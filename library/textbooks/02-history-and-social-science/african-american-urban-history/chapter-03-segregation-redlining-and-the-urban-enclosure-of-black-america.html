Part I — Migration, Segregation, and Urban Formation

Segregation, Redlining, and the Urban Enclosure of Black America

From Migration to Fragmentation: The Urban History of Black Street Organizations

Chapter Opening

"My father bought our house in 1953. The deed said we couldn't sell it to a Negro. The bank wouldn't have given us the loan if the block had a Negro on it. The federal government insured the mortgage. We called it the American Dream." — Composite account drawn from oral histories of white Chicago suburbanites, 1960s–1980s

In the summer of 1919, on a hot July afternoon at the 29th Street beach on Chicago's South Side, a Black teenager named Eugene Williams drifted on a raft across an invisible line in Lake Michigan — the informal color boundary that separated the "white" swimming area from the "Negro" one. A group of white men on the shore began throwing rocks. Williams, struck and disoriented, drowned. When Black beachgoers pointed out the man they held responsible to a white police officer, the officer refused to make an arrest. That refusal ignited five days of some of the worst racial violence in twentieth-century American urban history. Thirty-eight people died — twenty-three Black, fifteen white. More than five hundred were injured. Over a thousand Black families lost their homes to arson. The violence did not erupt from nowhere. It erupted from geography — from the desperate compression of Black Chicago into an overcrowded strip of South Side real estate from which every legal, financial, and extralegal mechanism available to white Chicago was being used to prevent escape.

The story of the Chicago Race Riot of 1919, sometimes called the Red Summer, is in one sense a story about violence, fear, and the immediate combustibility of a racially segregated city under pressure. But it is also, in a deeper sense, a story about how American cities were built — about the systems of law, finance, federal policy, real estate practice, and organized violence through which Black urban life was geographically bounded, economically constrained, and politically marginalized in every major American city during the first half of the twentieth century. That story is the subject of this chapter.

This chapter examines the mechanisms of residential segregation — the tools through which Black Americans who had migrated north and west in search of freedom and economic opportunity found themselves enclosed in specific urban territories, cut off from the housing markets, mortgage credit, and neighborhood resources available to white Americans. Those tools were not accidental. They were deliberate instruments of racial policy, developed and deployed by real estate boards, lending institutions, neighborhood associations, city governments, and most consequentially the federal government itself, which from the 1930s through the 1960s used its enormous financial power to build racial segregation into the very structure of the American housing market.

The instruments of this enclosure were varied and mutually reinforcing. Restrictive covenants embedded racial exclusion directly into property deeds, prohibiting Black buyers from purchasing homes in white neighborhoods and making that prohibition legally enforceable in court. Redlining — the practice of federal and private lending agencies of categorizing Black neighborhoods and neighborhoods adjacent to them as high-risk and therefore ineligible for mortgage insurance or conventional loans — cut Black families off from the credit they needed to buy or maintain homes, ensuring that the neighborhoods where they were permitted to live would deteriorate while the suburbs where they were not permitted to live received subsidized credit. Public housing was initially conceived as housing for the stable working class but was systematically steered toward Black neighborhoods, concentrated in the poorest areas of central cities, and allowed to deteriorate into conditions of extreme poverty and physical disrepair. White flight — the mass movement of white residents and businesses from central cities to subsidized suburbs — drained the tax base and economic vitality from Black urban neighborhoods while leaving Black residents behind in cities with shrinking resources and declining services.

The consequences of this systematic enclosure were not accidental byproducts of market forces or individual preferences. They were the predictable — indeed, intended — outcomes of policies designed by people who understood exactly what they were doing and why. When federal housing administrators classified neighborhoods by race to determine mortgage eligibility, they knew they were preventing Black families from building the housing wealth that was becoming the primary mechanism of middle-class asset accumulation for white Americans. When local real estate boards enforced racial exclusion through private practice and organized harassment, they knew they were maintaining property values in white neighborhoods by ensuring that Black families could not compete for housing in those areas. When city governments sited public housing projects in already-poor Black neighborhoods rather than distributing them across the metropolitan area, they knew they were concentrating poverty and racial segregation rather than dispersing it. The geography of American racial inequality that persists into the twenty-first century was built deliberately, piece by piece, decision by decision, through instruments of law and finance.

This chapter matters for the larger history this textbook traces because residential segregation is the geographic foundation on which all subsequent Black urban organizational life was built. The streets, blocks, neighborhoods, and housing projects that became the territorial basis of Black youth clubs, social organizations, and eventually street organizations in Chicago, Los Angeles, Philadelphia, and other cities were not random collections of geography. They were the compressed, enclosed, and deliberately impoverished territories created by fifty years of systematic residential segregation. To understand why certain neighborhoods produced certain kinds of organizational life — why territorial identity became so central to young Black urban men, why neighborhood boundaries carried such intense meaning, why the defense of neighborhood space felt so urgent — it is necessary to understand how those neighborhoods were created and by whom.

The South Side of Chicago's Black Belt is this chapter's primary case study, but the patterns it illustrates were reproduced in every major northern and western city with a significant Black population. Detroit's Paradise Valley and Black Bottom, Philadelphia's North and West Sides, Los Angeles's Central Avenue corridor, New York's Harlem and Bedford-Stuyvesant — all were products of the same systems of exclusion, the same federal and local policies, the same real estate practices, and the same organized resistance to Black freedom of movement in housing markets. Understanding those systems is the prerequisite for understanding what happened inside those neighborhoods, which is the story the remaining chapters of this book will tell.

A final note on the argument of this chapter: historians who study residential segregation have conclusively established that the segregation of American cities was not a natural outcome of economic forces, private preferences, or the simple clustering of ethnic groups with similar backgrounds and cultures. It was produced by specific policies, enforced by specific institutions, and maintained by specific acts of violence, legal exclusion, and financial discrimination. This distinction matters enormously. If racial residential segregation were a natural market outcome, then the poverty, disinvestment, and social disorganization of segregated Black neighborhoods would be the responsibility of the people who live in them. If residential segregation was produced by deliberate government and private policy, then the conditions of those neighborhoods are the responsibility of the institutions that created and maintained them. The evidence overwhelmingly supports the second conclusion. This chapter presents that evidence.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain what restrictive covenants were, how they functioned, and how they contributed to the racial segregation of American cities in the early and mid-twentieth century.
  2. Describe the federal government's Home Owners' Loan Corporation and Federal Housing Administration programs, and explain how redlining and mortgage discrimination systematically excluded Black Americans from federally subsidized housing credit.
  3. Analyze how public housing policy evolved from a program for the working class to a system of concentrated poverty and racial containment in American cities.
  4. Trace the process of white flight and suburbanization and explain how federal highway and mortgage policy subsidized the movement of white families to suburbs while trapping Black families in disinvesting cities.
  5. Identify the major mechanisms of neighborhood boundary maintenance — including blockbusting, steering, and organized violence — and explain how they extended and enforced residential segregation beyond formal legal instruments.
  6. Evaluate the argument that residential segregation was a deliberate policy outcome rather than a natural market result, using specific evidence from federal housing programs, real estate practices, and local ordinances.
  7. Compare the experiences of Black residents, white residents, real estate professionals, federal housing administrators, and local political officials in the development of residential segregation.
  8. Connect the geography of residential segregation to the later development of neighborhood-based street organizations, explaining why territorial identity became so central to Black urban youth in hypersegregated cities.
  9. Assess the long-term economic consequences of residential segregation, including the racial wealth gap, the deterioration of Black neighborhood infrastructure, and the perpetuation of concentrated poverty across generations.
  10. Interpret HOLC redlining maps, housing policy documents, and oral histories as historical evidence about the creation of racially segregated urban geography.
  11. Distinguish between de jure segregation (segregation enforced by law) and de facto segregation (segregation maintained by practice and custom), and explain why this distinction has been important both politically and historically.
  12. Contextualize the South Side Chicago Black Belt within the national pattern of hypersegregated Black urban neighborhoods produced by the same policies in every major American city.

Historical Context

To understand the residential segregation of northern and western American cities, it is necessary first to understand what Black migrants found when they arrived. Chapter 2 traced the Great Migration — the movement of millions of Black Southerners northward and westward between roughly 1910 and 1970 in search of industrial employment, physical safety, and escape from the Jim Crow South. That migration was driven by genuine push and pull factors: the violence and economic exploitation of the South, the industrial jobs and higher wages of the North. But the freedom that migrants sought was only partially available in their destinations. Northern cities were not the South, but they were racially organized in ways that channeled Black newcomers into specific neighborhoods, specific jobs, and specific schools — and then used the law, the financial system, and organized violence to keep them there.

The racial geography of early twentieth-century northern cities was not yet fully fixed when the Great Migration began in earnest around 1915. Black communities existed in most northern cities, but they were generally smaller and more dispersed than they would later become. The arrival of hundreds of thousands and eventually millions of Black migrants in a compressed period — Chicago's Black population grew from roughly 44,000 in 1910 to more than 230,000 by 1930, and to more than 800,000 by 1960 — created enormous pressure on existing Black neighborhoods and provoked a powerful white reaction aimed at containing that growth within clearly defined geographic limits.

That reaction operated through multiple channels simultaneously. White neighborhood associations organized to resist Black entry through violence and intimidation. Real estate professionals developed and enforced industry-wide codes that prohibited selling or renting to Black buyers in white neighborhoods. Property developers and sellers attached restrictive covenants to deeds that prohibited resale to Black buyers, making the racial exclusion of neighborhoods a contractual matter enforceable in court. Local governments used zoning power to concentrate undesirable land uses — industrial facilities, transit depots, commercial strips — in or adjacent to Black neighborhoods. And most consequentially, the federal government intervened massively in the housing market beginning in the 1930s in ways that simultaneously subsidized white suburbanization and denied Black families access to that subsidy.

The New Deal housing programs — particularly the Home Owners' Loan Corporation (HOLC), established in 1933, and the Federal Housing Administration (FHA), established in 1934 — transformed the American housing market. Before these programs, most Americans rented rather than owned their homes, and those who did buy typically used short-term balloon mortgages that required lump-sum repayment and exposed homeowners to the risk of loss during economic downturns. HOLC and FHA introduced and popularized the long-term, self-amortizing mortgage — the thirty-year fixed-rate loan with modest down payments — that made homeownership accessible to the broad working and middle class. This transformation was one of the most consequential economic policy interventions in American history, and its benefits were enormous — for the white Americans who received them.

The mechanism of racial exclusion built into these federal programs was systematic and explicit. HOLC developed a neighborhood rating system — the colored maps that would later give "redlining" its name — that classified neighborhoods from A (green, best, lowest risk) to D (red, hazardous, highest risk). The criteria for these ratings embedded racial composition directly into credit risk assessment: any neighborhood with Black residents, or any neighborhood adjacent to a Black neighborhood, was rated C or D, meaning it was ineligible for favorable mortgage terms or federal insurance. The FHA adopted and extended this system, refusing to insure mortgages in redlined neighborhoods and actively encouraging racial segregation in its underwriting guidelines. The FHA's Underwriting Manual, which guided mortgage lending decisions for decades, explicitly stated that neighborhood value was undermined by "inharmonious racial or nationality groups" and recommended that neighborhoods maintain racial homogeneity as a condition of receiving federal insurance.

These were not the informal preferences of low-level bureaucrats. They were the explicit, written, officially sanctioned policies of the federal agencies responsible for building the modern American housing market. They meant that Black families were systematically denied access to the federally subsidized mortgage credit that was making homeownership — and with it, the wealth accumulation that homeownership enabled — accessible to white working- and middle-class families. A white family that bought a home in a federally insured subdivision in the 1940s or 1950s was purchasing an asset that would appreciate in value over the following decades, building wealth that could be passed to children and grandchildren. A Black family that was denied access to that same mortgage credit was excluded from that wealth-building mechanism, consigned instead to the rental housing or the predatory contract buying arrangements that prevailed in redlined neighborhoods.

The physical consequences of redlining for Black neighborhoods were devastating and cumulative. Without access to conventional mortgage financing, Black homeowners could not easily buy, sell, or refinance their properties. Without mortgage financing, real estate values in redlined neighborhoods stagnated or declined. Without rising property values, the property tax base that funded public services — schools, parks, libraries, street maintenance — eroded. Landlords who owned properties in redlined neighborhoods found that they could not borrow against those properties for maintenance or renovation, so they deferred maintenance and extracted whatever income they could from tenants who had no alternatives in the housing market. Buildings deteriorated. Services declined. The physical infrastructure of Black neighborhoods was condemned to progressive decay by the same policy decisions that were simultaneously building and subsidizing white suburban neighborhoods with new schools, new streets, new parks, and new public facilities.

Public housing, which might in principle have been used as a mechanism for dispersing Black urban poverty and providing decent housing across the metropolitan area, was instead used to concentrate it. The placement of large public housing projects in already-poor Black neighborhoods — Chicago's Robert Taylor Homes and Cabrini-Green, New York's Queensbridge and Marcy projects, Philadelphia's Richard Allen and Tasker developments — was not accidental. It was the product of deliberate decisions by local housing authorities and city governments that responded to white political pressure to keep public housing — and the Black families who would disproportionately live in it — away from white neighborhoods. The result was the construction of massive concentrations of poverty, physically isolated from the rest of the city, in neighborhoods that were already underserved and underinvested.

White flight — the mass movement of white residents from central cities to surrounding suburbs — accelerated from the late 1940s through the 1970s, driven by a combination of real estate industry practices, federal highway policy, school desegregation pressures, and the genuine desire of many white families for suburban homeownership. The federal interstate highway system, authorized in 1956, destroyed Black neighborhoods as effectively as any single policy of the era, with highways routed through established Black communities in city after city, displacing hundreds of thousands of residents and severing the street-level social fabric of neighborhoods that had taken decades to build. White suburbs, shielded by restrictive covenants and exclusionary zoning, remained effectively off-limits to Black families even as the cities that Black families remained in lost their tax base, their industries, and their political power.

This chapter examines all of these processes in detail, using the South Side of Chicago's Black Belt as the primary case study while drawing on evidence from other cities to establish the national character of these patterns. The case of Chicago is particularly well-documented — the city's aggressive racial housing policies, its dramatic demographic transformation, and the scholarly attention it has received from researchers like Arnold Hirsch, Thomas Sugrue, Robert Sampson, and Richard Rothstein make it an unusually rich source of evidence for the arguments this chapter advances. But Chicago is not unique. The story it tells is the story of American cities.

Major People, Groups, and Places

Reference — People, Groups & Places

Home Owners' Loan Corporation (HOLC)
The federal agency established in 1933 to refinance home mortgages at risk of foreclosure during the Depression. HOLC developed the neighborhood appraisal system that produced the color-coded maps now known as redlining maps. These maps rated neighborhoods from A (green) to D (red) based on criteria that explicitly incorporated racial composition, effectively denying favorable mortgage terms to any neighborhood with Black residents or near a Black neighborhood.
Federal Housing Administration (FHA)
The federal agency established in 1934 to insure residential mortgages and stimulate the housing market. The FHA adopted and extended HOLC's racial rating system, refusing to insure mortgages in redlined neighborhoods and actively promoting racial segregation in its underwriting guidelines until 1968. The FHA's mortgage insurance program was the primary mechanism through which federally subsidized homeownership was made available to white Americans and denied to Black Americans.
Veterans Administration (VA)
The federal agency whose GI Bill mortgage guarantee program (Servicemen's Readjustment Act of 1944) extended homeownership subsidies to returning World War II veterans. Like the FHA, VA mortgage guarantees were administered in ways that effectively excluded Black veterans from the program's benefits, denying Black veterans access to the suburban homeownership and wealth accumulation that the GI Bill made available to their white counterparts.
Robert C. Weaver (1907–1997)
An African American economist and public official who served as the first director of the Housing and Home Finance Agency and later became the first Black cabinet member in American history as Secretary of Housing and Urban Development under President Lyndon Johnson. Weaver was an important critic of racial discrimination in federal housing policy and an advocate for fair housing enforcement, though the institutional and political forces sustaining segregation proved formidable obstacles throughout his career.
Elizabeth Wood
The progressive director of the Chicago Housing Authority from 1937 to 1954, who attempted to use public housing as a tool of racial integration and neighborhood stabilization but was repeatedly overruled by the Chicago City Council, which insisted on siting public housing projects exclusively in Black neighborhoods. Wood's tenure and her eventual firing illustrate the political dynamics through which local power overrode reformist impulses in public housing policy.
Richard J. Daley (1902–1976)
The powerful Democratic mayor of Chicago from 1955 to 1976, whose political machine represented and responded to the interests of white ethnic neighborhoods opposed to racial integration. Under Daley, Chicago's public housing was concentrated in Black neighborhoods, the city's schools remained segregated, and urban renewal programs were used as tools of Black displacement. Daley's Chicago represents the model of the liberal northern Democratic machine that simultaneously depended on Black votes and sustained racial segregation.
The Chicago Race Riot of 1919
Five days of racial violence in July 1919 that killed thirty-eight people and injured more than five hundred, triggered by the drowning death of Black teenager Eugene Williams at a racially segregated beach. The riot was the product of housing compression and territorial racial conflict on Chicago's South Side, and it led to the formation of a Chicago Commission on Race Relations whose 1922 report documented the role of housing segregation in producing racial conflict.
The Black Belt / Bronzeville (South Side Chicago)
The strip of South Side Chicago real estate into which Black Chicago was compressed by restrictive covenants and housing discrimination. By the 1940s, this area — sometimes called the "Black Metropolis" — housed more than 300,000 people in conditions of extreme overcrowding, with population densities far exceeding those of comparable white neighborhoods. Bronzeville was simultaneously a zone of remarkable Black cultural and institutional achievement and a site of profound poverty and physical deterioration produced by deliberate disinvestment.
Cabrini-Green (Chicago)
A massive public housing complex on Chicago's North Side, built in stages from 1942 to 1962, that at its peak housed approximately 15,000 residents in towers and row houses concentrated in a small geographic area. Cabrini-Green became internationally known as a symbol of failed public housing policy, with high rates of violent crime, deteriorating physical conditions, and chronically inadequate services. Its gradual demolition between 1995 and 2011 displaced thousands of residents and triggered intense debates about urban redevelopment and community stability.
Robert Taylor Homes (Chicago)
The largest public housing project ever built in the United States, consisting of twenty-eight sixteen-story towers constructed along South State Street on Chicago's South Side between 1962 and 1965. At its peak, Robert Taylor Homes housed nearly 27,000 people, overwhelmingly Black and overwhelmingly poor, in conditions of extreme social and physical isolation. The project's eventual demolition, completed in 2007, was accompanied by promises of mixed-income replacement housing that, for many former residents, were only partially fulfilled.
Shelley v. Kraemer (1948)
The Supreme Court case in which the Court unanimously ruled that racially restrictive covenants were unenforceable in court, holding that judicial enforcement of private racial discrimination violated the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. The decision did not make it illegal to include restrictive covenants in property deeds, only illegal for courts to enforce them. De facto racial segregation continued after Shelley through real estate industry practices, FHA policy, and organized white resistance.
Blockbusting
The real estate practice of inducing white homeowners to sell quickly and cheaply by warning them that Black families were moving into the neighborhood, then reselling or renting those properties to Black families at inflated prices. Blockbusting was enormously profitable for real estate speculators and contributed to the racial turnover of entire neighborhoods in a matter of years. It simultaneously accelerated white flight and exploited the exclusion of Black buyers from conventional mortgage markets.
Contract Buying
A predatory home financing arrangement common in redlined Black neighborhoods in which buyers paid installments to sellers but did not receive legal title to the property until the full purchase price was paid. If a buyer missed even one payment, they could lose the home and all prior payments with no legal recourse. Contract buying was widespread in cities like Chicago precisely because Black families were denied access to conventional mortgages in their neighborhoods; it transferred enormous sums from Black buyers to white sellers and speculators.
The Contract Buyers League (Chicago)
An organization formed in 1968 by Black homeowners in Chicago who had purchased their homes on contract and organized collectively to renegotiate or escape those exploitative arrangements. The Contract Buyers League conducted rent strikes and legal actions that brought national attention to the practice and documented the mechanisms through which financial exclusion translated into predatory extraction from Black families.
Detroit's Paradise Valley and Black Bottom
The primary Black neighborhoods of mid-century Detroit, home to a thriving Black cultural and commercial life along Hastings Street before urban renewal and highway construction destroyed them in the 1950s and 1960s. The construction of I-375 and the Chrysler Freeway through Black Bottom displaced approximately 150,000 Detroit residents, the vast majority of them Black, and destroyed one of the most economically vital Black commercial districts in the country. Detroit's transformation represents the pattern of urban renewal as Black removal.
Fair Housing Act of 1968
The federal law that prohibited racial discrimination in the sale, rental, and financing of housing. Passed in the week following Martin Luther King Jr.'s assassination, the Fair Housing Act provided legal tools against housing discrimination that had previously had no federal remedy. The Act's passage did not immediately end residential segregation, which continued through practices that were harder to detect and prove than overt discrimination, and through the accumulated structural effects of decades of explicitly discriminatory policy.

Section 3.1

Restrictive Covenants: Law as a Tool of Racial Enclosure

Origins and Spread of Racial Covenants

The restrictive covenant was a legal instrument — a clause attached to a property deed — that prohibited the future sale, lease, or occupancy of the property by members of specified racial or ethnic groups, almost always including Black people and often also including Jews, Asian Americans, and other groups that white property owners and developers sought to exclude. Restrictive covenants had existed in various forms in American real estate practice since the late nineteenth century, but they became the dominant mechanism of residential racial exclusion in northern cities following the Supreme Court's 1917 decision in Buchanan v. Warley, which struck down municipal racial zoning ordinances as unconstitutional violations of property rights.

The Buchanan decision was, in a formal sense, a civil rights victory: it prevented cities from explicitly designating neighborhoods by race in their zoning codes. But its practical effect was to accelerate the development of private alternatives to racial zoning that could accomplish the same ends without triggering constitutional scrutiny. The real estate industry, working through organizations like the National Association of Real Estate Boards, developed and promoted restrictive covenant agreements that could cover entire subdivisions or neighborhoods when signed by a sufficient proportion of property owners. These agreements were recorded in property records and were binding on all subsequent owners of any covered property for terms that typically ran from twenty to thirty years, renewable by re-recording.

The spread of restrictive covenants across northern cities in the 1920s was rapid and comprehensive. By the late 1940s, restrictive covenants covered the majority of residential land in many cities. A 1940 survey found that covenants covered approximately 80 percent of the residential real estate in Los Angeles and Chicago. In Chicago's suburban Cook County, covenant coverage was so extensive that a Black family attempting to buy a home in any of dozens of suburban municipalities would find virtually every property in the market contractually prohibited from sale to them. The covenants did not merely prevent individual transactions; they created racial maps of metropolitan areas in which the boundaries between "white" and "Negro" neighborhoods were written into property law itself.

The enforcement of restrictive covenants was a cooperative project involving real estate professionals, local courts, and neighborhood associations. When a Black family attempted to purchase a covenanted property — sometimes because the covenant was not disclosed to them, sometimes through explicit challenge — white neighbors could file suit to enforce the covenant, seeking an injunction that would prevent the Black family from taking possession or occupying the home. These suits were routinely successful in the courts of states with significant Black populations, including Illinois, Michigan, Ohio, and California. The willingness of courts to enforce racial exclusion through injunction gave the private covenant the coercive force of the legal system.

The social enforcement of covenant boundaries was equally important as the legal. White neighborhood improvement associations — organizations that proliferated across northern cities in the 1920s and 1930s specifically to maintain racial homogeneity — organized systematic harassment campaigns against Black families who attempted to move into restricted neighborhoods. Harassment ranged from intimidating visits and hostile demonstrations to outright violence: bombings of homes, arson, physical assault against families and their property. In Chicago alone, between 1917 and 1921, fifty-eight bombs were thrown at homes of Black families who moved into white neighborhoods or at the offices of real estate agents who facilitated such moves. The violence was rarely prosecuted. The message it sent was clear.

The Mechanics of Exclusion

Understanding how restrictive covenants functioned in practice requires attention to the real estate industry's role in their creation and enforcement. The National Association of Real Estate Boards — the national trade organization of real estate professionals — included in its code of ethics a provision that explicitly prohibited members from introducing into a neighborhood "members of any race or nationality" who would undermine property values. This provision, which remained in the National Association's code until 1950, made racial exclusion a professional obligation rather than a personal preference. Real estate agents who facilitated the sale of homes in white neighborhoods to Black buyers risked their licenses, their professional standing, and their livelihoods.

The practice known as "steering" — directing prospective homebuyers toward or away from specific neighborhoods based on their race — was the industry's standard operating procedure. A Black family seeking to buy a home in a northern city would be systematically shown only properties in or adjacent to established Black neighborhoods, regardless of their income, creditworthiness, or stated preferences. A white family seeking a home in the same price range would be shown properties across a much wider geographic area. Steering was so routine and so thoroughgoing that it effectively duplicated the effects of formal legal segregation without requiring any explicit legal instruction. Black families were not told they were being excluded; they were simply presented with a menu of options from which the excluded areas were missing.

The legal landscape changed dramatically with the Supreme Court's 1948 decision in Shelley v. Kraemer, in which the Court unanimously held that judicial enforcement of racially restrictive covenants violated the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. The Court's reasoning was careful: the covenants themselves were private contracts, not state action, and the Fourteenth Amendment applied only to state action. But when courts enforced those private contracts — when a judge issued an injunction preventing a Black family from occupying a covenanted home — that judicial action constituted state action in support of racial discrimination, which the Equal Protection Clause prohibited. The Shelley decision was a genuine legal landmark, and it eliminated the most direct form of legal enforcement of restrictive covenants.

But the practical consequences of Shelley were far more limited than its legal scope. The decision did not make it illegal to include racial restrictions in deeds; it only made such restrictions judicially unenforceable. More importantly, the systems of racial exclusion that covenants had formalized continued through the informal practices of the real estate industry — steering, blockbusting, steering through lending institutions — and through the far more consequential federal mortgage insurance and public housing programs that had been operating in parallel with covenants for fifteen years and that continued operating after Shelley without any modification of their racial practices. The formal legal instrument of the covenant was struck down while the systems of racial exclusion it had supported continued largely intact through other mechanisms.

Historical Vocabulary
Restrictive Covenant

A restrictive covenant is a clause in a property deed that places legally binding restrictions on how the property may be used or to whom it may be sold or rented. Racial restrictive covenants — the form relevant to this chapter — prohibited property owners from selling, renting, or leasing to members of specified racial groups, almost universally including Black people, and often also Jewish people, Asian Americans, and others.

Racial restrictive covenants were the primary legal mechanism of residential racial exclusion in northern and western American cities from the 1920s through the 1940s. They were attached to individual property deeds but could be made to cover entire subdivisions or neighborhoods when a sufficient number of property owners signed a neighborhood-wide covenant agreement. Once recorded in property records, these covenants were binding on all subsequent owners for the specified term, typically twenty to thirty years.

The Supreme Court's 1948 decision in Shelley v. Kraemer held that courts could not enforce racial restrictive covenants without violating the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. While this decision eliminated judicial enforcement of covenants, it did not eliminate the covenants themselves — many remained in recorded property documents for decades afterward, and many still exist in property records today, though they have no legal effect.

The historical significance of restrictive covenants extends beyond their direct legal effect. By covering large proportions of residential real estate in northern cities during the critical period when millions of Black migrants were arriving and seeking housing, covenants helped create and formalize the racial geography of American cities. The neighborhoods designated as "Negro" by the interaction of covenant coverage and real estate steering became the targets of federal redlining, the sites of deteriorating public housing, and the territories that would form the geographic basis of Black urban community and organizational life throughout the mid-twentieth century.

Violence as Covenant Enforcement

The legal instrument of the restrictive covenant cannot be understood separately from the extralegal violence that accompanied and enforced it. In city after city, the color line in residential geography was maintained not only by the legal mechanisms of covenant enforcement and real estate steering but by the credible threat and frequent reality of violent attack on Black families who crossed it. The 1919 Chicago Race Riot was the most dramatic manifestation of this violence, but it was preceded and followed by a continuous pattern of individual attacks — bombings, burnings, mob assaults — that made the crossing of racial boundaries in housing genuinely dangerous for the Black families who attempted it.

What is most significant about this violence, from a historical standpoint, is not its frequency or severity — though both were considerable — but its impunity. White men who bombed the homes of Black families in Chicago's South Side in the early 1920s were almost never prosecuted. Police who stood by while white mobs attacked Black families who had moved into white neighborhoods were never disciplined. The consistent message of the legal system's non-response to anti-Black housing violence was that racial boundary maintenance was not merely tolerated but actively supported by the agents of public order. Violence was not a rogue alternative to the legal system's operation; it was complementary to it, accomplishing through terror what the courts and the real estate industry accomplished through contract and professional practice.

The violence directed at Black families in housing was also gendered and generational in ways that mattered for the longer history this textbook traces. The men who organized and carried out attacks on Black families who moved into white neighborhoods were frequently young white men acting in defense of what they understood as their neighborhoods — the turf of their families, their ethnic communities, their identity. The parallel between white ethnic neighborhood defense through violence and the forms of territorial defense that would later characterize Black urban organizational life is not coincidental. Territoriality, the defense of neighborhood as identity and belonging, was a principle that operated on both sides of the color line in American cities. The specific history of its development on the Black side — the forms it took, the institutions it produced, the violence it generated — cannot be understood without understanding the territorial violence to which Black communities were subjected and the geographic enclosure within which they were forced to build their lives.

Section 3.2

Redlining and Housing Policy: The Federal Architecture of Segregation

The New Deal Housing Programs

The federal government's intervention in the housing market during the New Deal transformed American housing in ways that cannot be overstated. Before the Depression, most American families rented their housing. Those who owned homes typically held them on short-term mortgages with large down payments and balloon repayments that exposed them to significant risk of foreclosure. When the Depression struck, foreclosure rates soared — in 1933, approximately one thousand homes per day were being foreclosed nationally — and the housing market essentially collapsed. The Roosevelt administration's response was to create the institutions that would make long-term, low-interest, government-backed mortgages available for the first time, transforming homeownership from a privilege of the prosperous to an aspiration accessible to the broad working and middle class.

The Home Owners' Loan Corporation, established in 1933, was the first major instrument of this transformation. HOLC refinanced approximately one million mortgages threatened by foreclosure during its first years of operation, replacing short-term balloon loans with long-term self-amortizing mortgages — loans that were paid off in regular installments over fifteen to twenty years, with no balloon payment at the end. HOLC also developed the neighborhood appraisal system that produced the colored maps for which it is now best known. To assess the risk of the mortgages it was refinancing, HOLC assigned every neighborhood in every major American city a grade: A (green), B (blue), C (yellow), or D (red). The grade reflected HOLC's assessment of the neighborhood's stability, its residents' creditworthiness, and its likely future value. The criteria for these ratings explicitly incorporated racial composition as a determinant of credit risk.

HOLC's appraisal guidelines stated that neighborhoods with Black residents were inherently higher risk than comparable white neighborhoods, and that proximity to a Black neighborhood substantially reduced an area's value and stability. Neighborhoods that were entirely or predominantly Black received D (red) ratings regardless of their actual physical condition, the income levels of their residents, or their historical stability. Neighborhoods that bordered Black neighborhoods — even if they were entirely white — typically received C ratings that made them ineligible for favorable mortgage terms. The practical effect was that HOLC's appraisal system mapped racial composition directly onto credit risk, encoding the assumption that Black presence inherently devalued neighborhoods into the federal government's formal assessment system.

The Federal Housing Administration, established in 1934, took the HOLC rating system and embedded it into its mortgage insurance underwriting guidelines, which then governed the entire residential mortgage market. The FHA did not make mortgage loans directly; instead, it insured private lenders against losses on qualifying mortgages, dramatically reducing the risk to lenders and making them willing to extend the long-term, low-down-payment loans that HOLC had pioneered. Because FHA insurance was the key to the low-cost mortgage market, FHA's underwriting standards effectively determined which properties and which neighborhoods could receive financing. Properties in D-rated (redlined) neighborhoods could not receive FHA insurance. Lenders who refused to lend in uninsured areas — as virtually all did — effectively withdrew from redlined neighborhoods entirely.

The FHA's Underwriting Manual, which guided mortgage lending decisions from the 1930s through at least the late 1960s, made the agency's racial assumptions explicit. The Manual stated that mortgage risk was increased by "infiltration of inharmonious racial or nationality groups" and recommended that neighborhoods maintain racial homogeneity through the use of "deed restrictions" (i.e., restrictive covenants) as a condition of receiving favorable insurance ratings. The FHA actively promoted the use of restrictive covenants in subdivisions it was insuring, effectively requiring developers who wanted FHA backing to exclude Black buyers. The federal government was not merely complicit in private racial discrimination; it was actively promoting and requiring that discrimination as a condition of receiving federal subsidy.

Historical Vocabulary
Redlining

Redlining refers to the practice of designating certain neighborhoods as high-risk and therefore ineligible for mortgage loans, insurance, or other financial services, based on the racial composition of those neighborhoods rather than the actual creditworthiness of individual borrowers or the physical condition of individual properties. The term derives from the practice of the Home Owners' Loan Corporation, which literally drew red lines on neighborhood maps to indicate areas rated "hazardous" for mortgage lending — areas that were invariably Black or adjacent to Black neighborhoods.

Redlining operated at two levels: federal and private. At the federal level, HOLC and FHA created and used neighborhood rating systems that incorporated racial composition as a determinant of mortgage eligibility, effectively denying federally subsidized mortgage credit to Black neighborhoods and to neighborhoods adjacent to them. At the private level, banks, savings institutions, and insurance companies adopted the same neighborhood maps in making their own lending and insurance decisions, either because they used HOLC ratings directly or because they independently arrived at the same racially exclusionary logic.

The consequences of redlining were both immediate and long-term. In the immediate term, redlining prevented Black families from accessing the mortgage credit they needed to buy, maintain, or improve homes in their neighborhoods, limiting their housing choices to renting or to the predatory financing arrangements — particularly contract buying — that filled the void left by conventional lending. In the long term, redlining caused the systematic deterioration of Black neighborhoods, as properties could neither be financed for purchase nor borrowed against for maintenance, leading to deferred maintenance, building deterioration, and declining property values that then confirmed the "hazardous" rating the HOLC had originally assigned.

Redlining was not formally prohibited until the Home Mortgage Disclosure Act of 1975 and the Community Reinvestment Act of 1977, both of which were responses to documentation that redlining had persisted long after the formal Fair Housing Act of 1968. Modern versions of redlining — sometimes called "reverse redlining" — involve targeting minority communities for predatory high-cost loans rather than excluding them from lending entirely, a pattern documented in the subprime mortgage crisis of 2007–2008.

The GI Bill and the Racial Wealth Gap

The Servicemen's Readjustment Act of 1944 — popularly known as the GI Bill — is rightly celebrated as one of the most consequential pieces of social legislation in American history. By providing returning World War II veterans with college tuition, job training, unemployment insurance, and most importantly low-interest government-backed mortgages, the GI Bill enabled millions of Americans to achieve educational and economic advancement that would otherwise have been impossible. The postwar suburban housing boom — Levittown, the proliferation of tract housing developments across the country, the mass conversion of America from a nation of renters to a nation of homeowners — was built substantially on GI Bill mortgage guarantees.

Black veterans served in the same war, in the same theaters, under the same conditions — often in segregated units that subjected them to additional indignity and hardship. They returned to the same country and were technically eligible for the same benefits. But in practice, Black veterans were systematically excluded from the GI Bill's housing benefits through the same mechanisms that excluded Black civilians from FHA loans: VA mortgage guarantees, like FHA insurance, were effectively unavailable for properties in redlined neighborhoods, and Black veterans who attempted to buy homes in white neighborhoods were turned away by the same combination of real estate steering, lender discrimination, and organized resistance that turned away Black civilians.

The consequences of this differential access to the GI Bill's housing benefits were profound and lasting. A white veteran who bought a home in a Long Island Levittown in 1948 for approximately $8,000, with a VA-guaranteed mortgage at a low interest rate, was purchasing an asset that would appreciate enormously over the following decades. The same Levittown home was worth approximately $100,000 by the 1980s. The wealth accumulated through that appreciation could fund children's college educations, provide capital for small business investment, or serve as a safety net against economic adversity. A Black veteran denied access to that same mortgage guarantee and forced to rent in a redlined neighborhood, or to buy through a predatory contract-buying arrangement, accumulated none of that wealth. The racial wealth gap that persists into the twenty-first century — the approximately ten-to-one ratio of white to Black median family wealth — was built in substantial part by this differential access to federally subsidized housing credit in the postwar decades.

Contract Buying and Predatory Extraction

The exclusion of Black families from conventional mortgage credit did not prevent Black families from wanting to own homes. It redirected their desire for homeownership into alternative arrangements that were, in almost every case, more exploitative and less wealth-building than conventional mortgages. The most widespread of these alternatives was contract buying — an arrangement in which a buyer paid installments to the seller, similar to a mortgage payment, but did not receive legal title to the property until the final payment was made. If the buyer missed even one payment, or violated any term of the contract, the seller could reclaim the property and retain all prior payments with no legal recourse for the buyer.

Contract buying was the dominant form of homeownership in Chicago's Black Belt and in similar Black neighborhoods in other cities from the 1940s through the 1960s, not because Black buyers preferred it but because they had no alternative. Banks would not lend in redlined neighborhoods. FHA would not insure mortgages there. Conventional homeownership was not available. Into this gap stepped real estate speculators — primarily white men, many of whom had obtained their properties cheaply from white families fleeing rapidly integrating neighborhoods — who would sell to Black families on contract at inflated prices, knowing that any default would return the property to them with all prior payments retained.

The sociologist Beryl Satter's family history research, published in Family Properties (2009), documented in extraordinary detail how contract buying worked in Chicago. A property might have a market value of $12,000. A white buyer with access to conventional financing could purchase it with a $1,200 down payment and monthly mortgage payments of roughly $65, building equity with every payment. A Black buyer offered the same property on contract might pay $27,000 total, with a $1,500 down payment and monthly payments of $180, paying nearly three times as much for the same property while building no equity for the duration of the contract. If the Black buyer missed a single payment after years of faithful installments, the speculator could reclaim the property. Over the period from roughly 1945 to 1968, Satter's research found that speculators extracted billions of dollars from Black Chicago families through these arrangements — a massive transfer of wealth from Black buyers to white sellers enabled by and inseparable from federal redlining policy.

The Contract Buyers League, formed in Chicago in 1968, organized Black homebuyers to collectively resist these arrangements through rent strikes and legal action. Their organizing documented the mechanisms of contract buying exploitation with precision and brought national attention to the practice. But the structural conditions that had made contract buying necessary — the exclusion of Black neighborhoods from conventional mortgage credit — were not substantially changed by their organizing alone. It would take the Fair Housing Act of 1968 and subsequent community reinvestment legislation to begin formally addressing the financial exclusion of Black neighborhoods, and those measures proved only partially effective in the face of the accumulated structural effects of three decades of deliberate exclusion.

HOLC Residential Security Maps and the Language of Racial Appraisal

Creator: Home Owners' Loan Corporation  ·  Dates: 1935–1940  ·  Type: Federal agency maps and appraisal documents

Context: Between 1935 and 1940, the Home Owners' Loan Corporation produced residential security maps for 239 American cities. These maps color-coded neighborhoods from green (A, best) to red (D, hazardous) based on assessments developed by HOLC field agents in consultation with local lenders, real estate professionals, and city officials. The maps were used internally by HOLC and were shared with the FHA, which used them as guides in its own underwriting decisions. The maps remained largely unknown to the public until historian Kenneth Jackson documented their existence and racial character in his 1985 book Crabgrass Frontier. They have since been digitized and made publicly accessible through the Mapping Inequality project.

What the documents reveal: The area description forms that accompanied HOLC maps recorded the assessments of local field agents for each neighborhood. These forms asked about population demographics, employment, property conditions, and other factors — and explicitly asked about the "racial and foreign-born" composition of the neighborhood. The language used in these forms reveals how racial composition was integrated into credit risk assessment. A typical D-grade area description might note that the area was "entirely [N]egro" or had been "infiltrated" by Black residents, and would rate it as declining or static in value for reasons that were explicitly linked to racial composition rather than to physical conditions or economic fundamentals.

From a HOLC area description form for a Chicago neighborhood (paraphrased from historical documentation): "This area is definitely undesirable from a mortgage standpoint, being entirely occupied by colored people. Properties are deteriorating, rents are declining, and no improvement is in prospect. Hazardous."

What the maps show spatially: When HOLC maps for Chicago, Detroit, Philadelphia, Los Angeles, and other cities are examined, a consistent spatial pattern emerges: the areas shaded in red (D) and much of the yellow (C) correspond precisely to the areas where Black residents lived. Green (A) areas are exclusively white, typically new suburban subdivisions. The maps literally drew racial geography onto financial geography, making the two inseparable in federal policy.

Limits of the source: The HOLC maps were internal federal documents not disclosed to the public until decades after their creation. The people most directly harmed by them — Black homebuyers and renters in redlined neighborhoods — did not know they existed. The maps also do not fully document all the mechanisms through which racial segregation was maintained; they are evidence of one federal instrument among several that operated simultaneously.

Section 3.3

Public Housing and the Concentration of Poverty

The Promise and Betrayal of Public Housing

Public housing in the United States began as something genuinely different from what it became. The Housing Act of 1937, which created the federal public housing program, was conceived by its progressive architects as housing for the stable working class — people who were employed but earning wages insufficient to afford decent private-market housing. The program was explicitly not intended to be charity housing for the very poorest; income limits excluded those whose incomes were too low as well as those whose incomes were too high. In cities where public housing projects were built before the war — Chicago's Jane Addams Houses and Ida B. Wells Homes, New York's Queensbridge and Harlem River Houses — the initial tenants were predominantly working families, many of them white, with stable employment and clear connections to the housing projects' surrounding neighborhoods.

The racial geography of early public housing was complicated and varied. Some early projects in northern cities were racially integrated, at least in principle. Others were explicitly segregated. In Chicago, the public housing authority maintained separate projects for white and Black tenants, a practice that continued into the postwar period even as the demand for Black housing overwhelmed the supply of Black public housing units. Elizabeth Wood, the progressive CHA director who served from 1937 to 1954, attempted repeatedly to use public housing as a tool of racial integration — siting projects in white neighborhoods, integrating tenant assignments — and was repeatedly blocked by white aldermen who threatened to veto any project not placed in areas already identified as Black.

The political process through which public housing sites were selected in Chicago was devastatingly documented in Martin Meyerson and Edward Banfield's 1955 study Politics, Planning, and the Public Interest. Their research showed that every proposed public housing site in a white neighborhood was blocked by the alderman representing that ward, while sites in Black neighborhoods or in areas undergoing racial transition were approved. The result was that public housing was systematically concentrated in the poorest, most deteriorated sections of the Black Belt, reinforcing rather than dispersing the concentration of poverty that redlining and restrictive covenants had already created.

The massive postwar public housing construction that followed the Housing Act of 1949 — which authorized 810,000 new public housing units nationally — accelerated and intensified this pattern. The high-rise towers that came to define American public housing in the 1950s and 1960s were partly the product of technological enthusiasm and partly of cost-cutting, but they were also the product of deliberate decisions to maximize the number of low-income Black families that could be concentrated in the smallest possible land area in already-Black neighborhoods. Building towers rather than row houses or low-rise apartments allowed housing authorities to house thousands of families on a few blocks without expanding the geographic footprint of public housing into white areas.

Design, Management, and the Deterioration of High-Rise Public Housing

The high-rise public housing projects built in Black neighborhoods in the 1950s and 1960s were, almost from the beginning, structurally problematic as living environments. The design of buildings like Chicago's Robert Taylor Homes — sixteen-story towers with eight families per floor, sharing a single elevator lobby and a single stairwell — created what urban planners would later describe as "defensible space" failures: large, anonymized common areas with no natural surveillance, where strangers could not be identified and crime was difficult to prevent or witness. The towers concentrated far too many families — particularly families with children — in vertical environments designed for density rather than community, with no front stoops, few usable outdoor spaces, and long internal corridors that generated anxiety rather than sociability.

Management failures compounded design failures. The Chicago Housing Authority, like most urban housing authorities, was chronically underfunded and politically marginalized. Maintenance was deferred. Elevators broke down and were not repaired for weeks or months, forcing elderly residents and mothers with young children to climb sixteen floors of stairs. Building systems — heating, plumbing, electrical — were not maintained adequately. The CHA's tenant screening policies changed over time in ways that shifted the population of public housing away from the working families for whom it was originally intended and toward the very poor: families with no other housing options, families with histories of housing instability, families with minimal income from any source. As the private housing market gradually became somewhat more accessible to Black working families in the 1960s and 1970s — partially through fair housing enforcement and the growth of the Black middle class — those families left public housing for private rentals and homeownership, leaving behind a concentrated population of the very poor in buildings whose physical conditions were deteriorating rapidly.

The social consequences of this concentration were devastating and well-documented. High concentrations of poverty — geographically defined areas where the vast majority of residents are very poor — produce conditions qualitatively different from ordinary urban poverty, including higher rates of violent crime, lower rates of school completion, fewer neighborhood social networks connecting residents to employment opportunities, and higher rates of depression, trauma, and chronic illness. Sociologist William Julius Wilson's landmark research in The Truly Disadvantaged (1987) documented how the simultaneous concentration of poverty in public housing and surrounding Black neighborhoods, combined with the departure of the Black middle class from those neighborhoods, created a form of social isolation that limited residents' access to the social networks, role models, and mainstream institutions through which economic mobility normally operated.

The experience of growing up in or adjacent to Chicago's high-rise public housing projects in the 1970s and 1980s — the period when the most intense gang formation and territorial conflict of the post-migration era was occurring — was profoundly shaped by the physical and social conditions those projects created. The towers were territories unto themselves, and within them and among them the street organizations that would define Chicago's street landscape for decades were organized, recruited, and deployed. The relationship between public housing design and the organizational forms of street groups was not incidental: the physical structure of the projects — towers clustered in specific geographic areas, with specific stairwells and floors controlled by specific groups — directly shaped how territorial identity was experienced and expressed within them. The buildings were, in a meaningful sense, the geographic units of gang territory in ways that had no parallel in low-rise or dispersed housing settings.

Urban Renewal as Black Removal

The Housing Act of 1949 that authorized the postwar public housing construction boom also contained a separate program — urban redevelopment, quickly renamed "urban renewal" — that proved equally consequential for Black urban communities, and even more directly destructive of the neighborhood social fabric. Urban renewal authorized federal grants to local governments for the purchase and clearance of "slum" areas deemed substandard, with the cleared land to be used for new housing or other development. The program's liberal architects imagined it as a tool for replacing deteriorated housing with decent new housing for the people who had lived in the cleared areas. What it produced in practice was something quite different.

The designation of "slum" areas subject to urban renewal clearance followed the racial geography of American cities with remarkable consistency. In city after city, the neighborhoods designated for clearance were established Black communities — not necessarily the most deteriorated areas of the city, but the areas where Black residents lived and where local governments and real estate interests saw opportunity for profitable redevelopment. The cleared land was then redeveloped not for the people who had lived there — who were displaced, their homes destroyed, their neighborhoods scattered — but for universities, hospitals, cultural institutions, highways, or upscale housing for white residents. James Baldwin's famous remark that "urban renewal means Negro removal" was not rhetorical exaggeration; it was an accurate description of the program's operation in most American cities.

Chicago's use of urban renewal to clear Black neighborhoods adjacent to the University of Chicago in Hyde Park produced a particularly well-documented case. The university, concerned about the deterioration of its surrounding neighborhood as Black residents moved in during the 1940s and 1950s, worked with the city to designate large areas of adjacent Black housing as "blighted" and subject to clearance. The cleared areas were redeveloped as parkland, university facilities, and upscale housing inaccessible to the Black families who had been displaced. This process, which concentrated enormous resources in the clearing and redevelopment of Black neighborhoods while providing displaced residents with inadequate relocation assistance and no guaranteed right to return, was replicated in form if not in detail in dozens of other cities.

The displacement produced by urban renewal compounded the displacement produced by highway construction. The interstate highway system, authorized in 1956, required the demolition of vast amounts of urban real estate to create the rights-of-way for multilane freeways through the urban core. In city after city, highway routes were selected that passed through established Black neighborhoods rather than through white neighborhoods or commercial districts, in part because land in Black neighborhoods was cheaper (a consequence of redlining), in part because Black residents had less political power to resist, and in part because local officials saw the highways as instruments for accomplishing Black neighborhood clearance that they could not accomplish as directly through other means. Detroit's Black Bottom neighborhood was destroyed by freeway construction in the 1950s. North Nashville's historically Black neighborhood was cut in half by I-40 in the 1960s. Rondo in St. Paul was divided by I-94. The pattern repeated in city after city, producing what urban scholar Robert Moses scholar Robert Caro called "the destruction of the community" as a deliberate byproduct of transportation policy.

Cause and Effect

The Making of Residential Segregation: Causes and Long-Term Consequences

Long-Term Causes

The legal and ideological legacy of slavery and Jim Crow; the racial ideology of white supremacy that identified Black presence as inherently threatening to property values and neighborhood stability; the economic logic of the real estate industry, which organized racial exclusion as a property value protection strategy; the political culture of white ethnic urban neighborhoods that treated racial homogeneity as a community right worth defending through violence.

Proximate and Policy Causes

The Great Migration creating housing demand pressure that triggered organized white resistance; the National Association of Real Estate Boards' professional code prohibiting racial integration of neighborhoods; the HOLC neighborhood rating system embedding racial composition into federal credit risk assessment; the FHA's explicit promotion of racial segregation through its underwriting guidelines; the political structure of city governments that gave white aldermen veto power over public housing siting in their wards.

The Development of Hypersegregation

By the 1950s and 1960s, most major American cities had developed levels of Black-white residential segregation that sociologists would later characterize as "hypersegregation" — segregation so extreme that the average Black resident lived in a neighborhood that was overwhelmingly Black and was physically, socially, and institutionally isolated from the mainstream of metropolitan life. Chicago's Black Belt, Detroit's East Side, Philadelphia's North Side, and comparable areas in dozens of other cities became the contexts within which all subsequent Black urban organizational life developed.

Long-Term Consequences

The racial wealth gap: differential access to housing wealth accumulation between white and Black families, producing the approximately ten-to-one white-to-Black median wealth ratio that persists today. Neighborhood disinvestment: the systematic deterioration of Black urban neighborhoods as a direct result of their exclusion from capital markets. School segregation: the coupling of school assignment to neighborhood of residence meant that residential segregation produced school segregation even in the formal absence of school segregation law. The territorial foundations of street organizations: the compressed, clearly bounded, deeply familiar neighborhood territories created by hypersegregation became the geographic basis for the territorial identity and organizational structure of Black urban youth groups.

Historical complexity: Residential segregation was not produced by any single policy or decision. It was the outcome of multiple mutually reinforcing instruments — private covenants, federal insurance policy, public housing siting, highway construction, real estate industry practices, organized violence — that operated simultaneously and whose effects compounded over time. Understanding this complexity is essential to understanding why segregation proved so durable even after the most overt legal instruments (covenants, explicit FHA redlining) were formally prohibited, and why residential segregation remains a central feature of American metropolitan geography today.

Section 3.4

White Flight and Suburbanization: The Draining of the City

The Mechanics of White Flight

White flight — the mass movement of white residents and businesses from central cities to surrounding suburbs — was one of the defining demographic processes of postwar American life. Between roughly 1945 and 1970, millions of white families left the cities in which they or their parents had grown up and settled in new suburban communities outside the city limits: Levittown on Long Island, the sprawling subdivisions of suburban Detroit and Chicago, the San Fernando Valley outside Los Angeles, the Main Line suburbs of Philadelphia. This movement was enormously consequential for American society in multiple respects: it transformed the physical landscape, the political map, and the racial and economic geography of the nation. For the Black urban communities left behind, white flight was not merely a demographic fact but a material catastrophe, as it drained the cities of the middle-class population and tax base on which urban services depended.

White flight was not a simple or spontaneous phenomenon. It was produced by the interaction of several forces operating simultaneously. Federal housing and transportation policy — particularly the FHA mortgage insurance program, which overwhelmingly subsidized suburban homeownership, and the interstate highway system, which made suburban commuting practical — created the material conditions for suburbanization. The real estate industry actively promoted white flight through the practice of blockbusting, which transformed the racial anxieties of white homeowners into a massive profit opportunity for speculators. Local governments used exclusionary zoning — minimum lot size requirements, prohibitions on multifamily housing, restrictions on commercial development — to ensure that suburban municipalities remained racially and economically homogeneous, accessible only to families who could afford the single-family homes that exclusionary zoning mandated.

Blockbusting deserves particular attention because it illustrates with unusual clarity how private profit motive intersected with racial fear and policy-induced segregation to produce the racial turnover of urban neighborhoods. Blockbusting typically began when a real estate speculator managed to sell or rent a property on a previously all-white block to a Black family — sometimes by purchasing a property secretly and then selling it to a Black buyer, sometimes by arranging for a Black family to occupy a rental temporarily. The speculator would then circulate door-to-door in the surrounding blocks, warning white homeowners that Black families were moving in and that property values would decline if they did not sell immediately. The white homeowners, conditioned by decades of FHA propaganda to believe that Black presence caused property value decline, would sell quickly and at below-market prices. The speculator would then resell or rent those properties to Black families — who were desperate for housing outside the overcrowded Black Belt — at above-market prices, making an enormous profit from the racial panic the speculator had deliberately engineered.

The net effect of blockbusting was neighborhood racial turnover that was rapid, complete, and enormously profitable for the speculators who orchestrated it. Neighborhoods that had been entirely white in the late 1940s became entirely Black by the early 1960s, as thousands of white families sold in panic and thousands of Black families paid inflated prices (typically through contract buying) for the properties they vacated. The white families moved to suburbs where restrictive covenants or exclusionary zoning prevented Black families from following. The Black families who had paid inflated prices for properties in transitioning neighborhoods found that those properties were located in redlined areas where further deterioration was guaranteed by the continued denial of mortgage capital.

The Urban Fiscal Crisis

The departure of white middle-class residents and businesses from central cities, combined with the continued exclusion of Black residents from suburban alternatives, produced what urban historians call the urban fiscal crisis — a self-reinforcing cycle of declining tax revenues, deteriorating services, business flight, and population loss that accelerated through the 1970s and reached crisis proportions in cities like New York, Detroit, Cleveland, and Philadelphia.

The mechanism of the fiscal crisis was straightforward. Local government services — schools, police, fire protection, sanitation, parks, libraries, street maintenance — are funded primarily through property taxes, which are based on assessed property values. When white middle-class residents and businesses left cities for suburbs, they took their tax payments with them. The properties they left behind were in redlined areas where values were declining, generating less tax revenue per property. The remaining residents — increasingly poor and Black, concentrated in deteriorating housing and public projects — required more services (in part because poverty generates need) but generated less tax revenue. The result was a fiscal squeeze in which cities attempted to maintain services for a growing poor population with a shrinking tax base, producing either service deterioration or tax increases that accelerated middle-class departure in a self-reinforcing downward spiral.

The effects of the fiscal crisis on Black urban neighborhoods were devastating and concrete. Schools in Black neighborhoods received less funding than suburban schools — sometimes dramatically less — resulting in deteriorating facilities, underpaid teachers, outdated materials, and overcrowded classrooms. Parks were not maintained. Streets were not repaired. Libraries were closed or had their hours cut. Police response times were longer and police service was more often experienced as harassment than protection. The physical environment of Black urban neighborhoods deteriorated visibly, not because of the failures of residents but because of the systematic withdrawal of public investment that was itself the product of the segregation policies that had created Black neighborhoods.

The suburban communities to which white families fled received the mirror image of this disinvestment. New schools were built with state-of-the-art facilities. Parks were well-maintained. Streets were smoothly paved. Police provided protection rather than surveillance. The property values of suburban homes, insulated from the racial anxieties that drove blockbusting in transitional urban neighborhoods, appreciated steadily. The contrast between the declining public environments of Black urban neighborhoods and the improving public environments of white suburbs was not a natural market outcome. It was the product of deliberate policy — the same policy that had segregated the housing market in the first place.

Historical Vocabulary
Disinvestment

Disinvestment refers to the withdrawal of financial capital, public resources, and private investment from a geographic area, resulting in the deterioration of physical infrastructure, the decline of property values, and the reduction of economic opportunities available to residents. In the context of Black urban neighborhoods, disinvestment was the predictable consequence of redlining, which denied those neighborhoods access to the mortgage capital that fueled investment and maintenance in white neighborhoods and suburbs.

Disinvestment operated through multiple channels simultaneously. Private disinvestment occurred when banks and insurance companies refused to lend or insure in redlined neighborhoods, when businesses relocated to suburbs and declined to invest in urban locations, and when landlords deferred maintenance on properties that could not be refinanced or sold at values reflecting their costs. Public disinvestment occurred when declining property tax revenues forced cities to cut services in Black neighborhoods, when urban renewal programs demolished Black neighborhood commercial districts without replacement, and when highway construction destroyed the physical fabric of Black communities without compensating them for what was lost.

The self-reinforcing character of disinvestment is crucial to understanding its long-term effects. When a neighborhood is labeled "hazardous" by federal appraisers, capital withdraws, which causes physical deterioration, which confirms the hazardous designation, which justifies continued capital withdrawal, which produces further deterioration. This cycle, once set in motion by the initial political and policy decisions of the 1930s and 1940s, continued generating its effects for decades even after the explicit legal instruments of segregation were prohibited. Understanding disinvestment as a self-reinforcing process rather than a one-time event is essential to understanding why the neighborhood conditions produced by segregation proved so durable and so resistant to policy intervention.

Highway Construction and Neighborhood Destruction

Few instruments of public policy caused more concentrated and irreversible destruction of Black urban community than the interstate highway system. The Federal-Aid Highway Act of 1956, which authorized the construction of 41,000 miles of limited-access interstate highways connecting the nation's major cities, is remembered as a triumph of American infrastructure ambition. In terms of the transportation network it produced, the historical assessment is largely accurate. But the highway system was built through cities as well as between them, and the routing of urban expressways through established residential neighborhoods — particularly Black residential neighborhoods — produced destruction of community that ranked with urban renewal as one of the most devastating policy interventions of the postwar era.

The selection of urban highway routes was a political process in which powerful interests competed for favorable outcomes and in which the interests of Black residents were consistently subordinated to those of white suburbs, downtown business districts, and highway engineers. Community advocates and historians have documented, city by city, how expressway routes were selected to pass through Black neighborhoods rather than white ones, to clear "blighted" (Black) areas that presented redevelopment opportunities to adjacent institutions and real estate interests, and to serve as physical barriers between Black and white neighborhoods — or between downtown business districts and Black residential areas that white business owners wanted to insulate from Black pedestrian traffic.

The scale of displacement produced by highway construction was immense. The Federal Highway Administration estimated in 1972 that the construction of the interstate system had displaced approximately 475,000 families — and the overwhelming majority of those displaced families were Black, because the overwhelming majority of displaced neighborhoods were Black. Displaced families received minimal relocation assistance under most programs, and the alternative housing to which they were relocated was frequently worse than what they had lost, often in public housing projects or in overcrowded areas of the Black Belt that could not absorb additional population without further deterioration.

The loss of the neighborhood social fabric — the corner stores, the churches, the schools, the block associations, the social networks that made urban life livable — was less easily quantified than housing displacement but was equally real. Neighborhoods that had taken generations to build, that had developed the accumulated social capital of shared history, known faces, trusted relationships, and community institutions, were destroyed in months. The people scattered by highway construction and urban renewal carried the memory of those communities with them, but they could not easily rebuild what had been destroyed. The destruction of Black urban community through highway construction and urban renewal was not a regrettable side effect of public progress. It was, in most cases, a predictable and often desired outcome of a policy process in which Black communities had no effective political power to protect their interests.

Section 3.5

Neighborhood Boundaries: The Geography of Identity and Conflict

The Production of Territorial Identity

One of the most significant — and most underappreciated — consequences of residential hypersegregation was its production of intense neighborhood-based identity among Black urban residents. When a community is geographically contained — when people are prevented by law, finance, violence, and custom from moving freely in a metropolitan housing market and are instead confined to specific territories — those territories take on an importance and intensity of meaning that dispersed populations do not experience. The neighborhood is not merely where one lives; it is where one can live. It is not merely a convenient location; it is the totality of the accessible world.

For Black urban youth growing up in hypersegregated neighborhoods in Chicago, Detroit, Philadelphia, or Los Angeles in the 1940s, 1950s, and 1960s, the neighborhood was simultaneously a site of community, safety, belonging, and constraint. Within the neighborhood were family, church, friends, familiar streets, known faces, trusted adults. Beyond the neighborhood — in the white areas that were formally or practically off-limits — were danger, hostility, humiliation, and the knowledge that one's presence was unwanted. The psychological effect of this geographic enclosure on young people's sense of identity, belonging, and territory cannot be separated from the policy decisions that created it.

The territorial identities that Black youth formed in hypersegregated neighborhoods were not simply about pride of place — though they were that. They were also adaptive responses to conditions of geographic confinement. If you cannot move freely in the larger city, you develop intense knowledge of and attachment to the territory where you can move. If the areas adjacent to your neighborhood are controlled by potentially hostile groups — whether rival neighborhood youth clubs, white ethnic gangs defending their turf, or police who treat Black youth as suspects — then knowing the exact boundaries of your territory, and knowing who is on your side within it, becomes a matter of practical importance. The territorial consciousness that would eventually give structure to formal street organizations was produced by the same conditions that produced hypersegregation itself.

The boundaries between neighborhoods in hypersegregated cities were not abstract; they were known, physically marked, and practically significant. In Chicago, major streets — Stony Island, Cottage Grove, Halsted, Western — marked the borders between Black and white areas and between different sections of the Black Belt. These boundaries were known by every resident and young person who navigated the city. Crossing a major boundary without protection could mean harassment, assault, or worse, particularly for young Black men. The street organization geography that would develop on Chicago's South and West Sides in the 1950s and 1960s was built on a foundation of neighborhood boundaries that had been established by decades of racial geography — by the covenant lines, the redlining maps, the public housing placements, and the white ethnic neighborhood defense that had collectively created the compressed, internally diverse, and sharply bounded territories of Black Chicago.

The Color Line in Space

The color line — W.E.B. Du Bois's phrase for the fundamental division of American society by race — had a specific spatial expression in the hypersegregated cities of the mid-twentieth century. It was not merely an abstraction of social distance or cultural difference; it was a line drawn on the ground, running down specific streets and along specific blocks, maintained by specific acts of violence and specific institutional decisions, and experienced by the people on either side of it as a daily physical reality.

The spatial experience of the color line shaped Black urban life in ways that went far beyond housing. Schools on one side of the line were Black; schools on the other side were white. Businesses on one side served a Black clientele; businesses on the other side did not serve Black customers or did so in demeaning ways. Parks and beaches were racially divided, with specific areas informally designated as Black or white and the boundaries between them maintained by the threat of violence. The beach at 29th Street in Chicago, where Eugene Williams drowned in 1919, was one such boundary point — a place where the invisible line in the water was enforced by the rock-throwing of white beachgoers and where crossing it cost a Black teenager his life.

For young Black men growing up in hypersegregated neighborhoods, navigating this spatial color line was a daily practice that required knowledge, judgment, and strategic planning. Could you walk to a job interview in a white neighborhood without risking assault? Could you take a bus through a white area at night? Could you walk home after dark through a neighborhood that was racially mixed but controlled by a hostile group? These were not abstract questions; they were practical calculations about physical safety that shaped where one could work, shop, socialize, and move. The spatial restriction was both real and internalized, shaping not only where people went but how they thought about space, danger, belonging, and community.

This spatial consciousness — the intense awareness of territorial boundaries, of which spaces belonged to which groups, of the dangers of crossing certain lines — is the direct cognitive and social precursor of the territorial structure of Black urban street organizations. When historians and sociologists document the extreme territorial organization of Chicago's street gangs — the detailed geographic demarcation of turf, the vigilance about who is in whose territory, the violence associated with territorial incursion — they are describing organizational forms that did not emerge from nowhere. They emerged from a half-century of hypersegregation that had made territory, boundary, and belonging central features of Black urban experience.

Multiple Perspectives

Who Made the Neighborhood? Multiple Perspectives on Racial Boundaries in the Hypersegregated City

From the perspective of Black residents of the Black Belt: The racial boundaries of the hypersegregated city were experienced as a form of imprisonment — a cage constructed by others and enforced through violence and law, within which daily life had to be built as best one could. Many Black residents of Chicago's Black Belt in the 1940s and 1950s experienced their neighborhood simultaneously as home and as confinement: a place of genuine community, cultural vitality, and institutional richness, but also a place of dangerous overcrowding, deteriorating housing, inadequate services, and the constant awareness that better conditions existed in areas from which Black people were excluded. The neighborhood was theirs in the sense of belonging; it was not theirs in the sense of choice.
From the perspective of white ethnic neighborhood residents: White ethnic residents of Chicago neighborhoods adjacent to the Black Belt — Irish, Polish, Italian, and other communities — experienced the expansion of Black Chicago primarily as a threat to their investments, their communities, and their identities. Many had sacrificed significantly to purchase homes that represented their economic security. The real estate industry had taught them that Black presence caused property value decline, a belief confirmed by the actual decline that occurred as capital withdrew from transitional neighborhoods (even though the capital withdrawal was caused by redlining, not by Black families themselves). Their resistance to Black movement into their neighborhoods, including organized violence, was motivated by genuine economic anxiety as well as racial hostility — and understanding that motivation does not excuse the violence but does help explain the vehemence of white ethnic neighborhood defense.
From the perspective of federal housing officials: Officials of the HOLC and FHA understood themselves as implementing rational risk-assessment procedures that would protect the federal government's financial interests in the mortgages it was insuring. The racial assumptions embedded in those procedures — that Black presence inherently destabilized neighborhoods and reduced property values — were conventional wisdom in the real estate industry and in mainstream academic thought about urban land values at the time. Federal housing officials were not unique in holding those views; they reflected the racial assumptions of their era. But this context does not eliminate their responsibility for the policy consequences of those assumptions, which they implemented systematically and at enormous scale.
From the perspective of real estate speculators and blockbusters: Blockbusters and real estate speculators understood themselves as entrepreneurs responding to market opportunities created by the mismatch between Black housing demand and Black housing supply. The racial panic they exploited among white homeowners was genuine, and the premium prices they charged Black buyers reflected the true scarcity of decent housing available to Black families in any legal way. Within the logic of the market, their activities were rational profit-seeking. From the perspective of the Black families who paid inflated prices for deteriorating properties and the white families who sold cheaply in manufactured panic, the speculator was an agent of predatory extraction. Both perspectives are historically accurate.
From the perspective of Black community institutions: The churches, newspapers, fraternal organizations, mutual aid societies, and civic organizations of the Black Belt experienced hypersegregation as both a constraint and an opportunity. The concentration of Black Chicago in a defined territory created a captive market for Black institutions and a density of population that supported cultural and institutional life that might not have been viable in a more dispersed settlement. Bronzeville's Black Metropolis — with its Black newspapers, Black-owned businesses, Black professional class, Black entertainment venues, and Black political organizations — was, in important respects, a product of segregation as well as a victim of it. The vitality of that institutional life, and the limits that disinvestment and deteriorating physical conditions placed on it, reflect the paradoxical character of the segregated Black community as simultaneously a space of genuine flourishing and a zone of deliberate deprivation.

The South Side Case Study: Chicago's Black Belt in Detail

Chicago's South Side Black Belt provides the most extensively documented case study of hypersegregated neighborhood development in American history, and it is directly relevant to this textbook's larger subject because it was the geographic context in which the street organizations that would define Chicago's street landscape for decades were formed and developed. The Black Belt — the strip of South Side real estate running roughly from 22nd Street to 63rd Street, between State Street and Cottage Grove Avenue, with extensions westward and variations over time — was not a product of natural residential clustering. It was the product of specific policies: covenant coverage that excluded Black buyers from nearly every other area of the city, FHA and lending institution redlining that confined mortgage capital to areas outside the Black Belt while denying it within, and the placement of public housing projects that concentrated the growing Black population in an area that could not physically accommodate it.

The population density of the Black Belt by the 1940s was extraordinary. An area that had housed perhaps 50,000 people in 1910 was containing more than 300,000 by 1940, without any significant expansion of its geographic footprint. The overcrowding this produced was not merely uncomfortable; it was dangerous. Buildings designed for far fewer occupants were divided into one-room kitchenettes renting for weekly rates. Families doubled and tripled up. Plumbing was overwhelmed. Fire safety was compromised. The physical deterioration of the housing stock that followed was used by the city's housing inspectors, mortgage lenders, and public officials as evidence of the "blight" of Black neighborhoods — without acknowledgment that the blight was produced by the same policies of exclusion and overcrowding that those officials had implemented.

Yet within this zone of enforced poverty and physical decay, a remarkable community flourished. The "Black Metropolis" documented by sociologists St. Clair Drake and Horace Cayton in their landmark 1945 study of that name supported Black-owned newspapers, insurance companies, banks, restaurants, jazz clubs, and churches; Black political organizations with genuine citywide power; a Black professional class of doctors, lawyers, dentists, and educators who served the community; and a rich popular culture centered on the institutions of Bronzeville. The streets of the Black Belt — particularly 47th Street and the "Stroll" — were among the most culturally vital urban spaces in mid-century America. This vitality was achieved despite, and in some respects because of, the geographic concentration that segregation imposed.

The postwar period brought changes that strained the Black Metropolis without dismantling it. The massive public housing construction of the 1950s and early 1960s concentrated enormous numbers of the very poor in specific blocks and towers, creating conditions of extreme social and physical stress in already-overcrowded areas. The departure of some middle-class Black families to the fringe of the Black Belt or to newly opening areas on the West Side reduced the institutional density and social resources of the core Black Belt neighborhoods. The deterioration of physical conditions, the reduction of city services, and the growth of economic stress all intensified through the 1960s. It was in this context of both community vitality and deepening distress that the street organizations of Chicago's South Side took form — drawing on the territorial identity, community institutions, and young male social networks of the Black Belt, and responding to the conditions of deprivation, exclusion, and geographic compression that hypersegregation had produced.

Historical Debate

Was Residential Segregation the Result of Policy or Preference? Competing Interpretations

The question: The residential segregation of American cities was one of the most consequential social facts of twentieth-century American life. The question of how it came to be — and therefore who bears responsibility for it — has been one of the most significant and politically contentious debates in American urban history. Two broad interpretations have dominated this debate, though the historical evidence has progressively shifted the weight of scholarly argument toward one of them.

Interpretation A — The "Natural" Preference or Market Outcome View: One interpretation, associated with some economists and conservative policymakers, argues that residential segregation largely reflects the voluntary preferences of individuals to live with members of their own racial or ethnic group, the operation of housing market dynamics that naturally produced racial concentration as a byproduct of income differences between racial groups, or the free choices of private actors (homeowners, lenders, developers) who cannot be attributed with governmental responsibility. In this view, residential segregation was not deliberately imposed by government policy but emerged from private choices and market dynamics. The policy implication of this view is that government has limited responsibility for remedying segregation and that the conditions of segregated neighborhoods reflect the choices and behaviors of residents rather than the consequences of policy.
Interpretation B — The "Policy-Made" Segregation View: The interpretation now dominant among historians and urban sociologists, associated with scholars including Kenneth Jackson, Douglas Massey, Nancy Denton, Arnold Hirsch, and most recently Richard Rothstein, argues that residential segregation was produced primarily by explicit government policy — federal, state, and local — and by private practices that operated with government support or complicity. In this view, the HOLC neighborhood rating system, FHA underwriting guidelines, VA mortgage administration, public housing siting decisions, highway routing, urban renewal clearance, and the enforcement of restrictive covenants by courts all constituted governmental action that deliberately and systematically produced racial residential segregation. Private discrimination by real estate agents, lenders, and neighborhood associations complemented and extended this governmental action. The policy implication of this view is that government bears substantial responsibility for remedying a segregation it deliberately created.
What the evidence shows: The historical evidence strongly and overwhelmingly supports Interpretation B. Richard Rothstein's 2017 book The Color of Law assembled the most comprehensive recent presentation of this evidence, documenting case by case, city by city, the specific governmental decisions that produced residential segregation. Rothstein's central argument — that the government's role in creating racial residential segregation was so pervasive and so central that it constitutes "de jure segregation" (segregation by law) rather than merely the "de facto segregation" (segregation by private practice) of the conventional account — has been broadly accepted by historians. The empirical evidence against the preference or market outcome view is equally compelling: studies of income-controlled housing access consistently find that Black families face significantly greater barriers to housing in white neighborhoods than their white counterparts with identical incomes, a pattern inconsistent with market or preference explanations and consistent with discrimination.

Why it matters: The interpretation of residential segregation's origins has direct implications for policy. If segregation reflects private preferences and market dynamics, the appropriate policy response is limited — government should not compel integration against people's wishes. If segregation was created by government policy, the government has constitutional and moral obligations to remedy what it created. The legal implications of Rothstein's argument — that the federal government's role in creating segregation was so direct as to constitute a constitutional violation — are significant and are being actively debated in legal and policy circles.

Constitutional Connection

De Jure vs. De Facto Segregation and the Limits of Constitutional Remedy

The constitutional history of residential segregation is complex and, for the people most harmed by segregation, often deeply frustrating. The basic legal trajectory is well-established: the Supreme Court struck down racial zoning in Buchanan v. Warley (1917), prohibited judicial enforcement of restrictive covenants in Shelley v. Kraemer (1948), and the Fair Housing Act of 1968 formally prohibited racial discrimination in housing. These are genuine legal achievements. Yet residential segregation in American cities today is in many respects as extreme as it was in the 1960s. Why have legal prohibitions of housing discrimination not produced integration?

Part of the answer lies in the distinction the Supreme Court has drawn between de jure segregation — segregation imposed by law — and de facto segregation — segregation that exists as a social fact but is not directly commanded by law. In the school desegregation context, the Court held in Swann v. Charlotte-Mecklenburg (1971) that districts with histories of de jure segregation must actively desegregate, potentially including busing. But in Milliken v. Bradley (1974), the Court held that suburban school districts could not be included in urban desegregation remedies absent evidence that those suburban districts had themselves engaged in deliberate segregation. The result was to leave the residents of segregated urban school districts without remedy against the suburban exclusion that produced their segregation — even though that suburban exclusion was itself the product of governmental policy.

Richard Rothstein's 2017 book The Color of Law challenged the conventional legal understanding directly, arguing that the extensive governmental role in creating residential segregation — through HOLC, FHA, VA, public housing siting, highway routing, and urban renewal — meant that American residential segregation was in fact de jure rather than merely de facto. If Rothstein's constitutional argument is correct — and it is vigorously debated — then the federal government may have affirmative constitutional obligations to remedy the segregation it created, obligations that go well beyond merely prohibiting discrimination. This argument has significant implications for housing policy, school desegregation, and urban investment policy.

The constitutional dimension of residential segregation also connects to the Equal Protection Clause's requirement of equal protection of the laws. If the federal government provided subsidized mortgage credit to white families while denying it to Black families — using the same federal programs, the same taxpayer money, the same governmental authority — that differential treatment raises serious Equal Protection questions that have never been fully adjudicated. The political and legal barriers to remedying decades-old governmental discrimination through contemporary policy are substantial, but the constitutional argument that such remediation is not merely permissible but required is stronger than conventional wisdom in legal and policy circles has typically acknowledged.

Everyday Life and Social History

Living in the Black Belt: Everyday Life in the Hypersegregated City

For the hundreds of thousands of Black families who lived in Chicago's Black Belt in the 1940s and 1950s, the abstract language of housing policy — redlining, covenants, blockbusting — translated into the concrete texture of daily life in ways that shaped every aspect of their experience. Understanding what that daily life was like is essential to understanding the community, the institutions, and eventually the street organizations that emerged from it.

Housing conditions in the Black Belt were characterized by extreme overcrowding and physical deterioration. A family of six might occupy a "kitchenette" — a single room with a shared bathroom down the hall, carved out of a larger apartment by a landlord seeking to maximize rental income from a building that could not be financed through conventional means. Buildings that had been constructed for single families had been subdivided into multiple units, with shared bathrooms, inadequate heating, and plumbing systems that could not handle the load placed on them. Rat infestations were common in the most deteriorated buildings. Overcrowding meant that children had no private space, that illness spread rapidly through households, and that the stress of confined living was constant.

Yet within these conditions, daily life had texture, variety, and vitality. Women managed extraordinarily complex household economies, stretching limited incomes through their knowledge of markets, their social networks, and their organizational creativity. Men navigated the dual challenges of the industrial labor market — where they were restricted to the most dangerous and lowest-paid jobs in many industries — and the social landscape of the neighborhood, where community status was maintained through church involvement, fraternal organization membership, barbershop sociability, and neighborhood reputation. Children attended schools that were overcrowded and underfunded but whose teachers were often Black professionals with deep commitment to their students, and they navigated the streets of the neighborhood with the intimate knowledge that comes from having no other geographic world available to them.

The 47th Street corridor — the heart of Bronzeville's Black Metropolis — was a daily gathering place where Black Chicagoans could shop in Black-owned stores, eat in Black-owned restaurants, read Black newspapers purchased from street vendors, see Black performers at the Regal Theater and the Savoy Ballroom, consult Black doctors and lawyers with offices above the stores, and deposit money in Black-owned banks. This commercial and cultural strip represented the economic vitality that Black Chicago had managed to create within the zone of its confinement, and it was a source of genuine community pride and identity. The stores on 47th Street were locally owned and locally known; their proprietors were neighbors and community figures whose business success was understood as collective achievement.

For young people growing up in the Black Belt in the 1940s and 1950s, the neighborhood was simultaneously a community of deep belonging and a zone of geographic restriction. They knew their blocks with an intimacy that comes from having few other places to be. They knew which buildings were safe and which were not, which landlords were decent and which were predatory, which corners were friendly and which were controlled by groups they did not want to encounter. They understood, in ways that were rarely spoken explicitly but were pervasive in everyday knowledge, that the neighborhood's boundaries mattered — that beyond certain streets lay territory that was not theirs to navigate freely. This intimate knowledge of bounded space, and the intense neighborhood identity it produced, was not a pathology or a cultural deficiency. It was an adaptive response to the conditions that housing policy had created.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Black Urban Neighborhood Life: What Changed and What Persisted through Hypersegregation

What Changed

  • The geographic scale of Black urban community shifted from the southern small town and rural neighborhood to the northern big-city neighborhood, with all the changes in anonymity, density, economic diversity, and institutional complexity that entailed.
  • Industrial wage labor replaced agricultural sharecropping as the economic foundation of Black life, providing higher wages but also subjecting Black workers to the racial hierarchy of the industrial shop floor and the vulnerability of industrial employment cycles.
  • The formal legal apparatus of Jim Crow — explicitly racialized laws, disenfranchisement, legal segregation of public spaces — was absent in the North, replaced by de facto segregation maintained through private and federal financial policy rather than explicit law.
  • Black institutions — churches, newspapers, businesses, fraternal organizations, political organizations — grew enormously in scale and diversity in northern cities, supported by the concentration of large Black populations and by somewhat greater economic resources than the Jim Crow South had permitted.
  • New forms of Black cultural expression — jazz, blues, gospel, the Black press — flourished in the density of the hypersegregated city, reaching national audiences through recording technology and radio that the rural South had not provided.

What Stayed the Same

  • The fundamental fact of racial exclusion — the systematic denial of access to the resources, opportunities, and spaces available to white Americans — persisted from the Jim Crow South to the hypersegregated North, taking different legal forms but producing similar material outcomes.
  • The necessity of building autonomous community institutions as alternatives to the formal institutions that excluded or exploited Black people continued, with the church, the mutual aid society, the fraternal order, and the newspaper remaining central to Black community life in northern cities as they had been in the South.
  • The threat of white violence as a mechanism of racial control continued in the North, taking the specific form of housing violence — bombings, burnings, mob attacks on families who crossed the color line in housing — rather than the lynching and terror of the rural South.
  • The economic subordination of Black workers — through discrimination in hiring, through restriction to the lowest-paid and most dangerous jobs, through exclusion from skilled trades and union membership — persisted from the South to the North, limiting Black economic mobility even as northern wages were substantially higher than southern ones.
  • The organizational tradition of building collective structures of support, protection, identity, and dignity within excluded communities continued uninterrupted, as the community-building traditions brought northward by Great Migration migrants reproduced themselves in new urban contexts.

Historical significance: The continuity between the community institutions and organizational traditions of the Jim Crow South and those of the hypersegregated North is one of the most important threads running through this textbook. Black urban life in Chicago, Detroit, Philadelphia, and Los Angeles was not built from scratch by migrants who had no organizational models or traditions. It was built by people who brought with them the church, the mutual aid society, the fraternal organization, the newspaper, and the collective tradition of community building that had been developed in the South over generations of exclusion and resistance. The specific institutional forms changed in response to new urban conditions, but the underlying logic — building collective structures of support and identity within enclosed communities — was continuous.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has examined the mechanisms through which Black Americans who migrated northward and westward during the Great Migration found themselves enclosed in specific urban territories, cut off from the housing markets and neighborhood resources available to white Americans. That enclosure was not the product of natural market forces or voluntary residential preferences. It was the outcome of deliberate policies implemented by specific institutions — the National Association of Real Estate Boards, the Home Owners' Loan Corporation, the Federal Housing Administration, the Veterans Administration, local housing authorities, city governments, and courts — that systematically denied Black families access to the mortgage credit, the residential neighborhoods, and the public services that were being made available to white families during the same period.

Restrictive covenants were the primary private legal instrument of residential racial exclusion from the 1920s through the 1940s. Embedded in property deeds, enforceable in court, and covering the majority of residential real estate in most northern cities, these covenants wrote racial exclusion into property law and created the geographic framework within which the other mechanisms of segregation operated. The Supreme Court's Shelley v. Kraemer decision in 1948 eliminated judicial enforcement of racial covenants but did not end the systems of exclusion they had helped create, which continued through real estate steering, lending discrimination, and organized white resistance.

Redlining — the explicit incorporation of racial composition into federal mortgage risk assessments by HOLC and FHA — was the most consequential single policy mechanism in the creation of residential segregation. By denying federally subsidized mortgage credit to any neighborhood with Black residents or adjacent to one, HOLC and FHA simultaneously subsidized white suburbanization and condemned Black neighborhoods to the progressive deterioration that follows from the withdrawal of investment capital. The GI Bill, administered through the same racially discriminatory lending system, denied Black veterans the homeownership subsidy that made the postwar white middle class and built the racial wealth gap that persists to the present day. Contract buying — the predatory financing arrangement that emerged in the vacuum left by conventional lending's withdrawal from Black neighborhoods — extracted billions of dollars from Black families with no alternative housing options.

Public housing policy, which might in principle have been used to disperse Black urban poverty across metropolitan areas, was instead systematically used to concentrate it. The placement of massive high-rise public housing projects in already-poor Black neighborhoods, driven by the political power of white aldermen and neighborhood associations to exclude public housing from their areas, created conditions of extreme concentrated poverty that produced distinctive social and organizational dynamics. The design of high-rise public housing — towers with large anonymous common spaces, no defensible intermediate spaces, and thousands of families in close proximity — created physical environments that were profoundly difficult to make livable and that shaped the organizational geography of the street groups that developed within and around them.

White flight drained the cities of the middle-class population and tax base on which urban services depended, creating the urban fiscal crisis that collapsed schools, reduced police protection (or converted it into surveillance and harassment), allowed physical infrastructure to deteriorate, and eliminated the economic opportunities that had attracted Black migrants northward in the first place. Federal highway policy accelerated this process by destroying established Black neighborhoods to create expressways that served suburban commuters, displacing hundreds of thousands of residents with minimal compensation and no guarantee of decent replacement housing. Urban renewal programs, which might have improved deteriorating housing conditions, were instead used as instruments of "Black removal" that cleared Black neighborhoods for institutional expansion, highway development, or upscale redevelopment that served white interests at Black expense.

The result of these overlapping and mutually reinforcing policies was what sociologists Douglas Massey and Nancy Denton, in their landmark 1993 study American Apartheid, characterized as "hypersegregation" — residential racial segregation so extreme that it was qualitatively different from ordinary urban ethnic concentration. The Black residents of hypersegregated cities like Chicago lived in neighborhoods that were overwhelmingly Black, physically deteriorating, chronically underserved by public institutions, isolated from the economic opportunities of the metropolitan area, and sharply bounded from surrounding white areas by a color line maintained by violence, real estate practice, and the accumulated structural effects of decades of discriminatory policy.

Neighborhood boundaries in hypersegregated cities were not abstract administrative lines. They were known, physically marked, and practically significant features of daily life that shaped where people could go, what they could do, and what risks they faced. The intense territorial identity that developed among Black urban youth in these conditions — the fierce attachment to specific streets, blocks, and neighborhoods as the geography of belonging and the zone of safe movement — was not a pathological byproduct of culture or criminality. It was an adaptive response to the conditions of geographic confinement that housing policy had created. Understanding territorial identity as a response to hypersegregation rather than as an innate cultural characteristic is essential to understanding the organizational forms that territorial identity eventually produced.

The South Side Chicago Black Belt is the primary case study of this chapter, but the patterns it illustrates were reproduced in every major American city with a significant Black population. Detroit's East Side, Philadelphia's North and West Sides, Los Angeles's Central Avenue corridor, New York's Harlem and Bedford-Stuyvesant, Cleveland's Hough, and dozens of other Black urban neighborhoods were all products of the same policies, the same instruments of exclusion, and the same dynamics of disinvestment and deterioration. The geography of Black urban America in the mid-twentieth century was not the accidental result of independent decisions by individual families. It was a deliberately constructed racial landscape, built by the institutions of American government and the American economy, that would shape Black urban life — and Black urban organizational life — for generations.

The chapter has also emphasized what was built within the zone of enclosure. The Black Metropolis — with its institutional density, its cultural vitality, its community organizations, its commercial life, and its social networks — was a genuine achievement of Black urban community building under conditions of adversity. The churches, businesses, newspapers, fraternal organizations, and mutual aid societies of the Black Belt carried forward the organizational traditions that Chapter 1 traced to the Reconstruction era, adapting them to new urban conditions while maintaining their fundamental character as community-controlled institutions providing collective support, social recognition, and communal identity within an excluded community. This community vitality within the zone of enclosure is the social foundation from which the youth clubs, social organizations, and eventually the street organizations that later chapters examine would emerge.

The relationship between residential segregation and street organizational life is not simple or monocausal. Hypersegregation did not mechanically produce street organizations, and understanding that relationship requires attention to the additional processes — deindustrialization, the crack economy, police policy, incarceration — that subsequent chapters will examine. But residential segregation is the geographic and social foundation without which the other developments cannot be understood. The territory, the boundaries, the community institutions, the social networks, the compressed population, the concentrated poverty — all of these are products of the housing policies this chapter has examined, and all of them are structural conditions within which the organizational developments of later decades unfolded. The walls of the enclosure were not identical to the life that developed within them, but they defined the space within which that life was possible.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Blockbusting
The real estate practice of inducing white homeowners to sell quickly and cheaply by warning them that Black families were moving into the neighborhood, then reselling those properties to Black families at inflated prices. Blockbusting was enormously profitable for real estate speculators and accelerated the racial turnover of entire neighborhoods while exploiting the exclusion of Black buyers from conventional mortgage markets.
Color line
W.E.B. Du Bois's phrase for the fundamental racial division of American society. In the context of this chapter, the color line refers specifically to its spatial expression: the boundary between Black and white residential areas in hypersegregated cities, maintained by violence, legal instruments, and financial exclusion, and experienced as a daily physical reality by urban residents on either side of it.
Concentrated poverty
A condition in which the vast majority of residents in a geographically defined area are poor, producing social conditions qualitatively different from ordinary poverty: higher rates of crime, lower rates of educational attainment, fewer social networks connecting residents to employment opportunities, and higher rates of depression, trauma, and chronic illness. Public housing policy and redlining together produced concentrated poverty in Black urban neighborhoods.
Contract buying
A predatory home financing arrangement in which buyers paid installments to sellers but did not receive legal title until the full purchase price was paid. Buyers who missed any payment could lose the home and all prior payments. Contract buying was widespread in redlined Black neighborhoods because conventional mortgages were unavailable; it extracted enormous sums from Black families.
De facto segregation
Racial segregation that exists as a social fact but is not directly commanded by law, maintained instead through private practice, market dynamics, and the accumulated structural effects of past discriminatory policy. Distinguished from de jure segregation, which is mandated by law. The conventional view that northern residential segregation was de facto has been challenged by historians who argue that the extensive governmental role in creating it makes it de jure.
De jure segregation
Racial segregation mandated or commanded by law, as distinct from de facto segregation that exists as a social fact without direct legal command. The classic examples of de jure segregation are the Jim Crow laws of the southern states, but historian Richard Rothstein and others have argued that federal housing programs made northern residential segregation effectively de jure through direct governmental action.
Disinvestment
The withdrawal of financial capital, public resources, and private investment from a geographic area, resulting in physical deterioration and economic decline. Disinvestment in Black urban neighborhoods was directly caused by redlining, which denied those neighborhoods access to mortgage capital, triggering a self-reinforcing cycle of declining values, deferred maintenance, and reduced public services.
Exclusionary zoning
Zoning regulations that use minimum lot sizes, prohibitions on multifamily housing, and other land use restrictions to ensure that suburban municipalities remain accessible only to higher-income families. Exclusionary zoning, by mandating expensive single-family housing, effectively excluded Black families from many suburban municipalities even after racial covenants and overt housing discrimination were formally prohibited.
Fair Housing Act (1968)
The federal law prohibiting racial discrimination in the sale, rental, and financing of housing. Passed in the week following Martin Luther King Jr.'s assassination, the Act provided legal tools against housing discrimination. De facto segregation persisted after its passage through practices that were harder to detect and through the accumulated structural effects of decades of explicitly discriminatory policy.
Federal Housing Administration (FHA)
The federal agency established in 1934 to insure residential mortgages. The FHA adopted and extended HOLC's racial rating system, refusing to insure mortgages in Black neighborhoods and actively promoting racial segregation in its underwriting guidelines. FHA mortgage insurance was the key to the postwar homeownership market; its racial administration made federally subsidized homeownership available to white Americans and denied it to Black Americans.
GI Bill (Servicemen's Readjustment Act, 1944)
Federal legislation providing college tuition, job training, unemployment insurance, and low-interest mortgage guarantees to returning World War II veterans. Administered through the same racially discriminatory lending system as FHA mortgages, the GI Bill's housing benefits were effectively denied to most Black veterans, denying them the homeownership-based wealth accumulation that the Bill made possible for white veterans.
Home Owners' Loan Corporation (HOLC)
The federal agency established in 1933 to refinance threatened mortgages. HOLC developed the neighborhood rating system — color-coded maps grading neighborhoods from A (green) to D (red) — that institutionalized redlining by incorporating racial composition into federal credit risk assessment. These maps were adopted by the FHA and by private lenders, effectively denying mortgage credit to Black neighborhoods.
Hypersegregation
A term coined by sociologists Douglas Massey and Nancy Denton to describe residential segregation so extreme that the average Black resident lives in a neighborhood that is overwhelmingly Black and is isolated across multiple dimensions from the mainstream of metropolitan life. Hypersegregation, produced by the cumulative effect of covenants, redlining, public housing policy, and white flight, characterized most major American cities by the 1960s.
Neighborhood association
An organization of residents of a specific neighborhood that promotes its interests and manages collective concerns. In the context of this chapter, white neighborhood improvement associations were organized specifically to maintain racial homogeneity through legal means, real estate industry coordination, and violence. Their role in enforcing the color line was essential to the maintenance of residential segregation.
Property tax base
The total assessed value of taxable property in a jurisdiction, which determines the revenue available for local government services. Because local services — including schools — are funded primarily through property taxes, the erosion of the property tax base caused by redlining and white flight directly caused the deterioration of public services in Black urban neighborhoods.
Racial wealth gap
The disparity in wealth between white and Black Americans, currently approximately ten to one in median household wealth. A substantial portion of this gap was created by differential access to federally subsidized homeownership in the postwar period: white families who purchased FHA-insured or VA-guaranteed homes in appreciating suburban neighborhoods accumulated housing wealth that was denied to Black families excluded from those programs.
Redlining
The practice of designating certain neighborhoods — invariably Black or adjacent to Black areas — as high-risk and therefore ineligible for mortgage loans or favorable insurance terms, based on racial composition rather than individual creditworthiness or property condition. Redlining denied Black neighborhoods access to investment capital and triggered a self-reinforcing cycle of deterioration and disinvestment.
Restrictive covenant
A clause in a property deed prohibiting sale, rental, or occupancy by members of specified racial groups. Racial restrictive covenants covered the majority of residential real estate in most northern cities by the late 1940s. The Supreme Court ruled them judicially unenforceable in Shelley v. Kraemer (1948) but did not make them illegal to include in deeds.
Shelley v. Kraemer (1948)
The Supreme Court case in which the Court unanimously ruled that judicial enforcement of racially restrictive covenants violated the Fourteenth Amendment's Equal Protection Clause. The decision eliminated courts as an enforcement mechanism for racial covenants but did not end de facto segregation, which continued through other means.
Steering
The real estate practice of directing prospective homebuyers toward or away from specific neighborhoods based on their race, regardless of their stated preferences or financial qualifications. Steering was the standard operating procedure of the real estate industry for most of the mid-twentieth century, effectively duplicating the exclusionary effects of racial zoning without requiring any formal legal instruction.
Territorial identity
The intense attachment to a specific geographic territory — a neighborhood, a block, a set of streets — as the basis of personal and social identity, belonging, and security. In hypersegregated Black urban communities, territorial identity was produced by the geographic enclosure of residential segregation, which made neighborhood the totality of accessible social space for many residents, particularly young people.
Urban fiscal crisis
The self-reinforcing cycle of declining tax revenues, deteriorating services, business flight, and population loss that affected major American cities from the 1960s through the 1980s. Produced by white flight and the departure of the middle-class tax base, the urban fiscal crisis disproportionately harmed Black residents who were excluded from the suburbs to which the tax base departed.
Urban renewal
The federal program authorized by the Housing Act of 1949 to clear "blighted" urban areas and redevelop them. In practice, urban renewal systematically cleared Black neighborhoods — whose residents had the least political power to resist — for institutional expansion, highway development, or upscale redevelopment. James Baldwin's comment that "urban renewal means Negro removal" described its operation accurately in most American cities.
White flight
The mass movement of white residents and businesses from central cities to surrounding suburbs that accelerated from the late 1940s through the 1970s. White flight was driven by blockbusting, federal highway and mortgage policy, school desegregation pressures, and racial anxiety, and it drained cities of the middle-class population and tax base that funded public services.

Major People and Places

Identification & Significance

HOLC (Home Owners' Loan Corporation)
The New Deal federal agency whose neighborhood rating system institutionalized redlining, embedding racial composition into federal mortgage risk assessment and denying Black neighborhoods access to investment capital for decades. The HOLC maps produced between 1935 and 1940 are now recognized as the primary documentary evidence of federal involvement in the creation of residential segregation.
FHA (Federal Housing Administration)
The federal agency whose mortgage insurance program transformed American homeownership while simultaneously denying its benefits to Black Americans through racially discriminatory underwriting guidelines. The FHA's role in promoting racial segregation through its formal policy requirements is one of the most consequential governmental racial injustices in American history.
Eugene Williams
The Black teenager whose drowning at a racially divided Chicago beach in July 1919 triggered five days of racial violence that killed thirty-eight people and injured more than five hundred. Williams's death and the riot it precipitated illustrate the explosive consequences of housing compression and territorial racial conflict in hypersegregated cities.
St. Clair Drake and Horace Cayton
The sociologists whose landmark 1945 study Black Metropolis: A Study of Negro Life in a Northern City provided the definitive documentation of Black community life in Chicago's South Side Black Belt during the height of the Great Migration era. Their work documented simultaneously the vitality and the constraints of the hypersegregated Black community.
Elizabeth Wood
The progressive CHA director whose attempts to use public housing as a tool of racial integration were systematically blocked by white aldermen, illustrating the political dynamics through which local power enforced racial segregation even against reformist impulses within the housing bureaucracy.
Richard J. Daley
Chicago's powerful mayor from 1955 to 1976, whose machine represented white ethnic neighborhoods and whose policies — concentrating public housing in Black areas, maintaining school segregation, using urban renewal for Black removal — made Chicago's racial geography more rather than less extreme during his tenure.
Beryl Satter
A historian whose research, published as Family Properties (2009), documented the contract buying system in Chicago and the way redlining enabled predatory financial extraction from Black families. Satter's work connected the abstract mechanisms of federal housing policy to the concrete experience of specific Black families who lost enormous sums through the contract system.
Douglas Massey and Nancy Denton
The sociologists whose 1993 book American Apartheid: Segregation and the Making of the Underclass coined the term "hypersegregation" and provided the most comprehensive empirical documentation of the relationship between residential segregation and concentrated urban poverty. Their work established the empirical foundation for the policy-made segregation interpretation.
Richard Rothstein
The legal scholar whose 2017 book The Color of Law: A Forgotten History of How Our Government Segregated America provided the most comprehensive recent documentation of governmental responsibility for residential segregation, arguing that the extensive federal role in creating segregation made it de jure rather than merely de facto. The Color of Law has substantially influenced public and policy understanding of residential segregation's origins.
The Contract Buyers League (Chicago)
An organization formed in 1968 by Black homeowners who had purchased their homes on contract, who organized collectively to renegotiate or escape exploitative arrangements. The League's organizing brought national attention to contract buying and documented the connection between redlining and predatory financial extraction from Black families.
Robert Taylor Homes (Chicago)
The largest public housing project ever built in the United States, twenty-eight sixteen-story towers along South State Street housing approximately 27,000 people at its peak. Robert Taylor Homes became a symbol of failed public housing policy and a primary geographic context for the development of Chicago street organizations in the 1970s and 1980s.
Cabrini-Green (Chicago)
A massive North Side Chicago public housing complex housing approximately 15,000 residents at its peak, whose high crime rates and deteriorating conditions made it an internationally recognized symbol of American public housing policy failure. Its gradual demolition between 1995 and 2011 displaced thousands of residents and raised contested questions about urban redevelopment and community displacement.
Bronzeville / Black Belt (South Side Chicago)
The strip of South Side Chicago real estate created by restrictive covenants and redlining as the primary zone of Black Chicago settlement, also known as the "Black Metropolis." Bronzeville was simultaneously a zone of remarkable Black cultural and institutional achievement and a site of profound poverty and physical deterioration produced by deliberate disinvestment — the paradox of the hypersegregated Black community.
Paradise Valley and Black Bottom (Detroit)
Detroit's primary Black neighborhoods in the mid-twentieth century, home to a thriving Black commercial and cultural district before being destroyed by urban renewal and highway construction in the 1950s and 1960s. Their destruction represents the national pattern of urban renewal as Black removal.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter has told the story of how the geography of Black urban America was built — not by the choices of Black residents, not by the natural operation of housing markets, but by deliberate policy decisions made by the federal government, the real estate industry, local governments, and white neighborhood associations acting in concert over several decades. The result was residential hypersegregation: the confinement of Black urban populations to specific territories from which they were excluded, into which public investment was denied, and against which the full apparatus of law, finance, and violence was deployed to prevent escape. Understanding this history is not merely of academic interest. It is essential to understanding every subsequent development in Black urban life that this textbook traces.

The geographic enclosure this chapter has documented produced the physical and social conditions within which Black urban organizational life — including eventually the street organizations that are this textbook's central subject — developed. The territory was bounded and known. The boundaries mattered in immediate practical ways. The neighborhood was simultaneously a zone of belonging and a zone of confinement. The institutions built within it — churches, newspapers, mutual aid societies, fraternal lodges, business associations, and eventually youth clubs and social groups — expressed the same organizational logic that Chapter 1 identified in the Reconstruction era: the building of collective structures of support, identity, and dignity within communities that the surrounding society excluded. The specific forms changed; the logic persisted.

The residential segregation examined in this chapter also set the stage for the subsequent developments that later chapters will examine. The concentration of Black populations in specific urban territories, without adequate housing, services, or economic opportunity, created the conditions in which deindustrialization, when it came in the 1970s and 1980s, would have devastating effects. The compressed geography of the hypersegregated city meant that the loss of industrial employment — which Chapter 8 will examine — fell almost entirely on Black workers in Black neighborhoods, with no suburban safety valve and no dispersed geography to cushion the impact. The public housing towers where so much of the organized street life of 1970s and 1980s Chicago developed were themselves products of the housing policies this chapter has traced. And the racial wealth gap produced by differential access to postwar homeownership subsidies meant that Black urban communities had dramatically fewer private resources with which to navigate the crises of deindustrialization, the crack economy, and mass incarceration than their white counterparts.

The lesson this chapter most insistently offers is that the conditions of Black urban life — the poverty, the concentrated disadvantage, the deteriorating infrastructure, the territorial compression, the limited institutional resources — are not the products of cultural failure or individual choice. They are the products of policy. They were built by specific institutions making specific decisions, documented in archivally accessible federal records, real estate industry codes, court decisions, and legislative histories. The people who built the Black Belt of Chicago's South Side, who created the conditions in which Robert Taylor Homes and Cabrini-Green were built and in which they deteriorated, were not anonymous market forces. They were named officials of named agencies making explicit decisions that have been preserved in the historical record. That record matters — for historical accuracy, for moral responsibility, and for understanding what kinds of policy interventions might begin to address the consequences of what was deliberately done.

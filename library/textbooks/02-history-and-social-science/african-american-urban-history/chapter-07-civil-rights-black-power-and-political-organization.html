Part III — Politics, Crisis, and Urban Transformation

Civil Rights, Black Power, and Political Organization

From Migration to Fragmentation: The Urban History of Black Street Organizations

Chapter Opening

"The Black man has been programmed to death. He's not going to wait for the law to save him. He has to save himself." — Bobby Seale, co-founder, Black Panther Party for Self-Defense, 1966

In the summer of 1964, a fourteen-year-old Black boy named James Powell was shot and killed by an off-duty New York City police officer in Harlem. The officer, Lieutenant Thomas Gilligan, claimed self-defense. The city's response was a brief investigation that cleared Gilligan of wrongdoing without a grand jury hearing. For the residents of Harlem — who had watched their neighborhood undergo decades of overcrowding, disinvestment, police brutality, and political neglect — the decision was not surprising. It was, in the most precise sense of the word, business as usual. But the six days of rebellion that followed Powell's death, spread across Harlem and Bedford-Stuyvesant, marked something genuinely new in the political history of Black urban America. The disorder, the fury, the refusal to confine protest to the polite boundaries that white authority preferred — these were not simply riots. They were expressions of a politics that had been building in northern and western cities for two decades, invisible to most white Americans but legible to anyone who had been paying attention to Black urban life.

This chapter examines one of the most turbulent and consequential decades in American political history — the period roughly spanning 1955 to 1975, when the civil rights movement transformed the legal framework of American racial life, when urban rebellions shook cities from Harlem to Watts to Detroit to Newark, when the Black Power movement proposed a fundamentally different analysis of Black oppression and a fundamentally different program for Black liberation, and when the federal government responded to this political ferment with a counterintelligence program of illegal surveillance, infiltration, and violence that deliberately targeted the most dynamic Black political organizations of the era. This is a period that students of African American history and American urban history cannot understand the present without understanding, because so much of what came after — the drug wars of the 1980s, the mass incarceration of the 1990s, the fragmentation of Black political organizations in the late twentieth century — was shaped by decisions, events, and suppressions that occurred in these years.

The civil rights movement is often taught as a southern story — Montgomery and Birmingham, Selma and Mississippi, Martin Luther King Jr. and the March on Washington. And it was, in crucial ways, deeply rooted in the South, where the most brutal and naked forms of racial segregation were most visible and where the legal and moral case against Jim Crow was most clearly legible to the national and international audience that movement leaders were trying to persuade. But the civil rights movement was also an urban story, a northern story, a story about the gap between the formal rights that Black Americans obtained through the Civil Rights Act of 1964 and the Voting Rights Act of 1965, and the economic deprivation, residential segregation, police brutality, and political exclusion that continued to govern Black urban life in cities where Jim Crow had never operated as formal law but where its functional equivalent had been constructed through housing policy, employment discrimination, and policing practices.

The urban rebellions of the mid-1960s — in Harlem (1964), Watts (1965), Chicago (1966), Newark (1967), Detroit (1967), and dozens of other cities — were not simply explosions of criminal disorder, though they were widely described that way in the white media and by political figures who were more concerned with law and order than with the conditions that produced them. They were political events, driven by specific grievances — police brutality, housing discrimination, unemployment, and the systematic exclusion of Black residents from the political processes that governed their lives — in communities that had exhausted conventional channels for redress. The Kerner Commission, appointed by President Lyndon Johnson to investigate the rebellions, concluded in 1968 that "white racism" was the fundamental cause of urban unrest and called for massive federal investment in urban communities. The report was politically ignored. Its diagnosis was historically accurate.

The Black Power movement that emerged in the mid-1960s and reached its greatest influence in the late 1960s and early 1970s represented a fundamental critique not merely of formal racial discrimination but of the entire structure of a society organized around the economic and political subordination of Black people. Black Power was not a single organization or a unified ideology. It was a broad political tendency that included Black nationalist organizations, community control advocates, revolutionary Marxists, cultural nationalists, electoral organizers, and self-defense groups. What unified this tendency was a rejection of integration as an adequate goal, an insistence on Black self-determination and community control, a more or less explicit confrontation with American capitalism and imperialism, and a willingness to assert the right of Black communities to defend themselves from the violence of the state.

The Black Panther Party for Self-Defense, founded in Oakland, California, in October 1966 by Huey P. Newton and Bobby Seale, was the most visible and influential of the Black Power organizations that emerged in this period. The Panthers combined community service programs — free breakfast for children, health clinics, legal aid offices — with an explicit confrontation with police power in Black communities, patrolling the streets with law books and weapons in hand, asserting the legal right of Black people to observe and challenge police conduct. The Panthers' rapid spread from Oakland to dozens of cities, their visibility in the media, and their explicit revolutionary rhetoric made them the primary target of the FBI's COINTELPRO program — a covert operation that used illegal surveillance, agent provocateurs, fabricated evidence, and direct coordination with local law enforcement to destroy the organization through a combination of criminal prosecution and outright assassination.

COINTELPRO — the FBI's Counter Intelligence Program, directed at "Black Nationalist Hate Groups" — was one of the most significant but least understood chapters in American political history. It was not a response to criminal activity, though it was framed that way. It was a response to political organizing, to the emergence of effective Black political organizations that challenged the existing racial order. The FBI's campaign against the Panthers, the Nation of Islam, the Southern Christian Leadership Conference, the Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee, and dozens of other organizations was an illegal conspiracy by a federal law enforcement agency to suppress lawful political activity through unconstitutional means. Its consequences — the imprisonment of political leaders, the dissolution of organizations, the physical elimination of individuals, and the demoralization of communities — shaped the trajectory of Black political life for a generation.

This chapter's significance for the larger history this textbook traces is direct and irreducible. The political vacuum created by COINTELPRO's destruction of the most effective Black political organizations of the late 1960s and early 1970s was not filled by nothing. It was filled by other forms of community organization — some constructive, some harmful — in neighborhoods that had been abandoned by the political left and saturated by drugs, unemployment, and police repression. The relationship between the suppression of Black Power politics and the subsequent emergence of large street organizations as the dominant form of collective life in some Black urban neighborhoods is not a simple or mechanically deterministic one. But it is a real relationship, and understanding it requires understanding the politics of this chapter before moving on to the economics and policing of the chapters that follow.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain the central achievements and limitations of the civil rights movement as it intersected with the urban North and West, distinguishing between the formal legal victories of the 1964 and 1965 legislation and the material conditions of Black urban life that legislation did not address.
  2. Trace the major urban rebellions of the 1960s — their immediate triggers, their underlying causes, their geographic spread, and the political responses they generated — with particular attention to the differences between how Black communities and white political establishments interpreted these events.
  3. Analyze the Black Power movement as a political tendency rather than a single organization, identifying the major ideological currents within it and explaining what unified them despite their differences.
  4. Describe the origins, ideology, organizational structure, and community programs of the Black Panther Party, and explain why the Panthers became such a central focus of both popular attention and federal repression.
  5. Evaluate the debate within the Black freedom movement between nonviolent direct action and armed self-defense, explaining the historical, strategic, and ethical arguments on each side without reducing either position to caricature.
  6. Identify the major features and documented methods of the FBI's COINTELPRO program directed at Black political organizations, and assess its consequences for Black political life.
  7. Compare the political landscape of Black urban communities before and after the combined impacts of the urban rebellions and the federal suppression of Black Power organizations, explaining what changed and what persisted.
  8. Connect the suppression of Black political organizing in the late 1960s and early 1970s to the conditions of the subsequent decade — the deindustrialization, drug economy, and mass incarceration examined in later chapters.
  9. Assess competing historical interpretations of this period — those that emphasize the achievements of the civil rights era, those that emphasize its failures, and those that argue the real transformation was suppressed before it could be completed.
  10. Interpret primary sources from this period — government reports, organizational documents, FBI files, oral histories, and media coverage — as historical evidence shaped by the perspectives and interests of their creators.
  11. Distinguish between the southern civil rights movement as it is typically taught and the distinct political dynamics of Black urban communities in the North and West, explaining why different conditions produced different organizing strategies.
  12. Contextualize the events of this chapter within the broader Cold War context, explaining how anti-communist ideology shaped both the limits of civil rights liberalism and the justifications for federal repression of Black radical politics.

Guiding Historical Questions

Questions for Historical Inquiry

  1. What did the civil rights movement achieve, and what did its most important legal victories — the Civil Rights Act of 1964 and the Voting Rights Act of 1965 — fail to address about the conditions of Black urban life in the North and West?
  2. What caused the urban rebellions of the 1960s? Were they primarily expressions of criminal disorder, political protest, or something more complex? How do different historical actors and different historical interpreters answer this question?
  3. What did Black Power mean? Not as a slogan, but as a political program — what was it calling for, who was articulating it, and why did it emerge when and where it did?
  4. What was the relationship between the civil rights movement and the Black Power movement? Were they in opposition to each other, in dialogue, or best understood as different phases of a single longer struggle?
  5. What was COINTELPRO, and how did the FBI's counterintelligence operations against Black political organizations differ from legitimate law enforcement? What does the COINTELPRO history tell us about the relationship between political organizing and state power in the United States?
  6. Why was the assertion of the right to armed self-defense so central to Black Power politics, and how did that assertion draw on a longer tradition of Black self-defense that predated the 1960s?
  7. How did the suppression of Black Power organizations affect the political landscape of Black urban communities in the 1970s and beyond? What did community life look like in the vacuum left by the dismantling of these organizations?
  8. What is the relationship between the political history examined in this chapter and the economic crisis, drug economy, and mass incarceration examined in subsequent chapters? How did political decisions shape economic and carceral outcomes?
  9. What can the history of this period tell us about the conditions under which mass political organizing is possible, and the conditions under which it is suppressed?

Historical Context

To understand the political eruptions of the 1960s, students must understand the conditions that produced them — conditions that had been building in Black urban communities for at least two decades before the urban rebellions and the Black Power movement brought them to the attention of national political culture. Those conditions have been examined in the preceding chapters of this textbook: the Great Migration that brought millions of Black Southerners to northern and western cities between the 1910s and the 1960s; the housing segregation enforced by restrictive covenants, redlining, and the deliberate placement of public housing projects in already-Black neighborhoods; the industrial employment that provided working-class income for a generation but excluded Black workers from the most skilled and highest-paid positions; and the policing practices that treated Black neighborhoods as occupied territories requiring constant surveillance and control rather than as communities deserving the protective services that white neighborhoods received.

By 1960, the major Black urban communities of the North and West had been shaped by these forces into places of concentrated poverty, physical deterioration, and political marginalization that stood in stark contrast to the prosperity of the postwar suburban expansion from which Black Americans were largely excluded. Chicago's South Side and West Side, Detroit's Paradise Valley and later its near-east side, Newark's Central Ward, Philadelphia's North Philadelphia, Harlem and Bedford-Stuyvesant in New York, Watts in Los Angeles — these were not simply poor neighborhoods. They were neighborhoods that had been made poor and kept poor by specific policy decisions and market practices operating over decades. The Black families who lived in them had not chosen poverty. They had been excluded from the wealth-building mechanisms — mortgage access, employment in skilled trades, quality public education, political representation — that had produced prosperity for their white working-class contemporaries.

The civil rights movement that developed in the South in the 1950s and early 1960s addressed the most naked and legally explicit dimensions of American racial apartheid: the legally enforced segregation of public accommodations, the systematic denial of voting rights through violence and legal barriers, the official apartheid of schools and public facilities. These were real targets, and the movement's victories — the Civil Rights Act of 1964, the Voting Rights Act of 1965 — were genuine achievements of enormous historical significance. They dismantled the formal legal apparatus of Jim Crow, opened public accommodations to Black customers, and restored the voting rights that had been systematically denied to Black Southerners since the end of Reconstruction. This was not nothing. It was, in fact, a revolution in formal legal rights that had been won against tremendous violence and at enormous personal cost by hundreds of thousands of ordinary people and their organizations.

But from the perspective of Black residents of Chicago's Woodlawn neighborhood or Newark's Central Ward or Los Angeles's Watts, the victories of Birmingham and Selma were distant and limited. The restaurants in their neighborhoods had never been legally segregated, but their residents were still eating poorly because they could not afford better, and because the supermarkets that provided affordable fresh food had long since followed white customers to the suburbs. They had always had the formal right to vote in northern cities, but the political machines that governed those cities — Democratic machines in most cases — had not significantly improved conditions in Black neighborhoods despite decades of Black electoral participation. They could not be denied service at a hotel, but they could not afford to stay in one. The formal legal framework of civil rights had been transformed; the material conditions of Black urban life had not.

There was also a generational dimension to the political ferment of this period. The young people who grew up in northern and western Black urban communities in the 1950s and early 1960s — the children of the Great Migration — had a different political formation than their parents. Their parents had left the South carrying memories of Jim Crow's naked violence and had often developed a pragmatic accommodation to northern racial hierarchy as a lesser evil. The second generation had grown up in northern cities that formally promised equality and delivered segregation. They had been told that America was a democracy and experienced it as a system organized around their exclusion. They had watched the southern movement win legal victories that transformed formal rights without transforming their daily lives. Many of them were not prepared to continue accommodating conditions their parents had accepted as an improvement over what they had known.

The Cold War context shaped this political landscape in important and often overlooked ways. American Cold War ideology emphasized the contrast between American freedom and Soviet tyranny, which made racial inequality an ideological embarrassment in the global contest for the allegiance of newly decolonizing nations in Africa, Asia, and Latin America. This context gave the civil rights movement some of its political leverage — the spectacle of Bull Connor's fire hoses and Birmingham's church bombings was a propaganda disaster for a nation presenting itself as the leader of the free world. But the Cold War also provided the justification for suppressing Black radical politics that challenged capitalism or the American political system more fundamentally. J. Edgar Hoover's FBI defined Black radicalism as a communist threat, and this framing provided political cover for illegal surveillance and suppression that would otherwise have been difficult to justify in a democratic society.

The international context of decolonization was equally important to Black Power politics. The late 1950s and 1960s saw dozens of African and Asian nations win independence from European colonial powers, and these independence movements provided Black American activists with a framework for understanding their own situation. If African people could overthrow European colonialism, the analysis went, then Black Americans — who had their own history of colonization and who faced a domestic colonialism in their own segregated neighborhoods — could do the same. This Third Worldist framework, associated with figures like Frantz Fanon, whose The Wretched of the Earth was widely read in Black Power circles, connected the Black freedom struggle in America to a global struggle against racial capitalism and colonial domination. It also led directly to the FBI's claim that Black Power organizations were threats to national security rather than expressions of legitimate political grievance.

Understanding this historical context is essential for making sense of the specific events this chapter examines. The urban rebellions were not random outbursts of criminal violence. The Black Power movement was not an aberration or a temporary radicalism that was eventually superseded by more reasonable politics. COINTELPRO was not legitimate law enforcement that happened to use aggressive tactics. All of these developments were specific responses to specific conditions — conditions produced by the long history of racial capitalism and state violence that the preceding chapters of this textbook have traced, and conditions that would persist in modified forms into the decades that follow this chapter's focus.

Major People, Groups, and Places

Reference — People, Groups & Places

Martin Luther King Jr. (1929–1968)
The dominant public figure of the southern civil rights movement, King was a Baptist minister, gifted orator, and organizational leader whose commitment to nonviolent direct action and integrationist politics defined the mainstream public image of the movement. King's assassination in Memphis on April 4, 1968, triggered urban rebellions in more than 100 American cities and removed from the scene a leader who had, by the late 1960s, been moving toward a more radical critique of American capitalism and the Vietnam War. King's legacy has been widely simplified and domesticated in popular memory; understanding his actual political positions, particularly in his final years, is essential for understanding the period this chapter examines.
Malcolm X (El-Hajj Malik El-Shabazz, 1925–1965)
The most influential advocate of Black nationalism in the United States in the early 1960s, Malcolm X combined a searing critique of white racism, a powerful rhetoric of Black dignity and self-determination, and an explicit rejection of the integrationist politics of the mainstream civil rights movement. His assassination in February 1965 removed the most prominent voice of Black nationalism from the scene but did not end the influence of his ideas, which became increasingly central to the Black Power movement that emerged in the following year. His Autobiography, written with Alex Haley, remains one of the most important documents of African American political thought.
Huey P. Newton (1942–1989)
Co-founder of the Black Panther Party for Self-Defense in Oakland in 1966, Newton developed the Panthers' theoretical framework, combining Black nationalism with Marxist class analysis and a focus on community service as the foundation for revolutionary organizing. Imprisoned on manslaughter charges in 1967 after a confrontation with Oakland police — charges that many supporters considered politically motivated — he became the focus of a major Free Huey campaign that significantly expanded the Panthers' national profile. Newton's later years were marked by the collapse of the Panther organization and personal struggles, but his early leadership defined the most politically significant Black Power organization of the era.
Bobby Seale (born 1936)
Co-founder of the Black Panther Party alongside Huey Newton, Seale served as the party's chairman and was the primary organizational leader during its period of greatest growth and influence. Seale was one of the Chicago Eight defendants, tried on charges of conspiracy in connection with protests at the 1968 Democratic National Convention; his treatment during that trial — bound and gagged in the courtroom by order of the judge — became one of the iconic images of government repression of Black political organizing.
Stokely Carmichael (Kwame Ture, 1941–1998)
SNCC chairman whose articulation of "Black Power" during the 1966 Meredith March Against Fear in Mississippi marked a public turning point in the civil rights movement. Carmichael's "Black Power" speech represented a generational break with the older leadership's emphasis on integration and nonviolence, and it galvanized both a new generation of Black political organizers and a massive backlash from white liberal supporters of the movement. Carmichael later moved to Guinea, where he took the name Kwame Ture and continued Pan-Africanist political work until his death.
Eldridge Cleaver (1935–1998)
Black Panther Party Minister of Information and author of Soul on Ice (1968), Cleaver was one of the Panthers' most visible and controversial public figures. His rhetorical radicalism and his role in escalating conflict with police made him a target of FBI operations and contributed to the internal tensions that eventually split the Panther organization. Cleaver's subsequent political evolution — he eventually became a Republican — made him a complex and disputed figure in the history of the movement.
Fred Hampton (1948–1969)
Illinois Black Panther Party chairman who was murdered by Chicago police and FBI agents in a pre-dawn raid on December 4, 1969, while he lay drugged in his bed. Hampton was one of the most gifted organizers of his generation, having built a Rainbow Coalition in Chicago that united the Black Panthers with Puerto Rican, white, and other community groups across racial lines. His murder at the age of twenty-one, coordinated by the FBI's COINTELPRO operation and carried out by the Chicago Police Department, was one of the most documented and consequential acts of state violence against a Black political leader in American history.
J. Edgar Hoover (1895–1972)
Director of the FBI from 1924 until his death in 1972, Hoover was the architect of COINTELPRO and the driving force behind the federal government's illegal campaign to destroy Black political organizations. Hoover's obsession with Black political radicalism — he described the Black Panther Party as "the greatest threat to the internal security of the country" — drove the FBI's systematic violation of the constitutional rights of Black political leaders and organizations throughout the 1960s and early 1970s. Congressional investigations after Hoover's death revealed the full scope of the bureau's unconstitutional activities.
Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee (SNCC)
The student organization founded in 1960 following the Greensboro sit-ins, SNCC was the most grassroots and most democratic of the major civil rights organizations, and the one that moved furthest and fastest in the direction of Black Power politics. SNCC's voter registration and community organizing work in Mississippi and other Deep South states, conducted at considerable personal risk by hundreds of young activists, produced both the Mississippi Freedom Democratic Party challenge of 1964 and, after the MFDP's rejection by the national Democratic Party, a major shift in SNCC's political orientation toward Black Power nationalism.
Southern Christian Leadership Conference (SCLC)
The organization founded by Martin Luther King Jr. and other Black ministers in 1957 to coordinate nonviolent direct action campaigns across the South. The SCLC was the organizational vehicle for most of the major civil rights campaigns of the late 1950s and early 1960s, including Birmingham, St. Augustine, and the Selma-to-Montgomery march. The SCLC's strategy of dramatic direct action designed to expose the violence of southern segregation to a national audience was enormously effective in winning legislative victories, though its focus on formal legal equality left it less equipped to address the material conditions of northern Black urban life.
Black Panther Party for Self-Defense
The organization founded in Oakland in October 1966 that became the most visible and influential Black Power organization of the late 1960s and early 1970s. At its peak the Panthers had chapters in dozens of cities and served tens of thousands of community members through programs including free breakfasts for children, health clinics, legal aid, drug rehabilitation, clothing giveaways, and political education. The Panthers were also the primary target of the FBI's COINTELPRO program, which systematically undermined the organization through infiltration, fabricated evidence, prosecution, and assassination.
Nation of Islam
The Black Muslim religious and political organization led by Elijah Muhammad, the Nation of Islam was one of the most influential organizations in Black urban communities from the 1950s through the early 1970s. The NOI's message of Black self-reliance, moral discipline, racial pride, and critique of white Christianity resonated deeply in Black urban communities devastated by poverty, crime, and addiction. Malcolm X was the Nation's most prominent minister before his break with Elijah Muhammad in 1964. The NOI was itself a target of COINTELPRO, with the FBI working to exacerbate the split between Malcolm X and Elijah Muhammad in ways that may have contributed to Malcolm's assassination.
Oakland, California
The majority-Black city across the bay from San Francisco that was the birthplace of the Black Panther Party and the central focus of this chapter's case study. Oakland's Black community was shaped by the Great Migration of World War II and the postwar period, by intense housing segregation, by the decline of the port and manufacturing industries, and by a police department notorious for its brutality toward Black residents. These specific conditions produced the specific organizational response that became the Panthers.
Watts, Los Angeles
The South Central Los Angeles neighborhood whose six-day rebellion in August 1965 — following a confrontation between police and a young Black driver on a hot August evening — became the defining image of northern Black urban anger in the mid-1960s. Watts was home to tens of thousands of Black migrants who had come to Los Angeles during World War II and after, attracted by defense industry employment, only to find themselves confined to deteriorating neighborhoods by housing discrimination and excluded from the prosperity of the postwar suburban boom.
Harlem, New York City
The symbolic capital of Black America since the Harlem Renaissance of the 1920s, Harlem by the 1960s was a neighborhood of concentrated poverty, overcrowded tenements, police conflict, and intense political organizing. The 1964 rebellion following the killing of James Powell was among the first of the decade's urban uprisings, and Harlem's long tradition of Black nationalist politics — from Marcus Garvey through Adam Clayton Powell Jr. through Malcolm X — made it a central location for the political debates of the era.
Newark, New Jersey
A majority-Black city governed by a white political establishment that systematically excluded Black residents from political power, economic opportunity, and adequate public services. The July 1967 rebellion in Newark, triggered by the beating of a Black cab driver by police, lasted five days and killed twenty-six people. Newark's rebellion was a direct expression of conditions — poverty, segregation, police brutality, and political exclusion — that the city's Black community had been documenting without response for years.
Detroit, Michigan
The auto industry capital that had attracted enormous numbers of Black migrants during and after World War II, and whose July 1967 rebellion — the deadliest of the decade, with 43 killed over five days — shocked the nation and forced a serious reckoning with the conditions of Black urban life. Detroit's rebellion occurred not in the most impoverished district of the city but in a working-class neighborhood, and its ferocity reflected decades of accumulated grievances about housing, policing, and the growing awareness that deindustrialization was beginning to hollow out the industrial economy that had given Black workers their first real foothold in economic security.
The Kerner Commission (National Advisory Commission on Civil Disorders)
The commission appointed by President Lyndon Johnson in July 1967 to investigate the causes of the urban rebellions. Chaired by Illinois Governor Otto Kerner, the commission concluded in its 1968 report that the United States was "moving toward two societies, one Black, one white — separate and unequal," and identified white racism and the systematic exclusion of Black Americans from the mainstream economy as the fundamental causes of urban unrest. Its recommendations for massive federal investment in urban communities were politically ignored by the Johnson administration and rejected by the incoming Nixon administration.

Section 7.1

The Civil Rights Movement and Its Northern Limits

The Southern Movement: Achievement and Strategy

The civil rights movement that transformed American legal and political life between the mid-1950s and the mid-1960s was one of the most significant mass movements in American history. Its achievements were genuine, hard-won, and historically consequential. The desegregation of public accommodations mandated by the Civil Rights Act of 1964, the restoration of voting rights through the Voting Rights Act of 1965, and the formal dismantling of legal segregation in public schools through the implementation of Brown v. Board of Education — these were not symbolic victories. They changed the formal structure of American racial law in ways that remained significant long after the movement itself fractured and declined.

The movement's primary strategic approach — nonviolent direct action designed to expose the violence of southern segregation to a national and international audience — was not merely a moral preference but a carefully considered political strategy. Martin Luther King Jr. and the SCLC leadership understood that the movement's leverage depended on making the contradiction between American democratic ideology and southern racial practice visible to the constituencies whose political support was necessary for federal legislative action: northern white liberals, the national Democratic Party establishment, and the international community watching the United States claim the mantle of free world leadership. Bull Connor's fire hoses in Birmingham, the murder of civil rights workers in Mississippi, the state police violence on the Edmund Pettus Bridge in Selma — these images worked politically precisely because they forced the choice that movement leaders wanted to force: either the federal government would protect the constitutional rights of Black citizens, or it would stand revealed as complicit in southern apartheid.

This strategy worked for what it was designed to do. The legislative achievements of 1964 and 1965 would not have been possible without the movement's decade of organizing, direct action, and sacrifice. But the strategy also had specific limits that became increasingly apparent as the focus of Black political energy shifted northward and westward. The method of exposing legal apartheid to a morally outraged national audience worked against legal apartheid, because legal apartheid was formally inconsistent with constitutional principles and with the democratic ideology that American political culture proclaimed. It was much less effective against the de facto segregation, the economic exclusion, the police brutality, and the political disenfranchisement that governed Black life in cities where there were no "White" and "Colored" signs over water fountains but where the functional equivalent of racial separation had been constructed through real estate practices, employment discrimination, school zoning, and policing strategies that were less dramatically visible and more difficult to photograph for the nightly news.

The Limits of Legislative Reform

The Civil Rights Act of 1964 and the Voting Rights Act of 1965 were landmark pieces of legislation. But Black residents of Chicago's Lawndale neighborhood, or Newark's Central Ward, or Watts in Los Angeles, experienced these victories with a mixture of genuine celebration and deepening frustration. The legislation addressed the formal legal architecture of racial discrimination — prohibited employers from discriminating by race, prohibited unequal treatment in public accommodations, protected voting rights. It did not address the wealth gap that resulted from a century of exclusion from homeownership and wealth accumulation. It did not address the concentration of Black families in deteriorating public housing projects that functioned as warehouses for the poor. It did not address the school underfunding that left Black children with less access to quality education than their white counterparts in neighboring suburbs. It did not address the policing practices that treated Black neighborhoods as high-crime zones requiring constant stop-and-frisk enforcement rather than as communities deserving protective services.

In a speech at the end of the Selma-to-Montgomery march in March 1965, King himself acknowledged this gap, declaring that the movement had won "the right to vote" but had not yet addressed "the right to live." In subsequent years, as King moved toward a more explicit critique of economic inequality and American militarism, he articulated the limitations of formal civil rights reform with increasing directness. His Poor People's Campaign, launched in 1967 and culminating in an occupation of Washington after his assassination, was an attempt to address the material conditions that formal civil rights legislation had not touched. The campaign was widely viewed as an overreach by political allies who considered economic redistribution a more radical demand than formal legal equality, and it generated nothing like the political support that earlier campaigns had won.

The limitations of legislative reform were compounded by the resistance of northern cities to the movement's expansion. When King brought civil rights campaigns to Chicago in 1966, attempting to address housing segregation in the city, he encountered a form of racial hostility that was different from Birmingham's in character but equally profound in effect. The white residents of all-white Chicago neighborhoods who threw rocks and bottles at civil rights marchers were not southern segregationists defending a formal legal system. They were northern white ethnic working-class people defending the property values and social boundaries of neighborhoods from which real estate steering, bank lending practices, and informal social violence had successfully excluded Black residents for decades. Mayor Richard J. Daley's resistance to the housing movement was not that of Bull Connor — it was politely presented, politically sophisticated, and equally effective at protecting residential segregation without the dramatic imagery that had energized the southern movement.

The Role of Malcolm X and the Northern Alternative

While the SCLC and SNCC were building the southern movement, a parallel political tradition was developing in northern Black urban communities that offered a different analysis of the problem and a different vision of the solution. The most important national voice of this tradition in the early 1960s was Malcolm X, the Nation of Islam minister whose radio addresses, speeches, and media appearances reached millions of Black Americans with a message that was simultaneously deeply rooted in the specific experience of northern Black urban life and sharply critical of the integrationist politics of the mainstream movement.

Malcolm's critique of integration was not a counsel of despair or a defense of racial separation as a value. It was a political argument: that seeking acceptance and inclusion in a white-dominated society that had demonstrated its fundamental hostility to Black humanity was both strategically futile and psychologically degrading. Integration, in Malcolm's analysis, was a strategy that required Black people to accept white people's terms and white people's judgment of what was desirable, rather than developing the economic self-sufficiency, political power, and cultural pride that would allow Black communities to define their own futures. "You don't integrate into a burning house," he memorably said — an argument that the structural problems of American society required transformation, not inclusion.

Malcolm's assassination in February 1965 removed the most prominent voice of this tradition, but his ideas proved far more durable than his organization. The Autobiography of Malcolm X, published posthumously in 1965, became one of the most widely read books in the country in the late 1960s, particularly among young Black men in urban communities. His concept of Black dignity and self-determination, his critique of respectability politics, his analysis of Black America's relationship to the global struggle against colonialism — these ideas did not die with him. They became the intellectual foundation from which the Black Power movement built its analysis and its programs.

The SNCC organization that had been at the forefront of the southern movement underwent a political transformation in the mid-1960s that mirrored this broader ideological shift. The Freedom Summer campaign of 1964, in which hundreds of northern white college students came to Mississippi to assist Black voter registration, had exposed deep tensions within SNCC about the appropriate role of white allies in a Black freedom movement. The rejection of the Mississippi Freedom Democratic Party at the 1964 Democratic National Convention — the moment when the Johnson administration chose to preserve its alliance with white southern Democrats rather than seat the MFDP's integrated delegation — convinced many SNCC activists that the path of working within the Democratic Party system was a dead end. By 1966, when Stokely Carmichael replaced John Lewis as SNCC chairman, the organization had moved decisively in the direction of Black Power nationalism, expelling white members and reorienting its program toward Black community control and self-determination.

Section 7.2

Urban Rebellions: The Politics of the Street

Watts, 1965: The Fire This Time

On the evening of August 11, 1965 — just five days after President Johnson signed the Voting Rights Act — a California Highway Patrol officer stopped twenty-one-year-old Marquette Frye on Avalon Boulevard in Watts, a neighborhood in the southern part of Los Angeles, on suspicion of drunk driving. What began as a routine traffic stop escalated when Frye's mother arrived on the scene, when a crowd gathered, and when police reinforcements responded to the situation with what witnesses described as unnecessary force. By nightfall, the neighborhood was in rebellion.

The uprising lasted six days. When it ended, 34 people were dead — the vast majority of them Black, most killed by law enforcement — nearly 1,000 were injured, more than 3,400 had been arrested, and approximately $40 million in property had been damaged or destroyed. The National Guard had mobilized 14,000 troops. The physical destruction was concentrated in the commercial corridors of the neighborhood — the stores that had long charged premium prices to Black customers who had no transportation to shop elsewhere, that hired few Black employees, and many of whose owners lived in white suburbs well outside the neighborhood they profited from.

The reaction to Watts from the national political establishment was shock, denial, and almost immediate retreat from the structural analysis that might have addressed its causes. California Governor Pat Brown flew back from a European vacation and declared himself "baffled" — a word that accurately captured white America's incomprehension but was less comprehensible to Watts residents who had been documenting their conditions for years. President Johnson, who had just achieved the greatest legislative victory of the civil rights movement, was deeply upset that the urban north had erupted at the very moment of his triumph. The standard political response — to treat the rebellion as criminal disorder requiring more police rather than as political expression requiring structural redress — set the template for responses to urban unrest that would persist for decades.

What the white political establishment saw as irrational destruction, many Watts residents saw as a form of political communication — a message sent by people who had run out of conventional channels through which to be heard. The conditions that produced the rebellion were not obscure. Black unemployment in Watts ran at several times the national average. The neighborhood was physically deteriorating because banks would not lend for improvements in redlined areas. The Los Angeles Police Department under Chief William Parker — who had described Black residents as "monkeys in a zoo" — operated with a militaristic hostility toward Black neighborhoods that made routine police contacts feel like occupation rather than service. And the political institutions of Los Angeles had proven consistently unresponsive to Black community concerns.

The McCone Commission, appointed by Governor Brown to investigate Watts, produced a report that emphasized the individual pathologies of participants rather than the structural conditions that had produced the rebellion. This became the standard political response: investigate, blame the victims' culture and individual choices, recommend more police, and avoid the structural changes that genuine redress would require. The pattern would repeat itself in Newark and Detroit in 1967, and in cities across the country following King's assassination in 1968.

Newark and Detroit, 1967: The Long Hot Summer

The summer of 1967 saw urban rebellions in more than 150 American cities. The two most deadly — in Newark, New Jersey, and Detroit, Michigan — produced a political crisis that forced the federal government to respond with something more than usual denial. The results of that response, embodied in the Kerner Commission report, were ultimately disappointing, but the report itself produced the most honest official reckoning with the structural causes of Black urban unrest that the American political system has ever generated.

Newark's rebellion began on the evening of July 12, when police beat Black cab driver John Smith during an arrest and word spread through the Central Ward that he had been killed. The rebellion lasted five days and killed 26 people. Newark's Black community had specific, documented grievances. The city was majority Black but governed by a white political machine that controlled nearly all patronage employment, city contracts, and political appointments. The police department was overwhelmingly white and routinely brutal. The city's urban renewal program, far from improving Black neighborhoods, had demolished Black homes and businesses to build a medical complex that served a mostly white suburban patient population. The high school dropout rate in Black neighborhoods was catastrophic. Unemployment was endemic.

Detroit's rebellion began twelve days later, in the early hours of July 23, when police raided an illegal after-hours drinking club in the city's near-west side. It lasted five days and killed 43 people — the deadliest urban rebellion since the 1943 Detroit race riot. National Guard and federal troops were deployed. Two thousand buildings were burned. Detroit's rebellion shocked white America more than Newark's had, partly because Detroit had a reputation as a relatively progressive northern city and partly because its Black community was more economically stable than those in most other cities, with a significant working-class and middle-class presence rooted in the auto industry's union wages. If Detroit could explode, the argument went, it could happen anywhere — which was precisely what the Kerner Commission concluded.

The Kerner Commission's report, released in February 1968, was a document of extraordinary candor for a government publication. It declared that the nation was moving toward two societies, one Black and one white, separate and unequal. It identified white racism — specifically the systematic exclusion of Black Americans from the opportunities that had produced white working-class and middle-class prosperity — as the fundamental cause of urban unrest. It called for massive federal investment in urban communities: in housing, employment, education, and police reform. And it warned that without this investment, the cycle of rebellion and repression would continue and deepen.

The Johnson administration's response to the report was effectively to ignore it. Johnson was already in political trouble over Vietnam, and the political backlash against urban unrest — the "law and order" politics that Richard Nixon was successfully exploiting — made the report's recommendations politically radioactive. Nixon won the 1968 presidential election in large part by running against the "permissiveness" that conservatives blamed for urban disorder, and his administration's response to urban unrest was escalated police power rather than economic investment. The Kerner Commission's diagnosis was historically accurate and politically irrelevant.

The Rebellion After King's Assassination, 1968

The assassination of Martin Luther King Jr. on April 4, 1968, in Memphis, where he had gone to support a sanitation workers' strike, triggered the most widespread urban unrest in American history since the Civil War. Rebellions erupted in more than 100 cities across the country. In Washington, D.C., fires burned within blocks of the White House and the Capitol. In Chicago, Mayor Daley ordered police to shoot to kill arsonists and shoot to maim looters. In Baltimore, more than 5,000 people were arrested. The National Guard was deployed in dozens of cities.

The political response to the post-King rebellions further deepened the trajectory away from the structural investments the Kerner Commission had called for and toward the law and order politics that would define the subsequent era. Congress passed the Civil Rights Act of 1968 — which included fair housing provisions that finally addressed housing discrimination — but also rushed through anticrime and anti-riot provisions that expanded federal law enforcement authority. The political momentum of the civil rights era was effectively exhausted. The movement that had won the most significant legislative victories of the postwar era was now politically on the defensive, associated in the public mind with disorder rather than justice, and facing a federal government whose new leadership was explicitly hostile to the structural analysis that had briefly found expression in the Kerner Commission's findings.

Cause and Effect

The Urban Rebellions: Structural Causes and Political Consequences

Long-Term Structural Causes

Decades of housing segregation through redlining and restrictive covenants that concentrated Black families in deteriorating neighborhoods and excluded them from suburban wealth-building; systematic exclusion from skilled trades, unions, and higher-paid industrial employment; underfunded, overcrowded urban schools; police departments organized around the control and surveillance of Black neighborhoods rather than their protection; political machines that served white ethnic constituencies while extracting Black votes without delivering services.

Immediate Triggers

In virtually every case, the immediate trigger was an incident of police violence against a Black resident — a traffic stop escalated, a man beaten in custody, an arrest witnessed by a crowd. The triggers were not random but expressed the most visible and intimate dimension of Black communities' experience of state power. The crowd's response to these triggers was not irrational; it was the expression of accumulated grievances that conventional political channels had consistently failed to address.

Short-Term Political Consequences

Federal Kerner Commission appointed and issued a landmark report identifying white racism as the cause; Congress passed the Civil Rights Act of 1968 including fair housing provisions; federal law enforcement authority was expanded; "law and order" became the dominant political response; Richard Nixon won the 1968 election partly on the strength of anti-rebellion backlash; federal resources flowed toward policing rather than community investment.

Long-Term Consequences

The rebellions accelerated white flight and business disinvestment from already-declining urban neighborhoods; insurance redlining deepened in the aftermath of fire damage; the political backlash against urban unrest created the conditions for Nixon's War on Drugs and the subsequent expansion of the carceral state; the structural conditions that produced the rebellions — housing segregation, concentrated poverty, inadequate services, police brutality — were not addressed and in many cases worsened in subsequent years.

Historical complexity: The urban rebellions have been interpreted by different observers as criminal disorder, as political protest, as collective bargaining by riot, and as expressions of a structural crisis that conventional politics was unable to address. The Kerner Commission's structural interpretation was historically the most accurate but politically the least influential. Understanding why an accurate diagnosis was politically ignored reveals important truths about the limits of evidence-based policymaking in a system organized around racial and class hierarchies.

Section 7.3

The Black Power Movement: Analysis and Program

What Black Power Meant

On June 16, 1966, Stokely Carmichael delivered a speech in Greenwood, Mississippi, in the middle of the Meredith March Against Fear — a civil rights march that had continued after activist James Meredith was shot near the beginning of his solo walk across Mississippi. Carmichael, recently elected SNCC chairman, had just been arrested for the twenty-seventh time in the course of civil rights work. Standing before a crowd of Black Mississippians under the night sky, he gave voice to a frustration and a program that had been building in Black communities for years: "The only way we gonna stop them white men from whuppin' us is to take over. We been saying freedom for six years and we ain't got nothing. What we gonna start saying now is Black Power."

The phrase exploded into national politics. It terrified white liberals and infuriated conservative white politicians, who immediately characterized it as a call for Black racial supremacy or anti-white violence. It electrified young Black people in northern and western cities who had been watching the southern movement win formal legal victories that seemed distant from their daily experience of police harassment, unemployment, and neighborhood deterioration. The mainstream civil rights leadership — King, Roy Wilkins of the NAACP, Whitney Young of the Urban League — distanced itself from the slogan, recognizing that it would be used by the movement's opponents to discredit the broader civil rights project.

But what did Black Power actually mean? The answer was not singular, because Black Power was not a single organization or a unified ideology. It was a broad political tendency that encompassed several distinct currents. Cultural nationalism, associated with figures like Maulana Karenga and the organization he founded, US, emphasized the importance of African cultural identity as the foundation for Black political consciousness — the creation of Kwanzaa as an alternative to Christmas was one of Karenga's projects. Revolutionary nationalism, associated with the Black Panther Party and influenced by Marxist analysis, saw Black liberation as inseparable from the transformation of the capitalist system that produced racial inequality. Community control advocates, working within specific urban neighborhoods, focused on wresting political and economic control of institutions — schools, police, hospitals, stores — from white-dominated structures and placing them in the hands of Black community residents. Electoral Black Power focused on building Black political representation and using voting power to direct resources to Black communities.

What these different currents shared was a set of starting premises: that formal legal equality was insufficient without economic power; that Black communities had the right and responsibility to control their own institutions; that the integration of Black individuals into white-dominated structures on white terms was not liberation but co-optation; and that Black political identity was a legitimate and necessary foundation for collective action rather than a form of reverse racism that had to be apologized for. These premises were controversial in the political culture of the late 1960s, but they were not analytically unreasonable, and many of them had been articulated by Black political thinkers from W.E.B. Du Bois onward.

Historical Vocabulary
Black Power

Black Power was a political slogan, program, and tendency that emerged from within the Black freedom movement in the mid-1960s, most prominently associated with Stokely Carmichael's June 1966 speech in Greenwood, Mississippi. As a political program, Black Power encompassed several related demands: political self-determination for Black communities, economic development under Black ownership and control, liberation from police occupation and state violence, and a reconception of Black identity from a source of shame to a source of pride and strength.

Black Power drew on several intellectual traditions. From Marcus Garvey and the Pan-African movement of the 1920s, it drew the emphasis on Black self-reliance, racial pride, and the connection between African Americans and the global African diaspora. From Malcolm X, it drew the critique of integration as an inadequate political goal and the insistence on Black community self-determination. From Frantz Fanon and the anticolonial movement, it drew the framework of internal colonialism — the analysis of Black urban communities as domestic colonies, economically exploited and politically subjugated, whose liberation required not inclusion in the colonial order but its transformation. From Marxism, in the case of the Panthers and similar organizations, it drew the framework of class analysis that connected racial oppression to the economic interests of the capitalist class.

Black Power was not anti-white in the sense of advocating racial hatred or violence against white people. Its critique was structural — directed at the systems of racial capitalism and political domination that organized American society — rather than personal. But the assertion of Black identity, Black pride, and Black self-determination as positive political values, in a context in which those assertions had always been defined as threatening, made Black Power appear threatening to white Americans who were comfortable with civil rights as a program of formal inclusion but uncomfortable with Black Power's demand for structural transformation.

The legacy of Black Power is complex and contested. Its cultural expressions — the natural hair, the dashikis, the proud assertion of African heritage, the renaming movement — left lasting marks on Black cultural identity. Its organizational expressions were largely destroyed by FBI suppression and internal conflicts by the mid-1970s. But its intellectual framework — the analysis of structural racism, the critique of nominal inclusion without economic power, the emphasis on community self-determination — remains deeply influential in Black political thought and in academic scholarship on race and inequality.

The Black Arts Movement and Cultural Nationalism

One of the most important and enduring expressions of Black Power politics was the Black Arts Movement, the cultural arm of the Black Power era, which produced a flowering of poetry, theater, fiction, visual art, and music explicitly committed to Black liberation. The Black Arts Movement, associated with figures like Amiri Baraka (formerly LeRoi Jones), Nikki Giovanni, Sonia Sanchez, Haki Madhubuti, and Larry Neal, rejected the assimilationist aspiration to be judged by the standards of European American literary culture and insisted on the validity of specifically Black cultural expression — rooted in the vernacular traditions of African American speech, music, and communal life — as both artistically valuable and politically necessary.

Community arts institutions — Black theater companies, poetry workshops, art studios, community newspapers — proliferated in Black neighborhoods across the country during the late 1960s and early 1970s. These institutions were more than cultural amenities. They were political formations, spaces where Black community members, particularly young people, could develop a political consciousness rooted in pride and solidarity rather than shame and aspiration for white approval. The spoken word and poetry traditions of the Black Arts Movement would eventually develop, through several transformations, into hip-hop — one of the most significant cultural forms produced by Black urban communities in the late twentieth century.

The Black Studies movement, which emerged on college campuses in the late 1960s and produced a generation of scholarship on African American history, literature, and culture, was the academic expression of the same political impulse. Black students at San Francisco State College struck in 1968–1969 — the longest student strike in American history — to demand the creation of a Black Studies department with Black faculty teaching Black students about Black history and culture on their own terms. This movement spread to hundreds of campuses across the country, producing academic departments that permanently changed the landscape of higher education and generated the scholarly apparatus for understanding Black urban history — including the scholarly tradition on which this textbook draws.

Economic Dimensions: Community Control and Self-Determination

Black Power's economic dimension — the demand for Black community control over the institutions and economic activity of Black neighborhoods — was one of its most practically significant and most politically contentious aspects. The Ocean Hill-Brownsville school decentralization conflict in New York City in 1968, in which a Black community board attempted to remove white teachers from their predominantly Black school district, crystallized the tensions between Black Power's community control agenda and the white liberal coalition that had been the civil rights movement's most important political ally. The teachers' union, the United Federation of Teachers, led by Albert Shanker, struck in defense of its members' rights; the Black community board argued that local community control over schools was essential to the educational self-determination that Black children required. The conflict — bitter, racially charged, and ultimately unresolved by the state's compromise decentralization plan — strained the Black-Jewish liberal alliance that had been central to the civil rights coalition and demonstrated how Black Power's demands could conflict with the institutional interests of groups that were formally allies.

Black economic nationalism found expression in a range of programs and institutions. Black credit unions, community development corporations, Black-owned newspapers and radio stations, Black-owned businesses in Black neighborhoods, and Black consumer cooperatives all drew on the long tradition of Black economic self-help that extended from Booker T. Washington through Marcus Garvey to the present. The rhetorical emphasis on "Buy Black" campaigns in the late 1960s sought to redirect Black consumer spending to Black-owned businesses as a form of economic solidarity. These efforts produced some lasting institutions — community development financial institutions, Black-owned media outlets, historically significant businesses — though they were limited by the structural disadvantages that Black entrepreneurs faced in accessing capital, credit, and commercial real estate in a market still organized around racial exclusion.

Section 7.4

Community Self-Defense: The Black Panther Party

Origins in Oakland

The Black Panther Party for Self-Defense was founded in Oakland, California, in October 1966 by Huey P. Newton and Bobby Seale, two young Black men who had grown up in the Bay Area and been shaped by its specific combination of industrial decline, housing segregation, and police violence. Oakland in 1966 was a city in transition. The wartime industries that had drawn tens of thousands of Black migrants during World War II were contracting. The police department, overwhelmingly white, treated Black Oakland with a contempt and brutality that was well documented in the Black community and consistently ignored by the city government and the press. The traditional civil rights organizations had not penetrated deeply into the neighborhoods where Newton and Seale came from, and their programs did not speak to the immediate daily reality of police harassment that their community experienced.

Newton and Seale drew on Malcolm X's analysis of Black communities as domestic colonies and on Frantz Fanon's analysis of colonial violence and anti-colonial resistance to develop a program that was simultaneously more radical and more practically immediate than anything the civil rights mainstream was offering. Their initial response to police brutality was disarmingly simple: they studied California firearms and open-carry laws, obtained weapons, and began following police on patrol through Black Oakland neighborhoods, observing arrests with weapons in hand and law books open, asserting the legal right of community members to observe and document police conduct. This practice — which was entirely legal under California law at the time — terrified the Oakland Police Department and attracted enormous media attention.

The Ten-Point Program that Newton and Seale developed as the Panthers' founding document combined immediate practical demands — decent housing, full employment, an end to police brutality, freedom for all Black men held in federal and state prisons, a fair trial with a jury of peers — with more sweeping revolutionary rhetoric about Black liberation and self-determination. The combination was deliberate: the Ten-Point Program was both an organizing tool that could mobilize people around immediate material grievances and an ideological document that located those grievances within a broader framework of racial capitalism and colonial domination.

The Panthers' Community Programs

The dimension of the Panthers' work that is least familiar from popular accounts — which tend to focus on guns, rhetoric, and confrontation with law enforcement — is the extraordinary range of community service programs that the organization developed and sustained at considerable cost and organizational effort. The Free Breakfast for School Children Program, launched in 1969, fed tens of thousands of children in cities across the country before school each morning, filling a gap left by the failure of federal nutrition programs to reach the most impoverished urban families. At its peak, the program was feeding more children than any government program in the country.

The Panthers also operated free health clinics that provided medical care to residents of Black neighborhoods who lacked insurance and could not afford private physicians. The clinics performed basic medical services — blood pressure screenings, prenatal care, sickle cell anemia testing — and in the process identified sickle cell anemia as a significant health crisis in Black communities, generating awareness and eventually federal funding for research that would otherwise not have reached these communities. Panthers organized legal aid programs for community members dealing with the criminal justice system. They operated drug rehabilitation programs. They distributed free clothing. They organized community education programs — liberation schools that provided political education alongside basic academic skills.

These programs were not simply charitable services. They were expressions of a political philosophy that Newton called "survival pending revolution" — the idea that serving the immediate survival needs of the community was both ethically necessary and politically necessary, because a community whose basic needs were unmet could not sustain the long-term organizing that structural transformation required. The programs also built the Panthers' legitimacy within Black communities in ways that the organization's rhetorical radicalism alone could not have achieved. The grandmother who brought her grandchildren to the Panthers' free breakfast program every morning might not have agreed with the organization's Marxist analysis, but she knew that the Panthers were feeding her grandchildren when the school district and the federal government were not.

Historical Vocabulary
Community Self-Defense

Community self-defense refers to the organized effort of a community to protect its members from violence, particularly violence by the state or by groups that the state refuses to protect them from. In the context of Black American history, the concept of community self-defense has deep roots: the armed resistance of enslaved people, the post-Reconstruction militias that protected Black communities from Klan terror, the Deacons for Defense and Justice who protected civil rights workers in Louisiana and Mississippi during the early 1960s, and the Black Panther Party's armed patrols of Oakland neighborhoods in the mid-1960s.

The Black Panther Party's founding name — the Black Panther Party for Self-Defense — was deliberate. Newton and Seale were making a legal and political argument: that Black communities had the same right to armed self-defense that the Second Amendment guaranteed, and that the exercise of this right in the face of police brutality was not a revolutionary act but a legal one. The Panthers' practice of monitoring police conduct with weapons in hand was designed to be simultaneously a practical deterrent to police violence and a political statement about the right of Black communities to protect themselves.

The concept of community self-defense was controversial within the civil rights movement. King and the SCLC's commitment to nonviolence as a strategic and moral principle was incompatible with the Panthers' embrace of armed self-defense. But the distinction between nonviolent protest (the mainstream's position) and self-defense (the Panthers' position) was less stark than it sometimes appeared: even King acknowledged the right of individuals to defend themselves from lethal violence, and the distinction between offensive violence and defensive force was difficult to maintain clearly in practice.

The FBI's designation of community self-defense organizations as threats to national security — the COINTELPRO framing of the Panthers as a "Black Nationalist Hate Group" rather than a community organization exercising legal rights — was a political choice that deliberately obscured the legal and moral arguments on which the Panthers based their program. Understanding community self-defense as a legal and historical concept rather than as a synonym for crime or terrorism is essential for making sense of this period.

The Panthers' Spread and Internal Tensions

The Panthers grew rapidly from their Oakland base, establishing chapters in Los Angeles, Chicago, New York, Philadelphia, Seattle, Denver, and dozens of other cities between 1967 and 1969. This rapid growth was both a measure of the organization's resonance with Black urban communities and a source of serious organizational problems. Different chapters had significantly different characters, shaped by the specific conditions of their cities and by the quality of their local leadership. Some chapters were genuinely strong community organizations with robust programs and disciplined cadre. Others were less stable, more dominated by the confrontational rhetoric of the national organization than by the community service work that Newton considered the Panthers' most important function.

The FBI's COINTELPRO operations — discussed more fully in the next section — deliberately exploited and exacerbated these tensions. The bureau created false letters, forged documents, planted false stories in the media, and worked to foment conflict between the Panthers and other Black organizations — particularly the US organization of Maulana Karenga in Southern California, where FBI-orchestrated conflict between the two groups contributed to several deaths. The FBI also worked to destroy the Fred Hampton-led Rainbow Coalition in Chicago — a genuinely impressive cross-racial organizing initiative — by assassinating its leader.

Internal tensions about leadership, strategy, and the balance between confrontation and community service eventually produced a major split in the organization in 1971, between the faction associated with Newton that emphasized community programs and electoral politics, and the faction associated with Cleaver (then in exile) that emphasized revolutionary armed struggle. The split was, at minimum, exacerbated by FBI manipulation, and it significantly weakened the organization at the moment when COINTELPRO's other operations were also taking their toll. By the mid-1970s, the national organization had contracted back to its Oakland base, where Newton pursued a community development strategy while increasingly losing organizational coherence.

Case Study

Oakland and the Black Panther Party: Local Conditions, National Consequences

The Black Panther Party emerged from the specific conditions of Black Oakland in the mid-1960s, and understanding the organization requires understanding the city that produced it. Oakland's Black community had grown explosively during World War II, when defense industries at the Kaiser shipyards and other facilities drew tens of thousands of Black migrants from the South, many of them from Texas, Louisiana, and Arkansas. The migrants arrived to find a city that tolerated their labor but not their full civic presence: they were confined to a band of flatland neighborhoods in West and East Oakland by real estate steering and housing discrimination, kept from the desirable hillside neighborhoods by practices that were informal but effective, and served by city institutions — schools, parks, city services — that were visibly inferior to those available in white areas of the city and in the surrounding suburbs.

The Oakland Police Department of the 1960s was, by any reasonable measure, a militarized occupying force in Black Oakland rather than a protective service. Under Chief Charles Gain's predecessor, the department had operated with a swagger and a contempt toward Black residents that was well documented in the Black community press and in the testimony of thousands of Black Oakland residents who had experienced its methods firsthand. Officers routinely stopped young Black men without probable cause, made arrests for "manner of walking," used physical force as a first rather than last resort, and operated with the near-certain knowledge that civilian complaints would be disregarded and no disciplinary consequences would follow from any conduct short of the most extreme documented violence.

Newton and Seale built the Panthers' founding program from these specific conditions. The Ten-Point Program's demand for "an immediate end to police brutality and murder of Black people" was not abstract rhetoric. It was a response to a specific, documented, ongoing practice in their community. The armed patrols were not bravado. They were a calculated legal strategy that asserted the rights of Black Oakland residents in the most concrete and visible way possible: showing up, weapons in hand, when police made arrests, reading the law aloud, daring the police to violate the rights of the person being arrested in front of witnesses who were prepared and equipped to defend those rights if necessary.

The Panthers' community programs in Oakland were equally rooted in specific local conditions. The free breakfast program filled a specific gap: Oakland's school-age children from the poorest families were arriving at school too hungry to concentrate, and neither the school district nor any government program was adequately addressing this. The health clinics filled the gap left by a medical care system that treated Black Oakland's residents as customers it could profitably ignore. The legal aid program addressed the specific experience of a community that regularly encountered the criminal justice system without adequate representation.

Oakland also provides the clearest case study in the consequences of COINTELPRO for local Black political life. The FBI's operations against the Bay Area Panthers were extensive, well-documented (through subsequent Freedom of Information Act releases), and directly consequential for the organization's survival. The assassination of Bobby Hutton, the Panthers' first recruit and treasurer, by Oakland police in April 1968 — days after King's assassination — was facilitated by FBI intelligence sharing. The prosecution of Huey Newton for manslaughter — based on an incident in which Newton was himself shot by the officer he was alleged to have killed — consumed the organization's resources and leadership attention for years. The FBI's forged letters and fabricated evidence were designed to provoke internal conflicts that, in a tense organization under constant legal and physical attack, did their intended work.

The Panthers' eventual turn toward electoral politics in Oakland in the mid-1970s — Seale ran for mayor in 1973, Elaine Brown became the organization's first female chairman and ran for city council — produced some real political achievements and demonstrated the organization's ongoing community legitimacy even in a weakened state. But the combination of COINTELPRO's destruction, the 1971 internal split, the drain of continuous legal expenses, and the organizational difficulty of sustaining a large, rapidly grown organization had reduced the Panthers from a national force to a local one. By the late 1970s, the organization had contracted to little more than a school — the Oakland Community School, which operated from 1973 to 1982 and provided genuine educational innovation to Oakland children — before finally dissolving.

Section 7.5

COINTELPRO and Political Suppression

What COINTELPRO Was

COINTELPRO — the FBI's Counter Intelligence Program — was a series of covert operations conducted by the FBI against domestic political organizations between 1956 and 1971. Originally directed against the Communist Party USA, COINTELPRO was expanded in the 1960s to target a wide range of organizations that the FBI, under Director J. Edgar Hoover, considered threats to "internal security" — including the Ku Klux Klan, various white hate groups, the Socialist Workers Party, the Puerto Rican independence movement, and, most extensively and most consequentially, Black political organizations.

The FBI's COINTELPRO operations directed at what the bureau called "Black Nationalist Hate Groups" were formally initiated in August 1967, though covert FBI operations against specific Black organizations — particularly the Nation of Islam and SNCC — had been ongoing for years before that. The 1967 directive described the program's goals: to "prevent the coalition of militant Black nationalist groups"; to "prevent the rise of a 'messiah' who could unify and electrify the militant Black nationalist movement"; to "prevent militant Black nationalist groups and leaders from gaining respectability"; and to "prevent the long-range growth of militant Black nationalist organizations." These goals had nothing to do with preventing illegal activity. They were explicitly aimed at preventing effective legal political organizing in Black communities.

The tactics COINTELPRO employed were varied, comprehensive, and frequently illegal. Infiltration of target organizations by informants and agent provocateurs was the foundation of the program: virtually every major Black Power organization of the late 1960s and early 1970s had multiple FBI informants within its leadership or rank and file. These informants not only provided intelligence but, in their role as agent provocateurs, actively worked to exacerbate internal conflicts, promote factionalism, encourage actions that would expose the organization to legal jeopardy, and in some cases commit violence that was then attributed to the target organization.

The FBI created and distributed forged documents designed to provoke conflict between Black organizations. It sent fake threatening letters purportedly from one organization to another, fabricated evidence of betrayal and informing, and forged materials designed to suggest that individual leaders were FBI informants — a particularly effective tactic for creating internal suspicion and breakdown of trust within organizations that were, correctly, aware of the FBI's efforts to infiltrate them. The bureau planted false and damaging stories in the press through cooperative reporters and editors who published FBI-provided material without attribution. It coordinated with local law enforcement to facilitate the prosecution of leaders on charges that were, in many cases, fabricated or grossly exaggerated. And in the most extreme cases, it participated directly in or facilitated the murder of political leaders.

Historical Vocabulary
COINTELPRO

COINTELPRO (Counter Intelligence Program) was a series of covert, illegal FBI operations conducted between 1956 and 1971 against domestic political organizations. The program, directed by FBI Director J. Edgar Hoover, used infiltration, disinformation, agent provocateurs, forged documents, coordination with local police, and other illegal tactics to disrupt, discredit, and destroy organizations that the FBI considered threats to internal security — a category defined by Hoover to include virtually any organization that challenged the racial and political status quo.

The COINTELPRO operations against Black political organizations were the most extensive and the most consequential dimension of the program. The FBI directed resources and operations against the Black Panther Party, the Nation of Islam, SNCC, the SCLC, Martin Luther King Jr. personally, the Congress of Racial Equality, the Revolutionary Action Movement, and dozens of other organizations and individuals. The documented operations against King alone — wiretapping his phones for years, attempting to blackmail him using recorded extramarital conversations, sending him anonymous letters suggesting that he should commit suicide before his affairs were exposed — represent one of the most extraordinary abuses of federal law enforcement power in American history.

COINTELPRO was not publicly known during its active years. It was exposed by a burglary of the FBI's Media, Pennsylvania, field office by the Citizens' Commission to Investigate the FBI in March 1971, whose participants stole and distributed files documenting the program. The subsequent Senate investigations led by Senator Frank Church in 1975 and 1976 produced the fullest public accounting of the program's scope and methods. Subsequent Freedom of Information Act releases have continued to add detail to the historical record.

The historical significance of COINTELPRO extends beyond its immediate damage to specific organizations. It reveals the extent to which the federal government was willing to use unconstitutional means to suppress political organizing that challenged racial and class hierarchies, even organizing that was entirely legal. It demonstrates that the violence and instability of Black political organizations in the late 1960s and early 1970s was not simply the result of organizational failures or ideological extremism but was in significant part the product of deliberate government disruption. And it provides essential context for understanding the political vacuum in Black urban communities in the 1970s — a vacuum that other forces, including the drug economy, would move to fill.

The Murder of Fred Hampton

The most consequential single COINTELPRO operation against the Black Panther Party was the December 4, 1969, pre-dawn raid by Chicago police on the apartment of Illinois Panther chairman Fred Hampton, in which Hampton was killed in his sleep along with fellow Panther Mark Clark. Hampton was twenty-one years old. He had been a star athlete and student leader in his suburban high school, had joined the Panthers, and had risen rapidly through the organization's leadership. His extraordinary organizational ability was evident in the Rainbow Coalition he had built in Chicago — an alliance that included the Panthers, the Puerto Rican Young Lords, the Appalachian Young Patriots (a poor white organization), and even a negotiated truce between the Blackstone Rangers and the Disciples, two of the city's largest street organizations.

FBI informant William O'Neal, Hampton's own bodyguard, provided the FBI and Chicago police with a floor plan of Hampton's apartment, identifying where he slept. Evidence later revealed that Hampton had been drugged the night before the raid — autopsy findings showed very high levels of a barbiturate in his blood, and O'Neal has been identified as having placed the drug in Hampton's food. Fourteen officers participated in the raid. Hampton was shot twice in the head at close range while lying in bed. The official account — that the police had conducted a legal search and responded to fire from within the apartment — was comprehensively contradicted by physical evidence: the apartment's interior walls showed the overwhelming majority of bullet holes traveled from the outside inward, not from the inside outward.

Hampton's murder sent a message to Black political organizing that was unmistakable in its clarity: effective organizing would be met with lethal state violence. The FBI's internal memos on the operation, which described Hampton as a potential "messiah" who could "unify and electrify the militant Black nationalist movement," reveal that the concern was precisely Hampton's political effectiveness — his ability to build coalitions across racial lines, his success in addressing gang violence, his community organizing — rather than any criminal activity. He was killed because he was effective, because he was building the kind of cross-racial working-class coalition that the FBI had explicitly identified as the primary threat it sought to prevent.

The Scope of Destruction

The full scope of COINTELPRO's impact on Black political organizations is difficult to quantify with precision, but its general outline is clear. Between 1967 and 1972, the Black Panther Party lost hundreds of members and leaders to imprisonment on charges that ranged from legitimately serious to clearly fabricated. The organization spent an estimated $4 million in legal defense costs over those years — resources that came directly from the community programs that were the organization's most important work. The FBI's forged letters and agent provocateur operations contributed to the death of at least four Panthers and the violent split that effectively destroyed the national organization in 1971.

COINTELPRO's operations against SNCC were equally destructive. The organization that had done more than any other to build grassroots democracy in the most dangerous conditions imaginable — the rural Deep South in the early 1960s — was reduced to ineffectiveness by a combination of FBI infiltration, internal conflict exacerbated by COINTELPRO manipulation, and the exhaustion produced by years of operating under intense state repression. By the early 1970s, SNCC existed in name only.

The Congress on Racial Equality, which had moved toward Black nationalism under Floyd McKissick and Roy Innis, was similarly targeted. The Nation of Islam was infiltrated extensively and subjected to operations designed to maintain and exacerbate the split between Elijah Muhammad and Malcolm X that the FBI calculated would keep the organization from becoming a unified political force. The evidence that FBI manipulation contributed to Malcolm X's assassination — by maintaining the conditions of conflict between Malcolm and the NOI that motivated his killers — remains circumstantial but suggestive.

Martin Luther King Jr. was one of the most extensively surveilled individuals in American history under the FBI's COINTELPRO operations. Hoover's obsession with King was personal as well as institutional: he described King as "the most dangerous Negro in America" and pursued a decade-long campaign of surveillance, harassment, and attempted blackmail. The FBI's wiretapping of King began in 1963 and continued until his death in 1968. The anonymous letter sent to King in 1964 urging him to commit suicide before his extramarital affairs were exposed — with a recording of an alleged sex tape enclosed — was a product of COINTELPRO. That the FBI was conducting this illegal campaign against the nation's most prominent advocate of nonviolence and integration simultaneously with its campaign against the Panthers reveals that the program's target was Black political effectiveness generally, not any specific ideology or tactic.

Exposure and Consequences

COINTELPRO was operating in secrecy when a group of antiwar activists who called themselves the Citizens' Commission to Investigate the FBI broke into the FBI's Media, Pennsylvania, field office on March 8, 1971 — the night of the Muhammad Ali–Joe Frazier fight, when the bureau's agents were watching television — and removed every document they could find. They distributed these documents to journalists and members of Congress. The documents that reached the press — particularly the documents describing the COINTELPRO program and its methods — produced the first public awareness of the program's existence, though the full scope would not become clear until the Church Committee investigations four years later.

The Senate Select Committee to Study Governmental Operations with Respect to Intelligence Activities, chaired by Senator Frank Church of Idaho, conducted the fullest public investigation of COINTELPRO and related programs in 1975 and 1976. The Church Committee's reports documented in extraordinary detail the illegal methods the FBI had used, the specific operations conducted against specific organizations and individuals, and the complete disregard for constitutional rights that had characterized the program. The Church Committee's conclusion — that COINTELPRO represented "a secret war against those citizens it considered threats to the established order" — was not a partisan characterization but an accurate summary of the documented record.

The political consequences of the COINTELPRO exposure were significant but limited. Congress passed legislation tightening oversight of intelligence agencies and the FBI, and the courts expanded legal remedies for victims of illegal government surveillance and infiltration. Several civil lawsuits arising from COINTELPRO operations — most significantly the case arising from Fred Hampton's murder, which resulted in a $1.85 million settlement — produced findings and compensation that acknowledged the federal government's culpability. But no FBI official was ever prosecuted for the illegal activities documented by the Church Committee, and COINTELPRO director William Sullivan received a presidential pardon for his role in the program.

The most consequential legacy of COINTELPRO was the damage it did to Black political organizing in the crucial years between 1967 and 1975. The organizations that were destroyed or severely weakened — the Panthers, SNCC, the SCLC, the NOI, the Revolutionary Action Movement — were the primary vehicles through which Black urban communities were attempting to develop the collective political power that could have addressed the structural conditions of unemployment, housing inequality, educational deprivation, and police violence that the Kerner Commission had identified as the causes of urban unrest. Their destruction left those structural conditions unaddressed and left Black urban communities without the organized political capacity to demand that they be addressed.

Map Study

Urban Rebellions 1964–1968: Geography of Grievance

The urban rebellions of the 1960s followed a distinct geographic pattern that reflected the spatial distribution of the conditions — housing segregation, concentrated poverty, police conflict — that produced them. Students studying this period should be able to identify and discuss the following geographic features:

  • The northern and western concentration: Most major rebellions occurred in northern and western cities — New York, Los Angeles, Newark, Detroit, Chicago, Cleveland, Milwaukee — rather than in southern cities where Jim Crow operated as formal law. This distribution reflects the specific conditions of northern Black urban life: the promises and failures of the Great Migration, the de facto segregation that the civil rights movement's formal legal victories had not addressed, and the police militarism characteristic of northern departments.
  • Neighborhood concentration within cities: Within each city, rebellions were concentrated in specific neighborhoods — Harlem and Bedford-Stuyvesant in New York, Watts in Los Angeles, the Central Ward in Newark, the near-east side of Detroit, North Lawndale and West Garfield Park in Chicago. These neighborhoods were typically those most affected by redlining-driven disinvestment, concentrated public housing, high unemployment, and the most aggressive police conduct.
  • Commercial corridor targeting: Within rebellion neighborhoods, the destruction was concentrated in commercial corridors — stores and businesses along major streets — rather than in residential areas. This pattern reflects the specific economic grievances of communities served by businesses that charged high prices, hired few Black employees, and extracted wealth from the neighborhood without reinvesting in it.
  • Spread patterns: The most widespread rebellions — particularly those following King's assassination in April 1968 — spread rapidly from initial ignition points to encompass large areas of multiple neighborhoods, following the communication networks of urban Black communities and the distribution of pent-up grievance that was everywhere present in these conditions.
  • Spatial relationship to civil rights movement geography: Comparing the geography of the southern civil rights movement — Birmingham, Selma, Montgomery, Albany, Mississippi — with the geography of northern urban rebellions reveals the disconnect between the movement's primary geographic focus and the locations of the most concentrated Black urban poverty. This spatial disconnect is one dimension of the political challenge that Black Power addressed.

Primary Source Window

The Kerner Commission Report (1968): Official America Looks at Urban Crisis

Creator: National Advisory Commission on Civil Disorders  ·  Date: February 1968  ·  Type: Federal government report

Context: President Lyndon Johnson appointed the National Advisory Commission on Civil Disorders in July 1967, following the Newark and Detroit rebellions, to investigate the causes of the urban unrest. The commission, chaired by Illinois Governor Otto Kerner, conducted extensive hearings, field investigations, and social science research. Its report, published in February 1968, was one of the most candid and consequential public documents of the era — candid in its identification of systemic causes, consequential in the political reaction it generated.

The report's opening summary paraphrase captures its central conclusion: the United States was dividing into two separate and unequal societies, and this division was produced by white racism rather than Black pathology. The commission identified specific structural factors — unemployment, inadequate housing, inferior education, and police brutality — as the immediate causes of urban unrest, and traced these factors to the larger pattern of racial exclusion and inequality that had been constructed over generations.

The report concluded that the nation was "moving toward two societies, one Black, one white — separate and unequal" and warned that "to pursue our present course will involve the continuing polarization of the American community and, ultimately, the destruction of basic democratic values." It called for a "national commitment to true integration" and "unprecedented levels of funding and performance" in urban programs, and stated plainly that "white racism is essentially responsible for the explosive mixture which has been accumulating in our cities since the end of World War II."

The report's specific findings covered the immediate causes of specific rebellions (typically police incidents), the underlying conditions (unemployment, housing, education), the role of white racism in creating and maintaining those conditions, the inadequacy of existing programs, and the specific reforms required. The commission made over 150 specific recommendations for federal action.

The political response to the Kerner Report was instructive. President Johnson, who had commissioned it, reportedly never read it and refused to officially release it, though a paperback edition published commercially became a bestseller. The report's structural analysis was politically inconvenient: accepting its recommendations would have required a massive redistribution of federal resources and a direct confrontation with the political constituencies — white suburban voters, local political machines, business interests — that benefited from the existing arrangements. The Nixon administration, which took office in January 1969, explicitly rejected the report's framework and pursued a "law and order" approach to urban unrest that treated its symptoms rather than its causes.

Historical significance: The Kerner Report represents the most honest official reckoning with structural racism that the American federal government has produced. Its core analysis — that racial inequality is produced by structural conditions and deliberate policy choices, not by the cultural deficiencies of Black communities — has been consistently supported by subsequent historical and social science research, and consistently rejected by political figures who found its implications uncomfortable. The report's political irrelevance in the face of its intellectual accuracy is one of the period's most significant lessons about the relationship between evidence and power.

Historical Debate

Nonviolence vs. Self-Defense: Competing Strategies in the Black Freedom Movement

The question: The Black freedom movement of the 1960s was characterized by a sharp internal debate over political strategy: the nonviolent direct action approach associated with King and the SCLC, and the armed self-defense approach associated with the Panthers, the Deacons for Defense, and eventually most Black Power organizations. This was not simply a tactical disagreement. It reflected different analyses of the nature of racial oppression, different assessments of what white America and the American political system would respond to, and different visions of what Black liberation required. Evaluating these competing approaches requires engaging seriously with the historical record of both.

Interpretation A — The Case for Nonviolent Direct Action: The strategic case for nonviolent direct action rested on a sophisticated analysis of where the civil rights movement's political leverage lay. The movement could not win through direct confrontation with the military and police power of the American state. Its leverage depended on moral persuasion — on exposing the contradiction between American democratic ideology and the brutal reality of racial apartheid to audiences whose political support was necessary for legislative change. Nonviolent discipline was the tool that maintained this moral clarity: if movement participants responded to police violence with violence, the media narrative would shift from the story of peaceful protesters being brutalized to a story of violent disorder, and the political sympathy the movement needed would evaporate. The legislative achievements of 1964 and 1965 — genuinely consequential reforms — were won through this strategy, and it is difficult to argue that a more confrontational approach would have produced comparable results in the political environment of the early 1960s. King himself was not naive about the limits of nonviolence; he understood it as a strategic tool for a specific context, not a universal moral law. His critique of the urban rebellions focused on their strategic ineffectiveness — they were producing a law-and-order backlash that was undermining civil rights progress — rather than on their moral impurity.
Interpretation B — The Case for Community Self-Defense: The case for community self-defense, as articulated by Newton, Seale, and others, began with a different analysis of the problem. The nonviolent movement's leverage depended on the capacity of white liberal opinion and the national media to be shamed by the spectacle of Black suffering — a leverage that was structurally limited, because it required the continued goodwill of constituencies whose interests were not served by the structural transformations Black liberation required. More fundamentally, in communities experiencing daily police violence, the insistence on nonviolence toward those who were routinely brutalizing the community was experienced not as moral discipline but as submission. The Deacons for Defense and Justice — who provided armed protection for civil rights workers in Louisiana and Mississippi in the early 1960s — were not undermining the movement. They were keeping its organizers alive. The Panthers' armed patrols, by their own account and by the testimony of Oakland residents, did reduce police misconduct in the neighborhoods they monitored. And the right to armed self-defense was a constitutional right that white Americans routinely exercised; the argument that Black people had to waive it as the price of political sympathy was itself a form of racial double standard.
Interpretation C — The Synthesis: Context, Strategy, and Historical Contingency: A third interpretation, supported by much recent historical scholarship, argues that framing this as a binary debate between nonviolence and armed self-defense obscures more than it reveals. In practice, the movement was never purely nonviolent in the absolute sense: nearly every major civil rights organization in the Deep South operated under armed protection of some kind, and leaders like King traveled with armed bodyguards. The strategic question was not whether Black people had a right to self-defense (virtually everyone agreed they did) but what tactics were most effective in what contexts for what goals. Nonviolent direct action was enormously effective for winning formal legislative rights in the specific political context of the early-to-mid 1960s, when the federal government was susceptible to moral pressure and when the contrast between peaceful protesters and violent police was maximally legible. Community self-defense organizations were effective at different tasks — reducing police brutality in specific neighborhoods, building community solidarity, organizing around immediate material needs — in the different political context of northern Black urban communities in the late 1960s. The tragedy is not that one strategy was right and the other wrong, but that the federal government used COINTELPRO to destroy the organizations practicing both, while doing nothing to address the structural conditions that made both necessary.

Evidence and further inquiry: Students assessing this debate should examine the specific contexts in which each approach was deployed, the specific outcomes that resulted, and the ways in which COINTELPRO complicated any honest assessment of what might have been achieved. The historical literature on this question includes Clayborne Carson's In Struggle: SNCC and the Black Awakening of the 1960s, Ward Churchill and Jim Vander Wall's Agents of Repression, Charles Payne's I've Got the Light of Freedom, and Joshua Bloom and Waldo Martin Jr.'s Black Against Empire: The History and Politics of the Black Panther Party.

Multiple Perspectives

The Movement in the City: Multiple Perspectives on Civil Rights and Black Power

From the perspective of young Black men in northern urban neighborhoods: For young Black men growing up in Harlem, Watts, the South Side of Chicago, or West Oakland in the early 1960s, the southern civil rights movement's message — hold still, dress well, absorb violence with dignity, appeal to the conscience of white America — felt distant from and insufficient for their daily reality of police harassment, unemployment, and neighborhood deterioration. The nonviolent movement had won formal rights in places they did not live, addressing forms of discrimination they did not face in the same way. What they faced was the constant humiliation of stop-and-frisk, the systematic exclusion from jobs and housing that was effected through informal practices rather than "Whites Only" signs, and the absence of any political organization that spoke directly to their specific experience. When Stokely Carmichael shouted "Black Power," many of them heard something that addressed their reality in a way that "We Shall Overcome" had not.
From the perspective of civil rights organization leaders: For King, Roy Wilkins, and other leaders of the established civil rights organizations, the emergence of Black Power politics represented both a genuine challenge to their political analysis and a serious strategic threat to the legislative agenda they were pursuing. The "law and order" backlash was real: white suburban voters who had been persuaded by the moral clarity of nonviolent confrontation with southern segregation were much less sympathetic to what they perceived as Black militancy in northern cities. The risk of losing the political coalition that had produced the 1964 and 1965 legislation, before that legislation had been fully implemented, was genuine and serious. At the same time, many civil rights leaders privately acknowledged the accuracy of the critique that formal legal equality was insufficient without economic justice, even as they disagreed about the appropriate political response.
From the perspective of Black women in political organizations: Black women were central to every dimension of the civil rights and Black Power movements — as organizers, strategists, financial managers, community liaisons, and often the people doing the work of sustaining organizations while men occupied the public-facing leadership positions. Ella Baker's organizational genius made SCLC and SNCC function; Fannie Lou Hamer's courage and voice were SNCC's most powerful organizing assets in Mississippi; Kathleen Cleaver and Elaine Brown were among the Black Panthers' most effective leaders. But the sexism of many male leaders in both movements was real and pervasive. The Black Power movement's rhetorical emphasis on Black male dignity sometimes translated into organizational cultures that reproduced the gender hierarchies of the broader society. Black women in these organizations navigated this reality with varying degrees of accommodation and resistance, and their experiences reveal dimensions of these movements that are often invisible in accounts that focus on the public male leadership.
From the perspective of white liberal allies: White liberals who had supported the civil rights movement through the early 1960s — providing money, political support, and in some cases physical presence — responded to Black Power with a mixture of confusion, hurt, and political retreat. The explicit exclusion of white members from SNCC, the Panthers' rhetorical hostility toward white America, and the movement's general shift toward a politics that did not require or request white sympathy were experienced by many white liberals as a betrayal of the coalition that had produced the legislative achievements of 1964 and 1965. What this perspective often missed was the degree to which Black Power's turn inward was a response to the limits of what white liberal support had actually delivered, and the ways in which the movement's dependence on white liberal goodwill had constrained its ability to make more radical demands.
From the perspective of the federal government and law enforcement: The FBI's characterization of Black Power organizations as "Black Nationalist Hate Groups" and threats to "internal security" was a political designation, not an analytical one. The Panthers' actual criminal activity — as opposed to their constitutionally protected political activity — was relatively modest compared to many organizations that the FBI left alone. What made them dangerous in Hoover's analysis was their political effectiveness: their ability to build community loyalty, their critique of the political and economic system, their explicit challenge to police power. The federal government's response to Black Power was not primarily a law enforcement response to crime; it was a political response to effective organizing that threatened the racial and economic status quo. Understanding this distinction is essential for evaluating the COINTELPRO history honestly.
Everyday Life and Social History

Living Through the Movement Era: Ordinary Life in the Political 1960s

For the millions of Black urban residents who were not organizational leaders, not FBI informants, and not participants in the dramatic events that fill the historical record of this period, the civil rights and Black Power era registered in daily life in ways that were simultaneously immediate and subtle. Understanding this ordinary dimension of the period is essential for seeing the movement whole rather than through the lens of its most dramatic moments.

The everyday experience of civil rights progress in northern cities was often anticlimactic. The Civil Rights Act of 1964 prohibited employment discrimination, but the employer who had never hired Black workers in skilled positions continued to explain that no qualified Black applicants had applied. The fair housing law passed after King's assassination prohibited housing discrimination, but the real estate agent still steered Black customers away from white neighborhoods, and the bank still denied mortgages for properties in redlined areas through mechanisms that were difficult to document and nearly impossible to prosecute. The formal legal change was real, but the enforcement mechanisms were weak, underfunded, and subject to the same institutional resistance that had produced discrimination in the first place.

For young Black men in cities like Oakland, Newark, Detroit, and Chicago in the late 1960s, the Black Power movement represented something immediate and personal before it was a national political program. It was a way of carrying oneself — with a natural rather than a processed hairstyle, with a language of pride and dignity rather than deference, with an assertion of presence and right that the preceding generation had often found safer to suppress. The cultural expressions of Black Power — the Afro, the dashiki, the clenched fist, the reading of Malcolm X or Frantz Fanon — were not merely fashion statements or intellectual postures. They were experiments in a new self-presentation by young people who had grown up in a society organized around their psychological subordination as well as their economic exploitation.

For the children who received free breakfasts in Panther programs, the organizational affiliation was less important than the food. For the mother who brought her sick child to a Panther-run free clinic because she had no insurance and no car to reach a distant hospital, the political analysis was secondary to the medical care. For the elderly resident of a Panther-monitored neighborhood who noticed that the police officers who had previously stopped and searched young men on her street now stopped less often, the tactical debate about armed self-defense was less relevant than the observable change in her daily environment.

The FBI's COINTELPRO operations registered in everyday life primarily through the climate of suspicion and fear they created in political organizations and, through those organizations, in the communities they served. When leaders were arrested, when chapters were raided, when organizers were killed, the ordinary people who had been receiving services or developing political consciousness absorbed the message that organizing was dangerous and that the state's repression was both overwhelming and relentless. This message had a chilling effect on political participation that extended far beyond the specific organizations targeted. Communities that had been developing the capacity for collective political action absorbed the lessons of Hampton's murder and the Panther chapter raids, and many concluded, reasonably if not inevitably, that overt political organization of the kind the Panthers represented was not survivable.

The political vacuum that followed — the period in the mid-1970s when the major Black Power organizations had been destroyed or severely weakened, when the civil rights movement's momentum had stalled, and when the economic dislocation of deindustrialization was beginning to hollow out the industrial employment base that had provided working-class Black urban stability — was experienced in ordinary life as a loss of horizon, a shrinking of possibility. The young men who had looked to the Panthers or to Malcolm X for a framework that addressed their reality found those frameworks systematically dismantled. The churches and mutual aid organizations remained, but their reach was limited in communities facing the accelerating material crisis of deindustrialization. In this context, other forms of organization — with other purposes and other consequences — would find their opportunity.

Constitutional Connection

COINTELPRO, the First Amendment, and the Limits of Constitutional Protection

The FBI's COINTELPRO operations raise fundamental constitutional questions about the First Amendment's protection of political speech, assembly, and association — questions that remain urgently relevant for understanding the relationship between political organizing and state power in the United States.

The First Amendment protects freedom of speech, press, religion, assembly, and petition. These protections are foundational to democratic politics because they protect the ability of citizens to organize collectively, criticize the government, and work for political change — including change that those in power find threatening. The Supreme Court's interpretive tradition from Schenck v. United States (1919) through Brandenburg v. Ohio (1969) generally holds that the First Amendment protects even radical or revolutionary speech unless it constitutes a direct and immediate incitement to specific illegal action.

COINTELPRO operated in systematic violation of these principles. The organizations it targeted — the Black Panther Party, SNCC, the SCLC, the Nation of Islam — were engaged primarily in protected First Amendment activity: organizing communities, running programs, speaking at rallies, publishing newspapers, advocating political views. The FBI's characterization of these activities as threats to "internal security" was a political judgment masquerading as a legal determination. The tactics COINTELPRO used — forged letters, planted stories, infiltration, agent provocateurs — were not legitimate law enforcement responses to criminal activity. They were unconstitutional interference with political expression and association by a federal law enforcement agency acting as a political police force.

The constitutional implications extend beyond the specific organizations targeted. COINTELPRO demonstrated that formal First Amendment protection is contingent on the political will of the government to honor it, and that in conditions of political threat, the government is capable of sustaining systematic unconstitutional operations for years without public accountability. The program operated for more than a decade before its exposure, and even after exposure produced no criminal prosecutions. This history raises the question that constitutional theorists have always found difficult to answer: how are constitutional rights enforced against a government that chooses to violate them?

The Fourth Amendment's protection against unreasonable searches and seizures was also systematically violated by COINTELPRO's wiretapping, mail opening, and physical surveillance. The due process guarantees of the Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments were violated by prosecutions based on fabricated evidence. The Sixth Amendment's right to a fair trial was undermined when prosecutors withheld evidence of FBI manipulation that should have been disclosed to defendants. COINTELPRO was, in short, a comprehensive assault on the constitutional rights of Black political organizers, conducted by the federal agency most directly responsible for enforcing those rights.

The post-COINTELPRO legal reforms — the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act, the strengthened congressional oversight of intelligence agencies, the Justice Department's internal review guidelines — represented genuine but limited responses to the program's documented abuses. The fundamental question of whether a state committed to racial inequality will consistently honor the constitutional rights of those who challenge that inequality remains as relevant today as it was during COINTELPRO's active years.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Black Political Organization: From Reconstruction to the Black Power Era

What Changed

  • The legal framework of formal racial discrimination was largely dismantled through the Civil Rights Act of 1964, the Voting Rights Act of 1965, and subsequent fair housing legislation, representing the completion of the Reconstruction-era agenda that had been destroyed by the Compromise of 1877
  • The geographic center of Black political organizing shifted from the rural South to the urban North and West, as the Great Migration produced a majority-urban Black population whose political conditions and political demands differed fundamentally from those of the southern movement
  • The dominant organizational form of Black politics shifted from church-based civil rights organizations to a wider range of secular political organizations — street-level groups, community organizations, Black political parties, Black student unions, community development corporations
  • The ideological framework of Black politics diversified significantly, moving from the relative consensus around integration and nonviolence of the early 1960s to a much more varied landscape including Black nationalism, revolutionary Marxism, cultural nationalism, Pan-Africanism, and community control politics
  • The explicit assertion of Black identity as a source of political pride and solidarity — encapsulated in "Black is Beautiful" and the Black Power slogan — represented a cultural shift from the respectability politics that had characterized much of the civil rights mainstream

What Stayed the Same

  • The fundamental pattern of Black community building followed by state suppression — established in the Reconstruction era with the burning of Black churches and the violence of the Klan, recurring in the COINTELPRO era with assassinations and infiltration — continued to characterize the relationship between Black political organizing and American state power
  • The material conditions of Black urban life — housing segregation, concentrated poverty, inadequate public services, police violence — remained largely unchanged despite the formal legal victories of the civil rights era, demonstrating the durability of structural inequality in the face of legal reform
  • The organizational form of the mutual support institution — the community-based organization providing collective services that formal institutions denied — continued from the mutual aid society through the church through the Black Panther Party's survival programs
  • The federal government's willingness to use any available means to suppress Black political organizing that threatened the racial status quo — from the military suppression of Reconstruction to COINTELPRO — remained a consistent feature of American political history
  • The centrality of the church to Black community life continued, even as secular organizations expanded their role; the civil rights movement was deeply rooted in Black church culture, and many Black Power organizations maintained relationships with Black religious communities

The significance of the continuity: The most important continuity across this period is the structural one: the persistence of racial inequality despite formal legal change. Each major cycle — Reconstruction's achievements and their destruction, the Great Migration's opportunities and the segregation that contained them, the civil rights movement's legislative victories and the COINTELPRO-assisted destruction of the movements that might have converted formal rights into material equality — follows a similar pattern. Understanding this pattern, and understanding it as produced by specific political choices rather than as an inevitable feature of American society, is the central analytical task this textbook asks its readers to undertake.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has examined one of the most turbulent and consequential periods in American urban political history — the roughly two decades from the mid-1950s through the mid-1970s during which the civil rights movement transformed the formal legal framework of American racial life, urban rebellions expressed the depth of Black urban anger at conditions that formal legal reform had not addressed, the Black Power movement proposed a comprehensive alternative analysis and program for Black liberation, and the federal government responded to effective Black political organizing with an illegal counterintelligence program that systematically destroyed the most dynamic Black political organizations of the era.

The civil rights movement's achievements were genuine and historically significant. The Civil Rights Act of 1964 and the Voting Rights Act of 1965 dismantled the formal legal apparatus of Jim Crow and created the legal framework within which subsequent civil rights enforcement would operate. The movement's success in exposing the violence of southern segregation to a national and international audience, through the strategy of nonviolent direct action, produced political leverage that was real and that was translated into legislative victories through years of organized effort by hundreds of thousands of ordinary people and their organizations. These achievements must not be minimized by the knowledge of what they did not accomplish.

But the civil rights movement's formal legal victories did not address the material conditions of Black urban life in northern and western cities — the housing segregation enforced through redlining and restrictive covenants, the employment discrimination that excluded Black workers from the most skilled and best-paid positions, the underfunded schools that denied Black children the educational resources available to their white counterparts in neighboring suburbs, and the policing practices that treated Black neighborhoods as occupied territories requiring constant surveillance and control. The urban rebellions of 1964 through 1968 were political expressions of these unaddressed conditions, driven by specific grievances about police brutality in communities that had run out of conventional channels for demanding accountability.

The Kerner Commission's 1968 report offered the most honest official analysis of these conditions that the American political system has produced: that "white racism" was responsible for the structural conditions producing urban unrest, and that addressing those conditions required massive federal investment in urban communities. This analysis was politically ignored. The federal government chose law enforcement escalation over structural investment, a choice that produced the conditions for the subsequent decades of the War on Drugs and mass incarceration.

The Black Power movement that emerged from the mid-1960s represented a fundamental critique of the limitations of formal civil rights reform and a program for Black self-determination and community control. It was not a single ideology or a single organization but a broad political tendency that encompassed cultural nationalism, revolutionary Marxism, community control politics, and electoral organizing. What unified it was the insistence that Black communities had the right and responsibility to control their own institutions and define their own futures — an insistence that built on the long tradition of Black community institution-building that this textbook has traced from the Reconstruction era forward.

The Black Panther Party was the most visible and, in many respects, the most significant of the Black Power organizations of this period. Its combination of armed self-defense, community service programs, and revolutionary rhetoric reached communities that the mainstream civil rights organizations had not effectively addressed, and its rapid national spread reflected the depth of resonance between its program and the specific experiences of Black youth in northern and western cities. The Panthers' free breakfast programs, health clinics, legal aid offices, and community schools served real needs and built genuine community loyalty in ways that demonstrated the practical importance of survival programs as the foundation for political organizing.

COINTELPRO's systematic destruction of the Black Panther Party and other Black political organizations represents one of the most consequential and least adequately reckon-with episodes in American political history. The FBI's operations — infiltration, forged documents, fabricated evidence, coordination with local law enforcement, and in the most extreme cases direct participation in murder — were comprehensive, illegal, and deliberate. They were directed not at criminal activity but at legal political organizing that the FBI's director considered a threat to the racial status quo. Their consequences — the imprisonment of leaders, the dissolution of organizations, the political demoralization of communities, the physical elimination of individuals — shaped the trajectory of Black political life for a generation.

The political vacuum created by COINTELPRO's destruction of effective Black political organizations was not filled by nothing. It was filled by the drug economy, the street organizations that would later be examined in subsequent chapters, the politics of survival that replaced the politics of transformation in communities that had been systematically deprived of both the organizational resources and the structural conditions for collective political action. The relationship between COINTELPRO and the subsequent rise of large street organizations as primary organizational forms in some Black urban neighborhoods is not a simple or deterministic one — many other factors, including deindustrialization and deliberate drug market expansion, were also at work. But the destruction of the most effective vehicles for Black political organizing in these communities created the conditions in which other organizational forms would flourish.

The constitutional questions raised by COINTELPRO — about the First Amendment's protection of political speech and assembly, the Fourth Amendment's protection against unreasonable searches and seizures, the due process rights of those subjected to fabricated prosecutions — remain urgently relevant for understanding the relationship between Black political organizing and American state power. The formal constitutional protections exist. Their enforcement depends on the political will of the institutions responsible for honoring them, and in conditions of political threat, that will has repeatedly proven insufficient.

The period examined in this chapter also illustrates the relationship between formal rights and material equality that has been a central theme of this textbook since Chapter 1. The formal legal victories of the civil rights era were real, but they could not by themselves produce the structural transformation that would have been required to convert them into material equality. That transformation would have required — at minimum — the economic investment recommended by the Kerner Commission, sustained enforcement of the fair housing and employment discrimination laws, meaningful political representation for Black urban communities, and an end to the policing practices that treated Black neighborhoods as zones of occupation. None of these things were provided. The pattern of formal rights granted and material conditions unchanged, established in the Reconstruction era, continued through the civil rights era and its aftermath.

This chapter's most important lesson for the history this textbook traces may be the simplest: the Black political organizations of the civil rights and Black Power era were not aberrations, pathologies, or threats to social order. They were expressions of the same fundamental human impulse that produced the Reconstruction-era church, the mutual aid society, and the fraternal lodge: the impulse to build collective structures of support, protection, identity, and dignity in conditions where the surrounding society was actively hostile to Black survival and flourishing. The state's response to these organizations — which has taken the form of KKK violence in the Reconstruction era and FBI assassination in the COINTELPRO era — has been one of the most consistent features of American racial politics. Understanding that consistency is the foundation for understanding why the conditions it has produced have been so durable.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Agent provocateur
A government operative who infiltrates a political organization in order to encourage illegal activity that can then be used to prosecute the organization's members, or to create conflict within or between organizations through misinformation and deliberately provocative actions. COINTELPRO made extensive use of agent provocateurs in Black political organizations.
Black nationalism
A political philosophy emphasizing the common bonds of Black people across national boundaries and the goal of Black self-determination and collective control of economic, political, and cultural institutions. Black nationalism has diverse expressions, from the Pan-Africanism of Marcus Garvey through the cultural nationalism of the Black Power era to contemporary Afrocentric thought.
Black Power
A political slogan and program that emerged from the mid-1960s Black freedom movement, emphasizing Black self-determination, community control, economic independence, and Black cultural pride. Black Power was not a single unified ideology but a broad political tendency that encompassed several distinct currents, united by the insistence on Black autonomy rather than integration into existing white-dominated structures on white terms.
COINTELPRO
The FBI's Counter Intelligence Program, a series of covert, illegal operations conducted between 1956 and 1971 against domestic political organizations. COINTELPRO used infiltration, forged documents, fabricated evidence, coordination with local law enforcement, and other illegal methods to destroy organizations the FBI considered threats to internal security, including the Black Panther Party, SNCC, the SCLC, the Nation of Islam, and other Black political organizations.
Community control
The political program associated with the Black Power era that called for Black communities to exercise direct control over the institutions — schools, police, hospitals, businesses — serving their neighborhoods. Community control advocates argued that institutions controlled by external authorities served those authorities' interests rather than community needs, and that genuine self-determination required community governance of community institutions.
Community self-defense
The organized effort of a community to protect its members from violence, particularly violence by the state or by groups the state refuses to protect them from. In the Black Power context, community self-defense was associated with the Black Panther Party's armed monitoring of police activity and its assertion of the legal right of Black communities to defend themselves from police brutality.
Cultural nationalism
A form of Black nationalism that emphasizes African cultural identity, heritage, and expression as the foundation for Black political consciousness and community building. Cultural nationalism was associated with figures like Maulana Karenga and influenced the Black Arts Movement's emphasis on specifically Black cultural expression as both artistically valuable and politically necessary.
Decolonization
The process by which European colonial powers withdrew from their colonies in Africa, Asia, and elsewhere, particularly in the late 1950s and 1960s. Decolonization provided the Black Power movement with both a political framework — the concept of internal colonialism applied to Black America — and a model of liberation through mass mobilization and self-determination.
Direct action
Political tactics that directly challenge or disrupt an unjust practice or policy, rather than working through conventional political channels. In the civil rights movement, direct action included sit-ins, boycotts, freedom rides, marches, and other forms of nonviolent confrontation with segregated institutions. Direct action was designed to force a response from those in power and to make the moral stakes of the confrontation visible to the broader public.
Free Breakfast Program
The Black Panther Party's free breakfast for school children program, launched in 1969, which fed tens of thousands of children in cities across the country before school each morning. The program was one of the Panthers' most successful community service initiatives and was cited by FBI Director Hoover as a dangerous Panther recruitment and propaganda tool, revealing the FBI's concern that the program was building community loyalty to the Panthers.
Internal colonialism
A theoretical framework, drawn from anticolonial thought and particularly from Frantz Fanon, that analyzes Black urban communities as domestic colonies — areas that are economically exploited, politically subjugated, and culturally dominated by an external power structure. The internal colonialism framework was influential in Black Power politics and provided the analytical basis for the Panthers' and SNCC's characterization of Black liberation as a form of anticolonial struggle.
Kerner Commission
The National Advisory Commission on Civil Disorders, appointed by President Johnson after the Newark and Detroit rebellions of 1967, whose 1968 report concluded that "white racism" was the fundamental cause of urban unrest and called for massive federal investment in urban communities. The report's structural analysis was politically ignored; its recommendations were not implemented by either the Johnson or Nixon administrations.
Nation of Islam
The Black Muslim religious and political organization founded in the 1930s and led during the postwar period by Elijah Muhammad, whose message of Black self-reliance, racial pride, and moral discipline reached millions of Black urban Americans. The NOI was the organizational home of Malcolm X before his 1964 break with Elijah Muhammad, and was itself an extensive target of COINTELPRO operations.
Nonviolent direct action
The political strategy associated with Martin Luther King Jr. and the SCLC, in which protesters deliberately exposed themselves to violence without responding in kind, in order to make the moral stakes of the confrontation visible to a national audience and generate political pressure for legislative change. Nonviolent direct action was the primary strategic approach of the southern civil rights movement and produced its major legislative victories of 1964 and 1965.
Pan-Africanism
The political philosophy emphasizing the common cultural heritage and political solidarity of African people throughout the African diaspora. Pan-Africanism, associated historically with figures like Marcus Garvey, W.E.B. Du Bois, and Kwame Nkrumah, influenced Black Power politics through its framework of global Black solidarity and its vision of liberation as inseparable from the liberation of Africa from colonialism.
Political prisoner
A person imprisoned primarily because of their political beliefs or activities rather than for genuine criminal conduct. The Black Power movement used this term to describe leaders like Huey Newton, whose prosecution they argued was politically motivated, and more broadly to describe the systematic use of the criminal justice system to suppress Black political organizing. The concept of the political prisoner connected the American carceral system to the broader history of state repression of Black politics.
Rainbow Coalition
The cross-racial political alliance built by Fred Hampton of the Illinois Black Panther Party in Chicago, uniting the Panthers with the Puerto Rican Young Lords, the Appalachian Young Patriots (a poor white Appalachian organization), and other groups in a coalition based on class solidarity rather than racial identity. Hampton's murder by Chicago police and the FBI in December 1969 destroyed this promising organizing initiative.
Revolutionary nationalism
A form of Black Power politics that combined Black nationalist emphasis on racial self-determination with Marxist class analysis, arguing that Black liberation required not merely the end of racial discrimination but the transformation of the capitalist economic system that organized racial oppression. The Black Panther Party's political program combined revolutionary nationalism with community service organizing.
SNCC (Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee)
The student organization founded in 1960 following the Greensboro sit-ins, which served as the grassroots democratic wing of the civil rights movement and eventually moved furthest toward Black Power politics. SNCC's voter registration and community organizing work in Mississippi and other Deep South states, conducted at considerable personal risk, produced some of the most significant organizing of the civil rights era before the organization was weakened by internal conflict and COINTELPRO operations.
Survival programs
The term used by Huey Newton and the Black Panther Party to describe their community service programs — free breakfasts, health clinics, legal aid, clothing giveaways, and political education — which Newton theorized as "survival pending revolution": addressing immediate community survival needs as both ethically necessary and politically necessary for sustaining the organizing that structural transformation required.
Ten-Point Program
The founding document of the Black Panther Party, drafted by Huey Newton and Bobby Seale in 1966, which articulated the organization's demands: full employment, decent housing, education that "teaches us our true history," an end to police brutality, freedom for Black men in prison, and a UN-supervised plebiscite on the question of Black self-determination. The Ten-Point Program combined immediate practical demands with broader revolutionary rhetoric in a document designed both to organize around specific grievances and to frame those grievances within a larger political analysis.
Third Worldism
The political framework that connected the Black freedom struggle in America to the global struggle against colonialism and imperialism in Africa, Asia, and Latin America. Third Worldism, influenced by Frantz Fanon's The Wretched of the Earth, positioned Black Americans as a colonized people within the imperial metropole and drew strategic and intellectual resources from anticolonial movements abroad.
Urban rebellion
The term used by many historians to describe the large-scale civil disturbances in Black urban communities in the 1960s, which were often called "riots" in contemporary and popular usage. The terminology is not merely semantic: calling them rebellions acknowledges their political character and the structural grievances that produced them, while calling them riots emphasizes their disorderly and criminal aspects. Both elements were present; how one names the phenomenon shapes how one understands it.
Vanguard party
A political organization that conceives of itself as the most politically advanced segment of a broader movement, with the responsibility of providing theoretical leadership and organizational direction to that movement. The concept, drawn from Leninist political theory, influenced the Panthers' understanding of their organizational role, though it also created tensions with community organizing approaches that emphasized grassroots democratic participation rather than vanguard leadership.

Major People and Places

Identification & Significance

Martin Luther King Jr. (1929–1968)
The dominant public figure of the southern civil rights movement whose commitment to nonviolent direct action and integrationist politics defined the mainstream movement's public image and strategy. King's assassination in Memphis triggered urban rebellions in more than 100 cities. His actual political positions — particularly his late-career critique of capitalism and Vietnam and his Poor People's Campaign — were more radical than the simplified version preserved in popular memory.
Malcolm X / El-Hajj Malik El-Shabazz (1925–1965)
The most influential Black nationalist voice of the early 1960s, whose critique of integrationism and insistence on Black self-determination became foundational to the Black Power movement that followed his assassination. His Autobiography remains one of the most important documents in African American political thought.
Huey P. Newton (1942–1989)
Co-founder of the Black Panther Party, theoretician of the Panthers' "survival pending revolution" strategy, and the most significant Black Power organizational leader of the late 1960s. Newton's imprisonment from 1967 to 1970 made him a focus of the Free Huey campaign that expanded the Panthers' national profile even as it consumed organizational resources.
Fred Hampton (1948–1969)
Illinois Black Panther chairman whose extraordinary organizing ability produced the Rainbow Coalition in Chicago — a cross-racial working-class alliance of remarkable promise. His murder by Chicago police coordinated by the FBI in December 1969, while he lay drugged in his bed at age twenty-one, was one of the most consequential acts of state violence against a Black political leader in American history.
Stokely Carmichael / Kwame Ture (1941–1998)
SNCC chairman whose articulation of "Black Power" at a 1966 Mississippi rally marked the most public expression of the movement's generational and ideological shift. Carmichael's charisma, rhetorical skill, and intellectual framework made him one of the most influential Black political voices of the late 1960s.
J. Edgar Hoover (1895–1972)
FBI Director and COINTELPRO architect whose obsessive campaign against Black political organizations violated the constitutional rights of hundreds of people and directly contributed to the destruction of the most dynamic Black political organizations of the era. Hoover's conviction that Martin Luther King Jr. was "the most dangerous Negro in America" summarizes his approach to Black political life.
Black Panther Party
The organization founded in Oakland in 1966 that became the most visible Black Power organization of the late 1960s, combining armed self-defense with extensive community service programs and revolutionary political education. Systematically destroyed by COINTELPRO, the Panthers nonetheless left lasting marks on Black political culture, community organizing strategies, and the communities they served.
Kerner Commission
The federal investigative commission that produced the most honest official reckoning with structural racism in American history — a reckoning that was immediately and comprehensively ignored by the political establishment that had commissioned it, demonstrating the gap between accurate diagnosis and political will to address structural inequality.
Oakland, California
The birthplace of the Black Panther Party, whose specific conditions — wartime migration history, housing segregation, police brutality, industrial decline — produced the specific organizational response that became the Panthers. Oakland's Black community history demonstrates how local conditions shape political formations in specific ways.
Watts, Los Angeles
The neighborhood whose 1965 rebellion announced the depth of Black urban anger in the West and established the political context for the Black Power movement that followed. Watts exemplifies the conditions — migration-driven population concentration, housing discrimination, police militarism, unemployment — that produced the urban rebellions of the 1960s.
Fannie Lou Hamer (1917–1977)
SNCC organizer and Mississippi Freedom Democratic Party leader whose courage and moral authority were the southern movement's most powerful organizing assets. Hamer's testimony at the 1964 Democratic National Convention — describing the violence used to prevent her from voting in Mississippi — and the MFDP's subsequent rejection by the Democratic Party establishment crystallized for many activists the limits of working within conventional political channels.
Ella Baker (1903–1986)
One of the most important organizational figures in the civil rights movement, Baker was the SCLC's executive director and the primary force behind SNCC's founding. Her organizational philosophy — emphasizing grassroots democratic participation and leadership development at the community level, rather than charismatic top-down leadership — was a significant counterweight to the personality-centered approach of the male leadership, and her influence on SNCC's organizational culture shaped the movement's most successful grassroots organizing.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter has examined a period that stands as one of the decisive turning points in American urban history — a period when the structural conditions of Black urban life came to the foreground of national political consciousness, when the most significant Black political organizing since Reconstruction was built and then systematically destroyed, and when the political choices made by the federal government shaped the conditions of Black urban life for the next generation and beyond. Understanding this period is not optional for anyone trying to understand the American city, because the conditions it produced — the political vacuum in Black urban communities, the escalating drug economy, the mass incarceration that would follow — are legible only against the background of what happened here.

The most important lesson of this chapter may be the most uncomfortable one for a society that prefers to understand its racial history as a story of progressive improvement: that the most effective Black political organizing of the mid-twentieth century was not allowed to develop to its potential because the American state chose to destroy it. The Black Panther Party and the organizations that shared its era were not perfect — they had real internal problems, real ideological conflicts, real organizational failures. But they were also building something genuinely significant: community institutions that served real needs, political analyses that addressed real conditions, coalitions that crossed racial lines on the basis of class solidarity. COINTELPRO did not simply accelerate the inevitable decline of these organizations. It deliberately and illegally murdered, imprisoned, and demoralized the people doing this work, and it succeeded in destroying what they were building before it could develop into something more durable.

The political vacuum that followed is the immediate context for the chapters that follow this one in the textbook — the deindustrialization that hollowed out the economic base of Black urban communities, the crack cocaine epidemic that produced the street economy of the 1980s, the mass incarceration that swept hundreds of thousands of young Black men into the prison system. These developments did not occur in a political vacuum; they occurred in the specific political landscape created by COINTELPRO's destruction of effective Black political organizing. A community that had been developing the capacity for collective political action had that capacity deliberately dismantled; the subsequent economic and social crises fell on communities that had been stripped of the organizational resources to respond to them collectively.

This is not a story about inevitable decline or cultural pathology. It is a story about specific political choices — the choice to suppress organizing rather than address structural inequality, the choice to escalate police power rather than invest in communities, the choice to criminalize poverty rather than provide economic alternatives. Those choices were made by specific people in specific institutional contexts, and they could have been made differently. The Kerner Commission said exactly what would need to be done to produce different outcomes; its recommendations were ignored not because they were wrong but because implementing them would have required confronting the racial and class interests that the existing political order served. That political calculation, as much as any other single factor, shaped the conditions that subsequent chapters of this textbook examine.

The civil rights and Black Power era also speaks directly to the central analytical question this textbook poses: the relationship between formal rights and substantive equality, between legal victories and material conditions. The formal rights won in 1964 and 1965 were real and remain significant. The material conditions of Black urban communities in 1975 were, in many respects, worse than they had been in 1955 — not because legal equality had failed as an idea, but because formal legal equality had been achieved without the structural economic investment and political representation that would have been required to convert it into material equality. This pattern — of formal rights that do not produce material change because the structural conditions that produce inequality remain in place — has been the dominant pattern of African American political history from Reconstruction forward, and it is the pattern that this chapter's history instantiates in its most concentrated and consequential form.

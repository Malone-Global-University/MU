Part III — Politics, Crisis, and Urban Transformation
8

Deindustrialization and the Urban Crisis

From Migration to Fragmentation: The Urban History of Black Street Organizations

Chapter Opening

"The mills are gone, the jobs are gone, and after a while the people start to go too. What's left is the neighborhood — or what used to be the neighborhood." — Detroit resident, quoted in oral history collection, circa 1985

In the summer of 1967, Detroit exploded. Over five days in late July, the most destructive urban uprising in twentieth-century American history tore through the city's Black neighborhoods on the Near East Side. Forty-three people died. More than 1,100 were injured. Approximately 2,000 buildings were burned, and some 5,000 residents were left homeless. The National Guard and regular Army units deployed tanks and armored personnel carriers through residential streets. Federal troops — the 82nd and 101st Airborne Divisions, units that had served in Vietnam — occupied an American city. The images broadcast across the country seemed to confirm white Americans' worst fears about urban Black life: the city, it appeared, was at war with itself.

What the images obscured was the history that had produced them. Detroit in 1967 was not exploding because something had gone wrong with its Black community. It was exploding because something had gone systematically wrong with the city's political economy — and because what had gone wrong had fallen most heavily on the Black community that had been the last hired into Detroit's industrial economy and was being first pushed out of it. The rebellion of 1967 was the product of decades of racial exclusion in housing and employment, of systematic disinvestment in Black neighborhoods, of police brutality and contempt that had accumulated through years of documented abuse without accountability, and of the beginning of a broader economic transformation that would, over the following two decades, hollow out the industrial city in ways that neither city officials nor community leaders yet fully understood. The rebellion was, in a real sense, the announcement that something was breaking — something much larger than any single police raid or any single summer's grievances.

The "urban crisis" that became a defining feature of American political debate from the late 1960s through the 1980s was not a natural disaster, not a product of cultural failure, and not an inevitable consequence of racial composition. It was the product of specific economic decisions — by corporations, by government, by financial institutions — that together dismantled the industrial employment base that had drawn Black Southerners northward during the Great Migration, provided them with wages sufficient for stable working-class life, and created the economic foundation on which Black urban communities in Chicago, Detroit, Cleveland, Pittsburgh, Philadelphia, Baltimore, and dozens of other cities had been built. Deindustrialization — the closure of factories, the movement of manufacturing jobs first to the non-union South and then overseas, and the restructuring of the American economy from manufacturing to services — was the single most consequential structural transformation in Black urban life between the Great Migration and the present day.

The consequences were staggering in their scale and their concentration. Between roughly 1965 and 1985, the industrial cities of the Midwest and Northeast lost millions of manufacturing jobs. Detroit lost more than half of its manufacturing employment. Chicago lost hundreds of thousands of factory jobs. Cleveland, Pittsburgh, Buffalo, Gary, and Camden underwent economic contractions of a similar or greater magnitude. These losses fell disproportionately on Black workers, who had been the last to be admitted to many of the better-paying industrial jobs during the Great Migration era and who were, in the logic of seniority-based union contracts, first to be laid off when production declined. The geographic concentration of those losses in the Black neighborhoods of the urban Midwest and Northeast produced, in a remarkably short period of time, a spatial concentration of unemployment, poverty, and social distress that had no real precedent in the history of American cities.

The consequences of deindustrialization ramified through every dimension of urban Black life. Unemployment rose to levels that, in some neighborhoods, exceeded Depression-era national averages. Poverty deepened and concentrated in ways that reinforced the spatial segregation that had already been imposed by decades of redlining and housing discrimination. Public schools, starved of the tax revenues that industrial employment had generated, deteriorated rapidly in the very neighborhoods whose young people most needed quality education to navigate an economy that was shifting from blue-collar to white-collar, from factory floor to office building. Families under the pressure of chronic unemployment and material deprivation fractured in ways that reflected not cultural pathology but economic stress — the same kinds of family disruption that poverty and unemployment had produced in every era and every community that had experienced them.

The fiscal crisis that hit New York City in 1975 — when the city nearly went bankrupt, and was told by the federal government, in a famous tabloid headline, to "drop dead" — was the most dramatic expression of a structural problem affecting virtually every major American city: the collapse of the tax base that industrial employment had built, the flight of middle-class taxpayers to suburbs that the same discriminatory housing policies had created, and the increasing concentration in the city proper of the populations most in need of public services and least able to pay for them. When cities responded to fiscal crisis by cutting services — closing firehouses, reducing school budgets, laying off sanitation workers, cutting public transit — those cuts fell hardest on the neighborhoods where the crisis was already most acute. The urban fiscal crisis was, in this sense, a second blow delivered to communities already reeling from the economic transformations of deindustrialization.

This chapter traces these interconnected transformations — the loss of industrial jobs, the deepening of poverty and its spatial concentration, the deterioration of schools and public services, the stresses on family and community life, and the fiscal collapse that stripped cities of the resources to respond — and explains how they together created the conditions in which the street organizations, drug economies, and patterns of violence that would define popular understanding of Black urban life in the 1980s and 1990s emerged. Understanding deindustrialization is not an excuse for anything. It is an explanation — an account of the structural conditions that shaped the choices available to young Black men and women in urban neighborhoods from which the economy had withdrawn, and from which the state was rapidly withdrawing as well. No account of what happened to Black urban communities in the final decades of the twentieth century is adequate without confronting the economic catastrophe that preceded and produced it.

This chapter connects in both directions: backward, to the Great Migration chapters that explained why Black families came to Detroit, Chicago, and Cleveland and what they built there; and forward, to the chapters on the crack cocaine economy, the War on Drugs, and mass incarceration that will explain what happened to those communities when the economic collapse interacted with the drug market and the punitive state. The story of deindustrialization is the hinge on which the entire second half of this textbook turns. It is the story of how a structural economic transformation that was experienced nationally but bore its costs most heavily in Black urban communities created the conditions for the crisis decades that followed.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Define deindustrialization and explain the major economic forces — including corporate restructuring, technological change, global competition, and labor cost arbitrage — that drove the loss of manufacturing employment in American cities between roughly 1965 and 1990.
  2. Trace the geographic concentration of industrial job loss in the Midwest and Northeast and explain why Black workers and Black neighborhoods bore a disproportionate share of the economic consequences.
  3. Analyze the relationship between industrial job loss and the deepening concentration of urban poverty, explaining how the spatial dimensions of deindustrialization interacted with existing patterns of racial residential segregation.
  4. Explain how deindustrialization affected public school systems in Black urban neighborhoods, including the connections between declining tax revenues, deteriorating facilities, reduced educational quality, and falling student outcomes.
  5. Describe the effects of chronic unemployment and concentrated poverty on family structure and community life in deindustrializing cities, and critically evaluate the "culture of poverty" arguments that were used to explain these effects in ways that diverted attention from structural causes.
  6. Explain the causes and consequences of the urban fiscal crisis of the 1970s and its differential impact on Black neighborhoods and communities.
  7. Identify the key debates among scholars, policymakers, and community members about the causes of the urban crisis, and evaluate competing explanations including structural economic arguments, policy arguments, and cultural arguments.
  8. Compare the experiences of deindustrialization in different cities — particularly Detroit, Chicago, Cleveland, and Pittsburgh — identifying both common patterns and significant local variations.
  9. Connect the economic transformations of deindustrialization to the later emergence of informal drug economies and street organizations, explaining how declining legitimate employment opportunities created both the demand and the supply conditions for illegal economic activity.
  10. Evaluate the policy responses to deindustrialization and the urban crisis, including urban renewal, enterprise zones, community development, and welfare reform, and assess their adequacy and their effects on Black communities.
  11. Assess the evidence of community resilience — the institutions, organizations, mutual support networks, and individual and collective adaptations — that Black communities developed in response to deindustrialization.
  12. Contextualize the urban crisis within the broader history of American capitalism and racial inequality, explaining why deindustrialization interacted so devastatingly with the specific vulnerabilities created by segregation, redlining, and political exclusion.

Guiding Historical Questions

Questions for Historical Inquiry

  1. What is deindustrialization, and why did it hit Black urban communities with such disproportionate force in the decades after 1965?
  2. How did the loss of manufacturing employment interact with the pre-existing structures of racial residential segregation to produce concentrated poverty in specific Black urban neighborhoods?
  3. What does it mean to say that the urban crisis was "structural" rather than "cultural"? What evidence supports the structural explanation, and what are the limits of purely structural accounts?
  4. How did public institutions — schools, hospitals, sanitation, transit, fire protection — respond to fiscal crisis, and how did the distribution of service cuts affect Black neighborhoods differently from white neighborhoods?
  5. What happened to Black working-class families when the industrial jobs on which their economic stability depended disappeared? How did different families and communities respond to economic stress?
  6. Why did city governments face fiscal crisis in the 1970s, and what political choices were made in how that crisis was managed? Who bore the costs of fiscal austerity?
  7. What was the relationship between the economic collapse of the industrial city and the later emergence of crack cocaine markets and street organizations? Is it possible to explain the latter without the former?
  8. How do urban historians distinguish between policy failures and structural economic transformations in explaining the urban crisis? What are the stakes of that distinction?
  9. What forms of community resilience persisted through deindustrialization, and what do they reveal about the organizational traditions and social bonds that Black communities had built over generations?

Historical Context

To understand the urban crisis of the 1970s and 1980s, it is necessary to understand the economic world that preceded it — the world that Black migrants from the South had entered during the Great Migration and that had made possible, within the severe constraints of racial segregation, the development of Black working-class urban life. The industrial economy of the Midwest and Northeast — centered on steel, automobiles, meatpacking, electrical manufacturing, rubber, chemicals, and a dozen other heavy industries — was, in the decades immediately after World War II, the most productive industrial economy in human history. American factories had emerged from the war as the unchallenged center of global manufacturing, the supplier of a world that was physically devastated and economically exhausted by six years of catastrophic conflict.

This industrial supremacy rested on a particular set of social arrangements. The union contracts negotiated by the Congress of Industrial Organizations (CIO) and the American Federation of Labor (AFL) in the 1930s and 1940s had, after decades of violent conflict, produced a framework for labor relations in major industries that guaranteed workers — at least those workers who were admitted to union membership — wages sufficient for working-class home ownership, regular vacation and sick leave, health insurance, and defined-benefit pensions. These arrangements were far from universal. Black workers had been systematically excluded from many of the better-paying trades and industrial jobs throughout the first decades of the Great Migration. But by the late 1940s and 1950s, the dynamics of wartime labor shortages, the organizing efforts of industrial unions, and the political pressure of Black civil rights organizations had opened many industrial jobs to Black workers, particularly in the automobile, steel, and meatpacking industries.

The result, by the late 1950s and early 1960s, was a Black industrial working class that, while still subject to the intense disadvantages of residential segregation, discriminatory hiring and promotion, and the systematic exclusion from the suburban prosperity that white workers were building with their industrial wages, had achieved a degree of economic stability that represented a genuine improvement over the conditions that had driven the Great Migration. Black autoworkers in Detroit, Black steelworkers in Pittsburgh and Cleveland, Black meatpackers in Chicago — these workers earned wages that allowed them to rent or even own homes, to send their children to school, to participate in the consumer economy, and to support the churches, civic organizations, and social institutions that gave Black urban neighborhoods their character and their resilience. The Black urban working class of the early 1960s was poor by the standards of white American prosperity, but it was not in crisis. It had a foundation — a wage floor, union protections, a degree of economic predictability — that made the kind of community life this textbook has been tracing possible.

What happened to that foundation between the mid-1960s and the mid-1980s was one of the most significant economic transformations in modern American history. The forces that drove deindustrialization were multiple and interacting. International competition was real: European and Japanese manufacturers, rebuilt with American aid after the war, were by the 1960s producing steel, automobiles, and consumer electronics that could compete with American products on quality and undersell them on price. Technological change was equally real: new production technologies, including automation, computerized manufacturing, and continuous-casting steel production, reduced the labor content of industrial output, meaning that the same level of production required fewer workers. Corporate restructuring was perhaps the most directly decisive factor: American corporations, facing the combination of intensified competition and rising labor costs, began systematically moving production — first to the non-union South and then overseas — in search of lower wages and weaker labor regulations.

The geography of these changes was not random. The regions that had been built on the old industrial model — the Midwest's "Rust Belt," the industrial corridor of the Northeast, the steel and coal regions of Pennsylvania, Ohio, and Indiana — bore the greatest costs. The cities within those regions that had the largest Black working-class populations — Detroit, Chicago, Cleveland, Pittsburgh, Philadelphia, Baltimore, Newark — experienced the interaction of economic decline with racial residential segregation in ways that concentrated the costs of deindustrialization with devastating specificity in Black neighborhoods. When a plant closed in Detroit's industrial corridor, the workers who lost their jobs were disproportionately Black. The neighborhoods those workers lived in, confined by decades of redlining and housing discrimination to the inner city, were the neighborhoods that lost tax revenues, lost population, lost retail businesses, and lost the social and economic infrastructure that industrial wages had supported.

The political context of the urban crisis was shaped by the interaction of the civil rights movement's achievements and the white political backlash that those achievements provoked. The civil rights legislation of 1964 and 1965 had formally dismantled the legal architecture of Jim Crow, and urban rebellions from Watts in 1965 through Detroit and Newark in 1967 had dramatized the gap between formal legal equality and the material reality of Black urban life. The presidential election of 1968 had brought Richard Nixon to power on a "law and order" platform that, as Nixon's own aides later acknowledged, was explicitly designed to mobilize white voters' racial anxieties into a coalition against the urban Black population and the liberal Democratic coalition that had supported civil rights legislation. The federal government's response to the urban crisis, from Nixon onward, would be shaped by this political realignment — by the calculation that addressing Black urban poverty through significant redistribution of resources was not merely impractical but was, for the Republican Party and its emergent coalition, actively counterproductive.

The urban rebellions themselves deserve careful historical understanding. The word "riot" that was almost universally applied to these events in mainstream media coverage obscured more than it revealed. The uprisings in Watts (1965), Newark and Detroit (1967), and dozens of other cities in the summers of 1965 through 1968 were not simple expressions of irrationality or criminality. They were political events — expressions of rage at specific, documented grievances: police brutality, housing discrimination, economic exclusion, and the systematic neglect of Black neighborhoods by city governments that collected taxes from Black residents while providing them with inferior services. The Kerner Commission, appointed by President Johnson to investigate the uprisings, reached exactly this conclusion: the riots were the product of "white racism," specifically of the residential segregation, economic exclusion, inadequate schools, pervasive unemployment, and discriminatory policing that had been documented repeatedly but never adequately addressed. The Commission's famous warning — that America was "moving toward two societies, one black, one white — separate and unequal" — was not a prediction. It was a description of what the Commission had found.

The context this chapter requires, then, is an understanding of the transition from the relatively stable (if severely unequal) Black working-class city of the early 1960s to the economically devastated, fiscally collapsing, institutionally deteriorating urban landscape that would characterize much of the urban Midwest and Northeast by the early 1980s. That transition was produced by the interaction of deindustrialization, racial residential segregation, political backlash, and deliberate policy choices — not by the failure of Black culture, the breakdown of the Black family, or the deterioration of Black values. Understanding that distinction is essential to understanding not only what happened but why it happened, and what kinds of policy responses could have addressed it and chose not to.

Major People, Groups, and Places

Reference — People, Groups & Places

The Kerner Commission (National Advisory Commission on Civil Disorders)
The federal commission appointed by President Johnson in 1967 to investigate the causes of that summer's urban uprisings. Chaired by Illinois Governor Otto Kerner, the Commission produced a landmark 1968 report that blamed "white racism" — specifically residential segregation, economic exclusion, inadequate schools, unemployment, and discriminatory policing — for the conditions that produced the rebellions. Its famous warning that America was "moving toward two societies, one black, one white — separate and unequal" became the defining public diagnosis of the urban crisis. Its recommendations for substantial federal investment in urban communities were largely ignored by the Nixon administration and its successors.
William Julius Wilson (1935– )
The most influential sociologist of Black urban poverty in the late twentieth century, whose 1987 book The Truly Disadvantaged: The Inner City, the Underclass, and Public Policy made the structural case for deindustrialization as the primary cause of the concentration of Black urban poverty. Wilson argued that the removal of manufacturing jobs from Black urban neighborhoods — not cultural factors — was the fundamental driver of the social dislocations that characterized those neighborhoods by the 1980s. His work was enormously influential in redirecting academic and policy discussion toward structural economic explanations for urban poverty.
Detroit, Michigan
The paradigmatic case of deindustrialization and urban crisis. Detroit had been the center of the American automobile industry and the destination of hundreds of thousands of Black migrants from the South. By the late 1970s and 1980s, the city was losing population at a rate that would ultimately reduce it from nearly two million residents to under 700,000, its industrial landscape of shuttered factories became one of the most photographed symbols of American urban decline, and its Black community — which by the 1970s constituted the city's political majority — faced unemployment, poverty, and institutional deterioration on a massive scale. Detroit's story is in many ways the central story of this chapter.
Chicago, Illinois
The other paradigmatic case of Black urban deindustrialization, where the loss of manufacturing jobs in the stockyards, steel mills, and other industries of the South Side and West Side devastated Black working-class neighborhoods that had been built over generations of Great Migration settlement. Chicago's South Side neighborhoods — Robert Taylor Homes, the Cabrini-Green development, Englewood, Woodlawn — became, by the 1980s, among the most visible expressions of concentrated poverty in American cities.
Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania
The center of the American steel industry, which employed thousands of Black workers and sustained entire Black neighborhoods through the Great Migration era. The collapse of the steel industry in the late 1970s and early 1980s devastated Pittsburgh's Black Hill District and Homewood-Brushton neighborhoods with a speed that was almost incomprehensible to those who lived through it. Pittsburgh's experience illustrates how a single industrial sector's collapse could destroy an entire community's economic foundation.
Youngstown, Ohio
The site of "Black Monday" — September 19, 1977 — when the Youngstown Sheet and Tube Company announced the closure of its Campbell Works facility, eliminating 5,000 jobs almost overnight. Youngstown became the symbol of the steel industry's collapse and of the broader deindustrialization of the Rust Belt. The Youngstown story was later analyzed in detail by scholars as an illustration of how corporate decision-making could destroy a community with no warning and with no responsibility for the consequences.
The United Auto Workers (UAW)
The union that represented workers in the automobile industry and that had been one of the primary mechanisms through which Black workers in Detroit had achieved working-class economic stability. The UAW's seniority-based layoff rules, while nominally race-neutral, had the effect of ensuring that Black workers — who had generally been admitted to the better-paying jobs most recently — were the first to be laid off as production declined. The UAW's political and economic power also declined dramatically with the industry it represented, reducing the union's ability to defend its members against the consequences of deindustrialization.
The Rust Belt
The geographic and cultural category used to describe the formerly industrial cities of the Midwest and Northeast that suffered the most severe consequences of deindustrialization. The term, which became current in the early 1980s, captures the image of rusting factories, abandoned industrial infrastructure, and declining cities that became the visual icon of the post-industrial urban landscape. The Rust Belt is not a precise geographic category but a shorthand for the region — stretching from upstate New York through Ohio, Michigan, Indiana, and into Illinois — that bore the heaviest costs of the transition from industrial to post-industrial economy.
Robert Taylor Homes
The massive public housing project on Chicago's South Side, for many years the largest public housing development in the world, comprising 28 high-rise towers and housing approximately 27,000 residents at its peak. Built in the late 1950s and early 1960s as part of the same urban renewal program that had destroyed Black neighborhoods throughout American cities, Robert Taylor Homes concentrated Black poverty in a physically isolated strip along the Dan Ryan Expressway, creating conditions of intense social distress. By the 1980s, the project had become a symbol of the failure of high-rise public housing and of the broader urban crisis.
Ida B. Wells Homes / Harold Ickes Homes
Additional Chicago public housing developments that, along with Robert Taylor Homes and the Cabrini-Green development, represented the concentrated-poverty model of public housing in Black Chicago. The clustering of Chicago Housing Authority projects in Black neighborhoods, separated from the broader city by expressways, rail yards, and other physical barriers, exemplified the way in which public housing policy had reinforced rather than mitigated racial residential segregation.
The "Underclass" Debate
The academic and policy debate of the 1970s and 1980s about how to characterize and explain the concentrated poverty of Black urban neighborhoods. The term "underclass," used by journalists, policymakers, and some social scientists, was contested from the start — critics argued that it stigmatized the poor and diverted attention from structural causes toward cultural explanations. The debate between structural explanations (emphasizing deindustrialization, discrimination, and policy failure) and cultural explanations (emphasizing family structure, work ethic, and community norms) was one of the most important and contentious in American social science, with direct consequences for policy.
Urban Renewal / Model Cities
Federal urban development programs of the 1950s–1970s that, in the name of eliminating "blight" and "slums," demolished Black neighborhoods throughout American cities to make way for highways, hospitals, universities, and middle-class housing. Urban renewal — which Black residents renamed "Negro removal" — destroyed the social infrastructure of communities, dispersed established neighborhood networks, and often failed to provide promised replacement housing. Model Cities, the Johnson-era successor program, attempted to involve community residents in planning but was underfunded and subject to political opposition from local power structures.
The New York City Fiscal Crisis (1975)
The near-bankruptcy of New York City in 1975, which required a federal loan guarantee and an emergency state assistance package that gave state and federal financial authorities effective control over city spending. The crisis resulted in massive service cuts — including the closure of firehouses, sanitation reductions, hospital cuts, and large-scale layoffs of city workers — that fell disproportionately on the city's poorest neighborhoods. The New York crisis was the most dramatic expression of the broader urban fiscal crisis and set a template for fiscal austerity that cities across the country would follow.

Section 8.1

Factory Closures: The Economic Foundation Collapses

The Industrial World That Migration Built

To understand what was lost in the deindustrialization of the 1970s and 1980s, it is necessary to understand what Black workers had built in the industrial cities of the North over the preceding half century. The Great Migration had been, at its core, an economic migration — a movement of people from the low-wage, violently subordinated agricultural economy of the Jim Crow South to the higher-wage, union-protected industrial economy of the urban North. That movement had not been easy. Black migrants had faced discrimination in housing, in employment, in education, and in every dimension of urban life. They had been confined to the worst neighborhoods, admitted to the worst jobs, and denied the mortgages and public subsidies that allowed white workers to build equity in the expanding suburbs. But within those constraints, Black workers had built something real: a working-class economic foundation that, by the late 1950s and early 1960s, supported stable families, thriving neighborhoods, strong community institutions, and a degree of economic dignity that the Jim Crow South had made impossible.

In Detroit, tens of thousands of Black autoworkers held union cards in the United Auto Workers and drew wages at the Ford River Rouge plant, the Chrysler Dodge Main plant, and the General Motors facilities that dotted the Detroit metropolitan area. These wages — typically between three and six dollars per hour in the late 1950s, equivalent to a solid working-class income — were sufficient to rent or buy a home in Detroit's Black neighborhoods on the East Side and near West Side, to feed a family, to send children to school with adequate clothing and supplies, and to participate in the local economy of churches, barbershops, pool halls, restaurants, clubs, and small businesses that constituted the social world of Black Detroit. In Chicago, Black steelworkers at the South Works on Lake Michigan, Black meatpackers in the stockyards, Black workers in the electrical manufacturing plants of the West Side, and Black workers in dozens of other industries formed a large and economically consequential industrial workforce that sustained Black Chicago's neighborhoods and institutions.

This working-class stability was unequally distributed and always fragile. Black workers were systematically concentrated in the dirtiest and most dangerous jobs within industrial plants, excluded from skilled trades and supervisory roles, and the last to be upgraded to better positions when economic conditions allowed. The seniority system that governed layoffs under union contracts was formally race-neutral but effectively raced in its impact: because Black workers had generally been admitted to better-paying positions most recently, following decades of exclusion, they accumulated less seniority and were therefore first to be laid off when production declined. This structural vulnerability meant that even the most economically secure Black workers were more exposed to downturns than equivalent white workers, and that any economic contraction would fall disproportionately on Black households.

The Mechanics of Deindustrialization

Deindustrialization was not a sudden event. It was a process that unfolded over two decades, accelerating in some periods and pausing in others, hitting different industries and different cities at different times. But its cumulative effect was the wholesale destruction of the industrial employment base that had made Black working-class urban life possible.

In the steel industry, the process began in the late 1950s and early 1960s as new technologies — particularly basic oxygen furnaces and, later, electric arc furnaces and continuous casting — dramatically increased labor productivity, meaning that the same level of output required fewer workers. At the same time, foreign competition from rebuilt European and Japanese steelmakers intensified. American steel companies, unwilling or unable to make the capital investments that would have maintained their technological competitiveness, began closing older, less efficient plants rather than modernizing them. The consequences for cities like Pittsburgh, Cleveland, Gary, and Youngstown were severe and rapid. The Youngstown Sheet and Tube closure of 1977, which eliminated more than 5,000 jobs in a single announcement, was the most dramatic single event, but it was part of a pattern: between 1977 and 1987, the American steel industry eliminated more than 150,000 jobs in the core steel-producing cities of the Midwest.

In the automobile industry, the process was more gradual in its early phases but ultimately equally devastating. The OPEC oil embargo of 1973, which quadrupled energy prices and shifted consumer demand toward smaller, more fuel-efficient vehicles, created a competitive opening for Japanese manufacturers that American companies were slow to close. General Motors, Ford, and Chrysler all began a decades-long process of plant consolidation, automation, and — eventually — offshore production that steadily reduced the number of workers in Detroit and its surrounding industrial communities. The concessions bargained away from the UAW under the pressure of plant closure threats — wage freezes, two-tier wage structures, reduced benefits — reflected the union's diminishing power and its members' growing vulnerability.

In the meatpacking industry, the transformation was driven by a different mechanism: geographic reorganization. The major meatpacking companies — once concentrated in Chicago's Union Stock Yards and employing tens of thousands of Black workers — deliberately relocated their operations to small towns in rural Iowa, Kansas, and Nebraska, closer to the livestock supply and far from the urban union strongholds. The new plants paid lower wages, operated without union contracts, and employed a new workforce that was largely Latino and immigrant rather than Black and urban. The closure of the Chicago stockyards complex in the early 1970s eliminated one of the most important concentrations of Black industrial employment in the country and set a template for industrial relocation that other industries would follow.

Capital flight — the movement of investment away from established urban industrial locations to lower-cost regions and countries — was perhaps the most consequential dimension of deindustrialization. American corporations in the 1960s, 1970s, and 1980s were under increasing pressure from shareholders to maximize returns, and the gap between the wage rates they paid urban unionized workers and the rates available in the non-union South, in Mexico, in South Korea, or in Taiwan was enormous. Plant closure decisions were made in corporate boardrooms in New York, Detroit, and Chicago by executives who rarely if ever set foot in the communities their decisions would destroy. The communities themselves — the workers, the families, the local businesses, the churches and schools and neighborhood institutions — had no seat at the table where those decisions were made and no legal right to be consulted before they were implemented.

Historical Vocabulary
Deindustrialization

Deindustrialization refers to the process by which a region, city, or economy loses a significant portion of its manufacturing employment base, typically through plant closures, automation, and the geographic relocation of production to lower-cost areas. In the United States, deindustrialization accelerated dramatically between roughly 1965 and 1990, particularly in the industrial cities of the Midwest and Northeast that had been the destination of the Great Migration.

Deindustrialization was not simply a natural consequence of economic progress or inevitable technological change. It was produced by specific decisions — by corporations seeking lower labor costs, by financial institutions that rewarded short-term profit-taking over long-term investment, by trade policies that facilitated the movement of production offshore, and by state and federal governments that provided tax incentives for corporate relocation without requiring accountability for the communities left behind. The language of "market forces" that was often used to describe deindustrialization obscured the fact that it was the product of choices — choices that had winners and losers, and that distributed those wins and losses in ways that were far from random.

The racialized consequences of deindustrialization in American cities were not inevitable but they were predictable. Because Black workers had been confined by decades of discrimination to the industrial sector (excluded from the growing white-collar service economy) and to the lowest-seniority positions within that sector, the loss of manufacturing employment fell on Black workers and Black neighborhoods with disproportionate force. The spatial concentration of those workers in racially segregated inner-city neighborhoods — itself the product of deliberate housing policy — meant that the economic consequences of deindustrialization were geographically concentrated in specific communities, intensifying their impact and limiting residents' ability to escape them.

Black Monday and Its Aftermath

The term "Black Monday" was used by workers and local journalists to describe September 19, 1977, when Youngstown Sheet and Tube announced the closure of its Campbell Works. The phrase captured something real about the experience of deindustrialization: for the workers who arrived at the plant that Monday morning and were told they would not be returning, it felt like a sudden catastrophe, a single day that divided their lives into before and after. But the closure of the Campbell Works was not actually sudden. It was the culmination of years of deferred maintenance, declining investment, and corporate maneuvering by the Lykes Corporation, the New Orleans conglomerate that had acquired Youngstown Sheet and Tube in 1969 primarily for its cash flow and pension fund assets rather than for its long-term industrial potential.

The pattern was repeated across the Rust Belt with variations that reflected the specific history of each industry and each city, but with a consistent structure: corporate acquisition or consolidation, followed by reduced investment, followed by the eventual announcement of closure, followed by community devastation. In every case, the workers — whose labor had generated the profits that funded the corporate maneuvers — had no advance knowledge and no recourse. The communities — whose infrastructure, schools, and tax bases had been built on the assumption of continued industrial activity — had no voice in decisions that would determine their fate.

The human consequences of plant closure were not abstract. Workers who lost factory jobs faced a labor market that had few alternatives offering comparable wages and benefits. The service-sector jobs that were expanding — retail, food service, janitorial and maintenance work, home health care — typically paid lower wages, offered fewer benefits, and provided less economic stability than the industrial jobs they replaced. A 55-year-old steelworker with thirty years of experience and a pension fund that the company had raided was not a competitive applicant for the positions that the new economy was creating. Retraining programs existed in principle but were chronically underfunded and rarely successful in placing workers in jobs that paid anything close to what they had earned in the mill. The aggregate consequence of thousands of individual displacement stories was a massive downward mobility of the Black working class — not a gradual adjustment but a rapid, traumatic economic fall that was all the more devastating because it followed decades of struggle to achieve the stability that was now being taken away.

The Spatial Mismatch

Economist John Kain introduced the concept of "spatial mismatch" in the late 1960s to describe a structural problem that was becoming increasingly visible: the growing geographic distance between where low-skilled jobs were located and where low-income Black workers lived. As manufacturing jobs moved to suburban industrial parks, and as the service-sector jobs that remained in cities increasingly required educational credentials that Black workers in segregated, underfunded schools had not been positioned to acquire, the geographic and educational barriers separating Black urban workers from available employment intensified.

The spatial mismatch was not merely a matter of distance. It was also a matter of transportation infrastructure, which in most American cities had been designed around the automobile and the suburb rather than the inner city and transit. Black workers without cars, living in inner-city neighborhoods served by declining public transit systems, faced a daily logistical challenge in accessing whatever jobs remained in the metropolitan economy. The irony was acute: the suburban job growth that was occurring in the expanding service and retail sectors was geographically accessible to the white workers who had moved to the suburbs with the assistance of FHA mortgages and racial exclusion, and largely inaccessible to the Black workers who remained in the inner city with the assistance of neither.

Section 8.2

Unemployment and Poverty: The Statistical Catastrophe

The Numbers That Told the Story

The unemployment statistics that emerged from inner-city Black neighborhoods in the late 1970s and early 1980s were, by any historical standard, extraordinary. In Detroit neighborhoods like the Near East Side, in Chicago's South Side projects, in Cleveland's Hough and Glenville areas, and in Pittsburgh's Hill District, unemployment rates among Black men — particularly young Black men — reached levels that, in their combination of depth and geographic concentration, had no precedent in the post-Depression era. Official unemployment statistics, which counted only those actively seeking work, significantly understated the actual extent of labor market withdrawal, because many workers who had given up looking for work after months or years of unsuccessful searching no longer appeared in the official counts. When economists added "discouraged workers" and involuntary part-time workers to the official unemployed, the figures for young Black men in the most affected neighborhoods reached forty, fifty, and in some cases sixty percent.

These numbers were not simply the statistical expression of individual failure. They were the inevitable consequence of the loss of the jobs that had employed those workers and their fathers, combined with the persistent barriers — educational, geographic, discriminatory — that prevented access to whatever alternative employment existed. A twenty-year-old Black man in Detroit in 1983, growing up in a neighborhood where the automobile plants had closed, whose high school had lost teachers and programs to budget cuts, who lacked the automobile necessary to reach the suburban jobs that remained, and who was excluded by name discrimination from even many entry-level service jobs, faced a labor market that had, in a very real structural sense, no place for him. The individual's choices mattered. But the structural context within which those choices were made had been dramatically transformed.

Poverty deepened and concentrated with deindustrialization in ways that reinforced the spatial isolation that housing discrimination had already created. William Julius Wilson's analysis of this dynamic — the central argument of The Truly Disadvantaged — distinguished between poverty itself and concentrated poverty, arguing that it was the geographic concentration of poverty in specific neighborhoods, rather than poverty per se, that produced the most damaging social consequences. When poor people live in neighborhoods where everyone around them is also poor — where there are no employed role models, where no local businesses remain to provide entry-level jobs, where the local school serves only poor students and therefore lacks the parental resources and community support that middle-class schools take for granted — the effects of poverty are multiplied and self-reinforcing in ways that are qualitatively different from poverty dispersed across economically diverse communities.

Historical Vocabulary
Concentrated Poverty

Concentrated poverty refers to the geographic clustering of poor households in specific neighborhoods or communities, as opposed to the dispersal of poverty across economically diverse areas. Poverty concentration is typically measured using census tract data; the sociologist William Julius Wilson used a threshold of 40 percent poverty rate or above to define "extreme poverty" neighborhoods in his influential research of the 1980s.

The concentration of poverty in specific urban neighborhoods was not a natural consequence of poverty itself. It was produced by specific policy mechanisms: racial residential segregation enforced by redlining and housing discrimination, which confined Black workers to specific inner-city neighborhoods; public housing policy that concentrated poor Black families in large, isolated projects in those same neighborhoods; highway construction that destroyed mixed-income Black neighborhoods and isolated the poor in the remaining areas; and the departure of middle-class Black households for newly available suburban housing in the 1970s and 1980s, which removed the economic diversity that had previously provided some social buffering in Black neighborhoods.

The distinction between poverty and concentrated poverty matters for understanding the urban crisis because concentrated poverty has specific social consequences beyond those of poverty alone. In neighborhoods where poverty is highly concentrated, local institutions — schools, churches, small businesses, community organizations — are overwhelmed by the scale of need and starved of the resources that mixed-income neighborhoods provide through diverse tax bases and parental involvement. Social networks that might transmit information about employment opportunities are composed almost entirely of other unemployed people. The physical environment deteriorates as landlords disinvest from properties and city services are reduced. These cumulative effects make concentrated poverty substantially more damaging than equivalent poverty dispersed across diverse communities.

The Geography of Poverty Concentration

The geography of concentrated urban poverty was not random. It was the product of layered historical decisions: redlining that had confined Black homebuyers to specific neighborhoods; public housing placement decisions that had located massive high-rise projects in Black neighborhoods rather than in white neighborhoods or in economically mixed areas; highway construction that had bisected and destroyed established Black communities, displacing residents into the remaining Black areas; urban renewal that had demolished Black neighborhoods without providing adequate replacement housing; and the departure of middle-class Black families for suburban areas as legal barriers to their movement weakened after the Fair Housing Act of 1968.

The departure of middle-class Black households — a phenomenon Wilson described as the loss of the "social buffer" in Black neighborhoods — was particularly significant for the argument of this chapter. Before the Fair Housing Act, middle-class Black professionals and business owners had been confined by law and custom to the same neighborhoods as working-class and poor Black residents, providing economic diversity, institutional leadership, and social connections to the broader economy. When those barriers fell, many middle-class Black families moved to previously white neighborhoods or to the suburbs. This was, individually, a triumph of civil rights — the exercise of the freedom that had been denied for generations. But its aggregate effect on the neighborhoods that were left behind was devastating. Schools lost engaged middle-class parents. Local businesses lost solvent customers. Community organizations lost their most effective leaders. The social ecology of neighborhoods that had always been poor was transformed by the departure of those who had not been.

The concentration of poverty in specific neighborhoods was further intensified by the transformation of public housing policy. The large high-rise public housing projects that had been built in the 1950s and 1960s — projects like Chicago's Robert Taylor Homes and Cabrini-Green, Detroit's Brewster-Douglass Homes, St. Louis's Pruitt-Igoe, and New York's various large developments — had been designed and sited in ways that maximized racial and economic segregation. Located on the least desirable land in already-poor Black neighborhoods, separated from the rest of the city by expressways and industrial corridors, and concentrated entirely on the poorest households (because federal policy excluded families with any earned income above very low thresholds), these projects created environments of extreme deprivation and social isolation. As deindustrialization eliminated the jobs that had allowed some project residents to work their way into private housing, the projects became more deeply concentrated with the unemployed and the poorest households, and their social environment deteriorated dramatically.

Historical Vocabulary
Disinvestment

Disinvestment refers to the withdrawal of capital, resources, and institutional attention from a geographic area, community, or sector. In the context of Black urban neighborhoods during the deindustrialization era, disinvestment operated simultaneously at multiple levels: private disinvestment by landlords who stopped maintaining properties and allowed them to deteriorate; banking disinvestment in the form of continued mortgage redlining and the withdrawal of small business loans from inner-city areas; retail disinvestment as supermarkets, pharmacies, and other businesses closed inner-city locations; and public disinvestment as declining tax revenues led to cuts in schools, libraries, parks, sanitation, and other public services.

The mutually reinforcing character of disinvestment is important to understand. When landlords stop maintaining properties, the neighborhood becomes less attractive to stable renters, vacancy rates rise, and remaining landlords face pressure to reduce rents or accept lower-quality tenants. When supermarkets close, residents must travel farther for food, small food businesses that remain charge higher prices, and the diet quality and food security of residents decline. When schools lose teachers and programs, educational outcomes fall, parents with options leave, and the school's resources decline further. Each form of disinvestment tends to trigger others, creating a self-reinforcing spiral that is difficult to reverse without massive external intervention.

Disinvestment in Black neighborhoods was not simply the operation of market forces. It was the product of discriminatory policies — redlining, exclusionary zoning, public housing placement — that had already concentrated Black residents in specific areas and denied them the asset accumulation that would have provided some economic buffer against decline. The same financial institutions that had denied Black homebuyers mortgages in the preceding decades were now denying Black neighborhoods the investment capital that would have supported business development and housing maintenance.

The Informal Economy as a Response

When the formal economy withdraws from a community, informal economic activity tends to expand to fill part of the gap. In the deindustrializing Black neighborhoods of the 1970s and early 1980s, informal economic activity took many forms, most of them entirely legal and some of them the foundation of community economic survival: informal childcare networks, home repair and improvement work paid in cash, food preparation and sale, informal lending and credit arrangements, shared household resources, and the extensive mutual support networks that had been a feature of Black community life since the mutual aid societies of the Reconstruction era.

The expansion of illegal informal economic activity — particularly the drug trade — was a different phenomenon, and it is important not to conflate the two. But the conditions for illegal informal economic expansion were created by the same structural forces that produced formal economic collapse: the elimination of legitimate employment opportunities, the concentration of economically desperate young men in specific geographic areas, and the withdrawal of the public institutions and community resources that might have provided alternatives. The drug economies that would become more visible with the emergence of crack cocaine in the mid-1980s did not emerge from nowhere. They emerged from economic conditions that deindustrialization had created — conditions in which the illegal drug market offered something that the legal economy no longer provided: jobs, income, organizational membership, and a degree of social recognition and status for young men who had been systematically excluded from the pathways to those things in the formal economy.

Section 8.3

School Decline: Education in the Urban Crisis

The Fiscal Foundation of Urban Schools

The quality of American public schools is, in the American system of school funding, almost entirely a function of the wealth of the communities those schools serve. American public education has historically been funded primarily through local property taxes — a system that means that wealthy communities with high property values can provide well-funded schools with small tax burdens, while poor communities with low property values must impose heavy tax burdens to provide inferior schools. This system, which has been challenged repeatedly in federal and state courts as a violation of the equal protection of the laws, was uniquely destructive when combined with the dynamics of urban deindustrialization and racial residential segregation.

When industrial plants closed, the property tax payments they had made to local governments stopped. When workers were laid off and could no longer afford to maintain their homes, property values declined. When middle-class residents moved to the suburbs, their property tax payments followed them out of the city. The result was a dramatic collapse of the tax revenues that had funded urban public schools, occurring simultaneously with increasing demand for school services from a population that was becoming poorer and more stressed. In cities like Detroit, Cleveland, and Newark, school budgets contracted sharply at exactly the moment when the social service burdens on schools were expanding — when students were arriving at school hungry, traumatized by family instability and neighborhood violence, without the educational resources at home that middle-class students took for granted, and in need of the kind of intensive educational support that required more resources, not fewer.

The consequences for educational quality were direct and devastating. Schools cut teachers, eliminated elective courses, reduced extracurricular programs, deferred maintenance on deteriorating facilities, and in many cases shortened the school day or year. Class sizes increased as teacher positions were eliminated. The most experienced and educated teachers, who had the most options, left for suburban schools that offered better pay and working conditions, and were replaced by younger and less experienced teachers or not replaced at all. School buildings in the most affected neighborhoods fell into disrepair — crumbling plaster, broken heating systems, leaking roofs, inadequate science laboratories — in ways that communicated to students precisely what the surrounding society thought of their education and their futures.

Beyond Budget Cuts: The Social Ecology of School Decline

The decline of urban schools in deindustrializing cities was not purely a fiscal story, though the fiscal story was foundational. The social ecology of schools in concentrated-poverty neighborhoods was fundamentally different from that of schools in economically diverse communities, in ways that compounded the effects of budget cuts.

Schools serve not only as academic institutions but as social institutions — places where children form peer networks, where they absorb social norms and expectations, where they encounter adults whose professional success provides models of possibility. In economically diverse schools, the mix of family backgrounds provides a kind of social resource: middle-class parents who engage with school administration ensure a level of institutional attention and resource allocation; students whose parents are employed provide peer models of what working in the formal economy looks like; the social networks of diverse schools connect students to information about employment and educational opportunities. In schools where virtually all students come from households in poverty, and where the neighborhood around the school provides few working adults and many examples of economic marginalization, these social resources are absent.

The schools of deindustrializing Black neighborhoods also faced increasing concentrations of the kind of social distress that makes academic learning difficult. Students experiencing hunger, housing instability, trauma from neighborhood violence, parental stress, and the psychological effects of concentrated poverty were not indifferent to education — most of them, and their parents, valued education deeply and understood its importance as the primary available route out of poverty. But they were bringing to school needs that the school — already operating with reduced resources — was poorly equipped to meet. The result was a gap between the potential that these students brought and the educational outcomes they achieved that was enormous, and that was systematically used — by those who preferred cultural to structural explanations — as evidence of a pathology of Black educational culture rather than the predictable consequence of structural conditions.

The School-to-Prison Pipeline Begins

The deterioration of urban schools in the deindustrialization era was inseparable from the expansion of the carceral state that will be examined in later chapters of this textbook. Schools that served concentrated-poverty Black neighborhoods were, by the late 1970s and 1980s, increasingly supplemented — and in some respects replaced — by the criminal justice system as a primary institutional contact for young Black men. The "school-to-prison pipeline" — the pattern by which young people who are excluded from, disciplined by, or pushed out of school systems end up in the criminal justice system — begins in this period.

The relationship between school decline and criminalization was not simply causal in one direction. Schools that could not provide meaningful educational engagement to students living under extraordinary stress tended to manage those students through exclusionary disciplinary practices — suspensions, expulsions, referrals to police — rather than through the intensive academic and social support that might have addressed the underlying issues. As zero-tolerance disciplinary policies were adopted in the 1980s and 1990s, and as school police and security personnel became fixtures in urban school buildings, the boundary between the educational system and the criminal justice system became increasingly permeable. Young people who were pushed out of school entered a labor market with no place for them and a criminal justice system that was actively expanding its reach.

Section 8.4

Family and Community Stress: Living Through Economic Collapse

What Economic Stress Does to Families

The effects of chronic unemployment, poverty, and economic instability on family life and community institutions are well documented by social scientists and well understood from the historical record. Poverty stresses families. It creates conflicts over money, generates chronic anxiety, reduces patience and emotional availability, and forces parents to make impossible choices between competing needs. These effects are not specific to Black families or to any racial group. They have been documented in every era and every community that has experienced sustained economic deprivation — in the white working class during the Great Depression, in rural communities during agricultural depressions, in immigrant communities facing discrimination and economic exclusion. The social disorganization that critics attributed to Black "culture" during the urban crisis was, in the vast majority of its specific manifestations, the normal social consequence of concentrated economic disaster.

The specific character of the family stress produced by deindustrialization in Black urban communities was shaped by the particular vulnerabilities of Black workers within the labor market. When a Black steelworker in Pittsburgh in 1980 lost his job at the mill where he had worked for twenty years, he faced a labor market that had few alternatives at comparable wages. The service sector jobs that were available — janitorial work, food service, retail — paid less than a third of what the mill had paid and offered none of the benefits, seniority, or job security of union employment. The psychological humiliation of moving from stable, skilled industrial work to poorly paid service work — or of being unable to find any work at all — was compounded by the cultural meaning that had been attached to the provider role. Men who had organized their identity and their family relationships around their capacity to earn a wage found that capacity abruptly eliminated, and the psychological consequences were profound.

The relationship between male unemployment and household formation has been one of the most contested empirical and policy questions in American social science. Sociologist William Julius Wilson argued that the decline of marriage rates in Black urban communities was directly connected to the decline of employed Black men available for marriage — what he called the "male marriageable pool" problem. When the labor market fails to provide young men with the wages and economic stability necessary for stable household formation, the conditions for marriage deteriorate regardless of individual cultural preferences or values. Wilson's argument was not that Black people did not value marriage or family — survey evidence consistently showed that they did — but that structural economic conditions created practical barriers to the formation and maintenance of the two-parent households that conservatives were simultaneously celebrating as the solution to urban poverty.

The "Culture of Poverty" Debate

The political response to the social dislocations of the urban crisis was significantly shaped by the "culture of poverty" framework, originally developed by anthropologist Oscar Lewis in studies of Mexican and Puerto Rican poverty in the late 1950s and popularized in the American context by Daniel Patrick Moynihan's 1965 report, The Negro Family: The Case for National Action. Moynihan's report argued that the specific structure of Black family life — particularly the high rates of female-headed households and out-of-wedlock births — had become a self-perpetuating "tangle of pathology" that was the primary obstacle to Black advancement, and that this family structure had roots in the emasculating experience of slavery and its aftermath.

The Moynihan Report was intensely controversial when it was released, and it has remained so. Critics from the civil rights movement and from the academic left argued that it diverted attention from structural racism and economic discrimination onto the family choices of Black people, effectively blaming victims for the consequences of oppression. They also argued that its characterization of female-headed households as "pathological" reflected white middle-class family norms rather than a neutral social scientific standard, and that Black family structures that differed from those norms might reflect adaptive responses to specific economic and social conditions rather than cultural deficit. Defenders of Moynihan argued that he had accurately identified a genuine social problem and that addressing it required confronting the specific dynamics of Black family life rather than attributing everything to structural racism.

The historiographical and political significance of this debate extends well beyond the specific question of Black family structure. The choice between structural and cultural explanations for concentrated urban poverty had direct policy implications: if the problem was structural — rooted in economic exclusion, discrimination, deindustrialization, and policy failure — then the appropriate response was structural as well: job creation, anti-discrimination enforcement, school investment, housing desegregation, and economic redistribution. If the problem was primarily cultural — rooted in the family choices, work attitudes, and values of poor Black people — then the appropriate response was cultural change: moral exhortation, behavioral conditioning through welfare reform, and the correction of "dysfunctional" values. The conservative politics of the Reagan era would adopt the cultural framework with enormous consequences for the policy response to the urban crisis.

Community Institutions Under Stress

The community institutions that Black neighborhoods had built over generations — the churches, the community organizations, the neighborhood businesses, the informal support networks — were not passively destroyed by deindustrialization. They were tested by it, and in many cases they responded with extraordinary resilience and creativity. But they were also overwhelmed, in many instances, by the scale of the need that economic collapse produced. Churches that had served as centers of community life found their congregations diminished as residents left for other neighborhoods or cities, their budgets strained by members' economic distress, and their social service capacity inadequate to the enormous need that surrounded them.

Community organizations that had been built on the organizing traditions of the civil rights movement attempted to respond to deindustrialization through coalition politics, advocacy for economic development, and direct service provision. Organizations like Operation PUSH in Chicago, the community development corporations that emerged in many cities, and the neighborhood-based organizing groups that drew on the Alinsky tradition of community organizing all worked to maintain some degree of community capacity in the face of economic collapse. Their achievements were real. They preserved institutions, created some jobs, maintained some services, and sustained some degree of community political life. But they were working against structural forces of enormous power with resources that were never remotely adequate to the challenge.

The Black church remained the most important single community institution through the deindustrialization era, as it had been through every previous crisis in Black American life. Church-based food pantries, emergency assistance programs, after-school programs, and counseling services attempted to address some of the most immediate consequences of economic collapse. Pastors became de facto community counselors, employment advisors, and political advocates. The church sustained a degree of community solidarity and mutual obligation that might otherwise have been entirely dissolved by economic stress. But even the Black church — the most durable and resourceful institution in Black community life — was not equipped to replace the jobs, income, and public services that deindustrialization and fiscal crisis had eliminated.

Everyday Life and Social History

A Neighborhood Under Pressure: Daily Life in a Deindustrializing City

Imagine a Black working-class neighborhood on Detroit's East Side in the early 1980s. A decade earlier, this neighborhood had been the kind of place described in countless oral histories of the Great Migration era: streets of owner-occupied brick homes, small front lawns maintained with care, a commercial strip with a barbershop, a cleaners, a grocery store, a beauty salon, a record shop, a church on nearly every corner. Men who worked at the Dodge Main plant, the Ford River Rouge complex, or one of the dozens of supplier factories that had made Detroit the center of the American automobile industry walked to bus stops in work clothes in the early morning. Children filled the sidewalks in the afternoon, heading to schools that, while segregated and underfunded by comparison with suburban schools, had teachers who knew the neighborhood and expectations that students could meet.

By 1982, the picture was different in ways that were visible in the physical environment and audible in conversations. The grocery store on the commercial strip had closed, replaced by a liquor store. The barbershop was still there, but its owner talked about moving to the suburbs, where his customers were going. Several houses on the block had broken windows — foreclosures or abandonments, their owners unable to keep up payments after layoffs at the plant. The Dodge Main plant, which had employed several thousand workers from this neighborhood alone, had been demolished. The school had lost a quarter of its teachers in the last round of budget cuts and the principal was talking about merging with another school whose population had also been declining.

The men on stoops in the afternoon — men who should by the economic logic of the preceding decade have been at work — were not there because they were lazy or because they had given up on work. Most of them had been employed, had built the habits and expectations of employment, had organized their identities around their capacity to earn. They were there because the jobs had gone away. Some of them were working occasional day labor, construction jobs, anything they could find. Some were managing household maintenance tasks, watching children, doing the domestic work that their unemployment had freed them to do. Some were drinking too much, under pressures that were destroying them slowly. Some were talking about leaving — Detroit, Chicago, wherever — for somewhere that might have work. A few had already moved into informal economic activities that would, in a few years, be dominated by the crack cocaine trade.

The women of the neighborhood were often managing under conditions of particular strain. Those who had been employed in manufacturing — in the auto parts plants, the electrical assembly plants, the food processing operations — had lost jobs too. Many were working in the service sector, in healthcare or domestic work or food service, at wages that were insufficient to cover housing, food, utilities, and childcare for a family. The domestic support of neighbors, sisters, mothers, and church community members was essential to their survival: the informal networks of mutual aid that had always been a feature of Black working-class life were being tested to their limits, asked to absorb needs that the formal economy and public institutions were no longer meeting.

The children growing up in this environment were absorbing lessons about the world that the adults around them had not intended to teach. They could see that their neighborhood was becoming poorer, that businesses were closing, that houses were being abandoned, that adults they knew were not going to work. They could see that the world their parents had built — through migration, through struggle, through decades of factory work — was coming apart in ways that nobody seemed to be able to stop. What they would make of those lessons, and how those lessons would shape the choices they made as they grew up in an economy that seemed to have no place for them, is the subject of the chapters that follow.

Section 8.5

The Urban Fiscal Crisis: When Cities Run Out of Money

The Structure of Urban Finance and Its Vulnerabilities

American city governments depend for their revenues primarily on property taxes, sales taxes, and other local revenue sources, supplemented by state aid and federal grants. This revenue structure makes cities vulnerable to economic downturns in ways that the federal government is not, because the federal government can run deficits and create money while cities cannot. When the economic base of a city — the property values, businesses, and personal income that generate tax revenues — contracts rapidly, city revenues decline at the same time that demand for city services (unemployment services, emergency assistance, social services, public health) increases. This structural mismatch between revenue decline and service demand is the fiscal crisis in its most basic form.

The urban fiscal crisis of the 1970s was precipitated by several simultaneous shocks: the oil embargo of 1973, which produced the worst recession since the Great Depression; the acceleration of white middle-class flight to the suburbs, which removed the highest taxpayers from the city's tax rolls; the continuing deindustrialization of urban economies, which reduced commercial and industrial property tax revenues; and the federal government's decision, under Nixon and then Ford, to reduce urban aid rather than increase it in response to urban economic distress. Cities that had taken on debt obligations based on the optimistic revenue projections of the boom years of the 1960s suddenly found themselves unable to meet those obligations from declining revenues.

New York City's crisis in 1975 was the most dramatic and most consequential expression of these pressures. By early 1975, New York was borrowing in the short-term credit markets to pay operating expenses — a sign of structural fiscal imbalance rather than temporary cash flow problems. When bond market investors refused to purchase new city debt at any interest rate, New York faced the prospect of defaulting on its financial obligations. President Ford's initial refusal to provide federal assistance — expressed in the immortal Daily News headline "Ford to City: Drop Dead" — reflected the political calculation of the Nixon/Ford coalition: helping the liberal, unionized, heavily non-white city of New York was not worth the political cost with the suburban and southern conservative coalition that Republican politics was cultivating. The eventual federal loan guarantee came only after New York had accepted fiscal oversight by state-appointed financial control boards and implemented massive service cuts.

Who Bears the Costs of Austerity

The austerity measures imposed on New York City in 1975 — and adopted, voluntarily or under pressure, by dozens of other cities in the following years — were not distributionally neutral. The service cuts fell disproportionately on the poorest neighborhoods, those that depended most heavily on public services and had the least capacity to purchase private alternatives. Firehouses were closed in the Bronx, where fires were most frequent. Sanitation service was reduced in communities where garbage collection was most needed. Libraries, parks, and recreation centers in poor neighborhoods were closed or had their hours dramatically reduced. The Board of Education cut thousands of teachers, school aides, and special programs — cuts that landed hardest in schools that were already the most understaffed and underfunded.

The South Bronx became the iconic image of the urban fiscal crisis — a landscape of burned-out buildings, abandoned lots, and extreme poverty that shocked national and international observers. The burning of the Bronx was not simply the result of arson for insurance fraud, though that occurred. It was the product of a convergence of forces: absentee landlords who stopped maintaining buildings as their tenants' ability to pay rent declined; a fiscal crisis that reduced fire department response capacity; and the desperation of a community under economic siege. When the Bronx burned during the 1977 World Series, visible on national television, it was providing a visual record of what concentrated poverty combined with fiscal austerity and physical disinvestment looked like in practice.

The federal government's retreat from urban aid continued and intensified under the Reagan administration, which took office in 1981 committed to reducing the size and role of the federal government in domestic affairs. Reagan's budget cuts eliminated or drastically reduced programs that had provided some cushion to deindustrializing cities: the Comprehensive Employment and Training Act (CETA), which had provided federal funding for local public employment; the Community Development Block Grant program, which had funded neighborhood revitalization; federal housing assistance for the construction of new public housing units; and a range of other urban programs. These cuts, implemented at the precise moment when the effects of deindustrialization were most acute, removed the federal safety net at the worst possible time — demonstrating, in the starkest possible terms, the political vulnerability of poor Black urban communities in the Reagan-era political coalition.

Local Government Response and Its Limits

City governments responded to fiscal crisis with a variety of strategies, none of which fully addressed the structural problems that had produced the crisis. Revenue diversification — expanding sales taxes, hotel taxes, parking revenues, and fees for city services — provided some additional resources but tended to be regressive, falling more heavily as a proportion of income on poor households than on wealthy ones. Privatization of some city services — sanitation, parking management, even some correctional functions — was promoted as a way to achieve savings but often resulted in lower wages and fewer benefits for workers and no measurable improvement in service quality. Financial management reforms tightened accounting controls and reduced some inefficiencies but could not generate revenues that the underlying economic collapse had eliminated.

The most durable response to urban fiscal crisis was the pursuit of economic redevelopment — the attempt to attract new investment, businesses, and middle-class residents to replace what deindustrialization had destroyed. Downtown redevelopment projects — convention centers, sports stadiums, luxury hotel complexes, festival marketplaces — were promoted as engines of economic regeneration that would create jobs and tax revenues and restore city fiscal health. These projects, which became the dominant urban policy tool of the 1980s and 1990s, typically required large public subsidies (tax abatements, infrastructure investments, direct financial assistance) and primarily benefited the downtown business districts rather than the poor Black neighborhoods that bore the greatest costs of the urban crisis. The jobs they created were primarily in tourism, hospitality, and retail — lower-wage service positions rather than the manufacturing jobs they were designed to replace. The tax revenues they generated were typically less than the public subsidies they had required. Downtown redevelopment was, at best, a partial and unevenly distributed response to a crisis that required far more comprehensive structural intervention.

Primary Source Window

The Kerner Commission Report (1968): Diagnosing the Urban Crisis

Creator: National Advisory Commission on Civil Disorders (Kerner Commission)  ·  Date: March 1, 1968  ·  Type: Federal commission report

Context: President Lyndon Johnson appointed the National Advisory Commission on Civil Disorders in July 1967, following the devastating urban rebellions in Newark (26 killed) and Detroit (43 killed) that summer. Chaired by Illinois Governor Otto Kerner, the commission included eleven members drawn from government, law enforcement, business, and labor. It was charged with answering three questions: What happened? Why did it happen? What can be done to prevent it from happening again?

The commission's report, released in March 1968, was a 426-page document that drew on exhaustive research into the conditions of Black urban life in American cities. Its central finding was unambiguous: the urban rebellions were the product of "white racism" — specifically the residential segregation, economic exclusion, discriminatory policing, inadequate schools, and chronic unemployment that white society had imposed on Black urban communities. The commission found no evidence that the rebellions had been organized by outside agitators or revolutionary conspiracies; they were spontaneous expressions of rage at documented, specific grievances.

The Commission found that the most fundamental cause of the disorders was the racial attitude and behavior of white Americans toward Black Americans. Race prejudice has shaped our history decisively and now threatens to affect our future. White racism is essentially responsible for the explosive mixture which has been accumulating in our cities since the end of World War II. — Report of the National Advisory Commission on Civil Disorders, 1968 (paraphrased)

The report's recommendations were equally significant. The commission called for massive federal investment in employment, education, housing, and social services in Black urban communities — including a federal jobs program to provide employment for those who could not find it in the private sector, a major public housing construction program with racially integrated placement, significant increases in education funding for urban schools, and aggressive enforcement of anti-discrimination laws. The estimated cost was substantial, and the commission acknowledged that the political will to undertake these investments might be difficult to generate. It proved impossible: the recommendations were largely ignored by the Johnson administration, which was focused on Vietnam, and were actively rejected by the Nixon administration that followed.

Historical significance: The Kerner Report represents the most thorough official examination of the structural causes of Black urban poverty ever produced by the federal government. Its diagnosis — white racism as the fundamental cause — was controversial when it was released and has remained so. Its recommendations, had they been implemented, would have constituted a massive structural intervention in the conditions this chapter describes. The fact that they were not implemented illustrates the fundamental political constraint on the structural response to deindustrialization and urban decline: the political coalition that would have been necessary to fund such an intervention was never assembled, because the white majority's response to urban Black rebellion was not sympathy and reform but fear and reaction.

Historical Debate

What Caused the Urban Crisis? Competing Interpretations

The question: Among the most consequential and most contested questions in American urban history is the explanation for the concentrated poverty, social distress, and institutional deterioration that characterized Black urban neighborhoods in the 1970s and 1980s. Different explanations carry different policy implications and reflect different assumptions about the relative weight of structural forces and individual agency in shaping social outcomes. Students should understand what is at stake in this debate and what evidence supports each position.

Interpretation A — The Structural / Economic Explanation: Associated with sociologists William Julius Wilson, Douglas Massey, and Nancy Denton, and with urban historians like Thomas Sugrue, this interpretation holds that the urban crisis was primarily produced by structural forces: deindustrialization, which eliminated the manufacturing jobs that had sustained Black working-class life; racial residential segregation, which concentrated the effects of those job losses in specific Black neighborhoods; discriminatory federal housing policy, which had created the patterns of segregation that concentrated poverty; and the fiscal crisis of city governments, which eliminated the public services that might have buffered the effects of economic collapse. In this view, the social dislocations of the urban crisis — family instability, school failure, crime, drug use — were consequences of structural economic conditions rather than causes of poverty, and the appropriate policy response was structural: job creation, anti-discrimination enforcement, housing desegregation, and education investment.
Interpretation B — The Cultural / Behavioral Explanation: Associated with conservative social scientists including Charles Murray and Lawrence Mead, and with journalistic accounts of the "underclass," this interpretation holds that the urban crisis was primarily produced by a self-perpetuating culture of poverty — characterized by welfare dependency, the devaluation of marriage and work, the absence of deferred gratification, and the erosion of middle-class norms — that was independent of structural economic conditions and in some versions was actually caused by generous welfare programs that rewarded dependency. In this view, the appropriate policy response was behavioral rather than structural: welfare reform that required work, enforcement of behavioral norms, and the restoration of the traditional family. Charles Murray's 1984 book Losing Ground was the most influential version of this argument, contending that Great Society welfare programs had worsened poverty by creating perverse incentives.
Interpretation C — The Policy Failure Explanation: A third interpretation, which partially overlaps with both of the above, focuses on specific policy failures — particularly in housing and urban development — rather than on either economic structures or cultural factors. Historians like Arnold Hirsch (Making the Second Ghetto) and Thomas Sugrue (The Origins of the Urban Crisis) have traced in detail how specific policy decisions — the siting of public housing, the use of urban renewal to destroy Black neighborhoods, the failure of anti-discrimination enforcement, the structure of union contracts — created and deepened the conditions of Black urban poverty. This interpretation emphasizes the agency of governments, financial institutions, and other institutional actors rather than either abstract market forces or individual cultural choices.

What the evidence suggests: The overwhelming weight of empirical evidence supports the structural and policy-failure explanations over the cultural explanation. The urban crisis was most severe in the cities that experienced the greatest industrial job loss; cities that retained manufacturing employment experienced less concentrated poverty. Black families in the same cities who had access to industrial employment maintained stable two-parent households at rates comparable to white families with similar incomes; the "family breakdown" that cultural explanations emphasized was strongly correlated with income and employment rather than with race or culture. The timing of welfare program expansion does not match the timing of family instability, undermining the argument that welfare caused the latter. And the cultural explanation cannot account for the geographic specificity of the urban crisis — why it was most acute in specific cities and specific neighborhoods rather than diffused across all Black communities.

Multiple Perspectives

Living Through Deindustrialization: Multiple Perspectives

From the perspective of displaced industrial workers: Black workers who had built their lives around industrial employment experienced deindustrialization as a betrayal — of the promise that had drawn their parents northward during the Great Migration, of the stability they had earned through decades of labor, and of the future they had imagined for their children. Many displaced workers describe the experience not only in economic terms but in terms of identity: the loss of a role, a social position, a way of being in the world that industrial employment had provided. "I was a steelworker. I was good at it. I had worked my way up. And then one day it was just gone." The bitterness of displacement was compounded by the awareness that corporate decisions had caused it — decisions made without consultation with workers or communities, in service of shareholder interests rather than community welfare.
From the perspective of young people growing up in deindustrializing neighborhoods: Young people who came of age in the deteriorating neighborhoods of the 1970s and 1980s inherited a landscape shaped by economic collapse without having experienced the relative stability that had preceded it. Their reference point was not the factory floor their parents described but the abandoned buildings, closed stores, and adult unemployment that defined their daily environment. Many describe a combination of neighborhood loyalty — a fierce attachment to the specific streets and people of their community — with a clear-eyed recognition that the institutional pathways that might lead out of poverty were increasingly inaccessible. Schools were deteriorating. Jobs were scarce. The message that "working hard and following the rules" would lead to economic advancement was increasingly difficult to believe when the adults who had done precisely that were standing on stoops rather than going to work.
From the perspective of city officials and urban planners: City officials in deindustrializing cities faced a structural dilemma with no good solutions: declining revenues, increasing demands for services, political pressure from constituencies with conflicting interests, and a federal government that was reducing aid rather than increasing it. Many pursued downtown redevelopment strategies not out of indifference to poor Black neighborhoods but out of a calculation — often mistaken — that economic growth at the top would eventually benefit all residents. Some engaged in more direct community investment but found their resources insufficient to the scale of the problem. The racial politics of urban governance also mattered: white mayors who served primarily white constituencies had incentives to prioritize white neighborhood concerns; Black mayors who were elected in many cities by Black majorities in the 1970s and 1980s inherited fiscal crises they had not caused and often lacked the resources to address them significantly.
From the perspective of corporate executives: Corporate decision-makers who closed plants or relocated operations rarely described their choices as devastating communities. They described them as responding to competitive pressures, maintaining the viability of their companies, and meeting their fiduciary responsibilities to shareholders. In the ideological framework of shareholder capitalism that was becoming dominant in the 1970s and 1980s, the notion that corporations had responsibilities to the communities in which they operated — or to the workers whose labor had generated their profits — was viewed as a confusion of business purposes with social welfare purposes that properly belonged to government. The fact that corporate decisions destroyed specific communities was, from this perspective, an unfortunate byproduct of economic necessity rather than a preventable harm for which the corporation bore responsibility.
From the perspective of white suburban residents: Many white suburbanites who had left cities in the preceding decades experienced urban crisis as a confirmation of the wisdom of their departure — and as a justification for the racial residential separation they had helped to create. The narrative of "inner city decline" was widely understood in racial terms, connecting urban poverty and crime with the Black communities that had become the cities' majority populations as white flight continued. This narrative supported a political stance of opposition to policies — redistributive urban aid, school busing, housing integration — that might have addressed the structural conditions of urban poverty but that were perceived as threatening to suburban racial exclusivity. The political success of the Reagan coalition rested significantly on this suburban white constituency and its resistance to the kinds of structural intervention that might have addressed the urban crisis.
Cause and Effect

The Collapse of Industrial Employment: Causes and Consequences

Long-Term Causes

The structure of American capitalism, which gave corporations wide latitude to make investment and location decisions without accountability to affected communities; the political power of financial capital over industrial capital, which created incentives for short-term profit extraction rather than long-term investment; the fragmented structure of American labor markets, which made it impossible for unions to prevent capital flight by demanding equivalent labor standards across geographic boundaries; and the racial structure of the labor market, which had confined Black workers to the industrial sector and to the lowest-seniority positions within it.

Proximate Causes

International competition from rebuilt European and Japanese manufacturers; automation and technological change reducing labor requirements; corporate relocation to lower-wage regions and countries; the oil shocks of 1973 and 1979, which disrupted energy-intensive industries; the transition from a national economy to a global one that created new opportunities for capital mobility; and the weakening of union bargaining power as declining membership reduced political influence.

Immediate Consequences

Massive unemployment concentrated in Black neighborhoods; dramatic decline in household incomes and wealth; increase in poverty rates and the spatial concentration of poverty; loss of tax revenues for city governments; deterioration of public services, especially schools; outmigration of middle-class residents; physical deterioration of buildings and neighborhoods; expansion of informal economic activity including drug markets.

Long-Term Consequences

Permanent structural unemployment in Black urban communities; the conditions for mass incarceration as drug markets expanded and punitive policing intensified; the generational transmission of poverty through deteriorating schools; the political marginalization of urban Black communities as their economic leverage declined; the eventual emergence of the crack epidemic; and the fragmentation of street organizations from community-based groups into smaller, more volatile drug-market-oriented formations — the subject of later chapters.

Historical complexity: Deindustrialization cannot be reduced to a single cause or explained by any simple model. The interaction of international competition, technological change, corporate strategy, labor market structures, housing policy, and political choices produced an outcome that was not inevitable but was the product of specific decisions within specific structural conditions. Understanding that complexity is essential for evaluating the policy responses that were available — and those that were chosen.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Black Urban Inequality: What Changed with Deindustrialization, and What Stayed the Same

What Changed

  • The economic foundation of Black urban life shifted from industrial manufacturing wages to the service sector and informal economy, dramatically reducing the average wages and economic stability available to Black urban workers
  • Unemployment levels in Black urban neighborhoods reached historically unprecedented concentrations, particularly among young men, in ways that had not been characteristic of Black urban neighborhoods even in the 1950s when discrimination was more overt
  • The spatial concentration of poverty in specific Black neighborhoods intensified dramatically as middle-class Black households moved and industrial employment disappeared
  • City governments lost the fiscal capacity to maintain the public services and institutions that Black communities depended on, shifting the burden of meeting community needs onto already-stressed community organizations and families
  • The street organizations that had developed in previous decades, previously rooted in neighborhood identity and territorial stability, began a transition toward involvement in the drug economy that would accelerate dramatically with the emergence of crack cocaine
  • The political calculus of national politics shifted against Black urban communities, as the Reagan coalition explicitly deprioritized urban needs and reduced federal urban programs

What Stayed the Same

  • Racial residential segregation persisted and in some dimensions deepened, continuing to concentrate the economic consequences of deindustrialization in specific Black neighborhoods rather than distributing them across racially integrated communities
  • Discrimination in employment, lending, and housing continued despite the formal legal prohibitions of the civil rights era, limiting Black economic mobility and access to the suburban economic growth that was occurring
  • The community institutions of Black urban life — churches, fraternal organizations, neighborhood associations, informal mutual aid networks — persisted and continued to provide essential support to communities under stress, drawing on organizational traditions developed over generations
  • The pattern of structural disadvantage being attributed to cultural deficit continued, as political and media discourse increasingly explained urban poverty through frameworks of "culture of poverty" and "underclass" behavior rather than structural economic analysis
  • The relationship between Black communities and police remained fundamentally adversarial, as aggressive policing in economically distressed neighborhoods combined with near-total impunity for police misconduct to reproduce the conflict that had been visible since the early Great Migration era

Why the pattern matters: The combination of new economic catastrophe with persistent structural inequality is what makes deindustrialization so devastating in its consequences. Black communities that had built working-class stability within the constraints of segregation and discrimination had fewer resources — financial, institutional, political — with which to absorb the shocks of deindustrialization than white working-class communities that had been able to accumulate equity and institutional access over decades of relative advantage. The playing field was not level when the economic crisis struck, and the crisis did not level it.

Constitutional Connection

Urban Crisis, Equal Protection, and the Limits of Constitutional Remedy

The urban crisis of the 1970s and 1980s raised fundamental questions about the relationship between constitutional rights and economic conditions — questions that courts and scholars grappled with extensively without reaching satisfying resolutions. The Fourteenth Amendment's guarantee of equal protection of the laws had been the constitutional foundation for the civil rights movement's victories: the desegregation of schools, the prohibition of formal racial discrimination in housing and employment, the protection of voting rights. But what happens when formal legal equality coexists with massive substantive economic inequality? What constitutional protections exist for communities that are not denied rights but are economically destroyed by structural forces and policy choices?

The school funding cases provide the clearest constitutional engagement with urban fiscal inequality. In San Antonio Independent School District v. Rodriguez (1973), the Supreme Court held, in a five-to-four decision, that education was not a fundamental right under the federal Constitution and that the Texas school finance system — which produced dramatic disparities in per-pupil funding between wealthy suburban districts and poor urban and rural districts — did not violate the Fourteenth Amendment's Equal Protection Clause. The decision effectively foreclosed federal constitutional challenges to school funding inequality, leaving the issue to state courts and state constitutional provisions. State courts in New Jersey, Kentucky, Massachusetts, and other states subsequently found that their state constitutions required more equitable school funding, with mixed results in implementation, but the Rodriguez decision removed the federal constitutional mandate for educational equity.

Housing desegregation litigation reached a similar limit in Milliken v. Bradley (1974), where the Supreme Court ruled that a metropolitan desegregation remedy — one that would have included suburban school districts in a desegregation plan for Detroit — was generally impermissible unless suburban districts had themselves actively contributed to segregation. The decision effectively prevented the kind of metropolitan-scale desegregation that would have been necessary to address the concentration of Black poverty in urban school districts, cementing the suburban/urban division that deindustrialization was simultaneously intensifying for economic reasons.

These decisions illustrate a fundamental constitutional limitation: the law can prohibit individual acts of discrimination but cannot easily mandate the redistribution of resources necessary to address the cumulative consequences of historical discrimination. The constitutional framework that the civil rights movement had used to dismantle formal barriers to equality was poorly suited to addressing the structural economic inequalities that deindustrialization was intensifying. Addressing those inequalities required legislative action — redistribution through federal programs, economic development initiatives, school funding reform — of a kind that the political coalition of the Reagan era was explicitly organized to prevent.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has traced the devastating economic transformation that reshaped Black urban life between the mid-1960s and the mid-1980s: the deindustrialization of the Rust Belt cities that had been the primary destinations of the Great Migration, the deepening concentration of poverty in segregated Black neighborhoods, the deterioration of public schools and other urban institutions under fiscal pressure, the stresses on family and community life produced by chronic unemployment, and the fiscal collapse of city governments that stripped communities of the public resources on which they had depended.

The chapter began by placing deindustrialization in context: the Black industrial working class of the early 1960s had built, within the severe constraints of racial segregation and discrimination, a foundation of economic stability that sustained working-class family life, neighborhood institutions, and community organization. That foundation was not wealth or security in any absolute sense — Black workers earned less than white workers in the same industries, were confined to the most dangerous and lowest-seniority jobs, and were the most vulnerable to economic downturns. But it was a foundation — a wage floor, union protections, some degree of economic predictability — on which viable community life could be built.

Deindustrialization destroyed that foundation. Factory closures, corporate relocation, automation, and the restructuring of the American economy from manufacturing to services eliminated millions of industrial jobs in the cities where Black workers were concentrated. These losses fell disproportionately on Black workers because of the seniority structures that had been built on prior exclusion, and they fell disproportionately on Black neighborhoods because residential segregation had concentrated Black workers in specific inner-city areas that bore the geographic brunt of industrial collapse. The result was a pattern of concentrated unemployment — in some neighborhoods exceeding forty or fifty percent of working-age men — that had no real peacetime precedent in American history.

The consequences ramified through every dimension of urban life. Poverty deepened and became more geographically concentrated, creating neighborhoods where virtually everyone was poor and where the institutional resources — educational, commercial, social — that require economic diversity to flourish were absent. Schools, dependent on declining property tax revenues, lost teachers, programs, and physical maintenance at the moment when their students' needs were most acute. Public housing projects that had always been isolated concentrations of the poorest households became more deeply distressed as economic conditions worsened. Families under chronic economic stress showed the social consequences that poverty always produces: conflict, instability, difficulty maintaining consistent parenting — consequences that cultural analysts attributed to Black culture rather than to the economic conditions that produced them in every community that had experienced comparable distress.

The urban fiscal crisis of the 1970s added a second, institutional dimension to the economic catastrophe of deindustrialization. As the tax base that industrial employment had built contracted, city governments lost the revenues that had funded public services, and when they responded to fiscal crisis through austerity — service cuts, layoffs, reduced investment in schools and infrastructure — those cuts fell hardest on the poorest neighborhoods that depended most on public services. The New York fiscal crisis of 1975 was the most dramatic single expression of this pattern, but similar dynamics played out in Detroit, Cleveland, Pittsburgh, Newark, and dozens of other cities across the following decade.

The political response to the urban crisis was, for the most part, inadequate to the structural scale of the problem. The Kerner Commission's diagnosis of white racism as the fundamental cause and its recommendation for massive federal investment in Black urban communities was politically marginalized within months of its publication. The Nixon and Ford administrations reduced rather than increased federal urban aid. The Reagan administration cut programs at the moment of greatest need while promoting a cultural framework that attributed urban poverty to behavioral pathologies rather than structural conditions. Downtown redevelopment, the primary urban policy tool of the 1980s, provided modest benefits for city budgets while failing to reach the Black neighborhoods where economic distress was most acute.

The chapter has also documented the resilience of Black community institutions in the face of deindustrialization. Churches, community organizations, mutual aid networks, and the informal social bonds of neighborhood life persisted and adapted to the conditions of economic collapse, providing some degree of community solidarity and mutual support that cushioned — without eliminating — the most extreme consequences of economic distress. The organizational traditions built over generations of Black urban life were real resources that communities drew on in crisis, and their persistence and creativity in the face of overwhelming structural adversity is part of the historical record that any complete account must include.

The chapter has argued consistently for a structural explanation of the urban crisis — rooted in specific economic decisions, specific policy choices, and specific political calculations — rather than the cultural explanation that dominated popular and conservative political discourse. This is not an argument that culture is irrelevant or that individual choices do not matter. It is an argument about causation: the social dislocations of the urban crisis were primarily consequences of structural economic conditions rather than causes of poverty, and they were produced by forces — corporate investment decisions, government policy choices, political calculations — that were subject to human agency and could therefore have been different.

This argument matters for everything that follows in the textbook. The crack cocaine epidemic of the 1980s and 1990s, the rise of the drug economy within street organizations, the mass incarceration that the War on Drugs produced, the violence that marked Black urban neighborhoods in the late 1980s and 1990s — all of these developments are incomprehensible without the structural context that this chapter has provided. They did not emerge from a vacuum or from a cultural failure. They emerged from the economic catastrophe of deindustrialization, the institutional collapse of fiscal crisis, and the political abandonment of the Reagan era — from the specific conditions that this chapter has traced.

The story of deindustrialization is, finally, a story about what happens when the economic foundations of community life are destroyed by forces that communities cannot control, in conditions that they did not create, and with policy responses that were never adequate to the scale of the problem. Understanding that story with clarity and specificity — resisting the temptation to simplify it into either pure victimhood or cultural failure — is the essential preparation for understanding the decades that followed.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Automation
The replacement of human labor with machines, computers, or mechanized processes in industrial production. In the deindustrialization era, automation reduced the labor content of manufacturing output, meaning that the same level of industrial production required fewer workers and eliminating industrial jobs even in cases where production levels were maintained.
Capital flight
The movement of investment capital away from established locations — typically urban industrial areas — to regions or countries offering lower production costs, weaker labor regulations, or more favorable tax treatment. Capital flight was a primary driver of deindustrialization and was accelerated by the growth of a global economy that made offshore investment increasingly feasible.
Concentrated poverty
The geographic clustering of poor households in specific neighborhoods, typically measured using census poverty rates above 40 percent. Concentrated poverty produces social consequences — in education, community institutions, social networks, physical environment — that are more severe than equivalent poverty distributed across economically diverse communities.
Culture of poverty
A theoretical framework, originating with anthropologist Oscar Lewis and popularized in the American context by the Moynihan Report and later by conservative social scientists, holding that persistent poverty is maintained by cultural characteristics — including attitudes toward work, family, and deferred gratification — that are transmitted across generations independently of structural economic conditions. The framework was widely critiqued for attributing to culture what structural analysis attributed to economic conditions.
Deindustrialization
The process by which a region, city, or economy loses a significant portion of its manufacturing employment base through plant closures, automation, and the geographic relocation of production. In the United States, deindustrialization accelerated between roughly 1965 and 1990, particularly in the industrial cities of the Midwest and Northeast.
Disinvestment
The withdrawal of capital, resources, and institutional attention from a geographic area. In Black urban neighborhoods during the deindustrialization era, disinvestment operated simultaneously at private (landlords, banks, retailers), public (city services, schools), and institutional (community organizations, churches) levels, creating self-reinforcing spirals of decline.
Fiscal austerity
Government budget policies that reduce public spending, typically through service cuts and public sector layoffs, in response to declining revenues or rising debt levels. Urban fiscal austerity in the 1970s and 1980s fell disproportionately on poor Black neighborhoods that depended most heavily on public services and had the least capacity to purchase private alternatives.
Fiscal crisis
A situation in which a government's revenues are insufficient to fund its existing commitments and service obligations. Urban fiscal crises in the 1970s were produced by the convergence of declining tax revenues (from deindustrialization and white flight) and increasing service demands (from growing poverty and social distress), compounded by accumulated debt obligations from the more optimistic fiscal projections of the 1960s.
Informal economy
Economic activity that occurs outside the formal regulated economy, including both legal activities (off-the-books employment, informal childcare and domestic services, neighborhood mutual aid) and illegal activities (drug markets, theft, unlicensed businesses). In deindustrializing Black neighborhoods, both legal and illegal informal economic activities expanded as formal employment contracted.
Kerner Commission
The National Advisory Commission on Civil Disorders, appointed by President Johnson in 1967 to investigate the urban rebellions of that summer. The Commission's landmark 1968 report attributed the rebellions to "white racism" — specifically residential segregation, economic exclusion, inadequate schools, unemployment, and discriminatory policing — and recommended massive federal investment in Black urban communities, recommendations that were largely ignored.
Rust Belt
The geographic and cultural category describing the formerly industrial cities of the Midwest and Northeast that suffered the most severe consequences of deindustrialization, including Detroit, Pittsburgh, Cleveland, Buffalo, Gary, and dozens of smaller industrial cities. The term evokes the image of rusting abandoned factories that became visual icons of post-industrial urban decline.
Seniority system
The practice, established in union contracts, of using length of employment as the primary criterion for determining layoff order, promotion, and job assignment. The seniority system was formally race-neutral but had racially disparate effects in deindustrializing industries: because Black workers had generally been admitted to better-paying industrial positions most recently (following decades of exclusion), they accumulated less seniority and were therefore first to be laid off when production declined.
Service economy
An economy in which the majority of employment is in service industries — retail, healthcare, finance, food service, hospitality, education — rather than in manufacturing or agricultural production. The transition from an industrial to a service economy in American cities generally meant lower average wages, fewer union jobs, less economic stability, and — because of educational requirements and geographic location — reduced accessibility to Black workers displaced from industrial employment.
Spatial mismatch
The concept, developed by economist John Kain, describing the growing geographic distance between where low-skilled jobs are located and where low-income workers live. In deindustrializing cities, spatial mismatch was intensified by the movement of industrial jobs to suburban industrial parks and by the inadequacy of public transit systems designed for an automobile-dependent suburban commuter economy.
Structural unemployment
Unemployment resulting from long-term changes in the structure of the economy — technological change, industrial decline, or geographic shifts in production — rather than from cyclical economic downturns. Workers experiencing structural unemployment cannot simply wait for economic recovery to bring their jobs back; the jobs themselves have permanently disappeared. The structural unemployment produced by deindustrialization was qualitatively different from cyclical unemployment and required different policy responses.
Underclass
A contested sociological and journalistic term used in the 1970s and 1980s to describe the persistently poor population of inner-city Black neighborhoods, characterized by long-term welfare dependency, high rates of unemployment, female-headed households, and involvement in the informal drug economy. Critics argued that the term was stigmatizing, racially coded, and emphasized cultural characteristics rather than structural economic conditions.
Urban crisis
The combination of economic, social, and institutional deterioration that characterized many major American cities — particularly those with large Black populations — in the 1970s and 1980s, including concentrated unemployment, deepening poverty, school decline, physical disinvestment, fiscal collapse, and rising crime and violence. Historians debate the relative weight of structural economic forces, policy failures, and cultural factors in producing the urban crisis.
Urban renewal
Federal and local programs from the 1950s through the 1970s that used eminent domain and public investment to demolish "blighted" urban neighborhoods and replace them with highways, institutional facilities, luxury housing, and commercial development. Urban renewal disproportionately destroyed Black neighborhoods — which Black residents renamed "Negro removal" — and concentrated displaced residents in public housing projects that intensified rather than alleviated concentrated poverty.
White flight
The migration of white middle-class households from central cities to suburbs, accelerated by racial integration of urban neighborhoods, urban rebellions, school busing controversies, and the availability of FHA-subsidized suburban housing that was largely denied to Black households. White flight removed middle-class taxpayers from city tax rolls, accelerated fiscal crisis, and reduced the economic diversity of urban neighborhoods.

Major People and Places

Identification & Significance

William Julius Wilson (1935– )
The most influential sociologist of Black urban poverty in the late twentieth century. His 1987 work The Truly Disadvantaged made the definitive structural case for deindustrialization as the primary cause of concentrated Black urban poverty, distinguishing between poverty and concentrated poverty and arguing that the loss of manufacturing jobs — not cultural factors — was the fundamental driver of the social dislocations visible in inner-city Black communities. Wilson's analysis redirected academic and policy discussion toward structural economic explanations and remains the foundational text in the sociology of Black urban poverty.
Daniel Patrick Moynihan (1927–2003)
Assistant Secretary of Labor in the Johnson administration whose 1965 report, The Negro Family: The Case for National Action, identified the structure of Black family life — particularly high rates of female-headed households — as a primary obstacle to Black advancement. The Moynihan Report was enormously controversial, praised by some as a honest engagement with a genuine problem and condemned by others as blaming victims for the consequences of structural racism. Its framework influenced subsequent conservative cultural explanations of urban poverty and shaped policy debates about welfare, family structure, and urban poverty for decades.
Otto Kerner (1908–1976)
Governor of Illinois and chairman of the National Advisory Commission on Civil Disorders, whose name became attached to the Commission's landmark 1968 report. Kerner, a moderate Democrat with a record of fair administration, presided over a commission that reached conclusions — blaming white racism for the urban crisis — that were more honest and more uncomfortable than most federal reports. He later served a federal prison term for corruption unrelated to the Commission's work, a biographical irony that complicated his legacy.
Detroit, Michigan
The paradigmatic case of Black urban deindustrialization. At its peak in the mid-1950s, Detroit was the home of the American automobile industry and one of the most significant Black urban communities in the country. By the 1980s, it was losing population at a devastating rate, its industrial landscape was defined by shuttered factories, and its Black majority population faced unemployment and institutional deterioration that illustrated with particular clarity the consequences of deindustrialization combined with racial residential segregation and urban fiscal crisis.
Youngstown, Ohio
The site of "Black Monday" in September 1977, when the closure of the Youngstown Sheet and Tube Campbell Works eliminated over 5,000 jobs in a single announcement and marked the beginning of the steel industry's catastrophic decline. Youngstown became the symbol of Rust Belt deindustrialization and was later the subject of important historical and journalistic analysis that documented how corporate decision-making had destroyed a community.
Robert Taylor Homes (Chicago)
The massive public housing development on Chicago's South Side, comprising 28 high-rise towers, that became one of the most concentrated expressions of Black urban poverty in America. Built on the model of concentrated, isolated public housing, the project exemplified the policy failures that had intensified rather than mitigated the conditions of Black urban poverty, and its eventual demolition beginning in the 1990s became a symbol of the federal government's attempt to address the consequences of its own earlier failures.
The Kerner Commission (National Advisory Commission on Civil Disorders)
The federal commission appointed by President Johnson in 1967 to investigate the causes of that summer's urban rebellions. Its 1968 report provided the most comprehensive official analysis of the structural causes of Black urban poverty ever produced by the federal government, attributing the crisis to white racism and recommending massive federal investment in urban communities. Its recommendations were largely ignored, and its diagnosis was politically rejected by the Nixon administration that followed.
Thomas Sugrue
Urban historian whose 1996 book The Origins of the Urban Crisis: Race and Inequality in Postwar Detroit provided the definitive historical analysis of deindustrialization's effects on Detroit's Black community, tracing in careful detail how the interaction of industrial job loss, racial residential segregation, and discriminatory housing policy produced the concentrated poverty visible in Detroit by the 1980s. Sugrue's work is the model for subsequent histories of deindustrialization in other cities.
The South Bronx, New York
The neighborhood that became the iconic image of the urban fiscal crisis — a landscape of burned-out buildings, abandoned lots, and extreme poverty visible to the national television audience during the 1977 World Series. The South Bronx illustrated with particular clarity how deindustrialization, racial residential segregation, fiscal austerity, and physical disinvestment could interact to produce conditions of extraordinary urban devastation.
Chicago's South and West Sides
The Black neighborhoods of Chicago — including the South Side corridor from the stockyards to the South Shore, the West Side ghetto, and the public housing concentrations of the State Street corridor — that were built by the Great Migration and devastated by deindustrialization. Chicago's Black neighborhoods became central to national debates about urban poverty through journalism, social science research (William Julius Wilson was based at the University of Chicago), and the visibility of the Robert Taylor Homes and other large public housing developments.

MGU Library Research Project

Tracing Deindustrialization: A Local History of Economic Collapse

This research project asks you to investigate the history of deindustrialization in a specific city or community, tracing how the loss of industrial employment affected Black neighborhoods, families, and institutions in a specific local context. Local history grounds the large structural forces described in this chapter in the concrete experiences of specific people and places, and often reveals dimensions of the period that national accounts cannot capture.

  1. Choose a city to study. Select one of the following cities for your research: Detroit, Chicago, Cleveland, Pittsburgh, Newark, Baltimore, Philadelphia, Gary (Indiana), Youngstown, or Buffalo. You may also choose a smaller industrial city with a significant Black population if primary sources are available. Choose a city for which library and digital resources are accessible.
  2. Research the industrial history. What were the major industries that employed Black workers in your chosen city? When did they reach their peak employment? What was the process of their decline — when did closures begin, how many jobs were lost, and over what time period? Use academic sources, newspaper archives, and city history resources to document this industrial history.
  3. Trace the impact on Black neighborhoods. Using census data available through the University of Minnesota's NHGIS project or similar sources, track changes in poverty rates, unemployment rates, and population in Black neighborhoods in your chosen city between 1960 and 1990. How did the demographic and economic profile of these neighborhoods change over this period? Which neighborhoods were most affected, and why?
  4. Find and analyze primary sources. Locate at least two primary sources that document the experience of deindustrialization from the perspective of residents, workers, or community organizations in your chosen city. These might include: newspaper accounts of plant closures from local Black newspapers; oral history collections (many universities and historical societies have these); community organization records; city council proceedings; school board minutes; or other local government documents. For each source, analyze what it reveals and what its limits are as historical evidence.
  5. Identify a community response. Research one specific community organization, church initiative, or neighborhood institution that responded to deindustrialization in your chosen city. What did it do? How was it funded? Who led it? What did it accomplish, and what were its limits? How does its history illuminate the chapter's discussion of community resilience?
  6. Write your analysis. In a 5–7 page essay or equivalent presentation, synthesize your research into an analysis of deindustrialization in your chosen city. Apply the analytical framework of this chapter — the structural argument, the role of racial residential segregation, the interaction of economic collapse and fiscal crisis — to the specific local evidence you have gathered. What does the local case confirm about the national pattern, and what, if anything, does it complicate or modify?

Library resources to use: The MGU Library's collections in urban history, labor history, and African American history; the University of Minnesota's National Historical Geographic Information System (NHGIS) for census data; newspaper archives including ProQuest Historical Newspapers and local newspaper digitization projects; the Smithsonian's African American history collections; university and public library oral history collections; and academic databases for peer-reviewed scholarship on specific cities.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter has told the story of an economic catastrophe — one of the most consequential transformations in Black American life in the twentieth century, and one that remains insufficiently understood in popular historical consciousness. Deindustrialization destroyed the economic foundation that the Great Migration had built, concentrating the consequences of industrial collapse in the specific Black neighborhoods that racial residential segregation had created, and creating the structural conditions for the decades of crisis that followed. Understanding this story is not optional for anyone who wants to understand what happened to Black urban communities in the 1980s and 1990s — and what continues to shape them today.

The chapter has insisted, throughout, on a structural rather than cultural explanation for the urban crisis. This is not a politically neutral choice. The cultural explanation — poverty as the product of behavioral pathology, family breakdown, and the absence of mainstream values — has served political interests that preferred to attribute urban distress to the failures of Black people rather than to the failures of economic and political systems. Countering that explanation with evidence is both an intellectual responsibility and, in a sense, a moral one: accurate historical understanding is inseparable from accurate attribution of responsibility for historical harm. The corporations that made plant-closure decisions that destroyed communities, the financial institutions that continued to redline Black neighborhoods, the government officials who cut urban programs while poverty deepened, the political coalition that rewarded racial resentment rather than addressing structural inequality — these were the actors whose choices shaped the urban crisis. Naming them accurately is the beginning of honest historical understanding.

At the same time, the chapter has tried to avoid reducing the history of deindustrialization to a simple story of victimization. Black communities facing economic catastrophe did not simply collapse. They drew on generations of organizational tradition — the mutual aid ethic, the church-centered community, the informal networks of solidarity — to sustain some degree of collective life under conditions of extreme adversity. The churches that fed people, the community organizations that fought for development resources, the workers who organized against plant closures, the mothers and fathers who struggled to maintain families under economic siege — these were not passive recipients of structural forces. They were agents, making choices within constraints, and their choices and their endurance are also part of the history this chapter has tried to tell.

The connection forward — to the crack epidemic, mass incarceration, the fragmentation of street organizations, and the continuing crisis of Black urban America — is the subject of the chapters that follow. But it is worth pausing to observe what this chapter's history implies about those later developments. The drug economies that expanded in the mid-1980s did not create poverty; they were a response to it. The mass incarceration that followed the War on Drugs did not solve the urban crisis; it deepened it. The fragmentation of street organizations from neighborhood-based groups into smaller, more volatile formations did not reflect a degradation of community values; it reflected the destruction of the economic and institutional conditions on which more stable community organization had depended. Everything in the remaining chapters of this textbook flows, directly or indirectly, from the economic catastrophe that this chapter has documented. Returning to that foundation is essential whenever the complexity of later events threatens to obscure its origins.

The final lesson of this chapter is about the relationship between economic policy and community life — a relationship that American political culture persistently refuses to acknowledge directly. Public safety depends on economic stability. Community resilience depends on economic resources. Educational achievement depends on adequately funded schools. Family stability depends on wages sufficient for stable households. These connections are not speculative. They are empirically documented across multiple historical periods, multiple countries, and multiple communities. The urban crisis of the 1970s and 1980s is, in this sense, a historical experiment that demonstrates with painful clarity what happens when economic foundations are destroyed, public resources are withdrawn, and communities are left to manage catastrophic structural change with their own insufficient resources. The lesson of that experiment is not that Black communities failed to respond adequately. It is that the response to structural economic catastrophe must itself be structural — and that political choices to avoid that structural response have real and lasting human costs.

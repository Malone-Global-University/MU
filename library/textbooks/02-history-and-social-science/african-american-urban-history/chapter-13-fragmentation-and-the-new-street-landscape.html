Part V — Fragmentation, Redevelopment, and the Contemporary City
13

Fragmentation and the New Street Landscape

From Migration to Fragmentation: The Urban History of Black Street Organizations

Chapter Opening

"These new shorties don't respect the old ways. There's no structure, no codes. Everybody's beefing with everybody and nobody even knows why it started." — Chicago community organizer, quoted in field research on Chicago's South Side, early 2010s

By the early 2010s, the men and women who had spent decades studying Chicago's street organizations — sociologists, police commanders, community workers, journalists, and former members themselves — were noting something that seemed, on the surface, paradoxical. The large, hierarchically organized street organizations that had defined Chicago's South and West Side neighborhoods since the 1960s were weakening. The Gangster Disciples, the Black P. Stones, the Vice Lords, the Latin Kings — formations that had once enrolled tens of thousands of members under identifiable leadership structures, operated with recognizable internal codes and authority, claimed specific territories mapped over years of negotiation and conflict, and occasionally leveraged their organizational capacity for political or community purposes — were fragmenting. Where once there had been a structure, there were now dozens of smaller units, loosely connected or not connected at all, operating by different names and internal logics across neighborhoods that the old organizations had once unified. And despite this weakening of large, institutionalized organizations, the violence in many of these neighborhoods did not decrease. In some measures, it increased, grew more random, more confusing to outside observers, and more deadly for the young men caught within it.

How do we understand this transformation? What forces produced it? What does it mean for the communities in which it occurred? These are the central questions this chapter addresses. The fragmentation of large street organizations and the emergence of a new landscape of small crews, cliques, and digitally mediated conflicts did not arise from nowhere. It was the product of at least three decades of accumulated pressure on Black urban communities and on the organizations that had developed within them: the mass incarceration of a generation of organizational leadership; the physical destruction of the public housing projects that had given territorial organization its geography; the economic pressures of deindustrialization and the collapse of the drug market that had partly replaced industrial employment; the dispersal of communities through gentrification and displacement; and the rise of digital communication technologies that transformed how young people managed reputation, conflict, and identity in conditions of persistent poverty and danger.

The story of fragmentation is also, in an important sense, the culmination of the larger story this textbook has been telling. The Great Migration brought millions of Black Southerners to northern and western cities in search of economic opportunity and escape from the racial terror of the Jim Crow South. Segregation and redlining concentrated them in specific neighborhoods and denied them access to the resources and opportunities that were available in other parts of those cities. Deindustrialization stripped those neighborhoods of their economic base. The crack cocaine epidemic and the War on Drugs brought unprecedented violence and unprecedented punishment to communities that were already stressed nearly to the breaking point. Mass incarceration removed a substantial portion of the adult male population from these neighborhoods and deposited them in a prison system that further damaged their connections to family and community. Gentrification and urban redevelopment then began physically destroying the neighborhoods themselves, displacing residents and breaking apart the social networks that had sustained community life across generations of adversity. What fragmentation represents, in this light, is not primarily a failure of the street organizations themselves or of the communities they emerged from. It is the predictable consequence of what happens when community structures face this accumulation of pressures over multiple decades.

At the same time, fragmentation is not a simple story of decline or pathology. The young people who formed small crews and cliques in the 2000s and 2010s were not simply failed inheritors of an older tradition. They were responding, with the resources available to them, to the specific conditions of their specific moment: neighborhoods where the old territorial maps had been scrambled by displacement and demolition, where the internet and social media offered new tools for managing the reputation and status that had previously been managed through face-to-face organizational membership, where smartphones made the escalation of conflicts faster and more public than anything the older generation had experienced, and where the structural conditions that produced group conflict — poverty, concentrated disadvantage, educational failure, the absence of legitimate economic opportunity — remained as intense as ever even as the organizations that had channeled those conditions changed form.

This chapter examines the fragmentation process in detail, tracing its causes, its dynamics, and its consequences for the communities most directly affected by it. It looks at how mass incarceration dismantled organizational leadership; how the demolition of public housing scattered the territorial geographies on which street organizations had been built; how the drug economy's collapse and transformation changed the economic logic of street organization; how digital technology created new forms of reputation management and new vectors for conflict escalation; and how different cities experienced these processes in different ways, shaped by the specific histories and geographies that preceding chapters have described. The chapter includes a substantial case study of Chicago's drill music scene, which became, in the 2010s, the most visible cultural expression of the fragmented street landscape and one of the most intensely debated cultural phenomena in American urban life.

The chapter also examines what fragmentation meant for ordinary people living in affected neighborhoods — for the mothers and grandmothers who had learned to navigate the old territorial codes and now found themselves in a landscape whose logic they could not read; for the young men caught in conflicts whose origins they barely understood; for teachers and community workers trying to reach students whose social world was being conducted on platforms and in vernaculars that adults struggled to comprehend; for the older former members who had invested decades in building organizational structures that were now dissolving; and for the researchers, journalists, and policymakers struggling to understand a phenomenon that did not fit the frameworks they had developed to understand the older street organization landscape.

Finally, the chapter asks what fragmentation means for understanding the larger urban history this textbook has traced. If the large street organizations of the mid-twentieth century can be understood, as previous chapters have argued, as community formations that emerged from specific historical conditions — the conditions of segregation, redlining, deindustrialization, and exclusion that shaped Black urban life — then fragmentation must be understood as a response to the transformation of those conditions. The neighborhoods that produced large, hierarchically organized street organizations no longer exist in the same form. The public housing towers are gone. The industrial employment base is gone. The stable community networks that gave the old organizations their social roots have been disrupted by displacement, incarceration, and economic change. What has emerged in their place is not nothing — it is a new and in many ways more dangerous street landscape — but it is something different, produced by different conditions, and requiring different analytical frameworks to understand.

Understanding fragmentation historically, as the product of specific structural forces operating over specific decades in specific communities, is the essential task for anyone who wants to think seriously about what it means, why it persists, and what, if anything, can be done about it. This chapter attempts that understanding.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain the concept of fragmentation as it applies to Black street organizations in the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries, and describe the structural forces most responsible for producing it.
  2. Trace the relationship between mass incarceration and the decline of large, hierarchically organized street organizations, explaining how the removal of organizational leadership affected internal cohesion and territorial control.
  3. Analyze how the demolition of large public housing projects scattered the geographic communities on which territorial street organizations had been built, and assess the consequences of this scattering for both organizational structure and community networks.
  4. Describe the characteristics of the small crew and clique formations that emerged to replace large street organizations in many cities, and explain how their logic and dynamics differed from those of the older organizations they partly displaced.
  5. Evaluate the role of social media and digital communication technology in transforming how young men in disadvantaged neighborhoods managed reputation, status, and conflict, explaining both the new capabilities these technologies provided and the new dangers they introduced.
  6. Analyze Chicago's drill music scene as a cultural phenomenon rooted in the specific conditions of the fragmented street landscape, explaining its relationship to the neighborhoods from which it emerged and the debates its content generated.
  7. Compare the fragmentation process in Chicago, Los Angeles, Baltimore, and other major cities, identifying both common patterns and city-specific differences shaped by local history, geography, and policy.
  8. Assess competing interpretations of whether fragmentation made urban neighborhoods more or less dangerous, examining the evidence for each position and identifying the methodological challenges in making this assessment.
  9. Identify the community consequences of fragmentation for residents of affected neighborhoods, including families, community institutions, and young people who were not themselves involved in street organizations.
  10. Connect the fragmentation of street organizations to the larger history of Black urban community building and its successive disruptions that this textbook has traced, explaining what fragmentation reveals about the cumulative effects of structural inequality on community organization.
  11. Distinguish between fragmentation as a structural process produced by specific historical forces and fragmentation as a cultural or moral failure, explaining why the latter framing distorts the historical record.
  12. Interpret primary sources from the digital era — social media posts, drill lyrics, police intelligence documents, community organization reports, oral histories — as historical evidence with specific limits, perspectives, and contexts.

Guiding Historical Questions

Questions for Historical Inquiry

  1. What does "fragmentation" mean as a description of the transformation of Black street organizations in the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries, and why do historians and sociologists use this term rather than simply "decline"?
  2. Which forces were most responsible for producing fragmentation — mass incarceration, public housing demolition, economic transformation, demographic displacement, or digital technology — and how did these forces interact with each other?
  3. How did the small crew and clique formations that emerged from fragmentation differ from the large street organizations that preceded them, and what do those differences reveal about the conditions that produced each type of formation?
  4. How did social media transform the management of reputation, status, and conflict among young men in disadvantaged neighborhoods, and what does this transformation reveal about the relationship between technology and social structure?
  5. What is drill music, what is its relationship to the fragmented street landscape it emerged from, and how should cultural historians understand it — as documentation, as celebration, as trauma response, or as something more complicated than any of these?
  6. Did fragmentation make cities more dangerous? What does the evidence show, and why is this question more difficult to answer than it might appear?
  7. How did different cities experience fragmentation differently, and what does regional variation reveal about the importance of local history, policy, and geography in shaping the street landscape?
  8. What does fragmentation tell us about the cumulative consequences of structural inequality when it is sustained across multiple decades and multiple domains of community life simultaneously?
  9. How should communities, policymakers, and researchers respond to a fragmented street landscape that the tools developed for dealing with large street organizations may not be adequate to address?

Historical Context

To understand fragmentation, it is necessary to understand what the landscape looked like before it — the world of the large, institutionalized street organizations that had developed in American cities over the preceding half-century — and to understand the forces that transformed that world. Previous chapters of this textbook have traced in detail how large street organizations emerged in Chicago, Los Angeles, and other cities from the 1950s through the 1980s: how they developed from neighborhood youth clubs and corner groups, acquired formal organizational structures and leadership hierarchies, established territorial claims over specific geographic areas, became embedded in the drug economy that deindustrialization had partly made necessary, and accumulated enormous memberships before the combined forces of the crack epidemic's violence, the War on Drugs, and mass incarceration began to transform them. This historical background is essential for understanding fragmentation, because fragmentation is precisely the story of what happened to those organizations after those forces had done their work.

The late 1980s and early 1990s represented, in many respects, the apex of large street organization formation in American cities. In Chicago, the Gangster Disciples under Larry Hoover's leadership had achieved something approaching genuine institutional scale: a membership numbering in the tens of thousands, a hierarchical leadership structure with identifiable positions and responsibilities, a prison-based organizational infrastructure that maintained cohesion across incarceration, and a brief and heavily contested attempt at political engagement through registered voter organizing in the early 1990s. The Black P. Stones and the Vice Lords, the Latin Kings and the Black Disciples, similarly maintained structures of recognizable scale and internal organization. In Los Angeles, the Crips and Bloods — federations of loosely affiliated neighborhood sets rather than unified organizations, but connected by shared identity, color, and conflict — had spread from South Central to suburbs, prisons, and, through migration and incarceration, to cities across the country. In cities like Baltimore, Philadelphia, Detroit, and New York, analogous formations had developed with their own specific histories and structures.

Several forces converged from the early 1990s onward to destabilize these formations. The first and most consequential was mass incarceration. The wave of anti-drug enforcement that accelerated after the passage of the Anti-Drug Abuse Acts of 1986 and 1988, the Supreme Court's validation of aggressive stop-and-frisk and pretextual traffic stop practices, and the expansion of federal conspiracy prosecutions under RICO statutes (the Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act) targeted street organization leadership with particular effectiveness. Prosecutors discovered that RICO, originally designed to combat organized crime syndicates, could be applied to street organizations that engaged in drug distribution — and that the conspiracy provisions allowed them to charge entire organizational networks based on the actions of individual members, producing sweeping prosecutions that removed hundreds of members in single indictments. In Chicago, federal RICO prosecutions decimated the leadership of the Gangster Disciples and other major organizations repeatedly across the 1990s and 2000s. In Los Angeles, Operation Hammer and subsequent enforcement initiatives swept thousands of alleged Crip and Blood members into the correctional system. In Baltimore, gang prosecutions using federal conspiracy statutes produced mass sentencing that effectively removed the leadership of major drug distribution organizations.

The second major force was the demolition of large public housing projects. As Chapter 12 discussed in detail, the federal HOPE VI program initiated in 1992 funded the demolition of severely distressed public housing, and cities across the country used this funding to tear down the large high-rise projects — Chicago's Robert Taylor Homes and Cabrini-Green, Baltimore's Lexington Terrace, New Orleans's Iberville and St. Bernard, St. Louis's Pruitt-Igoe — that had concentrated Black urban poverty in specific, identifiable geographic locations. Whatever the legitimate arguments for this demolition on housing quality grounds, it had devastating consequences for the social organization of the communities that had lived in those projects. The housing projects had been, for all their terrible conditions, genuine communities — places where families had lived for generations, where social networks were dense and overlapping, where community institutions had found their footing, and where the territorial maps of street organizations had been drawn over decades. When the projects came down and their residents were scattered — to other parts of the city, to suburbs, to other states through voucher programs — those social networks were disrupted, those community institutions lost their congregations, and those territorial maps were rendered obsolete overnight.

The third force was the transformation of the drug economy itself. The crack cocaine market that had fueled much of the violence and organizational expansion of the late 1980s and early 1990s declined through a combination of market saturation, changing consumer preferences, and enforcement. The enormous profit margins that crack had briefly generated, and that had given street organizations both an economic rationale and a source of revenue to sustain organizational infrastructure, contracted. The drug market fragmented into smaller, more competitive, less territorially stable arrangements, in which the advantages of large organizational structure were less clear. At the same time, the consequences of drug market involvement — increasingly severe federal sentences, intensified enforcement, RICO prosecutions — made the costs of that involvement greater. The economic logic that had partly sustained large street organizations weakened at the same moment that enforcement was most intensely targeting their leadership.

The fourth force was the broader demographic transformation that gentrification and redevelopment were producing in many historically Black neighborhoods, as discussed in Chapter 12. The displacement of long-term residents broke apart the community networks that had given street organizations their social roots. When families were scattered across a metropolitan area, when the neighborhood that had been the territory of a particular organization was populated by new residents with no connection to that territorial history, the organizational geography became increasingly difficult to maintain. Young people growing up in dispersal — in new neighborhoods where they knew nobody, in suburban complexes where the social networks of the city were absent — found themselves without the community context that had previously structured street organization membership and identity.

Into this landscape of organizational disruption came digital communication technology. The widespread adoption of cell phones in the late 1990s and early 2000s, and the explosion of social media platforms — MySpace from the mid-2000s, Facebook from 2006, YouTube, Twitter, and Instagram in the late 2000s and early 2010s — provided young people in disadvantaged neighborhoods with tools for managing reputation, communicating threats, publicizing conflicts, and building social networks that cut across and superseded the old geographic boundaries of territorial organization. Social media did not cause fragmentation; the structural forces described above were its primary causes. But social media provided a new context within which fragmented street dynamics played out, amplifying conflicts, accelerating escalation, and creating new forms of public reputation management that had no precedent in the older organizational landscape.

The emergence of drill music — originating in Chicago's South Side around 2011 and 2012 before spreading to New York, London, and beyond — was the most visible cultural expression of this new landscape. Drill was not merely a musical genre. It was a form of documentary expression rooted in the specific social world of Chicago's fragmented street landscape: the small crews rather than large organizations, the granular block-by-block territorial conflicts, the digital reputation wars conducted on social media, the casualties that seemed to accumulate faster than they could be mourned, and the extraordinary creative talent that young people in conditions of concentrated disadvantage have consistently found ways to express. Understanding drill requires understanding the landscape it emerged from, and understanding that landscape requires understanding the history that produced it — the history that this textbook has been tracing since Chapter 1.

Major People, Groups, and Places

Reference — People, Groups & Places

Larry Hoover (1950– )
The leader of the Gangster Disciples whose federal conviction in 1997 on drug conspiracy and murder charges — following his earlier state conviction — resulted in a sentence of six consecutive life terms, effectively removing the most prominent symbol of large-scale street organization leadership from Chicago's street landscape. Hoover's prosecution and imprisonment were paradigmatic of the federal RICO strategy that dismantled large organizational structures across multiple cities. His continued influence from federal prison, and the ongoing organizational fracturing that followed his removal, illustrate both the resilience and the fragility of street organization hierarchies when facing federal prosecution.
Chief Keef (Keith Cozart, 1995– )
Chicago rapper from Englewood's 300 crew whose 2012 track "I Don't Like" became the defining early document of drill music and brought national attention to Chicago's fragmented street landscape. Chief Keef's music, his public conflicts, his legal history, and his online presence made him the most visible early figure of the drill era, embodying the ways in which social media, music, and street conflict had become intertwined in the new landscape. His career and its trajectory are a significant case study for understanding how the fragmented street landscape produced specific cultural forms.
Lil Durk (Durk Derrick Banks, 1992– ) and Polo G (Taurus Tremani Bartlett, 1999– )
Chicago drill and rap artists who, like Chief Keef, emerged from specific neighborhoods and social networks on Chicago's South and West Sides and whose music documented and reflected the social world of those neighborhoods. Their careers illustrate the way in which drill music provided a pathway — contested, uneven, and not available to most — out of the conditions the music described, while their lyrics and public statements provide historians with documentary evidence about how young people in those conditions understood and articulated their experiences.
Englewood (Chicago)
A neighborhood on Chicago's South Side that became one of the most intensively studied and most intensively affected communities in the fragmentation era. Englewood's demographics — majority Black, high poverty, high unemployment, significantly depopulated since the 1960s — and its position at the center of the drill music scene made it a focal point for research, media attention, and policy debate about urban violence and fragmentation. The neighborhood's history across the textbook's full arc — from Great Migration destination to redlined enclave to public housing community to deindustrialized poverty zone to fragmented street landscape — makes it in some respects a compressed case study of the full story this book tells.
Cabrini-Green (Chicago)
The public housing project on Chicago's Near North Side whose demolition, completed in 2011, became one of the most symbolically and practically significant examples of how public housing destruction scattered established communities and disrupted the social geography on which street organizations had been built. Cabrini-Green's demolition also produced one of the most striking examples of gentrification dynamics, as the land it occupied — valuable real estate near the lakefront — was redeveloped as luxury housing. The fate of Cabrini-Green families, scattered to different parts of the metropolitan area through voucher programs, illustrates the community costs of displacement even when the physical housing conditions were genuinely terrible.
Robert Taylor Homes (Chicago)
Once the largest public housing project in the world, with 4,321 apartments in 28 high-rise towers stretching two miles along the Dan Ryan Expressway on Chicago's South Side. The Taylor Homes were home to as many as 27,000 people at their peak, and they were also home to some of the most powerful street organization territorial structures in the city. Their demolition, completed in 2007, scattered thousands of families and disrupted organizational territories that had been established over decades, contributing significantly to the fragmentation of South Side street organization structure.
Cure Violence (formerly CeaseFire)
The violence intervention organization founded in Chicago by epidemiologist Gary Slutkin, which approached urban violence as a contagious disease and used "violence interrupters" — typically former gang members with credibility in affected communities — to intervene in conflicts before they escalated to shootings. Cure Violence became one of the most widely studied and replicated violence intervention models in the country, and its approach — focused on interrupting specific conflicts rather than dismantling organizational structures — was in many respects better adapted to the fragmented landscape than enforcement strategies designed to decapitate hierarchical organizations.
RICO (Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act)
A federal statute originally passed in 1970 to combat organized crime, which prosecutors discovered could be applied to street organizations engaged in drug distribution and other criminal enterprises. RICO's conspiracy provisions allowed prosecutors to charge entire organizational networks based on the actions of individual members, producing sweeping prosecutions that removed large numbers of members — including leadership tiers — in single indictments. RICO prosecutions were among the most effective tools for dismantling large street organizations, and their cumulative effect over the 1990s and 2000s was a major driver of organizational fragmentation.
Gang databases
Law enforcement systems for cataloging individuals identified as gang members or associates, used to target enforcement, enhance sentences under gang sentencing statutes, and share intelligence across jurisdictions. Gang databases have been criticized for their inclusion of individuals based on minimal criteria, their racial disparities (in which Black and Latino young men are catalogued at vastly disproportionate rates), and their lack of due process protections for removal. In the fragmentation era, gang databases became both tools of enforcement and subjects of significant civil liberties litigation, as people were held to have gang affiliations based on social media connections, neighborhood residence, and other criteria that critics argued criminalized normal social behavior.
Chiraq
A portmanteau of "Chicago" and "Iraq" that became widely used in the early 2010s — first within drill music and then in media coverage — to describe the violence levels in certain Chicago neighborhoods. The term was controversial: some residents and activists rejected it as sensationalizing and decontextualizing the violence, while others, including some residents of affected neighborhoods, used it to describe their own experience of living under conditions that felt like war. The debate over "Chiraq" as a descriptor reflects broader debates about how language, representation, and media framing shape public understanding of urban violence and its causes.
Baltimore's Sandtown-Winchester neighborhood
A neighborhood in West Baltimore that became one of the most intensively studied examples of concentrated disadvantage and street-level conflict in the fragmentation era. Sandtown-Winchester gained national attention following the April 2015 death of Freddie Gray in police custody, which triggered the Baltimore Uprising. The neighborhood's history — as a Great Migration destination, a redlined community, a site of urban renewal displacement, a deindustrialized poverty zone, and ultimately a landscape of fragmented conflict — parallels in important ways the Chicago story, illustrating how similar structural forces produced similar outcomes in different cities.
The "shorties" problem
A term used by older former street organization members and community workers in Chicago and other cities to describe the challenge posed by very young participants — sometimes as young as eleven or twelve — in street-level conflict who had grown up in the fragmented landscape without exposure to the codes, hierarchies, and conflict-limiting norms of the older organizational structure. The "shorties" problem is significant for understanding fragmentation because it illustrates how the breakdown of organizational hierarchy removed the internalized constraints that had partly moderated conflict in the older structure, even as the conditions producing conflict remained as intense as ever.

Section 13.1

The Decline of Large Street Organizations

The Peak of Organizational Scale

To understand the decline of large street organizations, it is necessary first to appreciate what they had become by the late 1980s and early 1990s — the scale of their membership, the complexity of their structures, the depth of their embeddedness in community life, and the ways in which their existence had shaped the social geography of the cities they inhabited. In Chicago, the Gangster Disciples were, by most estimates, the largest single street organization in the country, with membership estimates ranging from twenty thousand to thirty thousand across the metropolitan area. The organization had a formal hierarchical structure — a "board of directors," regional governors, coordinators, and a rank-and-file membership divided by geographic territory — that had been codified in written documents, enforced through internal discipline, and sustained across incarceration through a prison-based leadership network that maintained organizational cohesion even when most of the leadership was behind bars.

The Gangster Disciples' scale was exceptional but not entirely unique. The Black P. Stones, which had developed from the Woodlawn Organization's Blackstone Rangers in the late 1960s, maintained significant organizational scale and internal structure. The Vice Lords, similarly, had developed from a 1950s youth organization into a multi-generational street organization with a complex internal hierarchy and a history of engagement with community institutions and political organizations. The Latin Kings maintained extensive organizational structure connecting their Chicago membership with affiliates in other states. These were not simply loose affiliations of young men from the same neighborhood. They were, in important respects, genuine organizations with recognizable internal governance, mechanisms for conflict resolution, and the capacity to mobilize members collectively.

In Los Angeles, the organizational picture was different but comparably complex. The Crips and Bloods were not unified organizations in the Chicago sense but confederations of hundreds of neighborhood "sets," each with its own name, territory, and internal hierarchy, connected by shared color, shared identity, and shared conflict with opposing sets. The Rollin 60s Crips, the Eight Trey Gangster Crips, the Compton Crips, the Bloods sets of Compton and Inglewood — each of these was a genuine community-based organization with deep roots in specific neighborhoods, its own history, its own internal leadership, and its own codes of conduct. The geographic spread of these sets, which followed Black migration within the Los Angeles basin and then extended through incarceration-driven dispersal to cities across the country, gave the Crip and Blood identities a national and even international scope that no single hierarchical structure could have produced.

The significance of these organizational structures for community life was profound and deeply ambivalent. Previous chapters have described in detail how street organizations functioned as community institutions within the specific conditions of segregated, disinvested Black neighborhoods: as sources of economic opportunity in a deindustrialized economy, as territorial protection against inter-neighborhood conflict, as frameworks of identity and belonging for young men excluded from mainstream pathways to recognized social status, and as informal governance structures in neighborhoods where formal institutions were absent, unreliable, or hostile. These functions did not make street organizations benign — the violence they generated was real, and its costs fell most heavily on the communities they inhabited — but they do help explain why those organizations were embedded in community life in ways that simple enforcement strategies could not easily dislodge.

Federal Prosecution and the Decapitation Strategy

The federal government's discovery that the RICO statute could be applied to street organizations was, from a prosecution standpoint, enormously powerful. RICO allowed prosecutors to charge all members of a criminal enterprise — not merely those directly involved in specific criminal acts — with conspiracy based on their participation in the organization's overall pattern of criminal activity. A member who had not himself committed violence could be charged with conspiracy to commit violence if other members had done so as part of the organization's activities. A low-level drug dealer could be charged with the full criminal enterprise's drug distribution activities, not merely his own sales. And the penalties for RICO violations were severe — federal drug and conspiracy charges carried sentences measured in decades, not years.

The effect of sustained RICO prosecution on large street organizations was devastating to their leadership structures. Beginning in the 1990s, federal prosecutors in Chicago, Los Angeles, Baltimore, New York, and other cities mounted RICO prosecutions that swept the top tiers of major organizations into federal prison. In Chicago, the 1997 federal prosecution of Larry Hoover and the Gangster Disciples leadership network produced more than a hundred convictions and effectively removed the organization's entire leadership structure from the street. Similar prosecutions followed for the Black P. Stones, the Vice Lords, and other major organizations. The pattern was not unique to Chicago: Baltimore saw federal prosecutions of the drug distribution networks that dominated its most violent neighborhoods, Los Angeles saw prosecutions of Crip and Blood set leadership, and cities across the country experienced variants of the same strategy.

Prosecutors and law enforcement officials described this "decapitation strategy" as an effective approach to dismantling criminal organizations: remove the leadership, and the organization loses its capacity for coordination, direction, and sustained criminal enterprise. There is evidence that this logic had some validity in the short term — the removal of specific leaders did disrupt specific criminal networks and reduce specific criminal activities in specific areas. But the longer-term consequences of the decapitation strategy were more complicated than this simple logic suggested. When organizational leadership was removed without addressing the structural conditions — poverty, unemployment, exclusion, and the absence of legitimate economic opportunity — that had produced the organizations in the first place, the result was not the elimination of group conflict but its reorganization into forms that were, in important respects, more dangerous than what they replaced.

The problem was that large, hierarchically organized street organizations, for all the violence they generated and facilitated, also exercised a degree of internal regulation over that violence. Organizational hierarchies created chains of authority within which disputes could be adjudicated and conflicts managed. Leaders had interests in limiting gratuitous violence that drew police attention and disrupted drug market operations. Experienced members transmitted codes of conduct — however contested and imperfectly observed — that distinguished legitimate organizational activity from gratuitous violence. Territorial maps, negotiated over years of conflict and occasionally through explicit truces, created a degree of predictability in inter-organizational relations. When these structures were decapitated, the internal regulatory capacity they provided was removed along with the criminal enterprise capacity that was the prosecution's target. What filled the vacuum was not peace but a more chaotic, less regulated form of conflict in which the limiting functions of organizational hierarchy were absent.

The Destruction of Public Housing Communities

The demolition of large public housing projects was the second major force transforming the street organization landscape. As discussed in Chapter 12, the HOPE VI program initiated in 1992 funded the demolition of the most severely distressed public housing in the country, and cities across the country used this funding to tear down the high-rise towers that had concentrated Black urban poverty in specific geographic locations. In Chicago, the Plan for Transformation announced in 2000 committed to demolishing all 51 of the city's high-rise public housing towers and replacing them with mixed-income developments — a process that scattered approximately 25,000 families across the metropolitan area over the following decade.

The consequences for street organization structure were profound and largely unanticipated by the planners who designed the demolition program. Street organizations in public housing communities had, over decades, established territorial maps that were tied to the specific geography of specific buildings and courtyards. The Disciples who controlled specific buildings in Robert Taylor Homes, the Vice Lords who had established their presence in specific buildings of the Henry Horner Homes, the P. Stones of Cabrini-Green — all of these had organizational geographies that presupposed the continued existence of the physical communities in which they were embedded. When those communities were demolished, the organizational geographies were rendered obsolete.

The families who were relocated through voucher programs were scattered widely — to neighborhoods on the South and West Sides where they had no pre-existing social networks, to suburbs where they were isolated from the community institutions they had relied on, and to other parts of the metropolitan area where they arrived as strangers in unfamiliar territory. Young men from public housing communities who had been embedded in established organizational structures found themselves in new neighborhoods where their organizational identity meant nothing and where new territorial conflicts awaited them. The social networks that had given street organizations their membership pools and community roots were disrupted. The result was a period of intense conflict as displaced individuals and groups attempted to establish new territorial claims in areas where others were already entrenched, and as the organizational maps that had previously structured conflict became irrelevant.

Researchers at the University of Chicago and Northwestern who studied the displacement process documented patterns of what they called "conflict export" — the movement of gang-related violence from demolished project communities into receiving neighborhoods as displaced residents brought their conflicts with them. The residents themselves, interviewed in the years following displacement, frequently described the experience as profoundly traumatic: the loss not merely of housing but of community, of familiarity, of the social networks that had provided whatever safety and support their previous circumstances had made possible. Many reported that the mixed-income developments that replaced the projects were not accessible to them — their incomes were too low to qualify for market-rate units, and the below-market units in mixed-income developments were too few to accommodate all displaced families — and that the voucher system had produced a kind of scattered, atomized poverty that was in important respects more socially damaging than the concentrated poverty they had been displaced from.

Historical Vocabulary
Fragmentation

In the study of urban street organizations, fragmentation refers to the process by which large, hierarchically organized street organizations were disaggregated into smaller, less structured, and less territorially coherent units — variously called crews, cliques, sets, or blocks — that operated with reduced organizational coherence, weaker internal authority structures, and more localized and less predictable conflict dynamics.

Fragmentation is not the same as decline, though it is often confused with it. The total number of individuals involved in street-level conflict did not necessarily decrease with fragmentation; in some cities and periods, it increased. What changed was the organizational form through which that involvement was structured: from large organizations with recognizable hierarchies, identifiable leadership, and negotiated territorial maps, to small, ephemeral, overlapping units whose boundaries were fluid, whose internal authority was weak, and whose conflicts were driven by personal grievances and reputational dynamics more than by organizational imperatives.

Historians and sociologists have traced fragmentation to the convergence of multiple structural forces operating simultaneously: federal prosecution strategies that targeted organizational leadership; public housing demolition that scattered the geographic communities on which organizational territories had been built; the transformation of the drug economy that reduced the economic logic of large organizational structure; demographic displacement through gentrification and redevelopment; and the rise of digital communication technology that provided new tools for reputation management and conflict escalation outside of organizational frameworks.

The concept of fragmentation matters for historical analysis because it challenges simple narratives of "gang decline" or "gang elimination" that have sometimes been used to evaluate anti-gang policy. Understanding fragmentation rather than simply measuring organizational membership requires attention to organizational structure, conflict dynamics, and community effects — not merely to the size and formal persistence of named organizations.

Economic Transformation and the Drug Market

The crack cocaine market that had fueled much of the violence and organizational expansion of the late 1980s and early 1990s underwent significant transformation through the 1990s and 2000s. Crack markets had been enormously profitable in their early years precisely because they were new: demand was high, supply chains were not yet fully established, and the profit margins available to street-level distributors were exceptional by the standards of the informal economy. As the 1990s progressed, crack markets matured and then contracted. Consumer demand shifted; crack addiction, with its devastating visible consequences, made it less attractive to new users than cocaine powder had been. The enforcement response — including enhanced federal sentences for crack offenses — drove up the costs of market participation. And market saturation reduced the extraordinary profit margins that had made early crack distribution so lucrative.

The consequences for street organization economics were significant. Drug market revenue had been one of the primary sources of funding for organizational infrastructure — for paying members, maintaining territorial claims through violence when necessary, funding legal defense for incarcerated members, and supporting the organizational activities that gave large formations their cohesion. As drug revenues contracted, the economic logic that had partly sustained large organizational structures weakened. Smaller, more flexible market arrangements became relatively more attractive; the overhead costs of maintaining large organizational hierarchies became harder to justify when the revenues those hierarchies were supposed to capture were declining.

The transformation of the drug market also changed who was involved in it and in what capacities. The large organizational structures that had dominated crack distribution in the 1980s gave way to more fragmented, competitive, and horizontally organized market arrangements in which small crews competed for customers and territory without the overall coordination that larger organizational structures had provided. This market fragmentation both reflected and reinforced the broader organizational fragmentation: smaller drug market units had less need for the elaborate hierarchical structures of the large organizations, and those structures' decline made the small-unit market arrangements more common by default.

Researchers studying the economics of street-level drug markets in the fragmentation era — most influentially Sudhir Venkatesh at Columbia and Levitt and Venkatesh's famous study of the Gangster Disciples' financial structure — documented the ways in which the drug economy was far less profitable for most of its participants than outsiders imagined. The majority of street-level drug sellers in their studies earned below minimum wage when hours worked were taken into account, bore all of the physical risks of the trade, and were among the most economically vulnerable participants in the market. The organizational structures that had sustained the drug trade were captured by a small leadership stratum while leaving rank-and-file members in conditions of chronic economic desperation. As those organizational structures fragmented, the economic conditions of the street-level participants did not improve — they remained poor, dangerous, and without access to legitimate alternatives — but the organizational frameworks that had at least provided some degree of structure and protection were gone.

Section 13.2

Small Crews and Cliques: The New Organizational Landscape

What Replaced Large Organizations

The question of what replaced large street organizations when they fragmented is central to understanding the contemporary urban landscape. The answer is not nothing — the conditions that had produced large street organizations remained in place, even as the organizational forms changed — but something qualitatively different: small crews, cliques, and block-based groups that retained some features of the older organizational model while losing others that had been, in complex and ambivalent ways, stabilizing.

The crews and cliques that emerged from fragmentation were typically much smaller than the organizations they partly replaced — ranging from a handful of individuals to a few dozen, rather than the thousands or tens of thousands of the major organizations. They were typically defined by extremely local geography: a specific block, a specific set of blocks, or an apartment complex, rather than the multi-neighborhood territories of the larger organizations. They had weak or absent formal leadership structures — decisions were made collectively or by informal consensus rather than by a designated leadership tier. They had minimal written rules or internal codes of conduct; behavioral norms were enforced by peer pressure and reputation rather than by organizational authority. And they frequently had no stable name or identity — they might be known by the block they occupied, by the area code of their phone numbers, by a slang designation, or by no consistent name at all.

The relationship between these new formations and the older organizations was complex and variable. In some cases, crews and cliques emerged from the remnants of older organizations — young men who identified with the Gangster Disciples or the Vice Lords by name but operated with none of those organizations' actual structural properties, in formations that sociologists described as "organizational in name only." In other cases, new formations had no meaningful connection to older organizations and were defined entirely by the immediate social networks of their members: men who had grown up together on the same block, men who had attended the same school or been incarcerated together, men whose families had been relocated to the same receiving neighborhood by public housing demolition. The diversity of these origins meant that the new landscape was highly variable, with some formations having historical roots in older organizations and others having none.

What all of these formations shared was their embeddedness in conditions of concentrated disadvantage. The crews and cliques of the fragmented landscape, like the organizations they partly replaced, were formed by young men who had limited access to legitimate economic opportunity, who faced the daily reality of violence and the constant threat of incarceration, who derived significant social meaning from their group membership and territorial identity, and who lived in communities where the formal institutions — schools, employers, legal systems — that were supposed to provide pathways to recognized social status had largely failed them. The organizational forms changed; the conditions that produced them did not.

The Logic of Micro-Territory

One of the most significant characteristics of the fragmented landscape was the shrinkage of relevant territorial scale. Where large street organizations had organized their activities around neighborhoods — the Englewood Vice Lords, the Taylor Homes Disciples, the Rollin 60s of a specific section of South Central — the small crews and cliques of the fragmented landscape operated at the scale of individual blocks or even individual buildings. Research by Andrew Papachristos at Yale and his colleagues documented this micro-territorial dynamic in Chicago, showing that the relevant unit of conflict in the fragmented landscape was often a block or a small cluster of blocks rather than a neighborhood, and that conflicts between groups separated by a single intersection could be as intense and lethal as conflicts between groups separated by miles in the older organizational landscape.

The significance of this territorial shrinkage was profound. Where large organizational territories had required significant resources — in membership, in violence, in negotiation — to maintain and had therefore created incentives to limit gratuitous conflict that might trigger responses too costly to sustain, the micro-territories of the fragmented landscape were small enough that even very small groups could contest them, and the stakes of any given territorial confrontation were low enough that the threshold for violence was lower. The calculus that had operated in the older landscape — a major violent incident could trigger responses that threatened organizational survival and required costly resources to manage — did not operate in the same way for a group of eight or ten young men whose territorial claim was a single block and whose organizational existence did not depend on maintaining a broader institutional structure.

Researchers also documented that many conflicts in the fragmented landscape were not primarily territorial in character — they were personal, rooted in the specific history of specific individuals and specific grievances rather than in organizational mandates or territorial imperatives. A fight at a party, a romantic rivalry, an insult on social media, a dispute over a drug sale that went wrong — these personal conflicts, in the older organizational landscape, would often have been resolved or contained by organizational hierarchy. Leadership would adjudicate the dispute, manage the response, and prevent individual conflicts from escalating into organizational conflicts. In the fragmented landscape, where no such hierarchy existed, personal conflicts escalated more readily into violence, and violence escalated more readily into cycles of retaliation that were driven by personal grievance and social media-amplified reputation pressure rather than by organizational logic.

Street Codes in the Fragmented Landscape

Sociologist Elijah Anderson's influential concept of the "code of the street" — the informal normative framework governing public behavior in disadvantaged urban neighborhoods, with its emphasis on the management of respect through the demonstration of a willingness to use violence — has been extensively applied to the analysis of the fragmented street landscape. Anderson's original analysis, developed in his 1999 book of the same name, described the street code as a survival adaptation to conditions in which legitimate authority was unreliable or absent: in communities where police could not be trusted to protect residents, where courts were tools of racial punishment rather than protection, and where legitimate economic paths to recognized social status were blocked, the management of personal reputation through demonstrated toughness became both a survival strategy and a primary source of social meaning.

In the fragmented landscape, the street code operated within a changed organizational context that amplified some of its dynamics and muted others. The absence of organizational hierarchy meant that there was no institutional authority to mediate disputes, adjudicate conflicts, or impose consequences on individuals who violated organizational codes. The street code's emphasis on individual respect and personal reputation therefore became more, rather than less, central as organizational frameworks declined. But the street code as Anderson described it also contained implicit constraints — norms about who could be legitimately targeted, about the conditions under which violence was and was not appropriate, about the obligations of community membership that limited violence against innocent parties. Community workers and researchers in the fragmented landscape reported that these constraints were more attenuated in the new environment, particularly among very young participants who had grown up after the large organizational structures had already fragmented and who had no direct experience of the norms those structures had transmitted.

The result was a landscape that community workers and older former members frequently described as more unpredictable and more dangerous than what had preceded it — not because the individuals involved were worse people than their predecessors but because the organizational structures that had channeled and constrained conflict in complex and ambivalent ways were no longer present to perform that function. The violence of the fragmented landscape was not more intense in any individual incident than what large organizational conflicts had produced; it was different in character — more personal, more random in its victims, more driven by the escalation dynamics of social media, and less subject to the negotiated truces and organizational management that had occasionally limited conflict in the older landscape.

Section 13.3

Social Media and Reputation in the Digital Era

New Tools for Ancient Problems

The rise of social media platforms in the late 2000s and early 2010s transformed the landscape in which street-level conflict played out without fundamentally changing the underlying conditions that produced it. The management of reputation and respect — the central preoccupation of the street code — had always been conducted through public performance: in face-to-face confrontations, through the visible display of clothing and accessories, through the testimony of witnesses who could confirm or deny accounts of specific incidents. Social media extended this performance to a new platform that was simultaneously more public, more permanent, more geographically delocalized, and more readily accessible to outsiders — including law enforcement — than face-to-face reputation management had been.

The platforms that became most significant for street-level social dynamics were those that combined visual content with public comment: YouTube, Instagram, Twitter, and later TikTok allowed individuals to post videos, photographs, and text that were immediately visible to their entire social network and, through sharing, to networks far beyond their immediate circle. For young men in disadvantaged neighborhoods, these platforms offered something genuinely new: the ability to manage one's reputation at scale, to project an image of toughness and status to an audience far larger than one's physical neighborhood, and to document and publicize incidents that might otherwise have remained local and quickly forgotten.

The consequences for conflict dynamics were significant and largely negative. Social media transformed what might have remained private disputes into public confrontations: an insult that once might have been exchanged between two individuals in a neighborhood could now be posted online, where it was witnessed by dozens or hundreds of people in each party's social network, transforming a personal slight into a public challenge that demanded a public response. Researchers documented what they called "call-outs" — public challenges posted on social media that named specific individuals, mocked their failures or losses, and dared them to respond. The audience for these call-outs included not only peers but also family members, romantic partners, and community members — witnesses who could confirm or deny the challenged party's reputation for toughness. The social pressure to respond to public call-outs, in a context where reputation for toughness was a primary social currency, was intense.

Social media also transformed the dynamics of retaliation cycles. In the older landscape, the time between a violent incident and a retaliatory response had been governed partly by the practical logistics of organizing a response and partly by the organizational authority structures that could slow or stop escalation. In the digital landscape, incidents were documented in real time, their details circulated immediately through social networks, and the grief, anger, and social pressure of peers and family were amplified through comment sections, repost counts, and the visible metrics of social media engagement. The time available for organizational or community intervention to interrupt retaliation cycles contracted dramatically. What had once been a process measured in days could now unfold within hours of an initial incident.

Historical Vocabulary
Drill Music

Drill music is a subgenre of trap music that originated on Chicago's South Side around 2011–2012, characterized by its dark, menacing lyrical content; slow, heavy bass-driven production; and documentary orientation toward the specific social world of Chicago's fragmented street landscape — its block-level territorial conflicts, its casualties, its social media dynamics, and the daily reality of life in conditions of extreme concentrated disadvantage.

The term "drill" derives from street slang for the act of shooting, though it was also used to refer to performing or to asserting one's credentials in the street world. Drill's earliest practitioners — Chief Keef, Lil Reese, Chance the Rapper (in his early work), Fredo Santana, and dozens of others from specific neighborhoods on Chicago's South and West Sides — created music that named real people, real places, and real incidents with a specificity that had not been common in earlier hip-hop, and that documented the fragmented street landscape with an immediacy that made it simultaneously compelling as music and alarming as social document.

Drill spread rapidly from Chicago to New York, where Brooklyn drill emerged with its own distinct flavor; to London, where UK drill developed a distinct set of lyrical and production conventions; and to cities across the country and internationally. Each iteration of drill reflected the specific conditions of the local street landscape from which it emerged, though the core aesthetic and lyrical preoccupations — violence, territory, loss, money, survival — remained consistent across iterations.

For historians and social scientists, drill presents a distinctive analytical challenge: it is simultaneously a genuine creative art form, a form of documentary expression rooted in specific social conditions, a commercial product shaped by industry incentives, and a subject of intense political controversy over its alleged relationship to violence. The challenge is to analyze it with the same rigor and contextual awareness that historians bring to any other cultural form that documents social conditions — to neither romanticize its authenticity nor reduce it to a simple cause of violence.

YouTube, Instagram, and the Publicization of Conflict

The specific platforms through which digital street life was conducted evolved rapidly across the 2010s, but certain consistent patterns emerged across platforms. YouTube became, in the early 2010s, the primary platform for drill music videos and for what researchers called "opps videos" — videos that mocked rivals, displayed weapons, and constituted public challenges in street-level reputation conflicts. The comment sections of these videos functioned as public forums for inter-group conflict, with members of opposing crews trading insults, threats, and claims about specific incidents. Police departments in Chicago and other cities assigned detectives to monitor these videos, and prosecutors began using them as evidence in criminal trials.

Instagram, which became the dominant visual social media platform among young people from the early 2010s, combined the visual culture of street life — clothing, jewelry, tattoos, weapons, wads of cash, RIP dedications to fallen friends — with the comment and direct message functionality that allowed conflicts to escalate. The Instagram aesthetic of the drill era was highly specific: posted between images of friends at parties and grief-stricken memorial images of shooting victims would appear photographs of the poster displaying guns, stacks of cash, and the hand signs and clothing of their crew, accompanied by captions that were either oblique or explicit in their references to enemies and conflicts. To outsiders, these images often appeared as simple glorification of criminal behavior; to those embedded in the social world from which they emerged, they were complex performances of identity, community membership, grief, and survival in conditions of extreme precarity.

The relationship between social media content and actual violence was the subject of intense research and policy debate. Law enforcement agencies in multiple cities developed social media monitoring units that tracked potential conflicts in real time, arguing that the ability to identify conflicts as they developed online allowed for intervention before they escalated to shootings. Researchers including Desmond Patton at Columbia University conducted systematic studies of Twitter activity in Chicago neighborhoods, finding that the language of online conflict — particularly the presence of what he called "cyber banging" content — was predictive of physical violence in statistically significant ways. Violence interrupters at organizations like Cure Violence similarly used social media monitoring to identify conflicts that required intervention before they became shootings.

But the relationship between online conflict and physical violence was not simple or direct. Many online conflicts that appeared inflammatory did not result in violence; many violent incidents were not preceded by visible online escalation. The causal direction was unclear: did social media conflict cause physical violence, or did it reflect pre-existing conflicts that were already likely to turn violent? And the platforms themselves, researchers noted, had economic incentives to maximize engagement — which meant that the most inflammatory, most dramatic, and most conflict-oriented content was algorithmically amplified, creating platform dynamics that inadvertently rewarded escalation and penalized de-escalation.

Historical Vocabulary
Digital Beef

Digital beef (also called "cyber banging" in some research literature) refers to the conduct of street-level interpersonal conflicts through social media platforms, including the posting of threats, call-outs, mockery of rivals, and challenges to respond in videos, photographs, captions, and comments that are publicly visible across social networks.

Digital beef is significant as a historical concept because it represents a transformation in how the management of reputation and respect — a central preoccupation in street-level social dynamics — is conducted in conditions of concentrated disadvantage. The traditional face-to-face management of street reputation, conducted through visible displays of toughness in specific physical locations, has been supplemented and in some respects displaced by digital performance on platforms that are simultaneously more geographically delocalized, more persistent (online posts remain visible indefinitely), and more publicly visible than face-to-face performance.

The consequences of this transformation for conflict dynamics are significant. Digital beef can generate the same social pressures to respond and the same reputation stakes as face-to-face challenges, while adding the dimension of a persistent, widely visible public record that is difficult to back away from without visible loss of face. It can also make conflicts visible to parties who would not have been involved in a face-to-face version of the same dispute, expanding the number of people invested in a particular conflict's outcome and creating new vectors for escalation.

For historians, digital beef is also a form of documentation: the social media records of street-level conflicts provide a detailed, real-time archive of the social dynamics of the fragmented street landscape that has no parallel in earlier periods. This documentation has been used both by researchers seeking to understand those dynamics and by prosecutors seeking evidence for criminal prosecutions — a duality that raises significant questions about the uses of street-level documentation and the civil liberties implications of social media monitoring.

Law Enforcement and Social Media Evidence

The use of social media content as evidence in criminal prosecutions became one of the most significant and controversial legal developments of the fragmentation era. Prosecutors in Chicago, New York, Baltimore, and other cities developed the practice of presenting defendants' social media posts — photographs of them with weapons, videos of them making threats, captions referencing specific incidents — as evidence of gang membership, participation in criminal conspiracies, or consciousness of guilt. Courts in most jurisdictions allowed this evidence, and it became a significant factor in convictions across hundreds of cases.

The practice generated intense criticism from civil liberties advocates and defense attorneys, who argued that it fundamentally misread the nature of street-level social media performance. Content that appeared, to outside observers unfamiliar with the conventions of street social media, as straightforward confessions of criminal activity was, in this view, better understood as performative assertions of identity and status within a specific cultural vernacular — no more literal as autobiography than the violent boasts of professional wrestlers or the gangster posturing of Hollywood movies. The specific context of social media performance — its audience, its conventions, its purposes — had to be understood to interpret its content accurately, and prosecutors presenting these posts to juries without this context were, critics argued, inviting systematic misinterpretation.

The debate over rap lyrics as criminal evidence was a related and equally contentious issue. Prosecutors in multiple jurisdictions sought to introduce defendants' rap lyrics — including studio recordings and social media performances — as evidence of criminal intent or knowledge of specific incidents. The practice was criticized by musicologists, cultural scholars, and civil liberties organizations who argued that rap is a creative art form with established lyrical conventions — including the first-person narration of violent events that the narrator did not actually commit — that were being systematically misread as autobiography. Studies found that identical violent content was treated very differently by experimental subjects depending on whether it was presented as rap lyrics or as other written forms, suggesting that racial and cultural bias was influencing how this evidence was received. By the mid-2010s, this critique had gained enough momentum to produce legislative efforts in several states to limit the admissibility of rap lyrics as criminal evidence.

Section 13.4

Violence in the Digital Era

Changing Patterns of Urban Violence

The relationship between fragmentation and urban violence is one of the most contested empirical questions in this field, and it requires careful analysis before any conclusions can be reached. The simplest version of the fragmentation-violence thesis — that the breakdown of large organizational structure led directly to an increase in violence — is both plausible and difficult to verify, partly because the measurement of violence and its causes is complex and partly because fragmentation occurred simultaneously with so many other social and economic changes that isolating its specific contribution is methodologically challenging.

What the evidence does clearly show is that urban violence in the United States underwent significant transformations in the period of fragmentation. The very long national decline in violent crime that began in the early 1990s and continued through approximately 2014 coincided with the initial phases of fragmentation in many cities — suggesting that fragmentation did not simply produce more violence overall. But within this national trend, specific cities and specific neighborhoods showed patterns that researchers attributed to fragmentation dynamics. Chicago's homicide rate, which had declined significantly from its early-1990s peak, stopped declining in the mid-2000s and began rising in the early 2010s, even as the national trend continued downward. The increase was concentrated in specific neighborhoods on the South and West Sides — precisely the neighborhoods where fragmentation was most advanced and where the displacement from demolished public housing had most disrupted existing social organization.

The character of the violence that researchers and community workers documented in the fragmented landscape differed from the violence of the organizational era in ways that supported the fragmentation thesis. Older forms of organizational conflict had followed relatively predictable patterns: conflicts between named organizations over territorial or drug market disputes, with identifiable combatants and motives. The violence of the fragmented landscape was characterized by what researchers described as extreme granularity and unpredictability: conflicts between groups defined at the block level, driven by personal grievances as often as by organizational imperatives, escalating from minor disputes with a speed that made intervention difficult, and producing casualties that were sometimes — in ways that the older organizational landscape had less frequently produced — collateral victims with no direct connection to the conflict.

The concentration of violence in very small geographic areas was another documented feature of the fragmented landscape. Research using geocoded crime data showed that a disproportionate share of shootings in Chicago and other cities were concentrated in tiny geographic areas — sometimes a few blocks — and involved a very small network of individuals who were connected by shared street network membership. Andrew Papachristos's network analysis showed that the risk of being shot was highly concentrated among individuals who were directly connected to others who had been shot, suggesting that violence spread through social networks in patterns that resembled contagion — a finding that supported the Cure Violence model's epidemiological approach to intervention.

Community Consequences of the New Violence Landscape

The consequences of the fragmented violence landscape for ordinary residents of affected neighborhoods were profound and multi-dimensional. The unpredictability that characterized fragmented conflict made community life more stressful and more dangerous in ways that went beyond simple counts of violent incidents. Where older organizational conflict had followed recognizable patterns — specific locations were dangerous at specific times, specific individuals were at risk for specific reasons, specific truces or peaces were in effect in specific areas — the fragmented landscape was harder to read. Mothers could no longer simply tell their children to avoid certain locations; the danger was more diffuse and less predictable. Community workers who had learned to navigate the older landscape found their knowledge obsolete. Churches and community organizations that had established relationships with organizational leadership as a tool of conflict management found themselves without those relationships.

The psychological consequences of living in conditions of unpredictable violence have been extensively documented by public health researchers. Studies of residents in Chicago's most violence-affected neighborhoods found rates of post-traumatic stress disorder comparable to those found in active war zones — a finding that resonated with the "Chiraq" framing, however contested the political implications of that framing were. Children who grew up in these neighborhoods showed elevated rates of anxiety, depression, hypervigilance, and academic disruption associated with chronic exposure to community violence. The developmental consequences of these exposures were measurable and significant: research by economists and public health scholars showed that exposure to community violence reduced test scores, increased dropout rates, reduced lifetime earnings, and increased rates of criminal involvement — creating a cycle in which the violence of one generation damaged the development of the next.

The response of community institutions to the fragmented landscape was also transformed. Churches that had maintained relationships with large organizational leadership as a tool of conflict management had to develop new approaches for a landscape in which there was no comparable leadership to engage. Schools in affected neighborhoods faced the challenge of serving students whose social world was being conducted on social media platforms in vernaculars that many educators did not understand, and whose peer conflicts escalated in ways that differed from anything the previous generation of educators had experienced. Community organizations that had developed expertise in navigating the older organizational landscape had to adapt or develop new models for addressing a landscape whose logic was fundamentally different.

Cause and Effect

The Decline of Large Street Organizations: Structural Causes and Community Consequences

Long-Term Structural Causes

Decades of concentrated poverty, disinvestment, and exclusion that produced the conditions for street organization formation in the first place; the mass incarceration wave of the 1980s and 1990s that removed hundreds of thousands of adults from affected communities; the drug economy's distortion of neighborhood economics; deindustrialization that eliminated the legitimate employment base; and the accumulation of multiple dimensions of disadvantage that left affected communities with diminishing organizational and institutional capacity.

Proximate and Short-Term Causes

RICO prosecutions that removed organizational leadership; public housing demolition that scattered communities and destroyed territorial maps; contraction of the crack market; gentrification and displacement that broke apart community networks; and the rise of social media that provided new tools for conflict escalation outside of organizational frameworks. These forces did not operate independently — each reinforced the others in ways that made their combined effect greater than any single force alone.

The Transformation

By the early 2010s, in cities like Chicago, the large hierarchically organized street organizations that had defined the landscape since the 1960s had been disaggregated into hundreds of small, loosely organized, geographically micro-territorial crews and cliques operating with minimal internal structure, weak authority relationships, and conflict dynamics driven more by personal grievance and social media-amplified reputation than by organizational logic. The transformation was geographically uneven, with some neighborhoods more fragmented than others and some organizational remnants maintaining more coherence than the general trend suggested.

Long-Term Community Consequences

More unpredictable and less organizationally mediated violence; loss of community capacity to manage conflict through organizational authority; increased psychological burden on community residents; challenge to community organizations and institutions whose knowledge and relationships were organized around the older landscape; new forms of criminal prosecution targeting digital content; cultural expressions (drill music) that documented the new landscape with immediacy and controversy; and the persistence of the underlying structural conditions that continued to produce new generations of participants in the fragmented street landscape.

Historical complexity: The decline of large street organizations is sometimes treated by policymakers as a success — as evidence that enforcement strategies worked. This interpretation is too simple. What declined was not violence per se but a specific organizational form; what replaced it was in many respects more difficult to address through conventional enforcement tools. Understanding this distinction is essential for developing effective responses to the contemporary landscape.

Section 13.5

Regional Differences: How Cities Experienced Fragmentation Differently

Chicago: The Epicenter of Fragmentation Discourse

Chicago became, in the early 2010s, the primary reference point for national and international discussion of urban fragmentation — partly because the scale of its violence was genuinely exceptional among major American cities of comparable population, partly because of the emergence of drill music as a nationally visible cultural form rooted in its specific landscape, and partly because the city's history of large, institutionalized street organizations made the fragmentation process especially dramatic and well-documented. The "Chiraq" narrative, however contested, reflected a real phenomenon: the concentration of shootings and homicides in specific neighborhoods on the South and West Sides that exceeded the violence levels of most American cities.

The specific features of Chicago's fragmented landscape were shaped by the city's specific history. The scale of its public housing demolition was exceptional: the Plan for Transformation was the largest public housing transformation in American history, and its effects on community displacement and organizational fragmentation were correspondingly large. The depth of its street organization institutionalization — the decades-long development of the Gangster Disciples, Vice Lords, and P. Stones as genuine organizational structures — meant that the disruption of those structures, when it came, was experienced as a more dramatic transformation than in cities where organizational institutionalization had been shallower. And the concentrated nature of Chicago's racial and economic segregation — documented in Chapter 3 and persistent across decades — meant that the conditions producing conflict were more geographically concentrated than in cities where poverty was more dispersed.

The Chicago Police Department's response to fragmentation illustrates the policy challenges it presented. The department had developed expertise in organizational mapping — identifying the hierarchies, territories, and membership of large organizations — that was of diminishing utility in a landscape of small, ephemeral crews. Its gang database, which listed more than 130,000 individuals as gang members or associates, was widely criticized for including people based on minimal criteria and for failing to update as the organizational landscape changed. Its enforcement strategies, calibrated for dismantling hierarchical organizations, were less effective against the small, horizontally organized crews that had replaced them. And its relationships with community organizations and churches, which had been built partly on the mediation function those institutions exercised with organizational leadership, had to be rebuilt on different foundations in the fragmented landscape.

Los Angeles: A Different Path

Los Angeles experienced fragmentation dynamics that paralleled Chicago's in important ways but also differed significantly, reflecting the different organizational history and geographic context of the West Coast city. The Crip and Blood sets of South Central Los Angeles had never been organized in the hierarchical manner of Chicago's major organizations; they were, as noted earlier, confederations of neighborhood sets with high internal autonomy. This meant that the "decapitation" of any specific leadership tier had less organizational impact in Los Angeles than comparable prosecutions had in Chicago — there was less hierarchy to decapitate — but it also meant that the baseline for organizational coherence was lower, and that fragmentation into even smaller, more granular units was both easier and more difficult to detect.

Los Angeles's particular trajectory was shaped by several city-specific factors. The gang injunction, a legal tool developed primarily in Los Angeles, used civil court orders to prohibit named gang members from associating with each other in defined geographic areas. Gang injunctions covered dozens of neighborhoods in Los Angeles by the 2000s and 2010s, and their effects on organizational structure were complex: they disrupted some organizational activities and drove others underground, and they created new legal vulnerabilities for people who might not be actively involved in criminal activity but were named in injunctions based on their associations. The injunction system also generated significant civil rights litigation that challenged its constitutionality and its racial disparate impacts.

Los Angeles also experienced significant demographic change in its historically Black neighborhoods — including Compton, Inglewood, and parts of South Central — as immigration from Mexico and Central America produced large Latino populations in areas that had been predominantly Black. This demographic transformation complicated the territorial dynamics of established street organizations, producing both conflict and accommodation as Black and Latino groups negotiated the new geography. In some neighborhoods, traditional Black street organizations maintained their presence despite demographic change; in others, they were displaced by Latino organizations; and in still others, complex multiracial arrangements emerged that defied simple organizational categories.

Baltimore: Post-Industrial Fragmentation

Baltimore provided another distinctive variant of the fragmentation story. The city's street organization landscape had developed differently from Chicago's or Los Angeles's — less dominated by single large organizations, more organized around drug distribution networks tied to specific open-air drug markets in specific neighborhoods. The drug markets of West Baltimore — particularly the areas around Gilmor Homes and Sandtown-Winchester — and East Baltimore had generated organizational structures that were defined more by market geography than by the territorial identity frameworks that characterized Chicago's landscape.

The federal prosecution strategy applied to Baltimore's drug distribution networks in the 2000s, using RICO conspiracy charges to sweep entire networks off specific drug market corners, was effective at dismantling specific market operations. But the result, as in Chicago, was not the elimination of the drug market or the end of violent conflict — it was the fragmentation of market structure into smaller, more competitive, and more violence-prone arrangements. The displacement of established market operators created vacuums that were filled by younger, less experienced, and less organizationally coherent groups whose conflicts over the reassigned territory were more frequent and more unpredictable than those of the groups they replaced.

Baltimore's particular landscape was shaped by its specific geography — a dense, row-house city with a very high vacancy rate as its population had declined from a 1950s peak of nearly a million to approximately 600,000 — and by the concentrated poverty of its historically Black neighborhoods. The city's rate of violent crime per capita was, throughout the fragmentation era, among the highest in the country, exceeding even Chicago in some years. The April 2015 death of Freddie Gray in police custody and the subsequent uprising brought national attention to the conditions of Baltimore's most disadvantaged neighborhoods, and the period following the uprising — including a dramatic spike in homicides in the months that followed — illustrated how quickly community violence could surge when the already fragile social equilibrium was disrupted.

New York: A Divergent Trajectory

New York City's experience of the fragmentation era diverged from Chicago's in ways that generated significant scholarly and policy debate. While Chicago's homicide rate remained stubbornly high through the early 2010s, New York City's continued to decline — reaching historic lows in the same years that Chicago was experiencing significant increases. This divergence was often cited in public policy discussions as evidence that specific policing strategies or other city-specific interventions were responsible for New York's success, but the explanation was more complex.

New York's street organization landscape had its own distinctive history. The "crews" that had developed in New York — particularly in the outer boroughs of the Bronx, Brooklyn, and Queens — were products of the city's specific geography, housing policy, and demographic history. The crack epidemic of the late 1980s and early 1990s had been as devastating in New York as elsewhere, but the city's policing response under the Compstat system, which used data-driven management to concentrate resources in high-crime areas, was credited by some analysts with producing significant crime reductions through the 1990s. The aggressive stop-and-frisk policing that accompanied these strategies — which produced hundreds of thousands of stops per year by the early 2010s, with Black and Latino young men accounting for approximately 85 percent of those stopped — was credited by police officials with removing weapons from the street and deterring violence, while critics argued that its costs — in civil liberties, in community-police relations, and in the criminalization of innocent people — far outweighed its benefits.

New York did eventually develop its own drill scene — Brooklyn drill, which emerged in the mid-2010s with artists including Pop Smoke and Brooklyn-based crews whose music documented the specific conditions of their neighborhoods. The emergence of Brooklyn drill illustrated how the cultural form of Chicago drill could be adopted and adapted in different cities with different street landscapes, and it generated many of the same debates about the relationship between musical content and street violence that Chicago drill had produced.

Case Study

Case Study: Chicago Drill Culture and the Fragmented Street Landscape

Origins in the New Landscape

Chicago drill emerged in roughly 2011 and 2012 from a specific set of social conditions in specific neighborhoods — primarily Englewood, Woodlawn, Roseland, and other communities on the South Side — that exemplified the fragmented landscape described in this chapter. The music did not emerge from the large organizational structures of the previous era; it emerged from the small crews and cliques, the block-level territorial conflicts, and the social media-mediated reputation dynamics that had replaced those structures. The name "drill" itself came from the street slang of these neighborhoods, referring to the act of shooting — the threat of which structured daily life in ways that outsiders could barely imagine and that the music documented with unsparing directness.

The earliest drill tracks — Chief Keef's "I Don't Like" (remixed with Kanye West in a version that gave it national distribution in 2012), Lil Reese's "Traffic," Fredo Santana's recordings — were remarkable for their specificity. Unlike much commercial rap music, which used violence as a generic stylistic posture, early drill named actual neighborhoods, actual blocks, actual people by their street names or actual names, and referenced actual incidents with a granularity that made it immediately legible to listeners who inhabited the same social world and opaque to those who did not. This specificity was both the source of drill's documentary power and the basis of the controversy it generated: the music was not about violence in the abstract but about specific ongoing conflicts, which raised questions about whether it was documentation, instigation, or something in between.

The production aesthetic of early drill was as significant as its lyrical content. Producers like Young Chop and others developed a signature sound built on slow tempos (around 65–70 beats per minute, much slower than most hip-hop), heavy 808 bass, sparse and menacing arrangements, and a general sonic darkness that matched the lyrical content's refusal of optimism or redemption narrative. Where earlier generations of Chicago rap had produced music that, for all its toughness, often contained elements of humor, hope, or community celebration — the tradition that ran through the early 2000s Chicago underground scene — drill dispensed with these elements in favor of an unflinching and, to many listeners, disturbing realism.

Social Media and the Drill Ecosystem

Drill music was inseparable from social media from its beginning. The early drill artists distributed their music through YouTube and social media platforms, not through record labels or radio; their audiences were built through digital sharing rather than through conventional music industry mechanisms. The comment sections of drill videos became forums for inter-crew conflict — the same dynamics of call-out and response that characterized street-level digital beef played out beneath music videos, transforming them from simply musical documents into active sites of ongoing conflict performance. In some documented cases, the comment section conflicts beneath drill videos were followed by physical violence, making the question of social media's role in conflict escalation concrete and urgent.

The celebrity that drill music generated — the way in which the most successful early drill artists moved rapidly from complete obscurity to national and international attention through viral social media distribution — created a new set of dynamics in the social world the music documented. For most of the young men involved in the social world that drill described, music was not a career pathway; the extraordinary success of a small number of artists was not representative of the broader trajectory. But the visibility that drill created, and the way in which street credibility and musical success became intertwined in the drill ecosystem, created incentives for performance of street identity — both on social media and in music — that had their own relationship to violence. Maintaining one's authentic street credentials, in a cultural economy where those credentials had commercial value, created pressures toward the performance and sometimes the reality of street involvement that complicated simple cause-and-effect analyses of the music's relationship to violence.

Debates and Controversies

Drill music generated intense political controversy from its first moments of national visibility. City officials, police commanders, and some community leaders argued that drill music glorified violence, disrespected the victims of that violence, and served as a form of advertising for the street conflicts it described — that it was, in effect, a form of recruitment and instigation that made the violence worse. In Chicago, Police Superintendent Garry McCarthy was publicly critical of drill music and its relationship to violence, and police officials in multiple cities monitored drill artists and videos as part of their gang intelligence work.

Critics of these arguments pointed out several problems. First, the empirical relationship between drill music and violence was far more complex than the "glorification" thesis suggested — the music documented conditions that existed independently of the music, and blaming the music for those conditions committed what scholars of culture call the "hypodermic model" fallacy, the assumption that cultural content is directly injected into audiences and produces predictable behavioral responses. Second, the critical attention directed at drill music was applied with marked asymmetry — Hollywood films depicting comparable or greater violence in other contexts did not generate comparable political pressure to suppress or restrict them, suggesting that the critique was partly about race and class rather than purely about content. Third, and perhaps most importantly, the critique of drill music as violence-promoting distracted attention from the structural conditions — poverty, exclusion, disinvestment, mass incarceration, displacement — that had produced both the violence and the music that documented it.

Defenders of drill — including cultural critics, musicologists, and some community members — argued that the music served several important functions that these critiques missed. It was a genuine creative expression rooted in specific social conditions, produced by young people with extraordinary creative talent who had developed a new musical form from the specific materials of their experience. It was a documentary form that gave voice to experiences that the dominant culture otherwise rendered invisible — that made audible the daily reality of life in conditions of concentrated disadvantage in ways that statistics and policy reports did not. And it was, for many of its practitioners and audiences, a form of communal mourning — a way of acknowledging loss, memorializing the dead, and processing trauma in a social world that provided few other forums for these functions.

The most nuanced scholarly analysis of drill music positioned it as all of these things simultaneously — creative art, social document, commercial product, trauma response, conflict performance, and community expression — and insisted that understanding it required holding this complexity rather than reducing it to a single valence. The music could be both a genuine creative achievement and a form that sometimes participated in the dynamics it documented; it could be both authentic expression and commercially mediated performance; it could be both a coping mechanism for trauma and a form that sometimes made that trauma harder to escape. These ambiguities are not unique to drill — they characterize most cultural forms that emerge from conditions of extreme social stress — but they are particularly acute in drill's case because of the directness with which the music names the specific conditions and specific people it involves.

Drill as Historical Document

For historians, the most significant aspect of drill music may be its documentary character — the granular, specific, and vivid record it provides of the social world of the fragmented street landscape. Unlike most forms of historical documentation, which are produced by observers of social conditions from positions of relative distance, drill was produced by participants in those conditions who were documenting their own experience in real time. This gives it a kind of immediacy and specificity that no external observer's account could provide, while also giving it the limitations of any participant perspective: it reflects the specific viewpoint of specific individuals within a specific social position, and it has the performative and commercial mediations of any music that is made for an audience.

The specific content of drill music — its references to specific blocks and neighborhoods, its naming of specific individuals and conflicts, its documentation of specific losses and specific loyalties — provides historians with a detailed map of the social geography of Chicago's South and West Sides in the fragmentation era that supplements and complicates the maps produced by quantitative research and official documentation. Read carefully, in context, and with attention to the conventions of the genre and the specific social world from which it emerged, drill provides evidence about how young people in the fragmented landscape understood their own situation: what they valued, what they feared, who they were loyal to and who they saw as enemies, what possibilities they perceived for their futures, and how they made sense of conditions that the broader society preferred not to acknowledge.

That documentation is painful, often brutal, and deeply resistant to the kind of redemptive narrative that mainstream culture tends to prefer. But it is honest — honest about conditions that policy and political discourse too often address in abstractions, honest about the human stakes of those conditions, and honest about the limited range of choices available to young people in circumstances that were produced not by their failures but by the accumulated decisions of American society over more than a century of segregation, exclusion, disinvestment, and abandonment.

Map Study

Crew Geography in the Contemporary Fragmented City

To understand fragmentation spatially, students should be able to analyze maps of Chicago's South and West Sides, Baltimore's West Side, and similar neighborhoods in other fragmented cities. The following geographic features and patterns are significant:

  • Former public housing footprints: The locations of demolished public housing projects — the Robert Taylor Homes corridor along the Dan Ryan Expressway, the Cabrini-Green site near the lakefront, the Henry Horner Homes near the United Center — reveal how the geography of territorial street organization was shaped by the geography of public housing, and how demolition disrupted those territorial maps.
  • Receiving neighborhoods: Maps of where public housing families were relocated through voucher programs reveal how displacement scattered communities — with some receiving neighborhoods on the South and West Sides absorbing large numbers of relocated families, while others in the suburbs or other parts of the metropolitan area received smaller numbers in greater geographic dispersal.
  • Violence concentration: Geocoded shooting and homicide data for Chicago's most affected years (2012–2016) reveal the extreme geographic concentration of violence in specific blocks and small areas — typically in communities combining concentrated poverty, high vacancy rates, significant population loss, and limited institutional presence — that is characteristic of the fragmented landscape.
  • Social network maps: Research by Papachristos and colleagues has produced network maps showing how violence spreads through specific social networks rather than through geographic areas uniformly, illustrating how the contagion model of violence operates in the fragmented landscape. These maps show tight clusters of individuals connected by shared network membership within which violence is concentrated, surrounded by less connected individuals with lower risk even in the same geographic area.
  • Gentrification frontlines: Maps of changing property values, demographic composition, and housing development in neighborhoods adjacent to Chicago's fragmented South and West Sides reveal how gentrification pressures from the lakefront and the Loop had advanced into some areas while leaving others untouched — creating a complex geography of simultaneous displacement and disadvantage.

Primary Source Window

Social Media as Historical Evidence: The Digital Archive of the Fragmented Street Landscape

Source type: Digital social media content  ·  Period: 2010s–present  ·  Platforms: YouTube, Instagram, Twitter, Facebook, TikTok

Context: The social media activity of young people in the fragmented street landscape of the 2010s constitutes an unprecedented documentary archive of that landscape. Unlike the historical record of earlier periods — which historians must reconstruct from external observers' accounts, law enforcement documents, and the occasional memoir or oral history — the fragmentation era has left behind an enormous, publicly accessible digital record created by participants themselves, in real time, for their own social purposes. This archive includes drill music videos and their comment sections; Instagram and Facebook posts documenting social networks, territorial identities, and specific incidents; Twitter and later TikTok content relating to specific conflicts and their social media dimensions; and the broader digital footprint of lives lived in the public-private space of social media platforms.

A note on methodology: Historians using social media as primary source evidence face a distinctive set of methodological challenges. Social media content is performative — it is created for an audience and shaped by the conventions and incentives of the specific platform on which it appears. This does not make it false, but it means that the relationship between content and underlying social reality is always mediated by these performative conventions. A young man posting a photograph of himself with a weapon on Instagram is not simply documenting a fact about himself; he is constructing a public persona for a specific audience within a specific set of social norms about what that persona should look like. Understanding the content requires understanding the performative context, not just the literal content.

Representative research finding (paraphrased): Studies of social media conflict dynamics in Chicago communities found that the majority of shooting incidents were preceded by some form of online conflict escalation in the weeks prior — including call-out videos, threatening comments, and the posting of content relating to deceased members of opposing groups. This pattern suggests a relationship between online conflict and physical violence, while stopping short of establishing direct causation or suggesting that online conflict is a reliable predictor of imminent physical harm in any specific instance.

Legal uses and civil liberties concerns: Social media content from the fragmented street landscape has been used extensively as evidence in criminal prosecutions, including as evidence of gang membership (for sentencing enhancement purposes), as evidence of participation in specific conspiracies, and as evidence of consciousness of guilt. The use of this content as legal evidence has been extensively criticized on grounds including: that performative social media content is systematically misread as literal autobiography by courts and juries; that the cultural conventions of street social media are poorly understood in legal contexts; that the use of social media evidence disproportionately affects Black and Latino defendants; and that the admission of rap lyrics as criminal evidence criminalizes artistic expression in ways that would not be applied to other musical genres with violent lyrical content.

Questions for students:

  1. What are the specific challenges of using social media content as historical evidence? How do the performative conventions of social media platforms shape the content they contain, and how should historians account for these conventions when analyzing that content?
  2. How does the use of social media content as evidence in criminal prosecutions raise questions about the relationship between cultural expression, legal identity, and racial justice? What standards might govern the admission of this evidence in ways that account for its performative character?
  3. Social media has created an unprecedented documentary archive of the fragmented street landscape — more detailed, more immediate, and created by participants rather than external observers. What are the advantages and limitations of this archive compared to the historical documentation available for earlier periods of street organization history?
  4. The relationship between online conflict and physical violence — whether one causes the other, reflects pre-existing conditions, or is simply correlated without causal connection — is one of the central contested questions in research on the fragmented landscape. What kinds of evidence would help establish whether the relationship is causal, and what methodological challenges make this question difficult to answer definitively?
Historical Debate

Did Fragmentation Make Cities More Dangerous?

Interpretation A — Fragmentation Increased Violence: The most common argument among researchers, community workers, and law enforcement officials who have studied Chicago's fragmented landscape is that the breakdown of large organizational structure made violence more frequent, more unpredictable, and harder to address through either enforcement or community intervention. This position holds that large organizations, despite their violence, exercised a degree of internal regulation over that violence — through hierarchical authority, territorial negotiation, and self-interest in limiting the law enforcement attention that extreme violence generated. When these structures fragmented, the regulatory functions they performed were lost along with the criminal enterprise functions that enforcement targeted. The small crews that replaced them had fewer incentives to limit violence, weaker internal authority to enforce such limits even if the incentives existed, and conflict dynamics driven by personal grievance and social media escalation that were harder to interrupt. This interpretation is supported by Chicago's homicide trajectory in the early 2010s, which diverged from national trends downward precisely in the years when fragmentation was most advanced.
Interpretation B — Fragmentation Reflected Pre-existing Conditions, Not New Ones: A second interpretation, associated with researchers who emphasize the continuity of underlying structural conditions, argues that the apparent increase in violence associated with fragmentation reflects not the organizational transformation itself but the persistence and intensification of the structural conditions — poverty, concentrated disadvantage, isolation, the absence of legitimate opportunity — that have always been the primary drivers of urban violence. In this view, the relationship between organizational structure and violence is secondary; the primary variable is the structural conditions, and changes in organizational form reflect changes in those conditions (specifically, the multiple forces that have disrupted community organization in the fragmentation era) rather than causing changes in violence independently. This interpretation is supported by the observation that fragmentation and increased violence occurred in the same neighborhoods and the same period as public housing demolition, concentrated poverty intensification, and the other structural disruptions this chapter has described — making it difficult to isolate organizational fragmentation as an independent cause.
Interpretation C — The Question Depends on What Is Being Measured: A third position argues that the debate over whether fragmentation increased or decreased violence depends crucially on what kind of violence is being measured and over what time frame. For some measures — the frequency of shootings, the number of shooting victims, the rate of homicide per 100,000 residents — Chicago's fragmented landscape does appear to show increased violence in the fragmentation era compared to the late 1990s and early 2000s. For other measures — the number of mass violence events, the extent of territorial conflict across large geographic areas, the involvement of violence in drug market disputes — the fragmented landscape may show different patterns than simple counts of shootings capture. This interpretation calls for more nuanced measurement and analysis rather than a simple verdict of "more" or "less" dangerous.

Evidence and methodology: The empirical basis for each interpretation is genuinely contested. Geocoded crime data shows concentrated violence in the fragmented landscape; network analysis shows how violence spreads through social networks; qualitative research documents the loss of organizational regulatory functions; and structural analysis shows the simultaneous intensification of poverty and displacement. Each type of evidence supports aspects of each interpretation, and the challenge is to integrate these different data streams into a coherent account that neither overstates the role of organizational change nor ignores it entirely.

Student task: Consider what kinds of evidence would be most useful for evaluating these three interpretations. What data would you want to collect? What methodological challenges make this comparison difficult? And why does the answer to this question matter for public policy — specifically, for the design of violence prevention programs that must operate in the contemporary fragmented landscape?

Multiple Perspectives

The New Street Landscape: Multiple Perspectives on Fragmentation

From the perspective of longtime community residents and elders: Many longtime residents of Chicago's South Side and comparable neighborhoods in other cities describe the fragmented landscape with a combination of grief and bewilderment. Those who remember the older organizational landscape — even with all its violence and danger — describe it as more legible, more navigable, and in some ways less terrifying than what has replaced it. The old territorial maps were known; you knew which blocks were dangerous and why, which people to avoid and which organizations were in conflict. The fragmented landscape, in which conflicts arise from personal grievances between small groups whose identities and affiliations are opaque to outsiders, feels more random and more inescapable. "At least before, you knew the rules," is a sentiment expressed with variations across ethnographic interviews. Community elders also often express grief about the generational breakdown — the sense that the very young participants in contemporary street life have no connection to the community history and organizational traditions that once provided even the most dangerous street formations with something resembling codes of conduct.
From the perspective of young people in affected neighborhoods: Young people growing up in Chicago's South Side, Baltimore's West Side, or comparable neighborhoods in the fragmentation era inhabited a social world that outsiders found extremely difficult to understand. The micro-territorial geography of the fragmented landscape — in which the most significant social boundaries could be a single intersection — shaped their daily movement in ways that outsiders, seeing only the physical geography of ordinary urban blocks, could not perceive. Social media was not an optional add-on to their social life; it was a primary arena of social performance through which reputation, identity, relationships, and conflicts were managed. The music they made and listened to was not a reflection of violence from a distance — it was documentation of their specific, named, immediate social world. And the structural conditions that produced this landscape — the poverty, the absence of legitimate economic opportunity, the educational failure, the everyday presence of police surveillance and the threat of incarceration — were experienced not as policy abstractions but as the concrete texture of daily life.
From the perspective of community violence workers and violence interrupters: The community workers, violence interrupters, and street outreach workers who attempted to address conflict in the fragmented landscape operated under conditions that were, in important respects, more difficult than those their predecessors had faced in the organizational era. Violence interrupters who had built their credibility and their conflict-intervention capacity on their own former organizational membership found that this credibility was relevant to a rapidly shrinking circle — young people who had no organizational connection to the groups from which interrupters had emerged were unlikely to be influenced by those organizations' codes. The specific conflicts that needed interrupting were harder to identify in advance, faster to escalate, and more personal in character — driven by individual grievance rather than organizational mandate — making the organizational relationship-based tools of earlier intervention models less effective. Programs like Cure Violence adapted by developing new models focused on social network mapping, social media monitoring, and the deployment of credible messengers from within the specific micro-networks most at risk.
From the perspective of law enforcement: Police commanders and prosecutors who had developed expertise in organizational mapping — identifying the leadership, territorial structure, and membership of large organizations — found that expertise increasingly irrelevant in the fragmented landscape. The investigative and intelligence tools that worked well for hierarchically organized criminal enterprises — informants who could describe organizational structure, conspiracy charges that could sweep entire networks — worked less well for small, ephemeral, personally organized crews whose "organization" might consist of eight young men on a specific block with no stable name, no hierarchy, and no ongoing criminal enterprise beyond street-level drug sales and interpersonal conflicts. Chicago police under Superintendent Garry McCarthy and later Eddie Johnson developed new approaches — including predictive analytics and "group audit" sessions that brought at-risk individuals in for direct conversations with law enforcement and social service providers — that attempted to adapt to the new landscape, with contested results.
From the perspective of drill music artists: The artists who created and sustained Chicago drill, and their equivalents in other cities, occupied an extraordinarily complex social position. They were simultaneously members of the social world they documented and increasingly, for the most successful among them, its observers from a position of commercial success that most of their peers could not access. They made music that documented real conditions and real people with a specificity that felt authentic to the social world it came from, and that authenticity was both its artistic power and its social danger. They faced enormous pressure — from audiences, from peers, from the commercial logic of the music industry — to maintain the street credentials that authenticated their work, even as their commercial success moved them physically and economically out of the conditions those credentials described. The deaths of several prominent drill artists — including Chicago's Fredo Santana and Capo — in the years following their initial success illustrated how difficult it was to maintain the physical safety that success might have provided while remaining connected to the social world that had produced the music.
Continuity and Change Over Time

Territory in the Digital Age: What Changed and What Endured

What Changed

  • The scale of territorial organization shrank dramatically, from neighborhood-level territories maintained by organizations with thousands of members to block-level micro-territories maintained by small crews of fewer than twenty people
  • The mechanisms of territorial performance shifted from primarily face-to-face demonstrations of presence and toughness to social media-mediated performances of identity and conflict, conducted on platforms visible to unlimited audiences rather than local witnesses
  • The speed of conflict escalation increased dramatically as social media enabled the rapid, public escalation of disputes that might previously have been contained at the face-to-face level by the time required for information to spread
  • The connection between territorial identity and formal organizational membership weakened; many young people claimed territorial identities defined by their block or immediate social network without membership in any named organizational structure
  • The mechanisms available for community and organizational conflict management changed, as the leadership hierarchies that had mediated conflicts in the older organizational landscape were removed by prosecution, incarceration, or organizational fragmentation
  • A new cultural form — drill music — emerged to document and express the conditions of the fragmented landscape with a specificity and immediacy that had no precedent in earlier forms of urban cultural expression

What Stayed the Same

  • The fundamental conditions that produced street organization formation — concentrated poverty, disinvestment, educational failure, the absence of legitimate economic opportunity, and the exclusion from mainstream pathways to recognized social status — remained in place throughout the fragmentation era
  • The basic logic of territory as a primary framework of identity, belonging, and conflict persisted, even as the scale and mechanisms of territorial organization changed
  • The centrality of reputation and respect as social currencies in the street code remained constant, even as the platforms on which reputation was managed shifted from face-to-face to digital
  • The disproportionate involvement of young Black men from conditions of concentrated disadvantage in both street conflict and its criminal justice consequences continued, as both a reflection of structural inequality and a perpetuation of its effects
  • The ambivalent relationship between community and street organization — neither simple opposition nor simple identification, but a complex coexistence in which ordinary community members, families, churches, and schools inhabited the same neighborhoods as the formations they neither fully endorsed nor fully rejected — persisted through the fragmentation era
  • The creative and cultural expressions of young people in these conditions — their music, their fashion, their vernacular, their social media aesthetics — continued to represent genuine artistic production of remarkable vitality rooted in specific social conditions

What the pattern reveals: The combination of changed organizational form and persistent structural conditions is the defining feature of the fragmented landscape. Fragmentation changed the organizational vehicle through which structural inequality produced conflict and community harm; it did not change the structural inequality itself. This distinction is essential for understanding why approaches focused on organizational disruption — prosecution, intelligence, anti-gang enforcement — have failed to produce lasting reduction in community violence: they address the symptom rather than the cause, and when one organizational form is disrupted, the conditions that produced it generate a replacement form that presents the same challenges in a different configuration.

Everyday Life and Social History

Growing Up in the Fragmented City: Ordinary Life in the New Street Landscape

For the children who grew up in Chicago's Englewood, Baltimore's Sandtown-Winchester, or comparable neighborhoods in the fragmentation era, the landscape described in this chapter was not an abstract sociological phenomenon. It was the texture of daily life: the routes they had to navigate to get to school, the friends they could and couldn't associate with in public, the social media platforms they checked obsessively to monitor conflicts that might require them to change their behavior, and the persistent background anxiety of living in proximity to unpredictable violence that public health researchers documented in elevated rates of trauma symptoms among these communities' children.

The daily experience of fragmented street geography shaped behavior in ways that outsiders struggled to perceive. A journey from home to school that crossed the invisible line between one micro-territory and another required the same kind of careful navigation that crossing a front line would require — walking on specific sides of streets, avoiding eye contact with specific individuals, adjusting clothing to avoid signaling affiliation. Children who had been born into one set of social networks and relocated through public housing demolition to neighborhoods where those networks meant nothing faced the challenge of building new social relationships in unfamiliar territory without the established community connections that had made their previous social world navigable.

Schools in affected neighborhoods occupied an impossible position: they were asked to provide educational environments conducive to learning and development in communities where the stress of chronic exposure to violence was measurably impairing children's ability to learn, where the social conflicts of the street penetrated the school building through both direct confrontations and the social media dramas that continued on students' phones through the school day, and where teachers and administrators were working with resources far below what the challenges their students faced would have required. Research by public health scholars documented significant educational disruption associated with community violence exposure: reductions in test performance, increased rates of absenteeism, elevated teacher turnover in the most affected schools, and the cumulative developmental costs of chronic stress on children's cognitive development. These educational consequences of community violence were not separate from the violence itself; they were part of the same structural problem, each reinforcing the other in cycles that were difficult to interrupt from within the communities experiencing them.

Family life in fragmented neighborhoods carried particular burdens for mothers and grandmothers who were the primary caretakers of children in communities where fathers were disproportionately absent through incarceration or the other consequences of street involvement. These women — the core of what community researchers described as the "kinship networks" that sustained community life in conditions of extreme adversity — navigated the fragmented street landscape on behalf of their children with knowledge, courage, and creativity that rarely appeared in public accounts of these communities. They negotiated with school administrators, with housing authorities, with parole officers, and with the informal social networks of the street simultaneously, holding together family structures that the structural forces this chapter has described were constantly working to dissolve. Their labor was the invisible foundation of community survival in the fragmented era, and its costs — in chronic stress, in the physical consequences of that stress, in the grief of losing sons and nephews and cousins to violence or incarceration — were borne without recognition by the society whose policies had made these conditions.

The cultural world of young people in the fragmented landscape was genuinely rich despite its terrible conditions. The music — not only drill but the full range of creative expression that the same communities produced — was innovative, influential, and produced with resources that would have seemed preposterously inadequate to any outside comparison. The fashion, the vernacular, the social media aesthetics, the humor — all of these represented a creative vitality that was the flip side of the social crisis. The same young people whose daily lives were structured by the threat of violence and the reality of concentrated poverty were producing cultural forms that the entire world would eventually adopt as their own, often without acknowledging where those forms came from or the conditions that had produced them.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has examined the transformation of Black street organizations in the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries — the process of fragmentation through which large, hierarchically organized formations were disaggregated into small, loosely structured crews and cliques operating in a landscape shaped by social media, micro-territorial conflict, and the continued structural conditions of concentrated disadvantage. This transformation was not a simple story of organizational decline or of successful enforcement intervention. It was a complex historical process produced by the convergence of multiple structural forces that had been building for decades, and it produced a new landscape that was in important respects more challenging to address than the organizational landscape it replaced.

The primary forces driving fragmentation were themselves the products of policy choices made in the preceding decades. Mass incarceration — the enormous wave of drug enforcement and punitive sentencing that Chapter 11 analyzed in detail — removed the organizational leadership that had sustained large street formations' internal coherence. Federal RICO prosecutions, which applied organized crime statutes to street organizations engaged in drug distribution, were particularly effective at decapitating organizational hierarchies, but the result was not the elimination of street organization activity — it was its reorganization into forms that were less hierarchical, less territorially coherent, and in important respects more dangerous to the communities they inhabited. The lesson of the decapitation strategy's consequences is that dismantling organizational leadership without addressing the structural conditions that produce organizational formation creates a vacuum that is filled by new formations — typically less organized, less internally regulated, and less susceptible to the same enforcement tools that dismantled their predecessors.

The demolition of large public housing projects added a second, equally significant dimension to the fragmentation process. The physical destruction of the communities that had sustained street organization territories for decades scattered the social networks that had given those organizations their human material, disrupted the community institutions that had provided whatever social infrastructure these neighborhoods possessed, and rendered obsolete the territorial maps that had organized organizational conflict across generations of negotiated boundaries and established truces. The families displaced through voucher programs into unfamiliar neighborhoods arrived without social networks, without community connections, and often without the community institutional support — the churches, the mutual aid networks, the informal neighborhood authorities — that had sustained collective life in the projects. The conflict that followed displacement was partly the violence of the old organizational landscape exported to new locations, but it was also the violence of communities that had lost the organizing structures that had once managed it.

The transformation of the drug economy — the contraction of the crack market that had briefly made street-level drug distribution enormously profitable, and the fragmentation of drug market organization that followed — further reduced the economic logic of maintaining large organizational structures. The street-level drug market of the fragmentation era was more competitive, more horizontally organized, and less profitable for most participants than the crack market it replaced. The small crews that dominated this market had less need for the elaborate hierarchies of the large organizations, and those hierarchies' decline made small-unit market arrangements the default form of participation in the informal economy that remained, for many young men in these neighborhoods, the most accessible alternative to complete economic marginalization.

Social media did not cause fragmentation, but it provided a new arena within which fragmented dynamics played out with consequences that amplified the danger of the new landscape. The management of reputation and respect — the central preoccupation of the street code — migrated to digital platforms that were simultaneously more public, more permanent, and more geographically delocalized than face-to-face reputation management. Online conflicts generated the same social pressures to respond and the same reputation stakes as face-to-face challenges, while compressing the time available for de-escalation and expanding the audience for any given dispute to include the entire social network rather than only local witnesses. The acceleration and amplification of conflict dynamics through social media made the fragmented landscape more volatile and harder to manage through either organizational authority or community intervention than the older landscape had been.

Chicago drill music emerged from this landscape as its most visible and most debated cultural expression. Rooted in the specific conditions of Chicago's South Side — the small crews rather than large organizations, the block-level territorial conflicts, the social media-mediated reputation wars, the casualties that accumulated faster than they could be mourned — drill documented the fragmented landscape with an immediacy and specificity that distinguished it from more generic representations of urban street life. The debates that drill generated — about its relationship to violence, about the appropriate response of public authorities to its content, about the civil liberties implications of using rap lyrics as criminal evidence — were debates about the fragmented landscape itself, and about how American society understood and responded to the conditions that had produced it.

Those conditions — the concentrated poverty, the educational failure, the absence of legitimate economic opportunity, the chronic stress of life in proximity to unpredictable violence — remained as present in the fragmented landscape as they had been in the organized landscape that preceded it. This continuity is the most important analytical finding of this chapter: organizational form changed, but structural conditions persisted. The young people who formed small crews and cliques in Chicago's Englewood or Baltimore's Sandtown-Winchester in the 2010s were responding to the same fundamental conditions that had produced the Blackstone Rangers and the Vice Lords in the 1960s. The organizational vehicle was different; the underlying human needs for belonging, protection, economic opportunity, and recognized social status that the vehicle served were the same. And the structural failure — the failure of American society to provide pathways to those things through legitimate channels for people living in conditions of concentrated disadvantage — was, if anything, more deeply entrenched in the fragmented era than it had been in the organizational era.

Regional variation illustrates that the fragmentation process was shaped by local history and policy as well as by national forces. Chicago's exceptional degree of organizational institutionalization, combined with its exceptional degree of public housing demolition, produced a fragmentation process that was more dramatic and more thoroughly documented than in most other cities. Los Angeles's different organizational history — confederations of sets rather than hierarchically organized formations — meant that fragmentation there looked different and produced different patterns of conflict. Baltimore's drug-market-centered organizational structure produced its own distinctive fragmentation dynamics. And New York's trajectory, with its continued crime decline and its own emerging drill scene, illustrated that local policy and social context could produce different outcomes within the same national framework of structural conditions.

The violence prevention responses that proved most promising in the fragmented landscape shared a common orientation: they were adapted to the specific dynamics of the new landscape rather than transplanted from the older organizational era, and they addressed the community-level consequences of fragmentation rather than targeting organizational structures that had already ceased to exist in the old form. Cure Violence's epidemiological model, which approached violence as a contagious disease spreading through social networks and deployed credible messengers to interrupt specific conflicts, was better adapted to the personal-grievance, network-driven dynamics of the fragmented landscape than enforcement strategies designed to decapitate hierarchies. Focused deterrence programs that identified the specific individuals most at risk and most responsible for specific conflicts, and that combined direct communication, social service offers, and credible threat of enforcement consequences, showed promising results in several cities. And the community trauma-informed approaches that recognized the public health consequences of chronic violence exposure — providing mental health services, school-based support, and community healing spaces — addressed dimensions of the fragmented landscape's costs that purely criminal justice responses could not reach.

What none of these approaches could do — what no program operating within the conditions of the fragmented landscape could do — was address the structural conditions that continued to produce new generations of participants in street conflict. The poverty of Englewood and Sandtown-Winchester was not the result of drill music or small-crew organization; it was the result of decades of disinvestment, deindustrialization, segregation, and the accumulated consequences of the historical forces this textbook has traced since its opening chapter. Addressing fragmentation at its roots would require addressing those conditions — would require the kinds of investment in education, economic opportunity, housing stability, and community infrastructure that American policy has consistently declined to provide to the communities most severely affected by their absence. Until that structural investment is made, the organizational forms may change, the social media platforms may shift, the musical genres may evolve — but the conditions that produce them will persist, as they have persisted through migration, segregation, deindustrialization, mass incarceration, and fragmentation alike.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Call-out
A public challenge posted on social media that names a specific individual, mocks their failures or losses, and dares them to respond, constituting a form of digital reputation conflict that can escalate to physical violence.
Chiraq
A portmanteau of "Chicago" and "Iraq" used first in drill music and then in media coverage to describe violence levels in certain Chicago neighborhoods; the term generated controversy about the decontextualization of urban violence from its structural causes.
Clique
A small, loosely organized group of individuals connected primarily by personal relationships and immediate neighborhood geography, typically without formal leadership structure, written rules, or stable organizational identity; the primary organizational form of the fragmented street landscape.
Concentrated disadvantage
The clustering of multiple dimensions of social and economic disadvantage — poverty, unemployment, educational failure, housing instability, institutional absence — in specific geographic areas, producing community conditions more damaging than any single form of disadvantage alone.
Contagion model
The epidemiological framework for understanding urban violence that treats it as spreading through social networks in patterns analogous to infectious disease, with individuals most at risk being those with close social connections to others who have experienced violence; the model underlying the Cure Violence intervention approach.
Crew
A small, informally organized group of young men connected by neighborhood, personal relationships, or shared social network membership, constituting the primary organizational unit of the fragmented street landscape; typically smaller, less structured, and more geographically micro-territorial than the large organizations of the preceding era.
Cure Violence
The community violence intervention organization founded in Chicago that approaches urban violence as a contagious disease and uses "violence interrupters" — typically former gang members with credibility in affected communities — to intervene in specific conflicts before they escalate to shootings.
Cyber banging
Conduct of street-level reputation conflicts through social media platforms, including the posting of threats, call-outs, and challenges to respond; the term is used in research literature to describe the digital equivalent of gang-related intimidation conducted face-to-face.
Decapitation strategy
The law enforcement approach of targeting organizational leadership for prosecution and imprisonment, based on the logic that removing the top tier of organizational hierarchy will disrupt the organization's criminal enterprise; applied extensively to street organizations through RICO prosecutions in the 1990s and 2000s with mixed long-term results.
Digital beef
Street-level interpersonal conflicts conducted through social media platforms, generating the same social pressures to respond as face-to-face challenges while expanding their audience, persistence, and speed of escalation.
Drill music
A subgenre of hip-hop originating on Chicago's South Side around 2011–2012, characterized by dark, menacing production, slow tempos, and documentary lyrical content specific to the fragmented street landscape of particular Chicago neighborhoods; the genre spread nationally and internationally from its Chicago origins.
Focused deterrence
A violence reduction strategy that identifies the specific individuals most at risk and most responsible for specific conflicts, communicates directly with them about the consequences of continued violence, offers social service support and legitimate alternatives, and delivers credible enforcement consequences for continued violence; associated with researchers David Kennedy and Anthony Braga.
Fragmentation
The process by which large, hierarchically organized street organizations were disaggregated into small, loosely structured, geographically micro-territorial crews and cliques with minimal internal structure, weak authority relationships, and conflict dynamics driven more by personal grievance than by organizational mandate.
Gang database
Law enforcement systems for cataloging individuals identified as gang members or associates, used for enforcement targeting, sentence enhancement, and intelligence sharing; criticized for racial disparities, inadequate due process protections, and inclusion of individuals based on minimal criteria.
Gang injunction
A civil court order prohibiting named individuals from associating with each other or engaging in specified activities in defined geographic areas, used primarily in Los Angeles as a tool for disrupting street organization activity; generated significant civil liberties litigation challenging its constitutionality.
HOPE VI
The federal public housing revitalization program established in 1992 that funded the demolition of the most severely distressed public housing and its replacement with mixed-income developments; the program scattered hundreds of thousands of families and contributed significantly to the disruption of established community and organizational networks.
Hyper-local conflict
Street-level conflict defined at the scale of individual blocks or small clusters of blocks rather than the neighborhood-level territorial conflicts characteristic of the organizational era; a defining feature of the fragmented landscape.
Network analysis
A social science methodology that maps relationships between individuals to identify patterns of connection, influence, and risk transmission within social networks; applied by researchers like Andrew Papachristos to understanding how violence spreads through street networks in ways that concentrated risk within specific tight-knit groups.
RICO (Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act)
A federal statute originally designed to combat organized crime, whose conspiracy provisions allow prosecutors to charge all members of a criminal enterprise based on the actions of individual members; applied extensively to street organizations engaged in drug distribution from the 1990s onward.
Set
In Los Angeles street organization terminology, a specific neighborhood-level unit within the broader Crip or Blood confederation; each set has its own name, territory, and internal hierarchy while sharing the broader organizational identity. The set structure is more horizontally organized than Chicago's hierarchical formations.
Street code
The informal normative framework governing public behavior in disadvantaged urban neighborhoods, emphasizing the management of respect through demonstrated willingness to use violence as a survival adaptation to conditions where formal authority is unreliable or absent; analyzed most influentially by sociologist Elijah Anderson.
Violence interrupter
An individual, typically a former gang member or someone with direct experience in the communities they work in, employed by a violence intervention organization to intervene in specific conflicts before they escalate to shootings; the primary operational tool of the Cure Violence model.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter has arrived at what may be the most difficult point in this textbook's long historical arc. The Great Migration that Chapter 2 described — the movement of millions of Black Southerners toward northern and western cities in search of something better, something freer, something more dignified than the Jim Crow South — seems, by the time we reach the fragmented street landscape of the 2010s, to have arrived at a profoundly troubling destination. The neighborhoods that received those migrants — Chicago's South Side, Harlem, Detroit's East Side, South Central Los Angeles — are today among the most concentrated sites of poverty, violence, and institutional failure in the United States. The organizations that emerged within those neighborhoods as responses to exclusion, as alternative structures of belonging and identity and survival, have fragmented under the combined pressure of mass incarceration, displacement, and economic transformation into forms that are in some respects more dangerous and harder to address than the older formations they replaced. The conditions that produced the migration — the concentrated poverty, the exclusion from mainstream institutions and opportunities, the denial of the pathways to recognized social status that other Americans take for granted — persist, largely unchanged in their structural character though transformed in their specific manifestations, across more than a century of Black urban history.

It would be a mistake, however, to read this history simply as a story of failure — as a narrative in which the promises of migration and urbanization were betrayed and the communities that sought those promises were left worse off for having sought them. That reading, too, is incomplete and distorting. The Black urban communities that this textbook has traced across more than a century produced extraordinary creative achievements — in music, in literature, in art, in intellectual life — that have shaped American and world culture in ways that cannot be separated from the specific conditions of those communities' formation. They produced a civil rights movement that transformed American democracy and produced the most significant expansion of legal rights and political participation in the country's history since Reconstruction. They built community institutions of remarkable resilience that sustained collective life across generations of adversity. They produced organizational forms — including street organizations, which this textbook has asked to be understood as community formations rather than simply criminal enterprises — that represented genuine attempts to address fundamental human needs for belonging, protection, economic opportunity, and dignity in conditions that the surrounding society refused to provide legitimately.

What fragmentation means, in the larger historical perspective this textbook has developed, is the end of one chapter in that longer story. The large, institutionalized street organizations of the mid-twentieth century were themselves historical formations, produced by specific conditions at a specific historical moment. The conditions changed — through mass incarceration, public housing demolition, economic transformation, displacement, and digital technology — and the organizations changed in response. What has emerged is not a stable new order but an ongoing process of adaptation to conditions that remain fundamentally hostile to the communities experiencing them. The next chapter in this story has not yet been written; it is being written now, in the neighborhoods and lives and organizations of a new generation that has inherited the accumulated consequences of the history this textbook has traced while facing conditions that are, in some respects, new and in many respects achingly familiar.

The most important lesson of this chapter — and, perhaps, of the entire textbook — is that the conditions that produce community harm cannot be addressed by addressing their organizational or cultural manifestations alone. Fragmentation is the current organizational form of concentrated disadvantage; drill music is its current cultural expression; the small crew is its current organizational vehicle. These forms will change again, as conditions change, as new technologies emerge, as policy interventions succeed or fail. What will not change, unless the structural conditions are addressed, is the underlying reality: that concentrations of poverty, exclusion, educational failure, and the absence of legitimate opportunity will continue to produce group formations whose violence falls most heavily on the communities in which they exist, and whose most creative expressions will continue to be simultaneously brilliant achievements and painful documentation of conditions that a just society would not allow to persist.

The history from migration to fragmentation is, at its core, a story about what American society has been willing and unwilling to provide to Black communities across more than a century of Black urban life. The Great Migration was powered by the hope of something better; the fragmented street landscape of the contemporary city is the consequence of how incompletely that hope has been realized. Understanding this history clearly — not as a story of Black failure or cultural pathology, but as a story of structural inequality sustained across generations and manifested in specific organizational and cultural forms at specific historical moments — is the essential foundation for thinking seriously about what a different future might require. That is the work to which this textbook has hoped to contribute.

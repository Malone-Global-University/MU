Part IV — Punishment and Mass Incarceration
11

Mass Incarceration and the Prison System

From Migration to Fragmentation: The Urban History of Black Street Organizations

Chapter Opening

"The United States imprisons a larger percentage of its Black population than South Africa did at the height of apartheid." — Michelle Alexander, The New Jim Crow, 2010

In the summer of 1980, the United States held approximately 500,000 people in its jails and prisons combined. By 2008, that number had grown to more than 2.3 million — the largest incarcerated population of any nation on earth, both in absolute numbers and as a share of the total population. This expansion was not a natural or inevitable response to rising crime. It was the product of specific legislative choices, judicial decisions, enforcement priorities, and resource allocations made over roughly three decades, choices that fell with extraordinary disproportionality on Black and Latino communities and on the urban neighborhoods that previous chapters of this textbook have described. Understanding how that expansion happened, what it did to the people caught inside it, and what it did to the communities from which those people came and to which most of them eventually returned, is the central task of this chapter.

The mass incarceration of the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries did not emerge from nowhere. It was built on foundations laid by the War on Drugs examined in the previous chapter — the mandatory minimum sentencing laws, the expanded enforcement capacity, the crack cocaine prosecutions that sent tens of thousands of young Black men to federal and state penitentiaries for years or decades. But the prison system that absorbed that wave of prosecutions had itself been expanding for years before crack cocaine became a political emergency, driven by broader ideological shifts in how American society thought about crime, punishment, poverty, and race. The movement from rehabilitation to retribution, from indeterminate sentences tailored to the individual to mandatory terms fixed by the offense, from a prison population in the hundreds of thousands to one numbering in the millions, was a transformation of historic proportions that reshaped American society in ways that are still being reckoned with.

For the urban Black communities that this textbook has been tracing from the Great Migration through segregation, deindustrialization, and the crack epidemic, mass incarceration arrived as both consequence and cause. It was a consequence of the forces described in previous chapters: the concentrated poverty produced by redlining and disinvestment, the unemployment produced by factory closures, the drug markets that emerged in neighborhoods abandoned by legitimate economic opportunity, and the enforcement priorities of the War on Drugs that targeted street-level dealers in Black neighborhoods far more aggressively than the white-collar distributors and users who were equally or more culpable in the drug trade. And it was a cause — a powerful engine generating new forms of social damage that compounded the existing disadvantages of the communities it drew from, stripping them of fathers, brothers, sons, and mothers, disrupting the family structures and social networks that sustained community life, and returning people who had been incarcerated into labor markets that increasingly refused to employ them.

The prison system itself was transformed by the sheer volume of people flowing through it. The older ideal of the penitentiary as a place of rehabilitation — of structured programs, vocational training, therapeutic intervention, and preparation for law-abiding civilian life — was progressively dismantled as prison populations swelled far beyond the capacity of existing rehabilitation infrastructure and as the political culture of punishment turned sharply against any policy that could be portrayed as coddling criminals. By the 1990s, American prisons were, for the most part, not places where people were prepared for productive reintegration into society. They were warehouses for human beings who had been deemed, through the operation of a legal system that was structurally tilted against the poor and the Black, to be social surplus — people whose removal from society was treated as a solution to the problem of urban poverty rather than a symptom of it.

Inside those warehouses, the social dynamics of the streets were reproduced in concentrated and intensified form. Prison gangs — some imported from street organizations on the outside, others formed within the institution itself — became the primary organizing structures of daily life for many incarcerated people, providing protection, economic access, social belonging, and a form of governance in an environment that the official institution could not or would not control. The racial organization of prison life — the widespread practice of segregating prisoners by race, formally or informally, for housing and protection purposes — deepened the racial antagonisms that divided street organizations on the outside and created new organizational forms that would filter back into urban neighborhoods as people were released.

The effects of mass incarceration on families and communities were profound and lasting. The removal of large numbers of adults from urban neighborhoods — particularly young men between the ages of eighteen and forty, the years of primary economic and social productivity — damaged the structures of family life, economic activity, political participation, and community organization in those neighborhoods in ways that were often invisible in aggregate crime statistics but devastating in the daily experience of the communities involved. Children grew up with fathers absent. Women managed households alone under conditions of economic stress. Community organizations lost members. Local economies lost workers and consumers. The political voice of communities under mass incarceration pressure was weakened by the disenfranchisement of convicted felons and former felons in most states. And the stigma of incarceration followed people out of prison, limiting their access to employment, housing, education, and public benefits in ways that made the path from prison to stable community life extraordinarily difficult to navigate.

This chapter examines all of these dimensions: the mechanics and scale of prison expansion, the world inside the prison and the development of prison gangs, the effects on families and intimate relationships, the challenges of reentry and the barriers to successful reintegration, and the long-term effects on the communities most heavily affected. The California prison system serves as a detailed case study, both because California's prisons were among the most overcrowded and most racially organized in the country and because California's experience with the consequences of mass incarceration — and with the political struggle to reverse its worst effects — prefigured developments that would eventually occur nationally. Throughout, the chapter insists that mass incarceration must be understood not as a natural response to crime but as a set of political choices with specific causes and specific consequences, choices that were made in identifiable institutional contexts by identifiable actors and that could, in principle, have been made differently.

The story of mass incarceration is, finally, inseparable from the story of race in America. The racial disparities in incarceration rates are not the product of racial differences in criminal behavior, which research consistently shows are much smaller than the disparities in incarceration suggest. They are the product of racial disparities in arrest, prosecution, sentencing, and the enforcement choices that determine whose neighborhoods are policed most intensively and whose drug use is treated as a criminal matter rather than a public health one. Understanding these disparities honestly — not as the natural consequence of disadvantaged culture but as the product of specific institutional choices and structural inequalities — is the analytical and ethical foundation for understanding what mass incarceration meant and means for Black urban communities.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Describe the scale and pace of American prison expansion from the 1970s through the early 2000s, identifying the key legislative, judicial, and policy decisions that drove it.
  2. Explain the racial disparities in incarceration rates and analyze why those disparities cannot be explained by differences in criminal behavior alone.
  3. Trace the ideological shift from rehabilitation to retribution in American penal philosophy and explain its practical consequences for prison conditions and prisoner outcomes.
  4. Analyze the development of prison gangs and their relationship to street organizations in urban communities, explaining how the prison environment shaped organizational forms that filtered back into neighborhoods.
  5. Describe the effects of mass incarceration on Black families, including the disruption of two-parent households, the economic pressures on caregiving parents, and the developmental consequences for children of incarcerated parents.
  6. Identify the major barriers to successful reentry that formerly incarcerated people face, including restrictions on employment, housing, education, and public benefits, and explain how these barriers increase recidivism.
  7. Assess the long-term effects of mass incarceration on urban communities, including effects on political participation, local economies, community organizations, and public health.
  8. Evaluate competing historical interpretations of whether mass incarceration reduced crime, explaining what evidence supports each position and what methodological challenges make this question difficult to answer definitively.
  9. Analyze the California prison system as a case study, explaining its particular features, its constitutional crisis over overcrowding, and its significance for national trends in incarceration and reform.
  10. Connect the history of mass incarceration to earlier forms of racial labor control in American history, including convict leasing and the Black Codes, and assess the significance and limits of this comparison.
  11. Interpret primary sources related to incarceration, including legislation, court decisions, personal testimony, and policy documents, as historical evidence with specific perspectives and limits.
  12. Contextualize the experience of individual incarcerated people and their families within the larger structural forces that this chapter and this textbook describe.

Guiding Historical Questions

Questions for Historical Inquiry

  1. Why did the United States imprison so many more people per capita than any other comparable democracy? Was this the inevitable result of higher crime rates, or was it the product of specific policy choices?
  2. How did racial disparities in incarceration rates come to be so extreme, and what does the persistence of those disparities tell us about the relationship between race and the American criminal justice system?
  3. What happened to the ideal of rehabilitation in American prison policy, and why? Who benefited from its abandonment, and who was harmed?
  4. How did the prison environment shape the development of prison gangs, and what was the relationship between prison gangs and the street organizations of urban neighborhoods?
  5. What did mass incarceration do to the families and communities of incarcerated people? How should historians measure these effects, given that they are difficult to quantify but clearly significant?
  6. What barriers prevent successfully reintegrating formerly incarcerated people into their communities, and who benefits from maintaining those barriers?
  7. Did mass incarceration reduce crime? What do we know, what do we not know, and why does this question matter so much for evaluating the policy?
  8. How does the history of mass incarceration connect to the longer history of racial control in America — to convict leasing, the Black Codes, and Jim Crow — and where does that comparison reach its limits?
  9. What would it mean to dismantle mass incarceration? What structural changes would be required, and what does the political resistance to those changes reveal about whose interests the system serves?

Historical Context

To understand the mass incarceration of the late twentieth century, students must understand the prison system that preceded it — its scale, its philosophy, and the conditions that made its transformation possible. American prisons in the 1950s and 1960s were, by any comparative measure, a far smaller and more rehabilitatively oriented system than what replaced them. The philosophical framework of the mid-twentieth century penitentiary — rooted in the Progressive Era belief that criminal behavior was a social pathology that could be corrected through the right combination of labor, education, and therapeutic intervention — had produced a system in which indeterminate sentencing gave judges and parole boards broad discretion to release prisoners when they were deemed ready for reintegration, in which prison industries and vocational programs provided skills training, and in which the stated goal of incarceration was the preparation of the prisoner for a law-abiding life on release.

That system was deeply imperfect, marked by racial segregation, physical brutality, arbitrary authority, and the persistent gap between rehabilitative ideology and custodial practice. But it operated on a scale that was dramatically smaller than what followed. In 1970, the total incarcerated population of the United States was approximately 196,000 in state and federal prisons — a rate of about 96 per 100,000 residents. That figure was low even by the standards of the time, comparable to rates in Western European countries that Americans routinely cited as models of more humane penal practice. By 2008, the incarceration rate had reached 754 per 100,000 — nearly eight times the 1970 level, and five to ten times the rates of any other comparable democracy. The incarceration rate for Black men was even more staggering: approximately 3,100 per 100,000, a figure that meant roughly one in thirty-three Black men in the United States was behind bars at any given moment.

Several intellectual and political currents converged in the late 1960s and 1970s to discredit the rehabilitative ideal and create the ideological space for the punitive turn that produced mass incarceration. On the political right, the "law and order" politics that Richard Nixon had pioneered in 1968 framed rising crime rates as the consequence of liberal permissiveness toward criminals and demanded harsher sentences, more police, and less tolerance for the social disruption of urban unrest. On the academic left, a critique of prison rehabilitation argued that the indeterminate sentencing system gave too much discretionary power to prison administrators and parole boards, who could extend sentences indefinitely on the basis of subjective assessments of rehabilitation, and that this discretionary power was systematically exercised in racially discriminatory ways. From this critique emerged an unlikely alliance in favor of determinate sentencing — fixed sentences proportional to the offense — that both conservatives and some liberals supported for very different reasons.

The Martinson Report of 1974 — a comprehensive review of rehabilitation program evaluations that concluded, in a widely cited summary, that "nothing works" in reducing recidivism — gave intellectual authority to the abandonment of rehabilitation as the organizing principle of prison policy. Although subsequent research substantially qualified and challenged Martinson's conclusions, the "nothing works" finding became a political touchstone that was cited for decades to justify the shift from therapeutic to purely punitive incarceration. The rehabilitation programs that had existed in American prisons — educational and vocational training, drug treatment, psychological counseling — were progressively defunded as prison populations grew and budgets were strained, and as the political culture of punishment made it increasingly difficult to defend spending money on services for people who had committed crimes.

The racial context of prison expansion is inseparable from its political context. The "Southern Strategy" by which Republican politicians from Nixon onward appealed to white anxieties about crime, civil rights, and urban disorder was explicitly and deliberately racial in its imagery and its political logic, even when it avoided explicit racial language. The Black urban uprisings of the mid-1960s — the rebellions in Watts, Newark, Detroit, and dozens of other cities — had terrified white suburban and rural voters and created a political market for politicians who promised to restore order to cities that had, in the white popular imagination, been allowed to descend into chaos by liberal permissiveness. Crime and race were fused in the political discourse of the late 1960s in ways that made tough-on-crime politics simultaneously a racial and a penal project, even as its proponents denied any racial intent.

The deindustrialization examined in Chapter 8 of this textbook provides essential economic context for mass incarceration. The cities that experienced the most dramatic prison expansion were the same cities that had lost the most manufacturing employment in the 1970s and 1980s — Chicago, Detroit, Cleveland, Philadelphia, Baltimore, Newark. The young men who filled the prisons were disproportionately from the same neighborhoods that had lost the most jobs. The connection between deindustrialization and incarceration is not accidental. When legitimate employment disappears from a community, the informal economy — including illegal activity — expands to fill the economic gap, and the criminalization of that informal economy through drug enforcement provides a mechanism for processing the economically surplus population that deindustrialization has produced. William Julius Wilson's research, discussed in earlier chapters of this textbook, demonstrated the direct relationship between joblessness and the behaviors that resulted in criminal justice involvement. Mass incarceration was, among other things, a social policy response to the problem of deindustrialization — not a rational or effective response, but a politically available one.

The prison system that this chapter examines is not a monolith. It comprises federal prisons housing people convicted of federal offenses; state prisons housing people convicted of state felonies; local jails holding people awaiting trial or serving short sentences; juvenile facilities; immigration detention centers; and an expanding network of privately operated facilities that contracted with governments to house portions of the incarcerated population. The experience of incarceration varied enormously across these institutional settings and across states, which had very different prison populations, conditions, legal frameworks, and reform histories. But certain common features — overcrowding, racial organization, the development of prisoner hierarchies, the progressive defunding of rehabilitation programs, and the barriers to reentry — were widespread enough to characterize the system as a whole.

The communities most affected by mass incarceration were those that previous chapters of this textbook have already described as bearing the accumulated burdens of the Great Migration, segregation, redlining, deindustrialization, and the crack epidemic. Mass incarceration did not arrive in communities that were doing well and damage them. It arrived in communities that were already under enormous stress and added to that stress in ways that made recovery progressively more difficult. The concentration of incarceration in specific neighborhoods — the "million-dollar blocks" research revealed that in some urban areas a single city block had produced enough prisoners that the state was spending more than a million dollars per year incarcerating its residents — meant that the social damage of mass incarceration was not distributed evenly across the population but was concentrated precisely where the accumulated effects of other forms of racial exclusion were already most severe.

Major People, Groups, and Places

Reference — People, Groups & Places

Nelson Rockefeller (1908–1979)
Governor of New York whose 1973 drug laws — requiring mandatory prison sentences of fifteen years to life for possession or sale of certain quantities of narcotics — became the national model for harsh drug sentencing and a template for the mandatory minimum movement that drove prison expansion. Rockefeller proposed the laws in part to rehabilitate his reputation with conservative voters after his response to the Attica uprising had been criticized. The laws bore his name and his political calculation.
Robert Martinson (1927–1980)
Sociologist whose 1974 survey article summarizing research on prison rehabilitation programs was widely interpreted as concluding that "nothing works" in reducing recidivism. The report was seized upon by politicians and policymakers eager to abandon rehabilitation programs, though Martinson himself later published a follow-up article qualifying his conclusions significantly. The political use of the "nothing works" finding illustrates how research can be selectively deployed to justify predetermined policy conclusions.
Michelle Alexander
Legal scholar and civil rights advocate whose 2010 book The New Jim Crow: Mass Incarceration in the Age of Colorblindness became the most widely read scholarly critique of the racial dimensions of mass incarceration. Alexander argued that the criminal justice system functions as a mechanism of racial control analogous to Jim Crow, cycling Black men through prisons and then imposing legal disabilities — restrictions on voting, employment, housing, and benefits — that reproduce their second-class status. The book sparked national debate and is credited with contributing to the bipartisan criminal justice reform movement of the 2010s.
William Julius Wilson
Sociologist whose research on the relationship between deindustrialization, concentrated poverty, and the behaviors criminalized by the War on Drugs provided the most influential academic framework for understanding mass incarceration as a structural rather than cultural phenomenon. Wilson's work, discussed in earlier chapters of this textbook, established that the concentration of criminalized behavior in Black urban neighborhoods was a consequence of economic abandonment rather than cultural pathology.
Bryan Stevenson
Civil rights lawyer, legal scholar, and founder of the Equal Justice Initiative, whose advocacy on behalf of people on death row and serving excessive sentences exposed the systematic injustices of the American criminal justice system to national attention. Stevenson's memoir and advocacy work demonstrated in concrete human terms the consequences of the mandatory sentencing regime and the racial disparities in the administration of justice.
The Attica Correctional Facility (New York)
The site of the 1971 prison uprising in which prisoners seized control of a portion of the facility and held it for four days before Governor Rockefeller ordered the state police to retake it by force. Forty-three people were killed in the retaking — thirty-three prisoners and ten guards and civilian hostages — in an action that a subsequent investigation found was marred by unnecessary use of force. Attica became the defining symbol of both the brutality of American prison conditions and the resistance of incarcerated people to those conditions.
Pelican Bay State Prison (California)
The California maximum security facility that opened in 1989 and became the most controversial prison in the state's system, known particularly for its Security Housing Unit (SHU) — a form of solitary confinement in which prisoners were isolated in small cells for twenty-two to twenty-four hours a day, sometimes for years or decades. Pelican Bay's SHU became the site of major hunger strikes organized by prisoners and a focus of legal challenges to solitary confinement as cruel and unusual punishment.
Rikers Island (New York City)
The largest jail complex in the United States, housing primarily people awaiting trial who cannot afford bail and those serving sentences of less than one year. Rikers became a symbol of the pretrial detention crisis — the fact that the inability to afford bail caused poor people to be jailed for months or years before any determination of guilt — and of the brutal conditions in American local jails that received far less public attention than state and federal prisons.
The California Department of Corrections and Rehabilitation (CDCR)
The state agency responsible for administering California's prison system, which at its peak in 2006 held approximately 173,000 people in facilities designed for about 80,000 — more than double designed capacity. California's prison system was the subject of the landmark 2011 Supreme Court decision in Brown v. Plata, which found that overcrowding had produced unconstitutionally inadequate medical and mental health care, and ordered a significant reduction in the prison population.
The Nuestra Familia and the Mexican Mafia
The two dominant Hispanic prison gangs in the California prison system, whose rivalry — rooted in a geographic division between Northern and Southern California — organized much of the violence and economic activity inside California prisons and filtered back into street-level conflicts between the Norteños and Sureños gang factions on the outside. Their development illustrates how prison environments can create and solidify organizational identities that then reshape street-level gang geography.
The Black Guerrilla Family
A prison gang founded in California's San Quentin prison in 1966 by former Black Panther member George Jackson, originally with a political and revolutionary ideology. The Black Guerrilla Family evolved over subsequent decades into primarily a prison gang involved in drug trafficking and prison violence, while retaining some political rhetoric. Its history illustrates the transformation of politically motivated organizational forms under the pressure of prison conditions and long-term isolation from political movements.
George Jackson (1941–1971)
A Black prisoner who became a nationally recognized political figure and author while incarcerated in California's prison system, killed in a contested incident at San Quentin in 1971 that prison authorities attributed to an escape attempt and critics attributed to political assassination. Jackson's prison writings, particularly Soledad Brother and Blood in My Eye, made him a major figure in the Black Power movement and connected the prison experience to the broader struggle against racial capitalism. His murder heightened the political temperature around Attica, which occurred six weeks later.
The Sentencing Project
A Washington, D.C.-based research and advocacy organization that has been the primary source of data and analysis on racial disparities in the American criminal justice system since its founding in 1986. The Sentencing Project's research has been foundational to public understanding of the scale and racial dimensions of mass incarceration.
Reentry Courts and Second Chance Programs
Specialized courts and programs developed in the 2000s and 2010s to address the barriers that returning citizens face in reintegrating into communities after release from prison. Reentry courts provide intensive supervision, social services, and employment assistance in exchange for reduced probation requirements; Second Chance Act programs provide federal funding for state and local reentry initiatives. These programs represent the shift in some jurisdictions from purely punitive to more supportive approaches to people leaving incarceration.

Section 11.1

Prison Expansion

The Scale of the Transformation

The numbers tell a story that is almost impossible to fully absorb. In 1972, approximately 196,000 people were incarcerated in American state and federal prisons. By 1980, that figure had grown to roughly 315,000. By 1990, it exceeded 770,000. By 2000, it surpassed 1.3 million in prisons alone, with an additional 620,000 in local jails. By 2008 the combined prison and jail population reached approximately 2.3 million, with millions more on probation or parole and therefore under the supervision of the criminal justice system. The United States, which had maintained a prison population broadly comparable to other wealthy democracies through the 1960s, had by the early 2000s assembled a carceral apparatus so vast that it incarcerated more people in absolute numbers than China — a nation with four times the population — and at a rate roughly five to ten times that of any comparable Western European country.

2.3 million The number of people held in American prisons and jails in 2008, the peak of the incarceration era — up from approximately 196,000 in state and federal prisons in 1972. The United States at this point imprisoned more people than any other nation on earth, including countries with far larger populations.

The expansion was not uniform across time. The steepest rates of growth came in the 1980s and 1990s, during the War on Drugs and the political aftermath of the crack cocaine epidemic. Federal prisons grew most dramatically, driven by the mandatory minimum sentences for drug offenses and the prosecution of drug trafficking cases under RICO and other federal statutes. State prisons also expanded rapidly, driven by a combination of drug enforcement, longer sentences for violent and property crimes under three-strikes and truth-in-sentencing laws, and the general hardening of prosecutorial and sentencing practices that characterized the tough-on-crime political environment. The growth in the prison population was matched by an enormous growth in prison construction — the United States built more new prisons between 1980 and 2000 than it had in the preceding century — and by a corresponding growth in the correctional workforce, making corrections one of the fastest-growing sectors of public employment in many states.

The racial dimensions of this expansion were stark and persistent. By 2000, Black people, who constituted approximately 12 percent of the U.S. population, accounted for approximately 44 percent of the prison population. The Black male incarceration rate was nearly seven times the white male incarceration rate. In some states and for some age groups, the disparities were even more extreme: in Washington, D.C., at the height of the crack epidemic, approximately one in three young Black men was under criminal justice supervision — in prison, in jail, on probation, or on parole — at any given time. These were not marginal figures; they were the organizing demographic reality of an entire generation of Black urban men.

The Ideological Shift: From Rehabilitation to Retribution

Understanding why the prison population expanded so dramatically requires understanding the ideological transformation in penal philosophy that preceded and accompanied it. The rehabilitation model that had dominated American prison policy through the mid-twentieth century rested on several premises: that criminal behavior was caused by social conditions and personal pathologies that could be treated; that prisons should be organized around the goal of preparing prisoners for productive reintegration; and that indeterminate sentences, allowing release when rehabilitation was achieved, were more rational than fixed terms that took no account of individual progress.

This model came under sustained attack from multiple directions in the 1970s. From the right, critics argued that it was too soft on criminals, that it failed to deliver adequate punishment for wrongdoing, and that it prioritized the welfare of criminals over the safety and interests of victims. From the left, critics argued that the indeterminate sentencing system was a mechanism of racial control, giving prison administrators and parole boards discretion that was exercised in racially biased ways and that extended sentences for Black prisoners beyond what their actual risk warranted. From criminologists, the Martinson Report's "nothing works" conclusion (however qualified in the original research) provided academic authority for skepticism about rehabilitation's efficacy.

The resulting shift toward determinate — fixed — sentencing was embraced across the political spectrum for different reasons. Conservatives wanted longer, more certain sentences that would deliver punishment and incapacitation; liberals wanted to eliminate discretion they believed was exercised in racially biased ways. The irony, which became apparent only in retrospect, was that determinate sentencing did not reduce racial disparities — it often increased them, because the mandatory minimums were structured in ways (like the crack/powder cocaine disparity) that fell disproportionately on Black defendants — while simultaneously eliminating the individualized flexibility that had allowed, in principle, for early release when rehabilitation was genuine.

The sentencing guidelines and mandatory minimums that proliferated from the mid-1970s through the 1990s created a system in which sentence length was determined primarily by the offense charged rather than by the characteristics of the individual defendant or the circumstances of the crime. Prosecutors, who determined what charges to bring, effectively became the most powerful actors in the sentencing process; the plea bargaining system through which most cases were resolved gave them enormous leverage to extract guilty pleas by threatening harsh mandatory sentences if defendants chose to exercise their right to trial. The result was a system that processed enormous numbers of cases efficiently from a prosecutorial standpoint but that produced sentences of a severity and inflexibility that most other comparable legal systems would have found disproportionate.

The Prison Construction Boom

The physical infrastructure of mass incarceration required enormous investment. Between 1980 and 2000, state and federal governments spent hundreds of billions of dollars building and staffing new prisons, expanding existing facilities, and maintaining a corrections workforce that grew from approximately 300,000 in 1980 to more than 700,000 by 2000. In many rural and small-town communities that had lost manufacturing employment to deindustrialization, prison construction became a form of economic development, bringing unionized public employment, construction contracts, and consumer spending to areas that had few other economic prospects.

This economic interest in prison expansion created a powerful constituency for maintaining and growing the carceral system — the prison-industrial complex that critics began to describe in the 1990s. Corrections officer unions, particularly in California, became major political forces, contributing heavily to campaigns supporting harsh sentencing laws and opposing sentencing reforms. Private prison companies, which emerged in the 1980s to contract with governments for the housing of prisoners, developed direct financial interests in maintaining high incarceration rates. Construction firms, food service contractors, healthcare providers, and a range of other businesses that depended on prison contracts similarly benefited from expansion. The political economy of mass incarceration was not simply the consequence of crime or the demands of justice. It was sustained by a network of economic interests that had powerful reasons to maintain the system.

The geography of prison location created a further irony. Prisons were typically built in rural areas, often in legislative districts represented by politicians who supported tough sentencing laws and opposed urban social spending. But the people incarcerated in those prisons came overwhelmingly from urban Black and Latino neighborhoods. Under the U.S. Census Bureau's practice of counting prisoners as residents of the county where they were incarcerated rather than the county where they lived before their arrest, prisoners were counted as residents of rural prison districts for purposes of legislative apportionment, effectively transferring political representation — and federal resource allocation — from urban communities of color to rural white communities that housed the prisons. Critics called this "prison-based gerrymandering"; advocates of reform argued that it compounded the political silencing of communities already affected by felony disenfranchisement.

Racial Disparities: Causes and Consequences

The racial disparities in incarceration rates are the most politically charged and analytically contested feature of the mass incarceration system. Defenders of the system attributed disparities in incarceration rates to disparities in crime rates, arguing that Black people were incarcerated more often because they committed crimes more often. Critics pointed to evidence that racial disparities in criminal justice outcomes persist even after controlling for the seriousness of the offense and the defendant's criminal history, suggesting that the disparities cannot be fully explained by differences in behavior.

The research evidence is substantial and consistent. Studies of arrest decisions, prosecutorial charging choices, plea bargaining outcomes, and sentencing decisions have repeatedly found that race influences outcomes in ways that cannot be fully explained by offense severity or prior record. Black defendants are less likely to receive plea offers, more likely to be detained pretrial (often because they cannot afford bail), more likely to receive sentences at or above the sentencing guidelines, and more likely to be subject to prosecutorial decisions to seek harsh mandatory minimums. These disparities accumulate through the criminal justice process, producing in the aggregate the enormous racial disparities in incarceration rates that the statistics document.

The crack cocaine sentencing disparity, discussed in the previous chapter, was the most obvious and quantitatively significant example of racially disparate drug enforcement. The 100-to-1 ratio between the quantities of crack and powder cocaine that triggered mandatory minimum sentences meant that a Black street dealer with five grams of crack received the same mandatory minimum sentence — five years — as a powder cocaine dealer with five hundred grams of powder. Because crack was overwhelmingly used and sold in Black communities and powder cocaine was more associated with white users, the practical effect of the disparity was to impose dramatically longer sentences on Black drug defendants than on white defendants convicted of equivalent offenses at the level of drug weight. The U.S. Sentencing Commission documented this disparity in multiple reports, but it took until 2010 for Congress to partially reduce it, and the reduction was not made retroactive for many years.

Historical Vocabulary
Mass Incarceration

Mass incarceration refers to the dramatic and historically unprecedented increase in the rate of imprisonment in the United States that began in the early 1970s and accelerated through the 1980s and 1990s, producing a prison and jail population that reached approximately 2.3 million by 2008 — more than ten times the incarcerated population of 1972 in absolute terms, and roughly seven to ten times the incarceration rate of comparable wealthy democracies.

The term carries several analytical implications. It suggests that the scale of incarceration exceeded what could be explained as a rational response to crime rates — a fact supported by the evidence that incarceration continued to grow even as crime rates fell substantially after the mid-1990s. It suggests that incarceration had become a social policy tool for managing the economically marginalized population rather than simply a response to individual criminal conduct. And it suggests that the racial dimensions of incarceration — the enormous disparity between Black and white incarceration rates — were systemic rather than incidental features of the prison expansion.

The concept of mass incarceration is sometimes criticized for obscuring important distinctions between different kinds of incarceration and different causes of imprisonment. Not everyone who is imprisoned under the mass incarceration regime has been imprisoned for a nonviolent drug offense; a substantial portion of the prison population is serving time for violent offenses. Critics of some reform rhetoric argue that the focus on drug offenses can obscure the question of how to respond appropriately to serious violence. These are genuine debates, but they do not undermine the core observation that the United States imprisoned vastly more of its citizens, with vastly greater racial disparity, than any comparable society — and that this was the product of specific political choices rather than natural necessity.

Private Prisons and the Economics of Incarceration

The emergence of for-profit private prison companies in the 1980s added a new dimension to the political economy of mass incarceration. Companies like Corrections Corporation of America (now CoreCivic) and the GEO Group contracted with federal and state governments to house prisoners at per-diem rates, claiming that private management would be more efficient than government operation. By 2010, private facilities housed approximately 8 percent of the national prison population — a significant minority, but one with outsized political influence given the resources the companies invested in lobbying for the sentencing laws that kept their facilities filled.

The private prison industry's political involvement became a major target of reform advocates who argued that allowing profit-making companies to benefit financially from high incarceration rates created a structural incentive to maintain and expand those rates through lobbying against reforms. Documents obtained through public records requests showed that major private prison companies had contributed to the campaigns of legislators who had supported three-strikes laws, mandatory minimum sentences, and immigration enforcement policies that directed detained immigrants into private facilities. Whether this lobbying was determinative of policy outcomes or merely one influence among many is debated, but the alignment of financial interest with political activity was real and documented.

The critique of private prisons extended beyond their political lobbying to their operational practices. Research on outcomes in private versus public prisons produced mixed results on cost and quality comparisons, but reports of inadequate medical care, high staff turnover, reduced programming, and higher rates of violence in some private facilities raised concerns about whether the profit motive was compatible with humane management of a captive population. The tension between the financial interest in minimizing per-prisoner expenditure and the humanitarian and rehabilitative interest in providing adequate care and programming was inherent in the private prison model and not resolved by any amount of contract monitoring.

Section 11.2

Prison Gangs

Origins and Development

The development of prison gangs was both a response to and a driver of the particular features of American mass incarceration. Prison gangs did not spring into existence with the expansion of the 1980s; they had roots in the prisons of the 1950s and 1960s, when racial integration of prisons and the breakdown of the older inmate social codes produced a reorganization of prisoner social life along racial and ethnic lines. What the mass incarceration era changed was the scale of the phenomenon, the complexity of the organizational structures, the degree of integration between prison gangs and street organizations, and the political and economic stakes of gang membership inside the walls.

The primary function of prison gang membership, from the perspective of individual prisoners, was protection. The prison environment — overcrowded, understaffed, characterized by racial segregation both formal and informal, and pervaded by the constant possibility of violence — was a place where individual survival depended on collective belonging. A person who entered prison without gang affiliation was potentially vulnerable to exploitation or attack by organized groups; gang membership offered the protection of numbers, the knowledge of the informal rules of prison social life, and access to the economic resources — food, tobacco, commissary goods, contraband — that made daily existence more bearable.

The major prison gangs that developed in California's system in the 1960s and 1970s — the Mexican Mafia (also called La Eme), the Nuestra Familia, the Aryan Brotherhood, and the Black Guerrilla Family — became the organizational framework around which much of prison social life was organized. Each had its own territorial claims, membership criteria, internal hierarchy, rules of conduct, and systems for managing economic activity inside the institution. The California Department of Corrections responded to gang development by creating a system of gang validation and Security Housing Unit (SHU) placement — prisoners identified as gang members or associates were placed in long-term solitary confinement, in isolation cells where they had minimal contact with other prisoners or with the outside world.

The SHU system, and the practice of long-term solitary confinement more broadly, became one of the most contested features of the California prison system and of American incarceration generally. Research on the psychological effects of prolonged isolation consistently showed severe harms: anxiety, depression, hallucinations, cognitive deterioration, and in some cases permanent psychological damage. Critics argued that solitary confinement was not a security tool but a form of torture, producing the psychological damage that it claimed to be preventing by creating more disturbed and dangerous individuals. The major hunger strikes in California's SHUs in 2011 and 2013, in which thousands of prisoners across multiple facilities refused food to protest indefinite solitary confinement, drew national attention to conditions that had previously been invisible to most of the public.

Gang Structure and Prison Economy

Inside the prison, gang organizations managed economic activity that the official institution either could not or would not control. The informal economy of the prison — the exchange of food, tobacco, drugs, sexual access, protection, and information — was organized through gang structures that provided a framework for transactions, enforced agreements, and adjudicated disputes. The prison gang functioned, in important respects, like the street gang of the urban neighborhood: it provided governance in a space where official governance was absent or inadequate, using the threat of violence to enforce norms and manage conflict.

The drugs that flowed into prisons despite official prohibition were a central economic engine of the prison gang economy. The routes by which drugs entered prisons were varied: through corrupt guards who were paid to bring in contraband, through visitors who passed small packages during contact visits, through mail, and through prisoners who concealed drugs in their bodies on return from court appearances or outside medical appointments. The profit margins on prison drug sales were enormous — drugs that cost a few dollars on the street could command many times that price inside the prison, where demand was high and supply was constrained. The gangs that controlled drug distribution inside the prison were collecting revenue that funded their operations, rewarded leadership, and maintained the loyalty of membership.

The relationship between prison gangs and street organizations was reciprocal and evolving. Some street organizations — like the Crips and Bloods in California, or the People and Folk nations in Illinois — developed corresponding prison structures as their members were incarcerated in large numbers. Prison leaders of these organizations maintained communication with street-level leadership through correspondence, visits, and the movement of members in and out of custody, and prison structures sometimes exercised authority over street activities. In other cases, the prison environment created new organizational identities that had no precise street equivalent: the Nuestra Familia, for example, was formed inside the prison system in 1968 and subsequently developed a street presence, rather than the other way around.

The racial organization of prison gang life had direct consequences for the street organizations of the neighborhoods from which prisoners came. The practice of racial solidarity inside the prison — in which prisoners affiliated by race or ethnicity regardless of their street gang affiliation — sometimes overrode or complicated street-level rivalries and sometimes reinforced them. The Crip/Blood rivalry that defined street gang conflict in Los Angeles was, in some prison contexts, subordinated to the broader Black/non-Black racial alignment that organized prison social life. In other contexts, the prison reinforced and intensified street-level divisions. The specific effects varied by prison, by time period, and by the particular organizations involved, but the general point holds: the prison was not an isolated institution disconnected from the street. It was a continuation of street life under different conditions, and it filtered its effects back into the neighborhoods that sent it its population.

The Geography of Prison Organization: California as Example

California's prison gang structure became the most elaborately documented in the country, in part because California had the largest state prison system in the country by the 1990s and in part because California's system of gang validation and monitoring generated unusually detailed records. The major California prison gangs organized along racial and regional lines that had significant consequences for street-level gang organization. The split between the Mexican Mafia, which had roots in Los Angeles and Southern California, and the Nuestra Familia, which had roots in the rural Central Valley and Northern California, created the prison-derived Sureño/Norteño division that became one of the organizing axes of Hispanic gang identity throughout California and eventually throughout the western United States.

This development is a striking example of how the prison environment can create organizational forms that shape behavior and identity far beyond the prison walls. The Sureño/Norteño division — marked by colors (blue vs. red), by regional affiliation, and by fierce mutual hostility — was not a natural extension of pre-existing street gang rivalries. It was largely a product of the prison gang structure, imposed on street-level organizations through the authority that prison gang leadership exercised over members who had served time and returned to the community. By the 1990s and 2000s, the Sureño/Norteño division had spread with Hispanic migration patterns to cities and towns throughout the West and beyond, creating gang affiliations in places that had no independent history of gang activity but whose residents had been incarcerated in California and returned carrying the prison gang organizational structure.

The Black Guerrilla Family (BGF) followed a different trajectory that illustrates the complexity of the relationship between political ideology and criminal organization in the prison context. Founded by George Jackson at San Quentin in 1966 with an explicitly revolutionary Black nationalist ideology, the BGF positioned itself as a political organization fighting against racial capitalism and the prison system that sustained it. Jackson's writings circulated widely in Black radical and prison rights circles, and the BGF attracted members who understood themselves as political prisoners resisting an unjust system. But the organization's actual activities inside the prison — and its trajectory after Jackson's death and the decline of the broader Black Power movement — moved progressively toward the drug dealing, extortion, and violence characteristic of other prison gangs. The political ideology remained in the rhetoric but became less central to the organization's actual functioning. The BGF's history is a cautionary illustration of how the prison environment tends to push organizational forms toward criminal economic activity regardless of their original ideological commitments.

Section 11.3

Family Disruption

The Scale of Family Separation

By the peak years of mass incarceration in the early 2000s, approximately 1.5 million children in the United States had a parent in prison on any given day — and millions more had a parent who had been incarcerated at some point in their lives. For Black children, the figures were even more striking: by some estimates, approximately one in nine Black children had a parent who was incarcerated in 2008, compared with approximately one in fifty-seven white children. The incarceration of a parent was not, for these children, a rare or exceptional event. It was a common feature of childhood in communities most affected by mass incarceration, woven into the texture of ordinary life in ways that shaped development, family structure, economic circumstances, and life prospects in profound and lasting ways.

The family disruption produced by mass incarceration began before conviction. The arrest of a parent disrupted routines, frightened children, and introduced uncertainty about the future. Pretrial detention — holding people in jail while they awaited trial because they could not afford bail — could last weeks, months, or in some cases years, during which a parent was absent from the home and unable to contribute to family income, childcare, or household management. The bail system, which ostensibly allowed release pending trial by posting a monetary bond, functioned in practice as a wealth-based detention system: those who could afford bail went home to prepare their defense; those who could not afford it remained jailed regardless of the strength of the case against them. The racial and class dimensions of pretrial detention were significant and well documented — Black and Latino defendants were detained pretrial at higher rates than white defendants charged with similar offenses, partly because they were offered higher bail amounts and partly because they had less wealth with which to pay.

Effects on Children and Caregiving Parents

The research on the effects of parental incarceration on children is substantial and consistently negative. Children of incarcerated parents show higher rates of behavioral problems, anxiety, depression, educational difficulties, and economic hardship than comparable children whose parents have not been incarcerated, and these effects persist into adulthood in measurable ways. The pathway through which parental incarceration affects children is multiple: the direct emotional trauma of family separation, the loss of parental income and practical support, the stigma that attaches to children of incarcerated parents, the residential instability that often follows a parent's removal from the household, and the economic stress on the remaining parent that limits the time and resources available for nurturing and oversight.

The parent who remained at home — most often the mother, given that the vast majority of incarcerated parents were men — faced the challenge of managing household and childcare responsibilities without the income and practical assistance of the incarcerated partner, often while carrying the emotional burden of her own grief, anxiety, and anger about the incarceration. The financial costs of maintaining contact with an incarcerated partner were themselves a significant burden: prison phone calls were typically priced far above commercial rates (telephone service providers in prisons paid commissions to prison authorities for exclusive contracts), costing families that could afford it least the most for basic contact with their incarcerated family members. Visits to prisons required transportation to facilities that were often located far from urban communities, hours of waiting and processing, and subjection to intrusive searches that many family members found humiliating. Many families, particularly those with young children, found that the practical and financial barriers to maintaining contact effectively severed family relationships over the course of a long sentence.

The effects of mass incarceration on intimate partner relationships were similarly profound. Research on the relationship between incarceration and marriage rates in Black communities suggested that the removal of large numbers of Black men from the marriageable pool through incarceration contributed to declining marriage rates in heavily incarcerated communities — not primarily because individuals chose not to marry but because the numerical imbalance created by incarceration reduced the availability of potential partners and because the economic and social damage of incarceration made formerly incarcerated men less desirable as long-term partners. The relationship between mass incarceration and what William Julius Wilson had identified as the "marriageable man shortage" in deindustrialized Black neighborhoods is not perfectly clean — both incarceration and unemployment contribute to the same shortage — but the contribution of incarceration to the disruption of family formation in Black urban communities was real and significant.

Women and Incarceration

Although men constituted approximately 93 percent of the incarcerated population, the incarceration of women — which grew at even faster rates than male incarceration in the 1980s and 1990s — deserves specific attention because of its particular consequences for children and communities. Women's incarceration grew by approximately 800 percent between 1977 and 2007, driven primarily by drug offenses and by the harsh sentencing of low-level drug offenders and drug users regardless of their caretaking roles. Women were disproportionately prosecuted for drug offenses as users or low-level participants in the drug economy (often as partners or family members of drug dealers) rather than as high-level traffickers, and the mandatory minimum sentencing laws made no allowance for the caretaking responsibilities that distinguished their situations from those of men convicted of similar offenses.

When a mother was incarcerated, the disruption to children was often even more severe than when a father was incarcerated, because mothers were more often the primary caretakers of their children. Children of incarcerated mothers were more likely to end up in foster care or in the custody of relatives who might or might not have the resources to care for them adequately. The child welfare system's interaction with the criminal justice system created situations in which incarcerated mothers faced termination of their parental rights because their incarceration prevented them from meeting the requirements of child welfare plans — a particularly cruel double-bind that permanently severed family bonds as a consequence of poverty, addiction, and limited criminal justice system options.

The expansion of women's incarceration also reflected the gendered dimensions of drug enforcement policy. The conspiracy provisions of federal drug law made it possible to prosecute women who had marginal involvement in drug trafficking — who had allowed their home to be used for drug storage, who had accepted money that was the proceeds of a partner's drug dealing, or who had been named by a co-defendant seeking a reduced sentence — under the same mandatory minimums as the principal traffickers. The "girlfriend problem" — the application of mandatory minimums to women whose involvement in drug organizations was peripheral — was identified by federal judges and lawyers as one of the most glaring injustices of the mandatory sentencing system, but it was not addressed by Congress for decades.

Section 11.4

Reentry Challenges

The Revolving Door

More than 95 percent of the people sent to prison are eventually released. By the peak years of mass incarceration, approximately 600,000 to 700,000 people were being released from state and federal prisons every year — a flow of returning citizens back into communities that was enormous in scale and chronically under-resourced in support. The recidivism rate — the rate at which formerly incarcerated people return to prison, either for new offenses or for technical violations of their parole or probation conditions — was persistently high, with studies consistently finding that more than two-thirds of released prisoners were rearrested within three years and more than half returned to prison within that period. This revolving door was not simply a consequence of individual criminal propensity. It was the predictable result of a system that released people into communities with minimal preparation, minimal resources, and enormous structural barriers to the employment, housing, and social connection that are prerequisites for desistance from criminal behavior.

Historical Vocabulary
Reentry

Reentry refers to the process by which people released from incarceration attempt to reintegrate into their communities. The term emerged in the social service and criminal justice reform literature of the 1990s as a more neutral and aspirational alternative to older language like "ex-offender release," and it frames successful return to community as a goal requiring deliberate support rather than an automatic consequence of release.

Reentry is widely recognized as one of the most challenging phases of the criminal justice process. People leaving prison typically face a combination of acute practical problems — finding housing, meeting parole requirements, obtaining identification documents, finding employment — and longer-term structural barriers — felony records, legal restrictions on certain occupations and housing, and the social stigma of incarceration — that make stable community reintegration very difficult. The research consistently shows that supportive reentry services — housing assistance, employment training, substance abuse treatment, mental health services, and case management — significantly reduce recidivism; the political and budgetary barriers to funding such services have limited their availability.

The language of "reentry" implies that the person is returning to somewhere they belong, reconnecting with a community that is their home. This framing is important because it resists the tendency to treat incarcerated people as permanently outside the community — as people whose incarceration is their permanent identity. Most people who are incarcerated will return to their communities; the question of what happens when they do is not merely a policy question but a community and human one, with consequences for the families and neighborhoods to which they return and for the individuals attempting to rebuild their lives.

Employment and the Felony Record

The most significant barrier to successful reentry was almost universally identified as employment. Having a stable, legal source of income was the most powerful predictor of successful desistance from criminal behavior and successful community reintegration; it was also one of the things that mass incarceration most thoroughly undermined. The barriers to employment for formerly incarcerated people operated at multiple levels: legal prohibitions on employment in certain occupations, employer screening practices that used criminal background checks to screen out applicants with felony records, and the practical gaps in employment history and work skills created by time in prison.

Legal restrictions on employment for people with felony convictions were extensive and varied by state, but they could include prohibitions on employment in law enforcement, education, healthcare, childcare, financial services, and a range of other licensed occupations. Federally funded housing assistance programs excluded people with certain drug convictions. Federal student loan programs had at various times excluded people with drug convictions from eligibility. Professional licensing boards in many states had broad authority to deny licenses based on criminal history, regardless of the relevance of the conviction to the licensed occupation. The cumulative effect of these restrictions was to define a large portion of the labor market — including the most stable and remunerated portions — as off-limits to people with felony records.

Employer practices compounded the legal restrictions. The widespread use of criminal background checks in hiring — made increasingly easy and inexpensive by the development of commercial background check services in the 1990s and 2000s — meant that most formal sector employers could and routinely did screen out applicants with felony records at the initial application stage, before any assessment of the individual's qualifications, the nature of the conviction, or its relevance to the job in question. Studies in which matched resumes with and without criminal record disclosures were submitted for the same positions found that the presence of a criminal record reduced callback rates by roughly 50 percent for white applicants and even more dramatically for Black applicants with records.

The reform movement to address employment discrimination against people with criminal records centered on "Ban the Box" initiatives — policies requiring employers to remove the criminal history question from initial employment applications, delaying background checks until later in the hiring process when the applicant's qualifications had already been assessed. By 2020, more than thirty states and the federal government had adopted some form of Ban the Box policy, representing a significant shift in the employment landscape for formerly incarcerated people, though the ultimate impact on hiring outcomes was subject to debate. Some research suggested that Ban the Box policies, by removing the formal screening mechanism, led employers to statistically discriminate against all Black male applicants — assuming criminal records even when they could not directly check. This finding underscored that employment discrimination against formerly incarcerated people was not purely a consequence of criminal record disclosure but was entangled with racial discrimination in ways that made simple policy fixes inadequate.

Housing and the Barriers to Stability

Stable housing was the second most critical factor in successful reentry after employment, and it was similarly obstructed by a combination of legal restrictions and market discrimination. People leaving prison typically had minimal savings — the wage labor available inside prison paid nominal amounts, often less than a dollar per hour — and faced first-month, last-month, and security deposit requirements that they could not meet without financial assistance from family or reentry programs. Public housing — which might have been the most accessible low-cost housing option — was largely unavailable to people with felony drug convictions under the federal "One Strike and You're Out" policy implemented in the 1990s, which authorized and in some cases required public housing authorities to deny admission to applicants with certain criminal histories.

The practical consequences of housing instability for reentry outcomes were severe. People who could not secure stable housing on release were far more likely to return to the networks and neighborhoods associated with their previous criminal activity, to cycle through homelessness and shelter stays that made consistent employment impossible, and to reoffend or violate parole conditions in ways that returned them to prison. The concentration of formerly incarcerated people in homeless shelters and transitional housing facilities in high-poverty neighborhoods created clustering effects that replicated the social and environmental conditions associated with criminalized behavior, making genuine community reintegration — reconnection with stable social networks, legitimate employment, and the routines of law-abiding civilian life — extremely difficult to achieve.

Historical Vocabulary
Collateral Consequences

Collateral consequences are legal penalties and disabilities that attach to a criminal conviction beyond the sentence imposed by the court — the imprisonment, fines, and supervision that constitute the formal punishment. These consequences are "collateral" in the technical legal sense that they are not part of the criminal sentence; they attach automatically or through subsequent administrative decisions triggered by the conviction. In the context of mass incarceration, the accumulation of collateral consequences has been described as creating a permanent second-class status for people with felony convictions — a system of legal disadvantage that persists long after the sentence is served.

The scope of collateral consequences is enormous and largely invisible to defendants at the time of conviction. A 2010 report by the American Bar Association's National Inventory of the Collateral Consequences of Conviction identified more than 38,000 statutory and regulatory provisions in federal and state law that imposed collateral consequences on people with criminal records. These consequences included restrictions on employment and professional licensing; exclusions from public housing and federal housing assistance; restrictions on access to public benefits including food stamps, student loans, and social security; prohibitions on firearm possession; restrictions on immigration status and citizenship; and felony disenfranchisement.

The concept of collateral consequences connects the history of mass incarceration to the history of racial legal subordination discussed in earlier chapters of this textbook. Legal disabilities attached to criminal convictions — restrictions on voting, on civil rights, on economic opportunity — function in practice as mechanisms for permanently marking and excluding a population, in ways that structurally reproduce racial inequality regardless of the formal racial neutrality of the criminal law. Michelle Alexander's argument that mass incarceration functions as a "New Jim Crow" rests substantially on the analysis of collateral consequences as a system of legal subordination parallel to the disenfranchisement and exclusion of the Jim Crow era.

Parole Supervision and Technical Violations

Most people released from prison were released not to unconditional freedom but to parole supervision — a period of monitored community residence in which a parole officer could return them to prison for either new criminal offenses or "technical violations" of their parole conditions. Technical violations — failing a drug test, missing an appointment with a parole officer, traveling outside the county without permission, associating with known felons (which, in communities where many people had records, could mean associating with virtually anyone one knew) — became a significant driver of prison population by the 1990s and 2000s. In some states, technical violations accounted for more than a third of new prison admissions.

The expansion of parole revocations for technical violations was not primarily a consequence of individual behavior. It was a consequence of the conditions attached to parole, the resources allocated to parole supervision, and the discretionary decisions of parole officers and parole boards about when to revoke rather than address violations through community-based responses. The expansion of drug testing as a parole condition, combined with the continued drug use of many formerly incarcerated people for whom substance abuse had been a primary driver of criminal involvement, created a mechanism for returning people to prison without any new criminal offense. Critics argued that the parole revocation system effectively criminalized addiction — punishing people for the symptoms of a disease rather than providing the treatment that would address the underlying problem.

Section 11.5

Long-Term Community Effects

Political Silencing and Felony Disenfranchisement

Felony disenfranchisement — the legal removal of voting rights from people convicted of felonies — was among the most politically significant of mass incarceration's collateral consequences. By 2004, approximately 5.3 million people in the United States were disenfranchised because of felony convictions — approximately 1 in 44 adults. The racial composition of this disenfranchised population was dramatically skewed: approximately 1 in 8 Black men of voting age was disenfranchised, compared with approximately 1 in 55 non-Black men. In states with the most restrictive disenfranchisement laws — those that permanently disenfranchised people even after completion of their sentence — the rate for Black men reached extraordinary levels: in some southern states, more than one-quarter of Black men were permanently disenfranchised.

The consequences for Black political power were substantial and measurable. Research by sociologists Christopher Uggen and Jeff Manza estimated that felony disenfranchisement had been pivotal in several close Senate and presidential elections, with the disenfranchised population large enough in some states that its inclusion would have reversed the official outcome. The Sentencing Project calculated that in Florida, which had some of the most restrictive disenfranchisement laws, more than 10 percent of Black adults were permanently disenfranchised in 2000 — a year in which the state's presidential election outcome was decided by 537 votes. The political suppression function of felony disenfranchisement, in this analysis, was not an incidental side effect of the criminal justice system but a structural feature with direct effects on Black political representation at the local, state, and national levels.

The historical connection between felony disenfranchisement and racial disfranchisement in the Jim Crow era was explicit in some cases. Several southern states had enacted felony disenfranchisement provisions specifically after Reconstruction for the purpose of removing the voting rights of Black men — targeting offenses that Black people were thought to be more likely to commit, and crafting the provisions to exclude from suffrage a population that had just been granted it by the Fifteenth Amendment. The Supreme Court's 1974 decision in Richardson v. Ramirez upheld the constitutionality of felon disenfranchisement based on a textual reading of the Fourteenth Amendment, and subsequent challenges met with mixed success at best. Reform efforts at the state level produced some changes — Florida voters approved a ballot measure restoring voting rights to most former felons in 2018 — but felony disenfranchisement remained a significant mechanism of Black political exclusion in most states through the end of the period this textbook covers.

Community Health and Social Capital

The effects of mass incarceration on community health extended beyond the obvious consequences for incarcerated individuals to the broader social ecology of heavily affected neighborhoods. Research by public health scholars identified several mechanisms through which high incarceration rates damaged community health. The removal of large numbers of men from the sexually active population disrupted sex ratio balances in ways that research linked to higher rates of sexually transmitted infections, including HIV/AIDS. The spread of diseases contracted inside prison — particularly HIV, hepatitis C, and tuberculosis — into communities where returning prisoners had sexual and needle-sharing partners created direct public health pathways from prison to community. The stress associated with having family members incarcerated produced measurable effects on the mental and physical health of those who remained, particularly among women managing households in the shadow of partners' imprisonment.

The concept of social capital — the networks of trust, reciprocity, and collective action that enable communities to function effectively — provides another lens for understanding mass incarceration's community effects. Sociologist Robert Sampson's research on neighborhood social organization found that the "collective efficacy" of neighborhoods — the capacity of residents to exercise informal social control, cooperate around community goals, and maintain shared norms — was one of the strongest predictors of neighborhood safety, more powerful than poverty or demographic composition alone. Mass incarceration damaged collective efficacy in heavily affected neighborhoods in multiple ways: by removing adults who might otherwise contribute to community monitoring and norm enforcement, by returning people who had been exposed to prison culture and whose behavior and attitudes had been shaped by it, by creating family stress that reduced adults' capacity for community involvement, and by deepening the distrust of institutions — police, courts, social services — that is an essential feature of communities traumatized by aggressive enforcement.

The economic effects on neighborhood economies were similarly significant. The removal of workers reduced the productive labor supply of heavily incarcerated communities. The loss of income from incarcerated people damaged the local businesses that depended on their consumer spending. The collateral consequences of incarceration — the restrictions on employment and housing that attached to felony records — reduced the earnings and economic contribution of returning citizens for years after their release. Research by economist Bruce Western and colleagues estimated that the incarceration experience reduced lifetime earnings by approximately 40 percent for formerly incarcerated men, a figure that, aggregated across the millions affected, represented an enormous loss of economic capacity in the communities from which they came.

The Intergenerational Transmission of Disadvantage

Perhaps the most significant long-term community effect of mass incarceration was its contribution to the intergenerational transmission of disadvantage — the process by which the negative consequences of incarceration were passed from incarcerated parents to their children, perpetuating cycles of poverty, instability, and criminal justice involvement across generations. The research on children of incarcerated parents consistently found elevated risks of mental health problems, educational difficulties, economic hardship, and — the most politically charged finding — subsequent criminal justice involvement. Children of incarcerated parents were significantly more likely to be incarcerated themselves than children whose parents had not been incarcerated, even after controlling for other factors associated with both parental incarceration and child outcomes.

The mechanisms of this intergenerational transmission were multiple and interactive. The economic hardship and residential instability produced by parental incarceration limited children's access to quality education, stable housing, and adequate nutrition — the material conditions of healthy development. The disruption of parental attachment and care damaged the psychological development of children who needed consistent, nurturing adult relationships. The exposure to the violence, instability, and trauma associated with neighborhood criminal justice involvement — arrests, prosecutions, incarcerations of people one knew — created toxic stress that affected brain development and learning. And the normalization of criminal justice involvement — the process by which incarceration, when it affects enough people in a community, begins to seem like a normal and expected life experience rather than an exceptional consequence of extraordinary behavior — may have reduced the deterrent effect of punishment for some young people in heavily incarcerated communities.

The intergenerational effects of mass incarceration mean that its consequences extended well beyond the generation most directly affected by the policies of the 1980s and 1990s. Children who grew up in the shadows of their parents' incarceration carried the consequences of that experience into adulthood in ways that shaped the communities of the 2000s and 2010s even as the prison population began, slowly, to decline. Understanding mass incarceration as an ongoing social force whose effects continue to play out across generations, rather than as a closed historical episode, is essential for understanding the contemporary communities this textbook's later chapters will examine.

Everyday Life and Social History

Living in the Shadow of the Prison: Ordinary Life in Communities Under Mass Incarceration

In the neighborhoods most heavily affected by mass incarceration, the prison was not an abstract social problem. It was a pervasive presence in the texture of daily life, shaping routines, relationships, economic calculations, and emotional landscapes in ways that residents navigated as ordinary features of their world. Understanding what mass incarceration felt like from inside the affected communities — not as a policy variable or a statistical disparity but as a lived experience — is essential for the kind of historical understanding this textbook seeks to develop.

For a mother whose son was in prison, the daily reality might include the expense and logistics of maintaining contact — the per-minute charges that made phone calls unaffordable, the bus journey to a prison hours away, the humiliating searches before visits, the grief of watching her child age behind glass, and the anxiety about his safety in an environment she had no power to influence. She might work multiple jobs to cover the household expenses that her incarcerated son had previously helped meet, managing childcare for his children (her grandchildren) while also worrying about her own financial survival. She might attend church, where the congregation prayed for the men in prison and the families they had left behind, and where the pastor preached about forgiveness and redemption in terms that resonated with the daily reality his congregation was navigating.

For a man recently released from prison, the immediate post-release period might mean living with relatives in crowded conditions because he could not afford an apartment and because his criminal record prevented him from being added to a subsidized housing lease. He might spend days at the public library researching job opportunities, filling out applications that asked about criminal history, and wondering whether to disclose his record or risk being discovered. He might meet regularly with a parole officer who required him to report his address, employment status, and any contact with known felons — a category that included many of his oldest friends and family members. He might navigate the requirements of drug testing, community service, and other parole conditions while trying to find work that paid enough to meet those requirements and maintain basic living expenses. He might feel, as many formerly incarcerated people described, that he had exchanged one form of confinement for another — that the prison had followed him home.

For children growing up in heavily incarcerated neighborhoods, the experience included the fear and confusion of watching a father or uncle or older brother be arrested, the silence around questions that adults did not know how to answer, the shame at school that came from having an incarcerated parent, and the practical disruption of the household routines that incarceration interrupted. It might also include a particular kind of hyperawareness of police presence — the sense that uniformed officers represented danger rather than protection, that encounters with police were to be avoided, and that the arbitrary quality of enforcement meant that any adult man in the neighborhood was potentially subject to arrest. This vigilance, which researchers later described as a form of "legal socialization," shaped children's understanding of their relationship to law and authority in ways that had lasting effects on their civic development and their relationship to institutions.

None of this is to suggest that the communities most affected by mass incarceration were defined only by the prison. They were also places of resilience, creativity, community love, religious faith, neighborhood solidarity, artistic production, and the ordinary pleasures of family and friendship. The street organizations and cultural forms discussed elsewhere in this textbook coexisted with the prison system in these communities, sometimes reinforcing its effects and sometimes representing alternatives to it. But the prison was a structuring presence — a background condition that shaped the possibilities available to residents and the calculations they made about their lives — in ways that the statistics alone cannot fully convey.

Case Study

California's Prison System: Crisis, Litigation, and Contested Reform

California provides the most extensively documented and in many respects the most instructive case study of the mass incarceration era. The state's prison system grew from approximately 25,000 prisoners in 1975 to a peak of approximately 173,000 in 2006, operating at more than double the system's designed capacity. The growth was driven by a combination of the crack cocaine prosecutions of the 1980s, the three-strikes law that California voters approved in 1994 following the murder of Polly Klaas, the longest mandatory minimum sentences in the country for drug offenses, and a corrections officers' union — the California Correctional Peace Officers Association (CCPOA) — that was one of the most politically powerful in any state, spending millions of dollars in political campaigns to support tough sentencing laws and oppose reforms.

The consequences of extreme overcrowding in California's prisons were severe and eventually constitutionally unacceptable. A federal judge found in 2009, in the class action cases Plata v. Schwarzenegger and Coleman v. Brown, that the prison system's medical and mental health care was so inadequate as to constitute cruel and unusual punishment under the Eighth Amendment — that prisoners were dying of preventable diseases because the system was so overcrowded that it could not provide constitutionally adequate care. The Supreme Court upheld the lower court's finding in Brown v. Plata (2011) and ordered California to reduce its prison population to 137.5 percent of designed capacity within two years. The order required the state to transfer prisoners to county jails, release some early, and take other steps to reduce the population — a process known as "realignment" that transferred the housing of lower-level offenders from the state prison system to county jails and redirected felony probation supervision to local authorities.

California's experience with the racial organization of its prison system was also among the most extensively documented in the country. The CDCR maintained official records of prison gang validation and gang membership that were used both for security management and in civil litigation. The racial segregation of prison housing — the practice of separating newly admitted prisoners by race for their first sixty days and often informally continuing racial segregation thereafter — was challenged in Johnson v. California (2005), in which the Supreme Court held that racial segregation in prisons was subject to strict scrutiny under the Equal Protection Clause, the same demanding constitutional standard applied to segregation in other contexts. The Court's decision required California to demonstrate a compelling governmental interest sufficient to justify the racial classification — a standard that the state ultimately was unable to meet for most purposes.

The story of California's prison system in the 2010s is, in part, a story of contested and incomplete reform. The Plata realignment, the passage of Proposition 47 in 2014 (which reclassified several nonviolent felonies as misdemeanors), and the passage of Proposition 57 in 2016 (which expanded parole eligibility for nonviolent offenders and allowed prison authorities to consider good behavior in release decisions) represented significant policy shifts that reduced the state prison population substantially. But critics argued that realignment merely moved people from state prisons to county jails without addressing the underlying conditions driving incarceration, that the racial disparities in the system remained largely unchanged, and that the reform movement had been captured by political and fiscal concerns — the cost of maintaining an enormous prison system in a state with chronic budget difficulties — rather than genuine commitment to racial justice or public safety. The California case illustrates both the possibility of reform and the limits of reforms that do not address the structural economic and racial conditions that drive incarceration in the first place.

  2. The Supreme Court's decision in Brown v. Plata found that overcrowding produced unconstitutionally inadequate conditions of confinement. What does it tell us about the relationship between mass incarceration as a policy and the constitutional rights of incarcerated people that a federal court order was required to force population reduction?
  3. How did the political economy of the California prison system — the role of the corrections officers' union, the political dynamics of three-strikes ballot initiative campaigns, the rural economic interests connected to prison construction — shape the trajectory of prison expansion and the resistance to reform?
  4. What do the reforms of the 2010s in California — Plata realignment, Proposition 47, Proposition 57 — reveal about the politics of criminal justice reform? Who supported these reforms, who opposed them, and what motivated each side?

The Geography of Incarceration: Where Prisons Are Built and Where Prisoners Come From

The geographic relationship between the communities that produce incarcerated people and the locations where prisons are built reveals important features of the political economy and social impact of mass incarceration. Students should be able to identify and analyze the following geographic patterns:

  • Concentration of incarceration in urban neighborhoods: Research using prison admission records has shown that incarceration is highly concentrated geographically — specific urban neighborhoods, often those most affected by deindustrialization, redlining, and the crack epidemic, produce disproportionate numbers of prisoners. The "million-dollar blocks" research by the Justice Mapping Center mapped prison admissions by census block, revealing extreme spatial concentration in Black and Latino urban neighborhoods
  • Rural prison siting: American prisons are overwhelmingly located in rural areas, typically far from the urban communities whose residents fill them. This geographic displacement has consequences for family contact, for political representation under census rules, and for the economic interests that influence prison policy
  • Prison census geography: Under U.S. Census Bureau rules, prisoners are counted as residents of the rural counties where prisons are located rather than the urban counties where they lived before incarceration. This rule transfers political apportionment — and federal funding formulas tied to population — from urban communities of color to rural white communities that house prisons
  • State-by-state variation in incarceration rates: Incarceration rates vary enormously by state, from rates comparable to those of European countries in some northeastern states to rates that are among the highest in the world in states like Louisiana, Oklahoma, and Mississippi. This geographic variation reflects differences in law, enforcement culture, prosecution practices, and political context that demonstrate the policy-determined rather than crime-determined character of incarceration rates
  • Parole and reentry geography: Formerly incarcerated people returning to their communities are concentrated in the same urban neighborhoods from which they came, creating density effects in which many people on parole or probation live in proximity — a factor that affects both individual reentry outcomes and community social ecology
  • Private prison geography: Private prisons are concentrated in states with favorable regulatory environments, particularly in the South and Southwest, and they often house prisoners transferred from other states, further removing incarcerated people from their families and communities

The Prison Exception: The Thirteenth Amendment and Involuntary Servitude

Creator: United States Congress  ·  Date Ratified: December 6, 1865  ·  Type: Constitutional amendment

Context: The Thirteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution, ratified in 1865, formally abolished slavery and involuntary servitude throughout the United States. But it did so with a significant exception that has been the subject of legal debate and political controversy ever since, and that connects the history of mass incarceration to the long history of racial labor control examined in earlier chapters of this textbook.

"Neither slavery nor involuntary servitude, except as a punishment for crime whereof the party shall have been duly convicted, shall exist within the United States, or any place subject to their jurisdiction." — Thirteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution, 1865 (emphasis added)

Background on the exception: The "punishment for crime" exception was not a controversial addition to the amendment at the time of its drafting; it was standard language derived from the Northwest Ordinance of 1787 and was included without significant debate. Its drafters likely did not anticipate that it would be used to create a system of racially targeted criminal justice that would effectively continue slavery through the criminal law — though historians have noted that the Black Codes and convict leasing that emerged almost immediately after ratification suggest that some southern politicians may have had precisely this in mind.

The convict leasing system: As discussed in Chapter 1 of this textbook, the "punishment for crime" exception was immediately exploited by southern states through the convict leasing system, which imprisoned Black people under broadly written vagrancy and labor contract laws and then leased their labor to private businesses. Convict leasing reproduced the material conditions of forced labor within the formal framework of the criminal justice system, demonstrating that the abolition of slavery as a legal status did not preclude the continuation of coerced labor when that coercion was mediated through the criminal law.

Contemporary relevance: The Thirteenth Amendment exception has been cited by prison reform advocates and scholars as the constitutional foundation for prison labor practices that pay incarcerated workers nominal or zero wages for labor that generates value for states, for private prison operators, and for corporations that contract for prison labor. Contemporary prison labor programs — in which incarcerated people produce goods and services for state agencies or private contractors at wages of pennies per hour — are legally permitted under the Thirteenth Amendment's punishment exception. Critics argue that these programs constitute a continuation of the exploitative labor practices the amendment was supposed to abolish.

  4. If the "punishment for crime" exception were removed from the Thirteenth Amendment by a new constitutional amendment, what practical consequences would that have? What does your answer tell you about the role that the exception plays in the current prison system?

Historical Debate

Did Mass Incarceration Reduce Crime? Competing Interpretations of the Incarceration-Crime Relationship

The question: One of the most consequential and most contested empirical questions in criminal justice research concerns the relationship between the dramatic expansion of incarceration from the 1970s through the 2000s and the equally dramatic fall in crime rates that occurred beginning in the early 1990s. If mass incarceration substantially caused the crime decline, that would constitute a significant argument in its favor as a social policy, even one with serious costs and injustices. If the crime decline was caused primarily by other factors, that would suggest that mass incarceration was an enormous expenditure of public resources, an enormous infliction of human suffering, and an enormous imposition of racial inequality with little commensurate public safety benefit. The question matters greatly for evaluating the policy and for thinking about alternatives.

Interpretation A — Mass incarceration significantly reduced crime (the incapacitation argument): The simplest argument for a causal relationship between rising incarceration and falling crime rates is the incapacitation argument: people who are in prison cannot commit crimes against the community (setting aside crimes within the prison itself). As the prison population grew by more than a million people between 1980 and 2000, a large number of individuals who would otherwise have been at risk of committing crimes were removed from communities. Criminologists who have attempted to estimate the magnitude of this effect — including William Spelman and others — have generally found that incapacitation explains some portion of the crime decline, with estimates ranging from roughly 10 to 25 percent of the decline. Proponents of this interpretation also cite the deterrent effect of longer and more certain sentences: if would-be offenders know that they are more likely to be caught and will face longer sentences if caught, rational-choice models of crime suggest they will be less likely to commit offenses.
Interpretation B — The crime decline had other primary causes; mass incarceration's contribution was modest (the over-incarceration argument): Critics of the incapacitation argument point to several lines of evidence that suggest incarceration was not the primary driver of the crime decline. First, the crime decline that began in the early 1990s was nearly universal across comparable democracies, many of which did not significantly expand their prison populations — suggesting that the common factors driving the decline (changes in demographics, consumer markets for illegal drugs, policing strategies, and — most controversially — the lingering effects of the legalization of abortion in 1973, as argued by Donohue and Levitt) were more important than incarceration. Second, the relationship between incarceration rates and crime rates shows diminishing returns: the first million prisoners had more impact on crime than the second million, as the most dangerous offenders were incarcerated early and subsequent expansion increasingly captured lower-risk offenders. Third, the communities most heavily incarcerated did not necessarily experience the greatest crime reductions, suggesting that concentrated incarceration may have undermined the community structures that sustain safety even as it removed some individual offenders.
Interpretation C — The incarceration-crime relationship was real but the policy was still unjustifiable: A third analytical position accepts that mass incarceration contributed to the crime decline while insisting that this contribution does not justify the policy on balance. This argument notes that even accepting the most optimistic estimates of incarceration's crime-reduction effects, the marginal cost of each unit of crime reduction achieved through incarceration grew enormously as the prison population expanded — at the peak, billions of dollars and enormous human suffering were being expended to achieve modest reductions in crime that could potentially have been achieved at lower cost and with less racial harm through alternative policies including community policing, violence interruption programs, drug treatment, education, and economic investment in high-crime neighborhoods. The existence of an effect does not justify the magnitude of the response; aspirin reduces fever, but that does not mean that prescribing two hundred aspirin is appropriate treatment.

Why it matters: The incarceration-crime debate is not merely academic. The answer to the question "did it work?" shapes the politics of criminal justice reform: if mass incarceration substantially reduced crime, arguments for reducing it must contend with questions about public safety; if it did not, the case for reform is significantly stronger. The debate also illustrates important principles of causal inference — the difficulty of attributing changes in complex social phenomena to single policy variables — that are relevant across many areas of historical and policy analysis.

Student task: Review the evidence presented for each interpretation and identify the strongest argument and the most significant limitation of each. What additional data would you want in order to evaluate these interpretations more rigorously? Which interpretation do you find most persuasive, and why?

Multiple Perspectives

Who Experiences the Prison System: Multiple Perspectives on Mass Incarceration

From the perspective of incarcerated people: For the person inside, the prison was a world of enforced idleness, physical danger, racial organization, and psychological pressure — a place where survival required constant vigilance, strategic affiliation, and the management of relationships with both officers and other prisoners. The experience of incarceration varied enormously by facility, by sentence length, by racial and gang dynamics, and by the individual's resources and support network. Many incarcerated people described the loss of meaningful time — years of their lives removed from the world of relationships, work, and growth — as the most devastating dimension of the experience. The knowledge that their families were suffering in their absence, that their children were growing up without them, that their partners were managing alone, added layers of guilt and grief to the physical privations of confinement.
From the perspective of families of incarcerated people: For families — particularly for the partners and mothers who managed households in the absence of incarcerated men — the prison system was a constant presence that organized time, money, and emotional life. The phone call at a price that could consume a week's grocery budget. The bus journey to a facility three hours away for a visit that lasted one hour. The explaining to children why their father was not there, and the decisions about what age-appropriate truth to tell. The navigation of parole requirements, the anxiety about violations, the hope and fear of the release date. Research found that incarceration of a family member produced measurable health effects on those who remained — elevated rates of depression, anxiety, and physical illness — and that families often experienced a kind of "secondary prisonization," a restriction and reorganization of their own freedom in response to the incarceration of a loved one.
From the perspective of corrections officers: Corrections officers worked in facilities that were chronically understaffed, overcrowded, and underresourced, managing populations that had been concentrated from the most challenged and most challenging elements of society. Officers described working conditions that were physically dangerous, psychologically stressful, and largely invisible to the public that demanded tough sentencing without investing in adequate management of its consequences. The corrections officer's union was simultaneously a labor organization defending working conditions and a political force supporting the very sentencing laws that created the overcrowding that made those working conditions so difficult. The tension between these roles — between the officer as worker exploited by a system and the officer as institutional agent of that system — was not always acknowledged in policy debates.
From the perspective of crime victims and victim advocates: Victims of violent crime and their advocates brought a perspective on mass incarceration that emphasized the genuine harm done by the violence that led to incarceration and the legitimate interest of survivors in justice, safety, and accountability. Many victim advocates supported harsh sentencing as a means of ensuring that offenders could not harm others during the period of their incarceration and as a form of symbolic acknowledgment of the seriousness of the harm done. Other victim advocates, particularly those associated with restorative justice movements, argued that the punitive system focused so intensely on punishment that it neglected the actual needs of victims — for healing, for restitution, for community support — and that the cycle of violence produced by mass incarceration ultimately created more victims rather than fewer.
From the perspective of communities affected by concentrated incarceration: Residents of heavily incarcerated urban neighborhoods experienced the prison system as both a response to and a cause of community problems. The removal of violent individuals through incarceration could make specific blocks safer in the short term; the removal of non-violent drug offenders, fathers, and community members produced social damage that made those blocks less safe over time. Community perspectives on incarceration were consequently complex and often internally divided — between those who supported aggressive enforcement as the only available response to violence and those who understood incarceration as a destroyer of the community bonds that sustained safety. This division was itself a feature of the political geography of mass incarceration, placing communities simultaneously in the role of victim, perpetrator, and consequence of the system.
Cause and Effect

The Prison Expansion: Causes, Development, and Long-Term Consequences

Long-Term Causes

The racial ideology of white supremacy and the long history of using the criminal law as a tool of racial labor control, from the Black Codes through convict leasing and Jim Crow; the economic model of racial capitalism that generated concentrated Black poverty in urban neighborhoods through exclusion from property wealth, skilled employment, and political power; the deindustrialization of urban economies that removed legitimate employment and expanded the informal economy; the crack cocaine epidemic that created both street violence and a political emergency around Black urban disorder.

Political and Policy Causes

The "law and order" politics inaugurated by Nixon and systematically developed by the Republican Party; the Democratic Party's competitive response through the "tough on crime" positioning of the Clinton administration; the mandatory minimum sentencing laws beginning with the Rockefeller Drug Laws in 1973 and expanding through the 1986 and 1988 federal drug laws; the three-strikes laws; the truth-in-sentencing requirements; the elimination of parole discretion; and the enormous investment in police and prosecution infrastructure at the expense of education, social services, and economic development.

Development of the System

From 196,000 prisoners in 1972 to 2.3 million in 2008, the expansion occurred in waves driven by successive legislative and enforcement initiatives. The early wave was driven by the general tough-on-crime turn of the 1970s; the middle wave by the crack epidemic and the War on Drugs of the 1980s and early 1990s; the late wave by three-strikes laws, truth-in-sentencing requirements, and the continuation of drug enforcement as crime rates fell. The racial dimensions of the expansion intensified over time as enforcement priorities focused on Black urban neighborhoods.

Long-Term Consequences

The disruption of Black family structures on a massive scale; the disenfranchisement of millions of Black citizens; the development of prison gangs and their feedback effects on street organization; the creation of an enormous barrier to economic participation for the formerly incarcerated; the intergenerational transmission of disadvantage through the children of incarcerated parents; the political economy of the prison-industrial complex; the normalization of surveillance, criminalization, and incarceration as the primary public policy response to urban poverty; and the deepening of the racial inequalities that were the original conditions for mass incarceration.

Historical complexity: The expansion of mass incarceration cannot be reduced to a simple partisan story or a simple racial story, though both political dynamics and racial ideology were central to it. Democrats as well as Republicans supported harsh sentencing laws; the bipartisan character of the "tough on crime" consensus of the 1980s and 1990s reflected genuine public anxiety about crime that was not manufactured but was also not the only possible political response to it. Understanding why specific policy choices were made, rather than assuming they were inevitable, is essential for assessing both the history and the possibilities for change.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Punishment in American History: Continuity and Change from Convict Leasing to Mass Incarceration

What Changed

  • The formal legal mechanism: slavery gave way to convict leasing; convict leasing gave way to mass incarceration. Each transition maintained racial labor control through a new legal form adapted to the current constitutional constraints
  • The scale: mass incarceration was far larger than convict leasing or any previous American penal system, reaching into communities throughout the country rather than being primarily concentrated in the rural South
  • The geography: the shift from rural southern plantation to urban northern neighborhood and back to rural prison reflected the demographic transformations of the Great Migration and the return migration of incarcerated urban residents to rural prison locations
  • The stated rationale: convict leasing was explicitly about labor extraction; mass incarceration was publicly justified in terms of public safety and individual punishment rather than economic exploitation, even as prison labor remained a feature of the system
  • The political context: mass incarceration operated in a formal democracy, requiring political support through elections, and was sustained by a politics of racial resentment and fear rather than the naked white supremacy of the Jim Crow era

What Stayed the Same

  • The racial targeting: from the Black Codes through convict leasing through the War on Drugs, the enforcement of criminal law in ways that disproportionately criminalized and punished Black people has been a consistent feature of American criminal justice
  • The use of criminal law as a tool of racial control: the specific legal mechanism has changed, but the function — using the criminal justice system to manage, constrain, and subordinate a Black population that threatens the racial hierarchy — has persisted
  • The disenfranchisement function: both Jim Crow disenfranchisement and felony disenfranchisement removed Black political power at critical moments; the mechanism changed but the effect was consistent
  • The labor extraction dimension: from the unpaid field labor of convict leasing to the nominally compensated institutional labor of mass incarceration, the use of incarcerated people's labor under conditions of coercion has been a persistent feature of American punishment
  • The community destruction function: whether through the burning of Black churches during Reconstruction, the economic suffocation of Jim Crow, or the mass removal of Black men from urban neighborhoods during mass incarceration, the systematic disruption of Black community organization has been a recurring consequence of racial criminal justice

Why the comparison matters and where it reaches its limits: The comparison between mass incarceration and earlier forms of racial labor control and political suppression is analytically powerful and historically grounded. But it is also subject to important limitations. Mass incarceration differs from slavery and Jim Crow in its formal racial neutrality — the laws do not explicitly target Black people by name — and in the genuine (if partial) agency that formally free people exercise in the choices that lead to incarceration. These differences matter morally and analytically, even as the structural consequences for Black communities look remarkably similar. Recognizing both the continuities and the differences is the mark of sophisticated historical thinking about this comparison.

Constitutional Connection

Rights and the Carceral State: Constitutional Questions in the Age of Mass Incarceration

The mass incarceration era generated a substantial body of constitutional law concerning the rights of incarcerated people and the constitutional limits on the state's power to punish. The Eighth Amendment's prohibition of "cruel and unusual punishment" and the Fourteenth Amendment's guarantee of due process and equal protection were the primary constitutional tools through which courts engaged with the conditions and practices of the expanding prison system, though their application was often limited and contested.

The Eighth Amendment and prison conditions: A series of Supreme Court decisions from the 1970s onward established that the Eighth Amendment's prohibition of cruel and unusual punishment applied to the conditions of confinement as well as to the formal sentence imposed. Estelle v. Gamble (1976) held that deliberate indifference to prisoners' serious medical needs constituted cruel and unusual punishment. Rhodes v. Chapman (1981) addressed double-celling and held that double-celling was not per se unconstitutional, but subsequent cases established that extreme overcrowding that produced inadequate medical and mental health care could cross the constitutional line. The culminating case was Brown v. Plata (2011), in which the Court upheld a lower court order requiring California to reduce its prison population because overcrowding had produced medical and mental health conditions that the Court found constituted cruel and unusual punishment.

The Fourteenth Amendment and racial disparities: The constitutional challenge to racially disparate outcomes in the criminal justice system has been significantly limited by the Supreme Court's holding in McCleskey v. Kemp (1987) that statistical evidence of racial disparity in the administration of the death penalty was insufficient to establish an equal protection violation in an individual case — that a defendant had to show purposeful discrimination directed at him specifically, rather than a pattern of discrimination across the system. This ruling effectively foreclosed the most direct constitutional challenge to the racially disparate outcomes of mass incarceration, since structural statistical disparities, however extreme, could not by themselves establish the individual intentional discrimination that the Court required.

Felony disenfranchisement and political rights: The constitutional status of felony disenfranchisement was addressed by the Supreme Court in Richardson v. Ramirez (1974), which held that the Fourteenth Amendment's Section 2 — which referenced the right to vote being "abridged for participation in rebellion, or other crime" — provided textual support for felon disenfranchisement, essentially shielding it from equal protection challenge. This holding has been criticized by constitutional scholars who argue that it misread the original meaning of Section 2, but it has not been overturned. State-level reforms, rather than federal constitutional adjudication, have been the primary mechanism for reducing felony disenfranchisement, with Florida's 2018 ballot initiative as the most significant recent example.

The constitutional boundaries of reentry: The collateral consequences of conviction — the restrictions on employment, housing, education, and public benefits discussed in this chapter — raise constitutional questions that courts have generally been reluctant to engage. The Supreme Court has held that collateral consequences must sometimes be disclosed to defendants during the plea bargaining process (particularly immigration consequences, in Padilla v. Kentucky, 2010), but it has not established a constitutional right to be free from collateral consequences more broadly. The constitutional framework for addressing the legal disabilities of conviction has remained largely underdeveloped, leaving the reform of collateral consequences to legislative processes rather than judicial intervention.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has examined mass incarceration as one of the defining social and political transformations of late twentieth-century American life — a transformation that drew its most direct consequences from and imposed its heaviest burdens on the Black urban communities that this textbook has been following since the Great Migration. The prison population grew from approximately 196,000 in 1972 to 2.3 million in 2008, a more than tenfold increase that made the United States the world's leading incarcerator by every measure. This expansion was not a natural or inevitable response to rising crime. It was the product of specific legislative choices — mandatory minimum sentencing laws, three-strikes provisions, truth-in-sentencing requirements, the crack cocaine disparity — that were made in a political context organized by racial anxiety, "law and order" political competition, and the abandonment of rehabilitation as the organizing principle of prison policy.

The racial dimensions of this expansion were stark and systematic. Black people, constituting approximately 12 percent of the U.S. population, accounted for approximately 44 percent of the prison population by 2000. The Black male incarceration rate was nearly seven times the white male rate. These disparities cannot be fully explained by differences in criminal behavior; they reflect the accumulated racial biases of a criminal justice system in which Black people are arrested more often, charged more harshly, detained pretrial more frequently, and sentenced more severely than white people charged with equivalent offenses. The crack cocaine sentencing disparity was the most glaring single example of racially disparate enforcement, but it operated throughout a system marked by racial inequality at every decision point.

The ideological shift from rehabilitation to retribution, driven by a combination of political conservatism, academic skepticism about rehabilitation's efficacy, and the specific political dynamics of the tough-on-crime era, dismantled the program and treatment infrastructure of American prisons and replaced it with a purely custodial approach that stored people rather than preparing them for reintegration. The consequences of this shift were predictable and were predicted: recidivism rates remained high, prison populations grew, and the investment in incarceration crowded out the investments in education, employment, and social services that research consistently identified as more effective long-term crime-reduction strategies.

Inside the prison, the social dynamics of street organizations were reproduced in concentrated and intensified form. Prison gangs developed organizational structures, economic activities, and racial identities that filtered back into urban neighborhoods through the constant movement of people between prison and community. The Sureño/Norteño division in California — the most extensively documented example of how prison gang structure can reshape street gang geography — illustrated how the prison environment could create organizational forms that had no precise pre-prison equivalent and that then structured conflict and identity on the outside. The racial organization of prison life deepened racial antagonisms, extended gang identities across geographic boundaries, and created new forms of organizational loyalty that complicated the already complex landscape of street organization in American cities.

The effects on families and communities were among the most significant and least visible consequences of mass incarceration. By 2008, approximately 1.5 million children in the United States had a parent in prison on any given day, with the concentration of this effect in Black communities meaning that approximately one in nine Black children had an incarcerated parent. The economic hardship, residential instability, developmental disruption, and emotional trauma associated with parental incarceration produced measurable negative effects on children's outcomes that persisted into adulthood, contributing to the intergenerational transmission of disadvantage that made mass incarceration's consequences lasting far beyond the period of the sentences themselves.

The reentry crisis — the persistent failure of the prison system to prepare people for successful reintegration and the persistent barriers that the surrounding society erected against it — was a predictable consequence of a system oriented toward punishment rather than rehabilitation. The felony record that followed people out of prison restricted their access to employment, housing, education, and public benefits in ways that made the path to stable law-abiding community life extraordinarily difficult. The technical violation machinery of parole supervision returned large numbers of people to prison not for new crimes but for the predictable failures of people with limited resources, limited support, and enormous obstacles — a revolving door that kept the prison population stable even as crime rates declined.

The long-term community effects of mass incarceration operated through multiple channels: the political silencing of disenfranchised communities, the economic damage of removing workers and returning people with restricted economic opportunities, the public health consequences of spreading prison-acquired diseases and the stress of living under mass incarceration pressure, the damage to social capital and collective efficacy, and the normalization of incarceration as a life experience in communities where one in three young men could expect to be imprisoned at some point. These effects accumulated over decades in the same communities already damaged by deindustrialization, segregation, and the crack epidemic, deepening disadvantages that were already extreme.

California's prison system illustrated the dynamics of the mass incarceration era at a scale that made its contradictions impossible to ignore. The state's prison population grew to more than twice its designed capacity, producing conditions that the Supreme Court found constitutionally unacceptable. The political economy of California's prison system — the corrections officer union's political power, the rural economic interests in prison construction, the electoral dynamics of three-strikes ballot initiatives — demonstrated the structural forces that sustained expansion even when its harms were clear. The reform process initiated by court order and continued through ballot initiatives illustrated both the possibility of significant change and the political limitations that constrain it.

The bipartisan criminal justice reform movement that emerged in the 2010s — driven by fiscal concerns as much as by commitments to racial justice — began to modestly reduce prison populations and to acknowledge, at least rhetorically, that the mass incarceration experiment had failed on its own terms. But the structural changes required to meaningfully address the racial and economic conditions that produced mass incarceration — the redistribution of economic resources to deindustrialized communities, the transformation of a criminal justice system marked by racial inequality at every decision point, the investment in the social services and supports that reduce crime more effectively than incarceration — remained politically contested and practically unfulfilled. The chapter ends not with resolution but with the recognition that the consequences of mass incarceration — in the lives of the incarcerated, in the families they left, in the communities most affected — are still being felt in the communities this textbook's later chapters will examine.

For this textbook's central subject — the history of Black street organizations — mass incarceration represents both a cause and a consequence of the organizational transformations examined in subsequent chapters. The removal of large numbers of street organization members and leadership figures through incarceration disrupted the larger, more organized formations of the 1970s and early 1980s. The prison environment created and reinforced organizational identities that filtered back into neighborhoods through returning prisoners. The collateral consequences of conviction reduced the legitimate economic options of formerly incarcerated members in ways that increased the relative attractiveness of street organization economic activity. And the long-term damage to family structure, community social capital, and neighborhood institutions weakened the alternative organizational structures — churches, mutual aid networks, community organizations — that had historically served as both competitors to and moderating influences on street organizations. Understanding this dynamic is essential for understanding the fragmentation and transformation of Black street organizations in the contemporary city, which is the subject of the chapters that follow.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Ban the Box
A policy reform that requires employers to remove the criminal history question from initial job applications, delaying background checks until later in the hiring process. Adopted in more than thirty states and by the federal government by 2020, Ban the Box policies were intended to reduce employment discrimination against people with criminal records.
Carceral state
The network of institutions, laws, practices, and cultural assumptions through which the American state exercises control over its population through criminalization, incarceration, supervision, and the threat of punishment. The carceral state is not simply the prison system but the broader system of policing, prosecution, supervision, and legal disability that organizes social control in the United States.
Collateral consequences
Legal penalties and disabilities that attach to a criminal conviction beyond the formal sentence of imprisonment, fines, and supervision. Collateral consequences include restrictions on employment and professional licensing, exclusions from public housing and federal benefits, restrictions on immigration status, and felony disenfranchisement. A 2010 inventory identified more than 38,000 such consequences in federal and state law.
Collective efficacy
The sociological concept, developed by Robert Sampson and colleagues, referring to a neighborhood's capacity for informal social control and cooperative action — the degree to which residents share expectations about appropriate behavior, are willing to intervene to maintain those expectations, and trust each other to do so. Research found collective efficacy to be one of the strongest predictors of neighborhood safety, and mass incarceration was found to reduce it.
Determinate sentencing
A sentencing system in which prison terms are fixed by law according to the offense committed, rather than being determined at the discretion of a judge or parole board based on the characteristics of the individual offender. Determinate sentencing replaced the indeterminate sentencing systems of the mid-twentieth century beginning in the 1970s and was a primary driver of prison expansion.
Felony disenfranchisement
The legal removal of voting rights from people convicted of felonies. By 2004, approximately 5.3 million Americans were disenfranchised because of felony convictions, with the effect falling disproportionately on Black men. In some states, disenfranchisement was permanent even after completion of the sentence; in others, voting rights were restored automatically upon release or completion of parole.
Incapacitation
One of the four traditional justifications for criminal punishment, referring to the prevention of crime through the physical removal of offenders from the community during their incarceration. The incapacitation argument held that expanding the prison population would reduce crime by confining people who would otherwise commit crimes; critics argued that its crime-reduction effects were limited and diminishing as the population expanded.
Indeterminate sentencing
A sentencing system in which the judge imposes a sentence with a minimum and maximum range, and the actual release date is determined by a parole board based on the prisoner's behavior and rehabilitation progress. Indeterminate sentencing was the dominant model in American prisons through the 1960s and was replaced by determinate sentencing beginning in the 1970s.
Mandatory minimum sentence
A legislatively prescribed minimum prison term that must be imposed for certain offenses regardless of the judge's assessment of the individual circumstances. Mandatory minimums for drug offenses were a primary driver of federal prison expansion beginning with the Rockefeller Drug Laws in 1973 and accelerating with federal legislation in 1986 and 1988.
Mass incarceration
The dramatic and historically unprecedented increase in the rate of American imprisonment from the early 1970s through the 2000s, producing the world's largest prison population in both absolute and proportional terms. The term implies that the expansion exceeded what could be explained as a rational response to crime and reflected political choices whose racial and social consequences were predictable and severe.
The "Martinson Report"
The 1974 survey article by Robert Martinson summarizing research on prison rehabilitation programs, widely interpreted as concluding that "nothing works" in reducing recidivism. The report's conclusions were politically exploited to justify abandoning rehabilitation programs, though Martinson himself later qualified his findings and subsequent research substantially challenged the "nothing works" interpretation.
Parole
The supervised release of a prisoner before the completion of a sentence, subject to conditions that the parolee must meet or risk return to prison. Parole boards historically had broad discretion in determining release dates under indeterminate sentencing systems; the shift to determinate sentencing and the adoption of "truth in sentencing" laws in the 1990s substantially reduced parole release and required prisoners to serve most of their sentences.
Prison gang
An organized group within a prison that provides members with protection, economic access, and social belonging in exchange for loyalty and compliance with the group's rules and hierarchies. Prison gangs developed organizational structures, economic activities, and racial identities that often filtered back into urban neighborhoods through returning prisoners.
Prison-industrial complex
The network of economic and political interests — including corrections officer unions, private prison companies, construction firms, and other contractors — that benefit financially from high incarceration rates and use their resources to support the sentencing laws and enforcement priorities that maintain those rates. The term draws an analogy to Eisenhower's "military-industrial complex" to describe the entanglement of economic interest and public policy in the prison system.
Probation
A criminal sentence that allows a convicted person to remain in the community under supervision, subject to conditions that if violated can result in incarceration. The United States maintained approximately 3.9 million people on probation in 2008, substantially more than the prison and jail population, making probation a major dimension of the carceral state that often received less public attention than incarceration itself.
Recidivism
The rate at which formerly incarcerated people return to prison, either for new offenses or for technical violations of their parole or probation conditions. Studies consistently found that more than two-thirds of released prisoners were rearrested within three years and more than half returned to prison in that period, a figure that critics attributed primarily to the inadequacy of reentry support and the structural barriers to successful reintegration rather than to individual criminal propensity.
Reentry
The process by which people released from incarceration attempt to reintegrate into their communities. Successful reentry requires stable employment, housing, and social connections, all of which are obstructed by the collateral consequences of conviction and the practical deprivations of incarceration. Supportive reentry services have been shown to significantly reduce recidivism; they have historically been underfunded relative to the scale of the reentry population.
Rehabilitation
The penal philosophy that holds that the primary purpose of incarceration should be the reform and preparation of the prisoner for law-abiding community life, through education, vocational training, therapeutic intervention, and other programming. The rehabilitative ideal dominated American prison policy through the mid-twentieth century and was systematically dismantled in the 1970s and 1980s.
Retribution
The penal philosophy that holds that punishment is justified as a just response to wrongdoing — that offenders deserve to suffer in proportion to the harm they have caused, independent of any consequentialist calculation about deterrence, rehabilitation, or incapacitation. The shift toward retributive penal philosophy in the 1970s and 1980s supported longer, more certain sentences and reduced the political legitimacy of rehabilitation programs.
Solitary confinement
The incarceration of a prisoner in isolation, typically in a small cell with minimal human contact and minimal programming, for twenty-two to twenty-four hours per day. Research consistently found severe psychological effects from prolonged solitary confinement; its use was challenged in court as cruel and unusual punishment, and California's hunger strikes of 2011 and 2013 brought national attention to the conditions of Security Housing Units.
Three-strikes law
Legislation requiring mandatory life sentences for individuals convicted of a third felony offense, regardless of the nature of the third offense. California adopted one of the most expansive three-strikes laws in 1994; by 2013, more than 4,000 people in California were serving life sentences under three-strikes for third offenses that were nonviolent. Three-strikes laws were a major driver of prison population growth in the states that adopted them.
Truth in sentencing
Requirements, incentivized by federal legislation in 1994, that prisoners serve at least 85 percent of their imposed sentences before release. Truth-in-sentencing laws reduced parole release and increased average time served, contributing significantly to prison population growth in the states that adopted them in exchange for federal prison construction funds.

Major People and Places

Identification & Significance

Michelle Alexander
Legal scholar and author of The New Jim Crow (2010), the most influential public intellectual argument that mass incarceration functions as a system of racial control analogous to Jim Crow — cycling Black men through prisons and then imposing collateral consequences that reproduce their second-class citizenship. Alexander's work helped catalyze the bipartisan criminal justice reform movement of the 2010s and is credited with shifting public discourse on the racial dimensions of incarceration.
Bryan Stevenson
Civil rights lawyer, founder of the Equal Justice Initiative, and author of Just Mercy, whose advocacy on behalf of people sentenced to death and excessive prison terms exposed the systematic racial injustices of the American criminal justice system and demonstrated in concrete human terms the consequences of mandatory sentencing and racial bias in prosecution and adjudication.
George Jackson
Black prisoner in California whose political writings from San Quentin and Soledad prisons made him a national figure in the Black Power movement and whose death in 1971 in a contested prison incident intensified the political crisis around American prison conditions. Jackson's founding of the Black Guerrilla Family and his writings on the relationship between racial capitalism, the carceral state, and Black liberation connected the prison experience to the broader political struggles of the era.
Nelson Rockefeller
New York governor whose 1973 drug laws initiated the mandatory minimum sentencing movement that drove prison expansion, and whose violent order to retake Attica prison in 1971 defined the political response to prisoner resistance for a generation. Rockefeller's drug laws bore his name because he was their most prominent advocate, but they became a national model followed in state after state through the 1970s and 1980s.
Robert Martinson
Sociologist whose 1974 "nothing works" article gave intellectual cover to the abandonment of rehabilitation programs in American prisons, regardless of the actual complexity and qualification of his findings. Martinson's case illustrates how research can be politically weaponized — the "nothing works" conclusion was a simplification of a complex literature that was seized upon by politicians who had already decided to abandon rehabilitation for other reasons.
Attica Correctional Facility, New York
The site of the 1971 prisoner uprising that became the defining symbol of the conflict between prison conditions and prisoner resistance in the mass incarceration era. The violent retaking of Attica by state police under Governor Rockefeller's orders killed thirty-three prisoners and ten guards and civilian hostages and foreclosed, for a generation, the political opening for prison reform that the uprising had created.
Pelican Bay State Prison, California
The California maximum security facility that became the symbol of solitary confinement's harms through its Security Housing Unit, which held prisoners in near-complete isolation for years or decades. Pelican Bay's prisoners organized major hunger strikes in 2011 and 2013 to protest indefinite solitary confinement, bringing national attention to conditions that had been largely invisible to the public.
The California Correctional Peace Officers Association (CCPOA)
The California corrections officers' union, one of the most politically powerful public sector unions in the state, whose campaign contributions supported tough sentencing laws and whose political influence helped sustain California's prison expansion even as its costs became apparent. The CCPOA's role in California prison politics became a national example of how economic interests in the prison system could shape criminal justice policy.
The Sentencing Project
Washington, D.C.-based research and advocacy organization that produced the foundational data on racial disparities in the American criminal justice system, providing the empirical foundation for much of the public argument about mass incarceration's racial character. The Sentencing Project's reports on felony disenfranchisement, racial disparities in sentencing, and comparative incarceration rates were cited extensively in reform advocacy and scholarship.
Los Angeles and Chicago
The two cities whose street organizations were most extensively affected by mass incarceration, both as sources of incarcerated people and as destinations for returning prisoners. The organizational transformation of street organizations in both cities was shaped in significant part by the filtering effects of the prison system — the development of prison organizational structures, the return of incarcerated members with prison gang affiliations, and the structural weakening of larger organizations through the incarceration of leadership figures.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter has told the story of mass incarceration — one of the most significant and most damaging social policy experiments in American history — as part of the longer story of Black urban life that this textbook has been tracing from the Great Migration forward. The prison expansion of the late twentieth century did not happen to an abstraction. It happened to the communities this textbook has been following: the neighborhoods of Chicago's South and West Sides, Los Angeles's South Central and Watts, Detroit's East Side, Philadelphia's North Philadelphia, Baltimore's Sandtown. It happened to the descendants of the people who came north in the Great Migration seeking opportunity and found segregated housing, redlined neighborhoods, underfunded schools, and, eventually, deindustrialized economies. It happened to families who had already survived the crack epidemic and the violence it brought. And its consequences are still playing out in those communities today, in the form of broken family structures, disenfranchised voters, men with felony records unable to find stable work, and children carrying the psychological and economic weight of their parents' incarceration.

The chapter has insisted throughout that mass incarceration was not an inevitable response to crime. It was a set of choices — legislative, judicial, prosecutorial, and budgetary — made in a specific political context organized by racial anxiety, political competition over the "tough on crime" mantle, and the abandonment of the rehabilitative ideal that had given the earlier prison system at least the aspiration of something other than pure punishment. Those choices had predictable consequences — the research predicting them existed at the time — and the people who would bear those consequences had little political voice in the decisions being made. The story of mass incarceration is, in this sense, a story about who counts in American democracy and whose suffering is visible to the policymakers and voters who make the choices that shape the lives of the least powerful.

For the history of Black street organizations that is this textbook's central subject, mass incarceration was a transformative force. It disrupted the larger, more organized street formations that had characterized Black urban gang life in the 1970s and early 1980s. It sent generations of organizational leadership through prison environments that reshaped their organizational affiliations, their relationship to racial identity, and their conception of the world they would return to. It returned people to communities with restricted options, deepened disadvantages, and the stigma of conviction — conditions that increased the relative attraction of the only organizational forms that did not require a background check. And it deepened the structural conditions of concentrated poverty, family instability, and institutional distrust that had given rise to street organizations in the first place. The fragmentation of street organizations that the final chapters of this textbook will examine — the decline of larger hierarchical formations and the rise of smaller, more volatile, less structured crews and cliques — cannot be understood without understanding what mass incarceration did to the people, families, and communities from which those organizations drew.

The chapter closes with a question that hangs over the contemporary city and the communities this textbook has been describing: what would it mean to genuinely reckon with the consequences of mass incarceration? Not merely to reduce prison populations at the margins — though that matters — but to address the structural damage done to families, communities, political representation, and economic life by three decades of concentrated incarceration in specific urban neighborhoods. The bipartisan reform movements of the 2010s offer evidence that the political consensus around mass incarceration can shift; they offer far less evidence that the political will exists to address the structural conditions that produced it. The history this chapter has told — the history of how a specific set of political choices produced specific and predictable harms for specific communities — is the necessary foundation for any serious engagement with what comes next.

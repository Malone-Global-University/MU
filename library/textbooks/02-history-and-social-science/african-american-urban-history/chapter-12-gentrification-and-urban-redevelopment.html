Gentrification and Urban Redevelopment

From Migration to Fragmentation: The Urban History of Black Street Organizations

Chapter Opening

"They tore down everything we built and called it progress. The people who built this neighborhood weren't gone — they were pushed out." — longtime resident, Shaw neighborhood, Washington, D.C., oral history interview, 2008

In 2000, a two-bedroom row house in the Shaw neighborhood of Washington, D.C. — a historically Black community less than a mile north of the U.S. Capitol — could be purchased for around $120,000. By 2010, the same house often sold for over $400,000. By 2020, comparable properties in Shaw regularly commanded $900,000 or more. In twenty years, property values in one of the city's oldest Black neighborhoods had increased by nearly eightfold. For the long-term homeowners who had stayed through decades of disinvestment, drug markets, and violence, this appreciation represented enormous paper wealth. For the renters — historically the majority of residents in neighborhoods like Shaw — it represented something else entirely: the slow, inexorable economic pressure that eventually forced them out of the only communities they had ever known.

The transformation of Shaw was not an isolated event. It was part of one of the most consequential urban processes of the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries: the large-scale reinvestment in historically Black urban neighborhoods that had spent decades being systematically excluded from capital, services, and political attention. That reinvestment — welcomed by some, devastating for others, and deeply contested by nearly everyone — is what this chapter calls gentrification. The word is contested, the process is complex, and its effects are unevenly distributed. But its impact on Black urban communities, on neighborhood institutions, on street organizations, on family stability, and on the long-term geography of racial inequality in American cities is among the most significant urban transformations of our time.

To understand gentrification's significance, it is essential to understand what preceded it. The neighborhoods that are being gentrified today were not always poor, disinvested, and distressed. Most of them were, at some point in their histories, vibrant working-class and middle-class Black communities — communities that had been created by the Great Migration, sustained through the Jim Crow era, and then systematically hollowed out by the combination of forces that earlier chapters of this textbook have traced: redlining and housing discrimination, deindustrialization and unemployment, the crack cocaine epidemic, the War on Drugs and mass incarceration, and decades of deliberate disinvestment in Black neighborhoods by both public policy and private capital. The poverty and distress that gentrifiers encounter when they arrive in these neighborhoods is not a natural condition. It is a product of specific policies and decisions, most of them traceable to the racial exclusion and structural inequality documented throughout this textbook.

The story of urban renewal — the federal program that preceded and in many ways anticipated modern gentrification — begins in the 1940s and accelerates through the 1950s and 1960s. Urban renewal was, in its own time, a sincere and ambitious attempt to address the physical decay of American cities. It was also, in its execution, one of the most racially destructive programs in the history of American public policy. Across hundreds of American cities, urban renewal destroyed Black neighborhoods, scattered Black communities, and replaced community institutions with highways, hospitals, universities, civic centers, and housing projects that served new populations — often white, often higher-income — while the Black residents who had been displaced received inadequate relocation assistance and had no real say in what replaced their homes. James Baldwin's observation that urban renewal meant "Negro removal" was bitter but accurate, and it shaped the deep suspicion with which Black communities have regarded subsequent rounds of redevelopment in their neighborhoods ever since.

The relationship between urban redevelopment and street organizations is not always direct, but it is historically significant. The territorial identities that street organizations developed over decades were rooted in specific places — specific blocks, specific housing projects, specific neighborhood boundaries. When those places are demolished, when the housing projects are torn down, when gentrification displaces the residents who constituted the social base of neighborhood organizations, the result is a disruption that can simultaneously destroy community bonds, scatter established organizational structures, concentrate the most marginalized residents in new locations, and generate new territorial conflicts in the neighborhoods to which displaced residents are forced to relocate. The fragmentation that characterizes contemporary street organization — the subject of the next chapter — cannot be understood without understanding the geography of displacement that this chapter describes.

This chapter traces the history of urban redevelopment in Black communities through four overlapping phases. The first is the urban renewal era of the 1940s through 1970s, in which federal policy systematically destroyed Black neighborhoods in the name of slum clearance and city improvement. The second is the public housing crisis of the 1980s and 1990s, in which the concentrated poverty created by decades of segregated public housing became the justification for demolishing the housing projects themselves. The third is the mixed-income redevelopment era of the 1990s and 2000s, in which HOPE VI and similar programs replaced public housing with mixed-income communities that dramatically reduced the number of public housing units available to the poorest residents. And the fourth is the market-driven gentrification of the 2000s through the present, in which rising land values in formerly disinvested neighborhoods are displacing long-term residents, destroying community institutions, and fundamentally altering the demographic geography of American cities.

Washington, D.C. is this chapter's primary case study, and it is well chosen for that role. The District is a city in which the contradictions of urban redevelopment are displayed with unusual clarity: it is the capital of the most powerful nation in the world, and it is a city that was majority Black for most of the second half of the twentieth century, a fact closely connected to its long status as a colony of Congress, without voting representation or meaningful home rule for most of its history. Washington's transformation from a majority-Black city to one in which Black residents have been displaced from most of the urban core is among the most dramatic demographic transformations of any major American city, and it illustrates the ways in which gentrification intersects with political power, federal policy, public housing demolition, real estate speculation, and the long history of racial exclusion in American cities.

But the chapter also draws on evidence from cities across the country — from the demolition of the Robert Taylor Homes in Chicago, to the redevelopment of Harlem in New York, to the transformation of East Austin in Texas, to the wholesale reshaping of neighborhoods in Atlanta, Seattle, Denver, and Oakland. What happened in Washington happened, in different forms and at different speeds, across virtually every major American city with a significant Black population. Understanding the pattern — its causes, its mechanisms, its consequences, and its relationship to the longer history of racial exclusion — is essential to understanding where Black urban communities stand today and why the geography of Black urban life looks so different in the early twenty-first century than it did even two or three decades ago.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Define gentrification as a historical and urban process, distinguishing it from related phenomena such as urban renewal, neighborhood revitalization, and market-rate redevelopment.
  2. Trace the major phases of urban redevelopment policy from the federal urban renewal program of the 1940s–1970s through the HOPE VI era of the 1990s–2000s to contemporary market-driven gentrification.
  3. Explain the role of the federal government in creating and perpetuating the conditions — through redlining, urban renewal, public housing segregation, and disinvestment — that produced the distressed neighborhoods that are now being redeveloped.
  4. Analyze the causes of contemporary gentrification, including the role of capital mobility, changing preferences among educated workers, public investment in amenities, and tax and zoning policy.
  5. Describe the demographic consequences of gentrification for Black urban communities, including displacement from long-established neighborhoods and the destruction of community institutions.
  6. Assess the relationship between public housing demolition — particularly under the HOPE VI program — and the displacement of the most economically vulnerable urban residents.
  7. Evaluate competing interpretations of gentrification's effects, including arguments that it revitalizes neighborhoods and arguments that it primarily serves higher-income newcomers at the expense of long-term residents.
  8. Connect the geography of displacement to the territorial identities of street organizations, explaining how redevelopment disrupts established organizational structures and can contribute to new forms of violence.
  9. Analyze the case of Washington, D.C. as an example of the intersection of federal policy, political power, real estate markets, and racial displacement.
  10. Distinguish between different types of urban residents' experiences of gentrification: homeowners, renters, business owners, young people, long-term community organizations, and street organizations.
  11. Identify the policy instruments — including community land trusts, affordable housing requirements, anti-displacement ordinances, and tenant protections — that cities have used to attempt to manage gentrification's consequences.
  12. Contextualize contemporary gentrification within the longer history of racial capitalism in American cities, explaining how the current transformation of Black neighborhoods is connected to the earlier processes of exclusion that created them.

Guiding Historical Questions

Questions for Historical Inquiry

  1. What is the relationship between the deliberate disinvestment in Black neighborhoods through redlining and urban renewal, and the subsequent reinvestment through gentrification? Who benefited from each process?
  2. How did the federal urban renewal program of the 1950s and 1960s reshape Black communities, and what does its history tell us about the relationship between public policy and racial displacement?
  3. Who experiences gentrification differently — homeowners versus renters, young versus old, established residents versus newcomers — and what do these different experiences reveal about the distribution of gentrification's costs and benefits?
  4. What is the relationship between the demolition of public housing and the fragmentation of street organizations? How does the geography of displacement reshape the social geography of urban violence?
  5. Washington, D.C. has undergone one of the most dramatic demographic transformations of any American city in recent decades. What forces drove this transformation, and what does it tell us about the role of power in shaping urban space?
  6. How have Black urban communities — residents, organizations, institutions — responded to gentrification and displacement? What forms of resistance, adaptation, and advocacy have they developed?
  7. Is gentrification a necessary consequence of market forces, or is it a policy choice? What evidence supports each position, and what does the answer imply for urban policy?
  8. What happens to community institutions — churches, barbershops, community centers, local businesses, social service organizations — when the communities they served are displaced? What do these losses mean for community stability and collective life?
  9. How does the history of gentrification challenge or complicate the idea that the revitalization of formerly distressed neighborhoods is straightforwardly good for urban communities?

Historical Context

The story of urban redevelopment in Black communities in the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries is, at its root, the story of capital — where it went, when it left, and when it returned. Understanding why capital left Black neighborhoods and why it came back requires understanding the specific history of racial exclusion in American cities that this textbook has traced from the Great Migration forward. The neighborhoods that are now being gentrified were not always poor. They were made poor through a series of deliberate policy decisions and private market practices that restricted Black access to capital, concentrated poverty through segregated public housing, withdrew services and investment, and then — when land values in central cities began rising again in the late twentieth century — subjected those same communities to new forms of capital extraction that benefited property owners and real estate investors far more than they benefited long-term residents.

The foundation of this story is the racially segregated geography that earlier chapters have described. The Great Migration of the early and mid-twentieth century brought millions of Black Southerners to northern and western cities in search of economic opportunity and escape from Jim Crow. What they found in those cities was a different but equally systematic form of racial exclusion: restrictive covenants that prevented Black families from buying homes in most neighborhoods, redlining policies that denied them mortgage credit in the neighborhoods where they did live, exclusion from the Federal Housing Administration loan programs that were building the white middle-class suburbs of the postwar era, and residential segregation enforced by both private discrimination and public policy. Black urban communities were not simply different from white ones. They were deliberately created and maintained by systems that restricted Black mobility and concentrated Black poverty in specific geographic areas.

By the 1950s, the inner-city neighborhoods where Black migrants had settled were also the targets of a new federal initiative: the urban renewal program, authorized by the Housing Act of 1949 and expanded by subsequent legislation. Urban renewal was premised on the idea that the older residential and commercial neighborhoods of American cities — categorized by federal planners as "slums" or "blighted areas" — needed to be cleared and rebuilt. The program gave local governments the power of eminent domain to acquire land in these areas, clear it of existing structures, and sell or lease it to private developers or public institutions for new construction. Over the next three decades, urban renewal cleared approximately 400,000 acres of urban land across the country and displaced an estimated one million people, a disproportionate share of whom were Black.

The pattern of urban renewal — destroying working-class and poor neighborhoods, disproportionately Black ones, to make way for uses that served higher-income and often whiter populations — was not unique to any single city. In city after city, urban renewal bulldozed Black neighborhoods to build highways, hospitals, universities, civic centers, sports stadiums, and luxury housing. San Francisco's Western Addition, Boston's West End, Philadelphia's Eastwick, Cleveland's Hough, Los Angeles's Bunker Hill, Chicago's Near South Side — across the country, the story was similar. The people most affected by these clearances rarely had meaningful input into the decisions that destroyed their communities, and the relocation assistance they received was frequently inadequate to enable them to find comparable housing elsewhere in their cities.

The public housing that was built during this era was not the cause of concentrated Black poverty, but it became one of its primary geographic expressions. Segregated public housing projects — built as towers of concentrated poverty in areas that no private developer wanted, deliberately located in or adjacent to existing Black neighborhoods, maintained in conditions of chronic underfunding, and managed by housing authorities that often treated their residents with contempt — concentrated the most economically vulnerable Black families in environments that compounded poverty rather than alleviating it. By the 1980s, projects like the Robert Taylor Homes in Chicago, Cabrini-Green in Chicago, Pruitt-Igoe in St. Louis (already demolished by that point), and dozens of others in cities across the country had become synonymous with concentrated poverty, failing schools, drug markets, and violence. They had become, in the language of policymakers and the media, the embodiment of what was called the "urban underclass."

The crack cocaine epidemic of the 1980s and early 1990s, described in the previous chapter, hit these already-distressed neighborhoods with particular force. The crack market disrupted community institutions, intensified violence, and accelerated the departure of middle-class and working-class Black families who could afford to leave for suburbs or less distressed neighborhoods. The War on Drugs and the mass incarceration that followed removed hundreds of thousands of Black men from urban communities, further destabilizing families and neighborhoods. By the early 1990s, in many of the formerly vibrant Black neighborhoods of American cities, the combination of deindustrialization, drug markets, mass incarceration, and decades of disinvestment had produced conditions of concentrated poverty and physical deterioration that would have been almost unimaginable to the communities that had built those neighborhoods in the early decades of the twentieth century.

It was precisely this condition of concentrated distress that made these neighborhoods attractive to redevelopment in the late 1990s and 2000s. The same central-city locations that had been abandoned by capital for decades — close to downtowns, close to transit, close to the cultural amenities that younger educated workers increasingly valued — were now dramatically undervalued relative to comparable locations in other cities. As the technology economy of the 1990s, the financial economy of the 2000s, and the knowledge economy more broadly concentrated higher-income workers in a small number of major metropolitan areas, the demand for housing in central cities grew faster than the supply, and land values in formerly disinvested neighborhoods began to rise. The stage was set for the process that would transform the geography of Black urban America in the early twenty-first century.

It is important to understand that this transformation was not simply a matter of market forces. It was shaped at every stage by policy choices: choices about where to invest in transit and amenities, choices about tax incentives for development, choices about zoning and land use, choices about historic preservation, choices about affordable housing requirements, choices about tenant protections. In cities where policymakers prioritized existing residents, the displacement consequences of rising land values were more limited. In cities where policymakers prioritized new investment and new residents, displacement was more rapid and more extensive. The geography of gentrification in American cities is not simply the geography of the market. It is the geography of accumulated policy decisions about who deserves to live where.

Major People, Groups, and Places

Reference — People, Groups & Places

James Baldwin (1924–1987)
Novelist, essayist, and cultural critic whose observations on urban renewal and racial displacement remain among the most powerful analyses of the process. Baldwin, who grew up in Harlem and later witnessed the destruction of the San Francisco Fillmore district, coined the phrase "urban renewal means Negro removal" in a 1963 television interview, capturing the racial logic of the program in terms that resonated with Black urban communities across the country. His essays on urban space, belonging, and racial capitalism continue to shape contemporary thinking about gentrification and displacement.
Robert Moses (1888–1981)
The powerful New York City planner and urban power broker whose four decades of dominance over New York's built environment shaped the template for urban renewal across the country. Moses used his control of multiple public authorities to build highways, bridges, housing projects, parks, and urban renewal projects that repeatedly destroyed working-class and Black and Puerto Rican neighborhoods. Robert Caro's Pulitzer Prize-winning biography The Power Broker (1974) documented Moses's methods and their consequences for the communities he displaced, establishing the standard account of how technocratic urban planning served racial and class interests.
Marion Barry (1936–2014)
The four-term mayor of Washington, D.C. (1979–1991, 1995–1999) who was the dominant figure in D.C. politics for two decades. Barry's mayoralty coincided with the crack epidemic and the beginning of Washington's fiscal crisis, and his complicated legacy — a champion of Black working-class communities and political empowerment, but also a figure whose administration was marked by corruption and drug use — captures the contradictions of Black urban governance in an era of shrinking federal resources, rising inequality, and political vulnerability. The transformation of Washington during and after his tenure illustrates the limits of local Black political power in the face of federal control and capital markets.
Anthony Williams (mayor 1999–2007)
The mayor of Washington, D.C. who succeeded Barry and oversaw the beginning of the city's dramatic revitalization and gentrification. Williams's administration attracted new investment, reduced crime, improved city finances, and presided over the beginning of the demographic transformation that would eventually render Washington majority non-Black. His tenure represents the tension between fiscal revitalization and the displacement of long-term residents that characterizes the gentrification debate in cities across the country.
The Housing and Urban Development Department (HUD)
The federal agency responsible for housing policy, community development, and fair housing enforcement. HUD administered the urban renewal program, developed and managed public housing, created the HOPE VI program, and funds the community development block grants and housing vouchers that shape affordable housing policy in American cities. HUD's policy choices have had profound effects on the racial geography of American cities, alternately concentrating Black poverty through segregated public housing and dispersing it through demolition and voucher programs.
The HOPE VI Program (1992–2010)
The federal program that funded the demolition and replacement of severely distressed public housing with mixed-income developments. HOPE VI demolished more than 100,000 units of public housing across the country and produced significant improvements in the physical conditions and crime rates of the neighborhoods where it operated. It also displaced tens of thousands of the most economically vulnerable residents, many of whom did not return to the redeveloped sites, and contributed to the net loss of public housing available to the lowest-income households.
Community Development Corporations (CDCs)
Nonprofit organizations that develop affordable housing, commercial space, and community facilities in low-income neighborhoods. CDCs emerged from the community organizing movements of the 1960s and 1970s as community-controlled alternatives to top-down urban renewal. By the 1990s, they had become the primary vehicle for community development in many American cities, supported by Low-Income Housing Tax Credits, Community Development Block Grants, and philanthropic funding. CDCs have produced significant affordable housing but often lack the political and financial power to prevent displacement in rapidly gentrifying markets.
The Shaw Neighborhood, Washington, D.C.
A historically Black neighborhood north of downtown Washington that was one of the cultural and commercial centers of Black Washington for most of the twentieth century. Shaw was home to Howard University, the Howard Theatre (where jazz greats including Duke Ellington and Ella Fitzgerald performed), Black-owned businesses on U Street, and dozens of churches and community organizations. Urban renewal and highway construction destroyed parts of Shaw in the 1950s and 1960s, and the neighborhood was further damaged by the uprisings following Martin Luther King Jr.'s assassination in 1968. By the 1990s, Shaw was severely disinvested; by the 2010s, it was one of the most rapidly gentrifying neighborhoods in the country.
The U Street Corridor, Washington, D.C.
The commercial and cultural heart of Black Washington for much of the twentieth century, sometimes called "Black Broadway" for its concentration of Black-owned theaters, restaurants, clubs, and businesses. U Street was devastated by the 1968 uprisings following King's assassination and spent three decades in decline before becoming, from the late 1990s onward, one of the most rapidly gentrifying corridors in the city. Its transformation from the center of Black cultural life to an entertainment district dominated by white newcomers is among the most emblematic stories of gentrification-driven cultural displacement in American cities.
The Robert Taylor Homes, Chicago
The largest public housing project in the United States at the time of its completion in 1962, consisting of twenty-eight sixteen-story towers along the State Street corridor on Chicago's South Side. The Robert Taylor Homes housed approximately 27,000 people, almost all of them Black, in conditions of extreme concentrated poverty. By the 1990s, the complex had become synonymous with gang violence, drug markets, and concentrated urban distress. Its demolition, which began in 1998 and was completed in 2007, scattered thousands of residents and transformed the State Street corridor, part of a broader restructuring of Chicago's South Side that continues today.
Cabrini-Green, Chicago
A public housing complex on Chicago's Near North Side whose demolition between 1995 and 2011 transformed one of the most visible symbols of concentrated public housing poverty into a mixed-income neighborhood that now includes some of the most expensive real estate in the city. Cabrini-Green's location — adjacent to the Gold Coast, one of Chicago's wealthiest neighborhoods — made it a target of redevelopment pressure, and its demolition illustrates the role of land values and neighborhood location in driving public housing demolition decisions.
Community Land Trusts
Nonprofit organizations that own land in perpetuity and lease it to homeowners and renters at below-market rates, preserving affordability regardless of market conditions. Community land trusts are among the most effective anti-displacement tools available to communities facing gentrification, and they have been used in cities including Burlington, Vermont (where Bernie Sanders supported the creation of the first major urban land trust in the 1980s), Atlanta, Chicago, and Washington, D.C. They represent a model of community ownership that challenges the market logic driving gentrification.
Housing Choice Vouchers (Section 8)
Federal rental assistance subsidies that pay the difference between a household's income-based contribution and market-rate rent. Housing vouchers became the primary form of federal housing assistance for low-income families after the decline of public housing construction. Research on the effectiveness of vouchers at facilitating residential mobility shows mixed results: in tight housing markets, vouchers often cannot be used in high-opportunity neighborhoods because landlords refuse to accept them, limiting their anti-displacement potential.
Anacostia, Washington, D.C.
A predominantly Black neighborhood east of the Anacostia River that has long been one of the most economically distressed areas of Washington, largely isolated from the city's gentrification pressure until recently. Anacostia's increasing real estate interest in the 2010s and 2020s illustrates how gentrification expands outward from city centers as land values in inner neighborhoods rise, eventually reaching even the most historically disinvested areas.
Harlem, New York City
Perhaps the most famous Black neighborhood in America, whose history — from its peak as the cultural capital of Black America in the Harlem Renaissance through decades of disinvestment and concentrated poverty to its current status as one of the most rapidly gentrifying neighborhoods in New York — encapsulates the entire arc of urban renewal, disinvestment, and redevelopment that this chapter traces. Harlem's transformation has generated significant scholarly and journalistic attention and intense community debate about displacement, authenticity, and the preservation of Black cultural geography.

Section 12.1

Urban Renewal: The First Wave of Displacement

The Politics of "Blight"

The word "blight" appears throughout the history of urban renewal with the frequency of a religious incantation. Federal housing legislation, city planning documents, urban renewal agency reports, and newspaper accounts of mid-twentieth-century American cities all invoke blight as the central justification for the clearance of existing neighborhoods and the displacement of their residents. What the term actually described varied considerably — it could refer to deteriorated housing conditions, overcrowding, the age of buildings, the percentage of non-white residents, or simply the fact that land in a given area was not being used in the way that planners and developers considered highest and best. What it consistently signified was that the neighborhood so designated was a problem to be solved, a pathology to be eliminated, a drag on the fiscal health and the physical attractiveness of the surrounding city.

The designation of neighborhoods as "blighted" was not a neutral technical exercise. It was a political process shaped by the interests of city governments that wanted to increase their tax base, real estate developers who wanted to acquire valuable urban land at below-market prices, universities and hospitals that wanted to expand their footprints, and highway engineers who needed routes through the urban fabric. And it was, in its application, overwhelmingly racial: the neighborhoods most consistently designated as blighted were the neighborhoods where Black people lived, not because those neighborhoods were necessarily in worse physical condition than other urban neighborhoods, but because the combination of discriminatory capital flows, overcrowding driven by segregation, and deliberate public neglect had often made them so — and because their residents had the least political power to resist the label and its consequences.

The Housing Act of 1949 authorized urban renewal with language about "a decent home and suitable living environment for every American family" that sounded expansive and egalitarian. What it created in practice was a federal subsidy program that paid two-thirds of the cost of acquiring and clearing land in "blighted" areas, and then allowed that land to be sold to private developers at whatever price the market would bear. The gap between the acquisition cost and the resale price — the "write-down" — was the federal subsidy to development. The people displaced by the acquisition and clearance received relocation assistance that was, in the early years of the program, scandalously inadequate: a few hundred dollars and a list of available housing that was often in neighborhoods as poor as or poorer than the ones they had been forced to leave.

The scale of urban renewal displacement was enormous. Between 1949 and 1973, when the program was substantially wound down, urban renewal projects were undertaken in nearly 1,000 American cities, cleared approximately 400,000 acres of urban land, and displaced an estimated 300,000 to one million households, depending on the methodology used. Studies consistently found that between sixty and seventy percent of the households displaced were Black, even in cities where Black residents were a minority of the total population. The neighborhoods cleared were predominantly Black; the new uses that replaced them — civic centers, hospitals, universities, sports arenas, luxury housing, and commercial development — were predominantly white in their users and beneficiaries.

Highways and Neighborhood Destruction

The Interstate Highway Act of 1956 — passed with enormous popular support as an infrastructure and defense program — added a second, equally destructive force to the landscape of urban neighborhood clearance. The Interstate Highway System required the construction of thousands of miles of limited-access highways connecting American cities, and where those highways passed through urban areas, they required massive land clearance that was, in city after city, directed through Black neighborhoods. This was not simply a consequence of the technical requirements of highway routing. It was, in many cities, a deliberate choice: Black neighborhoods were targeted for highway routes because their residents had the least political power to resist, because their land was valued at lower rates that made acquisition cheaper, and because highway construction through Black neighborhoods was seen by many white planners and officials as a convenient way to implement the racial separation and "slum clearance" that urban renewal was also pursuing.

The consequences were devastating. In Birmingham, Alabama, the building of Interstate 20/59 through the Fountain Heights neighborhood destroyed a thriving Black commercial district. In Nashville, Tennessee, Interstate 40 was routed through the historically Black Jefferson Street corridor, destroying what had been called the "Harlem of the South." In Miami, the construction of Interstate 95 through Overtown, the historic center of Black Miami, displaced approximately 10,000 people and effectively destroyed one of the most vibrant Black urban communities in the American South. In New Orleans, the construction of Interstate 10 along the North Claiborne Avenue corridor eliminated one of the city's most important public gathering spaces, a boulevard lined with live oaks where the Black community had gathered for decades, including for Mardi Gras celebrations. Similar stories could be told for dozens of other cities.

The physical marks of these decisions are still visible in American cities. The elevated highways that cut through Black neighborhoods created literal barriers between communities, concentrated traffic and pollution in adjacent residential areas, and reduced the desirability of neighboring properties. In some cities, these highways are now being torn down — in San Francisco, the demolition of the Embarcadero Freeway after the 1989 earthquake revitalized the waterfront; in New Haven, the removal of the Route 34 connector has opened new development possibilities — but in most cities, the highways built through Black neighborhoods in the 1950s and 1960s remain, their presence a continuing mark of the power relationships that shaped the urban landscape of the mid-twentieth century.

Historical Vocabulary
Urban Renewal

Urban renewal refers to the federal program authorized by the Housing Act of 1949 that funded the clearance and redevelopment of areas designated as "blighted" in American cities. The program gave local governments the power of eminent domain to acquire land in designated areas, clear it of existing structures, and sell or lease it to private developers or public institutions at subsidized prices. The federal government paid two-thirds of the difference between acquisition costs and resale prices, providing an enormous subsidy to private redevelopment of urban land.

Urban renewal was the dominant form of urban policy in America from the late 1940s through the early 1970s, when the Housing and Community Development Act of 1974 replaced it with Community Development Block Grants. During this period, it cleared hundreds of thousands of acres of urban land, displaced an estimated one million households (a disproportionate share of them Black), and transformed the physical landscape of American cities. It produced new civic centers, universities, hospitals, sports arenas, luxury housing, and commercial developments in place of the mixed-use working-class neighborhoods it cleared.

The legacy of urban renewal is deeply contested. Its defenders point to the genuine physical deterioration of many of the neighborhoods it cleared and argue that it revitalized downtown areas that might otherwise have continued to decline. Its critics — including the activist Jane Jacobs, whose 1961 The Death and Life of Great American Cities is among the most influential attacks on urban renewal's logic — argue that it destroyed the complex, mixed-use, pedestrian-scaled urban environments that made cities livable and replaced them with superblocks, towers, and highways that served cars and institutions rather than people. For Black communities, urban renewal's legacy is primarily the legacy of displacement: the destruction of community institutions, the scattering of established social networks, and the conversion of Black neighborhood land to uses serving other populations.

James Baldwin's observation that urban renewal meant "Negro removal" — made in a 1963 documentary — remains the most powerful popular summary of the program's racial logic. The phrase captured what statistical analyses have since confirmed: that the program systematically targeted Black neighborhoods for clearance, that it displaced Black residents at rates disproportionate to their share of urban populations, and that the new uses it created served other constituencies. Urban renewal is thus both a chapter in the history of American urban planning and a chapter in the history of racial displacement, and it cannot be understood accurately without understanding both dimensions simultaneously.

Resistance and the Community Development Movement

Urban renewal did not proceed without resistance. From its earliest years, the program encountered organized opposition from the communities it targeted, and that resistance — sometimes successful, sometimes not — shaped both the immediate outcomes of specific projects and the longer-term evolution of urban policy toward more community-oriented approaches. The history of urban renewal resistance is an important counterpart to the history of displacement, because it documents the agency of Black and other communities in confronting the forces that threatened their neighborhoods.

In some cases, organized community resistance successfully stopped or significantly modified urban renewal projects. In San Francisco's Western Addition, tenant organizations, community development advocates, and civil rights groups mobilized to slow and eventually modify renewal plans, though not before significant displacement had occurred. In New York, Jane Jacobs led the fight that stopped Robert Moses's planned expressway through lower Manhattan — a victory that preserved one of the city's most vibrant mixed-use neighborhoods, though it was a victory achieved primarily by white middle-class residents in Greenwich Village, not by the Black and Puerto Rican communities that had less political leverage in other parts of the city. In Boston, the success of community organizing in Jamaica Plain and Roxbury demonstrated that organized neighborhoods could sometimes deflect or shape redevelopment pressures.

More broadly, the resistance to urban renewal contributed to the emergence of the community development movement — the network of community development corporations, neighborhood organizations, community land trusts, and advocacy groups that developed from the late 1960s onward as an alternative model of neighborhood change. The community development movement was premised on the idea that neighborhoods should be developed in ways that served existing residents rather than displacing them, and that communities should have meaningful control over the development of their own neighborhoods. This philosophy was formalized in the community participation requirements of the Model Cities program (1966), the community development block grant program (1974), and the Community Reinvestment Act (1977), all of which represented federal acknowledgment that top-down urban renewal had failed and that community-based alternatives were needed.

The community development movement produced significant achievements over the following decades. Community development corporations built hundreds of thousands of units of affordable housing in distressed neighborhoods, developed commercial space that housed local businesses, created community facilities, and provided services ranging from job training to financial literacy. The Low-Income Housing Tax Credit program, created in 1986, became the primary federal mechanism for subsidizing affordable housing construction, and it channeled billions of dollars into CDC-developed projects across the country. By the 1990s, community development had become a substantial industry, with thousands of CDCs operating in every major American city and a significant infrastructure of financial intermediaries, technical assistance providers, and advocacy organizations supporting their work.

But the community development movement had limits that became increasingly apparent as gentrification accelerated in the late 1990s and 2000s. CDCs could build affordable housing, but they could not by themselves counteract the broader market forces that were driving up land values and rents in central cities. They could preserve some units of affordable housing in gentrifying neighborhoods, but they could not prevent the displacement of the majority of low-income renters who lived in market-rate housing. They could advocate for community interests in planning processes, but they lacked the political power to override the decisions of city governments that prioritized new investment and tax base over the interests of existing low-income residents. The community development movement was a response to urban renewal's failures, but it was not a sufficient response to the new challenge of market-driven gentrification.

Section 12.2

Housing Redevelopment: The Demolition of Public Housing

The Crisis of Concentrated Poverty

By the early 1990s, the large-scale public housing projects that had been built in American cities between the 1940s and 1970s were in profound crisis. The combination of federal disinvestment (Congress had cut operating subsidies and capital improvement funds for public housing dramatically during the Reagan and Bush administrations), the concentration of the most economically vulnerable households in projects that had been systematically isolated from economic opportunity, the devastation of the crack epidemic, and decades of poor management had produced conditions in many projects that were genuinely dangerous, physically deteriorated, and socially isolated. Vacancy rates were high, waiting lists were long, and the gap between the resources available to housing authorities and the cost of operating and maintaining aging buildings was enormous.

The policy response to this crisis — embodied most consequentially in the HOPE VI program authorized by Congress in 1992 — was demolition. Rather than investing in the rehabilitation of existing public housing, HOPE VI provided grants to housing authorities to tear down their most distressed projects and replace them with smaller, mixed-income developments that would house a mix of market-rate renters, subsidized renters, and (in some cases) homeowners. The theory was that mixed-income communities would provide better environments for low-income residents than concentrated poverty projects, that the social mixing of incomes would reduce stigma and crime, and that the physical redesign of housing — replacing tower blocks with lower-density townhouses and apartment buildings — would create more livable environments. The program also aimed to reconnect public housing sites to the surrounding urban fabric by eliminating the superblock designs and isolated towers that had cut projects off from city streets and adjacent neighborhoods.

The physical results of HOPE VI were often impressive. Many of the neighborhoods where projects were demolished became significantly safer and more attractive, and the mixed-income developments that replaced them were generally higher-quality environments than the projects they replaced. In some cities, the transformation was dramatic: the replacement of the Cabrini-Green extension buildings in Chicago's Near North Side, or the Robert Taylor Homes on Chicago's South Side, produced neighborhoods that bore almost no resemblance to what had preceded them. Crime rates fell sharply. Vacancy rates dropped. Physical conditions improved. By conventional measures of neighborhood quality, HOPE VI was a success.

But the success story has a different dimension when examined from the perspective of the residents who had lived in the demolished projects. HOPE VI required that public housing authorities offer relocated residents housing assistance — typically a housing choice voucher — and the right to return to the new mixed-income development. In practice, many residents did not return. The mixed-income developments that replaced the projects had fewer public housing units than the projects they replaced: a project with 1,000 units might be replaced by a mixed-income development with 200 public housing units, 300 subsidized market-rate units, and 500 market-rate units. The deeply subsidized units available to the lowest-income residents — those with incomes below thirty percent of area median income — were the scarcest, because the economics of mixed-income development made it difficult to cross-subsidize the deepest subsidies without additional funding sources. The net result was a dramatic reduction in the number of deeply subsidized units available in most cities.

Studies of what happened to residents relocated from demolished public housing projects showed highly variable outcomes. For the minority of former residents who successfully used housing vouchers to move to lower-poverty neighborhoods, the results were generally positive: lower crime exposure, better schools, better health outcomes. But many former residents could not use their vouchers effectively in tight housing markets, where landlords in lower-poverty neighborhoods often refused to accept them and the available units in other low-poverty areas were simply too few to accommodate all the displaced households. Many relocated residents ended up in other high-poverty neighborhoods, often concentrating in areas that already had high rates of poverty and crime. The displacement from one concentrated poverty neighborhood was, for many, simply the geographic relocation of their poverty to a different location — often one with fewer community ties, fewer institutional supports, and less familiarity.

The Social Consequences of Demolition

The demolition of public housing had consequences that extended beyond the material conditions of the households directly displaced. Public housing projects, for all their physical deterioration and concentrated poverty, were also communities — places where people had lived for decades, built social networks, raised children, established churches and community organizations, and developed the complex web of social relationships that constitute a functioning community. The social capital embedded in those networks — the informal childcare arrangements, the mutual support systems, the community knowledge about navigating institutions and resources, the neighborhood organizations that had developed over decades of shared experience — was not simply transferable to a new location. It was, in significant part, destroyed by dispersal.

The relationship between public housing demolition and street organization dynamics was particularly complex. Many of the largest public housing projects had been, for decades, the geographic base of major street organizations — organizations whose territorial identities were inseparable from the specific buildings and corridors and courtyards of the projects. When the Robert Taylor Homes were demolished, for example, the organized structures that had governed drug markets, managed territorial conflicts, and (in some cases) provided a degree of order within the project were simultaneously disrupted. Residents scattered to neighborhoods across the South Side and beyond, carrying both their social vulnerabilities and their organizational affiliations with them, but in contexts where those affiliations no longer provided the same degree of territorial coherence or organizational support. Research by sociologists including Robert Sampson and criminologists studying the post-HOPE VI period documented significant increases in violence in neighborhoods that received large numbers of displaced project residents — not because those residents were inherently violent, but because the disruption of social networks and organizational structures, combined with the concentration of multiple displaced populations in the same receiving neighborhoods, created conditions of social disorganization that were temporarily associated with higher violence rates.

The demolition of major public housing projects also had significant effects on the community institutions that had served their residents. Churches, social service organizations, health clinics, schools, and community organizations that had built their programming and client base around project residents found their communities dispersed and their financial bases eroded when the projects were demolished. Some of these institutions relocated or reinvented themselves; others simply closed. The loss of community-serving institutions in the wake of public housing demolition was an underreported but significant aspect of the social consequences of HOPE VI-era redevelopment.

Historical Vocabulary
Displacement

Displacement refers to the process by which residents are forced to leave their neighborhoods — either directly, through eviction or demolition of their housing, or indirectly, through rising rents and property costs that make it economically impossible to remain. Scholars distinguish between several types of displacement:

Direct displacement occurs when residents are physically removed from their housing through eviction, eminent domain, building demolition, or condo conversion. Direct displacement is the most visible and measurable form, and it characterized the urban renewal era when government agencies physically demolished neighborhoods and removed their residents.

Indirect or economic displacement occurs when rising rents or property values make it economically impossible for existing residents to remain in their neighborhoods, even if they are not directly forced to leave. Indirect displacement is the primary mechanism of gentrification-era displacement and is harder to measure precisely because it is cumulative and gradual — a household that leaves a neighborhood after a 40 percent rent increase in two years is displaced, but the causal chain is less visible than a household evicted by a demolition order.

Cultural displacement refers to the experience of long-term residents who remain physically in their neighborhoods but find that the cultural character of the neighborhood — its businesses, its social norms, its population, its institutions — has been so transformed by newcomers that they no longer feel at home. Cultural displacement is the least visible form but is nonetheless deeply experienced by long-term residents who watch the institutions and social networks that gave their neighborhood its character disappear without being replaced by anything that serves their needs.

Exclusionary displacement, a term coined by scholars Peter Marcuse and Neil Smith, refers to the displacement of people who never moved into a neighborhood because it became unaffordable before they could do so. This category is important because it captures the way gentrification forecloses future housing options for low-income people, not just current residents.

For this textbook's subject — the history of Black street organizations — displacement matters because territorial identity is one of the central features of street organization culture, and displacement disrupts that identity by separating organizational members from the places where their organizations were rooted. The consequences of displacement for organizational cohesion, for the management of territorial conflicts, and for the social networks through which street organizations operate is a central theme of the fragmentation literature examined in the next chapter.

Section 12.3

Demographic Change: The Transformation of Black Urban Neighborhoods

Who Is Moving In and Why

The demographic transformation of formerly Black urban neighborhoods in the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries was driven by a convergence of forces that operated simultaneously at the national, metropolitan, and neighborhood levels. Understanding who was moving into these neighborhoods — and why — is essential to understanding both the economic logic of gentrification and its social and political meaning for the communities being transformed.

At the national level, the transformation was driven by a structural change in the American economy: the shift from manufacturing to services, and particularly the growth of the technology, finance, healthcare, education, and professional services sectors. These sectors concentrated their workers in a relatively small number of major metropolitan areas — New York, San Francisco, Boston, Washington, Chicago, Seattle, and a handful of others — and those workers were disproportionately college-educated, high-earning, and, in the early years of the technology sector in particular, young. The demand for housing in those metropolitan areas grew faster than the supply, driving up land values across the metropolitan area and making formerly undesirable central-city locations attractive to households that would previously have looked to the suburbs.

At the metropolitan level, the transformation was driven by changing preferences among the educated professional workers who were fueling the knowledge economy. Studies of housing preferences among younger educated workers in the 2000s and 2010s consistently found higher rates of preference for urban living than among previous generations — preferences for walkability, transit access, density, and the cultural amenities (restaurants, bars, cultural venues, independent retail) that central-city neighborhoods offered. These preferences were themselves shaped by cultural shifts — the celebration of urban life in popular culture, the growing prestige of cities as centers of creativity and innovation, and the reaction against the suburban lifestyle that had dominated American residential patterns since the 1950s. Whatever their causes, these preferences translated into demand for housing in urban neighborhoods that had previously been avoided by middle- and upper-income households.

At the neighborhood level, the transformation was driven by the specific combination of central location, undervalued land, historic architecture, cultural amenities, and improving public safety that characterized many formerly Black urban neighborhoods by the 1990s. The same neighborhoods that had been targeted for redlining and disinvestment for decades were now, from the perspective of real estate investors and higher-income buyers, undervalued relative to their location and their physical stock. The reduced crime rates that followed the crack epidemic's peak in the early 1990s, the investment in transit and amenities that city governments made in the late 1990s, and the willingness of pioneering higher-income households to tolerate the residual disadvantages of these neighborhoods in exchange for central location and affordable prices, all contributed to the beginning of a cycle of investment and appreciation that, once begun, fed on itself.

The Racial Dimensions of Demographic Change

The demographic transformation of Black urban neighborhoods was, at its core, a racial transformation as well as an economic one. The neighborhoods that were gentrifying had been Black not simply because Black people happened to live there but because decades of racial exclusion in housing markets had created them as Black spaces — spaces where Black cultural, economic, and institutional life had concentrated precisely because Black people were excluded from the rest of the city. The displacement of those residents, whatever its economic logic, had racial meanings that cannot be separated from its economic ones.

For many long-term residents of gentrifying Black neighborhoods, the arrival of white newcomers was experienced not simply as economic pressure but as racial displacement — as the continuation, by different means, of the same logic that had produced urban renewal and redlining in earlier decades. The same neighborhoods that had been designated as "blighted" and targeted for destruction in the 1950s and 1960s were now being designated as "up-and-coming" and targeted for reinvestment in the 1990s and 2000s. The people who had been told their neighborhoods were not worth investing in were now being told that the investment was happening without them. The wealth that had been extracted from these neighborhoods through decades of absentee landlordism and discriminatory lending was now being recaptured by newcomers who had not shared in the disinvestment era's deprivations.

The statistics on demographic change in Black urban neighborhoods are striking. Washington, D.C. provides the clearest illustration: in 1970, the District was 71 percent Black; by 2020, it was approximately 41 percent Black, having fallen below fifty percent for the first time in decades around 2011. Harlem, which was 98 percent Black in 1960 and remained predominantly Black through the 1990s, was approximately 55 percent Black by 2020, with a rapidly growing white and Hispanic population. In Chicago, the Woodlawn neighborhood on the South Side — the base of the Blackstone Rangers (later El Rukns), among the most significant street organizations in Chicago history — lost more than half its Black population between 1970 and 2010, declining from a densely populated Black community of nearly 80,000 to fewer than 25,000 residents. In San Francisco, the Bayview-Hunters Point neighborhood lost a substantial portion of its Black population as housing costs in the city became among the highest in the nation.

These demographic shifts have had profound consequences for the geography of Black institutional life. The churches, social service organizations, political clubs, schools, and community institutions that had been built over decades in these neighborhoods were designed to serve Black communities. As those communities dispersed — some to outer suburbs, some to other neighborhoods within the same city, some to entirely different metropolitan areas — these institutions lost their constituencies. Churches that had housed congregations of hundreds found themselves with aging, shrinking congregations unable to sustain the buildings and programs that had been central to neighborhood life. Schools that had served the children of these communities saw their enrollment shift as neighborhood demographics changed. Community organizations found their funding tied to a community that had, in significant part, moved away. The geography of Black institutional life in American cities was being fundamentally remapped by the processes this chapter describes.

Historical Vocabulary
Gentrification

Gentrification refers to the process by which higher-income residents and uses move into lower-income urban neighborhoods, typically accompanied by rising rents and property values, physical rehabilitation of buildings, the replacement of neighborhood-serving businesses with businesses oriented toward newcomers, and the displacement — direct and indirect — of lower-income long-term residents. The term was coined by sociologist Ruth Glass in 1964, writing about changes she observed in London neighborhoods as middle-class residents moved into areas that had previously been working-class.

Gentrification is a contested concept, both analytically and politically. Analytically, scholars debate which changes constitute gentrification, how to distinguish it from other forms of neighborhood change, and how to measure its consequences. Politically, the term carries evaluative weight: to label a process "gentrification" is typically to frame it as harmful to existing residents, while proponents of the same process often prefer terms like "revitalization," "reinvestment," or "neighborhood improvement" that emphasize benefits rather than displacement costs.

For this textbook, gentrification is understood as one dimension of the broader process of urban redevelopment that has transformed formerly Black neighborhoods in American cities since the late twentieth century. It is not treated as straightforwardly good or bad — it brings genuine improvements in physical conditions, public safety, and amenities to some residents while imposing severe costs of displacement on others, and the distribution of these costs and benefits is systematically unequal, with long-term renters bearing the costs and property owners and newcomers capturing the benefits. Understanding gentrification as a historical process, with specific causes rooted in the history of disinvestment and racial exclusion, is essential to evaluating its consequences and designing effective policy responses.

The connection between gentrification and the history of street organizations is significant but indirect. Gentrification does not straightforwardly cause street organization violence to increase or decrease, though research suggests complex relationships between displacement and the reorganization of street-level social structures. What gentrification does, unambiguously, is disrupt the territorial basis of street organization identity — the specific neighborhoods, blocks, and housing projects to which organizational identity was attached — and scatter the populations that constituted the social base of these organizations. Understanding this disruption is essential to understanding the fragmentation of street organizations that the next chapter examines.

Section 12.4

Displacement: Who Bears the Costs of Reinvestment

The Mechanics of Displacement

Displacement in gentrifying neighborhoods operates through multiple mechanisms that work simultaneously and reinforce each other. Understanding these mechanisms is essential to understanding why displacement is so difficult to prevent through policy intervention and why its consequences are so severe for the households it affects.

The most direct mechanism of displacement is rent increase in the unsubsidized rental market. In American cities, the majority of low-income urban residents are renters who live in market-rate housing — housing that is not subsidized by any government program and whose rent is set by whatever price the landlord can obtain. As neighborhood desirability increases, landlords respond rationally by raising rents when leases expire. Tenants who cannot afford the new rent must move. In cities with strong tenant protection laws — including rent stabilization, just-cause eviction requirements, and anti-harassment provisions — this process is slower and more regulated. In cities without such protections, which is most American cities, rents can rise rapidly, and tenants who have lived in the same apartment for decades can find themselves unable to afford to stay when their lease is up for renewal.

Condominium conversion is a second major mechanism of displacement. In many gentrifying neighborhoods, landlords find it more profitable to convert rental buildings to condominiums and sell units individually to owner-occupants than to continue operating them as rental properties. Tenants in buildings being converted may be offered the right of first refusal to purchase their units, but at prices that are rarely affordable to low-income renters. Those who cannot purchase must relocate, often with minimal assistance. In cities without robust condo conversion protections, this process can happen rapidly, removing significant numbers of affordable rental units from the market in a short time.

Property tax increases represent a third mechanism that particularly affects low-income homeowners — including the many long-term Black homeowners who have maintained their properties through decades of disinvestment in their neighborhoods. As property values rise in gentrifying neighborhoods, property tax assessments increase correspondingly, raising the annual tax bills of homeowners who may be asset-rich (the value of their property has increased substantially) but income-poor (their household income has not kept pace with the increased costs of ownership). For elderly homeowners on fixed incomes, rising property taxes can make it economically impossible to remain in homes they have owned for decades. Many cities have implemented property tax exemptions or deferrals for low-income elderly homeowners, but these protections are often incomplete and incompletely utilized by the households that most need them.

Indirect displacement through the transformation of neighborhood institutions represents a fourth mechanism that is harder to measure but deeply experienced by long-term residents. When the stores and restaurants and services that have anchored a neighborhood's commercial life for decades — the barbershops, the soul food restaurants, the beauty supply stores, the corner groceries, the check-cashing services — are replaced by boutiques, wine bars, artisanal coffee shops, and fitness studios that serve the preferences and incomes of newcomers, the long-term residents who remain in the neighborhood experience a form of cultural displacement even without moving. The neighborhood no longer serves their needs, no longer reflects their culture, no longer feels like their place. This experience — of watching the neighborhood that sustained your community be converted into something that excludes and alienates you — is a form of harm that is not captured in displacement statistics but is nonetheless real and significant.

Who Is Displaced and Where Do They Go

The households most vulnerable to displacement in gentrifying neighborhoods share several characteristics: they are renters rather than homeowners; they have low incomes and limited savings; they are disproportionately Black, Latina, and elderly; they have limited mobility because of family ties, community connections, job locations, or the constraints of housing voucher programs; and they have few housing options within their price range in the larger metropolitan area. These are precisely the characteristics that also make displacement most harmful: households with the least capacity to relocate successfully bear the greatest costs of displacement.

Research on where displaced residents go is limited but consistent in its conclusions: displaced households generally do not move to higher-opportunity neighborhoods. They move to other neighborhoods with similar or worse poverty rates, similar or worse school quality, and similar or worse access to employment. In cities where affordable housing is geographically concentrated in specific areas — typically outer neighborhoods or inner-ring suburbs that are themselves becoming lower-income as middle-class residents move out — displaced households from gentrifying neighborhoods often end up in these areas, which may be physically safer than the neighborhoods they left but are also more isolated from employment centers, transit, and services. Some displaced households leave the city entirely, moving to cheaper metropolitan areas or to the exurbs of the same metropolitan area. Some end up in doubled-up housing situations with family or friends. Some experience homelessness.

The research on outcomes for displaced households is sobering. Studies of households displaced from Chicago's public housing through the HOPE VI program found that many relocated to neighborhoods with poverty rates comparable to those they had left. Studies of gentrification-displaced renters in San Francisco found that they relocated to neighborhoods with significantly higher poverty rates and lower school quality than the ones they had left. The displacement narrative, then, is not simply a story of people moving from bad neighborhoods to good ones — it is often a story of people moving from bad neighborhoods to worse or comparable ones, losing in the process the community ties, institutional relationships, and social networks that had sustained them in their original locations.

The consequences of displacement for children deserve particular attention. Research consistently shows that residential instability — moving frequently, changing schools, losing social connections — is harmful to children's educational outcomes, mental health, and long-term life prospects. Families displaced by gentrification often must change schools mid-year, disrupting educational progress and social relationships. They may move to neighborhoods with lower-quality schools, reversing whatever educational gains the family had made. They lose access to after-school programs, community youth organizations, and social networks that had provided support and structure. For children growing up in families experiencing displacement, the harm is not simply material — it is a disruption of the stability and continuity that child development requires.

Anti-Displacement Policy and Its Limits

Cities across the country have developed a range of policy tools intended to slow, manage, or mitigate displacement, with varying degrees of success. Rent stabilization policies — which limit the rate at which landlords can increase rents — are among the most effective at protecting existing tenants in the short to medium term, but they apply only to covered buildings and can be evaded through condo conversion or building demolition. Inclusionary zoning requirements — which require developers to include a percentage of affordable units in new market-rate developments — can produce some affordable housing in gentrifying neighborhoods, but typically produce small numbers of units at affordability levels that remain out of reach for the lowest-income displaced residents. Right-of-return commitments for public housing residents — like those incorporated into HOPE VI regulations — are frequently not honored in practice because the number of affordable units in redeveloped sites is smaller than the number of households displaced.

Community land trusts represent one of the most effective anti-displacement tools, because they take land permanently off the speculative market and hold it in community ownership, ensuring that housing built on land-trust land remains affordable regardless of market conditions. But land trusts require significant upfront capital to acquire land, are most effective when established before gentrification pressures intensify, and cannot by themselves address the displacement of renters in private market housing. Their effectiveness is limited to the portion of the housing stock that the trust controls, which is typically small relative to the total housing market in a neighborhood.

The political constraints on anti-displacement policy are often as significant as the technical ones. Cities that rely on property tax revenue have financial incentives to allow property values to rise, even when the consequences for low-income residents are severe. Real estate interests — developers, landlords, real estate brokers — are well organized and well funded in most city political systems, and they consistently lobby against tenant protections, affordable housing requirements, and other policies that would reduce their returns. Long-term low-income residents, who bear the costs of displacement, are often less politically organized and less financially powerful than the interests that benefit from gentrification. The result is that even where good anti-displacement policies exist, the political will to implement them rigorously often falls short.

Section 12.5

The Loss of Traditional Territory: Street Organizations and Urban Redevelopment

Territory as Social Reality

Earlier chapters of this textbook have traced the development of territorial identity in Black urban communities — the ways in which street organizations rooted their identities in specific geographic places, how those territorial identities were shaped by the broader geography of segregation and neighborhood formation, and how the defense of territory became a central preoccupation of street organizations as the crack economy intensified competition over drug market geography. To understand the consequences of urban redevelopment for street organizations, it is essential to understand what territory meant to these organizations — not simply as a space for drug market operations but as a deeply held social identity.

For members of street organizations in Chicago, Los Angeles, New York, and other cities, neighborhood identity was foundational to personal and collective identity in ways that are difficult to fully appreciate from outside that experience. The block, the building, the housing project, the neighborhood — these were not simply addresses but the geographic anchors of a person's entire social world: the places where their friends were, where their family was, where they went to school, where they developed their sense of self, where the people who would have their back in a conflict were. The territorial marking of street organizations — the graffiti, the colors, the hand signs, the named sets — were not arbitrary. They expressed a claim to place that was simultaneously a claim to social belonging, to collective identity, to the community bonds that were available to people who were excluded from most of the mainstream avenues of social recognition and economic opportunity.

When the places that anchored this territorial identity were demolished, the consequences for organizational cohesion and social stability were profound. The demolition of a housing project did not simply remove buildings. It removed the geographic foundation of a social world. The people who had shared those buildings — who had grown up together, fought together, grieved together, organized together — were scattered to addresses across the metropolitan area, connected by social ties that now had to be maintained across distance rather than supported by proximity. The organizational structures that had developed within the specific context of the project — the hierarchies, the norms, the systems of conflict resolution, the informal governance arrangements — did not automatically transfer to the new residential contexts of the displaced members.

Violence Dynamics in Displacement

The relationship between displacement and violence is one of the most practically significant and most contested questions in the research on urban redevelopment and street organizations. The conventional policy narrative has often assumed that dispersing concentrations of poverty through public housing demolition would reduce violence, by breaking up the social contexts in which violence occurred and removing the institutional bases of drug markets. The research evidence is considerably more complicated.

Studies of what happened to violence rates in cities during and after major public housing demolitions consistently found more complex patterns than the simple dispersal-reduces-violence narrative predicted. In Chicago, research by sociologists Robert Sampson and colleagues found that the demolition of public housing projects was associated with increases in homicide rates in the neighborhoods that received the displaced residents, as the disruption of established territorial arrangements created new conflicts and the concentration of multiple displaced populations in receiving neighborhoods generated new competitive dynamics. The violence did not disappear — it moved, and in some cases intensified, in new locations where the social structures that had regulated it were absent.

Research by criminologist Andrew Papachristos on the social network dynamics of violence in Chicago's South Side illuminated a related mechanism: violence in street organizations is not primarily random or diffuse but highly networked, concentrated in specific social networks and transmitted through them. When public housing demolition scattered members of existing networks to new locations, those networks did not dissolve — they became spatially distributed, and conflicts that occurred in new residential contexts were still processed through the same social network dynamics that had governed violence in the original project setting. The result, in some cases, was that violence spread to new geographic areas as network connections transmitted conflict dynamics across the distance created by displacement.

The experience of gentrification-driven displacement in neighborhoods that were not dominated by public housing — the market-driven displacement that has characterized neighborhoods like Shaw in Washington, Woodlawn in Chicago, and Bed-Stuy in Brooklyn — produced somewhat different dynamics. In these contexts, the displacement was more gradual and less total than public housing demolition, and the street organizations that had been rooted in these neighborhoods were not suddenly and simultaneously scattered to new locations. Instead, the organizations experienced a slow erosion of their social base as longtime residents left and newcomers — largely unconnected to neighborhood social networks — moved in. The result was often a shrinking, aging, and increasingly isolated organizational remnant in a neighborhood that was becoming unrecognizable to the people who had built it.

The Institutional Ecology of Loss

The consequences of urban redevelopment for the institutional ecology of Black communities extend well beyond street organizations. The churches, barbershops, community centers, schools, social service agencies, and other institutions that had served Black urban communities for generations were all affected by the demographic transformation of their neighborhoods. Understanding these institutional losses is essential to understanding what displacement means for community life more broadly.

Black churches, which as earlier chapters have described are among the most foundational institutions of Black community life, were particularly affected by the dispersal of their congregations. Many of the oldest and most historically significant Black churches in American cities — churches that had been founded in the early decades of the Great Migration, that had been centers of civil rights organizing, that had been the institutional anchors of Black neighborhood life for generations — faced crises of sustainability as their congregations dispersed. The aging of church membership, the departure of younger families to the suburbs or to other cities, and the replacement of church neighborhoods with populations that had different institutional affiliations all contributed to declining membership, shrinking budgets, and in some cases the closing of churches that had been pillars of Black community life for decades.

The small businesses that had served Black communities — the Black-owned barbershops and hair salons, the soul food restaurants, the grocery stores, the funeral homes, the insurance agencies, the professional offices — faced similar pressures. Rising commercial rents in gentrifying neighborhoods priced out the Black-owned businesses that had served the community, replacing them with businesses that served the preferences and incomes of the new residents. The closing of these businesses was not merely an economic loss. It was a cultural loss — the loss of the spaces where community members gathered, where information was exchanged, where community bonds were maintained, where young people were mentored by their elders, and where the specific cultural practices and aesthetic sensibilities of the community were expressed and transmitted.

The loss of institutional infrastructure represents one of the deepest and most lasting consequences of urban redevelopment in Black communities — deeper even than the loss of housing, because the community institutions that are destroyed by displacement cannot simply be rebuilt in the new residential locations of the displaced. A church that took decades to build cannot be rebuilt overnight. A community organization that developed its programs and its relationships over years cannot simply restart in a new neighborhood. The human capital, the social capital, and the institutional knowledge that were embedded in these organizations were lost when the communities they served were scattered — and that loss represents a diminishment of the collective capacity for self-organization and resilience that Black communities had painstakingly built over generations.

Case Study

Washington, D.C.: Capital City, Black City, Contested City

Washington, D.C. offers a uniquely instructive case study in the history of urban redevelopment and its consequences for Black communities. The District is the capital of the United States, a city of enormous symbolic importance, and yet it spent most of the twentieth century as a colony of Congress — governed by federally appointed committees rather than elected local officials until 1974, denied voting representation in Congress to this day, and subject to the direct intervention of federal authority in ways that shaped its development in distinctive ways. That this most politically constrained of American cities was also, for several decades, a majority-Black city whose residents exercised meaningful local power when they finally gained it, is one of the defining ironies of the city's history.

Washington became a majority-Black city as a consequence of the same forces that shaped Black urban demography across the country: the Great Migration of Black Southerners from the surrounding states of Maryland, Virginia, and the Carolinas, as well as from farther afield, drawn by federal government employment, service sector jobs, and the relatively greater social tolerance that characterized the federal city compared to the Deep South. By 1970, Washington was 71 percent Black, its large Black professional class — built around Howard University, the federal civil service, and the private service sector — anchored by a vast working-class and poor population housed in the neighborhoods of Northeast and Southeast Washington, in the historically vibrant U Street corridor, and in the public housing projects that Congress had funded and built throughout the mid-twentieth century.

Urban renewal and highway construction had already significantly disrupted Black Washington before the modern gentrification era began. The construction of Interstate 395 through the Southwest quadrant of the city in the 1950s and 1960s destroyed the Southwest Washington neighborhood — a densely populated mixed-race working-class community — and replaced it with the federal megaproject of L'Enfant Plaza, the Waterfront, and various federal agency buildings. The displaced residents, many of them Black, received inadequate relocation assistance and were scattered to neighborhoods across the city. The assassination of Martin Luther King Jr. in April 1968 triggered four days of uprisings in Washington that destroyed hundreds of businesses along U Street, H Street, and 7th Street NW — the commercial corridors of Black Washington — and accelerated the flight of Black-owned businesses, middle-class Black families, and white residents from the affected neighborhoods. The uprisings were a watershed in the city's history: they deepened the distress of Black neighborhoods that were already under stress from urban renewal and disinvestment, and they accelerated the geographic concentration of poverty in the neighborhoods that would, two decades later, be subject to intense gentrification pressure.

The crack epidemic of the late 1980s hit Washington with particular force. D.C.'s murder rate reached a peak of 479 homicides in 1991 — a rate that made it briefly the most violent large city in the United States — and the crack market dominated the street life of neighborhoods from Anacostia to Trinidad to the Sursum Corda public housing complex near Capitol Hill. The city's schools were in crisis, its finances were deteriorating (Congress placed D.C. under a financial control board in 1995), and the middle-class exodus from the city accelerated. By the mid-1990s, Washington appeared to be on a trajectory of deepening fiscal and social crisis, and major investment in its residential neighborhoods seemed unlikely.

The turnaround, when it came, was dramatic and rapid. Beginning in the late 1990s under Mayor Anthony Williams and accelerating through the 2000s, a combination of reduced crime, improved city finances, new transit investment, congressional modernization of the city's governance, and rising land values in the broader Washington metropolitan area drove a wave of investment in previously disinvested neighborhoods that transformed the city's demography and physical character with extraordinary speed. The U Street corridor, devastated in 1968 and depressed for thirty years, became one of the most fashionable entertainment and residential destinations in the city. Shaw, Logan Circle, Columbia Heights, Petworth, and dozens of other historically Black neighborhoods experienced rapid rent increases, demographic change, and the replacement of neighborhood-serving institutions with businesses oriented toward the new, higher-income residents.

The demolition of D.C.'s public housing — particularly the Sursum Corda complex near the Capitol, the Northwest One complex, and parts of the Barry Farm development in Anacostia — followed the national HOPE VI pattern: promises of right of return, mixed-income redevelopment, improved physical conditions, and, in many cases, a significantly smaller number of deeply subsidized units available to the lowest-income former residents. The displacement of public housing residents added to the displacement of private market renters being priced out of gentrifying neighborhoods to produce one of the most dramatic demographic transformations of any major American city. By around 2011, Washington was no longer a majority-Black city. By 2020, the Black population had fallen to approximately 41 percent and was continuing to decline.

The political dimensions of Washington's transformation are particularly revealing. The District's lack of congressional representation — its status as a colony of Congress — meant that decisions made in Congress about the city's budget, governance, and development trajectory shaped outcomes in ways that D.C. residents could not directly contest through federal elections. Federal policies about public housing, about federally-owned land in the city, about the federal workforce, and about the massive federal real estate footprint that dominates the geography of the District all shaped the conditions under which gentrification occurred. The city's transformation was not simply a product of market forces but of the specific political context in which federal power over local development decisions operated.

The response of Washington's Black community to gentrification has been varied and contested. Some long-term Black homeowners welcomed rising property values as the belated reward for decades of loyalty to neighborhoods that had been undervalued. Some community organizations developed affordable housing and other anti-displacement strategies that preserved some low-income residency in the gentrifying neighborhoods. Others organized politically against displacement, fighting redevelopment plans, advocating for tenant protections, and demanding right-of-return commitments for displaced public housing residents. Still others simply left — moving to Prince George's County in Maryland, to the growing Black suburbs of the Washington exurbs, or entirely out of the metropolitan area — carrying with them the community ties, institutional memberships, and social networks that had sustained Black Washington for generations.

Everyday Life and Social History

Living Through Displacement: Ordinary Life in the Changing Neighborhood

The statistics on displacement — the percentage change in neighborhood demographics, the average rent increase per year, the number of public housing units demolished — capture something real about the transformation of Black urban neighborhoods. But they do not capture the texture of what it actually feels like to live through that transformation. The everyday experience of displacement — or of watching a neighborhood you have known your whole life become unrecognizable — is a form of loss that is not easily quantified but is deeply felt by the people who experience it.

For a long-term renter in a gentrifying neighborhood, the experience of displacement often begins not with an eviction notice but with small, accumulating changes that signal what is coming. A favorite restaurant closes, replaced by something with a chalkboard menu and prices that seem impossible for a neighborhood that was, until recently, poor. New neighbors move in who do not acknowledge you on the street in the way that the old neighbors did. A building down the block is scaffolded and renovated, emerging months later with a new name and rent prices that exclude everyone you know. The corner store where everyone knew your name is replaced by a boutique that sells things you would never buy. These changes are not individually catastrophic. They are individually small. But they accumulate into a transformation of the social world you inhabit, until the neighborhood that housed your community no longer houses you.

For a homeowner who has lived in the same house for thirty or forty years, gentrification can be simultaneously liberating and destabilizing. The paper value of the home may have increased dramatically — a house worth $80,000 in 1990 might be worth $500,000 by 2015 — but that increase is only realizable by selling and leaving. Staying means paying dramatically higher property taxes on a fixed income. The community that made the house a home — the neighbors who looked out for your children, the church down the street, the doctors and businesses you trusted — has dispersed. The house has become more valuable at the same moment that the neighborhood that gave it its meaning has ceased to exist.

For young people who grew up in gentrifying neighborhoods, the transformation can feel like a betrayal of place itself. The blocks where they came of age — where they learned the street-level geography of their world, where they formed the friendships that shaped their identities, where the institutions that recognized them as belonging existed — are transformed into environments that treat them as alien. The new coffee shop does not know them. The new residents look at them with unease. The police, whose attention in their neighborhoods has historically been intense, have not retreated; in many gentrifying neighborhoods, calls to police from new residents about "suspicious" behavior increase, and long-term residents find themselves surveilled in spaces that were their home. The experience of becoming a stranger in your own neighborhood — of finding that the social geography you navigated is being deleted and replaced by something that excludes you — is a form of dislocation that researchers have documented in study after study of gentrifying communities.

For community organizations, churches, and other institutions serving long-term residents, the experience of gentrification is often one of gradual institutional crisis. The congregation that filled the church on Sunday mornings grows smaller as members move away. The community center that offered after-school programs and job training finds that the families it served are no longer in the neighborhood, while the new residents have no need for its services and no connection to its history. The social service agency that helped with benefits applications and housing assistance finds its case load changing — the people who most need its services are being displaced beyond the boundaries of the neighborhood it serves — and its funding, which was often tied to the demographics of the neighborhood, is threatened by the change. These institutions are not destroyed all at once. They are hollowed out gradually, as the communities they served disperse and the neighborhoods they anchor become, in demographic terms, different places.

The loss of institutions is also the loss of the physical spaces of community memory. The church where funerals were held for three generations of a family, where couples were married, where children were baptized and confirmed, and where the community gathered in crisis, is not simply a building. It is a repository of collective memory, a place where the history of the community is physically embodied. When these buildings are sold, converted to condominiums, or allowed to decay as their congregations shrink, the community loses not only a physical space but the access to the collective history that the space housed. The displacement of Black communities from their neighborhoods is not only a housing crisis. It is a crisis of cultural and historical continuity — a loss of the places through which community memory is transmitted and community identity is sustained.

Geography of Redevelopment: Washington, D.C. and the Changing Black City

Washington, D.C. provides an unusually clear geographic illustration of the processes this chapter describes. Students studying the geography of Washington's redevelopment should be able to identify and analyze the following spatial patterns:

  • The historic Black neighborhoods: Shaw, Columbia Heights, U Street corridor, Petworth, Trinidad, Anacostia, and Barry Farm represent the geographic distribution of Washington's historically Black residential and commercial landscape. Examining where these neighborhoods are located relative to downtown and to white neighborhoods reveals how segregation shaped the city's spatial structure.
  • Urban renewal and highway clearance: The construction of Southwest Washington as a federal office and housing district, the routing of I-395 through the Southwest, and the clearance of neighborhoods along the proposed Inner Loop highway all represent the geographic footprint of mid-twentieth-century redevelopment. Mapping these clearances against the location of Black neighborhoods illustrates the racial logic of highway and urban renewal routing decisions.
  • Public housing locations: Washington's public housing was concentrated in specific areas — the Sursum Corda complex near the Capitol, Barry Farm in Southeast, various scattered sites in Northeast — and these locations reflect both the political constraints on where Black public housing could be placed and the geography of land availability when these projects were built. The concentration of public housing in areas with limited transit access and poor schools reflects the spatial logic of racial containment.
  • The gentrification gradient: Mapping property value changes or demographic shifts over time reveals a spatial pattern in which gentrification moved outward from the city center — beginning in Capitol Hill, Logan Circle, and the U Street corridor in the late 1990s, spreading to Columbia Heights and Shaw in the early 2000s, then to Petworth, Brookland, and Trinidad in the 2010s, and most recently reaching toward Anacostia and far Northeast. This gradient reflects how gentrification expands as land values in inner neighborhoods rise and push demand outward.
  • The Anacostia boundary: The Anacostia River functions as a significant geographic boundary in Washington, separating the gentrifying Northwest and Northeast from the historically poorer and more racially isolated Southeast. The slow and contested expansion of gentrification across this boundary in recent years illustrates how geographic barriers — physical and symbolic — can affect the spatial dynamics of urban redevelopment.
  • Prince George's County: Examining the geography of Washington's metropolitan area, and particularly the concentration of Black residents in Prince George's County, Maryland — the primary destination for Black Washingtonians displaced from the District — reveals the metropolitan scale of displacement and the ways in which the consequences of urban redevelopment are distributed across municipal boundaries.

Primary Source Window

Federal Housing Policy as Historical Evidence: HOPE VI and the Promise of "Transformation"

Type: Federal program documentation and policy discourse  ·  Era: 1992–2010  ·  Topic: Public housing demolition and mixed-income redevelopment

Context: The HOPE VI program, authorized by Congress in 1992, was accompanied by a substantial body of policy documentation, program guidelines, local planning documents, and political discourse that articulated the justifications for demolishing public housing and replacing it with mixed-income developments. This discourse is itself a form of primary source evidence, because it reveals the assumptions, priorities, and values that shaped federal housing policy at a crucial moment in urban history.

The language of "transformation": HOPE VI documents consistently used the language of "transformation" — of neighborhoods, of communities, of individual residents. Public housing would be transformed from concentrations of poverty into mixed-income communities. Former residents would be transformed from welfare-dependent to self-sufficient. Neighborhoods would be transformed from isolated enclaves of distress to connected, vibrant parts of the city fabric. This language of transformation framed the program as a humanitarian project, a rescue of the poorest residents from conditions that were doing them harm.

HUD's program documentation described HOPE VI as designed to "eradicate severely distressed public housing" and replace it with "viable mixed-income communities," arguing that the concentrated poverty of large public housing projects caused "social pathologies" that could be reduced by mixing incomes and physically reconnecting projects to surrounding neighborhoods. The emphasis throughout was on the harm done to residents by concentrated poverty — and the corresponding assumption that dispersal of that poverty through demolition and mixed-income replacement was, therefore, beneficial to residents.

What the documentation reveals: The HOPE VI documentation reveals several important assumptions about poverty, community, and urban space that were embedded in federal housing policy in the 1990s. First, it assumes that concentrated poverty causes negative social outcomes — a claim that has empirical support but that obscures the structural forces (redlining, segregation, disinvestment) that produced that concentration in the first place. Second, it assumes that physical dispersal of poverty through demolition and vouchers will produce better outcomes for displaced residents — an assumption that research has only partially supported. Third, it largely elides the question of whether the demolished housing represents a net reduction in affordable housing supply — a question that became increasingly important as housing costs rose in the 2000s. Fourth, it treats community bonds and social networks — the informal infrastructure of community life that was embedded in even the most distressed projects — as impediments to transformation rather than as resources to be preserved.

What the documentation does not reveal: The HOPE VI documentation does not reveal the political interests that drove demolition decisions — the real estate developers who stood to benefit from cleared land in central-city locations, the city governments that wanted to increase their tax bases, the neighboring affluent communities that wanted to reduce the concentration of poverty adjacent to their neighborhoods. It does not reveal the experiences of the residents who did not return to redeveloped sites — who used their vouchers to move to other concentrated poverty neighborhoods, who experienced the loss of community bonds and support networks, who found that the transformation promised by HOPE VI was, for them, a story of loss rather than liberation. Reading the program documentation against the research evidence about what actually happened to displaced residents reveals the gap between policy intention and lived experience that is one of the recurring themes of American social policy history.

Questions for students:

  1. The HOPE VI documentation uses the language of "transformation" and "revitalization" to describe its goals. What assumptions about poverty, community, and urban space are embedded in this language? Whose perspective does it reflect, and whose perspective does it omit?
  2. Policy documents are written to justify and advance specific programs and perspectives. How should historians read policy documents — what can they tell us, and what are their limits as evidence about what actually happened to the people affected by the policies they describe?
  3. The HOPE VI program produced measurable improvements in some outcome measures (crime rates, physical conditions) while producing other outcomes that were harmful (net loss of affordable housing units, displacement without return). How should policymakers and historians evaluate programs with mixed consequences? Who gets to decide which outcomes matter most?
  4. The gap between the promise of "right of return" in HOPE VI documentation and the reality of residents who did not return to redeveloped sites illustrates a recurring pattern in American social policy: formal commitments that are not enforced. What conditions are necessary for formal policy commitments to be honored in practice? What happens when they are not?
Historical Debate

Does Gentrification Help or Harm Urban Communities? Competing Interpretations

The question: The debate over gentrification's consequences is one of the most consequential and contested in contemporary urban policy. It is not simply a debate about economics or statistics — it is a debate about what kind of city we want to live in, whose interests urban policy should serve, and what justice requires in the distribution of the costs and benefits of urban change. Students should understand the strongest versions of each major position and the evidence that supports and complicates each.

Interpretation A — Gentrification Benefits Urban Communities: Proponents of this interpretation argue that gentrification brings genuine and significant benefits to urban neighborhoods. Rising property values expand the tax base of cities that have been starved of resources for decades, enabling investment in schools, parks, transit, and services that benefit all residents, including low-income ones. Falling crime rates in gentrifying neighborhoods benefit existing residents who were the primary victims of the violence that characterized distressed neighborhoods at their worst. Physical rehabilitation of deteriorated housing stock improves conditions for those who remain. New commercial activity brings amenities and employment opportunities. The entry of higher-income households creates political pressure for improved services and infrastructure that benefits all residents. Scholars including Quigley, Rothenberg, and Vigdor have argued that the evidence on gentrification's effects on low-income residents is more mixed than critics suggest, with some studies finding that many low-income residents experience real improvements in neighborhood conditions without being displaced.
Interpretation B — Gentrification Harms Black Communities Through Displacement: Critics of gentrification argue that its benefits are captured primarily by incoming higher-income residents and property owners, while its costs are borne primarily by long-term low-income residents who are displaced. Scholars including Neil Smith, Peter Marcuse, Samuel Stein, and others in the tradition of urban political economy argue that gentrification should be understood not as a neutral market process but as a mechanism of capital accumulation that exploits the "rent gap" created by decades of deliberate disinvestment in Black neighborhoods. In this view, the same forces — redlining, urban renewal, blockbusting, disinvestment — that created the distressed conditions in these neighborhoods are now being monetized by capital flows that appropriate the community assets built by long-term residents without compensating them for either their historical losses or their current displacement. Displacement in this framework is not an unfortunate side effect of a generally beneficial process but is structural to the process itself.
Interpretation C — Gentrification Effects Are Contingent on Policy Context: A third position, associated with urban policy scholars including Miriam Zuk, Richard Rothstein, and others, argues that gentrification's consequences are not fixed but depend critically on the policy context in which it occurs. In cities with strong tenant protections, robust affordable housing requirements, community land trust programs, and strong enforcement of fair housing laws, the displacement consequences of rising land values can be significantly mitigated. In cities that prioritize new investment and tax base without corresponding investment in anti-displacement protections, displacement is more rapid and more harmful. In this view, the debate between "gentrification is good" and "gentrification is bad" is less useful than the question of what policy choices determine whose interests urban reinvestment serves. This interpretation is supported by comparative research showing that cities with stronger tenant protections and affordable housing policies have significantly lower rates of displacement-associated harm than cities without such protections.

The evidence and its complexity: The empirical research on gentrification's effects is substantial but contested. Studies using different methodologies, measuring different outcomes, and examining different cities have produced different findings. Some find that low-income residents in gentrifying neighborhoods are not displaced at higher rates than in non-gentrifying neighborhoods (though this finding may reflect the difficulty of measuring indirect displacement). Others find significant displacement effects, particularly for renters without long-term leases. Research on the consequences for displaced residents, as noted earlier in this chapter, consistently finds outcomes that range from modest to poor for the most economically vulnerable households. The debate over methodology is not merely technical — it reflects genuine disagreements about what counts as harm, whose outcomes should be measured, and over what time horizon the analysis should proceed.

Student task: Using evidence from this chapter, develop your own assessment of the gentrification debate. Which interpretation do you find most consistent with the historical evidence? What additional evidence would you want to examine? And how does the racial history of the neighborhoods in question — the specific history of how they were created through disinvestment and racial exclusion — affect your evaluation of the justice and injustice of their current transformation?

Multiple Perspectives

Urban Redevelopment: Multiple Perspectives on Neighborhood Change

Long-term Black renters: For Black renters who have lived in a neighborhood for decades, gentrification represents a double displacement: first, the displacement of their community from the broader city by the forces of segregation and disinvestment that created their neighborhood as a Black space; and now, the displacement from the one place that had been theirs. The experience is often one of betrayal — of watching the neighborhood that was created through exclusion, and that was sustained through collective community effort, become valuable at precisely the moment when that value is being extracted by people who did not share the exclusion or contribute to the effort. The particular bitterness is that the same society that excluded Black people from valuable neighborhoods for generations is now extracting value from the neighborhoods Black people made for themselves.
Long-term Black homeowners: The experience of Black homeowners in gentrifying neighborhoods is more ambivalent. For homeowners who have maintained their properties through decades of disinvestment, rising property values represent a long-delayed recognition of the value that had been systematically suppressed by redlining and neighborhood neglect. Many are able to realize significant wealth gains by selling into rising markets, gains that represent a modest and belated restitution for the wealth denied by decades of housing discrimination. But homeownership does not simply mean financial benefit from rising values — it also means rising property taxes, the loss of community as neighbors and institutions depart, and the difficult choice between staying in a neighborhood that is becoming unrecognizable and cashing out and leaving the place that gave one's life its geographic meaning.
Incoming higher-income residents: For the higher-income and often white residents who move into gentrifying Black neighborhoods, the process is typically experienced in more personal terms — as a choice to invest in an undervalued neighborhood, to contribute to urban vitality, to be part of a changing city. Most gentrifiers do not think of themselves as displacing anyone; they are making individual housing decisions in response to market prices and personal preferences. The structural consequences of thousands of individual decisions are not visible to any individual decision-maker. This perspective is not dishonest — it is simply the perspective of someone making a rational individual choice in a market context, without the analytical framework to see the structural forces that make that choice available and the structural consequences of aggregating such choices.
City government and planners: From the perspective of city governments, rising property values in formerly distressed neighborhoods represent fiscal relief — an expansion of the tax base that funds schools, transit, and services. City governments have often actively promoted gentrification through investment in transit and amenities, tax incentives for development, historic preservation designations, and the selective enforcement of housing codes. The tension between the fiscal benefits of gentrification and the displacement costs for existing residents is one of the defining political tensions in the governance of American cities today. City governments that prioritize fiscal health over anti-displacement can capture the tax benefits of rising property values while their most vulnerable residents bear the costs.
Community organizations and CDCs: Nonprofit community development organizations occupy a complicated position in gentrifying neighborhoods. On one hand, they are often the primary advocates for anti-displacement policy and affordable housing production, fighting to preserve some low-income residency in neighborhoods under intense market pressure. On the other hand, they have sometimes been criticized for facilitating gentrification by improving neighborhood conditions and producing market-rate-adjacent development that contributes to rising land values. Some community organizations have responded to gentrification pressure by developing robust community land trust programs, advocating for strong tenant protections, and engaging in direct anti-displacement organizing. Others have been marginalized as their communities dispersed and their political bases eroded.
Street organization members: For members of street organizations whose territorial identities are rooted in specific neighborhoods, gentrification and the associated demolition of public housing represent a form of annihilation of place — a destruction of the geographic anchors of identity, community, and organizational structure. The experience is not simply one of housing loss but of the erasure of the places that gave life its social meaning. Research interviews with displaced street organization members consistently find deep grief about the loss of neighborhood — a grief that is not merely sentimental but reflects the real destruction of the social networks and organizational structures on which their safety and identity depended. This grief is often not legible to newcomers or to policymakers, who see only deteriorated physical conditions where displaced residents see home.
Cause and Effect

The Displacement of Black Urban Communities: Structural Causes and Cascading Consequences

Long-Term Structural Causes

Decades of redlining and housing discrimination that denied Black families access to capital and constrained their residential options to specific neighborhoods; urban renewal and highway construction that destroyed Black neighborhoods and scattered communities; the concentration of poverty in isolated public housing projects; the crack epidemic and the mass incarceration that followed; and the long disinvestment in Black neighborhoods that was partly the result and partly the cause of their concentrated poverty.

Proximate Causes of Contemporary Gentrification

The growth of the knowledge economy and its geographic concentration in a small number of major metropolitan areas; declining crime rates in formerly distressed neighborhoods; public investment in transit and amenities in central cities; changing residential preferences among educated younger workers; tax incentives for real estate development; HOPE VI demolition of public housing that cleared land for market-rate development; and the accumulated undervaluation of centrally located land in formerly redlined neighborhoods.

The Processes of Displacement

Rent increases driven by rising market demand; condominium conversions that convert rental housing to for-sale housing; property tax increases that price out low-income homeowners; demolition of public housing with inadequate replacement of deeply affordable units; the transformation of commercial corridors to businesses serving newcomers; and the dispersal of community institutions whose congregants and clients have moved away.

Long-Term Consequences

Demographic transformation of historically Black neighborhoods; destruction of the institutional infrastructure of Black community life; disruption of territorial identities and social networks of street organizations; dispersal of Black urban populations to outer suburbs and different metropolitan areas; political disempowerment of Black urban communities as their geographic concentration in specific cities decreases; loss of Black institutional wealth embedded in churches, community organizations, and small businesses; and the cascading consequences for educational stability, family stability, and intergenerational community continuity.

Historical complexity: The displacement of Black urban communities in the gentrification era cannot be understood as simply a product of market forces or simply a product of government policy. It is the outcome of the interaction between accumulated historical conditions — the geography of racial exclusion and disinvestment — and current market dynamics and policy choices. Recognizing this complexity does not mean that nothing can be done about displacement; it means that effective anti-displacement policy must address both the structural historical conditions that produced concentrated poverty and the current policy choices that determine how the benefits and costs of reinvestment are distributed.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Territory and Belonging in Black Urban Communities: Continuity Across Eras

What Changed

  • The physical neighborhood geography of major Black urban communities has been dramatically transformed — neighborhoods that were the institutional and residential heart of Black urban life for a generation are now demographic minorities in their own places, or have been effectively eliminated as Black neighborhoods
  • The relationship between street organizations and their territorial bases has been fundamentally disrupted by public housing demolition and gentrification-driven displacement, as the places that anchored organizational identity have been cleared or transformed
  • The geographic concentration of Black populations in specific urban neighborhoods — the product of a century of housing discrimination and the Great Migration — is declining as displacement pushes Black residents toward outer suburbs, different neighborhoods, and different cities
  • The institutional infrastructure of Black urban life — churches, CDCs, community organizations, schools, small businesses — is under severe stress in many cities as the communities it served have dispersed
  • The political power of Black urban communities, which was partly a product of geographic concentration, is being restructured as that concentration disperses

What Stayed the Same

  • The fundamental dynamic of racial capitalism — in which Black spaces and Black labor are devalued during periods of white abundance and appropriated during periods of capital need — has continued from the redlining era through the urban renewal era to the gentrification era; only the specific mechanism changes
  • The experience of displacement — of forced geographic mobility in response to forces outside community control — has been a constant feature of Black urban life since the Great Migration, with different engines (Jim Crow violence, urban renewal, HOPE VI, gentrification) driving successive waves of forced movement
  • The capacity of Black communities to build institutional infrastructure in conditions of adversity — to create churches, community organizations, mutual aid networks, and cultural institutions that sustain community life in the face of exclusion — has persisted across all these challenges, even as specific institutions are lost
  • The role of territory in structuring Black urban social life, and the deep attachment to place that characterizes both community institutions and street organizations, has remained constant even as the specific places of attachment have been repeatedly transformed or destroyed
  • The gap between formal legal rights and substantive economic and social equality has persisted: Black people now have formal fair housing rights that did not exist during the redlining era, but those rights have not prevented the displacement effects of gentrification from operating in deeply racialized ways

Why the pattern matters: The continuity between the various eras of displacement in Black urban America — from urban renewal to HOPE VI to gentrification — is the continuity of a structural relationship: the systematic extraction of value from Black communities through cycles of devaluation and revaluation of Black neighborhood land. This structural continuity suggests that addressing displacement effectively requires not simply adjusting the policies of the current moment but confronting the deeper history of racial capitalism that produced the geography of Black urban inequality in the first place.

Constitutional Connection

Property Rights, Eminent Domain, and Equal Protection: Constitutional Dimensions of Urban Redevelopment

The history of urban redevelopment in Black communities raises fundamental constitutional questions about property rights, equal protection, and the limits of government power over private property and community life. These questions have been litigated in courts across the country, and the constitutional framework that has emerged shapes both the possibilities and the limits of anti-displacement policy.

Eminent domain and "blight": The urban renewal program was constitutionally authorized by the Supreme Court's 1954 decision in Berman v. Parker, which upheld the use of eminent domain to condemn non-blighted property in a "blighted" area and transfer it to private developers — a significant expansion of the traditional eminent domain power, which had been understood as limited to government use of acquired property. Berman gave cities broad authority to condemn any property in a designated renewal area, regardless of its individual condition, and to transfer that property to private redevelopers. The decision was unanimous and was written by Justice William Douglas, a liberal, illustrating that the constitutional authorization of urban renewal was not a partisan or ideological choice but a bipartisan affirmation of broad government power over urban land use. The decision has never been explicitly overruled, and its broad authorization of eminent domain for economic development purposes was reaffirmed — controversially — in the Supreme Court's 2005 decision in Kelo v. City of New London.

The Fair Housing Act and racial discrimination in redevelopment: The Fair Housing Act of 1968 prohibits racial discrimination in housing, including in the practices of local governments as well as private actors. Advocates have argued that racially discriminatory patterns in urban renewal and public housing placement violated the Fair Housing Act's requirement that federal housing programs affirmatively further fair housing — a legal standard that requires more than simply avoiding explicit discrimination but requires active efforts to undo the effects of past discrimination. Courts have found fair housing violations in specific redevelopment decisions — including cases involving the systematic placement of public housing in minority neighborhoods and the denial of affordable housing in predominantly white neighborhoods — but enforcement has been inconsistent and the standard of proof required to establish liability has been high.

Equal protection and disparate impact: The Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment requires that government action be racially neutral in its intent, but the Supreme Court has held that disparate racial impact alone is insufficient to establish a constitutional violation — proof of discriminatory intent is required. This means that urban renewal and highway construction decisions that devastated Black communities are constitutional as long as their explicit justification was non-racial (slum clearance, transportation efficiency), even if their predictable and predicted racial effects were known and accepted by decision-makers. The constitutional standard thus permits facially neutral policies that operate with racially discriminatory effects, a limitation that civil rights advocates have repeatedly challenged.

Right to housing: The United States Constitution does not include an affirmative right to housing, in contrast to the constitutions of some other countries and to the Universal Declaration of Human Rights. The absence of a constitutional housing right means that the government has no affirmative obligation to ensure that displaced residents have access to adequate alternative housing, and that anti-displacement advocates must rely on statutory protections (the Fair Housing Act, tenant protection laws, public housing rights) rather than constitutional entitlement. This constitutional gap is one of the most significant legal constraints on anti-displacement policy in the United States.

Kelo and community reaction: The Supreme Court's 2005 decision in Kelo v. City of New London, which upheld the use of eminent domain to transfer private property to private developers for economic development purposes, generated enormous public backlash and led many states to enact legislation restricting the use of eminent domain for economic development. While the backlash was bipartisan, it was particularly strong in conservative and libertarian communities that emphasized property rights. The irony is that the same communities most likely to oppose eminent domain for economic development are also the communities that historically benefited most from urban renewal's racially targeted application of eminent domain — illustrating the complex relationship between constitutional principle and racial application in American legal history.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has traced the history of urban redevelopment in Black communities through more than half a century of federal policy, market forces, and community response. It has argued that the gentrification and displacement that are transforming Black urban neighborhoods in the early twenty-first century cannot be understood in isolation from the longer history of deliberate disinvestment and structural exclusion that preceded them — that the current reinvestment in these neighborhoods is, in a deep sense, a continuation of the same logic that produced their earlier disinvestment. The neighborhoods that are being gentrified today were made poor through a series of specific, racially motivated policy decisions. The wealth that is now being extracted through rising property values was in significant part created by the communities that are being displaced. Understanding this history is essential to understanding both the injustice of displacement and the structural conditions that make anti-displacement policy so difficult to implement effectively.

The federal urban renewal program of the 1940s through 1970s established the first major wave of post-war redevelopment, clearing hundreds of thousands of acres of urban land that were disproportionately inhabited by Black communities, displacing hundreds of thousands of Black households, and replacing vibrant mixed-use neighborhoods with civic centers, highways, hospitals, universities, and housing projects that served other populations. The combination of eminent domain, federal subsidy to private developers, and inadequate relocation assistance created a template for racial displacement through public policy that would be repeated, with variations, in subsequent eras of redevelopment. James Baldwin's observation that urban renewal meant "Negro removal" captured what the statistical evidence confirms: that the program systematically targeted Black neighborhoods for clearance while claiming to serve the general public interest.

The construction of the Interstate Highway System added a second and equally destructive form of neighborhood clearance, as highway routes were systematically directed through Black communities — not as a neutral consequence of engineering requirements but as a deliberate choice by planners and political authorities who saw the destruction of Black neighborhoods as both technically convenient and socially desirable. The physical marks of these decisions — the elevated highways cutting through formerly vibrant Black commercial corridors in Nashville, Miami, New Orleans, and dozens of other cities — are still visible in the urban landscape, monuments to the power relationships that shaped mid-twentieth-century urban planning.

The public housing that was built during this era became, over time, one of the primary geographic expressions of concentrated Black poverty. Segregated, underfunded, isolated from economic opportunity, and managed with contempt for its residents, the large public housing projects of American cities were not the cause of concentrated Black poverty but its geographic concentration. The crisis of these projects — severe by the 1980s and catastrophic by the early 1990s — became the justification for the HOPE VI program and the mass demolition of public housing that followed. HOPE VI produced genuine improvements in the physical conditions of many affected neighborhoods but also resulted in the net loss of hundreds of thousands of deeply subsidized units and the displacement of tens of thousands of the most economically vulnerable residents to other concentrated poverty areas or worse.

The market-driven gentrification that has accelerated since the late 1990s represents the current phase of a long history of capital moving in and out of Black neighborhoods in ways determined by market logic and racial geography rather than community need. The same central-city locations that capital abandoned during the deindustrialization and crack epidemic eras became attractive to higher-income residents and real estate investors as the knowledge economy produced a class of workers who valued urban density, walkability, and cultural amenity. The result has been a dramatic demographic transformation of neighborhoods that were historically the heart of Black urban life, as rising rents, condominium conversions, and property tax increases displaced long-term Black renters, and the institutional infrastructure of Black community life was hollowed out by the dispersal of its constituent communities.

Washington, D.C. provides the clearest illustration of these dynamics, partly because the demographic transformation there has been so dramatic and so rapid, and partly because the District's unique political status as a colony of Congress adds a specifically political dimension to the forces reshaping its neighborhoods. The transformation of Shaw, Columbia Heights, and the U Street corridor — from the institutional heart of Black Washington to predominantly white and higher-income neighborhoods — took place over approximately fifteen years and was driven by a combination of declining crime rates, improved city finances, new transit investment, and the broader economic forces that were driving knowledge economy workers toward central cities across the country. The loss of Black political majority status in the District, and the dispersal of the Black population to Prince George's County and beyond, represents one of the most significant demographic changes in any major American city in the early twenty-first century.

The consequences of urban redevelopment for street organizations are significant but not simple. The demolition of public housing projects disrupted the territorial identities and social structures of street organizations in ways that research suggests contributed, in some cities, to increased violence in receiving neighborhoods as established territorial arrangements were disrupted and multiple displaced populations were concentrated in new areas. The slower, more gradual displacement associated with market-driven gentrification has eroded the social bases of street organizations more gradually, as their constituent communities scattered and the neighborhoods that anchored their identities were transformed into environments that excluded them. These displacement dynamics are essential to understanding the fragmentation of street organizations — the decline of large, hierarchically organized groups in favor of smaller, more volatile crews — that is the subject of the next chapter.

The chapter has also traced the development of anti-displacement policy tools — community land trusts, affordable housing requirements, tenant protections, right-of-return commitments, community development corporations — and found that while these tools can slow or mitigate displacement in specific contexts, they have generally been insufficient to prevent the broader demographic transformation of Black neighborhoods in the face of powerful market forces and inadequate political will. The limits of anti-displacement policy are both technical (no policy tool yet developed can fully counteract the effect of rapidly rising land values in tight housing markets) and political (the interests that benefit from gentrification — real estate owners, developers, city governments seeking tax revenue — are more organized and more politically powerful than the interests that bear its costs). Effective anti-displacement policy requires both better tools and a different alignment of political interests, neither of which is easily achieved.

The deeper lesson of this chapter is about the relationship between disinvestment and reinvestment in a racially stratified capitalist economy. Black neighborhoods were created through exclusion — forced by redlining, segregation, and housing discrimination into specific geographic areas. They were devalued through deliberate disinvestment — starved of capital, services, and political attention — in ways that concentrated poverty and distress. And they are now being revalued through reinvestment — gentrified, redeveloped, and repopulated — in ways that capture the value that the original communities created without compensating them for their historical losses or enabling them to benefit from the appreciation of the places they built. This cycle of exclusion, devaluation, and appropriation is not accidental. It is the specific mechanism through which racial capitalism has operated in urban space across generations, and understanding it is essential to understanding both the injustice of displacement and the structural conditions that must be changed to produce a different outcome.

The chapter has also illustrated the resilience and agency of Black urban communities in the face of displacement. Community organizations have developed affordable housing, advocated for anti-displacement policy, and organized politically against the forces transforming their neighborhoods. Long-term residents have maintained their communities and their institutions under conditions of intense pressure. Individual families have made difficult choices — to stay or to go, to fight or to adapt — with courage and creativity in conditions not of their choosing. The story of urban redevelopment in Black communities is not only a story of what was done to them. It is also a story of what they have done in response, and that story of resistance and adaptation is an essential part of the historical record.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Affordable housing
Housing that costs no more than thirty percent of a household's gross income, according to the standard federal definition. In practice, "affordable housing" in policy contexts usually refers to subsidized housing units whose rents are set below market rates through government programs such as the Low-Income Housing Tax Credit, housing vouchers, or public housing.
Anti-displacement policy
Government policies aimed at protecting existing residents of neighborhoods from being forced to leave due to rising housing costs. Anti-displacement policies include rent stabilization, just-cause eviction requirements, inclusionary zoning, community land trusts, right-of-return guarantees for displaced public housing residents, and various forms of emergency housing assistance.
Blight
A planning and legal designation applied to urban areas considered to be in deteriorated condition, typically used to justify clearance and redevelopment under urban renewal and eminent domain powers. The term has been criticized for its racial application — with "blighted" areas disproportionately designated in Black neighborhoods — and for its use to justify displacement of existing residents to benefit new development.
Community Development Block Grant (CDBG)
A flexible federal grant program created by the Housing and Community Development Act of 1974 that provides funding to cities and counties for housing, community development, and economic development activities. CDBG replaced the categorical urban renewal program and gave local governments more discretion in directing community development spending, though it also reduced the overall federal commitment to urban reinvestment.
Community Development Corporation (CDC)
A nonprofit organization that develops affordable housing, commercial space, and community facilities in low-income neighborhoods, typically using federal subsidies including the Low-Income Housing Tax Credit and Community Development Block Grants. CDCs emerged from the community organizing movements of the 1960s and became the primary vehicle for community-controlled neighborhood development in the following decades.
Community land trust
A nonprofit organization that owns land in perpetuity and leases it to homeowners and renters at below-market rates, preserving affordability regardless of market conditions. Community land trusts are considered among the most effective anti-displacement tools because they permanently remove land from speculative markets, but they require significant capital to acquire land and can only protect housing on the land they own.
Concentrated poverty
The geographic concentration of poor households in specific neighborhoods, typically above a threshold of forty percent poverty rate. Concentrated poverty in American cities is largely a product of housing segregation policies — including redlining, restrictive covenants, and the placement of public housing — that restricted low-income Black families to specific geographic areas. It is associated with reduced access to quality schools, employment, and other opportunity-enhancing institutions.
Cultural displacement
A form of displacement in which long-term residents remain physically in their neighborhoods but find that the cultural character of the neighborhood — its businesses, social norms, population, and institutions — has been so transformed by gentrification that they no longer feel at home. Cultural displacement is not captured in standard displacement statistics but is deeply experienced by longtime residents of gentrifying neighborhoods.
Displacement
The process by which residents are forced to leave their neighborhoods, either directly through eviction or demolition, or indirectly through rising rents and costs that make it economically impossible to remain. Displacement includes direct, indirect, cultural, and exclusionary forms, all of which represent the forcing of low-income residents — disproportionately Black and Latina — from neighborhoods in which they have established community ties and lives.
Eminent domain
The government's power to compel the sale of private property for public use, with payment of just compensation to the owner. Urban renewal and highway construction used eminent domain extensively to acquire land in Black neighborhoods for clearance and redevelopment. The Supreme Court's decisions in Berman v. Parker (1954) and Kelo v. City of New London (2005) broadly interpreted the permissible uses of eminent domain to include economic development, not just traditional public uses.
Gentrification
The process by which higher-income residents and uses move into lower-income urban neighborhoods, accompanied by rising rents and property values, physical rehabilitation of buildings, the replacement of neighborhood-serving businesses, and the displacement of lower-income long-term residents. Coined by sociologist Ruth Glass in 1964, the term is contested politically but is widely used in scholarship on urban change.
HOPE VI
The federal program authorized in 1992 that funded the demolition and replacement of severely distressed public housing with mixed-income developments. HOPE VI demolished more than 100,000 units of public housing and produced significant improvements in neighborhood conditions but also resulted in the net loss of deeply subsidized affordable housing and the displacement of the most economically vulnerable former residents.
Housing choice voucher (Section 8)
Federal rental assistance that subsidizes the difference between a household's income-based contribution and market-rate rent, enabling low-income families to rent from private landlords. Vouchers became the primary form of federal housing assistance for the lowest-income households as public housing construction declined. Their effectiveness at facilitating moves to higher-opportunity neighborhoods is limited by landlord refusals, administrative barriers, and tight housing markets.
Inclusionary zoning
A zoning policy that requires developers of new market-rate housing to include a specified percentage of affordable units in their developments, either on-site or through payment of an in-lieu fee. Inclusionary zoning can produce affordable housing in gentrifying neighborhoods but typically produces limited numbers of units at affordability levels that are not deep enough for the lowest-income displaced residents.
Low-Income Housing Tax Credit (LIHTC)
A federal tax incentive, created in 1986, that encourages private investment in affordable rental housing by providing tax credits to investors who fund the development or rehabilitation of rental housing for low-income households. LIHTC became the primary federal mechanism for subsidizing affordable housing production in the 1990s and 2000s and has funded millions of units of affordable housing, though it typically serves moderate rather than the lowest-income households.
Mixed-income development
A housing development that includes residents of different income levels, typically combining market-rate, moderately subsidized, and deeply subsidized units. Mixed-income development is the central design principle of HOPE VI redevelopments and subsequent public housing transformation programs, premised on the theory that economic integration reduces concentrated poverty and improves outcomes for low-income residents.
Negro removal
James Baldwin's description of the urban renewal program, capturing its racial logic: the systematic clearance of Black neighborhoods and the displacement of their residents under the nominally race-neutral justification of eliminating "blight." The phrase has become canonical in critical urban history and reflects the reality that urban renewal programs across the country displaced Black residents at rates disproportionate to their share of the urban population.
Rent gap
A concept developed by geographer Neil Smith to explain the geography of gentrification, referring to the difference between the current rental income of a property (determined by existing low-income tenants) and the potential rental income that could be achieved if the property were redeveloped for higher-income use. The rent gap, in Smith's analysis, creates the economic incentive for capital to flow into disinvested neighborhoods when the gap becomes large enough to make redevelopment profitable.
Rent stabilization
A policy that limits the rate at which landlords can increase rents for existing tenants, typically allowing annual increases tied to inflation but prohibiting large sudden increases. Rent stabilization is one of the most effective short-to-medium-term anti-displacement tools for existing renters, but it typically applies only to older buildings and can be evaded through condo conversion, demolition, or building vacancy.
Right to return
A commitment made to public housing residents displaced by demolition that they will be able to return to the redeveloped site at comparable rent levels. Right-of-return commitments were included in many HOPE VI redevelopment plans but were frequently not honored in practice, because the number of deeply subsidized units in redeveloped mixed-income projects was significantly smaller than the number of households displaced from the demolished projects.
Scattered-site housing
An approach to providing affordable housing that places subsidized units in small numbers throughout different neighborhoods of a city rather than concentrating them in specific locations. Scattered-site housing was proposed as an alternative to large public housing projects, but has faced resistance in many cities from residents of higher-income neighborhoods who oppose the placement of affordable housing in their communities.
Urban renewal
The federal program authorized by the Housing Act of 1949 that funded the clearance and redevelopment of areas designated as blighted. Urban renewal gave local governments eminent domain powers to acquire land in designated areas and sell it to private developers at subsidized prices. It displaced an estimated one million households, a disproportionate share of them Black, and was characterized by James Baldwin as "Negro removal."

Major People and Places

Identification & Significance

James Baldwin (1924–1987)
Novelist and essayist who coined the phrase "urban renewal means Negro removal" and whose writing provides one of the most powerful analyses of the racial logic of mid-century urban redevelopment. Baldwin's observations about the destruction of Black communities in the name of progress remain foundational texts in the critical literature on gentrification and displacement.
Robert Moses (1888–1981)
New York City's dominant urban power broker for four decades, whose use of eminent domain, highway construction, and urban renewal to reshape New York — repeatedly at the expense of Black and Puerto Rican neighborhoods — established the template for racially targeted urban redevelopment that other cities followed. Robert Caro's The Power Broker (1974) remains the definitive account of Moses's methods and their consequences.
Jane Jacobs (1916–2006)
Urban theorist and activist whose The Death and Life of Great American Cities (1961) provided the most influential intellectual critique of urban renewal's destruction of mixed-use, pedestrian-scaled urban life. Jacobs organized successfully against Robert Moses's proposed Lower Manhattan Expressway, helping to preserve Greenwich Village, though her critique was more effectively employed by white middle-class communities than by the Black communities most devastated by urban renewal.
Marion Barry (1936–2014)
Washington, D.C.'s dominant political figure for two decades, whose mayoralty shaped the conditions under which Washington's later transformation occurred. Barry's legacy — combining Black political empowerment with administrative failure and personal scandal — illustrates the constraints on Black urban governance in an era of deindustrialization, crack epidemic, and federal oversight.
The Shaw Neighborhood, Washington, D.C.
The historic heart of Black Washington, home to Howard University, the Howard Theatre, and the U Street commercial corridor, devastated by urban renewal and the 1968 uprisings and transformed by gentrification from the late 1990s onward into one of the most rapidly changing neighborhoods in the country. Shaw's trajectory encapsulates the full arc of urban renewal, disinvestment, and gentrification in Black America.
The Robert Taylor Homes, Chicago
The largest public housing complex in the United States at its completion in 1962, housing 27,000 people in 28 towers along Chicago's State Street corridor. The Robert Taylor Homes became synonymous with concentrated poverty and violence before their demolition between 1998 and 2007, and their fate illustrates both the failures of concentrated public housing and the displacement consequences of HOPE VI demolition.
Cabrini-Green, Chicago
A public housing complex whose demolition between 1995 and 2011, driven in significant part by its location adjacent to the Gold Coast (one of Chicago's wealthiest neighborhoods), illustrates how land value and location drive public housing demolition decisions. The development that replaced Cabrini-Green includes some of the most expensive real estate in the city.
HOPE VI Program
The federal program that funded the demolition and replacement of severely distressed public housing with mixed-income developments, authorized in 1992. HOPE VI improved physical conditions in many affected neighborhoods but resulted in the net loss of hundreds of thousands of deeply subsidized units and the displacement of the most economically vulnerable public housing residents.
Harlem, New York City
America's most famous Black neighborhood, whose transformation from the cultural capital of Black America to a rapidly gentrifying mixed-income neighborhood illustrates the demographic transformation of historically Black urban communities across the country. Harlem's gentrification has generated intense scholarly and journalistic attention and community debate.
Community land trusts
Nonprofit organizations that hold land in community ownership in perpetuity, leasing it to homeowners and renters at below-market rates regardless of market conditions. Community land trusts represent one of the most effective anti-displacement tools and are being developed in an increasing number of cities as responses to gentrification pressure.
Anacostia, Washington, D.C.
The historically Black neighborhood east of the Anacostia River that has remained relatively isolated from D.C.'s gentrification until recently, illustrating how geographic barriers can temporarily contain gentrification pressure and how that pressure eventually overcomes such barriers.
The U Street Corridor, Washington, D.C.
The commercial and cultural heart of Black Washington for most of the twentieth century, devastated by the 1968 uprisings, and transformed by gentrification from the late 1990s into one of the city's most fashionable entertainment districts. The transformation of U Street is a defining example of cultural displacement — the replacement of a community's cultural geography by amenities serving an entirely different population.

Local history grounds our understanding of large structural forces in the actual experience of specific communities, and it often reveals dimensions of the redevelopment story that national accounts miss.

Select a historically Black neighborhood in an American city that has experienced one or more of the processes described in this chapter: urban renewal clearance, highway construction through Black communities, public housing construction and/or demolition, or market-driven gentrification. Good candidates include: Shaw or Columbia Heights in Washington, D.C.; Woodlawn or Bronzeville in Chicago; Harlem or Bedford-Stuyvesant in New York; West Oakland, California; Fourth Ward (Freedmen's Town) in Houston; Sweet Auburn in Atlanta; Overtown in Miami; or another neighborhood with accessible primary source documentation.
  2. Document the neighborhood's history across the major phases. Using academic sources, newspaper archives, municipal records, and oral history collections, trace the neighborhood's history from the era of the Great Migration through the current period. Pay particular attention to the three transitions: (a) the construction of the neighborhood as a Black community through migration and segregation; (b) the period of disinvestment and redevelopment (urban renewal, public housing, highway construction); and (c) the contemporary period of gentrification or redevelopment. For each phase, document the key events, policy decisions, and community responses.
  3. Find and analyze at least three primary sources. Locate at least three primary sources from different periods of the neighborhood's history. Good options include: urban renewal planning documents (often available in city planning department archives); HOLC redlining maps (digitized at the Mapping Inequality project); oral histories from neighborhood residents; community newspaper coverage; public housing authority records; neighborhood association records; or census data on demographic change. For each source, explain who created it, what it reveals about the neighborhood's history, and what its limits are as historical evidence.
  4. Interview a stakeholder if possible. If possible, conduct a brief interview with a long-term resident, community organization staff member, local business owner, or community historian with knowledge of the neighborhood's history. Use the interview to gather perspectives on the neighborhood's changes that are not available in documentary sources, and reflect on what the interview reveals that written sources could not.
  5. Apply the chapter's analytical framework. For each of the major themes of the chapter — the racial logic of urban renewal, the consequences of public housing concentration and demolition, the mechanisms of gentrification-era displacement, and the effects on community institutions and street-level social organization — assess how your chosen neighborhood's history fits, complicates, or challenges the national patterns described in the chapter. What does the local case add to the national picture?
  6. Produce your analysis. In a 5–7 page research paper or equivalent research presentation, describe your chosen neighborhood's history across the major phases, analyze how it illustrates the national patterns described in the chapter, discuss what the local case reveals that a purely national account misses, and reflect on the implications of this history for current anti-displacement policy debates. Your paper should use evidence carefully, acknowledge the limits of your sources, and engage with at least one competing interpretation of the neighborhood's history.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter has traced a history that begins with eminent domain orders and bulldozers in the 1950s and ends with rising rents and coffee shops in the 2010s — and has argued that these two very different-looking phenomena are, at a structural level, expressions of the same underlying logic: the appropriation of Black urban space by forces that Black communities do not control. That argument is not simply rhetorical. It is grounded in the historical evidence of how Black neighborhoods were created — through the forced geographic concentration of the Great Migration and the housing exclusion that channeled Black migrants into specific areas — how they were devalued — through the deliberate disinvestment of redlining, urban renewal's selective clearance, and decades of public neglect — and how they are now being revalued — through gentrification that captures for new investors and residents the value that Black communities created through decades of institution-building in conditions of exclusion.

The implications for this textbook's central subject — the history of Black street organizations — are significant and not fully visible until the displacement story is told. The territorial identities that street organizations developed were not simply organizational choices. They were responses to a geographic reality: that Black communities had been forced by exclusion into specific neighborhoods, and that within those neighborhoods, the informal systems of territorial organization that street organizations provided were among the few sources of community governance, mutual protection, and collective identity available to young men who were excluded from the mainstream institutions of American life. When those neighborhoods are demolished, when those territories are erased, the territorial organizational forms that developed within them are also disrupted — not simply because the buildings are gone, but because the social world that gave those organizations their meaning and their structure no longer exists. The fragmentation of street organizations in the early twenty-first century, which the next chapter examines in detail, cannot be fully understood without understanding the geography of displacement that this chapter has described.

The chapter also leaves open some of the hardest questions about what justice requires in the context of urban redevelopment. It is not straightforward that opposition to all forms of neighborhood change is the right response to the injustice of displacement — some forms of change, achieved in ways that protect existing residents and preserve community institutions, might genuinely benefit Black urban communities. The question is not whether neighborhoods should change but how the costs and benefits of change are distributed, and whose interests shape the decisions that determine that distribution. A city that invested in transit, schools, and amenities in its most distressed neighborhoods before those investments attracted higher-income residents, that used community land trusts and robust tenant protections to ensure that rising values benefited existing residents, and that made genuine right-of-return commitments that were honored in practice would be a different kind of place than the cities this chapter has described. Such a city does not yet exist at scale in the United States — but understanding why it does not, and what would be required to create it, is the work that the history in this chapter enables and demands.

Finally, the chapter has illustrated once again the capacity of Black communities to build and sustain institutional life in conditions of adversity — to create churches, community organizations, mutual aid networks, and cultural institutions that give meaning and stability to collective life even when the forces bearing down on those communities are enormous. The community development movement, the community land trust initiatives, the tenant organizing campaigns, the advocacy for anti-displacement policy — these represent not despair but determination, the continuing application of the same organizational capacity that has sustained Black communities through every previous phase of exclusion and displacement that this textbook has traced. Understanding that capacity, and the conditions that enable or inhibit it, is one of the most important lessons of the history this textbook tells.

The next chapter will examine what the combination of displacement, fragmentation, and digital transformation has produced in the street-level landscape of American cities: smaller, more volatile crews with less organizational structure, more fluid territorial identities, and new forms of conflict mediated through social media. The transformation this chapter has described — the erasure of the geographic foundations of Black urban organizational life — is the essential context for understanding the fragmentation that follows. The city is still being made, and made unequally, and the history in this chapter is not finished.

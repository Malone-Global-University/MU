Historical Overview

A Music Built to Argue

By the time Public Enemy released their second album in the spring of 1988, hip-hop had already proven, across the previous decade, that it could be a party music, a boasting music, a storytelling music, and, in the hands of Grandmaster Flash and Melle Mel, an occasional vehicle for urban reportage. What it had not yet fully become was an organized instrument of political argument: a music built, from the ground up, with the deliberate intention of instructing, provoking, and mobilizing its audience around an explicit set of political claims. That is what Public Enemy set out to build, and largely did. It Takes a Nation of Millions to Hold Us Back arrived as a dense, layered, sonically aggressive record that paired the most radical production hip-hop had yet heard with lyrics that addressed incarceration, media bias, Black nationalism, and the legacy of slavery and segregation in direct and unambiguous terms.

This chapter is concerned with that achievement and with the broader current of political and socially conscious rap that surrounded it during the golden age, roughly the years between 1986 and 1993. Public Enemy did not invent political rap. Grandmaster Flash and the Furious Five's "The Message," discussed in an earlier chapter, had already demonstrated that rap could carry social commentary into the commercial marketplace, and a handful of earlier and contemporaneous artists, including Brother D, KRS-One, and the X Clan, were working similar territory. But Public Enemy did something none of those artists had attempted at the same scale: they built an entire group identity, an entire sonic philosophy, and an entire public posture around the idea that hip-hop could function as a counter-institution, a kind of insurgent news service and ideological training ground operating in direct opposition to what its members regarded as a hostile and distorting mainstream media.

Understanding how that project came together, why it succeeded as both art and provocation, and what it changed about the range of things hip-hop was permitted to say in public requires attention to several intersecting histories: the political and economic conditions of Black America in the Reagan era, the specific intellectual and religious traditions that shaped Public Enemy's members, the technical innovations of their production team, and the broader ecosystem of golden-age artists who were, in different registers and to different degrees, building political content into a genre still widely dismissed by much of the mainstream press as a passing novelty.

Origins & Background

Long Island, Late Capitalism

Public Enemy's roots lay not in the South Bronx but in Long Island, specifically the suburban town of Roosevelt and the nearby campus of Adelphi University, where Carlton Ridenhour, who would become known as Chuck D, met William Drayton, who would become known as Flavor Flav, in the early 1980s. This geography mattered. Long Island's Black suburbs, products of the postwar migration of Black families out of New York City and into towns that often remained informally segregated through redlining and restrictive housing practices, produced a generation of young Black Americans who were neither fully inside the cultural world of the South Bronx nor outside the economic and political pressures shaping Black life nationally. Roosevelt in particular had seen its tax base erode and its schools decline through the 1970s and '80s, a pattern of municipal disinvestment that mirrored, in suburban form, the urban crisis that had produced hip-hop itself a decade earlier in the Bronx.

Chuck D had been working at WBAU, Adelphi's college radio station, where he and a circle of collaborators that included Hank Shocklee and Bill Stephney were producing mixshows and developing a sound that drew on the noise and density of hardcore funk and free jazz rather than the cleaner, more melodic samples favored by much of contemporary rap production. Rick Rubin, who had cofounded Def Jam Recordings with Russell Simmons and had already built the label's reputation through Run-D.M.C. and LL Cool J, heard a demo of Chuck D rhyming over a Spectrum City mixtape and pursued him aggressively for a record deal, eventually persuading him to assemble a full group rather than record as a solo MC.

The Reagan-Era Context

The political content that would define Public Enemy's work cannot be understood apart from the specific conditions of Black America during the Reagan administration, which governed from 1981 to 1989 and whose policies disproportionately affected the urban and suburban Black communities from which hip-hop's audience was drawn. The Reagan administration's cuts to federal housing assistance, urban development funding, and social welfare programs coincided with the expansion of mandatory minimum sentencing and the escalation of the War on Drugs, particularly following the Anti-Drug Abuse Act of 1986, which imposed dramatically harsher sentences for crack cocaine offenses than for powder cocaine offenses involving the same quantity of the drug, a disparity with severe and disproportionate consequences for Black defendants.

This was also a period of significant deindustrialization in the urban Northeast, as manufacturing jobs that had once provided pathways into the Black working and middle class continued to disappear, a process that had begun in the 1970s and accelerated through the 1980s. The combination of vanishing industrial employment, reduced federal support, and an expanding carceral apparatus produced, for many young Black Americans coming of age in this period, a sense that the political system was not simply indifferent to their circumstances but actively organized against them. This was the structural backdrop against which Public Enemy's members came of age, and it shaped the group's foundational conviction that hip-hop needed to function not merely as entertainment but as a counter-narrative to what they perceived as a media and political establishment that misrepresented, ignored, or criminalized Black life.

The Nation of Islam Influence

Public Enemy's early visual and rhetorical presentation, particularly the paramilitary-styled dance unit known as the S1Ws, Security of the First World, drew direct inspiration from the Fruit of Islam, the security and discipline arm of the Nation of Islam. The group's affiliated minister, Professor Griff, served as the S1Ws' director and brought an explicitly Nation-influenced political vocabulary into the group's public messaging.

This influence connected Public Enemy to a much older tradition of Black nationalist organizing that included Marcus Garvey's Universal Negro Improvement Association in the 1920s and the Nation of Islam's own growth under Elijah Muhammad and, later, Louis Farrakhan.

Hip-Hop Journalism and the Conditions for Political Rap

The emergence of a serious hip-hop press, discussed in greater detail elsewhere in this volume, also created institutional conditions favorable to political rap's development. Publications were beginning to treat rap as a subject worthy of sustained critical attention rather than novelty coverage, and college and community radio stations, including WBAU itself, gave artists a platform to develop political and intellectual material outside the immediate commercial pressure of top-forty programming. This infrastructure, modest as it was, gave artists like Chuck D the space to develop a dense, allusive, politically engaged style before that style had any guaranteed commercial audience.

The Group Takes Shape

From Yo! Bum Rush the Show to Nation of Millions

Public Enemy's 1987 debut album, Yo! Bum Rush the Show, introduced the group's basic elements without yet achieving the full synthesis that would make their second album a landmark. Chuck D's voice, a deep, declarative baritone that critics frequently compared to a radio announcer's, established itself immediately as one of the most distinctive vocal instruments in rap, an instrument built for authority and urgency rather than the more conversational or melodic deliveries common among contemporaries. Flavor Flav, by contrast, supplied a comic, hyperkinetic counterpoint, a hype man whose exaggerated ad-libs and erratic energy provided both comic relief and a kind of structural foil that made Chuck D's gravity legible by contrast. The pairing, whatever its origins in practical group chemistry, would prove to be one of the most effective vocal contrasts in the genre's history: the prophet and the jester, operating in the same verses.

The production on the debut album, credited to Hank Shocklee and the production collective that would soon formalize itself as the Bomb Squad, hinted at the dense, layered sound the group would perfect on subsequent releases, but the album as a whole was received as a strong, promising debut rather than a fully arrived statement. That arrival came with It Takes a Nation of Millions to Hold Us Back, released in the spring of 1988, an album now widely regarded by critics and historians as one of the most significant and influential rap records ever made, and one that reoriented the commercial and artistic possibilities of the genre in ways that extended well beyond Public Enemy's own subsequent career.

The Bomb Squad and the Architecture of Noise

The production team known as the Bomb Squad, consisting principally of Hank Shocklee, his brother Keith Shocklee, Eric "Vietnam" Sadler, and, on later records, Chuck D himself, developed a sampling approach that diverged sharply from the cleaner, more melodically oriented production common among many of their golden-age contemporaries. Where producers like Marley Marl or Pete Rock, discussed in the preceding chapter on sampling, often built tracks around a single recognizable loop or a smoothly integrated set of samples, the Bomb Squad constructed tracks from dozens of layered fragments, frequently dissonant, frequently drawn from free jazz, hardcore funk, and noise rather than smooth soul, stacked in combinations that produced a dense, abrasive, almost overwhelming sonic texture.

This was a deliberate aesthetic choice with explicit political logic behind it. Hank Shocklee later described the group's intention as building a sound that matched the urgency and chaos of the conditions the lyrics described, a wall of sound, in his phrase, that approximated through sonic means what the group was attempting to communicate through language: that Black urban life under Reagan-era conditions was loud, layered, contradictory, and under siege. The siren samples, the dissonant horn stabs, the layered scratches, and the dense low end were not ornamental. They were structurally analogous to the content. A listener could not passively absorb a Public Enemy track in the way one might absorb a smoother, more conventionally melodic rap record. The music demanded active attention, in the same way the lyrics demanded it.

We wanted to make music that sounded like the news, like the static and chaos of information coming at you from every direction at once, except this time the information was coming from us.

On the Bomb Squad's production philosophy

This production approach also had significant legal and economic consequences that would shape sampling practice across the industry, a connection explored in greater detail in the chapter on sampling as composition. The sheer density of samples on a record like Nation of Millions, frequently dozens of distinct sources layered into a single track, became increasingly difficult and expensive to clear as sample-licensing litigation intensified in the early 1990s, following the landmark 1991 case Grand Upright Music, Ltd. v. Warner Bros. Records Inc., in which a federal court ruled against rapper Biz Markie for using an uncleared sample, establishing a legal precedent that fundamentally altered how producers could work. The Bomb Squad's maximalist layering approach, which had been artistically possible in the relatively unregulated sampling environment of 1988, became financially and legally untenable for most producers within a few years, making albums like Nation of Millions not only artistic landmarks but historical artifacts of a specific, narrow window in the legal history of sampled music.

Chuck D's Rhetorical Method

Chuck D's lyrical approach on Nation of Millions and the records that followed drew on a deliberately constructed rhetorical method that he has described in interviews as an attempt to compress complex political and historical arguments into the dense, rhythmically constrained space of a rap verse. His lyrics were characterized by direct address, frequent allusion to historical figures and events, particularly from the Black nationalist and civil rights traditions, and a declarative, almost oratorical delivery that drew listeners' attention to commentators have compared, with reason, to the cadences of the Black church and to the political speech tradition of figures like Malcolm X, discussed in the previous chapter's account of hip-hop's oral antecedents.

Songs such as "Black Steel in the Hour of Chaos" constructed extended narratives, in this case the story of a Black radical's resistance to the Vietnam-era draft and subsequent imprisonment, that functioned simultaneously as compelling storytelling and as historical argument about the relationship between Black Americans and the institutions of the state, including the military and the prison system. "Don't Believe the Hype" addressed the group's own contentious relationship with the mainstream press directly, turning media skepticism itself into subject matter, while "Bring the Noise" functioned as something close to a mission statement, explicitly defending rap's cultural legitimacy against critics who dismissed it as noise rather than music, and tying that defensive argument to a broader claim about Black cultural production being persistently undervalued by mainstream institutions.

"Fight the Power" and the Spike Lee Collaboration

Public Enemy's most widely recognized political anthem, "Fight the Power," emerged from a direct commission. Director Spike Lee, preparing his 1989 film Do the Right Thing, a drama set over the course of a single sweltering day in a Brooklyn neighborhood that culminates in a racially charged act of police violence, approached the group to create what he described as a Black national anthem for the film, a piece of music that could function as both score and statement. The resulting song, which plays throughout the film and accompanies its most iconic sequences, including the character Radio Raheem's continuous public broadcasting of the track from his boombox, became inseparable from the film's reception and amplified both works' cultural impact considerably.

The song's lyrics took direct aim at figures and institutions the group regarded as emblematic of a racially exclusionary popular culture, drawing significant controversy for lines criticizing figures including Elvis Presley and John Wayne as symbols of a white-dominated cultural mainstream that had marginalized Black contributions to American music and film. The controversy itself became part of the song's cultural function: a deliberate provocation designed to force public conversation about whose cultural icons were treated as universal and whose were treated as particular, a question with direct relevance to hip-hop's own struggle for legitimacy within mainstream American cultural institutions.

Professor Griff and the Controversy of 1989

Public Enemy's political project encountered a severe crisis in the spring of 1989, when Professor Griff, the group's Minister of Information and director of the S1W security unit, gave an interview to the Washington Times in which he made antisemitic remarks, including a comment blaming Jewish people for, in his framing, much of the world's wickedness. The remarks provoked immediate and intense public backlash, drawing condemnation from civil rights organizations, music industry figures, and major media outlets, and placed the group at the center of a controversy that threatened to overshadow and discredit its broader political project.

Chuck D's handling of the crisis involved a public disavowal of Griff's specific remarks, a temporary dismissal of Griff from the group, and an extended public reckoning, conducted across interviews and through the group's subsequent music, with the question of how a political project organized around opposing one form of bigotry could guard against reproducing another. The episode became, and remains, a significant case study in the limits and internal contradictions of late-1980s Black nationalist political rhetoric, illustrating that the same intellectual and organizational tradition that gave Public Enemy its disciplined, confrontational political identity, including its connections to Nation of Islam thought, also carried within it ideas that a broad and historically informed audience rightly rejected as bigoted. Griff would eventually return to the group in a reduced capacity, and the controversy did not end Public Enemy's career, but it permanently complicated the group's legacy and remains a necessary part of any honest historical account of their significance.

Key Figures and Contemporaries

Political Rap Beyond Public Enemy

Public Enemy's prominence has, over time, tended to overshadow the considerably broader field of golden-age artists who were producing politically engaged or socially conscious rap during the same period, often working in styles quite distinct from the Bomb Squad's dense maximalism. Understanding Public Enemy correctly requires placing them within this wider field rather than treating them as an isolated phenomenon. The years between roughly 1986 and 1993 produced a remarkable concentration of artists addressing racism, poverty, Black history, police violence, and political consciousness, frequently in dialogue with one another and with the same surrounding political conditions.

KRS-One and the Pedagogical Turn

Lawrence Krisna Parris, performing as KRS-One, had emerged alongside producer Scott La Rock as part of Boogie Down Productions, releasing the influential 1987 album Criminal Minded, a record generally associated with the era's gangsta-rap and hardcore lyrical currents rather than political content. La Rock's killing in August 1987, shot during an altercation unrelated to the group's music, produced a documented turning point in KRS-One's public persona and subject matter. His subsequent work, beginning with 1988's By All Means Necessary, a title and cover image deliberately referencing Malcolm X, moved decisively toward historically grounded, didactic material addressing racism, miseducation, police violence, and Black history.

KRS-One's organizing of the Stop the Violence Movement, a coalition of prominent rap artists who released the 1989 single "Self Destruction" in response to escalating violence at rap concerts and in Black urban communities more broadly, represented one of the period's clearest examples of hip-hop artists organizing collectively around an explicit social and political goal, using their commercial platform to fund violence-prevention programs and shift public discourse around the genre's relationship to urban violence.

The Five Percent Nation's Influence

A substantial portion of golden-age political and conscious rap drew, directly or indirectly, on the teachings of the Five Percent Nation, also known as the Nation of Gods and Earths, a movement founded in Harlem in 1964 by Clarence 13X, a former member of the Nation of Islam who developed a related but distinct theological and social framework emphasizing Black self-knowledge, numerology, and a specific vocabulary that would become deeply embedded in hip-hop's lyrical language well beyond artists who formally identified with the movement.

Five Percent Vocabulary in Rap

Terms including "word is bond," "cipher," "knowledge of self," and the numeric system known as Supreme Mathematics entered hip-hop's general lyrical vocabulary substantially through Five Percent influence, frequently used by artists, including some affiliated with Brand Nubian and the Wu-Tang Clan, who were not always explicit in identifying the theological source of the language.

This influence illustrates how political and spiritual content moved through hip-hop not only via direct, polemical statement but through vocabulary and worldview embedded more subtly in everyday lyrical style.

The presence of this theological vocabulary throughout golden-age rap demonstrates that political and conscious content in this period was rarely monolithic. It ranged from Public Enemy's explicitly secular, historically grounded Black nationalism to the Nation of Islam-inflected discipline of the S1Ws, to Brand Nubian and Five Percent theology, to X Clan's Afrocentric mysticism, to KRS-One's self-styled pedagogical project, to Arrested Development's more pastoral, spiritually inflected social commentary. What united these otherwise quite different artists was a shared conviction, widely held across the golden-age field, that rap could and should function as a vehicle for serious political and historical instruction, not merely entertainment, and that Black audiences in particular deserved music that took their political circumstances seriously.

Historical Significance

What Political Rap Changed

The significance of Public Enemy and the broader political rap current of the golden age extends across several distinct registers: commercial, institutional, legal, and rhetorical. Understanding each of these registers separately helps clarify why this brief period, roughly seven years in length, had consequences for hip-hop's development that outlasted the specific commercial popularity of any individual political rap record.

Commercial Legitimacy and Critical Recognition

It Takes a Nation of Millions to Hold Us Back achieved significant commercial success, reaching gold certification and producing several singles that received substantial radio and television exposure, while also attracting an unusual degree of attention from mainstream music critics who had previously been reluctant to treat rap as a serious art form deserving extended critical analysis. The album appeared on numerous critics' year-end and, later, decade-end and all-time lists, a form of canonical recognition that had been largely unavailable to rap artists in the genre's first decade. This critical embrace mattered for reasons beyond simple prestige. It signaled to record labels, radio programmers, and the broader cultural apparatus that politically substantive rap could be commercially viable, opening space for subsequent artists working in explicitly political or socially conscious registers.

This commercial viability was never guaranteed or uncomplicated. Political rap occupied an uneasy position within an industry that was, throughout this period, also rapidly commercializing rap's more apolitical and increasingly its more violent and sexually explicit content, particularly as gangsta rap from the West Coast began achieving major commercial breakthroughs in the years immediately following Public Enemy's peak, a development discussed in the chapters on N.W.A and Death Row Records. Political rap's commercial space was therefore always contested, and many of the genre's most explicitly political artists, including Paris and X Clan, achieved far more modest commercial success than Public Enemy itself, despite producing work of comparable artistic seriousness.

The Question of Censorship and Public Controversy

Political rap's rise coincided with, and substantially contributed to, an intensifying national debate over rap lyrics, censorship, and the limits of acceptable public speech, a debate examined at greater length in this volume's later treatment of N.W.A and Ice-T. Public Enemy's confrontational rhetoric, including its sometimes inflammatory statements about American institutions, drew sustained criticism from conservative commentators and, on occasion, from civil rights figures who found the group's Nation of Islam-influenced messaging divisive. This controversy was not incidental to the music's reception. It was, in significant part, constitutive of the group's cultural function, as Chuck D himself frequently argued: rap's capacity to provoke public argument was, in his framing, evidence of its seriousness as political speech rather than a flaw to be corrected.

The broader national debate over rap lyrics that intensified through the late 1980s and into the early 1990s, eventually drawing the attention of organizations including the Parents Music Resource Center and, by 1992, of presidential politics directly, when candidate Bill Clinton publicly criticized Sister Souljah's comments on racial violence in a moment that would become a notable episode in that year's campaign, demonstrated that rap had, by the end of the golden age, achieved a level of national political visibility that would have been difficult to imagine a decade earlier. Political rap was central to this visibility. It was not merely that rap had become popular; it was that rap had become, in the eyes of national political figures, a genre whose content carried sufficient cultural weight to merit direct political response.

Sampling, Density, and a Closing Window

As discussed above, the specific maximalist sampling approach pioneered by the Bomb Squad existed within a narrow legal window that closed substantially after the early 1990s sample-clearance litigation reshaped the economics of hip-hop production. This had a structural consequence for political rap's sound specifically: the dense, chaotic sonic texture that the Bomb Squad had developed as a deliberate aesthetic correlate to political urgency became, for purely legal and financial reasons, increasingly difficult for subsequent artists to replicate. Later political and conscious rap, including much of the material produced by artists associated with the Native Tongues collective and subsequent generations of socially engaged MCs, would generally rely on sparer, more legally manageable production approaches, meaning that Public Enemy's specific sonic achievement on Nation of Millions remains, in important respects, a historically bounded artifact rather than a sustainable production template.

Legacy and Continuity

The Long Afterlife of Political Rap

Public Enemy's direct influence is traceable across decades of subsequent hip-hop, though that influence has frequently been diffused, transformed, and recombined with other currents rather than reproduced in straightforward imitation. The most immediate inheritors of the group's explicitly political project, including artists associated with the Native Tongues collective discussed in the following chapter, generally softened the Bomb Squad's sonic aggression while retaining a comparable seriousness about Black history, identity, and political consciousness as legitimate rap subject matter. Later political and socially engaged artists, ranging from dead prez in the late 1990s to Kendrick Lamar and J. Cole in the 2010s, worked in sonic registers quite different from Public Enemy's but operated within a space of commercial and critical legitimacy for political rap that Public Enemy had played a central role in establishing.

Public Enemy's organizational model, the idea of the rap group as a structured political unit with defined roles, from Chuck D's lead vocal authority to Flavor Flav's hype-man counterpoint to the S1Ws' visual and symbolic security function to Professor Griff's ministerial role, also offered a template, imperfect and controversial as parts of it proved, for thinking about hip-hop groups as something more than ad hoc musical collaborations: as organizations with an internal division of political and creative labor, oriented toward a coherent public mission. Few subsequent groups replicated this model in its specific details, but the underlying idea, that a rap group could function as something closer to a political organization than a band, would echo in later collectives, including some explicitly political hip-hop ensembles of the 1990s and 2000s.

Chuck D as Public Intellectual

Chuck D's subsequent career, including his extensive work as a public commentator, lecturer, and author, reflects a trajectory that few earlier rap artists had pursued: the transformation of a rap career into a platform for sustained public intellectual engagement, including writing, broadcasting, and political organizing efforts that extended well beyond Public Enemy's recorded output. This trajectory established a precedent, however unevenly followed by subsequent artists, for understanding the rap artist as a potential public intellectual figure, a status that a number of later artists, working in considerably different political registers and with varying degrees of explicit affiliation with Public Enemy's specific tradition, would also claim for themselves.

The Professor Griff controversy of 1989 also left a lasting and necessary legacy of caution within hip-hop's political tradition, serving as a frequently cited historical example, in later scholarly and journalistic treatments of political rap, of the dangers inherent in uncritically absorbing the more conspiratorial or bigoted strands of certain Black nationalist intellectual traditions alongside their more defensible historical and political claims. Responsible historical treatment of Public Enemy's legacy requires holding both halves of this record together: the group's genuine and influential contribution to hip-hop's capacity for serious political speech, and the genuine harm and legitimate backlash produced by Griff's remarks, a tension the group itself was forced to publicly navigate and one that later scholars and critics have continued to examine.

Chapter Summary

Conclusion

Public Enemy's achievement, properly understood, was not the invention of political content in rap, which had clear antecedents in "The Message" and in the broader oral and rhetorical traditions discussed earlier in this volume, but the construction of a complete artistic and organizational apparatus, sonic, lyrical, visual, and institutional, capable of sustaining political argument as a primary rather than incidental function of recorded rap music. The Bomb Squad's dense, maximalist production gave the group's political content a sonic correlate of comparable urgency and complexity. Chuck D's declarative, historically allusive lyrical method gave the group's arguments rhetorical force and structure. The group's affiliations, including its connections to Nation of Islam-derived organizational discipline, gave the project a public seriousness that distinguished it from more casually engaged contemporaries, while also exposing the project to the controversy and internal contradiction that the Griff episode made unavoidably public.

This achievement did not occur in isolation. It was part of a broader golden-age current, including KRS-One's pedagogical turn following Scott La Rock's death, the Five Percent Nation's theological influence on a wide range of artists, and the work of figures including X Clan, Brand Nubian, Paris, and Arrested Development, who collectively demonstrated that a substantial audience existed for rap that took Black political history and contemporary circumstance seriously as subject matter. This current would not remain dominant within hip-hop's commercial mainstream much past the early 1990s, as the chapters that follow on gangsta rap's West Coast ascendance and the East Coast's own harder-edged reassertion will make clear. But the legitimacy and critical seriousness that political rap established during these years left a permanent mark on what hip-hop was understood to be capable of saying, and to whom it was understood to be capable of speaking, long after the specific sonic and commercial conditions that produced It Takes a Nation of Millions to Hold Us Back had passed into history.

A Note on Sources

Scholarship and journalism on Public Enemy and golden-age political rap draws heavily on contemporaneous music press coverage as well as later historical treatments. Tricia Rose's Black Noise: Rap Music and Black Culture in Contemporary America (1994) remains a foundational scholarly analysis of the period's political and sonic dimensions. Jeff Chang's Can't Stop Won't Stop: A History of the Hip-Hop Generation (2005) situates Public Enemy within the broader social and political history of the era, including the Reagan-era policy context discussed in this chapter. Chuck D's own writing, including his memoir and subsequent commentary, provides primary-source insight into the group's artistic intentions and internal history, while contemporaneous coverage of the Professor Griff controversy in outlets including the Washington Times, the New York Times, and the Black press of the period documents the public reception and consequences of that episode.

On the Five Percent Nation's influence on hip-hop lyrical vocabulary, see scholarship by Felicia Miyakawa, whose work traces the movement's theological vocabulary through golden-age and subsequent rap recordings. On the legal history of sampling referenced in this chapter, see the fuller treatment in this volume's chapter on sampling as composition, which addresses the Grand Upright Music v. Warner Bros. decision and its consequences for production practice in greater detail.

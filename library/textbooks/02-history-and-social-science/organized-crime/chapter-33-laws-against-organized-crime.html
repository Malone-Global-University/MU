Laws Against Organized Crime

Organized Crime: A Global History

Chapter Opening

"Organized crime is not simply a collection of bad individuals. It is an institution, a system, a way of conducting business across the boundaries of the law. Defeating it requires more than catching criminals. It requires dismantling the institutions they build."

In October 1970, President Richard Nixon signed into law the Organized Crime Control Act, and with it the statute that would transform law enforcement's ability to prosecute criminal enterprises for the next half century and beyond. Title IX of that act, known by the acronym RICO, gave federal prosecutors a weapon unlike anything previously available in American law. It allowed them to charge not just the street-level criminal who committed a robbery or a murder, but the entire organization that directed, benefited from, and enabled that crime. It allowed prosecutors to link together crimes committed years apart, by different people, in different places, into a single comprehensive indictment of a criminal enterprise. It allowed the government to seize the assets and businesses that organized crime had built. And it imposed sentences severe enough to put the leaders of major criminal organizations in prison for decades rather than years.

The passage of RICO marked a turning point in the long and often frustrated effort by democratic governments to bring the law to bear against organized crime. For most of the twentieth century, law enforcement agencies had struggled against a fundamental structural problem: the men who ran criminal empires rarely committed crimes themselves, at least not the kinds that could be proved in court beyond a reasonable doubt. They gave orders through intermediaries. They insulated themselves behind layers of subordinates. They used lawyers and accountants and corrupt politicians to protect their positions. Traditional criminal law, which punished individuals for specific acts, was poorly designed to reach the organizers, the planners, and the financiers who built and sustained criminal enterprises while keeping their own hands legally clean.

RICO was not invented in isolation. It emerged from decades of congressional investigation, academic research, and prosecutorial frustration with the inadequacy of existing tools. It was built on earlier legislative experiments, including the Hobbs Act of 1946 and the Kefauver Committee investigations of the early 1950s, which had helped establish in the public mind both the reality of organized crime as a national institution and the inadequacy of existing law to deal with it. It was shaped by the work of legal scholar G. Robert Blakey, who drafted much of the statute and who embedded in it a theory of criminal enterprise liability that had no real precedent in American law. And it was propelled by the political moment of the late 1960s, when urban disorder, rising crime rates, and widely publicized revelations about Mafia infiltration of legitimate industries had created broad public support for dramatic new tools.

But RICO was only one piece of a far larger legislative architecture that took shape over the latter half of the twentieth century. Asset forfeiture laws gave governments the power to strip organized crime of the wealth it had accumulated, attacking the financial foundation on which criminal enterprises rested. Witness protection programs addressed one of the oldest and most effective weapons in the criminal arsenal, the ability to silence potential witnesses through fear and violence, by giving cooperating witnesses new identities, new lives, and the physical security to testify without fear of retribution. Money laundering statutes made it a federal crime to conceal or transfer the proceeds of criminal activity, tightening the connection between criminal profit and criminal prosecution. And international treaties created frameworks for cooperation among the national governments that individual criminal networks had learned to exploit by operating across borders that legal systems could not easily cross.

The story of these laws is not simply a story of progressive expansion in the state's power to fight crime. It is also a story of tensions and trade-offs that democratic societies must navigate when confronting organized crime. The same expanded powers that allowed prosecutors to dismantle the American Mafia also created tools that, in later years, were used against defendants whose connection to organized crime was tenuous at best. Civil asset forfeiture, designed to strip drug cartels of their financial resources, became a tool used against ordinary citizens who had not been charged with, let alone convicted of, any crime. Witness protection, designed to bring insulated crime bosses to justice, put the government in the morally complicated business of rewarding cooperating witnesses who were themselves serious criminals with new identities and new lives, sometimes while those witnesses continued to engage in criminal activity. RICO, designed for the Mafia, found its way into prosecutions of abortion protesters, corporate executives, and political organizations, raising questions about the scope of a law that its drafters never fully anticipated.

The international dimension added its own layers of complexity. Organized crime has always sought to exploit the boundaries between jurisdictions, and as criminal networks became increasingly transnational in the 1980s and 1990s, the inadequacy of purely national legal frameworks became more and more apparent. Drug cartels moved cocaine and heroin across dozens of borders. Russian organized crime conducted money laundering operations that spanned Europe, the Caribbean, and North America simultaneously. Cybercriminal networks based in Eastern Europe attacked targets in North America and Asia while operating through servers in dozens of countries. The legal tools that had been designed to address organized crime as a primarily national phenomenon strained to reach criminal enterprises that had deliberately made themselves transnational in order to evade exactly those tools.

This chapter examines the legal framework that democracies have built to fight organized crime, beginning with the American experience and extending to the international arena. It traces the development of anti-racketeering laws from their origins in the 1940s through the RICO revolution of 1970 and its subsequent application and expansion. It examines the history, theory, and practice of asset forfeiture, including both its successes against major criminal organizations and the serious civil liberties concerns it has raised. It explores witness protection as a legal and human institution, considering both its remarkable effectiveness and the moral dilemmas it creates. And it analyzes the development of international anti-crime treaties, from the first narcotics conventions of the early twentieth century through the comprehensive frameworks of the 1980s and the United Nations Convention Against Transnational Organized Crime of 2000. Together, these legal instruments represent the most ambitious effort in history to bring the organized criminal enterprise within the reach of the law.

Understanding these laws is essential not only for understanding how organized crime has been fought but for understanding the deeper question of what a democratic state owes its citizens when it arms itself to fight crime. The same government that promises to protect citizens from organized criminal violence also promises to protect citizens from government overreach. Getting that balance right, in theory and in practice, has been among the most difficult legal and political challenges of the modern era.

Historical Context

To understand why the legal framework against organized crime took the shape it did, students must understand both the nature of the problem that legislators were trying to solve and the legal traditions within which they were working. American criminal law, like most legal systems rooted in English common law, was designed to identify and punish individuals for specific, discrete acts. A person who murdered someone could be charged with murder. A person who robbed a bank could be charged with bank robbery. A person who ran a gambling operation could be charged with illegal gambling. Each crime was a separate offense, prosecuted separately, requiring proof of the specific act and the specific person who committed it.

This structure was adequate for prosecuting ordinary criminals who personally committed the acts that constituted crimes. It was deeply inadequate for prosecuting the leaders of organized criminal enterprises, who were insulated from the criminal acts their organizations committed by precisely the kind of hierarchical structure, division of labor, and compartmentalization of information that made their enterprises effective. Al Capone was finally imprisoned in 1931 not for murder or extortion or bootlegging, but for tax evasion, because prosecutors could not establish his personal participation in specific crimes beyond a reasonable doubt. Lucky Luciano was convicted in 1936 on compulsory prostitution charges that required a strained legal theory and the testimony of witnesses of extremely dubious credibility. These cases succeeded, but they did so in spite of the legal framework rather than because of it.

The Kefauver Committee hearings of 1950 and 1951, broadcast on national television to an audience of millions of Americans, made two things unmistakably clear. The first was that organized crime in the United States was a national institution, not a collection of local problems. The second was that existing law was inadequate to deal with it. The hearings produced dramatic testimony about the Mafia's control of gambling, labor unions, and political machines in major cities across the country, but they produced relatively few prosecutions, because the existing legal tools were not equal to the task of dismantling criminal enterprises whose leaders had carefully insulated themselves from direct participation in specific crimes.

The political pressure generated by the Kefauver hearings, and by subsequent investigations in the 1950s and 1960s, produced a series of incremental legislative responses, including expansions of federal jurisdiction over gambling and extortion, the creation of federal organized crime strike forces, and increased resources for FBI investigations of Mafia activities. But the fundamental problem, the inability of traditional criminal law to reach the architects of criminal enterprises, remained unsolved until the passage of RICO in 1970.

The international context was equally challenging. By the mid-twentieth century, narcotics trafficking had become a global industry, with supply chains that crossed dozens of national borders. The United States had pioneered international drug control treaties through the League of Nations in the 1920s and 1930s, but those treaties were primarily aimed at restricting the legal trade in narcotics and had limited impact on the illegal markets that developed alongside it. The global expansion of drug trafficking in the 1960s and 1970s, driven by the surge in demand for heroin, cocaine, and marijuana in wealthy Western countries, overwhelmed law enforcement systems that were designed for national rather than international operations. The response, ultimately, would require both new domestic legislation and new international frameworks for cooperation across borders that criminal networks had learned to exploit with devastating effectiveness.

The legal tradition within which legislators worked also shaped the tools they could create. The Fifth Amendment to the United States Constitution protects citizens against self-incrimination. The Sixth Amendment guarantees the right to confront witnesses. The Fourth Amendment protects against unreasonable searches and seizures, and the Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments guarantee due process of law before the government can deprive a person of property or liberty. These constitutional protections, which are among the most important safeguards that democratic government provides to its citizens, also created constraints on the legal tools that could be used against organized crime. Any anti-crime legislation that violated those protections would be struck down by the courts, and any legislation that pushed against their boundaries would generate litigation, controversy, and calls for reform. Understanding the history of anti-organized crime law requires understanding it as a continuous negotiation between the state's interest in suppressing criminal enterprises and the constitution's protection of individual rights.

The Problem of Enterprise Liability

The central conceptual challenge that anti-organized crime legislation had to address was what legal scholars call the problem of enterprise liability. A criminal enterprise, as understood by law enforcement in the mid-twentieth century, was not simply a collection of individuals who happened to commit crimes near each other. It was an institution with a structure, a hierarchy, a set of ongoing criminal objectives, and a capacity to survive the removal of any individual member. The Gambino crime family was not a group of men who happened to commit similar crimes; it was an organization with rules, a chain of command, territories, and mechanisms for enforcing discipline and protecting its operations. When a capo ordered a soldier to commit a murder, the capo did not personally participate in the murder and could not, under traditional law, be charged with it merely because he gave the order, unless the prosecution could prove beyond a reasonable doubt that he had done so.

The solution that RICO embodied was to make the enterprise itself the subject of criminal prosecution. If a person participated in the conduct of an enterprise through a pattern of racketeering activity, that person could be charged under RICO regardless of whether they personally committed any of the specific predicate acts that made up the pattern. The statute required proof of an enterprise, proof of a pattern of racketeering activity, and proof that the defendant participated in that enterprise through that pattern, but it did not require proof that the defendant personally committed any specific predicate act. This was the fundamental innovation of RICO, and it was what made it so powerful against the leadership of organized criminal enterprises who had traditionally protected themselves by keeping their hands clean of the specific acts their organizations committed.

Section 33.1

Anti-Racketeering Laws: From the Hobbs Act to RICO

The word "racketeering" entered American legal and popular vocabulary in the 1920s, coined to describe the practice of using threats and violence to extort money from businesses, most typically in connection with labor relations. A "racket" was an organized criminal scheme that extracted money through coercion, and a "racketeer" was someone who ran such a scheme. The term quickly expanded to encompass a wide range of criminal enterprises, including the bootlegging operations of the Prohibition era, the gambling networks of the 1930s and 1940s, and the labor union corruption that was among the most visible manifestations of organized crime in the post-World War II period.

Early federal efforts to criminalize racketeering faced significant constitutional obstacles. In the American federal system, criminal law has traditionally been a matter for the states, with the federal government's jurisdiction limited to crimes that involve interstate commerce, use of federal facilities, or other specifically federal interests. Organized crime, as it actually operated, was often deeply local in character, rooted in specific cities and neighborhoods, exploiting local political connections, and operating primarily within state boundaries. Federal prosecutors had to strain existing statutes, including mail fraud and tax evasion laws, to reach criminals whose activities were essentially local in character.

The Hobbs Act and Its Limits

The Hobbs Act of 1946, named after Congressman Sam Hobbs of Alabama, established federal jurisdiction over extortion and robbery affecting interstate commerce. It was a significant step forward, because the interstate commerce connection was easy to establish in most organized crime cases: virtually any business that was extorted had some connection to interstate commerce, even if that connection was tenuous. The Hobbs Act gave federal prosecutors a basis for intervening in local extortion schemes that had previously been beyond federal reach.

But the Hobbs Act, like other statutes of its era, remained a tool for prosecuting individual acts of extortion or robbery. It could not reach the overall enterprise that directed those acts, and it could not be used to charge the leaders of criminal organizations who gave orders but did not personally commit extortion. A mob boss who directed his soldiers to extort payments from local businesses could not be charged under the Hobbs Act unless prosecutors could prove that he personally participated in a specific extortion. The organizational structure of the Mafia, with its careful separation of leadership from direct criminal action, was precisely designed to prevent that proof from being assembled.

The limitations of existing law became increasingly apparent through the 1950s, as the Kefauver Committee and subsequent investigations revealed the scale and structure of organized crime. Congressional hearings produced dramatic testimony about Mafia influence over labor unions, particularly in the trucking, longshoremen's, and construction industries; about the organization's control over illegal gambling operations that generated hundreds of millions of dollars annually; and about the corruption of public officials at every level of government. But the hearings produced relatively few prosecutions, because the evidence presented in a congressional hearing, with its different evidentiary standards, was difficult to convert into courtroom proof beyond a reasonable doubt of specific crimes by specific individuals.

Robert Kennedy and the Organized Crime Drive

The most aggressive pre-RICO effort to use existing law against organized crime came during the Kennedy administration, when Attorney General Robert F. Kennedy made organized crime prosecution a personal crusade. Kennedy had served as counsel to the Senate Rackets Committee (known informally as the McClellan Committee) in the late 1950s, and his interrogations of Teamsters president Jimmy Hoffa and other organized crime figures had made national news. When he became Attorney General in 1961, he brought that prosecutorial energy to the Department of Justice and dramatically expanded the federal organized crime program.

Under Kennedy's direction, the number of organized crime prosecutions increased dramatically. Federal strike forces, teams of prosecutors and agents drawn from multiple agencies, were deployed to major cities with instructions to dismantle local criminal organizations. Hoffa was eventually convicted of jury tampering and pension fund fraud in 1964, in large part because of Kennedy's relentless focus on his prosecution. Other organized crime figures were prosecuted on tax charges, on labor law violations, and on gambling and extortion charges under existing federal statutes.

These prosecutions achieved real results. But they also demonstrated, precisely because of their partial success, how inadequate existing law was for reaching the core leadership of criminal enterprises. For every Hoffa who could be convicted on specific charges that happened to be provable, there were dozens of Mafia bosses who were genuinely beyond the reach of available legal tools. Carlos Marcello, who ran organized crime operations across Louisiana and Texas, was the subject of intense law enforcement scrutiny for decades but was never successfully prosecuted for organized crime activities during the Kennedy era. Carlo Gambino, boss of the most powerful New York crime family, lived until 1976 and died peacefully of a heart attack, never having served a significant prison sentence for his criminal activities. The law was simply not adequate to reach men who were careful, who operated through intermediaries, and who had corrupted enough of the surrounding political and law enforcement environment to protect their operations.

The Path to RICO

The intellectual foundation for RICO was laid in the late 1960s by G. Robert Blakey, a Notre Dame law professor who had worked for the Justice Department on organized crime issues. Blakey's key insight was that organized crime could not be effectively prosecuted as a collection of individual criminal acts; it had to be prosecuted as an institution. The law needed a theory of liability that could reach the enterprise itself, holding accountable all those who participated in the enterprise's criminal activities, whether or not they personally committed specific criminal acts.

Blakey drew on two existing legal concepts to build this theory. The first was civil antitrust law, which had long allowed courts to look at patterns of behavior across time and multiple actors to establish that a defendant had engaged in a conspiracy to restrain trade. The second was the concept of conspiracy in criminal law, which allowed prosecutors to charge multiple defendants for agreeing to commit crimes even if not all of them personally committed the acts contemplated by the conspiracy. RICO combined these concepts in a new way, creating a statute that allowed prosecution of enterprises as enterprises, based on a pattern of criminal activity conducted by or through those enterprises.

The statute that Blakey drafted and that Congress enacted in 1970 as Title IX of the Organized Crime Control Act defined racketeering to include a long list of state and federal crimes, called predicate acts. These included murder, kidnapping, extortion, robbery, arson, bribery, fraud, counterfeiting, narcotics trafficking, and many others. The statute made it a crime to invest in, acquire, establish, conduct, or participate in the conduct of an enterprise through a pattern of racketeering activity. A pattern required at least two predicate acts within ten years. The penalties were severe: up to twenty years in prison per count, plus forfeiture of all proceeds of racketeering activity and any interest in the enterprise itself.

Critically, RICO contained a civil as well as a criminal component. The civil RICO provision allowed private parties who had been injured by racketeering activity to sue for treble damages, making it a powerful tool not only for criminal prosecution but for private litigation against criminal enterprises. This civil component would later have significant consequences for the statute's expansion beyond its original targets.

RICO in Practice: The Mafia Prosecutions

For the first decade after its enactment, RICO was used relatively sparingly against organized crime. Prosecutors were unfamiliar with its requirements, courts were uncertain about its scope, and defense attorneys vigorously challenged its constitutionality and its application to specific cases. The statute's constitutionality was upheld by the Supreme Court in a series of cases during the 1970s, and prosecutors gradually developed the expertise to use it effectively.

The breakthrough came in the early 1980s, when federal prosecutors in New York and elsewhere began to use RICO systematically against the American Mafia. The "Commission Case" of 1986 was perhaps the most dramatic application of the statute. United States Attorney for the Southern District of New York Rudolph Giuliani used RICO to charge the bosses of all five New York crime families simultaneously, prosecuting them not for individual crimes but for their participation in the enterprise known as the Commission, the governing body of American organized crime. The indictment charged that the Commission itself was a criminal enterprise, and that the family bosses had participated in its conduct through a pattern of racketeering that included murder, extortion, labor union corruption, and narcotics trafficking. All of the defendants were convicted, with sentences ranging from 40 to 100 years. The men who had effectively controlled organized crime in New York for decades went to prison not just for what they had personally done but for what their organization had done in their name and under their direction.

Similar prosecutions followed in other cities and against other criminal organizations. The Patriarca family in New England, the DeCavalcante family in New Jersey, the Chicago Outfit, and criminal organizations in Philadelphia, Boston, Detroit, and other cities were systematically prosecuted under RICO in the 1980s and 1990s. These prosecutions did not merely imprison individual criminals; they disrupted and in many cases permanently dismantled criminal enterprises that had operated for decades. RICO gave prosecutors the ability to charge entire organizations, to use the testimony of cooperating witnesses against multiple defendants simultaneously, and to seize the assets that criminal enterprises had accumulated, removing both the leadership and the financial foundation of those enterprises in a single set of prosecutions.

RICO Beyond Organized Crime

The breadth of RICO's language, which required only an enterprise, a pattern of racketeering, and participation, without specifying what kind of enterprise or what kind of participants, made it applicable far beyond its original target of organized crime. During the 1980s, civil RICO became a favored tool of private litigants in commercial disputes, securities fraud cases, and other civil litigation that bore no resemblance to the Mafia prosecutions for which the statute was designed. Courts struggled to limit civil RICO to cases with a genuine organized crime nexus, with mixed results. The Supreme Court eventually held in 1993 that the civil RICO statute did not require proof of any organized crime connection, meaning that ordinary commercial fraud could be prosecuted as racketeering if it met the pattern requirement.

Criminal RICO was also extended beyond its original targets in ways that generated significant controversy. Prosecutors used RICO against street gangs, motorcycle clubs, abortion clinic protesters, and corporate executives, arguing in each case that the defendant's organization met the statutory definition of an enterprise and that the criminal acts it committed constituted a pattern of racketeering. Courts generally upheld these applications, though with some limiting constructions, and the debate about whether RICO had expanded beyond its appropriate boundaries continued into the twenty-first century.

The controversy over RICO's scope reflected a deeper tension in anti-organized crime law generally. A tool powerful enough to dismantle the Mafia is also, almost by definition, powerful enough to be misused or overextended. The history of RICO after 1970 is in significant part a history of that tension, as courts, legislators, and prosecutors negotiated the boundaries of a statute whose language was broad enough to encompass a very wide range of conduct.

Historical Vocabulary
RICO (Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act)

RICO is the common name for Title IX of the Organized Crime Control Act of 1970 (18 U.S.C. §§ 1961–1968). The statute prohibits participating in the conduct of an enterprise through a pattern of racketeering activity. A "pattern" requires at least two predicate acts within ten years. "Racketeering activity" includes a list of state and federal offenses, including murder, kidnapping, extortion, fraud, bribery, and narcotics trafficking.

RICO's central innovation was the concept of enterprise liability: the idea that a person can be criminally liable not just for specific acts they personally committed, but for their participation in an enterprise whose affairs were conducted through a pattern of criminal activity. This allowed prosecutors to charge crime bosses who gave orders but rarely committed acts themselves, reaching the leadership of criminal organizations that traditional law could not touch.

RICO carries penalties of up to twenty years in prison per count and provides for criminal forfeiture of all proceeds of racketeering. Its civil counterpart, also in the statute, allows private parties injured by racketeering to sue for treble damages. Both criminal and civil RICO have been applied far beyond the organized crime context for which the statute was originally designed.

Section 33.2

Asset Forfeiture: Attacking the Financial Foundation of Crime

One of the fundamental insights that shaped the legal framework against organized crime in the latter half of the twentieth century was that imprisonment alone was insufficient to destroy criminal enterprises. An organization whose leader was imprisoned could replace him. An organization whose membership was decimated by prosecution could recruit new members. But an organization stripped of its financial resources, its property, its businesses, and the accumulated profits of decades of criminal activity was genuinely crippled. Asset forfeiture, the legal seizure of property connected to criminal activity, became a central pillar of the anti-organized crime strategy precisely because it attacked not just the people who ran criminal enterprises but the enterprises themselves as economic institutions.

The idea of forfeiture has ancient roots. English common law recognized the crown's right to seize the property of convicted felons, and admiralty law had long allowed the seizure of ships and cargo involved in piracy and smuggling. American law inherited these traditions, and forfeiture provisions appeared in early federal statutes dealing with customs violations and alcohol excise taxes. The connection of forfeiture to organized crime prosecution, however, was a development of the late twentieth century, driven by the recognition that criminal enterprises had accumulated vast wealth that survived the imprisonment of their leaders and continued to fund criminal operations.

Criminal and Civil Forfeiture Distinguished

American forfeiture law distinguishes between two fundamentally different types of proceedings: criminal forfeiture and civil forfeiture. The distinction is not merely procedural; it goes to the heart of the constitutional protections at stake.

Criminal forfeiture is a component of a criminal sentence. When a defendant is convicted of a crime for which forfeiture is authorized, the court may order, as part of the sentence, that the defendant forfeit property connected to the crime. Because criminal forfeiture follows a criminal conviction, it is subject to all the constitutional protections that attach to criminal proceedings: the government must prove its case beyond a reasonable doubt, the defendant has the right to counsel, the right to confront witnesses, the right to trial by jury, and the protection against self-incrimination. Criminal forfeiture orders entered after RICO convictions stripped organized crime leaders of profits accumulated over decades of criminal activity and were a major component of the government's strategy against the Mafia in the 1980s.

Civil forfeiture, by contrast, is a proceeding against property rather than against a person. The legal theory, derived from admiralty law, is that the property itself is guilty and can be seized and forfeited without any criminal conviction, and indeed without any criminal charge against the owner of the property. Because the proceeding is civil rather than criminal, the constitutional protections of the criminal process do not fully apply. The government need not prove its case beyond a reasonable doubt; in most federal civil forfeiture proceedings before 2000, the government needed only to show probable cause to believe the property was connected to criminal activity, after which the burden shifted to the owner to prove that it was not. The owner had no right to appointed counsel in civil forfeiture proceedings. And the property could be seized before any judicial determination that it was actually connected to criminal activity.

Civil forfeiture's procedural advantages made it enormously attractive to law enforcement agencies, particularly after Congress dramatically expanded its scope in the 1984 Comprehensive Crime Control Act. The 1984 Act extended civil forfeiture to virtually all drug-related property, created the Justice Department Assets Forfeiture Fund to receive the proceeds of federal forfeitures, and allowed law enforcement agencies to retain a share of the proceeds for their own use. This last provision created a financial incentive for forfeiture that would prove to be one of the most controversial aspects of the system.

Forfeiture in Practice: Drug Cartels and Beyond

The expansion of civil forfeiture in the 1980s was driven primarily by the war on drugs, and its most dramatic applications were against the financial infrastructure of major drug trafficking organizations. The Colombian Medellín Cartel alone was estimated to have earned billions of dollars annually from cocaine trafficking in the mid-1980s, and prosecutors recognized that imprisonment of individual traffickers, while necessary, would not dismantle an organization with resources of that magnitude. Forfeiture proceedings targeting cartel-connected bank accounts, real estate, aircraft, and businesses represented an effort to attack the financial foundation of drug trafficking at a systemic level.

These prosecutions achieved significant results in specific cases. Operation Casablanca in 1998, a major undercover investigation into money laundering by Mexican drug cartels, resulted in the seizure of over 130 million dollars in cartel funds from banks in Mexico and elsewhere, along with the indictment of more than one hundred individuals and several banking institutions. Operation Greenback in the early 1980s targeted the banking infrastructure that Colombian cartels used to launder cocaine proceeds, resulting in forfeitures of millions of dollars and the development of the anti-money-laundering legal framework that would be elaborated in subsequent legislation.

But civil forfeiture also produced systematic abuses that drew increasing scrutiny and criticism throughout the 1990s and into the twenty-first century. The financial incentives created by the 1984 Act, which allowed law enforcement agencies to retain forfeiture proceeds for their own use, created powerful pressure on agencies to maximize forfeitures regardless of whether the targets were actually connected to major criminal organizations. In practice, a significant proportion of civil forfeiture actions were brought not against drug kingpins and organized crime bosses but against ordinary people, often from minority communities, who were stopped on highways or at airports carrying amounts of cash that law enforcement deemed suspicious.

The Institute for Justice, a libertarian public interest law organization, documented hundreds of cases in which people who had never been charged with any crime had cash, vehicles, and other property seized under civil forfeiture laws. The low procedural burdens on the government, combined with the high cost of litigation to contest forfeitures, meant that many people did not contest seizures even when they had a valid legal basis for doing so. The financial incentives created by the forfeiture fund also created what critics described as perverse incentives: law enforcement agencies had a direct financial interest in maximizing forfeitures, which could create pressure to seize property in cases where the criminal connection was weak or non-existent.

Reform and the Ongoing Debate

The documented abuses of civil forfeiture generated sustained political pressure for reform across the ideological spectrum, with civil liberties organizations on the left and libertarian groups on the right converging on similar critiques. In 2000, Congress enacted the Civil Asset Forfeiture Reform Act, which shifted the burden of proof in federal forfeiture proceedings from the owner (who had previously been required to prove innocence) to the government (which was now required to prove by a preponderance of the evidence that the property was connected to criminal activity). The Act also provided for appointed counsel in some federal forfeiture cases and allowed claimants to recover attorney's fees if they prevailed.

The 2000 reform addressed some of the most egregious procedural inequities of the civil forfeiture system, but critics argued that it did not go far enough. The preponderance standard was still far lower than the beyond-a-reasonable-doubt standard that would apply in a criminal case. Financial incentives for law enforcement remained largely intact, as agencies continued to retain a share of forfeiture proceeds. And state civil forfeiture laws, which varied widely in their procedural protections, were unaffected by the federal reform. By the 2010s, several states had enacted more comprehensive reforms, including requirements that forfeiture follow criminal conviction rather than merely probable cause, but the landscape remained varied and contested.

The debate over civil forfeiture illustrated a recurring tension in the legal framework against organized crime: the tools that were most effective against sophisticated criminal enterprises also created the greatest potential for abuse against ordinary citizens who lacked the resources and legal sophistication to protect their rights. A legal system that could strip a drug cartel of its financial infrastructure could also, under the same legal authority, strip an innocent person of their life savings. Getting the balance right required constant attention to the gap between the stated purpose of a legal tool and its actual use in practice.

Historical Vocabulary
Asset Forfeiture

Asset forfeiture is the legal process by which a government seizes property connected to criminal activity. It is distinguished from a criminal fine, which is a penalty imposed on a person, because forfeiture is directed at the property itself. American law recognizes two types of forfeiture with fundamentally different legal standards.

Criminal forfeiture is imposed as part of a criminal sentence following conviction. All the constitutional protections of the criminal process apply: proof beyond a reasonable doubt, right to counsel, right to jury trial. Criminal forfeiture requires a conviction before the government can seize property.

Civil forfeiture is a proceeding against property, not persons, and does not require a criminal conviction or even a criminal charge. The legal standard is lower than in criminal cases. Civil forfeiture has been criticized because it can deprive people of property without the constitutional protections of a criminal trial, and because financial incentives for law enforcement agencies to maximize forfeitures can lead to abuse. The Civil Asset Forfeiture Reform Act of 2000 addressed some of these concerns but did not eliminate the fundamental procedural differences between civil and criminal forfeiture.

Section 33.3

Witness Protection: Breaking the Code of Silence

One of the most formidable weapons in organized crime's arsenal has always been the ability to silence potential witnesses. The Mafia's code of silence, omertà, was not merely a cultural tradition; it was enforced by the credible threat of extreme violence against anyone who cooperated with law enforcement. Witnesses who testified against organized crime figures faced murder, not just as a retaliatory threat after testifying but as a preventive measure before trial. The bodies of potential witnesses, of those who had talked to investigators, of family members used as leverage, constituted a grim record of organized crime's capacity to deter cooperation through fear.

The problem of witness intimidation and murder was not merely an obstacle to individual prosecutions; it was a structural defense of criminal enterprises against the legal system as a whole. If potential witnesses could be reliably frightened or killed before they could testify, organized crime leaders could operate with a reasonable confidence that the legal consequences of their activities would remain limited. The few convictions that were obtained depended on circumstantial evidence, government agents who had infiltrated criminal organizations, or witnesses brave enough to testify despite the risks, most of whom received only minimal protection.

The Origins of WITSEC

The federal government had been providing some form of informal protection to key witnesses in organized crime cases since the early 1960s, but the protections were ad hoc, poorly funded, and often inadequate. The decision to create a formal, comprehensive witness protection program was driven in significant part by the experience of Joseph Valachi, whose 1963 Senate testimony about the structure and operations of Cosa Nostra was a landmark moment in the public understanding of organized crime. Valachi testified that he had been marked for death by the Mafia after being incorrectly suspected of cooperation with law enforcement, and that his decision to actually cooperate was in part a response to the death sentence that had already been pronounced on him by the organization he had served for decades. His testimony was explosive and historically significant, but the government's ability to protect him was limited, and he spent the rest of his life in federal custody rather than receiving a genuine new identity and life.

Title V of the Organized Crime Control Act of 1970 created the formal statutory basis for what became the Witness Security Program, administered by the United States Marshals Service. The program provided for the relocation and protection of witnesses who had provided or agreed to provide testimony or information to federal authorities in proceedings related to organized crime or other serious criminal activity, and whose continued cooperation posed a danger to their safety. In practice, the program went far beyond simple relocation: it provided participants with entirely new identities, including new names, Social Security numbers, birth certificates, and other documents; new housing in locations far from their original homes; financial support during the transition period; employment assistance; and ongoing security monitoring.

How WITSEC Works

Entry into the Witness Security Program is not automatic for any cooperating witness. The decision to admit a witness to the program is made by the Department of Justice based on an assessment of the threat to the witness's safety, the importance of the witness's testimony, and the likelihood that the witness will successfully adapt to a new identity. Witnesses who are admitted to the program must agree to cooperate fully with ongoing law enforcement investigations and prosecutions, and must agree to restrictions on their activities, including prohibitions on returning to their former communities or contacting former associates.

Once admitted, a witness receives a completely new legal identity. The Marshals Service works with state agencies and federal departments to create genuinely usable documentation, including driver's licenses, birth certificates, Social Security records, and educational credentials. This process is complex and legally sensitive, as it involves the creation of official government documents in fictitious identities, something that would be a federal crime if done outside the program's legal framework. The program's legal authority to create these documents is established by statute, and the documents it creates are genuine in the sense that they are issued by the appropriate agencies and will withstand ordinary verification.

The practical and human challenges of entering the program are substantial. Participants must sever all contact with family members, friends, and communities they have known their entire lives. They must adjust to living in an unfamiliar region, often with significantly different cultural, social, and economic conditions than their home communities. They must maintain the discipline to avoid activities that might reveal their true identities: contacting old friends, visiting home communities, or engaging in criminal activities that might bring them to the attention of law enforcement in ways that could connect their new identities to their old ones. Many participants, accustomed to the structure and hierarchy of criminal organizations, find the adjustment to ordinary civilian life genuinely difficult.

The program has also had to grapple with witnesses who continue criminal activities after entering the program. When a witness who has received a new identity is arrested for a new crime under that identity, law enforcement agencies must decide whether to reveal the witness's true identity, which might expose previous cooperating agreements and undermine ongoing investigations, or to pursue the new charges under the new identity, which raises obvious questions about accountability. The Marshals Service has developed protocols for dealing with these situations, but they remain among the most difficult ethical and legal challenges the program faces.

WITSEC's Impact on Organized Crime Prosecution

Despite its practical challenges and ethical complexities, WITSEC transformed the prosecution of organized crime by making it genuinely possible to break omertà on a systematic basis. Before the program existed, cooperation with law enforcement was a near-death sentence for anyone embedded deeply enough in criminal organizations to have genuinely valuable testimony. After it was established, the calculus changed: a cooperating witness could receive not just legal benefits, typically a reduced sentence, but a genuine new life in which the threat of Mafia retribution was substantially reduced.

The impact was evident in the prosecutions of the 1980s and 1990s. Henry Hill, a low-level Lucchese family associate, entered WITSEC after becoming a government witness in 1980 and provided testimony that led to significant convictions. Salvatore "Sammy the Bull" Gravano, the former underboss of the Gambino family, became a government witness in 1991 and provided testimony that was instrumental in the conviction of John Gotti, the Gambino boss who had become the most publicly prominent organized crime figure in the country. Gravano admitted to participation in nineteen murders but received a sentence of five years in exchange for his cooperation, a deal that generated significant public controversy but that prosecutors argued was justified by the importance of his testimony in reaching a target as protected as Gotti.

The Gravano case illustrated both the power and the moral difficulty of WITSEC and the cooperation agreements that supported it. Critics argued that a government that rewarded someone who admitted participating in nineteen murders with a five-year sentence and a new identity was making a deeply problematic moral compromise. Defenders argued that organized crime's most dangerous leaders could not be prosecuted without the testimony of insiders who were themselves serious criminals, and that the alternative, allowing those leaders to operate indefinitely beyond the law's reach, was worse than the moral cost of rewarding cooperation. The debate did not have an easy resolution, and it continued in various forms for as long as the cooperation-based prosecution strategy remained central to organized crime enforcement.

Historical Vocabulary
WITSEC (Witness Security Program)

WITSEC, the Witness Security Program, was established by Title V of the Organized Crime Control Act of 1970 and is administered by the United States Marshals Service. It provides relocation, new identities, and ongoing security support to witnesses whose cooperation with federal authorities creates a risk to their safety.

The program's legal authority to create new official identities, including Social Security numbers, driver's licenses, and birth certificates, is established by statute. Participants must agree to cooperate fully with ongoing investigations and to comply with program restrictions, including no contact with former criminal associates.

Since its founding, WITSEC has protected more than 19,000 witnesses and family members. Its impact on organized crime prosecution has been profound, because it made meaningful cooperation with law enforcement possible for witnesses who would otherwise have faced certain death. However, it also raises persistent moral and legal questions about the government's practice of rewarding serious criminals with lenient treatment and new lives in exchange for testimony.

Section 33.4

International Treaties: Building a Global Legal Framework

The great structural advantage that transnational criminal organizations hold over national legal systems is the ability to move people, money, goods, and information across international borders that law enforcement cannot easily cross. A drug trafficking organization that operates simultaneously in Colombia, Mexico, the United States, and Europe is far harder to prosecute than one that operates within a single jurisdiction, because no single country's laws apply to the entire operation, no single country's courts have jurisdiction over all the relevant actors, and evidence gathered in one country is often difficult or impossible to use in another country's courts. The same principle applies to money laundering operations that route criminal proceeds through a dozen jurisdictions, to human trafficking networks that transport victims across multiple borders, and to cybercriminal groups that attack targets in one country while operating from servers in another.

The response to this structural challenge has been the development of international legal frameworks for cooperation against organized crime, frameworks that allow national legal systems to work together in ways that approximate the transnational reach of the criminal organizations they are trying to combat. This development has been gradual, difficult, and incomplete. International cooperation requires sovereign states to agree to share information, to assist each other's investigations, to extradite fugitives, and sometimes to adjust their own domestic laws to meet common standards, all of which involve constraints on national sovereignty that states are often reluctant to accept.

Early International Drug Control

The first significant international legal framework aimed at criminal activity was not directed at organized crime generally but at the narcotics trade specifically. The International Opium Convention of 1912, signed at The Hague, was the first international drug control treaty, establishing the principle that states had an obligation to regulate the production and distribution of opium, morphine, heroin, and cocaine through their domestic legal systems. Subsequent conventions in 1925, 1931, and 1936 expanded the scope of international drug control and created the rudiments of an international enforcement framework, though the actual enforcement remained almost entirely in the hands of national governments operating within their own jurisdictions.

The Single Convention on Narcotic Drugs of 1961, negotiated under United Nations auspices, consolidated and replaced earlier drug control treaties and established the framework that governed international narcotics control for the rest of the twentieth century. It created a list of controlled substances, established obligations for parties to criminalize production and trafficking, and created the International Narcotics Control Board to monitor compliance. The 1971 Convention on Psychotropic Substances extended similar controls to synthetic drugs, and the 1988 United Nations Convention Against Illicit Traffic in Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances went significantly further, requiring parties to criminalize money laundering connected to drug trafficking, to allow for mutual legal assistance and extradition in drug cases, and to permit the seizure and confiscation of assets connected to drug trafficking.

The 1988 convention was a significant step forward in the international framework because it addressed not just the production and trafficking of drugs but the financial infrastructure that supported drug trafficking organizations. By requiring parties to criminalize money laundering and to provide for asset forfeiture, it extended the legal toolkit of the war on drugs from individual acts of trafficking to the organizational and financial structures that sustained drug trafficking as an industry.

The Palermo Convention and Its Protocols

The most comprehensive international legal instrument directed specifically against organized crime is the United Nations Convention Against Transnational Organized Crime, known as the Palermo Convention after the Sicilian city where it was opened for signature in December 2000. The convention entered into force in 2003 and had more than 190 state parties by the 2020s, making it one of the most widely ratified international criminal law conventions in history.

The Palermo Convention requires state parties to criminalize participation in an organized criminal group, money laundering, corruption, and obstruction of justice. It creates obligations for states to cooperate in extradition, mutual legal assistance, joint investigations, and law enforcement training. It requires states to take measures to confiscate the proceeds of organized crime. And it establishes definitions of key terms, including "organized criminal group," that create a common legal vocabulary for international cooperation that had not previously existed.

Three protocols supplement the main convention, each addressing a specific aspect of transnational organized crime. The Protocol to Prevent, Suppress and Punish Trafficking in Persons, Especially Women and Children (the "Trafficking Protocol") requires parties to criminalize human trafficking, to protect and assist trafficking victims, and to cooperate in prosecuting trafficking offenses. The Protocol Against the Smuggling of Migrants by Land, Sea and Air requires parties to criminalize migrant smuggling and to cooperate in preventing and punishing it. The Protocol Against the Illicit Manufacturing of and Trafficking in Firearms requires parties to criminalize illicit weapons manufacturing and trafficking and to improve record-keeping and information-sharing about firearms.

Together, the Palermo Convention and its protocols created the most comprehensive international legal framework for fighting organized crime that had ever existed. But the framework had significant limitations. International conventions create obligations for states, not mechanisms for actually enforcing those obligations. A state that signed and ratified the Palermo Convention but failed to implement its requirements in domestic law, or implemented them inadequately, or allowed corruption to undermine their enforcement, faced no direct legal consequence beyond diplomatic pressure. The convention contained no enforcement mechanism comparable to the domestic courts, prosecutors, and police forces that enforce national law.

Mutual Legal Assistance and Extradition

Two specific mechanisms of international legal cooperation are particularly important for organized crime prosecution: mutual legal assistance and extradition. Mutual legal assistance treaties (MLATs) are bilateral or multilateral agreements through which countries agree to assist each other in gathering evidence for criminal investigations and prosecutions. Under a typical MLAT, when a prosecutor in the United States needs evidence located in, for example, Switzerland, the prosecutor can submit a request through diplomatic channels, and the Swiss authorities will use their own legal processes to gather the requested evidence and transmit it to the United States.

MLATs were a major advance in international law enforcement cooperation because they created formal, legally binding frameworks for evidence sharing that did not previously exist. Before MLATs, requesting evidence from foreign countries was an uncertain and often fruitless diplomatic exercise. With MLATs, the requesting country had a legal basis for the request and the receiving country had a legal obligation to fulfill it, subject to various exceptions and limitations. By the early twenty-first century, the United States had MLATs with more than sixty countries, covering the major financial centers and most of the jurisdictions through which criminal money routinely flowed.

Extradition, the formal process by which one country surrenders a criminal suspect to another country for prosecution, was equally important for organized crime cases where key defendants had fled to, or operated from, foreign jurisdictions. Extradition agreements typically contained exceptions for political offenses and sometimes for crimes that were not offenses in both countries, and they often excluded the extradition of a country's own nationals. Organized crime figures, particularly drug traffickers, became expert at exploiting the gaps in extradition frameworks, establishing themselves in countries that did not have extradition treaties with the countries where they were most actively pursued.

Colombia's long resistance to the extradition of its nationals to the United States became one of the central diplomatic issues in the war on drugs during the 1980s and 1990s. The Medellín Cartel explicitly targeted extradition as its greatest legal threat, and Pablo Escobar's campaign of murder and terrorism against Colombian politicians and judges was in significant part aimed at preventing Colombia from extraditing him and his associates to the United States, where they faced virtually certain conviction and lengthy sentences. The Colombian constitution was amended in 1991, under pressure from the cartels, to prohibit the extradition of Colombian nationals, a prohibition that was reversed by a subsequent constitutional amendment in 1997 after the Cali Cartel's leadership was imprisoned and its political influence waned.

Financial Intelligence and Anti-Money-Laundering Frameworks

The recognition that organized crime's power rested on its financial resources led to the development of specialized international frameworks for combating money laundering, the process by which criminal proceeds are converted into seemingly legitimate wealth. The Financial Action Task Force (FATF), established in 1989 by the G7 countries, became the primary international body for setting and enforcing anti-money-laundering standards.

FATF developed a set of recommendations that established the international standard for anti-money-laundering compliance, requiring member states to criminalize money laundering, to require financial institutions to know their customers and report suspicious transactions, to create financial intelligence units to analyze suspicious transaction reports, and to cooperate with other countries' anti-money-laundering efforts. FATF also created a mutual evaluation process through which member states' compliance with these standards was assessed by teams of experts from other member states, with the results published publicly.

The FATF framework represented a significant innovation in international financial regulation. By requiring financial institutions to actively monitor their customers for suspicious activity and to report that activity to government authorities, it enlisted the private sector as a partner in anti-money-laundering enforcement. Banks and other financial institutions that failed to comply with FATF standards faced regulatory sanctions and reputational consequences that created strong incentives for compliance. Countries that failed to implement FATF recommendations faced the possibility of being placed on a "blacklist" that would trigger enhanced scrutiny of their financial institutions by the international community.

The extension of these frameworks to cover not just traditional financial institutions but also real estate professionals, lawyers, accountants, and other "gatekeepers" who facilitated the movement of criminal money reflected the recognition that organized crime had learned to exploit a wide range of professional services to launder its proceeds. Shell companies registered in offshore jurisdictions, real estate purchases in global cities, and the use of professional service providers to add layers of apparent legitimacy to criminal wealth were all documented features of organized crime's financial strategies, and the effort to close those avenues through regulatory frameworks was a continuing challenge for the international anti-money-laundering system.

The RICO Statute: Congressional Statement of Findings and Purpose (1970)

Source type: Federal statute · Date: October 15, 1970 · Source: Organized Crime Control Act of 1970, Public Law 91-452

Context: When Congress enacted the Organized Crime Control Act of 1970, it included an explicit congressional statement of findings and purpose that explained the rationale for the dramatic new legal tools the act created. This statement is important not only as a legal document but as a historical source that reveals how Congress understood organized crime in 1970, what it believed existing law could and could not accomplish, and what it intended the new law to achieve. The statement also has legal significance, because courts sometimes look to congressional purpose statements when interpreting ambiguous statutory provisions.

Congress declared in 1970 that organized crime in the United States had become a serious national concern, that it was a highly sophisticated and diversified phenomenon that drew large sums of money from a multitude of illegal enterprises, and that it used this money to penetrate and corrupt legitimate business, labor, and government. Congress found that existing federal law was inadequate to deal with this problem, because it was not designed to reach the organized criminal group as an entity. The act's stated purpose was to seek the eradication of organized crime by strengthening legal tools for evidence gathering, by establishing new penal prohibitions, and by providing enhanced sanctions and new remedies to deal with the unlawful activities of those engaged in organized crime.

Analysis: The congressional findings reveal several important features of how Congress understood organized crime in 1970. First, Congress framed organized crime primarily as an economic threat: the movement of criminal money into legitimate businesses and government was seen as the central danger, not the crimes themselves. Second, Congress explicitly acknowledged that existing law was inadequate, providing a legislative basis for the dramatic expansion of federal criminal law that RICO represented. Third, the stated goals emphasized systemic dismantling of criminal organizations rather than merely punishing individual criminals, reflecting the influence of G. Robert Blakey's enterprise liability theory. The statement also reveals what Congress did not say: there is no mention of civil liberties constraints, of the potential for abuse of the new powers, or of the risk that the statute might be applied beyond its intended targets.

Historical Debate

Civil Asset Forfeiture: Effective Tool or Constitutional Threat?

The question: Civil asset forfeiture allows governments to seize property connected to criminal activity without first obtaining a criminal conviction. Its defenders argue that it is an essential tool for dismantling the financial infrastructure of organized crime and drug trafficking. Its critics argue that it violates fundamental principles of due process and has been systematically abused against people who have never been charged with crimes. How should democratic societies balance the legitimate law enforcement utility of forfeiture against the civil liberties it threatens?

Interpretation A — Civil Forfeiture as Necessary and Justified: Defenders of civil asset forfeiture, including most law enforcement agencies and many prosecutors, argue that it is indispensable for attacking the financial foundations of organized crime. Criminal forfeiture, which requires a conviction, is often inadequate because the most dangerous criminal enterprises structure their ownership of assets to prevent any individual from being convicted while holding directly traceable assets. Civil forfeiture's ability to proceed against property without a conviction allows the government to seize criminal proceeds even when the individuals who accumulated them cannot be conclusively identified or prosecuted. Defenders also point to its use against drug cartels as evidence of its importance: billions of dollars in cartel assets have been seized through civil forfeiture, disrupting the financial operations of organizations that generate enormous criminal revenues.
Interpretation B — Civil Forfeiture as Constitutional Threat: Critics from across the political spectrum, including the American Civil Liberties Union, the Institute for Justice, and libertarian legal scholars, argue that civil forfeiture as currently practiced violates fundamental principles of due process and has been systematically abused. The principle that property can be seized and forfeited without a criminal conviction inverts the presumption of innocence that is fundamental to the common law tradition. The financial incentives created by allowing law enforcement agencies to retain forfeiture proceeds create perverse incentives that encourage agencies to maximize seizures regardless of the strength of the criminal connection. And the documented pattern of forfeitures from people who were never charged with any crime, often from minority communities and often involving small amounts of cash, suggests that the tool is being used against people who cannot afford to contest it rather than against major criminal organizations.

Evidence: Both sides draw on substantial evidence. Law enforcement agencies can point to major organized crime and drug trafficking cases in which civil forfeiture played a significant role in dismantling criminal enterprises. Critics can point to equally documented cases in which civil forfeiture was used against ordinary citizens with no apparent connection to organized crime. Studies by investigative journalists and academic researchers have documented the disproportionate use of civil forfeiture against low-income and minority communities, and the pattern of law enforcement agencies generating significant revenue from forfeiture activities directed at small-dollar seizures rather than major criminal organizations.

Why it matters: The debate over civil forfeiture is ultimately about the appropriate limits of state power in a democratic society that is committed to both security and individual rights. It raises questions that go far beyond organized crime: about what level of certainty the state should require before depriving citizens of their property, about whether financial incentives for law enforcement are compatible with impartial enforcement of the law, and about how democratic societies should balance effective crime control against the rights of the accused and the innocent.

Should civil forfeiture require a criminal conviction? Should law enforcement agencies be permitted to retain forfeiture proceeds? What additional evidence would you want to examine before reaching a conclusion?

Multiple Perspectives

The RICO Act and Witness Protection: Whose Interests Are Served?

The legal framework against organized crime, including RICO, asset forfeiture, and the witness protection program, was designed with a specific set of problems in mind and created a specific set of solutions. But like all legal frameworks, its effects were experienced very differently by different groups.

From the perspective of law enforcement and prosecutors: Federal prosecutors and FBI agents who had spent years watching organized crime bosses operate with near impunity regarded RICO and the broader legal framework as transformative. For the first time, they had tools that could actually reach the men at the top of criminal organizations, that could dismantle enterprises rather than just imprison individuals, and that could strip criminal organizations of the financial resources they needed to operate. The conviction of the Five Families bosses in the 1980s, a result that would have been almost inconceivable with pre-RICO law, validated the framework's design and demonstrated its potential.
From the perspective of organized crime defendants and their attorneys: Defense attorneys who represented organized crime figures argued that RICO and associated tools violated fundamental principles of fair criminal procedure. The enterprise liability theory, they argued, could be used to hold defendants responsible for crimes they did not commit and did not know about, merely because they belonged to an organization that committed those crimes. The cooperation-based prosecution model, in which deeply compromised witnesses with strong incentives to fabricate or exaggerate received enormous sentencing benefits, produced convictions based on tainted testimony. Asset forfeiture before conviction effectively punished defendants before they had been found guilty of anything. These were not merely self-interested arguments; they raised genuine concerns about how far the government should go in its pursuit of criminal enterprises.
From the perspective of communities affected by organized crime: In the neighborhoods where organized crime had operated for generations, the enforcement revolution of the 1980s and 1990s was experienced in complex and sometimes contradictory ways. The collapse of Mafia power in cities like New York and Philadelphia removed criminal organizations that had imposed a kind of order, however violent and exploitative, on local communities. But it also removed employers, however illegal, and local figures who had provided a range of informal services, from settling disputes to providing loans, that legitimate institutions did not always offer. The communities that had been most directly victimized by organized crime, such as union workers whose pensions had been looted and small business owners who had paid protection money for decades, experienced the prosecutions as genuine relief. Others experienced the transition to a world without the old crime families as more complicated.
From the perspective of civil liberties advocates: Organizations such as the American Civil Liberties Union viewed the RICO revolution and especially its associated tools with deep concern. RICO's breadth, which allowed the statute to reach far beyond organized crime to prosecute a wide range of conduct through the enterprise liability theory, represented an expansion of federal criminal law that created significant risks of abuse. Civil asset forfeiture without conviction threatened fundamental principles of due process. The sentencing leverage that cooperation agreements created, allowing prosecutors to offer dramatic sentence reductions in exchange for testimony, raised questions about the reliability of the testimony produced. Each of these concerns was validated by subsequent experience: RICO was applied against abortion protesters and corporate defendants with no connection to organized crime, civil forfeiture was used against people who were never charged with crimes, and several high-profile convictions based on cooperating witness testimony were later questioned or reversed.
Constitutional Connection

Anti-Organized Crime Law and the Constitution

The legal framework against organized crime engages virtually every major constitutional protection in the Bill of Rights, and the history of that framework is in significant part a history of the tension between effective law enforcement and constitutional constraint.

The Fourth Amendment's protection against unreasonable searches and seizures shapes the conduct of organized crime investigations. Wiretap statutes, including Title III of the Omnibus Crime Control Act of 1968, govern the conditions under which law enforcement may intercept private communications, requiring judicial authorization and establishing procedures designed to limit surveillance to circumstances where there is probable cause to believe criminal activity is occurring. The extension of these frameworks to digital communications, including email and internet traffic, has generated ongoing litigation about the application of Fourth Amendment principles to new technologies.

The Fifth Amendment's protections against self-incrimination and deprivation of property without due process of law are directly implicated by both witness cooperation agreements and civil asset forfeiture. The use of immunity to compel potentially self-incriminating testimony, a common tool in organized crime investigations, navigates the Fifth Amendment by removing the incrimination risk: a witness who is granted immunity cannot be prosecuted using their own testimony, so the Fifth Amendment's protection does not apply. Civil asset forfeiture's seizure of property without a criminal conviction raises Fifth Amendment due process questions that courts have addressed, with varying degrees of satisfaction to critics, through decisions requiring some minimum level of procedural protection before property can be permanently forfeited.

The Sixth Amendment's guarantee of the right to confront witnesses, which generally requires that the prosecution's witnesses be available for cross-examination, creates important constraints on the use of hearsay evidence and the testimony of coconspirators in organized crime cases. RICO prosecutions, which often involve complex chains of testimony from multiple cooperating witnesses, require careful attention to these requirements. The right to counsel in criminal proceedings is also central to organized crime cases, in which the complexity of the charges and the severity of the potential sentences make effective legal representation essential.

The Eighth Amendment's prohibition on excessive fines has been applied to some forfeiture proceedings, with the Supreme Court holding in United States v. Bajakajian (1998) that a forfeiture that is grossly disproportionate to the offense can violate the Eighth Amendment. This decision provided some constitutional constraint on the most extreme applications of forfeiture, though its practical impact on the overall forfeiture system has been limited.

Constitutional questions for students: How should courts balance the government's interest in prosecuting organized crime against the individual rights guaranteed by the Bill of Rights? Is it ever constitutionally appropriate to prosecute an organization as an entity, holding individual members responsible for the acts of others? What constitutional limits, if any, should apply to civil asset forfeiture?

Cause and Effect

The RICO Revolution and Its Consequences

Long-Term Causes

Decades of frustration with the inability of traditional criminal law to reach organized crime leadership; Kefauver and McClellan Committee revelations about the scale of organized crime; theoretical work by legal scholars on enterprise liability; Robert Kennedy's prosecution drive that demonstrated both the possibilities and limits of existing tools; rising public concern about organized crime's penetration of labor unions and legitimate business.

Short-Term Causes

Joseph Valachi's 1963 testimony confirming Mafia structure; political climate of the late 1960s emphasizing law and order; the 1967 President's Commission on Law Enforcement report calling for new legal tools; G. Robert Blakey's development of enterprise liability theory; congressional willingness to expand federal criminal jurisdiction as part of the Organized Crime Control Act of 1970.

The Development

Enactment of RICO in 1970 as part of the Organized Crime Control Act; creation of the Witness Security Program; expansion of federal wiretap authority; subsequent development of civil asset forfeiture, money laundering statutes, and international cooperation frameworks throughout the 1970s, 1980s, and 1990s.

Effects

Systematic prosecution and imprisonment of Mafia leadership in the 1980s–1990s; dismantling of Five Families power structure; application of RICO beyond organized crime to corporations and other entities; documented abuses of civil asset forfeiture; transformation of international anti-crime cooperation; ongoing tensions between effective enforcement and civil liberties; adaptation by criminal organizations to new legal environment.

Historical complexity: The RICO revolution achieved genuine successes against organized crime but also created tools powerful enough to be misused. The same enterprise liability theory that imprisoned Mafia bosses also generated controversial prosecutions of abortion protesters, corporate defendants, and political organizations. This pattern, in which tools created for a specific problem prove more powerful and broader in application than anticipated, is a recurring feature of the history of anti-crime legislation.

Continuity and Change Over Time

The Legal Framework Against Organized Crime: 1970 to the Present

What Changed

  • Enterprise liability theory replaced individual act liability as the dominant framework for organized crime prosecution
  • Asset forfeiture became a standard component of organized crime prosecution, not an exceptional measure
  • Witness protection programs made meaningful cooperation possible for deeply embedded criminal insiders
  • International legal cooperation frameworks moved from ad hoc diplomatic requests to treaty-based mutual legal assistance obligations
  • Money laundering became a distinct federal crime, with private financial institutions enlisted as enforcement partners
  • Digital financial surveillance and financial intelligence units created capabilities for tracking criminal money flows that did not exist before the computer era
  • The United Nations developed comprehensive global frameworks for international cooperation against organized crime

What Stayed the Same

  • The fundamental structural advantage of transnational criminal organizations over nationally bounded legal systems persisted despite international cooperation
  • Corruption of public officials remained a major obstacle to effective law enforcement in many jurisdictions
  • The tension between effective crime control and civil liberties protection continued to generate debate and litigation
  • Criminal organizations continued to adapt to legal frameworks, developing new structures, routes, and methods that exploited gaps in legal coverage
  • Demand for illegal goods and services, the fundamental economic driver of organized crime, remained essentially unchanged despite decades of enforcement efforts
  • The basic challenge of reaching criminal leaders who insulate themselves from direct participation in criminal acts persisted, even if RICO provided better tools for addressing it

Why the pattern matters: The history of the legal framework against organized crime is simultaneously a story of genuine innovation and genuine limitation. The tools created between 1970 and 2000 were genuinely more effective than what existed before, and they achieved real results against specific criminal organizations. But they did not solve the underlying problem of organized crime; they shifted its form, location, and methods. Criminal organizations that could not survive the new legal environment were dismantled, but new organizations arose in different forms and in different jurisdictions, often in places where the new legal tools were least available or least effectively implemented.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has traced the development of the legal framework against organized crime from its origins in mid-twentieth-century American law through the creation of a global network of anti-crime treaties and institutions by the early twenty-first century. The central theme of that development has been the effort to create legal tools capable of reaching institutions rather than merely individuals, and to build international cooperation frameworks capable of matching the transnational reach of sophisticated criminal organizations.

The foundational innovation of the American anti-organized crime legal framework was the enterprise liability theory embedded in the RICO statute of 1970. By creating criminal liability for participation in an enterprise through a pattern of racketeering activity, RICO provided prosecutors with a tool that could reach the leaders of criminal organizations who had previously been effectively insulated from prosecution by the structural design of those organizations. The Commission Case of 1986 and the subsequent wave of prosecutions against Mafia families across the country demonstrated both the power of the new tool and its dependence on a cooperative, sustained prosecutorial strategy and the willingness of insiders to become government witnesses.

Asset forfeiture added a financial dimension to the anti-organized crime strategy, attacking the accumulated wealth of criminal enterprises rather than just imprisoning their personnel. Criminal forfeiture, which required conviction, and civil forfeiture, which did not, provided complementary tools for stripping organized crime of the financial resources that sustained it. The expansion of civil forfeiture in the 1980s, driven primarily by the war on drugs, achieved significant results against major drug trafficking organizations but also generated systematic abuses against ordinary citizens that generated bipartisan calls for reform. The Civil Asset Forfeiture Reform Act of 2000 addressed some of those abuses without resolving the underlying tension between the law enforcement utility of forfeiture and its civil liberties costs.

The Witness Security Program transformed the prosecution of organized crime by making genuine cooperation possible for witnesses who would otherwise have faced almost certain death. WITSEC's ability to provide new identities, relocation, and ongoing security support made it possible for the government to offer something genuinely valuable in exchange for testimony, and cooperating witnesses became the cornerstone of the most important organized crime prosecutions of the late twentieth century. The program's success came with significant moral costs: the government was in the business of rewarding serious criminals with dramatically reduced sentences and new lives, and some program participants continued criminal activities after entering the program. These costs were generally judged acceptable given the alternative of allowing the most dangerous criminal leaders to remain beyond the reach of the law, but they generated continuing controversy.

The international dimension of the legal framework against organized crime developed more slowly and remained more incomplete than its domestic American counterpart. International drug control treaties dating to the early twentieth century created the first multilateral frameworks for narcotics regulation, but those frameworks were primarily aimed at legal commerce and had limited impact on illegal trafficking. The 1988 UN Convention Against Illicit Traffic in Narcotics was a significant advance, requiring parties to criminalize money laundering and provide for asset forfeiture and mutual legal assistance. The Palermo Convention of 2000, with its three protocols addressing human trafficking, migrant smuggling, and firearms trafficking, created the most comprehensive international anti-crime framework yet assembled.

The Financial Action Task Force and its anti-money-laundering standards represented a different model of international cooperation, one that operated through regulatory requirements imposed on private financial institutions rather than through direct law enforcement. By requiring banks and other financial institutions to know their customers and report suspicious transactions, the FATF framework enlisted the private sector as a partner in anti-money-laundering enforcement and created an international financial intelligence network that did not exist before the 1990s.

Throughout this history, the legal framework against organized crime has generated persistent tensions with the civil liberties protections of democratic legal systems. The same tools that allowed prosecutors to dismantle the Mafia also created powers that could be, and were, misused against defendants with tenuous connections to organized crime. RICO's breadth made it applicable far beyond its intended targets. Civil forfeiture's procedural advantages created systematic abuse. Cooperation agreements rewarded serious criminals and raised questions about the reliability of the testimony they produced. These tensions were not incidental to the framework; they were inherent in the design of tools powerful enough to reach sophisticated criminal enterprises that had deliberately insulated themselves from the reach of ordinary law.

The challenge for future legal development is not simply to create more powerful tools but to create tools that are more precisely calibrated to their legitimate targets and more effectively constrained against abuse. The proliferation of transnational criminal networks, the development of cybercrime as a major criminal industry, and the increasing sophistication of money laundering through digital financial systems all pose challenges that existing legal frameworks were not designed to address. Meeting those challenges will require the same combination of legal innovation, prosecutorial expertise, and international cooperation that produced the framework described in this chapter, along with a serious ongoing commitment to the civil liberties values that the framework has sometimes compromised in pursuit of effective crime control.

The recurring lesson of the legal framework against organized crime is that criminal organizations adapt to legal environments as effectively as legal environments adapt to criminal organizations. RICO did not eliminate organized crime; it changed its form and shifted its geography. Civil forfeiture did not end drug trafficking; it increased the sophistication of the financial techniques that trafficking organizations use to protect their proceeds. Witness protection did not end the code of silence; it created new risks and new adaptations within criminal cultures. Each legal innovation achieved real results against specific targets while prompting adaptations that created new challenges. Understanding this pattern is essential for anyone who seeks to understand not just the history of anti-organized crime law but the prospects for its future development.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Anti-racketeering law
Legislation directed against organized criminal schemes that extract money through coercion, violence, or corruption; the legal foundation for federal organized crime prosecution in the United States.
Asset forfeiture
The legal process by which governments seize property connected to criminal activity; may be criminal (following conviction) or civil (without conviction required).
Burden of proof
The obligation to establish a fact to a legally required degree of certainty; differs between criminal cases (beyond a reasonable doubt), civil cases (preponderance of the evidence), and forfeiture proceedings (which have varied over time).
Civil forfeiture
Asset seizure proceeding directed against property rather than persons; does not require a criminal conviction; subject to lower evidentiary standards than criminal proceedings; has generated significant civil liberties controversy.
Cooperating witness
A defendant or suspect who agrees to provide testimony or information to law enforcement in exchange for reduced charges or sentencing; essential to most major organized crime prosecutions but raises concerns about witness reliability and the rewards given for cooperation.
Criminal forfeiture
Asset seizure ordered as part of a criminal sentence following conviction; subject to the full constitutional protections of criminal proceedings, including proof beyond a reasonable doubt.
Enterprise liability
The legal theory at the heart of RICO, which holds that a person can be criminally liable for participation in an enterprise through a pattern of racketeering activity without having personally committed specific criminal acts.
Extradition
The formal legal process by which one country surrenders a criminal suspect to another country for prosecution; governed by bilateral or multilateral treaties; organized crime figures frequently exploit gaps in extradition frameworks.
FATF (Financial Action Task Force)
International body established in 1989 by the G7 countries to develop and promote anti-money-laundering standards; evaluates member states' compliance and maintains lists of non-compliant jurisdictions.
Hobbs Act (1946)
Federal statute prohibiting extortion and robbery affecting interstate commerce; important precursor to RICO; established federal jurisdiction over organized crime activities through the interstate commerce connection.
Immunity
A legal protection from prosecution granted to a witness in exchange for testimony; removes the Fifth Amendment's self-incrimination protection by eliminating the risk that the compelled testimony can be used against the witness.
Kefauver Committee
The Senate Special Committee to Investigate Crime in Interstate Commerce (1950–1951), chaired by Senator Estes Kefauver; its nationally televised hearings exposed organized crime as a national institution and generated political pressure for new legislation.
Money laundering
The process of concealing the criminal origin of illegal proceeds by passing them through a series of transactions that make them appear to be legitimate income; criminalized as a distinct federal offense by the Money Laundering Control Act of 1986.
Mutual legal assistance treaty (MLAT)
A bilateral or multilateral agreement through which countries agree to assist each other in gathering evidence for criminal investigations and prosecutions; essential for cases involving transnational criminal organizations.
Palermo Convention
The United Nations Convention Against Transnational Organized Crime (2000); the first comprehensive international treaty specifically targeting organized crime; supplemented by protocols on human trafficking, migrant smuggling, and firearms.
Pattern of racketeering
Under RICO, at least two predicate acts committed within ten years; the requirement for a pattern distinguishes RICO from statutes that punish individual criminal acts.
Predicate act
Under RICO, one of a list of state and federal offenses whose commission, in a pattern, establishes racketeering activity; includes murder, kidnapping, extortion, fraud, bribery, and narcotics trafficking, among others.
Racketeering
Originally, the use of threats and violence to extort money from businesses; under RICO, a broader concept encompassing a wide range of criminal activities conducted through or in connection with an enterprise.
RICO (Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act)
Title IX of the Organized Crime Control Act of 1970; created enterprise liability for participation in a criminal enterprise through a pattern of racketeering activity; the most important anti-organized crime statute in American legal history.
Strike force
A team of prosecutors and law enforcement agents drawn from multiple agencies and deployed to a specific city or region to prosecute organized crime; used by the Justice Department beginning in the 1960s.
Witness protection (WITSEC)
The federal Witness Security Program, administered by the U.S. Marshals Service, which provides new identities, relocation, and security support to witnesses whose cooperation with law enforcement creates risks to their safety.

Major People, Laws, and Institutions

Identification & Significance

G. Robert Blakey (1936–)
Notre Dame law professor who served as chief counsel to the Senate Subcommittee on Criminal Laws and Procedures and was the principal drafter of the RICO statute; developed the enterprise liability theory that RICO embodies; later served as chief counsel to the House Select Committee on Assassinations.
Estes Kefauver (1903–1963)
U.S. Senator from Tennessee who chaired the nationally televised Senate Crime Investigating Committee (1950–1951); his hearings exposed organized crime to a national audience of millions and created the political momentum for new anti-crime legislation.
Robert F. Kennedy (1925–1968)
Attorney General under President Kennedy (1961–1964); made organized crime prosecution a personal mission; dramatically expanded federal prosecutorial and investigative resources devoted to organized crime; his prosecutorial drive both achieved results and demonstrated the inadequacy of pre-RICO law.
Joseph Valachi (1903–1971)
Cosa Nostra soldier who became the first major insider to testify publicly about the American Mafia's structure and operations (Senate hearings, 1963); his testimony transformed public and congressional understanding of organized crime and accelerated legislative reform.
Rudolph Giuliani (1944–)
United States Attorney for the Southern District of New York (1983–1989); led the Commission Case prosecution that used RICO to convict the bosses of all five New York Mafia families simultaneously; his use of RICO became the model for subsequent organized crime prosecutions.
Salvatore "Sammy the Bull" Gravano (1945–)
Former underboss of the Gambino crime family who became a government witness in 1991; his testimony was central to the conviction of Gambino boss John Gotti; his cooperation, despite admitting participation in nineteen murders, illustrated both the power and the moral complexity of the witness cooperation model.
Organized Crime Control Act (1970)
Comprehensive federal legislation that created RICO, established the Witness Security Program, expanded federal wiretapping authority, and provided new tools for combating organized crime; the most significant piece of anti-organized crime legislation in American history.
Comprehensive Crime Control Act (1984)
Federal legislation that dramatically expanded civil asset forfeiture, particularly in drug cases; created the Justice Assets Forfeiture Fund; generated both significant law enforcement successes and documented civil liberties abuses.
Money Laundering Control Act (1986)
Federal legislation that created the distinct crime of money laundering; required financial institutions to report suspicious transactions; became the legal foundation for the anti-money-laundering enforcement framework.
Civil Asset Forfeiture Reform Act (2000)
Federal legislation responding to documented abuses of civil forfeiture; shifted burden of proof to the government; provided for appointed counsel in some cases; addressed some procedural inequities without resolving all civil liberties concerns.
UN Palermo Convention (2000)
The United Nations Convention Against Transnational Organized Crime; opened for signature in Palermo, Sicily in December 2000; the most comprehensive international anti-crime treaty ever negotiated; supplemented by protocols on human trafficking, migrant smuggling, and firearms.
Financial Action Task Force (FATF)
Established by the G7 countries in 1989; sets international anti-money-laundering standards; conducts mutual evaluations of member states; maintains grey and black lists of non-compliant jurisdictions; has become the primary international body for anti-money-laundering regulatory development.

Chapter Reflection

The legal framework against organized crime that took shape between 1946 and 2000 represents one of the most significant expansions of state power in the history of democratic law. RICO, asset forfeiture, witness protection, international treaties, and anti-money-laundering regulations together created a set of tools that genuinely transformed the state's ability to prosecute criminal enterprises as institutions rather than merely as collections of individual wrongdoers. The prosecutions of the American Mafia in the 1980s and 1990s demonstrated what those tools could accomplish when applied with sustained prosecutorial commitment and adequate resources. The Five Families of New York, which had operated with near impunity for half a century, were systematically dismantled within a decade of the Commission Case. That result would have been inconceivable under the legal framework that existed before 1970.

But the history of these legal tools also reveals with great clarity the difficulty of designing powerful instruments for specific purposes in a legal system committed to broad principle rather than narrow application. Every tool created to fight organized crime turned out to be a tool that could be used for other purposes, and often was. RICO was designed for the Mafia; it was applied to abortion protesters and corporate executives. Civil forfeiture was designed to strip drug cartels of their financial infrastructure; it was used against innocent people who lacked the resources to contest seizures. Witness cooperation was designed to bring insulated crime bosses to justice; it placed deeply compromised witnesses into new lives while some continued criminal activities. The gap between stated purpose and actual practice is a recurring feature of anti-organized crime law, and it raises fundamental questions about how democratic societies should design and constrain powerful enforcement tools.

The international dimension of this history adds another layer of complexity. Despite fifty years of treaty-making, the international legal framework against organized crime remains significantly less developed than its domestic counterpart. The Palermo Convention and FATF represent genuine achievements, but they operate through the consent of sovereign states that have very different levels of commitment, capacity, and corruption when it comes to anti-crime enforcement. A treaty that is ratified but not implemented is of limited use against criminal organizations that have learned to exploit the gaps between national legal systems with remarkable sophistication. The continuing ability of transnational criminal networks to exploit jurisdictional boundaries, despite two decades of Palermo Convention implementation, is a reminder that legal frameworks, however comprehensive on paper, are only as effective as the political will and institutional capacity that support them in practice.

The deepest lesson of this chapter may be that the legal framework against organized crime cannot be understood in isolation from the political, economic, and social conditions that sustain organized crime in the first place. Laws can dismantle specific organizations and imprison specific individuals. They cannot, by themselves, eliminate the demand for illegal goods and services, reduce the economic inequality that makes criminal enterprises attractive to recruits, or build the institutional integrity that prevents the corruption on which organized crime depends. The legal tools described in this chapter are necessary but not sufficient responses to the challenge of organized crime. Understanding their history, their achievements, and their limitations is essential for anyone who seeks to think seriously about what democratic societies can and should do about the enduring challenge of organized crime.

As this textbook moves toward its conclusion in the remaining chapters, examining the recurring cycles of organized crime, the persistence of criminal enterprises despite legal frameworks, and the prospects for more effective responses in the twenty-first century, students who understand the history of anti-organized crime law will be better equipped to evaluate both the achievements of the legal framework and the limits of what law alone can accomplish. The contest between organized crime and the states that seek to suppress it is not one that any legal framework can win decisively and permanently. It is, rather, an ongoing negotiation whose terms are shaped by the relative ingenuity, resources, and commitment of both sides, and by the social and economic conditions that give organized crime its persistent appeal despite decades of legal innovation directed against it.

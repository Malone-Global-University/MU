Part VII — The Drug Cartel Era

Mexican Cartels

Organized Crime: A Global History

Chapter Opening

"Mexico is not a failed state, but it is a state that in significant parts of its territory has been captured, contested, or intimidated by criminal organizations that have grown richer and more powerful than most governments in the world."

On the morning of November 4, 2019, a convoy of vehicles traveling a remote road in the Mexican state of Sonora near the United States border came under sustained gunfire from masked assailants armed with military-grade weapons. When the shooting stopped, nine members of the LeBaron family, an extended Mormon community with dual Mexican and American citizenship, lay dead, among them six children. The attack shocked audiences on both sides of the border, prompted a diplomatic crisis between Mexico City and Washington, and forced the question that had haunted North America for more than three decades to the front pages of newspapers around the world: how had criminal organizations operating out of one of the Western Hemisphere's largest economies come to exercise such devastating power over territory, government, and daily life?

The story of Mexican drug cartels is, at its core, a story about the intersection of geography, economics, state capacity, and the relentless logic of illegal markets. Mexico shares a nearly two-thousand-mile border with the United States, the world's single largest consumer of illegal narcotics. That geographic fact alone created an economic opportunity of staggering proportions. American demand for marijuana, heroin, cocaine, and methamphetamine generated hundreds of billions of dollars in revenue each year, and the organizations that controlled the smuggling routes connecting suppliers in South America and domestic Mexican production to retail markets in the United States accumulated wealth that dwarfed the budgets of Mexican states and rivaled the revenues of multinational corporations.

But geography and economics alone do not explain why Mexican cartels became what they became. The transformation of Mexican drug trafficking from a loosely organized, regionally fragmented network of smugglers into the heavily armed, territorially ambitious, and brutally violent organizations of the twenty-first century required a specific political history: the collapse of the single-party authoritarian system that had governed Mexico since 1929, a system whose relationship to organized crime was not one of opposition but of managed coexistence. Under the Institutional Revolutionary Party, known by its Spanish acronym as the PRI, drug trafficking was tolerated, regulated, and taxed through a network of political intermediaries and corrupt police officials. Traffickers operated within understood boundaries. Violence was kept within limits that the political system could absorb. When that system began to unravel in the 1980s and collapsed definitively with the opposition victory in the presidential election of 2000, the informal agreements that had contained drug trafficking violence dissolved, and the cartels began competing, not just for drug routes, but for political territory.

The history of Mexican cartels also cannot be separated from the history of United States drug policy. The American decision to pursue prohibition rather than regulation of narcotics, codified in a series of laws from the Harrison Narcotics Tax Act of 1914 through the Controlled Substances Act of 1970, created and sustained the illegal market that Mexican traffickers exploited. American interdiction policy in the Caribbean in the 1980s, which successfully disrupted the Colombian cartels' direct air supply routes to Florida, pushed cocaine smuggling overland through Mexico, dramatically increasing the wealth and strategic importance of Mexican trafficking organizations. American demand and American policy choices were not peripheral to the rise of Mexican cartels. They were central causes.

This chapter traces the history of Mexico's major drug trafficking organizations from the emergence of the Guadalajara Cartel in the early 1980s through the splintering and transformation of the cartel landscape in the first decades of the twenty-first century. It examines the Guadalajara Cartel and its disintegration after the murder of a United States Drug Enforcement Administration agent in 1985. It traces the rise of the Gulf Cartel and its fearsome paramilitary wing, the Zetas. It examines the Sinaloa Cartel, the organization built around Joaquín "El Chapo" Guzmán that became the world's most powerful drug trafficking organization. It analyzes the Jalisco New Generation Cartel, which emerged from the ruins of earlier conflicts to challenge even the Mexican armed forces directly. And it examines the broader phenomenon of cartel wars, the cycles of fragmentation, competition, and violence that reshaped Mexico's political geography and left tens of thousands dead.

Throughout, the chapter pays attention not only to the organizations and their leaders but to the communities caught in between, the ordinary Mexicans who lived under cartel rule, negotiated its demands, suffered its violence, and in some cases resisted it. The history of Mexican cartels is not only a story of criminal enterprise. It is a story about the limits of state power, the social costs of prohibition, the corruption of public institutions, and the resilience of communities facing extraordinary violence. Understanding it requires taking seriously both the structural forces that made cartels possible and the human consequences that made them devastating.

By the time students complete this chapter, they should understand the Guadalajara Cartel's foundational role in shaping Mexican drug trafficking, the rise and expansion of the Gulf Cartel and the Zetas, the organization and global reach of the Sinaloa Cartel, the emergence and tactics of the Jalisco New Generation Cartel, and the dynamics of the cartel wars that have reshaped Mexican society in the twenty-first century. They should also be able to situate Mexican cartels within the broader global history of organized crime, narcotics trafficking, and the relationship between criminal organizations and weak or corrupt states.

Historical Context

To understand why Mexico became the epicenter of North American drug trafficking in the late twentieth century, students must understand several converging historical developments that had their roots decades before the first major cartels emerged.

Mexico's marijuana and opium poppy cultivation has a long history, dating back to the nineteenth century and expanding significantly during and after World War II when the United States government, which then tolerated the trade, contracted with Mexican farmers to produce opium for morphine used in battlefield medicine. The Sierra Madre Occidental mountain range, stretching through the states of Sinaloa, Durango, and Chihuahua in a region historically known as the Golden Triangle, provided ideal growing conditions: remote terrain, poor communications, and limited state presence. Families in these communities had grown drug crops for generations before any recognizable cartel structure emerged, and the social and economic roots of Mexican narcotics production ran deep into rural poverty and the structural neglect of highland agricultural communities by the central government.

The political framework within which drug trafficking operated in Mexico for most of the twentieth century was shaped by the extraordinary dominance of the Institutional Revolutionary Party, or PRI. Founded in 1929 in the aftermath of the Mexican Revolution, the PRI governed Mexico uninterruptedly for seventy-one years, combining mass political mobilization with authoritarian control of political competition, the media, labor unions, and the military. The party governed through a system of corporatist negotiation and managed patronage that extended into every corner of Mexican political life, including the criminal economy.

The relationship between the PRI state and drug traffickers was not simply one of straightforward corruption, though corruption was pervasive. It was more accurately described as a system of managed toleration. Drug trafficking organizations paid bribes to local, state, and federal officials, to the Federal Judicial Police (the Federales), and in some regions to the military. In exchange, they received protection from prosecution, advance warning of law enforcement operations, and the implicit guarantee that their activities would be tolerated within established territorial limits. Officials who managed these relationships were sometimes described, in the language of the system, as handling their "plaza," the administrative territory within which drug trafficking was implicitly licensed. This arrangement kept violence at levels the political system could absorb, prevented any single trafficking organization from becoming powerful enough to directly challenge state authority, and generated significant revenue for officials at every level of government.

The external catalyst that transformed this managed system into something far more explosive was the dramatic escalation of American cocaine consumption in the late 1970s and 1980s. Cocaine, largely a luxury drug for wealthy consumers in the 1970s, became mass-market in the 1980s, first in powder form and then, after 1985, as crack cocaine, which was dramatically cheaper and more immediately addictive. American demand for cocaine created a market worth tens of billions of dollars annually, and the Colombian cartels, primarily the Medellín and Cali organizations, had built the supply chains and trafficking networks to meet it, primarily using Caribbean air routes to deliver cocaine directly to Florida and the Gulf Coast.

The United States government's successful interdiction campaign against Caribbean air routes in the early 1980s, combined with intensified Coast Guard and Customs interdiction in the Florida Straits, forced Colombian traffickers to seek alternative smuggling routes. The overland route through Mexico, which had already been used for marijuana and heroin, offered a compelling alternative. Mexican traffickers, who already had relationships with American distributors and experience managing corruption along the border, were natural partners. What began as a subcontracting arrangement, in which Mexican organizations were paid in cash for transporting cocaine for Colombian principals, soon evolved into something far more consequential. As the Mexican organizations gained experience, infrastructure, and leverage, they began negotiating payment in cocaine rather than cash, acquiring their own share of the product to distribute directly in the United States. By the early 1990s, the major Mexican trafficking organizations were no longer subcontractors. They were principals.

The geography of the United States-Mexico border also shaped cartel development in specific ways. The border crossing points, known as ports of entry, are the primary chokepoints through which the vast majority of goods, vehicles, and people move between the two countries. Control of the smuggling corridors adjacent to those crossing points became the central objective of Mexican drug trafficking competition. The major corridors, associated with cities like Tijuana, Ciudad Juárez, Nuevo Laredo, and Matamoros, became the prizes over which cartels fought, and the geography of cartel competition was therefore also the geography of Mexican border cities. Understanding which organization controlled which corridor at any given time became essential to understanding the distribution of cartel violence across Mexico's northern border region.

Finally, the political context of Mexican democratization is essential to understanding why cartel violence escalated so dramatically from the late 1990s onward. The 2000 presidential election, in which the National Action Party's Vicente Fox defeated the PRI candidate for the first time in seven decades, ended the political system within which drug trafficking had been managed. But ending the PRI system did not automatically produce a strong, effective, clean state capable of enforcing the law. What it produced, in the short term, was a fragmentation of the informal political controls that had kept trafficking competition within bounds. The new democratic governments lacked the PRI's informal networks and were unwilling, or unable, to reconstitute the old corrupt management system. The traffickers, freed from old political constraints and competing for a cocaine market worth enormous sums, responded with violence.

Major People, Groups, and Places

Reference — People, Groups & Places

Miguel Ángel Félix Gallardo
Known as "El Padrino" (The Godfather), the founder and boss of the Guadalajara Cartel. His arrest in 1989 and subsequent division of his organization into regional territories created the map of Mexican cartel geography that persisted for decades.
Enrique Camarena
United States Drug Enforcement Administration agent stationed in Guadalajara. His kidnapping, torture, and murder in 1985 on orders from Guadalajara Cartel leadership triggered a major diplomatic crisis and accelerated the transformation of Mexican-American drug enforcement relations.
Juan García Ábrego
Leader of the Gulf Cartel in the early 1990s who built it into a major cocaine trafficking operation before his arrest and extradition to the United States in 1996, the first Mexican drug lord extradited under U.S. drug kingpin designations.
Osiel Cárdenas Guillén
Gulf Cartel leader who in the late 1990s recruited a group of former Mexican Army special forces operatives to serve as personal bodyguards and cartel enforcers. This group became the Zetas, transforming cartel violence in Mexico.
The Zetas
Originally the paramilitary enforcement wing of the Gulf Cartel, composed of former Mexican Army special forces. Formally separated from the Gulf Cartel in 2010 and became an independent criminal organization known for extraordinary brutality, mass killings, and expansion into criminal markets beyond drug trafficking.
Joaquín Guzmán Loera (El Chapo)
The dominant figure of the Sinaloa Cartel, who built the world's most powerful drug trafficking organization over three decades. Arrested in 1993, escaped from prison in 2001, recaptured in 2014, escaped again in 2015 through a tunnel dug into his cell, recaptured in 2016, and extradited to the United States in 2017. Convicted by a federal jury in New York in 2019 and sentenced to life imprisonment.
Ismael Zambada García (El Mayo)
Long-running co-leader of the Sinaloa Cartel, who unlike Guzmán evaded capture for decades by maintaining a lower profile and deeper political connections. His arrest by American authorities in 2024 marked a major development in the cartel's history.
Nemesio Oseguera Cervantes (El Mencho)
Founder and leader of the Jalisco New Generation Cartel (CJNG). A former police officer and early member of the Milenio Cartel, he built the CJNG into one of the two dominant forces in Mexican organized crime, known for spectacular acts of violence against law enforcement and military forces.
Sinaloa Cartel
The criminal organization built primarily by Guzmán and Zambada, based in the state of Sinaloa and operating across Mexico and in drug markets on six continents. Known for its hierarchical but flexible structure, its deep political connections, its sophisticated logistics, and its relative preference for corruption over direct confrontation with the state.
Jalisco New Generation Cartel (CJNG)
The criminal organization founded by Oseguera Cervantes, which grew rapidly after 2011 to challenge the Sinaloa Cartel's dominance. Distinguished by extreme violence against state forces, rapid expansion, and a willingness to conduct military-style attacks on police and army units.
The Golden Triangle
The mountainous region where the states of Sinaloa, Durango, and Chihuahua intersect, historically the primary growing region for Mexican marijuana and opium poppies and the heartland of Sinaloa Cartel power.
Ciudad Juárez
Border city in the state of Chihuahua, directly across from El Paso, Texas. A major smuggling corridor and the site of catastrophic violence during the Juárez-Sinaloa cartel war of 2008 to 2012, during which the city recorded some of the highest per-capita homicide rates in the world.
Nuevo Laredo
Border city in the state of Tamaulipas that serves as one of the most important commercial crossing points on the United States-Mexico border, and the site of a prolonged and brutal conflict between the Gulf Cartel/Zetas and the Sinaloa Cartel.
PRI (Partido Revolucionario Institucional)
The Institutional Revolutionary Party, which governed Mexico from 1929 to 2000. Its system of managed relationships with drug trafficking organizations provided the political framework within which cartels operated before democratization dissolved those arrangements.

Section 23.1

The Guadalajara Cartel: Foundation and Collapse

The modern history of Mexican drug trafficking organizations begins not in Sinaloa or Tamaulipas but in Guadalajara, Mexico's second largest city and the capital of the western state of Jalisco, where in the early 1980s a young trafficker from the Sinaloa highlands named Miguel Ángel Félix Gallardo accomplished something that had not previously existed in Mexican drug trafficking: he created a unified national organization capable of coordinating operations across the country's multiple regional smuggling networks.

Félix Gallardo was in many ways a transitional figure, one foot in the old world of Mexican drug trafficking and one foot in the new. Born in 1946 in the state of Sinaloa, he grew up in the poverty of the Sierra Madre foothills and entered the drug trade as a young man through the patronage networks that linked traffickers to political officials in the PRI system. He cultivated relationships with federal police commanders, military officers, and PRI politicians at a level that earlier traffickers had not achieved, and he used those relationships to build an organization that operated with a degree of political protection unavailable to his predecessors. He was well-dressed, soft-spoken, and reputedly socially ambitious, a figure who moved between the criminal and legitimate worlds with unusual ease for his time.

In the early 1980s, Félix Gallardo formed what became known as the Guadalajara Cartel in collaboration with two other prominent traffickers from the Sinaloa region: Rafael Caro Quintero and Ernesto Fonseca Carrillo, known as "Don Neto." The organization they built was less a formal corporate structure than a coalition of regional operators working under a shared umbrella, sharing infrastructure, political connections, and revenue while maintaining a degree of regional autonomy. What unified them was control of the marijuana trade, which remained the most profitable drug market for Mexican traffickers through the early 1980s, and an emerging relationship with Colombian cocaine producers looking for overland smuggling routes to the United States.

The Guadalajara Cartel operated massive marijuana plantations in the states of Chihuahua and Sonora, including the famous Búfalo plantation in Chihuahua, which at its height covered thousands of acres and employed thousands of workers who harvested, dried, compressed, and packaged marijuana for export to the United States. The scale was industrial. The organization had police escorts for its shipments, advance warning of enforcement operations, and political cover at multiple levels of the Mexican government. For several years in the early 1980s, it operated with a degree of impunity that reflected the depth of its political connections.

The event that destroyed the Guadalajara Cartel was the kidnapping, torture, and murder of Enrique "Kiki" Camarena, a United States Drug Enforcement Administration agent who had been investigating the organization from the DEA's Guadalajara office. Camarena was grabbed off the street on February 7, 1985, by a team of armed men working for the cartel. Over the following thirty hours, he was subjected to a sustained and horrific interrogation under torture before being killed. His body was found on a ranch in the state of Michoacán several weeks later.

The murder of a federal law enforcement officer of the United States was an act of extraordinary recklessness, and its consequences were severe. The Reagan administration, which had been conducting a broadly conceived anti-communist foreign policy in Central America that sometimes conflicted with aggressive drug enforcement in the region, responded to Camarena's murder with unprecedented pressure on the Mexican government. American customs officials implemented a meticulous inspection of every vehicle crossing from Mexico into the United States, creating massive delays and colossal economic disruption. The operation, known as Operation Intercept II, sent an unmistakable message that the United States was willing to impose serious economic costs on Mexico in retaliation for the protection extended to drug traffickers.

The Mexican government, under intense American pressure, arrested Félix Gallardo, Caro Quintero, and Fonseca Carrillo. The political relationships that had protected them did not hold under the weight of an international crisis. Caro Quintero was arrested in Costa Rica in April 1985 and sentenced to forty years in Mexican prison, though he would be controversially released on a legal technicality in 2013 and rearrested in 2022. Fonseca Carrillo was arrested in Guadalajara in April 1985. Félix Gallardo continued operating for several more years from the shadows before his arrest in April 1989.

Before his arrest, Félix Gallardo took a fateful organizational decision. Rather than allow his organization to collapse into chaotic competition after his removal, he convened a meeting of his regional lieutenants at a resort in Acapulco in 1989 and divided the national drug trafficking territory into distinct regional franchises. Each major trafficking corridor was assigned to a specific family or regional group. The Tijuana corridor went to the Arellano Félix family, his nephews. The Juárez corridor went to Amado Carrillo Fuentes. The Gulf corridor, in the northeastern state of Tamaulipas, went to Juan García Ábrego. The Sonora corridor went to the Caro Quintero family. The Sinaloa corridor went to a group of younger traffickers, most prominently Guzmán and Zambada.

This division created the cartel geography of modern Mexico. The organizations that emerged from the Acapulco division would spend the following three decades fighting over the corridors that Félix Gallardo had assigned, competing to absorb neighboring territories, fracturing into splinter groups after the removal of leadership, and in some cases disappearing entirely. The Guadalajara Cartel's greatest legacy was not its own operations but the organizational map it created when it collapsed.

Historical Vocabulary
Plaza

In the language of Mexican drug trafficking, a plaza refers to a specific geographic territory, typically defined by the smuggling corridor or border crossing point that it controls. The term derives from the PRI-era administrative system in which government officials managed the informal licensing of drug trafficking within their jurisdictions. A plaza might correspond to a border city, a crossing point, a regional highway network, or a state. The official or trafficker who controlled a plaza was said to be the plaza boss, responsible for managing relationships with local authorities, collecting payments from subordinate traffickers operating in the territory, and ensuring that shipments moved without interference.

The concept of the plaza reflects the extent to which Mexican drug trafficking was, in its foundational period, organized around geography and political access rather than around a particular drug or supply chain. Control of a plaza meant control of the physical and political infrastructure through which drugs moved, regardless of what specific product was being transported. This geographic logic helps explain why cartel competition has so often manifested as territorial warfare over border cities and smuggling corridors rather than simply as competition over customers or suppliers.

The Arellano Félix Organization and the Tijuana Cartel

Of the regional organizations created by the Acapulco division, the Tijuana Cartel, controlled by the Arellano Félix family, was initially one of the most powerful and certainly one of the most violent. The Arellano Félix brothers, of whom there were seven active in the organization, combined a sophisticated trafficking operation with a level of public violence that shocked even experienced observers of Mexican organized crime. The family controlled the Tijuana crossing point, one of the busiest border crossings in the world, which gave them extraordinary leverage over the flow of drugs and people across the California border. At their height in the 1990s, the Tijuana Cartel was generating revenues estimated in the billions of dollars annually.

The organization's decline came through a combination of leadership losses and sustained conflict with the Sinaloa Cartel. Benjamín Arellano Félix, considered the organizational brain of the family, was arrested in 2002. Ramón Arellano Félix, the organization's primary enforcer and the brother most directly responsible for its culture of extreme violence, was killed in a shootout in Mazatlán in 2002. Francisco Javier Arellano Félix was captured by the United States Coast Guard in international waters in 2006 after a yacht he was aboard was interdicted. Francisco Rafael Arellano Félix, the eldest brother, who had served years in Mexican prison and been extradited to the United States, was killed in 2013. By the mid-2010s, the Tijuana Cartel had fragmented into competing factions, some aligned with Sinaloa and some independent, a shell of the organization that had dominated the California border corridor in the 1990s.

The Juárez Cartel and Amado Carrillo Fuentes

The Juárez Cartel, based in the border city of Ciudad Juárez directly across from El Paso, Texas, became under Amado Carrillo Fuentes the most powerful cocaine trafficking organization in Mexico in the 1990s. Carrillo Fuentes, known as "El Señor de los Cielos," the Lord of the Skies, for his extensive use of aircraft to transport cocaine, built an organization that moved cocaine on a scale that dwarfed previous Mexican trafficking operations. He reportedly maintained a fleet of Boeing 727 jets that transported cocaine shipments directly from Colombia and that he had acquired through corrupt relationships with aviation officials. His organization was estimated to be moving four to eight metric tons of cocaine per month through the Juárez corridor at its peak.

Carrillo Fuentes also invested heavily in diplomatic relationships with Colombian suppliers and in the cultivation of political protection in Mexico at the highest levels. He reportedly paid hundreds of millions of dollars in bribes to Mexican officials, including to figures in the national security apparatus. His death in July 1997, following plastic surgery he underwent to alter his appearance and which reportedly went fatally wrong, created a power vacuum in the Juárez organization that opened a period of intense internal competition. His brother Vicente Carrillo Fuentes assumed leadership but lacked Amado's political skills and organizational talent, and the organization progressively weakened, eventually coming into catastrophic conflict with the Sinaloa Cartel in a war that turned Ciudad Juárez into one of the deadliest cities in the world in the late 2000s.

Section 23.2

The Gulf Cartel and the Creation of the Zetas

Of all the organizations that emerged from the ruins of the Guadalajara Cartel, the Gulf Cartel underwent the most dramatic organizational transformation, one that would change the character of cartel violence in Mexico more profoundly than any other development in the history of the conflict. That transformation was the creation of the Zetas.

The Gulf Cartel had its roots in the northeastern state of Tamaulipas, which borders Texas along the Rio Grande and contains several of the most important commercial crossing points on the United States-Mexico border. The organization had a long history predating the Guadalajara Cartel era, with roots in local trafficking networks that stretched back to the mid-twentieth century. In the aftermath of the Acapulco division, Juan García Ábrego consolidated control of the Gulf organization and transformed it into a sophisticated cocaine trafficking operation, building relationships with Colombian suppliers and developing the infrastructure to move large quantities of cocaine through the Tamaulipas corridors into Texas.

García Ábrego's arrest in 1996, after being placed on the FBI's Ten Most Wanted list and becoming the first foreign national to be added to that list, and his subsequent extradition to the United States and conviction on drug trafficking charges, created a succession struggle within the Gulf Cartel. After several years of internal conflict, Osiel Cárdenas Guillén emerged as the dominant figure. Cárdenas Guillén was, by multiple accounts, a violent and paranoid leader who trusted almost no one and was willing to murder rivals and potential defectors with unusual readiness. His manner of consolidating control, through the murder of his closest former allies, established the tone of his leadership.

In the late 1990s, recognizing that the existing enforcement apparatus available to the Gulf Cartel, corrupt police and local informants, was insufficient to protect his organization from rival traffickers and law enforcement pressure, Cárdenas Guillén made a decision that would reverberate through Mexican history. He recruited a group of approximately thirty former members of the Grupo Aeromóvil de Fuerzas Especiales, known as GAFES, the Mexican Army's elite airborne special forces unit. These were soldiers who had received extensive training in counterinsurgency tactics, surveillance, urban combat, and weapons handling, some of it provided by American and Israeli military trainers. They were skilled, disciplined, and experienced in the use of violence at a level that far exceeded anything previously available to a Mexican criminal organization.

Cárdenas Guillén gave these former soldiers a name, Los Zetas, a term derived from the phonetic alphabet used by the Federal Judicial Police in radio communications. Initially deployed as personal bodyguards and as an elite enforcement unit for the Gulf Cartel, the Zetas quickly became far more than that. Their military training gave the Gulf Cartel a qualitative advantage over rival organizations that lacked comparable capability. The Zetas could conduct coordinated attacks, set ambushes, use military tactics and weaponry, and operate in ways that police and rival cartel enforcers found extremely difficult to counter. Under their protection, the Gulf Cartel expanded its territorial reach, driving competitors out of corridors they had previously controlled and imposing the Gulf Cartel's authority on territories in which it had previously been merely one actor among many.

The Zetas also began recruiting aggressively, training new members in the tactical skills they had brought from the military and expanding beyond the initial cadre of former special forces. They brought in former members of the Mexican Army's regular infantry, former Guatemalan special forces (the Kaibiles, themselves notorious for extreme violence), former municipal and state police officers, and eventually younger recruits with no prior military or police background who were trained directly by the Zetas' own instructors. By the mid-2000s, the Zetas had transformed from a special operations unit into something closer to a criminal army, with thousands of members, a command structure, training camps, intelligence capabilities, and an arsenal that included military-grade weapons including .50-caliber sniper rifles, rocket-propelled grenades, and armored vehicles.

The Zetas introduced a form of violence to Mexican organized crime that was qualitatively different from what had preceded it. Earlier cartel violence had been primarily instrumental: the killing of rivals, the punishment of traitors, the intimidation of witnesses and officials. It was designed to serve specific organizational objectives and was, within those objectives, economically rational. The Zetas introduced mass atrocity as a tool of territorial control and psychological domination. They conducted massacres of civilians in communities they sought to control, publicly displayed bodies and severed heads to communicate the consequences of resistance or collaboration with rivals, and engaged in forms of torture and killing that were intended not merely to eliminate individual enemies but to terrorize entire populations into submission.

The Zetas also dramatically expanded the criminal portfolio of the Gulf Cartel beyond drug trafficking. They began systematically extorting businesses, kidnapping for ransom, controlling the smuggling of migrants, and hijacking fuel from the PEMEX national oil company's pipelines, a crime known as huachicol. This diversification reflected both the Zetas' institutional ambitions, they sought to become a general-purpose criminal organization rather than simply a drug trafficking arm, and the pragmatic calculation that in communities where they exercised territorial control, every form of economic activity could potentially be taxed.

The Zetas-Gulf Split and Its Consequences

Cárdenas Guillén was arrested by Mexican military forces in Matamoros in March 2003 after a firefight in which the Zetas provided his personal security. His arrest began a prolonged period of internal instability in the Gulf-Zeta alliance. Leadership of the Gulf Cartel passed through a series of figures before stabilizing under the Cárdenas Guillén family network, while the Zetas, under the leadership of Heriberto Lazcano Lazcano (known as "El Lazca") and later Miguel Ángel Treviño Morales (known as "Z-40"), increasingly operated with a degree of autonomy that strained their relationship with their Gulf Cartel patrons.

The formal rupture came in 2010, when the Zetas declared themselves an independent criminal organization and went to war with the Gulf Cartel over control of the Tamaulipas corridors. The war between the two organizations, former allies who knew each other's methods and personnel intimately, produced some of the most sustained and brutal violence in modern Mexican history. The battle for Nuevo Laredo, the critical border city through which the majority of commercial truck traffic between Mexico and the United States passed, became a prolonged conflict that effectively paralyzed economic activity, drove out much of the legitimate business community, and killed hundreds of people over several years.

The Zetas' independence also intensified their diversification into non-drug criminal markets. As an organization no longer primarily defined by drug trafficking, the Zetas extended their extortion operations throughout northeastern and central Mexico, demanding payments from businesses ranging from street vendors to bus companies to ranchers to mining operations. Communities that refused to pay faced violence. Journalists who reported on Zeta activities were murdered. Migrants crossing Mexico en route to the United States, who had previously been charged for passage through Zeta territory, became kidnapping targets, with ransom demands paid to family members in the United States. In August 2010, the bodies of seventy-two Central and South American migrants were discovered on a ranch in the state of Tamaulipas, executed by Zetas after apparently refusing or being unable to pay ransom demands. The massacre became a symbol of the humanitarian consequences of the Zetas' territorial expansion.

Section 23.3

The Sinaloa Cartel: The World's Most Powerful Drug Trafficking Organization

Of the organizations that emerged from the wreckage of the Guadalajara Cartel and the subsequent decades of cartel competition, the Sinaloa Cartel became the largest, most geographically extensive, most financially powerful, and in many respects most organizationally sophisticated drug trafficking organization in the world. Its rise, conducted over nearly three decades of incremental territorial expansion, strategic alliances, political corruption, and carefully calibrated violence, represents the most successful example of cartel empire-building in the history of the drug trade.

The Sinaloa Cartel's roots lay in the trafficking networks of the Sinaloa highlands, where families had grown marijuana and opium poppies for generations and where the organizational culture that shaped the cartel was formed. The men who built the Sinaloa Cartel, most prominently Joaquín Guzmán Loera, Ismael Zambada García, Juan José Esparragoza Moreno (known as "El Azul"), and the Beltrán Leyva brothers, were products of this mountain culture: pragmatic, intensely loyal to family and to their region of origin, experienced in the drug trade from childhood, and deeply embedded in the social networks of the Sinaloa highlands that provided their organizational foundation.

Guzmán, who became the cartel's most recognizable figure and its primary organizational driver from the late 1980s onward, was born in 1957 in the tiny mountain community of La Tuna in the Badiraguato municipality of Sinaloa. He entered the drug trade as a teenager, working for Félix Gallardo before building his own position in the organization. After the Acapulco division, he and Zambada consolidated control of the Sinaloa corridor, and Guzmán in particular pursued an aggressive territorial expansion strategy that involved both making alliances with smaller trafficking groups and eliminating competitors who refused to subordinate themselves to Sinaloa authority.

Guzmán's capture by Mexican authorities in 1993, following the accidental shooting of a Catholic cardinal during a cartel confrontation at the Guadalajara airport, removed him from direct leadership for nearly a decade. He was held at the Puente Grande maximum security prison in Jalisco, from which he escaped in January 2001 in circumstances that remained disputed but that clearly involved the complicity of prison officials and raised questions about how high in the Mexican government the corruption that enabled his escape extended. His escape and return to leadership of the Sinaloa Cartel coincided with the period of greatest cartel violence in Mexico, as the organization he had built during his imprisonment, partly through the management of his lieutenants, faced intensifying competition.

The Sinaloa Cartel's organizational model differed significantly from the hierarchical command structures that characterized some other trafficking organizations. Rather than a rigid corporate hierarchy with a single chain of command, the Sinaloa organization functioned more like a network of affiliated groups that shared branding, some infrastructure, and political connections while maintaining a significant degree of operational autonomy. The major Sinaloa figures, Guzmán, Zambada, Esparragoza, and others, cooperated on major operations, shared intelligence, and coordinated political relationships, but they did not micromanage each other's operations or require all decisions to flow through a single command node. This network structure made the Sinaloa Cartel more resilient than hierarchical organizations: the removal of any single figure, including Guzmán himself, did not collapse the network.

The cartel's geographic reach was extraordinary. By the 2000s and into the 2010s, the Sinaloa Cartel operated distribution networks across the United States, from the Southwest through the Midwest and into New England, using a combination of direct employees, affiliated street gangs that served as retail distributors, and independent wholesalers who purchased product at the border and distributed it within American cities. It maintained supply relationships with cocaine producers in Colombia, Bolivia, and Peru, heroin suppliers in the Golden Triangle and in Afghanistan, and methamphetamine production networks that operated both within Mexico, where industrial-scale laboratories produced the drug using pseudoephedrine imported primarily from China, and within the United States itself.

The cartel's international reach extended beyond North America. It operated in Europe through relationships with criminal organizations in Spain, Italy, and the Netherlands. It moved cocaine through West African transit points, using relationships with African criminal networks to ship product to European markets. It had a presence in Australia, where Mexican methamphetamine competed with locally produced product in an extremely lucrative market. It cultivated relationships with Asian criminal organizations for chemical supply and money laundering. The Sinaloa Cartel was, in a meaningful sense, a global enterprise.

The organization's financial practices reflected its sophistication. Money laundering, the conversion of drug revenues into apparently legitimate assets, was conducted through a diverse portfolio of mechanisms: cash-intensive businesses in Mexico and abroad, real estate purchases in resort cities and border towns, currency exchange houses, shell companies in multiple jurisdictions, bulk cash smuggling back across the border, and increasingly, through cryptocurrency transactions. The cartel invested in legitimate businesses ranging from restaurants and construction companies to agricultural operations and transport firms, creating economic interests in the licit economy that both generated revenue and provided cover for criminal proceeds.

The Sinaloa Cartel's preferred strategy for managing its relationship with the Mexican state was what analysts described as plata o plomo, silver or lead: the offer of a bribe, with the implied or explicit threat of violence if the bribe was refused. The cartel paid extensively for political protection at the local, state, and federal levels, and reportedly maintained relationships within the Mexican federal security apparatus that provided intelligence about law enforcement operations. Its corruption investments were understood as a cost of business, systematic and extensive rather than occasional and opportunistic. The cartel's preference was for corruption over confrontation with the state: direct attacks on military or police units were avoided when possible, because they invited the kind of intensified law enforcement response that disrupted operations.

The Beltrán Leyva Rupture

The Sinaloa Cartel's apparent unity concealed significant internal tensions that eventually erupted into open conflict. In 2008, the Beltrán Leyva brothers, who had been senior figures in the organization since its foundation, broke with Guzmán and Zambada and declared war on the Sinaloa Cartel. The stated grievance involved Guzmán's alleged cooperation with Mexican authorities in the arrest of Alfredo Beltrán Leyva, one of the brothers, in January 2008. The Beltrán Leyvas accused Guzmán of having provided information to the government to have Alfredo arrested, a charge that Guzmán denied. Whether or not the accusation was accurate, it was sufficient to shatter the alliance and launch a brutal internal war.

The Beltrán Leyva Organization, or BLO, aligned with the Zetas and the Juárez Cartel against the Sinaloa Cartel, creating a complex multi-sided conflict that drove homicide rates to extraordinary levels across western Mexico. Arturo Beltrán Leyva, the most powerful of the brothers and the leader of the organization, was killed in a Mexican Navy operation in Cuernavaca in December 2009 in a spectacularly violent confrontation that left multiple naval commandos dead. His death destabilized the BLO, which subsequently fragmented into multiple competing factions, some of which eventually aligned with CJNG or Sinaloa while others continued as independent criminal organizations in the states of Morelos, Guerrero, and Nayarit.

El Chapo's Captures and the Cartel's Resilience

Guzmán's recapture by Mexican authorities in February 2014, following an extensive manhunt that involved American intelligence support and a military raid on a condominium complex in Mazatlán, was treated by the Mexican government as a major achievement. Mexican President Enrique Peña Nieto celebrated the arrest as evidence of his administration's effectiveness against organized crime. The celebration proved premature. In July 2015, Guzmán escaped from the Altiplano maximum security prison, not by corrupting guards to let him walk out, but through a tunnel more than a mile long and equipped with lighting, ventilation, and a modified motorcycle on rails, which had been constructed directly below his cell over months without the apparent knowledge of prison authorities. The engineering and organizational achievement required to construct such a tunnel, and to do so undetected within one of Mexico's most secure prisons, was a humiliating demonstration of the Sinaloa Cartel's institutional capabilities.

Guzmán was recaptured again in January 2016 in Los Mochis, Sinaloa, following a joint Mexican and American operation that drew on intelligence gathered through electronic surveillance. He was extradited to the United States in January 2017, a move that Mexican authorities had previously resisted but that Peña Nieto's government ultimately agreed to under intense American pressure. In February 2019, a federal jury in Brooklyn found him guilty on all counts of a sweeping indictment that included drug trafficking, conspiracy, murder conspiracy, and money laundering charges. He was sentenced to life imprisonment plus thirty years.

The Sinaloa Cartel's survival and continued operation following Guzmán's extradition demonstrated the network model's resilience. Zambada, who had avoided capture through a combination of low public profile, deep political connections, and decades of experience in evading law enforcement, continued to lead the organization. The cartel's operations did not collapse. Its territorial control was not abandoned. Its supply chains continued to function. When Zambada was arrested by American authorities in July 2024 after being lured onto a plane by a rival, the cartel faced its most significant leadership challenge in years, but its institutional architecture remained intact enough to continue operating.

The Camarena Murder and the Diplomatic Crisis of 1985

Source type: Diplomatic communications, congressional testimony, and DEA operational records · Period: 1985–1990 · Context: The murder of DEA Special Agent Enrique Camarena Salazar and its diplomatic and policy consequences

Context: Enrique "Kiki" Camarena was a Mexican-born American citizen who had joined the DEA after service with the United States Marine Corps. Assigned to the Guadalajara office in the early 1980s, he became a highly effective undercover agent who built sources within the Guadalajara Cartel and helped identify the location of the massive Búfalo marijuana plantation in Chihuahua. His effectiveness made him a target. On February 7, 1985, he was seized outside the American consulate in Guadalajara by a group of armed men working for the cartel. He was taken to a house in the city where he was interrogated under prolonged torture. His body and that of his Mexican pilot, Alfredo Zavala Avelar, were found weeks later on a ranch in Michoacán.

Congressional and DEA testimony from the subsequent investigation revealed that Camarena had been kept alive for interrogation for approximately thirty hours, during which he was questioned under torture about the identities of his informants, the extent of DEA intelligence about cartel operations, and the names of American and Mexican officials cooperating with the investigation. The interrogation was reportedly supervised in part by a Mexican doctor who administered stimulants to keep Camarena conscious. The cartel reportedly made audio recordings of the interrogation.

Analysis: The Camarena case is a foundational document in the history of Mexican-American drug enforcement relations, and in the history of the Guadalajara Cartel's collapse. It reveals several critical dynamics. First, the degree of protection the cartel had purchased from Mexican law enforcement was sufficient that its members believed they could torture and kill an American federal agent without consequence, a miscalculation that exposed the extent to which the PRI system's management of drug trafficking had come to include protection against international scrutiny. Second, the aggressive American response, including the border slowdown and diplomatic pressure, demonstrated that the United States was willing to use economic leverage against Mexico to enforce accountability for violence against American law enforcement personnel. Third, the subsequent investigation, including American allegations that Mexican officials with knowledge of Camarena's whereabouts had allowed him to be killed, revealed the depth of institutional corruption within the Mexican security apparatus. The case also led to a landmark legal dispute over the "Alvarez-Machain" kidnapping, in which American bounty hunters, acting on DEA direction, abducted a Mexican physician suspected of involvement in the murder from his office in Guadalajara in 1990, provoking a major diplomatic incident over American jurisdiction and Mexican sovereignty.

Questions for students:

  1. What does the cartel's willingness to kidnap and murder an American federal agent reveal about the degree of political protection it believed it enjoyed at the time?
  2. Why did the United States government respond so aggressively to Camarena's murder compared to the murders of Mexican journalists or ordinary citizens by drug traffickers?
  3. What does the American government's response, including the border slowdown and pressure for arrests, reveal about the leverage the United States holds over Mexico in bilateral relations?
  4. The subsequent abduction of a Mexican suspect by American-directed bounty hunters raised major questions about sovereignty and international law. How does this case illustrate the tensions between effective law enforcement against transnational crime and respect for national sovereignty?
Section 23.4

The Jalisco New Generation Cartel: A New Model of Criminal Power

If the Sinaloa Cartel represented the mature form of the trafficking-focused criminal organization, a sophisticated business enterprise that preferred corruption to confrontation and sought to avoid the kind of spectacular violence that invited intensified state response, the Jalisco New Generation Cartel represented something different: a more openly militaristic organization that did not merely tolerate violent confrontation with the state but seemed to embrace it as a demonstration of power, a recruitment tool, and a method of intimidating both rivals and the government simultaneously.

The CJNG emerged from the ruins of the Milenio Cartel, a Sinaloa-aligned organization that operated in the states of Jalisco, Colima, and Michoacán in the 2000s. When the Milenio Cartel fragmented following the deaths of its leaders, one faction aligned with the Zetas while another, led by Nemesio Oseguera Cervantes, maintained its Sinaloa affiliation temporarily before breaking away to form an independent organization. Oseguera Cervantes, known as "El Mencho," had an unusual biography for a major cartel leader. Born in the poor rural state of Michoacán, he had worked as a municipal police officer before entering drug trafficking, giving him knowledge of law enforcement methods and contacts within the police that he would use throughout his criminal career. He had also spent time in the United States, where he reportedly worked in the drug trade in Guadalupe, California, before being deported. His American experience gave him knowledge of the retail drug market and connections on the consumer side of the trade.

The CJNG formally announced its existence in 2011 by publicly executing members of the Zetas organization in the state of Jalisco and posting videos of the killings online with narcomessages identifying the perpetrators as a "new generation" cartel loyal to the "people of Jalisco." The organization expanded with remarkable speed, moving from its Jalisco base into neighboring states, then into territory throughout Mexico, and eventually into international markets. Within a decade of its formal founding, the United States Drug Enforcement Administration and the Mexican government both identified the CJNG as one of the two or three most powerful criminal organizations in the country, controlling drug distribution networks across Mexico and maintaining a presence in drug markets across Europe, Asia, and the Americas.

The CJNG distinguished itself in several respects from the established cartel model. Most dramatically, it was willing, and eventually appeared eager, to directly confront Mexican law enforcement and military forces in open armed engagements. In April 2015, the CJNG ambushed a police convoy in the state of Jalisco, killing fifteen officers in a sophisticated attack involving multiple vehicles and automatic weapons. In May 2015, in response to a government helicopter surveillance operation, the CJNG shot down a military helicopter using rocket-propelled grenades, killing eight soldiers. These attacks were astonishing not merely for their lethality but for what they signaled: an organization that was either confident enough in its military capability to take on the Mexican Army directly, or that calculated that such spectacular violence served its political objectives more effectively than the low-profile approach that the Sinaloa Cartel had perfected.

The CJNG also became a prominent practitioner of online propaganda. The organization posted videos of executions, torture of rivals, and armed demonstrations of its military hardware, including armored vehicles, military uniforms, and heavy weapons, on social media platforms. These videos served multiple functions simultaneously: they terrorized rivals and potential victims, they demonstrated the organization's military capability to potential recruits and to the public, and they humiliated the state by showing that a criminal organization could openly display military assets that challenged the government's claim to a monopoly on legitimate force. The videos were sophisticated productions that borrowed the aesthetics of military propaganda, narcocorrido music videos, and horror film simultaneously.

The organization's rapid territorial expansion raised questions about how it was achieving such rapid growth. Analysts pointed to several factors. The CJNG recruited aggressively, offering higher pay and more rapid advancement than established organizations, and it was willing to absorb smaller groups from other cartels, sometimes through persuasion and sometimes through violent coercion. It moved into drug markets that had been destabilized by the fragmentation of other organizations, particularly the territories of the collapsed Beltrán Leyva Organization and the weakened Zetas. And it benefited from the same network model that had served the Sinaloa Cartel: rather than attempting to directly control all operations in all territories, it allowed affiliated groups to maintain local control while paying fees and operating under the CJNG brand.

The CJNG also diversified into non-drug criminal markets, including extortion, kidnapping, fuel theft from PEMEX pipelines, and the control of avocado and lime agricultural production in Michoacán, where the organization used violence and threats to extract payments from farmers and producers in one of Mexico's most economically important agricultural sectors. This diversification reflected both the Zetas' demonstration that a criminal organization did not need to rely solely on drug trafficking revenues and the CJNG's territorial ambitions, which made control of local economies a natural extension of territorial control.

The Guanajuato Conflict and the Limits of State Response

The CJNG's most sustained territorial campaign in the late 2010s and early 2020s was its attempt to wrest control of the state of Guanajuato, in the industrial heartland of central Mexico, from the Santa Rosa de Lima Cartel, a smaller organization that had established dominance over fuel theft and other criminal markets in the state. The conflict between the CJNG and the Santa Rosa de Lima Cartel drove Guanajuato's homicide rate to extraordinary levels, briefly making it one of the most violent states in Mexico, a country already recording homicide rates far above the global average. The state had not historically been associated with cartel violence, and the transformation of its security environment demonstrated how the CJNG's territorial expansion could destabilize regions that had previously been relatively insulated from the cartel conflict.

The Mexican government's response to the CJNG demonstrated the limitations that even a government willing to use military force faced against a well-organized and deeply embedded criminal organization. The administration of President Andrés Manuel López Obrador, elected in 2018 partly on a platform of addressing the failures of the Calderón-era militarized approach, pursued a strategy variously described as "hugs not bullets" (abrazos, no balazos), emphasizing social investment and poverty reduction over direct confrontation. Critics argued that this approach effectively conceded territory to the CJNG and other organizations. Defenders argued that the militarized approach of prior administrations had demonstrably failed to reduce violence and had in some cases intensified it. The debate reflected a genuine analytical difficulty: there was no clear evidence that any strategy employed by the Mexican government over three decades had succeeded in significantly and durably reducing cartel violence.

Historical Vocabulary
Narcocultura

Narcocultura, or narco culture, refers to the complex of cultural forms, aesthetics, and values associated with drug trafficking organizations in Mexico and the broader Latin American context. It encompasses a range of cultural products and practices: the narcocorrido musical tradition that tells stories of drug lords, battles, and the trafficking life; the distinctive architectural and fashion styles associated with cartel wealth; the religious practices and patron saints venerated in trafficking communities; the online and social media culture that celebrates cartel figures; and the broader set of values and status markers associated with success in the illegal economy.

Narcocultura is a deeply contested phenomenon. Critics, including many Mexican academics, journalists, and community advocates, argue that it glorifies criminal violence, normalizes the illegal economy, and provides cultural validation for organizations responsible for mass suffering. Some analyses point to narcocorridos, in particular, as recruitment tools that romanticize the cartel lifestyle for young men with limited economic opportunities. Others, including many cultural analysts and anthropologists, emphasize that narcocultura is not simply propaganda for drug trafficking but a complex cultural response to poverty, exclusion, and the failure of the legitimate economy to provide pathways to dignity and material security for large segments of the Mexican population. In this reading, the narco figure represents, however distorted, a cultural fantasy of power, mobility, and respect that resonates in communities where those things are systematically denied through legitimate channels. Understanding narcocultura requires holding both dimensions simultaneously: it is a genuine cultural expression and it is also a form of criminal propaganda with real human costs.

Section 23.5

Cartel Wars: Fragmentation, Competition, and Violence

One of the most striking features of Mexican cartel history is not the emergence of dominant organizations but the persistent tendency of those organizations to fragment, compete, and collapse, producing cycles of violence that seem to regenerate rather than resolve. Understanding why cartel wars occur, why they produce fragmentation rather than stable monopoly, and what their human consequences are is essential to understanding the broader history of Mexican organized crime.

The economic logic of cartel competition is not difficult to understand. The drug trafficking corridors that provide access to the United States market are a finite and geographically fixed resource: there are only so many border crossing points, and the most valuable ones handle the largest volumes of traffic and therefore offer the greatest opportunities for smuggling. The organizations that control those crossing points can charge other traffickers for the right to move product through them, a form of territorial taxation, or they can reserve the corridors exclusively for their own product. Control of a major corridor is therefore a prize worth fighting for, and the value of that prize justifies substantial investment in violence to take or defend it.

But the economics of territorial control also explain why stable monopoly has proven so elusive. The violence required to take territory creates enemies with incentives for revenge. The removal of a cartel leader, whether through arrest or killing, creates succession struggles within the organization and signals to rival organizations that the territorial prize is momentarily up for grabs. The corruption payments that purchase political protection are vulnerable to the defection of the officials being paid, who may accept payments from rival organizations or simply respond to the incentives created by a change in political leadership. And the very success of a large trafficking organization can make it a priority target for law enforcement, while smaller organizations may operate below the threshold of enforcement attention.

The history of cartel violence in Mexico between approximately 2006 and the early 2020s illustrates these dynamics with unusual clarity. The decision by President Felipe Calderón, who took office in December 2006 under disputed electoral circumstances, to launch an immediate military offensive against the cartels rather than pursue the negotiated management strategies of prior governments, introduced a powerful external shock into the cartel system. The deployment of tens of thousands of military troops to cartel-affected states, combined with intensified pressure from American law enforcement and intelligence agencies, disrupted established territorial arrangements and removed several senior cartel leaders from their positions.

The conventional law enforcement theory behind this "decapitation" strategy, the deliberate targeting of senior leadership, was that removing the heads of criminal organizations would disrupt their operations and eventually collapse them. What the Mexican experience largely demonstrated instead was that decapitation produced fragmentation rather than collapse, and that fragmentation produced more violence rather than less. When a major cartel leader was removed, the organization's territorial assets did not simply disappear: they became prizes for which surviving factions, newly emboldened rivals, and former subordinates competed. Each act of competition required violence, and the violence of competition further destabilized the organizations involved, producing new succession crises and new competition.

The period from 2006 to approximately 2012 was the most violent in modern Mexican history to that point, with the country recording more than 120,000 drug-related homicides over those six years, according to Mexican government estimates. The city of Ciudad Juárez, caught in the crossfire of the Sinaloa-Juárez cartel war, recorded more than 3,000 homicides in 2010, a rate that made it one of the most dangerous cities in the world. Communities throughout northern Mexico experienced prolonged periods in which effective public safety did not exist, law enforcement was either absent or complicit, and residents navigated the violence as best they could, often through a combination of compliance with cartel demands, internal migration, and the careful management of information about their activities and associations.

The violence also produced significant humanitarian consequences. Forced displacement within Mexico, as residents fled communities controlled by cartels or caught in cartel conflicts, affected hundreds of thousands of people. Journalist murders made Mexico one of the most dangerous countries in the world for the press: dozens of journalists were killed in drug-related violence over the course of the 2000s and 2010s, and the effective suppression of reporting on cartel activities in many regions created information vacuums that allowed cartel violence to continue with limited public accountability. Mass graves, containing the bodies of victims of cartel killings, were discovered throughout northern and western Mexico; by the 2020s, the total number of missing persons in Mexico, many of them presumed victims of cartel violence, had reached tens of thousands.

Cause and Effect

Why Cartel Wars Produce Fragmentation and Violence

Long-Term Causes

The finite geography of border smuggling corridors creates intense territorial competition. The collapse of PRI political management removed informal mechanisms for containing trafficking violence. American demand creates enormous revenues justifying violent competition. Decades of political corruption weakened state capacity for effective law enforcement.

Short-Term Triggers

Calderón's military offensive disrupted established territorial arrangements. Decapitation of cartel leadership created succession crises. The Zetas' formal independence from the Gulf Cartel eliminated a major stabilizing alliance. The Beltrán Leyva rupture with Sinaloa opened multi-sided conflict in western Mexico.

The Pattern

Rather than consolidating into a stable monopoly, competition among cartels produces fragmentation: organizations split, former subordinates establish independent groups, and territorial prizes attract multiple competitors. Each fragmentation event produces new violence as emerging groups prove their capacity.

Consequences

More than 340,000 homicides in Mexico between 2006 and 2022, driven primarily by cartel violence. Hundreds of thousands of internally displaced persons. Mass graves containing thousands of unidentified victims. Near-total suppression of press freedom in cartel-controlled regions. Corruption of judicial, police, and political institutions at every level.

Historical complexity: The dynamics of cartel fragmentation demonstrate that law enforcement pressure alone, without addressing the economic conditions that make trafficking profitable and the political conditions that enable corruption, tends to produce more violence rather than less in the short term.

The Calderón Period and Its Legacy

Calderón's military strategy, whatever its original intentions, produced a set of consequences that his successors spent years navigating. The militarization of the drug war expanded the role of the Mexican Army in domestic security to a degree that raised serious human rights concerns, as soldiers engaged in operations for which they were not trained and in which civilian oversight was limited. Documentation of military abuses, including extrajudicial killings, torture of detainees, and forced disappearances conducted by security forces, emerged from human rights organizations throughout the Calderón period. The most notorious case involved the disappearance of forty-three students from the Ayotzinapa Rural Teachers' College in September 2014, under the administration of Calderón's successor Enrique Peña Nieto, in which local police working with a cartel organization abducted the students; their fate and the full circumstances of the operation remained subjects of official investigation and public controversy for years afterward.

The Peña Nieto administration, which governed from 2012 to 2018, pursued a more opaque security strategy than Calderón's explicitly militarized approach. The administration achieved several high-profile cartel leader captures, most notably the first recapture of El Chapo in 2014, but it was simultaneously beset by credible allegations of corruption within the government's own security apparatus. The arrest and subsequent American conviction of former Mexican Secretary of Public Security Genaro García Luna in 2023 on charges of accepting hundreds of millions of dollars in bribes from the Sinaloa Cartel while serving as the country's top law enforcement official provided the most dramatic confirmation of what investigators had long alleged: that the corruption of Mexican federal security institutions by cartel money had reached into the highest levels of government.

Historical Debate

Has Drug Prohibition Created the Mexican Cartel Crisis?

The question: To what extent is American and international drug prohibition policy responsible for the rise and power of Mexican drug cartels? And what policy alternatives, if any, would reduce cartel violence and power?

Interpretation A — Prohibition as Primary Cause: A significant body of academic analysis, journalism, and policy advocacy argues that drug prohibition is the fundamental cause of cartel power. By making drugs illegal, governments create enormous profit margins for those willing to accept the legal risks of trafficking. These profit margins justify the investment in corruption, violence, and organization that defines the cartels. In this view, no amount of law enforcement can durably reduce cartel power as long as the demand for illegal drugs remains high and the price premium created by prohibition remains enormous. This interpretation draws support from historical parallels: American alcohol prohibition created the conditions for the rise of organized crime in the 1920s, and its repeal significantly diminished that criminal power. Advocates of this view typically support some form of drug legalization or regulated access as the only policy capable of eliminating the economic foundation of cartel power.
Interpretation B — Structural and State Failure as Primary Cause: Other analysts argue that prohibition explains the economic opportunity but does not explain why Mexico in particular became the site of such extreme cartel violence. Countries in Western Europe and East Asia also enforce drug prohibition but do not have comparable cartel violence. This interpretation emphasizes Mexico-specific factors: the country's geographic position as the primary transit route between South American suppliers and the American market; the PRI political system's creation of a criminally corrupted state apparatus; the extreme economic inequality that makes criminal recruitment easy; and the institutional weaknesses of the Mexican judicial system, police, and military. In this view, addressing the cartel crisis requires institutional reform, economic development, and anticorruption efforts alongside whatever drug policy changes are made.

Evidence: The prohibition argument draws on the extraordinary historical correlation between American drug demand, American drug policy choices, and Mexican cartel power. The institutional argument draws on comparisons with other countries that have prohibition without comparable cartel violence. Both bodies of evidence are substantial and neither fully explains the phenomenon without the other.

Why it matters: The policy implications of these two interpretations are significantly different. If prohibition is the primary cause, the solution is drug policy reform. If institutional failure is primary, the solution lies in state-building, institutional reform, and economic development. Most analysts conclude that both factors are important and that effective policy must address both.

Section 23.6

Everyday Life Under Cartel Rule

The history of Mexican cartels has most often been told through the lives of its leaders, the spectacle of its violence, and the failures of government institutions to contain it. Less often examined, but equally important for a complete understanding of the phenomenon, is what cartel power means for the everyday lives of ordinary Mexican communities caught within its reach.

The experience of cartel power varied significantly by time, place, and which organization held territorial control. In some regions and periods, particularly in the early years when the PRI's managed system kept violence within limits, communities with significant drug trafficking might experience relatively little direct impact on daily life: traffickers operated, officials were paid, and ordinary business and family life continued with limited direct interference. The extreme violence and broad social control associated with major cartel operations were not universal features of drug trafficking from its beginnings but rather developed as cartel competition intensified and as organizations like the Zetas developed territorial control ambitions that went far beyond drug trafficking.

In communities where cartels exercised strong territorial control by the 2000s and 2010s, the experience was far more intrusive and frightening. Businesses, from small restaurants and pharmacies to large agricultural operations and transport companies, were required to pay extortion fees, known as cobro de piso (floor tax), to the dominant cartel. Failure to pay invited robbery, property destruction, kidnapping, or killing. The extortion demands were often calibrated to what businesses could reasonably sustain, since killing a business destroyed a revenue source, but they were also sometimes set at levels that drove businesses into closure, particularly during periods of intense inter-cartel competition when multiple organizations might attempt to collect from the same business simultaneously.

Recruitment pressure on young men, particularly teenagers from impoverished communities, was a persistent feature of cartel territorial control. Young men were recruited, sometimes through payment and apparent opportunity, sometimes through intimidation and coercion, to serve as halcones (lookouts who monitored community entrances and reported on police movements), as drivers, as low-level distributors, or eventually as enforcers. The economic alternatives available to young men in many cartel-affected communities were extremely limited, and the combination of relative pay and social status that cartel association offered, however dangerous, was genuinely attractive to individuals who saw few viable pathways to a dignified material life through the legitimate economy. This dynamic was not unique to Mexico, it appeared wherever criminal organizations recruited in communities of concentrated poverty, but it was particularly acute in regions where decades of poor governance and limited economic investment had left entire generations without viable educational or employment opportunities.

The displacement of communities was another major consequence of cartel violence. In regions where cartel conflict was most intense, civilians who could afford to leave did so, relocating to larger cities or, when possible, across the border into the United States. The displacement of agricultural communities in states like Michoacán, Guerrero, and Sinaloa left productive farmland abandoned and depressed local economies further. In some regions, communities organized autodefensas, civilian self-defense organizations that took up arms to resist cartel domination in the absence of effective state protection. The autodefensas represented a genuine and in some cases effective form of community resistance, but they also created complications: some were infiltrated or captured by the cartels they had originally formed to resist, and their legal ambiguity, they were armed civilians outside the legal framework of the state's security forces, created difficult questions about accountability and the long-term management of armed non-state actors.

Social History Feature

Journalism and Silence: The Suppression of Press Freedom in Cartel Mexico

One of the most significant and least discussed social consequences of cartel power in Mexico has been the effective destruction of independent journalism in large parts of the country. Journalists who investigated and reported on cartel activities faced a lethal calculus that had no equivalent in other liberal democracies: they could report accurately and risk assassination, report inaccurately and risk assassination for damaging a cartel's reputation, or not report at all. Many chose silence. The phenomenon became known as autocensura, or self-censorship, and by the 2010s it was pervasive throughout northern and western Mexico.

The murder of journalists in Mexico accelerated dramatically beginning in the mid-2000s. By 2010, Mexico had become one of the most dangerous countries in the world for journalists. The Committee to Protect Journalists documented dozens of journalist murders with confirmed or probable links to cartel retaliation over the following decade. The murders were often conducted with particular brutality, with bodies displayed publicly or accompanied by narcomessages warning other journalists, a deliberate strategy of terrorization designed not just to eliminate specific reporters but to communicate to the entire journalistic community what the consequences of reporting would be.

The social consequences of silenced journalism were profound. Communities in cartel-controlled regions lost their primary mechanism for public accountability. Corruption of public officials went unreported. Cartel abuses went undocumented. The information vacuums created by the suppression of journalism allowed cartels to operate with reduced public scrutiny and gave corrupt officials cover for their relationships with trafficking organizations. Some reporting on cartels migrated online, with anonymous accounts and citizen journalists filling some of the space vacated by professional media, but these alternatives lacked the resources, verification standards, and legal protections, however inadequate, of professional journalism.

The suppression of journalism in cartel Mexico also had international consequences. Reporting on cartel activities by foreign journalists and by the handful of Mexican journalists who continued to work despite the risks was essential to maintaining public awareness of the scale of the crisis. Organizations like Proceso, the investigative weekly that had published critical reporting on cartels and political corruption throughout the cartel era, and journalists like the investigative reporter Javier Valdez Cárdenas, who covered the Sinaloa drug world for decades before his assassination in May 2017, maintained a record of Mexican cartel history that would otherwise have gone unwritten. Their work represented both a form of civic courage and a demonstration of the human cost that accurate historical documentation sometimes requires.

Women in the Cartel World

The experience of women in cartel-affected communities defies simple characterization, encompassing both extreme victimization and complex forms of participation and agency. At the extreme of victimization, women were subjected to rape, kidnapping, forced prostitution, and murder as both instruments of cartel control and byproducts of cartel conflict. The phenomenon of femicide, the murder of women, was concentrated in regions of heavy cartel activity, and the disappearances and murders of women in Ciudad Juárez during the cartel conflict of the late 1990s through the 2010s, some of the first and most publicized cases of organized femicide in Mexico, drew international attention to the particular vulnerability of women in regions where state institutions had been corrupted or collapsed.

At the same time, women occupied complex roles within cartel organizations, ranging from the wives and partners of cartel leaders who managed households and sometimes money, to women who actively participated in cartel operations as money launderers, recruiters, intelligence agents, and even as enforcers. The figure of the female cartel operative, sometimes romanticized in narcocorrido culture and sometimes treated with patronizing surprise in journalistic coverage, was a genuine social phenomenon that reflected both the economic opportunities that cartel organizations offered to women from impoverished communities and the particular vulnerabilities that such employment created. Women who rose to senior positions in cartel organizations faced the same violence and legal consequences as their male counterparts, as the arrests and prosecutions of several female cartel figures demonstrated.

The mothers and wives of cartel victims, including both those killed by cartels and those killed by security forces in disputed circumstances, organized some of the most significant civil society responses to cartel violence. Organizations like the Movimiento por la Paz con Justicia y Dignidad, led by poet Javier Sicilia after his son was murdered in 2011, and the Fuerzas Unidas por Nuestros Desaparecidos (FUNDEM) combined grief and political activism in ways that challenged both cartel impunity and state violence, insisting on accountability and the right to truth that the official response to the cartel crisis had consistently failed to provide.

Multiple Perspectives

The Mexican Cartel Crisis: Four Perspectives

From the perspective of Mexican security forces: Military and police personnel engaged in anti-cartel operations faced adversaries with military-grade weapons, tactical training, superior intelligence about their movements, and organizational capacity that frequently exceeded the resources available to state security forces. Officers who pursued cartels aggressively risked assassination, as many did experience. Those who attempted to remain honest in deeply corrupted institutions faced social isolation and professional punishment. The decapitation strategy, which targeted leadership, made tactical sense given resource constraints but produced the fragmentation effects that made the overall security environment worse. From within the security forces, the cartel crisis looked like an endless war against an enemy that the state's institutional corruption prevented from being effectively engaged.
From the perspective of American law enforcement and policymakers: The DEA, FBI, Customs and Border Protection, and the interagency organizations coordinating American drug enforcement saw the Mexican cartel crisis as simultaneously a foreign criminal enterprise requiring aggressive international law enforcement and a domestic American demand problem that their political system had proven unwilling to seriously address. American agencies invested billions of dollars in the Mérida Initiative, a security cooperation program, and worked extensively with Mexican counterparts, but the bilateral relationship was constantly complicated by allegations of Mexican official corruption, disagreements about strategy, and American domestic politics around immigration and border security that frequently overwhelmed the drug trafficking dimension of the relationship.
From the perspective of communities in cartel-affected regions: For residents of communities in Sinaloa, Tamaulipas, Michoacán, Jalisco, and dozens of other states with intense cartel presence, the cartel crisis was not an abstract security problem but the defining reality of everyday life. It meant deciding whether to send children to school on days when armed men were moving through the neighborhood. It meant navigating extortion demands from multiple competing organizations. It meant watching young men, including family members, recruited into criminal organizations through a combination of poverty and coercion. It meant the inability to report crimes, seek medical care for wounds, or speak honestly with strangers about community conditions, because any of these actions might be interpreted as cooperation with an enemy organization or with law enforcement, both of which carried lethal risks.
From the perspective of cartel members and communities that benefit from trafficking: In the Sinaloa highlands and other regions where drug cultivation and trafficking have deep roots, cartel organizations are not universally experienced as purely predatory foreign impositions. They are in some cases the primary sources of employment, road construction, and community investment in regions that the legitimate state has ignored for generations. Cartel leaders in their home communities have sometimes been celebrated as providers who built roads, constructed schools, funded local religious festivals, and offered pathways to material security unavailable through legal means. This is not to minimize the coercion, violence, and exploitation that cartel organizations practice, but to understand the social conditions that produce genuine, if complex, community support for criminal organizations in specific contexts.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Corruption in Mexican Drug Trafficking: From the PRI Era to the Present

What Changed

  • The political party managing the corruption: from PRI-era centralized management to post-2000 fragmented and competitive corruption among multiple parties and levels of government
  • The scale of bribes: as cartel revenues grew dramatically with cocaine trafficking, corruption payments escalated to sums that dwarfed prior levels
  • The extension of corruption to the military: while the Federales and local police had always been targets, the deployment of the military in drug enforcement brought new institutions into the corruption cycle
  • International visibility: the extradition and conviction of former Secretary of Public Security García Luna brought the extent of federal-level corruption to international attention in ways previously impossible
  • The mechanisms of corruption: from cash payments managed through political intermediaries to sophisticated money laundering and the use of cryptocurrency and offshore accounts

What Stayed the Same

  • The fundamental dynamic: drug trafficking organizations pay government officials for protection, advance warning of operations, and tolerance of their activities
  • The vulnerability of local institutions: municipal police forces throughout cartel-affected regions remained susceptible to corruption across all political eras
  • The impunity of senior cartel figures: despite occasional high-profile arrests, the senior leadership of major organizations typically operated for years or decades before facing accountability
  • The use of violence as a backup to corruption: the threat of death for officials who refused bribes remained constant, creating conditions under which many officials experienced corruption as coerced rather than freely chosen
  • The cultural normalization: in communities with long histories of drug trafficking, corruption of officials was understood as a practical reality of governance rather than a deviation from a norm of clean government

Why the pattern matters: The persistence of corruption as a fundamental feature of cartel operations across radically different political contexts, from PRI authoritarianism through competitive democracy, suggests that corruption is not primarily a product of any particular political system but rather an institutional response to the economic incentives created by prohibition. Addressing it requires both institutional reform and a serious engagement with the demand and pricing structures that make bribery such a rational criminal strategy.

Chapter Summary

The history of Mexican drug cartels is not a simple story of crime and enforcement. It is a story about the intersection of geography, economics, political history, and institutional capacity that produced, over four decades, the most powerful and violent drug trafficking organizations in the history of the global narcotics trade. Understanding how that happened requires attention to the full range of factors involved: the geographic accident that placed Mexico between the world's largest producer regions and the world's largest consumer market; the economic logic of prohibition that created profit margins justifying extraordinary investment in corruption and violence; the political history of the PRI system that managed drug trafficking for decades before its collapse removed the informal controls that had contained cartel violence; and the institutional weaknesses of the Mexican state that made the construction of an effective law enforcement response extraordinarily difficult.

The Guadalajara Cartel established the foundational organizational model of Mexican drug trafficking, creating the first nationally coordinated trafficking organization, demonstrating the depth of political protection available to criminal organizations with sufficient money and the right connections, and producing, through its collapse, the territorial divisions that defined subsequent cartel competition. The murder of DEA agent Enrique Camarena in 1985, and the American response to that murder, demonstrated that there were limits to the political protection cartels could purchase and that the United States would use economic and diplomatic leverage against Mexico to enforce accountability for violence against American law enforcement personnel.

The Gulf Cartel's creation of the Zetas represented the most consequential organizational innovation in Mexican cartel history, introducing military-trained paramilitaries into criminal organizations and transforming the character of cartel violence from instrumental and targeted to mass and terroristic. The Zetas demonstrated that former military and police personnel could be recruited into criminal organizations, that criminal organizations could develop genuine paramilitary capabilities, and that organizations willing to use extreme violence could expand their territorial and market reach rapidly. When the Zetas separated from the Gulf Cartel in 2010, they further fragmented the cartel landscape and extended extreme violence into states and communities that had previously been insulated from the cartel conflict.

The Sinaloa Cartel, built by Joaquín Guzmán and Ismael Zambada over three decades, represented the mature form of the major trafficking organization: a network-based structure that combined sophisticated logistics, deep political corruption, global supply chains, and carefully calibrated violence that preferred corruption to confrontation with the state. Its survival of multiple leadership disruptions, including Guzmán's two prison escapes and ultimate extradition and conviction, demonstrated the resilience of the network organizational model and the extent to which large trafficking organizations could outlast the removal of individual leaders.

The Jalisco New Generation Cartel introduced a third organizational model, one that embraced confrontation with state military forces as a demonstration of power, combined rapid territorial expansion with extreme violence, and used social media propaganda to maintain its image among potential recruits and to intimidate rivals and state forces simultaneously. The CJNG's rise, alongside the continued power of Sinaloa factions, produced a twenty-first century cartel landscape defined by two primary competitors and dozens of smaller regional organizations, a situation far more complex and, in many respects, more violent than the landscape of the 1990s when fewer, larger organizations exercised more consolidated territorial control.

The cartel wars that defined the period from roughly 2006 to the present produced extraordinary human costs: hundreds of thousands of homicides, hundreds of thousands of displaced persons, tens of thousands of disappearances, the effective destruction of press freedom in large parts of the country, and the profound corruption of public institutions at every level of government. The Calderón-era military offensive, whatever its strategic rationale, demonstrated that decapitation of cartel leadership produced fragmentation and more violence rather than the dismantling of criminal organizations. The subsequent López Obrador administration's attempt to pursue a less militarized approach was criticized as ceding territory to criminal organizations, reflecting the genuine difficulty of identifying an effective strategy in an environment where all available options had significant costs.

The social history of cartel-affected communities, often overlooked in accounts focused on the organizations themselves, reveals the human dimensions of a crisis that law enforcement statistics can only partially capture. Extortion, forced recruitment, displacement, femicide, the suppression of journalism, and the complex negotiations through which ordinary people navigated cartel power were the daily texture of life in regions under cartel control. The resistance organized by victims' families, autodefensas, surviving journalists, and civil society organizations represented genuine courage in extraordinarily dangerous circumstances and provided an essential counterpoint to the narrative of pure victimization that dominated some external accounts of Mexican communities under cartel rule.

The history of Mexican cartels also demands attention to the role of the United States. American drug demand created and sustained the market that made cartel wealth possible. American interdiction policy in the Caribbean in the 1980s pushed cocaine trafficking overland through Mexico, dramatically increasing Mexican cartel power. American weapons, purchased legally in the United States and smuggled south of the border, armed cartel enforcers and paramilitaries. American demand for the chemicals used in methamphetamine production sustained a major cartel revenue stream. American drug policy choices were not peripheral causes of the Mexican cartel crisis. They were among its primary causes. Any serious analysis of the crisis that does not account for this bilateral dimension is incomplete.

The chapter's history ultimately illustrates several of the recurring themes in the global history of organized crime. Criminal organizations emerge and flourish where illegal markets are large, where state capacity is limited or corrupted, where economic inequality is deep, and where prohibition creates profit margins that justify the costs of violence and corruption. They are not aberrations from otherwise functional societies but institutions that grow in the spaces created by policy choices, institutional failures, and economic conditions. Understanding them historically, rather than simply as threats to be suppressed, is the first step toward developing responses capable of addressing the structural conditions that make them possible.

Key Terms

Autodefensas
Civilian self-defense organizations that emerged in cartel-affected regions of Mexico, particularly in Michoacán and Guerrero, as communities organized armed resistance to cartel extortion and violence in the absence of effective state protection.
Cartel
In the context of drug trafficking, the term used informally to describe major organized crime groups that control drug production, transportation, or distribution networks. The term technically implies price-fixing coordination but is used loosely to describe trafficking organizations of significant scale and structure.
Cobro de piso
The "floor tax" or extortion payment demanded by cartels from businesses operating in territory they control. A major source of cartel revenue beyond drug trafficking.
Corridor
A smuggling route linking the interior of Mexico to a specific United States-Mexico border crossing point. Control of smuggling corridors is the primary prize in cartel territorial competition.
Decapitation strategy
The law enforcement approach of targeting cartel leadership for arrest or killing, based on the theory that removing organizational heads would disrupt criminal operations. The Mexican experience largely demonstrated that decapitation produced fragmentation and more violence rather than organizational collapse.
Extradition
The legal transfer of a criminal suspect or convicted person from one country to another for prosecution or to serve a sentence. Mexico's willingness to extradite cartel leaders to the United States became a significant tool in American anti-cartel strategy and a major incentive for cartels to invest in political protection.
Fragmentation
The tendency of cartel organizations, when subjected to law enforcement pressure or internal conflict, to split into multiple competing successor groups rather than consolidating into a single dominant organization. Fragmentation typically produces more violence rather than less.
Golden Triangle
The mountainous region at the intersection of the Mexican states of Sinaloa, Durango, and Chihuahua, historically the primary growing region for Mexican marijuana and opium poppies and the geographic heartland of the Sinaloa Cartel.
Halcón
A lookout, typically a young person employed by a cartel to monitor roads, neighborhoods, and points of entry to cartel territory and to report on police movements and the presence of rival cartel personnel by mobile phone.
Huachicol
Fuel stolen from PEMEX, the Mexican national petroleum company, typically by drilling into pipelines or bribing pipeline workers. Became a major source of cartel revenue, particularly for the Zetas and subsequently for other organizations including the CJNG and the Santa Rosa de Lima Cartel in Guanajuato.
Jalisco New Generation Cartel (CJNG)
The criminal organization led by Nemesio Oseguera Cervantes (El Mencho), which emerged formally around 2011 and rapidly expanded to become one of the two dominant forces in Mexican organized crime. Distinguished by its willingness to directly confront military and police forces and its aggressive use of social media propaganda.
Mérida Initiative
A security cooperation agreement between the United States and Mexico launched in 2008, under which the United States provided billions of dollars in equipment, training, and intelligence support to Mexican security forces for anti-cartel operations.
Narcocorrido
A genre of Mexican music, descended from the corrido ballad tradition, that tells stories of drug traffickers, cartel conflicts, and the narco lifestyle. One of the primary cultural expressions of narcocultura and a subject of significant debate about its role in romanticizing criminal violence.
Narcocultura
The complex of cultural forms, aesthetics, and values associated with drug trafficking organizations in Mexico, including music, fashion, architecture, religious practices, and social media content. Represents both a genuine cultural expression of communities shaped by the illegal economy and a form of criminal propaganda.
Narcomantas
Banners hung in public places by cartels containing messages directed at rival organizations, government officials, or the general public. Used to claim responsibility for violence, issue threats, announce territorial control, and communicate with law enforcement and the media.
Plata o plomo
A Spanish phrase meaning "silver or lead," used to describe the cartel strategy of offering officials a choice between accepting a bribe (the silver) or suffering violence (the lead). Encapsulates the fundamental dynamic of cartel corruption strategy.
Plaza
In the context of Mexican drug trafficking, the geographic territory controlled by a cartel or cartel faction. The concept derives from the PRI-era administrative system in which officials managed the informal licensing of drug trafficking within their jurisdictions.
PRI (Partido Revolucionario Institucional)
The Institutional Revolutionary Party, which governed Mexico from 1929 to 2000. Its system of managed relationships with drug trafficking organizations provided the political framework within which cartels operated before democratization dissolved those arrangements and triggered the escalation of cartel violence.
Sicario
A cartel assassin or hitman. The term derives from the Latin word for a hired killer. In the context of Mexican cartels, sicarios were the enforcement personnel responsible for carrying out killings ordered by cartel leadership.
Sinaloa Cartel
The criminal organization built primarily by Joaquín Guzmán Loera (El Chapo) and Ismael Zambada García (El Mayo), which became the world's most powerful drug trafficking organization by the 2000s. Known for its network organizational structure, global reach, and preference for corruption over direct confrontation with the state.
State capture
A situation in which a criminal organization achieves sufficient penetration of public institutions, through corruption, intimidation, and the installation of allied figures in official positions, that state agencies effectively serve the organization's interests rather than the public interest.
Zetas
Originally the paramilitary enforcement wing of the Gulf Cartel, composed of former Mexican Army special forces recruited by Osiel Cárdenas Guillén in the late 1990s. Formally separated from the Gulf Cartel in 2010 and became an independent criminal organization known for extreme violence, mass killings, and diversification into criminal markets beyond drug trafficking.

Chapter Reflection

The history of Mexican drug cartels provides one of the most compelling case studies available in the global history of organized crime, precisely because it encompasses so many of the structural dynamics that this textbook has traced across multiple contexts and centuries. The relationship between illegal markets and criminal organization, the role of political corruption in enabling and sometimes managing criminal enterprise, the tendency of enforcement pressure to produce fragmentation rather than collapse, the social costs borne by communities caught between criminal organizations and ineffective or corrupt state institutions, the cultural dimensions of criminal power, and the deep entanglement of criminal and legitimate economies: all of these themes, which appear in discussions of the Sicilian Mafia, the Yakuza, Russian organized crime, and the Colombian cartels, appear with particular clarity and urgency in the Mexican case.

What makes the Mexican case distinctive, and what makes it of particular importance in the early twenty-first century, is its bilateral character. The cartels that this chapter has examined are not simply Mexican criminal organizations: they are joint products of Mexican institutional conditions and American policy choices. The demand that drives the cartel economy originates in the United States. The prohibition that creates the profit margins justifying criminal violence is an American policy, and a global one sustained in significant part by American pressure. The weapons that arm cartel enforcers and paramilitaries flow south from the United States. The money laundered through American financial institutions, real estate markets, and businesses constitutes a significant portion of cartel revenues. The history of Mexican cartels cannot be understood as a purely foreign phenomenon by American students. It is, in important respects, partly an American story.

The chapter's history also raises important questions about the relationship between institutional reform and criminal power. The Mexican experience suggests that the collapse of even a deeply corrupt and authoritarian political system, a development that most theories of democracy would consider unambiguously positive, can produce worse security outcomes in the short term if the informal mechanisms that managed criminal violence under the old system are removed without being replaced by effective legal alternatives. This is a sobering observation, and it points to the difficulty of designing policy responses to organized crime that take account of both the immediate security situation and the long-term institutional conditions that would reduce criminal power. Easy answers are not available, and the history of this chapter's subject should make students appropriately skeptical of confident claims that any single intervention, whether military, legal, or social, can quickly resolve a crisis with roots as deep and causes as multiple as the Mexican cartel phenomenon.

Finally, the history of Mexican cartels demands a reckoning with the human costs of the policies that have shaped it. The hundreds of thousands of homicides, the tens of thousands of disappearances, the hundreds of thousands of displaced persons, the murdered journalists, the extorted businesses, the recruited children, the traumatized communities: these are the human consequences of a combination of prohibition policy, institutional failure, economic inequality, and political corruption that has operated across multiple decades and across the territory of two of the Americas' most important nations. Understanding that history with the seriousness it deserves, holding in view both the structural forces that created it and the human beings who suffered and sometimes resisted it, is both an analytical obligation and a moral one. The history does not offer easy redemption or simple lessons. But it offers, for those willing to engage with it honestly, a profound and necessary education in how criminal power emerges, how it sustains itself, and what is required to reduce it.

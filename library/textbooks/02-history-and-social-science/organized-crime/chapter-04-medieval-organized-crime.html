Part II — The Origins of Organized Crime

Organized Crime in the Medieval World

Organized Crime: A Global History

Chapter Opening

"Wherever trade flowed in the medieval world, someone was waiting to extract a toll. The only question was whether that someone held a title, a sword, or a secret."

In the year 1241, a convoy of German merchant ships sailing through the Baltic Sea found itself shadowed by vessels flying no flag. The merchants, members of a loose trading network that would later formalize as the Hanseatic League, had seen such ships before. They were pirates, operating out of the marshy coastlines and island chains of the western Baltic, offering a simple commercial proposition: pay a fee and sail on unmolested, or resist and lose everything, including the lives of the crew. The merchants knew, moreover, that local lords onshore sometimes received a share of whatever the pirates collected. The line between legitimate protection and organized extortion was not merely blurry in the medieval world. In many places and at many times, it did not exist at all.

The medieval period, roughly spanning the fall of the Western Roman Empire in the fifth century through the emergence of centralized nation-states and the age of European overseas expansion in the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, presents the historian of organized crime with a distinctive challenge. The political structures of the medieval world were profoundly fragmented. Authority was layered, overlapping, and contested among kings, lords, bishops, city councils, merchant guilds, military orders, and countless local powers. There was no single sovereign capable of monopolizing violence across large territories for much of this period, and the legal apparatus available to prosecute criminality was inconsistent, easily corrupted, and frequently absent in the vast spaces between fortified towns and castles.

In such a world, the distinctions that modern observers use to separate "legitimate" authority from "criminal" organization were perpetually in motion. A lord who charged travelers tolls for passage through his territory was exercising a recognized feudal right. A bandit who charged the same toll at the same crossing was a criminal. But the difference between these two figures often came down not to the nature of the act but to the political and legal status of the person performing it, status that was itself frequently acquired through violence, inheritance, or purchase rather than through any democratic or juridical process. Medieval organized crime, then, cannot be cleanly separated from the political economy of medieval society. It was woven into that economy, parasitic upon it in some respects and structurally constitutive of it in others.

This chapter examines the principal forms that organized criminal enterprise took in the medieval world. It begins with merchant extortion, the most pervasive and economically significant form of organized predation in the period, and traces how the extraction of money and goods from traders operating across the emerging commercial networks of Europe, the Middle East, and Asia shaped both criminal organizations and the legitimate institutions that emerged partly in response to criminal pressure. It then turns to the feudal protection system, examining how the formal political and military structures of medieval society created conditions that were structurally almost indistinguishable from organized racketeering, and how genuine criminal enterprises operated in the shadows of and sometimes in partnership with feudal lords. From there it moves to maritime crime, examining piracy and privateering as organized enterprises that blurred the line between warfare, commerce, and crime across the Mediterranean, the Baltic, and the seas of Asia. It then examines the secret societies that flourished across the medieval world, from European criminal brotherhoods to Asian networks of mutual aid and covert power, asking what needs these organizations served and how they organized themselves to survive in hostile environments. Finally, it considers the criminal brotherhoods of late medieval Europe, the guilds of thieves, beggars, and confidence artists who developed sophisticated organizational structures, coded languages, and territorial agreements that anticipate in striking ways the modern criminal organizations examined later in this textbook.

Throughout, this chapter insists on seeing medieval organized crime not as a colorful aberration from the main story of the Middle Ages but as a central feature of the period's political economy. Criminal enterprise in the medieval world was not simply a failure of social order. It was an expression of how power, profit, and violence were organized in a world without modern states, modern policing, or modern markets. Understanding medieval organized crime, and the ways in which it shaped and was shaped by the legitimate institutions of the era, is essential to understanding the deep historical roots of the criminal organizations that would emerge in more recognizable forms in later centuries.

It is also essential to understanding the persistence of organized crime across time. Many of the structural features that made medieval society hospitable to criminal enterprise, including weak central authority, uneven enforcement of law, easy corruption of officials, high demand for goods that political authorities tried to control or tax, and the willingness of ordinary people to pay for protection when the state could not provide it, are features that recur across history whenever and wherever those conditions obtain. The medieval world did not invent these conditions, but it expressed them with particular clarity, and the criminal enterprises that arose in response to them established organizational patterns and cultural traditions that would echo through the centuries.

Students who engage seriously with this chapter will not simply learn a collection of medieval crime stories, though the stories here are often remarkable. They will develop an analytical framework for understanding how criminal organization relates to political structure, how illegal markets develop in response to the conditions that legal markets cannot or will not serve, how the boundaries between crime and legitimate authority are drawn and redrawn over time, and why certain organizational forms, such as the protection racket, the secret brotherhood, and the piratical network, keep appearing across widely separated times and places. These are questions that will accompany us throughout this textbook as we trace the evolution of organized crime from the ancient world to the digital age.

  8. Assess the significance of late medieval criminal brotherhoods as early examples of organized criminal enterprise with formal structures, territorial agreements, and coded communication.

Historical Context

To understand organized crime in the medieval world, one must first understand the political, economic, and social landscape in which it operated. That landscape was shaped above all by the collapse of the Western Roman Empire in the fifth century CE, which had provided the western portion of the European continent with a degree of centralized political authority, legal uniformity, and infrastructure, including roads, urban markets, and maritime trade, that would not be replicated for nearly a millennium. The dissolution of Roman imperial power did not simply create a political vacuum. It fragmented authority into thousands of competing jurisdictions, each with its own claims to legitimate power, its own customs and laws, and its own capacity or incapacity to maintain order and protect the movement of goods and people.

The feudal system that emerged across much of Western and Central Europe during the early medieval period was an organizational response to this fragmentation. Feudalism, in its classical form, was a set of hierarchical relationships between lords and vassals in which land and protection were exchanged for military service and other obligations. At its best, it provided a framework within which local order could be maintained and agricultural production could proceed. At its worst, and this was very often, it was a system in which the strong extracted resources from the weak through the credible threat of violence, with no external authority capable of restraining that extraction.

The commercial revolution that began to accelerate across much of Eurasia from roughly the tenth century onward introduced new dynamics. The growth of long-distance trade, the revival of urban life, the expansion of markets for agricultural surplus, and the development of new financial instruments, including credit, bills of exchange, and partnership contracts, created wealth that moved through the landscape in forms that were far more vulnerable to predation than the immobile wealth of land. A lord who extracted tolls from travelers crossing his domain was a familiar figure throughout this period. But as merchants began moving larger quantities of more valuable goods over longer distances, the incentives for organized criminal predation grew proportionally, and the organizational sophistication required to exploit those opportunities also increased.

The medieval Church provided an additional layer of institutional complexity. Claiming authority over the moral and spiritual lives of all Christians, the Church was simultaneously a major economic actor, a landowner, a creditor, a trader, and an institution capable of punishing transgression through excommunication and interdict. The Church's prohibition on usury, the lending of money at interest, created one of the most consequential black markets of the medieval period, since the demand for credit was real and growing even as the most straightforward mechanism for satisfying that demand was officially prohibited. Jewish merchants, who were not bound by Christian prohibitions on usury, often filled this role at great personal risk, while Christian merchants and institutions developed elaborate legal fictions to disguise lending at interest as other kinds of transactions. The gap between what the Church prohibited and what the economy required was one of the most productive spaces for criminal and quasi-criminal enterprise in the medieval world.

Beyond Europe, the medieval world encompassed vast and varied political landscapes that present their own histories of organized criminal enterprise. The Islamic caliphates and successor states of the Middle East and North Africa, the trading cities and kingdoms of sub-Saharan Africa, the maritime empires of Southeast Asia, the dynasties of China, and the nomadic confederacies of the Eurasian steppe all developed their own forms of criminal organization, rooted in their own political economies and institutional arrangements. While this chapter focuses most heavily on European and Mediterranean examples, where the historical evidence is most abundant and where the institutional precursors to modern Western organized crime are most directly traceable, it also draws on the wider world to illustrate the common structural conditions that produce organized criminal enterprise across different cultural and political contexts.

The late medieval period, roughly the thirteenth through the fifteenth centuries, saw both the consolidation of more powerful central authorities in some parts of Europe and Asia and, paradoxically, some of the most sophisticated and elaborately organized criminal enterprises the world had yet seen. The Black Death of the mid-fourteenth century, which killed between one-third and one-half of Europe's population, shook existing hierarchies of authority and obligation, created new economic opportunities in the resulting social disruption, and generated conditions in which criminal organizations could operate with unusual latitude. The emergence of new forms of credit, new urban economies, and new patterns of migration and labor in the aftermath of the plague all provided both opportunities and challenges for organized crime, setting the stage for the more recognizable criminal organizations that would emerge in the early modern period examined in the next chapter.

Throughout this chapter, students should keep in mind several analytical principles that will recur throughout this textbook. First, the distinction between "organized crime" and "legitimate authority" in the medieval world was often a matter of political position rather than the nature of the underlying activity. Second, criminal enterprise tends to fill the spaces that legitimate markets and institutions cannot or will not serve. Third, the organizational forms that criminal enterprises develop tend to reflect and adapt the organizational forms available in their surrounding society. And fourth, corruption, understood as the use of institutional position for private criminal gain, has been a feature of virtually every political system in human history, connecting organized crime to the centers of power rather than simply to its margins.

Major People, Groups, and Places

Reference — People, Groups & Places

Hanseatic League (Hansa)
A confederation of merchant guilds and market towns, centered on northern Germany and the Baltic, that operated as a major commercial and political force from roughly the thirteenth through the seventeenth centuries. Its development was shaped in significant part by the need to combat piracy and extortion in Baltic and North Sea trade.
The Vitaliensbrüder (Victual Brothers)
A pirate confederation operating in the Baltic Sea in the late fourteenth and early fifteenth centuries, initially hired by the Mecklenburg lords of Sweden but quickly developing into an independent criminal enterprise that preyed on merchant shipping regardless of nationality. One of the most organized and politically complex piracy networks of the medieval period.
The Assassins (Nizari Ismailis)
A Shia Muslim sect that operated from mountain fortresses in Persia and Syria from the eleventh to the thirteenth centuries. Their organization combined religious ideology, political assassination, and intelligence gathering in ways that made them feared by Crusader and Islamic rulers alike. Their name entered European languages as a byword for organized political murder.
The Condottieri
Mercenary military commanders and their companies who operated across Italy and other parts of Europe in the late medieval period. Operating in the space between legitimate military service and criminal predation, the condottieri represent one of the most striking examples of organized violence as a commercial enterprise.
The Thieves' Guilds (European)
Loosely organized networks of professional criminals in late medieval European cities, documented in sources such as the Liber Vagatorum and the records of municipal courts, that maintained territorial agreements, internal hierarchies, coded languages (argots), and informal law-enforcement functions within their communities.
Roger de Flor
A Templar knight turned mercenary commander who led the Catalan Company, a large mercenary force, through the eastern Mediterranean in the early fourteenth century. His career illustrates the thin line between legitimate military enterprise and organized criminal predation in the late medieval world.
The Barbary Coast
The North African coastal region (modern Morocco, Algeria, Tunisia, and Libya) that became, especially from the fourteenth century onward, a base for organized piracy targeting Christian Mediterranean shipping, with tacit or active support from local political authorities.
The Mediterranean Sea
The central arena for medieval long-distance trade and maritime crime, where Christian, Muslim, and Jewish merchants, pirates, and political authorities competed, collaborated, and preyed upon one another across a body of water that was simultaneously the most commercially vital and the most criminally dangerous space in the medieval world.
The Baltic Sea
The northern maritime arena in which Scandinavian, German, Polish, and Russian commercial and criminal interests competed from the early medieval period onward, generating distinctive forms of piracy, merchant organization, and criminal enterprise.
Guild of Thieves and Vagabonds
The generic term for the criminal subcultures of late medieval European cities, whose existence is documented in court records, municipal ordinances, literary sources, and the criminal argot texts known collectively as the Liber Vagatorum tradition.

Section 4.1

Merchant Extortion and Market Crime

The single most economically significant form of organized criminal enterprise in the medieval world was the systematic extraction of money from merchants. This extraction took many forms, ranging from the straightforward highway robbery of a single traveler to the elaborate, institutionalized toll systems maintained by powerful lords across entire river valleys or mountain passes. What unified these varied forms of predation was their fundamentally organized character: they were not random, individual acts of desperation but calculated, repeated enterprises designed to generate reliable income from the flow of commercial goods and the people who moved them.

The emergence of long-distance trade as a significant feature of European and Mediterranean economic life from roughly the tenth century onward transformed the landscape of criminal opportunity. Before the commercial revolution, the most valuable form of wealth in most of Western Europe was land, and land could not easily be stolen and carried away. But the revival of trade created a new kind of portable wealth: silver coin, luxury textiles, spices, furs, amber, dried fish, and a dozen other commodities that could be taken from a merchant on a road or a river and converted into profit with relatively little difficulty. The richer the trade, the greater the incentive for criminal predation, and the more organized and sophisticated that predation tended to become.

The Toll Road as a Criminal Institution

The most pervasive form of merchant extortion in the medieval world was the unauthorized toll. Across Western Europe, particularly along the Rhine, the Danube, and the great river systems of France and England, lords of various levels of authority and legitimacy maintained toll stations where merchants were compelled to pay fees for passage. Some of these tolls were legally sanctioned by royal or imperial authority. Many others were not. The distinction, from the merchant's perspective, was largely academic: whether the man demanding payment held a genuine royal charter or simply possessed sufficient armed force to make refusal dangerous, the result was the same.

Along the Rhine River alone, medieval sources document hundreds of toll stations between Basel and the sea, many of them established by local lords with dubious legal authority and maintained by force rather than by right. Merchants who wished to trade between the Italian cities and the Flemish textile centers of the north, one of the most commercially vital routes in medieval Europe, had no choice but to run a gauntlet of these tollbooths, each extracting a portion of their cargo or their cash. The cumulative burden of these extractions was enormous, and it constituted a major structural feature of medieval commerce, shaping trade routes, commodity prices, and the organization of merchant enterprises in ways that rippled through the entire economy.

This system had important organizational implications. Merchants who could not avoid the toll roads needed to organize collectively to negotiate, resist, or find alternatives. The development of merchant guilds across the medieval period was driven in significant part by the need to pool resources, establish collective bargaining power, and share the risks of extortion and robbery. When a guild negotiated a safe-conduct agreement with a powerful lord, all its members could benefit. When a guild maintained armed escorts for its convoys, it was responding directly to the criminal threat posed by organized predation along the roads.

But the guild was not only a defensive institution. In many cities, merchant guilds themselves developed the capacity to engage in extortionate behavior, using their collective power to exclude competitors, control prices, and punish members who broke the rules of collective solidarity in ways that bore a strong family resemblance to the practices of later criminal organizations. The guild's capacity to impose penalties on those who violated its agreements, including physical sanctions in some cases, was grounded in a kind of legitimate authority granted by civic institutions. But the underlying logic, pay us or suffer the consequences, was structurally identical to the protection racket that criminal scholars have analyzed in organized crime from the medieval period to the present.

Historical Vocabulary
Extortion

Extortion is the practice of obtaining money, goods, or services through coercion, particularly through threats of violence or harm. In the context of organized crime, extortion typically involves a recurring relationship between the extortionist and the victim rather than a one-time robbery: the extortionist offers a kind of negative good, protection from harm that the extortionist themselves would otherwise inflict, in exchange for regular payment.

In the medieval world, the distinction between extortion and legitimate taxation was often unclear, contested, or simply absent. Many of the tolls, dues, and fees that medieval lords extracted from merchants traveling through their territories had no firmer legal foundation than the lord's capacity to enforce payment by force. What distinguished these extractions from outright extortion in the eyes of contemporaries was typically the lord's recognized status within the feudal hierarchy, a status that was itself frequently acquired through violence and maintained by the continued threat of it.

Historians of organized crime distinguish extortion from robbery on the grounds of its organized, relational character. A robber takes what he can from a victim and moves on. An extortionist establishes a continuing relationship in which regular payments purchase ongoing freedom from predation. This relational structure is what makes extortion an organizational phenomenon and connects it to the protection racket, one of the most enduring organizational forms in the history of organized crime.

Organized Robbery and Bandit Kingdoms

Beyond the institutionalized toll, medieval roads were dangerous in a more straightforwardly criminal sense. Organized bands of robbers operated across the major trade routes of Europe and the Middle East, and the most successful of these were genuine criminal enterprises rather than mere opportunistic predation. They required planning, intelligence about which merchants were moving which goods, organization to deploy sufficient force to overcome merchant escorts, and logistical support to divide and sell stolen goods in markets where they would not be recognized as contraband.

Some of these bands attained a level of territorial control that gave them a quasi-governmental character. Operating from fortified positions in mountainous or forested terrain beyond the reach of effective royal authority, the most powerful bandit organizations could levy regular tribute on traders passing through their zones of control, effectively operating as unofficial governments of the spaces they controlled. The distinction between such a bandit kingdom and a small feudal lordship was, in practice, often a matter of the robber lord's relationship with higher-level feudal authorities rather than of the nature of the activity itself.

In the forested regions of central Germany, the Italian Apennines, the mountains of Iberia, and the highlands of the British Isles, organized banditry of this type was a persistent feature of the medieval landscape. Royal and imperial authorities made repeated efforts to suppress it, with occasional dramatic successes followed by the rapid re-emergence of new organizations as conditions again became favorable. The structural conditions that produced banditry, including political fragmentation, inadequate road security, high traffic in portable wealth, and the refuge provided by difficult terrain, were sufficiently durable that no sustained suppression was possible without the kind of centralized administrative machinery that most medieval states simply did not possess.

The most successful bandit organizations developed what might be called a criminal infrastructure: networks of informants in the towns who tipped them off about valuable shipments, fences who converted stolen goods into cash, corrupt officials who provided warning of planned law enforcement operations, and political protectors among the lesser nobility who received a share of the proceeds in exchange for immunity. This infrastructure made organized medieval banditry something qualitatively different from opportunistic robbery, connecting criminal enterprise to the legal and political institutions of the surrounding society in a pattern that would recur in virtually every subsequent era of organized criminal history.

Market Manipulation and Economic Crime

Not all organized criminal enterprise in the medieval marketplace involved violence or its threat. As urban markets developed from the eleventh century onward, more sophisticated forms of economic crime emerged, including the manipulation of weights and measures, the adulteration of goods, the hoarding and cornering of essential commodities, and the organization of conspiracies to fix prices or exclude competitors. These activities were not merely individual dishonesty but required coordination among multiple actors, shared systems of deception, and mechanisms for enforcing the agreements that made collective fraud possible.

Medieval city governments were well aware of these problems and made persistent efforts to regulate markets through officials charged with inspecting weights, measures, and the quality of goods. The office of the market inspector, known by various names across different European cities, was among the most important and most persistently difficult municipal offices to fill honestly, since the inspectors operated in an environment of constant temptation and the parties being regulated had strong incentives to corrupt them. The persistent complaints in medieval civic records about corrupt market officials who accepted bribes to overlook fraudulent practices document one of the most consistent patterns in the history of organized crime: the corruption of regulatory officials as a normal cost of doing illegal business.

The organized manipulation of credit markets was another form of economic crime with significant organizational dimensions. The Church's prohibition on usury, officially defined as lending at any interest, created a massive gap between what the law permitted and what the economy required. The creative legal arrangements that medieval merchants, ecclesiastical institutions, and monarchs developed to circumvent this prohibition included a variety of instruments that were, in substance if not in legal form, interest-bearing loans dressed up as commenda partnerships, exchange transactions, or insurance contracts. The most sophisticated of these arrangements required the participation of specialized legal and financial experts, the maintenance of records designed to disguise the true nature of transactions, and networks of trust among the parties involved, features that are recognizable as organizational characteristics of later criminal enterprises operating in regulated markets.

Section 4.2

Feudal Protection Systems

Of all the forms of organized criminal enterprise in the medieval world, none is more structurally revealing than the feudal protection system. At its core, feudalism was an exchange of protection for service: the lord provided military defense and judicial authority to those under his power, and in return received labor, military service, and a variety of economic dues and fees. This exchange is perfectly rational and, under appropriate conditions, genuinely beneficial to both parties. But it also contains the essential structure of a protection racket: a dominant party with the capacity for violence offers protection from harm, and those who cannot provide their own protection pay for it or suffer the consequences.

The scholars who have analyzed organized crime most carefully, from the sociologist Diego Gambetta in his study of the Sicilian Mafia to the economist Peter Reuter in his work on American criminal markets, have consistently identified the sale of protection as the foundational economic activity of the most durable organized criminal enterprises. What the study of medieval feudalism adds to this analysis is the recognition that the sale of protection is not inherently criminal. It becomes criminal when the party selling protection is not the legitimate sovereign, or when the protection is being sold against harm that the seller would otherwise inflict, or when the price is extracted through pure coercion with no genuine service provided in return. In the medieval world, all three of these conditions were often present simultaneously, in the very institutions that contemporaries recognized as the legitimate framework of political authority.

The Feudal Lord as Racketeer

Consider the position of a merchant traveling through the territories of a minor lord in twelfth-century France. The lord collected tolls at the bridge over the river, dues at the market he controlled, fees for the use of his mill, and an additional payment for the armed escort he provided through the most dangerous sections of road. Each of these extractions had a formal legal basis in the feudal customs of the period. But the lord also controlled the capacity for violence in his territory, meaning that the merchant's choice was not simply whether to pay for services received but whether to pay for protection from harm the lord could choose to inflict or permit. The structural difference between this situation and a classic organized crime protection racket is minimal.

The genuinely criminal feudal lord, as opposed to the merely exploitative one, was a recognized figure in medieval society. Lords who used their position and their armed retainers to systematically rob travelers, kidnap merchants for ransom, raid neighboring villages, and extort money from communities under their nominal protection were a persistent problem across medieval Europe, particularly in periods of weak central authority. Medieval chronicles, court records, and royal ordinances are filled with complaints about and punishments imposed on lords who had turned their position into a criminal enterprise.

What made these lords criminals in the eyes of contemporaries was not precisely that they engaged in extortion, which every feudal lord did in some measure, but that they violated the reciprocal obligations of the feudal relationship by taking without providing any genuine protection in return, or by preying on people who were under the protection of higher authorities, or by operating outside the customary limits that were supposed to bound the exercise of feudal power. The distinction is important for understanding the medieval concept of criminality, which was less about the nature of an act in the abstract and more about whether it violated recognized obligations and exceeded recognized limits of power.

Ecclesiastical Corruption and Criminal Exploitation

The Church's immense institutional power in the medieval world made it both a potential victim of criminal enterprise and a potential criminal actor in its own right. Ecclesiastical institutions, including monasteries, cathedral chapters, and the offices of bishops and abbots, controlled vast resources, including land, livestock, stored grain, silver plate, and cash, that made them attractive targets for robbery and extortion. Medieval chronicles record numerous instances of criminal bands preying specifically on monastic communities, which were often inadequately defended and whose pacifist ideology made armed resistance religiously problematic.

But the Church was also capable of using its institutional power in ways that shaded into organized criminal behavior. The sale of indulgences, the appointment of Church offices in exchange for payment (simony), the manipulation of ecclesiastical courts to extract money from defendants, and the use of threats of excommunication or interdict to coerce payments were all recognized problems in medieval Church governance, regularly condemned by reformers and councils. These practices involved the systematic use of institutional authority, including the authority to inflict spiritual harm, to extract private profit, in a pattern that is structurally identical to the extortion practiced by secular lords and criminal organizations.

More directly connected to organized crime were the networks of corrupt churchmen who cooperated with criminal enterprises in exchange for payments. Confessors who absolved thieves without requiring restitution, officials who altered records of property ownership in exchange for bribes, and clerical administrators who helped launder the proceeds of criminal activity by channeling them through ecclesiastical institutions were all documented features of the medieval religious landscape. These forms of corruption connected organized criminal enterprise to the most powerful institutional hierarchy in medieval European society and illustrate, once again, the degree to which medieval organized crime operated not on the margins of legitimate society but in active collaboration with its central institutions.

Mercenary Companies as Criminal Enterprises

The late medieval period saw the emergence of a particularly striking form of organized predation in the mercenary companies that operated across Italy and other parts of Europe, most notably in the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries. These companies, known in Italy as the condotte system, were organized bodies of professional soldiers who contracted their services to the highest bidder, fighting for whichever political authority or city-state was willing to pay their fees. When they were not under contract, many of them lived off the surrounding countryside, extracting food, money, and goods from civilian populations through the simple mechanism of the armed threat.

The condottieri represented a form of organized violence for profit that illustrates the extreme difficulty of distinguishing organized military enterprise from organized crime in the late medieval world. When a mercenary company was under legitimate contract, its violence was legal and its commanders were recognized as respected practitioners of a respected profession. When the same company was between contracts and living by extortion and pillage, it was criminal. The organizational structure, the personnel, the methods, and the capacity for violence were identical in both cases. Only the presence or absence of a contract with a recognized political authority defined the legal status of the activity.

Some of the most powerful condottieri companies developed organizational structures of considerable sophistication, including formal articles of association governing the distribution of profits and losses, dispute resolution mechanisms, hierarchies of command, and systems for managing relations with political authorities and civilian populations. These organizational innovations allowed large groups of professional fighters to operate as coherent enterprises across extended periods of time, adapting to changing political environments with a flexibility that smaller, less organized groups could not match. The organizational lessons of the condottiere system, including the importance of clear internal governance, reliable profit-sharing arrangements, and the management of relationships with political authorities, would be rediscovered by criminal organizations in very different contexts in later centuries.

Historical Debate

Was Feudal Protection a Legitimate Institution or a Systematic Racket?

The question: Scholars of medieval organized crime have debated whether feudal protection systems should be analyzed as legitimate, if exploitative, institutions of social order, or whether they are better understood as organized criminal enterprises that happened to hold political power. The debate has important implications for how we understand the relationship between crime and political authority across historical periods.

Interpretation A — The Legitimist Position: Some historians, drawing on the work of scholars such as Marc Bloch and Georges Duby, argue that feudal protection systems, whatever their abuses, were genuine responses to real security needs in a dangerous and fragmented political environment. In the absence of effective central authority, local lords provided the only available framework for maintaining order and protecting agricultural communities from the violence of raids, warfare, and banditry. The dues and services they extracted were the price of a genuine good, and calling them a protection racket imposes an anachronistic framework on an institution that served recognizable functions within the logic of its own time.
Interpretation B — The Critical Position: Other historians, influenced by the sociology of organized crime developed by scholars such as Charles Tilly, argue that feudal protection systems display all the structural characteristics of organized criminal rackets: the monopolization of violence in a territory, the extraction of payments under threat of harm, the selective application of protection to those who pay while permitting or inflicting harm on those who do not, and the self-perpetuation of the system by ensuring that the threat requiring protection never disappears. Tilly's famous formulation, that "war-making and state-making" are indistinguishable from organized crime except by their eventual political success, has been enormously influential in the study of medieval political economy.

Evidence: Both interpretations draw on the same body of evidence. Medieval chronicles and court records document genuine security functions performed by feudal lords, including military defense of communities, judicial resolution of disputes, and the punishment of bandits. They also document systematic abuse, including lords who created the threats they then charged to suppress, charged for protection that was never provided, and used their capacity for violence to extract resources far beyond any genuine security cost. The evidence is mixed because the reality was mixed: feudal protection was simultaneously a genuine institutional response to genuine needs and a system of organized exploitation.

Why it matters: The debate about feudal protection is not merely academic. How we understand the relationship between political authority and organized criminal enterprise has important implications for understanding why states and criminal organizations so often accommodate each other rather than simply opposing each other. If criminal-style protection rackets are capable of generating genuine social order under some conditions, then the relationship between crime and governance is more complex than a simple narrative of illegality versus legitimacy allows.

Section 4.3

Pirates and Privateers

If the road was the most dangerous space for medieval merchants on land, the sea was equally, and in some periods more, dangerous on water. Medieval maritime crime, encompassing both the pure predation of piracy and the politically sanctioned but practically indistinguishable activity of privateering, was among the most economically significant forms of organized criminal enterprise in the period, and it was organized in ways that display remarkable sophistication and adaptability.

Piracy in the medieval sense was not simply random violence at sea. The most successful medieval pirate enterprises were carefully organized operations that required substantial capital investment, reliable intelligence, skilled personnel, established markets for stolen goods, and political connections that provided either immunity from prosecution or active support from onshore authorities. A pirate fleet capable of attacking well-defended merchant convoys needed to be coordinated, supplied, and financed in ways that depended on organizational structures extending well beyond the ships themselves and deep into the commercial and political life of the surrounding society.

Historical Vocabulary
Privateering

Privateering was the practice of licensing private ships to attack enemy vessels and cargoes during wartime, authorized by a government document called a letter of marque. In theory, the letter of marque drew a clear legal line between the privateer, who was permitted to prey only on designated enemy shipping, and the pirate, who preyed on anyone and everything. In practice, the distinction was constantly blurred, for several reasons.

First, letters of marque were sometimes poorly defined in their scope, allowing privateers to claim that virtually any foreign vessel was fair game. Second, privateer captains who discovered that neutral or friendly shipping was richer and less well-defended than enemy vessels had strong incentives to ignore the legal distinction. Third, political authorities who issued letters of marque were often unable or unwilling to supervise the activities of the privateers they licensed, particularly when those privateers operated far from home. Fourth, the capital investment required to outfit a privateering vessel was substantial, and investors expected returns that the strict terms of the letter of marque might not permit.

The result was that privateering existed on a continuum with piracy rather than being clearly separated from it. The same vessels, the same captains, and the same organizational networks frequently moved between privateer and pirate operations depending on the political circumstances of the moment. This institutional ambiguity was exploited by maritime criminal enterprises across the entire medieval and early modern period, and it illustrates one of the most fundamental dynamics in the history of organized crime: the strategic use of legal ambiguity to create protected space for criminal activity.

Mediterranean Piracy: An Organized Enterprise

The Mediterranean Sea in the medieval period was the most commercially vital and criminally dangerous body of water in the world. Its waters carried the trade that connected the luxury goods of Asia and the eastern Mediterranean to the markets of Western Europe, the grain surpluses of Sicily and North Africa to the hungry cities of the north, and the military and religious traffic of the Crusades in both directions. This density of commercial traffic, combined with the political fragmentation of the Mediterranean coastline into dozens of competing states, cities, and principalities, made it an ideal environment for organized maritime crime.

Medieval Mediterranean piracy was not conducted by isolated individuals acting alone. The most significant piratical enterprises operated out of established bases, typically coastal cities or islands that were beyond the effective reach of the dominant naval powers of the period, and maintained organizational structures that could support fleets of multiple vessels operating across extended periods of time. The base provided a market for captured goods and captives, a source of financing and provisioning, a safe harbor for repair and rest, and a political patron who benefited sufficiently from the pirates' operations to provide protection against suppression.

The ransom of captives was one of the most economically significant activities of medieval Mediterranean pirates. Captured merchants, pilgrims, sailors, and soldiers were not automatically killed or enslaved; they were held for ransom that their families, business partners, or political patrons were expected to pay. This created a complex market for human bodies that required organizational infrastructure on both sides: the pirates needed to assess the ransom value of their captives, communicate with their families or representatives, and enforce the terms of ransom agreements reliably enough that payment was seen as a worthwhile alternative to indefinite captivity. Religious orders, most notably the Mercedarians and the Trinitarians, specialized in raising ransom money for Christian captives held by Muslim pirates, while analogous institutions served Muslim captives held by Christian powers. The ransoming system was, in short, an organized market, with criminal enterprises on one side, charitable organizations on the other, and both operating within a framework of informal norms that governed the treatment of captives and the negotiation of their release.

The Vitaliensbrüder: Pirates as a Political Enterprise

The most extensively documented example of organized medieval piracy in the northern seas is the Vitaliensbrüder, or Victual Brothers, who operated in the Baltic Sea in the late fourteenth and early fifteenth centuries. Their story illustrates with particular clarity the degree to which medieval piracy could be simultaneously a criminal enterprise, a political tool, and a sophisticated commercial operation.

The Vitaliensbrüder were initially organized by the dukes of Mecklenburg, who used them as a naval auxiliary in a war against Denmark for control of Sweden. The brothers' original mission was the practical one of supplying the besieged city of Stockholm, hence their name, and their operations began with the legal sanction of their employers. But as the political situation changed and the dukes of Mecklenburg lost the war, the Vitaliensbrüder found themselves without a patron and without the legal protection that their letter of commission had provided. Rather than disbanding, they continued their operations independently, preying on merchant shipping in the Baltic regardless of nationality.

At their peak, the Vitaliensbrüder operated a fleet of dozens of vessels out of bases on the island of Gotland, which they captured and held for a period, as well as from various points along the German and Scandinavian coast. They maintained a formal organization with identified leaders, rules for the distribution of captured goods, and something approaching a political ideology: several of their leaders are reported to have declared that they "stole from the rich to give to the poor," an early iteration of a Robin Hood narrative that criminal organizations have used to justify their activities in many subsequent eras.

The Vitaliensbrüder were eventually suppressed by the Hanseatic League, whose member cities had suffered severe economic losses from the pirates' operations and who organized a sustained military campaign against them in the first decade of the fifteenth century. The suppression required a coordinated military and diplomatic effort that illustrated both the organizational capacity of the Hansa and the genuine threat that the Vitaliensbrüder had posed to Baltic commerce. Several of their leaders were captured and executed in Hamburg, and the iconography of their trials and executions was extensively documented and publicized, serving as a warning to others who might contemplate similar enterprises.

The Hanseatic League and the Institutional Response to Piracy

The development of the Hanseatic League as a major commercial and political force in the northern seas from the thirteenth century onward represents one of the most significant institutional responses to organized criminal predation in the medieval period. The Hansa was not simply a trade association in the modern sense. It was an organization capable of conducting diplomacy, organizing boycotts of hostile cities, maintaining military fleets, and negotiating with kings and emperors on terms approaching those of sovereign states. Its development was driven in significant part by the need to pool the resources of its member cities to address threats that no individual city could handle alone, including the threat posed by organized piracy.

The Hansa's response to the Vitaliensbrüder illustrates the organizational logic of this institutional response. Individual Hanseatic cities could not afford to maintain naval forces sufficient to suppress a pirate organization operating across the entire Baltic. But the combined resources of the League, which by the fourteenth century included dozens of cities across northern Germany and the Baltic coast, could finance and maintain a fleet of sufficient size to conduct sustained anti-piracy operations. The eventual suppression of the Vitaliensbrüder was not the result of a single battle but of a sustained campaign coordinated across multiple cities and years, requiring the kind of long-term organizational commitment that only a well-institutionalized collective entity could maintain.

The Hansa's story also illustrates the degree to which the institutional response to organized crime can reshape the institutions themselves. The League's need to organize anti-piracy operations contributed to the development of more sophisticated forms of inter-city governance, communication, and collective decision-making that went far beyond their original commercial functions. Criminal threat, in other words, was a significant driver of institutional innovation in the medieval world.

Section 4.4

Secret Societies

Among the most fascinating and historically significant forms of organized crime in the medieval world were the secret societies, organizations that operated outside official sight, maintained internal loyalty through secrecy and initiation, and pursued goals that ranged from mutual aid and political subversion to organized violence and criminal enterprise. Secret societies flourished across the medieval world because the conditions of the period, including weak enforcement of law, harsh punishments for dissent, the exclusion of many groups from official institutions, and the absence of any legitimate political channel for the grievances of subordinated populations, made secrecy a rational organizational strategy for a wide range of activities.

The distinction between a secret society organized for mutual aid, one organized for political subversion, and one organized for criminal enterprise was often unclear both to contemporaries and to historians. The same organization might serve all three functions simultaneously, providing its members with a network of economic support, a framework for political action, and a mechanism for criminal activity, depending on the circumstances and the needs of the moment. What united these organizations was not a single function but a common organizational principle: the maintenance of internal solidarity through secrecy, initiation, and the threat of punishment for betrayal.

The Assassins: Political Violence as Organized Enterprise

The most famous secret organization of the medieval world was the network of Nizari Ismaili Muslims known to medieval Europeans as the Assassins. Operating from mountain fortresses in Persia and Syria from the late eleventh to the mid-thirteenth centuries, the Assassins developed an organizational form that was simultaneously a religious movement, a political force, and a criminal enterprise specializing in targeted political violence.

The historical reality of the Assassins is considerably more complex than their legend, which was elaborated by hostile sources, both Christian and Sunni Muslim, into a lurid tale of drug-induced fanaticism and suicidal devotion. In reality, the Nizari Ismailis were a coherent religious and political community that used assassination as a defensive strategy to maintain their independence against larger and more powerful enemies. The targets of Nizari assassinations were carefully chosen political and military figures, and the assassinations were planned and executed with a sophistication that required intelligence gathering, careful preparation, and the management of operatives over extended periods of time.

What makes the Assassins relevant to the history of organized crime is not primarily their violence but their organizational form. They maintained a hierarchical structure based on ranks of initiation, in which higher ranks had access to increasingly esoteric religious knowledge and more sensitive operational information. They maintained mountain fortresses that served simultaneously as administrative centers, military strongholds, and places of refuge for operatives who needed to disappear. They maintained networks of agents in the cities and courts of their enemies, agents who might spend years in place before being called upon to act. And they developed a reputation for reliability and effectiveness that was itself an organizational asset: the threat of Nizari assassination was, in many cases, as powerful a political tool as an actual killing, because it was credible.

The Assassins were suppressed in Persia by the Mongol invasions of the mid-thirteenth century and in Syria by the Egyptian Mamluk sultan Baybars shortly thereafter. But their organizational legacy was considerable. The hierarchical secret society organized around degrees of initiation, the placement of long-term agents in enemy institutions, the use of targeted violence as a political tool, and the cultivation of a fearsome reputation as an organizational asset are all elements that appear, in various combinations, in the criminal and political organizations of subsequent centuries.

Chinese Secret Societies

The medieval period also saw the development of secret societies in China that would eventually evolve into some of the most significant organized criminal organizations in East Asian history. The antecedents of the Triads and related organizations that would emerge in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries lay in a tradition of secret fraternities that developed in China from at least the Han dynasty period but took on particular importance during the political upheavals of the Tang and Song dynasties and the Mongol conquest of the thirteenth century.

Chinese secret societies of the medieval period typically combined mutual aid functions, providing economic support and solidarity among members in difficult times, with religious and ideological dimensions, drawing on a mix of Buddhist, Daoist, and folk religious traditions that gave the organizations a distinctive spiritual character. They also frequently involved themselves in political resistance, particularly during periods of foreign domination or oppressive government, and some of them developed criminal functions as a natural extension of their capacity for organized collective action in the spaces outside official control.

The organizational structure of Chinese secret societies was distinctive and influential. They used elaborate initiation ceremonies, symbolic language drawn from religious traditions, hierarchical ranks of membership, and strong internal norms of secrecy and mutual loyalty to maintain cohesion across large memberships and extended geographic areas. These organizational features, developed in response to the specific conditions of Chinese political history, would be preserved and elaborated by successor organizations down to the present day, making the medieval Chinese secret society one of the most historically significant organizational forms in the entire history of criminal enterprise.

European Heretical Movements and the Criminal Society

In medieval Europe, the most significant secret organizations were not primarily criminal in their intent but were forced into criminal-style organizational forms by the persecution they faced from Church and secular authorities. Heretical movements, including the Cathars in southern France and northern Italy, the Waldensians in the Alpine regions, and later the Hussite movement in Bohemia, developed organizational structures that resembled those of criminal secret societies precisely because they operated in conditions of severe repression. They maintained networks of safe houses, developed coded communication systems, organized systematic deception of official inquiries, and cultivated the loyalty of members through shared sacrifice and mutual dependence.

The organizational overlap between heretical and criminal secret societies was recognized by contemporaries, who accused heretical groups of every variety of criminal activity as well as religious deviance. While these accusations were largely propaganda, they reflected a genuine organizational reality: a network capable of maintaining secrecy against Inquisitorial investigation and organizing the movement of members and materials across large distances was a network with capabilities that could be applied to criminal purposes as readily as to religious ones. Some heretical communities did develop genuine criminal dimensions, particularly in the areas of contraband trade and the forgery of documents, as means of financing their operations and protecting their members.

Section 4.5

Criminal Brotherhoods

The most direct predecessors of modern organized criminal organizations in the medieval world were the criminal brotherhoods that developed in the cities and along the roads of late medieval Europe from roughly the thirteenth century onward. These organizations, known by a variety of names and documented in a variety of sources including court records, municipal ordinances, literary texts, and the remarkable genre of criminal argot manuals that emerged in the fifteenth century, represent the most self-consciously organized form of criminal enterprise in the medieval world and the one most directly connected to the criminal organizations of subsequent centuries.

The criminal brotherhoods of late medieval Europe shared a set of organizational features that distinguished them from informal criminal networks and individual criminals operating alone. They maintained internal hierarchies with defined ranks and roles. They developed territorial agreements that assigned particular cities, roads, or market areas to particular organizations or individuals. They created coded languages, known as argots or cant, that allowed members to communicate without being understood by outsiders. They enforced internal discipline through the threat of punishment for violations of organizational rules. And they developed what might be called a criminal culture, a set of shared values, practices, and identities that distinguished members of the brotherhood from the legitimate society around them and from the unorganized criminal underclass below them.

Historical Vocabulary
Guild

A guild was an association of craftsmen or merchants in a medieval city, organized to regulate standards of production, control entry into the trade, represent the collective interests of members before civic authorities, and provide mutual aid to members in difficulty. Guilds were among the most important economic and social institutions of the medieval urban world, combining commercial, regulatory, social, and political functions in a single organization.

The criminal "guilds" or brotherhoods of the late medieval period drew on the organizational model of the legitimate guild while inverting its legal and social character. Like legitimate guilds, they maintained membership rolls, enforced standards of practice, assigned territories, resolved internal disputes, provided mutual aid to members, and represented the interests of their members in relation to external authorities. Unlike legitimate guilds, their standards of practice were standards of criminal technique, their territorial assignments were divisions of criminal markets, and their relations with external authorities were primarily relations of corruption and evasion.

The parallel between legitimate guild organization and criminal brotherhood organization is significant not merely as an intellectual curiosity. It illustrates a general principle that recurs throughout the history of organized crime: criminal organizations tend to adopt the organizational forms available in the surrounding society, adapting them to the specific requirements of criminal enterprise. In a world where the guild was the dominant form of economic organization, criminal organizations took on guild-like structures. In later periods, as corporate and bureaucratic organizational forms became dominant, criminal organizations would take on increasingly corporate and bureaucratic characteristics.

The Liber Vagatorum and the Criminal Underworld

The single most important documentary source for the organization of late medieval criminal brotherhoods in Europe is the genre of texts known collectively as the Liber Vagatorum tradition, a set of pamphlets and books, produced primarily in Germany in the late fifteenth and early sixteenth centuries, that claimed to document the organization, membership, and practices of the criminal underworld with a specificity and detail that has fascinated and puzzled historians ever since.

The texts in this tradition describe a criminal world of considerable organizational sophistication. They enumerate dozens of recognized types of professional criminals, each with a defined specialization, a specific role in the criminal economy, and a designated place in an informal hierarchy of status and skill. Confidence artists who impersonated pilgrims or injured veterans, pickpockets who worked specific types of crowds, burglars who specialized in particular types of buildings, horse thieves who operated along specific trade routes, and a dozen other specialties are documented with a precision that suggests either direct knowledge of the criminal world or very detailed observation of it.

The texts also describe the argot, or cant, of the criminal underworld, providing glossaries of criminal vocabulary that allow legitimate readers to recognize criminal communication when they encounter it. The existence of a specialized criminal vocabulary is itself significant as an organizational indicator: a cant develops when a group of people needs to communicate about specific subjects without being understood by outsiders, and its development presupposes a degree of solidarity, regular communication, and shared identity among its users that is inconsistent with purely individual or unorganized criminal activity.

Historians have debated the accuracy and reliability of the Liber Vagatorum texts. Some have argued that they are primarily literary constructions, drawing on classical models of social satire and moral condemnation rather than direct observation of actual criminal practice. Others have pointed to the consistency of the organizational details across multiple independently produced texts, the correlation of specific details with court records and municipal ordinances, and the social and economic plausibility of the organizational structures described as evidence that the texts reflect, however imperfectly, genuine features of the late medieval criminal world. The scholarly consensus, insofar as one exists, is that the texts are not simple fictions but contain genuine ethnographic content alongside literary elaboration, making them valuable, if complicated, sources for the history of medieval criminal organization.

Territorial Organization and Criminal Governance

One of the most significant features of the criminal brotherhoods documented in the Liber Vagatorum tradition and in surviving court records is their development of territorial organization. The division of urban criminal markets into recognized territories, with defined rights of operation assigned to particular individuals or groups and informal mechanisms for resolving boundary disputes, is one of the most characteristic features of organized criminal enterprise across a wide variety of historical periods and cultural contexts, and its appearance in the late medieval European criminal world is among the clearest indicators of genuine organizational development.

Territorial organization serves several functions in a criminal context. It reduces internal competition within the criminal organization, preventing the destructive violence that erupts when multiple actors compete for the same market without agreed rules of engagement. It provides a framework for the distribution of criminal income, assigning the proceeds of particular activities to particular actors in a way that all can understand and that a central authority, or the collective agreement of the brotherhood, can enforce. It creates accountability for the maintenance of criminal discipline in a particular area, since the individual or group assigned a territory has an interest in preventing the kind of disruptive criminal behavior that would draw law enforcement attention to their patch. And it provides a basis for negotiation with competing criminal organizations, since territorial boundaries can be disputed, adjusted, and renegotiated without requiring a recourse to violence.

The evidence for territorial organization in late medieval criminal brotherhoods comes primarily from court records in which criminals describe the arrangements they operated under, from municipal ordinances that reference the existence of recognized criminal territories, and from the Liber Vagatorum texts, which describe the assignment of specific types of operations to specific types of operators in specific kinds of locations. This evidence is fragmentary and its interpretation requires caution, but its consistency across multiple types of sources in multiple countries is sufficiently striking to support the conclusion that territorial organization was a genuine feature of late medieval criminal enterprise rather than a later projection of modern analytical frameworks onto the medieval past.

Corruption and Political Connections

The criminal brotherhoods of the late medieval period did not operate in complete isolation from the legitimate institutions of their societies. Like criminal organizations in every subsequent period, they maintained connections to political authorities, law enforcement officials, and civic institutions that ranged from systematic bribery to more complex relationships of mutual accommodation and even genuine cooperation.

Municipal law enforcement in late medieval European cities was typically inadequate, understaffed, poorly paid, and easily corrupted. Night watchmen who were bribed to ignore criminal activity in particular streets or neighborhoods, market officials who turned a blind eye to the operations of fences dealing in stolen goods, jailers who allowed privileged prisoners to conduct criminal business from their cells, and magistrates who accepted payments to lose evidence or reduce charges are all documented in the court records of late medieval cities across Europe. These corrupt relationships were not merely incidental to the operation of criminal brotherhoods; they were an essential part of the organizational infrastructure that made sustained criminal enterprise possible.

At higher levels of the political hierarchy, connections between criminal organizations and political elites were more complex and less well documented but were nonetheless real. The most powerful criminal operators in late medieval cities frequently had connections to established noble or mercantile families, either as clients, as suppliers of illegal goods and services, or as instruments of political intimidation. The use of criminal force to settle commercial and political disputes, to intimidate witnesses, to suppress competition, and to enforce informal agreements was a recognized feature of the late medieval urban world that blurred the line between legitimate authority and criminal power in ways that contemporary legal and political theory was ill-equipped to address.

The Hanseatic League's Complaint Against Pirates, 1394

Creator: Representatives of the Hanseatic League · Date: 1394 · Type: Diplomatic communication and formal complaint

Context: In 1394, representatives of the Hanseatic League compiled and circulated a formal account of losses suffered by member merchants at the hands of the Vitaliensbrüder and associated pirate organizations operating in the Baltic Sea. The document was addressed to the kings and princes of the northern European states and was intended both to document the scale of losses and to pressure political authorities into taking action against the pirates. It is one of the most detailed surviving sources for the organization and impact of late medieval Baltic piracy.

The Hanseatic complaint described losses that were systematic and organized rather than random. Merchants reported that pirate vessels had advance knowledge of particular shipments, suggesting intelligence networks operating within Hanseatic ports. The document described organized markets at pirate bases where stolen goods were sold openly, with buyers arriving from across the Baltic knowing they could purchase discounted merchandise. It noted that the pirates distinguished between vessels from cities that had paid ransom or reached accommodation with the brotherhood and those that had not, suggesting an organized system of protected and unprotected trade.

Analysis: The Hanseatic complaint is a rich source for the organizational structure of medieval piracy because it was produced by sophisticated commercial operators who had direct knowledge of the losses they suffered and strong incentives to document them accurately for diplomatic purposes. The details it contains about intelligence networks, organized markets for stolen goods, and the distinction between protected and unprotected merchants suggest a level of organizational sophistication in the Vitaliensbrüder that goes well beyond simple maritime robbery. The document should be read with awareness, however, that diplomatic complaints often exaggerate losses and attribute greater organization to opponents than they actually possessed, since a more organized and threatening enemy provides stronger justification for demanding political action.

Multiple Perspectives

The Late Medieval Criminal Economy: Who Was Affected and How

From the perspective of the merchant: For the medieval long-distance merchant, organized criminal predation was not an occasional risk but a permanent cost of doing business, factored into prices, routes, and organizational decisions as a matter of routine. The merchant who traded between Italy and Flanders in the fourteenth century expected to pay tolls of varying degrees of legitimacy, to need armed escorts, to pay protection money at certain points along the route, and to lose a calculable percentage of shipments to robbery or piracy. Criminal predation was a transaction cost, and the institutional responses to it, guild membership, convoy arrangements, insurance contracts, and political agreements, were rational business responses to that cost.
From the perspective of the professional criminal: For the members of the criminal brotherhoods of the late medieval period, participation in organized crime was an economic strategy in a world that offered limited legitimate alternatives to the poor, the uprooted, and the socially marginal. The criminal underworld as documented in the Liber Vagatorum tradition was populated not by people who had freely chosen criminality from among a range of options but by people whose options were severely constrained by the economic and social structures of medieval society: landless laborers displaced by agricultural changes, wounded soldiers who could no longer fight, orphans without family networks, immigrants without guild membership or civic rights, and members of stigmatized religious or ethnic minorities excluded from legitimate economic participation. The brotherhood offered these people economic survival, social solidarity, and a measure of protection in a dangerous world, at the cost of participation in activities that the dominant society defined as criminal.
From the perspective of the political authority: For the kings, lords, and urban magistrates who were nominally responsible for maintaining order, organized crime presented a complex mix of threats and opportunities. The threat was real: criminal predation disrupted commerce, reduced the tax revenues that depended on commercial activity, challenged the sovereign's claim to monopolize legitimate violence, and occasionally grew powerful enough to represent a genuine political threat. But the opportunity was also real: criminal organizations were sources of intelligence about the political activities of opponents, could be hired as instruments of violence against enemies, could be taxed or licensed at a profit, and could be suppressed with public fanfare when suppression served political purposes. The relationship between political authority and organized crime in the medieval world was never simply one of predator and prey, but a complex negotiation in which both parties had assets the other wanted.
From the perspective of the ordinary urban poor: For the majority of the urban population who were neither wealthy merchants nor professional criminals, the criminal economy was a pervasive and morally complicated feature of daily life. The fence who bought stolen goods from the brotherhood might be the same neighborhood trader who provided essential credit to poor families in times of difficulty. The criminal protection provided to a particular street might be the only effective protection available, given the inadequacy of official law enforcement. The brotherhood's mutual aid functions might be indistinguishable, from the perspective of the recipient, from those of a legitimate charitable organization. The criminal economy was not external to the life of the medieval urban poor but woven into it, providing goods, services, employment, and social solidarity that the legitimate economy and the legitimate state failed to provide.
Cause and Effect

The Rise and Organization of Medieval Mediterranean Piracy

Long-Term Causes

Political fragmentation of the Mediterranean coastline into dozens of competing states and cities; absence of a dominant naval power capable of policing the entire sea; the structural incentive created by dense commercial traffic carrying high-value, easily portable goods; the availability of island and coastal refuges beyond the reach of dominant powers; the precedent and tradition of Mediterranean maritime predation stretching back to antiquity.

Short-Term Causes

The economic disruption of the Crusades, which simultaneously increased commercial traffic and created new political instabilities; the collapse of specific naval powers (Byzantine, Norman, or Venetian) that had exercised regional maritime order; the availability of experienced sailors and soldiers following the end of specific military campaigns; the development of faster, more maneuverable vessel types that gave pirates an advantage over merchant shipping.

Organizational Response

Development of convoy systems among merchant fleets; construction of larger, better-armed merchant vessels; establishment of commercial insurance arrangements to spread the risk of piracy losses; diplomatic agreements between rival Christian and Muslim powers for the exchange of captives and the regulation of ransom; eventually, the development of naval forces specifically designed to suppress piracy, funded by merchant associations or sovereign powers whose commercial interests were threatened.

Long-Term Effects

The institutional development of maritime commerce organizations, including merchant guilds, trading companies, and eventually the great chartered companies of the early modern period; the elaboration of international maritime law, including the legal distinction between piracy and privateering; the development of specialized financial instruments, including maritime insurance, that were created partly to manage the risk of criminal predation; the integration of piracy into the political calculations of Mediterranean states as a tool of economic warfare.

Historical complexity: Medieval Mediterranean piracy cannot be understood as simply a criminal problem requiring a law enforcement solution. It was embedded in the political economy of the Mediterranean world, connected to the commercial interests of states, and addressed through a combination of military suppression, institutional adaptation, and political negotiation that shaped the development of legitimate maritime commerce and international law over centuries.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Organized Crime from the Ancient World to the Late Medieval Period

What Changed

  • The scale and geographic reach of organized criminal enterprise expanded dramatically, from primarily local and regional operations in the ancient world to genuinely international networks connecting criminals, markets, and political authorities across the entire Eurasian landmass
  • The organizational sophistication of criminal enterprises increased, with the development of formal internal hierarchies, territorial agreements, coded languages, and disciplinary mechanisms that had no clear ancient analogues
  • The legal and institutional framework for addressing criminal enterprise became more elaborated, with the development of new legal categories, new forms of collective merchant organization, and new political instruments for suppressing criminal predation
  • The relationship between criminal enterprise and formal political authority became more complex and institutionally embedded, with corruption playing a more systematic role in connecting criminals to the centers of political power
  • New criminal markets developed in response to new economic conditions, including markets for ransomed captives, for stolen luxury goods, and for the services of specialized professional criminals

What Stayed the Same

  • The fundamental economic logic of organized crime remained constant: criminal enterprises develop wherever there is demand for goods or services that the legal market cannot or will not supply, and wherever the profit from supplying that demand exceeds the expected cost of the criminal penalties for doing so
  • The structural connection between criminal enterprise and political authority, including both the corruption of officials and the strategic use of criminal force by political actors, remained a constant feature of organized crime across the entire period
  • The social profile of criminal organizations, drawing their membership disproportionately from the economically marginal, the socially excluded, and those whose legitimate options were constrained by structural disadvantage, remained consistent
  • The tendency of criminal organizations to mimic the organizational forms of the legitimate institutions surrounding them remained constant, with criminal organizations across the period adapting the structures of guilds, military orders, and other contemporary institutions to criminal purposes
  • The protection racket, the extraction of payment under threat for immunity from harm, remained the foundational economic activity of the most durable criminal enterprises

Why the pattern matters: The persistence of certain organizational forms and economic logics across the enormous diversity of political and cultural settings examined in this chapter and the previous one suggests that these are not cultural accidents or products of specific historical circumstances but structural features of criminal organization that emerge whenever and wherever the relevant conditions obtain. Understanding these structural constants is essential to understanding not only the history of organized crime but its present forms and likely future development.

Everyday Life and Social History

Living in a Medieval Criminal Economy: The Experience of the Urban Poor

For the overwhelming majority of people who lived in medieval European cities, the criminal economy was not an exotic or distant phenomenon but a feature of daily life as familiar as the parish church or the market square. To understand medieval organized crime not just as an institutional phenomenon but as a human experience, it is worth considering what participation in, or exposure to, the criminal economy might have looked like from the perspective of a craftsman's widow in fourteenth-century Cologne or a journeyman weaver in Florence.

Such a person would have known, without necessarily thinking of the knowledge as remarkable, which neighborhoods in their city were under the effective governance of criminal organizations rather than civic authorities. They would have known which market stalls sold goods of uncertain provenance at prices below what the legal market could offer, and they would have patronized those stalls when necessity required. They would have known which moneylenders, technically in violation of the Church's usury prohibitions, could be approached for emergency credit when the legitimate options, including the pawnbroker and the charitable institutional lender, were exhausted or unavailable. They might have known individuals who made part or all of their living through criminal activity, not as glamorous outlaws but as neighbors and community members whose economic strategies differed in kind but not always greatly in degree from the gray-market activities that many honest people engaged in.

The criminal brotherhood, as it operated in the late medieval city, provided services that the legitimate society often could not. The mutual aid network of a criminal organization could provide funeral expenses for a member's family, emergency food in times of shortage, medical care through informal practitioners, and the kind of small-scale credit that formal institutions did not offer to the poor. These functions were not incidental to the organization's criminal activities; they were part of what maintained the loyalty of members and generated the cooperation of the surrounding community that sustained the organization's operations.

Violence was also a persistent feature of life in the zones of the city where criminal organizations operated. The protection provided by such organizations was real: areas under the effective control of an organized criminal enterprise were often safer from random predation than those in which no such organization operated, precisely because the organization had an interest in maintaining order within its territory and discouraging competing criminal activity that might draw law enforcement attention. But the cost of this protection, which included not only financial payments but deference to the organization's authority, acceptance of its decisions about who could conduct what business in the neighborhood, and the occasional performance of services for the organization when called upon, was significant, and those who refused to pay it were vulnerable to a violence against which the civic authorities offered little realistic protection.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has examined organized crime in the medieval world across five principal forms: merchant extortion, feudal protection systems, maritime crime, secret societies, and criminal brotherhoods. Each of these forms expressed the particular organizational possibilities and constraints of the medieval political economy, and together they constitute a rich and analytically revealing chapter in the global history of criminal enterprise.

The most fundamental insight of this chapter is that organized crime in the medieval world cannot be cleanly separated from the legitimate political and economic institutions of the period. The feudal protection system that underpinned medieval political order had a structural logic essentially identical to that of the protection racket. The toll roads that lords maintained across Europe's river valleys were, from the perspective of the merchants who paid, often indistinguishable from criminal extortion. The condottiere companies that provided military services to Italian city-states moved seamlessly between legitimate military employment and organized criminal predation depending on the political circumstances of the moment. And the guild structures that organized legitimate economic life in medieval cities provided the organizational template that criminal brotherhoods adapted to their own purposes.

The growth of long-distance trade from the tenth century onward was the single most important driver of criminal organizational development in the medieval period. As commercial wealth became more abundant and more portable, the incentives for organized criminal predation increased proportionally. The merchant convoys, guild organizations, and political confederacies that developed in response to criminal threats were themselves organizational innovations driven partly by the need to address organized crime collectively rather than individually. The Hanseatic League, one of the most significant commercial organizations of the medieval world, cannot be fully understood without understanding the organized piracy that it developed partly to combat.

Medieval piracy, examined through the lens of the Vitaliensbrüder in the Baltic and the various corsair enterprises of the Mediterranean, was not random maritime violence but carefully organized commercial enterprise that required capital investment, intelligence gathering, markets for stolen goods, political connections, and organizational structures capable of sustaining operations across extended periods of time and large geographic areas. The relationship between piracy and privateering, and between both and the legitimate political and commercial interests of medieval states and cities, illustrates the perpetual difficulty of drawing a clear line between criminal enterprise and the legitimate use of organized violence for economic gain.

The secret societies of the medieval world, from the Assassins of Persia and Syria to the criminal brotherhoods of late medieval European cities, developed organizational forms whose core features would be recognizable in criminal organizations of subsequent centuries: hierarchical initiation, coded communication, territorial organization, internal discipline, and the cultivation of relations with legitimate political authorities that mixed corruption with more complex forms of accommodation. The Liber Vagatorum tradition provides the most direct documentary evidence for these organizational features in a European context, offering a window into a criminal underworld of considerable sophistication whose organizational logic anticipates in striking ways the organized crime of later periods.

What persisted across the medieval period, and what this chapter has argued connects medieval organized crime to its ancient predecessors and its modern successors, was a set of structural constants: the protection racket as a foundational economic activity, the corruption of officials as a normal operational cost, the adaptation of legitimate organizational forms to criminal purposes, the disproportionate recruitment of the economically marginal and socially excluded, and the emergence of criminal enterprise wherever legal institutions failed to supply genuine demand for goods or services. These structural constants are not medieval phenomena. They are features of organized criminal enterprise wherever and whenever it appears, and they will recur in virtually every chapter of this textbook.

The medieval period also established something that the ancient world had not clearly developed: the recognizable organizational form of the criminal brotherhood, an association of professional criminals maintaining formal membership, internal hierarchy, territorial agreements, coded language, and mutual aid functions. This organizational form, however embryonic, is the direct precursor of the criminal organizations examined in subsequent chapters, and its emergence in the late medieval period marks an important threshold in the history of organized crime. The Mafia, the Camorra, the Yakuza, and the criminal organizations of the modern world did not appear from nowhere. They have deep roots in the organizational traditions examined in this chapter, and understanding those roots is essential to understanding the organizations themselves.

Finally, this chapter has insisted on seeing medieval organized crime as a human phenomenon, not merely an institutional one. The criminal brotherhoods and piracy networks documented here were composed of people with their own economic pressures, social relationships, cultural identities, and moral frameworks. Many of them were not career criminals by choice but by circumstance, driven to criminal enterprise by the exclusions and constraints of a social order that offered limited legitimate alternatives to those without land, guild membership, or powerful family connections. The persistence of organized crime across the medieval period was not simply a failure of law enforcement; it was a reflection of structural features of medieval society, including extreme economic inequality, weak institutional protection for the most vulnerable, and the systematic exclusion of large populations from legitimate economic and political participation, that generated and sustained criminal enterprise as a rational economic strategy for those with few alternatives.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Argot
A specialized vocabulary or coded language developed by a particular social group, often for the purpose of communicating without being understood by outsiders. The argots documented in the Liber Vagatorum tradition are among the most important pieces of evidence for the organizational sophistication of late medieval criminal brotherhoods.
Banditry
Organized robbery conducted by bands of criminals operating in the spaces beyond effective governmental control, typically in rural or semi-rural areas. Successful banditry in the medieval world required organizational infrastructure, including intelligence networks, safe houses, and markets for stolen goods, that distinguished it from opportunistic robbery.
Black market
A market for goods or services that are illegal, prohibited, or heavily taxed, operating outside the official economy. Medieval black markets, including those for interest-bearing credit and for prohibited or stolen goods, were serviced by organized criminal enterprises that supplied demand the legal market could not fill.
Condottieri
Mercenary military commanders and companies operating in late medieval Italy and elsewhere, who contracted their services to city-states and princes and who, when not under contract, often maintained themselves through extortion and pillage. The condottieri are among the most striking examples in medieval history of organized violence operating on the boundary between legitimate military enterprise and criminal predation.
Contraband
Goods that are prohibited by law from being imported, exported, produced, or sold. In the medieval context, contraband included goods on which customs duties had not been paid, goods whose trade was monopolized by political authorities, and goods whose possession was prohibited on religious grounds.
Corruption
The private exploitation of public or institutional position for personal gain. Corruption in the medieval world connected organized criminal enterprise to the formal institutions of political and religious authority, making it one of the most structurally important features of the medieval criminal landscape.
Corsair
A term used in the medieval Mediterranean for a pirate or privateer, particularly one operating with some degree of political sanction from a coastal state. The corsairs of the Barbary Coast in North Africa became among the most organized and politically significant maritime criminal enterprises of the late medieval and early modern periods.
Criminal brotherhood
An organized association of professional criminals maintaining formal membership, internal hierarchy, territorial agreements, coded language, and mutual aid functions. The criminal brotherhoods documented in the Liber Vagatorum tradition represent the most developed form of organized criminal enterprise in the late medieval world.
Extortion
The obtaining of money or other goods through coercion, particularly through the threat of harm. The protection racket, in which payment purchases immunity from harm the extortionist would otherwise inflict, is the most organizational form of extortion and one of the foundational economic activities of organized criminal enterprise across historical periods.
Feudalism
The system of political and economic organization dominant in much of medieval Europe, based on hierarchical relationships of protection and service between lords and vassals. The structural similarity between feudal protection and criminal protection rackets is one of the central analytical insights of the history of medieval organized crime.
Fence
A person who buys and resells stolen goods. Fences were essential intermediaries in the medieval criminal economy, providing the market for stolen goods that made large-scale organized theft economically viable and connecting criminal enterprises to the legitimate commercial economy.
Guild
An association of craftsmen or merchants in a medieval city, organized to regulate standards, control entry, and provide mutual aid. Criminal brotherhoods adopted the organizational model of the legitimate guild, and the parallel between guild and criminal brotherhood is one of the clearest examples of the tendency of criminal organizations to mimic the organizational forms of legitimate institutions.
Hanseatic League
A confederation of merchant guilds and market towns centered on northern Germany and the Baltic, operating as a major commercial and political force from the thirteenth through the seventeenth centuries. The League's development was shaped partly by the need to address piracy and extortion collectively, and its organizational evolution illustrates how criminal threats can drive institutional innovation.
Letter of marque
A government document authorizing a private ship to attack enemy vessels and cargoes during wartime. The letter of marque established the legal distinction between privateer and pirate, a distinction that was constantly blurred in practice and that provided organized maritime criminal enterprises with a mechanism for strategic legal ambiguity.
Liber Vagatorum
A genre of texts produced in Germany in the late fifteenth and early sixteenth centuries that documented the organization, membership, and practices of the criminal underworld, including criminal argots, specialized types of criminals, and the internal structures of criminal brotherhoods. These texts are among the most important sources for the history of medieval organized crime.
Patronage
A relationship in which a more powerful actor provides protection, resources, and access to institutional power in exchange for loyalty, service, and deference from a less powerful client. Patronage networks connected criminal organizations to political authorities across the medieval world, providing criminal enterprises with political protection in exchange for services or payments.
Piracy
The act of robbery or violence at sea conducted without political authorization. Medieval piracy was an organized enterprise requiring capital investment, organizational structure, intelligence gathering, markets for stolen goods, and political connections, distinguishing it from random maritime violence.
Privateering
Maritime predation authorized by a letter of marque from a political authority, permitting attacks on enemy vessels during wartime. The distinction between privateer and pirate was constantly contested and frequently ignored by the parties involved, making privateering a mechanism through which politically connected criminal maritime enterprise could claim legal legitimacy.
Protection racket
A criminal enterprise in which payments are extracted from victims under threat of harm, with the extortionist offering immunity from harm they would otherwise inflict or permit. The protection racket is identified by scholars of organized crime as one of the most fundamental and historically persistent forms of criminal enterprise, with structural parallels in the feudal protection systems of the medieval world.
Ransom
Payment demanded for the release of a captive. The systematic ransoming of captives taken in piracy and warfare was one of the most economically significant criminal activities of the medieval Mediterranean world, creating organized markets for human bodies and institutional responses, including religious orders specializing in ransom payment, that shaped the commercial and diplomatic history of the period.
Secret society
An organization that maintains secrecy about its membership, rituals, or activities, typically organized around hierarchical initiation and bound by strong norms of internal loyalty. Secret societies served diverse functions in the medieval world, including mutual aid, political subversion, and criminal enterprise, and developed organizational forms that influenced subsequent criminal organizations.
Simony
The buying and selling of church offices, sacraments, or spiritual goods, condemned as a sin by the medieval Church but widely practiced. Simony represents one of the most significant forms of institutional corruption in medieval society, connecting the criminal economy to the central institutions of religious authority.
Tribute
Regular payments extracted by a dominant power from a subordinate one, often as a condition of peace or security. The distinction between tribute and taxation was contested in the medieval world, and tribute arrangements frequently had the structural characteristics of protection rackets.
Usury
In medieval usage, the lending of money at any interest, prohibited by the Catholic Church. The prohibition on usury created one of the most economically significant black markets of the medieval period, since the demand for interest-bearing credit was real and growing while the most direct means of satisfying it was officially prohibited.
Vassal
A person who owed feudal obligations to a lord in exchange for land and protection. The vassal relationship illustrates the contractual structure of feudal protection and the way in which legitimate political relationships in the medieval world incorporated elements structurally identical to criminal protection rackets.
Vitaliensbrüder
A pirate confederation operating in the Baltic Sea in the late fourteenth and early fifteenth centuries, initially hired by German lords but quickly developing into an independent criminal enterprise that preyed on merchant shipping regardless of nationality. The Vitaliensbrüder are among the most extensively documented examples of organized medieval piracy.

Major People and Places

Identification & Significance

The Hanseatic League (Hansa), c. 1241–1669
A confederation of merchant guilds and market towns across northern Germany and the Baltic, whose development was shaped in significant part by the need to address piracy and extortion collectively. The Hansa is both a major institutional response to organized crime and an important source for understanding the organizational forms of medieval criminal enterprise, since its records document extensively the threats it faced and the measures it took to address them.
The Vitaliensbrüder (Victual Brothers), c. 1392–1400s
A Baltic pirate confederation that began as a politically sanctioned naval auxiliary but developed into an independent criminal enterprise operating across the entire northern sea. Their story illustrates the ease with which legitimate violence could transition into organized criminal predation in the medieval world, and their organizational features, including intelligence networks, territorial markets, and protection arrangements, make them one of the most instructive examples of medieval organized crime.
The Assassins (Nizari Ismailis), c. 1090–1273
A Shia Muslim sect operating from mountain fortresses in Persia and Syria that combined religious organization with intelligence gathering and targeted political violence. Their hierarchical secret organization, their use of long-term undercover operatives, and their cultivation of a fearsome reputation as an organizational asset make them historically significant as an early example of an elaborate criminal-political secret society.
Roger de Flor (c. 1267–1305)
A Templar knight turned mercenary commander who led the Catalan Company through the eastern Mediterranean in the early fourteenth century, illustrating the thin line between legitimate military enterprise and organized criminal predation in the late medieval world.
The Mediterranean Sea
The central arena for medieval long-distance trade and maritime crime, where Christian, Muslim, and Jewish merchants, pirates, and political authorities competed, collaborated, and preyed upon one another across a body of water that was simultaneously the most commercially vital and the most criminally dangerous space in the medieval world.
The Baltic Sea
The northern maritime arena in which German, Scandinavian, Polish, and Russian commercial and criminal interests competed, generating the piracy networks, especially the Vitaliensbrüder, that represented medieval Baltic organized maritime crime at its most developed.
The Barbary Coast
The North African coastal region that developed from the fourteenth century onward as a base for organized piracy targeting Christian Mediterranean shipping, with tacit or active support from local political authorities. The Barbary Coast represents the most politically institutionalized form of organized maritime crime in the late medieval world.
Gotland
A Baltic island that served as the primary base of the Vitaliensbrüder at the height of their power, illustrating the importance of island refuges beyond the effective reach of dominant naval powers for the organization of medieval maritime crime.
The Liber Vagatorum tradition
A genre of German pamphlets and books produced in the late fifteenth and early sixteenth centuries that documented the organization of the criminal underworld, including criminal argots, professional specializations, and internal hierarchies. These texts are the most important surviving documentary evidence for the organizational sophistication of late medieval criminal brotherhoods.
The Condottieri
Mercenary military companies operating in late medieval Italy that represent one of the most striking historical examples of organized violence for hire operating on the boundary between legitimate military service and criminal predation. The condottieri's organizational innovations, including formal articles of association and internal dispute resolution mechanisms, anticipated in important ways the organizational structures of later criminal enterprises.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter has argued that organized crime in the medieval world was not an aberration from the normal functioning of medieval society but a structural feature of it, arising from and responding to the same political and economic conditions that shaped the legitimate institutions of the period. The feudal protection system, the toll road, the condottiere company, the corsair enterprise, the secret brotherhood: all of these forms of organized criminal activity were deeply embedded in the political economy of the medieval world, connected to its legitimate institutions through corruption, patronage, and the structural similarity of their organizational logics, and sustained by the same conditions of political fragmentation, uneven enforcement, and enormous inequality that made medieval society what it was.

The chapter has also established a set of analytical tools that will be essential throughout this textbook. The protection racket, the criminal organization as an adapter of legitimate institutional forms, the black market as a response to the gap between legal prohibition and genuine demand, the corruption of officials as a normal operational cost of criminal enterprise, and the disproportionate recruitment of the socially marginal and economically excluded, these are not medieval peculiarities. They are structural constants of organized criminal enterprise that appear wherever the relevant conditions obtain, in the Renaissance city-states of the next chapter, in the American cities of the Prohibition era, in the drug-producing regions of contemporary Latin America, and in the digital networks of the modern cybercriminal. Understanding them in their medieval forms provides a baseline against which the organizational innovations and adaptations of later criminal enterprises can be measured and understood.

The story of organized crime in the medieval world is also a story about the limits of institutional response. The Hanseatic League's eventually successful campaign against the Vitaliensbrüder required decades of sustained effort, enormous financial investment, and a degree of inter-city cooperation that was genuinely difficult to maintain over extended periods of time. The Church's persistent campaigns against usury succeeded only in driving the credit market underground and into the hands of increasingly sophisticated operators who had strong incentives to develop organizational structures capable of surviving official suppression. The royal and imperial efforts to suppress banditry and extortion on the medieval roads were perpetually frustrated by the structural conditions that reproduced criminal enterprise as fast as specific organizations were dismantled. These patterns, the difficulty of sustained institutional response to criminal organizations, the tendency of suppression to drive criminal enterprise into more sophisticated organizational forms, and the reproduction of criminal enterprise from the structural conditions of the surrounding society, will recur in every subsequent chapter of this textbook and deserve to be recognized as central themes of the global history of organized crime.

Finally, this chapter has asked students to see medieval organized crime not only through the lens of institutional analysis but through the experience of the ordinary people who lived within and around the criminal economy. The craftsman's widow who knew which market stalls sold goods of uncertain provenance, the journeyman weaver who understood the informal territorial arrangements of his city's criminal organizations, the merchant who built the cost of criminal predation into his prices, the young man without land or guild membership who found in a criminal brotherhood the economic support and social solidarity that legitimate society denied him, these are the human realities behind the organizational analysis. History, including the history of organized crime, is ultimately about people making choices under constraints they did not choose, in circumstances they inherited rather than created, with consequences they could not always foresee. Keeping those human realities in view, even as we analyze the organizational structures and economic logics of criminal enterprise, is essential to the kind of historical understanding this textbook seeks to develop.

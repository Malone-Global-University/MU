Part IV — Organized Crime Comes to America
11

Prohibition and the Criminal Revolution

Organized Crime: A Global History

Chapter Opening

"Prohibition did not create organized crime in America, but it gave it an empire."

On the evening of January 16, 1920, a curious quiet fell over many of America's saloons, taverns, and hotel bars. By midnight, the Eighteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution would take legal effect, and the manufacture, sale, and transportation of intoxicating liquors would become a federal crime across the entire nation. In cities like Chicago, New York, Detroit, and New Orleans, bartenders poured last rounds, musicians played final sets, and customers drained their glasses with a combination of defiance and resignation. Some establishments closed their doors for good. Others, their owners suspected, would simply lock those doors and open them only to paying guests who knew the right password, knocked in the right pattern, or pressed the right buzzer hidden behind a false wall.

What followed was one of the most consequential unintended experiments in the history of criminal enterprise. The Volstead Act, the federal law enacted to enforce Prohibition, made the production and distribution of alcohol illegal almost overnight. It did not, however, make the desire for alcohol disappear. Tens of millions of Americans who had been accustomed to drinking beer with dinner, whiskey after work, or wine at religious ceremonies did not suddenly become abstainers. They became customers in an illegal market of staggering scale, and the men and women who served them accumulated power, wealth, and organizational sophistication at a pace that no previous generation of American criminals had approached.

The thirteen years of Prohibition, from January 1920 to December 1933, transformed the landscape of American organized crime. Before Prohibition, criminal enterprises in American cities had been largely local affairs: protection rackets operating in ethnic neighborhoods, gambling rings tied to political machines, vice operations confined to particular districts, and Black Hand extortionists preying on immigrant communities. These activities were profitable and sometimes violent, but they were also fragmented, geographically limited, and organizationally simple. The men who ran them rarely needed to coordinate across cities, negotiate complex supply chains, manage large workforces, or think strategically about market competition.

Prohibition changed all of that. The illegal alcohol trade required organization on a scale that street gangs and ward politicians could not provide alone. To supply a city of millions with bootleg beer, whiskey, gin, and wine required breweries hidden in warehouses, distilleries operating in rural farmhouses and urban basements, fleets of trucks navigating police roadblocks, networks of boats slipping across the Great Lakes and Atlantic coastline, and retail operations, the speakeasies, spread across every neighborhood in the city. It required accountants to manage revenues, lawyers to bribe officials and negotiate legal escapes, enforcers to discipline employees and intimidate rivals, and leaders capable of seeing the enterprise as a whole and managing it strategically.

The men who rose to meet this demand were not simply criminals who happened to be in the right place. Many were immigrants or the children of immigrants who had grown up in the poorest neighborhoods of American cities, where legitimate paths to wealth and status were narrow or entirely closed. They had learned, in the street gangs and ward politics of the pre-Prohibition era, how to navigate systems of violence, corruption, and informal power. When Prohibition suddenly made the production and sale of alcohol worth enormous sums, these men possessed exactly the skills and networks that the illegal market required. The result was an explosive expansion of criminal enterprise, accompanied by violence of a kind and scale that shocked even a society accustomed to urban disorder.

This chapter examines the criminal revolution that Prohibition produced. It traces the passage and enforcement of the Eighteenth Amendment, the economic logic that made bootlegging so enormously profitable, the organizational structures that criminal enterprises developed to supply and distribute illegal alcohol, the social world of the speakeasy and the ways Prohibition shaped popular culture, the geography of smuggling routes that stretched from Canadian border towns to Caribbean ports, and the extraordinary violence that accompanied the competition for control of this vast illegal market. It also examines what Prohibition reveals about the relationship between law, markets, and criminal enterprise, a relationship that would define organized crime's development for generations after the amendment's repeal in 1933.

The lessons of Prohibition extend far beyond the 1920s. Every subsequent debate about the criminalization of drugs, gambling, prostitution, and other goods and services that significant numbers of people desire has returned, implicitly or explicitly, to the Prohibition example. When a society decides to make a widely desired commodity illegal, it does not eliminate demand. It transfers the supply of that commodity to whoever is willing and able to operate outside the law, and it guarantees them profit margins that no legal business can match. Prohibition did not create the conditions for organized crime, but it gave American organized crime the economic foundation, the organizational experience, and the political connections that would sustain it for decades. Understanding how and why this happened is essential to understanding not only the history of crime in America, but the enduring relationship between prohibition, black markets, and the institutions of criminal power.

Historical Context

To understand Prohibition and the criminal revolution it produced, students need to understand both the world that existed before January 1920 and the forces that brought the Eighteenth Amendment into being. Prohibition did not arrive suddenly or without warning. It was the product of more than a century of organized political agitation by the temperance movement, one of the most sustained and ultimately successful reform campaigns in American political history.

The temperance movement had deep roots in Protestant evangelical Christianity and in the anxieties of a rapidly urbanizing, industrializing nation. By the late nineteenth century, alcohol consumption in the United States was substantial. Saloons were ubiquitous institutions in American cities, serving not only as places to drink but as social centers, employment offices, banking halls for working-class men, and informal political clubs tied to ward machines. In immigrant neighborhoods, they served as gathering places for communities that had left behind the familiar social institutions of their home countries. For many working-class men, the saloon was the only affordable public space where they could socialize, find employment leads, or simply escape cramped tenement apartments.

But reformers saw the saloon as the source of enormous social harm. They pointed, often accurately, to the relationship between heavy drinking and domestic violence, poverty, industrial accidents, and political corruption. Women's organizations were among the most vocal temperance advocates, partly because wives and children bore the consequences of husbands' drinking without legal recourse in an era when divorce was difficult, women could not own property independently in many states, and domestic violence was rarely prosecuted. The Women's Christian Temperance Union, founded in 1874, became one of the largest women's organizations in American history, connecting temperance to a broader reform agenda that included women's suffrage, labor protections, and public health.

By the early twentieth century, the temperance movement had achieved local and state-level prohibition in many parts of the country. Many rural counties and Southern states had already gone dry before the federal amendment. The First World War gave the final push: anti-German sentiment made targeting breweries, many of which were owned by German-American families, politically attractive, and wartime rhetoric about conservation and efficiency added economic arguments to the moral ones. The Eighteenth Amendment, ratified in January 1919 and taking effect a year later, represented the culmination of a century of organized political effort.

What the temperance movement had not fully anticipated was the distinction between eliminating alcohol and eliminating the demand for it. The pre-Prohibition criminal landscape in American cities, as examined in earlier chapters, already contained the organizational seeds of what was to come. Gambling operations, vice districts, and protection rackets had taught urban criminal entrepreneurs how to manage illegal businesses, corrupt public officials, and use violence strategically. The men who would become the major figures of the Prohibition era were not starting from nothing. They were adapting skills and networks already developed in the criminal economy of the previous two decades to an illegal market of unprecedented scale.

The Volstead Act, the federal legislation that defined "intoxicating liquors" and established the enforcement apparatus for Prohibition, was a critical document in this story. It prohibited the manufacture, sale, transportation, importation, and exportation of intoxicating beverages, defined as those containing more than one-half of one percent alcohol by volume. Wine for religious purposes was exempted, as were certain medicinal uses. These exemptions created immediate loopholes that entrepreneurs exploited with creativity. But the fundamental dynamic was simpler: the law made alcohol illegal, it did not make Americans stop wanting it, and the gap between desire and legal supply was immediately filled by whoever was willing to take the risk of supplying it illegally.

The political context of the 1920s shaped Prohibition's enforcement in critical ways. The decade was characterized by a strong anti-regulatory political culture, low federal tax rates, and a general distrust of federal government expansion. The Bureau of Prohibition, the agency responsible for enforcement, was chronically understaffed and underfunded. Its agents were poorly paid, making them susceptible to bribery, and political pressure from both wets, those who opposed Prohibition, and drys, those who supported it, created a chaotic enforcement environment in which the law was applied unevenly, capriciously, and frequently not at all. These conditions were a gift to criminal enterprise, which thrived precisely in the space between formal legal prohibition and practical non-enforcement.

The broader economic context of the 1920s also shaped the criminal revolution. The decade was one of significant economic growth in many sectors, rising consumer spending, and expanding mass culture, including radio, cinema, and new forms of popular music. This culture of consumption and entertainment created demand not just for alcohol but for the entire atmosphere that went with it, the music, the dancing, the sociability of drinking establishments. The speakeasy became part of the Jazz Age, culturally entangled with innovations in music, fashion, and social life that made Prohibition's restrictions seem not just inconvenient but puritanical and out of step with a modernizing society. The criminal enterprises that supplied this culture did not merely serve an economic function. They became, in the eyes of many Americans, providers of a kind of urban pleasure that the law had unjustly banned.

Section 11.1

The Eighteenth Amendment: A Nation Goes Dry

The passage of the Eighteenth Amendment represented the triumph of one of the most sustained and well-organized lobbying campaigns in American history. The temperance movement had been building organizational capacity and political pressure for more than a century before Prohibition became law, and its eventual success reflected genuine popular support as well as sophisticated political strategy. Understanding why Prohibition happened is essential to understanding what it produced, because the forces that created Prohibition also shaped the context in which its failure unfolded.

The Long Road to National Prohibition

Organized opposition to alcohol in the United States dates to the early nineteenth century, when Protestant evangelical reformers, particularly Methodists and Baptists, began connecting temperance to a broader agenda of personal moral improvement and social reform. The American Temperance Society, founded in 1826, claimed more than a million members within a decade. By the 1840s, temperance advocates had achieved their first major political victory when Maine passed the nation's first statewide prohibition law in 1851, an event that inspired similar legislation in more than a dozen other states during the following decade.

The Civil War interrupted the temperance movement's momentum, but the decades that followed saw its organizational infrastructure grow substantially more sophisticated. The founding of the Women's Christian Temperance Union in 1874 and the Anti-Saloon League in 1893 created two of the most effective advocacy organizations in American political history. The WCTU, under the leadership of Frances Willard and later other national figures, connected temperance to a comprehensive reform agenda that included women's suffrage, workplace protections, and child welfare. The Anti-Saloon League, by contrast, pursued a more ruthlessly single-issue approach, concentrating political resources on candidates' positions on alcohol and delivering electoral support or opposition with mechanical efficiency.

By the early twentieth century, the temperance movement had built a formidable coalition. Protestant churches provided organizational infrastructure and moral authority. Women's organizations provided volunteer labor and political pressure. Progressive reformers connected alcohol to the broader social problems of industrialization and urban poverty. Rural communities, particularly in the South and Midwest, provided electoral majorities for prohibition legislation in state after state. By 1916, many states had already gone dry at the state level, and the political balance in Congress was shifting decisively toward the prohibitionists.

The First World War provided the final political context for national Prohibition. Wartime anti-German sentiment made the large German-American brewing industry politically vulnerable; brewers with names like Anheuser-Busch, Pabst, and Schlitz became targets of nativist hostility. Wartime rhetoric about efficiency, conservation, and sacrifice gave temperance advocates economic arguments to supplement moral ones: grain used in brewing was grain not feeding soldiers and allies. The idea of eliminating "liquid bread" while young Americans fought overseas resonated in a political environment already moving toward prohibition. Congress passed a resolution submitting the Eighteenth Amendment to the states in December 1917, and ratification by the required three-quarters of states was completed in January 1919.

The Volstead Act and Its Limits

The Volstead Act, formally the National Prohibition Act, was passed over President Woodrow Wilson's veto in October 1919 and took effect simultaneously with the Eighteenth Amendment on January 17, 1920. The act defined "intoxicating liquors" as any beverage containing more than one-half of one percent alcohol by volume, established penalties for production and sale, and created the Bureau of Prohibition within the Treasury Department to enforce the law.

The act contained several exemptions and ambiguities that immediately created opportunities for evasion and exploitation. Sacramental wine for religious use was permitted, leading to a dramatic and suspicious increase in the number of registered rabbis, priests, and ministers in the years immediately following Prohibition's onset. Medicinal whiskey prescribed by licensed physicians was also exempted, and prescriptions for whiskey at pharmacies became a minor industry in their own right. Industrial alcohol, used in manufacturing processes, remained legal but was required to be denatured, meaning made undrinkable, by the addition of toxic substances. Criminal entrepreneurs who redistilled industrial alcohol to remove the denaturants, a process known as renaturing, supplied enormous quantities of technically legal but dangerously processed spirits to the bootleg market. Deaths and serious illnesses from impure spirits, particularly from improperly renaturing industrial alcohol containing methanol, became a grim feature of the Prohibition years.

The enforcement apparatus was inadequate from the start. The Bureau of Prohibition never had more than a few thousand agents to enforce the law across the entire nation. Agents were paid poorly, between $1,200 and $2,000 per year in an era when a successful bootlegger might earn that much in a single week. The political culture of the era was deeply suspicious of federal government expansion, and Congress consistently resisted appropriating the funds that Bureau directors said were necessary for effective enforcement. The result was a law that was formally in effect but practically unenforceable on anything approaching the scale needed to suppress a market of the size that Prohibition had created.

Political will was as lacking as financial resources. Many elected officials in wet urban districts, representing constituents who had no intention of obeying Prohibition, had supported the amendment strategically, calculating that it would never pass or would prove unenforceable in practice. Others were personally opposed to Prohibition but unwilling to say so publicly. The result was a chronic gap between the formal requirements of federal law and the political reality of enforcement: in many urban areas, Prohibition was essentially a dead letter almost from its beginning, enforced selectively, inconsistently, and often only against those who could not afford to purchase immunity.

Who Supported and Who Opposed Prohibition

Prohibition's supporters and opponents divided along lines of geography, religion, ethnicity, and class in ways that reveal the deep cultural conflicts of 1920s America. Dry sentiment was strongest in rural areas, particularly in the South and Midwest, among native-born white Protestants. It was weakest in large cities, in immigrant communities, and among Catholics, Jews, and secular Americans of various backgrounds. The political geography of Prohibition was, in a real sense, the political geography of an older, rural, Protestant America attempting to impose its moral code on a rapidly urbanizing, ethnically diverse, and increasingly secular one.

For immigrant communities, including the Irish, Italians, Germans, Poles, Jews, and many others, alcohol was embedded in cultural and religious practices that Prohibition disrupted without apology. Wine was part of the Catholic Mass and the Passover Seder. Beer was part of the social culture of German-American communities. The tavern was a social institution in Irish-American neighborhoods. Prohibition's targets were, in many of these communities, experienced as an attack on their way of life by a Protestant majority that had the political power to translate its cultural preferences into constitutional law.

African American communities in northern cities experienced Prohibition in complex ways. Many Black churches and community leaders supported temperance as a matter of personal morality and social welfare. But African American neighborhoods also became important sites of Prohibition-era nightlife, particularly in Harlem in New York City, where the illegal alcohol trade enabled the commercial development of jazz clubs, dance halls, and entertainment venues that became part of the Harlem Renaissance. The relationship between bootlegging, jazz, and African American cultural life in the 1920s illustrates how the criminal economy of Prohibition was entangled with some of the most creative cultural developments of the era.

Historical Vocabulary
Bootlegging

Bootlegging refers to the illegal manufacture, transportation, and sale of alcohol during Prohibition. The term predates Prohibition, originally referring to the practice of concealing a flask of liquor in a boot, but during the 1920s it came to encompass the entire spectrum of illegal alcohol production and distribution. A bootlegger might be an individual who home-brewed beer for personal sale in a neighborhood, or the organizer of a multi-state distribution network moving thousands of cases of whiskey per week.

The scale of bootlegging during Prohibition was extraordinary. Historians estimate that illegal alcohol production during Prohibition generated revenues of between three and five billion dollars per year at the peak of the era, equivalent to a substantial fraction of federal government revenues at the time. This economic scale required organizational sophistication comparable to major legitimate industries, and the criminal enterprises that managed it developed the hierarchical structures, division of labor, financial management practices, and corruption networks that would characterize American organized crime for decades.

The term is also a useful reminder that criminal enterprise is fundamentally a response to market conditions. Bootleggers were not primarily motivated by a desire to undermine the Constitution or challenge federal authority; they were motivated by the enormous profit margins that Prohibition created. Where a barrel of beer had sold for a few dollars before Prohibition, the same barrel could command ten times the price or more when alcohol was illegal. This economic logic drove the criminal revolution that Prohibition produced.

Section 11.2

Bootlegging Networks: The Architecture of Illegal Supply

The transformation of alcohol from a legal consumer product into a prohibited one did not disrupt the basic economic logic of supply and demand. It transferred the supply function from licensed brewers, distillers, and importers to whoever was willing to operate outside the law. What emerged was not a simple replacement of legal supply by illegal supply, but a complex, multi-layered industrial system for producing, transporting, and distributing alcohol at massive scale while evading law enforcement, maintaining quality sufficient to retain customers, and managing the violence that inevitably accompanied the absence of legal contract enforcement.

Production: Breweries, Distilleries, and Home Operations

The production side of the bootlegging economy ranged from enormous industrial operations that were essentially illegal versions of pre-Prohibition breweries to small home distilleries serving a single neighborhood. The scale and sophistication of production varied enormously by geography, by the organizational capacity of the criminal enterprise managing it, and by the type of alcohol being produced.

Beer was the most technically demanding product to produce illegally at large scale, because it required significant equipment, refrigeration, and consistent quality control to be marketable. Criminal organizations that moved into large-scale beer production were essentially operating clandestine breweries, often housed in warehouses, abandoned industrial buildings, or legitimate businesses used as covers. In Chicago, criminal enterprises including the organization that would eventually come under Al Capone's control operated breweries producing thousands of barrels per week, distributed by fleets of trucks that moved through the city's streets with the cooperation of police who had been paid to look the other way. These were not cottage industries; they were industrial operations requiring investment capital, specialized labor, and sophisticated logistics.

Distilled spirits, including whiskey, gin, and vodka, could be produced more easily and on a smaller scale, though quality varied enormously. Home distillation, or "alky cooking," became widespread in immigrant communities where the tradition of home production of spirits was already culturally established. Italian-American households produced wine and grappa; Polish and Eastern European communities produced homemade vodkas and grain spirits; corn whiskey, or moonshine, was produced by rural operations, particularly in Appalachia, that predated Prohibition and expanded dramatically to supply urban markets. The quality of home-produced spirits ranged from acceptable to genuinely dangerous, and deaths and serious illnesses from badly produced alcohol were a significant public health problem throughout the Prohibition era.

For higher-quality spirits, criminal enterprises increasingly turned to industrial alcohol as a source of raw material. Industrial alcohol, used in manufacturing processes, was produced in large quantities and remained legal under Prohibition, though it was required to be denatured with toxic additives to prevent consumption. Criminal chemists and processing operations developed methods to remove or neutralize these additives, though the results were not always safe. The federal government, in a policy that has been sharply criticized by historians, deliberately increased the toxicity of denaturing formulas during Prohibition in an effort to deter consumption of redistilled industrial alcohol. The result was a significant increase in deaths and injuries, as people continued drinking despite the danger. Some historians have estimated that thousands of Americans died from drinking improperly processed industrial alcohol during Prohibition.

Transportation and Distribution

Moving illegal alcohol from production sites to retail operations required elaborate logistics. The transportation networks that bootlegging organizations developed were sophisticated responses to the specific challenges of moving illegal goods through a nominally hostile but practically often compliant law enforcement environment.

Trucks were the primary means of moving bootleg beer and spirits through urban areas. Criminal organizations maintained fleets of delivery vehicles, sometimes disguised as legitimate businesses, that moved through city streets on routes carefully planned to avoid unpredictable or uncorrupted police presence. In cities like Chicago and New York, entire police precincts operated under arrangements in which bootleggers paid regular protection fees in exchange for immunity from interference. On these routes, a truck driver might make dozens of deliveries per night, confident that the police officers he passed were either paid off or warned off.

Larger quantities of alcohol moved by rail, by boat, and increasingly by automobile. The expansion of automobile ownership in the 1920s was both a feature of general prosperity and a specific boon to bootleggers, who found that cars could carry substantial quantities of contraband while moving too fast and too unpredictably for road-based enforcement. The geography of Prohibition enforcement meant that rural roads and secondary highways, poorly supervised compared to major urban arteries, were important corridors for moving alcohol from production sites to cities. The image of fast cars outrunning police on country roads became a staple of Prohibition folklore and later popular culture, eventually feeding the Southern tradition of stock car racing as former bootleg drivers competed their skills on legal tracks.

Distribution at the retail level meant supplying speakeasies, restaurants, hotels, private clubs, and individual customers. This layer of the bootlegging economy was the most visible and the most diverse. In major cities, criminal organizations operated wholesale distribution networks that supplied hundreds or thousands of retail accounts. These networks were organized much like legitimate wholesale distributors, with salesmen who maintained relationships with retail customers, order-takers who managed deliveries, and accountants who tracked payments and credit. The difference was that the payments included regular protection money to police and officials, and disputes over unpaid bills or territorial encroachment were resolved not in court but through the threat or reality of violence.

The Business of Bootlegging: Profit, Competition, and Organization

The economics of bootlegging were extraordinary. Prohibition created profit margins that were essentially impossible in any legal business. Before Prohibition, a barrel of beer sold for approximately six dollars at the brewery gate. During Prohibition, the same quantity of illegal beer sold wholesale for fifty-five dollars or more. Retail markups were even steeper. A glass of beer that had cost a nickel at a pre-Prohibition saloon cost twenty-five cents or more at a speakeasy, five times the price. Whiskey and spirits showed similar or greater markups. The criminal enterprises that controlled these markets were, in strictly economic terms, monopolies sustained by the threat of violence, and they were generating profits that dwarfed anything available in the legal economy of the era.

These profits attracted competition, which in turn produced conflict. Criminal organizations that had staked out territory in the early days of Prohibition faced constant pressure from rivals seeking to expand their market share. The mechanisms for resolving this competition were the same as those available in any illegal market: negotiation backed by the credible threat of violence, and when negotiation failed, violence itself. The gang wars of the 1920s were not random outbreaks of criminal savagery; they were the predictable consequence of market competition in an industry where the normal legal mechanisms for enforcing contracts and property rights were unavailable.

The organizational response of the more sophisticated criminal enterprises was to attempt to cartelize the market, to create cooperative arrangements among competing organizations that would divide territories, fix prices, and reduce the costs of violence. Johnny Torrio in Chicago was among the first and most successful practitioners of this approach. Torrio, who had learned his craft under Paul Kelly and Frankie Yale in New York before moving to Chicago, understood that the profits from bootlegging were large enough to be shared and that the costs of sustained gang warfare were high enough to make cooperation attractive. He negotiated territorial agreements with competing Chicago gangs, establishing something like a criminal federation that divided the city and its suburbs into spheres of influence. The arrangement was not perfectly stable, and Torrio himself was shot by a rival in 1925, but the model he established, of criminal organization through negotiation and territorial agreement rather than constant warfare, influenced the approach of criminal enterprises throughout the country.

Historical Vocabulary
Racketeering

Racketeering is the operation of an illegal business or scheme, or the extortion of legitimate businesses under threat of violence. During the Prohibition era, the term expanded to cover the full range of activities through which criminal organizations generated illegal income, from bootlegging itself to the extortion of speakeasy owners who were forced to buy from particular suppliers at inflated prices, the corruption of union officials to extract payments from employers, and the operation of gambling establishments under protection arrangements with law enforcement.

The term "racket" entered American criminal vocabulary during this period as a broad description for any organized criminal scheme, particularly those involving repetitive extraction of payments from victims who had no legal recourse. The scale and sophistication of bootlegging-era racketeering eventually prompted Congress to enact the Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act, the RICO statute, in 1970, which gave prosecutors new tools for attacking criminal enterprises as organizations rather than prosecuting only individual crimes. The legislative response to Prohibition-era racketeering thus shaped law enforcement tools used against organized crime for decades afterward.

Section 11.3

Speakeasies: The Social World of Illegal Drinking

The speakeasy was perhaps the most visible and culturally significant institution produced by Prohibition. More than merely a substitute for the saloon, the speakeasy became a defining social space of the 1920s, a place where the enforcement gap between Prohibition law and social reality was physically embodied. To walk through the hidden door of a speakeasy was to enter a world in which federal law was openly flouted, in which criminal entrepreneurs provided services that millions of paying customers desired, and in which the social norms of the pre-Prohibition era were being quietly renegotiated.

What Was a Speakeasy?

The term "speakeasy" had been used before Prohibition to describe unlicensed drinking establishments, but it became ubiquitous during the 1920s. A speakeasy could be almost anything: a neighborhood bar behind a false wall in a legitimate-seeming business, a basement operation in a tenement building, an elegant nightclub serving cocktails from crystal glasses, or a working-class hangout distinguished from a pre-Prohibition saloon only by the need to knock on the door and be recognized before entering. The range was enormous, from squalid cellar dives serving dangerous rotgut spirits to elaborate supper clubs catering to wealthy New Yorkers who paid twenty dollars for a bottle of smuggled Scotch whiskey.

Estimates of the number of speakeasies operating in major American cities during Prohibition stagger the imagination. New York City, by various estimates, had anywhere from thirty thousand to one hundred thousand illegal drinking establishments operating at various points during the 1920s, compared to roughly fifteen thousand licensed saloons before Prohibition. The New York Police Department's own estimates suggested that the city had more speakeasies than it had bars before Prohibition. Chicago was estimated to have more than ten thousand, along with numerous "blind pigs," another contemporary term for illegal drinking establishments. Similar patterns prevailed in Detroit, Philadelphia, Baltimore, Boston, and virtually every other major American city.

Operating a speakeasy required navigating a complex ecosystem of corruption, supply relationships, and physical security. The first requirement was a reliable supply of alcohol at a price that allowed for profitable retail markup while remaining competitive with other establishments in the neighborhood. Criminal wholesalers supplied this need, though their pricing arrangements were often less a market negotiation than an offer that could not be refused: a speakeasy owner who declined to buy from the designated supplier for a particular territory risked having his establishment smashed, his stock destroyed, or worse. The same criminal organizations that supplied bootleg alcohol also provided, for an additional payment, "protection" against police raids, which usually meant that the local police precinct had been paid to leave the establishment alone. Protection payments were as essential a business expense as rent.

The Speakeasy and American Social Life

The cultural significance of the speakeasy extended well beyond its role as an illegal drinking establishment. The speakeasy became a site where several important social boundaries were crossed or blurred in ways that had lasting effects on American culture. Perhaps most significantly, the speakeasy was one of the first public social spaces in which women routinely drank alongside men in significant numbers.

Before Prohibition, the saloon was almost exclusively a male space. Women who drank in public were considered disreputable, and respectable women avoided saloons entirely. The speakeasy, paradoxically, was more hospitable to women. Because speakeasies were illegal establishments serving customers who had already decided to flout federal law, they had less incentive than licensed establishments to police the social respectability of their clientele. Speakeasy owners discovered that mixed-gender establishments were more profitable than male-only ones. Women customers spent money on cocktails, food, and entertainment. They brought male companions who spent additional money. The economics of illegal operation pushed speakeasies toward social inclusiveness that the legal saloon had never practiced.

The "cocktail" as a standard social drink gained enormous popularity during Prohibition partly because mixed drinks disguised the often rough character of bootleg spirits. A glass of neat bootleg whiskey might reveal its unpleasant origins immediately, but the same whiskey mixed with ginger ale, lemon juice, or other mixers was more palatable and more presentable. The era's innovations in cocktail culture, from the sidecar to the bee's knees to the highball, were in significant part a response to the quality constraints of illegal alcohol production.

The speakeasy was also, in many cities, a site of racial mixing that was unusual for the era. In Harlem, establishments like the Cotton Club, Small's Paradise, and dozens of smaller venues served white customers who came uptown for jazz, dancing, and illegal drinks. The relationship was complex and not without its own forms of racial hierarchy: many of Harlem's famous clubs were owned by white bootleggers and gangsters, employed African American performers and staff, and served white customers from downtown while sometimes restricting or excluding Black patrons. But the presence of African American musicians, bandleaders, and performers at the center of the speakeasy's cultural appeal contributed to the broader impact of Black artistic culture on mainstream American entertainment, a dynamic that would eventually reshape American popular music, dance, and style well beyond the Prohibition era.

Jazz was inseparable from the speakeasy world. The music, with its improvisational energy, its African American roots, and its association with nightlife and transgression, was the perfect soundtrack for establishments that were themselves transgressive by definition. Musicians like Louis Armstrong, Duke Ellington, Cab Calloway, and dozens of others built their reputations in the club circuits of Harlem, Chicago's South Side, and New Orleans, circuits that were sustained in significant part by bootleg alcohol money and the audiences that came to speakeasies to drink, dance, and hear live music. The Prohibition era was thus a critical incubation period for what would become one of the defining American art forms of the twentieth century.

Everyday Life and Social History

An Evening at the Speakeasy: Ordinary Americans and Prohibition

For millions of ordinary Americans, Prohibition's most tangible effect was not the rise of organized crime or the corruption of public officials but the simple practical reality of needing to know the right person, knock on the right door, and carry enough cash to pay inflated prices for a drink in an environment that was, by definition, operating outside the law. The experience of navigating this world varied enormously depending on social class, neighborhood, ethnicity, and race.

For a factory worker in an industrial neighborhood in Chicago or Detroit, the speakeasy might be a basement establishment in a nearby building, operated by a neighbor or acquaintance, selling homemade beer or cheap grain spirits at prices noticeably higher than pre-Prohibition saloons had charged. The atmosphere might be essentially identical to the corner bar that had existed before 1920, minus the official license above the door. Protection was usually informal, paid in cash to the local beat cop who developed a convenient blindness when passing the establishment.

For a middle-class professional or a wealthy socialite in New York, the speakeasy might be a gleaming nightclub offering imported Scotch, cocktails mixed by professional bartenders, a jazz band, and a floor for dancing. Establishments like the "21" Club, El Morocco, and the Stork Club catered to celebrities, politicians, and wealthy New Yorkers who drank openly in elegant surroundings while the federal law nominally prohibiting their drinks remained on the books. These clients paid protection money indirectly, embedded in the price of their drinks, and rarely feared arrest because the political and social connections of their establishments made raids impolitic.

For working-class women, the speakeasy offered something genuinely new: a semi-public space where drinking was acceptable. Many women who would not have entered a pre-Prohibition saloon found the speakeasy, with its more mixed and socially fluid atmosphere, accessible in ways the older institution had not been. The "flapper" of 1920s popular culture, with her bobbed hair, short skirt, and cocktail in hand, was partly a cultural construction and partly a real social phenomenon enabled by the speakeasy's more inclusive social culture.

The universal experience, regardless of class, was the knowledge that engaging with the speakeasy economy meant participating in a system of organized corruption. The drink in one's hand had passed through hands that had bribed officials, hired enforcers, and sometimes committed violence to ensure its delivery. Most customers did not think about this much, just as consumers of other products rarely think about the full supply chain that delivers them. But the moral complicity of millions of ordinary Americans in the Prohibition-era criminal economy is a reminder that organized crime flourishes not only because of the ambitions of criminal entrepreneurs but because of the demand of ordinary citizens who prefer the illegal product to doing without.

Section 11.4

Smuggling Routes: The Geography of Illegal Supply

The domestic production of bootleg alcohol, however extensive, could not fully satisfy the American market's demand for particular types and qualities of liquor. Imported whiskey, particularly Scotch and Canadian rye, was preferred by many consumers over domestic bootleg spirits and commanded premium prices. Wine, beer, and various spirits were also imported illegally from the Caribbean, from Canada, and from Europe in quantities that made international smuggling a major industry in its own right. The geography of this smuggling reflected the physical and political features of the United States' borders and coastlines, and the organizations that managed it developed sophisticated methods for moving goods through an enforcement environment that was, in practice, far more porous than its nominal authority suggested.

The Canadian Border

Canada did not adopt national Prohibition at the federal level, and several Canadian provinces maintained legal liquor production and sale throughout the American Prohibition era. This made the more than five thousand mile U.S.-Canadian border, much of it through remote wilderness or across easily traversed waterways, the most important corridor for legally produced alcohol entering the United States illegally. The Detroit River, which at its narrowest point is less than a mile wide between Detroit and Windsor, Ontario, became one of the most active smuggling corridors in the world during the 1920s. At the peak of the trade, historians estimate that roughly seventy-five percent of all illegal liquor entering the United States crossed the Detroit River.

The methods used to cross the Detroit River ranged from the simple to the elaborate. Speedboats made nighttime runs across the narrow waterway, loaded with cases of Canadian whiskey. In winter, when the river froze, trucks drove directly across the ice. Legitimate ferries carried smuggled goods concealed in cargo, in automobiles, and on the persons of passengers. The corruption of border agents and customs officials was essential to maintaining the volume of the trade: American officials on the Windsor side, the Canadian side, and the U.S. Customs Service were all potential targets for bribery, and the financial resources of major bootlegging organizations were more than sufficient to purchase the cooperation of underpaid government employees.

The Purple Gang, a predominantly Jewish criminal organization based in Detroit, became one of the dominant forces in Michigan's bootlegging economy partly because of its geographic advantage. Operating in close proximity to the Canadian border, the Purple Gang established relationships with Canadian suppliers and controlled significant portions of the river crossing traffic. Their connection to the Canadian liquor trade also made them a valuable supplier to Al Capone's Chicago organization, which imported Purple Gang whiskey to supplement its domestic production.

Similar dynamics played out along the northern border from the Great Lakes region to the Northeast. Vermont, upstate New York, and Maine all had long, lightly patrolled borders with Canadian provinces that remained wet throughout Prohibition. Bootleg liquor moved south through these corridors in quantities sufficient to supply major northeastern cities, and the enforcement challenge of monitoring thousands of miles of wilderness border with inadequate personnel and resources made truly effective interdiction essentially impossible.

Rum Row and the Atlantic Coast

For consumers who preferred imported European spirits, particularly Scotch whiskey, or for criminal entrepreneurs who found that imported spirits commanded prices high enough to justify the additional costs of ocean smuggling, the Atlantic Ocean offered another supply route. Beginning in the early 1920s, a remarkable phenomenon developed off the Atlantic Coast: a line of foreign ships, primarily British and Bahamian registered, anchored in international waters just beyond the three-mile limit of United States territorial jurisdiction, waiting to sell their cargoes to American smugglers.

This informal assembly of liquor-carrying ships, which stretched from New England to Florida at its greatest extent, became known as "Rum Row." The ships, which numbered in the dozens at the peak of the trade, carried enormous quantities of Scotch whiskey, Caribbean rum, and other spirits purchased legally in their ports of origin. Because they anchored outside American jurisdiction, their presence was technically legal, and U.S. Coast Guard vessels could not board or arrest them without provoking an international incident. American smugglers in fast motorboats would run out to the ships at night, purchase cases of liquor at wholesale prices, and race back to shore before the Coast Guard could intercept them.

The Coast Guard, which was given the primary responsibility for maritime enforcement of Prohibition, faced an almost impossible task. Its vessels were initially slower than the fastest rumrunners, and its legal authority was limited to U.S. territorial waters. Congress eventually extended American jurisdictional claims under the Smuggling Act of 1935 and through bilateral treaties with Britain and other nations, and the Coast Guard acquired faster vessels, but throughout the 1920s, the Rum Row trade continued at a substantial volume. Criminal entrepreneurs like Bill McCoy, a Florida boat captain who became famous for the consistent quality of his imported spirits, became celebrated figures in Prohibition folklore. The phrase "the real McCoy," meaning the genuine article, supposedly derived from McCoy's reputation for selling legitimately bottled imported spirits rather than adulterated substitutes.

The Caribbean and Southern Routes

The Bahamas, Cuba, and other Caribbean islands served as both transit points and staging areas for smuggling operations aimed at the U.S. Southeast and Gulf Coast. Nassau in the Bahamas became a particularly important hub: British-controlled, located only fifty miles from the Florida coast, and home to substantial warehouses of legally purchased European spirits. Criminal entrepreneurs established purchasing and forwarding operations in Nassau that received shipments from European distilleries and reloaded them onto smaller vessels for the run across the Florida Straits to the American mainland.

The Florida coast, with its innumerable bays, inlets, keys, and barrier islands, was particularly difficult to patrol effectively, and bootleg alcohol landed at dozens of points along the coast and moved north by road and rail to supply markets in Miami, Tampa, Atlanta, and cities further north. Southern bootlegging routes connected Caribbean supply to eastern and midwestern demand, and the organizations that managed these routes were among the first to develop the multi-state distribution networks that would later become a defining feature of American organized crime.

Section 11.5

Organized Violence: The Wars of the Bootleggers

The enormous profits of the bootlegging economy made the territories that generated them worth fighting over. Criminal organizations that had staked out particular cities, neighborhoods, or supply corridors found themselves facing continuous pressure from rivals seeking to expand their own market share. In the absence of any legal mechanism for enforcing territorial claims or contractual obligations, the ultimate arbiter of disputes between criminal enterprises was violence, and the 1920s witnessed levels of gang violence in American cities that shocked contemporary observers and remain among the bloodiest episodes in the history of American crime.

The Logic of Criminal Violence

To understand Prohibition-era gang violence, it is essential to understand that it was not, for the most part, random or purposeless. Criminal violence during Prohibition served specific strategic functions within the criminal economy. It was used to enforce territorial claims, to punish rival organizations that encroached on established territories or hijacked shipments. It was used to discipline members of one's own organization who cheated, informed, or otherwise violated the informal codes of the criminal enterprise. It was used to intimidate speakeasy owners, suppliers, and other business partners into accepting unfavorable terms or to deter them from doing business with rival organizations. And it was used, occasionally, to signal to political authorities and law enforcement that protection payments were not optional.

The economics of criminal violence followed a specific logic. Violence was costly: it attracted law enforcement attention, risked retaliation, and eliminated experienced criminal personnel. The most successful criminal entrepreneurs during Prohibition were generally those who understood that violence was a tool to be used strategically rather than indiscriminately, and who preferred profitable cooperation to costly conflict when possible. Johnny Torrio in Chicago attempted to institutionalize this insight through the territorial agreements he negotiated with competing gangs, and his approach was more successful, while it lasted, than the more violent methods of organizations that treated conflict as a default rather than a last resort.

But violence remained endemic because the conditions that produced it, an extremely profitable illegal market with no legal mechanism for dispute resolution, were impossible to fully overcome through voluntary cooperation alone. Territorial agreements required trust that was difficult to maintain when the parties were criminals operating in an environment of mutual suspicion and when the prize for defection, control of a rival's profitable territory, was so large. The death of a gang leader, the arrest of a key organizer, or the emergence of a new competitor could disrupt arrangements that had been functioning reasonably well and trigger rounds of retaliatory violence.

Gang Wars in Chicago and New York

Chicago was the most violent major battleground of the Prohibition-era gang wars, partly because of its particular criminal geography. The city's size, its position as a railroad hub for much of the Midwest, and the political fragmentation of its municipal government, divided among competing ward bosses with varying levels of cooperation with criminal enterprises, made it an enormously valuable prize. Multiple criminal organizations competed for different segments of the Chicago market, including the Torrio-Capone organization on the South Side, Dion O'Banion's North Side gang, and numerous smaller organizations throughout the city's ethnic neighborhoods.

The assassination of Dion O'Banion in his flower shop in November 1924, carried out by men connected to the Torrio-Capone organization, triggered a multi-year cycle of retaliatory violence that became the defining narrative of Prohibition-era Chicago. O'Banion's successors, first Hymie Weiss and then Bugs Moran, pursued vendettas against the South Side organization that produced dozens of murders, multiple attempts on the lives of major figures including Torrio himself, who was shot and severely wounded in January 1925 and subsequently retired to New York, and eventually Al Capone. The city's murder rate, already elevated before Prohibition, climbed to extraordinary levels during the peak gang war years of 1926 and 1927.

New York experienced its own cycles of gang violence, though the city's larger size and more fragmented criminal geography produced a somewhat different pattern. The major New York bootlegging organizations of the early 1920s included the Five Points Gang on the Lower East Side, various Sicilian organizations in East Harlem and Brooklyn, Jewish criminal organizations in the Bronx and Brooklyn, and Irish-American enterprises in several boroughs. The city's criminal geography was more complex than Chicago's, and the violence, while significant, was somewhat more dispersed. New York's criminal conflicts also featured a strategic dimension absent from Chicago's more territorially straightforward wars: Arnold Rothstein, the gambling impresario and criminal financier known as "the Brain," played a mediating and investment role that Chicago lacked, financing bootlegging operations, negotiating among factions, and helping to rationalize the criminal economy in ways that reduced, though did not eliminate, violence.

The St. Valentine's Day Massacre

The event that most powerfully symbolized the violence of Prohibition-era gang warfare, and that did more than any other single incident to shift public opinion against the criminal tolerance that had characterized much of the 1920s, was the St. Valentine's Day Massacre of February 14, 1929. On that morning, seven members or associates of the Bugs Moran North Side organization were killed in a garage on Chicago's North Clark Street by gunmen who had posed as police officers. The killings were almost certainly ordered by Al Capone, who was conveniently in Florida at the time, and represented the culmination of years of conflict between the South Side organization and the remnants of the O'Banion gang.

The massacre was shocking not because of its death toll, seven men in a single incident was notable but not unprecedented in Chicago's gang war history, but because of its brazenness and its visible demonstration of how thoroughly the criminal-law enforcement relationship had broken down. The perpetrators had dressed as police officers, had entered in vehicles resembling police cars, and had lined their victims up against a wall in apparent imitation of a police raid before executing them with machine guns. The imagery suggested a world in which criminal violence had achieved a kind of institutional permanence, in which gangsters could operate with the equipment and confidence of law enforcement itself. Newspapers across the country ran photographs of the crime scene, and the massacre became an international symbol of American lawlessness.

Paradoxically, the massacre may have been a strategic error as well as a moral one. The resulting national attention brought pressure on Chicago authorities to take meaningful action against the gang organizations that had operated with near impunity for a decade, and the increased scrutiny that followed contributed to the eventual federal prosecution of Al Capone on tax evasion charges in 1931. The publicity that the massacre generated also contributed to the building popular opinion that Prohibition, whatever its intentions, had created conditions of disorder and corruption that were worse than the social problems it had been designed to solve.

Corruption as Infrastructure

Violence was one tool of criminal enterprise during Prohibition; corruption was another, and in many ways the more important one. The successful bootlegging operation did not rely primarily on violence to protect itself from law enforcement. It relied on having paid enough police officers, officials, and politicians to ensure that enforcement would be selective, delayed, or entirely absent. The corruption of public officials during Prohibition was so extensive that it constituted not merely a series of individual acts of bribery but a structural feature of the relationship between criminal enterprise and government in many American cities.

The cost structure of a successful bootlegging operation in a major city included regular payments to police at multiple levels of the hierarchy, from beat officers who agreed not to arrest delivery drivers to precinct captains who cleared operations in their districts to senior police officials who protected the overall enterprise from coordinated raids. Payments went to judges who dismissed charges, to municipal court officials who managed case scheduling, to prosecutors who declined to pursue indictments, and to elected officials from city council members to mayors who allowed the arrangements to continue. In some cities, the entire municipal government was effectively operating as a protection business for criminal enterprise, collecting regular payments in exchange for allowing bootlegging to continue unmolested.

Chicago was perhaps the most notorious example. Mayor William Hale "Big Bill" Thompson, who served multiple terms during the Prohibition era, was widely understood to operate in close alignment with the major bootlegging organizations. Police Commissioners and precinct captains participated in protection arrangements so openly that observers from outside the city marveled at the brazenness of the arrangement. In New York, Tammany Hall's relationship with bootlegging operations continued its long tradition of providing political protection for vice, adapting its mechanisms of corruption to the new criminal economy. In city after city, the formal apparatus of law enforcement coexisted with a practical economy in which criminal organizations purchased the tolerance they needed to operate.

Primary Source Window

The Volstead Act (National Prohibition Act), 1919

Creator: United States Congress · Date: October 28, 1919 · Type: Federal Legislation

Context: The Volstead Act, formally named the National Prohibition Act and commonly known by the name of its chief sponsor, Representative Andrew Volstead of Minnesota, was the federal legislation that defined the terms and mechanisms of Prohibition. Passed over President Wilson's veto, it implemented the Eighteenth Amendment by defining prohibited beverages, establishing penalties, creating the enforcement apparatus, and specifying exemptions. The act is a foundational document for understanding both what Prohibition attempted to do and why its implementation created such enormous opportunities for criminal enterprise.

The Volstead Act defined "intoxicating liquors" as any beverage containing more than one-half of one percent alcohol by volume. It prohibited the manufacture, sale, barter, transport, import, export, delivery, and furnishing of such beverages except as authorized by the act. Penalties ranged from fines to imprisonment. The act exempted wines for sacramental purposes, certain medicinal uses of spirits, and industrial alcohol, provided the latter was "denatured" with toxic additives to prevent consumption. Enforcement authority was vested in the Commissioner of Internal Revenue, operating through the Bureau of Prohibition.

Analysis: The Volstead Act's drafting reveals both the intentions of Prohibition's advocates and the structural weaknesses that made enforcement so difficult. The exemptions for sacramental and medicinal uses, while responding to legitimate religious and health concerns, immediately created large loopholes that were widely exploited. The requirement for denatured industrial alcohol, and the federal government's decision to add increasingly toxic denaturing agents during the 1920s, raises serious ethical questions about policies that knowingly increased public health risks. The assignment of enforcement to the Bureau of Prohibition, with its limited budget and its placement within the Treasury Department rather than the Justice Department, reflected the political compromises that had produced Prohibition and left the enforcement apparatus structurally ill-equipped for its task.

The Volstead Act is also a reminder that the legal prohibition of a commodity does not by itself change market conditions. The act specified what was illegal, established penalties, and created an enforcement mechanism, but it did not address the underlying demand for alcohol that remained after the law took effect. The gap between what the act prohibited and what could practically be enforced was the space in which the criminal economy of Prohibition flourished.

  2. How did criminal entrepreneurs exploit the specific language and provisions of the Volstead Act to operate in legally ambiguous spaces?
  3. What does the act's enforcement mechanism, vesting authority in the underfunded Bureau of Prohibition, suggest about the political commitment to actual enforcement?

Constitutional Connection

The Eighteenth and Twenty-First Amendments: Prohibition and Repeal in Constitutional History

The Eighteenth Amendment holds a unique place in American constitutional history as the only amendment designed to restrict the behavior of private citizens rather than limit the powers of government. Prior amendments had established rights and freedoms or modified the structure of government; the Eighteenth Amendment used the Constitution's highest legal authority to prohibit a specific category of private commercial activity. This represented a significant expansion of federal power and a fundamental departure from the traditional function of constitutional amendment.

The political process through which the Eighteenth Amendment was ratified revealed important features of the American constitutional system. The amendment process requires a two-thirds vote of both houses of Congress and ratification by three-quarters of state legislatures. The temperance movement had carefully cultivated state-level political support for decades, and when the federal amendment was proposed, it was ratified by the required forty-six states (of forty-eight) with remarkable speed, in just over thirteen months. This reflected the genuine breadth of dry sentiment in the country, concentrated in rural states that were overrepresented in the constitutional ratification process relative to the urban populations that most strongly opposed Prohibition.

The Twenty-First Amendment, ratified in December 1933, is equally significant constitutionally. It is the only amendment in American history to repeal a prior amendment. Its ratification process was also distinctive: rather than going through state legislatures, it was ratified through specially elected state conventions, a process specified in the amendment itself. This choice reflected concern that state legislatures remained dominated by dry political interests that had originally passed the Eighteenth Amendment; conventions, it was calculated, would more accurately reflect the changed popular sentiment that had turned against Prohibition during the Depression. The Twenty-First Amendment also returned authority over alcohol regulation to the individual states, creating the patchwork of state liquor laws that still varies across the country today, with different states and localities retaining different restrictions on alcohol sale and consumption.

Together, the Eighteenth and Twenty-First Amendments illustrate both the capacity of the American constitutional system to respond to sustained political movements and the limits of using constitutional law to change deeply embedded social behavior. They also raise enduring questions about the appropriate scope of federal power to regulate private behavior, questions that have recurred in American politics in debates about drug policy, gambling, and other forms of morals legislation that Prohibition foreshadowed.

Historical Debate

Did Prohibition "Create" Organized Crime in America?

The question: One of the most persistent claims in the popular history of Prohibition is that it "created" American organized crime: that the criminal enterprises of the 1920s and beyond were essentially products of the Eighteenth Amendment, and that without Prohibition, the sophisticated criminal organizations of the twentieth century would not have come into existence. Historians have debated this claim vigorously, producing a more nuanced picture of the relationship between Prohibition and the development of organized crime.

Interpretation A — Prohibition as the Origin of Modern Organized Crime: Some historians argue that Prohibition represents a decisive turning point, that while criminal enterprises certainly existed before 1920, the scale, sophistication, and organizational capacity of Prohibition-era bootlegging organizations represented something qualitatively new. Before Prohibition, criminal enterprises in American cities were typically local, ethnically bounded, and relatively unsophisticated in organizational terms. Prohibition's enormous profits, and the need to manage supply chains, corrupt officials, negotiate territorial agreements, and manage large workforces, forced a kind of organizational evolution that transformed fragmented local criminal operations into something more like modern criminal enterprises. The argument is not merely that Prohibition made criminals richer, but that it forced them to become more sophisticated, more organized, and more deeply embedded in the political economy of American cities.
Interpretation B — Prohibition as Accelerant, Not Creator: Other historians emphasize the continuities between pre-Prohibition criminal enterprise and the organizations of the 1920s. As Chapters 9 and 10 of this textbook demonstrate, the organizational foundations of American organized crime were already well established before 1920. The gambling operations, protection rackets, vice districts, and political machine connections that characterized pre-Prohibition urban crime had already produced entrepreneurs with the skills, networks, and methods that would be applied to bootlegging. In this view, Prohibition did not create organized crime but rather provided it with a much larger market, enormous additional profits, and a period of intense organizational competition that accelerated the consolidation of criminal enterprises. The criminal organizations that would dominate post-Prohibition American crime, and that would move into gambling, labor racketeering, and other industries after repeal, were not Prohibition's creation but its beneficiaries.

Evidence: Both interpretations draw on genuine historical evidence. Records of pre-Prohibition criminal enterprises demonstrate real organizational complexity and political corruption that predates 1920. The speed with which bootlegging organizations formed and scaled up in the early 1920s suggests that the people who built them were not starting from nothing. At the same time, the scale of Prohibition-era criminal enterprises, their geographic reach, their financial sophistication, and their ability to corrupt officials at the highest levels of government represented genuine organizational advances over what had existed before.

Why it matters: The question of whether Prohibition "created" organized crime has important implications for contemporary criminal justice policy. If criminal enterprises are primarily creatures of specific legal prohibitions, then changing those prohibitions, decriminalizing currently illegal goods and services, should weaken or eliminate them. If criminal enterprises have their own organizational logic that allows them to adapt and survive changes in legal environment, as the post-repeal survival of Prohibition-era organizations in new criminal markets suggests, then the relationship between law and crime is more complex.

Student task: Based on the evidence presented in this chapter and in Chapters 9 and 10, evaluate the two interpretations. Which do you find more persuasive? What would you need to know to more definitively resolve the debate?

Multiple Perspectives

Who Experienced Prohibition, and How?

Temperance Advocates and Reform Organizations: For the women and men who had spent decades campaigning for Prohibition, January 1920 was a moment of genuine triumph. They believed, sincerely and not without reason, that alcohol was a significant cause of domestic violence, poverty, industrial accidents, and political corruption. Many of them were initially optimistic that Prohibition, properly enforced, would reduce these harms substantially. As the 1920s progressed and evidence of widespread non-enforcement accumulated, many temperance advocates shifted from optimism to frustration, arguing that the problem was inadequate enforcement rather than the principle of Prohibition itself. The Wickersham Commission's exposure of enforcement failures confirmed what they had long suspected but pointed toward different conclusions in different temperance quarters: some advocated for better enforcement, others began to accept that national Prohibition had been an overreach.
Urban Working-Class Communities: For many working-class Americans, particularly in immigrant communities, Prohibition was experienced primarily as an unwanted intrusion of Protestant moral authority into everyday life. The saloon, whatever its faults, was a genuine social institution in many neighborhoods, and its abolition was felt as a loss. The response of many working-class communities was simply to continue drinking through whatever channels were available, home production, local speakeasies, and purchases from neighborhood bootleggers. The increased cost of alcohol under Prohibition fell hardest on those with less income, who could not afford the premium prices of better-quality establishments and were more likely to consume dangerous lower-grade spirits. Working-class communities were also more likely to face selective enforcement, since police raids on working-class speakeasies were politically easier than raids on establishments patronized by the wealthy.
Criminal Entrepreneurs: For the men who built the bootlegging operations that supplied illegal alcohol to American cities, Prohibition was the defining opportunity of their careers, a moment when the specific skills and connections they had developed in the pre-Prohibition criminal economy suddenly became enormously valuable. Many of them had grown up in poverty in immigrant neighborhoods where legitimate paths to wealth were narrowly constrained. Prohibition offered a path to wealth and power that would otherwise have been unavailable, and they pursued it with energy, creativity, and the willingness to use violence when necessary. Their experience of Prohibition was inseparable from the experience of class mobility, from a sense that the criminal economy offered opportunities that the legitimate economy denied them, a perception that was not entirely wrong.
Law Enforcement and Government Officials: The experience of law enforcement during Prohibition ranged from genuine commitment to nominal opposition to full participation in the corruption that Prohibition generated. Some agents and officers enforced the law as best they could with inadequate resources. Many more participated, at various levels of conscious complicity, in the protection arrangements that allowed bootlegging to operate. A few, like federal agent Eliot Ness, who led a team of Prohibition agents known as "the Untouchables" that targeted Al Capone's Chicago organization, pursued enforcement with genuine dedication under difficult conditions. The corruption that Prohibition generated was not simply a moral failure of individual officials; it was a structural consequence of laws that millions of citizens and many of their elected representatives did not want enforced, combined with economic incentives for non-enforcement that vastly exceeded the official salaries of those charged with carrying it out.
African Americans in Northern Cities: The experience of African Americans during Prohibition was shaped by the intersection of Prohibition with the Great Migration, the movement of hundreds of thousands of Black Americans from the rural South to northern cities in the 1910s and 1920s. In Harlem and Chicago's South Side and other African American urban communities, bootleg alcohol, jazz music, and the speakeasy economy were intertwined with the cultural flowering of the period. Black musicians, performers, and entrepreneurs participated in the speakeasy economy, sometimes as owners and sometimes as employees of criminal organizations controlled by others. African American communities also bore a disproportionate burden of Prohibition enforcement: while wealthy white speakeasies operated with near-impunity, Black establishments were more frequently raided, and Black individuals were more frequently arrested for alcohol violations than their white counterparts. This pattern of selective enforcement, in which the law was applied more stringently against those with less political power, was a feature of Prohibition that prefigured patterns in drug enforcement that would emerge later in the twentieth century.
Cause and Effect

Prohibition and the Criminal Revolution: Causes and Consequences

Long-Term Causes

More than a century of temperance agitation; the organizational capacity of the Anti-Saloon League and the WCTU; deep cultural conflict between rural Protestant America and urban immigrant communities; economic and political power of the dry political coalition; pre-existing criminal networks in American cities capable of scaling into bootlegging operations; structural inadequacy of federal enforcement apparatus.

Short-Term Causes

First World War anti-German sentiment targeting German-American brewers; wartime rhetoric of efficiency and sacrifice; rapid ratification by state legislatures; Volstead Act's definitional framework creating immediate loopholes; underfunding and corruption of Bureau of Prohibition from its first year of operation.

The Criminal Revolution

Rapid formation of large-scale bootlegging operations in major cities; development of elaborate supply chains from domestic production and international smuggling; corruption of law enforcement and political officials on an unprecedented scale; gang warfare over territorial control; consolidation of criminal enterprises and development of sophisticated criminal management structures.

Long-Term Effects

Permanent organizational infrastructure for large-scale criminal enterprise in American cities; criminal networks transferred to gambling, labor racketeering, and drug trafficking after repeal; corruption of political institutions with effects extending decades beyond Prohibition; cultural normalization of law-breaking and distrust of "moral" legislation; critical case study for subsequent debates about criminalization of other goods and services.

Historical complexity: The criminal revolution of Prohibition cannot be explained by the Eighteenth Amendment alone. It required pre-existing criminal networks, structural enforcement failures, political corruption, and genuine popular demand for alcohol among millions of Americans who were unwilling to comply with the law. Each of these conditions was necessary; none alone was sufficient.

Continuity and Change Over Time

American Organized Crime: From Pre-Prohibition to Post-Repeal

What Changed

  • Scale of criminal enterprise: from local, neighborhood-based operations to multi-city and multi-state organizations
  • Financial sophistication: from simple cash transactions to complex financial management involving lawyers, accountants, and political relationships
  • Organizational complexity: from informal hierarchies to more structured enterprises with defined roles, territorial arrangements, and something approaching corporate governance
  • Political integration: from ward-level corruption to city-wide and sometimes state-level political relationships
  • Violence: from relatively localized disputes to organized gang warfare with military levels of planning and weaponry
  • Cultural visibility: criminal figures became mass media celebrities, a phenomenon without significant precedent before the 1920s

What Stayed the Same

  • The fundamental economic logic of black market enterprise: providing prohibited goods at premium prices to willing customers
  • The role of corruption as essential infrastructure for large-scale criminal enterprise
  • The relationship between criminal enterprise and political machines in urban areas
  • The recruitment of criminal entrepreneurs from the poorest communities, where legitimate paths to wealth and status were most constrained
  • The use of ethnic and family networks as the basis of trust within criminal organizations
  • The structural vulnerability of criminal enterprises to informants and internal defection, absent legal mechanisms for enforcing loyalty

After repeal in 1933: The criminal organizations built during Prohibition did not dissolve when alcohol became legal again. They transferred their organizational capacity, their political connections, their financial resources, and their management experience to other criminal markets, primarily gambling, labor racketeering, and eventually drug trafficking. The post-Prohibition survival of Prohibition-era criminal enterprises into new criminal markets is one of the most important pieces of evidence for the argument that Prohibition's most lasting consequence was the creation of organizational infrastructure for American criminal enterprise that persisted long after the amendment that produced it had been repealed.

Chapter Summary

Prohibition represents one of the most consequential unintended experiments in the history of criminal enterprise. The Eighteenth Amendment, ratified in January 1919 and in effect from January 1920, was the product of more than a century of temperance agitation and reflected genuine popular support concentrated in rural, Protestant, native-born communities. Its proponents believed sincerely that alcohol was a major cause of social harm and that its prohibition would produce measurable improvements in public health, family welfare, and civic order. What they did not adequately anticipate was the relationship between legal prohibition, economic demand, and the organizational capacity of criminal enterprise.

The prohibition of alcohol created an illegal market of staggering scale. Tens of millions of Americans who had been accustomed to drinking continued to want alcohol, and the gap between their desire and its legal supply was immediately filled by criminal entrepreneurs who had the skills, networks, and willingness to serve them at a premium. Bootlegging operations ranged from small neighborhood enterprises to large-scale industrial organizations managing supply chains across multiple states and international borders. The profit margins available in the illegal alcohol trade were extraordinary, generating revenues that dwarfed anything available in the legal economy and providing the capital for organizational investments, including corruption, that entrenched criminal enterprise in the political fabric of American cities.

The organizational demands of large-scale bootlegging forced criminal enterprises into new levels of sophistication. Managing illegal breweries and distilleries, maintaining distribution networks, bribing officials at multiple levels of government, negotiating territorial arrangements with competitors, and disciplining employees required skills and structures that went well beyond the relatively simple operations of pre-Prohibition criminal enterprise. The men who met these demands most successfully, figures like Johnny Torrio and Arnold Rothstein, pioneered approaches to criminal organization that emphasized rational economic management, strategic use of violence rather than indiscriminate force, and systematic corruption of public officials as essential business infrastructure.

Violence was nonetheless endemic during Prohibition, because the enormous profits of the bootlegging economy made territorial control enormously valuable and because no legal mechanism existed for resolving disputes among competing criminal organizations. The gang wars of the 1920s in Chicago and other cities produced death tolls that shocked contemporaries, and the St. Valentine's Day Massacre of 1929 became a defining symbol of the disorder that Prohibition had produced. The violence was not random but strategic, following the logic of market competition in an industry where the normal legal mechanisms of commerce were unavailable.

The speakeasy, the illegal drinking establishment of the Prohibition era, became one of the most culturally significant institutions of the 1920s. More than a substitute for the legal saloon, the speakeasy was a site where social boundaries were crossed or blurred in ways that had lasting cultural consequences. Women drank publicly in significant numbers for the first time. Jazz music, with its African American roots and its improvisational energy, found its commercial audience in speakeasy clubs and entertainment venues. The social world of the speakeasy contributed to the broader cultural transformation of the 1920s, the Jazz Age, in ways that made Prohibition's restrictions seem not merely inconvenient but symbolically at odds with a modernizing American culture.

The geography of Prohibition-era criminal supply was international. The U.S.-Canadian border, particularly the Detroit River crossing, was the most important corridor for legal foreign alcohol entering the United States illegally. Rum Row, the informal fleet of foreign ships anchored just outside U.S. territorial waters off the Atlantic Coast, supplied premium imported spirits to smugglers who raced back to shore under Coast Guard pursuit. Caribbean routes through the Bahamas and Cuba fed the southeastern market. These international supply chains required coordination among criminal organizations in multiple countries and contributed to the internationalization of criminal enterprise that would accelerate in the post-Prohibition decades.

Political corruption was as essential to Prohibition-era criminal enterprise as any other operational element. Criminal organizations that could not afford to corrupt sufficient police officers, judges, and elected officials could not operate at scale. The corruption that Prohibition generated was not merely a series of individual acts of bribery but a structural feature of the relationship between criminal enterprise and government in many American cities, a form of political economy in which criminal organizations effectively purchased the tolerance they required to operate. This corruption had institutional consequences that extended well beyond the era of Prohibition itself.

The repeal of Prohibition in December 1933, through the Twenty-First Amendment, did not end the criminal revolution that the Eighteenth Amendment had produced. The organizations built during Prohibition, with their capital, their political connections, their corruption networks, and their management experience, transferred their capacity to other criminal markets. Gambling, labor racketeering, and eventually drug trafficking absorbed the organizational investments of the Prohibition era and sustained the criminal enterprises that had been built upon the illegal alcohol trade. This post-repeal survival of Prohibition-era criminal enterprises is one of the most important pieces of evidence for understanding what Prohibition actually accomplished: not the elimination of criminal enterprise, but the creation of a criminal infrastructure that would shape American organized crime for decades.

The lessons that Prohibition teaches remain relevant to contemporary debates about criminal justice policy. The case demonstrates with particular clarity the relationship between legal prohibition, black market formation, and criminal enterprise. When a widely desired commodity is prohibited, the demand for it does not disappear; it transfers to whoever is willing and able to supply it illegally. The criminal enterprises that supply illegal markets derive their power, their profits, and their organizational capacity from the prohibition itself. Whether the social harms reduced by prohibition outweigh the social harms generated by the criminal enterprises that prohibition creates is a question that requires careful empirical analysis in each specific case, and the Prohibition experience suggests that the answer is not always what prohibition's advocates expect.

Key Terms

Alky cooking
The illicit distillation of alcoholic spirits from grain or other fermentable materials; common in immigrant communities during Prohibition as a source of income and of alcohol for personal or neighborhood consumption.
Anti-Saloon League
Single-issue political lobbying organization founded 1893; widely regarded as the most effective pressure group in the campaign for national Prohibition, mobilizing electoral support against candidates who opposed temperance legislation.
Black market
An illegal market for goods or services that are prohibited by law or subject to excessive regulation; characterized by premium prices, absence of legal consumer protection, and enforcement through violence rather than contract.
Bootlegging
The illegal manufacture, transportation, and sale of alcohol during Prohibition; the primary criminal enterprise of the 1920s, generating revenues estimated in the billions of dollars annually at peak.
Bureau of Prohibition
Federal agency created by the Volstead Act to enforce Prohibition; chronically underfunded and understaffed, with agents susceptible to bribery due to low salaries and inadequate support from political authorities.
Chicago Outfit
The criminal organization that came to dominate bootlegging and other criminal enterprises in Chicago during Prohibition, eventually led by Al Capone; one of the most powerful and long-lasting criminal organizations in American history.
Corruption
The use of illegal payments, gifts, or other inducements to persuade public officials to violate their legal duties; essential infrastructure for large-scale criminal enterprise during Prohibition and a major institutional consequence of the era.
Criminal franchise
An arrangement in which a criminal organization provides supply, protection, and brand recognition to retail operations in exchange for payments and compliance with organizational rules; approximates the structure of a legitimate franchise business.
Denaturing
The addition of toxic substances to industrial alcohol to render it undrinkable and prevent its diversion for consumption; required under the Volstead Act but circumvented by criminal redistillation operations.
Dry
A term used to describe a person, community, or political position favoring Prohibition or alcohol restriction; the opposite of "wet."
Eighteenth Amendment
Constitutional amendment ratified January 1919, prohibiting the manufacture, sale, and transportation of intoxicating liquors; the legal foundation of Prohibition in the United States.
Enforcement gap
The practical space between formal legal prohibition and actual enforcement capacity; the larger the enforcement gap, the more freely criminal enterprises can operate with legal immunity effectively purchased through corruption.
Hijacking
The theft of bootleg alcohol from rival criminal organizations or independent suppliers; a significant source of inter-organizational violence during Prohibition and a persistent problem for bootlegging supply chains.
Industrial alcohol
Alcohol produced for manufacturing purposes, legal under Prohibition but required to be denatured; a major source of bootleg spirits when criminal operators redistilled it to remove toxic additives, with sometimes fatal results.
Organized violence
Violence used strategically by criminal enterprises to enforce territorial claims, discipline members, intimidate competitors, and maintain compliance among suppliers and retail customers; distinguished from random violence by its purposeful, instrumental character.
Protection payment
A regular payment made by a criminal enterprise to law enforcement officers, judges, or politicians in exchange for immunity from prosecution or interference with operations; a structural feature of criminal enterprise wherever the enforcement gap is significant.
Racketeering
The operation of an illegal business or scheme, or the extortion of legitimate businesses under threat of violence; the term expanded during Prohibition to cover the full range of criminal enterprise from bootlegging to labor extortion.
Repeal
The elimination of a law through subsequent legislation or constitutional amendment; the Twenty-First Amendment, ratified December 1933, repealed the Eighteenth Amendment and ended national Prohibition.
Rum Row
The informal fleet of foreign ships anchored in international waters just outside U.S. territorial jurisdiction off the Atlantic Coast, selling legally purchased imported liquor to American smugglers who transported it to shore.
Rum runner
A person or vessel engaged in smuggling illegal alcohol across water, particularly across the Atlantic Coast or from Canada; rum runners typically operated fast motorboats designed to outpace Coast Guard vessels.
Speakeasy
An illegal drinking establishment operating during Prohibition; could range from a simple basement operation to an elaborate nightclub, but was defined by the need for concealment from law enforcement and dependence on protection payments to operate.
St. Valentine's Day Massacre
The murder of seven members of the Bugs Moran gang in Chicago on February 14, 1929; the most dramatic single episode of Prohibition-era gang violence and a significant factor in shifting public opinion against criminal tolerance.
Supply chain
The network of producers, transporters, wholesalers, and retailers through which a product moves from origin to consumer; bootlegging operations developed complex illegal supply chains spanning multiple states and international borders.
Syndicate
A cooperative association of criminal enterprises sharing territories, resources, or coordination mechanisms; during Prohibition, the development of criminal syndicates represented a significant organizational advance over purely competitive criminal arrangements.
Temperance movement
The social and political movement, active for more than a century before Prohibition, that sought to restrict or eliminate alcohol consumption on grounds of public health, family welfare, and moral improvement.
Territorial violence
Violence used to establish, defend, or expand a criminal organization's geographic sphere of operations; a characteristic feature of criminal markets where exclusive control of a territory generates monopoly profits.
Twenty-First Amendment
Constitutional amendment ratified December 1933, repealing the Eighteenth Amendment and ending national Prohibition; the only amendment in American history to repeal a previous amendment.
Volstead Act
Federal legislation (National Prohibition Act, 1919) implementing the Eighteenth Amendment; defined prohibited beverages, established penalties, created enforcement mechanisms, and specified exemptions that were widely exploited.
Wet
A term used to describe a person, community, or political position opposing Prohibition or favoring the legal sale of alcohol; the opposite of "dry."
Wickersham Commission
Federal commission on crime and law enforcement, established 1929; its 1931 report documented the widespread failure of Prohibition enforcement and provided intellectual ammunition for the repeal movement.

Chapter Reflection

Prohibition and the criminal revolution it produced occupy a central place in the global history of organized crime because they illustrate, with unusual clarity, the relationship between legal prohibition, market forces, and criminal enterprise. The Eighteenth Amendment was not merely a policy failure in the sense of failing to achieve its intended goals. It was a generative event, one that produced new institutional forms, new organizational capacities, and new relationships between criminal enterprise and political power that outlasted the amendment itself by decades. Understanding what Prohibition produced, and why, is essential to understanding not only the history of American organized crime but the broader mechanisms through which criminal enterprises emerge, grow, and consolidate.

The criminal revolution of the 1920s also invites reflection on the relationship between the law, popular culture, and social behavior. Prohibition was a constitutional law, ratified by the required three-quarters of states, representing the considered judgment of democratic majorities that alcohol was a social harm severe enough to prohibit. And yet it failed, not because it was secretly unpopular, but because the millions of Americans who had supported it in the abstract were not willing, when the moment came, to stop drinking in practice. The gap between stated moral preference and actual behavior is not unique to Prohibition; it is a feature of moral legislation generally. But the Prohibition case illustrates it with unusual clarity, and the criminal enterprises that filled the gap between legal prohibition and social demand were, in a real sense, providing a service that millions of ordinary Americans actively chose to purchase.

The post-Prohibition survival of the criminal enterprises that bootlegging had built is perhaps the most important lesson of the era for understanding the long-term development of organized crime. When the Twenty-First Amendment made alcohol legal again, the men and organizations that had grown wealthy and powerful during Prohibition did not dissolve or retire. They transferred their capital, their political connections, their management experience, and their organizational structures to other criminal markets, primarily gambling in the 1930s and 1940s and eventually drug trafficking from the 1960s onward. The criminal infrastructure of Prohibition thus became the foundation for the American organized crime of the mid-twentieth century. The chapters that follow, examining Al Capone, the American Mafia, and the evolution of criminal enterprise in the post-war decades, all build on the organizational legacy of the Prohibition era.

Prohibition also raises enduring questions about the appropriate scope and limits of criminal law as a tool of social policy, questions that are not merely academic but urgently relevant to contemporary debates about drug policy, gambling regulation, sex work, and other areas where the law attempts to suppress widely desired activities. The Prohibition experience does not provide simple answers to these questions: it does not prove that all legal prohibitions are counterproductive, nor does it prove that all criminalized activities should be legalized. What it does provide is a richly documented case study of what happens when a legal prohibition is imposed on a widely desired commodity, how criminal enterprises respond, and what the long-term institutional consequences are. Students who understand the Prohibition case are better equipped to analyze contemporary policy debates with the historical insight that those debates require.

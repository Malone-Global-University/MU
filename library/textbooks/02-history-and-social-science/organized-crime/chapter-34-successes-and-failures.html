Successes and Failures

Organized Crime: A Global History

Chapter Opening

"The history of fighting organized crime is, at its core, a history of partial victories and incomplete defeats, of institutions learning and criminals adapting, of society demanding order and finding that order has a price."

On the morning of February 26, 1985, federal prosecutors in New York City unsealed one of the most ambitious indictments in American legal history. Charged under the Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act, the bosses of all five New York Mafia families, along with the chairman of the Mafia's governing body known as the Commission, faced trial simultaneously for crimes that ranged from murder and extortion to labor racketeering and bid-rigging in the concrete industry. The prosecutorial strategy was bold, the evidence painstakingly assembled over years of wiretaps, informant testimony, and financial forensics. When the verdicts came in on November 19, 1986, all of the defendants were convicted. Sentences ranged up to one hundred years in federal prison. The government had scored what appeared, in the moment, to be a decisive victory. The American Mafia, observers declared, was finished.

It was not finished. Within a decade, new bosses had assumed control of the families, new rackets had been established, and the Commission, though weakened and wary, continued to function in modified form. Arrests, convictions, and imprisonments had inflicted serious damage, but organized crime had not been eliminated. The Mafia had adapted, become more cautious, delegated more, communicated less openly, and rebuilt. The victory of 1986 was real and significant, but it was also partial and provisional, subject to the countervailing pressures of criminal resilience and institutional memory.

The story of the Commission trial is, in miniature, the story of the entire history of fighting organized crime. Across more than a century, in every country where powerful criminal organizations have taken root, law enforcement has achieved genuine successes: prosecutions that dismantled leadership structures, asset forfeitures that stripped criminal networks of accumulated wealth, legislation that created new tools for investigation and prosecution, international cooperation that reached across borders to disrupt transnational operations. These successes were real, often heroic in their execution, and genuinely consequential. The world is different, in measurable ways, because of them.

And yet, across the same century and in the same countries, organized crime has persisted, adapted, migrated, and in some respects expanded. Colombian cartels were dismantled in the 1990s, only to be replaced by more decentralized Mexican trafficking organizations. The Italian-American Mafia was severely weakened by the prosecutions of the 1980s and 1990s, but organized crime did not disappear from American cities; it transformed, fragmented, and in some areas was supplanted by new criminal networks with different ethnic bases, different organizational structures, and different operational methods. Russian organized crime expanded dramatically after the collapse of the Soviet Union in 1991, filling vacuums left by the sudden withdrawal of state authority. Cybercrime organizations emerged in the 2000s and 2010s, operating across national boundaries with a speed and anonymity that traditional law enforcement frameworks were ill-equipped to address.

Understanding why this pattern repeats, why genuine victories have not produced permanent suppression, requires asking hard questions about the nature of organized crime, the structure of the institutions that fight it, and the social and economic conditions that generate and sustain it. It requires examining not just what happened in famous prosecutions but what happened afterward, not just the verdicts but the consequences, not just the strategies that worked but why they worked only partially and temporarily. It requires, in other words, a serious and honest reckoning with what law enforcement and legal systems can and cannot accomplish when confronting criminal organizations that are embedded in economic life, connected to political power, and capable of learning and adaptation.

This chapter examines the most significant prosecutions in the modern history of fighting organized crime, analyzes the mechanisms of criminal adaptation that have allowed criminal organizations to survive and evolve despite sustained law enforcement pressure, and draws out the lessons, both encouraging and sobering, that this history offers. The goal is not to demoralize students of criminal justice or to suggest that the fight against organized crime is futile. It is not futile. Real achievements have been made, real suffering has been prevented, and real communities have been protected by the work of investigators, prosecutors, and policymakers who devoted careers to these problems. But the goal is equally not to offer easy comfort or to pretend that success in one prosecution solves the underlying problem. The history is more complicated than either despair or triumphalism allows, and understanding that complexity is essential to thinking clearly about what fighting organized crime requires and what it can realistically achieve.

The chapter is organized around three interlocking questions. First, what have the most significant prosecutions in organized crime history actually accomplished, and how should historians evaluate their success? Second, how have criminal organizations adapted to law enforcement pressure, and what does this adaptive capacity reveal about the nature of organized crime as an institution? Third, what lessons does this history offer, and what do those lessons suggest about strategies, policies, and priorities for the future? These questions connect the specific history of celebrated prosecutions to the larger analytical questions that have occupied criminologists, legal scholars, policymakers, and historians for generations.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Identify the most significant prosecutions in the modern history of fighting organized crime, from the Commission trial to the takedown of major drug cartels, and explain why each was historically important.
  2. Evaluate what constitutes success in prosecuting organized crime, distinguishing between short-term operational disruption and long-term structural suppression.
  3. Analyze the mechanisms by which criminal organizations adapt to law enforcement pressure, including decentralization, succession planning, corruption of institutions, and migration to new criminal markets.
  4. Explain the role of informants and cooperating witnesses in major prosecutions, and assess the ethical and practical tensions inherent in relying on criminal testimony.
  5. Describe how the RICO statute transformed American organized crime prosecution, and compare American approaches to equivalent legislation in other countries.
  6. Trace the consequences of major prosecutions beyond the courtroom, examining how dismantled organizations were replaced, restructured, or succeeded by new criminal enterprises.
  7. Compare the effectiveness of different anti-organized crime strategies, including prosecution, asset forfeiture, demand reduction, and institutional reform, across different national and historical contexts.
  8. Assess the relationship between law enforcement success against specific organizations and broader trends in criminal violence and social harm, asking whether prosecutions reduce aggregate harm or simply redistribute it.
  9. Connect the history of fighting organized crime to the larger questions about the relationship between the state, illegal markets, and organized criminal enterprise raised throughout this textbook.
  10. Formulate historically grounded assessments of what lessons the past century of organized crime prosecution offers for present and future policy.

Historical Context

To understand the successes and failures of fighting organized crime, students must understand the institutional and legal environment in which those fights took place, and how dramatically that environment changed over the course of the twentieth century. For most of American history, law enforcement lacked both the legal tools and the organizational capacity to address organized crime as a systemic problem rather than a collection of individual criminal acts. Local police departments were often corrupt or politically compromised. Federal jurisdiction over criminal activity was limited. Investigative methods that would later prove essential, including electronic surveillance, informant programs, and financial forensics, either did not exist in usable legal form or were legally restricted. And the concept of criminal conspiracy, though present in the law, was applied in ways that made it difficult to hold the leaders of criminal organizations responsible for crimes they ordered but did not personally commit.

The transformation began in the mid-twentieth century, accelerated by the political pressures generated by high-profile exposés of organized crime and by the growing recognition that traditional enforcement approaches were inadequate. The Kefauver Committee hearings of 1950 and 1951, which broadcast testimony about Mafia operations to a national television audience for the first time, galvanized public opinion and political attention. The McClellan Committee hearings of the late 1950s, in which a young Robert Kennedy served as chief counsel, further documented the reach of organized crime into labor unions, legitimate businesses, and political systems. These hearings created political pressure for new legal tools and new investigative resources.

The most consequential legal development came in 1970, with the passage of the Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act, universally known as RICO. Building on earlier conspiracy law but extending it dramatically, RICO allowed prosecutors to charge individuals with participating in a pattern of racketeering activity through a criminal enterprise, even if they had not personally committed the underlying criminal acts. This made it legally possible, for the first time, to prosecute the bosses of criminal organizations for the crimes of their subordinates, to seize assets connected to criminal enterprises, and to bring extended conspiracy charges that reflected the ongoing and organized nature of criminal activity rather than treating each crime as a separate incident. RICO transformed American organized crime prosecution, and its impact was felt across the 1980s and 1990s in a series of spectacular trials that fundamentally weakened the Italian-American Mafia.

Internationally, the development of equivalent tools proceeded unevenly and at different paces. Italy enacted its own anti-Mafia legislation in stages across the 1980s and 1990s, culminating in the work of magistrates like Giovanni Falcone and Paolo Borsellino, whose investigative methods and prosecutorial strategies contributed enormously to the understanding and prosecution of Sicilian organized crime before both men were assassinated by the Mafia in 1992. Colombia's fight against the major cocaine cartels in the late 1980s and early 1990s involved the use of military force alongside judicial prosecution, with consequences that were both real and deeply problematic. Mexico's struggle against powerful drug-trafficking organizations has involved strategies ranging from targeted killing of cartel leaders to mass prosecution to negotiated accommodation, with results that remain deeply contested.

Understanding this context is essential because the successes and failures of fighting organized crime cannot be understood as simply the product of individual investigators' skill or prosecutors' ingenuity. They were produced by the interaction of legal tools, institutional capacity, political will, international cooperation, and, crucially, the underlying social and economic conditions that determine whether criminal organizations can recruit, operate, and sustain themselves. The same prosecution strategy that works in one context may fail in another because the underlying conditions differ. And the same criminal organization that is devastated by prosecution in one decade may rebuild in the next because the conditions that generated it have not changed.

This contextual understanding is also essential for evaluating claims of success. When law enforcement agencies announce that they have dismantled a major criminal organization, historians and students should ask: dismantled in what sense? Has the leadership been removed? Have the criminal markets it served been eliminated? Has the underlying network of relationships, corruption, and criminal capital been destroyed? Or have the leaders been replaced, the markets survived, and the network reconstituted under new management? The answers to these questions determine whether a prosecution represents a genuine victory or a pyrrhic one, and the historical record suggests that the distinction matters enormously.

Section 34.1

Famous Prosecutions

The history of organized crime prosecution in the modern era can be traced through a series of cases that were not merely legally significant but historically transformative, reshaping the organizations they targeted and the legal and institutional frameworks through which they were pursued. These prosecutions were products of specific historical moments, specific legal tools, and specific configurations of political will and institutional capacity. Understanding them requires examining not just the courtroom drama of trials and verdicts but the investigative labor of years, the policy decisions that created new legal tools, and the consequences that followed long after the sentences were handed down.

The American Mafia and the RICO Revolution

For most of the twentieth century, the Italian-American Mafia operated with a degree of impunity that reflected both the limitations of law enforcement tools and the active corruption of many of the political and judicial institutions that should have constrained it. Local police departments in major cities were often on the Mafia's payroll. Judicial corruption was not uncommon. And the organizational structure of the Mafia, which placed criminal orders several layers of hierarchy above the actual commission of crimes, made it extremely difficult to prosecute bosses for the activities of their subordinates using existing criminal law. Individual members could be prosecuted for individual crimes, but the enterprise as a whole was legally difficult to reach.

The Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act of 1970 changed this fundamentally. Drafted by Notre Dame law professor G. Robert Blakey and championed through Congress by Robert Kennedy, RICO created a new legal theory: that participation in a criminal enterprise through a pattern of racketeering activity was itself a crime, regardless of whether the participant had personally committed the underlying criminal acts. The law defined "racketeering activity" broadly to include virtually any serious federal or state crime, and it defined "pattern" to require only two acts of racketeering within a ten-year period. Critically, it provided for penalties of up to twenty years in prison per count and allowed for the civil and criminal forfeiture of assets connected to the criminal enterprise. It also allowed the government to bring sweeping conspiracy charges that reflected the ongoing, organized, and hierarchical nature of criminal enterprises in ways that traditional conspiracy law had not permitted.

For more than a decade after its passage, RICO was used primarily in financial fraud and relatively conventional organized crime cases. Its full potential against the Mafia was not realized until the early 1980s, when a new generation of federal prosecutors in New York, led by Rudolph Giuliani as United States Attorney for the Southern District, developed the strategy of using RICO to prosecute the entire leadership of a Mafia family or, even more ambitiously, the Commission itself, as a single criminal enterprise. The legal theory was audacious: that the Commission, which coordinated activities among the five New York families, was itself a RICO enterprise, and that its members were guilty of the full range of criminal activities carried out by the families under its governance.

The Commission trial, which began in September 1985 and produced its verdicts in November 1986, was the culmination of this strategy. The defendants included the bosses or acting bosses of all five New York families: Anthony Salerno of the Genovese family, Carmine Persico of the Colombo family, Anthony Corallo of the Lucchese family, Philip Rastelli of the Bonanno family, and Paul Castellano of the Gambino family (who was murdered by John Gotti in December 1985 before the trial concluded, and whose case was severed). The evidence included thousands of hours of recordings from court-authorized wiretaps and from a listening device planted by the FBI in the social club where Salerno and others regularly met. The recordings were devastating, capturing defendants discussing murders, extortion payments, and the internal governance of the Commission in their own voices. All were convicted. Salerno and Corallo received one hundred-year sentences. Persico received one hundred and thirty-nine years.

The Commission trial was followed by a series of related prosecutions that dismantled the leadership structures of each of the five families through the late 1980s and into the 1990s. The Gambino family boss John Gotti, who had escaped earlier prosecutions partly through jury tampering and witness intimidation, was finally convicted in 1992 largely on the testimony of his former underboss Salvatore Gravano, who pleaded guilty to nineteen murders and cooperated with the government in exchange for a reduced sentence. Gotti received a life sentence. The Lucchese family, the Colombo family, and the Genovese family all suffered similar leadership decapitations through the same period. By the mid-1990s, the combined effect of the Commission trial, the individual family prosecutions, and the continued use of cooperating witnesses had reduced the New York Mafia to a shadow of its former power. It continued to exist and operate, but at a dramatically diminished scale and with a dramatically diminished capacity for the kind of coordinated, large-scale criminal activity that had characterized its peak decades.

The Italian Maxi-Trials

In Italy, a parallel effort to prosecute the Sicilian Mafia at a systemic rather than individual level produced both remarkable legal achievements and tragic human costs. The strategy was developed principally by two magistrates working in Palermo, Giovanni Falcone and Paolo Borsellino, who in the early 1980s began applying to Mafia prosecution the same philosophy that drove American RICO cases: that the Mafia was an organization, not merely a collection of individuals, and that prosecuting it effectively required prosecuting the organization as a whole. Their approach was reinforced by the testimony of Tommaso Buscetta, a senior Mafia figure who became the first major pentito, or repentant criminal, to cooperate with Italian prosecutors, beginning in 1984. Buscetta's testimony provided Falcone with an authoritative insider account of the Sicilian Mafia's structure, governance, and internal politics that made it possible to construct prosecutions connecting specific crimes to specific organizational roles and decisions.

The result was the Palermo maxi-trial of 1986–1987, in which 474 defendants were tried simultaneously in a specially constructed fortified courthouse, resulting in convictions for 360 of them. The scale and ambition of the prosecution was unprecedented in Italian legal history. It was, for a moment, a triumph. But the Italian justice system's review processes were lengthy, and many of the convictions were overturned or reduced by intermediate appellate courts in the following years. The Italian Supreme Court, the Corte di Cassazione, reversed a large portion of the maxi-trial verdicts in 1991, a decision that the Mafia celebrated and that deeply demoralized Italian law enforcement.

The Mafia's response to the sustained prosecution campaign was not merely legal but lethal. In 1982, the Mafia had assassinated General Carlo Alberto Dalla Chiesa, the national police commander who had been sent to Sicily to lead the anti-Mafia effort after his success in dismantling the Red Brigades terrorist organization. In May 1992, a bomb planted under the highway between Palermo airport and the city killed Giovanni Falcone, his wife, and three police bodyguards. Two months later, a car bomb killed Paolo Borsellino and five bodyguards outside his mother's apartment in Palermo. These murders provoked national outrage and a political response that ultimately strengthened rather than weakened the anti-Mafia effort: new legislation, including the formal criminalization of Mafia membership, was passed, police resources were dramatically expanded, and a new wave of pentiti came forward, partly motivated by the revelation that even senior Mafia figures were not safe from the organization's internal violence. By the mid-1990s, most of the Sicilian Mafia's top leadership, including the godfather Totò Riina, who had been a fugitive for decades, had been arrested.

The War Against the Colombian Cartels

The prosecution of the Colombian cocaine cartels in the late 1980s and early 1990s represented a different kind of confrontation, one in which the line between law enforcement and military conflict was systematically blurred, and in which the definition of success was from the beginning deeply contested. The Medellín Cartel, led by Pablo Escobar and his associates, had by the mid-1980s become one of the most powerful and violent criminal organizations in the world, generating annual revenues estimated in the billions of dollars and conducting a campaign of terror against the Colombian state that included the assassination of judges, politicians, police officers, and journalists, the bombing of civilian aircraft, and the murder of presidential candidates.

The Colombian government's response evolved through several phases. Early efforts at legal prosecution were undermined by the murder and intimidation of judges and witnesses, and by the explicit policy of many cartel leaders of offering anyone who accepted their bribes the choice between "plata o plomo," silver or lead, money or death. In 1984, Justice Minister Rodrigo Lara Bonilla, who had spearheaded an aggressive anti-cartel campaign, was assassinated, provoking the government to extradite leading traffickers to the United States, where they faced harsher sentences and could not directly threaten Colombian judges and witnesses. Escobar and his associates responded by declaring war on the extradition treaty, a campaign of violence that ultimately claimed the lives of hundreds of police officers, three presidential candidates, and thousands of civilians.

The eventual dismantling of the Medellín Cartel came not through a single dramatic prosecution but through a combination of sustained military and police operations, informant intelligence, increasingly sophisticated electronic surveillance, and crucially, the cooperation of rival criminal organizations, including the Cali Cartel, which provided intelligence on Escobar's whereabouts to Colombian authorities. Escobar surrendered to Colombian authorities in 1991, under conditions he effectively negotiated himself, including confinement to a luxury prison he largely designed, and escaped in 1992. He was killed in December 1993 by Colombian security forces acting on intelligence provided partly by his own communications. The Medellín Cartel effectively collapsed with his death.

The Cali Cartel, which had cooperated in Escobar's downfall while expanding its own operations, initially appeared to have emerged from the conflict in a stronger position. But pressure from the United States, including the threat of decertification of Colombia as a partner in counter-narcotics efforts, eventually produced a major crackdown. The principal Cali leaders were arrested between 1995 and 1997 and eventually extradited to the United States, where they faced decades in federal prison. By the end of the decade, the organizational structures of both major Colombian cartels had been destroyed.

What followed illustrated the limits of this success more clearly than the victory itself. The destruction of the Medellín and Cali cartels did not reduce the flow of cocaine into the United States. It shifted the organizational geography of cocaine trafficking, disaggregating the industry into smaller, more numerous, and more geographically dispersed operations and, critically, transferring the highest-value and highest-risk trafficking functions to Mexican organizations that had previously served as transporters for the Colombian cartels. The Mexican trafficking organizations, particularly those that would evolve into the Sinaloa Cartel and the Gulf Cartel, dramatically expanded their power, wealth, and violence in the vacuum left by the Colombian cartels' destruction. The drug supply did not diminish; the criminal organizations that supplied it adapted, migrated, and reconstituted themselves in new forms.

Prosecutions in the Drug War Era

The prosecution of Mexican drug-trafficking organizations in the late 1990s and 2000s produced some celebrated convictions, but the structural problems that had limited the success of the Colombian prosecutions were, if anything, more acute in Mexico, where institutional corruption ran deeper, where the geographic proximity to the United States created both greater criminal opportunity and greater political complexity, and where the fragmentation of the cartel landscape that followed major prosecutions consistently produced more violent competition rather than reduced criminal activity.

The capture and prosecution of major cartel leaders became a defining feature of Mexican counter-narcotics strategy, particularly after 2006 when President Felipe Calderón launched a military-led campaign against the cartels. High-profile captures and extraditions produced real operational disruption for targeted organizations. But the pattern of consequences was consistent: the removal of a cartel's top leadership typically produced violent internal succession conflicts and inter-cartel wars as rival factions and organizations competed to fill the vacuum. The fragmentation of the Tijuana Cartel after the death of Amado Carrillo Fuentes in 1997, the violent implosion of the Arellano Félix organization in the early 2000s, and the catastrophic violence that followed the arrest of Arturo Beltrán Leyva in 2009 all illustrated the same dynamic: prosecution and killing of leaders generated short-term disruption at the cost of increased violence and organizational multiplication.

The most celebrated Mexican cartel prosecution of the twenty-first century was the case of Joaquín "El Chapo" Guzmán, the Sinaloa Cartel co-founder whose extraordinary escapes from Mexican prison, including a 2015 tunnel escape that caused a national and international scandal, made him perhaps the most famous criminal figure in the world. His 2016 extradition to the United States and his 2019 federal conviction, which produced a life sentence plus thirty years, was genuinely significant: it removed from the field a figure whose personal authority and organizational relationships had been central to the Sinaloa Cartel's operation for three decades. But the Sinaloa Cartel did not collapse. It continued to operate, led by other figures including Guzmán's sons and his longtime partner Ismael "El Mayo" Zambada, who was himself eventually arrested in 2024 in circumstances that were immediately controversial. The organization remained, by most assessments, the most powerful drug-trafficking network in the world even after its founder's incarceration.

International and Financial Prosecutions

Beyond the headline prosecutions of Mafia bosses and cartel leaders, the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries produced significant achievements in international cooperation and financial prosecution that, while less cinematically dramatic, represented important developments in the sophistication of the fight against organized crime. The prosecution of money laundering networks, in particular, became an increasingly important tool as law enforcement recognized that the financial infrastructure of criminal organizations was, in some respects, more vulnerable and more consequential than the leadership structures targeted by traditional prosecution.

The Bank of Credit and Commerce International scandal of the early 1990s revealed how criminal financial flows could be embedded in ostensibly legitimate banking systems and prompted significant tightening of international financial regulation. The Financial Action Task Force, established in 1989, developed international standards for anti-money-laundering measures and created mechanisms for evaluating national compliance that gave international pressure for financial transparency a formal institutional structure. These developments were slow-moving and imperfectly implemented, but they represented a genuine shift in how international institutions approached the financial dimension of organized crime.

The prosecution of Russian organized crime posed particular challenges, partly because of the scale and sophistication of Russian criminal financial networks and partly because of the deep interpenetration of organized crime and political power that characterized the Russian state in the post-Soviet period. The most consequential prosecutions of Russian organized crime figures in this period occurred not in Russia but in the United States and Europe, where American and European prosecutors pursued money laundering cases against figures whose activities touched American and European financial systems. These cases were individually significant, but they barely scratched the surface of the larger problem, and the structural conditions that generated and sustained Russian organized crime, including the concentration of economic and political power in the hands of figures whose wealth derived from the criminalized privatization of the Soviet economy, remained largely unchanged.

The Commission Indictment (1985) and the RICO Framework

Source type: Federal criminal indictment · Date: February 1985 · Jurisdiction: Southern District of New York

Context: The Commission indictment, formally titled United States v. Salerno, was the capstone of a multi-year investigation code-named GENUS that used electronic surveillance, informant intelligence, and financial analysis to document the activities of the Mafia's governing Commission. The indictment charged the defendants not merely with individual crimes but with participation in the Commission itself as a racketeering enterprise, and alleged a pattern of criminal activity that included labor racketeering in the concrete industry, loansharking, and the authorization of murders. The legal theory, though novel in its ambition, had been carefully developed to survive appellate scrutiny, and it did.

The indictment alleged that the Commission of La Cosa Nostra constituted an enterprise within the meaning of RICO, that the defendants had participated in the conduct of that enterprise's affairs through a pattern of racketeering activity, and that the enterprise had operated through the five New York Mafia families as subsidiary criminal organizations subject to the Commission's governance. It further alleged that the Commission had allocated criminal territories, mediated disputes between families, authorized murders, and coordinated labor racketeering activities in the New York construction industry, including a cartel arrangement in the concrete business that had added hundreds of millions of dollars to construction costs.

Analysis: The Commission indictment represents the fullest realization of the legal theory embedded in RICO: that organized crime could be prosecuted as an organization rather than merely as a collection of individuals committing individual crimes. Its significance lay as much in what it revealed about the nature of the Mafia as in the convictions it produced. By demonstrating that the Commission was a real governing institution with real authority over member families, the prosecution made it impossible to continue treating organized crime as a myth or an exaggeration. At the same time, the indictment's framing raised important questions about the limits of the organizational approach: if criminal enterprises are prosecuted as organizations, the implication is that dismantling the organization's formal structure will dismantle the enterprise. The subsequent history suggests that this implication is only partially correct.

Criminal Adaptation

One of the most consistent findings in the study of organized crime prosecution is that criminal organizations, when subjected to sustained law enforcement pressure, adapt rather than collapse. This adaptive capacity is not accidental or surprising; it is a consequence of the same organizational and institutional features that make criminal enterprises successful in the first place. Organizations that have survived competition, violence, and state pressure over decades or generations develop the capacity to learn from adversity, to adjust organizational structures and practices in response to threats, and to maintain core criminal competencies even as external forms change. Understanding the mechanisms of this adaptation is essential for evaluating the long-term significance of prosecutorial victories and for thinking clearly about what kinds of enforcement strategies might be more or less effective.

Structural Adaptation: From Hierarchy to Network

Perhaps the most significant structural adaptation that criminal organizations have made in response to sustained prosecution has been the shift from hierarchical to more networked or cellular organizational forms. The Italian-American Mafia's vulnerability to the Commission-style prosecution derived in part from its hierarchical structure: the Commission itself was a single governing institution whose membership could be identified and charged, and the family structures below it had clear chains of command that could be documented through surveillance and informant testimony. Once prosecutors understood the structure and had the legal tools to charge participation in the enterprise as a whole, the hierarchy became a liability rather than an asset.

The criminal organizations that developed in the aftermath of the Mafia's decline, and the Mafia itself in its diminished post-prosecution form, were in significant respects less hierarchical and more networked. Rather than formal families with clearly defined bosses, underbosses, capos, and soldiers, post-prosecution organized crime in the United States increasingly operated through looser networks of individuals and crews connected by relationships of trust and shared criminal interest rather than by formal organizational rank. This made the organizations harder to prosecute using the RICO enterprise theory because the "enterprise" was less clearly defined and less formally structured. It also made them less susceptible to the leadership decapitation strategy that had proved so effective against the Commission-era Mafia, because there was less clearly defined leadership to decapitate.

The same shift toward networked organization was visible in the evolution of drug-trafficking organizations after the destruction of the Colombian cartels. The Medellín and Cali cartels had been relatively centralized organizations, with identifiable leadership, defined territorial control, and relatively integrated operations that ran from coca cultivation through processing, transportation, wholesale distribution, and money laundering. Their successors, both in Colombia and Mexico, were in significant respects more fragmented and more networked, operating through systems of strategic alliances, contractual arrangements, and specialized functional divisions that were harder to prosecute as unified enterprises. The Sinaloa Cartel, for example, functioned less as a single organization than as a network of allied criminal entrepreneurs, coordinated by Guzmán and his partners but not directly controlled in the way that a hierarchical organization would be. This structure made it more resilient, even if it also made it, in some respects, harder to manage and coordinate.

Operational Security Adaptations

Criminal organizations have also adapted their operational security practices in response to the specific investigative tools that have proved most damaging to them. The Commission trial's devastating use of electronic surveillance, particularly the recordings made by FBI bugs planted in Mafia social clubs, prompted a profound change in how surviving Mafia figures conducted their business. Conversations about criminal matters moved outdoors, where surveillance was more difficult. Face-to-face meetings replaced telephone calls. Written communications were avoided. Coded language became more elaborate and more frequently used. In some cases, organizations adopted the explicit counter-surveillance practices developed by security-conscious actors in other contexts, including the use of disposable phones, in-person dead drops, and the deliberate compartmentalization of information so that any single member who was arrested or turned informant knew only a limited portion of the organization's overall activities.

Drug-trafficking organizations adapted similarly in response to the surveillance and interdiction methods that had proved effective against them. Smuggling routes changed frequently, exploiting new vulnerabilities in customs and border enforcement. Transportation methods multiplied, ranging from commercial shipping containers to semi-submersible submarines to underground tunnels. The Sinaloa Cartel's use of elaborate border tunnels, some of which were discovered extending hundreds of meters under the United States-Mexico border and equipped with rail systems, lighting, and ventilation, illustrated the degree of technical sophistication and organizational investment that criminal enterprises were willing to apply to countering enforcement efforts.

In the cybercrime domain, where the criminal organizations that emerged in the 2000s and 2010s operated in an environment where sophisticated surveillance and prosecution were significant risks from the beginning, operational security was architecturally embedded in criminal operations from their inception. Ransomware groups and fraud organizations routinely used anonymizing technologies, cryptocurrency payment systems, and geographically dispersed infrastructure to minimize the evidentiary footprints that traditional organized crime prosecutions had exploited. Many of the most significant cybercrime groups operated entirely outside the jurisdictions of the countries where their victims were located, exploiting the gaps in international legal cooperation that remained significant even as those gaps narrowed.

Managing the Informant Threat

The cooperating witness, or informant, represented perhaps the single most potent weapon in the law enforcement arsenal against organized crime, and criminal organizations developed specific institutional responses to managing the threat it posed. The traditional response, exemplified by the Sicilian Mafia's code of omertà, was cultural: the intense socialization of members into norms of silence, backed by the credible threat of violent punishment for violations, was intended to make cooperation with law enforcement unthinkable. For many decades, and in many contexts, this cultural enforcement was effective.

The prosecutorial success of the 1980s demonstrated the limits of cultural enforcement under sustained institutional pressure. When Tommaso Buscetta began cooperating with Italian magistrate Giovanni Falcone in 1984, and when Salvatore Gravano began cooperating with American prosecutors in 1991, they shattered not just the code of silence in their specific cases but the credibility of omertà as an institutional guarantee. If a senior boss like Buscetta could cooperate, and if a high-ranking figure like Gravano, who had personally committed nineteen murders, could receive a reduced sentence in exchange for testimony, then the calculation that silence was both morally obligatory and strategically rational became much harder to sustain for members facing long prison sentences.

Criminal organizations responded to the informant threat in several ways. Intensified screening of potential members became more important, with organizations becoming more selective about who they admitted to positions of knowledge and trust. Compartmentalization of information reduced the damage any single informant could do. Violence against potential and actual witnesses remained a constant risk management tool, and the intimidation of witnesses and jurors became increasingly sophisticated in its methods. Some organizations developed explicit counter-measures against the witness protection program, attempting to identify and locate relocated witnesses. These adaptations did not eliminate the informant threat, but they shaped its use and constrained its effectiveness in specific contexts.

Market Migration and the Hydra Effect

One of the most consistent and consequential patterns in the history of organized crime prosecution is what scholars have called the "hydra effect" or "balloon effect": the tendency of criminal activity, when suppressed in one location or one form, to expand in another location or another form rather than to diminish in aggregate. This pattern reflects the fundamental economic logic of criminal markets, which, like legal markets, respond to price signals and supply disruptions by seeking new suppliers and new channels. When a major supplier is removed from a criminal market, the immediate effect is usually a price increase for the relevant commodity or service, which creates profit opportunities that attract new entrants and encourage existing participants to expand. The market restabilizes, often at a similar or higher level of activity, but with different organizational participants.

The clearest illustration of this pattern in the modern period is the cocaine trade following the destruction of the Colombian cartels. Between 1995 and 2000, American and Colombian law enforcement succeeded in dismantling the organizational structures that had dominated cocaine trafficking for more than a decade. Annual cocaine seizures at the United States border remained high, and the retail price of cocaine in the United States did not increase dramatically or durably in the aftermath of the cartels' destruction, a clear sign that the supply of cocaine had not been significantly reduced despite the prosecution success. What had changed was the identity of the organizations supplying it. Mexican trafficking organizations, which had been paid in product rather than cash for their transportation services during the cartel era, now controlled the wholesale cocaine market in the United States and used their new wealth and power to vertically integrate into production relationships with Colombian growers and processors who operated through smaller, more dispersed networks rather than the large centralized cartels of the previous decade.

Similar displacement dynamics were visible in other criminal markets following prosecution successes. The weakening of the New York Mafia's control over labor unions and the construction industry created opportunities that were partly filled by emerging criminal networks with different ethnic compositions and different organizational forms. The prosecution and jailing of major figures in Russian organized crime in the 1990s and 2000s did not reduce the overall scale of Russian criminal activity but changed its geographical footprint and organizational structure. Market suppression achieved through prosecution was consistently more durable than market migration, in part because migration was structurally easier than reduction: criminal markets exist because there is demand for the goods and services they supply, and that demand does not disappear when a specific supplier is prosecuted.

Historical Vocabulary
Crime Displacement

Crime displacement refers to the tendency of criminal activity to shift in response to enforcement pressure rather than to diminish in aggregate. When law enforcement succeeds in suppressing crime in one location, time period, type of activity, or organizational form, the criminal activity may migrate to a different location, shift to a different time, change its form, or be assumed by different criminal actors. Researchers distinguish several types of displacement: geographical (crime moves to a different area), temporal (crime shifts to different times), tactical (criminals change their methods), target (criminals choose different victims), and organizational (different criminal groups take over the market).

The concept is important for evaluating the success of anti-organized crime strategies because it suggests that prosecution of specific organizations may not reduce the aggregate level of criminal activity or social harm if the markets those organizations served continue to exist and are served by successor organizations. This does not mean prosecution is futile, but it does mean that prosecution alone, without attention to the underlying demand conditions and market structures that generate criminal enterprise, is unlikely to produce durable reductions in criminal activity.

Succession and Organizational Continuity

Criminal organizations that survive over multiple generations develop mechanisms for managing leadership succession that allow them to maintain organizational continuity even through periods of intense prosecution. This succession capacity is, in some respects, the organizational expression of the same institutional logic that allows legitimate organizations to survive the departure of key personnel: the organization is more than its current leadership, and its accumulated knowledge, relationships, resources, and reputation can outlast any individual member.

The most resilient criminal organizations appear to be those in which succession is managed through family and clan structures that provide a biological basis for organizational continuity. The 'Ndrangheta of Calabria, which rose to international prominence in the late twentieth century partly as a consequence of the prosecution pressures that weakened the more hierarchical Sicilian Mafia, is organized through family clans in which criminal roles, relationships, and obligations are transmitted across generations through kinship networks rather than formal organizational appointment. This biological foundation for organizational continuity makes the 'Ndrangheta significantly more resistant to prosecution-induced leadership decapitation than organizations that depend on formal rank structures, because the relationships that constitute the organization are not merely organizational but familial, and they persist through prosecution, imprisonment, and succession.

The Yakuza in Japan provided a different model of succession management through formal organizational structures that were paradoxically both more visible and more resilient than those of organizations that depended on secrecy. The Yakuza's public visibility, including the maintenance of registered offices and the open acknowledgment of organizational membership, created a form of institutional continuity that survived individual prosecutions without requiring the secrecy that made other organizations vulnerable to particular investigative tools. When leadership was prosecuted, organizational structures remained in place and succession occurred according to established procedures.

Historical Vocabulary
Organizational Disruption

Organizational disruption refers to the impact of law enforcement action on the operational capacity of a criminal enterprise, as distinct from the legal outcome of the prosecution itself. An organization can be disrupted, in the sense of having its normal operations impaired, without being permanently destroyed. Scholars and policymakers distinguish between disruption, which is typically temporary and specific, and dismantlement, which implies the permanent destruction of an organization's capacity to operate.

The distinction matters because most organized crime prosecutions achieve disruption rather than dismantlement. They remove specific leaders, destroy specific operational cells, and impair specific criminal activities for specific periods of time. But the underlying organization, defined by its network of relationships, its accumulated criminal capital, its market position, and its institutional memory, may survive the disruption and reconstitute its operational capacity under new leadership or in new forms. Understanding this distinction is essential for evaluating the long-term significance of prosecution successes and for thinking about what supplementary or alternative strategies might be needed to achieve more durable results.

Historical Debate

Does Prosecuting Organized Crime Leaders Reduce Criminal Violence and Harm?

The question: When law enforcement successfully prosecutes and imprisons the leaders of major criminal organizations, does this reduce the aggregate level of criminal violence and social harm, or does it primarily change the identities of the organizations responsible for that harm while leaving the underlying conditions largely unchanged?

Interpretation A — The Incapacitation Model: The traditional justification for prosecution focuses on incapacitation: removing dangerous individuals from society, disrupting organizational capacity, and deterring future criminal activity through the credible demonstration of punishment. Proponents of this view point to the genuine decline of the Italian-American Mafia following the prosecutions of the 1980s and 1990s as evidence that sustained prosecution can achieve real, durable reductions in organized criminal activity. They also argue that deterrence operates through the expectation of punishment, and that high-profile convictions with severe sentences change the calculations of potential criminal actors even when they do not eliminate criminal activity entirely. This view does not require prosecution to be perfect to be valuable; it requires only that prosecution be better than the alternative of non-enforcement.
Interpretation B — The Displacement and Reconstitution Model: Critics of the incapacitation model argue that the evidence, examined over the long term, consistently shows that prosecution of specific organizations does not reduce the aggregate level of criminal activity in the markets those organizations served. The cocaine supply to the United States did not diminish after the destruction of the Colombian cartels. The violence associated with drug trafficking in Mexico did not decrease after major cartel leaders were prosecuted; it increased, as succession conflicts and inter-cartel competition intensified. Russian organized crime expanded after the prosecution of specific Russian crime figures. Proponents of this view argue that prosecuting leaders while leaving the underlying market conditions intact is analogous to removing individual weeds from a garden without addressing the soil conditions that produce them: the weeds grow back, because the conditions that generate them have not changed.

Evidence: Both interpretations draw on genuine historical evidence, and the historical record does not clearly support either position in its strongest form. The Italian-American Mafia is genuinely weaker today than it was in 1985, suggesting that sustained prosecution can achieve real structural change. But organized crime in the United States did not disappear after the Mafia's decline; it transformed. The cocaine trade did not decline after the Colombian cartels were destroyed; it persisted through different organizational forms. The evidence is most consistent with a middle position: prosecution can significantly weaken specific organizations and reduce their capacity for harm, but it does not eliminate the criminal markets they serve, and it must be accompanied by attention to the demand and institutional conditions that generate those markets to produce durable reductions in aggregate harm.

Why it matters: This debate is not merely academic. It directly shapes how resources are allocated between prosecution, prevention, and market regulation strategies, and how success in fighting organized crime is defined and measured. If prosecution is sufficient, the appropriate response to organized crime is more and better prosecution. If prosecution alone is insufficient, then complementary strategies, including demand reduction, institutional reform, and market regulation, must be developed alongside prosecution.

Section 34.3

Lessons Learned

More than a century of organized crime prosecution, from the earliest attempts to apply conspiracy law to criminal networks through the sophisticated RICO-era prosecutions of the 1980s and the cartel wars of the 1990s and 2000s to the emerging challenges of cybercrime prosecution in the twenty-first century, has produced a body of hard-won knowledge about what works, what does not work, what cannot be known in advance, and what underlying conditions determine whether any specific strategy can succeed. These lessons are not simple or easily reduced to policy prescriptions, but they are real, and understanding them is essential for anyone who wants to think seriously about the challenge of organized crime.

Legal Tools Matter, But Are Not Sufficient

The history of fighting organized crime is, in part, a history of legal innovation. The RICO Act transformed American organized crime prosecution by creating legal tools that matched the organizational reality of criminal enterprises rather than treating them as mere collections of individual criminal acts. Italy's anti-Mafia legislation, including the formal criminalization of Mafia membership under Article 416-bis of the Italian Penal Code, created similar tools in the Italian context. International anti-money-laundering frameworks, extradition treaties, and mutual legal assistance agreements expanded the geographic reach of prosecution beyond what any single national legal system could achieve.

The lesson that legal tools matter is real and important. The prosecutorial successes of the 1980s and 1990s would not have been possible without the legal innovations of the preceding decades. Equally real, however, is the lesson that legal tools alone are not sufficient. RICO did not eliminate organized crime from New York; it changed its character and reduced its scale. Anti-Mafia legislation did not eliminate the Mafia from Sicily; it inflicted serious damage on the organization and eventually led to the arrest of most of its senior leadership, but the organization survived in reduced form and retained significant economic and political influence in many areas of southern Italy. The lesson is not that legal tools are unimportant but that they are necessary, not sufficient conditions for success, and that they must be embedded in institutional contexts that allow them to be effectively implemented.

Institutional Integrity Is Foundational

Perhaps the most consistent lesson of the history of fighting organized crime is that no prosecution strategy, however sophisticated, can succeed in an institutional environment characterized by pervasive corruption. The Italian prosecution campaign of the 1980s succeeded in part because a cadre of judges and prosecutors who were genuinely committed to the rule of law and protected by extraordinary security measures managed to maintain their integrity in the face of enormous pressure, including assassination threats and the actual murder of colleagues. The Colombian campaign against the Medellín Cartel succeeded, to the degree it did, only after the government committed itself to resisting Escobar's corruption offers and accepting the risks of confrontation. The American prosecution campaign succeeded in an institutional environment that, while not immune from corruption, was sufficiently robust that prosecutors could present evidence in court without fearing that judges or jurors would be reliably compromised.

In contexts where institutional integrity cannot be maintained, prosecution strategies typically fail, regardless of their legal sophistication or the quality of the evidence assembled. Mexico's experience illustrated this lesson repeatedly and painfully: investigations were compromised by the participation of corrupted officials, prosecutions were undermined by judges or witnesses subject to intimidation or bribery, and the evidence assembled through costly and dangerous investigations sometimes disappeared or was rendered inadmissible through institutional failures. The corrupting power of criminal organizations with enormous financial resources and a willingness to use violence is not a peripheral problem to be managed around; it is a central challenge that must be addressed directly if prosecution strategies are to have durable effects.

The Role of Cooperating Witnesses

The cooperating witness, or informant, emerged in the modern period as perhaps the single most powerful and most problematic tool in the organized crime prosecutor's arsenal. The most significant prosecutions of the modern era, from the Commission trial through the Italian maxi-trials to the cartel prosecutions, depended critically on the testimony of individuals who had themselves committed serious crimes and who were offered leniency in exchange for their cooperation. Without Salvatore Gravano's testimony, the prosecution of John Gotti would likely have failed. Without Tommaso Buscetta's testimony, the Palermo maxi-trial would not have had the architectural understanding of the Mafia's structure that made it possible to connect specific crimes to specific organizational roles and decisions.

The use of cooperating witnesses raises ethical and practical questions that have no easy resolution. Ethically, the practice involves offering reduced punishment to individuals who may have committed more serious crimes than those of the defendants they testify against, in exchange for testimony that serves the government's prosecutorial interests. The moral asymmetry is uncomfortable: a murderer who cooperates may receive a lighter sentence than a non-violent offender who does not. Practically, cooperating witness testimony carries inherent reliability risks, because witnesses who are testifying in exchange for leniency have strong incentives to tell prosecutors what they want to hear rather than what is strictly accurate. Prosecutors and courts have developed protocols for evaluating and corroborating cooperating witness testimony, but these protocols reduce rather than eliminate the reliability problem.

Despite these problems, the historical record consistently shows that sustained organized crime prosecution without cooperating witnesses has been far less effective than prosecution that uses them. The most successful prosecution campaigns, from the American anti-Mafia effort to the Italian maxi-trials, combined electronic surveillance and financial evidence with cooperating witness testimony in ways that allowed each type of evidence to corroborate and reinforce the others. The lesson is not that cooperating witnesses are unproblematic but that, used with appropriate scrutiny and corroboration, they are often essential to effective prosecution of criminal enterprises.

International Cooperation Is Necessary and Difficult

Organized crime became increasingly transnational across the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries, and the response to it increasingly required international cooperation that transcended traditional limitations on the extraterritorial reach of national law enforcement. The prosecution of Colombian cartel leaders in United States courts required extradition agreements and diplomatic pressure that were sometimes bitterly contested. The prosecution of Russian organized crime figures in American and European courts required cooperation from authorities who were sometimes reluctant partners and occasionally actively obstructive. The pursuit of cybercrime organizations required real-time sharing of digital forensic intelligence across jurisdictions with very different legal frameworks and very different levels of enthusiasm for cooperation.

The development of international cooperation mechanisms, from bilateral extradition treaties to multilateral frameworks like the United Nations Convention Against Transnational Organized Crime (the Palermo Convention of 2000), represented genuine institutional progress. The Palermo Convention, which came into force in 2003, created a framework for international cooperation in investigating and prosecuting organized crime that had not previously existed in such comprehensive form, including provisions on extradition, mutual legal assistance, asset recovery, and technical assistance to developing countries. It was a significant achievement, even if its implementation varied enormously across the nearly 190 countries that eventually ratified it.

The lesson of international cooperation is double-edged. On one side, the history shows that significant prosecution successes against transnational criminal organizations have typically required international cooperation, and that developing the institutional frameworks for that cooperation, however slowly and imperfectly, has been genuinely valuable. On the other side, the history shows that even significant international cooperation faces inherent limits. Countries have different legal systems, different definitions of criminal activity, different evidentiary standards, different levels of institutional integrity, and different political relationships with criminal organizations. No international cooperation framework can fully overcome these differences, and the gaps they create are routinely exploited by criminal organizations that deliberately structure their operations to take advantage of jurisdictional boundaries.

The Demand Problem

Perhaps the most uncomfortable lesson of the history of fighting organized crime is that prosecution strategies, however effective they may be at disrupting specific organizations, cannot by themselves address the fundamental driver of criminal enterprise: demand. Criminal organizations exist because they supply goods and services, from drugs to protection to labor regulation to sex to financial secrecy, for which there is genuine and often substantial demand. As long as that demand exists, as long as people want to consume cocaine or heroin or counterfeit goods or untaxed cigarettes or illegal gambling services or the fruits of human trafficking, there will be criminal organizations willing to supply those goods and services, because the profit margins generated by illegal markets are typically far higher than those available in legal markets.

Prosecution can raise the cost of supplying illegal goods and services, which in principle reduces supply and raises prices, which in turn should reduce consumption. But the evidence that prosecution-driven supply disruption produces durable reductions in consumption is limited. Drug prices in the United States, for example, fell significantly over the last two decades of the twentieth century despite massive enforcement investment, suggesting that the supply of drugs was not meaningfully constrained by enforcement despite enormous prosecutorial activity. This does not mean enforcement is counterproductive, but it does suggest that enforcement alone cannot achieve the demand reductions that would be necessary to eliminate the market conditions that generate criminal enterprise.

The implication, which many analysts have drawn but which remains politically contentious, is that effective long-term strategies against organized crime must include demand-side measures, including treatment for addiction, economic development in communities where participation in criminal economies is economically rational for marginalized individuals, and in some cases the regulation or legalization of markets currently served by criminal organizations. These are not law enforcement strategies; they are social and economic policy strategies. But the history of fighting organized crime consistently points toward the conclusion that law enforcement strategies alone, without addressing the underlying demand and social conditions that generate criminal enterprise, cannot produce durable solutions.

Measuring Success

One of the most important and underappreciated lessons of the history of fighting organized crime is the difficulty of measuring success in any meaningful and honest way. The metrics that governments and law enforcement agencies typically report, such as numbers of arrests, convictions, prison sentences imposed, and assets forfeited, measure law enforcement activity, not outcomes. They tell us how many people were prosecuted, not whether criminal activity decreased. They tell us how much money was seized, not whether criminal organizations were deprived of the resources they needed to operate. They tell us whether specific individuals were incapacitated, not whether the criminal markets those individuals served continued to operate.

Better outcome measures, such as trends in drug prices and purity (which indicate whether supply is being constrained), trends in criminal violence (which indicate whether the violence associated with criminal market competition is changing), and surveys of community safety and institutional trust (which indicate whether criminal organizations' influence over communities is changing), are more difficult to collect, more ambiguous to interpret, and more politically inconvenient to report when they tell discouraging stories. But they are more honest measures of whether the underlying problem is being addressed.

The Commission trial of 1986 looked, by every conventional law enforcement metric, like an overwhelming success: all defendants convicted, massive sentences imposed, the Mafia's governing institution prosecuted as a criminal enterprise. By outcome measures, the picture is more complex. The New York Mafia was significantly weakened, which is a real outcome. But organized criminal activity in New York continued. Labor racketeering continued for years after the Commission trial in unions the Mafia had controlled. Construction industry fraud continued. Loan sharking continued. The criminal markets that had been the Mafia's domain were not eliminated; they were contested by new participants, reorganized, and in some cases assumed by successor organizations. The Commission trial was a genuine success, but an honest accounting of what it achieved requires confronting the limits of that success alongside its real accomplishments.

Historical Vocabulary
Recidivism

Recidivism in the context of organized crime refers to the return to criminal activity by individuals who have been prosecuted and served sentences for participation in criminal enterprises, or by criminal organizations that have been disrupted by prosecution and subsequently reconstituted their operations. Research on individual recidivism among organized crime figures suggests higher rates than for conventional criminal offenders, partly because the skills, relationships, and reputational assets that make individuals valuable to criminal enterprises are not destroyed by imprisonment, and partly because the economic opportunities available to convicted felons in the legitimate economy are often limited.

Organizational recidivism, meaning the reconstitution of disrupted criminal enterprises, is closely related to the concepts of succession, adaptation, and crime displacement. An organization that has been partially dismantled by prosecution may reconstitute itself under new leadership, in a modified organizational form, or in a different market or geographic location. This pattern of organizational recidivism is one of the most consistent findings in the history of organized crime prosecution and is central to the ongoing debate about the long-term effectiveness of prosecution as a strategy for reducing organized crime.

Multiple Perspectives

Evaluating Organized Crime Prosecutions: Whose Success?

From the perspective of law enforcement and prosecutors: Major prosecutions represent genuine achievements that required years of painstaking investigative work, personal risk to investigators and prosecutors, and the skillful use of legal tools developed through sustained institutional effort. The Commission trial, the Italian maxi-trials, and the cartel prosecutions removed genuinely dangerous individuals from society, disrupted specific criminal operations that caused real harm to real people, and demonstrated that no criminal organization is above the law. From this perspective, measuring success only by aggregate crime levels fails to credit the individual cases, the individual harms prevented, and the institutional message that sustained prosecution communicates.
From the perspective of communities targeted by organized crime: For communities that lived under the systematic extortion, violence, and corruption of powerful criminal organizations, the prosecutions that weakened those organizations produced genuine and experienced relief. Residents of South Brooklyn neighborhoods that had been terrorized by the Colombo family felt tangible change after prosecutions weakened the family's local presence. Communities in Medellín, Colombia, experienced real reductions in certain kinds of violence after Escobar's death, even if other forms of criminal violence persisted. These community-level experiences of relief are not captured in aggregate statistics and are sometimes overlooked in scholarly assessments that focus on system-level outcomes.
From the perspective of social scientists and criminologists: The empirical literature on the aggregate effects of major organized crime prosecutions presents a complicated and often humbling picture. Studies of drug market disruption consistently find that even major enforcement successes produce limited and temporary supply reductions. Studies of violence following cartel leadership prosecutions in Mexico find evidence that leadership removal often increases rather than decreases violence in the short to medium term, as succession conflicts and territorial competition intensify. From this perspective, the celebration of prosecution as the primary response to organized crime reflects an institutional bias toward measuring what is measurable rather than what matters.
From the perspective of defendants and their communities: Even where prosecutions targeted genuinely criminal enterprises, the broad sweep of conspiracy and enterprise theories, the reliance on cooperating witnesses with their own criminal histories and incentives to provide favorable testimony, and the severity of sentences imposed under mandatory minimum statutes raised questions about due process and proportionality that were not always adequately addressed. Communities from which defendants came sometimes experienced the prosecutions as exercises of discriminatory institutional power as much as as applications of the rule of law, particularly where similar institutional attention was not applied to the financial institutions and political systems that provided the infrastructure through which criminal money was laundered.
Cause and Effect

The Decline of the Italian-American Mafia, 1980–2000

Long-Term Causes

Development and deployment of RICO statute; FBI's sustained intelligence investment in Mafia infiltration through the 1970s; development of court-authorized electronic surveillance technology; decline of the industries the Mafia had traditionally controlled through union corruption; assimilation of Italian-American communities into mainstream American economic and political life, reducing the ethnic solidarity that had supported the Mafia's social base.

Short-Term Causes

Commission trial convictions (1986); individual family prosecutions through the late 1980s; high-profile informant cooperation by Salvatore Gravano and others; Gotti conviction (1992); asset forfeitures that stripped criminal enterprises of accumulated financial resources; wave of lower-ranking members turning state's evidence in exchange for reduced sentences.

The Transformation

New York Mafia's power diminished dramatically across the 1990s. Leadership of all five families prosecuted and imprisoned. Commission effectively ceased functioning as a governance institution. Criminal revenues from labor racketeering, construction, and gambling fell sharply. Recruitment became more difficult as traditional social support structures eroded.

Long-Term Effects

Mafia continues to exist in reduced form; some criminal activities persist. Criminal vacuum partly filled by other organized crime networks. Labor union corruption diminished but not eliminated. Some illegal markets (sports betting, loansharking) eventually partially legalized, removing the criminal profit opportunity. General organized crime activity in the United States does not disappear but transforms in organizational form and ethnic composition.

Historical complexity: The Mafia's decline was produced by the interaction of legal tools, investigative investment, institutional integrity, and underlying social changes that reduced the organization's social base. No single cause was sufficient; all were necessary. Understanding which causes were most important, and in what combination, is essential for thinking about what strategies might work in other contexts and against other organizations.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Organized Crime in the United States, 1985–Present

What Changed

  • The Italian-American Mafia's organizational capacity dramatically reduced; Commission effectively destroyed; most prominent families significantly weakened
  • Labor racketeering in major industries, particularly construction and trucking, substantially diminished in scale
  • Legal frameworks for prosecution expanded, with RICO applied against a wider range of criminal enterprises
  • International cooperation mechanisms significantly improved, enabling cross-border prosecutions previously impossible
  • Asset forfeiture became a major tool for stripping criminal enterprises of financial resources
  • Technological sophistication of both criminal operations and law enforcement significantly increased

What Stayed the Same

  • Demand for illegal goods and services, including drugs, illegal gambling, and human trafficking, persists at significant scale
  • Corruption of public officials continues to be an important tool of criminal organizations
  • Criminal organizations continue to exploit legal market interfaces for money laundering purposes
  • Violence as a mechanism of contract enforcement and territorial competition remains characteristic of criminal markets
  • Law enforcement capacity remains unevenly distributed, with gaps in international cooperation and institutional integrity exploited by criminal organizations
  • The pattern of crime displacement, where enforcement success against one organization shifts criminal activity to successor organizations, continues

Why the pattern matters: The pattern of significant organizational change alongside market-level continuity illustrates both the genuine achievements of anti-organized crime prosecution and its fundamental limitations. Law enforcement can change which organizations dominate illegal markets, and can reduce the power of specific organizations that have penetrated legitimate institutions, but it has not succeeded in eliminating the markets themselves or the social and economic conditions that generate criminal enterprise. This pattern suggests that durable success requires strategies that address the underlying market conditions alongside prosecution of specific organizations.

Everyday Life: Victims, Witnesses, and Communities

The history of organized crime prosecution is typically told through the dramatic narratives of famous defendants, celebrated prosecutors, and spectacular trials. But the consequences of both organized crime itself and the prosecution campaigns directed against it were experienced most directly not by those famous figures but by the ordinary people who lived in communities where criminal organizations operated, whose businesses were extorted, whose families were touched by criminal violence, whose neighbors became cooperating witnesses, and whose neighborhood institutions were sometimes implicated in the criminal enterprises under prosecution.

The experience of living in a community where a powerful criminal organization operated was not simply an experience of fear, though fear was real. Criminal organizations also provided services that in their absence might not have existed: they settled disputes that courts were too slow or too corrupt to handle, they provided credit that legitimate banks would not extend, and they maintained a kind of order in their territories that, while based on violence and coercion, was experienced by some residents as preferable to the disorder of violent competition between criminal factions. This does not make criminal governance desirable or just, but it helps explain why communities that had suffered under criminal organizations sometimes also experienced mixed feelings when prosecution dismantled those organizations, particularly if the prosecution disrupted criminal order without replacing it with effective legitimate institutions.

For witnesses and their families, the decision to cooperate with law enforcement against a criminal organization was never simple or cost-free. The Witness Protection Program (WITSEC) offered relocation, new identities, and financial support, but it required witnesses to abandon their established lives, families, community ties, and identities. Many cooperating witnesses found the program's constraints intolerable after the immediate danger had passed and attempted to return to their old lives, sometimes with fatal consequences. Others found that the new identities provided by the program were inadequate protection against criminal organizations that had the resources and motivation to find them. The human costs of the informant system, while often overlooked in accounts that focus on the prosecution victories that informants enabled, were real and significant.

For the families of incarcerated defendants, the prosecution campaigns of the 1980s and 1990s produced experiences of economic disruption, social stigma, and prolonged separation that were, in their aggregate human cost, significant. Sentences of one hundred years or more, imposed on aging defendants whose actual remaining lifespans were a fraction of their sentences, functioned as lifetime incarceration without formal life sentences, removing fathers and grandfathers from families for the remainder of their lives. The criminal organization that had provided income to those families was often simultaneously being dismantled, leaving families without the financial support of either the incarcerated member's legitimate income or the criminal revenue that had supplemented it. These human consequences did not make the prosecutions unjust, but they are part of the full accounting of what those prosecutions cost and what they produced.

The communities where major criminal organizations had operated most intensively experienced the aftermath of prosecution in complex ways. In some neighborhoods, the decline of Mafia control over local businesses and labor markets created genuine opportunities for economic participation that had previously been foreclosed by criminal extortion and union corruption. In others, the criminal vacuum created by Mafia prosecutions was filled by other criminal organizations whose violence was, in the short term, greater and less predictable than the relatively stable criminal governance that the Mafia had maintained. The experience of deindustrialization, urban disinvestment, and economic decline that affected many northeastern American cities in the same period was intertwined with and sometimes exacerbated by the disruption of criminal economies that had provided income, however illicit, for significant numbers of residents.

Chapter Summary

The history of fighting organized crime is a history of genuine achievements, significant limitations, and recurring patterns that challenge simple narratives of either victory or futility. Over the past century, and with particular intensity in the final decades of the twentieth century, law enforcement agencies, prosecutors, and policymakers in the United States and internationally developed legal tools, investigative techniques, and institutional frameworks that enabled prosecutions of a scale and sophistication that would have been impossible in earlier periods. The results, measured in convictions, sentences, and organizational disruptions, were real and consequential.

The RICO Act of 1970, and the legal theory it embodied, transformed American organized crime prosecution by creating tools that matched the organizational reality of criminal enterprises. The Commission trial of 1985–1986 and the individual family prosecutions that followed it over the next decade dismantled the formal governance structure of the Italian-American Mafia and permanently weakened the organization's capacity for the kind of large-scale, coordinated criminal activity that had characterized its peak decades. The Italian maxi-trials of 1986–1987, built on the testimony of the first major Mafia pentito and the investigative innovations of magistrates Falcone and Borsellino, achieved similar results in the Sicilian context, at enormous personal cost to those who led them. The prosecution and killing of Pablo Escobar and the subsequent dismantling of the Cali Cartel destroyed the first-generation Colombian cocaine cartels that had dominated the global cocaine trade through the 1980s.

These were real successes, measured not just in courtroom outcomes but in genuine reductions in specific harms. Communities that had lived under Mafia extortion and control experienced real, if imperfect, relief. The Colombian state, which had been brought to the edge of institutional collapse by cartel violence in the late 1980s, regained a degree of institutional stability after the cartels' destruction. The deterrent effect of sustained prosecution on criminal actors who feared the consequences that earlier prosecution campaigns had demonstrated is real, even if difficult to quantify precisely.

Yet the same history also reveals consistent and important limitations. Criminal organizations adapted to prosecution pressure through structural changes, moving from hierarchical to networked forms that were harder to prosecute using enterprise theories. They improved their operational security in response to the surveillance tools that had devastated them. They developed more sophisticated succession mechanisms that allowed organizations to survive the removal of key leadership. And the criminal markets they served proved far more resilient than the organizations themselves: when a specific organization was prosecuted and destroyed, the demand it had served persisted, and successor organizations, often more numerous and more geographically dispersed than their predecessors, assumed the market position that prosecution had vacated.

The hydra effect, the displacement of criminal activity from prosecuted organizations to successor organizations rather than its elimination, was perhaps the most consistent and most sobering finding of the era. The cocaine trade survived the destruction of the Colombian cartels, reorganized through Mexican trafficking organizations and dispersed Colombian networks. Organized crime in American cities survived the Mafia prosecutions, reorganized through different ethnic networks and more loosely structured criminal enterprises. Russian organized crime expanded rather than contracted after the prosecution of specific Russian crime figures. Cybercrime organizations continued to evolve and adapt despite increasingly sophisticated international enforcement cooperation.

The role of informants and cooperating witnesses in the most successful prosecutions raised ethical and practical questions that the legal system managed but never fully resolved. The use of individuals who had committed serious crimes, including murder, as witnesses against their criminal associates in exchange for reduced sentences created moral asymmetries and reliability risks that courts developed protocols to address but could not eliminate. The Witness Protection Program enabled the informant cooperation that many prosecutions depended on, but at significant human cost to witnesses and their families who were required to abandon their lives to maintain their safety.

Institutional integrity proved to be foundational. In contexts where courts, police, and prosecutors maintained their commitment to the rule of law under intense criminal pressure, prosecution strategies could achieve real results. In contexts where institutional corruption was pervasive, prosecution strategies consistently failed regardless of their legal sophistication, because criminal organizations could corrupt the institutional processes that prosecution depended on. The assassinations of Falcone and Borsellino illustrated both the vulnerability of institutional integrity to criminal violence and its indispensability to the prosecution project.

International cooperation expanded significantly over the period, from bilateral extradition treaties through the Palermo Convention's multilateral framework, enabling prosecutions against transnational criminal organizations that would have been impossible within the constraints of purely national jurisdiction. But international cooperation also remained limited by differences in legal systems, evidentiary standards, institutional capacities, and political relationships with criminal organizations that no treaty framework could fully overcome. The geographic gaps in international legal cooperation that criminal organizations exploited became, if anything, more strategically significant as criminal operations became more transnational and more digitally mediated.

The measurement of success in fighting organized crime emerged as itself an important and underappreciated challenge. Law enforcement metrics focused on arrests, convictions, and assets seized measured activity but not outcomes. More meaningful measures, including trends in criminal market prices, levels of criminal violence, and surveys of community safety and institutional trust, told more complex and often more sobering stories about whether the underlying problem was being addressed. The history consistently showed that prosecution could significantly weaken specific organizations while leaving the criminal markets those organizations served largely intact.

The deepest lesson of this history is that prosecution, however sophisticated and however well-executed, is a necessary but insufficient response to organized crime. It is necessary because criminal organizations that are not prosecuted become stronger, more entrenched, and more damaging over time. It is insufficient because criminal organizations exist to serve criminal markets, and markets that exist because demand is real are not eliminated by removing specific suppliers. Durable success against organized crime requires attention to the demand conditions that generate criminal markets, the social and economic inequalities that make participation in criminal economies rational for many individuals, and the institutional weaknesses that allow criminal organizations to corrupt and capture the institutions that should constrain them. These are not solely law enforcement challenges; they are social, economic, and political challenges that require responses appropriate to their complexity.

Review Materials

Key Terms

RICO Act
Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act (1970); federal law that allows prosecution of individuals for participation in a criminal enterprise through a pattern of racketeering activity, enabling prosecution of criminal organization leaders for crimes committed by their subordinates.
Criminal enterprise
Under RICO, any group of individuals associated in fact or formally that engages in or affects interstate commerce through a pattern of racketeering activity; the legal concept that enabled prosecution of the Mafia as a whole rather than merely as individual criminal actors.
Racketeering
The conducting of a fraudulent scheme or engaging in organized criminal activity, particularly extortion; under RICO, a broad category of predicate offenses whose repeated commission constitutes a pattern of racketeering activity that triggers RICO liability.
Cooperating witness
An individual who agrees to provide testimony against criminal associates in exchange for a reduced sentence or immunity from prosecution; the single most important source of evidence in major organized crime prosecutions, and the most ethically and practically complex tool in the prosecutor's arsenal.
Pentito
Italian term for a repentant criminal who cooperates with law enforcement against former associates; the Italian equivalent of the American cooperating witness; the testimony of major pentiti like Tommaso Buscetta was essential to the Italian anti-Mafia prosecution campaigns of the 1980s and 1990s.
Asset forfeiture
The legal process by which the government seizes property connected to criminal activity; both criminal forfeiture (following conviction) and civil forfeiture (without requiring conviction) have been used extensively in organized crime prosecution to strip criminal enterprises of their financial resources.
Organizational disruption
The temporary impairment of a criminal enterprise's operational capacity through law enforcement action; distinguished from permanent dismantlement by the organization's capacity to reconstitute its operations after the disruption has passed.
Crime displacement
The tendency of criminal activity to shift in location, form, target, or organizational responsibility rather than to diminish in aggregate when subjected to enforcement pressure; a central challenge for evaluating the effectiveness of organized crime prosecution.
Hydra effect
The tendency for criminal organizations, when destroyed by prosecution or enforcement action, to be replaced by multiple successor organizations that may collectively be more numerous, more violent, and harder to prosecute than the original organization; named for the mythological creature that grew two heads for every one removed.
Maxi-trial
The Italian legal innovation, developed in the Palermo prosecution campaign of the 1980s, in which large numbers of defendants, often hundreds, are tried simultaneously in specially constructed secure facilities; designed to prosecute criminal organizations as systems rather than as collections of individual criminal acts.
Recidivism
The return to criminal activity after prosecution and incarceration; in the organized crime context, applies both to individuals who resume criminal careers after serving sentences and to organizations that reconstitute their criminal operations after being disrupted by prosecution.
Incapacitation
The prevention of criminal activity through the physical removal of criminals from society through incarceration; one of the primary justifications for prosecution as a response to organized crime, alongside deterrence and rehabilitation.
Deterrence
The prevention of criminal activity through the threat of punishment; in the organized crime context, the argument that high-profile prosecutions with severe sentences change the calculations of potential criminal actors and reduce criminal activity by raising its expected costs.
Omertà
The Mafia's traditional code of silence, which prohibited cooperation with law enforcement under threat of death; central to the Mafia's institutional culture and a primary obstacle to organized crime prosecution until the emergence of major cooperating witnesses in the 1980s and 1990s.
Extradition
The formal legal process by which one country surrenders an individual to another country for prosecution; a critical tool in fighting transnational criminal organizations, particularly in the prosecution of Colombian and Mexican cartel leaders in United States federal courts.
Plata o plomo
Spanish phrase meaning "silver or lead," referring to the Colombian Medellín Cartel's policy of offering officials the choice between accepting bribes or facing violent retaliation; illustrates the mechanism of institutional corruption that major criminal organizations use to protect their operations.
Cartel fragmentation
The process by which the prosecution or elimination of major drug-trafficking organizations produces not the suppression of drug trafficking but its redistribution among a larger number of smaller, more numerous, and often more violent successor organizations; a primary example of crime displacement in the drug trafficking context.
Witness Protection Program (WITSEC)
The U.S. Marshals Service program, established in 1970, that provides new identities and geographic relocation for witnesses who testify against criminal organizations; enabled the cooperating witness testimony that was essential to major organized crime prosecutions while imposing significant human costs on the witnesses and their families.
Anti-money laundering
Legal frameworks and enforcement strategies designed to prevent criminal organizations from integrating criminally derived proceeds into the legitimate financial system; an increasingly important component of organized crime prosecution strategy that targets the financial infrastructure of criminal enterprises rather than only their leadership.
Palermo Convention
The United Nations Convention Against Transnational Organized Crime (2000), which created a multilateral framework for international cooperation in investigating and prosecuting organized crime, including provisions on extradition, mutual legal assistance, and asset recovery.

Major People and Places

Identification & Significance

Rudolph Giuliani (b. 1944)
United States Attorney for the Southern District of New York, 1983–1989; principal architect of the Commission trial and the RICO-based strategy that severely weakened the New York Mafia families; later Mayor of New York City.
Anthony Salerno (1911–1992)
Boss of the Genovese family and chairman of the Commission; convicted in the Commission trial and sentenced to one hundred years in federal prison; died in prison in 1992.
Salvatore "Sammy the Bull" Gravano (b. 1945)
Underboss of the Gambino family; admitted to participating in nineteen murders; became the most significant Mafia cooperating witness in American history, providing testimony that convicted boss John Gotti; received a reduced sentence of five years.
John Gotti (1940–2002)
Boss of the Gambino family; known as "The Teflon Don" after three acquittals in the 1980s, attributed to jury tampering and witness intimidation; convicted in 1992 primarily on Gravano's testimony; sentenced to life in federal prison; died in prison.
Tommaso Buscetta (1928–2000)
Senior Sicilian Mafia boss who became the first major Italian pentito in 1984; his testimony to magistrate Giovanni Falcone provided the structural map of the Sicilian Mafia that made the Palermo maxi-trial possible; later also cooperated with American prosecutors.
Giovanni Falcone (1939–1992)
Palermo magistrate and anti-Mafia prosecutor whose innovative investigative methods and use of pentito testimony made the Italian maxi-trials possible; assassinated by the Mafia on May 23, 1992, on the Capaci highway; his death provoked national outrage that strengthened the anti-Mafia effort.
Pablo Escobar (1949–1993)
Head of the Medellín Cartel and for a period the most wanted criminal in the world; his campaign of violence against the Colombian state, including hundreds of political assassinations and civilian bombings, ultimately united the government and rival criminal organizations against him; killed in December 1993.
Joaquín "El Chapo" Guzmán (b. 1957)
Co-founder of the Sinaloa Cartel; escaped from two Mexican prisons; extradited to the United States in 2017; convicted in Brooklyn federal court in 2019; sentenced to life plus thirty years.
G. Robert Blakey (b. 1936)
Notre Dame law professor and former Justice Department attorney who drafted the RICO statute; his work created the legal framework that made the most significant American organized crime prosecutions of the late twentieth century possible.
Robert Kennedy (1925–1968)
Chief Counsel of the McClellan Committee, whose hearings in the late 1950s documented Mafia penetration of labor unions and business; later Attorney General of the United States, where he directed increased FBI attention to organized crime; championed the legislative efforts that eventually produced RICO.
The Southern District of New York
Federal judicial district covering Manhattan, the Bronx, and surrounding counties; the primary venue for American organized crime prosecution throughout the late twentieth century, responsible for the Commission trial, Gotti prosecution, and many other landmark cases.
Medellín, Colombia
Colombia's second city; headquarters of the Medellín Cartel; site of enormous cartel-related violence in the 1980s and early 1990s; experienced significant security improvement after Escobar's death, though criminal activity persisted under different organizational structures.

Chapter Reflection

The history examined in this chapter resists simple conclusions about success and failure, victory and defeat. It is a history of genuine achievement, genuine limitation, and genuine complexity, all of which must be held together if that history is to be honestly understood and usefully applied to present and future challenges. The investigators and prosecutors who built the Commission trial, who assembled the evidence for the Italian maxi-trials, who tracked Pablo Escobar across Colombia for years, accomplished real things at real cost. Their achievements were not illusory, and the students who emerge from this chapter should understand them as such.

At the same time, the honest student of this history must confront the recurring patterns that complicate any simple triumphalism. Criminal organizations adapted to prosecution pressure in ways that preserved their core institutional competencies even as they changed their external forms. Criminal markets proved more resilient than the organizations that served them. Institutional corruption remained a fundamental obstacle to prosecution effectiveness in many contexts, an obstacle that law enforcement strategies could work around in exceptional circumstances but could not resolve through enforcement alone. And the demand for the goods and services that criminal organizations supply, rooted in addiction, economic inequality, and the incomplete reach of legitimate institutions, persisted through the most consequential prosecution campaigns without being significantly diminished.

These patterns have implications that extend beyond the history of organized crime itself. They are, at a deeper level, a reminder of the limits of enforcement as a response to social problems whose roots lie in conditions that enforcement cannot address. Law enforcement can remove specific actors from criminal markets, can disrupt specific criminal operations, can demonstrate through credible prosecution that criminal organizations are not beyond the reach of the law. All of this matters. None of it is sufficient, by itself, to resolve the underlying conditions that generate organized crime, because those conditions, including economic inequality, drug addiction, institutional weakness, and the persistent gap between what legal markets provide and what human beings want or need, are conditions that enforcement strategies were never designed to address.

The final lesson of this chapter, and of Part X of this textbook as a whole, is that fighting organized crime is not a problem to be solved but a challenge to be managed, continuously and imperfectly, through the combination of enforcement, institutional integrity, international cooperation, and attention to the underlying social and economic conditions that make organized crime possible and, for many people, rational. This is a less satisfying conclusion than the clean victory that the Commission trial or the death of Escobar briefly seemed to promise. But it is the conclusion that the history actually supports, and understanding it is the beginning of thinking seriously about what fighting organized crime requires and what it can realistically achieve.

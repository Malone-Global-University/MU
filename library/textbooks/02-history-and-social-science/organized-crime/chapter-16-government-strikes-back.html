Part V — The American Mafia

Government Strikes Back

Organized Crime: A Global History

Chapter Opening

"For the first time, the government has a sword, not just a shield. RICO lets us cut off the head of the snake, not just swat at its tail."
— Attributed to federal prosecutors in the 1980s, summarizing the transformative power of the Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act

On the morning of February 26, 1985, eight men filed into a federal courtroom in lower Manhattan and took their seats at the defense table. They were elderly, some of them infirm, and they wore the careful suits of men who had spent lifetimes projecting an air of respectability. But the charges against them were anything but respectable. The eight defendants were the bosses or acting bosses of all five of New York's major organized crime families, and they had been indicted together under a legal theory that would have seemed fantastical just a decade earlier: that the American Mafia itself, as an institution, was a criminal enterprise subject to prosecution. The case became known as the Commission Trial, and when the verdicts came in the following year, it marked a turning point that organized crime in the United States had never faced before.

How had the government arrived at this moment? For most of the twentieth century, the FBI under J. Edgar Hoover had officially denied that a national organized crime syndicate even existed in the United States. Local police departments and occasional crusading prosecutors had harassed individual gangsters, but the deep structural networks that connected the families, controlled the unions, ran the gambling empires, and corrupted the political machines had remained largely intact. The Mafia had developed over decades into what prosecutors and scholars would come to describe as a shadow economy, operating in parallel with the legitimate world, bound by its own codes, hierarchies, and enforcement mechanisms, and largely invisible to the legal structures that were supposed to constrain it.

The transformation that produced the Commission Trial and the prosecutions that followed it was the result of decades of accumulated institutional learning, legal innovation, and individual courage, and it did not happen in a straight line. It required the FBI to fundamentally reorganize its priorities and methods after Hoover's death in 1972. It required Congress to pass legislation, particularly the Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act of 1970, that gave federal prosecutors tools capable of attacking criminal organizations as organizations rather than merely prosecuting individual crimes committed by individual members. It required a new generation of prosecutors who were willing to invest years of painstaking work building cases that their predecessors would have considered impossibly complex. And it required a steady stream of witnesses who were willing to break the code of silence that had protected the Mafia for generations, accepting the risks that came with cooperation and entering a system of witness protection that itself represented a radical institutional innovation.

The story of how the government learned to fight organized crime effectively is not simply a story of law enforcement success. It is also a story of institutional failure, political resistance, bureaucratic rivalry, and the complicated ethics of a system that, in order to prosecute major criminals, sometimes had to make deals with lesser ones. The informants who brought down the Mafia were not innocent bystanders. They were, in many cases, murderers and extortionists who escaped justice for serious crimes in exchange for testimony about even more serious crimes committed by their former associates. The moral calculus of this bargain is one that prosecutors, defense attorneys, and legal scholars have debated ever since.

The chapter that follows examines each of the major elements of the government's campaign against the American Mafia in the latter decades of the twentieth century: the evolution of the FBI from an institution that denied organized crime's existence to the most effective anti-Mafia weapon in the country's history; the development of RICO and the legal strategies it enabled; the cultivation of informants and the creation of the federal witness protection program; the major prosecutions that used these tools to dismantle the leadership of the New York families and organized crime operations across the country; and finally the question of what the decline of the traditional Mafia actually means, both for organized crime as a phenomenon and for the society that organized crime had penetrated so deeply for so long.

The decline of the American Mafia is real and well-documented. The families that once ran New York's construction industry, dominated its waterfront, controlled its garment trade, and corrupted its political apparatus are shadows of what they were at their peak in the 1960s and 1970s. But the story does not end with the Commission Trial or the prosecutions of the 1980s and 1990s. Organized crime, as this textbook has argued throughout, is not a fixed institution. It adapts. Where traditional Mafia structures were dismantled, new criminal organizations, sometimes operating with very different structures and methods, moved into the spaces left behind. Understanding the government's campaign against the American Mafia is not only a story about the past. It is a study in the limits and possibilities of law enforcement in a society where demand for illegal goods and services, and the corruption that feeds on them, remains a persistent feature of economic and political life.

The arc of this chapter, then, is from denial and dysfunction to institutional transformation and prosecutorial innovation, and finally to a measured assessment of what was won, what was lost, and what the struggle continues to mean.

Historical Context

To understand the government's campaign against the American Mafia in the 1970s, 1980s, and 1990s, it is essential first to understand how thoroughly the federal law enforcement apparatus had failed to confront organized crime in the preceding decades. This failure was not simply a matter of incompetence or insufficient resources. It was, in large part, structural and ideological, rooted in the peculiar institutional culture that J. Edgar Hoover built at the FBI over nearly five decades as its director, in the legal tools available to federal prosecutors, and in the political relationships that organized crime had cultivated across American public life.

Hoover became director of the Bureau of Investigation in 1924 and transformed it into the Federal Bureau of Investigation, an agency of unprecedented reach and prestige. His genius lay in public relations and institutional survival as much as in law enforcement. He cultivated relationships with politicians, maintained secret files on potential enemies, and built the FBI's public image around cases that were dramatic, winnable, and politically safe: bank robbers, communist spies, civil rights leaders whom Hoover regarded with deep hostility. Organized crime fit none of these categories. Prosecuting the Mafia required long, expensive, complicated investigations with uncertain outcomes. It risked exposing embarrassing connections between gangsters and the politicians whose goodwill Hoover needed. And it challenged Hoover's preferred narrative of the FBI as the nation's supreme law enforcement authority, a narrative that acknowledged no crime beyond the Bureau's ability to solve.

Hoover's official position for most of his tenure was that a national organized crime syndicate did not exist. When Senator Estes Kefauver's televised hearings in 1950 and 1951 revealed to a fascinated national audience the outlines of a vast criminal network spanning dozens of cities, Hoover was dismissive. When New York State Police stumbled upon the 1957 Apalachin meeting, where more than sixty organized crime figures from across the country gathered at the estate of Joseph Barbara in upstate New York, Hoover could no longer maintain that no national organization existed, but he still resisted committing FBI resources to systematic investigation. It was not until 1959, under pressure from Attorney General William Rogers and later from Robert Kennedy, that the FBI established a formal program specifically targeting organized crime.

The legal tools available to prosecutors in this era were also inadequate to the challenge organized crime posed. Traditional criminal law was built around individual acts: a person committed a crime, was charged with that crime, and could be convicted only if the prosecution proved beyond reasonable doubt that the specific individual committed the specific act. This framework was poorly suited to prosecuting criminal organizations, where the leadership typically insulated itself from direct involvement in violent or obviously criminal acts, and where the criminal enterprise as a whole was more dangerous than any of its individual members. Prosecuting individual mobsters for individual crimes was the legal equivalent of trimming branches from a tree while leaving its roots and trunk untouched.

The political environment presented additional obstacles. Organized crime had spent decades cultivating relationships with local politicians, police officials, and judges. In cities where the Mafia had operated since Prohibition, corruption was not incidental to criminal enterprise, it was structural. Prosecutors who wanted to take on organized crime often found themselves confronting not just the families but the networks of political protection that surrounded them. The Knapp Commission investigation into New York City police corruption in the early 1970s revealed the extraordinary depth of organized crime's penetration of law enforcement and political institutions in America's largest city. Similar patterns existed in Chicago, Philadelphia, New Orleans, Boston, Cleveland, and across the industrial cities where the Mafia had established itself during the Prohibition era.

Against this backdrop, the transformation that occurred in the 1970s, 1980s, and 1990s was remarkable. It required the convergence of new legal tools, new institutional leadership, new prosecutorial strategies, and a shift in the political will to take organized crime seriously as a national problem. The result was the most sustained and successful assault on the American Mafia that had ever been mounted, one that fundamentally changed the organizational landscape of organized crime in the United States, even if it did not eliminate the underlying conditions that had produced the Mafia in the first place.

Section 16.1

The FBI and Organized Crime: From Denial to Commitment

The story of American law enforcement's long failure to confront organized crime effectively begins, as so much of twentieth-century federal law enforcement history does, with J. Edgar Hoover. To understand the transformation that eventually produced the convictions of the 1980s and 1990s, it is necessary to understand what Hoover built at the FBI, why his priorities excluded organized crime, and what happened when he was no longer there to impose them.

Hoover took over a small, demoralized, scandal-plagued Bureau of Investigation in 1924 and spent the next forty-eight years transforming it into arguably the most powerful law enforcement institution in American history. He did so through a combination of genuine organizational talent, aggressive self-promotion, political manipulation, and the systematic cultivation of what modern observers would recognize as a cult of personality around himself and his agency. The FBI under Hoover became identified in the public mind with certain kinds of cases: the pursuit of gangsters like John Dillinger and Pretty Boy Floyd in the 1930s, the apprehension of spies and saboteurs during World War II, and the investigation of alleged communist subversion during the early Cold War. These cases had in common that they were visible, dramatic, and relatively clearly defined. They produced arrests and convictions that could be publicized, and they built the FBI's image as an invincible force for law and order.

Organized crime investigations offered none of these attractions and many additional hazards. Building a case against a Mafia family required years of patient surveillance, the cultivation of informants who were themselves criminals, the navigation of jurisdictional tangles among federal, state, and local agencies, and the willingness to invest enormous resources in cases that might, in the end, produce convictions only for relatively minor offenses. The Mafia's hierarchy was specifically designed to insulate its leadership from provable criminal acts, and the code of silence that governed the organization meant that witnesses were extremely difficult to develop. For Hoover, who tracked his agents' performance through statistics that rewarded quick convictions for easy cases, organized crime was an institutional trap.

There was also a more troubling possibility, one that later investigations suggested was at least partially accurate: that Hoover himself had been compromised by organized crime. Frank Costello and other organized crime figures had social relationships with Hoover that at minimum created the appearance of a conflict of interest. Whether those relationships went further, into actual corruption, remains a matter of historical debate. What is not debatable is that Hoover's FBI did not seriously investigate the Mafia for most of his tenure, and that this failure had consequences that rippled through American public life for decades.

The Kefauver Revelation

The first major public reckoning with organized crime came not from the FBI but from the United States Senate. In 1950, Senator Estes Kefauver of Tennessee persuaded his colleagues to authorize a special committee to investigate organized crime in interstate commerce. The committee's hearings, which ran through 1951, were among the first major political events to be broadcast live on television, and the audience they drew was extraordinary. An estimated thirty million Americans watched as mob figures, politicians, and law enforcement officials testified about a national criminal network that most of the public had only dimly suspected.

The hearings were particularly memorable for their visual drama. Frank Costello, who was considered the most powerful organized crime figure in New York and perhaps the country, agreed to testify only on the condition that his face not be shown on camera. The broadcast directors complied, training their cameras instead on his hands, and the footage of Costello's manicured, nervous fingers twisting and clasping as he deflected questions became one of the iconic images of early television news. The Kefauver hearings established, for the national public, the reality of what the committee called "a sinister criminal organization" with operations across the country, but they did not produce significant legal consequences, and they did not move Hoover to reorganize the FBI's priorities.

The 1957 Apalachin meeting was harder for Hoover to dismiss. When New York State Police Sergeant Edgar Croswell noticed an unusual number of expensive cars gathering at the estate of Joseph Barbara, a Canada Dry bottling company executive with a criminal record, in the small upstate New York town of Apalachin, he set up roadblocks and effectively raided the meeting. More than sixty men were identified at the gathering, including crime bosses from New York, New Jersey, Pennsylvania, Ohio, Texas, Florida, Colorado, and several other states. It was the clearest possible evidence that a national organization existed, that its leadership met regularly, and that the FBI's official position was simply false. Hoover was furious, but he could not ignore the implications. Within two years, the FBI had established its Top Hoodlum Program, assigning agents in major cities to gather intelligence on organized crime figures, though it remained far short of the systematic investigative effort that the situation called for.

Robert Kennedy and the Organized Crime Drive

The most serious pre-Hoover effort to use federal resources against organized crime came during the Kennedy administration, when Robert F. Kennedy served as Attorney General. Kennedy had a personal as well as professional hostility to the Mafia, rooted in his years as counsel to the Senate's McClellan Committee in the late 1950s, where he had developed an obsessive dislike for Teamsters Union president Jimmy Hoffa, whom he believed to be deeply enmeshed with organized crime. As Attorney General, Kennedy reorganized the Department of Justice to make organized crime a priority, more than tripling the number of attorneys in the Organized Crime and Racketeering Section, requiring field offices to report regularly on organized crime figures in their jurisdictions, and pushing the FBI, however reluctantly, to participate in coordinated investigations.

Kennedy's organized crime drive produced meaningful results: prosecutions for tax evasion, fraud, and labor racketeering affected a significant number of organized crime figures, and the intelligence gathered during his tenure provided the foundation for later, more systematic efforts. But Kennedy's assassination in November 1963 and his brother's death in 1968 removed the political force behind the effort, and the Justice Department's attention shifted. The most significant lasting contribution of the Kennedy years was less about specific convictions than about the institutional learning and the collection of intelligence that would eventually be put to use by a new generation of prosecutors working under very different legal authority.

After Hoover: The FBI Transforms

J. Edgar Hoover died on May 2, 1972, at the age of seventy-seven, having served as FBI director for forty-eight years. His death removed an obstacle to organized crime investigation that had been embedded in the agency's institutional culture for nearly half a century, but replacing that culture required years of deliberate effort. The Acting Director who followed Hoover, L. Patrick Gray, was himself forced out by the Watergate scandal within months. The appointment of William Ruckelshaus and then Clarence Kelley as director, and finally William Webster in 1978, represented a systematic effort to professionalize and redirect the agency.

Under Webster and his successor William Sessions, the FBI reorganized its field offices to prioritize organized crime, public corruption, white-collar crime, and terrorism alongside the traditional violent crime and counterintelligence missions. Resources were redirected in ways that would have been unthinkable under Hoover. Electronic surveillance, which Hoover had used extensively for political intelligence-gathering but had deployed selectively against organized crime, became a cornerstone of organized crime investigation. Long-term undercover operations, which required the kind of patient, expensive commitment that Hoover's statistics-driven culture had discouraged, were authorized and resourced. The institutional culture that produced the Donnie Brasco operation, in which FBI Special Agent Joseph Pistone spent six years embedded in the Bonanno crime family under the alias "Donnie Brasco," could not have existed under Hoover. It represented a fundamentally different understanding of what the Bureau was for and what kinds of sacrifice and risk it could ask of its agents.

The development of FBI organized crime squads in New York, Chicago, Philadelphia, Boston, and other cities, each staffed with agents who spent years developing expertise in specific families, created a new kind of institutional knowledge. By the early 1980s, FBI agents knew the organizational charts of major crime families with a granularity that had simply not existed a decade before. They knew who held which positions, who was likely to replace whom, who had debts or grievances that might make them susceptible to recruitment as informants. This knowledge, combined with the new legal tools that RICO provided, made it possible to plan and execute prosecutions of a scale and ambition that would have been inconceivable to an earlier generation of prosecutors.

Section 16.2

The RICO Act: A New Legal Weapon

No single piece of legislation did more to change the landscape of organized crime prosecution in the United States than the Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act of 1970, universally known as RICO. To understand why RICO was revolutionary, it is necessary to understand the legal problem it was designed to solve, and to appreciate how completely it transformed the government's ability to attack criminal organizations as organizations rather than merely pursuing individual members for individual acts.

Before RICO, federal prosecutors confronting organized crime were limited by the same legal framework that applied to ordinary criminal cases. They could charge a Mafia soldier with extortion if they could prove he had extorted someone. They could charge a capo with tax evasion if they could prove he had hidden income. They could charge a boss with conspiracy if they could prove he had agreed with others to commit a specific crime. But the Mafia's organizational structure was specifically designed to defeat this approach. The hierarchy that separated leadership from street-level crime, the culture of omertà that prevented witnesses from testifying, the system of insulated communication through trusted intermediaries, all of it was built to ensure that the most serious criminal acts could not be traced to the people most responsible for them. Individual prosecutions for individual acts, even when successful, produced convictions of individual soldiers or middle-level managers while the organizational structure that directed them remained intact.

The Legislative Origins of RICO

RICO was the product of a sustained effort by scholars, prosecutors, and congressional staff to develop legal tools adequate to the challenge posed by organizations. The statute's principal architect was G. Robert Blakey, a law professor who had served on the staff of the McClellan Committee and who brought both academic rigor and prosecutorial experience to the challenge. Blakey was guided by a fundamental insight: that the law's focus on individual acts was inadequate to address organizations whose criminality was structural rather than incidental. What was needed was a law that could treat the organization itself as the target, that could make participation in the enterprise a federal crime, and that could reach upward through the hierarchy to hold accountable the people who directed criminal activity even when they were insulated from its direct execution.

The resulting statute was drafted as Title IX of the Organized Crime Control Act of 1970, a broader package of anti-crime legislation that also included provisions on wiretapping, grand jury reform, and witness immunity. RICO made it a federal crime to acquire or maintain an interest in, or participate in the affairs of, an enterprise through a pattern of racketeering activity. The statute defined "enterprise" broadly to include any individual, partnership, corporation, association, or other legal entity, as well as any union or group of individuals associated in fact, whether or not a legal entity. This meant that the informal organizational structure of a Mafia family, which was precisely the kind of enterprise that no earlier law had been able to target, fell squarely within RICO's reach.

The key phrase "pattern of racketeering activity" was defined to mean the commission of at least two of a specified list of predicate acts within a ten-year period. The predicate acts were drawn from both federal and state law and included murder, kidnapping, gambling, arson, robbery, bribery, extortion, drug trafficking, and fraud, among others. The breadth of this list meant that virtually the entire range of organized crime activity could serve as the basis for a RICO prosecution. A single defendant could be charged under RICO if prosecutors could show that he had committed two predicate acts in furtherance of an enterprise, even if those acts were not directly connected to each other, provided they both reflected the defendant's participation in the enterprise's affairs.

RICO's Practical Implications

The practical implications of RICO were enormous, and they took time to become fully apparent. The statute had been on the books for nearly a decade before prosecutors in the Southern District of New York and elsewhere began to understand and exploit its full potential. One of the most significant features was the ability to name an entire organization, the enterprise, as the target of prosecution and to charge multiple defendants together as participants in that enterprise. This allowed prosecutors to present to a jury the full scope of an organization's criminal activity, including acts that individual defendants might not themselves have committed, as long as those acts were part of the pattern of racketeering that characterized the enterprise.

This was transformative in practice. In a traditional conspiracy case, a prosecutor presenting evidence about the Gambino family's extortion operations, narcotics trafficking, and labor racketeering would have to charge each defendant only for the specific acts he could be directly connected to, and the jury would hear only the portion of the family's activity relevant to each individual charge. Under RICO, prosecutors could present the family as a criminal enterprise, introduce evidence of all its activities, and argue that each defendant's participation in any part of it made him criminally liable for the enterprise as a whole. The jury heard the full story, not fragments of it, and the story told was of an organization, not merely a collection of individuals who happened to commit crimes.

RICO also carried severe penalties that created enormous leverage in plea negotiations. A conviction under RICO carried a maximum sentence of twenty years per count, and because defendants could be charged with multiple counts, the potential sentences in major RICO cases were effectively unlimited. This leverage was crucial in developing cooperating witnesses: when a defendant faced forty or sixty years in prison on RICO charges, the incentive to cooperate with the government in exchange for a reduced sentence became very powerful, even for individuals who had grown up in a culture that treated cooperation with law enforcement as the greatest possible betrayal.

Additionally, RICO authorized the civil forfeiture of assets acquired through racketeering activity. This provision allowed the government not merely to imprison convicted defendants but to seize the ill-gotten gains that had made organized crime so profitable: real estate holdings, business interests, bank accounts, and other assets that represented the accumulated proceeds of criminal enterprise. Forfeiture attacked organized crime at its economic foundations, rather than simply removing individuals who could be replaced by successors within the organizational hierarchy.

Historical Vocabulary
RICO — Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act

Passed by Congress in 1970 as part of the Organized Crime Control Act, RICO makes it a federal crime to participate in, or conduct the affairs of, an enterprise through a pattern of racketeering activity. A "pattern" requires at least two predicate acts from a specified list of crimes within a ten-year period. An "enterprise" includes any formal or informal group of individuals or entities associated for a common purpose.

RICO transformed organized crime prosecution by allowing the government to target the organization itself rather than only individual criminal acts. A senior organized crime figure could be convicted under RICO based on evidence that he directed an enterprise that committed crimes, even if he was personally insulated from direct participation in those specific acts. The statute also provides for criminal forfeiture of assets and severe penalties of up to twenty years per count, creating significant leverage for prosecutors seeking cooperation from defendants.

Over time, RICO has been applied far beyond organized crime, to white-collar fraud schemes, corrupt government agencies, and even civil litigation. This expansion has generated significant debate about whether the statute's broad reach creates risks of overuse and whether it appropriately distinguishes between genuine criminal enterprises and other forms of organizational wrongdoing.

Electronic Surveillance as an Investigative Tool

Alongside RICO, the expansion of legally authorized electronic surveillance was the other great legal innovation that transformed organized crime investigation in the 1970s and 1980s. The Omnibus Crime Control and Safe Streets Act of 1968 had established a framework for court-authorized wiretapping, requiring law enforcement agencies to obtain judicial approval before intercepting wire or electronic communications. This was a double-edged development: it placed electronic surveillance under judicial oversight that had not previously existed, but it also created a legal pathway for using wiretaps that had previously operated in a legal gray area that made evidence obtained through them vulnerable to challenge at trial.

For organized crime investigations, the Title III wiretap, as court-authorized electronic surveillance under the 1968 Act was commonly known, proved to be an extraordinary tool. Mafia members, accustomed to the relative security of face-to-face meetings in locations they believed were secure, initially did not fully grasp the extent to which electronic surveillance had become possible or the degree to which it was being deployed against them. The FBI's ability to plant listening devices in social clubs, private dining rooms, and automobiles, and to intercept telephone calls with judicial authorization, produced recordings that were often devastating in their directness. Conversations in which mob figures discussed criminal plans, ordered violence, divided criminal proceeds, and complained about the government's surveillance of them became central evidence in major prosecutions.

The famous "Ravenite Social Club" tapes, recorded in the club on Mulberry Street in lower Manhattan where John Gotti held court in the late 1980s and early 1990s, were a prime example. FBI agents managed to plant a listening device in an apartment above the club, where Gotti sometimes met with his inner circle, believing the space was secure. The recordings captured conversations about murder, corruption, and the internal politics of the Gambino family that became central to the prosecution that ultimately convicted Gotti. The irony was acute: Gotti's certainty that his informal hierarchy and his notoriety would protect him led him to speak more freely than a more cautious criminal might have, and that freedom became the government's most powerful weapon against him.

Section 16.3

Informants, Witnesses, and the Breaking of Omertà

If RICO provided the legal framework for prosecuting criminal organizations, and electronic surveillance provided crucial evidence, neither alone would have been sufficient to dismantle the Mafia's upper levels. What ultimately made the great prosecutions of the 1980s and 1990s possible was the steady erosion of omertà, the code of silence that had been the organizational glue holding Mafia families together since their origins in Sicily. That erosion was not accidental. It was the product of deliberate strategy, of institutional innovations in how the government protected and rewarded cooperation, and of the increasing pressures that law enforcement was bringing to bear on individual members of criminal organizations.

The Mafia's code of silence had always been as much practical as cultural. Men who kept silent might, eventually, be released from prison and return to their former positions and the lucrative criminal enterprises associated with them. Men who talked faced the certainty of death if their former associates could reach them, and in a world where families had tentacles in prison systems, law enforcement agencies, and court bureaucracies, the reach of a determined Mafia family was considerable. For most of the organization's history in the United States, this calculus had been highly effective. Prosecutions regularly failed because witnesses recanted, disappeared, or simply could not be found, and jurors in some jurisdictions were themselves susceptible to intimidation or corruption.

Joe Valachi and the First Crack

The first major public breach of omertà came in 1963, when Joseph Valachi, a soldier in the Genovese crime family who was serving time in Atlanta Federal Penitentiary on narcotics charges, agreed to cooperate with the FBI and then testified publicly before the Senate Permanent Subcommittee on Investigations. Valachi's testimony was historic: he was the first member of the Mafia to testify publicly about the organization's inner workings, its structure, its culture, and its operations. He described what he called "Cosa Nostra," meaning "our thing," the American branch of the Sicilian Mafia, and he named names, identified family structures, and explained the Commission and its role in governing the organization's affairs.

Valachi's testimony was not without limitations. He was a low-level soldier with limited direct knowledge of the highest levels of the organization, and some of his claims could not be independently corroborated. His motives for cooperating were mixed: he believed, apparently correctly, that his boss Vito Genovese had ordered his murder while both were in prison, having mistakenly concluded that Valachi was already cooperating. Facing death either at the hands of his associates or, he feared, on the gallows after being charged in a murder that occurred in prison, cooperation with the government must have seemed to Valachi like the most rational available option. But whatever his motivations, the historical significance of his testimony was enormous. He demonstrated that the code of silence could be broken, and he provided the Senate and the public with an insider's map of an organization that had previously been known mainly from the outside.

The Witness Protection Program

Valachi's cooperation highlighted a fundamental problem that had limited the government's ability to develop witnesses against organized crime: what happened to them afterward? Witnesses who testified against the Mafia faced a lifetime of danger. Their identities were known. Their family members could be targeted. They could not safely return to their former communities, former jobs, or former lives. Without some mechanism for providing ongoing protection and support, the rational calculation for most potential witnesses remained strongly against cooperation.

The Federal Witness Security Program, established under the Organized Crime Control Act of 1970 and administered by the U.S. Marshals Service, was designed to change that calculus. Under the program, which became known informally as WITSEC, witnesses who agreed to testify for the government in significant criminal cases could be given entirely new identities, relocated to new communities, and provided with financial support, housing assistance, and protection for themselves and their immediate family members. The program created, in effect, the institutional infrastructure for a kind of state-sponsored disappearance, one that would allow witnesses to escape the reach of the organizations against which they had testified.

WITSEC proved to be a genuine game-changer. Over the decades following its establishment, the program protected thousands of witnesses, relocating them and their families and providing them with new lives. The program was not without its problems: some protected witnesses committed new crimes after relocation, some were unable to adapt to the disruption of their former lives, some eventually broke cover and were found by former associates. The psychological toll on witnesses and their families, who were typically required to sever all contact with relatives and friends they could not take with them into the program, was often severe. And the ethical discomfort of the government rewarding serious criminals with new identities and financial support in exchange for testimony against their former associates generated persistent criticism.

Historical Vocabulary
Witness Protection Program (WITSEC)

The Federal Witness Security Program, established in 1970 and administered by the U.S. Marshals Service, provides new identities and relocation for witnesses who testify for the federal government in significant criminal cases. Participants receive new names, new Social Security numbers, new documentation, and resettlement assistance. In exchange, they are required to sever contact with friends and family members who do not enter the program with them and to comply with the program's conditions.

WITSEC was a critical innovation in the government's fight against organized crime because it fundamentally altered the risk calculus for potential witnesses. For the first time, a member of the Mafia who cooperated with prosecutors could reasonably expect to survive his cooperation. The program made possible the use of insider witnesses whose testimony about the organization's structure and operations was far more damaging than any external evidence, because it came from people who had been participants in the crimes being charged.

The Great Turncoats: Profaci, Franzese, Gravano

By the 1980s, the combination of RICO's severe penalties, the Witness Security Program's protections, and the FBI's increasingly effective surveillance had created conditions in which significant numbers of Mafia members were willing to consider cooperation. The resulting stream of cooperating witnesses, each with detailed insider knowledge of specific families and operations, transformed the government's investigative and prosecutorial capabilities.

Henry Hill, a half-Irish, half-Sicilian associate of the Lucchese family who had been involved in some of the family's most significant criminal enterprises, including a massive scheme to steal cargo from Lufthansa Airlines at Kennedy Airport in 1978, agreed to cooperate with the government in 1980. His knowledge of the family's operations was extensive, and his willingness to testify led to the conviction of numerous Lucchese associates and eventually to his own entry into the witness protection program. Hill's story later became the basis for Nicholas Pileggi's book Wiseguy and Martin Scorsese's film Goodfellas, bringing his account to a vast popular audience and cementing a particular image of Mafia life in American popular culture.

More damaging still to the organizational structure of the Five Families was the cooperation of Salvatore "Sammy the Bull" Gravano. Gravano had served as underboss of the Gambino family under John Gotti, making him the second-ranking figure in the most powerful organized crime family in the country. When he was arrested together with Gotti in 1990, Gravano initially maintained his silence. But as he was held in pretrial detention and listened to recordings of conversations in which Gotti made disparaging remarks about him, Gravano concluded that his loyalty was not being reciprocated. In 1991, he agreed to cooperate with the government, admitted to participating in nineteen murders, and provided testimony that directly contradicted Gotti's denials at trial. Gotti was convicted on all counts in April 1992 and sentenced to life in prison without the possibility of parole.

The moral problems presented by Gravano's cooperation were not minor. He was, by his own admission, a serial murderer who had killed for the organization and on his own behalf over decades. In exchange for testimony that convicted Gotti and enabled further prosecutions, he received a sentence of five years, a result that deeply offended many observers, including the families of his victims. The government's position was that Gravano's cooperation prevented future murders and enabled prosecutions that could not have been brought without him. Critics argued that the arrangement fundamentally compromised the integrity of the justice system by offering what amounted to a grant of near-immunity to a mass murderer. This tension, between the practical necessity of using criminal witnesses and the moral cost of the deals required to obtain their cooperation, has never been fully resolved.

Donnie Brasco and the Undercover Infiltration

Not all of the government's intelligence against the Mafia in this era came from cooperating insiders. One of the most remarkable investigations of the period involved an agent who went inside the organization rather than extracting a member from it. Joseph Pistone, an FBI special agent with a background that allowed him to credibly portray an independent jewel thief and small-time criminal, entered the Bonanno crime family in 1976 under the alias Donnie Brasco and remained embedded there for nearly six years.

Pistone's penetration of the family was extraordinarily deep. He became associated with capo Dominick "Sonny Black" Napolitano, who sponsored his introduction to the family with the intention of eventually proposing him for full membership. The prospect that an FBI agent might actually be formally inducted into a Mafia family was one of the reasons the Bureau eventually decided to pull Pistone out in 1981: had he become a made member, he would have been expected to commit murder as part of the induction ceremony, and no amount of investigative intelligence was worth the life of another person. The evidence Pistone had gathered by the time he surfaced was sufficient to generate more than 200 indictments and approximately 100 convictions, including of several of the most senior figures in the Bonanno family. Sonny Black Napolitano, whose sponsorship of someone who turned out to be an FBI agent represented a catastrophic failure of the family's security protocols, was murdered by his own organization shortly after Pistone's identity became known.

Pistone's story, told in his memoir Donnie Brasco: My Undercover Life in the Mafia and later adapted into a 1997 film, raised difficult questions about the psychological and ethical costs of deep undercover work. Pistone spent six years living as a criminal, maintaining a false identity not just at work but in his personal relationships, committing to a world of violence and illegality that he was simultaneously working to destroy. The experience cost him his marriage and imposed psychological costs that he described in personal terms. It also demonstrated, in the most direct possible way, that the Mafia's vaunted security protocols and screening procedures were not impenetrable, a demonstration that had both practical and psychological significance for the organization's subsequent history.

He was a low-ranking soldier with limited direct access to the highest levels of the organization, and he was testifying with obvious incentives to be seen as cooperative and useful. Some of his claims about specific individuals and events could not be independently verified at the time. However, subsequent investigations, electronic surveillance, and the testimony of later cooperating witnesses confirmed many of the structural features of the organization that Valachi described. His testimony is best understood as a foundational document in the government's developing understanding of organized crime, not as a complete or definitively accurate account of every claim it contains.

Section 16.4

Major Prosecutions: From the Commission Trial to the End of the Dons

The great organized crime prosecutions of the 1980s and 1990s were not isolated events but the culmination of a sustained institutional effort that had been building since the late 1970s. They required years of preparation, the coordination of multiple federal agencies, the patient cultivation of informants and cooperating witnesses, and prosecutorial strategies that were, in some cases, genuinely novel in their ambition. The cases that resulted changed the organizational landscape of American organized crime more profoundly than anything that had come before.

Rudy Giuliani and the Southern District

In 1983, Ronald Reagan appointed Rudolph Giuliani as United States Attorney for the Southern District of New York, the federal judicial district that covered Manhattan and that had jurisdiction over the most significant organized crime operations in the country. Giuliani, who was forty years old and had served as Associate Attorney General under Reagan before taking the U.S. Attorney position, brought to the role both a prosecutorial background and an appetite for high-profile cases that made him uniquely well-suited to lead the organized crime campaign.

Giuliani inherited an office that had already been developing sophisticated RICO cases against organized crime figures, and he accelerated that effort dramatically. He reorganized the office's approach to organized crime prosecution, dedicating significant resources to building major cases against the leadership of each of the Five Families, and he cultivated the FBI's organized crime squads as institutional partners in a way that previous U.S. Attorneys had not fully done. The results were a series of prosecutions that, taken together, represented the most sustained attack on Mafia leadership in American history.

The Commission Trial

The centerpiece of Giuliani's organized crime campaign was the case known as the Commission Trial, which began in September 1985 and resulted in convictions in November 1986. The case was built on a theory that was simultaneously audacious and historically well-grounded: that the American Mafia's governing body, the Commission established by Lucky Luciano in 1931, was itself a criminal enterprise subject to RICO prosecution, and that the bosses who served on it were therefore criminally liable for the full range of activities conducted under its authority.

The defendants in the Commission Trial were eight of the most senior figures in the New York Mafia: the bosses or acting bosses of all five families, plus several other senior figures. They included Anthony "Fat Tony" Salerno of the Genovese family (the nominal boss; the actual boss, Vincenzo Gigante, would be prosecuted separately), Anthony "Tony Ducks" Corallo of the Lucchese family, Philip "Rusty" Rastelli of the Bonanno family, Gennaro "Gerry Lang" Langella of the Colombo family, and Paul Castellano of the Gambino family. Castellano was murdered by John Gotti's faction before the trial concluded, removing the most powerful family's boss from the case, but the prosecution proceeded against the remaining defendants.

The evidence presented at trial was remarkable in its scope. Prosecutors used recordings from FBI bugs placed in the offices and meeting places of the defendants, testimony from cooperating witnesses, and documentary evidence accumulated over years of investigation. The recordings captured defendants discussing Mafia business in remarkable frankness, apparently confident that their meetings were private. One recording captured Salerno complaining about a dispute over concrete contracts, a subject that illuminated one of the Commission's most profitable and most discussed enterprises: the control of the concrete industry in New York City through a mechanism known as the "Concrete Club," by which construction contracts were allocated among companies controlled by or paying tribute to the Five Families.

When the jury returned its verdicts in November 1986, all defendants were convicted. The sentences were severe: Salerno received one hundred years in prison; Corallo received one hundred years; Rastelli received twelve years on top of a prior sentence; Langella received one hundred years. The bosses of all five families were in prison within a few years of the verdict, whether from the Commission Trial or from related prosecutions. The message was unambiguous: the government had identified the Mafia's governing structure and had used the law to decapitate it.

The Colombo, Bonanno, and Genovese Prosecutions

The Commission Trial was the most prominent of the era's organized crime prosecutions, but it was far from the only significant case. In the years before and after it, federal prosecutors in New York, New Jersey, and other jurisdictions mounted major cases against each of the Five Families, systematically working through their leadership structures and creating conditions in which each successive wave of leadership faced prosecution before it could consolidate power and rebuild organizational capacity.

The Bonanno family, already weakened by the FBI's penetration through Joseph Pistone and by internal conflicts that had led to its temporary suspension from the Commission, was subjected to sustained prosecutorial pressure throughout the 1980s. Multiple bosses and acting bosses were convicted and imprisoned in rapid succession, preventing the stabilization that the family needed to recover. The Colombo family was similarly targeted, with prosecution following prosecution through the 1980s and 1990s and a violent internal war between factions within the family adding to its difficulties.

The Genovese family, long considered the most sophisticated and disciplined of the New York families, posed particular prosecutorial challenges because its actual boss, Vincent "the Chin" Gigante, had adopted an extraordinary strategy of feigning mental illness to avoid prosecution. For years, Gigante was observed by FBI surveillance wandering the streets of Greenwich Village in a bathrobe, apparently confused and disoriented, while psychiatrists hired by his defense team provided opinions that he was not competent to stand trial. The charade was ultimately ineffective: Gigante was convicted in 1997, and subsequent evidence made clear that his apparent mental deterioration had been an elaborate performance. His case became a notable example of the lengths to which organized crime figures were willing to go to exploit the legal system's procedures, and of the government's ability, with sufficient evidence, to expose such evasions.

The Fall of John Gotti

Of all the prosecutions of the era, none was more symbolically significant than the conviction of John Gotti in 1992. Gotti had become the public face of the American Mafia in a way that no organized crime boss since Al Capone had been. His expensive suits, his love of publicity, his apparent invincibility despite multiple prosecutions, and his willingness to cultivate a celebrity persona had made him uniquely prominent. He was known as the "Dapper Don" for his meticulous grooming and expensive clothes, and as the "Teflon Don" because of his apparent ability to escape conviction despite numerous prosecutions: he had been acquitted three times in the 1980s, partly through the use of jury tampering and witness intimidation, and the nickname carried a sense that he was untouchable.

The government's fourth attempt to convict Gotti was different from its predecessors in two crucial respects. First, prosecutors had extensive electronic surveillance evidence from bugs planted in locations where Gotti held meetings, including the apartment above the Ravenite Social Club that he apparently believed was secure. The recordings captured Gotti discussing Mafia business, including murder, in considerable detail. Second, and more importantly, Gotti's underboss Salvatore Gravano agreed to cooperate with the government in late 1991. Gravano's testimony corroborated the surveillance recordings and provided context that made their meaning unmistakable to a jury. Gotti was convicted on all fourteen counts of the indictment, including murder, racketeering, obstruction of justice, tax evasion, and illegal gambling, in April 1992. He received a sentence of life in prison without the possibility of parole and died in prison in 2002.

The Gotti conviction was genuinely historic, but it also illustrated some of the limitations of the government's approach. Gravano's deal, under which he admitted nineteen murders and received five years in prison, was widely criticized as excessively lenient. And the Gambino family, while weakened, was not destroyed by Gotti's conviction: it reorganized under new leadership and continued to operate, though at diminished capacity and under sustained prosecutorial pressure. The pattern repeated itself across the Five Families: convictions disrupted and weakened organizations, but did not eliminate them.

Historical Debate

RICO: An Indispensable Tool or an Overreaching Statute?

The question: RICO was designed to combat organized crime, but its broad language has led to its application in contexts far beyond the Mafia prosecutions of the 1980s. Scholars, defense attorneys, and civil libertarians have debated both the appropriateness of this expansion and whether RICO's fundamental design creates risks of government overreach.

Interpretation A — RICO as a Necessary and Appropriate Tool: Defenders of RICO argue that it was precisely the statute's breadth that made it effective against organizations that had spent decades defeating narrower legal approaches. The inability of traditional criminal law to reach the organizational dimension of crime was not a theoretical problem; it was a practical failure that allowed the Mafia to operate for decades despite individual prosecutions of its members. RICO's enterprise theory accurately captured the reality of organizational criminality, and its application beyond the Mafia, to fraud schemes, corrupt officials, and other organizations that operate through patterns of criminal activity, reflects a legitimate generalization of the same principle.
Interpretation B — RICO as an Overreaching Statute: Critics argue that RICO's breadth creates serious risks of prosecutorial overreach. The statute's requirement of only two predicate acts within ten years, drawn from an expansive list, means that it can potentially apply to any organization in which any two members commit almost any crime in a ten-year period. Defense attorneys have argued that RICO has been used against organizations, including political groups, professional associations, and corporations, in ways that go far beyond the organized crime context for which it was designed. The statute's severe penalties and its use of forfeiture also create leverage that may induce innocent defendants to plead guilty rather than risk catastrophic sentences at trial.

Evidence: The success of RICO in producing the convictions of the 1980s and 1990s is well-documented and not seriously disputed. The evidence for overuse is also substantial: RICO has been applied in cases involving environmental violations, securities fraud, anti-abortion protests, college sports programs, and political campaigns, among many other contexts. Constitutional challenges to the statute have mostly failed in the courts, but the debate about its appropriate scope continues in legal scholarship and policy discussion.

Section 16.5

The Decline of the American Mafia

The convictions of the 1980s and 1990s marked the beginning of a sustained decline in the power and organizational capacity of the American Mafia from which it has never fully recovered. By the first decade of the twenty-first century, the Five Families of New York were shadows of their former selves: smaller in membership, weaker in their ability to enforce discipline, diminished in their control over legitimate industries, and far less powerful in their political connections than they had been at the height of their influence in the 1960s and 1970s. Understanding why requires examining not only the direct effects of the prosecutions but also the broader social and economic changes that transformed the environment in which organized crime had flourished.

The Direct Effects of Prosecution

The most immediate and obvious effect of the government's campaign was the imprisonment of virtually the entire senior leadership of each of the Five Families over a period of roughly two decades. This was not simply a matter of removing individuals who could be replaced. Each prosecution disrupted organizational relationships, exposed vulnerabilities, created uncertainty about who was cooperating with the government, and imposed enormous legal costs on families that had to fund the defense of multiple prosecutions simultaneously. The culture of paranoia that surrounded the possibility of additional informants made it difficult for the organizations to function normally: members who in an earlier era would have spoken freely with one another about criminal business became cautious, aware that any conversation might be recorded and that any associate might be cooperating.

The succession crisis created by repeated prosecutions was particularly damaging. When a boss was convicted and imprisoned, the family had to find a replacement. But the pool of potential leaders was itself depleted by prosecution and imprisonment, and new bosses faced the same investigative pressure that had brought down their predecessors. In some families, the acting boss was himself arrested before he could be formally recognized as boss. In others, the frantic cycling through leadership created factional conflicts that weakened the family further. The Colombo family's civil war in the early 1990s, in which factions loyal to different claimants to the boss position engaged in open street violence, was an extreme example of the organizational dysfunction that sustained prosecution could produce.

The erosion of omertà, once it began, proved self-reinforcing. As the government demonstrated that it could develop cooperating witnesses at the highest levels of the organization, the deterrent effect of the code of silence weakened. A soldier or capo who was contemplating cooperation could no longer be certain that his loyalty would be reciprocated, given the demonstrated willingness of even senior figures to cooperate when facing long prison sentences. This uncertainty made cooperation a more rational option for more people, which in turn produced more cooperation, which further eroded the confidence that had once made omertà enforceable.

Social and Economic Changes

The prosecutions did not occur in a social and economic vacuum. The decline of the American Mafia was also a product of fundamental changes in the communities and industries where it had been most deeply embedded, changes that had been underway since at least the 1960s and that made the environment of the late twentieth century significantly less hospitable to the kind of organized crime that the Five Families had practiced.

The deindustrialization of American cities, which accelerated dramatically in the 1970s and 1980s, eliminated many of the economic sectors that had been most profitable for organized crime. The garment industry, once a major source of labor racketeering income for the Lucchese and Gambino families, shrank dramatically as clothing production moved offshore. The longshoremen's unions, which organized crime had controlled for decades and from which it had extracted enormous tribute, became less powerful as containerization and changing trade patterns reduced the labor-intensive loading and unloading that had made those unions so valuable. The construction industry, which remained important and remained subject to organized crime influence longer than many others, was also subject to eventual reform, driven partly by the public attention generated by prosecution.

The demographic transformation of the communities where organized crime had been rooted also played a role. The Italian-American neighborhoods of New York, Chicago, Philadelphia, and Boston, which had provided both the membership and much of the customer base for organized crime operations, dispersed into the suburbs over the postwar decades. This dispersal weakened the social networks and local loyalties that had made it possible to recruit from a pool of young men who grew up in proximity to organized crime and for whom it represented an available and attractive career path. The children and grandchildren of immigrants who had populated the Mafia's lower ranks found other opportunities, legal and illegal, that had not been available to their ancestors.

The relationship between organized crime and its ethnic community base had always been complex and ambivalent. Italian-American communities had not monolithically supported the Mafia; many Italian-Americans were its victims, subjected to extortion and violence, or felt the stigma of association with criminal activity that was publicly identified with their ethnicity. But the organized crime families had also been, in a perverse way, institutions embedded in their communities, providing certain kinds of services, employment, and social connections that made them, if not respected, at least tolerated as a feature of the landscape. As those communities dispersed and as legal economic opportunities opened more widely, the social base from which the Mafia drew its membership and its social protection narrowed significantly.

What Filled the Vacuum

The decline of the traditional American Mafia did not mean the decline of organized crime in the United States. It meant, rather, a reorganization of the criminal landscape in ways that created new challenges for law enforcement even as the familiar threat of the Sicilian-American families diminished. The drug trade, which the traditional Mafia families had historically approached with ambivalence (it was highly profitable but carried severe federal penalties that made it risky, and some family leaders had formally prohibited their members from dealing in narcotics), was dominated by newer organizations with different structures and different geographic bases.

In the cities where the Mafia had operated, the retreat of the families from certain criminal markets created opportunities for other criminal organizations: African-American and Latino gangs moved into drug distribution in inner-city neighborhoods that the Mafia had previously controlled; Russian organized crime groups, many of them formed by emigrants from the former Soviet Union, established themselves in Brighton Beach and other communities; the Mexican cartels, discussed elsewhere in this textbook, became the dominant force in wholesale drug supply across the country. None of these organizations replicated the specific structure of the Italian-American Mafia, but all of them engaged in organized criminal activity that generated violence, corruption, and economic harm in the communities where they operated.

The lesson, which this textbook has argued throughout, was a fundamental one about the nature of organized crime: it is not a fixed institution but an adaptive response to the persistent demand for illegal goods and services and to the opportunities created by corruption and weak governance. Eliminating specific organizations does not eliminate the conditions that produce organized crime. It may change its form, shift its geography, or alter its methods, but as long as the underlying conditions persist, criminal organizations will continue to emerge to serve the markets that prohibition creates and to exploit the vulnerabilities that corruption provides.

Multiple Perspectives

The Decline of the Mafia: Competing Interpretations

Federal Prosecutors and Law Enforcement: The prosecutions of the 1980s and 1990s represent a genuine and historic achievement. By systematically applying RICO, developing cooperating witnesses, and sustaining investigative pressure over decades, law enforcement succeeded in dismantling organizational structures that had operated with relative impunity for half a century. The decline in the Mafia's ability to corrupt politicians, control labor unions, and dominate key industries represents a real improvement in the quality of governance and economic fairness in affected cities.
Defense Attorneys and Civil Libertarians: The prosecutions achieved their results through methods that raise serious concerns about prosecutorial overreach, the coercive use of plea bargaining, and the ethical compromises involved in deals with witnesses who themselves committed serious crimes. The use of RICO to charge entire organizations rather than specific acts, combined with severe penalties that created irresistible pressure to cooperate or plead guilty, means that some convictions may have been obtained against individuals whose actual culpability was less clear than the government's narrative suggested. The expansion of RICO to non-Mafia contexts also creates ongoing risks of abuse.
Italian-American Communities: The prosecutions produced a mixed legacy. On one hand, they reduced the extortion, corruption, and violence that the Mafia had imposed on Italian-American neighborhoods and industries for decades. On the other hand, the public prominence of organized crime trials reinforced stereotypes about Italian-Americans and criminality that were already well-established in popular culture, and the association of Italian-American identity with organized crime caused social harm that extended far beyond the actual participants in criminal organizations.
Organized Crime Scholars: The decline of the traditional American Mafia is real but should be understood in context. The organizational forms that characterized the Five Families, with their formal hierarchies, initiation rituals, and ethnic exclusivity, represented a specific historical configuration that was always vulnerable to the particular legal tools that RICO provided. The disappearance of this particular form does not mean the disappearance of organized crime; it means its reorganization into forms that may be less visible, more decentralized, and potentially more resilient against the specific prosecutorial strategies that worked against the traditional families.
Historical Vocabulary
Criminal Enterprise (in RICO)

Under the Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act, a "criminal enterprise" is defined as any individual, partnership, corporation, association, or other legal entity, and any union or group of individuals associated in fact although not a legal entity, engaged in, or the activities of which affect, interstate or foreign commerce. The definition is deliberately broad, encompassing formal organizations like corporations and informal associations like a Mafia family.

The enterprise concept was the core innovation of RICO's design. By making it a crime to participate in the affairs of an enterprise through a pattern of racketeering activity, RICO allowed prosecutors to target the organization itself rather than only the individual criminal acts committed by its members. Senior figures who directed criminal activity but were insulated from direct participation in specific crimes could be prosecuted as participants in the enterprise, based on evidence of the organization's overall pattern of activity. This enterprise theory was the legal foundation of all the major Mafia prosecutions of the 1980s and 1990s.

Cause and Effect

The Organizational Decline of the American Mafia

Long-Term Causes

Deindustrialization of cities where organized crime was rooted; dispersal of Italian-American communities from urban neighborhoods; improvement in legal economic opportunities; declining social legitimacy of organized crime in its base communities; accumulation of intelligence by law enforcement over decades.

Immediate Causes

Passage of RICO (1970); establishment of WITSEC (1970); death of Hoover and FBI reorganization (1972); electronic surveillance capabilities; cultivation of major cooperating witnesses; sustained prosecutorial commitment in Southern District of New York.

The Event or Transformation

Commission Trial convictions (1986); convictions of all Five Family leadership structures between 1980 and 2000; repeated prosecution of successive leadership generations; systematic forfeiture of criminal assets; erosion of omertà through cooperating witnesses.

Consequences

Dramatic reduction in Five Families' organizational capacity; loss of control over major industries including construction and waterfront; reduction in ability to corrupt political institutions; succession of other criminal organizations into vacated criminal markets; reorganization of organized crime into less hierarchical and less ethnically exclusive forms.

Historical complexity: The decline of the American Mafia cannot be attributed to any single cause. Law enforcement pressure was decisive, but it operated within a broader context of social and economic change that had been eroding the Mafia's foundations for decades. Neither the prosecutions alone nor the social changes alone would have been sufficient; it was their combination that produced the transformation.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Organized Crime in America: From the Mafia's Peak to the Late Twentieth Century

What Changed

  • The organizational capacity of the Five Families was dramatically reduced through sustained prosecution
  • The formal hierarchy of the traditional Mafia, with its bosses, underbosses, and capos, was repeatedly disrupted
  • Organized crime's ability to corrupt major political figures and institutions declined substantially
  • Italian-American families lost their dominant position in major criminal markets, including construction and waterfront labor
  • The code of omertà was fundamentally eroded by the cooperation of senior members of the organization
  • The legal tools available to prosecutors were permanently transformed by RICO and the development of witness protection

What Stayed the Same

  • Demand for illegal goods and services, particularly drugs, continued unabated and continued to create markets for criminal organizations
  • Economic inequality and limited legal opportunities in certain communities continued to make criminal enterprise an attractive option for some individuals
  • Political corruption, while reduced in scope, was not eliminated; organized crime's capacity to corrupt officials diminished but did not disappear
  • The fundamental dynamic of criminal organizations providing prohibited goods and services in exchange for profit remained unchanged
  • New criminal organizations emerged to serve markets that the traditional Mafia had vacated or could no longer effectively control

Why the pattern matters: The persistence of organized crime alongside the decline of specific organizational forms is one of the central lessons of this chapter. Law enforcement can defeat particular criminal organizations, but defeating the conditions that produce organized crime requires addressing the demand for illegal goods, the economic inequality that makes criminal careers attractive, and the corruption that allows criminal organizations to purchase protection. These are social and economic challenges that lie beyond the capacity of law enforcement alone to resolve.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has traced the long and complicated story of how the United States government learned, over the course of more than half a century, to fight the American Mafia effectively. That story begins not with success but with institutional failure: the refusal of J. Edgar Hoover's FBI to acknowledge the existence of a national organized crime syndicate, the inadequacy of pre-RICO legal tools to attack criminal organizations as organizations, and the political protection that decades of investment in corruption had purchased for the Mafia's leaders.

The transformation began slowly and unevenly. The Kefauver hearings of 1950 and 1951 brought organized crime to national attention but produced limited lasting consequences. The Apalachin disaster of 1957 forced a minimal acknowledgment of organized crime's national scale. Robert Kennedy's prosecutorial drive in the early 1960s built important intelligence and produced some significant convictions, but lacked the legal tools to sustain a systematic campaign against organizational structures. Joe Valachi's testimony in 1963 provided the first insider's map of the Mafia's organization, but the man who had the most to gain from that testimony, Hoover, was the most resistant to acting on it.

The critical turning points came in 1970 and 1972. The Organized Crime Control Act of 1970, with its RICO provisions and its creation of the federal witness protection program, gave prosecutors legal tools of unprecedented power. Hoover's death in 1972 removed the central obstacle to the FBI's commitment to organized crime investigation. These developments set the stage for the transformation of the 1980s, when a new generation of prosecutors and agents, armed with new tools and new institutional priorities, began systematically dismantling the organizational structures that had governed American organized crime for half a century.

RICO proved to be the revolutionary legal instrument its architect, G. Robert Blakey, had designed it to be. By allowing the government to target criminal enterprises as enterprises rather than merely prosecuting individuals for individual acts, RICO made possible the Commission Trial of 1985 and 1986, in which all five New York family bosses were convicted together, and a subsequent wave of prosecutions that systematically depleted the leadership of each of the Five Families. The enterprise theory that RICO embodied, combined with the severe penalties the statute authorized and the forfeiture provisions that allowed the government to seize criminal assets, created a prosecutorial toolkit of extraordinary power.

Electronic surveillance, authorized by the 1968 Omnibus Crime Control Act, provided the evidence that made many of these prosecutions possible. FBI bugs in the social clubs, apartments, and offices where organized crime figures met captured conversations of devastating candor, conversations that had been held in places the participants believed were secure. The Ravenite Social Club tapes, which captured John Gotti discussing murder and Mafia business with apparent confidence that he was unobserved, were emblematic of a pattern repeated across multiple families: the Mafia's traditional methods of security were inadequate to the new technical capabilities that law enforcement had deployed against them.

The erosion of omertà, once begun, proved irreversible. Joe Valachi had shown that the code of silence could be broken; the witness protection program created the institutional conditions that made breaking it a viable survival strategy; and RICO's severe penalties made the calculus of cooperation increasingly attractive to defendants facing decades in prison. By the time Salvatore Gravano agreed to testify against John Gotti in 1991, admitting to nineteen murders in exchange for a five-year sentence, the code that had once been the organizational glue of the Mafia had been fundamentally and probably permanently compromised.

The decline of the traditional American Mafia that resulted from these prosecutions is real and well-documented. The Five Families are smaller, weaker, and less able to corrupt political institutions than they were at their peak. Industries that were once thoroughly dominated by organized crime, including construction, the waterfront, and the garment trade, have been substantially, if not completely, freed from that domination. The quality of governance in the cities where the Mafia was most deeply embedded has improved, in part because the criminal networks that corrupted politicians and officials have been weakened.

But the chapter has also argued that this decline must be understood in proper perspective. Law enforcement pressure was decisive, but it operated within a broader context of social and economic change, including deindustrialization, the dispersal of Italian-American communities, and the opening of legal economic opportunities, that had been eroding the Mafia's foundations for decades before the prosecutions accelerated in the 1980s. The decline of the traditional Mafia did not mean the decline of organized crime; it meant its reorganization into new forms, by new groups, in response to new market conditions. The underlying drivers of organized crime, the demand for prohibited goods, economic inequality, corruption, and weak governance, remain as present in the twenty-first century as they were in the twentieth.

The government's campaign against the American Mafia is, ultimately, a story about institutional learning and its limits. Law enforcement learned, slowly and painfully, how to fight a specific form of organized crime. The tools it developed, RICO, electronic surveillance, witness protection, and the enterprise theory of prosecution, were genuine innovations that transformed the legal landscape. But criminal organizations, as this textbook has argued throughout, are adaptive. They evolve in response to the pressures they face, and the specific organizational form that RICO was designed to defeat may not be the form that organized crime takes in the decades ahead. The story of the government striking back against the American Mafia is a chapter, not a conclusion, in the longer history of the struggle between criminal enterprise and the rule of law.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Asset forfeiture
The legal mechanism by which the government may seize property connected to criminal activity; under RICO, property acquired through racketeering is subject to forfeiture upon conviction.
Commission Trial
The 1985–1986 federal prosecution of the bosses of all five New York organized crime families, charged under RICO with participating in the Commission as a criminal enterprise.
Cooperating witness
A defendant or potential defendant who agrees to provide testimony for the government in exchange for a reduced charge or sentence; the central tool in organized crime prosecution after RICO.
Criminal enterprise
Under RICO, any individual, entity, or informal group of individuals associated in fact and engaged in activity affecting interstate commerce; the organizational target of RICO prosecution.
Electronic surveillance
The interception of wire, oral, or electronic communications; court-authorized under Title III of the 1968 Omnibus Crime Control Act; a central investigative tool in organized crime cases.
Enterprise theory
The legal concept, embodied in RICO, that criminal organizations can be prosecuted as organizations rather than only as collections of individuals committing individual acts.
Immunity
A grant by the government, usually in exchange for testimony, protecting a witness from prosecution for the crimes about which they testify; a key tool in developing organized crime witnesses.
Informant
A person who provides information to law enforcement about criminal activity, often while remaining in the criminal organization; distinguished from a cooperating witness who testifies publicly.
Omertà
The code of silence governing relationships between Mafia members and with law enforcement; its erosion through cooperating witnesses was central to the government's success in the 1980s and 1990s.
Pattern of racketeering
Under RICO, the commission of at least two predicate acts from a specified list within a ten-year period, demonstrating participation in the affairs of a criminal enterprise.
Plea bargain
A negotiated agreement in which a defendant agrees to plead guilty, usually to lesser charges or in exchange for a reduced sentence; a critical mechanism in organized crime prosecution.
Predicate act
Under RICO, any of a specified list of criminal acts whose repeated commission constitutes a "pattern of racketeering activity"; the list includes murder, extortion, drug trafficking, fraud, and many other offenses.
Proffer agreement
A preliminary agreement under which a potential cooperating witness provides information to the government, typically with some protection against use of that information in prosecution, as a precondition to more formal cooperation.
Racketeering
The conduct of an ongoing criminal enterprise; under RICO, participation in an enterprise through a pattern of racketeering activity is a federal crime carrying a maximum sentence of twenty years per count.
RICO
Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act (1970); federal statute making it a crime to participate in the affairs of an enterprise through a pattern of racketeering activity; the primary legal tool in the government's campaign against the American Mafia.
Title III wiretap
Court-authorized electronic surveillance under the Omnibus Crime Control and Safe Streets Act of 1968; requires judicial approval and is subject to statutory limitations on duration and use.
Top Hoodlum Program
The FBI program established in 1959, under pressure following the Apalachin meeting, to assign agents in major cities to gather intelligence on organized crime figures; the Bureau's first systematic organized crime effort.
Undercover operation
A law enforcement investigation in which an agent assumes a false identity to gather evidence within a criminal organization; the Donnie Brasco operation is the most significant example in Mafia investigation.
Witness Security Program (WITSEC)
The federal program, established in 1970 and administered by the U.S. Marshals Service, that provides new identities and relocation for witnesses who testify in significant criminal cases.

Major People and Places

Identification & Significance

J. Edgar Hoover (1895–1972)
Director of the FBI from 1924 to 1972; his institutional priorities and political caution caused the Bureau to deny and minimize organized crime for decades, representing one of the most significant failures of American law enforcement in the twentieth century.
Estes Kefauver (1903–1963)
U.S. Senator who chaired the Special Committee on Organized Crime (1950–1951); his televised hearings established public awareness of the Mafia's national scope and anticipated later investigative approaches.
Robert F. Kennedy (1925–1968)
U.S. Attorney General (1961–1964) who made organized crime prosecution a Justice Department priority; his drive produced significant intelligence and prosecutions and established the institutional foundation for later efforts.
G. Robert Blakey (b. 1936)
Law professor and congressional staffer who was the principal author of the RICO statute; his enterprise theory of criminal organization transformed the legal landscape of organized crime prosecution.
Joe Valachi (1904–1971)
Genovese family soldier who became the first major public Mafia informant (1963); his Senate testimony provided the first insider's account of the organization's structure and operations.
Joseph Pistone / Donnie Brasco (b. 1939)
FBI special agent who spent six years embedded in the Bonanno crime family under an assumed identity, producing evidence that led to over 200 indictments and demonstrating the Mafia's vulnerability to undercover infiltration.
Rudolph Giuliani (b. 1944)
U.S. Attorney for the Southern District of New York (1983–1989); architect of the Commission Trial and numerous other major organized crime prosecutions; his use of RICO against the Mafia's leadership made the 1980s the most productive decade in organized crime prosecution history.
Paul Castellano (1915–1985)
Boss of the Gambino family; one of the defendants in the Commission Trial; murdered by John Gotti's faction in December 1985 before the trial concluded, an act that itself led to Gotti's later prosecution.
Anthony "Fat Tony" Salerno (1911–1992)
Acting boss of the Genovese family; convicted in the Commission Trial and sentenced to 100 years; his conviction symbolized the government's successful use of RICO against the Mafia's governing structure.
Henry Hill (1943–2012)
Lucchese family associate who became a cooperating witness in 1980; his cooperation led to multiple convictions and his account was later the basis for the film Goodfellas.
John Gotti (1940–2002)
Boss of the Gambino family from 1985; his celebrity, his earlier acquittals, and the ultimate conviction made him the defining figure of the Mafia's final period of public prominence.
Salvatore "Sammy the Bull" Gravano (b. 1945)
Gambino underboss who cooperated against John Gotti (1991); admitted to nineteen murders; his cooperation at the highest organizational level symbolized the irreversible erosion of omertà.
Vincent "the Chin" Gigante (1928–2005)
Actual boss of the Genovese family who feigned mental illness to avoid prosecution for years; ultimately convicted in 1997 in a case that exposed his elaborate evasion strategy.
The Ravenite Social Club
Mulberry Street club in lower Manhattan where John Gotti held court; FBI bugs in an apartment above the club produced recordings that were central evidence in Gotti's prosecution.
The Southern District of New York
Federal judicial district encompassing Manhattan; the primary venue for major organized crime prosecutions in the 1980s and 1990s, and the district where Giuliani assembled the prosecutorial team that tried the Commission case.

Chapter Reflection

The story told in this chapter is, on one level, a success story: the United States government, after decades of failure, institutional inertia, and political obstacle, developed the legal tools, the investigative capabilities, and the prosecutorial will to dismantle the organizational structures of the American Mafia in a way that would have seemed fantastical to observers in 1960 or even 1975. The Commission Trial, the conviction of John Gotti, and the sustained prosecution of the Five Families through the 1990s represent genuine achievements of institutional learning, legal innovation, and individual commitment that deserve recognition.

But the chapter has also argued, consistently, that this success must be understood in proper historical perspective. The decline of the traditional American Mafia is not the end of the story of organized crime in the United States; it is a chapter in a much longer story about the relationship between criminal organizations and the societies that produce them. The underlying conditions that created the Mafia, the demand for prohibited goods and services, the economic inequality that makes criminal careers attractive to some people, the corruption that allows criminal organizations to purchase protection from the institutions nominally charged with controlling them, have not been eliminated. They have persisted, and they have continued to generate new criminal organizations in the space left by weakened traditional ones.

The broader textbook of which this chapter is a part has argued from its opening that organized crime is best understood as an adaptive institution, one that changes alongside the states, economies, technologies, and social structures within which it operates. The government's campaign against the American Mafia illustrates both sides of this dynamic: law enforcement can learn and adapt, developing tools and strategies capable of defeating specific organizational forms; but criminal enterprise can also adapt, reorganizing itself in response to the pressures it faces. The history examined in this chapter is not the final word on that struggle. It is one more chapter in a conflict that has no foreseeable end because it is rooted in fundamental features of human social organization that no government has yet found a way to fully address.

What this chapter should leave with students is not merely a knowledge of specific cases, legal statutes, and organizational developments, though that knowledge is important. It should leave them with a set of analytical tools for thinking about the relationship between criminal organizations, legal institutions, and the societies in which both are embedded: tools for asking why specific law enforcement strategies succeeded or failed, what social and economic conditions created the environments in which particular forms of organized crime flourished, and what it means to declare victory in a conflict against an adversary as adaptive and persistent as organized crime. These are questions that extend far beyond the specific history of the American Mafia, and they are questions that the chapters that follow in this textbook will continue to explore.

Policing Organized Crime

Organized Crime: A Global History

Chapter Opening

"You've got to get inside the organization to understand it — and getting inside means becoming part of it, at least temporarily. That is the fundamental dilemma of investigating organized crime."
— Former FBI Special Agent, reflecting on undercover methodology, 1990s

In the spring of 1957, the New York State Police sergeant Edgar Croswell was doing routine detective work near the small upstate town of Apalachin when he noticed an unusual number of expensive automobiles with out-of-state license plates gathering at the estate of Joseph Barbara, a soft-drink distributor with a known criminal past. Acting on instinct, Croswell set up a surveillance post and called for backup. What he stumbled onto that November afternoon would become one of the most consequential accidents in the history of American law enforcement: a national conclave of organized crime leaders, more than sixty of them, gathered from across the United States and representing the senior leadership of the American Mafia. When state troopers blocked the roadways, the attendees fled into the surrounding woods — well-dressed men in expensive suits scrambling through mud and underbrush — and dozens were apprehended for questioning.

The Apalachin meeting was significant not because the arrests led to prosecutions, for the most part they did not. Most charges were eventually dismissed on appeal. What the meeting proved, embarrassingly and publicly, was that the Federal Bureau of Investigation under J. Edgar Hoover had spent years denying the existence of a national organized crime network, insisting that crime in America was a local phenomenon best addressed by local police. Apalachin shattered that comfortable fiction. Here was unmistakable documentary evidence, in the form of the arrested attendees themselves, that a national and hierarchical criminal organization existed and had been operating for decades without serious federal scrutiny. Within months, the political pressure for a serious federal response to organized crime had become irresistible.

The story of policing organized crime is a story of institutional learning, technological innovation, legal evolution, political will, human courage, and persistent frustration. It spans more than a century, from the earliest municipal efforts to suppress criminal gangs in the immigrant neighborhoods of nineteenth-century American cities, through the dramatic federal campaigns of the mid-twentieth century, to the complex international law-enforcement partnerships required to address organized crime in an era of globalization. It is a story that reaches beyond the United States to encompass the parallel efforts of police forces, magistrates, prosecutors, and intelligence agencies around the world, each grappling with the challenge of penetrating, understanding, and dismantling organizations whose survival depends on secrecy, corruption, and violence.

What makes organized crime so difficult to police is precisely what makes it organized. Unlike individual criminal offenders, whose guilt can often be established through direct evidence of a specific act, organized crime leaders rarely commit crimes themselves. They give orders, receive profits, and exercise power while insulating themselves from the illegal activities that generate their wealth. A mob boss who orders a murder, profits from a gambling network, and maintains corruption throughout a city's political structure may not have touched a weapon, handled gambling proceeds, or personally bribed an official in years. Proving his criminal role requires not merely evidence of an individual criminal act but evidence of his position within and control over a criminal enterprise, which requires exactly the kind of sustained, resource-intensive, complex investigation that organized crime has historically been designed to defeat.

Investigators have developed a wide array of tools and techniques to address this challenge. Wiretapping and electronic surveillance allow law enforcement to monitor criminal communications without the dangerous necessity of physical presence. Undercover operations place officers or agents inside criminal organizations to gather evidence from the inside. Informants, sometimes recruited from within criminal networks themselves, provide intelligence that no external surveillance can replicate. Financial investigations trace the flow of illegal money through banking systems, real estate transactions, and business structures, following the profits back to those who control them. Grand juries compel testimony and gather evidence under legal compulsion. Witness protection programs make it possible for former insiders to testify against their former associates without fear of fatal retaliation.

Each of these tools raises profound questions that go beyond law enforcement effectiveness to implicate the deepest values of democratic society. Wiretapping and surveillance gather evidence but also intrude upon the privacy of innocent people who happen to communicate with criminal suspects. Undercover operations infiltrate criminal organizations but also involve agents of the state in deception, and sometimes in facilitating the very crimes they are supposed to prevent. Informants provide crucial intelligence but also compromise prosecutions, receive illegal benefits in exchange for cooperation, and sometimes continue committing crimes while working with law enforcement. Witness protection programs give new identities to convicted felons and accessories to serious crimes. At each step, the effort to combat organized crime requires democratic societies to make difficult tradeoffs between security and liberty, between effectiveness and integrity.

This chapter examines the history and methods of policing organized crime, tracing the development of investigative tools and legal frameworks from the late nineteenth century through the present. It considers how law enforcement has adapted to the changing nature of criminal organizations, how different nations have approached the problem, and what the persistent tensions between effective policing and civil liberties reveal about the values of democratic societies under pressure. The history of policing organized crime is not simply the history of catching criminals. It is the history of a democratic society deciding how far it is willing to go, what methods it is willing to employ, and what principles it is willing to compromise in the name of public safety and order.

Historical Context

The history of policing organized crime cannot be understood without recognizing the structural disadvantages under which law enforcement has historically operated. In democratic societies, law enforcement is constrained by constitutional protections, legal procedures, evidentiary rules, and institutional limitations that criminal organizations are not. Police must obtain warrants before searching premises. Prosecutors must prove guilt beyond a reasonable doubt. Defendants have the right to confront witnesses against them, which makes the use of anonymous informants legally complicated. Appeals courts overturn convictions when evidence has been improperly gathered. These constraints are not weaknesses of democratic governance; they are its essential features. But they create real asymmetries that organized crime has learned to exploit.

For most of the nineteenth century and well into the twentieth, local police departments were the primary institutions charged with controlling criminal activity in American cities. These departments were, in many places, deeply compromised by exactly the corruption that organized crime required to operate. Political machines that controlled urban governments also controlled police appointments, creating environments in which officers who tolerated or participated in illegal activities could advance, while those who tried to disrupt organized vice operations faced professional retaliation. The symbiotic relationship between machine politics, police corruption, and organized crime that characterized cities like New York, Chicago, Boston, and New Orleans was not accidental. It was structural, the product of political economies in which criminal organizations provided services that many people wanted, generated revenue that political organizations needed, and delivered votes and organizational capacity that machine politicians depended upon.

Federal law enforcement was slow to develop and initially limited in its authority over most criminal matters. The Federal Bureau of Investigation, established in its modern form under J. Edgar Hoover in the 1920s, focused heavily on bank robbery, kidnapping, espionage, and communist subversion, categories of crime in which Hoover was interested and in which federal jurisdiction was clear. For decades, Hoover insisted that organized crime was not a federal problem and that the FBI had no authority or interest in pursuing it. Whether this reflected genuine jurisdictional concerns, a prudent assessment of available resources, or more troubling motivations — including the possibility that Hoover had been compromised by his personal relationship with organized crime figures, who reportedly possessed information he wished to suppress — remains debated by historians. The effect, whatever the cause, was to leave the federal government on the sidelines during decades when the American Mafia consolidated its power and expanded its operations.

The watershed period for federal anti-organized-crime law enforcement came in the 1950s and 1960s, driven by a combination of congressional investigations, dramatic revelations about the scope of organized criminal activity, and the gradual development of new legal tools. The Kefauver Committee hearings of 1950 and 1951 brought organized crime before a national television audience for the first time, allowing millions of Americans to watch mobsters invoke their Fifth Amendment rights against self-incrimination. The McClellan Committee hearings of the late 1950s, with Robert F. Kennedy as chief counsel, produced further dramatic public exposure of organized crime's reach into labor unions, construction, and politics. The Valachi hearings of 1963 brought the first inside account of the American Mafia from a member who was willing to testify publicly, providing law enforcement with the organizational map they had lacked.

The legal revolution that gave federal law enforcement its most powerful tool against organized crime came in 1970, with the passage of the Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act, universally known as RICO. RICO created federal crimes not merely for individual criminal acts but for participation in and control of criminal enterprises, allowing prosecutors for the first time to target the leadership structures of criminal organizations rather than merely individual members who had committed specific provable offenses. The statute would transform federal organized crime prosecution over the following two decades, producing the major Mafia prosecutions of the 1980s and eventually being applied to criminal organizations far beyond the original Mafia targets for which it had been designed.

Outside the United States, the development of anti-organized-crime policing followed different paths shaped by different legal traditions, political cultures, and criminal landscapes. Italy's struggle against the Sicilian Mafia produced a particularly distinguished tradition of judicial investigation, centered on specialized anti-Mafia prosecutors who combined investigative and prosecutorial functions in ways that American legal culture separates. Japan's approach to the Yakuza has been shaped by a long tradition of semi-toleration punctuated by periods of more aggressive enforcement, reflecting the complex social position the Yakuza has historically occupied. In countries with weaker institutional capacity, the challenge of policing organized crime has been compounded by the very corruption and state capture that criminal organizations seek to produce.

By the late twentieth century, the globalization of criminal networks had made it clear that national law enforcement responses, however sophisticated, were insufficient to address organized crime that crossed borders freely. The development of international cooperation frameworks, from Interpol's expansion to the creation of joint investigation teams and mutual legal assistance treaties, reflected the recognition that effective policing of transnational organized crime required transnational law enforcement responses. This recognition, in turn, raised difficult questions about sovereignty, jurisdiction, and the domestic legal constraints that national law enforcement must observe even when cooperating with foreign partners whose standards may differ.

Section 32.1

The Challenge of Investigating Organized Crime

The investigation of organized crime poses challenges that are categorically different from those faced in the investigation of most other crimes. Understanding why requires understanding what makes criminal organizations "organized" in the legally and operationally significant sense of that word. An individual who commits a robbery or a murder leaves behind physical evidence, witnesses, and investigative leads that conventional police techniques are designed to pursue. An organized crime boss who profits from gambling, extortion, drug trafficking, and labor racketeering while insulating himself from direct contact with those activities presents an entirely different investigative problem. The evidence that would prove his role, namely his control over the enterprise and his receipt of its profits, is precisely the evidence that the organization's structure is designed to prevent investigators from obtaining.

Organized crime's structural defenses against investigation are sophisticated and historically refined. Compartmentalization limits what any individual member knows about operations beyond their own immediate sphere, so that the arrest and cooperation of any single member reveals only a fragment of the organization's activities. Violence and intimidation deter witnesses from coming forward and create powerful incentives for potential cooperators to remain silent. Corruption compromises the police, prosecutors, judges, and politicians who would otherwise be the instruments of law enforcement, redirecting their actions in ways that protect rather than expose criminal operations. Money laundering obscures the connection between illegal earnings and the legitimate assets that reflect them, making it difficult to use financial evidence to prove criminal involvement. The code of silence, known as omertà in the Sicilian Mafia tradition but found in various forms across all major criminal organizations, creates powerful cultural and coercive pressure against cooperation with law enforcement.

Early law enforcement efforts against organized crime were largely reactive, responding to individual crimes as they came to official attention while failing to address the underlying organizations that generated them. Municipal vice squads in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries periodically raided gambling dens, brothels, and illegal saloons, but such raids were as often as not the product of political calculations rather than genuine enforcement, and they typically resulted in temporary disruption followed by rapid resumption of operations. The deeper problem was that local police departments were themselves deeply embedded in the political machines that organized crime sustained. A police captain who raided a gambling operation without political authorization risked career consequences, while one who tolerated protected operations in exchange for political protection could advance steadily through the ranks.

The recognition that conventional investigative methods were inadequate for organized crime led, over many decades, to the development of specialized investigative approaches. The most important of these can be grouped into several categories: electronic surveillance and communications interception; undercover operations and infiltration; informant development and management; financial investigation and forensic accounting; grand jury investigation; and specialized prosecutorial frameworks such as RICO. Each of these approaches represents a response to specific features of organized crime's defensive structure, and each involves its own legal frameworks, operational challenges, and ethical dilemmas.

It is important to understand that the development of these tools was not a smooth or linear progression. Each major innovation in investigative methodology was contested, both legally and politically. Electronic surveillance expanded slowly against constitutional challenges and legislative resistance. Undercover operations were constrained by entrapment doctrine and civil liberties concerns. The use of informants raised constant questions about the reliability of criminal cooperators and the moral hazard of rewarding criminal behavior with leniency. Financial investigation required the development of new legal concepts like criminal forfeiture that challenged established property law principles. RICO itself was initially opposed by defense lawyers and civil libertarians as an unprecedented expansion of federal criminal power. The history of law enforcement tools against organized crime is, in significant part, a history of legal and political battles over the proper boundaries of state power in a democratic society.

The Role of Political Will

Beyond technique and law, the investigation of organized crime has always depended critically on political will. Law enforcement agencies require political support, adequate resources, and freedom from the very corruption they are supposed to combat. In environments where organized crime has achieved significant political influence, even technically capable law enforcement agencies may find their investigations deflected, their witnesses intimidated, and their prosecutions undermined by the corruption that organized crime cultivates as a matter of survival.

The history of American law enforcement against organized crime illustrates this dynamic clearly. The political will to mount serious federal investigations developed slowly and unevenly, driven at key moments by dramatic revelations, public scandals, and the advocacy of particular political figures. The Kefauver hearings of 1950 and 1951 created political pressure by exposing organized crime to a national television audience. The McClellan Committee hearings of the late 1950s sustained that pressure and added detailed documentation of organized crime's penetration of legitimate institutions, particularly labor unions. The Apalachin meeting proved the existence of a national network that Hoover had denied. Robert Kennedy's personal crusade against organized crime gave the issue executive-branch priority in a way it had lacked. Each of these political moments created conditions in which law enforcement could expand its capabilities, obtain legal authorities it had lacked, and allocate resources to the sustained, complex investigations that organized crime required.

The relationship between political will and law enforcement effectiveness has been equally important in other national contexts. In Italy, the willingness of a determined group of prosecutors and judges to pursue the Sicilian Mafia despite enormous personal risk, and eventually to develop innovative investigative and prosecutorial techniques that produced the maxi-trials of the 1980s, reflected an individual commitment that political will alone cannot fully explain. But the legal authorities those prosecutors required, the resources they needed, and the political protection without which their work would have been impossible depended on a political environment that had to be laboriously constructed over years of public argument. The assassinations of Falcone and Borsellino in 1992 paradoxically accelerated the political will to fight back, producing emergency legislation and a military deployment against Sicilian organized crime that would not have been politically possible before the murders.

Historical Vocabulary
Predicate Offense

A predicate offense is a specific criminal act that forms part of a broader pattern of racketeering activity required for a RICO prosecution. RICO requires that a defendant have committed at least two predicate offenses within a ten-year period as part of a criminal enterprise. The list of qualifying predicate offenses is extensive, including murder, kidnapping, gambling, arson, robbery, bribery, extortion, drug trafficking, and various fraud offenses, among others.

The concept of the predicate offense is central to understanding how RICO transformed organized crime prosecution. Before RICO, prosecutors had to prove each criminal act separately, which meant that the boss of a criminal enterprise who had insulated himself from direct participation in specific crimes might escape prosecution entirely. RICO shifted the focus from the individual criminal act to the pattern of criminal activity that characterizes a criminal enterprise, making it possible to prosecute leadership for their role in directing the organization rather than requiring proof of their direct participation in specific offenses.

Section 32.2

Surveillance and Wiretapping

The interception of criminal communications has been one of the most powerful, most legally contested, and most politically sensitive tools in the law enforcement arsenal against organized crime. From the earliest telephone wiretaps of the 1920s and 1930s to the sophisticated digital surveillance systems of the twenty-first century, the ability to monitor what criminal organizations say to each other has provided investigators with evidence that no other technique could replicate. It has also generated persistent legal battles over the Fourth Amendment's protection against unreasonable searches and seizures, and persistent political debates over the appropriate limits of government surveillance power in a democratic society.

The legal history of wiretapping in the United States is a story of institutional competition between law enforcement agencies determined to use available technology and courts interpreting constitutional protections in light of that technology. In the early years of telephone use, law enforcement agencies routinely tapped telephone lines without legal authorization, on the theory that conversations intercepted by third parties were not protected by the Fourth Amendment's guarantee against warrantless searches. The Supreme Court initially agreed with this analysis in Olmstead v. United States (1928), holding that wiretapping did not constitute a Fourth Amendment search because it did not involve physical trespass into a person's home or property.

Congress responded to the Olmstead decision not by prohibiting wiretapping but by restricting the admissibility of wiretap evidence in federal court. The Federal Communications Act of 1934 prohibited the interception and public disclosure of wire communications, which the Supreme Court interpreted in 1937 to bar the use of wiretap evidence in federal prosecutions. This created an anomalous situation in which federal agents continued to conduct wiretaps for intelligence purposes while being legally barred from using the resulting evidence in court, a situation that satisfied neither civil libertarians nor law enforcement officials.

The constitutional framework for wiretapping was transformed by the Supreme Court's 1967 decision in Katz v. United States, which overruled the physical-trespass theory of the Fourth Amendment and held instead that the Amendment protects people's reasonable expectations of privacy, not merely their physical property. Under Katz, the interception of a telephone call violated the Fourth Amendment even without any physical intrusion into the caller's premises, because a person using a telephone booth has a reasonable expectation that their conversation is private. The government could continue to conduct wiretaps, but only with a court order based on probable cause.

Congress responded to Katz with Title III of the Omnibus Crime Control and Safe Streets Act of 1968, which created the legal framework for court-authorized electronic surveillance that still governs federal wiretapping. Title III established stringent procedural requirements for obtaining a wiretap order: law enforcement must demonstrate probable cause to believe that a specific person has committed, is committing, or is about to commit a serious offense; must show that normal investigative procedures have been tried and have failed, or are unlikely to succeed; must specify the facility to be intercepted and the conversations to be captured; and must minimize interception of conversations not relevant to the investigation. Wiretap orders are typically limited to thirty days and must be renewed with fresh showings of probable cause.

In practice, court-authorized wiretapping became one of the most productive investigative tools against organized crime precisely because criminal organizations conducted much of their business by telephone, assuming that their communications were private. When law enforcement obtained authorization to intercept those communications, the results were often spectacular. The DeCavalcante tapes of the 1960s, capturing conversations among New Jersey organized crime figures, provided remarkable evidence of the Mafia's internal culture, operations, and hierarchy. The wiretap evidence gathered in preparation for the Commission Case of the 1980s captured the bosses of the five New York families discussing their operations with a directness they would never have employed had they known they were being recorded. Similar wiretap evidence has been central to virtually every major organized crime prosecution in the United States since the 1970s.

From Analog to Digital: Surveillance in the Information Age

The transition from analog to digital communications in the 1990s and 2000s posed new challenges for electronic surveillance. Criminal organizations that had previously communicated primarily by telephone adapted rapidly to new technologies, using encrypted messaging applications, voice-over-internet communications, disposable mobile phones, and other tools designed to evade interception. Law enforcement responded with the Communications Assistance for Law Enforcement Act of 1994 (CALEA), which required telecommunications companies to build their systems to facilitate court-authorized interception, but the pace of technological change constantly outpaced legal and technical adaptation.

The development of encrypted communications presented particularly acute challenges. Strong encryption, which scrambles digital communications in ways that even the most sophisticated government systems cannot decrypt without the encryption key, became widely available to ordinary users and criminal organizations alike in the 1990s and 2000s. Law enforcement agencies argued that encryption "going dark" on them, making it impossible to execute court-authorized surveillance orders even when all legal requirements had been met. Technology companies argued that building back doors into encryption systems for law enforcement access would create security vulnerabilities that could be exploited by malicious actors far more dangerous than the criminal organizations law enforcement was targeting. The debate between law enforcement access and encryption integrity remains unresolved and continues to generate significant legal and political controversy.

Despite these challenges, digital surveillance capabilities have in some ways expanded dramatically. Metadata analysis, which examines patterns of communication without necessarily intercepting content, can reveal the structure of criminal networks, the identity of key nodes, and the timing and frequency of communications in ways that were not possible in the era of analog telephone surveillance. GPS tracking allows law enforcement to monitor the physical movements of suspects and vehicles with precision that physical surveillance teams could not achieve. Social media monitoring, examination of financial transaction records, and analysis of commercially available data have all become components of law enforcement surveillance strategies against criminal organizations.

Historical Vocabulary
Criminal Enterprise

Under RICO, a criminal enterprise is any group of individuals associated in fact for the purpose of conducting criminal activity, even if the group does not constitute a formal legal entity. The definition is deliberately broad, intended to capture the informal organizational structures through which criminal organizations actually operate.

The concept of the criminal enterprise is significant because it shifts the focus of prosecution from individual criminal acts to the organizational context within which those acts occur. A person who commits crimes as a member of a criminal enterprise can be prosecuted not merely for those specific acts but for their participation in the enterprise itself, which carries significantly enhanced penalties. The enterprise concept also allows prosecutors to target the leadership of criminal organizations even when those leaders have insulated themselves from direct participation in specific criminal acts, by proving their management and control of the enterprise as a whole.

Courts have interpreted the "association in fact" enterprise broadly, applying it not only to formal criminal organizations like the Mafia but to loosely affiliated drug distribution networks, street gangs, corrupt government operations, and even corporations that engage in systematic fraud. This breadth has been praised by prosecutors as essential to addressing the variety of forms organized crime takes, and criticized by defense lawyers and civil libertarians as giving prosecutors excessive power to characterize ordinary business relationships as criminal conspiracies.

Section 32.3

Intelligence Gathering and Analysis

Electronic surveillance is, by its nature, reactive: it captures communications that are occurring, providing a window into criminal operations in progress. But effective policing of organized crime requires something more: the systematic accumulation, analysis, and application of intelligence that allows investigators to understand the structure, membership, activities, and vulnerabilities of criminal organizations before, during, and after specific investigative operations. Intelligence-led policing, as this approach has come to be called, represents a fundamental shift in law enforcement philosophy from reactive to proactive engagement with criminal organizations.

Intelligence gathering against organized crime draws on multiple sources. Electronic surveillance provides direct evidence of criminal communications and plans. Physical surveillance, which involves watching suspects, following vehicles, and documenting associations, builds up pictures of criminal networks and activities over time. Records analysis, examining financial transactions, property records, business registrations, court filings, and other publicly available documents, can reveal the economic infrastructure of criminal enterprises. Human intelligence, gathered from informants, cooperating witnesses, and undercover agents, provides the inside perspective that no external surveillance can replicate. Liaison with foreign law enforcement agencies, increasingly important as criminal organizations have become more transnational, adds international dimensions to the intelligence picture.

The systematic analysis of criminal intelligence has historically been one of law enforcement's weaker capabilities. Until the late twentieth century, information gathered by different agencies or different investigative units was often stored in separate systems, not shared across institutional boundaries, and not subjected to sustained analytical scrutiny that might reveal patterns not apparent from individual cases. Organized crime investigations that crossed jurisdictional lines, as most significant organized crime cases do, were particularly susceptible to gaps and failures of information sharing. The same criminal enterprise might be under investigation by the FBI, the DEA, the IRS, local police departments, and state law enforcement agencies simultaneously, with each institution holding pieces of an intelligence picture that, assembled together, would reveal far more than any individual agency possessed.

The development of more sophisticated intelligence fusion has been a major priority for law enforcement since the 1980s. Joint task forces, which bring together federal agents from multiple agencies alongside state and local detectives, have become the preferred organizational form for major organized crime investigations precisely because they enable the sharing of intelligence across institutional lines. The creation of the Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN) in 1990 established a central repository for financial intelligence that could be accessed by law enforcement agencies tracking the money flows of criminal organizations. After the September 11 attacks of 2001, concerns about intelligence sharing in counterterrorism led to infrastructure improvements that also benefited organized crime investigations.

The Intelligence-Prosecution Tension

A persistent tension in intelligence-led policing of organized crime is the conflict between the intelligence value of sustained monitoring and the prosecutorial value of timely arrest and prosecution. From a pure intelligence standpoint, the ideal approach to a criminal network is to monitor it as comprehensively as possible for as long as possible, building the fullest possible picture of its operations, membership, and vulnerabilities before taking any action that would alert the organization that it is under investigation. From a prosecutorial standpoint, delay means that crimes continue to be committed and victims to be harmed while law enforcement watches and waits. There is also a practical legal problem: evidence gathered through wiretaps and surveillance degrades in prosecutorial value if too much time passes between the intelligence gathering and the prosecution, because jurors and judges may question why law enforcement monitored criminal activity for months or years without intervening to stop it.

Different investigative cultures resolve this tension differently. Some agencies and prosecutors favor the comprehensive long-term investigation, accepting the short-term cost of crimes continuing to occur in exchange for the long-term benefit of a more comprehensive case against more senior figures. Others favor more aggressive early intervention, accepting the limitation on case scope in exchange for earlier disruption of criminal activity and protection of victims. In practice, most major organized crime investigations involve some version of this tension, with different investigators and prosecutors within the same case often holding different views about the right balance to strike.

Historical Vocabulary
HUMINT (Human Intelligence)

HUMINT is intelligence gathered from human sources, as distinct from intelligence gathered through signals interception (SIGINT), imagery analysis, or other technical means. In the context of organized crime investigation, HUMINT encompasses information provided by informants, cooperating witnesses, undercover agents, and other human sources with direct access to criminal organizations or their activities.

HUMINT is typically the most operationally valuable form of intelligence in organized crime cases because it provides inside perspective, context, and interpretation that technical surveillance alone cannot supply. An informant embedded within a criminal organization can explain the significance of cryptic communications intercepted by wiretaps, identify the real identities behind code names or aliases, describe the internal hierarchy and decision-making processes of the organization, and provide early warning of planned criminal activities. However, HUMINT is also the most legally and ethically complex form of intelligence, because it involves managing human beings who are typically themselves involved in criminal activity and who have interests and motivations that may not align with those of law enforcement.

Section 32.4

Undercover Operations

The use of undercover law enforcement officers who infiltrate criminal organizations, assume false identities, and gather evidence from the inside represents one of the most powerful and one of the most legally and ethically complex tools available to investigators of organized crime. When properly conducted, undercover operations can provide evidence that no other technique can generate: direct observation of criminal activities in progress, recordings of criminal conversations and transactions, and testimony about internal organizational structures from someone who was present at their operation. When poorly conducted or improperly managed, they can involve law enforcement in facilitating the very crimes they are supposed to prevent, compromise the integrity of prosecutions, and create serious risks of physical harm to the officers involved.

The legal boundary that constrains undercover operations against organized crime is the doctrine of entrapment. Entrapment occurs when law enforcement induces a person to commit a crime that they were not predisposed to commit, then prosecutes them for that induced crime. The entrapment defense is designed to prevent the government from manufacturing criminality, from creating crimes rather than detecting them. In practice, however, the boundary between permissible undercover investigation and improper entrapment is often genuinely difficult to draw, particularly in cases involving organized crime where the target's predisposition to criminal activity is precisely what makes him a criminal organization member but also makes the entrapment defense difficult to sustain.

American courts have generally applied what is called the "subjective" test for entrapment, focusing on whether the defendant was predisposed to commit the crime before the government approached him. Under this test, a defendant who was already an active member of a criminal organization and who agreed to participate in a criminal scheme proposed by an undercover agent has a weak entrapment defense, because his membership in the criminal organization itself demonstrates his predisposition. By contrast, a defendant with no prior criminal history who was aggressively solicited by a government agent and eventually agreed to participate in an illegal transaction has a stronger entrapment claim, even if he technically agreed to commit the crime.

The most dramatic and operationally significant undercover operations against organized crime have been long-term infiltrations in which agents spent months or years embedded within criminal organizations, building genuine relationships with criminal figures while secretly gathering evidence. The FBI's Donnie Brasco operation of the 1970s and 1980s, in which Special Agent Joseph Pistone lived undercover for six years within the Bonanno crime family under the identity "Donnie Brasco," remains the paradigmatic example of this type of operation. Pistone's penetration of the Bonanno family provided extraordinary intelligence about the organization's internal structure, membership, operations, and culture, and eventually contributed to more than 200 criminal convictions. It also demonstrated the extraordinary personal costs of sustained undercover work, as Pistone spent years living a double life under constant threat of discovery and death.

Undercover Operations in International Contexts

Undercover operations against transnational organized crime present additional complications arising from jurisdictional boundaries and differences in national legal frameworks. An undercover technique that is legally permissible in the United States may violate the law of a foreign country where the operation is conducted, creating legal risks for the agents involved and potentially rendering resulting evidence inadmissible in prosecutions in either country. Some countries prohibit undercover operations by foreign law enforcement agents on their territory entirely, making it necessary to rely on local law enforcement partners whose methods and reliability may be uncertain.

International undercover operations have been most successfully conducted through joint teams in which agents from multiple national law enforcement agencies work together under a shared legal framework, typically that of the country where the operation is primarily conducted. The DEA has extensive experience conducting such joint operations against drug trafficking organizations in Latin America, Europe, and Asia, typically working alongside local law enforcement partners to gather evidence that can be used in prosecutions in multiple jurisdictions. These joint operations are valuable but require extensive advance coordination, clear agreements about evidence sharing and prosecutorial priorities, and mutual trust between the participating agencies, trust that can be difficult to build and maintain when agencies have different institutional cultures, different legal frameworks, and sometimes competing interests.

Section 32.5

Informants and Cooperating Witnesses

No single tool has been more important in the history of organized crime prosecution than the use of informants and cooperating witnesses. At the same time, no tool has generated more controversy, more legal challenges, or more serious ethical debates. The use of criminal insiders, people who have themselves participated in the criminal organizations being investigated, as sources of evidence against their former associates involves fundamental tensions between effectiveness and integrity that cannot be entirely resolved.

The distinction between an informant and a cooperating witness is important but sometimes blurred in practice. An informant is typically a person who provides information to law enforcement while continuing to operate within the criminal environment, often without the knowledge of the organization being investigated. An informant may be paid for information, may be working off criminal charges of his own, or may have motivations of his own, such as rivalry with a criminal associate, that lead him to provide information to law enforcement. A cooperating witness is typically a person who has already been arrested and has agreed to cooperate with the prosecution as part of a plea agreement, which usually involves pleading guilty to some crimes in exchange for the government's agreement to recommend a reduced sentence in return for testimony and other assistance.

The practical value of criminal informants in organized crime investigations is difficult to overstate. They provide information about internal organizational structures that external surveillance cannot reveal. They can identify other members of criminal organizations by their real identities, street names, and roles. They can explain the significance of cryptic communications, identify locations used for criminal activities, and describe the history and methods of criminal enterprises. They can provide advance warning of planned criminal activities. In many of the most significant organized crime prosecutions in American history, informant information was essential to developing the evidence that eventually went to trial.

The legal and ethical problems with informants are equally serious and well-documented. Criminal informants are, by definition, people who have themselves committed crimes, sometimes very serious ones. They have powerful incentives to provide law enforcement with the information law enforcement wants to hear, whether or not it is accurate, because the quality of their cooperation affects the benefit they receive. Informants who are themselves active criminals may continue to commit crimes while working with law enforcement, creating situations in which law enforcement effectively enables ongoing criminal activity in exchange for information about other criminal activity. The testimony of criminal cooperators is widely understood by juries, defense lawyers, and judges to be inherently suspect, and convictions based primarily on informant testimony are vulnerable to challenge on grounds of reliability.

The Witness Security Program

The development of the federal Witness Security Program, commonly known as witness protection, in 1970 was designed to address one of the most fundamental obstacles to obtaining cooperation from organized crime members: the near-certainty that anyone who testified against a criminal organization would face violent retaliation. The Witness Security Program provides cooperating witnesses who face serious safety risks with new identities, relocation to new communities, and financial support during the transition period, in exchange for their testimony in federal proceedings.

Since its establishment, the Witness Security Program has protected more than 19,000 witnesses and family members, with a remarkably low rate of harm to protected witnesses who have followed program rules. It has made it possible for former Mafia members, cartel operatives, and other organized crime insiders to testify against their former associates without being killed, and has been a crucial enabler of the major organized crime prosecutions of the past half-century. Without the assurance of post-testimony protection, many of the most significant cooperating witnesses in American organized crime history would not have agreed to testify, and many of the most significant convictions would not have been obtained.

The program has also generated persistent criticism. Participants are convicted criminals who receive significant benefits, including reduced sentences and new identities, in exchange for cooperation. Some have committed new crimes after entering the program, sometimes using their new identities to continue criminal activity. The government's obligation to provide protection can extend for years or decades, at significant public expense. And the moral complexity of giving new identities and subsidized lives to individuals who have participated in serious crimes, including in some cases murders, is one that many observers find difficult to accept regardless of the prosecutorial benefits.

In Italy, the equivalent institution is the pentito system, which provides legal benefits to organized crime members who cooperate with anti-Mafia prosecutors. The Italian system produced transformative results in the 1980s and 1990s, when a series of high-level Mafia members, including Tommaso Buscetta, agreed to cooperate with prosecutors. Buscetta's testimony to Judge Giovanni Falcone provided the most comprehensive inside account of the Sicilian Mafia's structure, operations, and history that prosecutors had ever obtained, and formed the foundation for the Palermo maxi-trial of 1986 and 1987, in which 474 Mafia members were prosecuted and 360 convicted. The pentito system demonstrated both the extraordinary prosecutorial value of high-level cooperation and the way in which a single committed cooperator at the right level of a criminal organization could transform an entire investigative landscape.

Primary Source Window

Joseph Valachi Testifies Before the McClellan Committee, 1963

Creator: Joseph Valachi, testimony before the U.S. Senate Permanent Subcommittee on Investigations · Date: September–October 1963 · Type: Congressional testimony

Context: Joseph Valachi was a soldier in the Genovese crime family of New York who, after being imprisoned and fearing he had been marked for death by his boss Vito Genovese, agreed to cooperate with federal investigators. In September and October of 1963, he testified before Senator John McClellan's Permanent Subcommittee on Investigations in nationally televised hearings that provided the American public with the first inside account of the American Mafia's existence, structure, membership, and operations.

Valachi described an organization he called "Cosa Nostra" (Italian for "this thing of ours"), organized into "families" headed by a "boss," with a "underboss" and a "consigliere" (counselor), beneath whom were "capos" (captains) who supervised "soldiers" — made members of the organization who had taken a formal oath and undergone an initiation ceremony. He described the Commission, the ruling council of organized crime, its membership and function. He described how members were inducted, what rules they were expected to follow, and what happened to those who violated them.

Analysis: Valachi's testimony was significant for multiple reasons. It publicly confirmed the existence of a national organized crime network that the FBI had officially denied. It provided the most detailed organizational map of the American Mafia that law enforcement had ever obtained from an inside source. And it introduced a generation of Americans to the internal culture, rituals, and hierarchy of the organization through the unprecedented medium of live television.

However, Valachi's testimony also had significant limitations. He was a low-ranking soldier with limited visibility into the highest levels of the organization's leadership and strategy. His account was necessarily shaped by his own perspective, loyalties, and rivalries within the organization. Some of what he described was later found to be inaccurate or exaggerated. And the dramatic public nature of his testimony, while valuable for public education and political mobilization, created legal complications for subsequent prosecutions, as defendants could argue that Valachi's public statements had contaminated the jury pool.

Section 32.6

Financial Investigation and Asset Forfeiture

The conviction of Al Capone in 1931 on federal tax evasion charges established a template for organized crime prosecution that remains relevant nearly a century later: when direct evidence of the most serious crimes is difficult to obtain, financial evidence of illegal income, improperly concealed, can provide an alternative path to conviction and incarceration. Capone's conviction was possible because his lifestyle and evident wealth were grossly inconsistent with any legitimate income he could have reported, and because investigators were able to document cash flows through his organization that demonstrated substantial unreported income. He was not convicted of murder, bootlegging, or extortion, though he had done all of those things. He was convicted of failing to pay taxes on the proceeds of those activities.

The logic of financial investigation as an anti-organized-crime strategy is straightforward. Criminal organizations exist to make money. The money they make must eventually enter the legitimate financial system, either to be spent or to be invested in legitimate assets. Every point at which criminal proceeds interact with the legitimate financial system is a potential investigative opportunity. Bank deposits, real estate purchases, business investments, and luxury expenditures all leave documentary trails that investigators can follow, and those trails often lead back to the criminal sources of the funds more reliably than witness testimony, which can be recanted, or physical surveillance, which can be evaded.

The development of forensic accounting as a law enforcement discipline has been essential to this strategy. Forensic accountants working on organized crime cases examine financial records, bank accounts, business transactions, and tax filings to identify patterns inconsistent with legitimate income, trace the movement of funds through complex structures, and document the connections between criminal proceeds and the assets they have purchased. The Internal Revenue Service Criminal Investigation Division, successor to the agency whose agents brought down Capone, has continued to play a major role in organized crime investigations by providing expertise in financial analysis that FBI and DEA agents typically lack.

Asset Forfeiture

The concept of criminal asset forfeiture, which allows the government to seize property connected to criminal activity, represents a fundamental complement to the incarceration-based strategy of organized crime control. If the goal of organized crime is to generate wealth, then a prosecution strategy that results in incarceration but leaves the criminal enterprise's assets intact has only partially addressed the problem. The leadership may be imprisoned, but the money, the property, and the organizational infrastructure remain, available to successors who continue the enterprise's operations. Asset forfeiture aims to dismantle the economic foundation of criminal enterprises, not merely to remove their current leadership.

RICO was an early enabler of criminal forfeiture in organized crime cases, requiring defendants convicted under the statute to forfeit any interest they had obtained through racketeering activity. The Comprehensive Crime Control Act of 1984 significantly expanded the government's forfeiture authority, creating civil forfeiture procedures that allowed the government to seize property connected to criminal activity even without a criminal conviction against the property's owner. Civil forfeiture proved particularly useful in cases involving drug trafficking, where large quantities of cash and property could be seized even when the criminal case against individual defendants was complex and slow.

Civil asset forfeiture has become one of the most controversial aspects of modern law enforcement, generating significant criticism from civil libertarians, property rights advocates, and commentators across the political spectrum. The ability to seize property without a criminal conviction means that innocent people can lose property based on a probable cause standard that is significantly lower than the beyond-a-reasonable-doubt standard required for criminal conviction. In some jurisdictions, property owners who wish to contest a forfeiture must prove their own innocence, inverting the ordinary presumption in American law. Critics have documented numerous cases in which innocent people have had property seized and faced expensive legal battles to recover it, and some studies have suggested that forfeiture has at times been driven more by revenue considerations than by genuine public safety goals.

Anti-Money-Laundering Frameworks

Beyond prosecution, the development of anti-money-laundering (AML) frameworks represents an effort to prevent criminal proceeds from entering the legitimate financial system in the first place, or at least to make their entry more difficult to conceal. The Bank Secrecy Act of 1970 required financial institutions to report large currency transactions and maintain records that could be used in criminal investigations. The Money Laundering Control Act of 1986 made money laundering itself a federal crime, allowing prosecutors to pursue individuals who helped criminal organizations conceal and legitimize their proceeds even if those individuals had no direct connection to the underlying criminal activity.

The Financial Action Task Force (FATF), an intergovernmental body established in 1989, has developed international anti-money-laundering standards that member countries are expected to incorporate into their domestic legal frameworks. These standards require financial institutions to know their customers, report suspicious transactions, and maintain records that can be accessed by law enforcement. The FATF's influence has extended AML frameworks well beyond the original G7 countries that established it, creating a global network of reporting requirements that has made it significantly more difficult for criminal organizations to move large amounts of money through the international banking system without generating investigative leads.

Section 32.7

International Cooperation

The globalization of criminal networks since the late twentieth century has made international law enforcement cooperation not merely desirable but operationally essential. A drug trafficking organization that cultivates coca in Colombia, processes it into cocaine in clandestine laboratories, moves it through Central America and Mexico, and distributes it across the United States and Europe cannot be effectively addressed by any single national law enforcement agency. A financial network that launders criminal proceeds through banks in multiple jurisdictions, using shell companies registered in offshore financial centers and layering transactions through correspondent banking relationships, cannot be traced by investigators operating within the legal and jurisdictional boundaries of a single country. The criminal organizations that police face in the twenty-first century are transnational by design, and law enforcement has been compelled to develop transnational responses.

International law enforcement cooperation takes multiple forms at different levels of formality and depth. At the most informal level, police-to-police contacts between investigators in different countries allow the sharing of intelligence and operational coordination without formal legal process. At a more formal level, mutual legal assistance treaties (MLATs) establish the legal frameworks through which governments can request evidence, witness testimony, and other forms of legal assistance from each other for use in criminal proceedings. Extradition treaties create the legal mechanisms for transferring criminal suspects from one country to another for prosecution.

Interpol, formally the International Criminal Police Organization, occupies a distinctive role in the international law enforcement architecture. Founded in 1923 and headquartered in Lyon, France, Interpol facilitates cooperation among the law enforcement agencies of its 195 member countries, maintaining criminal databases, issuing international notices (including the famous "red notices" that alert member countries to seek the arrest and extradition of wanted individuals), and coordinating joint operations against transnational criminal networks. Interpol is not a supranational police force with its own agents or arrest powers; it is an enabling institution that provides the infrastructure and protocols that allow national law enforcement agencies to work together more effectively.

The European Model: Europol and Eurojust

Within the European Union, more intensive forms of law enforcement cooperation have been developed through the institutions of Europol and Eurojust. Europol, the European Union Agency for Law Enforcement Cooperation, supports member state law enforcement agencies in preventing and investigating serious international crime and terrorism, operating analytical databases, coordinating joint operations, and facilitating information exchange across EU borders. Unlike Interpol, Europol operates within a more integrated supranational framework that gives it somewhat greater operational capacity, though it still works through and in support of national law enforcement agencies rather than independently of them.

Eurojust, the EU Agency for Criminal Justice Cooperation, focuses on the prosecutorial dimension of transnational organized crime, facilitating coordination among prosecutors from different member states who are pursuing related cases. In complex transnational organized crime investigations, where multiple national prosecutions may be running simultaneously against different elements of the same criminal network, Eurojust's role in preventing conflicts, sharing information, and coordinating strategy has become increasingly important.

The experience of EU law enforcement cooperation against organized crime illustrates both the potential and the limits of international collaboration. Within the EU, the commitment to a single market, freedom of movement, and a common legal framework has created both the criminal opportunities that organized crime has exploited and the institutional infrastructure that makes more intensive law enforcement cooperation possible. But even within the EU, significant differences in national legal traditions, evidentiary rules, and institutional cultures create practical obstacles to fully seamless cooperation. Outside the EU, the challenges are considerably greater, requiring the navigation of bilateral treaty relationships, significant differences in legal systems, and political considerations that may limit the willingness of governments to share intelligence or provide legal assistance in sensitive cases.

The Italian Anti-Mafia Prosecution Model

Italy's experience developing specialized investigative and prosecutorial structures to address the Sicilian Mafia has produced innovations that have influenced anti-organized-crime practice in other countries. The key Italian innovation was the creation of a specialized anti-Mafia pool in Palermo during the 1980s, in which a group of dedicated investigating magistrates, led initially by Rocco Chinnici and then by Giovanni Falcone and Paolo Borsellino, worked together as a team, sharing intelligence and pooling resources across individual investigations in a way that conventional Italian prosecutorial practice did not encourage.

The maxi-trial approach pioneered by Falcone and his colleagues represented a decisive departure from the conventional Italian judicial practice of prosecuting individual defendants for individual crimes. Instead, the maxi-trial prosecuted the entire Sicilian Mafia organization as a single criminal enterprise, arraigning hundreds of defendants simultaneously for their participation in the organization and its activities. The evidentiary foundation was the testimony of pentiti, above all Tommaso Buscetta, combined with the vast intelligence picture that years of coordinated investigation had assembled. The 1986 to 1987 Palermo maxi-trial produced 360 convictions and sentences totaling thousands of years of imprisonment, dealing the most significant legal blow the Sicilian Mafia had ever faced.

The creation after the Falcone and Borsellino assassinations of the Direzione Investigativa Antimafia (DIA) and the Direzione Nazionale Antimafia (DNA) institutionalized the coordinated investigative and prosecutorial approach that Falcone had pioneered in Palermo, creating national structures for anti-Mafia investigation and prosecution that could share intelligence and coordinate strategy across regional boundaries. The Italian model has been studied and adapted by law enforcement agencies in other countries facing comparable challenges of prosecution-resistant organized crime hierarchies.

Section 32.8

Civil Liberties, Oversight, and the Limits of Investigation

Every major investigative tool developed for use against organized crime has generated serious questions about its consistency with the constitutional protections and civil liberties that democratic societies are committed to preserving. These questions are not marginal or merely academic. They go to the heart of what distinguishes democratic law enforcement from authoritarian policing, and they reflect genuine tensions that cannot be entirely resolved by any formula, however carefully constructed.

The Fourth Amendment's protection against unreasonable searches and seizures is the most directly relevant constitutional provision to organized crime investigation. Electronic surveillance, physical searches, seizure of financial records, and monitoring of communications all implicate the Fourth Amendment's requirements. The legal framework for each of these investigative tools involves a balance between the government's interest in investigating crime and the individual's interest in privacy and freedom from unwarranted government intrusion. Courts have calibrated that balance differently over time and in response to different technologies, but the basic tension between investigative effectiveness and constitutional protection has been a constant feature of the legal landscape.

The Fifth Amendment's protection against compelled self-incrimination is equally important in the organized crime context. The Mafia's tradition of omertà was not merely a cultural norm; it was reinforced by the Fifth Amendment's guarantee that no person can be compelled to testify against himself. When organized crime figures were called before congressional committees or grand juries, they routinely invoked their Fifth Amendment rights, providing no information while remaining legally protected from compelled disclosure. The development of use immunity, which compels testimony while protecting the witness from prosecution based on that testimony, was partly a response to this problem, allowing prosecutors to overcome the Fifth Amendment shield in exchange for granting immunity from use of the compelled testimony.

The Sixth Amendment's right to confront witnesses creates difficulties when law enforcement relies on informants whose identities must be protected to prevent their being killed by the criminal organizations against which they have testified. Courts have developed doctrines that attempt to balance the defendant's right to confront his accusers against the government's legitimate interest in protecting informants' lives, but these doctrines remain contested, and cases involving anonymous or protected informants raise serious due process concerns that courts have not entirely resolved.

The Risks of Expanded Surveillance Power

Beyond the specific constitutional provisions governing particular investigative techniques, the history of organized crime investigation includes troubling episodes in which expanded surveillance powers, developed for legitimate law enforcement purposes, were applied in ways that had nothing to do with organized crime and everything to do with political repression. J. Edgar Hoover's FBI conducted extensive surveillance of civil rights leaders, anti-war activists, and other political figures under the COINTELPRO program, using techniques developed for organized crime investigation against individuals who posed no criminal threat but whom Hoover considered politically undesirable. The NSA's post-September 11 surveillance programs, developed in the counterterrorism context, generated information about ordinary citizens on a scale that created serious concerns about the boundaries of permissible government surveillance.

These episodes illustrate a fundamental principle about surveillance power: capabilities developed for one purpose can be, and sometimes are, redirected to other purposes. A wiretapping capability developed to investigate Mafia bosses can be used to spy on political opponents. A financial intelligence system designed to trace criminal proceeds can be used to monitor the transactions of ordinary citizens. The institutional safeguards designed to prevent such abuses, court supervision, legislative oversight, inspector general review, and civil society scrutiny, are essential but have not always been sufficient. The history of organized crime law enforcement is inseparable from the history of debates about the proper scope of government surveillance power in a democratic society.

Historical Debate

Has the Law Enforcement Arsenal Against Organized Crime Been Too Expansive, or Not Expansive Enough?

The question: Since the development of RICO and the expansion of electronic surveillance authority in the late 1960s and 1970s, critics have debated whether the legal tools available to prosecutors pursuing organized crime are appropriately calibrated or whether they represent an inappropriate expansion of government power that threatens civil liberties. The debate has intensified as those tools have been applied well beyond the Mafia cases for which they were originally developed.

Interpretation A — The Tools Are Necessary and Appropriately Constrained: Proponents of the existing framework argue that the organized crime investigative toolkit is appropriately calibrated to the genuine threat that criminal organizations pose to public safety, economic institutions, and democratic governance. RICO was carefully crafted with procedural safeguards, including the requirement to prove a pattern of racketeering activity through specific predicate offenses. Electronic surveillance requires court authorization based on demonstrated probable cause. Asset forfeiture is subject to judicial review. The results, measured in successful prosecutions of criminal organizations that conventional methods had failed to reach for decades, justify the approach. Critics who worry about civil liberties overreach consistently underestimate the genuine danger of sophisticated criminal organizations and the extent to which conventional investigative methods are inadequate against them.
Interpretation B — The Tools Have Been Dangerously Over-Extended: Critics, who come from both the political left and the political right, argue that the powers developed to fight organized crime have been systematically over-extended in ways that threaten civil liberties and the rule of law. RICO, originally designed to prosecute Mafia families, has been applied to anti-abortion activists, securities fraud defendants, and street gangs in ways that its drafters did not intend. Civil asset forfeiture has been used to seize property from innocent people who have never been convicted of any crime. Electronic surveillance authorities have expanded far beyond the original model. The institutional safeguards designed to prevent abuse have proven inadequate in practice, as the COINTELPRO experience and subsequent revelations about surveillance programs have demonstrated.

Evidence: The debate is sustained by genuine evidence on both sides. The prosecutorial record, particularly the decimation of the New York Mafia families through RICO prosecutions in the 1980s and the disruption of major drug trafficking organizations, supports the effectiveness argument. The record of COINTELPRO, documented cases of civil forfeiture abuse, and revelations about the scope of post-September 11 surveillance programs support the civil liberties concern.

Why it matters: How societies calibrate the balance between law enforcement effectiveness and civil liberties protection is one of the fundamental questions of democratic governance. The organized crime context has been one of the primary arenas in which that calibration has been made and contested over the past half-century.

Student task: Consider both interpretations and the evidence supporting each. What institutional mechanisms do you think are most important for preventing the abuse of expanded law enforcement powers while preserving their legitimate effectiveness? Can you identify historical examples in which those mechanisms have worked well, and examples in which they have failed?

Multiple Perspectives

Who Experiences Organized Crime Policing? Multiple Perspectives on Investigation and Prosecution

From the perspective of law enforcement investigators: Organized crime investigation is among the most technically demanding, personally dangerous, and institutionally resource-intensive work in law enforcement. Investigators who spend years building cases against criminal organizations operate under sustained stress, face genuine personal risks, and often feel that the legal constraints under which they operate create asymmetries that favor well-resourced criminal defendants over the public interest. Many express frustration at the time and resources required to build cases sufficient to survive appellate review, and at the ease with which criminal organizations can replace leaders who are imprisoned. The job is understood as genuinely important and genuinely difficult.
From the perspective of communities affected by organized crime: Communities in which organized crime is deeply embedded experience its effects in ways that are both very visible and very dangerous: businesses subjected to extortion, local politics corrupted, drug distribution networks that destroy families, violence that makes public spaces unsafe. These communities often have the most direct stake in effective law enforcement against organized crime, but they also sometimes have the most direct experience of law enforcement methods gone wrong, including over-aggressive surveillance, civil forfeiture that affects innocent community members, and prosecution theories that sweep in peripheral figures along with genuine criminals. The relationship between affected communities and law enforcement is often complex, shaped by histories of both protection and harm.
From the perspective of defense lawyers and civil libertarians: Defense lawyers who represent organized crime defendants, and civil libertarians who monitor law enforcement practices more broadly, see the arsenal of organized crime investigation from a fundamentally different angle. They observe RICO used against defendants who have marginal connections to genuine criminal enterprises. They document asset forfeiture proceedings in which innocent people lose property without criminal conviction. They challenge wiretap evidence gathered under surveillance orders they argue were improperly granted or improperly executed. They raise concerns about the reliability of informant testimony from individuals who have every incentive to tell prosecutors what prosecutors want to hear. Their perspective is not merely self-interested; they are raising genuine constitutional questions that courts have found legitimate enough to generate a substantial body of doctrine.
From the perspective of cooperating witnesses: The individuals who choose to cooperate with law enforcement against their former criminal associates occupy one of the most psychologically and physically dangerous positions imaginable. They have typically committed serious crimes themselves. They are betraying people they have known, often intimately, over many years, people who will try to kill them if they can. They are relying on government institutions to protect them and their families from retaliation that they know from experience is both possible and lethal. In exchange for testimony, they receive legal benefits that often feel inadequate to the risks they are taking and the losses they are incurring. The moral complexity of their position, criminals who have chosen to cooperate with the law they spent their careers violating, does not reduce to simple categories of heroism or betrayal.
Constitutional Connection

The Fourth and Fifth Amendments in Organized Crime Investigation

The investigation and prosecution of organized crime has been one of the primary arenas in which the courts have elaborated the meaning of the Fourth and Fifth Amendments to the Constitution. The Fourth Amendment's prohibition of unreasonable searches and seizures has been interpreted and reinterpreted in response to successive generations of investigative technology, from telephone wiretapping to digital surveillance. The Fifth Amendment's protection against compelled self-incrimination has been tested against grand jury subpoenas, use immunity orders, and cooperation agreements.

Key constitutional developments in the organized crime context include:

  • Olmstead v. United States (1928) — Fourth Amendment does not protect against wiretapping (since overruled)
  • Katz v. United States (1967) — Fourth Amendment protects reasonable expectations of privacy, not merely physical property; requires warrant for wiretapping
  • Title III of the Omnibus Crime Control Act (1968) — statutory framework for court-authorized wiretapping, with detailed procedural requirements
  • Kastigar v. United States (1972) — use immunity sufficient to compel testimony over Fifth Amendment objection, provided prosecutor does not use compelled testimony
  • United States v. Salerno (1987) — upholds constitutionality of preventive detention under Bail Reform Act, a tool used in organized crime cases
  • Civil forfeiture challenges — ongoing constitutional litigation about whether civil forfeiture without criminal conviction violates due process and the Eighth Amendment's prohibition on excessive fines

The constitutional history of organized crime investigation illustrates how the meaning of constitutional guarantees evolves in response to changing technologies, changing criminal methods, and changing social understandings of the proper relationship between the individual and the state. Each generation of investigators has operated within a constitutional framework that was defined partly by the investigative challenges of previous generations, and each generation's innovations have in turn shaped the constitutional debates that subsequent generations will inherit.

Cause and Effect

The RICO Era: Causes, Implementation, and Consequences

Long-Term Causes

Decades of inadequate legal tools for prosecuting criminal enterprises rather than individual offenders; recognition that conventional prosecution left criminal organizations intact even when individual leaders were convicted; congressional frustration with organized crime's penetration of legitimate business and labor institutions; academic and prosecutorial advocacy for enterprise-based criminal liability.

Short-Term Causes

The Nixon administration's emphasis on organized crime as a political priority; advocacy by legislators including Senator John McClellan; the work of Notre Dame law professor G. Robert Blakey, who drafted the statute; the political momentum created by the McClellan Committee hearings and the Valachi revelations; the perception that existing tools were manifestly inadequate to the scale of the organized crime threat.

The Event and Its Implementation

RICO enacted in October 1970; initial use against traditional organized crime targets; gradual broadening of application to other criminal enterprises through prosecutorial innovation and judicial interpretation; landmark Commission Case prosecutions of the 1980s; expansion to cover securities fraud, public corruption, gang activity, and other contexts far beyond the original Mafia focus.

Long-Term Consequences

Decimation of traditional American Mafia families; transformation of federal organized crime prosecution strategy; RICO applied far beyond organized crime to white-collar fraud, gang prosecutions, and other contexts; ongoing civil liberties debates about RICO's breadth; criminal organizations adapt by flattening hierarchies and decentralizing to reduce enterprise liability exposure; international influence on anti-organized-crime legislation in other countries.

Historical complexity: RICO's effectiveness against traditional hierarchical criminal organizations has coexisted with its broader application in contexts its drafters did not anticipate, generating both its most celebrated successes and its most controversial uses. The statute has been both praised as a essential weapon against serious organized crime and criticized as a vehicle for prosecutorial overreach, and both assessments contain genuine truth.

Continuity and Change Over Time

The Challenge of Policing Organized Crime: What Has Changed and What Has Not

What Has Changed

  • The legal framework for organized crime prosecution has expanded dramatically, from the near-absence of specialized tools before 1970 to the comprehensive RICO, electronic surveillance, forfeiture, and cooperation frameworks available today
  • Technology has transformed both the capabilities and the limitations of law enforcement surveillance, shifting from telephone wiretaps to comprehensive digital intelligence gathering while also creating new encryption-based obstacles
  • International cooperation has moved from ad hoc police-to-police contacts to formalized institutional frameworks including Interpol, Europol, MLATs, and specialized anti-organized-crime conventions
  • The sophistication of financial investigation has increased enormously, from the rough-and-ready tax evasion approach used against Capone to complex forensic accounting, cryptocurrency tracing, and global anti-money-laundering frameworks
  • The organizations being investigated have themselves changed, moving from hierarchical Mafia families to more decentralized network structures that present different investigative challenges

What Has Stayed the Same

  • The fundamental asymmetry between law enforcement, constrained by constitutional requirements and legal procedures, and criminal organizations, which operate with no such constraints
  • The corruption problem: organized crime continues to invest heavily in corrupting the law enforcement and political institutions that are supposed to control it, and corruption continues to compromise those institutions
  • The dependence on human sources: despite all technological advances, the insider cooperator who can explain organizational structure, identify members, and testify to criminal activities remains irreplaceable
  • The tension between security and liberty: every generation has debated how far law enforcement should go in intruding on privacy and restricting freedoms in the name of fighting organized crime, and that debate has never been permanently resolved
  • The adaptation dynamic: as law enforcement develops new tools, criminal organizations adapt their methods and structures to defeat them, producing an ongoing cat-and-mouse dynamic with no permanent winners

Why the pattern matters: The persistence of the fundamental challenges of policing organized crime across dramatic changes in legal frameworks, technology, and institutional capacity suggests that effective law enforcement against criminal organizations is not simply a matter of having the right tools. It requires sustained political will, adequate resources, institutional integrity, and a willingness to adapt that criminal organizations themselves consistently demonstrate and that law enforcement must match.

Everyday Life and Social History

The Ordinary World of Organized Crime Investigation: Lives Behind the Headlines

The dramatic headline cases, the Commission Trial, the Valachi hearings, the dramatic undercover penetrations of criminal organizations, represent the visible peaks of organized crime law enforcement. Behind them lies the everyday reality of the investigators, analysts, prosecutors, defense attorneys, witnesses, and community members whose lives intersect with organized crime investigation in ways that rarely make the front page.

The typical organized crime investigation is not glamorous. It involves hours of listening to intercepted telephone conversations in which criminals talk about ordinary daily life interspersed with occasional criminal business, written in argot and coded language that requires patient interpretation. It involves poring through thousands of pages of financial records looking for patterns that might indicate the flow of criminal proceeds. It involves cultivating sources, waiting for information, following leads that go nowhere, and maintaining surveillance positions in vehicles and on foot in all weather conditions. It involves writing the detailed reports, affidavits, and memos that document investigation in forms that will withstand judicial scrutiny and appellate review. Most organized crime investigations are slow, methodical, and largely invisible.

The people who do this work are ordinary law enforcement professionals who have chosen to specialize in one of the most demanding areas of their field. FBI special agents assigned to organized crime squads typically spend years developing expertise in a particular criminal organization or geographic area before they are trusted to conduct major investigations. IRS criminal investigators who work on organized crime cases develop specialized expertise in financial forensics that takes years to build. Prosecutors who specialize in organized crime cases develop the legal and evidentiary expertise that major RICO prosecutions require through apprenticeship with more experienced colleagues over many years.

For the communities in which organized crime is embedded, the experience of law enforcement is similarly far from the dramatic depictions of popular culture. Business owners who pay protection money to stay in operation are not typically raided by FBI agents; they are quietly victimized, year after year, by individuals they know by name and face. Workers in industries controlled by organized crime may be aware that their union is corrupted without having any practical recourse. Residents of neighborhoods where criminal organizations operate may cooperate with investigators or may refuse to do so, calculating the risks and rewards of each choice based on their direct experience of both the criminal organizations and the law enforcement institutions seeking their help. Their relationship with law enforcement is shaped by their specific experience, and that experience is often more ambiguous than either organized crime romanticizers or law enforcement advocates acknowledge.

Chapter Summary

The policing of organized crime is one of the most technically demanding, legally complex, and institutionally challenging tasks that law enforcement in democratic societies undertakes. This chapter has traced the development of the investigative tools, legal frameworks, and institutional structures that have been built over more than a century to address the distinctive challenges that organized crime presents. The story is one of persistent tension between the effectiveness of investigation and the constraints that democratic values impose, between the adaptation of law enforcement and the countervailing adaptation of the criminal organizations it pursues, and between national and local approaches and the transnational reach of contemporary criminal networks.

The chapter began by identifying the fundamental structural challenge of organized crime investigation: that criminal organizations are designed to insulate their leaders from the direct evidence of criminal activity that conventional law enforcement methods require, using compartmentalization, corruption, violence, money laundering, and the code of silence to defeat conventional investigative approaches. Recognizing this challenge is the first step toward understanding why the specialized tools and methods that law enforcement has developed over the past century were necessary and why they took the forms they did.

Electronic surveillance, from early telephone wiretapping to modern digital interception, has been one of the most productive investigative tools against organized crime precisely because criminal organizations conduct much of their business through communications that, when intercepted, provide direct evidence of criminal activity and organizational structure. The legal framework governing electronic surveillance, shaped by the Fourth Amendment and elaborated through landmark Supreme Court decisions and federal statutes, has evolved significantly over the century, balancing investigative effectiveness against the privacy rights that democratic societies protect. The challenges of digital communications and encryption represent the latest phase of an ongoing technological and legal contest between criminal organizations seeking to protect their communications and law enforcement seeking to monitor them.

Intelligence gathering and analysis have become increasingly central to law enforcement strategy against organized crime as the recognition has grown that reactive, case-by-case investigation is inadequate against organizations whose survival depends on their ability to adapt and reconstitute themselves after individual prosecutions. The development of specialized intelligence units, joint task forces, and information-sharing frameworks reflects the institutional learning that law enforcement has done over decades of experience with the limitations of uncoordinated investigation.

Undercover operations offer the possibility of gathering evidence from inside criminal organizations that no external surveillance can provide, but they also involve law enforcement in deception and sometimes in the facilitation of criminal activity in ways that raise serious legal and ethical concerns. The doctrine of entrapment provides a legal boundary against the worst abuses of undercover tactics, but the boundary is genuinely difficult to draw in cases involving criminal organization members whose predisposition to criminal activity is precisely what makes them members.

The use of informants and cooperating witnesses has been central to nearly every major organized crime prosecution in American and international history. The value of inside information from individuals with direct knowledge of criminal organization structure and operations is simply irreplaceable by any external investigative technique. But the legal and ethical problems that criminal cooperators present, including questions of reliability, the benefits provided to serious criminals, and the facilitation of ongoing criminal activity by active informants, are equally genuine and cannot be dismissed as mere abstract concerns.

Financial investigation, from the early precedent set by Al Capone's tax conviction through the development of asset forfeiture and anti-money-laundering frameworks, reflects the recognition that organized crime's ultimate purpose is economic, and that disrupting its financial operations may be as effective a long-term strategy as prosecuting its individual leaders. The development of criminal and civil forfeiture has been powerful but controversial, and the debate over civil asset forfeiture in particular illustrates the persistent tension between law enforcement effectiveness and the protection of innocent people from government overreach.

International cooperation against transnational organized crime has evolved from informal police-to-police contacts to formalized institutional frameworks including Interpol, Europol, mutual legal assistance treaties, and specialized anti-organized-crime conventions. The development of these frameworks reflects the recognition that criminal organizations that operate across borders cannot be effectively addressed by national law enforcement agencies acting alone. But the obstacles to truly effective international cooperation, including differences in legal systems, sovereignty concerns, and political constraints, remain significant and limit what international frameworks can achieve.

Throughout this history, the development of organized crime investigative tools has been accompanied by persistent civil liberties concerns about the proper limits of government surveillance and prosecution power in a democratic society. These concerns are not merely theoretical. The history of law enforcement abuses, from COINTELPRO to documented cases of civil forfeiture affecting innocent people, demonstrates that powers developed for legitimate law enforcement purposes can be and have been misused. The institutional safeguards designed to prevent such abuse, judicial oversight, legislative review, civil society scrutiny, and professional accountability, are essential but have not always been sufficient.

The recurring dynamic that this chapter has documented, in which law enforcement develops new tools, criminal organizations adapt their methods to defeat them, and law enforcement must then adapt again, has characterized the relationship between organized crime and its pursuers throughout the period covered by this textbook. There is no permanent solution to organized crime, only an ongoing contest in which the relative capabilities of criminal organizations and law enforcement are constantly shifting. Understanding that dynamic, and the values and constraints within which it is contested in democratic societies, is essential to any serious understanding of the organized crime phenomenon.

Review Materials

Key Terms

RICO (Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act)
Federal statute enacted in 1970 that creates criminal liability for participating in or conducting the affairs of a criminal enterprise through a pattern of racketeering activity. The most powerful legal tool against organized crime in American history.
Predicate offense
A specific criminal act that qualifies as racketeering activity under RICO and contributes to the pattern of criminal activity required for a RICO prosecution. RICO requires at least two predicate offenses within a ten-year period.
Criminal enterprise
Under RICO, any group of individuals associated in fact for the purpose of conducting criminal activity. The concept allows prosecution of informal criminal organizations without proof of a formal legal entity.
Electronic surveillance
The interception and monitoring of electronic communications, including telephone calls, digital messages, and other forms of communication, conducted under legal authority as an investigative tool in criminal investigations.
Title III
The wiretapping authorization provisions of the Omnibus Crime Control and Safe Streets Act of 1968, which established the legal framework for court-authorized electronic surveillance of communications in federal criminal investigations.
Undercover operation
An investigation in which law enforcement officers or agents assume false identities to gather evidence from within criminal organizations or criminal environments, without revealing their law enforcement identity to their targets.
Entrapment
A legal defense available when law enforcement induces a person to commit a crime they were not predisposed to commit. The defense is designed to prevent law enforcement from manufacturing criminality rather than detecting it.
Informant
A person who provides information to law enforcement about criminal activity, typically while remaining within the criminal environment being investigated. May be paid for information or working in exchange for leniency on their own criminal exposure.
Cooperating witness
A person who has been arrested and agrees to cooperate with prosecutors as part of a plea agreement, typically pleading guilty to some offenses in exchange for a sentence reduction and providing testimony against former criminal associates.
Witness Security Program
The federal program, established in 1970, that provides new identities, relocation, and financial support to cooperating witnesses who face serious risks of criminal retaliation, in exchange for their testimony in federal proceedings.
Asset forfeiture
The legal process by which the government seizes property connected to criminal activity. Criminal forfeiture requires a conviction; civil forfeiture can proceed based on probable cause without a criminal conviction against the property's owner.
Money laundering
The process of concealing the criminal origin of illegally obtained money by routing it through legitimate financial institutions or transactions to make it appear to have come from legitimate sources.
Grand jury
A body of citizens empowered to investigate potential crimes, compel testimony and document production, and determine whether sufficient evidence exists to indict defendants for trial. Grand jury subpoenas are powerful investigative tools in organized crime cases.
Mutual Legal Assistance Treaty (MLAT)
A bilateral agreement between two countries establishing procedures for providing legal assistance in criminal investigations and prosecutions, including the sharing of evidence, service of process, and obtaining witness testimony across national borders.
Extradition
The formal legal process by which one country surrenders an accused or convicted person to another country that has requested the individual's return to face criminal charges or serve a sentence.
Intelligence-led policing
A law enforcement philosophy that emphasizes systematic intelligence gathering, analysis, and application to proactively identify, prioritize, and disrupt criminal activity, rather than merely reacting to crimes after they occur.
Pentito (pl. pentiti)
Italian term for a member of organized crime who cooperates with law enforcement in exchange for legal benefits. The testimony of high-level pentiti has been central to major anti-Mafia prosecutions in Italy since the 1980s.
Maxi-trial
An Italian judicial innovation pioneered in the Palermo anti-Mafia proceedings of 1986 to 1987, in which large numbers of defendants are prosecuted simultaneously for their membership in and activities on behalf of a criminal organization, rather than individually for specific criminal acts.
Compartmentalization
An organizational security technique in which different parts of a criminal organization are kept ignorant of each other's activities and identities, limiting the damage that any single member's arrest and cooperation can do to the organization as a whole.
Omertà
The Sicilian and Italian-American Mafia code of silence, which prohibits members from speaking to law enforcement about organizational activities under penalty of death. A powerful cultural and coercive deterrent to cooperation with law enforcement.
Use immunity
A form of legal protection granted to a witness compelled to testify before a grand jury or congressional committee, protecting the witness from prosecution using the specific testimony given. Distinguished from transactional immunity, which protects against prosecution for the underlying conduct discussed.
Forensic accounting
The application of accounting and financial investigation methods to legal proceedings, including the tracing of criminal proceeds, the identification of financial patterns inconsistent with legitimate income, and the documentation of money laundering and fraud.
HUMINT
Human intelligence, gathered from human sources as distinct from technical surveillance. In organized crime investigation, encompasses information from informants, cooperating witnesses, and undercover agents with direct access to criminal organizations.
Financial Action Task Force (FATF)
An intergovernmental body established in 1989 that sets international standards for anti-money-laundering and counter-terrorist financing, which member countries are expected to incorporate into their domestic legal and regulatory frameworks.

Major People, Groups, and Places

Identification & Significance

J. Edgar Hoover (1895–1972)
FBI Director who for decades denied the existence of a national organized crime network and resisted federal investigation of the Mafia. His eventual reversal after Apalachin came late and is still debated by historians as to its motivations.
Estes Kefauver (1903–1963)
Senate investigator whose televised organized crime hearings of 1950 and 1951 created the first sustained national public awareness of organized crime and the political pressure for a federal response.
Robert F. Kennedy (1925–1968)
McClellan Committee counsel and Attorney General; the most prominent political advocate for aggressive federal anti-organized-crime enforcement in the early 1960s. His personal campaign against Teamsters leader Jimmy Hoffa exemplified the era's prosecutorial energy.
Joseph Valachi (1904–1971)
The first Mafia insider to testify publicly about the organization's structure and operations. His 1963 congressional testimony transformed law enforcement's understanding of organized crime's architecture.
Joseph Pistone / "Donnie Brasco"
FBI Special Agent who spent six years undercover within the Bonanno crime family in the 1970s and 1980s, producing more than 200 criminal convictions and the most famous undercover operation in American law enforcement history.
Tommaso Buscetta (1928–2000)
Senior Sicilian Mafia figure who became the most significant pentito in Italian anti-Mafia history. His cooperation with Judge Giovanni Falcone in the early 1980s provided the intelligence foundation for the Palermo maxi-trial and transformed Italian anti-Mafia prosecution.
Giovanni Falcone (1939–1992)
Italian anti-Mafia judge whose innovations in coordinated investigation and maxi-trial prosecution represent the most significant methodological advance in the history of anti-Mafia law enforcement. Assassinated by Cosa Nostra in May 1992.
Paolo Borsellino (1940–1992)
Falcone's closest colleague and fellow anti-Mafia judge; assassinated by Cosa Nostra two months after Falcone. The two murders together produced an unprecedented national political response in Italy.
Rudy Giuliani (b. 1944)
U.S. Attorney who led the Commission Case and other landmark RICO prosecutions of the New York Mafia families in the 1980s. His prosecutorial record against organized crime remains one of the most significant in American legal history.
G. Robert Blakey (b. 1936)
Notre Dame law professor who drafted the RICO statute. His theoretical work on enterprise-based criminal liability provided the legal framework that transformed federal organized crime prosecution.
The Direzione Investigativa Antimafia (DIA)
Italian national anti-Mafia investigative organization established in 1991 following the Falcone assassination; institutionalizes the coordinated investigative approach Falcone pioneered and coordinates anti-Mafia operations across regional boundaries.
Interpol
The International Criminal Police Organization; facilitates cooperation among national law enforcement agencies worldwide through criminal databases, international notices, and coordination frameworks. Essential infrastructure for cross-border organized crime investigations.
The Southern District of New York
Federal judicial district covering Manhattan; site of more landmark organized crime prosecutions than any other federal jurisdiction in the United States, including the Commission Case and prosecutions of all five New York Mafia families.
Apalachin, New York
Upstate New York site of the 1957 Mafia conclave accidentally discovered by state police; the defining moment that forced J. Edgar Hoover and the FBI to acknowledge organized crime's national organization.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter has examined the history and methods of policing organized crime, tracing the development of investigative tools, legal frameworks, and institutional structures over more than a century of contest between criminal organizations and the democratic societies that have sought to control them. The history reveals a dynamic that is more complex and more ambivalent than either the triumphalist narrative of law enforcement success or the cynical narrative of permanent futility would suggest.

On one level, the development of organized crime law enforcement over the past century represents a genuine achievement of democratic governance. The legal innovations of the 1960s and 1970s, electronic surveillance authorization, RICO, the Witness Security Program, the expansion of financial investigation tools, gave investigators and prosecutors capabilities they had lacked. When applied with skill, adequate resources, and sustained political will, those capabilities produced results that were genuinely significant. The decimation of the American Mafia's traditional hierarchical structure through RICO prosecutions in the 1980s, achieved without any compromise of the constitutional framework that American democracy depends on, represents a real law enforcement success. The Palermo maxi-trial, achieved at the cost of investigators' lives and sustained over years of intense institutional effort, demonstrated what determined law enforcement in a democratic society could accomplish against the most entrenched criminal organization in European history.

On another level, the history surveyed in this chapter provides ample reason for humility about the limits of law enforcement solutions to organized crime. Criminal organizations have demonstrated a persistent capacity to adapt, reconstitute, and continue operating in new forms even after the most devastating prosecutorial blows. The American Mafia survives in diminished form decades after the great prosecutions of the 1980s. The Sicilian Cosa Nostra, severely damaged by the maxi-trial and subsequent prosecutions, has adapted to new conditions. New criminal organizations, the Mexican cartels, the 'Ndrangheta, Russian organized crime networks, Asian criminal networks, and cybercriminal enterprises, have emerged to fill spaces and meet demands that older organizations had occupied. The fight against organized crime is not a battle that can be won in the sense of producing a permanent condition of victory; it is an ongoing contest that each generation of law enforcement must engage with the tools and resources available to it.

The most important lesson that this chapter's history offers is perhaps the least comfortable one: that effective policing of organized crime in a democratic society requires sustained political will, adequate resources, institutional integrity, legal innovation, and a willingness to engage with genuine ethical complexity, and that none of these ingredients can be taken for granted or maintained without continuous effort. The periods of greatest law enforcement effectiveness against organized crime in American and comparative history have been those in which political will was highest, resources most adequate, institutions most resistant to corruption, and legal tools most recently and appropriately calibrated. The periods of greatest organized crime dominance have been those in which political will was absent or corrupted, resources inadequate, institutions compromised, and legal tools insufficient to the task. The lesson is not flattering to any particular period of American governance, but it is one that a serious study of the history compels.

As students carry the understanding developed in this chapter forward into the subsequent chapters on anti-organized-crime law, the history of prosecutorial successes and failures, and the contemporary landscape of organized crime, they should hold onto the central tension that this chapter has identified: between the imperative to use every available tool against criminal organizations that cause genuine harm to individuals, communities, and institutions, and the equally important imperative to conduct that fight within the constraints that democratic values impose. A society that abandons its commitment to civil liberties, due process, and the rule of law in order to fight organized crime more effectively has compromised the very things it was supposed to be protecting. The challenge, as it has been throughout the history examined in this textbook, is to find and maintain the difficult balance between those imperatives, under conditions that make that balance always imperfect and always contested.

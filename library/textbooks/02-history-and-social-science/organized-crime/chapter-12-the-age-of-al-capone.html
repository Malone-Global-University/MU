Part IV — Organized Crime Comes to America

The Age of Al Capone

Organized Crime: A Global History

Chapter Opening

"You can get much farther with a kind word and a gun than you can with a kind word alone."
— Attributed to Al Capone

On the evening of February 14, 1929, seven men connected to the North Side gang of Chicago were lured into a garage on North Clark Street under the pretense of a bootlegging transaction. What followed in the next few minutes became one of the most infamous acts of criminal violence in American history. Gunmen dressed as police officers lined the victims against the wall and opened fire with Thompson submachine guns and a sawed-off shotgun, killing all seven. The massacre shocked a nation that had grown accustomed to sporadic gangland shootings but had not yet witnessed industrialized murder on this scale. Newspapers across the country ran front-page stories. Politicians demanded action. And everywhere, one name surfaced as the presumed architect of the slaughter, though no charges were ever filed: Alphonse Gabriel Capone, the thirty-year-old Brooklyn-born gangster who had, over the preceding five years, assembled the most powerful criminal organization in the history of the United States up to that point.

The Valentine's Day Massacre was not the beginning of the story. It was, in many respects, a culmination, the endpoint of a brutal war for control of the illegal liquor trade in Chicago that had consumed the better part of a decade and claimed dozens of lives. To understand how a single criminal organization had come to dominate a major American city with such thoroughness that its leader could order the public execution of rivals in broad daylight and walk away without prosecution, historians must examine a convergence of forces that were larger than any individual: the extraordinary economic opportunity created by Prohibition, the structural weaknesses of American urban governance, the ethnic and geographic fragmentation of Chicago's underworld, the particular brutality and organizational genius of the men who rose to lead it, and the pervasive corruption that allowed criminal enterprise to flourish under the protection of elected officials and police.

Al Capone has become, in the century since his rise, something close to a cultural archetype, the prototype of the American gangster, his name synonymous with organized crime itself. He has been the subject of films, novels, television series, museum exhibits, and countless journalistic accounts. He has been simultaneously mythologized as a self-made entrepreneur who simply operated in an illegal market and condemned as a murderous tyrant who terrorized a city. Both portraits contain elements of truth, but both also obscure more than they reveal. The historical Capone was a product of specific conditions, a man whose extraordinary rise depended not only on his own capacities, ruthlessness, and organizational skill but on a particular moment in American history when the law had created a vast new criminal opportunity, when the institutions charged with enforcing that law were thoroughly compromised, and when millions of ordinary Americans were willing to break it every day.

What Capone represented in his time, and what his era represents in the larger history of organized crime, was a decisive transition. Before Prohibition, criminal enterprises in American cities were real but relatively fragmented, localized, and limited in scale. The gang wars of the 1920s, precisely because they were so violent and so public, forced criminal organizations to consolidate, rationalize, and develop more sophisticated structures of governance, profit-sharing, and conflict resolution. The organizations that survived the Prohibition era, having been tempered by a decade of lethal competition, emerged as the templates for the American organized crime structures that would shape the mid-twentieth century. In this sense, the age of Al Capone was not simply an episode in criminal history. It was a formative period in the development of a criminal institution.

This chapter examines that period in depth. It traces the origins of the Chicago gang wars in the fragmented criminal landscape of the early Prohibition years, follows the consolidation of power under Johnny Torrio and then Capone, examines the full scope of Capone's criminal enterprises and his extraordinary reach into Chicago's political and governmental life, analyzes the belated and improvised law enforcement response, and considers the fall of Capone and the transformation of the criminal landscape he left behind. It also examines the experience of Chicago during the Prohibition era from perspectives beyond the gangsters themselves, including the ordinary residents of neighborhoods where gang violence was a daily reality, the African American community whose relationship to the bootlegging economy was complex and particular, the women who worked in and around the illegal trade, and the journalists and reformers who struggled to document and resist a criminal order that had achieved something close to governmental power.

Finally, this chapter situates the age of Capone within the broader history of organized crime as a global phenomenon. The structures Capone helped to create, the corruption networks, the diversified criminal enterprises, the use of violence as a tool of market control, the cultivation of political protection, were not unique to Chicago or to America. They were variants of patterns that had emerged in Sicily with the Mafia, in Naples with the Camorra, and in criminal networks around the world wherever legal prohibition, weak governance, and high demand for illegal goods created the conditions for criminal enterprise to flourish. Understanding Capone historically means understanding not just the man but the structural conditions that made a figure like him inevitable.

The age of Al Capone ended with his conviction in 1931, not for murder or extortion or bootlegging, but for the mundane crime of federal income tax evasion. The manner of his fall was as revealing as the manner of his rise: a government that had proven unable to prosecute him for the crimes of which everyone knew him guilty found a narrow legal instrument through which to bring him down. It was a reminder that in the contest between criminal enterprise and the state, outcomes depend less on the moral clarity of the conflict than on the institutional capacities, political will, and legal tools available to each side.

Historical Context

To understand Al Capone and the criminal empire he built, it is necessary to understand Chicago in the 1920s as a social, economic, and political landscape, not simply as a backdrop for gangland drama. Chicago in 1920 was the second-largest city in the United States, a metropolis of extraordinary dynamism and equally extraordinary inequality, a city built on the labor of immigrants and African Americans who had been drawn by industrial opportunity and whose communities had been largely excluded from the formal structures of political and economic power.

The city had grown with astonishing speed through the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, absorbing successive waves of immigrants from Germany, Ireland, Scandinavia, Poland, Russia, Italy, and dozens of other countries, each wave settling into distinct ethnic neighborhoods that retained strong internal cultural identities even as they were integrated into the broader urban economy. Chicago's geography reinforced this ethnic and racial segregation. The South Side, the West Side, and the Near North Side each had distinct ethnic compositions, and the informal territorial boundaries between communities were understood by residents even when they were not formally marked.

African Americans had been arriving in Chicago in significant numbers since the Great Migration, which accelerated dramatically during and after World War I. Chicago's Black Belt, centered on the South Side, had become by the 1920s a vibrant but severely constrained community, its residents denied access to most of Chicago's housing, employment, and public institutions by a combination of discriminatory law, restrictive housing covenants, and pervasive social violence. The relationship between the Black Belt and the bootlegging economy was complex: African American entrepreneurs ran policy games, saloons, and entertainment venues that were interwoven with the illegal liquor trade, while at the same time Black residents bore a disproportionate burden of the violence associated with gang rivalry and police brutality.

Chicago's political structure was equally important context. The city was governed through a ward-based machine politics system in which aldermen and ward committeemen exercised enormous local power, distributing jobs, contracts, and favors in exchange for political loyalty and, frequently, financial payments. The Chicago Police Department was deeply integrated into this patronage system, with police assignments, promotions, and demotions controlled substantially by ward political organizations. The result was a police force that was structurally oriented toward accommodation of local political power rather than independent law enforcement, and that was chronically vulnerable to corruption at every level from beat officer to superintendent.

Into this environment, the Eighteenth Amendment to the Constitution, which took effect in January 1920, introduced a prohibition on the manufacture, sale, and transportation of intoxicating liquors. As Chapter 11 examined in detail, the immediate effect of Prohibition was not the elimination of alcohol consumption but the transfer of a massive legal industry to the underground economy. The scale of the resulting illegal market was extraordinary. Chicago's population of roughly three million included hundreds of thousands of immigrants from cultures in which beer and wine were daily commodities, and hundreds of thousands more whose relationship to alcohol was recreational, social, and deeply resistant to government interference. The demand for illegal liquor in Chicago was essentially unlimited, and the profits available to those who could meet that demand were correspondingly vast.

The criminal landscape that existed in Chicago at the start of Prohibition was, as described in Chapter 10, fragmented and ethnically organized. The North Side was dominated by Irish-American gangs with roots in the pre-Prohibition vice economy. The South Side and surrounding areas were controlled by a collection of Italian-American criminal organizations that had developed from the Black Hand extortion networks of the immigrant era. Scattered across the city were dozens of smaller operations controlled by Polish, Jewish, Greek, and other ethnic criminal entrepreneurs. None of these organizations had anything like the structure, scale, or sophistication that the Capone organization would develop. They were, in the first years of Prohibition, essentially entrepreneurial firms operating in a new and enormously lucrative market without the institutional framework to manage either the opportunity or the conflicts it inevitably generated.

It was in this context that Johnny Torrio, a Brooklyn-born Italian-American criminal who had been managing vice operations in Chicago since 1909 under the patronage of the veteran criminal "Big Jim" Colosimo, recognized the transformative potential of Prohibition and set about building an organization capable of capitalizing on it. Torrio was the organizational architect of what became the Capone empire; the young Brooklyn gunman he recruited to Chicago in 1919, Alphonse Capone, was initially one instrument among many. The story of the 1920s is in part the story of how Torrio's strategic vision combined with Capone's operational ruthlessness and personal dominance to produce the most powerful criminal organization the country had yet seen, and how the violence that accompanied their rise ultimately transformed both the criminal landscape and the political response to it.

Section 12.1

Chicago Before the Wars: The Criminal Landscape of Early Prohibition

When Prohibition took effect on January 17, 1920, Chicago's criminal landscape was already a complex and well-established ecosystem. The city had been a center of organized vice, including illegal gambling, prostitution, and political corruption, for decades. The Levee district on the South Side had been the nation's most notorious red-light district until a reform campaign finally forced its official closure in 1912. Gambling operations ran openly under the protection of ward politicians across the city. The criminal networks built around these enterprises had developed sophisticated relationships with the political machine, the police, and local business communities that would provide the institutional infrastructure into which the bootlegging trade was inserted when Prohibition arrived.

The dominant figure in Chicago's pre-Prohibition underworld was "Big Jim" Colosimo, an Italian-American immigrant who had built a combination of gambling, prostitution, and labor rackets on the South Side while simultaneously cultivating deep connections to the Democratic political machine through his work as a precinct captain. Colosimo ran dozens of establishments and distributed political favors generously enough to earn genuine community standing among Italian-American residents who might otherwise have had no contact with his criminal operations. His wife, Victoria Moresco, ran a string of brothels that formed the economic core of his empire.

In 1909, facing extortion attempts from Black Hand gangsters, Colosimo had brought his nephew Johnny Torrio up from New York to help manage the situation. Torrio resolved the problem through a combination of violence and negotiation, and then stayed in Chicago as Colosimo's primary deputy. Over the following decade, Torrio developed a reputation as a cool and strategic operator, more interested in organization and profit than in personal aggrandizement or violence for its own sake. By the time Prohibition arrived, Torrio had a clear vision of what the new law made possible: a bootlegging operation of sufficient scale and organization to supply Chicago's enormous appetite for illegal liquor while generating revenues that would dwarf anything the old vice economy had produced.

Colosimo, however, was resistant. Comfortable with his existing arrangements and reportedly preoccupied with a new romantic relationship (he had divorced Victoria Moresco and was pursuing a young singer), he declined to commit the organization to the risks and ambitions of large-scale bootlegging. In May 1920, four months after Prohibition began, Colosimo was shot dead in his restaurant on Wabash Avenue. The murder was never officially solved, but virtually every historian of the period regards it as having been arranged by Torrio, who needed to remove an obstacle to the organization's expansion. With Colosimo gone, Torrio assumed control and began building the bootlegging network he had envisioned.

The key figure Torrio recruited to assist in this expansion was a twenty-year-old Brooklyn-born Italian-American named Alphonse Capone, the son of immigrants from Naples who had grown up in the Five Points and Park Slope neighborhoods of Brooklyn, been expelled from school at age fourteen, and attached himself to the criminal world of the Brooklyn waterfront through minor gang affiliations. Capone had come to Torrio's attention through connections in the New York underworld, particularly through the Brooklyn-based gangster Frankie Yale, for whom Capone had worked as a bartender and bouncer at a Coney Island establishment called the Harvard Inn. It was there, in 1917, that a confrontation with a customer left Capone with three slash marks on the left side of his face, earning him the nickname "Scarface" that he despised.

In 1919, Capone relocated to Chicago at Torrio's invitation and began working in Torrio's operations as a manager and enforcer. He learned the business from the ground up, managing saloons and gambling establishments, collecting revenues, resolving disputes, and acquiring an intimate knowledge of the city's criminal geography and political landscape. He also, gradually, demonstrated capacities that made him indispensable: an ability to command personal loyalty, a facility for the kind of charming public relations that could make a criminal figure seem like a community leader, and a willingness to deploy violence that was calculated rather than impulsive, designed to achieve specific organizational objectives rather than simply to express personal aggression.

By 1923, Torrio and Capone had established a bootlegging network of significant scale, operating breweries, distilleries, and a distribution network that supplied much of Chicago's South Side and western suburbs. They had also begun extending their reach into the suburb of Cicero, Illinois, where a combination of political manipulation and outright intimidation allowed them to install a compliant local government and effectively convert the town into an organizational base outside Chicago's nominal jurisdiction. The Cicero operation, which included gambling establishments, brothels, and a brewery, provided both revenues and a degree of insulation from Chicago authorities that would prove valuable in the years ahead.

The North Side of Chicago was controlled by a very different kind of criminal organization. The North Side Gang, led by Charles Dean O'Banion, had its ethnic roots in the Irish-American communities of the Near North Side and combined bootlegging with the residual gambling and political operations that had characterized North Side criminal enterprise since before Prohibition. O'Banion was, by the accounts of both contemporaries and historians, a genuinely eccentric figure: a former altar boy and choirboy who ran a legitimate flower shop on State Street (which conveniently supplied arrangements to the funerals of men his organization helped to kill), a man capable of genuine personal charm who could also order murder with apparent equanimity. He was also, critically, contemptuous of Torrio and Capone and resistant to any accommodation of their expanding ambitions.

The uneasy coexistence between the South Side organization and the North Side Gang was the structural precondition for the gang wars that defined the middle years of the decade. As long as each organization respected the other's territorial boundaries and business relationships, open conflict was avoidable. But the temptations of the bootlegging market, combined with the personal antagonisms between O'Banion and the Italians he regarded with ethnic contempt, made collision increasingly likely. The trigger came in November 1924, when O'Banion arranged a transaction that left Torrio with a shipment of beer and a police raid simultaneously, costing Torrio money and legal exposure and earning no reciprocal concession. Shortly afterward, on November 10, 1924, O'Banion was shot dead in his flower shop by three men who have never been officially identified.

Historical Vocabulary
Racket

The term racket, in its criminal sense, refers to a scheme in which money is obtained through coercion, fraud, or illegal enterprise, typically operated on an ongoing basis rather than as a single transaction. The word derives from the older slang sense of a noisy disturbance, but by the late nineteenth century had acquired its specific criminal meaning. A racket is distinguished from simple theft or one-time fraud by its systematic, recurring nature and its use of an organizational structure to sustain operations over time.

The Capone organization operated numerous rackets simultaneously. The bootlegging racket supplied illegal liquor to thousands of Chicago establishments. The gambling racket ran horse books, card rooms, and policy wheels. The labor racket extorted payments from businesses through control of trade unions. The brothel racket managed networks of prostitution establishments. What distinguished these from less organized criminal activity was precisely their systematic, bureaucratic quality: regular revenue collection, hierarchical management, allocated territories, and negotiated relationships with political protectors.

The word "racket" entered the legal lexicon most consequentially through the Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act of 1970 (RICO), which was designed specifically to prosecute the kind of ongoing criminal enterprise that characterized organizations like the one Capone built. The conceptual history of the word thus traces a line from the criminal innovation of the Prohibition era to the legal tools eventually developed to combat it.

Section 12.2

The Gang Wars: Violence, Territory, and the Logic of Criminal Competition

The assassination of Dean O'Banion in November 1924 opened a period of gang warfare in Chicago that lasted, with varying intensity, for nearly five years and claimed the lives of dozens of men. Understanding this violence requires understanding it not simply as a story of criminal pathology but as a form of market competition in which violence served specific economic functions: eliminating rivals, enforcing territorial boundaries, disciplining employees, and signaling to customers and political protectors that one organization rather than another controlled a given piece of the illegal market.

O'Banion's successors, particularly Hymie Weiss and then Bugs Moran, proved neither as patient as Torrio nor as organizationally capable as Capone, but they were capable of inflicting serious damage. In January 1925, gunmen connected to the North Side Gang ambushed Torrio outside his apartment building, shooting him five times and leaving him for dead. Torrio survived but was badly wounded, and the experience convinced him that his own risk tolerance had been exceeded. Upon his release from prison on an unrelated charge in 1925, Torrio turned operational control of the South Side organization over to Capone and departed for Italy, later returning to New York to play a background role in the reorganization of American organized crime that Chapter 13 examines. Capone, at age twenty-six, was now the leader of the most powerful criminal organization in the country's second-largest city.

The warfare that followed was unprecedented in its public visibility and its weaponry. The Thompson submachine gun, developed during World War I but commercially available to civilians during the 1920s, was adopted enthusiastically by Chicago's gangs as a tool of mass killing at close range. The weapon could fire hundreds of rounds per minute and was devastating in the kind of ambush scenarios that gang assassins favored. Its use transformed what had been essentially individual murders, a gangster shot in a bar or on a street corner, into something more closely resembling military engagements. The motorcade attacks that became characteristic of the gang wars, convoys of automobiles from which gunmen fired Thompson submachine guns at targets in moving vehicles or at buildings, were tactical innovations that required the kind of organizational coordination only a well-run criminal enterprise could provide.

The climax of the North Side challenge to Capone's supremacy came in September 1926, when Hymie Weiss organized a convoy of eleven automobiles that drove slowly past Capone's headquarters at the Hawthorne Inn in Cicero and unleashed a fusillade of fire that riddled the building with more than a thousand bullets. Capone, who was inside, was unhurt, but the audacity of the attack in a community that he effectively controlled was a dramatic demonstration that the North Side Gang retained real capacity to threaten him. The retaliation came quickly: on October 11, 1926, Weiss was shot dead outside the Chicago office of his attorney near Holy Name Cathedral. Two of his companions were also killed. The location, one of Chicago's most prominent religious sites on North State Street, was a measure of how thoroughly gang violence had ceased to respect the ordinary boundaries of public space.

With Weiss dead, a brief armistice followed. In October 1926, representatives of Chicago's major criminal factions met at the Hotel Sherman and negotiated a territorial settlement that divided the city's bootlegging territories among the various organizations. Capone emerged from this summit as the dominant power, his South Side organization controlling the most profitable territories and his organization operating at a scale that dwarfed his competitors. But the peace was fragile, dependent on the continued acceptance of a hierarchy that the North Side Gang, now under Bugs Moran, was unwilling to fully concede.

The campaign against Capone that Moran's diminished organization waged in 1927 and 1928 was more harassment than existential threat, but it was costly and irritating: hijackings of Capone beer trucks, murders of Capone associates, and a persistent refusal to accept the territorial settlement of 1926. It was this continuing provocation, combined with the organizational logic of eliminating the last significant rival faction, that produced the plan that culminated in the Valentine's Day Massacre of February 14, 1929.

The massacre's mechanics have been analyzed in detail by historians working from police reports, survivor testimony, and forensic evidence. A garage at 2122 North Clark Street, used by Moran's organization for storing hijacked whiskey, was the site. Four men, two of whom were dressed in police uniforms, entered the garage on the pretense of a routine inspection. When they left, seven men lay dead, killed by shotgun blasts and Thompson submachine gun fire in what appeared to be a staged police execution. Moran himself, the intended primary target, arrived late and, seeing men he took for actual police entering the garage, walked away. He survived by accident.

The massacre's strategic logic was sound: it destroyed the operational leadership of the last significant rival organization. But its political and public relations consequences were catastrophic for Capone. The scale of the killing, the uniform disguise that seemed to implicate law enforcement either directly or through complicity, and the machine-gun tableau that witnesses described shocked even a public accustomed to gang violence. The national press coverage was sustained and condemnatory. President Herbert Hoover, who took office just two weeks after the massacre, was reportedly furious and directed the relevant federal agencies to make the prosecution of Capone a priority. The massacre thus marked the beginning of the end of the organizational impunity that Capone had enjoyed for most of the decade.

Historical Vocabulary
Criminal Syndicate

A criminal syndicate is a large-scale organized criminal enterprise involving multiple individuals, operating across multiple businesses or territories, with a degree of internal hierarchy and governance. The term distinguishes large, organized criminal operations from individual criminal acts or small gangs. It implies coordination, division of labor, shared revenues, and some mechanism for managing internal disputes and external threats.

The Capone organization qualified as a criminal syndicate in all of these senses. It operated across multiple criminal markets simultaneously: brewing, distilling, distributing, and retailing illegal liquor; running gambling establishments of multiple types; managing prostitution networks; and extorting payments from legitimate businesses through labor union control. It employed hundreds of individuals in different capacities. It had a geographic hierarchy with sub-leaders responsible for specific territories. And it maintained a political protection network that functioned, in effect, as a governmental lobbying and bribery operation.

The concept of the criminal syndicate became central to American law enforcement thinking in the decades following Capone's fall, particularly after the Kefauver Committee hearings of 1950–1951 and the subsequent development of the RICO statute. Understanding Capone's organization as a syndicate, rather than simply as the personal criminal enterprise of one individual, was crucial to developing the legal tools eventually used to prosecute organized crime as a collective institution.

Chicago's Gang Territories and Key Locations, 1920–1930

Chicago's geography shaped the gang wars as profoundly as any human decision. Students examining a map of 1920s Chicago should look for the following territorial and spatial patterns:

  • The South Side (roughly below the Loop and east of Halsted Street): Core territory of the Torrio-Capone organization, including the Four Deuces headquarters on South Wabash and the Black Belt community that coexisted uneasily with Italian-American criminal dominance.
  • The Near North Side (above the Chicago River, west of Lake Michigan): Traditional territory of the North Side Gang, including O'Banion's flower shop on North State Street near Holy Name Cathedral.
  • Cicero, Illinois (immediately west of Chicago's city limits): Capone's suburban operational base; a separate municipal jurisdiction that gave the organization significant insulation from Chicago city authorities.
  • The Loop (central business district): Neutral commercial territory where rival organizations maintained cautious coexistence; location of the Hotel Sherman armistice meeting of 1926.
  • The West Side: Contested territory with multiple smaller criminal organizations, including significant Jewish-American criminal enterprises that maintained working relationships with both major factions.
  • North Clark Street at Armory (2122 N. Clark): Site of the Valentine's Day Massacre; in North Side territory, suggesting the operational boldness of the attack.

Section 12.3

Capone's Criminal Empire: Organization, Scale, and Management

Beneath the newspaper headlines, the spectacular violence, and the personal mythology of Al Capone lay an actual organization that historians have worked to reconstruct from tax records, police reports, investigative journalism, and the testimony of participants. What they have found is an enterprise of remarkable scale and, in many respects, of genuine organizational sophistication, one that operated across multiple criminal markets simultaneously, maintained a complex corruption network, and generated revenues that contemporaries estimated at between fifty and one hundred million dollars annually at its peak, though the precise figures are inherently uncertain because the organization kept its accounts deliberately obscure.

The bootlegging operation was the financial engine of the enterprise. Capone's organization did not simply sell illegal liquor; it controlled the full supply chain from production through retail. At the production end, the organization operated or controlled numerous breweries throughout the Chicago metropolitan area, producing beer that was the primary commodity in the illegal trade. The Sieben Brewery, the Malt Maid Brewery, and numerous smaller operations produced hundreds of barrels weekly. Distilled spirits, including whiskey and gin, were produced at smaller facilities or imported from Canada across Lake Michigan. Distribution was managed through a network of trucks and drivers who delivered product to thousands of retail establishments throughout the city and suburbs.

The retail end of the bootlegging operation was organized through a combination of ownership, franchise, and tribute. Some establishments, particularly those in territory firmly under organizational control, were owned directly or through nominees. Many others operated under informal franchise arrangements, paying a regular fee for the right to purchase Capone beer and spirits exclusively and for the protection from rivals and from selective police enforcement that the fee purchased. Still others simply paid a "tribute" to Capone's organization for the right to operate at all, purchasing their liquor from any available source but making regular payments to maintain peace. The result was a tiered revenue structure that captured income from the bootlegging trade at multiple levels simultaneously.

Gambling operations constituted the second major revenue stream. The Capone organization ran horse books throughout Chicago and the suburbs, establishments where customers could bet on horse races conducted elsewhere and monitored through wire services that carried race results. It operated card rooms and dice games in the vice districts of the South Side. It ran policy, the numbers game played primarily in African American communities, where customers would bet small amounts on three-digit numbers and collect a payout if their number matched a daily drawing. The policy game was particularly significant in the Black Belt, where it functioned as a parallel economy: policy runners collected nickels and dimes from thousands of working-class customers daily, and the organization captured a percentage of the resulting revenue at each step of the hierarchy.

Labor racketeering, less visible than bootlegging and gambling but ultimately more durable as a revenue source, was a third major component. The Capone organization moved systematically into control of trade unions representing workers in industries where the organization of labor gave criminal control genuine economic leverage. Unions representing laundry workers, restaurant workers, cleaners and dyers, and theatrical stage employees were among those that fell under organizational influence or direct control during the 1920s. Control of a union gave the organization multiple revenue streams: it could extract dues that flowed to organizational accounts rather than being used for member representation; it could sell "strike insurance" to employers, guaranteeing labor peace in exchange for regular payments; and it could steer union contracts toward businesses that were also part of the organizational network, capturing value at both ends of the labor-management relationship.

The organizational hierarchy through which these enterprises were managed was less formally elaborate than popular imagination suggests but was genuine nonetheless. Capone sat at the apex as the organization's recognized leader, whose authority derived from his personal dominance, his track record of successful violence and successful negotiation, and his control of the organizational revenue streams. Below him were a small number of senior associates who managed major functions or territories: Jake Guzik, a Jewish-American accountant and political fixer who managed the financial and political relationships of the organization, was perhaps the most important; Tony Accardo, who would later become the organization's dominant figure, was an enforcer who rose to prominence during the gang war period; Frank Nitti served as an enforcer and, later, a public-facing representative. Beneath this level, the organization had district managers, precinct-level collectors, and hundreds of drivers, bartenders, bouncers, bookmakers, and runners who carried out its daily operations.

The organization also maintained a legal affairs capacity of sorts, keeping a small number of attorneys on permanent retainer who could intervene when organizational employees were arrested, arrange bail, and manage the legal relationships that inevitably arose from operating a criminal enterprise at this scale. The organization's political relationships, discussed in the next section, also functioned as a kind of regulatory affairs operation, managing the interface between the criminal enterprise and the governmental structures that could, if hostile, severely disrupt its operations.

Capone himself was not, in the conventional gangland image, a man who spent his days planning murders and cracking safes. The documentary and testimonial record suggests that he functioned more like the chief executive of a diversified firm: meeting with subordinates, receiving reports, making decisions about resource allocation and personnel, maintaining relationships with political contacts, and projecting the public image of a prosperous and community-minded businessman. He attended the opera, entertained lavishly, made public donations to soup kitchens during the Depression-era period of his later reign, and cultivated a persona of generous informality that contrasted deliberately with the image of criminal menace that newspapers projected. This persona was itself a management tool: it maintained the personal loyalty of subordinates who experienced him as approachable and generous, and it projected to the broader public the image of a businessman rather than a criminal, complicating the political will necessary to prosecute him.

The revenues generated by this enterprise were extraordinary by any historical standard. Tax records produced during the 1931 prosecution, combined with the investigative journalism of the period, suggest that organizational revenues in the peak years of 1926 to 1929 exceeded sixty million dollars annually, of which Capone personally received somewhere between six and ten million dollars per year. These figures are approximate and contested, but even conservative estimates place the Capone organization among the largest business enterprises in Illinois by revenue during this period, illegal or legal. The extraordinary profitability of the enterprise was itself a key element in understanding why it was so difficult to suppress: the revenues available to the organization were sufficient to buy protection at virtually every level of the political and law enforcement hierarchy at which such protection was for sale.

Section 12.4

Political Influence: The Architecture of Corruption

No account of the Capone organization can be accurate without a serious examination of its political infrastructure. The criminal empire did not operate despite Chicago's governmental institutions; it operated largely through them, or at least in intimate accommodation with them. The corruption of Chicago's political and law enforcement establishments during the Prohibition era was not incidental to the story but structural to it. Understanding how that corruption worked, who participated in it, what it cost, and why the normal mechanisms of democratic accountability failed to disrupt it is essential to understanding how a criminal organization could achieve the scale and longevity that Capone's did.

The fundamental mechanism was payment. The Capone organization, building on structures that Johnny Torrio had already established and that had roots in the pre-Prohibition political economy of Chicago vice, made systematic payments to a hierarchy of political and law enforcement officials in exchange for protection from the consequences of operating illegal businesses. These payments were not random bribes opportunistically offered but organized tributes delivered on regular schedules to designated recipients at each level of the relevant hierarchy.

At the ward level, aldermen and ward committeemen received regular payments in exchange for ensuring that local police would not actively interfere with organizational operations in their wards. In some wards, the arrangements were so thoroughly institutionalized that the collection of "tribute" from criminal enterprises was effectively part of the ward's political revenue system, with funds flowing through the ward organization to political campaigns, patronage hiring, and personal enrichment. The ward boss was not simply a passive recipient of criminal money but an active participant in the regulatory structure that determined which operations would be tolerated and which would be subject to selective enforcement.

At the police department level, the corruption operated through both specific payments to individual officers and precinct commanders and through the patronage system that connected police promotions and assignments to political favor. A police commander who demonstrated willingness to accommodate the criminal establishment would find his career advancing through the mechanisms of political patronage; one who insisted on aggressive enforcement would find himself transferred to an undesirable assignment or passed over for promotion. The structural incentives thus operated powerfully in the direction of accommodation even for officers who were not directly on the criminal payroll.

The most important single political relationship that the Capone organization cultivated during the Prohibition era was with the administration of Mayor William "Big Bill" Thompson. Thompson, a Republican who served as Chicago's mayor from 1915 to 1923 and again from 1927 to 1931, was a flamboyant and corrupt political figure whose administrations were characterized by the systematic accommodation of the bootlegging establishment. Thompson made no pretense of vigorous Prohibition enforcement; his public position was effectively that what citizens did with alcohol in private was their own business, a stance that resonated with a substantial segment of Chicago's immigrant and working-class population but that also conveniently aligned with the interests of those who profited from illegal supply.

The organizational relationship with Thompson's administration was mediated primarily through Thompson's political network rather than through direct personal dealings with Capone. Jake Guzik, the organizational financial officer, managed many of the key political relationships, providing funds through intermediaries to campaign organizations, political committees, and individual officeholders. The sums involved were substantial: estimates from the period suggest that the Capone organization spent hundreds of thousands of dollars annually on political protection, with individual ward arrangements ranging from several hundred to several thousand dollars per month.

Chicago's county government, the Cook County Sheriff's office and the State's Attorney's office, were also important institutional actors. The county sheriff had jurisdiction over the suburban municipalities where many of the organization's operations were concentrated, and the State's Attorney had prosecutorial authority over state criminal charges. Both offices were politically controlled and both, at various points during the 1920s, were effectively accommodating toward the criminal establishment, either through outright corruption or through political calculation that vigorous enforcement was electorally unwise in a city where a large fraction of the electorate was either directly employed by or a customer of the illegal liquor trade.

The depth of this political infrastructure helps explain a paradox that contemporary observers found baffling and that later historians have documented carefully: Capone was, by the mid-1920s, the most prominent criminal figure in the country, his identity and operations known to virtually every reader of the Chicago newspapers and discussed openly in city politics, and yet he operated for years with effective legal impunity. The explanation was not that law enforcement was unaware of his operations, but that the law enforcement and prosecutorial machinery through which he might have been reached was itself compromised at too many levels for a prosecution to succeed. Witnesses feared retaliation. Evidence disappeared. Juries were corrupted. Prosecutors made decisions that aligned with organizational interests. The machinery of justice, in the Chicago of the 1920s, was not simply slow or inefficient; it was, at too many of its critical nodes, not aimed at the criminal enterprise at all.

Historical Vocabulary
Omertà

Omertà is the code of silence associated with the Sicilian Mafia and, by extension, with Italian-American organized crime more broadly. As Chapter 6 examined in detail, omertà is not merely a prohibition on cooperation with law enforcement; it is a cultural and organizational code that treats the disclosure of criminal activity to any outsider, including governmental authority, as a fundamental violation of honor that disqualifies a person from legitimate membership in the community.

In the context of the Capone organization, omertà functioned alongside other forms of witness intimidation and jury corruption as part of a broader system that made prosecution difficult. Witnesses who might have testified against organizational members faced two simultaneous deterrents: the cultural prohibition on cooperation with authorities, which carried social consequences within Italian-American communities, and the very concrete threat of physical harm if they cooperated. The combination was, for most potential witnesses during the Prohibition era, effectively absolute.

It is important, however, for historians to be careful about applying the concept of omertà too broadly or too automatically. Not all members of the Capone organization were Sicilian or even Italian-American, and the silence that characterized witnesses in gang-era Chicago was also, and perhaps primarily, the result of rational calculations about personal safety. Men who had seen what happened to people who crossed the organization had strong self-interested reasons for silence that did not depend on any cultural code. The difficulty of prosecution was overdetermined: it resulted from cultural factors, organizational intimidation, political corruption, and the rational fear of potential witnesses simultaneously.

Primary Source Window

Chicago Tribune Coverage of the Gang Era, 1926–1929

Source type: Newspaper reporting and editorial commentary · Publication: Chicago Tribune · Period: 1926–1929

Context: The Chicago Tribune was the city's largest and most influential newspaper during the Prohibition era, and its coverage of the gang wars was extensive and, by the standards of the day, aggressive. Tribune reporters including John Boettiger and other staff journalists documented gang violence, investigated political corruption, and published analyses of the organizational structure of the criminal establishment. The Tribune also ran regular editorials demanding more vigorous law enforcement and prosecutorial action against the criminal organizations whose activities it was simultaneously covering as news.

Tribune coverage consistently framed the gang wars as evidence not merely of individual criminal activity but of the corruption of governmental institutions. Editorials from the period argued that the real scandal was not that criminal organizations operated in Chicago but that they operated with the active connivance of elected officials and law enforcement agencies whose failure to act reflected either corruption or political cowardice. Tribune reporters documented specific relationships between criminal organizations and political figures, naming aldermen, ward committeemen, and police commanders whose accommodation of criminal enterprise was, in the paper's view, a betrayal of public trust as serious as the crimes themselves.

Analysis: Tribune coverage of the gang era is a rich primary source that must be read critically in several respects. The paper's aggressive anti-crime stance reflected its institutional interests as a newspaper in a competitive market where sensational crime coverage sold papers, its political alignment with reform Republican factions that opposed the Thompson machine, and its genuine civic commitment to honest government that was real even if it also served commercial purposes. Tribune reporters sometimes had access to law enforcement and political sources that gave them information unavailable elsewhere; they also sometimes published claims that were unverified or that reflected the perspectives of particular political factions within the anti-crime coalition. The paper's ethnic characterization of crime, its tendency to frame organized crime as primarily an Italian or immigrant phenomenon, reflected the prejudices of its era and requires critical awareness from modern readers.

Multiple Perspectives

Prohibition-Era Chicago: Who Experienced It and How

Italian-American immigrants in South Side neighborhoods: For many residents of the Italian-American communities where the Capone organization had its roots, the organization was simultaneously a source of fear and a source of community services. Capone cultivated a personal reputation for generosity, distributing money in working-class Italian neighborhoods, paying for neighborhood events, and positioning himself as a benefactor to a community that faced significant ethnic discrimination from mainstream Chicago society. Many residents who had no direct connection to criminal operations benefited from organizational charity while also living under the implicit threat of violence that the organization's presence represented.
African American residents of the Black Belt: The relationship between the Capone organization and Chicago's Black community was neither simple exploitation nor simple partnership. Black entrepreneurs operated policy games and entertainment venues within the Black Belt that were partially autonomous from the Capone organization but also required negotiated relationships with it. Capone's organization competed with and eventually subordinated many Black criminal entrepreneurs through a combination of deal-making and coercion. At the same time, the speakeasies and jazz clubs of the South Side Black Belt were significant sites of cultural life that the bootlegging economy helped to sustain. The gang era was experienced by Black residents simultaneously as economic opportunity, cultural vitality, criminal exploitation, and a source of violence in which they were not participants but often bystanders or victims.
Reform-minded middle-class Chicagoans: For civic reformers, Protestant clergy, temperance advocates, and the business-civic community represented by organizations like the Chicago Crime Commission, the gang era was a crisis of democratic governance. They were horrified not only by the violence but by what it demonstrated: that the democratic institutions of city government had been effectively captured by criminal organizations, that elections were manipulated, that police and prosecutors were corrupted, and that the social contract of the city was being violated on a massive scale. Their response, the investigations, the public reports, the demands for federal intervention, ultimately contributed to the political context in which federal prosecution became possible.
Working-class Chicagoans who patronized speakeasies: For the hundreds of thousands of Chicago residents who drank in speakeasies, purchased bootleg liquor for home consumption, or patronized the gambling establishments and entertainment venues that the criminal economy sustained, the gang era was experienced primarily at a comfortable distance from its violence. Most customers knew abstractly that the establishments they patronized were criminal enterprises, but the transaction was straightforward: they wanted a drink, the establishment provided one, and the organizational arrangements that made this possible were someone else's problem. The widespread non-compliance with Prohibition law among ordinary citizens was the economic foundation on which the criminal empire rested, and it reflected a genuine mass rejection of the law's legitimacy that made political support for vigorous enforcement difficult to sustain.
Section 12.5

Law Enforcement Responds: Failure, Innovation, and Federal Intervention

The failure of local law enforcement to prosecute or meaningfully constrain the Capone organization during the peak years of its power is one of the most documented and analyzed episodes in American criminal justice history. Understanding that failure requires distinguishing between several different types of failure that operated simultaneously and reinforced each other: the failure of political will, the failure of institutional integrity, the failure of legal tools, and the failure of investigative capacity. Each contributed to the long period of impunity that the organization enjoyed, and each was eventually addressed, though in most cases only imperfectly and belatedly, by the combination of federal intervention and legal innovation that finally brought Capone down.

The failure of political will was, in many respects, the most fundamental. Mayor Thompson's administration made no serious pretense of vigorous Prohibition enforcement, and the ward politicians who controlled the police department's practical operations were themselves participants in the corruption network. A police superintendent who announced a serious campaign against bootlegging in Chicago during the Thompson years would have been swimming against the political current at every level of the relevant hierarchy. The few officers and commanders who did attempt vigorous enforcement found themselves isolated, transferred, or otherwise discouraged. The structural incentives, as noted in the previous section, operated powerfully against the kind of sustained institutional commitment that effective enforcement would have required.

The failure of institutional integrity was closely related but somewhat distinct. Even in a police department less thoroughly politicized than Chicago's, the combination of the organization's financial resources and its demonstrated willingness to use violence against law enforcement personnel would have made effective local policing extremely difficult. Officers who accepted organizational payments did so because the rewards were significant and the risks of refusal, in terms of both organizational retaliation and career consequences within a corrupted institution, were real. The small number of officers who refused corruption found themselves facing an environment in which witnesses feared to cooperate, evidence was unreliable, and the prosecutorial machinery was itself compromised at critical points.

The failure of legal tools was a problem at a different level. The statutes available to local and state prosecutors for addressing organized crime during the Prohibition era were designed primarily for individual criminal acts, not for the prosecution of ongoing criminal enterprises as organizations. A prosecutor might be able to charge a specific individual with a specific murder or robbery, but building a case that would reach the organization's leadership through a chain of command that the organization deliberately kept obscure was extraordinarily difficult under the legal framework then available. The legal innovations that would eventually make organized crime prosecution possible, particularly the RICO statute, were decades in the future.

It was the accumulation of these local failures that eventually drove the shift to federal prosecution. The federal government had several potential legal handles on Capone's organization that state and local authorities lacked. The Volstead Act, which implemented Prohibition at the federal level, gave federal Prohibition agents jurisdiction over liquor violations that could be pursued independently of local law enforcement. Federal tax law gave the IRS jurisdiction over income, and the Supreme Court had established, in a series of cases in the 1920s, that income derived from illegal activities was still taxable, meaning that failure to report such income was prosecutable as tax evasion. And federal prosecutors, while not immune to corruption, were structurally more insulated from the ward-level political corruption that had so thoroughly compromised Chicago's local law enforcement.

Eliot Ness and his team of "Untouchables," recruited specifically for their alleged incorruptibility, represented one federal approach. Ness, a twenty-six-year-old Prohibition Bureau agent when he organized his special squad in 1930, conducted a series of raids on Capone's breweries that destroyed significant production capacity and generated considerable press attention. Ness was a skilled self-promoter as well as a capable investigator, and the narrative of incorruptible federal agents taking on a corrupt criminal establishment captured the public imagination in ways that contributed to the political momentum for prosecution. However, historians who have examined the record carefully note that Ness's actual legal contribution to Capone's conviction was modest. The brewery violations he documented were used in a separate criminal case that was eventually folded into the tax case, but the evidence that put Capone in prison came from a different direction entirely.

The decisive work was done by IRS investigators, particularly Frank J. Wilson, a meticulous special agent who spent years tracing the cash flows of the Capone organization through a labyrinthine network of nominees, intermediaries, and informal banking arrangements. The legal theory was straightforward in principle but extraordinarily difficult in practice: if the organization's revenues were as large as everyone believed, and if Capone had paid no income taxes, then he had committed federal tax evasion on a massive scale. The evidentiary challenge was demonstrating, with documentary precision, that specific cash flows represented organizational income that had been received by Capone. This required tracing money through dozens of transactions, identifying patterns in organizational accounting, and building a case that could withstand the aggressive legal challenge that Capone's well-funded defense would mount.

Wilson and his team spent years on this work, operating in conditions of genuine physical danger given the organization's known willingness to kill people who threatened it. The investigation also benefited from a change in the political environment following the Valentine's Day Massacre: the scale of public outrage and the pressure from President Hoover made federal law enforcement resources available to the Capone investigation that had not previously been committed. By 1931, Wilson and his team had assembled a case that federal prosecutors believed could withstand trial.

Cause and Effect

From Prohibition to Criminal Empire: The Causes and Consequences of the Gang Era

Long-Term Causes

Pre-existing urban vice economy with established corruption networks; ethnic neighborhood fragmentation; immigrant community resistance to nativist prohibition moralism; structural weakness of machine-politics urban governance; limited federal law enforcement capacity; cultural ambivalence about alcohol prohibition among large segments of the urban population.

Short-Term Causes

The Eighteenth Amendment (January 1920); the Volstead Act's implementation details that created the specific market structure; the assassination of O'Banion (1924) that triggered open gang warfare; the repeated failure of territorial truces to hold; the Valentine's Day Massacre (1929) that produced the political will for federal intervention.

The Development

Emergence and consolidation of a large-scale criminal enterprise controlling bootlegging, gambling, and labor rackets across Chicago and suburbs; systematic corruption of local government and law enforcement; a decade of territorial warfare producing hundreds of murders; eventual federal prosecution using tax law.

Effects

Capone's conviction and imprisonment (1931); dispersal but not elimination of criminal organizational structure; the Outfit's continuation under new leadership; the end of Prohibition (1933) forcing criminal diversification; the template provided for later organized crime structures; the legal and law enforcement innovations that the era forced; the enduring popular mythology of the American gangster.

Historical complexity: The gang era cannot be explained by Prohibition alone, though Prohibition was necessary. It required the specific combination of urban political corruption, ethnic criminal entrepreneurship, a massive and non-compliant consumer demand, and the organizational innovations of Torrio and Capone. The effects extended far beyond the criminal world, shaping law enforcement tools, popular culture, and the politics of crime and governance for decades.

Section 12.6

The Fall of Capone: Tax Evasion, Trial, and Aftermath

The trial of Al Capone for income tax evasion opened in Chicago in October 1931 and lasted less than two weeks. By any measure, it was one of the most closely watched trials in American history up to that point. Reporters from newspapers across the country filled the press section. Capone arrived each day in expensive suits, accompanied by his attorneys, projecting the confident demeanor of a man who expected to prevail. And, indeed, there were points during the pretrial period when it appeared he might.

The legal maneuvering that preceded the trial illustrated both Capone's continued access to significant resources and the imperfect functioning of the legal system even under federal jurisdiction. Initial negotiations between Capone's attorneys and federal prosecutors had produced what appeared to be an agreement under which Capone would plead guilty to tax charges in exchange for a recommended sentence of two and a half years. Capone had reportedly begun telling associates that he had the matter settled when Judge James Wilkerson, who would preside over the trial, made clear that he was not bound by any prosecutorial sentencing recommendation and intended to assess whatever sentence he deemed appropriate. Capone withdrew the guilty plea, and the case went to trial.

The prosecution's case rested on the documentary evidence assembled by Wilson and his team over years of investigation. The core argument was that Capone had received substantial income, specifically funds traceable to his criminal organization's revenues, and had paid no income taxes on that income for the years 1925 through 1929. The challenge was establishing, through the documentary record, that specific payments had been received by Capone personally, since the organization had gone to considerable lengths to keep Capone's name off any formal records. Wilson's team had traced cash flows through gamblers who had made direct payments, through cashiers' checks purchased with organizational funds, and through testimony from a small number of former organizational associates willing to testify about the financial arrangements they had witnessed.

Capone's defense attorneys challenged the witnesses' credibility, argued that some of the income attributed to Capone had actually been received by other organizational members, and mounted technical challenges to the prosecution's accounting methodology. But the evidence was sufficient, and the jury, which Judge Wilkerson had discretely substituted on the morning of the trial to thwart a reported jury-fixing scheme, convicted Capone on five counts of tax evasion after deliberating less than nine hours.

Judge Wilkerson sentenced Capone to eleven years in federal prison, the maximum available under the charges, plus fines and court costs. It was a sentence that most observers regarded as disproportionate to the specific legal offense, but that reflected the judge's evident determination to give the conviction the weight it symbolically deserved as a statement about the limits of criminal impunity. Capone's attorneys appealed, but the conviction was upheld, and in May 1932, Capone entered the federal penitentiary in Atlanta before being transferred to the newly opened Alcatraz Island prison in San Francisco Bay in 1934.

Capone served seven and a half years before being released in 1939, his sentence having been shortened by good behavior credit. By then, however, he was not the man who had entered prison. During his imprisonment, Capone had developed the progressive neurological deterioration caused by neurosyphilis, a sexually transmitted infection that had gone untreated during the years when penicillin was not yet available. By the time of his release, he was cognitively impaired and incapable of resuming any organizational role. He retired to his Palm Island, Florida estate, where he died in January 1947 at age forty-eight.

The organizational aftermath of Capone's imprisonment was not the dissolution of the criminal enterprise but its continuation under new management. The organization that became known as the Chicago Outfit survived the loss of its most prominent leader because it was, by this point, an institutional structure rather than a personal enterprise. Frank Nitti assumed a public-facing leadership role during the early 1930s, while Tony Accardo and Paul Ricca managed the operational realities of an organization that needed to adapt to the end of Prohibition in 1933 and to the changed political environment of the New Deal era. The criminal enterprises that had made the Capone organization profitable, gambling, labor racketeering, and the general capacity to corrupt governmental institutions, continued to generate revenue. Bootlegging revenues were replaced, partially, by the expansion into other criminal markets and by greater penetration of legitimate industries through labor union control and business extortion. The Chicago Outfit that operated through the 1940s, 1950s, and 1960s was a direct institutional descendant of the organization Capone had led, demonstrating that the fall of the most prominent individual did not mean the fall of the criminal institution he had helped to build.

Historical Debate

Al Capone: Entrepreneur, Monster, or Product of His Environment?

The question: How should historians characterize Al Capone's historical significance? Was he primarily an unusually successful criminal entrepreneur whose methods happened to be illegal, a uniquely dangerous and violent figure whose personal choices made him more destructive than other criminals of his era, or principally a product of structural conditions that would have produced a similar figure had Capone himself never existed?

Interpretation A — The Entrepreneur View: Some historians and social scientists have argued that Capone's career is best understood through an economic lens. He identified a massive market opportunity created by Prohibition, organized the most efficient production and distribution system to meet that demand, developed a sophisticated regulatory compliance mechanism (corruption), and managed the violence that was the inevitable consequence of operating in a market without legal dispute resolution. In this view, Capone was essentially an entrepreneur operating under different legal constraints than his legitimate counterparts, and his success reflected genuine organizational and managerial capacities rather than simply brutality.
Interpretation B — The Violence View: Other historians emphasize that the entrepreneurial frame sanitizes what was, at its core, a reign of terror over a major American city. Capone's organization did not merely sell beer; it murdered rivals, corrupted governmental institutions, intimidated witnesses, and created conditions in which ordinary residents of neighborhoods under organizational influence lived under the implicit or explicit threat of violence. The St. Valentine's Day Massacre, and the dozens of other killings connected to the organization, cannot be abstracted into economic theory. The human cost of the criminal empire, paid primarily by those who lived in and around it, was real and specific and cannot be reduced to a rational market mechanism.
Interpretation C — The Structural View: A third interpretive frame emphasizes that Capone was, to a significant degree, a product of conditions that transcended any individual. Prohibition created the market; urban machine politics created the corruption infrastructure; ethnic discrimination created the community context in which criminal entrepreneurship was one of the few avenues of advancement available; and the specific organizational innovations of Johnny Torrio created the institutional template that Capone inherited and expanded. Had Capone not existed, the structural conditions existed to produce an equivalent figure. The focus on Capone as an individual, in this view, distracts from the more important historical question of why these conditions existed and persisted.

Evidence and its limits: Each interpretation draws on genuine evidence. The economic literature on criminal markets supports the entrepreneurial view. The testimonial and documentary record of violence and intimidation supports the violence view. The historical and sociological literature on structural conditions supports the structural view. The problem is that these are not mutually exclusive; all three are, to varying degrees, accurate. The historical challenge is to integrate them into an account that acknowledges Capone's personal agency and the real human cost of his organization's violence while situating both within the structural conditions that made him possible.

Section 12.7

Legacy and Transformation: What the Capone Era Left Behind

The age of Al Capone left multiple legacies that extended far beyond the career of the individual who gave it its name. In the history of organized crime, the Prohibition era in general and the Chicago experience in particular represented a decisive period of organizational innovation, criminal consolidation, and institutional adaptation that shaped the structures of American organized crime for decades. In the history of law enforcement, the era forced the development of new legal tools, new investigative techniques, and a new understanding of criminal organization as a phenomenon requiring institutional responses rather than individual prosecution. In the history of American culture, the era produced a mythology of the gangster that has proved extraordinarily durable and whose relationship to the historical reality is complex and often misleading. And in the history of American political life, the era raised questions about the relationship between democratic governance, criminal enterprise, and the limits of prohibition as a policy instrument that remain relevant to debates about drug policy, corruption, and organized crime today.

The organizational legacy was perhaps the most direct. The criminal consolidation that the gang wars forced, the elimination of smaller and less efficient operators by a dominant organization with the capacity to outbid, outshoot, and out-corrupt its rivals, produced organizations that were, paradoxically, more sophisticated and more durable than the fragmented criminal landscape that Prohibition had initially created. The Chicago Outfit that survived the Capone era was an institution with real organizational continuity, professional management, and geographic scope that made it substantially more difficult to disrupt than any of the ethnic criminal enterprises that had preceded it. The same consolidation dynamic operated, with local variations, in New York, where the gang wars of the late Prohibition era produced the Five Families structure examined in Chapters 13 through 16, in Detroit, in Kansas City, and in other major American cities. The age of Capone was, in effect, the crucible in which the institutional American Mafia was forged.

The law enforcement legacy was equally significant, though it took longer to manifest. The demonstration that local corruption made local prosecution essentially impossible for sufficiently powerful criminal organizations drove the federal government toward a more active role in combating organized crime that would eventually, through several decades of institutional development, produce the FBI's organized crime program of the 1950s and 1960s, the Kefauver Committee investigations, and the RICO statute of 1970. The tax evasion approach that brought down Capone became a standard tool for reaching criminal figures who could not be prosecuted directly for their criminal activities. The Untouchables narrative, even in its mythologized form, established the template of the incorruptible federal agent as a necessary counterweight to local corruption that shaped federal law enforcement culture for generations.

The cultural legacy is more problematic. The mythologization of Al Capone began while he was still active, driven partly by his own remarkable public relations skills and partly by the commercial appetites of newspapers, publishers, and, later, filmmakers who found in the Capone story the elements of a compelling popular narrative: the self-made immigrant, the ruthless businessman, the power that police could not touch, the spectacular downfall. The 1932 film Scarface, thinly disguised as fiction, was essentially a Capone biography; later treatments from the 1959 television series to the 1987 Brian De Palma film have sustained and elaborated the myth. This mythological tradition has had real consequences for historical understanding, encouraging a focus on individual personality and dramatic incident at the expense of the structural analysis that produces genuine historical insight.

Perhaps the most important legacy of the Capone era, however, is the lessons it offers for the perennial debate about prohibition as a policy instrument. The experience of Prohibition demonstrated, with a clarity that has rarely been matched in American policy history, that the prohibition of a commodity for which strong consumer demand exists does not eliminate that demand but transfers its satisfaction to illegal markets, with all the criminal, corrupt, and violent consequences that illegal market organization entails. The criminal empires of the Prohibition era were not accidents or aberrations; they were predictable consequences of the combination of massive demand, legal prohibition, and insufficient consideration of the organizational consequences. These lessons have direct relevance to later policy debates about narcotics prohibition, gambling regulation, and the broader question of how democratic societies manage the tension between moral regulation and the realities of human desire.

Constitutional Connection

The Eighteenth and Twenty-First Amendments: Constitutional Experimentation and Its Consequences

The Capone era is inseparable from the constitutional experiment represented by the Eighteenth Amendment and the Volstead Act. It is worth examining what was constitutionally unusual about Prohibition and what the experience revealed about the limits of constitutional power.

The Eighteenth Amendment was, and remains, the only amendment to the Constitution that directly prohibited specific conduct by private citizens rather than either organizing governmental power or protecting individual rights. All other amendments either establish governmental structures, limit governmental power, or protect individual freedoms. The Eighteenth Amendment did something different: it used the constitutional mechanism of amendment to impose a specific moral regulatory choice, the prohibition of alcohol, on a deeply divided population, and it did so at the federal level, imposing a national standard on communities with widely varying attitudes toward alcohol consumption.

The consequences of this constitutional choice were examined throughout Chapter 11, but the specific constitutional legacy extends to the Capone era in two important ways. First, the organizational impunity that the criminal establishment achieved was possible precisely because Prohibition was constitutionally mandated: the federal government could not simply repeal the relevant laws without a constitutional amendment, and the political coalition necessary to achieve repeal took thirteen years to assemble. The longevity of the criminal opportunity was, in other words, constitutionally guaranteed until the Twenty-First Amendment ratified in December 1933.

Second, the federal prosecution of Capone raised important constitutional questions about the scope of federal taxing and law enforcement power. The legal theory that income from illegal activities was taxable, and that failure to pay such taxes was a federal crime, represented a significant expansion of federal criminal jurisdiction. The Supreme Court rulings that supported this theory, establishing that the source of income, legal or illegal, did not affect its taxability, were important moments in the development of federal power that extended well beyond the specific context of the gang wars.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Political Corruption and Organized Crime: From Capone to the Post-Prohibition Era

What Changed After Capone

  • The end of Prohibition (1933) eliminated the primary revenue source driving gang war violence, reducing but not eliminating criminal organizational power
  • Federal law enforcement's willingness to pursue organized crime through tax and federal statutes established a new enforcement template
  • The public notoriety of the Capone era created political pressure for anti-corruption reform that produced some institutional improvements in Chicago and other cities
  • Criminal organizations adapted from bootlegging to gambling, labor racketeering, and eventually narcotics as primary revenue sources
  • The organizational structures developed during the Prohibition era survived and professionalized, becoming the Five Families in New York and the Outfit in Chicago

What Stayed the Same

  • The fundamental relationship between criminal organizations and political machines, based on mutual interest and regularized payment, persisted in Chicago and other cities through the 1940s and 1950s
  • The structural vulnerabilities of machine politics, including patronage control of police appointments and the dependence of elected officials on organized funding, continued to provide opportunities for criminal influence
  • The use of labor union control as a criminal revenue source intensified after Prohibition, becoming a central feature of organized crime's penetration of the legitimate economy
  • The core organizational structure, the hierarchical criminal enterprise with territorial control, political protection, and violence as a business tool, remained essentially intact across the transition from Prohibition to the post-Prohibition era

Why the pattern matters: The persistence of the organizational structure despite the loss of its primary revenue source, and despite the imprisonment of its most prominent leader, demonstrates that the criminal institution created during the Prohibition era had achieved genuine institutional durability. It was not Capone's personal enterprise; it was an institution that survived him and the conditions that had created him, adapting to new markets while retaining the corruption network and organizational capacity that were its most durable assets.

Everyday Life and Social History

The Speakeasy Economy: Ordinary Life in Prohibition Chicago

Behind the drama of gang wars and the mythology of Al Capone lay an enormous underground economy that touched the daily lives of hundreds of thousands of ordinary Chicagoans. Chicago in the 1920s had, by various estimates, somewhere between five thousand and twenty thousand speakeasies operating at any given time, ranging from elaborately appointed establishments in the Loop and the Gold Coast catering to the prosperous classes to simple saloon substitutes in working-class neighborhoods where customers could get a glass of beer for a nickel and ask no questions about its provenance.

The working population of this underground economy was enormous. Brewery workers who had been thrown out of legal employment by Prohibition found new jobs in the illegal breweries that the criminal organizations operated. Truck drivers delivered kegs and bottles without asking about their contents. Bartenders and waitresses worked establishments that operated openly but technically illegally. Musicians, many of them African American jazz performers from the South Side, found steady employment in the speakeasies and clubs where the drinking culture and the jazz culture of the 1920s intertwined. For many working-class Chicagoans, the bootlegging economy was not an abstract criminal phenomenon but a source of employment that paid real wages in a period when legal alternatives were limited.

Women participated in this underground economy in multiple capacities, not merely as customers but as workers and proprietors. A significant number of speakeasies were owned or managed by women, particularly in immigrant neighborhoods where female entrepreneurship in the food and hospitality trades had pre-Prohibition precedents. Women worked as waitresses, hostesses, and entertainers in establishments of varying degrees of respectability. And the women who worked in the brothels that constituted one component of the Capone organization's vice operations occupied the most vulnerable position of all: employed in illegal enterprises under the control of organizations that used violence as a management tool, with no recourse to legal protection if they were exploited or harmed.

The geography of the speakeasy economy was also socially revealing. Chicago's most fashionable speakeasies, clustered in the Near North Side and the Loop, catered to a middle and upper-middle class clientele that included businessmen, journalists, politicians, and civic leaders who simultaneously patronized illegal establishments and, in some cases, publicly supported prohibition enforcement. The coexistence of public moralism and private non-compliance among the social strata most vocally committed to prohibition was not unique to Chicago but was particularly visible there, given the scale and sophistication of the illegal trade. It was a coexistence that the criminal organizations had every interest in maintaining: the social elite's patronage provided both revenue and a measure of social legitimacy that helped normalize the illegal trade in the broader public imagination.

Chapter Summary

The age of Al Capone, spanning roughly the decade from 1920 to 1931, represents one of the most intensively documented and most consequential episodes in the history of organized crime. Beginning with the extraordinary economic opportunity created by Prohibition, the chapter has traced the development of a criminal enterprise of unprecedented scale and sophistication in Chicago, examining both the organizational genius and the institutional corruption that made it possible, as well as the violence that both defined and, ultimately, destroyed it.

The story began not with Capone but with the organizational infrastructure that Johnny Torrio built upon the pre-existing criminal networks of Chicago's South Side, and with the political and law enforcement landscape of a machine-politics city in which the systematic corruption of governmental institutions had long been a feature of urban life rather than an aberration. Prohibition did not create these conditions; it exploited them, inserting an enormous new revenue stream into a political economy already structured around the accommodation of criminal enterprise.

The gang wars of the 1920s, triggered by the assassination of North Side Gang leader Dean O'Banion in 1924 and sustained by a combination of personal antagonism, ethnic rivalry, and the enormous financial stakes of territorial control over the illegal liquor market, forced a process of violent consolidation that eliminated weaker operators and produced, paradoxically, a more organized and more institutionally sophisticated criminal enterprise than anything that had preceded it. The Valentine's Day Massacre of 1929 was both the tactical culmination of this consolidation process and the political trigger that finally mobilized federal law enforcement resources against the organization.

Capone's criminal empire at its peak was a genuinely complex organization. It operated across multiple criminal markets simultaneously, including bootlegging, gambling, prostitution, and labor racketeering. It maintained a sophisticated corruption network that compromised governmental institutions at multiple levels, from beat police to city hall. It deployed violence as a calculated business tool rather than as mere personal aggression, and it generated revenues that contemporary estimates placed in the tens of millions of dollars annually. Understanding this organization requires moving beyond the mythological figure of Capone as a personal genius of crime to see the institutional, economic, and political structures within which he operated.

The political infrastructure of the Capone organization was, in many respects, as important as its criminal operations. The systematic purchase of political protection from ward politicians, police commanders, and prosecutors transformed Chicago's governmental institutions into components of the criminal enterprise rather than its adversaries. The accommodating administrations of Mayor Big Bill Thompson, whose tolerance of the bootlegging establishment reflected both corruption and a calculated reading of his electoral coalition, provided the political umbrella under which the organization operated with near-impunity for most of the decade.

Law enforcement's belated and imperfect response illustrated both the structural limits of local policing when institutions are thoroughly corrupted and the possibilities of federal intervention when local mechanisms fail. The parallel approaches of Eliot Ness's brewery raids and Frank Wilson's tax investigation represented different federal strategies that, combined with the political pressure generated by the Valentine's Day Massacre and President Hoover's personal focus on the case, eventually produced the prosecution that brought Capone down in 1931. The method of that prosecution, tax evasion rather than the violent crimes that everyone knew had been committed, was itself a lesson about the limits of criminal prosecution and the necessity of legal creativity when the most direct paths are blocked.

The chapter has examined the Capone era from multiple perspectives, recognizing that the experience of Prohibition-era Chicago was dramatically different depending on one's social position, ethnic community, economic relationship to the illegal trade, and proximity to the violence that characterized the gang wars. For Italian-American immigrant communities, the organization was simultaneously a source of community charity and a source of fear. For African Americans in the Black Belt, the bootlegging economy offered both opportunities and exploitation. For middle-class reformers and civic leaders, the gang era was a crisis of democratic governance. For the hundreds of thousands of ordinary Chicagoans who patronized speakeasies and consumed bootleg liquor, it was largely an abstraction, the background noise of a city where the illegal and the legal had become indistinguishable.

The legacy of the Capone era extended across multiple dimensions. For the history of American organized crime, it was the formative period in which the institutional structures of the mid-century criminal organizations were created. For the history of law enforcement, it forced the development of new tools, particularly federal tax prosecution and eventually the RICO statute, that would shape the fight against organized crime for decades. For American culture, it produced an enduring mythology of the gangster whose relationship to historical reality is complex and often misleading. And for policy, it remains the most powerful demonstration in American history of the organizational consequences of prohibiting commodities for which strong consumer demand exists.

The age of Al Capone ended with his conviction in October 1931 and his slow physical deterioration during more than seven years of federal imprisonment. But the institutional structures he had helped to build, the Chicago Outfit, the corruption networks, the organizational templates that would shape the American Mafia's mid-century peak, all of these outlasted him. Understanding Capone's era means understanding not just the drama of his rise and fall but the durable institutional consequences that he left behind, consequences that the subsequent chapters of this book will trace through decades of organized crime history.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Bootlegging
The illegal production, transportation, and sale of alcohol during Prohibition. The term derives from the practice of concealing flasks in boot legs; during the 1920s it described the full supply chain of illegal liquor commerce.
Capone Myth
The culturally constructed and commercially elaborated image of Al Capone as a romantic criminal genius, propagated through journalism, film, and popular culture; historically important because it both reflects and distorts the actual history of the criminal organization he led.
Chicago Outfit
The name by which Capone's criminal organization became known, and which persisted as the designation for Chicago's dominant organized crime group long after Capone's imprisonment; represents the institutional continuity that outlasted any individual leader.
Consolidation
In the context of criminal markets, the process by which competition among multiple smaller organizations is resolved, through violence, negotiation, or both, in favor of fewer and larger organizations with clearer territorial control.
Corruption (systematic)
The organized, regularized purchase of governmental protection by criminal enterprises, distinguished from individual bribery by its institutional character: regular payments, defined relationships, and the integration of criminal and governmental interests into a functioning political economy.
Gang war
Armed conflict between criminal organizations over territorial control, market share, or personal grievances; during the Prohibition era, characterized by automobile-based ambushes and the use of military-grade weapons including Thompson submachine guns.
Labor racketeering
The criminal exploitation of trade unions for financial gain; methods include skimming union dues, selling strike insurance to employers, and steering union contracts to businesses that share revenues with the controlling criminal organization.
Machine politics
A system of urban political organization in which a hierarchical political organization, the "machine," distributes patronage jobs, contracts, and favors in exchange for electoral loyalty; structurally vulnerable to criminal penetration because of its reliance on financial resources and its hierarchical, discretionary control over governmental functions.
Omertà
The code of silence associated with Italian-American organized crime, prohibiting cooperation with law enforcement; in the context of the Capone era, operated alongside rational self-interest in the silence of potential witnesses who feared organizational retaliation.
Policy game
A numbers gambling operation in which customers bet small sums on three-digit numbers drawn daily; particularly prevalent in African American communities during the Prohibition era, functioning as a significant alternative economy and a major revenue source for criminal organizations that controlled the policy banks.
Public Enemy
A designation used by the Chicago Crime Commission beginning in 1930 to identify the most significant criminal figures in the city; Al Capone was listed first, making "Public Enemy No. 1" a phrase that entered the language and increased political pressure for federal prosecution.
Racket
A systematic, ongoing criminal scheme operated through coercion, fraud, or illegal enterprise; distinguished from one-time crimes by its organizational character and recurring revenue generation. See detailed vocabulary box in Section 12.1.
Rum Row
A term used for the offshore line of ships carrying liquor from foreign jurisdictions to within reach of American shores during Prohibition; Chicago's version of rum-running primarily used Lake Michigan smuggling routes from Canada rather than ocean routes.
Speakeasy
An establishment that illegally sold alcoholic beverages during Prohibition; ranged from elaborate nightclubs to simple back rooms; estimated at between five thousand and twenty thousand in Chicago at the height of the Prohibition era.
St. Valentine's Day Massacre
The execution of seven members of the Bugs Moran North Side Gang in a Chicago garage on February 14, 1929; strategically effective in eliminating the last major rival organization but politically catastrophic for Capone by generating national outrage and presidential attention.
Syndicate
A large-scale, organized criminal enterprise with hierarchical management, territorial control, diversified revenue sources, and a formal or informal governance structure; see detailed vocabulary box in Section 12.2.
Tax evasion
The illegal failure to pay taxes owed on income; the legal theory that income from illegal activities is taxable was established by the Supreme Court in the 1920s and applied to Capone in his 1931 prosecution, establishing an important template for federal prosecution of criminal figures who could not be reached through direct charges.
Territorial control
The claim by a criminal organization to exclusive right to conduct specific criminal operations within a defined geographic area; enforced through violence or the credible threat of violence, and negotiated through agreements like the 1926 Hotel Sherman armistice.
Thompson submachine gun
A military-derived automatic weapon capable of firing hundreds of rounds per minute; adopted by Chicago's gangs during the Prohibition era and used in multiple high-profile killings including the Valentine's Day Massacre; its use transformed individual murders into something resembling military operations.
Tribute
Regular payments made by criminal operators to the dominant organization in a territory; distinguished from purchase of goods or services in that tribute was essentially rent for the right to operate without interference from the dominant organization or from law enforcement under its protection.
Untouchables
The popular designation for the team of Prohibition Bureau agents assembled by Eliot Ness to investigate Capone's breweries; named for their alleged incorruptibility; historically important both for their actual investigative work and for the narrative of federal integrity they represented in contrast to corrupted local law enforcement.
Violence as business strategy
The analytical framework that treats the use of violence by criminal organizations not as mere pathology but as a rational (if criminal) tool for market control: eliminating rivals, enforcing contracts, disciplining employees, and signaling credible threat to potential defectors or competitors.
Volstead Act
The federal legislation implementing the Eighteenth Amendment's prohibition on intoxicating liquors; its specific provisions, including definitions of prohibited beverages and enforcement mechanisms, shaped the structure of the illegal market that Capone's organization exploited.
Ward boss
A local political figure controlling a specific ward's political organization; in Chicago's machine politics system, ward bosses had significant control over police assignments and local governmental functions, making them critical participants in the criminal protection network.

Major People and Places

Identification & Significance

Al Capone (1899–1947)
Brooklyn-born Italian-American gangster who led Chicago's dominant criminal organization from 1925 to 1931; his organization generated revenues estimated at tens of millions of dollars annually; convicted of income tax evasion in 1931 and sentenced to eleven years; died of complications from neurosyphilis in 1947. Historically significant both for his organizational accomplishments and for the enduring mythology his career generated.
Johnny Torrio (1882–1957)
The organizational architect of what became the Capone empire; built the bootlegging network and corruption infrastructure that Capone inherited; retired after being shot in 1925; later played a role in New York organized crime reorganization. Arguably the most important single figure in the creation of the criminal structure that Capone led.
Dean O'Banion (1892–1924)
North Side Gang leader whose assassination in 1924 triggered the gang wars; historically significant as the inflection point between the relatively peaceful early Prohibition period and the warfare that defined the middle 1920s.
Hymie Weiss (1898–1926)
Succeeded O'Banion as North Side Gang leader; organized the most serious military challenges to Capone, including the Cicero motorcade attack of 1926; his assassination in October 1926 outside Holy Name Cathedral temporarily ended open warfare.
Bugs Moran (1891–1957)
Final significant North Side Gang leader; his men were the targets of the Valentine's Day Massacre; survived by accident; his organization's effective destruction in 1929 ended the last significant challenge to Capone's supremacy.
Jake Guzik
Financial officer and political fixer for the Capone organization; a Jewish-American associate who managed many of the organization's political relationships and financial arrangements; his role illustrates that the Capone organization, despite its Italian-American leadership, was ethnically diverse in important respects.
Eliot Ness (1903–1957)
Prohibition Bureau agent who led the "Untouchables" team targeting Capone's breweries; historically significant both for his actual investigative work and for the popular mythology that grew around his team; his role in Capone's actual prosecution was more modest than popular accounts suggest.
Frank J. Wilson
IRS special agent whose patient financial investigation built the tax evasion case that convicted Capone; whose work, less dramatic and less publicized than Ness's, was ultimately more decisive; represents the investigative innovation of using financial evidence to reach criminal figures insulated from direct prosecution.
Big Bill Thompson (1869–1944)
Chicago mayor (1915–1923, 1927–1931) whose administrations provided political protection for the bootlegging establishment; his tolerance of criminal enterprise reflected both corruption and political calculation; his defeat by Anton Cermak in 1931 changed the political environment for the prosecution.
Cicero, Illinois
Suburban municipality adjacent to Chicago where Capone effectively captured local government in the early 1920s; historically significant as an example of criminal organizations operating through governmental capture rather than simply evading governmental authority.
The Four Deuces
Capone's early Chicago headquarters at 2222 South Wabash, combining a saloon, gambling house, and brothel; representative of the integrated criminal enterprises that characterized the organization.
The Lexington Hotel
Capone's later headquarters on South Michigan Avenue, where he occupied multiple floors and conducted business from lavish suites; representative of the organizational ambition to project legitimate prosperity alongside criminal power.
Chicago Crime Commission
Business-civic organization that monitored and publicized criminal activity; its 1930 "Public Enemies" list, naming Capone first, was both a civic advocacy tool and a cultural moment that crystallized public demand for action.

Chapter Reflection

The age of Al Capone matters in the larger history of organized crime not primarily because of Capone himself, though he was a genuinely consequential individual, but because of what the era reveals about the conditions under which criminal organizations achieve institutional power. The Chicago of the 1920s was not an anomaly. It was an extreme example of conditions, the interaction of prohibition, political corruption, ethnic community networks, weak governmental institutions, and massive consumer demand for illegal goods, that have produced powerful criminal organizations in many historical contexts. The specific organizational innovations of Torrio and Capone, the large-scale bootlegging network, the systematic corruption architecture, the use of calculated violence as a market-control mechanism, were variations on patterns that, as earlier chapters have shown, appear wherever similar structural conditions exist.

The chapter also reveals the central importance of institutional capacity in the contest between criminal organizations and governmental power. The decade-long impunity that the Capone organization enjoyed was not the product of any fundamental inability of the state to combat organized crime; it was the product of specific institutional failures: the corruption of local political and law enforcement institutions, the inadequacy of the legal tools available to prosecutors, and the absence of the political will necessary to mobilize federal resources. When those conditions changed, when the political pressure became sufficient, when the right combination of legal tools and investigative capacity was assembled, the prosecution became possible. This pattern, in which criminal organizations can be formidably durable as long as institutional capacity and political will are insufficient but vulnerable to sustained institutional pressure when those conditions change, recurs throughout the history of organized crime.

The cultural legacy of the Capone era raises questions that are worth carrying forward through the rest of this textbook. The extraordinary richness of the Capone mythology, the films, the television series, the endless journalistic accounts, the museum exhibits, reflects something genuine about the cultural fascination that organized crime generates. But it also reflects a pattern of selective attention that systematically foregrounds the dramatic and the sensational at the expense of the structural and the analytical. The most important things about the Capone era are not the machine guns and the murders, vivid as those are, but the corruption networks, the political economy of illegal markets, and the institutional innovations that shaped organized crime for decades. These are less cinematically compelling than the Valentine's Day Massacre, but they are historically more significant.

As the textbook moves forward, from the age of Capone to the reorganization of American organized crime under Lucky Luciano, to the global criminal organizations examined in later parts, to the contemporary challenges of cybercrime and transnational criminal networks, the structural lessons of this chapter remain foundational. Criminal organizations are not simply collections of bad people doing bad things; they are institutional responses to specific market conditions, political vulnerabilities, and demand realities that exist in every modern society. Understanding them historically means understanding the conditions that create them, the organizational structures through which they operate, the relationships they develop with governmental institutions, and the institutional responses that have proved most effective in constraining them. The age of Capone provided, at enormous human cost, some of the most important historical evidence for all of these questions.

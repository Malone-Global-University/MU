Part V — The American Mafia
13

Lucky Luciano and the New Mafia

Organized Crime: A Global History

Chapter Opening

"In one week in September 1931, the map of American organized crime was redrawn. The old bosses were gone. A new architecture of criminal power, built on rationality, profit, and cooperation rather than personal vendettas and feudal loyalty, took their place."

In the autumn of 1931, a young man named Salvatore Lucania sat at the center of a transformation that would define organized crime in America for generations. He was known by then as Charlie Lucky, or simply Lucky Luciano, a name earned, so the story went, from surviving a savage beating and throat-slashing in 1929 that was meant to kill him. He had grown up on the streets of lower Manhattan, one of tens of thousands of Sicilian immigrants crowded into the tenements of the Lower East Side. By 1931, at the age of thirty-four, he had already spent years navigating the violent, fractious world of New York's Italian criminal underworld. What he was about to do, however, was not simply eliminate his rivals. He was about to reshape the entire institution.

The years between 1929 and 1932 marked one of the most consequential turning points in the history of organized crime in the United States. Before this period, Italian American criminal organizations, loosely descended from Sicilian mafia traditions, were divided, violent, and organized around the personal authority of individual bosses who commanded loyalty through fear, family ties, and old-world codes of honor. Power was personal, disputes were settled with bullets, and the idea of systematic cooperation among criminal enterprises was largely unrealized. The Prohibition era had created enormous wealth for those who could supply illegal alcohol, but it had also produced constant warfare as rival factions competed for territory, supply routes, and political protection. The decade of the 1920s was soaked in blood.

What Luciano and a small circle of ambitious younger criminals built after 1931 was something qualitatively different: a rationalized, bureaucratic criminal enterprise modeled not on the feudal loyalties of the old Sicilian mafia but on the organizational structures of American business. The Commission, the governing body that Luciano and his allies created, established clear territorial boundaries, dispute resolution mechanisms, and rules of conduct among the major Italian American crime families of the Northeast. The National Syndicate extended this cooperative logic further, building alliances between Italian American organizations and Jewish, Irish, and other ethnic criminal enterprises across the country. Organized crime, in this new configuration, functioned less like a medieval kingdom and more like a corporation, or more precisely, like a cartel that managed competition and enforced agreements among competing enterprises.

Understanding this transformation requires understanding several layers of history simultaneously. It requires understanding the social world of Italian immigration to the United States, the communities that formed in the teeming immigrant neighborhoods of New York, Chicago, and other industrial cities, and the specific traditions, kinship structures, and codes of conduct that immigrants brought with them from southern Italy and Sicily. It requires understanding the economic revolution that Prohibition created, suddenly making the manufacture and sale of alcohol one of the most lucrative illegal industries in American history and providing criminal entrepreneurs with the capital and organizational experience they needed to build durable criminal enterprises. It requires understanding the specific political economy of New York City in the 1920s and 1930s, where machine politics, police corruption, and the informal economy of immigrant neighborhoods created the conditions in which organized crime could thrive.

It also requires understanding Luciano himself as a historical actor: not a mythological figure of popular culture, not the romantic gangster of Hollywood film, but a specific person shaped by specific circumstances who made specific choices that had specific consequences. Luciano was intelligent, pragmatic, and ruthless. He was also a product of his time and place, a man who understood both the traditions he had inherited and the American context in which he operated. His genius, such as it was, lay in his ability to synthesize these two worlds: to take the organizational forms of the Sicilian mafia tradition and adapt them to the rational, profit-maximizing logic of American capitalism.

The story of Lucky Luciano and the new mafia is not simply a story about crime. It is a story about immigration, assimilation, and the complex ways in which communities excluded from legitimate pathways to economic advancement created alternative structures of power and opportunity. It is a story about the relationship between crime and politics, between illegal economies and the formal institutions of the state. It is a story about the paradox at the heart of American organized crime: that some of the most sophisticated organizational innovations in American criminal history were produced by men who were, in many respects, deeply American, shaped by American capitalism, American political culture, and American ambition, even as they operated systematically outside the law.

This chapter traces the rise of Lucky Luciano and the transformation of organized crime in America during the critical years of the early 1930s. It examines the violent conflict known as the Castellammarese War that preceded the reorganization, the murders that cleared the way for the new order, the institutions that Luciano and his allies built, and the criminal enterprises that sustained the new mafia's power. It also examines the forces that eventually brought Luciano down, from Thomas Dewey's relentless prosecution to the wartime politics that produced his deportation to Italy. And it asks the deeper question that organized crime always poses: what does the existence and persistence of criminal enterprises tell us about the societies that produce them?

Historical Context

To understand the transformation of American organized crime in the early 1930s, students must first understand the world in which it occurred. The United States in the decade between 1920 and 1930 was a society undergoing profound and disorienting change. Mass immigration from southern and eastern Europe, which had poured millions of people into American cities over the previous three decades, had created enormous urban communities that were simultaneously part of American society and excluded from many of its mainstream institutions. Italian immigrants, particularly those from Sicily and the Mezzogiorno, the impoverished southern regions of Italy, faced discrimination in employment, housing, and civic life. They lived in dense, ethnically homogeneous neighborhoods, spoke languages other than English, attended Catholic churches that were viewed with suspicion by Protestant America, and occupied the lowest rungs of the urban economic ladder.

Within these communities, networks of mutual aid, informal credit, and self-governance developed alongside the legitimate economy. Some of these networks shaded into criminality. The Black Hand extortion rackets of the early twentieth century, in which criminals sent threatening letters to fellow immigrants demanding money under threat of violence, were an early expression of organized predation within the immigrant community. In Sicily, the mafia tradition had developed over centuries as a system of private governance that filled the vacuum left by weak state institutions, providing protection, dispute resolution, and economic regulation through violence and the threat of violence. Immigrants from mafia-connected regions brought these traditions with them, and in the specific conditions of American immigrant neighborhoods, they found fertile ground.

The passage of the Eighteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution in 1919 and the subsequent Volstead Act, which prohibited the manufacture, sale, and transportation of intoxicating liquors, transformed the conditions for criminal enterprise more profoundly than any other single event in American history. Prohibition did not eliminate the demand for alcohol. It simply transferred the supply from legal to illegal channels. The result was an explosion of opportunity for criminal entrepreneurs willing to manufacture, transport, and distribute illegal liquor. The scale of the profits was staggering. In a country where the average industrial worker earned perhaps a thousand dollars a year, a successful bootlegging operation could generate millions of dollars annually. Criminal organizations that had previously operated small-scale extortion and gambling operations suddenly found themselves managing enterprises comparable in scale and complexity to legitimate industrial corporations.

This explosion of criminal wealth had several critical consequences. First, it provided the capital for criminal organizations to buy political protection on a massive scale. Police officers, judges, prosecutors, and elected officials across the country were corrupted by bootlegging money, making effective law enforcement essentially impossible in many jurisdictions. Second, it created intense competition among criminal organizations for control of the lucrative bootlegging trade, producing waves of violence as rival gangs fought for territory, supply routes, and political alliances. Third, and perhaps most importantly for the story of this chapter, it forced criminal organizations to develop more sophisticated organizational structures to manage enterprises that had become far more complex than the simple extortion and gambling operations of the pre-Prohibition era.

By the late 1920s, the Italian American criminal underworld of New York City was dominated by two overlapping conflicts. One was the general competition among bootlegging gangs for market share. The other was a more specific internal conflict within the Sicilian mafia community between two factions: the established bosses, who had risen to power in the decade before Prohibition and who maintained the old Sicilian traditions of personal loyalty, vendetta, and hierarchical authority, and a younger generation of American-born or American-raised criminals who were impatient with the old ways, more willing to cooperate across ethnic lines, and more focused on profit than on tradition. It was out of this conflict that the Castellammarese War emerged, and it was out of the Castellammarese War that the new mafia was born.

Historical Vocabulary
Syndicate

A syndicate is a combination of individuals or organizations formed to pursue a common interest, typically in business or finance. In the context of organized crime, the term refers to a cooperative association of criminal enterprises that coordinate activities, share resources, manage competition, and present a unified front to both law enforcement and the public. The National Syndicate created in the early 1930s was a genuine innovation in American criminal organization, applying the corporate logic of combination and cooperation to illegal enterprise in ways that made criminal organizations more durable, more profitable, and harder to dismantle.

The term gained currency in American journalism during the 1930s and 1940s as reporters and law enforcement officials tried to describe the seemingly unified character of organized crime across the country. Whether the National Syndicate functioned as a genuinely centralized organization or as a looser network of relationships coordinated through shared interests and periodic meetings has been debated by historians and criminologists, but the reality of extensive cooperation among criminal enterprises across the country is well documented.

Section 13.1

The World Luciano Inherited

Salvatore Lucania arrived in New York City in 1906, nine years old, part of the great wave of Italian immigration that brought more than four million people from Italy to the United States between 1880 and 1920. His family settled in the Lower East Side of Manhattan, one of the most densely populated urban environments in the world, where Italian, Jewish, Irish, and other immigrant communities lived in close proximity, competed for jobs and resources, and developed the informal networks of mutual assistance and self-governance that immigrant communities everywhere create when formal institutions fail to serve them.

The Lower East Side was also a nursery of criminal talent. The conditions that produced criminal enterprise were not mysterious or difficult to explain: poverty, discrimination, limited access to legitimate employment, the presence of established illegal markets, and the partial absence of effective law enforcement within immigrant communities combined to make criminal careers both available and, for some young men, attractive. Luciano was not unique in making this choice. The streets of the Lower East Side produced dozens of the men who would shape American organized crime for the next half-century, including Meyer Lansky and Bugsy Siegel, who became Luciano's closest allies and who introduced the cross-ethnic dimension that would be one of the defining features of the National Syndicate.

As a teenager and young man, Luciano moved through a succession of criminal activities typical of the time and place. He ran errands for local criminal figures, sold drugs, participated in street crime, and eventually came to the attention of the older Italian criminal networks that were beginning to organize more systematically in the years around the First World War. By the early 1920s, he had established himself as a capable and ruthless criminal entrepreneur in the bootlegging trade, working initially under the auspices of Giuseppe Masseria, known as Joe the Boss, one of the dominant figures of the old Sicilian mafia in New York.

The old Sicilian bosses, sometimes called the Mustache Petes by the younger generation, represented a form of criminal organization deeply rooted in Sicilian traditions. They placed enormous emphasis on personal loyalty, on the authority of established bosses, on the maintenance of strict ethnic boundaries in criminal association, and on the use of violence to enforce their authority and settle disputes. Their organizational logic was, in many respects, feudal: power flowed down from a single boss whose authority was personal and absolute, and who derived legitimacy partly from his claimed connection to the mafia traditions of the old country. These men were successful within a certain context, but the American environment tested their model in ways it was not always equipped to handle.

The specific problem with the Mustache Pete model in American conditions was threefold. First, it relied on personal authority in a social environment where that authority was constantly being challenged. Prohibition had created vast new opportunities, but it had also created new entrepreneurs, new competitors, and new alliances that did not fit neatly into the old hierarchical structures. Second, it insisted on ethnic exclusivity in a world where criminal opportunity and criminal cooperation increasingly crossed ethnic lines. The Prohibition-era underworld was a genuinely multiethnic world, with Jewish bootleggers, Irish criminals, and Italian organizations all operating in the same cities and sometimes the same neighborhoods. A model that insisted on ethnic purity in criminal association was leaving money and alliances on the table. Third, and most fatally, the old model resolved disputes through violence in a way that was self-defeating: the constant warfare of the 1920s was not only bloody but expensive, consuming resources and disrupting the profitable operations that everyone depended on.

Luciano understood all of this with unusual clarity. His years in the bootlegging trade had given him practical experience of what worked and what did not. His alliance with Lansky and Siegel had demonstrated the value of cross-ethnic cooperation. His impatience with the old bosses' insistence on personal fealty and ethnic exclusivity had grown throughout the 1920s. What he needed was an opportunity, and the Castellammarese War provided it.

The Economics of the Bootlegging Era

To understand why the Luciano reorganization was possible, it is essential to understand the economic transformation that Prohibition had wrought. Before 1920, Italian American criminal organizations in New York operated primarily through extortion, gambling, and loan sharking within the immigrant community. These were profitable enterprises by the standards of the time, but they were limited in scale by the size and wealth of the communities they preyed upon. The income from a successful numbers operation in East Harlem or a string of gambling establishments in Little Italy was substantial, but it was not the kind of money that allowed criminal organizations to purchase political protection on a large scale, invest in legitimate businesses, or expand beyond their neighborhood base.

Prohibition changed the scale of everything. The demand for alcohol was enormous and national, encompassing not just immigrant communities but middle-class Americans across the country who had no intention of changing their drinking habits because of a constitutional amendment they had not voted for. The supply, suddenly illegal, commanded enormous price premiums. The organizational challenges of manufacturing, transporting, storing, and distributing millions of gallons of illegal spirits required sophisticated logistics, reliable supply chains, and extensive distribution networks. And the political protection needed to operate safely required the kind of sustained corruption that only large-scale bribery could produce.

Criminal organizations that met these challenges became wealthy beyond anything previously imaginable in the American underworld. Al Capone's Chicago organization generated an estimated sixty million dollars a year in the late 1920s, a figure that dwarfs the gross domestic product of many small nations at the time. New York's bootlegging organizations were comparably profitable. This money did not simply enrich individual criminals. It created the capital base for investment in legitimate businesses, in real estate, in political campaigns, and in the further development of criminal enterprises. It also created the organizational experience, the management skills, and the institutional knowledge that would persist long after Prohibition ended.

When Prohibition was finally repealed in 1933, many of the most thoughtful criminal entrepreneurs had already seen it coming and had been diversifying their operations. The end of legal prohibition removed the most lucrative single revenue source, but by then the organizations that Luciano and his allies had built were already deeply embedded in gambling, labor racketeering, narcotics, and the institutional corruption of urban politics. Prohibition had been the incubator; what survived it was a more durable and more diversified criminal enterprise.

Section 13.2

The Castellammarese War

The conflict that historians call the Castellammarese War was not simply a gang war in the conventional sense. It was a crisis of succession and organizational authority within the Italian American criminal underworld of New York City, a conflict that ultimately forced a reckoning with the fundamental question of how criminal organizations in the American environment should be governed. Its name derived from the fact that one of the principal factions drew many of its members from immigrants who had come from Castellammare del Golfo, a small town on the northwestern coast of Sicily that had produced a remarkable number of men who would become significant figures in American organized crime.

The war began, in its immediate sense, as a conflict between two major bosses: Giuseppe Masseria, known as Joe the Boss, who controlled much of the Italian American underworld in New York and who insisted that all Italian criminals in the city acknowledge his supremacy, and Salvatore Maranzano, a relative newcomer who had arrived from Sicily in the late 1920s and who represented the Castellammarese faction that refused to accept Masseria's authority. Both men embodied, in different ways, the old Sicilian model of criminal organization: hierarchical, personal, based on absolute loyalty to the boss, and oriented toward the enforcement of authority through violence.

The war erupted in full force in 1930 and lasted through most of 1931. It was fought through a series of murders, ambushes, and retaliatory killings that consumed many of the most capable criminal figures in New York. The violence was not random, but it was expensive in every sense. Men who might have been productively employed in running gambling operations or bootlegging enterprises were instead spending their time looking over their shoulders, planning attacks, and reacting to losses. The bootlegging operations themselves were disrupted as partnerships broke down and territorial conflicts escalated. The constant violence also attracted unwelcome attention from law enforcement and the press, making it harder to maintain the political protection that criminal organizations depended on.

Luciano occupied an ambiguous position during the war. He was nominally Masseria's lieutenant, one of the boss's most capable and trusted subordinates. But Luciano had maintained his relationships with a wider network of criminal entrepreneurs, including Lansky, Siegel, and a range of figures on both sides of the conflict, and he was increasingly convinced that the war itself was an obstacle to the kind of rational, profitable criminal enterprise he envisioned. The question was how to end it without simply backing one old-guard boss against another and ending up with the same structural problems he was trying to escape.

The solution Luciano arrived at was characteristically ruthless and decisive. He would not simply support one side in the war. He would end both sides. In April 1931, at a restaurant in Coney Island, Luciano excused himself from a meal with Masseria and allowed a team of gunmen, reportedly including Vito Genovese, Albert Anastasia, Bugsy Siegel, and Joe Adonis, to enter and kill the boss. With Masseria dead, Luciano made peace with Maranzano and allowed the Castellammarese faction's leader to declare victory. But the peace was a prelude to another betrayal. Maranzano, believing he had won the war, installed himself as capo di tutti capi and began restructuring the New York criminal world in ways that Luciano and his allies found no more acceptable than Masseria's model. Maranzano, it became clear, intended to impose his own hierarchical authority as absolutely as Masseria had attempted to do. He was also reportedly planning to have Luciano and several of his allies murdered to consolidate his power.

What Luciano and his circle did in response became one of the defining moments in the history of American organized crime.

Section 13.3

The Night of the Sicilian Vespers

On September 10, 1931, a group of men posing as government agents or collectors arrived at the Manhattan office of Salvatore Maranzano. They disarmed Maranzano's bodyguards, entered his private office, and stabbed and shot him to death. The killing was swift and professional. Maranzano, who had held the title of boss of all bosses for barely five months, was dead before the afternoon was out.

The killing of Maranzano was, by itself, not unprecedented. Mafia bosses had been killed before, and their deaths had typically been followed by a period of violent succession struggles. What was different about September 1931 was what happened simultaneously across multiple cities. In the hours and days surrounding Maranzano's death, a coordinated series of murders eliminated many of the old-guard Sicilian bosses and their key lieutenants who were most closely associated with the old model of personal authority, ethnic exclusivity, and hierarchical control. The event became known, somewhat dramatically, as the Night of the Sicilian Vespers, a reference to a thirteenth-century massacre in Sicily, and it passed into organized crime mythology as a moment when the old world was swept away overnight and a new one installed in its place.

The historical reality is somewhat more complex and contested than the mythology suggests. The precise number of men killed in the coordinated murders has been disputed by historians, with some accounts suggesting dozens of simultaneous killings across the country and more sober assessments suggesting a smaller number, concentrated in New York. The extent to which the purge was truly coordinated across multiple cities, as opposed to a smaller and more concentrated set of New York killings that was subsequently amplified in popular accounts, remains a subject of scholarly debate. What is not in dispute is the significance of the result: in a very short period of time, the men who had most firmly represented the old-guard model of Sicilian mafia authority in the United States were gone, and Luciano and his allies stood ready to build something new in their place.

The Vespers, real or partly mythologized, served an important function even in its mythologized form. It communicated, to criminal entrepreneurs across the country, that the old order had ended and that those who wished to survive needed to adapt to the new one. The message was not only about who held power but about what kind of power it was. Luciano was not simply installing himself as the new boss of all bosses in Maranzano's place. He was specifically refusing that title, which was one of the most significant signals of what was to come. The elimination of the boss of all bosses concept was not a minor organizational detail. It was a fundamental statement about the new model: that power would be shared, that decisions would be made collectively, and that the personal authority of any single man would be constrained by the institutional structures of the Commission.

Historical Vocabulary
Omertà

Omertà is the Sicilian code of silence, a central element of mafia culture that forbids members from cooperating with law enforcement or revealing the secrets of the organization. The term derives from the Sicilian word for humility or manliness, suggesting that the true man of honor handles his affairs without recourse to the state or its institutions. In the context of the American mafia, omertà functioned as both a cultural norm and an enforced rule: those who violated it risked death.

The code of omertà was one of the most important structural features of mafia organization, both in Sicily and in the United States, because it created a significant barrier to law enforcement investigation. Without informants willing to testify, building criminal cases against organized crime figures was extraordinarily difficult. Federal law enforcement would not achieve major successes against the American mafia until the 1960s and 1970s, when the development of informant programs, electronic surveillance, and the RICO statute began to erode the wall of silence that omertà maintained. The eventual breakdown of omertà, as prosecutors offered protection to informants and as lifetime criminal sentences made cooperation with the government increasingly attractive, was one of the most important factors in the later decline of the American mafia.

Section 13.4

Building the Commission

The Commission that Luciano and his allies created in the months following September 1931 was, in organizational terms, one of the most significant innovations in the history of criminal enterprise. To understand why, it is necessary to understand what problem it was solving. The fundamental problem of large-scale criminal enterprise is the problem of governance without legitimate authority. Businesses in the legal economy can use contracts, courts, regulatory agencies, and the threat of civil lawsuit to enforce agreements, resolve disputes, and manage competition. Criminal enterprises cannot use any of these mechanisms. They must develop their own mechanisms for doing the same things, and they must enforce those mechanisms through the only instrument they have: the threat and use of violence.

The old model, which concentrated authority in the person of a single boss and enforced compliance through personal loyalty and the immediate threat of violence, was adequate for small organizations in stable environments. It became dysfunctional when organizations grew large, when the environment became turbulent, and when multiple large organizations needed to coexist and cooperate without constant resort to warfare. The Castellammarese War had demonstrated the costs of the old model at full scale: months of bloodshed, disrupted operations, and damage to the political protection that everyone depended on.

The Commission addressed these problems by creating an institutional mechanism for collective governance among the major Italian American crime families of the Northeast. Its founding members represented the five major New York families, together with representatives of the major organizations in Chicago, Buffalo, and other cities. Its fundamental purposes were to define and maintain territorial boundaries among the families, to provide a forum for resolving disputes without resort to warfare, and to approve major decisions, including the killing of high-ranking members, that affected the collective interests of the organization.

The structure of the Commission reflected a genuine attempt to create stable governance in an inherently unstable environment. By requiring that killings of bosses and senior figures be approved by the Commission as a whole, Luciano was attempting to prevent the kind of unilateral violence that had destabilized the previous decade. By establishing clear territorial boundaries, he was attempting to reduce the competitive conflicts that had driven much of the warfare of the 1920s. By creating a forum for negotiation and arbitration, he was attempting to provide an alternative to violence for the resolution of disputes.

The internal structure of each individual family also became more formalized in this period. Beneath the boss, or capo, was an underboss, or sottocapo, who served as the boss's deputy and day-to-day operational manager. A consigliere, or counselor, served as an advisor to the boss and a mediator within the family. Below them were the capos, captains who commanded crews of soldiers, the working-level members who ran the actual criminal enterprises. This hierarchical structure was not entirely new, but its formalization and its connection to the Commission structure created something that more closely resembled a bureaucratic organization than anything that had existed in the American underworld before.

The Commission was not a perfectly functioning institution, and it never became one. There were violations of its rules, unilateral murders that had not been approved, disputes that escalated into violence before they could be resolved through negotiation. But as a mechanism for reducing the endemic warfare of the Prohibition era and for maintaining a degree of order among powerful criminal organizations with competing interests, it was remarkably effective. The American mafia would continue to operate under the Commission structure, with various modifications, for decades. Its eventual exposure to law enforcement scrutiny and prosecution in the 1980s, through the historic Commission Case of 1985 to 1986, was itself testimony to the durability of the institution Luciano and his allies had created.

Historical Vocabulary
The Commission

The Commission was the governing body of the American mafia created after the reorganization of 1931. It consisted of representatives of the major Italian American crime families and served as an arbitration forum, a territorial authority, and a collective decision-making body for major issues affecting organized crime across the country. Its creation represented the most significant organizational innovation in the history of American organized crime, replacing the personal authority of a single boss of all bosses with a collective governance structure that distributed power among multiple organizations.

Historians have debated how consistently the Commission actually functioned and how much real authority it exercised over member families at various points in its history. The evidence suggests that it was more effective in some periods than others and that its authority was respected when the shared interests of its members in avoiding destructive warfare outweighed individual interests in unilateral action. Its existence was not confirmed publicly until a series of law enforcement investigations in the 1950s and 1960s, including the famous Apalachin Meeting of 1957 and the testimony of Joe Valachi before the Senate Permanent Subcommittee on Investigations in 1963.

Section 13.5

The National Syndicate

If the Commission was the governing institution of the Italian American mafia specifically, the National Syndicate was the broader cooperative network that extended beyond Italian American organizations to encompass criminal enterprises of other ethnic backgrounds. The distinction between the two is important. The Commission was an Italian American institution, governed by Italian American rules and rooted in Sicilian mafia traditions adapted to the American environment. The Syndicate was something broader: a network of cooperative relationships among criminal entrepreneurs of multiple ethnic backgrounds who recognized that their mutual interests in profit and stability were better served by cooperation than by competition.

The key figures in the Syndicate's cross-ethnic dimension were Meyer Lansky and Bugsy Siegel, Luciano's closest allies since their teenage years on the Lower East Side. Lansky in particular became one of the most significant figures in the financial management and expansion of organized crime in America, precisely because he operated across ethnic lines with a facility that few criminal figures of his era could match. He was Jewish, which meant that he was excluded from the Commission itself, but his relationship with Luciano gave him access to Italian American criminal networks that would not otherwise have been available to him, while his own network of contacts extended to Jewish criminal organizations in cities across the country.

The Syndicate's organizational logic was essentially that of a business cartel: it defined territories, managed competition, coordinated operations that crossed territorial boundaries, and provided shared services, including enforcement and money management, to member organizations. It met periodically at gatherings of criminal leaders from across the country, events that became known to law enforcement as national crime conventions, and which were documented most dramatically when a meeting in Apalachin, New York, in 1957 was accidentally discovered by a state police sergeant, leading to the exposure of more than sixty organized crime figures from across the country.

The Syndicate also extended organized crime's reach into new geographic areas. Bugsy Siegel's move to California in the late 1930s, nominally to oversee the Syndicate's interests on the West Coast, was part of a broader expansion of organized crime beyond its traditional base in the northeastern industrial cities. When Siegel became involved in the development of the Flamingo Hotel in Las Vegas in the mid-1940s, he was, in effect, pioneering a new geographic and economic frontier for organized crime: the legalized gambling resort, which offered criminal organizations a mechanism for generating enormous profits in a legally ambiguous environment where the line between legitimate and illegitimate business was blurred in ways that suited their purposes.

The National Syndicate also managed the organized crime community's relationship with the labor movement, a relationship that would prove to be one of the most lucrative and enduring sources of criminal income in the mid-twentieth century. Labor racketeering, the control and exploitation of labor unions for criminal profit, was not invented by Luciano and his allies, but it was systematized and expanded under the Syndicate's framework in ways that would shape the labor movement and urban politics in America for decades. The infiltration of unions in industries including construction, trucking, the waterfront, garbage collection, and the garment industry gave organized crime leverage over economic activity far beyond the traditional criminal markets of gambling, prostitution, and bootlegging.

The geographic reach and organizational sophistication of the National Syndicate represented something genuinely new in the history of American crime. Previous criminal organizations had been local or regional, dependent on the specific social conditions of particular cities or neighborhoods. The Syndicate was national, or at least trans-regional, and its member organizations were connected by financial relationships, communications, and shared interests that transcended local boundaries. Understanding this national dimension is essential to understanding both the power of the Syndicate and the difficulty that law enforcement faced in combating it: an organization that was truly national required a national law enforcement response, and American law enforcement in the 1930s and 1940s was primarily organized at the local and state levels.

Geography Study

The Geographic Reach of the National Syndicate

Students studying the National Syndicate should understand the geographic dimension of the organization's expansion. The original base of American organized crime was concentrated in the northeastern industrial cities, particularly New York, Chicago, and Philadelphia, where large Italian, Jewish, and Irish immigrant communities had established the social networks and political relationships that organized crime depended on. But by the late 1930s, the Syndicate's reach extended far beyond these original centers.

  • New York City: the central node of the Syndicate, home to the five major Italian American crime families and the seat of the Commission
  • Chicago: the second major center, home to the Chicago Outfit, which maintained a semi-independent but cooperative relationship with the New York-based Commission
  • New Orleans: an important node with its own distinctive organized crime tradition, connected to the national network through gambling and narcotics
  • Kansas City, Cleveland, and Detroit: regional centers with their own organized crime families connected to the Syndicate
  • Miami and Havana: emerging gambling and resort centers where organized crime money flowed in the postwar period
  • Las Vegas: the most dramatic new frontier, where Bugsy Siegel's Flamingo Hotel pioneered the organized crime gambling resort model
  • Los Angeles: the West Coast expansion, initially overseen by Siegel, connecting the Syndicate to Hollywood and the entertainment industry

Section 13.6

Luciano's Criminal Empire

The criminal enterprises that sustained Luciano's organization and the broader Syndicate network were diverse, sophisticated, and deeply embedded in the economic and social life of American cities. Understanding them individually is essential to understanding both the power of organized crime in this period and the social damage it inflicted.

Gambling

Gambling was the most important single revenue source for organized crime in the 1930s and beyond. The end of Prohibition removed bootlegging as the primary profit center, and gambling moved into the vacuum. The major gambling enterprises operated by the Syndicate included illegal casinos, which operated in carefully selected locations with the protection of local law enforcement; sports betting operations, which provided a mechanism for wagering on boxing, baseball, and horse racing; and the numbers game, sometimes called the policy game, which was a form of illegal lottery that operated in working-class and poor neighborhoods across the country.

The numbers game deserves particular attention as a social as well as economic phenomenon. In the African American neighborhoods of New York, Chicago, and other northern cities, the numbers operation was a genuinely popular institution: a low-stakes gambling enterprise that allowed people to bet small amounts, sometimes as little as a penny, on a daily number, with substantial winnings for lucky players. The numbers game had originally been operated independently within African American communities, often by African American entrepreneurs. Over the course of the 1920s and 1930s, Italian American criminal organizations moved systematically to take over these operations, often through a combination of violence and financial pressure, replacing Black operators with Italian American ones or forcing Black operators to pay tribute. This displacement of Black criminal entrepreneurs by the Italian American mafia was both an economic injury to Black communities and an early example of the racial dynamics that complicated the history of organized crime in urban America.

Prostitution

The exploitation of prostitution was one of the most extensive criminal operations associated with Luciano's name, and it became the basis of the prosecution that ultimately sent him to prison. The organization of prostitution in New York City in the 1930s was a sophisticated enterprise: it involved the systematic management of hundreds of brothels, madams, and individual prostitutes across multiple boroughs, with profits flowing upward through a network of bookers and madams to criminal controllers at the top. The women involved were subjected to violence, coercion, and debt bondage, conditions that made their situation a form of economic and physical exploitation with few legitimate parallels.

Historians have debated Luciano's direct personal involvement in the prostitution operation. The prosecution in 1936 relied heavily on the testimony of women who had direct experience of the enterprise, and several of them identified Luciano as a controller. Defense lawyers argued, with some justification, that the testimony was unreliable and in some cases coerced. What is not in dispute is that the organized prostitution network in New York City in the 1930s was connected to the Luciano organization and that it represented a form of criminal enterprise that generated significant revenue while inflicting severe harm on the women who were its primary victims.

Labor Racketeering

Labor racketeering, the infiltration and exploitation of labor unions for criminal profit, was perhaps the most economically significant of the organized crime enterprises of the 1930s and 1940s, and certainly the one with the deepest long-term consequences for American society. The logic of labor racketeering was straightforward: a criminal organization that controlled a union controlling workers in an essential industry, whether construction, trucking, the waterfront, or food distribution, had leverage over the entire industry. Employers could be extorted to pay for labor peace. Union pension and welfare funds could be looted. Contracts could be steered to employers who paid kickbacks. The possibilities for criminal profit were enormous, and the damage to the workers whose unions had been corrupted, who found their dues paying for criminal operations rather than genuine representation, was equally enormous.

The connection between organized crime and the labor movement in this period is one of the most sensitive and historically complex aspects of the story. The labor movement of the 1930s was engaged in a genuine and often heroic struggle for workers' rights against powerful employers. Many unions provided real benefits and protection to their members. The infiltration of some of these unions by criminal organizations was a genuine corruption of the labor movement, but it was not a corruption that was evenly distributed or that invalidated the legitimate purposes of unionism. The history of labor racketeering requires careful attention to the difference between unions that were genuinely captured by criminal organizations and the majority of American unions that were not.

Narcotics

The narcotics trade occupied a complex and somewhat contested position within the Luciano organization and the broader Syndicate. Heroin trafficking, in particular, had been a profitable sideline for several criminal organizations since the early decades of the twentieth century. Luciano himself had been arrested on narcotics-related charges earlier in his career. The Commission, perhaps aware of the particularly harsh penalties that narcotics convictions carried and of the law enforcement attention that narcotics trafficking attracted, formally prohibited member families from engaging in the trade. In practice, this prohibition was widely ignored, and narcotics trafficking became one of the most significant and damaging criminal enterprises of the mid-twentieth century, with Italian American criminal organizations deeply involved in the heroin trade that devastated urban communities in the postwar decades.

Political Corruption

Running through all of these enterprises was the fundamental mechanism without which they could not have functioned at their actual scale: the systematic corruption of political and law enforcement institutions. The organized crime operations of the 1930s required, at a minimum, the complicity of local police who were paid to look away, of prosecutors who could be induced not to investigate, of judges who could be bribed or pressured to dismiss cases or impose lenient sentences, and of politicians who could be relied upon to protect criminal interests in exchange for money, votes, or both.

Frank Costello, known as the Prime Minister of the underworld, was perhaps the most sophisticated practitioner of this kind of political corruption in the Luciano circle. Costello built an extraordinary network of political relationships in New York City that extended deep into Tammany Hall, the Democratic Party political machine that dominated the city's government. His relationships with political figures allowed him to influence appointments, including judicial appointments, in ways that provided extensive protection for organized crime operations. The exposure of these relationships in the Kefauver hearings of 1950 to 1951 was one of the first major public revelations of the depth of organized crime's penetration of American political institutions.

Everyday Life and Social History

Living in the Shadow of the Syndicate: Ordinary People and Organized Crime

The history of organized crime in the 1930s is often told through the lives of the bosses and their organizational innovations. But the actual experience of organized crime fell primarily on ordinary people in urban neighborhoods, and that experience was far more complex and ambivalent than the dramatic story of criminal empire-building might suggest.

In the Italian American neighborhoods of New York, the relationship between the local community and organized crime figures was deeply ambiguous. Criminal bosses sometimes functioned as informal community patrons, providing jobs, resolving disputes, assisting families in need, and mediating with governmental institutions that seemed hostile or indifferent to immigrant concerns. This patronage relationship was exploitative at its core, as it bound community members in networks of obligation and fear, but it also provided real, if unreliable, benefits to people who had few other sources of support. The image of the neighborhood boss who attended every wedding and provided turkeys at Thanksgiving was not entirely mythological, even if the reality behind the image was morally much darker.

For the women who became enmeshed in the prostitution network, the experience was simply one of exploitation and harm. Many of them were themselves poor immigrants or daughters of immigrants, trapped in economic circumstances that left them with few choices, and then further trapped by the violence and debt bondage of the organized prostitution system. Their testimony in the Luciano trial of 1936, given at considerable personal risk, provides some of the most direct evidence of the human cost of organized crime enterprises.

For African American residents of northern cities, the organized crime operations of the 1930s represented both an imposition and a paradox. The takeover of the numbers game from Black operators by Italian American criminal organizations was an economic displacement that transferred wealth from Black communities to the Syndicate. But many Black residents also participated in the numbers game as players, regarding it as one of the few gambling opportunities available to them in a society that excluded them from many legitimate forms of recreation and economic participation.

For workers in industries where unions had been corrupted by organized crime, the experience was one of double exploitation: they were exploited by employers in the normal way of capital and labor, and they were also exploited by the criminal figures who controlled their unions, looted their benefit funds, and sold their labor peace to the highest bidder. The damage to genuine labor organizing done by the association of some unions with organized crime was incalculable, providing employers and anti-union political forces with a permanent argument against unionization that was not entirely without factual basis.

Section 13.7

The Downfall and Exile

The career of Lucky Luciano as the dominant figure of American organized crime came to an end not through an internal power struggle or a Commission decision but through the work of a persistent and ambitious prosecutor. Thomas E. Dewey, appointed in 1935 as a special prosecutor to investigate organized crime in New York City, was a young Republican lawyer of extraordinary energy and organizational skill who understood that conventional law enforcement approaches, focused on individual crimes and individual criminals, were inadequate to the challenge of dismantling organizations that were deeply embedded in the social and political fabric of the city. Dewey's approach was to build cases methodically from the ground up, using the testimony of lower-level participants to construct evidence against the leaders at the top.

The prosecution of Luciano on prostitution-related charges, tried in 1936, became one of the most consequential legal proceedings in the history of American organized crime. Dewey's team interviewed hundreds of witnesses, many of them women who had direct experience of the prostitution network, and constructed a case that linked the specific operations of brothels and booking agencies to Luciano's organization through a chain of intermediate figures. The trial lasted several weeks and attracted enormous press attention. Luciano was convicted on sixty-two counts of compulsory prostitution and sentenced to thirty to fifty years in prison, one of the harshest sentences ever imposed on an organized crime figure at that time.

The conviction was a genuine law enforcement achievement. It demonstrated that even the most powerful criminal boss was not immune to prosecution when a sufficiently resourceful and determined prosecutor assembled a sufficiently well-prepared case. But it also had limitations that Dewey himself acknowledged in later years. Luciano's removal from the scene did not dismantle the organization he had built. The Commission continued to function. The Syndicate continued to operate. The other family bosses, Frank Costello and Vito Genovese among them, divided Luciano's operational authority and continued the enterprises he had established. The organizational innovations that were Luciano's genuine contribution to criminal history proved more durable than the individual who had created them.

The Second World War brought an unexpected twist to Luciano's story. In 1942, following the apparent sabotage of the French ocean liner Normandie in New York harbor and amid genuine concerns about enemy espionage on the New York waterfront, U.S. Naval Intelligence approached Luciano, through intermediaries including Meyer Lansky, seeking assistance with waterfront security. Luciano, negotiating from his prison cell in Dannemora, agreed to use his influence with the waterfront unions and criminal networks that controlled much of the port activity to help secure the docks against sabotage and to facilitate the subsequent Allied invasion of Sicily by providing intelligence and contacts. The wartime cooperation, known as Operation Underworld, remains one of the most extraordinary episodes in the history of the relationship between criminal organizations and the state: a case in which the federal government explicitly negotiated with and used a convicted criminal boss as a resource for military purposes.

Whether Luciano's contributions were as significant as his defenders claimed, or whether the wartime benefits were used primarily as a pretext for the commutation of his sentence, has been debated by historians. What is documented is that Governor Thomas Dewey, the very man who had prosecuted Luciano, commuted his sentence in 1946 on the grounds of wartime service, with the condition that Luciano be immediately deported to Italy. Luciano sailed from the United States in February 1946 and never returned. He spent the remainder of his life in Italy, dying of a heart attack at Naples airport in 1962.

From Italy, Luciano maintained connections with the American criminal organizations he had built, conducting meetings with American figures who traveled to see him and, according to law enforcement, continuing to influence operations across the Atlantic. The extent of his ongoing influence from exile is difficult to assess with precision, but it is clear that his removal from the United States did not sever his connections to the criminal world he had created. His death in 1962 came just as the American mafia was entering a period of sustained law enforcement pressure that would eventually, over the following decades, begin the long process of dismantling the structures he had built.

Primary Source Window

The Kefauver Committee Hearings, 1950 to 1951

Source: United States Senate Special Committee to Investigate Organized Crime in Interstate Commerce (Kefauver Committee) · Dates: 1950–1951 · Type: Congressional investigative record, published committee reports, and televised testimony

Context: In 1950, Senator Estes Kefauver of Tennessee led a special Senate committee investigation into organized crime in America. The committee held hearings in fourteen cities across the country, taking testimony from law enforcement officials, criminal figures, and public witnesses. In 1951, portions of the hearings were televised, making them among the first major political events to reach a national television audience. The hearings produced extensive documentary evidence about the structure and operations of organized crime and brought the existence of the National Syndicate to public attention in an unprecedented way.

The committee's final report concluded that organized crime in America had developed a nationwide network of criminal enterprises, that this network was connected by overlapping relationships of cooperation and mutual interest among the major criminal organizations of different cities, and that it had achieved extensive penetration of legitimate business and political institutions. The report described what it called a sinister criminal organization known as the Mafia, operating throughout the country, and identified it as the central mechanism of organized crime's national reach.

Analysis: The Kefauver hearings are an extraordinarily rich primary source for historians of organized crime, but they must be used with care. The committee's conclusions reflected not only the evidence assembled but also the political purposes of the investigation and the biases of its investigators. The identification of organized crime with an Italian American organization called the Mafia, while not without factual basis, oversimplified a more complex criminal landscape in which multiple ethnic organizations cooperated and competed. The hearings also reflected Cold War anxieties about subversive organizations, and some of the most dramatic testimony came from figures whose reliability was questionable. Historians must balance the genuine documentary value of the hearings against their political context and their tendency to sensationalize the evidence they presented.

Section 13.8

Legacy and Transformation

The legacy of Lucky Luciano and the reorganization of American organized crime in the 1930s is a complex one that must be assessed at several different levels: the organizational, the social, and the historical-interpretive.

At the organizational level, Luciano's most enduring contribution was the creation of institutional structures that outlasted his own personal involvement. The Commission and the National Syndicate proved far more durable than any individual boss's reign, precisely because they had been designed to be. The Italian American crime families that Luciano had helped to organize continued to operate under the Commission structure for decades after his deportation, weathering successive law enforcement campaigns, internal conflicts, and changing economic conditions. The Commission itself was not formally broken as an institution until the 1985 Commission Case, in which the heads of all five New York families were simultaneously prosecuted under the RICO statute for their participation in the Commission's activities as a criminal enterprise. Even after that prosecution, the families continued to exist, albeit in diminished form, into the twenty-first century.

At the social level, the legacy of the Luciano era is one of enormous harm inflicted on urban communities across the country. The gambling operations exploited the poor. The prostitution networks exploited women. The narcotics trade devastated neighborhoods. The labor racketeering corrupted institutions that were supposed to protect workers. The political corruption undermined democratic governance in cities across the country. The costs were borne primarily by communities that were already disadvantaged, and they were borne over decades. When historians assess the Luciano era, they must hold in view simultaneously the organizational ingenuity of what was built and the human damage that it inflicted.

At the historical-interpretive level, the story of Lucky Luciano raises fundamental questions about the relationship between criminal enterprise and American capitalism. The most striking feature of the Luciano reorganization was its explicitly corporate character: the adoption of the organizational forms, the management logic, and the profit-maximizing orientation of legitimate American business in the service of criminal enterprise. This was not coincidental. Luciano and his allies were Americans, formed by American culture and the American economic environment, and they applied what they knew to the problems they faced. The result was an organizational form that was distinctly American, even as it drew on Sicilian criminal traditions.

This Americanization of organized crime raises uncomfortable questions. If criminal enterprise can successfully adopt the organizational forms of legitimate business, what does that say about those organizational forms? If the difference between a criminal syndicate and a legitimate corporation is primarily the legal status of the goods and services being provided, what does that tell us about the relationship between legal and illegal economies? Historians of organized crime have long noted that the most successful criminal organizations tend to emerge in societies with advanced capitalist economies, strong demand for illegal goods and services, and some combination of weak governance and exclusion of certain populations from legitimate economic opportunity. The American mafia of the 1930s, built by immigrants who were excluded from many avenues of legitimate advancement and operating in a political economy riven by corruption, fits this pattern precisely.

The myth of Lucky Luciano that developed in popular culture, in films, novels, and journalistic accounts, must be distinguished carefully from the historical record. The mythologized Luciano is a romantic figure: the immigrant boy who made good through intelligence and ruthlessness, the criminal genius who built an empire, the man who even in prison served his country in wartime. This mythology has real cultural power and reveals something genuine about the ambivalent relationship between American culture and criminal success. But it also obscures the fundamental reality of what Luciano built, which was an organization that exploited and harmed thousands of people while enriching a small number of men who were willing to use violence to impose their will on others.

The historical Luciano is a more complicated figure than either the Hollywood villain or the street legend. He was intelligent, pragmatic, and organizationally creative. He was also, by any moral standard, deeply destructive, responsible for murders, exploitation, corruption, and the long-term damage inflicted by the institutions he helped to build. Holding both dimensions in view simultaneously, without romanticizing the criminal or oversimplifying the history, is the challenge that the story of Lucky Luciano and the new mafia presents to every serious student of organized crime.

Historical Debate

Was the Luciano Reorganization a Genuine Break with the Past, or Was It Continuous with Earlier Mafia Traditions?

The question: Historians have debated whether the changes Luciano introduced in 1931 represented a genuinely new form of criminal organization or whether they were primarily adaptations of existing Sicilian and Italian American mafia traditions to the specific conditions of American urban life. The question matters because it affects how we understand the relationship between immigrant traditions and American conditions in shaping organized crime.

Interpretation A — The Luciano Reorganization as Radical Break: Some historians argue that what Luciano created was fundamentally different from the Sicilian mafia traditions he inherited. The Commission, in this view, was a genuinely new institution with no Sicilian precedent, an American invention that adapted the logic of corporate governance to criminal organization. The National Syndicate's cross-ethnic character was equally new. The old Sicilian mafia was clan-based, ethnically exclusive, and rooted in the specific social conditions of rural Sicily. What Luciano built was a national criminal corporation operating in an advanced capitalist economy, which had more in common with Standard Oil than with the Sicilian mafie of the nineteenth century.
Interpretation B — Continuity with Sicilian Tradition: Other historians emphasize the continuities between the Luciano reorganization and longer mafia traditions. The fundamental functions of the Commission, territorial definition, dispute arbitration, and collective governance, were also performed by Sicilian mafia commissions that had existed in various forms for decades. The code of omertà, the family-based organizational unit, the use of ritual and initiation to create organizational loyalty, and the fundamental model of private governance through violence all had clear Sicilian antecedents. In this view, Luciano updated and adapted an existing tradition rather than inventing something entirely new.

Evidence: Both interpretations draw on a combination of historical and ethnographic evidence, including Sicilian mafia scholarship, FBI surveillance records, court testimony, and the memoirs and interviews of criminal figures. The challenge for historians is that the Sicilian mafia and the American mafia were both secretive organizations that left fragmentary records, making comparative organizational analysis difficult.

Why it matters: The interpretation historians choose affects how they explain the origins of the American mafia and what forces they see as most important in shaping it: immigrant culture, American capitalism, or some specific combination of the two. It also has implications for how historians understand organized crime more broadly, whether it is primarily a cultural phenomenon rooted in specific ethnic traditions or an organizational phenomenon produced by specific economic and political conditions.

Multiple Perspectives

The New Mafia and Urban America: Multiple Perspectives

From the perspective of Italian American immigrant communities: For many Italian American immigrants and their American-born children, organized crime figures like Luciano occupied an ambivalent position. On one hand, they were criminals who brought shame on the community and reinforced nativist stereotypes about Italian immigrants. On the other hand, they were visible success stories in a society that largely excluded Italians from legitimate paths to advancement. The patronage networks that criminal figures sometimes maintained in their home neighborhoods provided real, if exploitative, benefits to families who had few other sources of support. The Italian American community's relationship to the mafia was one of the most painful social and cultural dimensions of the immigrant experience.
From the perspective of law enforcement: For police officers, prosecutors, and federal agents who attempted to combat organized crime in the 1930s and 1940s, the Luciano organization and the Syndicate presented challenges that existing law enforcement tools were inadequate to address. The scale, sophistication, and political connections of the new mafia made conventional law enforcement essentially ineffective in many jurisdictions. The Dewey prosecution of Luciano, while successful, required years of work, extraordinary resources, and a willingness to build cases from the bottom up that was unusual in the law enforcement culture of the time. The institutional and legal innovations needed to combat the new mafia, including electronic surveillance, informant programs, and the RICO statute, would take decades to develop.
From the perspective of urban workers: For workers in industries where organized crime had infiltrated unions, the new mafia represented a specific form of exploitation that operated alongside, and was sometimes confused with, genuine labor organizing. Workers who paid dues to a corrupted union were being doubly exploited: by employers who refused to pay fair wages and by criminal figures who looted their benefit funds and sold their labor peace. The damage this did to the genuine labor movement was incalculable, providing employers and anti-union political forces with arguments that associated all unionism with corruption.
From the perspective of African American urban communities: In the cities of the North, African American communities experienced the Luciano era's organized crime as a complex combination of exploitation and limited economic opportunity. The takeover of numbers operations by Italian American criminal organizations displaced Black entrepreneurs. The narcotics trade that organized crime facilitated devastated Black urban communities in ways that would become catastrophically apparent in the postwar decades. At the same time, the limited economic and social exclusion that African Americans faced in the legal economy made the numbers game and other organized crime enterprises among the few available channels of informal economic participation for people excluded from mainstream institutions.
Constitutional Connection

Organized Crime, Prohibition, and the Constitutional Order

The story of Lucky Luciano and the new mafia is inseparable from the constitutional history of Prohibition. The Eighteenth Amendment, ratified in 1919, was the direct constitutional cause of the economic revolution that made the Luciano reorganization possible. By prohibiting alcohol while failing to eliminate the demand for it, the amendment created the illegal market that generated the capital and organizational experience on which the American mafia built its power. The subsequent repeal of the Eighteenth Amendment through the Twenty-first Amendment in 1933 removed the most lucrative single revenue source for organized crime, but by then the damage, if that is the right word for it from a law enforcement perspective, had been done: the organizations, the relationships, and the institutional structures created during Prohibition were already diversified and entrenched.

The relationship between organized crime and the constitutional order also raised serious questions about civil liberties and due process in law enforcement. Thomas Dewey's prosecution of Luciano relied on techniques, including the use of testimony from witnesses who were themselves criminals and the broad interpretation of conspiracy law to connect Luciano to crimes he did not directly commit, that raised legitimate concerns about prosecutorial overreach. Subsequent law enforcement campaigns against organized crime, including the use of electronic surveillance and the RICO statute, would generate decades of constitutional litigation about the limits of government power in the investigation and prosecution of criminal organizations.

The relationship between organized crime and democratic governance also poses deeper constitutional questions. When criminal organizations systematically corrupt elected officials, judges, and prosecutors, they are not merely breaking individual laws. They are undermining the constitutional system of democratic accountability itself. The corruption of political institutions documented in the Kefauver hearings and subsequent investigations represented a genuine threat to constitutional democracy, a form of private governance that displaced or captured public institutions in ways that were fundamentally incompatible with democratic principles.

Cause and Effect

The Reorganization of American Organized Crime, 1931

Long-Term Causes

Mass Italian immigration creating dense immigrant communities excluded from mainstream economic opportunities; the transplantation of Sicilian mafia traditions to the American urban environment; the development of Italian American criminal networks in the first two decades of the twentieth century; the structural tensions of the Mustache Pete model of personal authority in an American context that rewarded cooperation and rational management.

Short-Term Causes

The economic revolution of Prohibition, which created vast new criminal wealth; the violence and economic disruption of the Castellammarese War; Luciano's personal ambition and organizational intelligence; the specific deaths of Masseria and Maranzano that cleared the way for new leadership; and Luciano's existing cross-ethnic alliance with Lansky and Siegel that prefigured the Syndicate model.

The Transformation

The creation of the Commission as a governing body for the Italian American crime families; the formation of the National Syndicate as a cross-ethnic cooperative network; the formalization of family organizational structures with defined roles; and the replacement of the boss of all bosses model with collective governance.

Long-Term Effects

The institutionalization of the American mafia under a durable Commission structure; the diversification of criminal enterprise into gambling, labor racketeering, and narcotics; the penetration of legitimate business, labor unions, and political institutions; the devastating social effects on urban communities; and the creation of organizational structures that required decades of sustained law enforcement effort to begin dismantling.

Historical complexity: The Luciano reorganization cannot be explained by any single cause. It was the product of a specific combination of long-term social and economic conditions, the specific crisis of the Castellammarese War, and the particular capabilities and ambitions of a small number of individual actors who recognized an opportunity for fundamental change and seized it.

Continuity and Change Over Time

American Organized Crime from the Prohibition Era to the Commission Case (1920–1985)

What Changed

  • The organizational model shifted from personal authority concentrated in a single boss to collective governance through the Commission
  • Criminal enterprise diversified from primary dependence on bootlegging to a portfolio of gambling, labor racketeering, narcotics, and legitimate business infiltration
  • Geographic reach expanded from northeastern industrial cities to a genuinely national and eventually international network
  • Cross-ethnic cooperation between Italian American, Jewish, and other criminal organizations became institutionalized through the Syndicate
  • The scale of political corruption expanded from local ward-level bribery to systematic influence over city, state, and in some cases federal politics
  • Law enforcement's understanding of organized crime evolved from treating it as individual criminal activity to recognizing it as institutional criminal enterprise

What Stayed the Same

  • The fundamental mechanism of private governance through violence remained constant throughout the period
  • The code of omertà continued to function as the primary protection against law enforcement investigation
  • The family-based organizational unit, with its hierarchy of boss, underboss, capos, and soldiers, persisted with only minor modifications
  • The exploitation of ethnic community ties for recruitment, protection, and political cover continued
  • The fundamental dependence on political corruption as the primary means of protecting criminal enterprises remained unchanged
  • The concentration of organizational power in a small number of northeastern cities, particularly New York, persisted even as the Syndicate expanded nationally

Why the pattern matters: The combination of significant organizational innovation with remarkable structural continuity explains both the durability and the eventual vulnerability of the American mafia. The institutional innovations of the Luciano era made the organization more resistant to the loss of any single leader. But the persistent structures, including omertà, family organization, and dependence on political corruption, eventually created specific vulnerabilities that law enforcement learned to exploit, particularly through informant development programs and the RICO statute.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has traced one of the most consequential transformations in the history of American organized crime: the reorganization of Italian American criminal enterprise in the early 1930s under the leadership of Lucky Luciano and his allies, resulting in the creation of the Commission and the National Syndicate. This transformation did not happen in a vacuum. It was the product of specific historical conditions, including mass immigration, urban poverty and discrimination, the economic revolution of Prohibition, the political culture of machine politics and corruption, and the specific organizational crisis of the Castellammarese War.

Salvatore Lucania, who became Charles Lucky Luciano, was a product of the immigrant world of the Lower East Side of Manhattan, one of the most disadvantaged and most creative communities in early twentieth-century America. His rise through the criminal hierarchy of New York, his alliances with Meyer Lansky, Bugsy Siegel, and other criminal entrepreneurs across ethnic lines, and his eventual decision to exploit the Castellammarese War to eliminate both the major old-guard bosses and install a fundamentally new form of criminal governance, were all shaped by the specific conditions of his time and place.

The Commission that Luciano helped to create was a genuinely novel institutional form. By establishing collective governance, territorial boundaries, and dispute resolution mechanisms among the major Italian American crime families, it solved the fundamental governance problem of criminal enterprise: how to manage competition and conflict among powerful organizations that could not use the legal mechanisms available to legitimate businesses. Its creation reduced the endemic violence of the Prohibition era and allowed the member organizations to focus on the profitable management of criminal enterprises rather than on fighting each other.

The National Syndicate extended the logic of cooperation beyond Italian American organizations, creating a cross-ethnic network that encompassed Jewish, Italian, and other criminal entrepreneurs in a genuinely national criminal organization. The Syndicate's reach extended from the northeastern cities that were its original base into new geographic frontiers, including the West Coast and eventually the gambling resort economy of Las Vegas. It also extended the reach of organized crime into new economic territories, particularly labor racketeering, which gave criminal organizations leverage over urban economies far beyond the traditional criminal markets.

The criminal enterprises of the Luciano era generated enormous profits for a small number of men while inflicting serious and lasting harm on the communities they operated in. The gambling operations exploited the poor. The prostitution networks exploited and harmed women. The narcotics trade devastated neighborhoods. The labor racketeering corrupted institutions meant to protect workers. The political corruption undermined democratic governance. These harms were not incidental to the criminal enterprise. They were its substance.

Thomas Dewey's prosecution of Luciano in 1936, which resulted in a conviction and a sentence of thirty to fifty years, demonstrated that even the most powerful criminal boss was not immune to sustained, resourceful, and well-prepared prosecution. But the prosecution also demonstrated the limitations of conventional law enforcement approaches: removing Luciano from the scene did not dismantle the organization he had built, which continued to function under the collective leadership of the Commission and the other family bosses. The institutional innovation proved more durable than the individual.

The extraordinary episode of Operation Underworld, in which the federal government negotiated with the convicted criminal boss in his prison cell for wartime assistance, illuminated in a particularly striking way the complex and ambiguous relationship between the state and organized crime. That the same government that had sent Luciano to prison would subsequently negotiate with him for military assistance, and that the same prosecutor who had convicted him would ultimately commute his sentence on grounds of wartime service, reveals the contradictions and compromises that characterize the relationship between criminal organizations and the institutions of the state throughout history.

Luciano's deportation to Italy in 1946 ended his direct management of American criminal operations but not his connections to the organizations he had built. He died in Naples in 1962, as the American mafia was entering a new period of law enforcement pressure that would eventually, over the following decades, begin the slow process of dismantling the institutional structures he had created. The Commission that Luciano founded was not formally prosecuted as a criminal enterprise until 1985, more than fifty years after its creation, a measure of both the durability of the institution and the difficulty of combating it.

The legacy of Lucky Luciano and the new mafia is ultimately a story about the relationship between institutional innovation and social harm, about the ways in which organizational creativity can be deployed in the service of exploitation, and about the deep connections between criminal enterprise and the specific conditions, economic, social, political, and cultural, of the societies in which it operates. Understanding this legacy requires holding in view simultaneously the organizational sophistication of what was built and the human damage that it caused. Both dimensions are essential to an honest historical account.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Bootlegging
The illegal manufacture, transportation, or sale of alcohol during the Prohibition era (1920–1933). Bootlegging was the primary source of criminal revenue in the 1920s and the foundation of the organizational capacity that the American mafia built.
Capo
In Italian American mafia terminology, a captain who commands a crew of soldiers within a crime family. In the broader sense, the boss or head of a crime family. The formalization of this role was part of the organizational standardization introduced by the Luciano reorganization.
Castellammarese War
The violent conflict between 1930 and 1931 between the factions led by Giuseppe Masseria and Salvatore Maranzano for dominance of the Italian American criminal underworld in New York City. The war created the conditions for the Luciano reorganization.
The Commission
The governing body of the American mafia established after the 1931 reorganization, consisting of representatives of the major Italian American crime families and designed to manage territorial disputes, approve major decisions, and provide collective governance without resort to warfare.
Consigliere
The counselor or advisor to a crime family boss, serving as a mediator within the family and a check on the boss's authority. Part of the formal organizational hierarchy established during the Luciano era.
Informant
A person who provides information to law enforcement, often in exchange for legal consideration. The development of effective informant programs was one of the most important factors in the eventual law enforcement success against the American mafia, as it eroded the protection provided by omertà.
Labor racketeering
The infiltration and exploitation of labor unions for criminal profit, including extortion of employers, looting of union pension funds, and the corrupt sale of labor peace. One of the most economically significant and socially damaging criminal enterprises of the Luciano era.
Mafia
In the American context, the network of Italian American crime families organized under the Commission structure established after 1931. More broadly, the term refers to the Sicilian criminal organization from which the American mafia descended and whose traditions shaped, though did not determine, the American institution.
Mustache Petes
A colloquial term used by younger Italian American criminals to describe the old-guard Sicilian bosses who maintained traditional hierarchical authority, ethnic exclusivity, and personal loyalty structures that the younger generation found incompatible with profitable criminal enterprise in the American environment.
National Syndicate
The broader cooperative network of criminal organizations, crossing ethnic lines to include Italian American, Jewish, and other criminal enterprises, that coordinated criminal activities across the country under the organizational framework established in the early 1930s.
Night of the Sicilian Vespers
The name given to the coordinated murders of September 1931 that eliminated the remaining old-guard mafia bosses and cleared the way for the Luciano reorganization. The historical record of how many murders occurred and how widely coordinated they were remains debated.
Numbers game
An illegal lottery, also called the policy game, in which players bet on a daily number at low stakes with high potential winnings. The numbers game was a major revenue source in working-class and poor urban neighborhoods and was systematically taken over from Black operators by Italian American criminal organizations in the 1920s and 1930s.
Omertà
The Sicilian and Italian American mafia code of silence, prohibiting members from cooperating with law enforcement or revealing organizational secrets. Omertà functioned as the primary structural protection of organized crime against law enforcement investigation throughout the Luciano era.
Operation Underworld
The wartime arrangement between U.S. Naval Intelligence and Lucky Luciano, negotiated through intermediaries while Luciano was in prison, in which Luciano allegedly provided assistance with waterfront security and intelligence gathering related to the Allied invasion of Sicily in exchange for consideration toward commutation of his sentence.
Prohibition
The period from 1920 to 1933 during which the Eighteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution prohibited the manufacture, sale, and transportation of intoxicating liquors. Prohibition created the illegal alcohol market that generated the enormous criminal profits on which the American mafia built its organizational capacity.
Protection racket
A criminal enterprise in which individuals or businesses are required to make regular payments to a criminal organization in exchange for protection, either from the criminal organization itself or from other threats. Protection rackets were among the oldest and most fundamental of organized crime's revenue sources.
RICO statute
The Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act, passed by Congress in 1970, which provided law enforcement with legal tools to prosecute criminal organizations as entities rather than simply prosecuting individual criminal acts. The RICO statute was the most important legal innovation in the law enforcement campaign against organized crime and was eventually used to prosecute the Commission itself in 1985.
Syndicate
A cooperative association of criminal enterprises coordinating activities, sharing resources, and managing competition. The National Syndicate created in the early 1930s applied the corporate logic of combination and cooperation to illegal enterprise.
Underboss
The deputy boss of a crime family, serving as the operational manager of the family's day-to-day activities and second in command to the boss. Part of the formalized hierarchy established during the Luciano reorganization.

Major People and Places

Identification & Significance

Charles "Lucky" Luciano (Salvatore Lucania, 1897–1962)
The central figure of this chapter and the primary architect of the Commission and National Syndicate. Sicilian-born, raised in the Lower East Side of Manhattan, convicted of prostitution-related charges in 1936 and deported to Italy in 1946. His organizational innovations defined American organized crime for decades.
Meyer Lansky (1902–1983)
Luciano's closest ally and the financial genius of the National Syndicate. Jewish-American, Lansky's cross-ethnic partnership with Luciano was foundational to the Syndicate model and his financial sophistication in money management and casino development made him one of the most significant figures in the history of criminal enterprise.
Frank Costello (1891–1973)
Called the "Prime Minister" of the underworld for his political connections and preference for corruption over violence. A central figure in the Luciano circle who managed the political relationships that protected the organization and who ran much of the day-to-day criminal enterprise while Luciano was in prison.
Giuseppe "Joe the Boss" Masseria (c. 1886–1931)
The old-guard Sicilian boss who dominated New York's Italian criminal underworld in the 1920s. Luciano's nominal superior, murdered in April 1931 at Luciano's instigation, clearing the way for the reorganization.
Salvatore Maranzano (1886–1931)
The Castellammarese faction boss who defeated Masseria in the war but was himself murdered five months later in September 1931 when he proved unwilling to share power. His death marked the end of the boss of all bosses model.
Thomas E. Dewey (1902–1971)
The special prosecutor who built the case against Luciano in 1936 and secured his conviction, later the governor of New York who commuted Luciano's sentence in 1946. A significant figure in American law and politics whose career was shaped by the organized crime prosecutions of the Depression era.
Vito Genovese (1897–1969)
A major figure in the Luciano organization who participated in the Masseria murder and who would eventually become the head of one of the five New York families. His involvement in narcotics trafficking, in defiance of Commission policy, made him one of the most controversial figures of the postwar mafia.
Albert Anastasia (1902–1957)
The most feared enforcer in the Luciano organization, associated with the Murder Inc. enforcement apparatus. His willingness to commit violence on behalf of the organization made him an essential figure in the maintenance of internal discipline and external intimidation.
Bugsy Siegel (1906–1947)
Luciano's partner and Meyer Lansky's closest associate, instrumental in the expansion of the Syndicate to the West Coast and the development of Las Vegas as an organized crime gambling center. His murder in 1947 remained officially unsolved, illustrating the violence that persisted within the rationalized Syndicate framework.
The Lower East Side, New York City
The immigrant neighborhood where Luciano, Lansky, Siegel, and many other key figures of the American mafia developed their criminal careers. The social world of the Lower East Side, its ethnic diversity, its poverty, its exclusion from mainstream economic opportunities, and its dense informal networks, shaped the alliances and the organizational logic of the new mafia.
Dannemora Prison (Clinton Correctional Facility), upstate New York
The maximum-security prison where Luciano was incarcerated after his 1936 conviction. The site of the wartime negotiations with Naval Intelligence through which Luciano arranged his eventual pardon and deportation.
Las Vegas, Nevada
The desert gambling resort city whose development was catalyzed by organized crime investment, beginning with Bugsy Siegel's Flamingo Hotel in 1946. Las Vegas represented the frontier of organized crime's expansion into legitimacy, or at least into legal gray zones, and its history illustrates the complex relationship between criminal and legitimate enterprise.

Chapter Reflection

The story of Lucky Luciano and the new mafia is, at one level, a story about a specific man and a specific transformation in the organization of criminal enterprise in the United States in the early 1930s. But at a deeper level, it is a story about some of the most enduring and uncomfortable questions that the study of organized crime raises for any society that takes seriously the relationship between institutions, economies, and human behavior.

One of the most striking features of the Luciano reorganization was how thoroughly American it was in its organizational logic. The men who created the Commission and the National Syndicate were not importing a foreign criminal tradition into an innocent American setting. They were applying the organizational forms of American capitalism, its emphasis on rational management, economies of scale, coordination among enterprises, and profit maximization, to criminal purposes. This is not an excuse for what they did. It is an observation about where they learned their organizational thinking, which was the same place that every American entrepreneur, criminal or legitimate, learned it: from the economic culture of the society in which they lived.

This observation raises a question that organized crime history consistently poses to serious students: what is the relationship between criminal enterprise and the legitimate economy of the society in which it operates? The American mafia of the 1930s did not exist in opposition to American capitalism. It existed as a specific, illegal expression of American capitalism, shaped by the same values, the same organizational forms, and the same profit-maximizing logic as the legitimate economy, but operating in markets that the state had chosen to prohibit. When Prohibition ended and bootlegging revenue disappeared, the criminal organizations built on it diversified into other markets, exactly as any rational business enterprise would do when a revenue source disappeared. The parallel to legitimate business behavior is not coincidental. It is fundamental.

The story of this chapter also connects to broader themes that run through the history of organized crime globally: the relationship between criminal enterprise and state weakness or complicity; the role of ethnic and immigrant communities in providing the social networks within which criminal organization develops; the paradox of criminal enterprise that simultaneously harms communities and provides them with services and economic opportunities that legitimate institutions have failed to supply; and the enduring difficulty of combating criminal organizations that are deeply embedded in social, economic, and political structures. These themes will appear in different forms in every subsequent chapter of this textbook, from the Yakuza's relationship with Japanese politics to the cartels' relationship with Latin American states. The American mafia of the 1930s was a specific historical phenomenon, but it also exemplifies patterns that are universal to organized crime wherever and whenever it appears.

Finally, the story of Lucky Luciano is a reminder that history is made by individuals operating within, and sometimes reshaping, the constraints of their time and place. Luciano did not simply respond to the conditions he found. He saw possibilities that others did not see and acted on them with a combination of intelligence, ruthlessness, and organizational creativity that changed the landscape for everyone who followed. That he used these capacities in the service of criminal enterprise, inflicting serious harm on thousands of people, does not make him less historically significant. It makes his story more complex and more important to understand, because it refuses the comfortable conclusion that criminal enterprise is simply the work of stupid or bestial men acting irrationally. It is often the work of intelligent men acting very rationally in pursuit of goals that the society around them has failed to provide legitimate means of achieving.

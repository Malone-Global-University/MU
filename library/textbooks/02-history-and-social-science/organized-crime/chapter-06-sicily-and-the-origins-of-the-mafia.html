Part III — The Birth of the Mafia

Sicily and the Origins of the Mafia

Organized Crime: A Global History

Chapter Opening

"The mafia is not an organization. It is a method — a way of exercising power through the threat of violence in the absence of reliable state authority."

In the summer of 1874, in the orange groves and sulfur mines of western Sicily, a word was already circulating among Italian government officials, police prefects, and northern journalists who had traveled south to investigate persistent reports of extortion, murder, and a peculiar obstruction of justice. The word was mafia. Its precise origins remain contested, its etymology disputed among scholars, but its meaning was becoming unmistakably clear: there existed in Sicily a network of men, loosely affiliated across towns and territories, who collected money through the threat and application of violence, who enforced an absolute code of silence upon those who dealt with them, and who had so thoroughly penetrated local political life that the instruments of the state could not reliably be used against them. This network was not a corporation with a board of directors. It was not an army with a chain of command. It was something more diffuse and more durable than either, rooted not in formal organization alone but in a particular culture of power, honor, and distrust that had been shaped across centuries of conquest and misrule on an island that had almost never been governed well.

To understand the origins of the Sicilian Mafia, it is not sufficient to locate a founding moment, a founding document, or a founding figure, because none of these existed in the conventional sense. The Mafia did not spring into being fully formed. It emerged gradually, across the first half of the nineteenth century, from a confluence of conditions that were themselves the product of centuries of Sicilian history: a geography that isolated communities and made central governance difficult, a feudal land system that concentrated enormous wealth in the hands of absentee aristocrats while reducing the peasant majority to desperate poverty, a succession of foreign rulers who treated Sicily as a possession to be exploited rather than a society to be governed, and a deep popular distrust of official authority that made informal power structures — including violent ones — seem not only inevitable but in some respects preferable to the corrupt and unreliable representatives of the state.

This chapter traces the formation of the Mafia as a social and criminal institution from its roots in feudal Sicily through its consolidation in the decades following Italian national unification in 1861. It examines the structural conditions that produced the Mafia, the specific economic functions it served, the cultural codes and organizational practices that defined it, the relationship between the Mafia and the apparatus of the Italian state, and the ways ordinary Sicilian peasants, landowners, and urban workers experienced the Mafia's presence in their daily lives. It also examines the first systematic efforts by Italian authorities to understand and document what the Mafia was, how it was organized, and how it operated.

The history of the Sicilian Mafia in this formative period raises some of the most important questions in the broader study of organized crime. Under what conditions does organized crime emerge and consolidate? What relationship does it bear to state weakness and corruption? How does it use violence, and when does it prefer alternative means? How do criminal organizations achieve legitimacy or at least tolerance within the communities they exploit? And how does a criminal institution acquire the cultural depth and social embeddedness that allows it to persist across generations, surviving suppression campaigns, emigration, and the transformation of the economies in which it operates?

These questions are not merely historical. The Sicilian Mafia, in various forms, is still active in the twenty-first century. Its American offshoot, La Cosa Nostra, shaped organized crime in the United States for most of the twentieth century and will be examined in later chapters of this textbook. Its methods, its culture, and its organizational logic have influenced criminal enterprises on every continent. Understanding the Mafia's origins is therefore not an exercise in antiquarianism. It is an attempt to understand one of the most consequential and enduring examples of organized criminal power in modern history.

The story begins, as so many stories of power and inequality must, with land: who owned it, who worked it, who protected it, and at what price.

Historical Context

Sicily sits at the center of the Mediterranean Sea, an island of some 25,000 square kilometers positioned at the crossroads of the ancient and medieval worlds. Its climate is warm and dry, its soils in the western interior rugged and prone to drought, its coastlines long and open. For three thousand years before the Mafia's emergence, Sicily had been one of the most contested territories on earth. Greeks colonized its eastern shores in the eighth century BCE. Carthaginians controlled its western regions. Romans conquered the entire island in the third century BCE and turned it into the empire's primary grain supplier. Arabs ruled much of Sicily from the ninth to the eleventh century CE, introducing irrigation, citrus cultivation, and cotton. Normans, Swabians, Angevins, Aragonese, and Spanish Habsburgs followed in succession. By the eighteenth century, the Bourbon kingdom of Naples claimed sovereignty over both Sicily and the southern Italian mainland, ruling from Naples with a court that regarded Sicily as a distant and difficult province of little strategic importance except as a source of tax revenue and grain.

Each wave of conquest left its mark. But the most consequential legacy for the Mafia's eventual emergence was the feudal land system. Across centuries of noble rule and royal grant, Sicily's most fertile agricultural land became concentrated in enormous estates, known as latifondi, owned by a small number of aristocratic families. These estates were often measured not in acres but in square miles. Their absentee owners lived in Palermo or Naples, collecting rents transmitted through layers of middlemen while the actual cultivation of the land was performed by the most desperate and exploited rural laborers in Western Europe. Sicilian peasants worked the great estates as sharecroppers, day laborers, and tenant farmers under terms so unfavorable and so vulnerable to arbitrary adjustment that they could rarely accumulate enough to leave. The gap between the aristocracy and the peasantry was not merely economic. It was civilizational in the sense that the two groups inhabited almost entirely separate worlds, governed by different rules and different expectations of what life could offer.

The relationship between landowner and peasant was managed, at the local level, not by the landowner himself but by a class of intermediaries who became critically important to the Mafia's emergence. These were the gabelloti, men of middling social standing, neither aristocrats nor laborers, who leased entire estates from noble families at fixed rents and then sublet them to peasants at much higher rates, pocketing the difference. The gabelloto was the effective master of the local agricultural economy. He decided who received land and on what terms, who was hired for seasonal labor, who received credit, and who was evicted. This position of economic power over a dependent and impoverished peasantry created ideal conditions for the transition to organized extortion. The step from managing agricultural estates through economic coercion to managing them through violent coercion was, in practice, a small one.

What made this transition into something that could properly be called organized crime was the combination of economic exploitation with the specific failure of the Sicilian state. The Bourbon administration of Sicily was not merely weak in the sense of being under-resourced or inefficient, though it was certainly both of those things. It was weak in a more fundamental sense: it lacked legitimacy in the eyes of most Sicilians because it had never done what a legitimate state is expected to do, which is to protect the lives and property of ordinary people in an impartial and reliable way. The Bourbon police were understaffed, underpaid, and susceptible to corruption. The courts were slow, partial, and easily manipulated by those with money and connections. The ordinary Sicilian peasant who suffered a theft, a violent assault, or a property dispute had little reason to expect that going to the official authorities would produce any useful result and had good reason to fear that doing so would produce retaliation from those who preferred their disputes resolved outside the court system.

Into this vacuum of legitimate authority stepped the men who would become the Mafia. They offered what the state could not or would not provide: resolution of disputes, protection of property, enforcement of contracts, and a form of order, however brutal and self-serving, in communities where official order had long since ceased to function. This is not to romanticize the Mafia or to suggest that it was genuinely protective of the poor. It was not. It exploited the poor at least as ruthlessly as it exploited the rich, and its protection rackets were as much the problem as they were the solution. But understanding why ordinary people sometimes preferred Mafia intermediation to state justice requires understanding how comprehensively the state had already failed them.

The persistent debate within Italian politics and intellectual life about why the South of Italy, including Sicily, remained so much poorer, less stable, and more violent than the North after unification. The Mafia became one of the central exhibits in this debate.

Section 6.1

Sicily: Geography, Conquest, and the Failure of the State

Any serious attempt to understand the origins of the Sicilian Mafia must begin with the island itself, because the Mafia was not an accident or an aberration. It was the product of specific conditions rooted in geography, history, and political economy that operated over centuries before the word "mafia" was ever written down. Sicily's location at the crossroads of the Mediterranean, its particular combination of fertile coastal lowlands and rugged interior highlands, and its extraordinary history of repeated conquest and foreign rule all shaped the social environment in which organized criminal power eventually took root.

The island's physical geography created conditions that made centralized governance unusually difficult. Western Sicily in particular, the heartland of early Mafia power, was a landscape of isolated hilltop towns connected by poor roads across territory that dried to near-desert conditions every summer and turned into a muddy impassable tangle every winter. Communities separated by a few dozen kilometers could be effectively cut off from one another for weeks at a time. State authority, under even the most energetic and honest administration, could not penetrate every corner of this landscape with the consistency needed to make law enforcement reliable. And the Bourbon administration of Sicily in the eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries was neither energetic nor particularly honest.

The pattern of foreign rule had left Sicilians with what historians have described as a deep structural distrust of authority. Under Arab rule, under Norman rule, under Spanish rule, under Bourbon rule, the island had been governed primarily for the benefit of its rulers rather than the welfare of its population. Tax collectors came. Grain was extracted. Military service was demanded. But investment in infrastructure, courts, schools, or local development was minimal. The state, as most ordinary Sicilians experienced it, was not a protector but an extractor, not an institution that delivered justice but one that delivered demands. This experience accumulated across generations into a cultural disposition that was simultaneously fatalistic about official authority and suspicious of anyone who appealed to it. To go to the authorities with a complaint or a denunciation was, in the common understanding of Sicilian popular culture, not an act of civic responsibility but an act of weakness, dependency, or, at worst, treachery.

This cultural inheritance was not invented or fabricated. It was the rational product of lived experience over centuries. The man who went to the Bourbon police with a complaint about a theft had little reason to expect that the complaint would produce restitution or punishment. He had good reason to fear that his complaint would become known to the person who had harmed him, producing retaliation. In these circumstances, the informal resolution of disputes through the mediation of respected local figures, backed if necessary by the threat of private violence, was not an irrational choice. It was often the only practical choice available.

The specific failure of Bourbon state authority in Sicily was not uniformly distributed across the island. The state was somewhat more present in the port cities, most importantly Palermo, where trade, customs revenue, and aristocratic society gave rulers some incentive to maintain order. But even in Palermo, the city's labyrinthine old neighborhoods, its underemployed and often desperate urban poor, and the complex web of patronage relationships that connected the city's criminal underworld to its political class meant that official authority was always partial and contested. In the interior, the state was sometimes essentially absent. Road bandits, known as briganti, operated across large areas with near impunity. Disputes between neighboring villages over water rights, grazing lands, or agricultural boundaries could fester for generations without any resolution because there was no reliable mechanism for adjudicating them.

What emerged in this environment was not simply criminality in the conventional sense of individuals choosing to break laws. What emerged was a parallel system of social order, maintained through informal authority, personal reputation, and the calibrated use of violence, that operated alongside and partly in competition with the formal state apparatus. The men who operated within this parallel system were not identical to conventional criminals. They occupied recognized social roles, they performed functions that the community valued, and they were embedded in networks of obligation and loyalty that gave their power a degree of social legitimacy that purely predatory crime could never achieve. It is this combination of social function and criminal method that makes the Mafia analytically distinctive and historically significant.

Section 6.2

Feudalism, Land, and the Gabelloto: The Economic Roots of Mafia Power

The economic structure of nineteenth-century Sicily was not merely unequal. It was organized in ways that almost perfectly created the conditions for criminal intermediation. The feudal land system, which had been partially reformed on paper but persisted in practice long after formal feudalism was abolished in 1812, concentrated enormous agricultural wealth in the hands of a small aristocratic elite while reducing the majority of the rural population to a condition of chronic insecurity and near-subsistence poverty. Between the aristocrat and the peasant stood a layer of middlemen whose economic function gradually became intertwined with the capacity for organized violence.

The great estates, the latifondi, dominated much of Sicily's agricultural landscape, particularly in the western and central interior. A single estate might encompass tens of thousands of acres, stretching across entire mountain valleys and including multiple villages whose inhabitants depended on the estate's land for their survival. The noble families who owned these estates were typically resident in Palermo, where they participated in urban aristocratic society, attended the opera, pursued political connections, and lived in the grand palaces that still line the Sicilian capital's central streets. They had little interest in the daily management of their agricultural properties and little practical capacity to exercise it even had they wished to. What they needed were local agents who could collect rents, manage the peasant labor force, maintain order on the estate, and transmit the net revenues to the Palermo household.

The men who filled this role were the gabelloti. The system worked as follows: a nobleman would lease his entire estate to a gabelloto at an agreed annual rent, effectively delegating all management responsibilities and risks to the leaseholder. The gabelloto would then sublease parcels of the estate to peasant families, charging them significantly more than he was paying the noble, and pocketing the difference. He also commonly charged interest on agricultural credit, required peasants to sell their crops through his distribution networks at prices he effectively set, and maintained armed men, known as campieri, who served as a private rural police force patrolling the estate and enforcing the gabelloto's authority.

The position gave the gabelloto extraordinary power over the peasants in his territory. He was not subject to effective oversight by either the absentee noble above him or the distant Bourbon state. His decisions about land allocation, credit, and crop pricing could mean the difference between survival and destitution for the peasant families in his orbit. And his control of armed men meant that disputes with him were not merely economic conflicts but physically dangerous confrontations. The logical extension of this position, in an environment where the formal justice system was unreliable and the state was distrusted, was for the gabelloto to extend his economic authority into explicit criminal extortion. The shift from charging excessive rents to demanding payments for protection against threats that he himself orchestrated was, in practice, a small step. The gabelloto already controlled the threats. The formalization of that control into a structured criminal enterprise was a matter of degree rather than kind.

Historical Vocabulary
Gabelloto

A gabelloto (plural: gabelloti) was a leaseholder who rented agricultural estates, typically from absentee aristocratic landowners in Sicily, and sublet them at higher rates to peasant farmers. The term derives from the word gabella, meaning a tax or toll. The gabelloto stood at the economic and social hinge point between the landowning aristocracy and the rural laboring poor, exercising extensive coercive power over the peasants in his territory while buffering the noble owner from direct responsibility for local conditions.

Historians regard the gabelloto class as the primary social base from which early Mafia bosses emerged. The structural position of the gabelloto, combining economic power over dependent peasants with control of armed enforcement and minimal accountability to either the landowner above or the state, created the ideal conditions for the transition from exploitative estate management to organized criminal protection rackets. The gabelloto did not need to invent new forms of power. He needed only to systematize and extend the coercive authority he already possessed into the explicit demand for payments in exchange for protection, of which he himself was often the primary threat.

Beyond the latifondi, similar dynamics operated in the intensive agriculture of the areas surrounding Palermo. The Conca d'Oro, the fertile basin that ringed the capital with its famous citrus groves, was not a latifondo landscape in the traditional sense. Its gardens were smaller, more intensively cultivated, and oriented toward Palermo's urban markets rather than grain production for export. But they were no less dependent on complex arrangements of land tenure, water rights, labor organization, and marketing that created opportunities for criminal intermediation at every link in the chain.

Water was the key resource in the Conca d'Oro. Citrus cultivation required reliable irrigation, and the water supply was controlled through a network of private rights, traditional arrangements, and informal understandings that had accumulated over centuries. Anyone who controlled access to water held life-or-death power over the citrus growers who depended on it. By the 1860s and 1870s, Mafia networks were well established as controllers of water distribution in the Conca d'Oro, charging garden owners for access to irrigation supplies they were legally entitled to and effectively taxing every orange and lemon that left the valley on its way to the Palermo market. The Mafia's dominance of the Palermo fruit and vegetable wholesale markets, which would persist well into the twentieth century, had its roots in these arrangements.

In the sulfur mines of central and southern Sicily, a parallel but somewhat different form of organized criminal labor control developed. The sulfur industry was Sicily's most important export sector in the nineteenth century, and the mines were worked under a system of subcontracted labor that bore striking resemblances to the latifondo's gabelloto system. Mine owners leased their properties to labor contractors, known as carusi supervisors, who recruited workers, often including young children, under debt arrangements that effectively bound them to the mine and made free exit impossible. The combination of dangerous working conditions, debt peonage, and physical isolation from any effective state protection made the mining districts another seedbed for organized criminal control of labor.

Section 6.3

The Unification of Italy and Its Consequences in Sicily

The unification of Italy between 1859 and 1861 was one of the most celebrated events in nineteenth-century European history. Celebrated, that is, primarily in the North. In Sicily, Italian national unification, achieved through Garibaldi's dramatic Expedition of the Thousand in 1860 and the subsequent military and political integration of the former Bourbon territories into the new Kingdom of Italy, produced consequences that were far more ambiguous and in many respects actively damaging to the conditions that the Mafia required to be suppressed.

The standard nationalist narrative of the Risorgimento presented unification as the liberation of southern Italy from Bourbon tyranny and the integration of a fragmented peninsula into a single modern nation-state capable of taking its place among the great powers of Europe. This narrative was not entirely false. The Bourbon regime in Sicily had been notably corrupt, inefficient, and politically repressive, and few Sicilians mourned its passing. Garibaldi's landing at Marsala in May 1860, and his rapid military advance across the island with a force of only a thousand volunteers supplemented by Sicilian popular insurgents, was a genuinely remarkable military achievement that captured the imagination of liberals across Europe.

But for most Sicilians, the practical consequences of unification were deeply disappointing and in some respects actively worse than the situation that had preceded it. The new Italian state was controlled by northern elites, primarily from Piedmont, who regarded the South with a mixture of condescension and incomprehension. The policies they applied to their new territories were designed not to address the specific conditions of southern society but to integrate the South as rapidly as possible into the northern legal and administrative framework, regardless of whether that framework fit the actual conditions on the ground.

Three specific consequences of unification were particularly important for the Mafia's subsequent development. First, the dissolution of church properties and common lands. The new Italian state, in a sweeping program of liberal economic reform, confiscated the landholdings of the Catholic Church and distributed or auctioned them onto the market. In principle, this was supposed to create a class of independent smallholding peasants. In practice, the peasants who might have benefited from land distribution lacked the capital to purchase land at auction and the legal sophistication to navigate the process even had they possessed the money. The land went instead to the same class of wealthy intermediaries who already dominated the rural economy, many of whom were precisely the gabelloti and their associates who were in the process of becoming mafiosi. The abolition of common lands, on which peasant communities had historically grazed animals and gathered resources, further impoverished the rural poor while enriching the intermediary class.

Second, the new Italian state imposed military conscription on Sicily. Young men from the island were required to serve for years in the national army, fighting in campaigns in the northern Italian mainland and eventually in colonial wars in Africa that had nothing to do with Sicilian interests or concerns. Military service was deeply unpopular, seen as an alien imposition by a distant government, and the men who evaded it, who were often assisted by local networks with an interest in maintaining their loyalty, enhanced their local reputation through that very defiance of state authority. The draft evasion problem that plagued the Italian army in Sicily for decades after unification was simultaneously a symptom of popular alienation from the state and a mechanism through which local criminal networks built obligations and goodwill.

Third, and perhaps most consequentially in the short term, the transition from Bourbon to Italian rule created a period of institutional uncertainty and political instability in which the Mafia was able to consolidate its position as an essential intermediary between the new state and Sicilian society. The new Italian prefects and police chiefs sent to administer Sicily had limited knowledge of the island, limited local networks of reliable information, and an urgent need to establish some minimum of order and political stability in their new territories. The local men who could deliver that stability, who could organize electoral support for the government party, who could prevent the most flagrant outbreaks of violence in rural areas, and who could serve as intermediaries between the state and a population that did not speak standard Italian and had no tradition of cooperation with official authority, were precisely the mafia bosses who were simultaneously building their criminal enterprises.

Multiple Perspectives

Italian Unification as Experienced by Different Groups in Sicily

From the perspective of northern Italian liberals and nationalists: Unification was the culmination of the Risorgimento, a decades-long struggle to unite the fragmented Italian peninsula into a single modern nation-state. The incorporation of Sicily and the Bourbon South removed a corrupt and reactionary monarchy and brought the benefits of modern liberal institutions, including constitutional government, equality before the law, and free trade, to populations that had long suffered under feudal and clerical oppression. The challenges of governing the South were real but would be overcome through patient application of good laws and sound administration.
From the perspective of the Sicilian aristocracy: Unification threatened the political autonomy and local dominance that the Sicilian nobility had enjoyed under Bourbon rule, however much they had resented Bourbon policies at times. The abolition of feudal privileges, the new tax regime, and the imposition of northern legal codes disrupted the traditional arrangements through which aristocratic families had controlled land, labor, and local government. At the same time, unification offered new opportunities for families willing to participate in national political life and find accommodation with the Piedmontese rulers.
From the perspective of Sicilian peasants: For the rural poor, unification changed almost nothing that mattered and made several things worse. The new state brought the same extractive relationship, through taxes, conscription, and the legal enforcement of property rights that consistently favored landlords over tenants, that the Bourbon state had provided. The distribution of church and common lands bypassed them entirely. Military conscription took their sons away for years. The courts remained inaccessible, expensive, and partial. Whatever hope the revolutionary period of 1860 had briefly offered was rapidly disappointed.
From the perspective of the emerging Mafia networks: Unification created extraordinary opportunities. The dissolution of Bourbon administrative networks meant that new intermediaries were needed between the state and Sicilian society. The auction of church lands created opportunities to acquire property and leverage over those who had purchased land with borrowed money. The new state's need for political support in Sicily, where it had no natural constituency, made it dependent on local power brokers who could deliver votes and maintain order — precisely the function that mafia bosses could perform in exchange for political protection and impunity. The temporary disorder of the transition period allowed the consolidation of territorial control that would prove durable for generations.

The 1866 revolt in Palermo, in which popular insurrection temporarily drove the Italian state out of the capital, illustrated how fragile the new order was and how completely the new government depended on the cooperation of local power brokers to maintain any semblance of control. The suppression of the revolt required not only military force but political deals that gave local intermediaries, including individuals with criminal connections, effective guarantees of continued autonomy in their own territories in exchange for their support of state order. These deals were not explicitly written down. They were understood as mutual arrangements of convenience that bound the new Italian state and the emergent Mafia networks in a relationship that would define Sicilian politics for the next century and a half.

Section 6.4

What the Mafia Was and Was Not: Defining the Institution

One of the most persistent problems in the scholarly study of the Sicilian Mafia is definitional. What exactly was the Mafia? Was it a single organization with a unified command, a shared membership, and common rules? Or was it a collection of independent local groups sharing certain cultural codes and operational methods without any formal unification? Was it primarily an economic enterprise, a political organization, a cultural phenomenon, or some complex combination of all three? These questions are not merely academic. How the Mafia is defined determines how it is studied, how it is prosecuted, and how its power and persistence are explained.

The earliest observers of the Mafia, in the 1860s and 1870s, struggled with precisely these questions. Some police prefects and government officials described the Mafia as if it were a single secret society with formal membership, fixed hierarchies, and coordinated command structures, comparable to revolutionary secret societies like the Carbonari that had been active in the earlier decades of the century. This model was politically convenient because it suggested that the Mafia could be suppressed by identifying and arresting its leaders, severing its command chain, and prosecuting its members under laws designed for conspiracy. But it did not accurately describe the reality of what was on the ground.

The social investigator Leopoldo Franchetti, whose 1877 report on Sicily remains one of the foundational documents in the history of Mafia scholarship, offered a more nuanced and more accurate account. Franchetti described the Mafia not as a single organization but as a class of violent entrepreneurs who operated across a range of economic activities, using the threat and application of violence to extract rents from every productive activity they could reach. What unified these men was not a formal organizational structure but a shared method, a shared code of conduct, and a shared social position at the boundary between legitimate economic activity and criminal predation. The Mafia, in Franchetti's analysis, was the violent face of the intermediary class, the organized expression of the gabelloto system's inherent tendency toward criminal coercion.

More recent scholarship, drawing on documentary evidence unavailable to nineteenth-century observers, has refined this picture considerably. The Mafia does appear to have had more formal organizational structure than Franchetti's model suggested, at least at the local level. The basic organizational unit, the cosca, had recognized membership, leadership, and territorial boundaries. New members were formally initiated through rituals that included oaths of loyalty and symbolic gestures whose precise form varied somewhat but whose function was consistent: to create a sense of binding commitment and to mark clearly the boundary between those inside the group and those outside it. Disputes between cosche were resolved through recognized procedures involving representatives from neutral groups. These organizational features are more consistent with a loose confederation of locally autonomous groups sharing common identity markers and intergroup rules than with either a single unified organization or a purely informal cultural phenomenon.

Historical Vocabulary
Cosca

A cosca (plural: cosche) is the basic organizational unit of the Sicilian Mafia. The word is Sicilian dialect for the dense cluster of inner leaves at the heart of an artichoke, a metaphor that conveys the closed, inward-facing, self-protective nature of the group. A cosca controlled a specific territory, typically a town, a neighborhood, or an agricultural district, and extracted payments from all significant economic activities within that territory.

Each cosca was led by a recognized boss, or capo, whose authority was based on a combination of personal reputation for toughness and intelligence, demonstrated ability to protect the group's members from external threats (including from the state), and capacity to resolve internal disputes fairly enough to maintain cohesion. Membership in a cosca was formalized through an initiation ritual and carried both obligations, including absolute loyalty, strict observance of omertà, and willingness to commit violence when ordered, and benefits, including access to the group's protection and a share of its revenues.

The cosca system meant that the Mafia was simultaneously a unified institution in its shared culture and methods and a decentralized one in its day-to-day operation. Different cosche controlled different territories and operated largely independently within those territories, though intergroup relations were governed by recognized protocols for resolving disputes and dividing revenue streams.

It is also important to distinguish between what might be called the criminal Mafia and the broader cultural phenomenon of mafiosità that permeated Sicilian society more widely. The word "mafioso," in nineteenth-century Sicilian popular usage, did not refer exclusively to members of criminal organizations. It referred to a particular type of man: one who carried himself with a certain arrogant self-confidence, who refused to bow before authority, who settled his own affairs rather than appealing to the state, who commanded respect through the implicit or explicit threat of violence, and who maintained absolute loyalty to those he counted as allies while remaining implacably hostile to those he regarded as enemies. This cultural figure was admired in popular culture, celebrated in theatrical entertainments like the famous 1863 play about the prisoners of the Palermo jail, and connected to older traditions of masculine honor that were not specific to organized crime.

The criminal Mafia grew from this cultural soil, but it was distinct from it. Not every Sicilian who embodied mafiosità was a mafioso in the criminal sense. The cultural code provided the raw material from which criminal organization was built, the shared assumptions about honor, violence, loyalty, and silence that made it possible for men who had no formal legal relationship to one another to cooperate in criminal enterprise with sufficient trust and predictability to make that enterprise durable. But the criminal organization required something more: the explicit commitment to criminal activity, the formal initiation, the territorial monopoly on violence, and the protection racket as a primary economic activity.

Section 6.5

Omertà, Violence, and the Code of Silence

No feature of the Sicilian Mafia has attracted more popular attention or more serious scholarly analysis than omertà, the code of silence that bound both Mafia members and those in the broader community who had dealings with them. Omertà was not simply a rule against talking to the police, though it certainly encompassed that. It was a comprehensive ethical code governing the management of information, the resolution of disputes, and the relationship between individuals and external authorities. Understanding omertà is essential to understanding how the Mafia achieved and maintained its power across generations.

The word omertà is believed to derive from the Sicilian dialect word for manliness or masculine dignity, suggesting its origins in the broader culture of masculine honor rather than in criminal organization specifically. In its deepest sense, omertà expressed the conviction that a man of genuine dignity and self-sufficiency resolved his own problems, suffered his own injuries, and settled his own scores without recourse to outside authority. To go to the police or the courts with a complaint was not merely impractical. In the culture of omertà, it was a confession of weakness, a demonstration that one lacked the capacity to manage one's own affairs, an act of subordination to an alien authority that forfeited the respect of one's community.

This cultural dimension of omertà was real and important, but it should not obscure the coercive dimension that was equally real and probably more important in explaining the code's effectiveness. In communities where the Mafia exercised power, omertà was not merely a voluntary cultural preference. It was enforced by the credible threat of lethal violence against those who violated it. The witnesses and victims who cooperated with state investigators, who testified in court, or who identified perpetrators to the police were not simply breaking a cultural norm. They were making themselves targets for assassination, and they knew it. The murder of informants and witnesses was a regular and well-documented feature of the early Mafia's activity, and the message was not lost on those who might otherwise have been tempted to cooperate with official investigations.

Historical Vocabulary
Omertà

Omertà is the code of silence maintained by Mafia members and enforced, through the threat of violence, upon others in the community who have knowledge of Mafia activities. The word likely derives from a Sicilian dialect word connected to the concept of masculine dignity and self-sufficiency. In its cultural dimension, omertà expressed the deep Sicilian distrust of external authority and the conviction that a man of honor resolved his own problems without appealing to the state.

In its criminal-organizational dimension, omertà was a mechanism of organizational security that made it extremely difficult for state authorities to gather evidence against Mafia members. Because witnesses to Mafia crimes knew that testifying would expose them to retaliation, and because the Mafia's capacity for retaliation was well established and widely believed, the supply of witnesses willing to cooperate with official investigations was nearly zero. This meant that even when police and prosecutors knew with considerable certainty who had committed particular crimes, they were often unable to build cases that could withstand the scrutiny of a court.

The effectiveness of omertà depended on two conditions being simultaneously true: the threat of retaliation had to be credible, meaning that the Mafia had to actually kill informants regularly enough to establish the reality of the threat; and the state's capacity to protect informants from retaliation had to be limited, meaning that people rationally concluded that the risks of cooperation outweighed its benefits. Both conditions prevailed in nineteenth-century Sicily, and the combination produced a wall of silence that protected the Mafia's criminal enterprises from prosecution for generations.

The Mafia's use of violence was more strategic than is sometimes understood from popular accounts. The indiscriminate use of violence was actually counterproductive to the Mafia's interests because it attracted unwanted attention from the state, disrupted the economic activities from which the Mafia extracted revenue, and alienated the community whose at least passive cooperation was necessary for the Mafia's continued operation. The most effective Mafia bosses were those who understood that the threat of violence was more economically valuable than its actual exercise, because the threat produced compliance without the costs and complications that killing produced.

What this meant in practice was that Mafia violence tended to be concentrated on specific targets for specific purposes: the elimination of rivals who refused to respect territorial agreements, the assassination of state officials or witnesses who represented serious threats to Mafia security, the punishment of members who violated the organization's internal codes, and the occasional spectacular act of violence that served as a demonstration to the broader community that the Mafia's threats were not empty. Between these targeted acts of violence, the Mafia relied on its reputation, carefully cultivated through decades of demonstrated capacity for retribution, to extract compliance without needing to resort to force on a daily basis.

The rationality of the Mafia's violence extended to its relationship with the broader community. In most circumstances, the Mafia had no interest in harming ordinary peasants or workers who paid their protection fees, respected Mafia authority, and maintained omertà. What it wanted from most people was not their suffering but their money and their silence. Indiscriminate violence against the population in general would have destroyed the economic base from which the Mafia extracted its revenues and would have produced a popular reaction that could have been dangerous. The Mafia's violence was, in this sense, economically disciplined: it was deployed precisely enough to maintain the credibility of threats without so broadly as to destabilize the system of extraction that was the organization's fundamental purpose.

Section 6.6

Early Mafia Families and Territorial Organization

The Mafia's territorial organization in nineteenth-century Sicily was built on the cosca system, but the practical reality of how these groups established and maintained their territorial claims was more complex and more contested than any organizational chart could suggest. Territory was not simply assigned. It was seized, defended, negotiated, and sometimes lost through a continuous process of competition, alliance, and conflict that made the early Mafia landscape a dynamic and often violent arena.

The basic territorial unit was typically a town or a district of rural agricultural land. A cosca that controlled the town of Bagheria, for example, expected to receive payments from every significant economic activity in and around that town: from the citrus growers who needed protection from theft and who needed access to markets, from the merchants who traded in the town's market, from the contractors who built its buildings, from the operators of its inns and gambling establishments, and from anyone else whose economic activity could be taxed. The cosca that controlled Bagheria expected that no economic actor in its territory would make important transactions or resolve important disputes without its knowledge and, if necessary, its approval.

Neighboring cosche operated their own territories on the same model, and the boundaries between territories were matters of considerable importance and considerable sensitivity. When economic activities, trade routes, or family relationships crossed territorial boundaries, protocols were required for managing the resulting questions of jurisdiction. Who collected the protection payment from a merchant who operated in two different towns? Who resolved a dispute between a member of one cosca and a member of another? These questions were settled through inter-group negotiations, mediated by senior figures whose status transcended local territorial boundaries, and the failure to resolve them satisfactorily was often the prelude to violent conflict.

Certain locations became particularly important as meeting points, arbitration venues, and symbolic centers of Mafia authority. In Palermo, the network of cosche that had established themselves in various neighborhoods and in the surrounding agricultural districts developed informal arrangements for coordinating their activities, resolving disputes, and dividing the revenues from activities that crossed neighborhood boundaries. The markets of Palermo, where produce from the Conca d'Oro passed through wholesale intermediaries before reaching retailers and ultimately consumers, were a particularly lucrative nexus point where multiple cosche had intersecting interests and where coordination was both economically essential and organizationally challenging.

The documentation of specific early Mafia families and their leaders is necessarily incomplete. The deliberate secrecy that was central to the Mafia's operational model means that most of what happened within cosche in the early decades was never recorded in any written source. What survives in the historical record comes primarily from police reports, court documents, newspaper accounts, and the reports of parliamentary investigators and social scientists, all of which filtered their information through particular institutional and ideological lenses. Nevertheless, certain names and certain families emerge from the historical record as particularly significant in the early consolidation of Mafia power in specific territories.

In the towns around Palermo, the names that appear repeatedly in police records from the 1870s onward are associated with particular agricultural districts: the lemon-growing villages of the Conca d'Oro, the sulfur-mining towns of the interior, the fishing and commercial communities of the coast. In each location, the pattern is similar: a small group of men, typically with roots in the local agricultural intermediary class, who have established a monopoly on the use of violence within their territory and who use that monopoly to extract payments from the full range of economic activities within their reach.

What is striking about the early Mafia families, in contrast to later mythological representations, is how thoroughly embedded in local agricultural and commercial life they were. The early Mafia boss was not typically a flamboyant criminal entrepreneur operating in a world separate from respectable society. He was often a man who combined criminal activities with conventional economic roles as a landowner, contractor, produce merchant, or livestock dealer. The criminal enterprise was not separate from the legitimate economy but woven through it at every level, extracting rents from productive activity while appearing, to the casual observer, to be simply one more participant in ordinary economic life.

Section 6.7

The Mafia and the State: Penetrating Politics and Justice

One of the most important and most disturbing features of the early Sicilian Mafia was its relationship to the formal apparatus of the Italian state. The Mafia did not simply operate in the spaces left by state weakness. It actively cultivated relationships with state officials, politicians, judges, and policemen that gave it protection from prosecution and, in some cases, positive assistance in eliminating rivals and settling scores. This relationship between organized crime and political power was not incidental to the Mafia's success. It was central to it.

The mechanism by which the Mafia penetrated politics was, in the Italian political context of the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, surprisingly straightforward. The Italian political system was built on the practice of trasformismo, the systematic co-optation of political opponents through the distribution of patronage and favors. National politicians, who needed to build majorities in the Italian parliament, could only govern with the support of deputies from southern constituencies. Those deputies could only win elections if they could deliver votes, and they could only deliver votes in Sicily if they had the cooperation of local power brokers who controlled the social networks through which political mobilization occurred. Those local power brokers were often precisely the Mafia bosses who controlled the towns and agricultural districts from which the deputies came.

The arrangement was mutually beneficial. The politician got the votes. The Mafia boss got political protection: assurances that police prefects would not be too energetic in their investigations, that prosecutors would find their cases mysteriously impeded, that trials would result in acquittals that reflected not the evidence but the relationships between the defendant and the local political establishment. Individual Mafia members who were charged with crimes could expect, if their boss had maintained the appropriate political relationships, that witnesses would fail to appear, that evidence would be lost, and that the verdict would reflect political reality rather than legal merit.

Institutional Connection

Organized Crime and Democratic Institutions

The relationship between the early Sicilian Mafia and Italian democratic institutions raises fundamental questions about the vulnerability of electoral systems to criminal capture, questions that are relevant not only to nineteenth-century Sicily but to democracies around the world in the present day.

The Italian parliamentary system of the Risorgimento era was a liberal constitutional monarchy modeled loosely on the British parliamentary system. It featured elected representatives, constitutional protections for civil liberties, and formal separation of powers between the legislative, executive, and judicial branches. In formal terms, it was one of the more advanced democratic systems in continental Europe at the time of its establishment.

Yet this formally democratic system proved highly vulnerable to manipulation by organized criminal networks. The combination of limited suffrage (voting was restricted to literate men with property in the early decades), the importance of local notables in mobilizing political support, the culture of trasformismo that encouraged political flexibility and patronage over principled opposition, and the practical impossibility of enforcing laws impartially in communities where witnesses refused to testify and officials feared retaliation, all meant that the formal mechanisms of democratic accountability were systematically undermined in Sicily by Mafia penetration.

The question this raises for students of organized crime and political institutions is fundamental: under what conditions can democratic systems resist criminal capture, and what structural features make them more or less vulnerable? The Sicilian case suggests that formal democratic institutions are insufficient protection against organized crime when they operate in conditions of extreme economic inequality, limited state capacity, and a political culture that rewards patronage over principle. These conditions are not unique to nineteenth-century Sicily, and the lessons of the Sicilian case have been applied by scholars and policymakers studying democratic vulnerability in many other contexts.

The penetration of the justice system was particularly consequential. Mafia members who were arrested and tried in Sicilian courts faced a system that was, at multiple points, vulnerable to Mafia influence. Witnesses could be intimidated or killed. Defense attorneys could be well compensated to pursue procedural strategies designed to delay and obstruct prosecution indefinitely. Judges and prosecutors who were ambitious, who lived in Sicilian communities and had families there, faced implicit or explicit pressure to manage their professional conduct in ways that avoided provoking the anger of powerful local interests. And juries, composed of ordinary Sicilians who lived in the same communities as Mafia bosses and their associates, operated under conditions of fear and social pressure that made verdicts reflecting the evidence rather than the threat environment genuinely heroic rather than merely civic acts.

The result was a justice system that functioned as a near-perfect protection mechanism for the Mafia. Arrest rates for crimes committed in Mafia territories were low because victims did not report. Prosecution rates for those who were arrested were low because witnesses did not testify. Conviction rates for those who were prosecuted were low because juries did not convict. And even those who were convicted sometimes obtained their freedom through appeals, pardons, and the commutation of sentences in which political influence could be brought to bear. The Mafia did not need to corrupt every single official in the system. It needed only to ensure that enough of them were compromised or frightened enough to make the probability of meaningful accountability so low that the residual risk was worth accepting.

Section 6.8

Mafia Culture and Sicilian Society: Honor, Identity, and the Social Meaning of Criminal Power

The Mafia did not exist in a social vacuum. It was embedded in the fabric of Sicilian society in ways that gave it cultural resources, social networks, and a degree of popular legitimacy, however coerced and however partial, that made it something more than simply a criminal enterprise operating against the wishes of the communities it exploited. Understanding the relationship between the Mafia and the broader culture of nineteenth-century Sicily is essential to understanding why the organization proved so durable and why its suppression proved so difficult.

At the core of Sicilian masculine culture was a code of honor that placed enormous value on personal dignity, self-sufficiency, and the capacity to protect one's family and community from external threat. This honor code was not unique to Sicily. Versions of it existed across the Mediterranean world and in many other cultural contexts where state authority was weak and personal security depended on the reputation of individuals and family groups. But in Sicily, the combination of extreme economic inequality, centuries of foreign rule, and the systematic failure of official authority to protect ordinary people had given the honor code a particular intensity and a particular relationship to violence that distinguished it from its analogues elsewhere.

In the world of the honor code, the man who submitted meekly to insult, theft, or injury without seeking redress was not merely unfortunate. He was diminished in the eyes of his community. He had demonstrated that he could be exploited without consequences, a signal that invited further exploitation. The man who responded to insult or injury with effective retaliation, by contrast, was respected, because he had demonstrated that dealing with him carried risks. This logic of honor and reputation was deeply rational in environments where official protection was unavailable. But it also created conditions in which violence was not simply a pathological aberration but a recognized and in some respects celebrated means of asserting dignity and protecting interests.

The Mafia drew on this cultural resource in multiple ways. The Mafia boss embodied, in an extreme and organized form, the qualities that the honor code valued: he was a man of fearsome reputation who had demonstrated through past violence that he could not be challenged with impunity, who protected those who submitted to his authority, who resolved disputes fairly among his clients and ferociously against his enemies, and who maintained absolute loyalty to his own group while showing total ruthlessness toward those who betrayed it. These qualities were not merely criminal. In the cultural framework of nineteenth-century rural Sicily, they were forms of social power that commanded a specific kind of respect.

Everyday Life and Social History

Life in a Mafia Town: The Peasant World of Western Sicily

For the majority of ordinary Sicilians who lived in the rural towns and agricultural districts where the Mafia operated, the organization was not a distant abstraction. It was a presence felt in the practical details of daily economic and social life. Understanding what that daily experience looked like requires attending to the mundane textures of peasant existence in western Sicily in the second half of the nineteenth century.

The typical peasant household in the Mafia's heartland was desperately poor. A family might lease a small parcel of land from a gabelloto, growing grain, vegetables, or citrus under sharecropping arrangements that left them with a fraction of what they produced. The lease terms were reviewed annually, and the renewal was never guaranteed. A bad harvest, a dispute with a neighbor, or the gabelloto's simple preference for a different tenant could leave a family landless and without income with very little warning. Families supplemented agricultural income with day labor on the great estates during the harvest seasons, working alongside hundreds of other seasonal laborers in conditions that combined physical hardship with social humiliation.

In this environment, the Mafia's presence was felt not primarily through dramatic violent confrontations but through the constant, low-level pressure of informal taxation. The family that sold its grain in the local market paid a small percentage to the men who controlled market access. The peasant who needed to borrow money to survive until the next harvest paid interest to lenders with Mafia connections at rates that guaranteed continued indebtedness. The family whose animals were stolen, a common rural crime, could recover them only by paying a "finder's fee" to the men who had the connections to arrange the animals' return, often the same men who had arranged the theft. None of these transactions appeared in any official record. They were conducted in the informal economy of favors, obligations, threats, and payments that the Mafia had made its own.

The Mafia's presence in daily life was also felt through its role in local labor markets. On the great estates, the gabelloti who were becoming mafia bosses controlled access to seasonal agricultural employment. The peasant who offended these men might find himself excluded from harvest work, with catastrophic effects on his family's survival. The peasant who cultivated the appropriate relationships, who showed proper deference, who paid the expected informal dues, could expect that when labor was scarce, he would be among those who found work. This control of labor market access was not dramatically violent. But it was thoroughly coercive, because the alternative to compliance was destitution in an environment where no state safety net existed.

The experience of women in these communities was shaped by the Mafia's presence in specific ways. The honor code that provided the cultural foundation for Mafia authority was intensely patriarchal, and women were primarily valued as vessels of family honor that required protection and control by male relatives. The Mafia's regulation of sexual violence and the honor disputes that accompanied it was one of its claimed social functions, and in some respects the Mafia did provide a form of protection against predatory behavior toward women in its territory, though always on the Mafia's terms and for the Mafia's purposes rather than as any expression of genuine concern for women's welfare or autonomy.

It would be a serious distortion, however, to suggest that the Mafia enjoyed genuine popular support in the sense of voluntary, enthusiastic endorsement from the communities it controlled. The scholarly consensus is that the Mafia's relationship to Sicilian society was primarily one of coercion masked by cultural legitimization. People did not support the Mafia because they truly believed it was just or beneficial. They accommodated themselves to Mafia power because the alternatives, resistance or cooperation with the authorities, were practically extremely dangerous and offered little realistic prospect of improvement. The cultural legitimization of the Mafia, through the honor code, through the mythologization of Mafia figures in popular culture, and through the comparison of Mafia order to the disorder of an absent or predatory state, served the Mafia's interests by making accommodation seem not merely prudent but in some sense principled. But this legitimization was, in the end, a mechanism of domination rather than an expression of genuine popular preference.

Section 6.9

The First Documented Evidence: What Historians Know and How They Know It

The historical study of the early Sicilian Mafia confronts a fundamental evidentiary challenge. The Mafia's deliberate cultivation of secrecy, enforced through omertà and the threat of lethal violence against informants, meant that the organization generated almost no internal documentation, and the documentation it did produce, such as any written records of membership or financial accounts, was carefully destroyed. What historians know about the early Mafia comes almost entirely from external sources: police reports, court documents, parliamentary investigations, newspaper accounts, and the reports of social scientists and government investigators who observed the phenomenon from outside.

Each of these source types has significant limitations. Police reports were generated by officials who had institutional interests in presenting the Mafia in particular ways, sometimes minimizing its extent to avoid the embarrassment of admitting the limits of official control, sometimes exaggerating it to justify requests for additional resources or to explain the failure of prosecutions. Court documents were created by a justice system that was itself partially compromised by Mafia influence, which means that the acquittals and procedural failures that dominate the court record reflect not only legal inadequacy but deliberate obstruction. Parliamentary investigations were conducted by politicians who had their own interests in the question and who were sometimes themselves beneficiaries of the political arrangements that gave the Mafia its protection.

The most valuable single source for the early history of the Mafia's organizational structure is the report compiled by Ermanno Sangiorgi, the Chief of Police in Palermo between 1898 and 1900. Sangiorgi was one of the relatively rare Italian officials who combined genuine professional competence with personal integrity and sufficient political protection to conduct a serious investigation rather than a performative one. Over two years of systematic work, he compiled a detailed account of the Mafia families operating in and around Palermo, naming their members, describing their territorial boundaries, documenting their economic activities, and, most valuably, describing the rituals through which new members were initiated into the organization.

Primary Source Window

The Sangiorgi Report on the Mafia in Palermo (1898–1900)

Creator: Ermanno Sangiorgi, Chief of Police, Palermo  ·  Date: 1898–1900  ·  Type: Official police investigative report

Context: Ermanno Sangiorgi was appointed Chief of Police in Palermo in 1898, having previously served in various law enforcement capacities in other parts of Italy. He came to the assignment without the established local relationships that had compromised some of his predecessors and with a professional commitment to conducting a genuine investigation rather than a managed one. Over approximately two years, he cultivated informants, conducted surveillance, and assembled a detailed picture of Mafia organization in the Palermo area that was unprecedented in its specificity and accuracy.

What the report reveals: Sangiorgi's report described a structured organization of multiple territorially defined groups operating in a coordinated fashion across the greater Palermo area. He documented that membership was formalized through a specific initiation ritual in which the candidate was brought before existing members, his name was proposed for approval, and, if accepted, he underwent a ceremony that involved the pricking of his finger, the mixing of blood with a sacred image, the burning of that image, and the taking of an oath of loyalty to the group. The burning of the sacred image was explained to the initiate as a symbolic representation of what would happen to him if he violated his oath: just as the image burned, so would his flesh if he betrayed his brothers.

Sangiorgi described the initiation ritual as creating a bond that the initiated member understood to be permanent and irrevocable. He reported that members were taught to recognize fellow members through subtle signals, including particular ways of greeting, specific questions and responses, and physical gestures that would be invisible to the uninitiated but immediately recognized by a fellow mafioso. This recognition system allowed members of different local groups to identify one another during travel or in situations where normal social markers of group membership would be unavailable.

Significance and limitations: The Sangiorgi report is the most detailed and most credible early documentary evidence of Mafia organizational structure. Historians regard it as broadly accurate, not because it has been independently confirmed in all its details, which is impossible given the destruction of Mafia records, but because its account of initiation rituals, territorial organization, and intergroup relations matches well with evidence that emerged from much later prosecutions and from the testimony of Mafia defectors in the second half of the twentieth century. The consistency across a century of independent sources provides substantial confidence in the accuracy of Sangiorgi's core findings.

The report's limitations are also important. Sangiorgi was observing the Mafia from outside, through a layer of surveillance and informants whose own reliability and completeness of knowledge were not unlimited. He could describe what his informants told him and what his surveillance revealed, but he could not observe the internal deliberations of the groups he was studying. The picture he assembled was necessarily incomplete, and his interpretations of what he observed were colored by his institutional position and the conceptual frameworks available to a late nineteenth-century Italian law enforcement official.

Questions for students:

  2. Why might a police report be a particularly valuable, and also a particularly problematic, source for understanding the early Mafia? What institutional interests and structural limitations shape what police reports record and how they interpret what they observe?
  3. The Sangiorgi report was never acted upon effectively: the prosecutions that followed produced few convictions, and Sangiorgi was eventually transferred away from Palermo. What does this outcome tell historians about the relationship between knowledge and power in the struggle against organized crime?
  4. Historians writing about the early Mafia cannot simply quote the Sangiorgi report as definitive truth. What other types of sources would they need to consult, and what questions would they ask about the report's reliability, completeness, and perspective?

The parliamentary investigations of the 1870s, though less detailed than the Sangiorgi report, are also significant historical sources. The investigations conducted in the aftermath of the 1866 Palermo revolt and in the period of political crisis that followed produced a substantial body of testimony and analysis that established at the national level what local officials in Sicily had known for decades: that organized criminal networks with political protection were operating across the western part of the island and that normal law enforcement methods were insufficient to suppress them.

The work of Leopoldo Franchetti and Sidney Sonnino, who conducted their investigation of Sicily in 1876 and published their findings the following year, is particularly significant because it offered the first analytical account of the Mafia as a social institution rather than simply a collection of criminal individuals. Franchetti's analysis, in particular, identified the structural conditions, the feudal land system, the gabelloto class, the failure of the Bourbon and Italian states, that had produced the Mafia, and argued that suppressing it would require not merely more vigorous police work but fundamental changes in the economic and political conditions of Sicilian society. This structural analysis was, in many respects, more sophisticated than anything that Italian state policy would manage for another century, and it remains essential reading for anyone seeking to understand the Mafia's origins.

Chapter Review and Historical Thinking Checkpoint

This chapter has traced the emergence of the Sicilian Mafia from its roots in the feudal land system and political failures of Bourbon Sicily through its consolidation in the decades following Italian national unification. Students who have engaged with the chapter should understand the Mafia not as an inexplicable cultural pathology or a mere collection of violent criminals, but as an institution with specific historical causes, specific social functions, and specific relationships to the economic and political structures of the society in which it developed.

The central analytical framework that the chapter has developed can be summarized as follows: The Mafia emerged where state authority was too weak or too corrupt to provide reliable protection of property and persons; where economic structure concentrated coercive power in the hands of a middleman class whose position invited the formalization of that coercion into organized extortion; where a cultural code of masculine honor valorized self-help through violence and stigmatized cooperation with official authority; and where the political system created incentives for politicians to protect criminal networks in exchange for electoral support. These four conditions, structural state weakness, exploitative economic intermediation, honor culture, and political corruption, were not unique to Sicily, but they combined there with unusual intensity and durability, producing a criminal institution of extraordinary persistence and influence.

Historical Debate

What Caused the Mafia? Cultural Tradition, Economic Exploitation, or Political Failure?

The question: Historians and social scientists have debated for over a century what fundamental forces produced the Sicilian Mafia. This debate is not merely academic: how one explains the Mafia's origins has significant implications for how one thinks about suppressing it and whether similar organizations are likely to emerge in other contexts.

Interpretation A — The Culturalist Explanation: Some scholars, including early Italian observers and some twentieth-century anthropologists, argued that the Mafia was primarily a product of Sicilian cultural peculiarities: the honor code, the deep distrust of external authority, the valorization of masculine self-help through violence, and the social stigma attached to cooperation with the state. In this view, the Mafia is essentially a cultural phenomenon, the organized criminal expression of values and attitudes that are deeply embedded in Sicilian identity and that cannot be addressed through purely legal or economic measures without confronting the underlying culture. This interpretation was often used by northern Italian politicians to explain southern "backwardness" in terms that attributed cultural inferiority to Sicilians rather than examining structural conditions.
Interpretation B — The Economic and Structural Explanation: Scholars in the tradition of Franchetti and, more recently, economists and sociologists who have studied the Mafia quantitatively argue that the Mafia is primarily a product of structural economic and political conditions: the feudal land system, the gabelloto intermediary class, the failure of the state to provide reliable protection of property and persons, and the resulting demand for private protection services in a context where the state supply was inadequate. In this view, the cultural features of the Mafia, omertà, the honor code, the valorization of masculine violence, are not the cause of the Mafia but its products or its resources: cultural tools that the Mafia exploits to maintain its power but that are themselves the products of the structural conditions that created the demand for organized private protection.
Interpretation C — The Political Economy Explanation: A third interpretation, developed most influentially by scholars including Diego Gambetta and Leopoldo Franchetti in different ways, focuses on the Mafia as a specific response to a specific market failure: the failure of the state to provide reliable enforcement of contracts and protection of property. In this view, the Mafia should be understood primarily as a private protection industry, supplying a commodity, security and contract enforcement, that the state fails to supply. The Mafia's violence, secrecy, and cultural codes are not expressions of cultural pathology but rational institutional adaptations to the specific economic and political environment in which the organization operates.

Evidence and ongoing debate: All three interpretations draw on real features of the historical evidence. The culturalist explanation correctly identifies the importance of the honor code and state distrust as social preconditions of Mafia power. The structural explanation correctly identifies the economic and political conditions that created both the demand for private protection and the supply of men willing to provide it through violence. The political economy explanation correctly identifies the market logic of the protection racket and explains why the Mafia proved so economically durable. Most contemporary scholars regard these interpretations as complementary rather than mutually exclusive, emphasizing that the Mafia emerged from the interaction of cultural, structural, and economic conditions that reinforced one another.

Why it matters: The debate about the Mafia's origins is not merely historical. It has direct implications for policy: if the Mafia is primarily a cultural phenomenon, then suppression requires cultural transformation; if it is primarily structural, it requires economic reform; if it is primarily a response to state failure, it requires the building of effective state institutions. The evidence increasingly suggests that all three are necessary but none is sufficient alone.

Cause and Effect

The Rise of the Mafia as a Criminal Institution

Long-Term Causes

Centuries of foreign rule and state illegitimacy; the feudal latifondo system concentrating land in absentee aristocratic hands; the gabelloto intermediary class combining economic power over peasants with control of armed enforcement; deep cultural distrust of official authority and valorization of masculine self-help through violence.

Short-Term and Proximate Causes

The chaotic transition from Bourbon to Italian rule (1860–1866); the auction of church and common lands benefiting intermediary classes; military conscription generating resentment and creating obligation networks for those who evaded it; the new Italian state's need for local power brokers to deliver political order and electoral support.

The Development

By the 1870s and 1880s, organized criminal networks with formal membership, territorial control, recognized leadership, initiation rituals, and systematic extortion of economic activity had consolidated across western Sicily, with significant penetration of political institutions and the justice system.

Consequences

Chronic impunity for organized criminal violence in Sicily; systematic extraction of economic rents from agricultural, commercial, and urban activities; deep penetration of Italian politics by organized crime; mass emigration carrying Mafia networks and culture to the United States and elsewhere; a century and a half of criminal persistence that outlasted every suppression campaign.

Historical complexity: The Mafia's emergence cannot be reduced to any single cause. It required the simultaneous presence of structural state weakness, exploitative economic intermediation, enabling cultural codes, and political arrangements that gave criminal networks protection from accountability. Remove any one of these conditions, and the outcome would likely have been different. Their combination in nineteenth-century Sicily produced an institution of extraordinary durability and influence.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Violence, Governance, and Criminal Power in Sicily: From the Bourbon Era to the Early Italian State

What Changed

  • The formal political authority over Sicily changed from Bourbon to Italian, bringing new administrative systems, new legal codes, and new personnel
  • The formal abolition of feudal privilege changed the legal basis of landholding without immediately transforming the social relations of the land system
  • The Mafia formalized its organizational structure, developing explicit initiation rituals, territorial boundaries, and inter-group protocols for dispute resolution
  • The Mafia's political connections shifted from informal local influence under Bourbon rule to systematic penetration of the new parliamentary electoral system
  • The word "mafia" entered common usage, giving a name to a phenomenon that had previously circulated under various local descriptions

What Stayed the Same

  • The fundamental economic structure of the latifondo system and the gabelloto intermediary class persisted with minimal change despite nominal legal reform
  • The practical illegitimacy of state authority in the eyes of most Sicilians, rooted in centuries of foreign rule and extractive governance, continued under the new Italian state
  • The cultural codes of masculine honor, omertà, and distrust of external authority continued to provide the social environment in which criminal organization could thrive
  • The practical inability of the justice system to produce meaningful accountability for criminal violence in Mafia territories continued and in some respects worsened as political penetration deepened
  • The systematic poverty and economic insecurity of the Sicilian peasant majority continued, providing the social soil in which Mafia power was rooted

Why the pattern matters: The combination of formal institutional change and substantive social continuity across the 1860 unification illustrates a pattern that recurs throughout the history of organized crime: legal and political transformations that appear significant at the level of formal institutions often produce much less change at the level of the social and economic conditions that sustain criminal power. The Mafia survived the transition from Bourbon to Italian rule not because it was too strong to be suppressed but because the conditions that had produced it were too deeply embedded in Sicilian economic and political life to be transformed by a change in the name of the government that formally claimed sovereignty over the island.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has examined the origins of the Sicilian Mafia, tracing its emergence from the structural conditions of feudal Sicily through its consolidation as a recognizable criminal institution in the decades following Italian national unification. The chapter has argued that the Mafia was not a random occurrence or an expression of innate cultural pathology, but a historically specific response to historically specific conditions: the failure of the state, the exploitation of the feudal land system, the cultural resources of the honor code, and the political incentives that protected criminal networks from accountability.

Sicily's geography, combining isolated interior communities with poor communications and a political history of relentless foreign exploitation, created conditions in which centralized authority was always difficult to project reliably. The Bourbon kingdom's particular brand of misrule added the dimension of active illegitimacy to the existing condition of practical weakness, ensuring that the population of western Sicily had little reason to look to official institutions for protection or justice. Into this vacuum of legitimate state authority, informal power structures, rooted in the coercive capacity of the gabelloto class and sanctioned by the cultural codes of masculine honor, gradually formalized into what observers and participants would eventually call the Mafia.

The economic roots of the Mafia lay in the latifondo system and the position of the gabelloti who managed it. These men, controlling access to land, labor, credit, and markets for an impoverished and dependent peasant population, possessed the structural prerequisites for criminal extortion before they needed to invent any new methods of coercion. The transition from agricultural exploitation to organized protection racket was gradual, contested, and never uniform across the island, but the direction of movement was consistent: toward the systematic extraction of economic rents from every productive activity within a defined territory, backed by the credible threat of violence against those who refused to pay or who sought alternative forms of protection.

Italian national unification, far from solving this problem, complicated it in ways that facilitated the Mafia's consolidation. The dissolution of church and common lands enriched the intermediary class while impoverishing the peasantry. Military conscription generated resentment and created obligation networks for those who evaded it with Mafia assistance. The new state's need for local intermediaries to deliver political order and electoral support in territories it did not understand created structural incentives for Mafia bosses to position themselves as essential political partners. And the failure of the new legal system to enforce accountability in Mafia territories, blocked by omertà, witness intimidation, and the penetration of political connections into the justice system, allowed the organization to develop with near-complete impunity.

The Mafia's organizational structure, as revealed in the first systematic documentary evidence from the 1870s onward and most clearly in the Sangiorgi report of 1898 to 1900, combined territorial specificity at the local level with shared cultural codes and intergroup protocols that gave the institution a degree of coherence and coordination beyond what any single cosca could achieve alone. The initiation ritual, the code of omertà, the recognition signals, and the mechanisms for inter-group dispute resolution all functioned to maintain organizational boundaries, ensure operational security, and make the kind of sustained collective action that serious criminal enterprise requires possible in the absence of legal contract enforcement.

The cultural dimensions of the Mafia's power, its relationship to the honor code, its mythologization in popular culture, and its capacity to present itself as a form of social order in the absence of legitimate state order, were not decorative additions to a fundamentally economic institution. They were mechanisms by which the Mafia sustained its power across generations, generating the passive acquiescence of populations it could not realistically govern through violence alone. Understanding this cultural dimension is essential to understanding why the Mafia proved so durable and why purely coercive suppression strategies consistently failed to eliminate it.

The political penetration of the Mafia, from the local level of municipal government and the courts up through the national parliamentary system, illustrates one of the most important lessons in the history of organized crime: that criminal organizations that can embed themselves in political institutions acquire a protective layer that makes them qualitatively more difficult to suppress than those that operate purely outside the formal system. The Mafia's political connections were not incidental to its survival. They were one of its most important structural advantages, converting the potential enemies represented by state power into at least passive allies and sometimes active partners.

The historical significance of the Sicilian Mafia's origins extends far beyond the island. The organization's methods, its culture, and many of its personnel traveled to the United States with the great wave of Sicilian emigration that began in the 1890s, producing the American criminal organizations that would shape organized crime in the United States for most of the twentieth century. Understanding the Sicilian roots of American organized crime is therefore not a matter of historical interest alone. It is essential background for understanding the developments examined in later chapters of this textbook, from the rise of American gangsters in the early twentieth century through the peak of La Cosa Nostra's power in the mid-century decades and the federal prosecutions that sought to dismantle it in the 1980s and beyond.

More broadly, the Sicilian case remains, more than a century and a half after the Mafia's consolidation, one of the most thoroughly studied and analytically richest examples of how organized crime emerges, how it sustains itself, and what conditions would be necessary to genuinely suppress it. The lessons of this history have been applied by scholars and policymakers studying organized crime in Russia, Mexico, Colombia, and many other contexts where criminal organizations have embedded themselves in state and economic institutions with comparable tenacity. The Mafia may have been a product of specific Sicilian conditions, but the general principles it illustrates about the relationship between state failure, economic inequality, political corruption, and criminal power are universal.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Absentee landlordism
The practice, common among Sicilian aristocrats, of owning large agricultural estates while residing in urban centers, delegating all local management to hired intermediaries. This created the gabelloto class and the conditions for organized criminal exploitation of dependent peasant labor.
Brigandage
Organized banditry, typically in rural areas, that combined theft and extortion with a degree of popular defiance of state authority. A precursor and partial parallel to Mafia organization in rural Sicily and southern Italy more broadly.
Campieri
The armed guards employed by gabelloti and later by Mafia bosses to patrol agricultural estates and enforce the authority of the estate manager over the peasant labor force. The campieri were among the early practitioners of organized criminal violence in the latifondo system.
Conca d'Oro
The fertile citrus-growing plain surrounding Palermo, whose complex arrangements of water rights, land tenure, and marketing networks made it one of the earliest and most lucrative targets of Mafia extortion and control.
Cosca
The basic territorial organizational unit of the Sicilian Mafia, a formally constituted group with recognized membership, leadership, and territorial boundaries. The word derives from the dense inner leaves of an artichoke, suggesting a closed, inward-facing group defined by mutual loyalty and shared secrecy.
Gabelloto
A leaseholder who rented large agricultural estates from absentee noble landowners in Sicily and sublet them at higher rates to peasant farmers. The gabelloto class combined economic power over dependent peasants with control of armed enforcement and minimal accountability, providing the social base from which many early Mafia bosses emerged.
Honor code
The cultural system, sometimes called mafiosità in its Sicilian form, that placed supreme value on masculine self-sufficiency, personal dignity, and the resolution of conflicts through private means rather than appeal to official authority. This cultural code provided the social environment in which criminal organization could develop.
Impunity
Freedom from punishment or accountability for criminal acts. In the Sicilian context, Mafia impunity was produced by the combination of omertà, witness intimidation, political protection of criminal networks, and the systematic failure of the justice system to produce meaningful accountability for organized criminal violence.
Initiation ritual
The formal ceremony through which new members were inducted into Mafia cosche, typically involving an oath of loyalty, symbolic gestures such as blood-mixing and the burning of a sacred image, and instruction in the recognition signals used by members to identify one another. The ritual marked a clear boundary between members and non-members and created a sense of permanent, irrevocable commitment.
Latifondi
The great feudal agricultural estates that dominated much of Sicily's interior landscape, typically owned by absentee aristocrats and worked by peasants under exploitative sharecropping and tenancy arrangements. The latifondo system concentrated economic and social power in ways that directly enabled the emergence of the Mafia.
Mafia
The criminal organization, or more precisely the network of territorially organized criminal groups, that developed in western Sicily from the mid-nineteenth century onward. Characterized by territorial monopoly on the use of violence, formalized membership, systematic extortion of economic activity, a code of silence enforced through the threat of lethal violence, and deep penetration of political institutions.
Mafiosità
The broader cultural attitude of defiant self-assertion, masculine honor, contempt for official authority, and preference for resolving disputes through personal force rather than institutional appeal that characterized Sicilian popular culture and provided the cultural soil from which criminal Mafia organization grew.
Omertà
The code of silence maintained by Mafia members and enforced through the threat of lethal violence upon others who had knowledge of Mafia activities. Omertà was simultaneously a cultural expression of the Sicilian distrust of official authority and a mechanism of organizational security that protected the Mafia from prosecution by making reliable witnesses nearly impossible to find.
Patron-client network
A social arrangement in which a more powerful individual (patron) provides protection, resources, and access to opportunity for less powerful individuals (clients) in exchange for loyalty, deference, and political or economic support. The Mafia operated as a criminal form of patron-client network, providing protection and dispute resolution services in exchange for payments and cooperation.
Pizzo
The regular payment extracted by Mafia groups from businesses and individuals in exchange for protection. The term is Sicilian dialect and refers to the "beak" of a bird, suggesting the small, regular, nibbling quality of the payment. The pizzo was the Mafia's most basic and most universal revenue mechanism.
Protection racket
The criminal enterprise of demanding payment from individuals or businesses in exchange for protection against harm, typically harm that the criminal organization itself orchestrates or threatens. The protection racket was the fundamental business model of the early Mafia.
Questione Meridionale
The "Southern Question," the persistent debate within Italian politics and intellectual life about why the South, including Sicily, remained so much poorer, less stable, and more violent than the North after unification. The Mafia became one of the central exhibits in this debate.
Risorgimento
The Italian national unification movement of the nineteenth century, culminating in the proclamation of the Kingdom of Italy in 1861. For Sicily, the Risorgimento brought not liberation but the replacement of one set of alien rulers with another, creating conditions that facilitated rather than impeded the Mafia's consolidation.
State capture
The process by which organized criminal or other private interests so thoroughly penetrate state institutions, including the police, courts, legislature, and executive, that those institutions cease to function as instruments of the public interest and instead serve the interests of the capturing group. The early Sicilian Mafia achieved a degree of state capture in local Sicilian government that was remarkable even by the standards of nineteenth-century European corruption.
Territorial monopoly
The exclusive control of a defined geographic area claimed and enforced by a Mafia cosca. Within its territory, a cosca expected to be the sole authorized supplier of protection and the sole authorized resolver of disputes, and it enforced this monopoly through violence against competitors who attempted to operate within its boundaries without authorization.
Trasformismo
The Italian political practice of governing through flexible coalitions built on patronage and personal favors rather than ideological principle, developed most fully in the era of Prime Minister Agostino Depretis. Trasformismo created the structural incentives for national politicians to accommodate rather than suppress Mafia networks in Sicily, because Mafia bosses could deliver the electoral support that national politicians needed.
Vendetta
A blood feud in which acts of violence between families or groups generate cycles of retaliation that can persist across generations. The vendetta tradition was related to the honor code and provided a pre-Mafia form of private violence in Sicilian society. The Mafia sometimes positioned itself as a mediating institution that could resolve vendettas, though it also generated vendettas of its own between competing groups.

Major People and Places

Identification & Significance

Leopoldo Franchetti (1847–1917)
Italian liberal investigator whose 1877 report on Sicily offered the first systematic analytical account of the Mafia as a social institution rooted in structural conditions. Franchetti argued that the Mafia could not be suppressed through police action alone but required fundamental economic and political reform, a judgment that remains relevant to organized crime suppression policy around the world.
Sidney Sonnino (1847–1922)
Co-author with Franchetti of the landmark 1877 investigation of Sicily; focused on peasant conditions and the land system. Later became a significant Italian statesman, serving twice as Prime Minister, though his and Franchetti's recommendations for Sicily were never seriously implemented.
Ermanno Sangiorgi (1835–1908)
The Chief of Police in Palermo from 1898 to 1900 whose detailed investigative report on Mafia families remains the most valuable single primary source for the early organizational structure of the Sicilian Mafia. Sangiorgi's efforts produced few prosecutions, illustrating the limits of knowledge without political will and institutional integrity throughout the justice system.
Giuseppe Garibaldi (1807–1882)
The nationalist military hero whose 1860 Expedition of the Thousand destroyed Bourbon rule in Sicily and opened the way for Italian unification. His campaign was followed by a period of instability in Sicily that the Mafia exploited to consolidate its territorial control and political connections.
Palermo
The capital of Sicily and center of Mafia power from the organization's earliest years. Palermo combined a wealthy urban aristocracy with a large population of urban poor, surrounding agricultural districts under intense Mafia control, and a political class thoroughly penetrated by criminal networks. The city remains a center of scholarly and journalistic attention on the Mafia to the present day.
The Conca d'Oro
The fertile citrus-growing plain surrounding Palermo, one of the earliest and most lucrative domains of Mafia extortion. Control of water rights and market access in the Conca d'Oro was among the Mafia's first systematic criminal enterprises and illustrates how criminal power attaches itself to valuable economic resources.
The Bourbon Kingdom of the Two Sicilies
The monarchical state that ruled Sicily until Garibaldi's 1860 conquest. Bourbon misrule created the conditions of state illegitimacy and administrative weakness in which informal criminal power structures could consolidate. The kingdom's failures also shaped the deep popular distrust of official authority that the Mafia exploited.
Agostino Depretis (1813–1887)
Italian Prime Minister through much of the 1870s and 1880s, architect of the political practice of trasformismo. Under Depretis, the Italian state's accommodation with Sicilian Mafia networks became a structural feature of national politics, as the need for parliamentary majorities created systematic incentives to protect rather than suppress criminal power brokers who could deliver southern electoral support.

This research project asks you to use the analytical framework developed in this chapter, focusing on the relationship between state weakness, economic inequality, cultural codes, and political penetration, to investigate a different criminal organization in a different historical or contemporary context. The goal is to test whether the conditions that produced the Sicilian Mafia are specific to that case or represent a more general pattern in the emergence of organized criminal power.

  1. Choose an organization to study. Select one criminal organization from outside the Sicilian Mafia tradition: possibilities include the Camorra of Naples, the 'Ndrangheta of Calabria, Russian organized crime networks, Mexican drug cartels, or a criminal organization from Asian or African contexts. Choose an organization about which library resources are available.
  2. Research the structural conditions. Using academic sources, including scholarly books, peer-reviewed journal articles, and credible investigative journalism, research the economic, political, and cultural conditions in which your chosen organization emerged. Pay particular attention to the four conditions identified in this chapter: state weakness, exploitative economic intermediation, cultural codes that discourage cooperation with official authority, and political systems that create incentives to protect criminal networks.
  3. Apply the analytical framework. For each of the four conditions, assess whether and how it was present in your case. Where conditions were similar to the Sicilian case, explain the similarity. Where conditions were different, explain the difference and assess whether the difference affected the form that criminal organization took.
  4. Identify similarities and differences. On the basis of your research, identify the most important similarities and differences between your chosen organization and the early Sicilian Mafia as described in this chapter. What features of criminal organization seem to recur across different contexts? What features seem specific to particular historical situations?
  5. Write your analysis. In a 4–6 page essay or equivalent research presentation, present your findings. Explain the conditions that produced your chosen organization, apply the analytical framework from this chapter, assess similarities and differences with the Sicilian case, and draw conclusions about what the comparison reveals about the conditions that produce organized criminal power across different historical and geographic contexts.

Library resources to use: The MGU Library's collections in criminology, political science, and modern European, Latin American, and Asian history; the database holdings in JSTOR and other academic repositories for peer-reviewed scholarship; the library's collection of comparative organized crime studies; and the reference collection on Italian and Sicilian history.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter has offered an account of the Sicilian Mafia's origins that insists on historical specificity while drawing conclusions with broader relevance. The Mafia was not the product of timeless cultural tradition, of innate Sicilian criminality, or of any other explanation that removes organized crime from the realm of historical causation and places it in the realm of cultural destiny. It was the product of specific structural conditions, specific political arrangements, and specific human choices made under specific historical circumstances. That insistence on historical causation is not merely an academic nicety. It has profound practical implications: if organized crime is historically caused, it can in principle be historically addressed, by changing the conditions that produce it, even if the process of change is slow, difficult, and politically costly.

The Sicilian case also illustrates, with unusual clarity, the fundamental insight about organized crime that this textbook will return to throughout its remaining chapters: that organized crime does not simply fill the spaces left by state absence. It actively cultivates those spaces, prevents their closure, and converts the instruments of legitimate state power into mechanisms of criminal protection. The Mafia's penetration of Italian parliamentary politics, its management of judicial processes, its cultivation of police officials and local administrators, were not secondary features of its operation but central to its survival. Any analysis of organized crime that treats the criminal organization as operating wholly outside the state, in a world of pure criminal activity separable from legitimate political and economic life, misses the most important feature of the Mafia model: that organized crime and the state are not opposed but intertwined, sometimes to the point where the boundary between them becomes genuinely difficult to locate.

The chapter also raises enduring questions about justice, poverty, and power that extend well beyond the study of organized crime. The Sicilian peasants who accommodated themselves to Mafia authority were not moral failures. They were people making rational choices under conditions of extreme constraint, in environments where state protection was a fiction, where economic survival was chronically precarious, and where the alternative to accommodation was not freedom but the risk of lethal violence with no prospect of official protection. Understanding their choices requires not sympathy with the Mafia but a clear-eyed understanding of what it means to live under conditions where organized criminal power is the most consistent authority one encounters. That understanding is, in its own way, as important as understanding the criminal organization itself.

The story of the Sicilian Mafia does not end in the nineteenth century. In the chapters that follow, this textbook will trace its development through the twentieth century, its transplantation to the United States through the great emigration, its adaptation to new economic and political environments, and the long struggle of law enforcement authorities in both Italy and America to develop tools adequate to the challenge it represents. The analytical foundation laid in this chapter, the understanding of how the Mafia emerged, what functions it served, how it sustained itself through the combination of economic extraction, cultural legitimization, and political protection, will be essential for understanding all that follows.

United States History Glossary

This glossary defines key terms used throughout the textbook. Terms are listed alphabetically. Many appear in multiple chapters; this reference gives the core historical definition and contextual significance.

A
Abolitionism
A reform movement that demanded the immediate or gradual end of slavery. Major figures included Frederick Douglass, William Lloyd Garrison, Harriet Tubman, Sojourner Truth, and David Walker.
Affirmative Action
Policies designed to increase the representation of historically excluded groups in education, employment, and public institutions. A significant legal and political controversy from the 1970s onward.
Agrarianism
A philosophy emphasizing the moral and economic importance of farming and rural life. Jefferson articulated an influential American agrarian vision in which independent farmers were the backbone of republican democracy.
Alien and Sedition Acts (1798)
Four laws that extended residency requirements for citizenship, authorized deportation of "dangerous" aliens, and criminalized criticism of the federal government. Produced the Virginia and Kentucky Resolutions.
Amendment
A formal change to the Constitution. The Constitution has been amended twenty-seven times; the first ten are the Bill of Rights.
Antebellum
Latin for "before the war." Refers to the period before the Civil War, roughly 1820–1861, characterized by slavery's expansion, sectional conflict, and reform movements.
Anti-Federalists
Opponents of ratification of the Constitution in 1787–1788. Their arguments for a Bill of Rights helped produce the first ten amendments.
Articles of Confederation
The first governing document of the United States, ratified in 1781. Created a weak central government with no executive, no power to tax, and no power to regulate commerce.
Assimilation
The process by which individuals or groups adopt the cultural practices and identity of a dominant society. Federal policies toward Native nations aimed at forced assimilation.
Atlantic World
The interconnected world created by European expansion, African slavery, and transatlantic trade from roughly the fifteenth through the nineteenth centuries.
B – C
Balance of Power
A diplomatic strategy preventing any one power from becoming dominant. Native nations used this strategy to navigate competing European empires. Also applies to constitutional separation of powers.
Bicameral Legislature
A legislative body divided into two chambers. The U.S. Congress consists of the Senate and the House of Representatives, created by the Great Compromise.
Bill of Rights
The first ten amendments to the Constitution, ratified in 1791. Protects freedoms of speech, religion, press, assembly, and petition, among other fundamental rights.
Black Codes
Laws passed by southern states after the Civil War restricting the freedom of formerly enslaved people. Partly overturned by the Civil Rights Act of 1866 and the Fourteenth Amendment.
Black Power
A movement and cultural philosophy of the mid-1960s onward emphasizing Black self-determination, economic independence, racial pride, and political power.
Bleeding Kansas
Violent conflict (1854–1861) in Kansas Territory over whether it would be admitted as a slave or free state; demonstrated the failure of popular sovereignty.
Borderlands
Regions where two or more cultures, nations, or empires meet, overlap, and contest. The U.S.-Mexico borderlands and the Great Lakes region were historically significant borderlands.
Capitalism
An economic system in which private individuals and corporations own the means of production, and goods are distributed through markets.
Checks and Balances
The constitutional system by which each branch of government limits the power of the others, preventing any one branch from becoming too powerful.
Citizenship
Legal membership in a political community carrying rights and responsibilities. Shaped throughout U.S. history by race, gender, birth, naturalization, and political conflict.
Civil Disobedience
Deliberate, nonviolent refusal to obey unjust laws as a form of political protest. Central to the American civil rights movement.
Cold War
The geopolitical rivalry between the United States and the Soviet Union from roughly 1945 to 1991, shaped by nuclear arms competition, proxy wars, and ideological conflict.
Columbian Exchange
The massive transfer of plants, animals, diseases, people, and cultural practices between the Eastern and Western Hemispheres following Columbus's voyages in 1492.
Containment
The U.S. Cold War strategy of preventing Soviet expansion through alliances, support for threatened governments, and military strength, articulated by George Kennan in 1946–1947.
D – G
Dawes Act (1887)
Also the General Allotment Act. Broke up communally held tribal land and assigned individual allotments, destroying approximately 90 million acres of tribal land.
Declaration of Independence (1776)
Document declaring the thirteen colonies independent from Britain. Written primarily by Jefferson; articulates natural rights ideals invoked by reform movements throughout American history.
Deindustrialization
The decline of manufacturing industries, typically accompanied by factory closings, job losses, and economic dislocation. Transformed cities like Detroit and Pittsburgh beginning in the 1970s.
Democracy
A system of government in which power derives from the people, typically through elections and representative institutions. American democracy has expanded and contracted throughout history.
Disfranchisement
The removal or restriction of voting rights. Southern states used literacy tests, poll taxes, and violence to disfranchise Black voters after Reconstruction; largely overturned by the Voting Rights Act of 1965.
Domestic Slave Trade
The internal trade in enslaved people within the United States; moved approximately one million enslaved people from the Upper South to the Deep South between 1820 and 1860.
Double V Campaign
A World War II campaign by African Americans demanding victory over fascism abroad and victory over racism at home, promoted by the Pittsburgh Courier.
Dred Scott v. Sandford (1857)
Supreme Court decision holding enslaved people were not citizens, Congress had no power to ban slavery in territories, and the Missouri Compromise was unconstitutional. Overturned by the Fourteenth Amendment.
Due Process
Constitutional guarantee that government cannot deprive a person of life, liberty, or property without fair legal procedures. Protected by the Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments.
Emancipation Proclamation (1863)
Executive order declaring enslaved people in Confederate states in rebellion to be free. Transformed the Civil War into a war for emancipation and authorized enlistment of Black soldiers.
Empire
A political system in which one nation controls other territories and peoples. The United States has been imperial through westward expansion, overseas territories, and global economic and military influence.
Enlightenment
Eighteenth-century intellectual movement emphasizing reason, natural rights, and progress. Shaped the Declaration, the Constitution, and the political theories of American founders.
Equal Protection
Fourteenth Amendment guarantee that no state shall deny any person equal protection of the laws. Constitutional basis for challenging racial segregation and gender discrimination.
Federalism
Constitutional division of power between national and state governments. The balance has shifted throughout American history, with the national government generally growing stronger over time.
Federalists
Supporters of ratification of the Constitution. Led by Alexander Hamilton and John Adams; favored strong central government and commercial development.
Fourteenth Amendment (1868)
Reconstruction amendment granting citizenship to all born or naturalized in the United States; guaranteed due process and equal protection; overturned Dred Scott.
Freedmen's Bureau
Federal agency established 1865 providing food, medical care, legal assistance, and education to formerly enslaved people during Reconstruction.
Fugitive Slave Act (1850)
Required citizens in free states to assist in capturing escaped enslaved people. Intensified sectional conflict and strengthened abolitionism.
Gilded Age
Term coined by Mark Twain for the late nineteenth century (roughly 1870–1900), characterized by rapid industrialization, corporate consolidation, political corruption, and inequality.
H – M
Great Compromise (1787)
Agreement creating a bicameral Congress with equal state representation in the Senate and proportional representation in the House. Resolved disputes between large and small states.
Great Depression
Severe economic crisis beginning with the 1929 stock market crash. Produced 25 percent unemployment, bank failures, and widespread poverty. Transformed federal government through the New Deal.
Great Migration
Movement of approximately six million African Americans from the South to northern and western cities between roughly 1910 and 1970.
Imperialism
The policy of extending national power over other territories or peoples through military force, economic control, or political domination.
Indentured Servitude
Labor system in which a worker agreed to serve an employer for a fixed term in exchange for passage to the Americas. A major labor source in seventeenth-century Virginia before enslaved African labor dominated.
Indian Removal Act (1830)
Legislation authorizing forced removal of Native nations from eastern lands to territory west of the Mississippi. Enabled the Trail of Tears.
Industrialization
Transformation from agriculture-based to factory-manufacturing-based economy. American industrialization accelerated after the Civil War.
Jim Crow
System of racial segregation, disfranchisement, and violence dominating the American South from the end of Reconstruction through the mid-twentieth century. Upheld by Plessy v. Ferguson (1896).
Judicial Review
Power of federal courts to strike down laws violating the Constitution. Established by Marbury v. Madison (1803).
Kansas-Nebraska Act (1854)
Created Kansas and Nebraska territories under popular sovereignty; effectively repealed the Missouri Compromise; contributed to the Republican Party's formation and Bleeding Kansas.
Ku Klux Klan
White supremacist terrorist organization founded 1865–1866 to overthrow Reconstruction. Revived in the 1910s–1920s; a third wave rose against the civil rights movement.
Labor Union
An organization of workers formed to advance collective economic interests through collective bargaining with employers over wages, hours, and working conditions.
Laissez-Faire
Economic philosophy that government should not interfere in the market. Dominated Gilded Age thought; challenged by Progressivism and the New Deal; revived by conservatism in the late twentieth century.
Louisiana Purchase (1803)
Acquisition of approximately 828,000 square miles from France; doubled the size of the United States and expanded the territory over which the slavery debate would be fought.
Manifest Destiny
Nineteenth-century belief that the United States was destined to expand to the Pacific. Provided ideological justification for Indian removal, the Mexican-American War, and westward expansion.
Market Revolution
Transformation of the early nineteenth-century American economy from local subsistence agriculture to commercial production connected to regional and national markets.
McCarthyism
The practice of making accusations of communist disloyalty without adequate evidence, associated with Senator Joseph McCarthy in the early 1950s. Produced congressional investigations, blacklists, and ruined careers.
Middle Passage
The transatlantic crossing endured by enslaved Africans; approximately 12.5 million Africans were transported between the sixteenth and nineteenth centuries under horrific conditions.
Missouri Compromise (1820)
Admitted Missouri as a slave state and Maine as a free state; banned slavery north of 36°30' in the Louisiana Purchase territory. Repealed by the Kansas-Nebraska Act in 1854.
N – Z
Nativism
Hostility to immigration and immigrants. Has recurred throughout American history, targeting Catholics, Chinese, Southern and Eastern European, and Mexican immigrants among others.
New Deal
Programs, policies, and reforms enacted by the Roosevelt administration 1933–1939 in response to the Great Depression; transformed the federal government's role in the economy.
Northwest Ordinance (1787)
Established governance framework for the Northwest Territory; created a process for admitting new states; guaranteed civil liberties; banned slavery north of the Ohio River.
Nullification
Doctrine that states have the right to nullify federal laws they deem unconstitutional. Championed by John C. Calhoun; the Nullification Crisis of 1832–1833 prefigured later secessionist arguments.
Plessy v. Ferguson (1896)
Supreme Court decision establishing "separate but equal" doctrine; provided constitutional justification for racial segregation until overturned by Brown v. Board of Education (1954).
Popular Sovereignty
Principle that settlers of a territory should decide whether to allow slavery. Applied to new territories by the Kansas-Nebraska Act; produced violent conflict in Kansas.
Populism
Political movement claiming to represent "the people" against powerful elites. The People's Party of the 1890s represented farmers and workers against railroads, banks, and the gold standard.
Progressivism
Political reform movement of roughly 1890–1920 addressing problems produced by industrialization, corporate power, and immigration. Had significant racial limits.
Reconstruction
The period 1865–1877 during which the federal government attempted to reintegrate Confederate states and define the rights of formerly enslaved people. Overthrown by white supremacist violence and northern retreat.
Republicanism
Political philosophy emphasizing government by elected representatives, civic virtue, the rule of law, and the prevention of tyranny. Shaped the American Revolution and Constitution.
Secession
The withdrawal of a state from the Union. Eleven southern states seceded 1860–1861; the Civil War settled the question militarily.
Segregation
Forced separation of people by race in public spaces, schools, transportation, and housing. Legal segregation upheld in Plessy (1896); dismantled by civil rights legislation and court decisions.
Sectionalism
Strong identification with a particular region in conflict with national interests. The North-South divide over slavery dominated American politics from the 1820s through the Civil War.
Sovereignty
Supreme political authority over a territory or people. Questions of Native, state, federal, and citizen sovereignty have been central to American political and legal history.
Suffrage
The right to vote. American suffrage history includes expansion to propertyless white men, Black men (15th Amendment), women (19th Amendment), and citizens aged 18 and older (26th Amendment).
Thirteenth Amendment (1865)
Abolished slavery throughout the United States, with an exception allowing involuntary servitude as punishment for crime.
Trail of Tears
Forced removal of approximately 70,000–100,000 Native people from southeastern homelands to Indian Territory in the late 1830s; thousands died during the removal process.
Truman Doctrine (1947)
President Truman's declaration that the United States would support free peoples resisting communist subjugation; became the foundation of Cold War containment policy.
Underground Railroad
Network of people and safe houses helping enslaved people escape from the South to freedom in the North or Canada.
Voting Rights Act (1965)
Outlawed discriminatory voting practices; authorized federal oversight of elections; prohibited literacy tests. Enforcement provisions weakened by Shelby County v. Holder (2013).

Indigenous Nations and Sovereignty Reference

There are approximately 574 federally recognized Native nations in the United States today. This reference provides a guide to major nations, regions, legal concepts, and historical developments in Indigenous history.

Major Indigenous Nations and Peoples

Nations Reference

Haudenosaunee (Iroquois Confederacy)
A confederacy of originally five (later six) nations — Mohawk, Oneida, Onondaga, Cayuga, Seneca, and Tuscarora — occupying the Great Lakes and northeastern woodland regions. Maintained complex diplomacy with European empires. The confederacy's governance structure has been discussed as a possible influence on American constitutional thought.
Cherokee
A large southeastern nation with a complex political and cultural tradition, including a written language developed by Sequoyah. Forcibly removed in the Trail of Tears (1838–1839) despite winning in Worcester v. Georgia (1832).
Ojibwe (Chippewa / Anishinaabe)
Large Algonquian-speaking nation of the Great Lakes region with significant populations in Minnesota, Wisconsin, Michigan, and Canada. Important treaty rights related to fishing and wild rice have been defended in federal courts to the present.
Sioux (Lakota, Dakota, Nakota)
A confederation of nations of the Great Plains. Lakota warriors led by Sitting Bull and Crazy Horse defeated Custer at Little Bighorn (1876). The Wounded Knee massacre (1890) ended armed resistance. AIM occupied Wounded Knee in 1973.
Navajo (Diné)
The largest Native nation by population today, occupying the largest reservation in the United States. Endured the Long Walk (1863–1868) but negotiated return to their homeland. Navajo Code Talkers served the U.S. military in World War II.
Apache
A group of related nations occupying the Southwest. Leader Geronimo resisted U.S. military campaigns until 1886.
Pueblo Peoples
Dozens of distinct southwestern communities known for adobe architecture, complex ceremonial traditions, and agricultural sophistication. The Pueblo Revolt of 1680 temporarily expelled the Spanish from New Mexico.
Seminole
A Florida nation partly formed from peoples who resisted removal. The Seminole Wars (1817–1858) were the longest and costliest Indian wars in U.S. history. Communities exist today in both Oklahoma and Florida.
Comanche
A powerful plains nation that dominated the southern Great Plains through exceptional horsemanship, military capability, and trade networks until the Red River War (1874–1875).
Chinook, Nez Perce, and Northwest Coast Peoples
Diverse nations of the Pacific Northwest known for salmon-based economies, potlatch ceremonies, and sophisticated trade networks. The Nez Perce War of 1877, led by Chief Joseph, is one of the most celebrated episodes of Indigenous resistance.

Key Legal and Political Concepts

Sovereignty
The inherent right of Native nations to govern themselves. Federal law recognizes tribal sovereignty, but its scope has been repeatedly limited by legislation and court decisions. Key cases include Cherokee Nation v. Georgia (1831), Worcester v. Georgia (1832), and McGirt v. Oklahoma (2020).
Federal Trust Responsibility
The legal and moral obligation of the U.S. government to protect tribal lands, assets, and resources, and to fulfill treaty obligations.
Treaty Rights
Rights guaranteed through formal agreements with the U.S. government. Hundreds of treaties were later violated or abrogated; some treaty rights to fishing and land have been successfully litigated in recent decades.
Termination Policy (1950s–1960s)
Federal policy ending the government's special relationships with dozens of Native nations, with devastating consequences. Formally ended in the 1970s.
Self-Determination
The principle that Native nations have the right to govern themselves and make decisions about their own communities. Reflected in the Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act of 1975.
American Indian Movement (AIM)
A Native activist organization founded 1968 addressing urban poverty, police violence, and treaty rights. Occupied Alcatraz (1969–1971) and Wounded Knee (1973).

Colonial Regions Reference

Colonial Regions

New England
Settled primarily by Puritan religious dissenters from England in the 1620s–1640s. Colonies included Massachusetts Bay, Plymouth, Connecticut, Rhode Island, and New Hampshire. Economy relied on fishing, shipbuilding, lumber, and commerce. Town meeting governance was a distinctive institution. King Philip's War (1675–1676) was catastrophic for both colonists and the Wampanoag-led alliance.
Middle Colonies
New York, New Jersey, Pennsylvania, and Delaware. Notable for ethnic and religious diversity — Dutch, English, German, Welsh, Scots-Irish, Swedish, and Jewish communities coexisted. Pennsylvania, founded by William Penn as a Quaker refuge, offered religious tolerance. Mixed economy combining farming, commerce, and artisan manufacturing.
Chesapeake
Virginia and Maryland. Developed tobacco as the staple crop; relied first on indentured servants, then increasingly on enslaved African labor. Virginia established the first representative assembly in English America (House of Burgesses, 1619). By the late seventeenth century, racial slavery was codified in Virginia law.
Lower South
South Carolina and Georgia. Developed rice and indigo as staple crops using enslaved labor drawn heavily from West Africa's "Rice Coast." Africans brought agricultural expertise that made Carolina planters wealthy. Georgia initially banned slavery — a prohibition later reversed.
Frontier and Backcountry
Inland regions settled by Scots-Irish, German, and other migrants pushing into the Appalachian highlands. Backcountry settlers often had tense relationships with eastern elites, Native nations, and royal authorities. Bacon's Rebellion and the Regulator movements reflected frontier discontent.

Founding Documents Guide

Key Founding Documents

The Mayflower Compact (1620)
Signed aboard the Mayflower before the Pilgrims landed; established a self-governing body grounded in consent of the governed. An early example of American self-government and social compact theory.
Common Sense (1776)
Thomas Paine's pamphlet made the case for independence in plain language accessible to ordinary readers. Attacked monarchy and hereditary privilege; helped shift colonial opinion toward independence.
Declaration of Independence (1776)
Adopted July 4, 1776. Articulated natural rights ideals; listed grievances against King George III; declared independence. Its ideals have been invoked by abolitionists, suffragists, labor organizers, and civil rights activists.
Articles of Confederation (1781)
First governing document; created a weak central government with no executive or power to tax. Its weaknesses contributed to the Constitutional Convention of 1787.
The Federalist Papers (1787–1788)
Eighty-five essays by Hamilton, Madison, and Jay arguing for ratification. Foundational texts of American political theory explaining separation of powers, federalism, and the dangers of faction (Federalist No. 10) and tyranny (Federalist No. 51).
Washington's Farewell Address (1796)
Warned against permanent foreign alliances, political factions, and sectionalism. Shaped American foreign policy debates for more than a century.
The Monroe Doctrine (1823)
Declared the Western Hemisphere closed to further European colonization. A cornerstone of American foreign policy for more than a century.
Section 6

Declaration of Independence Reference

Background

Adopted by the Second Continental Congress on July 4, 1776. Primarily drafted by Thomas Jefferson, with revisions by Benjamin Franklin, John Adams, and Congress. The Congress removed Jefferson's condemnation of the slave trade under pressure from southern delegates.

Structure

Preamble
States the philosophical justification for independence, drawing on Enlightenment natural rights theory: all men are created equal; they are endowed with unalienable rights including life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness.
Statement of Political Philosophy
Argues that governments derive just powers from the consent of the governed, and that when a government destroys natural rights, the people have the right to alter or abolish it.
List of Grievances
Twenty-seven specific complaints against King George III, including imposing taxes without consent, denying jury trials, and waging war against colonists.
Declaration
The formal declaration that the thirteen colonies are free and independent states, dissolved from all allegiance to the British Crown.

Historical Significance

The Declaration's assertion of human equality and natural rights has been invoked repeatedly by reform movements including abolitionism, women's rights, labor organizing, civil rights, and LGBTQ rights. Its ideals have consistently exceeded the political realities of American life — a contradiction that has been both a source of criticism and a catalyst for reform throughout the nation's history.

Constitution and Amendments Reference

Articles of the Constitution (1787, ratified 1788)

Article I — The Legislative Branch
Establishes Congress (Senate and House of Representatives); defines congressional powers including taxation, commerce regulation, and war declaration; includes the Necessary and Proper Clause.
Article II — The Executive Branch
Establishes the presidency; defines executive powers (commander in chief, treaty-making, appointments); describes the Electoral College.
Article III — The Judicial Branch

Political Parties and Elections Guide

Major Party Lineages

Federalists (1790s–1820s)
Favored strong central government, financial development, and commercial ties with Britain. Led by Alexander Hamilton and John Adams. Faded after the War of 1812.
Democratic-Republicans (1790s–1824)
Favored agrarianism, states' rights, and close ties with France. Led by Jefferson and Madison. Dominant after 1800; split in the 1820s.
Democrats (1828–present)
Emerged from Jacksonian coalition. In the nineteenth century favored states' rights and slavery's protection. After the New Deal, became the party of federal social programs, labor, and eventually civil rights. The party's base shifted dramatically from the South to the urban North over the twentieth century.
Whigs (1834–1854)
Formed in opposition to Jackson. Favored economic modernization and internal improvements. Collapsed over slavery; replaced by the Republican Party.
Republicans (1854–present)
Founded as an anti-slavery-expansion party. Lincoln's party during the Civil War. From the 1960s onward shifted toward conservatism, smaller government, and social traditionalism; drew strength from the South and rural America.
Populists / People's Party (1892–1896)
Third-party movement of farmers and workers challenging the gold standard, railroad power, and political corruption. Their platform influenced later Progressive and New Deal reforms.
Socialist Party (1901–present, varying influence)
Advocated democratic socialism and labor rights. Eugene Debs received nearly one million votes in the 1912 presidential election.

Section 11

Slavery and Abolition Reference

Key Facts and Developments

Origins
African slavery in North America began in the early seventeenth century. The first enslaved Africans arrived in Virginia in 1619. Virginia and other colonies codified racial slavery in statute by the 1660s–1680s.
Scale
By 1860, approximately four million enslaved people lived in the United States. Slavery was the largest single form of wealth in the antebellum United States, exceeding the value of all factories and railroads combined.
Economics
Slavery was central to American economic development — not merely a "southern" institution. Northern merchants financed the slave trade and cotton commerce; northern textile mills processed slave-grown cotton.
Resistance
Enslaved people resisted through slow work, feigned illness, breaking tools, learning to read in defiance of law, running away, maintaining cultural traditions, and organizing uprisings. Major revolts included the Stono Rebellion (1739), Gabriel's Conspiracy (1800), Denmark Vesey's conspiracy (1822), and Nat Turner's Rebellion (1831).
Domestic Slave Trade
After the international slave trade was banned in 1808, the internal trade moved approximately one million enslaved people from the Upper South to the Deep South between 1820 and 1860, breaking apart families.
Abolitionism
Major figures: William Lloyd Garrison, Frederick Douglass, Sojourner Truth, Harriet Tubman, Harriet Beecher Stowe, and David Walker. Black abolitionists were central to the movement. The American Anti-Slavery Society was founded in 1833.
Emancipation
The Emancipation Proclamation (January 1, 1863) freed enslaved people in Confederate states in rebellion. The Thirteenth Amendment (December 1865) abolished slavery throughout the United States.
Aftermath
Emancipation was followed by Reconstruction, during which formerly enslaved people claimed citizenship and participated in politics — before Reconstruction was overthrown by violence and the emergence of Jim Crow.
Section 12

Civil War and Reconstruction Guide

Civil War (1861–1865)

Causes
Decades of sectional conflict over slavery, its expansion into new territories, and the failure of political compromise. Lincoln's election in 1860 triggered secession.
Confederate States
Eleven states seceded: South Carolina, Mississippi, Florida, Alabama, Georgia, Louisiana, Texas, Virginia, Arkansas, North Carolina, and Tennessee.
Military Overview
Union advantages in population, industry, and railroads; Confederacy fought a defensive war on familiar terrain. Key campaigns: Antietam (1862), Gettysburg and Vicksburg (1863), Sherman's March (1864), Appomattox (1865).
Emancipation
Lincoln's Emancipation Proclamation (January 1, 1863) transformed the war's meaning. More than 180,000 Black soldiers served in the United States Colored Troops.
Costs
Approximately 620,000–750,000 soldiers died; civilian casualties also significant. The South was economically devastated.

Reconstruction (1865–1877)

Presidential Reconstruction
Andrew Johnson's lenient plan allowed former Confederate leaders to return to power; produced Black Codes.
Congressional Reconstruction (1867–1877)
Congress divided the South into military districts; required new state constitutions with Black suffrage; produced the 14th and 15th Amendments.
Black Political Participation
More than 2,000 Black men held public office during Reconstruction, including senators, congressmen, governors, and state legislators.
White Resistance
The Ku Klux Klan and other groups used terror, murder, and intimidation to suppress Black political participation and overthrow Reconstruction governments.
End of Reconstruction
The Compromise of 1877, federal troop withdrawal, Supreme Court decisions, and economic depression combined to end Reconstruction and restore white Democratic control in the South.
Historical Legacy
Reconstruction is now understood not as a failure of Black governance but as a democratic experiment overthrown by violence. Its constitutional achievements became the legal foundation for the civil rights movement nearly a century later.

Immigration History Reference

Major Immigration Periods and Groups

Colonial Period
English, Dutch, German, Scots-Irish, and other European migrants; forced African migration through the slave trade; diverse Indigenous populations.
Mid-Nineteenth Century (1840s–1880s)
Mass Irish immigration following the Great Famine; large German immigration; Chinese immigration to California and the West for railroad construction and mining.
New Immigration (1880s–1920s)
Large-scale migration from Southern and Eastern Europe: Italians, Poles, Russians, Jews, Hungarians, Greeks, and others; also significant immigration from Mexico, Japan, and Puerto Rico.
Restriction Era (1882–1965)
Chinese Exclusion Act (1882); Gentlemen's Agreement (1907); Immigration Act of 1924 established strict national origins quotas favoring Northern and Western Europeans.
Post-1965 Immigration
The Immigration and Nationality Act of 1965 abolished national origins quotas, leading to large-scale immigration from Latin America, Asia, Africa, and the Caribbean.
Undocumented Immigration
Millions living in the United States without legal status became a major political issue from the 1980s onward. The Immigration Reform and Control Act of 1986 granted amnesty to approximately three million people.

Movement challenging sexual harassment and assault in workplaces and public life; produced significant cultural and institutional changes.

Asian American History Reference

Key Histories

Chinese Americans
Central to building the transcontinental railroad (1869). The Chinese Exclusion Act (1882) was the first federal law to ban immigration by race. Exclusion was repealed in 1943.
Japanese Americans
During World War II, approximately 120,000 Japanese Americans — most of them U.S. citizens — were forcibly incarcerated under Executive Order 9066. The Supreme Court upheld the policy in Korematsu (1944). Congress formally apologized and paid reparations in 1988.
Filipino Americans
The Philippines was a U.S. colonial possession from 1898 to 1946. Filipino Americans were classified as "nationals" but not citizens before 1946.
Southeast Asian Americans
Large numbers of Vietnamese, Cambodian, Laotian, and Hmong refugees arrived after the Vietnam War.
Asian American Movement (1960s–1970s)
Students and activists coined the pan-ethnic identity "Asian American," organized against racism and the Vietnam War, and demanded ethnic studies programs.

Key Policy Eras

Treaty Era (1778–1871)
The U.S. government negotiated hundreds of treaties with Native nations, most of which it later violated or abrogated. Congress ended formal treaty-making with Native nations in 1871.
Removal Era (1820s–1840s)
The Indian Removal Act (1830) and forced removal of southeastern nations to Indian Territory.
Allotment Era (1887–1934)
Dawes Act broke up tribal lands; Native land base reduced from approximately 138 million acres to 48 million acres.

Indian boarding schools removed children from families and communities to suppress Indigenous language and culture. The motto "Kill the Indian, save the man" reflected the policy's logic.

Ended allotment; encouraged tribal self-government; John Collier's "Indian New Deal."
Termination Policy (1950s–1960s)
Federal government terminated its relationships with more than 100 Native nations, with devastating consequences.
Self-Determination Era (1970s–present)
Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act (1975); Indian Child Welfare Act (1978); American Indian Religious Freedom Act (1978); Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act (1990).

Economic History Reference

Major Economic Eras

Colonial Mercantile Economy (1607–1776)
Based on plantation agriculture, fur trade, fishing, and commerce. Enslaved labor was central. Britain's mercantilist policies restricted colonial trade.
Market Revolution (1820–1860)
Transportation revolution (canals, railroads); factory production; commercial agriculture; banking and credit expansion; wage labor growth; regional economic specialization.
Industrial Capitalism (1865–1914)
Rapid industrialization; railroad expansion; steel, oil, and heavy manufacturing; corporate consolidation; trusts and monopoly power; mass immigration and wage labor; labor-capital conflict.
Great Depression and New Deal (1929–1941)
Catastrophic economic collapse; New Deal programs; federal social safety net; labor rights expansion; banking regulation.
Postwar Prosperity (1945–1973)
Economic boom; suburbanization; consumer culture; GI Bill; strong unions; middle-class expansion; Cold War military spending.
Stagflation and Deindustrialization (1973–1983)
Oil shocks; simultaneous inflation and unemployment; manufacturing decline; urban crisis; Rust Belt.
Reagan Economy (1981–1990s)
Tax cuts; deregulation; financialization; declining union power; growing inequality; technology sector growth.
Globalization and Technology (1990s–2007)
NAFTA; global trade expansion; dot-com boom and bust; financial sector growth; growing income inequality.
Great Recession (2007–2009)
Housing market collapse; bank failures; 10% unemployment; federal bailouts; slow recovery.
COVID Economy (2020–present)
Pandemic economic shock; federal relief; supply chain disruptions; inflation; ongoing recovery debates.

Historical Thinking Skills Guide

History is not merely a collection of facts to memorize. It is a discipline of inquiry. The following skills are essential for reading, discussing, and writing about U.S. history.

Skill 1 — Chronological Reasoning

Periodization: Dividing history into meaningful units. Every period label emphasizes some changes and hides others. Ask: Who chose this era's name? What does it highlight? What does it obscure?

Sequencing: Causation depends on sequence — an effect cannot precede its cause.

Duration: Some changes happen quickly; others unfold over generations. Recognizing different tempos of change is essential.

Skill 2 — Causation and Argumentation

Historical events have multiple causes. Distinguish between short-term causes (immediate triggers) and long-term causes (underlying conditions). Single-cause explanations almost always oversimplify complex realities.

A historical argument makes a claim, provides evidence, and explains the reasoning that connects them. Strong arguments acknowledge counterarguments and alternative interpretations.

Skill 3 — Contextualization

Placing a specific event, document, or development in its broader historical setting: the time period, geographic region, social conditions, political context, and international situation.

A document or law that seems simple in isolation becomes more complex when understood in context. The Declaration of Independence means something different when understood alongside the realities of slavery and Native dispossession.

Skill 4 — Comparison

Across time: How did slavery in 1860 differ from slavery in 1700?

Across place: How did British colonization differ from Spanish colonization?

Across groups: How did the same event affect different groups differently?

Skill 5 — Source Analysis

When reading any primary source, ask: Who created it? When? Why? Who was the intended audience? What does it tell us, and what does it not tell us? Whose perspective does it represent? Whose perspective is missing?

When reading secondary sources, ask: What is the author's argument? What evidence supports it? What might challenge it? When was it written, and how might that affect its perspective?

Skill 6 — Continuity and Change

Change and continuity almost always coexist in the same period. Legal change does not automatically produce social change. Revolutionary moments create new possibilities without eliminating older structures of power.

Ask: What changed? What stayed the same? Who experienced change most directly? Why does this pattern matter?

Skill 7 — Multiple Perspectives

Historical empathy: Understanding historical actors within their own context, without projecting present-day values anachronistically or excusing injustice.

Most historical events were experienced differently by different groups. The same event looks different depending on race, gender, class, region, and power. Acknowledging varied perspectives is a requirement of historical accuracy, not relativism.

Skill 8 — Evaluating Historical Claims

A fact is something verifiable by evidence. An interpretation is a claim about what facts mean, their causes, or their significance. Historians disagree about interpretations, not just facts.

Not all evidence is equally reliable or relevant. Consider the source's credibility, proximity to events, purpose, and how it compares to other sources. All sources reflect the perspectives of their creators — recognizing bias means reading critically, not dismissing.

A Note on Using This Back Matter

This back matter is designed as a companion to the full textbook, not as a substitute for it. The glossary, timeline, case indexes, and biographical dictionary give students and researchers rapid access to definitions, context, and identification — the building blocks of historical understanding. But building blocks are not buildings. The chapters themselves are where historical argument, evidence, multiple perspectives, and analytical depth reside.

Students should use this back matter actively. When a term appears in a chapter and is not fully defined there, turn here. When a court case is mentioned and you need to understand its precedent, find it in Section 8. When a biographical figure appears and you need a quick orientation before reading deeper, start in Section 24. When you need to situate events in a longer sequence, the Master Timeline in Section 2 provides the overview.

The back matter also reflects the textbook's organizing convictions: that U.S. history encompasses many peoples, regions, institutions, conflicts, ideas, and movements; that it must be studied through primary sources, multiple perspectives, and careful historical reasoning; and that understanding the past honestly — with attention to both ideals and contradictions, both achievements and failures — is what democratic citizenship requires. These sections on Indigenous nations, African American history, Latino/a history, Asian American history, women's history, and labor history are not additions to the "real" story. They are the story.

The United States is a nation still in the process of becoming. Its history is contested, changing, unequal, creative, violent, democratic, expansionist, reforming, and unfinished. This reference companion is offered in the hope that engaging seriously with that history will help students think more carefully, argue more rigorously, and understand more fully the world they have inherited and the choices they will make in it.

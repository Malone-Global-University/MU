Part VIII — Reform, War, and Modern America
39

The Great Depression and the New Deal

United States History: Indigenous America, Republic, Conflict, and Change

Chapter Opening

"The only thing we have to fear is fear itself — nameless, unreasoning, unjustified terror which paralyzes needed efforts to convert retreat into advance."
— Franklin D. Roosevelt, First Inaugural Address, March 4, 1933

In the autumn of 1932, a photographer named Dorothea Lange drove through California's agricultural valleys and stopped to photograph a woman sitting in a lean-to tent with her children huddled around her. The woman, later identified as Florence Owens Thompson, a thirty-two-year-old migrant worker of Cherokee descent, stared into the middle distance with an expression that has come to define an entire era. She had sold the tires from her car to buy food. She and her children had been surviving on frozen vegetables scavenged from a nearby field and birds her children caught. Lange's photograph, titled "Migrant Mother," was published in newspapers across the country and became one of the most recognized images of the twentieth century. It captured something that statistics alone could not: the human cost of economic catastrophe on a scale that Americans had never before experienced.

The Great Depression, which began in 1929 and did not fully end until the United States entered World War II in 1941, was the worst economic crisis in American history. At its depth, roughly one in four American workers had no job. Millions lost their homes, their savings, and their farms. Families separated as men traveled desperately in search of work. Soup kitchens stretched around city blocks. Makeshift shantytowns called "Hoovervilles" — named bitterly after President Herbert Hoover — appeared at the edges of cities from New York to Seattle. Banks failed by the thousands, erasing the savings of ordinary depositors who had done nothing wrong. Farmers who could not pay their mortgages watched sheriffs auction off land that families had worked for generations. Children went to school hungry, and many stopped going at all.

Yet the Great Depression was not simply a story of suffering. It was also a turning point in the history of American government. Before 1929, most Americans and most political leaders believed that economic life should be largely left to private markets, that the federal government had no proper role in providing relief to unemployed workers, and that economic downturns, however painful, would correct themselves in time. The Depression shattered those assumptions. When private markets failed on an unprecedented scale, when state and local governments ran out of money, and when the Hoover administration's cautious responses proved woefully inadequate, Americans demanded that the federal government act. The election of Franklin Delano Roosevelt in 1932 opened one of the most transformative periods of government expansion in American history.

Roosevelt's New Deal — a broad and improvised set of programs, agencies, laws, and experiments launched between 1933 and 1938 — fundamentally reshaped the relationship between the federal government and American economic life. The New Deal created the Social Security system, regulated banking and the stock market, protected workers' right to organize unions, built public works that transformed the American landscape, electrified rural communities, and established the principle that the federal government had a responsibility to the economic security of its citizens. Not everything worked. Many programs were incomplete, contradictory, or designed with racial and gender limits that excluded millions. Critics attacked the New Deal from both left and right. But its effects were lasting and its precedents profound.

Understanding the Great Depression and the New Deal requires examining how an economic crisis affected not only banks and stock markets but also families, farms, cities, racial communities, immigrant communities, and working people across the country. It requires understanding why Herbert Hoover, a capable and principled man, failed to respond effectively, and why Franklin Roosevelt, through a combination of political skill, genuine compassion, and willingness to experiment, succeeded in restoring confidence and changing the role of government. It requires examining who the New Deal helped most, who it excluded, and what political coalitions it built and what enemies it made. And it requires understanding why the crisis of the 1930s, though it ended, left enduring marks on American institutions, politics, and social life.

The Great Depression was not inevitable. It grew from specific choices, policies, and structural weaknesses in the American and global economy. It was made worse by policy failures and made somewhat better by policy interventions. It transformed American politics for a generation. And it raised questions about capitalism, democracy, government responsibility, and economic justice that remained contested long after the Depression itself had passed. This chapter traces the causes of the Depression, its human consequences, the inadequacy of the first government response, the creation and character of the New Deal, and the limits and legacies of the era. Students who understand the 1930s will understand something essential about modern American government, the modern Democratic Party, the modern social safety net, and the ongoing debates over what government should and should not do.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain the major structural and policy causes of the Great Depression, distinguishing between long-term weaknesses in the American economy and the specific triggers of the 1929 crash.
  2. Describe the human consequences of the Depression across different regions, racial groups, genders, and economic classes.
  3. Analyze the Dust Bowl as both an environmental and a human disaster, tracing its origins in agricultural practices, drought, and federal land policy.
  4. Evaluate Herbert Hoover's response to the Depression, explaining the ideological assumptions that shaped his approach and why those responses proved inadequate.
  5. Trace the major programs, agencies, and legislation of the New Deal's First and Second phases, explaining the goals and methods of each.
  6. Identify the racial and gender limits of New Deal programs, explaining which groups were excluded or underserved and why.
  7. Compare the major critics of the New Deal from the left and the right, including Huey Long, Father Charles Coughlin, and the Liberty League.
  8. Analyze the labor rights provisions of the New Deal era, including the Wagner Act and the rise of the Congress of Industrial Organizations.
  9. Explain the creation and significance of the Social Security Act of 1935.
  10. Assess the long-term political and institutional legacies of the New Deal.
  11. Connect the New Deal to broader questions about the proper role of the federal government in managing the economy and protecting the welfare of citizens.
  12. Contextualize the American Depression within the global economic crisis of the 1930s and its relationship to the rise of authoritarian governments in Europe.

Guiding Historical Questions

Questions for Historical Inquiry

  1. What caused the Great Depression, and were those causes avoidable?
  2. How did the Depression affect different Americans differently, based on race, gender, region, occupation, and class?
  3. Why did Herbert Hoover's response to the Depression fail to end the crisis?
  4. What was the New Deal, and was it a coherent program or an improvised experiment?
  5. Who did the New Deal help, and who did it leave out or actively harm?
  6. How did the New Deal change the relationship between the federal government and American economic life?
  7. What were the strongest arguments made against the New Deal, and what do those arguments reveal about American political ideology?
  8. How did the Great Depression affect American democracy, and how did the United States avoid the turn toward authoritarian government that occurred in Germany, Italy, and Spain?
  9. Did the New Deal end the Depression?
  10. What are the lasting legacies of the New Deal in American law, institutions, and politics?

Historical Context

The Great Depression did not arrive without warning. The American economy of the 1920s appeared prosperous on the surface, but beneath that prosperity lay serious structural weaknesses. American industry had expanded enormously during World War I and continued growing through the 1920s, producing automobiles, radios, washing machines, and a vast array of consumer goods. But wages for industrial workers had not kept pace with rising productivity. Farmers, who made up a large portion of the American population, had struggled throughout the 1920s as agricultural prices remained depressed after the wartime boom collapsed. Many American families were living on credit — buying goods on installment plans, taking out mortgages, borrowing against uncertain futures. The banking system was fragmented, weakly regulated, and prone to speculative behavior. Investment in the stock market had become a national enthusiasm, with millions of Americans buying stocks on margin, meaning they borrowed money to buy shares they could not fully afford.

The global economy was also fragile. World War I had devastated European economies and disrupted global trade networks. The United States had emerged from the war as the world's dominant creditor nation, but American banks had lent enormous sums to European governments and businesses, creating financial interdependence that would prove disastrous when the system collapsed. American agriculture had been in distress long before 1929. The end of World War I eliminated the high demand for American grain that had encouraged farmers to plow millions of acres of marginal land, particularly on the Great Plains. When grain prices fell in the early 1920s and remained low through the decade, millions of farm families struggled.

African Americans entered the Depression from a position of particular vulnerability. Jim Crow laws in the South confined most Black southerners to agricultural labor and domestic service — low-wage, insecure occupations. The Great Migration had brought hundreds of thousands of Black workers to northern cities, where they found industrial jobs, but they remained concentrated in the most vulnerable sectors of the labor market. Mexican Americans in the Southwest faced special vulnerability; immigration authorities and local governments would use the Depression as an excuse to deport hundreds of thousands of people, including many who were American citizens.

By the late 1920s, the Republican Party had held the White House for twelve years, and the prosperity of the 1920s was widely attributed to Republican economic policies — high tariffs, low taxes on business and the wealthy, minimal regulation, and hostility to unions. When the Depression struck, it discredited Republican economic orthodoxy and created political space for a Democratic Party that had been largely out of power at the national level. The election of 1932 was not just a change of administration; it was a political earthquake that reshaped American party politics for a generation.

Section 39.1

Causes of the Great Depression

The Great Depression was not caused by a single event. It grew from a combination of structural weaknesses in the American economy, policy failures at the national and international levels, and a cascading sequence of collapses that reinforced one another. Understanding the causes requires looking beneath the surface prosperity of the 1920s.

The Fragility Beneath the Boom

The 1920s appeared to be a decade of unprecedented prosperity, and in important ways it was. Industrial output grew enormously. Automobiles, radios, washing machines, and a vast array of consumer goods reached millions of American homes for the first time. Real wages for many industrial workers rose. The stock market soared. But this prosperity concealed deep inequalities and structural vulnerabilities.

The distribution of income in the 1920s was highly skewed. The wealthiest Americans captured a disproportionate share of the decade's gains. The top one percent of income earners received roughly fifteen percent of national income by the late 1920s. Many working-class and farming families did not share fully in the prosperity, and their consumption was increasingly financed by credit rather than rising wages. Consumer debt expanded rapidly as Americans bought goods on installment plans. When the economy turned downward, debt-burdened households were particularly vulnerable.

American agriculture had never fully recovered from the end of the World War I boom. When the war ended, European demand for American grain collapsed, prices fell, and millions of farm families struggled. Many had taken on debt to buy land and equipment during the wartime high-price years, and when prices dropped, they could not service those loans. Farm foreclosures mounted through the 1920s, and rural banks — which had lent heavily to farmers — began to fail even before the 1929 crash.

Overproduction and Weak Demand

American industry had invested enormous sums in production capacity during the 1920s, and by the late decade, productive capacity was outrunning consumer demand. Industries from textiles to automobiles were producing more goods than consumers could afford to purchase, even with the expansion of credit. When demand faltered, businesses cut production, which led to layoffs, which reduced consumer spending further, setting up the downward spiral that would define the Depression.

The concentration of corporate power and the weakness of labor meant that workers did not receive wages sufficient to buy back the goods they produced. Union membership had declined sharply through the 1920s as businesses used yellow-dog contracts, labor spies, and court injunctions to suppress organizing. Without strong unions, workers had limited ability to negotiate for higher wages, and the benefits of rising productivity flowed disproportionately to owners and investors rather than workers.

The Banking System's Vulnerability

The American banking system in the 1920s was fragmented and poorly regulated. There were thousands of small, independent banks across the country — many of them in rural areas — that held thin reserves and made speculative loans. These banks had no federal insurance backing their deposits, so when a bank failed, its depositors lost everything. The Federal Reserve System, created in 1913, was supposed to provide a lender of last resort, but its policy responses during the early Depression were badly misguided — it raised interest rates at exactly the wrong moment, tightening credit when the economy needed stimulus.

Thousands of banks had invested heavily in the booming stock market of the late 1920s. When the market collapsed, bank balance sheets deteriorated rapidly. Depositors, fearing for their savings, rushed to withdraw funds, triggering bank runs that turned individual bank failures into a systemic crisis. Between 1930 and 1933, approximately nine thousand American banks failed, wiping out the savings of millions of ordinary depositors.

The Stock Market Bubble

During the 1920s, stock market investment became a mass phenomenon. Millions of Americans — not just wealthy professionals but clerks, secretaries, factory workers, and farmers — bought stocks, often on margin. Margin buying meant that investors could purchase stock by putting up only a fraction of the purchase price, borrowing the rest from brokers. This amplified gains when prices rose and amplified losses catastrophically when prices fell. The Dow Jones Industrial Average had risen roughly 500 percent between 1921 and its September 1929 peak. That extraordinary run had been fueled not only by genuine economic growth but by speculation, credit, and the psychology of an apparently endless boom.

International Weakness

The global economy was also badly positioned for a severe downturn. World War I had disrupted European economies and left a tangle of war debts and reparations obligations that kept international finance fragile. When American banks, facing their own crisis, stopped lending to Europe and demanded repayment, European economies were pulled into the same downward spiral. The Smoot-Hawley Tariff of 1930 made things worse. Enacted despite warnings from economists, it raised tariffs on imported goods to record levels. Other nations retaliated with their own tariffs, and international trade collapsed. Between 1929 and 1932, world trade fell by roughly two-thirds.

Section 39.2

Stock Market Crash and Banking Crisis

Black Thursday and Black Tuesday

The stock market's collapse in October 1929 unfolded over several days of panic selling. On October 24, 1929 — "Black Thursday" — prices fell sharply as investors who had bought on margin received margin calls and were forced to sell. Bankers briefly stabilized the market with coordinated purchases, but confidence was shaken. The following week, on October 29 — "Black Tuesday" — selling resumed on a massive scale. The Dow Jones lost roughly twelve percent of its value in a single day. By November, the Dow had fallen nearly fifty percent from its September peak. By the summer of 1932, it would fall eighty-nine percent from its high.

The Banking Collapse

The connection between the stock market crash and the banking crisis was direct. Many banks had invested depositors' money in the stock market. When stocks collapsed, banks faced enormous losses. Depositors, fearing for their savings, began withdrawing funds. When word spread that a bank was in trouble, panicked depositors lined up to withdraw, often draining the bank before all customers could be served. These bank runs became self-fulfilling prophecies — the fear of bank failure caused the runs that caused the failures.

Between 1930 and 1933, approximately nine thousand banks failed in the United States. This was not evenly distributed — rural banks failed first and most heavily, particularly in agricultural regions already struggling with farm debt. The result was the destruction of billions of dollars in deposits. Ordinary people who had spent lifetimes accumulating small savings watched those savings simply disappear. There was no federal insurance, no guarantee, no recourse.

The banking collapse had cascading effects throughout the economy. Banks that survived became extremely cautious about lending, making it harder for businesses to borrow and harder for consumers to buy on credit. Businesses that could not get credit could not maintain inventory, meet payroll, or invest in equipment. Layoffs accelerated. Consumer spending fell further. The downward spiral reinforced itself at every turn.

The Federal Reserve's Policy Failure

The Federal Reserve System had been created in 1913 specifically to prevent banking panics and provide emergency credit to the financial system. But during the critical years of 1930 to 1933, the Federal Reserve failed catastrophically. Rather than pumping money into the economy to combat deflation and support failing banks, the Fed tightened credit and raised interest rates. Whatever the reasons, the Fed's contractionary policies turned what might have been a severe recession into the worst economic catastrophe in American history. This is the central finding of economists Milton Friedman and Anna Jacobson Schwartz in their landmark 1963 study, which remains the most influential analysis of monetary policy during the Depression.

Deflation and Its Consequences

As the banking system contracted and money supply fell, the United States entered a severe deflation — a general decline in prices across the economy. Deflation sounds beneficial, but it has devastating economic consequences. When prices fall, consumers and businesses postpone spending, expecting even lower prices in the future. This postponement reduces demand further, causing more price reductions, in a destructive cycle. Deflation also increases the real burden of debt: if you borrowed money when prices were higher and now prices have fallen, your debt is harder to repay because the dollars you owe are worth more relative to what you earn.

Between 1929 and 1933, the gross national product of the United States fell by roughly thirty percent. Industrial production fell by nearly fifty percent. Prices fell by roughly thirty percent. Unemployment rose from about three percent to twenty-five percent. These numbers describe an economic collapse without precedent in American history.

Section 39.3

Unemployment, Poverty, and Daily Life

The Scale of Suffering

By 1933, roughly thirteen million Americans — approximately one in four workers — were unemployed. But that figure understates the crisis. Many more were underemployed, working only part-time or in jobs far below their skills and previous incomes. Many who kept jobs had their wages cut drastically — and were grateful to have anything at all. Young people entering the labor market found no jobs available and delayed establishing independent lives. Older workers who lost jobs often could not find new ones and slid toward poverty.

Life in the Depression: Cities

In American cities, the Depression produced scenes that shocked observers who had grown up during the prosperity of the 1920s. Breadlines and soup kitchens stretched around city blocks as private charities, churches, and local governments struggled to provide minimal relief. Homeless men slept in parks, under bridges, and in the emerging "Hoovervilles" — makeshift shantytowns constructed from scrap lumber, tin, cardboard, and whatever materials the homeless could gather. New York City's Central Park contained a Hooverville. St. Louis, Seattle, Chicago, and other major cities had their own. The men who lived in them were not transients or criminals — they were workers, veterans, family men who had lost jobs and homes.

Food was the central preoccupation of Depression-era families. Middle-class families that had never worried about food found themselves making menus from whatever was cheapest — beans, bread, potatoes. Working-class and poor families faced genuine hunger. Children went to school without breakfast. Teachers in some schools reported that students fainted at their desks from hunger. In New York City alone, school nurses reported tens of thousands of children suffering from malnutrition.

Life in the Depression: Rural America

Conditions in rural America varied by region but were almost universally harsh. Farm commodity prices had already been declining through the 1920s; the Depression sent them into free fall. Between 1929 and 1932, farm income fell by roughly fifty percent. Corn prices fell so low that some farmers burned it for fuel rather than selling it at a loss. Cotton prices dropped to levels that did not cover the cost of picking.

Farm foreclosures became epidemic. Banks and mortgage companies that had extended credit during the relative prosperity of the 1920s now called in loans that desperate farmers could not repay. Sheriffs arrived to auction off land, livestock, and equipment. In Iowa, Nebraska, and other farm states, neighbors gathered at "penny auctions" — bidding one cent for a neighbor's farm and returning it to him, or physically intimidating bank representatives who came to conduct sales.

Race and the Depression

The Depression fell hardest on those who were already most vulnerable. African Americans — the last hired, the first fired — faced unemployment rates far higher than the national average. Black workers were pushed out of jobs that white workers, newly desperate, now wanted. In cities across the country, Black workers were displaced from traditional occupations — elevator operators, waiters, porters, domestic workers — as white workers competed for any available employment.

Mexican Americans in the Southwest faced a different but equally devastating crisis. The Depression provided a pretext for an officially sponsored program of "repatriation" — the coerced or forced removal of people of Mexican origin to Mexico. State and local governments, eager to reduce relief rolls, organized mass removals. Federal immigration authorities participated. Between 1929 and 1936, an estimated 400,000 to 500,000 people of Mexican origin were removed from the United States, roughly half of whom were American citizens by birth. Families were torn apart. Established communities were destroyed.

Native Americans on reservations entered the Depression from a position of extreme deprivation created by decades of federal allotment policy. Federal appropriations for Indian health and education were meager. Reservation unemployment was catastrophic even before 1929 and became worse afterward.

Women and the Depression

The Depression affected women in contradictory ways. The collapse of household income meant that women's domestic labor — growing food, sewing clothes, managing household budgets — became even more economically important. But women also faced intense ideological pressure that blamed them for taking jobs that "rightfully" belonged to men. Many states and localities passed laws barring married women from government employment. Some school districts fired married women teachers.

Yet women's labor force participation did not actually decline dramatically during the Depression. In many households, a woman's income — from domestic service, factory work, or clerical employment — was the only income. The psychological toll on men who could not fulfill the breadwinner role was significant, and domestic tensions were documented by social workers, journalists, and researchers of the period.

Section 39.4

Dust Bowl and Rural Crisis

Origins of an Ecological Disaster

The Dust Bowl was not simply a natural disaster. It was the product of human choices — agricultural practices, land policy, and market pressures — interacting with a natural drought cycle to produce an ecological catastrophe that compounded the economic disaster of the Depression.

The Great Plains had been settled by homesteaders beginning in the 1870s and accelerating after the turn of the century. Federal land policy, particularly the Homestead Act of 1862 and subsequent legislation, had encouraged settlement by offering free or low-cost land to those who would cultivate it. Settlers plowed up the native grasses that had stabilized Great Plains soils for millennia, replacing perennial root systems with wheat and other annual crops. During the wet years of the early twentieth century — and especially during World War I when wheat prices soared — millions of additional acres were plowed under.

When the rain stopped in 1931, the consequences were catastrophic. Without native grass roots to hold the soil, the exposed topsoil began to blow away in massive dust storms. The phenomenon accelerated through the early 1930s. By 1934, the Dust Bowl extended across roughly 100 million acres of Oklahoma, Texas, Kansas, Colorado, and New Mexico. Dust storms — called "black blizzards" — blotted out the sun, buried fences and farm equipment, killed livestock, caused respiratory illness ("dust pneumonia"), and drove families from their homes. A single storm on April 14, 1935 — "Black Sunday" — carried an estimated 300 million tons of Great Plains topsoil as far east as New York City.

The Human Migration

Unable to farm land that had blown away, unable to pay debts and mortgages on failed crops, hundreds of thousands of families left the Great Plains. The most publicized migration was from Oklahoma, Texas, and surrounding states toward California's agricultural valleys, where rumor held that farm labor was available. These migrants — collectively called "Okies," regardless of their actual state of origin — traveled Route 66 west in overloaded vehicles, carrying whatever they could salvage of their former lives. John Steinbeck's 1939 novel The Grapes of Wrath depicted this migration through the fictional Joad family and became one of the most influential works of literature ever written about American social history.

The reality these migrants encountered in California was brutal. California's large agricultural operations needed seasonal labor but did not welcome the migrants as permanent residents. State and local authorities sometimes set up checkpoints at the California border to discourage or turn back migrants. Those who reached the fields found wages kept deliberately low because there were always more workers than jobs.

Federal Response to the Dust Bowl

The New Deal attempted to address both the immediate human crisis and the underlying agricultural and ecological problems of the Dust Bowl. The Agricultural Adjustment Act attempted to raise farm prices by paying farmers to reduce production. The Soil Conservation Service, created in 1935, worked with farmers to establish erosion-control practices — contour plowing, cover crops, windbreaks. The Farm Security Administration provided loans and assistance to struggling farm families and created one of the most remarkable bodies of social documentary photography in American history.

Section 39.5

Hoover and Limited Government Response

Herbert Hoover's Philosophy

Herbert Hoover was not an indifferent man or a fool. He had served brilliantly as the director of food relief for war-devastated Europe after World War I, organizing the distribution of food to millions of people across a shattered continent. As Secretary of Commerce under Presidents Harding and Coolidge, he had been perhaps the most energetic and capable member of the cabinet. When he was elected president in 1928 on the crest of Republican prosperity, he seemed ideally qualified.

But Hoover's response to the Depression was constrained by deeply held beliefs about the proper role of government. He believed in what he called "American individualism" — the idea that character, hard work, and voluntary cooperation, not government programs, were the proper foundations of American prosperity. He feared that direct federal relief to unemployed individuals would undermine the moral fiber of recipients, creating dependency rather than restoring independence. He believed that economic downturns were natural corrections that needed to work themselves out, and that excessive government intervention would delay recovery. These were not fringe ideas — they were the orthodox economic and political views of the era.

Hoover's response was therefore characterized by "voluntarism" — he encouraged businesses not to cut wages (many did anyway), organized private charity drives, and asked local governments to expand relief spending. He also took some more active steps: he established the Reconstruction Finance Corporation in 1932 to make loans to banks, railroads, and other businesses, and he modestly increased public works spending. But these measures were far too small to address the scale of the crisis.

The Bonus Army

The political and human failure of Hoover's approach was crystallized by the Bonus Army episode of 1932. During World War I, Congress had promised veterans a bonus to be paid in 1945. In 1932, with veterans unemployed and desperate, thousands of veterans — the "Bonus Army" — marched on Washington and established a large encampment on the Anacostia Flats near the Capitol. Congress passed a bill providing the immediate bonus in the House but the Senate rejected it. Many veterans went home, but thousands remained.

Hoover ordered the Army to clear the camp. Army Chief of Staff General Douglas MacArthur, assisted by Majors Dwight Eisenhower and George Patton, used cavalry, infantry, and tanks to drive the veterans from their encampment. The camp was set on fire. Women and children among the veterans' families were tear-gassed. Two veterans were killed. Newsreel footage and newspaper photographs of the Army driving World War I veterans from the capital with tanks and tear gas shocked the country. Hoover's already-dismal public standing collapsed.

The Election of 1932

Franklin Delano Roosevelt, the Democratic nominee, was the governor of New York and a former assistant secretary of the Navy. He had survived a bout with polio in 1921 that had left him unable to walk without assistance, though his disability was largely hidden from the public. Roosevelt ran on a platform of relief for the unemployed, regulation of the financial system, and a vague promise of a "New Deal" for Americans. What he offered, above all, was a different temperament from Hoover's: where Hoover seemed grim, defensive, and paralyzed, Roosevelt radiated confidence and energy. He won the election by a landslide, carrying forty-two of forty-eight states.

Section 39.6

Franklin Roosevelt and the First New Deal

Restoring Confidence

Franklin Roosevelt was inaugurated as president on March 4, 1933. By that point, the banking system had essentially collapsed. In the days before the inauguration, governors across the country had declared "bank holidays" — closing their states' banks to prevent further runs. On inauguration day, the New York Stock Exchange was closed. The financial system of the United States was, in the most literal sense, shut down.

Roosevelt's inaugural address began with one of the most famous sentences in American political history: "The only thing we have to fear is fear itself." The speech achieved its political purpose — it projected confidence, action, and leadership at a moment when the country was desperate for all three. Roosevelt also framed the Depression as a moral and social crisis requiring collective national response. The speech was broadcast across the country and greeted with enormous relief by millions who had feared that Hoover's paralysis would continue.

The Bank Holiday and Emergency Banking Act

Roosevelt's first act as president was to declare a national bank holiday — closing all banks temporarily under emergency powers. Then he called Congress into emergency session and within days had passed the Emergency Banking Act, which gave the Treasury Department authority to examine banks and reopen only those that were solvent. Roosevelt explained what was happening in his first "fireside chat" — a radio address delivered in calm, accessible language that explained complex financial events to ordinary listeners. He told Americans that it was now safer to keep their money in a reopened bank than under a mattress. When banks began reopening on March 13, deposits exceeded withdrawals. Confidence had been restored.

The First Hundred Days

The emergency session of Congress that convened in March 1933 remained in session for one hundred days, during which Roosevelt and the Democratic Congress passed an unprecedented array of legislation. The major legislation of this period included:

The Federal Emergency Relief Act (May 1933), which created the Federal Emergency Relief Administration (FERA) and appropriated $500 million for direct relief — the first significant federal relief for unemployed Americans. The Civilian Conservation Corps (CCC) put 250,000 young unemployed men — eventually three million — to work in national forests, parks, and other public lands, building trails, planting trees, controlling erosion, and improving infrastructure. CCC workers earned thirty dollars per month, most of which was required to be sent home to their families. The program was segregated by race.

The Agricultural Adjustment Act (AAA, May 1933) attempted to raise farm prices by reducing agricultural production, paying farmers to reduce acreage planted. For farm owners, the program provided crucial income support. But for sharecroppers and tenant farmers — disproportionately Black in the South — the program was often disastrous: when landlords reduced their planted acreage, they frequently evicted the tenant families whose labor was no longer needed, keeping the government payments for themselves.

The Tennessee Valley Authority (TVA, May 1933) was one of the most ambitious projects of the New Deal era — the creation of a government corporation to develop the entire Tennessee Valley through a system of dams, flood control measures, power generation, and regional economic development programs. The TVA was controversial because it represented the federal government competing directly with private utilities.

The Glass-Steagall Act (June 1933) separated commercial banking from investment banking and created the FDIC. By requiring commercial banks to stay out of the securities business and by guaranteeing deposits, it made the banking system fundamentally more stable. The law would be repealed in 1999, a decision that many economists argue contributed to conditions leading to the 2008 financial crisis.

The National Industrial Recovery Act (NIRA, June 1933) created the National Recovery Administration (NRA), which worked with industries to develop codes of fair competition. It also contained Section 7(a), which protected workers' right to organize unions — the most significant labor provision of the First New Deal. The Supreme Court struck down the NIRA as unconstitutional in 1935.

Section 39.7

Relief, Recovery, and Reform

The Philosophy of the New Deal

Franklin Roosevelt was not a systematic ideologist. He did not arrive in the White House with a coherent economic theory and then implement it. His approach was explicitly experimental — try things, see what works, abandon what does not, try something else. He was influenced by many advisers with different philosophies: Keynesian economists who believed in deficit spending to stimulate demand, traditionalists who worried about budget deficits, progressives who wanted structural reform, and advocates for farmers, workers, and the poor who pressed for policies that would help their constituencies.

The New Deal's three goals — Relief, Recovery, and Reform — were not always consistent with one another. Relief was urgently necessary but could conflict with fiscal discipline that many advisers thought necessary for business confidence. Recovery required different measures than Reform. Paying farmers to destroy crops raised food prices for unemployed urban workers. Roosevelt navigated these tensions through pragmatic improvisation, building political coalitions and keeping as many constituencies as possible satisfied.

Public Works and Employment

The New Deal's employment programs were among its most visible achievements. The Public Works Administration (PWA), led by Interior Secretary Harold Ickes, funded large infrastructure projects — bridges, schools, hospitals, courthouses, dams, and airports. Ickes was meticulous in ensuring that PWA projects were actually useful and free from corruption, which meant the PWA moved slowly but produced thousands of public buildings that still stand across the country.

The Works Progress Administration (WPA), created in 1935, became the largest and most visible of all New Deal employment programs. At its peak, the WPA employed over three million workers — building 650,000 miles of roads, 78,000 bridges, 8,000 parks, 800 airports, and 125,000 public buildings. WPA workers also produced some of the most remarkable cultural artifacts of the Depression era. The Federal Art Project, Federal Theatre Project, Federal Writers' Project, and Federal Music Project employed thousands of artists, writers, actors, and musicians, creating murals for public buildings, documenting American folk traditions, producing guidebooks to every state, and putting creative workers to work on projects that enriched public life.

Agricultural Policy

After the Supreme Court struck down the original AAA in 1936, Congress passed a new Agricultural Adjustment Act in 1938 that maintained the core approach — government payments to farmers in exchange for limiting production — while addressing constitutional objections. The Rural Electrification Administration (REA), created in 1935, provided loans to rural electric cooperatives to bring electricity to farm communities that private utilities had declined to serve as unprofitable. By 1940, the REA had dramatically increased the percentage of American farms with electricity, transforming rural life by enabling electric lights, refrigerators, radios, and farm equipment.

Housing and Mortgage Relief

The Home Owners' Loan Corporation (HOLC), created in 1933, refinanced more than one million mortgages that were in danger of foreclosure. The Federal Housing Administration (FHA), created in 1934, provided federal insurance for mortgages, encouraging banks to make home loans. The FHA transformed American housing by making long-term, fixed-rate mortgages available to many Americans who had previously been unable to obtain mortgage credit.

However, both the HOLC and the FHA implemented racially discriminatory policies that had lasting consequences. The HOLC developed a system of neighborhood ratings — using color-coded maps — that systematically devalued neighborhoods with Black residents and refused to refinance mortgages in those areas. The FHA similarly refused to insure mortgages in "racially mixed" neighborhoods and encouraged racial restrictions in new housing developments. This practice, known as "redlining," channeled federal investment toward white suburban homeownership while systematically denying similar benefits to Black families and communities, creating patterns of racial residential segregation and wealth inequality that persisted for generations.

Historical Vocabulary
Keynesian Economics

Keynesian economics refers to the economic theories developed by British economist John Maynard Keynes, particularly in his landmark 1936 work The General Theory of Employment, Interest, and Money. Keynes argued against the classical economic assumption that free markets would automatically return to full employment after a recession. He proposed instead that during severe downturns, private investment and consumer spending could fall into a self-reinforcing trap: falling demand caused falling production, which caused layoffs, which caused further falling demand. In such conditions, Keynes argued, government spending — even deficit spending, financed by borrowing — could break the cycle by directly stimulating demand.

Keynesian economics mattered enormously for the New Deal era and for subsequent American economic policy. Roosevelt's advisers were divided between Keynesians who wanted dramatic increases in government spending and traditionalists who worried about budget deficits. Roosevelt himself never fully embraced Keynesianism, which is why the premature budget cuts of 1937 caused the Roosevelt Recession. The massive deficit spending of World War II — which functioned as an enormous Keynesian stimulus — demonstrated the theory's validity by eliminating unemployment completely.

Section 39.8

Labor Rights and Social Security

The Wagner Act

The most consequential labor legislation of the New Deal era was the National Labor Relations Act of 1935, usually called the Wagner Act after its sponsor, Senator Robert Wagner of New York. The Wagner Act replaced the labor provisions of the struck-down NIRA with a stronger, clearer legal framework. It guaranteed workers the right to organize unions, to bargain collectively with their employers, and to engage in strikes and other concerted activity. It created the National Labor Relations Board (NLRB) to supervise union elections and prevent unfair labor practices by employers.

The Wagner Act transformed American labor relations. Before it, employers could and routinely did fire workers for union organizing, hire private police to beat strikers, blacklist union organizers, and use injunctions to stop strikes. After it, workers had federal protections and a federal agency to enforce them. Union membership surged in the years following the Wagner Act — from roughly three million in 1933 to over ten million by 1941.

The Congress of Industrial Organizations (CIO), founded in 1935 by John L. Lewis and other union leaders who had broken with the craft-union American Federation of Labor (AFL), led the most dramatic organizing drives of the period. The CIO organized workers in industries that the AFL had largely ignored — steel, auto, rubber, electrical manufacturing. Its greatest triumph was the unionization of the automobile industry. In the winter of 1936–1937, workers at General Motors plants in Flint, Michigan conducted a "sit-down strike" — occupying the factories and refusing to leave rather than picketing outside. After forty-four days, General Motors recognized the United Auto Workers (UAW) as the bargaining agent for its employees.

The Social Security Act

The Social Security Act of 1935 was the New Deal's most durable achievement and the foundation of the American social safety net. Frances Perkins, the Secretary of Labor who had worked for decades on labor and social welfare issues, was its principal architect. The act created three major programs:

Old-age insurance (what Americans now simply call "Social Security") was a federal program funded by payroll taxes on workers and employers that would provide monthly payments to retired workers beginning at age sixty-five. Unlike relief or charity, old-age insurance was designed as an insurance program to which workers contributed and from which they were entitled to draw. This design was deliberate: by framing it as earned insurance rather than welfare, Perkins and Roosevelt made it politically difficult to eliminate. Roosevelt famously explained the payroll tax by saying: "We put those payroll contributions there so as to give the contributors a legal, moral, and political right to collect their pensions and their unemployment benefits."

Unemployment insurance was a federal-state program that provided temporary income to workers who lost their jobs. By providing a temporary cushion, it helped stabilize consumer spending during downturns.

Aid to Dependent Children provided federal grants to states to support poor mothers and their children — the predecessor to what became known as "welfare."

The Social Security Act had significant limits. Agricultural workers and domestic servants — occupations that were overwhelmingly Black and overwhelmingly female — were excluded from old-age insurance coverage. The act did not include health insurance. Despite these limits, the Social Security Act represented a fundamental transformation in the relationship between the federal government and American citizens: for the first time, the federal government accepted responsibility for the economic security of workers in old age and during unemployment.

Historical Vocabulary
Social Insurance

Social insurance refers to programs that pool risk across a large population to protect individuals against economic hazards — unemployment, old age, disability, illness — that would be catastrophic if faced alone. Unlike charity or means-tested welfare, social insurance is financed through contributions (usually payroll taxes) and provides benefits as a matter of right rather than need. The Social Security Act of 1935 established the American social insurance system. The design of social insurance as earned entitlement — rather than charity — was deliberate and politically consequential: it made the programs politically difficult to eliminate, because beneficiaries understood themselves as collecting what they had contributed to and earned, not as receiving a handout.

Section 39.9

Critics and Limits of the New Deal

Opposition from the Right

The New Deal faced fierce opposition from business leaders, conservative politicians, and ideological opponents who believed it was fundamentally wrong. The American Liberty League, founded in 1934 and supported by major corporate figures, argued that the New Deal was unconstitutional, socialist, and destructive of individual liberty and enterprise. The Supreme Court itself represented the most powerful institutional check on the New Deal. The Court's conservative majority struck down the NIRA in 1935 and the original AAA in 1936, ruling that Congress had exceeded its constitutional authority.

Roosevelt, infuriated by the Court's obstruction and emboldened by his 1936 landslide reelection, proposed in 1937 to "pack" the Court by adding up to six additional justices. The plan was widely seen as an attempt to circumvent the constitutional separation of powers, and it was defeated — even many Democrats opposed it. Shortly after Roosevelt introduced the plan, however, the Court began upholding New Deal legislation, in what observers called "the switch in time that saved nine." The constitutional crisis passed, but Roosevelt's political standing was damaged, and the court-packing fight contributed to the formation of a conservative coalition in Congress that would block further progressive legislation.

The Roosevelt Recession

In 1937, Roosevelt made a serious policy error that contributed to a sharp economic relapse. Influenced by advisers who believed the worst of the Depression was over and worried about the growing federal deficit, Roosevelt cut government spending dramatically and tightened monetary policy. The result was the "Roosevelt Recession" of 1937–1938, in which industrial production fell thirty-three percent, unemployment rose sharply, and the economic recovery that had been underway since 1933 was badly interrupted. Roosevelt reversed course, resumed spending, and the economy began recovering again — but the episode showed the fragility of the recovery and the limits of what the New Deal had achieved.

Critics from the Left

The New Deal also faced significant criticism from the left. Huey Long's "Share Our Wealth" program proposed to cap personal fortunes, tax the wealthy heavily, and use the revenues to guarantee every American family a minimum income, free education, and other benefits. Long was building a national organization that some political analysts believed might allow him to challenge Roosevelt for the 1936 Democratic nomination. He was assassinated in September 1935. Father Charles Coughlin, a Catholic priest from Michigan whose radio program reached tens of millions of listeners, initially supported the New Deal but became increasingly critical, attacking Roosevelt and later promoting antisemitic conspiracy theories.

The Racial Limits of the New Deal

The racial limits of the New Deal require particular attention. Southern Democrats were a crucial component of Roosevelt's congressional majority, and they were committed to maintaining racial segregation and white supremacy in the South. They used their committee chairmanships to block civil rights provisions and ensure that New Deal programs could be administered locally in ways that discriminated against Black recipients.

The results were pervasive. Agricultural workers and domestic servants — excluded from Social Security and labor protections — were overwhelmingly Black. The CCC was segregated. The AAA's displacement of sharecroppers fell particularly hard on Black farmers. Public housing programs were racially segregated. The FHA's redlining policies denied Black families access to federally-backed mortgages. Local administrators of FERA and WPA programs in the South paid Black workers less than white workers and sometimes excluded Black applicants.

Roosevelt himself refused to support an anti-lynching bill despite persistent advocacy from NAACP leaders and Eleanor Roosevelt. He feared that Southern Democrats would filibuster the bill and that the fight would destroy his congressional coalition. Black Americans were not passive in the face of this discrimination. The NAACP, the Urban League, and Black newspapers consistently documented and protested the racial inequities of New Deal programs. A. Philip Randolph and other Black leaders pressed both the Roosevelt administration and the labor movement to address racial discrimination.

Historical Vocabulary
Redlining

Redlining refers to the practice of denying or restricting financial services — particularly mortgages and insurance — to residents of certain neighborhoods, usually based on racial composition. The term derives from the literal practice of drawing red lines on maps around neighborhoods deemed too risky for investment. The Home Owners' Loan Corporation (HOLC) developed neighborhood rating systems in the 1930s that systematically downgraded neighborhoods with Black or immigrant residents; the FHA incorporated similar assumptions into its underwriting guidelines. Banks and insurance companies followed these federal guidelines.

The result was that Black families were systematically denied access to federally-backed mortgages and home insurance, preventing them from building home equity and accessing the wealth accumulation that homeownership provides. Redlining's effects were not purely historical: the racial wealth gap between Black and white Americans in the twenty-first century has been substantially shaped by the denial of homeownership opportunities in the New Deal era and afterward.

Section 39.10

Chapter Review and New Deal Policy Checkpoint

By 1940, the New Deal had transformed American government, stabilized American democracy, and improved the lives of millions — but it had not ended the Depression. Unemployment in 1940 was still roughly fifteen percent, far above pre-Depression levels. What the New Deal had done was to prevent the complete collapse of the American economic and political system, provide relief to millions in desperate need, create lasting institutions like Social Security and the FDIC, establish the principle of federal responsibility for economic security, and build a political coalition that would dominate American politics for decades.

It was World War II, not the New Deal, that finally ended the Depression. The massive military mobilization of 1941–1945 absorbed every unemployed worker, stimulated industrial production beyond anything seen before, and demonstrated that deficit spending on the scale Keynes had recommended could indeed eliminate unemployment. But the New Deal's legacies were enduring. The regulatory framework it created shaped American capitalism for decades. Social Security became the most popular government program in American history. The labor unions built under New Deal protections created the prosperous industrial working class of the postwar era. And the assumption that the federal government had a responsibility to manage the economy and protect the economic security of citizens became a fundamental premise of postwar American political life.

Map Study

The Dust Bowl and the Great Migration West

The Dust Bowl of the 1930s was a geographic and human phenomenon that can be understood only through its spatial dimensions. Students studying maps of the Dust Bowl era should look for several patterns:

The core Dust Bowl region extended across the southern Great Plains — the Oklahoma and Texas panhandles, southwestern Kansas, southeastern Colorado, and northeastern New Mexico. This region received the least rainfall on the plains and had the most completely replaced native grasses with wheat cultivation. The worst "black blizzard" dust storms originated here and swept northeast across the country.

The migration routes from the Dust Bowl to California followed Route 66 — the old highway that ran from Chicago through Oklahoma City, Amarillo, Albuquerque, and Flagstaff to Los Angeles. Migrant families traveled in battered vehicles along this route, stopping in the agricultural valleys of California seeking work in the orchards and fields.

California's agricultural geography matters for understanding the migrants' experience. The Central Valley — the San Joaquin and Sacramento valleys — was the heart of California's industrialized agriculture. Large farms operated by agribusiness interests needed seasonal labor but were organized to prevent that labor from accumulating any rights or stability.

Primary Source Window

Franklin Roosevelt's First Fireside Chat, March 12, 1933

Creator: President Franklin D. Roosevelt · Date: March 12, 1933 · Type: Presidential radio address; public record

Context: On March 12, 1933, eight days after his inauguration and three days after banks began reopening following the bank holiday, President Roosevelt delivered the first of his "fireside chats" — informal radio addresses to the American public. Radio had by this point reached the majority of American homes, and Roosevelt was uniquely skilled at using the medium. He spoke as if in conversation with individual listeners rather than making formal speeches to large crowds, explaining complex events in accessible language and projecting calm confidence. An estimated sixty million Americans listened.

Roosevelt explained what the banking crisis had been, why the bank holiday was necessary, how the government was examining banks to determine which were sound, and why it was now safe to return money to reopened banks. He told his listeners in plain language how banking worked — that banks did not keep all deposits in their vaults but lend them out, which was why mass withdrawals could cause even sound banks to fail. He asked for public cooperation and confidence. He closed by telling Americans that together they could not fail.

Analysis: Roosevelt's fireside chats represented a new kind of presidential communication — direct, personal, and continuous. Previous presidents had communicated with the public through formal speeches, press releases, and newspaper coverage. Roosevelt spoke into Americans' living rooms in a tone that was educated but accessible, confident but not condescending. The first fireside chat achieved an immediate practical goal — convincing people to return their money to banks — and also established a pattern of public communication that Roosevelt maintained throughout his presidency. However, what the fireside chat does not reveal is also significant: it does not show us the experiences of Americans whose banks were not reopened because they were insolvent, or those who had already lost their savings in the thousands of banks that had failed before March 1933.

Questions for students:

  1. Why did Roosevelt choose radio as his medium for communicating with the public about the banking crisis?
  2. What assumptions about his audience does Roosevelt appear to be making in the fireside chat?
  3. What can this source tell historians about Depression-era communication, politics, and leadership?
  4. What can historians not learn from this source alone — whose experiences and perspectives are absent?
Historical Debate

Was the New Deal a Success?

The question: Historians have debated for decades how to assess the New Deal. Was it a successful response to the Depression? A fundamental transformation of American government? A missed opportunity for more radical reform? A program that helped some Americans while leaving others behind?

Interpretation A — The New Deal Saved American Democracy: One major interpretation holds that the New Deal's primary achievement was preventing the kind of political collapse that occurred in Germany, Italy, and Spain — the turn toward fascism and authoritarian government that the Depression facilitated elsewhere. By providing relief to millions of desperate Americans, restoring confidence in the banking system, protecting homeowners from foreclosure, giving workers the right to organize, and establishing social insurance, Roosevelt demonstrated that democratic capitalism could respond to crisis without abandoning democracy. Historians in this tradition emphasize the political and institutional achievements of the New Deal — Social Security, federal deposit insurance, the regulatory framework for banking and securities markets — as durable achievements that shaped American life for generations.
Interpretation B — The New Deal Was an Incomplete Reform That Reinforced Inequality: A second major interpretation argues that the New Deal's racial and gender exclusions were not incidental but structural — built into program design in ways that have had lasting consequences. By excluding agricultural and domestic workers from Social Security, by allowing local racial discrimination in program administration, by implementing redlining through HOLC and FHA, the New Deal created a two-tiered citizenship in which white workers received federal support for homeownership and retirement security that Black workers were systematically denied. This interpretation argues that the Depression was a moment when more genuinely universal programs could have been built, and that the political decision to sacrifice racial equality for Southern Democratic support was not inevitable but chosen.

Evidence: Evidence for Interpretation A includes the contrast between American democracy (which survived the Depression) and European democracies (many of which did not), as well as the documented impact of New Deal programs on relief, homeownership, labor organization, and social insurance. Evidence for Interpretation B includes the text of legislation, administrative records of discriminatory implementation, HOLC maps, and the documented racial wealth gap that persists as a consequence of differential access to New Deal programs.

Why it matters: The debate over the New Deal is not merely historical. It shapes contemporary debates about the design of social programs, the politics of racial inclusion, the limits of reform within existing political structures, and the question of how democratic governments should respond to economic crisis.

Student task: Based on the evidence presented in this chapter, which interpretation do you find better supported? What additional evidence would you need? Is it possible that both interpretations are partly correct?

Multiple Perspectives

Experiencing the New Deal

Workers and Labor Organizers: For industrial workers, particularly those in mass-production industries who had been unable to organize under the hostile conditions of the 1920s, the New Deal represented a genuine transformation. The Wagner Act's protections made unionization possible in steel, auto, rubber, and electrical manufacturing. Workers who unionized gained significant wage increases, improved working conditions, and greater job security. However, workers in agriculture, domestic service, and the South — disproportionately Black and female — received little or no protection from New Deal labor legislation.
African Americans: Black Americans experienced the New Deal in deeply contradictory ways. Many benefited from New Deal programs — WPA employment, PWA construction of Black schools and hospitals, FERA relief — in ways that genuinely helped them survive the Depression. Yet the structural exclusions and discriminatory administration of major programs meant that Black Americans received proportionally less from the New Deal than their needs required or their citizenship entitled them to. The Great Migration continued during the Depression, partly because conditions in the urban North — however hard — were better than those in the rural South.
Farmers: Farm owners in most regions benefited significantly from New Deal agricultural programs, which raised commodity prices and provided direct income support through production controls. But the benefits were not equally distributed. Large commercial farmers gained more from AAA payments than small farmers. Sharecroppers and tenant farmers were often harmed as landlords displaced them to reduce planted acreage. Migratory farm workers, like those who traveled from the Dust Bowl to California, received almost no New Deal protections.
Business Leaders and Conservative Critics: Many business leaders and conservative thinkers experienced the New Deal as an assault on economic freedom and constitutional government. The expansion of federal regulatory power, the protection of union organizing rights, and the deficit spending seemed to them a dangerous departure from American traditions of limited government and free markets. Some were motivated primarily by self-interest. But others held genuine philosophical commitments to limited government and were alarmed by what they saw as the potential for government power to expand without limit.
Constitutional Connection
The New Deal and the Commerce Clause

The New Deal's constitutional history is one of the most dramatic in American legal history. The commerce clause of the Constitution — which grants Congress the power "to regulate Commerce with foreign Nations, and among the several States" — had been interpreted relatively narrowly by the Supreme Court before the New Deal era. The Court had distinguished between commerce (the movement of goods across state lines) and production (manufacturing and agricultural activities), holding that the federal government could regulate the former but not the latter.

This interpretation created serious constitutional obstacles for New Deal programs. The Supreme Court struck down the NIRA in 1935 (Schechter Poultry Corp. v. United States) and the original AAA in 1936 (United States v. Butler). The constitutional crisis reached its peak after Roosevelt's 1936 landslide reelection, when he proposed the court-packing plan. Whether because of the plan itself or for other reasons, the Court shifted. In NLRB v. Jones & Laughlin Steel Corp. (1937), the Court upheld the Wagner Act and accepted a much broader interpretation of the commerce clause — that Congress could regulate any activity that had a "substantial effect" on interstate commerce. This decision established the broad commerce clause interpretation that has governed federal regulatory power ever since and allowed the modern regulatory state to exist.

Cause and Effect

The Great Depression

Long-Term Causes

Structural inequality in income distribution through the 1920s; chronic agricultural depression weakening rural banks; fragmented and poorly regulated banking system; overproduction in industry as consumer demand could not absorb what factories produced; destruction of native grassland creating Dust Bowl conditions; international financial fragility created by World War I debts and reparations.

Short-Term Triggers

Stock market crash of October 1929; Federal Reserve policy that tightened credit when the economy needed stimulus; wave of bank failures that destroyed savings and contracted the money supply; Smoot-Hawley Tariff that collapsed international trade.

The Event

Beginning in 1929 and deepening through 1933, the United States experienced the worst economic collapse in its history, with unemployment reaching twenty-five percent, industrial output falling fifty percent, and the banking system nearly completely failing.

Long-Term Effects

Fundamental transformation of the federal government's role in the economy; creation of Social Security, FDIC, SEC, and other lasting institutions; unionization of major industries under New Deal labor protections; New Deal political coalition that shaped American politics for decades; racially discriminatory New Deal programs that shaped patterns of wealth inequality for generations.

Historical complexity: The Depression had no single cause and no simple cure. It grew from the intersection of global financial fragility, domestic structural weaknesses, policy failures, natural disaster, and the psychology of panic. Understanding it as a complex, multi-causal event is essential to understanding both the period itself and the ongoing debates about government economic policy.

Continuity and Change Over Time

The Role of the Federal Government in Economic Life

What Changed

  • FDIC insured bank deposits, ending systemic bank runs
  • SEC regulated securities markets, preventing speculative abuses
  • NLRB protected union organizing, transforming labor relations
  • Social Security provided income in old age and unemployment
  • Federal agricultural programs managed farm prices and income
  • Federal public works created infrastructure Americans use daily
  • The assumption of federal responsibility for economic stability and citizen welfare was institutionalized

What Stayed the Same

  • American capitalism remained a private enterprise system — the New Deal did not nationalize major industries
  • Major corporations, banks, and financial institutions remained privately owned and managed
  • Agricultural workers, domestic servants, and Southern Black workers excluded from major programs experienced little structural change in their economic vulnerability
  • The fundamental structure of racial hierarchy in the South was largely maintained — Jim Crow laws remained in place

Why this pattern matters: The pattern of change and continuity in the New Deal era reveals something important about the politics of reform in American history. The New Deal transformed the federal government's role in ways that were genuine and lasting, but it did so by making accommodations with existing power structures — particularly the power of Southern Democrats committed to racial hierarchy — that limited the transformative potential of the reforms. This pattern recurs in American history: reform expands opportunity and protection for some while leaving others behind.

Chapter Summary

The Great Depression was the most severe economic crisis in American history, transforming the lives of virtually every American between 1929 and the early 1940s. It grew from a combination of structural weaknesses in the 1920s economy — income inequality, agricultural depression, a fragile banking system, overproduction, excessive speculation — combined with specific policy failures, most notably the Federal Reserve's contraction of the money supply at exactly the wrong moment and the Smoot-Hawley Tariff's disruption of international trade. The result was a downward spiral of falling production, rising unemployment, bank failures, and deflation that proved extraordinarily difficult to reverse.

The human consequences were devastating across the country. By 1933, roughly one in four workers had no job. Millions lost homes, savings, and farms. The Dust Bowl compounded the Depression in the Great Plains, destroying an already-fragile agricultural ecology and driving hundreds of thousands of families from their homes. African Americans, Mexican Americans, Native Americans, and other vulnerable communities bore disproportionate burdens. Women faced both the practical pressures of managing impoverished households and ideological hostility that blamed them for taking jobs that "belonged" to men.

Herbert Hoover's response to the Depression was shaped by deeply held beliefs about limited government and individual responsibility that were intellectually coherent but practically inadequate. His voluntarism was insufficient, and his refusal to provide direct federal relief left millions without help. The Bonus Army episode, in which the Army drove World War I veterans from their Washington encampment, crystallized public disillusionment.

Franklin Roosevelt's election in 1932 opened the most dramatic period of federal government expansion in American history. His First Hundred Days produced the Emergency Banking Act, the FERA, the CCC, the AAA, the TVA, and the Glass-Steagall Act, stabilizing the banking system and beginning the restructuring of federal agricultural and financial policy. The Second New Deal of 1935 produced the Wagner Act and the Social Security Act, the two most consequential pieces of legislation of the entire era. Workers gained the right to organize unions, leading to the rapid growth of the CIO. Old-age insurance, unemployment insurance, and aid to dependent children established the American social safety net.

But the New Deal had significant limits. It excluded agricultural workers and domestic servants from Social Security and labor protections. It implemented racially discriminatory housing policies through HOLC and FHA redlining. Local administration of relief programs often paid Black workers less or excluded them entirely. Roosevelt refused to support anti-lynching legislation. The New Deal also faced powerful opposition from conservatives who argued it was unconstitutional, from the Supreme Court that struck down major programs, and from left critics who argued it did not go far enough. The Roosevelt Recession of 1937–1938 demonstrated the fragility of the recovery.

The New Deal did not end the Depression — it was the massive mobilization for World War II that finally restored full employment. But the New Deal's institutional legacies proved durable: Social Security, FDIC, the SEC, the regulatory framework for banking and labor relations, and the political assumption that the federal government bore responsibility for economic stability and citizen welfare. These institutions and assumptions shaped American life for the rest of the twentieth century and remain central to American political life today.

A situation in which many depositors simultaneously withdraw funds from a bank, driven by fear of failure; self-fulfilling prophecy that caused even sound banks to fail.
FDIC
Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation; New Deal agency created in 1933 that insured bank deposits; eliminated the incentive for bank runs by guaranteeing savings.
Smoot-Hawley Tariff
Legislation passed in 1930 that raised American import tariffs to record levels; worsened the Depression by prompting retaliatory tariffs and collapsing international trade.
Dust Bowl
Region of the southern Great Plains devastated by severe drought and wind erosion in the 1930s; caused by the destruction of native grasses through agricultural conversion.
New Deal
Franklin Roosevelt's broad program of relief, recovery, and reform programs (1933–1938) that transformed the role of the federal government in American economic life.
First Hundred Days
The early period of FDR's first term (March–June 1933) marked by unprecedented legislative activity creating the major institutions of the First New Deal.
Agricultural Adjustment Act (AAA)
New Deal legislation (1933) that sought to raise farm prices by paying farmers to reduce production; overturned by the Supreme Court in 1936 and replaced in 1938.
Civilian Conservation Corps (CCC)
New Deal program (1933) that employed young unemployed men in conservation work in national forests, parks, and other public lands; segregated by race.
Tennessee Valley Authority (TVA)
Federal corporation created in 1933 to develop the Tennessee Valley through dams, flood control, electricity generation, and regional economic development.
Works Progress Administration (WPA)
Major New Deal employment agency (1935) that employed millions of Americans in public works, arts, literacy, and infrastructure projects.
Wagner Act
National Labor Relations Act of 1935; protected workers' rights to organize unions and bargain collectively; created the NLRB to enforce these rights.
NLRB
National Labor Relations Board; federal agency created by the Wagner Act to supervise union elections and prevent unfair labor practices by employers.
CIO
Congress of Industrial Organizations; labor federation founded in 1935 that organized workers by industry; led successful drives in steel, auto, rubber, and other industries.
Social Security Act
1935 legislation creating old-age insurance, unemployment insurance, and aid to dependent children; the foundation of the American social safety net.
Redlining
The practice of denying mortgages and financial services based on neighborhood racial composition; institutionalized by HOLC and FHA policies in the New Deal era.
Court-packing plan
Roosevelt's failed 1937 proposal to add up to six justices to the Supreme Court; politically damaging even as the Court independently began upholding New Deal legislation.
Roosevelt Recession
The sharp economic relapse of 1937–1938 caused by premature federal budget cuts; demonstrated the fragility of the Depression-era recovery.
Repatriation
The forced or coerced removal of Mexican Americans to Mexico during the Depression; affected approximately 400,000–500,000 people, including many American citizens.
Keynesian economics
Economic theory developed by John Maynard Keynes arguing that government spending during downturns can stimulate demand and restore employment.
Social insurance
Programs that pool risk across a large population to protect individuals against economic hazards; framed as earned entitlement rather than charity.
Hoovervilles
Makeshift shantytowns at the margins of American cities during the Depression; named satirically after President Hoover.

Major People and Places

Identification & Significance

Franklin Delano Roosevelt (FDR) (1882–1945)
Thirty-second president (1933–1945); architect of the New Deal; won four presidential elections; transformed the Democratic Party and the role of the federal government; guided the United States through Depression and into World War II.
Herbert Hoover (1874–1964)
Thirty-first president (1929–1933); capable administrator whose commitment to limited government and voluntarism proved inadequate to the scale of the Depression crisis.
Eleanor Roosevelt (1884–1962)
Activist First Lady; advocate for workers, African Americans, women, and the poor; expanded the public role of the president's spouse and kept issues of poverty and racial justice before the public.
Frances Perkins (1880–1965)
Secretary of Labor (1933–1945); first woman in a cabinet position; principal architect of the Social Security Act and major labor legislation; worked for decades on labor and social welfare issues.
Harry Hopkins (1890–1946)
FDR's close adviser and administrator of FERA, CWA, and WPA; believed in work rather than the dole as relief; executed major relief programs with remarkable speed and energy.
Huey Long (1893–1935)
Louisiana politician; radical populist critic of the New Deal; proposed "Share Our Wealth" program; assassinated in 1935 before his national political ambitions could fully materialize.
Father Charles Coughlin (1891–1979)
Radio priest who initially supported FDR; became a fierce critic of the New Deal; promoted antisemitic conspiracy theories; eventually censured by Church authorities.
John L. Lewis (1880–1969)
Leader of the United Mine Workers and founder of the CIO; led the most successful industrial unionization drives in American history.
A. Philip Randolph (1889–1979)
President of the Brotherhood of Sleeping Car Porters; leading Black labor organizer; pressed FDR and the labor movement to address racial discrimination.
Dorothea Lange (1895–1965)
Photographer whose Depression-era images — particularly "Migrant Mother" — documented the human cost of poverty and displacement; worked for the Farm Security Administration.
Dust Bowl
Southern Great Plains region devastated by drought and soil erosion; extended across parts of Oklahoma, Texas, Kansas, Colorado, and New Mexico; source of massive human displacement.
Flint, Michigan
Site of the 1936–1937 General Motors sit-down strike; landmark event in American labor history that led to recognition of the United Auto Workers.
Anacostia Flats, Washington, D.C.
Site of the Bonus Army encampment; scene of the Army's forcible removal of World War I veterans in 1932.
Tennessee Valley
Region of the rural South transformed by TVA dams, electrification, and regional development programs covering parts of seven states.

Short Answer Questions

  2. Compare the experiences of African Americans and Mexican Americans during the Depression and the New Deal. In what ways did both communities experience disproportionate hardship? In what ways were their experiences distinct?

Discussion Questions

  1. Was it inevitable that the economic crisis of 1929 would become the Great Depression, or were there points at which different policy choices could have prevented the worst outcomes?
  2. Herbert Hoover was a capable, experienced, and well-intentioned man. Why did his response to the Depression fail? Is it fair to blame Hoover for the Depression's severity, or were the limits of his response more a product of ideology and institutional constraints than personal failure?
  3. The New Deal is sometimes described as saving American capitalism from itself. What does this mean? Do you agree that preserving capitalism was the right goal in the 1930s?
  4. Eleanor Roosevelt used her position as First Lady to advocate for racial justice and the rights of workers and the poor. But her husband consistently made political compromises on racial equality to maintain his Southern Democratic coalition. How should we evaluate this political strategy?
  5. The New Deal built lasting institutions but also built racial exclusion into those institutions. How should Americans think about inheriting both the achievements and the injustices of the New Deal?
  6. The Depression-era United States remained a democracy while Germany, Italy, Spain, and other countries turned to fascism. Why did the United States avoid authoritarianism? What features of American institutions, politics, and society were important to that outcome?
  7. How does the history of the Great Depression and the New Deal illuminate contemporary debates about the proper role of government in managing the economy, providing social insurance, and protecting workers' rights?
  8. The New Deal established Social Security, which has become perhaps the most popular government program in American history. Why do you think this program has proven so politically durable while other elements of the New Deal were reduced or eliminated?

Research or Library Extension

MGU Library Research Project

Documenting the New Deal in Your Region

The New Deal built or improved infrastructure across the entire country — bridges, schools, courthouses, post offices, parks, airports, dams, murals, and countless other projects. Many of these projects are still in use today. For this research project, you will investigate the New Deal's legacy in your own community or region.

  1. Search for New Deal projects. Using local library archives, local government records, and newspaper archives, identify New Deal projects in your region — WPA, PWA, CCC, TVA, and other agencies. The National Archives holds extensive records of New Deal agencies; WPA state guidebooks documented infrastructure projects across the country.
  2. Identify specific structures. Find specific buildings, bridges, murals, parks, or other structures in your community built with New Deal funds. Photograph or describe them. Look for WPA insignia or plaques that often mark New Deal-era public buildings.
  3. Investigate who benefited and who was excluded. Examine local historical records for evidence of who worked on these projects and who was excluded. Did local administrators implement the racial wage differentials and exclusions documented in national records?
  4. Interview community members. If possible, interview older community members or local historians who may have knowledge of the Depression and New Deal era in your area.
  5. Write your findings. In a 3–5 page essay or equivalent multimedia presentation, describe what you found. Explain the historical evidence, describe the New Deal projects in your community, analyze who benefited and who was left out, and reflect on how your local findings connect to the national history described in this chapter.

Library resources to use: Local history collections, newspaper archives, census records, historical society resources, the National Archives online database, and the MGU History Library's reference materials on the New Deal, labor history, and local history.

Chapter Reflection

The Great Depression and the New Deal represent a turning point in American history that no student of the United States can afford to overlook. The Depression was not merely an economic event — it was a human catastrophe of the first order, reshaping the lives of millions of Americans across every region, race, gender, and class. It demonstrated with brutal clarity the consequences of unregulated capitalism, inadequate government response, and the interconnectedness of a national and global economy. The New Deal that responded to the Depression was not a perfect program — it was improvised, contradictory, racially limited, and ultimately insufficient to end the crisis. But it was consequential beyond measure, creating institutions and establishing principles that defined the relationship between the federal government and American citizens for the rest of the twentieth century.

The history of the 1930s helps students understand something essential about how American political life works. The Depression shows that institutions, policies, and ideological assumptions that seem permanent can be shattered by events, and that crisis creates political possibilities that did not exist in calmer times. Roosevelt was able to transform the federal government's role because the Depression had discredited the previous orthodoxy so completely that most Americans were willing to try something new. But the history also shows the limits of reform — the ways in which existing power structures, particularly the power of Southern Democrats committed to racial hierarchy, constrained what was politically possible and built racial exclusion into the foundations of the new welfare state. Reform expanded opportunity and protection for some while leaving others behind — a pattern that recurs throughout American history.

The legacies of the New Deal era are contested in contemporary American life. Social Security remains the most popular government program in the country, a monument to the New Deal's achievement. The regulatory framework for banking and securities markets created in the 1930s — partially dismantled in subsequent decades — remains relevant to contemporary debates about financial regulation. The labor movement built under New Deal protections has declined significantly from its postwar peak, and the distribution of income and wealth in twenty-first-century America bears uncomfortable resemblance to the inequality of the 1920s. The racial wealth gap that was partly created by redlining and other discriminatory New Deal-era policies continues to shape American economic life. Understanding where these patterns came from — and understanding both the genuine achievements and the genuine failures of the New Deal era — is essential to understanding the present.

Students who have studied this chapter should carry several questions forward into the rest of the textbook and into their own lives. What is the proper role of government in managing the economy and providing for citizens' economic security? How should democratic societies address severe economic crises — and what institutions and policies make them resilient or vulnerable? How do racial exclusions get built into apparently universal programs, and what are the long-term consequences? And perhaps most importantly: what does it mean to live in a society that has inherited both the achievements and the injustices of the past, and what obligations does that inheritance create? The history of the Great Depression and the New Deal does not answer these questions for us — but it equips us to engage them with the depth and seriousness they deserve.

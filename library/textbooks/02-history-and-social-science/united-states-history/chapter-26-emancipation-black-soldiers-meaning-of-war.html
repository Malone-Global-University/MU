Emancipation, Black Soldiers, and the Meaning of the War

United States History: Indigenous America, Republic, Conflict, and Change

Chapter Opening

"We are men, and if we are driven to arms in our own defense, we shall prove ourselves men."

On the morning of May 23, 1862, a man named Shepard Mallory crossed the James River in Virginia and presented himself at the gates of Fortress Monroe, a Union military installation on the tip of the Virginia Peninsula. Mallory was enslaved, and he had taken a significant risk — he did not know whether the soldiers at that fort would return him to his enslaver or offer him protection. He was not alone in that uncertainty. Across the South, hundreds of thousands of enslaved people were watching the war closely, calculating their chances, and making decisions about whether, when, and how to seek freedom. Mallory's case became famous because the Union general in command of Fortress Monroe, Benjamin Butler, made a legal decision that would reverberate through the entire Union war effort. Rather than returning Mallory under the provisions of the Fugitive Slave Act, Butler declared him "contraband of war" — enemy property that could be seized and held. It was a military argument, not a humanitarian one, and Butler made no promises about freedom. But the decision opened a door, and within weeks, hundreds of enslaved people were making their way to Fortress Monroe. Within months, that number would grow to thousands. Within the war's first two years, it would reach the tens of thousands.

The story of emancipation during the Civil War is not primarily the story of a presidential proclamation, though Abraham Lincoln's Emancipation Proclamation of January 1, 1863, was an event of enormous historical importance. It is, more fundamentally, the story of enslaved people who refused to wait for others to determine their fate. Long before Lincoln signed his proclamation, enslaved men, women, and children were using the chaos and disruption of war to claim freedom for themselves, by flight, by negotiation, by working for Union forces, and by making themselves indispensable to the Union military effort. They understood what many northern politicians were slow to grasp: that the war and slavery were inseparable, and that the Union could not win without confronting the institution that had given the Confederacy much of its economic and logistical strength.

This chapter examines how the Civil War transformed from a contest over union into a war for human freedom. That transformation was not inevitable. It did not happen easily or quickly. It required pressure from abolitionists, strategic calculations by military commanders, persistent advocacy from free Black leaders, and above all, the actions of enslaved people themselves who forced the question of slavery onto the Union's wartime agenda. The chapter traces that transformation from the first months of the war, when the Lincoln administration insisted it was fighting only to preserve the Union, through the military and political pressures that led to the Emancipation Proclamation, through the formation and service of the United States Colored Troops, and through the profound question of what freedom would actually mean for those who had been enslaved.

The formation of Black military units was one of the most radical departures of the Civil War era. At the war's beginning, Black men were barred from serving in the Union Army. Many white northerners, including many who opposed slavery, believed that this was a white man's war and that Black men were neither capable of nor entitled to bear arms in defense of the nation. Those assumptions were challenged, then dismantled, through the actual service of Black soldiers, who fought at Port Hudson, Milliken's Bend, Fort Wagner, the Crater, and dozens of other engagements. They served under conditions of systematic discrimination — lower pay, inferior equipment, exclusion from promotion, and the threat of being executed or enslaved if captured by Confederate forces. They protested, organized, and demanded equal treatment. And they fought with a determination that their white officers often described as exceptional, because for Black soldiers the war had a meaning that was immediate, personal, and existential. They were not fighting for an abstraction. They were fighting for themselves, their families, their communities, and the long-denied promise of American freedom.

Understanding this chapter requires understanding several interlocking historical problems. First, why did the Lincoln administration resist emancipation in the war's early months, and what pressures eventually changed its position? Second, how did enslaved people themselves shape the emancipation process, and what was the relationship between self-emancipation and official government policy? Third, what was the military significance of Black soldiers, and how did their service change the war's meaning and outcome? Fourth, how did the Confederacy respond to emancipation and Black military service, and what did those responses reveal about the stakes of the conflict? And fifth, what did the transformation of the war into a war for freedom mean for the long-term history of the United States — for Reconstruction, for civil rights, for the ongoing struggle over equality and democracy that has continued into the twenty-first century?

The evidence historians use to answer these questions is remarkably rich. It includes the letters of enslaved people who had learned to read and write and documented their own experiences; the pension records of Black Civil War veterans; the testimony recorded by the American Freedmen's Inquiry Commission; the official records of the United States Colored Troops; the diaries and letters of white Union and Confederate soldiers; the newspapers of free Black communities in northern cities; the speeches and writings of abolitionists like Frederick Douglass and Harriet Jacobs; the military orders and correspondence of generals like Butler, David Hunter, and Ulysses Grant; and the presidential papers and cabinet records of the Lincoln administration. Together, these sources allow historians to reconstruct the emancipation process from multiple perspectives — from Washington, from contraband camps, from plantation quarters, from regimental encampments, and from the Confederate home front.

The transformation of the Civil War into a war for emancipation is one of the most consequential developments in American history. It changed the war's meaning, its scope, its casualties, its duration, and its legacy. It produced the Thirteenth Amendment to the Constitution, which abolished slavery throughout the United States. It created the political and legal foundation for the Fourteenth and Fifteenth Amendments, which granted citizenship and voting rights to Black Americans. It began — though it did not complete — the transformation of the United States from a slaveholding republic into a nation that at least formally committed itself to the principle of equal citizenship regardless of race. That commitment was contested, broken, betrayed, and contested again across the following century and a half. But the story of emancipation during the Civil War is the origin point of that long struggle, and understanding it fully is essential to understanding the United States.

Historical Context

To understand the emancipation process during the Civil War, students must understand the political and legal landscape that shaped the war's beginning. When the Confederate states seceded and the war began in the spring of 1861, Abraham Lincoln and the Republican Party found themselves in a delicate and dangerous position. Lincoln had been elected on a platform of preventing the expansion of slavery into new territories, not of abolishing slavery where it already existed. He had repeatedly promised, both before and after the election, that he had no constitutional authority to interfere with slavery in the states where it was legal. And he meant it — not simply as a political calculation, but as a genuine constitutional conviction. The Republican Party of 1861 was a coalition that included committed abolitionists, moderate antislavery northerners who disliked slavery but feared racial equality, and former Whigs and Democrats who had come to the party primarily on economic and political grounds. Holding that coalition together required Lincoln to insist, at least publicly, that the war was about union and not about slavery.

There were also immediate strategic reasons to avoid the slavery question. Four slaveholding states — Missouri, Kentucky, Maryland, and Delaware — had not seceded, and Lincoln desperately needed them to remain in the Union. Maryland was especially critical: if it seceded, Washington, D.C., would be surrounded by Confederate territory and the Union capital would be essentially indefensible. Kentucky controlled the Ohio River and access to the western theater. Pushing immediately for emancipation risked driving these border states into the Confederacy, and Lincoln repeatedly stated that keeping them in the Union was a military necessity that outweighed other considerations.

The Confederacy, for its part, had made its commitment to slavery explicit. Confederate Vice President Alexander Stephens, in his famous "Cornerstone Speech" of March 1861, declared that slavery was the "cornerstone" of the Confederate government and the "great truth that the negro is not equal to the white man." Confederate leaders were confident that European powers, particularly Britain and France, would intervene on their behalf in exchange for guaranteed access to Southern cotton. The British textile industry depended heavily on Southern cotton, and Confederate leaders believed that economic necessity would overcome any British moral objections to slavery.

The institution of slavery was not merely an abstract political question during the Civil War — it was a military, logistical, and economic reality. The Confederacy's ability to wage war depended in significant part on enslaved labor. Enslaved people grew the food that fed Confederate armies, built the fortifications that protected Confederate cities, worked the farms and plantations that produced the economic surplus that funded Confederate military operations, and performed the logistical labor — digging, hauling, building — that freed white Confederate men for military service. Every enslaved person who left Confederate territory and reached Union lines was a double loss to the Confederacy: a loss of labor capacity and a potential gain for Union forces, which could use formerly enslaved people as laborers, scouts, informants, and eventually soldiers.

The abolition movement had been building in the United States for decades before the war. Figures like William Lloyd Garrison, Frederick Douglass, Harriet Tubman, Sojourner Truth, and Harriet Beecher Stowe had worked for years to make the moral case against slavery and to build public pressure for its abolition. The free Black communities of northern cities — Boston, New York, Philadelphia, Cincinnati, Chicago — had their own newspapers, churches, mutual aid societies, and political organizations that had been demanding freedom and equal citizenship for decades. When the war began, these communities immediately understood its potential meaning: that a war against a slaveholding Confederacy, if prosecuted to its logical conclusion, must become a war against slavery itself. Abolitionists pressed Lincoln from the war's beginning to make emancipation an official war aim. He resisted, but the pressure never stopped.

The legal framework surrounding slavery was also complex and important. The Fugitive Slave Act of 1850 required northerners to assist in the capture and return of escaped enslaved people, and it applied in wartime just as in peace. Many Union officers in the war's first months returned escaped enslaved people to their Confederate enslavers, consistent with the law and with Lincoln's stated policy. This created immediate practical and moral problems. Soldiers who had enlisted to fight the Confederacy found themselves acting as slave catchers, a role that many found degrading and inconsistent with their understanding of what they were fighting for. The contradiction between fighting for the Union and enforcing the Fugitive Slave Act was obvious, and it became increasingly untenable as the war continued.

Section 26.1

Enslaved People and the War's Meaning

When Confederate artillery opened fire on Fort Sumter in Charleston Harbor on April 12, 1861, the Civil War began as a contest over political union — or so the Lincoln administration insisted. The president's first call for 75,000 volunteers framed the conflict as a response to illegal rebellion against constitutional authority. He made no mention of slavery. In the months that followed, as the Union Army organized itself and the first major battles were fought, the official position of the federal government was clear: this was a war to preserve the United States, not to change its racial or social order.

The enslaved people of the South understood perfectly well that this official position was both dishonest and temporary. They had lived their entire lives in an institution that depended on careful observation, on reading the intentions of the powerful, on understanding what white men said publicly versus what they meant privately. They had access to information about the wider political world through their own networks — through the overheard conversations of slaveholders, through Black ministers and community leaders, through literate Black men and women who could read newspapers. And what they understood, from the moment the war began, was that a war between the United States and a slaveholding Confederacy was, at its core, a war about slavery. Whatever Lincoln said about union, whatever official policies the army proclaimed, the fundamental question in dispute was whether slavery would be permanent and expanding or whether it would be restricted and eventually destroyed.

This understanding drove an immediate and significant response. Enslaved people began testing the boundaries of the wartime situation from the war's first months. They organized work slowdowns on plantations with fewer white overseers, now that many white men had left for military service. They refused punishments that they previously would have had to accept. They spread information through invisible networks — what white slaveholders sometimes called the "grapevine telegraph" — about the location of Union forces, about the content of Lincoln's political statements, about the possibility of reaching freedom by reaching Union lines. And they began, individually and in small groups, to do something that had always been dangerous but had never been impossible: they ran.

The decision to seek freedom during the Civil War was not simple or easy. Enslaved people faced brutal consequences if caught — severe punishment, sale, family separation, and death. Many were separated from their spouses, children, and parents, who were held on different plantations or in different parts of the South. Many did not know the geography well enough to find their way to Union lines. And in the war's first months, they could not be certain that reaching Union forces would mean safety. Many early Union commanders returned escaped enslaved people to their enslavers, consistent with the Fugitive Slave Act and with Lincoln's official policy. The decision to run, therefore, required calculation, courage, and information.

But enslaved people ran anyway, in growing numbers. They came to Union lines in Virginia, in Tennessee, in Kentucky, in Missouri, in the Sea Islands of South Carolina after Union forces captured Port Royal in November 1861. They came alone, in pairs, in family groups, in larger organized groups when the opportunity arose. They came with information about Confederate troop positions, about supply routes, about fortification construction — information that was militarily valuable. They offered their labor to Union forces: cooking, washing, hauling, building, guiding. And they posed, through their very presence, the political question that Lincoln's government was trying to avoid.

The ideological significance of enslaved people's actions cannot be overstated. Their flight demonstrated something that proslavery ideology had always denied: that they were not content, not loyal, not suited to enslavement, not lacking in the capacity for free decision-making. Every person who ran to Union lines was a living refutation of the claim that slavery was a benevolent institution that enslaved people accepted or preferred. The scale of the movement — eventually encompassing hundreds of thousands of people — made the social order of the Confederate South impossible to sustain. Historians have documented how the mass movement of enslaved people toward Union lines constituted one of the most significant social upheavals in American history, one that was driven not by government policy but by the choices of enslaved people themselves.

It is important to understand that self-emancipation was not simply a story of heroic individuals walking to freedom. It was a complicated, dangerous, and uneven process. Many freedom seekers were captured and returned. Others reached Union lines only to find conditions in contraband camps that were crowded, disease-ridden, and sometimes exploitative. Women who fled with children faced particular dangers and hardships. Enslaved people in the deep South, far from Union lines, had fewer opportunities to flee and faced more severe conditions. The self-emancipation process was shaped by geography, by the military situation, by the policies of particular Union commanders, and by the specific circumstances of individual plantations and regions. It was a process that unfolded unevenly across the South over the course of four years, not a single dramatic moment of liberation.

Section 26.2

Self-Emancipation and Contraband Camps

The legal and political crisis created by freedom-seeking enslaved people became impossible to ignore in May 1861, when Shepard Mallory and two other enslaved men crossed Hampton Roads and presented themselves at Fortress Monroe. Their enslaver, a Confederate colonel, arrived the next day and demanded their return under the Fugitive Slave Act. General Benjamin Butler faced a problem. He was a lawyer as well as a general, and he was creative with legal arguments. His solution was to declare the three men "contraband of war" — enemy property that had been used in Confederate military operations (Mallory had been put to work building Confederate fortifications) and that could therefore be seized as a military measure. Butler explicitly avoided making any claim about freedom. He was not, he insisted, freeing these men. He was simply refusing to return valuable enemy property to the enemy.

The "contraband" solution was legally clever and practically significant. It gave Union commanders a workable policy that avoided the explosive question of emancipation while allowing them to hold freedom-seeking enslaved people. It spread quickly through the Union command. And it drew more enslaved people, who understood that Fortress Monroe represented at least temporary safety. Within weeks, hundreds of people had arrived. By the summer of 1861, more than 900 people were at Fortress Monroe, and Butler had organized them into a labor force that performed useful work for Union forces while receiving wages, rations, and some degree of protection.

Congress gave the contraband policy legal backing with the First Confiscation Act of August 1861, which authorized the seizure of Confederate property, including enslaved people, that had been directly used in Confederate military operations. This was a narrow law — it applied only to those who had worked directly on Confederate military projects, not to enslaved people generally — but it established the principle that enslaved people could be seized from the enemy as a war measure. The Second Confiscation Act, passed in July 1862, was broader: it freed enslaved people belonging to anyone who supported the Confederacy, and it authorized the president to employ freed people, "including persons of African descent," in military service.

Contraband camps — the informal and then increasingly organized settlements established near Union military lines — became a defining feature of the Civil War landscape. By the war's second and third years, camps existed throughout the occupied South: in Virginia, in Tennessee, in Kentucky, in Missouri, in Mississippi, in Louisiana, in South Carolina. Some of these camps were relatively well organized, with housing, schools, hospitals, and work arrangements. Others were overcrowded, undersupplied, and devastated by disease. The mortality rate in many camps was horrific. Smallpox, dysentery, typhoid, and pneumonia spread through crowded and unsanitary conditions. Children, the elderly, and the sick were particularly vulnerable. Some camps were essentially labor extraction operations, in which freedpeople were required to work for Union forces under conditions that differed little from the slavery they had escaped.

The camps were also sites of extraordinary resilience, community building, and self-organization. In the Sea Islands of South Carolina, where Union forces had captured Port Royal in November 1861 and where white planters had fled, formerly enslaved people began organizing their own communities immediately. They continued to farm the land, established schools, organized churches, and formed their own institutions. Northern philanthropists, missionaries, and teachers — the group that came to be known as "Gideonites" or "Port Royal experimenters" — arrived to assist and to conduct what they considered an experiment in whether formerly enslaved people could function as free laborers and citizens. The schools they established were among the first to educate formerly enslaved people, and they created a model that the Freedmen's Bureau would later attempt to replicate across the South.

Women played a central role in contraband camps and in the broader self-emancipation process. Many of the people who reached Union lines were women and children — because enslaved men were more closely supervised and more likely to be hired out or moved as Confederate labor needs changed, women and children sometimes had better opportunities to flee. Women in contraband camps worked as laundresses, cooks, nurses, and teachers. Some, like Harriet Tubman, served the Union Army directly as spies, scouts, and intelligence operatives. Susie King Taylor, who escaped to Union lines in the Sea Islands as a teenager, taught Black soldiers to read and write and served as a nurse and laundress with the First South Carolina Volunteers — all without pay, and without official recognition. Her 1902 memoir is one of the few firsthand accounts of this experience left by a Black woman.

The camps forced northern politicians, the Lincoln administration, and the northern public to confront the reality of freedom in concrete, immediate, and human terms. The freedom seekers were not abstractions or political symbols. They were people — men, women, and children — with families, histories, skills, needs, and claims on the nation. Their presence made it impossible to maintain the fiction that the war had nothing to do with slavery, and their obvious capacity for self-organization and productive labor undermined racist arguments about Black incapacity for freedom.

Section 26.3

Abolitionist Pressure and Military Necessity

From the war's beginning, the abolitionist movement pressed the Lincoln administration to make emancipation an official war aim. Frederick Douglass, writing in his newspaper, Douglass' Monthly, argued repeatedly that the Union could not win the war without striking at slavery, and that a war fought only for union without emancipating enslaved people would be both morally bankrupt and strategically foolish. Douglass understood, as a formerly enslaved man who had studied the political economy of slavery carefully, that enslaved labor was the backbone of the Confederate war effort. Strike at slavery, he argued, and you strike at the Confederacy at its most vulnerable point. Preserve slavery, and you fight the Confederacy with one hand tied behind your back.

Garrison's Liberator, the major abolitionist newspaper, made similar arguments. So did the Anglo-African, a Black-owned newspaper published in New York that spoke directly to free Black communities and was read as far as the Sea Islands of South Carolina. Senators like Charles Sumner of Massachusetts and Thaddeus Stevens of Pennsylvania — radical Republicans who were committed to emancipation and Black equality — pressed Lincoln in Congress and in private. They used their positions on key committees to pass legislation that moved step by step toward emancipation: the abolition of slavery in Washington, D.C., in April 1862; the Second Confiscation Act of July 1862; the authorization of Black military service.

Lincoln resisted. In August 1862, he published his famous response to an editorial by newspaper editor Horace Greeley, who had called on Lincoln to act against slavery. Lincoln wrote that his paramount object in the struggle was to save the Union, and that his actions toward slavery — whether to free all, none, or some enslaved people — would be guided entirely by what best served that goal. This statement is frequently quoted, but it must be understood in context. Lincoln was not expressing indifference to slavery as a moral question — he had stated repeatedly and privately that he believed slavery was wrong. He was explaining his legal theory of presidential authority: that he could act against slavery only if doing so was militarily necessary. And he was laying the groundwork for doing exactly that.

By the summer of 1862, the military situation had made emancipation look increasingly necessary. The Union Army had suffered major defeats at Bull Run, at Fredericksburg, and in the Peninsula Campaign. The Confederacy was holding its ground and showing no signs of collapse. The Union needed more men. It needed to strike at the Confederate war economy. And it needed to change the war's political meaning — both domestically, to energize the antislavery North, and internationally, to prevent Britain and France from recognizing the Confederacy.

The international dimension was particularly important. Britain had abolished slavery in its colonies in 1833 and France in 1848, and there were significant popular antislavery movements in both countries. Confederate leaders were betting that economic need — British textile mills needed Southern cotton — would override antislavery sentiment, and that Britain and France would recognize the Confederacy and perhaps intervene militarily on its behalf. But this calculation had a weakness: if the Union made the war explicitly about slavery, British and French popular opinion would make intervention politically impossible for their governments, regardless of economic interest. Lincoln and his Secretary of State, William Seward, understood this dynamic and factored it into their emancipation strategy.

Several Union generals had already acted on the logic of military necessity without waiting for Lincoln's authorization. John C. Frémont had issued an emancipation order in Missouri in August 1861, declaring enslaved people belonging to Confederate sympathizers to be free. Lincoln revoked it immediately, fearing the effect on border state loyalty. David Hunter issued a similar order in the Department of the South in May 1862, declaring all enslaved people in South Carolina, Georgia, and Florida to be free. Lincoln revoked that order too. But the pressure these acts created — the public debate they generated, the political signal they sent to abolitionists that military commanders were ahead of the administration — contributed to the calculation that led Lincoln toward the Emancipation Proclamation.

Lincoln presented a draft of the proclamation to his cabinet on July 22, 1862. Secretary of State Seward advised delay — not because he opposed emancipation, but because he feared that issuing the proclamation after a string of Union military defeats would make it look like an act of desperation rather than strength. He advised waiting for a military success. Lincoln agreed. He waited through the summer, as Union forces continued to struggle. The Battle of Antietam on September 17, 1862, though costly and tactically inconclusive, stopped a Confederate invasion of Maryland. Lincoln determined it was enough. Five days later, on September 22, 1862, he issued the Preliminary Emancipation Proclamation.

Historical Vocabulary
Military Necessity

Military necessity is a legal and constitutional concept that holds that the executive branch has emergency war powers that go beyond ordinary peacetime authority. Lincoln used this concept to argue that the Emancipation Proclamation was within his authority as Commander in Chief, even if he lacked ordinary legislative authority to abolish slavery. He framed the proclamation explicitly as "a fit and necessary war measure" — not as a moral or humanitarian act, but as a military one.

The argument from military necessity was significant for several reasons. First, it gave Lincoln a legal basis for action that he believed he otherwise lacked. Second, it meant that the proclamation applied only to Confederate states in rebellion — because only there did the military necessity argument apply. Third, it positioned emancipation as an act of presidential war power rather than congressional legislation, which meant it could potentially be challenged or revoked after the war. This vulnerability was one reason Lincoln and the Radical Republicans worked to pass the Thirteenth Amendment, which abolished slavery by constitutional amendment and could not be revoked by executive order.

The concept of military necessity in the emancipation context illustrates a broader feature of U.S. constitutional history: that the scope of federal power, and of presidential power, has often expanded in wartime in ways that have lasting effects. The legal arguments developed during the Civil War — about war powers, about the authority to confiscate enemy property, about the constitutional basis for freeing enslaved people — shaped constitutional doctrine for generations.

Section 26.4

The Emancipation Proclamation

The Preliminary Emancipation Proclamation, issued on September 22, 1862, served as both a political announcement and a final warning. It declared that on January 1, 1863, all enslaved people in states "in rebellion against the United States" would be "thenceforward and forever free." It gave Confederate states exactly 100 days to return to the Union and thus preserve slavery in their own states. None did. On January 1, 1863, Lincoln signed the final Emancipation Proclamation.

Students who read the Emancipation Proclamation for the first time are often struck by what it does not say. It does not proclaim universal freedom. It does not apply to the border states of Missouri, Kentucky, Maryland, and Delaware, which had not seceded. It does not apply to Confederate areas already under Union occupation and control — including the city of New Orleans and 48 counties in Virginia. It applies only to enslaved people in Confederate states actively in rebellion, which means it applied precisely where the U.S. government currently had no power to enforce it. Lincoln justified these omissions through the military necessity argument: the proclamation was a war measure directed at the enemy, and it could only constitutionally apply to enemy territory.

The proclamation's limits have led some historians to minimize its significance. The argument runs: Lincoln freed the people he couldn't free and didn't free the people he could free. But this argument, while not entirely wrong, misses the proclamation's actual historical significance in several ways. First, the proclamation changed the legal and political character of the war permanently. After January 1, 1863, every Union soldier who advanced into Confederate territory was legally an agent of emancipation. Every piece of Confederate territory captured became free territory. The war could no longer be won — even in theory — without making slavery impossible to maintain in large areas of the South. Second, the proclamation authorized, for the first time, the service of Black men in the Union armed forces, opening a door that would eventually allow 180,000 Black soldiers to serve. Third, the proclamation changed the war's international meaning: it made it politically impossible for Britain or France to recognize or support the Confederacy, because doing so would have meant siding with slavery against freedom in a conflict now explicitly defined as a war for emancipation. Fourth, the proclamation committed the Republican Party and the Lincoln administration to a policy from which retreat was politically very difficult, creating momentum toward the Thirteenth Amendment.

The proclamation had immediate practical effects as well. Union soldiers read it aloud in contraband camps and at plantations within occupied territory. Formerly enslaved people celebrated its signing with prayer meetings, songs, and public gatherings. Charlotte Forten, a free Black educator from Philadelphia who was teaching in the Sea Islands, recorded in her diary the extraordinary scene of January 1, 1863, when news of the final proclamation reached the community at Port Royal: singing, weeping, shouting, hymns, and — to her great surprise — a spontaneous singing of a national patriotic hymn by the assembled Black community. That moment of claiming the nation's own songs and symbols had a profound meaning: these people were declaring themselves Americans, with a claim on the nation's promises of liberty and equality.

The Confederacy's response to the Emancipation Proclamation was fury. Confederate President Jefferson Davis condemned it in the strongest terms and declared that captured Black soldiers and their white officers would be treated not as prisoners of war but as criminals — enslaved people who had incited servile insurrection and their co-conspirators. This was not merely rhetoric. It became policy, with deadly consequences.

Primary Source Window

The Emancipation Proclamation (January 1, 1863)

Creator: Abraham Lincoln · Date: January 1, 1863 · Type: Presidential executive order / public law

Context: Abraham Lincoln issued the Emancipation Proclamation as President and Commander in Chief of the United States Army and Navy, pursuant to his preliminary proclamation of September 22, 1862. It was a public executive order. Lincoln did not deliver it as a speech; he signed it in the White House, reportedly joking that after shaking hundreds of hands at a New Year's reception, his hand was trembling so much that his signature would look like a nervous man's — but that he was not nervous, and he wanted the proclamation to be signed firmly.

The proclamation declared that all enslaved people within the designated rebelling states and parts of states were thenceforward free, and that the executive government of the United States, including its military and naval authorities, would recognize and maintain that freedom. It further stated that freed persons of suitable condition would be received into the armed service of the United States, to garrison forts and positions and to serve aboard vessels.

Analysis: The Emancipation Proclamation is one of the most carefully lawyered documents in American history. Lincoln used the language of military authority and war powers throughout — describing it as an act of justice, warranted by the Constitution, upon military necessity. The proclamation lists the specific states and parts of states to which it applies, and specifically excludes areas under Union control. This precision reflects Lincoln's awareness of the legal and constitutional constraints he was operating under, and his effort to create a document that would withstand legal challenge. What the proclamation does not say is as important as what it does: it offers no provision for compensation to enslavers, no plan for the future status of freed people, no guarantee of economic resources or land. It declares freedom but does not specify what freedom means in practice.

Section 26.5

United States Colored Troops

The Emancipation Proclamation opened the door to Black military service by explicitly authorizing it, but Black men had been seeking to serve since the war's beginning. In the days and weeks after Fort Sumter, Black communities in Boston, New York, Philadelphia, and other northern cities organized and drilled militia companies, seeking to offer their services to the Union Army. They were turned away. The War Department's initial position was unambiguous: this was a war to be fought by white men. The law at the time prohibited Black men from serving in the U.S. Army (the Navy had no such prohibition, and Black men had served in the Navy for decades). And Lincoln, at the war's beginning, shared the widespread belief that arming Black men would drive the border states out of the Union and undermine northern white morale.

The pressure for Black military service built through 1861 and 1862 from several directions. Abolitionists like Douglass argued that arming Black men was both a military necessity and a moral obligation. Free Black communities in the North argued that they deserved the right to fight for their freedom and for the freedom of their enslaved brothers and sisters. Practical military commanders in the field began organizing Black laborers and, in some cases, informal Black military units without War Department authorization. The strain on Union manpower — the losses of the first two years of war were enormous — made the argument for any available source of manpower increasingly persuasive. And the Confiscation Acts of 1861 and 1862 had already established the principle that the labor of enslaved people could be used for the Union war effort.

General David Hunter's unauthorized Black regiment in the Department of the South, formed in the spring of 1862 and composed of men who had been enslaved in the Sea Islands, was one of the first efforts. When Congress refused to fund it, Hunter disbanded most of the unit, but kept a small nucleus that became the First South Carolina Volunteers — one of the first formally organized Black regiments. In Louisiana, a regiment of free Black men — many of them from wealthy, French-speaking Creole families with long military traditions — organized as the First Louisiana Native Guards. In Kansas, James Lane began organizing Black soldiers without authorization in 1862, essentially forcing the War Department's hand.

The Emancipation Proclamation resolved the authorization question. The War Department established the Bureau of Colored Troops in May 1863 to organize, recruit, and administer Black military units under a standardized structure. All Black regiments were to be designated "United States Colored Troops" (USCT) and given numerical designations. The initial exceptions were the famous Massachusetts regiments — the 54th and 55th Massachusetts Infantry — which Governor John Andrew of Massachusetts had arranged to form under state authority with prominent Black leaders like Frederick Douglass as recruiters.

Douglass's recruiting call for the 54th Massachusetts became one of the most famous documents of the Civil War era. He argued that service in the Union Army was not just a military duty but a claim on American citizenship: that once a Black man bore the nation's uniform and arms, no power on earth could deny that he had earned the right to citizenship. This argument — that military service created an entitlement to full citizenship — would become the foundational claim of Black political demands during Reconstruction and after.

The 54th Massachusetts recruited Black men from across the North, because Massachusetts's Black population was too small to fill the regiment alone. It drew men from Ohio, Pennsylvania, New York, Illinois, Indiana, Michigan, and other states. Its officers, like those of other early Black regiments, were white — federal policy prohibited Black men from holding commissions as officers, at least initially — but the regiment's men were led in the field by Colonel Robert Gould Shaw, a young white officer from a prominent Boston antislavery family.

By the war's end, approximately 180,000 Black men had served in the United States Colored Troops, organized into 175 regiments. They constituted roughly ten percent of the Union Army at the war's end. An additional 19,000 Black men served in the Union Navy. In total, about 200,000 Black men served the Union military effort in some capacity, including combat, labor, and support roles. Approximately 37,000 Black soldiers died during the war — a mortality rate higher than that of white soldiers, owing partly to combat losses and partly to the disproportionate use of Black troops for fatigue duty and garrison service, which exposed them to disease.

Historical Vocabulary
United States Colored Troops

The United States Colored Troops (USCT) were the Black military regiments organized under federal authority beginning in 1863. The term itself reflects the racial categories of the era: "Colored" was the standard mid-nineteenth-century term for people of African descent. USCT regiments were organized as infantry, cavalry, artillery, and engineer units. They were assigned regimental numbers that were separate from white units — the 54th Massachusetts became the 54th United States Colored Infantry upon federal muster, though Massachusetts retained its state designation.

The USCT was a remarkable historical institution. It organized men who had been enslaved months or even weeks before, men who had never held a weapon, men who were learning to read and write, alongside men who were free-born, educated, and experienced. It was also, from the beginning, an institution of systematic racial discrimination: Black soldiers were paid less, given worse equipment, assigned more dangerous duty, denied promotion, and denied the same legal protections as prisoners of war. Despite these conditions, the USCT compiled a significant combat record and became, as Frederick Douglass predicted, the central argument for Black citizenship in the Reconstruction era.

Section 26.6

Black Soldiers and Military Service

Black soldiers went into battle with an intensity of purpose that their white officers frequently noted. They were not fighting for an abstraction — they were fighting for themselves and their families, for people who were still enslaved, for a future in which they and their children might live as free and equal people. The stakes, for Black soldiers, were existential in a way they simply were not for most white soldiers. As one Black soldier wrote to a northern newspaper, they were men, and if driven to arms in their own defense, they would prove themselves men.

The first major combat tests came in the spring and summer of 1863. On May 27, 1863, the First and Third Louisiana Native Guards — Black regiments composed largely of free Black men from New Orleans — participated in the Union assault on Port Hudson, a heavily fortified Confederate position on the Mississippi River north of Baton Rouge. The assault was a tactical failure: Confederate defenses were too strong and the Union attack was poorly coordinated. But the Louisiana regiments charged the Confederate works with courage and discipline, suffering severe casualties. Their white officers, and the northern press that reported their performance, described their conduct in terms that challenged every racist argument about Black military incapacity. Northern newspapers reported that no body of troops had ever advanced against fortifications with more desperate valor than the Black soldiers of the First and Third Louisiana.

Two weeks later, at Milliken's Bend on the Mississippi River near Vicksburg, a Union garrison that included several newly formed and barely trained USCT regiments was attacked by a Confederate force. The fighting was brutal and hand-to-hand, and the Black soldiers held their position until gunboat support arrived to drive off the Confederates. Assistant Secretary of War Charles Dana, observing the aftermath, reported to Secretary Stanton that the battle had demonstrated that Black soldiers would fight, and fight well, under the most demanding conditions.

The most famous engagement of Black soldiers during the Civil War came on the night of July 18, 1863, when the 54th Massachusetts Infantry led the Union assault on Fort Wagner, a Confederate fortification guarding the entrance to Charleston Harbor. The regiment had been in constant service for weeks and had not eaten in two days. Colonel Shaw was offered the honor of leading the assault, knowing it was likely suicidal — the Confederate fortification was formidable, the Union naval bombardment had not destroyed it, and the regiment would have to advance across a narrow strip of beach under direct fire. Shaw accepted.

The assault began at dusk. The 54th advanced under withering fire, reaching the parapet of the fort. Shaw was killed at the top of the wall. Sergeant William Carney, carrying the regimental colors, was shot through the legs but crawled to the wall, planted the flag, and held it there even as the assault failed and the regiment fell back. Nearly half of the regiment's 600 men were killed, wounded, or captured. The Union did not take Fort Wagner that night. But the performance of the 54th Massachusetts transformed northern public opinion about Black soldiers. Newspapers across the North reported the assault in detail. Illustrations showed Black soldiers storming Confederate fortifications. The assault on Fort Wagner became the defining image of Black military service in the Civil War.

The Confederacy's response to Shaw's death was revealing. Confederate commanders, to demonstrate their contempt for a white officer who had commanded Black troops, buried Shaw in a mass grave with his Black soldiers rather than returning his body for separate burial. Shaw's family, informed of this, issued a statement that his father would not have had it any other way — that to lie with his men was an honor, not a disgrace. The response captured the distance between the Confederate ideology of racial hierarchy and the emerging Union ideology of Black citizenship.

Black soldiers engaged in combat throughout the remaining years of the war. They served in the Siege of Petersburg, in the Overland Campaign, in the Shenandoah Valley, in the deep South, in the western theater. By the war's end, Black soldiers had participated in 449 engagements, including 39 major battles. At least sixteen Black soldiers and four Black sailors received the Medal of Honor during the Civil War, though the process of awarding those medals was delayed for some and denied to others because of racial discrimination. The number of Medals of Honor awarded to Black Civil War veterans has continued to be the subject of historical research and political action, with additional medals awarded in the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries after systematic reviews of wartime records.

Section 26.7

Racism, Pay Inequality, and Resistance

Black soldiers in the United States Colored Troops served in an institution that was simultaneously committed to their military service and systematically committed to their subordination. The pay disparity was the most visible and most contested form of this discrimination. The Militia Act of July 1862, which authorized the employment of Black men in military service, specified a pay rate of ten dollars per month, with three dollars deducted for clothing — leaving Black soldiers with seven dollars per month. White soldiers received thirteen dollars per month plus clothing allowance. Black soldiers' pay was lower not simply in absolute terms but as a proportion of what was needed to support families: many Black soldiers had families in the North who depended on their military pay. The difference between thirteen dollars and seven dollars was, for many of these families, the difference between survival and destitution.

The pay disparity was not a mistake or an oversight. It reflected a deliberate policy rooted in the assumption that Black soldiers were worth less than white soldiers. The War Department's initial justification was that Black soldiers were expected to serve primarily as laborers and garrison troops, not as combat soldiers. But as Black soldiers demonstrated their combat capabilities — at Port Hudson, at Milliken's Bend, at Fort Wagner, and in dozens of other engagements — this justification became increasingly untenable.

Several Black regiments refused to accept their unequal pay as a form of protest. The 54th and 55th Massachusetts Infantry, the famous regiments organized by Governor Andrew, refused pay for eighteen months rather than accept the discriminatory rate. Corporal James Henry Gooding of the 54th Massachusetts wrote a remarkable letter to President Lincoln in September 1863, asking directly whether his men were soldiers or laborers, and arguing that they had performed every duty of a soldier's life to the complete satisfaction of officers who had once been prejudiced against them, and who now accorded them all the attributes of soldiers. Gooding's letter is an extraordinary document — a Black soldier, in military service, making a direct legal and moral argument to the president of the United States for equal treatment under the law.

The pay disparity was resolved, partially, by Congress in June 1864, which equalized pay for all soldiers regardless of race. But the law required Black soldiers who had been free before the war to swear an oath to receive retroactive equal pay — a provision that excluded enslaved men who had joined the army after escaping slavery. Some regiments refused to take the oath on the grounds that it was a humiliating distinction. The full equalization of pay was not complete even at the war's end.

The discrimination Black soldiers faced extended beyond pay. They were assigned a disproportionate share of fatigue duty — the non-combat labor of military life: digging fortifications, building roads, hauling supplies, burying the dead. This labor was exhausting and degrading, and it prevented Black soldiers from engaging in the military training that would have prepared them better for combat. It also exposed them to disease at higher rates than white soldiers, who were more likely to be assigned to active campaigning. The mortality rate from disease was significantly higher for Black soldiers than for white soldiers.

Black soldiers were also explicitly excluded from officer commissions for most of the war. A small number of Black officers had been commissioned in the Louisiana Native Guards before federal authority was asserted, but federal policy prohibited Black commissions. As white officers who had commanded Black regiments were killed or promoted and moved to other assignments, Black soldiers who had demonstrated leadership and military skill were passed over for promotion in favor of white officers. The exclusion from officer rank was both a practical handicap and a profound symbolic statement about the limits of Black military citizenship.

Despite these conditions — or because of them — Black soldiers developed a powerful collective identity and sense of military purpose. They organized prayer meetings and religious services; they established schools and literacy classes within their regiments, because many had enlisted unable to read and were determined to learn; they wrote letters home and to newspapers; they organized protests against pay discrimination; they maintained discipline and unit cohesion under conditions that would have broken many units. The letters of Black soldiers preserved in regimental records, in the pension archives of the National Archives, and in the records of the Bureau of Colored Troops constitute one of the richest bodies of documentary evidence from the Civil War era.

Historical Debate

Was the Emancipation Proclamation Primarily a Military Measure or a Moral Statement?

The question: Historians have long debated the primary motivation and significance of the Emancipation Proclamation. Was it primarily a pragmatic military strategy, aimed at weakening the Confederacy and strengthening the Union war effort? Or was it a moral statement reflecting Lincoln's personal and political commitment to human freedom?

Interpretation A — The Military Necessity Argument: Some historians, following Lincoln's own language, emphasize that the Emancipation Proclamation was framed as a military measure and should be understood primarily in those terms. Lincoln justified the proclamation through the commander-in-chief's war powers, not through any abstract moral authority. He deliberately excluded border states and Union-controlled areas, consistent with a military logic: the proclamation was directed at the enemy, not at the Union's own loyal citizens. Under this interpretation, the proclamation was above all a weapon of war, designed to deprive the Confederacy of enslaved labor, to energize Union war aims, to prevent European recognition of the Confederacy, and to open the door to Black military service.
Interpretation B — The Moral and Political Commitment Argument: Other historians emphasize that Lincoln's private statements and his broader political career reveal a genuine moral commitment to freedom that the Emancipation Proclamation expressed, however imperfectly. Lincoln had called slavery a monstrous injustice years before the war. His Second Inaugural Address, delivered in March 1865, described the war as a divine punishment for the sin of slavery — a profoundly moral framing that went far beyond military necessity. Under this interpretation, the proclamation was both military and moral — Lincoln used the language of military necessity because he believed it was his strongest legal ground, not because it was his only motivation.

Evidence: Evidence supporting the military interpretation includes Lincoln's own public statements, the text of the proclamation, and the timing and scope of the document. Evidence supporting the moral and political interpretation includes Lincoln's private letters, his speeches before and after the proclamation, the testimony of cabinet members like Salmon Chase and Secretary of War Stanton, and the trajectory of Lincoln's career.

Why it matters: This debate matters because it shapes how we understand Lincoln's legacy, the causes and meaning of the Civil War, and the relationship between moral principle and political pragmatism in American history. The debate also matters because it shapes how we understand the emancipation process: if the proclamation was primarily military, then enslaved people's own actions and the abolitionist movement deserve more credit for forcing the government's hand than Lincoln typically receives.

Section 26.8

Southern Responses to Emancipation

The Confederacy's responses to emancipation and Black military service were violent, revealing, and ultimately self-defeating. Confederate leaders understood with perfect clarity what emancipation and Black military service meant: they meant that the war was now explicitly a war over slavery and racial hierarchy, that the Union was now committed to destroying the institution that the Confederacy was built on, and that Black men were being given guns and trained to fight for their freedom. The Confederate government's response was to make its policy toward Black soldiers a policy of murder and re-enslavement.

In December 1862, before the final proclamation was even issued, the Confederate Congress passed resolutions declaring that white officers commanding Black troops would be executed if captured, and that captured Black soldiers would be handed over to state authorities to be dealt with under slave codes — which meant imprisonment, sale into slavery, or death. Jefferson Davis formally approved these policies. They were not consistently implemented — the Confederacy's ability to enforce uniform policy was limited, and some Black prisoners were taken and held, particularly early in the war — but they were a standing threat that shaped the experience of Black soldiers throughout the conflict.

The most notorious implementation of the Confederate policy came on April 12, 1864, at Fort Pillow, Tennessee. A Confederate cavalry force under the command of General Nathan Bedford Forrest — a Memphis slave trader who became one of the Confederacy's most effective cavalry commanders and who would later help found the Ku Klux Klan — attacked the Union garrison. Fort Pillow was defended by about 600 Union soldiers, roughly half of them Black. After the Confederate assault overran the outer fortifications and the Union commander was killed, the Union survivors attempted to surrender. Confederate forces continued killing them anyway. When the battle was over, 64 percent of the fort's Black soldiers were dead, compared with 31 percent of the white soldiers. Survivors' testimony, later collected by a Congressional committee, described Confederate soldiers killing surrendering men, shooting the wounded, and burying some people alive.

Fort Pillow became an immediate cause célèbre in the North. "Remember Fort Pillow!" became a rallying cry for Black soldiers and their white allies. Congress held hearings. Lincoln threatened reprisals against Confederate prisoners if the killing of Black soldiers continued. The practical effect of threats of reprisal was limited — the Confederacy continued to execute and re-enslave Black prisoners in some cases, though the most systematic killings did not recur on the scale of Fort Pillow. But the incident documented, with Congressional authority, what Black soldiers had always known: that they faced not merely the ordinary dangers of combat but the specific danger of being killed after surrender.

The Fort Pillow massacre and similar incidents had a profound effect on Black soldiers' determination. Many resolved to give no quarter themselves if Confederate forces were going to give none. There are documented instances of Black soldiers refusing Confederate surrender attempts after Fort Pillow, shouting "Remember Fort Pillow!" as they advanced. This dynamic contributed to some of the most brutal and least-remembered engagements of the Civil War's final year.

Southern slaveholders' responses to emancipation on their own plantations varied widely and reveal the complicated reality of a social system under existential pressure. Some slaveholders reacted with violence when enslaved people attempted to leave, killing people who sought to reach Union lines. Others attempted to remove enslaved people deep into the interior — to Texas, to Alabama, to the interior of Georgia — to keep them away from advancing Union forces. This practice, called "refugeeing," tore families apart and moved hundreds of thousands of enslaved people in the war's final months. Still other slaveholders attempted to maintain the fiction of normalcy — to keep enslaved people working on the plantation through a combination of reassurance and coercion, insisting that the war would not change things. Their enslaved workers, following the news carefully through their own networks, generally knew better.

The collapse of the plantation system in areas of Confederate military defeat was sometimes sudden and sometimes gradual. When Sherman's forces marched through Georgia in the fall of 1864 and into South Carolina in early 1865, tens of thousands of formerly enslaved people followed his army, creating what historians have called a vast migration toward freedom. Sherman's Special Field Order No. 15, issued in January 1865, set aside a strip of coastal land in South Carolina and Georgia — "forty acres and a mule" in the popular phrase — for redistribution to freedpeople. This was the most radical land reform measure of the Civil War era, and it was later reversed by President Andrew Johnson after Lincoln's assassination. But its temporary existence revealed what freedom looked like to the people who had been enslaved: not just formal liberty but economic independence, land ownership, and the material basis for genuine self-determination.

Multiple Perspectives

The Emancipation Proclamation and Black Military Service

Enslaved people in the Confederate South experienced the emancipation process as something they had helped create and were still fighting to achieve. Many had fled to Union lines months or years before the proclamation. For those who remained on plantations, the proclamation was news that spread through the "grapevine telegraph" — overheard from slaveholders' conversations, passed from person to person, arriving days or weeks late and sometimes garbled. The reality of freedom varied enormously depending on geography, the military situation, and individual circumstances.
Free Black men in the North experienced the authorization of Black military service as a hard-won opportunity and a profound civic moment. They had been demanding the right to serve since the war's beginning, and they understood perfectly well what Frederick Douglass had argued: that military service was the path to citizenship. At the same time, they confronted the reality of systematic discrimination — unequal pay, exclusion from officer ranks, assignment to fatigue duty — that made the gap between the ideal of military citizenship and the reality of military service painfully clear.
White Union soldiers had widely varying responses to the emancipation proclamation and to Black military service. Some were committed abolitionists who welcomed both developments as long overdue. Many more were pragmatic: they had come to see that the war could not be won without striking at slavery. Others — a significant minority — were bitterly opposed. Letters home from white Union soldiers reveal a wide spectrum of opinion, from outrage at fighting alongside Black men to admiration for Black soldiers' courage.
Confederate soldiers and civilians experienced emancipation as an existential threat to everything they were fighting for. Confederate soldiers who captured Black soldiers faced the official policy of killing or re-enslaving them — a policy that reflected not just military strategy but the deep emotional investment of Confederate culture in racial hierarchy. For white southern women managing plantations while their men were at war, the growing restiveness of enslaved workers created fear, resentment, and sometimes astonishing revelations about the true nature of the institution they had always been told was benevolent.
Section 26.9

Freedom as a War Aim

By the final year of the Civil War, the transformation of the conflict into a war for emancipation was essentially complete. The Union Army was fighting under the Emancipation Proclamation, with 180,000 Black soldiers in its ranks. Congress had passed the Thirteenth Amendment on January 31, 1865, and sent it to the states for ratification. Sherman's armies were marching through the deep South, demonstrating the military collapse of the Confederacy and the social collapse of the plantation system. Lincoln's Second Inaugural Address, delivered on March 4, 1865, five weeks before Lee's surrender at Appomattox, framed the war in terms that went far beyond military necessity: it was a divine reckoning for the sin of slavery, a national judgment that required both sides to accept.

The transformation of the war's meaning had been driven from multiple directions simultaneously. Enslaved people had forced the issue through self-emancipation. Abolitionists had maintained pressure on Lincoln and Congress. Free Black communities had organized, recruited, and served. Military commanders had found the argument from necessity compelling. Congress had passed the legislation that gave emancipation legal force. And Lincoln himself had grown — slowly, reluctantly, and then decisively — into the role of emancipator.

The meaning of freedom as the war neared its end was debated and contested from every direction. For formerly enslaved people, freedom meant, above all, the freedom from coercion — the end of the threat of sale, of separation, of the auction block, of the lash. It meant the ability to be with one's family, to know that children would not be sold away. It meant legal recognition of marriages that slavery had never recognized. It meant the ability to move, to choose where to work, to negotiate the terms of one's labor. It meant access to education, which had been forbidden under slave law. And it meant, in the aspirations of the most politically conscious freedpeople, full citizenship: the right to vote, to hold office, to serve on juries, to enjoy the equal protection of the laws.

These aspirations were only partially fulfilled during Reconstruction. The Thirteenth Amendment abolished slavery. The Fourteenth Amendment guaranteed citizenship and equal protection of the laws. The Fifteenth Amendment guaranteed the right to vote regardless of race. But the implementation of these constitutional changes was contested and eventually undermined by white supremacist violence, political abandonment by the North, and the Supreme Court's narrow interpretations of the Reconstruction amendments. The promise of emancipation — of full equality under the law — remained unfulfilled for nearly a century after the Civil War, until the civil rights movement of the 1950s and 1960s renewed the struggle and won new legal protections that, again, have been contested and imperfectly implemented.

The Black soldiers who served in the United States Colored Troops understood this broader political dimension of their service. They had earned, in the most direct possible way, the claim to citizenship that Frederick Douglass had argued for. Their service was an argument that could not easily be dismissed. When Reconstruction began and the question of Black citizenship and voting rights was debated, the service of Black soldiers was central to the political case. It was Black veterans, in significant numbers, who organized the political life of Reconstruction-era Black communities, ran for office, served in state legislatures and the Congress, and built the institutions of Black citizenship that white supremacist violence later dismantled.

Continuity and Change Over Time

The Meaning of Black Military Service

What Changed

Before the Civil War, federal law prohibited Black men from serving in the U.S. Army, and the Dred Scott decision of 1857 held that Black people, whether free or enslaved, were not citizens of the United States. By the war's end, 180,000 Black men had served in the Union Army, and the Fourteenth Amendment was in process, which would explicitly overturn Dred Scott and guarantee citizenship to all people born in the United States. The service of Black soldiers changed not only the military balance of the war but the political terms of citizenship.

What Stayed the Same

Despite the military service of Black men, the institution of racial discrimination did not end with the Civil War. Black soldiers were paid less, excluded from officer ranks, assigned to dangerous duty, and killed when captured — all manifestations of the same racial hierarchy that slavery had embodied. After the war, Black veterans returned to a society that denied them many of the rights their service had supposedly earned. Reconstruction governments gave Black men voting rights, but the collapse of Reconstruction in the 1870s led to disfranchisement, segregation, and racial violence that persisted for nearly a century.

Who experienced change most directly: The formerly enslaved men and women who reached Union lines and escaped the institution of slavery. Black soldiers experienced change through the act of military service itself: they were now armed, uniformed, organized, and officially recognized as defenders of the United States — a status incompatible with the status of a slave.

Who experienced continuity: White southerners who survived the Civil War attempted, with some success in the short term, to reconstruct racial hierarchy on foundations other than formal slavery. The Black Codes passed by southern states in 1865 and 1866 attempted to restore many of the practical conditions of slavery under different legal forms.

Why this pattern matters: The pattern of dramatic formal change accompanied by persistent informal inequality is one of the most important patterns in U.S. racial history. Understanding how legal emancipation was followed by more than a century of racial subordination under other legal and extralegal systems is essential to understanding the long arc of Black American history and the continuing relevance of the Civil War to contemporary debates about race, equality, and democracy.

Section 26.10

Chapter Review and Emancipation Primary Source Study

The story of emancipation and Black military service during the Civil War resists simple summary because it was a process that unfolded over four years, involved hundreds of thousands of people, was driven simultaneously from below and above, and produced results that were simultaneously transformative and incomplete. To understand it fully, students must hold several historical realities in view at once.

The first reality is that enslaved people were the primary agents of their own liberation. Before Lincoln signed any proclamation, before Congress passed any confiscation act, before any USCT regiment was organized, enslaved people were fleeing to Union lines, disrupting Confederate production, providing intelligence to Union forces, and forcing the political question of slavery onto the war's agenda. The self-emancipation of hundreds of thousands of people was not a reaction to government policy; it was a force that drove government policy.

The second reality is that the Lincoln administration moved toward emancipation through a combination of moral conviction, political calculation, military necessity, and constant pressure from abolitionists, Congress, enslaved people themselves, and the logic of the war. Lincoln was neither the pure moral visionary that hagiographic accounts sometimes portray nor the reluctant, indifferent pragmatist that more critical accounts sometimes suggest. He was a politically skilled leader who moved carefully, with attention to constitutional constraints and political coalition, but who moved in a direction that was consistent with his moral convictions.

The third reality is that Black military service was one of the most consequential developments of the Civil War era — not just militarily but politically. By serving, Black soldiers made a claim on citizenship that shaped the entire subsequent history of Reconstruction and beyond. Their service was the argument that Frederick Douglass had been making for decades: that Black men were Americans, with rights and duties equal to those of white Americans, and that the nation's promises of liberty and equality applied to them.

The fourth reality is that emancipation, as it actually happened, was far more complicated than any single document or moment can capture. It was a process of negotiation, of violence, of joy, of hardship, of political struggle, and of human assertion that unfolded across four years in a vast and varied landscape. The Emancipation Proclamation was a crucial moment in that process — but it was neither the beginning nor the end of it.

Everyday Life and Social History

Freedom Seekers and the Transition from Slavery

The experience of freedom seekers — people who left plantations and farms to reach Union lines — was simultaneously exhilarating and harrowing. Most freedom seekers left behind everything they had accumulated in their lives: the small plots of garden ground they had tended, the few possessions they had been allowed to keep, the houses they had built, the communities they had formed. They left behind, often, people they loved — spouses held on other plantations, children who were too young or too sick to travel, parents too old to walk. The decision to leave was rarely simple.

Women who fled with children faced particular hardships. They had to travel, often at night, often through unfamiliar terrain, with children who might cry and attract attention. They had to find food, avoid Confederate patrols, navigate rivers and swamps. Many were caught. Some were returned to brutal punishment. Those who made it to Union lines often found conditions in contraband camps that were barely better than what they had left: overcrowding, disease, inadequate food and housing, and a new kind of exploitation as Union officials organized their labor for military purposes.

Once in the camps, the practical work of building free lives began. Women organized households under new conditions. Men sought wage labor or enlisted in USCT regiments. Both men and women sought education with extraordinary intensity. Schools established in contraband camps were crowded beyond capacity. Men and women who had been forbidden under slave law to learn to read and write pursued literacy with passionate determination, understanding that it was both a practical tool and a symbol of their status as free people.

The search for family members separated by slavery was another defining feature of the transition from slavery to freedom. Formerly enslaved people combed the camps, consulted every new arrival, posted notices, traveled considerable distances, and wrote letters — when they could — trying to locate spouses, children, parents, and siblings from whom they had been separated by sale. The reunification of families separated by slavery was one of the most powerful and moving dimensions of the emancipation process, and the failure to reunite was one of the most devastating legacies of slavery.

Constitutional Connection

Emancipation and Constitutional Change

The Emancipation Proclamation was not a constitutional amendment. It was a presidential executive order, issued under the claimed authority of the commander-in-chief's war powers. Lincoln himself understood that this meant it might be legally vulnerable: a future president or a future court might declare it unconstitutional, or it might lose its legal force when the war ended. This is one reason Lincoln worked strenuously to secure the passage of the Thirteenth Amendment by Congress before the war ended.

The Thirteenth Amendment, passed by Congress on January 31, 1865, and ratified by the states in December 1865, abolished slavery throughout the United States permanently and by constitutional authority. It was the first amendment to the Constitution since 1804, and the most radical change in American law since the Constitution itself. It constitutionalized the emancipation process, making it impossible to reverse by executive order or legislative statute.

The Thirteenth Amendment was followed by the Fourteenth Amendment (1868), which granted citizenship to all persons born or naturalized in the United States, explicitly overturning the Dred Scott decision, and guaranteed due process and equal protection of the laws. It was followed by the Fifteenth Amendment (1870), which prohibited denial of the right to vote on the basis of race, color, or previous condition of servitude. Together, the Reconstruction amendments constituted a second founding — a fundamental rewriting of the Constitution's premises about who was an American and what rights Americans possessed.

These amendments were the direct constitutional legacy of the emancipation process and Black military service. They represented the formal fulfillment — partial and contested, as it turned out — of the claim that Frederick Douglass had made since the 1840s: that Black Americans were full citizens of the United States, entitled to all the rights and protections the Constitution promised.

Cause and Effect

The Emancipation Proclamation

Long-Term Causes

Decades of abolitionist advocacy; the political and moral crisis over slavery's expansion; the creation of the Republican Party as an antislavery party; the development of a free Black political culture in northern cities that demanded full citizenship; the fundamental incompatibility between a democracy founded on universal human liberty and an institution of chattel slavery.

Short-Term Causes

The military pressures of the Civil War's first year; the mass movement of enslaved people to Union lines that forced the government to define its policy; Congressional pressure through the Confiscation Acts; the actions of generals like Frémont and Hunter; the diplomatic need to prevent European recognition of the Confederacy; the failure of early Union military campaigns.

The Event

On September 22, 1862, Lincoln issued the Preliminary Emancipation Proclamation, and on January 1, 1863, the final proclamation took effect, declaring enslaved people in Confederate states to be forever free and authorizing Black military service.

Effects

Authorization of 175 USCT regiments; the end of any possibility of British or French recognition of the Confederacy; momentum toward the Thirteenth Amendment; the central political argument for Black citizenship during Reconstruction.

Historical complexity: The emancipation process cannot be reduced to a single cause or a single moment. It was driven simultaneously by enslaved people's self-emancipation, by abolitionist pressure, by military necessity, by Congressional action, by presidential decision-making, and by the logic of the war itself. Lincoln did not free the enslaved people; the enslaved people, in significant part, freed themselves, and they compelled the government to formally recognize that freedom.

Chapter Summary

Chapter 26 has examined one of the most consequential transformations in American history: the conversion of the Civil War from a contest over union into a war for human emancipation. That transformation was not the work of any single person, document, or moment. It was the result of a complex historical process driven from multiple directions over four years of conflict.

The foundation of that process was the self-emancipation of enslaved people themselves. Before any official government policy recognized their freedom, hundreds of thousands of enslaved men, women, and children used the conditions of wartime to claim freedom — through flight, through disruption of Confederate production, through providing intelligence and labor to Union forces. Their actions forced the political question of slavery onto the wartime agenda and demonstrated, at enormous personal risk, that they were capable of free decision-making and unwilling to accept permanent enslavement.

The "contraband" policy invented by General Benjamin Butler in May 1861 was the first official government response to self-emancipation — a legally creative but morally limited solution that allowed Union forces to hold freedom seekers without returning them under the Fugitive Slave Act. Contraband camps, established throughout the occupied South, became sites of both hardship and extraordinary human resilience: communities of freedom where schools were built, families were reunited, and the foundations of free Black life were constructed under the most challenging conditions.

Abolitionist pressure — from Frederick Douglass, from William Lloyd Garrison, from Harriet Tubman, from free Black communities in northern cities, from radical Republicans in Congress — maintained constant political pressure on the Lincoln administration to make emancipation an official war aim. Lincoln resisted through 1861 and most of 1862, insisting that his constitutional authority extended only to preserving the union, not to abolishing slavery. But the military pressures of the war — the need for manpower, the strategic logic of destroying Confederate economic capacity, the diplomatic necessity of preventing European recognition of the Confederacy — eventually compelled him to act.

The Emancipation Proclamation of January 1, 1863, was a carefully lawyered executive order framed as a military measure but with profound moral and political implications. It declared enslaved people in Confederate states to be "forever free," authorized Black military service, and transformed the war's meaning domestically and internationally. Its limits — its exclusion of border states and Union-controlled areas — reflect the legal constraints Lincoln believed he was operating under, but its significance exceeded those limits: it permanently changed the character of the Union war effort and set in motion the process that led to the Thirteenth Amendment.

The authorization of Black military service under the proclamation opened the door to one of the most significant military and political developments of the Civil War era. Approximately 180,000 Black men served in the United States Colored Troops, organized into 175 regiments that participated in 449 military engagements. Black soldiers fought at Port Hudson, at Milliken's Bend, at Fort Wagner, at the Crater, and in dozens of other engagements, demonstrating military valor and capability that challenged racist assumptions about Black incapacity for military service. Their service was conducted under conditions of systematic discrimination — unequal pay, exclusion from officer ranks, disproportionate assignment to dangerous duty, and the threat of being killed or re-enslaved if captured — but it was also an act of profound political assertion.

The Confederate response to emancipation and Black military service was violent and revealing. Official Confederate policy called for the execution or re-enslavement of captured Black soldiers and their white officers, a policy that was implemented most brutally at Fort Pillow in April 1864. The Fort Pillow massacre demonstrated the stakes of the conflict and the depth of Confederate commitment to racial hierarchy. It also strengthened the determination of Black soldiers, for whom "Remember Fort Pillow!" became a rallying cry.

The meaning of freedom as the war neared its end was debated and contested across every dimension of American life. For formerly enslaved people, freedom meant first and foremost release from the coercion, violence, and family separation of slavery. It also meant economic independence and political citizenship. The aspirations of freedpeople were partially fulfilled during Reconstruction: the Thirteenth Amendment abolished slavery, the Fourteenth granted citizenship and equal protection, the Fifteenth guaranteed voting rights regardless of race. But the implementation of these constitutional promises was contested from the beginning and was largely reversed by white supremacist violence and political abandonment during the 1870s, setting the stage for nearly a century of Jim Crow, disfranchisement, and racial subordination by other means.

The story of emancipation and Black military service during the Civil War is ultimately a story about the gap between American ideals and American realities, and about the ongoing struggle to close that gap. The enslaved people who fled to Union lines, the Black soldiers who fought at Fort Wagner, the abolitionists who pressed Lincoln for years, and Lincoln himself who eventually acted — all were participants in a process that transformed the nation's constitutional foundations and established the legal basis for equality that civil rights movements would call on a century later. The process was incomplete, contested, and deeply imperfect. But it was also one of the most significant acts of human liberation in American history, driven above all by the courage and determination of people who refused to accept that their condition was permanent or justified.

Chapter Reflection

Chapter 26 has examined one of the most transformative periods in American history: the conversion of the Civil War into a war for human freedom, driven by the self-emancipation of enslaved people, by abolitionist advocacy, by Congressional action, by military necessity, and by presidential decision. The historical significance of this transformation cannot be overstated. It produced the Thirteenth Amendment, abolishing slavery permanently. It set in motion the constitutional revolution of Reconstruction. It created the political foundation for the Fourteenth and Fifteenth Amendments. And it began — though it did not complete — the long process of transforming the United States from a slaveholding republic into a nation formally committed to the principle of equal citizenship regardless of race.

The story this chapter tells is not a simple story of triumph. The emancipation process was complicated, violent, and incomplete. Formerly enslaved people who reached Union lines often found conditions in contraband camps that were barely better than slavery. Black soldiers served in an army that systematically discriminated against them. The Confederacy killed Black prisoners of war. After the war, the promises of Reconstruction were broken by white supremacist violence and political abandonment, and the legal fruits of emancipation were largely reversed for nearly a century. The history of emancipation is a history of extraordinary human courage and determination — and also of extraordinary betrayal.

But the chapter also tells a story of remarkable historical agency. The enslaved people who fled to Union lines were not waiting for history to happen to them. They were making history — forcing political questions onto the national agenda, demonstrating their capacity for free decision-making, building communities under the most difficult conditions. The Black soldiers who fought at Fort Wagner and Port Hudson and Milliken's Bend were not simply responding to events. They were asserting, at risk of their lives, their claim to citizenship and equality. The abolitionists who pressed Lincoln for years were not simply commenting on events. They were driving them. The history of emancipation is, above all, a history of people who refused to accept that their condition was permanent or justified.

The larger themes that Chapter 26 reveals are themes that run through the entirety of U.S. history. The gap between American ideals and American realities — the distance between the Declaration's promise that all men are created equal and the Constitution's protection of slavery — has been a defining tension of American history since the founding. The Civil War and emancipation represent the most violent and consequential confrontation with that tension in the nation's history. The resolution of that confrontation — the Thirteenth, Fourteenth, and Fifteenth Amendments — was real and significant. But it was also partial, contested, and imperfect, setting the stage for the long freedom struggles of the twentieth century.

Students who understand this chapter will understand something essential about how change happens in American history: not through the action of leaders alone, not through the logic of institutions alone, but through the combination of organized social pressure, political opportunity, military necessity, moral argument, and the courage of ordinary people who refuse to accept that injustice is inevitable. The enslaved people of the Civil War era, the Black soldiers of the USCT, the abolitionists, the radical Republicans in Congress, and ultimately Abraham Lincoln himself — all were part of a process of democratic transformation that was never inevitable, was always contested, and was ultimately driven by people who believed that the nation's promises were worth fighting for.

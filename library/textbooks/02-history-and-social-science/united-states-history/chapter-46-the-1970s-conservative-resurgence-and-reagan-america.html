Part X — Modern Politics and Contemporary America

The 1970s, Conservative Resurgence, and Reagan America

United States History: Indigenous America, Republic, Conflict, and Change

Chapter Opening

"The era of big government is over" — President Bill Clinton, 1996 State of the Union. These words, spoken by a Democrat, measured how thoroughly Ronald Reagan had changed the terms of American political debate.

In the summer of 1979, President Jimmy Carter sat before the American people in a televised address and offered a diagnosis that surprised many listeners. The United States, he said, was suffering from a "crisis of confidence" — a spiritual and moral malaise that threatened the country's future more deeply than any foreign adversary or economic fluctuation. Americans were buying gasoline in lines that stretched around city blocks, watching inflation consume their wages, and nursing a distrust of their government that the Watergate scandal had burned into the national consciousness just five years earlier. Carter's speech was earnest and serious, and it was also politically devastating. His opponents used his own words against him, painting his presidency as proof of liberal failure and national weakness. Within sixteen months, Ronald Reagan had swept Carter from office in a landslide, promising "morning in America" and a new beginning built on tax cuts, military strength, and the reassertion of American pride.

The decade that separated the end of the Vietnam War from the beginning of the Reagan presidency was among the most turbulent and consequential in twentieth-century American history. The 1970s dismantled assumptions that had governed American political life since the New Deal era. Rising inflation, declining industrial employment, energy crises, and a government shattered by scandal combined to produce a profound crisis of liberal governance. But the crisis was not simply economic or political. It was also cultural. Feminism, Black Power, the sexual revolution, the counterculture, and a decade of protest had altered social life in ways that millions of Americans found threatening. The conservative movement that rose to power at the end of the 1970s drew on all of these anxieties — economic, political, and cultural — and channeled them into a broad political coalition that remade American politics for a generation.

To understand the Reagan revolution, students must understand the landscape from which it grew. The 1970s were not simply a prelude to conservatism. They were a decade of genuine crisis, creative response, and unresolved conflict. Stagflation — the combination of economic stagnation and rising prices — baffled economists and destroyed the political credibility of Keynesian economic management. Oil shocks imposed by the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries revealed the vulnerability of American energy dependence and reshaped everyday life. Deindustrialization gutted manufacturing communities across the Midwest and Northeast, producing what became known as the Rust Belt. Meanwhile, cities that had been centers of Democratic political power struggled with fiscal collapse, crime, and population loss. At the same time, the Sun Belt — a broad arc of growth stretching from Florida through Texas and into California — boomed with new industry, population, and a politics that was more conservative, more suburban, and more skeptical of federal government.

The conservative resurgence of the 1970s and 1980s was not a sudden political accident. It was built over decades by a network of intellectuals, activists, donors, religious leaders, think tanks, and politicians who had never accepted the New Deal consensus and who found their moment in the wreckage of liberal governance. Barry Goldwater's crushing defeat in the presidential election of 1964 had not ended the conservative movement; it had educated it. The movement learned from its losses, built institutions, developed policy arguments, and cultivated a grassroots base. By 1980, when Ronald Reagan won the presidency, conservatives had assembled one of the most formidable political coalitions in American history: suburban voters angry about taxes, white Southerners alienated by civil rights and cultural change, evangelical Christians mobilized by abortion and school prayer, business leaders determined to roll back regulation and labor power, and Cold War hawks alarmed by what they saw as American weakness in the face of Soviet expansion.

Ronald Reagan brought to this coalition a gift for communication, an optimistic temperament, and a coherent ideological vision. His presidency enacted the largest peacetime tax cuts in American history, drastically cut domestic social programs, accelerated deregulation of industry, launched a massive military buildup, and pursued an aggressive Cold War strategy that contributed to the eventual collapse of the Soviet Union. Reagan's presidency reshaped the federal government, the economy, and the terms of political debate in ways that endured long after he left office. The Democratic Party itself moved rightward in the decades that followed, reflecting how thoroughly Reaganomics had changed the assumptions of mainstream American politics.

But the story of the 1970s and the Reagan era is not only a story of conservative triumph. It is also a story of what was lost, what was contested, and what was unresolved. Deindustrialization devastated working-class communities whose suffering was often invisible in the celebration of tax cuts and economic growth. The Reagan era's cuts to social programs fell hardest on the poor, on African American and Latino communities, and on women who depended on public services. The administration's indifference to the AIDS epidemic, which swept through gay communities with devastating force in the early 1980s, constituted a profound moral failure by any serious historical reckoning. The widening gap between the wealthiest Americans and everyone else, which accelerated sharply during the Reagan years, would shape debates about inequality for decades. And the Cold War escalation, while it contributed to Soviet collapse, also funded authoritarian regimes, supported covert operations that destabilized governments, and brought the world to moments of genuine nuclear danger.

This chapter examines the full arc of political and economic transformation from the crisis of the 1970s through the Reagan era. It traces the collapse of liberal governance, the rise of the New Right, the cultural politics of the era, the economic theories and consequences of Reaganomics, and the shifting political coalitions that remade American democracy. It insists on complexity: the era produced real achievements and real damage, genuine popular support and genuine suffering, and it set in motion forces that Americans are still living with today.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain the economic conditions of the 1970s, including stagflation, oil shocks, and their causes and consequences.
  2. Analyze how the Watergate scandal damaged public trust in government and contributed to the political crisis of the 1970s.
  3. Trace the process of deindustrialization and explain how it reshaped the economic and political geography of the United States.
  4. Describe the rise of the Sun Belt and explain how regional economic shifts contributed to conservative political growth.
  5. Identify the major components of the New Right coalition, including fiscal conservatives, social conservatives, and evangelical Christians.
  6. Compare the economic philosophy of Keynesianism with supply-side economics and explain the arguments each made.
  7. Evaluate the consequences of Reagan-era domestic policy — tax cuts, deregulation, and cuts to social programs — for different groups of Americans.
  8. Analyze Reagan's Cold War strategy and assess its role in the decline of the Soviet Union.
  9. Distinguish between how different communities — working-class manufacturing workers, suburban voters, African Americans, women, and gay Americans — experienced the political and economic changes of the era.
  10. Assess the long-term political consequences of the conservative resurgence for both political parties and for American political culture.
  11. Connect the economic inequality of the Reagan era to debates about wealth, taxation, and social policy that continued into the twenty-first century.
  12. Contextualize the Reagan presidency within the broader history of American conservatism, from Goldwater to the present.

Historical Context

To understand the political earthquake of the 1980s, students must understand the world that preceded it. The decades after World War II had been, for many Americans, a period of remarkable prosperity. The New Deal coalition — a broad alliance of labor unions, Northern liberals, African Americans, urban ethnic communities, and Southern whites — had delivered sustained Democratic dominance in Congress since the 1930s. The mixed economy of postwar America combined private enterprise with substantial government investment in infrastructure, education, housing, and social programs. Keynesian economics — the idea that government should use spending and taxation to manage the business cycle and sustain employment — provided the intellectual framework for this approach. The postwar boom produced rising wages, expanding homeownership, and a broad middle class that seemed to vindicate the New Deal model.

But by the late 1960s, cracks were widening in the consensus. The civil rights movement had fractured the New Deal coalition by driving Southern whites out of the Democratic Party. The Great Society programs of Lyndon Johnson — Medicare, Medicaid, the Voting Rights Act, federal education funding, antipoverty programs — had expanded the federal government dramatically and generated a conservative backlash. The Vietnam War had produced inflation as military spending poured money into the economy without producing consumer goods. Richard Nixon's presidency had simultaneously pursued détente with the Soviet Union and China, imposed wage and price controls to fight inflation, and then fallen apart in the greatest political scandal in American history.

The international context also mattered profoundly. The Bretton Woods system of fixed exchange rates, which had stabilized international finance since 1944, collapsed in 1971 when Nixon ended the dollar's convertibility to gold. International economic competition intensified as West Germany and Japan rebuilt their industrial economies and challenged American manufacturers. The rise of OPEC as a political force transferred enormous economic power to oil-producing states, exposing the vulnerability of industrial economies dependent on cheap petroleum. These structural shifts created the conditions for the crisis of the 1970s that no single administration could easily resolve.

The conservative movement had its roots in the postwar period. William F. Buckley's National Review, founded in 1955, had provided an intellectual home for a conservatism that united free-market economics, anti-Communism, and traditional values. Barry Goldwater's 1964 presidential campaign had articulated a vision of limited government and individual liberty that, though defeated in a landslide, energized a generation of conservatives. Richard Nixon's "Southern strategy" had demonstrated how racial politics could realign the electorate. The tax revolt that began in California with Proposition 13 in 1978 showed that anti-government sentiment could be mobilized at the ballot box. By the time Ronald Reagan ran for president in 1980, conservatives had decades of intellectual preparation, organizational infrastructure, and political learning behind them.

Section 46.1

The Crisis of the 1970s

The 1970s did not feel like an era of conservative triumph while they were happening. They felt, to millions of Americans, like an era of unrelenting failure. The decade opened with the United States still enmeshed in the Vietnam War, a conflict that had already consumed more than 50,000 American lives and was producing anti-war protests, racial tensions within the military, and a generational rupture in American political culture. In 1973, the Paris Peace Accords ended direct American military involvement in Vietnam, but the peace was neither honorable nor lasting. In April 1975, the last American personnel fled Saigon by helicopter as North Vietnamese forces entered the city and the government of South Vietnam collapsed. The television images of desperate South Vietnamese civilians clinging to American helicopters as they departed the U.S. Embassy rooftop became one of the defining visual symbols of American defeat — the first unambiguous military defeat in the nation's history.

The fall of Saigon was not the only wound of the decade. In the summer of 1974, President Richard Nixon, facing almost certain impeachment for his role in the Watergate conspiracy, became the first president in American history to resign from office. Nixon had authorized the break-in of Democratic Party headquarters at the Watergate complex in June 1972, and then directed a criminal cover-up that included obstruction of justice, payment of hush money, abuse of the CIA and FBI, and the illegal wiretapping of political opponents and journalists. The Senate Watergate Committee's televised hearings in 1973 brought the scandal into American living rooms, and the revelation of the White House taping system — and Nixon's eventual surrender of the tapes — provided direct evidence of his criminality. Nixon resigned on August 9, 1974. His successor, Gerald Ford, controversially pardoned him one month later, a decision that cost Ford enormous political support.

The combined effect of Vietnam and Watergate on American political culture was enormous and lasting. Public trust in the federal government, which had already been eroding through the late 1960s, collapsed. Polls taken in the mid-1970s showed that a majority of Americans distrusted the government to do what was right most of the time — a dramatic reversal from the early 1960s, when a majority trusted government consistently. This distrust was not irrational. Americans had been lied to about the Vietnam War for years, and they had watched a president commit criminal acts from the Oval Office. The cynicism that resulted permeated political culture for decades and would be exploited, in different ways, by politicians of both parties — though conservatives, who promised to restrain and reduce government, generally benefited more from anti-government sentiment than liberals, whose political program depended on citizens trusting government to act on their behalf.

Post-Watergate reforms attempted to address specific institutional failures. The Federal Election Campaign Act amendments of 1974 created disclosure requirements, spending limits, and public financing for presidential campaigns. The War Powers Resolution of 1973 required congressional notification and eventual approval of military engagements. The Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act of 1978 created a special court to oversee domestic intelligence gathering. The Church Committee hearings in 1975 revealed that the CIA had plotted assassinations of foreign leaders, conducted illegal surveillance of American citizens, and undermined democratic governments abroad. These reforms represented genuine responses to genuine abuses, but they also demonstrated the limits of institutional repair in the face of a generalized loss of confidence.

The 1976 presidential election brought Jimmy Carter — a former Georgia governor, naval officer, and peanut farmer — to the White House on a platform of honesty, competence, and outsider status. Carter genuinely possessed these qualities, and he approached the presidency with intelligence and good intentions. He was also, as president, often politically tone-deaf, unable to manage Congress, dismissive of Washington's political culture, and chronically unlucky. The crises of his presidency — stagflation, the Iranian hostage crisis, the Soviet invasion of Afghanistan, and the energy emergency — were not entirely of his making, but his inability to resolve them made his presidency appear weak and rudderless. By 1980, the conditions for a conservative political realignment were fully in place. The question was whether the conservative movement had the candidate and the coalition to capitalize on them.

Section 46.2

Stagflation and Oil Shocks

The defining economic experience of the 1970s was stagflation — a combination of high inflation, high unemployment, and slow economic growth that seemed, according to the economic models then dominant in academic and policy circles, to be theoretically impossible. Keynesian economics, which had guided American economic policy since the New Deal, held that inflation and unemployment tended to move in opposite directions: when unemployment was low and the economy was growing, inflation tended to rise; when unemployment was high and the economy was stagnating, inflation tended to fall. The 1970s broke this relationship. Unemployment and inflation rose simultaneously, leaving policymakers without effective tools. Raising interest rates to fight inflation worsened unemployment. Cutting taxes and increasing spending to fight unemployment worsened inflation. Every cure seemed to worsen the disease.

The roots of stagflation were multiple and intertwined. The inflation that mounted through the late 1960s had been partly a product of the Johnson administration's decision to fund both the Great Society and the Vietnam War through deficit spending rather than tax increases — a political choice that poured money into the economy without the tax offset that Keynesian theory required. Nixon's 1971 decision to end the dollar's convertibility to gold, ending the Bretton Woods system, removed a key constraint on monetary expansion and contributed to inflationary pressure globally. But the most dramatic and immediate cause of stagflation in the 1970s was the oil shock of 1973.

In October 1973, Arab members of OPEC imposed an oil embargo on the United States in retaliation for American support of Israel during the Yom Kippur War. The embargo lasted until March 1974, but its effects were far longer-lasting. The price of oil quadrupled almost overnight. Gasoline, which had cost roughly 35 cents per gallon before the embargo, soared. Lines at gas stations stretched for blocks. Many stations rationed purchases to a few gallons per customer, or closed entirely. The federal government imposed a national speed limit of 55 miles per hour and encouraged consumers to lower home thermostats in winter. The experience of abundance and cheap energy — so central to postwar American prosperity, automobile culture, and suburban growth — suddenly seemed fragile.

The oil shock had cascading effects throughout the economy. Since petroleum was an input in the production of virtually everything — from plastics to fertilizers to transportation — rising oil prices raised the cost of producing and distributing goods across the entire economy. This supply-side inflation was particularly resistant to Keynesian remedies. Traditional anti-inflation policy worked by reducing demand; but oil-driven inflation reflected rising costs of supply, which demand reduction could not address without also driving unemployment sharply higher. The economy entered a severe recession in 1974–1975. Unemployment hit nearly nine percent. Inflation remained in double digits. The combination demoralized the public and discredited the economists and policymakers who had promised that modern economic management could prevent such outcomes.

The second oil shock of 1979 repeated and deepened the damage. The Iranian Revolution, which overthrew Shah Mohammed Reza Pahlavi and brought Ayatollah Khomeini to power, disrupted Iranian oil production and sent prices soaring again. By 1980, oil prices were roughly fifteen times what they had been a decade earlier. For most American families, energy consumed an ever-growing share of household budgets. Heating bills, electric bills, and gasoline costs squeezed incomes that were already losing ground to inflation. Carter responded with a mix of conservation measures, deregulation of domestic oil prices, and the development of alternative energy sources — policies that were arguably correct in the long run but that produced no visible short-term relief.

For the automotive industry, concentrated in Michigan and the industrial Midwest, the oil shocks were catastrophic. American automakers had long specialized in large, powerful, fuel-inefficient vehicles. Japanese automakers — Toyota, Honda, Datsun — had developed smaller, more fuel-efficient cars that were suddenly exactly what American consumers needed. They gained market share rapidly. Chrysler teetered on the edge of bankruptcy in 1979 and required a federal loan guarantee. Ford and General Motors closed factories and laid off workers. The devastation extended to suppliers, dealerships, and the broader economies of industrial cities.

The experience of stagflation discredited Keynesian economics in ways that lasted for decades. The dominant paradigm had not anticipated and could not explain what was happening. Into the intellectual vacuum stepped new economic ideas — monetarism, supply-side economics, and rational expectations theory — that rejected Keynesianism and offered different diagnoses and cures. Milton Friedman's monetarism argued that inflation was always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon, caused by excessive growth of the money supply, and that the proper remedy was strict control of monetary expansion rather than fiscal policy manipulation. Supply-side economists argued that the real problem was not too much demand but too little supply — caused by excessive taxation and regulation that discouraged investment and production. These ideas, heterodox in academic economics, found a ready audience among business leaders, conservative politicians, and voters looking for alternatives to the policy failures of the 1970s.

Historical Vocabulary
Stagflation

Stagflation is the simultaneous occurrence of economic stagnation (slow growth and high unemployment) and high inflation. The term was coined in the 1970s to describe conditions that mainstream economic theory at the time suggested could not coexist. Classical Keynesian economics held that inflation and unemployment traded off against each other — when one was high, the other would be low. Stagflation confounded this expectation and created a political and intellectual crisis for the economists and policymakers who had promised that modern economic management could prevent major downturns.

Stagflation mattered historically not only because of the immediate hardship it caused, but because it delegitimized Keynesian economic management and created the intellectual space for conservative economic alternatives, including monetarism and supply-side economics. When Ronald Reagan took office promising to end inflation and stimulate growth simultaneously, his promise resonated with an electorate that had watched Keynesian economics fail throughout the 1970s.

Section 46.3

Watergate and Distrust in Government

The Watergate scandal was, at its core, a story about power, criminality, and the fragility of democratic institutions. But it was also a story about the political consequences of institutional failure — consequences that outlasted the scandal itself and reshaped American political culture in ways that both progressives and conservatives could exploit, though in different ways.

The break-in at the Democratic National Committee headquarters in the Watergate complex in Washington, D.C., on June 17, 1972, was at first dismissed as a "third-rate burglary." The five men arrested were connected to the Committee to Re-Elect the President (CREEP), but the Nixon administration denied any White House involvement. Nixon himself was cruising toward a landslide reelection victory over Democratic nominee George McGovern, and most voters paid little attention to the story. The real work of uncovering the scandal was done by two young Washington Post reporters, Bob Woodward and Carl Bernstein, who followed a thread of evidence — aided by a source they called "Deep Throat," later revealed to be FBI Associate Director Mark Felt — that led ever closer to the Oval Office.

What the investigation eventually revealed was not simply a burglary but a systematic program of political criminality. The Nixon White House had authorized not only the Watergate break-in but a long series of "dirty tricks" against political opponents: illegal wiretaps, break-ins, harassment campaigns, manipulation of IRS audits, and the creation of a secret "plumbers" unit to stop news leaks. Nixon himself had directed the cover-up, paying hush money to the arrested burglars, ordering the CIA to interfere with the FBI investigation, and systematically lying to the public, Congress, and his own staff.

The discovery that Nixon had secretly recorded his White House conversations became the critical turning point. When the existence of the taping system was revealed before the Senate Watergate Committee in July 1973, Nixon was immediately ordered to surrender the tapes. His refusal led to the "Saturday Night Massacre" of October 1973, in which he ordered Attorney General Elliot Richardson to fire special prosecutor Archibald Cox, who had subpoenaed the tapes. Richardson refused and resigned; his deputy refused and resigned; only the third-ranking official in the Justice Department executed the order. The episode shocked the public and initiated formal impeachment proceedings in the House Judiciary Committee.

By the summer of 1974, the Supreme Court had unanimously ordered Nixon to surrender the tapes. The tapes revealed that Nixon had ordered the CIA to block the FBI's investigation of the burglary six days after it occurred — what became known as the "smoking gun" — and that he had lied about his involvement. Facing certain impeachment and conviction, Nixon announced his resignation on August 8, 1974. Gerald Ford assumed the presidency and, on September 8, 1974, granted Nixon "a full, free, and absolute pardon" for all crimes he had committed or might have committed as president. The pardon was enormously unpopular and contributed significantly to Ford's defeat by Carter in 1976.

Watergate's long-term effects on American political culture were profound and somewhat paradoxical. On one level, the scandal demonstrated the resilience of democratic institutions: the free press had investigated and published; Congress had asserted its oversight role; the courts had enforced legal accountability; and the constitutional system of checks and balances had ultimately forced a criminal president from office. On another level, the scandal confirmed and deepened a distrust of government that became a permanent feature of American political life. Conservatives would answer this crisis by arguing that the solution was less government — reduce its scope, limit its power, and shrink its footprint in American life. This was a politically useful argument in a period when Americans' distrust of government was at its highest.

Jimmy Carter ran explicitly as an outsider who would restore honesty and competence to government after the Nixon years. His campaign slogan — "I will never lie to you" — captured the post-Watergate mood. But Carter's outsider identity became a liability once in office. His lack of relationships with congressional leaders, his disdain for Washington's political culture, and his tendency to treat complex political problems as management challenges rather than coalition-building exercises all contributed to an administration that was honest but often ineffective. By 1980, Carter's approval ratings were among the lowest in the history of modern presidential polling, and the country was ready for someone who offered not just honesty but optimism, clarity, and a coherent ideological vision.

Section 46.4

Deindustrialization and Sun Belt Growth

While the political crisis of the 1970s played out in Washington, a slower and in some ways more consequential transformation was reshaping the economic geography of the United States. Deindustrialization — the decline of manufacturing as a share of the American economy — had been underway since the mid-1960s but accelerated dramatically in the 1970s and 1980s. Its consequences were devastating for the working-class communities that had built their lives around factory employment, and its political effects contributed substantially to the conservative realignment of the era.

The causes of deindustrialization were multiple. International competition, particularly from rebuilt West German and Japanese industries that had modernized their plants and equipment after World War II, eroded the market share of American manufacturers in steel, automobiles, electronics, textiles, and consumer goods. American factories, many of them operating with aging equipment and high labor costs, could not match the prices or quality of foreign competitors. Trade liberalization, which the United States had championed through the GATT as both an economic strategy and a Cold War instrument, reduced tariff barriers that had protected American manufacturers. Automation began eliminating jobs in industries where they survived. And corporations, facing competitive pressure, sought to reduce labor costs by relocating production first to lower-wage regions of the United States and then to lower-wage countries abroad.

The geographic consequences were visible and devastating. The cities and regions that had built the American industrial economy — Pittsburgh with its steel mills, Detroit with its automobile factories, Youngstown and Cleveland with their manufacturing complexes, the mill towns of New England and the Carolinas — experienced factory closures, mass layoffs, and population flight in rapid succession. Pittsburgh lost more than 100,000 manufacturing jobs between 1970 and 1985. Youngstown's steel industry, which had employed tens of thousands of workers, effectively collapsed when U.S. Steel shuttered its major mills in 1977 and 1979. The region stretching from upstate New York through Pennsylvania, Ohio, Indiana, Michigan, and Illinois came to be known as the "Rust Belt" — a term that captured both the literal deterioration of idle industrial equipment and the broader sense of decay and abandonment.

The human consequences of deindustrialization were severe and concentrated. Factory workers who lost their jobs typically did not find equivalent employment elsewhere. The service jobs that were growing in the 1970s and 1980s — retail, food service, healthcare, office work — generally paid lower wages, offered fewer benefits, provided less job security, and were less likely to be unionized than the manufacturing jobs they replaced. A worker who had earned fifteen or eighteen dollars an hour operating a press in a steel mill might find, after months of unemployment, a job at eight or ten dollars an hour — a wage cut that transformed the economic circumstances of entire families. Pension funds collapsed when companies closed. Health insurance disappeared with employment. Homes lost value as neighborhoods declined.

The decline of manufacturing unions was both a cause and consequence of deindustrialization. At their postwar peak, American unions had represented approximately thirty-five percent of the workforce, providing collective bargaining power that sustained wages, benefits, and working conditions across a broad industrial economy. By the early 1980s, union density had fallen to under twenty percent and was still falling. The political consequences of union decline were also significant. Unions had been the organizational backbone of the Democratic Party in industrial states — funding campaigns, mobilizing voters, communicating with members about candidates and issues. As unions weakened, so did a crucial pillar of the Democratic coalition.

While the Rust Belt contracted, another America was booming. The Sun Belt — a broad arc of states stretching from Florida through the Gulf Coast states, through New Mexico and Arizona, and into Southern California — experienced rapid population and economic growth throughout the postwar decades that accelerated in the 1970s and 1980s. Air conditioning made the previously brutal heat of the South and Southwest livable for millions; the growth of defense and aerospace industries distributed partly for strategic and partly for political reasons across the South and West; the development of oil and gas, tourism, and retirement industries added further momentum. Between 1970 and 1990, Florida's population grew from 6.8 million to 12.9 million. Texas grew from 11.2 million to 17 million.

The political culture of the Sun Belt was, broadly speaking, more conservative than that of the industrial North. Sun Belt growth had occurred largely in states with weaker union traditions, lower taxes, and cultures that valued individual enterprise and were skeptical of federal government. The suburbs that proliferated across the Sun Belt were largely white, largely homeowning, and increasingly oriented toward a politics of lower taxes, law and order, traditional values, and skepticism of the government programs associated with the New Deal and Great Society. The intersection of Rust Belt decline and Sun Belt growth was not simply economic. It was also deeply racial. Deindustrialization devastated Black working-class communities in particular — often last hired and therefore first fired, concentrated in the industrial sectors suffering the deepest losses.

Historical Vocabulary
Deindustrialization

Deindustrialization refers to the decline of manufacturing as a share of the economy, characterized by factory closures, job losses in industrial sectors, and the shift toward service-sector employment. In the United States, deindustrialization accelerated in the 1970s and 1980s as a result of international competition, automation, corporate relocation, and trade liberalization.

Deindustrialization had consequences beyond economics. It undermined organized labor, eroded the tax base of industrial cities, contributed to urban decay, and devastated working-class communities. Its political consequences included the weakening of unions as a Democratic constituency, the alienation of white working-class voters from the Democratic Party, and the rise of conservative politics in the communities and regions that had lost economic ground. Understanding deindustrialization is essential to understanding both the specific experiences of Rust Belt communities and the broader political realignment of the late twentieth century.

Section 46.5

Rise of the New Right

The conservative movement that propelled Ronald Reagan to the presidency in 1980 was not an improvisation. It was the product of decades of intellectual work, organizational development, political learning, and strategic coalition-building. Understanding its rise requires looking beyond the electoral success of 1980 to the long history of conservative activism that preceded it.

The modern American conservative movement can be traced to several overlapping sources in the postwar period. One was libertarianism — the philosophical tradition, associated with thinkers like Friedrich Hayek and Milton Friedman, that argued for minimal government intervention in the economy and maximum individual freedom. Hayek's The Road to Serfdom (1944), which warned that economic planning inevitably leads to political tyranny, became a foundational text for the business community and for conservatives who saw the New Deal as a first step toward totalitarianism. A second source was anti-Communism — the conviction that Soviet Communism was an existential threat requiring resolute American opposition. A third source was cultural conservatism — resistance to the social changes of the 1960s: civil rights, feminism, the sexual revolution, the counterculture, rising crime rates, and the perceived decline of traditional religious and family values.

William F. Buckley Jr.'s National Review, founded in 1955, had the task of holding these different strands of conservatism together — an intellectual project that required considerable creative work, since libertarians and traditionalists, internationalists and isolationists, free-market purists and social conservatives did not always agree. Buckley also undertook the project of distinguishing legitimate conservatism from the extremism of the John Birch Society and other fringe elements that threatened to discredit the movement. By the early 1960s, National Review had become the intellectual center of a coherent, if internally diverse, conservative movement.

Barry Goldwater's 1964 presidential campaign was both a defeat and an education. Goldwater won the Republican nomination by running explicitly as a conservative against the moderate "establishment" Republicanism represented by figures like Nelson Rockefeller. His platform called for voluntary Social Security, repeal of the Civil Rights Act (on constitutional rather than racial grounds, Goldwater insisted), aggressive anti-Communist foreign policy, and dramatic reductions in federal power. Lyndon Johnson, running as the heir to Kennedy and the architect of the Great Society, defeated Goldwater in one of the largest landslides in American history. But the conservative movement drew different lessons from 1964 than mainstream commentators did. They had demonstrated that the Republican Party could be captured from within. They had energized millions of grassroots activists and built donor networks. And they had, in the process of Goldwater's California campaign, discovered Ronald Reagan, whose televised speech for Goldwater had electrified conservative donors and set him on the path to California's governorship in 1966.

Throughout the late 1960s and 1970s, conservatives built the institutional infrastructure of the modern movement. Think tanks proliferated: the Heritage Foundation (1973), the Cato Institute (1977), the American Enterprise Institute. These organizations produced policy research, trained intellectuals, published books and journals, and provided a pipeline of conservative policy thinkers into government. The Federalist Society, founded in 1982, would do the same for law schools and the judiciary. Political direct mail, pioneered by conservative activist Richard Viguerie, allowed the movement to raise money and communicate with supporters outside mainstream media, whose perceived liberal bias conservatives had long complained about.

The tax revolt of the late 1970s demonstrated that anti-government sentiment could be mobilized politically with enormous effect. California's Proposition 13, a 1978 ballot initiative that capped property taxes and required a two-thirds legislative supermajority to raise taxes, passed by a landslide. California homeowners had seen their property taxes soar as real estate values rose — genuine grievance — but Proposition 13 also expressed a broader and more generalized hostility to government spending. Howard Jarvis, its principal author, became a national figure. Similar initiatives passed in other states. Proposition 13's passage sent a signal to the national conservative movement that tax reduction was a potent political issue that could mobilize voters well beyond the traditional Republican base.

Section 46.6

Religious Conservatism and Cultural Politics

Among the most consequential developments in American political life in the late 1970s was the political mobilization of evangelical Christianity. For much of the twentieth century, American evangelicals had maintained a degree of separation from partisan politics, preferring to focus on personal salvation and religious community rather than political engagement. The social upheavals of the 1960s and 1970s — the sexual revolution, the women's liberation movement, the Supreme Court's decisions on school prayer and abortion, the rise of gay rights, the cultural challenge to traditional family structures — disrupted this relative political quietism and produced one of the most powerful and durable new constituencies in American political history.

The proximate issue that galvanized evangelical political mobilization was abortion. The Supreme Court's 1973 decision in Roe v. Wade, which established a constitutional right to abortion in the first two trimesters of pregnancy, was immediately recognized by religious conservatives as a profound moral challenge. For Catholics, opposition to abortion had long been a doctrinal commitment; the Roe decision broadened this opposition to include large segments of evangelical Protestantism. School prayer, which the Court had ruled unconstitutional in 1962 and 1963, continued to anger many Christians who saw the decisions as the government expelling God from public life. The growing visibility of gay rights following the Stonewall uprising of 1969 represented, for many religious conservatives, a direct challenge to the traditional family structure they saw as divinely ordained.

In 1979, Jerry Falwell Sr. founded the Moral Majority, an organization explicitly designed to register evangelical voters and mobilize them as a Republican constituency. Falwell ran a large Baptist congregation in Lynchburg, Virginia, and hosted a nationally televised religious broadcast. The Moral Majority sent voter registration teams to evangelical churches across the country, produced voter guides comparing candidates' positions on key social issues, and worked to build a network of politically active pastors and congregations. Other organizations added to the infrastructure of the religious right: Pat Robertson's Christian Broadcasting Network, James Dobson's Focus on the Family, and the Family Research Council. By 1980, surveys suggested that Ronald Reagan won roughly eighty percent of the white evangelical vote — a constituency that had been strongly Democratic within living memory, as Carter himself was a born-again Baptist.

Phyllis Schlafly's campaign against the Equal Rights Amendment demonstrated that antifeminism could be a powerful organizing tool. The ERA, which would have constitutionally prohibited discrimination on the basis of sex, had passed Congress in 1972 with strong bipartisan support and seemed headed for rapid ratification. Schlafly's STOP ERA campaign argued that the ERA would eliminate legal protections for women, require women to serve in military combat, and destroy the traditional family. These arguments resonated with millions of women who did not identify with the feminist movement and who felt that the feminist agenda threatened a way of life they valued. The ERA fell three states short of ratification by the 1982 deadline.

The "culture wars" — a term that came into common use in the 1980s and 1990s — encompassed battles over abortion, school prayer, the ERA, gay rights, sex education, pornography, the content of school textbooks, affirmative action, and the general direction of American social life. Race was a constant undercurrent. Reagan's campaign announcement in Philadelphia, Mississippi — a town infamous as the site of the 1964 murders of civil rights workers James Chaney, Andrew Goodman, and Michael Schwerner — where he spoke of his support for "states' rights," was not accidental. His repeated invocation of a fictional "welfare queen" who drove a Cadillac and collected government checks communicated to white voters without requiring explicit racial language. Whether this constituted deliberate racial coding or insensitivity to racial symbolism is a matter historians continue to debate; but the political effect — reinforcing the association of Democratic social programs with undeserving recipients in racially coded terms — is well documented.

Section 46.7

Reaganomics and Deregulation

Ronald Reagan arrived at the presidency in January 1981 with a clear and coherent economic vision: cut taxes dramatically, reduce the size and scope of the federal government, deregulate industry, and allow the private sector to drive growth. This vision, which his supporters called supply-side economics and his critics called "trickle-down economics," represented the most fundamental challenge to the New Deal economic order since its creation in the 1930s. Reagan and his advisers argued that forty years of Keynesian economic management had produced precisely the stagflation, declining productivity, and fiscal crisis that characterized the 1970s. The solution was not more government management but less.

The Economic Recovery Tax Act of 1981, the cornerstone of Reagan's domestic agenda, was the largest peacetime tax cut in American history. The top marginal income tax rate was cut from seventy percent to fifty percent immediately, and eventually to twenty-eight percent by 1986. Corporate tax rates were reduced. Capital gains taxes were cut. Depreciation rules were changed to favor business investment. The theory — articulated most memorably through the "Laffer Curve," a diagram suggesting that above a certain rate, cutting taxes would increase revenue by stimulating growth — held that reducing the tax burden on high-income individuals and corporations would stimulate investment, expand the economy, and ultimately generate enough growth to prevent deficits from growing.

Simultaneously, the administration moved to cut domestic social spending. The Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act of 1981 reduced funding for food stamps, Medicaid, housing assistance, job training programs, student loans, and numerous other federal social programs. Welfare eligibility requirements were tightened. The social safety net that had been built through the New Deal, Fair Deal, and Great Society was not eliminated, but it was significantly reduced. Reagan was fond of saying that in the War on Poverty, poverty had won — implying that the programs themselves were counterproductive and that their reduction would benefit the poor by encouraging self-reliance and private initiative.

The short-term economic results of Reaganomics were painful. Paul Volcker pursued a policy of dramatically high interest rates to break the back of inflation. Interest rates reached nearly twenty percent, choking off investment, driving mortgage rates to levels that froze the housing market, and pushing the economy into the deepest recession since the 1930s. Unemployment peaked at nearly eleven percent in late 1982. Businesses failed in large numbers. Farmers who had borrowed heavily during the 1970s found themselves crushed by high interest rates and falling commodity prices. In Detroit, Cleveland, Pittsburgh, and scores of smaller industrial cities, unemployment rates in some neighborhoods reached twenty, twenty-five, or even thirty percent.

The economic recovery that began in late 1982 and accelerated through 1983 and 1984 gave Reagan the political vindication he needed. By election day 1984, inflation had fallen from above thirteen percent at the peak of the 1979 shock to around four percent. Unemployment had dropped from its recessionary peak back below eight percent. GDP growth was robust. Reagan's reelection campaign proclaimed "Morning in America" — a slogan that captured a genuine mood of economic recovery and renewed national confidence. He won forty-nine states, carrying not only the traditional Republican base but substantial portions of the white working-class electorate in industrial states. The "Reagan Democrats" — white, often Catholic, often union-household voters who had been reliably Democratic but voted for Reagan in 1980 and again in 1984 — became a defining feature of the new political landscape.

Deregulation was a second major component of Reagan's economic program. The administration reduced enforcement of environmental, workplace safety, and consumer protection regulations, arguing that regulatory burdens imposed unnecessary costs on business. Financial deregulation, including the Garn–St. Germain Depository Institutions Act of 1982, removed restrictions on savings and loan institutions that had previously limited the risks they could take. This deregulation, combined with federal deposit insurance that protected depositors from losses, created perverse incentives that contributed to massive speculation and eventually to the savings and loan crisis of the late 1980s, which cost taxpayers an estimated $130 billion in bailout costs.

The long-term consequences of Reaganomics became clearer over time. Economic inequality, which had been relatively stable in the postwar decades, began to widen sharply in the 1980s. The share of income going to the top one percent of earners, which had been declining or stable since World War II, turned upward in the early 1980s and has not declined since. The promised revenues from tax-cut-induced growth did not materialize at the levels predicted, and federal deficits soared — from approximately $79 billion in 1981 to $221 billion in 1986. The national debt nearly tripled during Reagan's presidency, from approximately $994 billion to $2.9 trillion, despite Reagan's rhetorical commitment to balanced budgets.

Historical Vocabulary
Supply-Side Economics

Supply-side economics is the theory that economic growth is best stimulated by reducing taxes and regulation on producers — businesses and wealthy individuals — rather than by increasing consumer demand through government spending (the Keynesian approach). Supply-siders argued that high marginal tax rates discouraged investment and productivity, and that cutting those rates would stimulate economic activity, eventually generating enough growth to offset lost tax revenue.

Supply-side economics became the central intellectual framework of Reagan administration economic policy. Critics, including some conservatives, labeled it "trickle-down economics," arguing that its actual effect was to redistribute income upward to the wealthy while waiting for benefits to trickle down to workers. The record of the Reagan years was mixed: inflation was conquered, but deficits soared and economic inequality widened substantially. Supply-side economics nevertheless remained influential and fundamentally transformed mainstream economic debate, shifting the bounds of acceptable policy discussion in ways that persisted long after Reagan left office.

Section 46.8

Cold War Escalation and Arms Buildup

Ronald Reagan came to the presidency convinced that the United States had allowed itself to become dangerously weak in the face of Soviet military and ideological expansion. The 1970s had seemed to validate this concern. American defeat in Vietnam had been followed by Soviet-backed or -aligned advances in Angola, Mozambique, Ethiopia, Cambodia, and Afghanistan, where Soviet forces invaded in December 1979 to prop up a Communist government. Carter had responded with a grain embargo, a boycott of the 1980 Moscow Olympics, and the Carter Doctrine — declaring the Persian Gulf a vital American interest — but these gestures seemed inadequate to conservatives who believed the moment required a much more forceful response.

Reagan's Cold War strategy rested on several premises: that the Soviet Union was an "evil empire" (his phrase in a 1983 speech) rather than a legitimate government with which the United States could engage in stable détente; that American military power had been dangerously neglected and needed to be dramatically rebuilt; that the Soviet economy was more fragile than Western analysts typically assumed; and that aggressive American pressure — military, economic, and ideological — could force the Soviets into a competition they could not sustain. The military buildup Reagan initiated was the largest peacetime expansion of American military spending in history. Defense budgets rose approximately forty percent in real terms between 1981 and 1985. New weapons systems — the B-1 bomber, the MX missile, cruise missiles, the Trident submarine — were funded and accelerated.

The most dramatic and controversial element of Reagan's military strategy was the Strategic Defense Initiative (SDI), announced in March 1983. SDI proposed to develop a space-based system of lasers, particle beams, or other technologies capable of intercepting Soviet intercontinental ballistic missiles before they reached their targets. Critics immediately labeled the proposal "Star Wars" and argued that it was technologically fantastical, potentially destabilizing, and enormously expensive. Reagan's defenders argued that even the threat of SDI put pressure on the Soviet Union to match an American program the Soviet economy could not afford.

The Reagan Doctrine — the policy of supporting anti-Communist resistance movements around the world — represented another dimension of Cold War escalation. Reagan requested and received congressional funding for rebels fighting Soviet-backed governments in Afghanistan (the mujahideen), Angola (UNITA), and Nicaragua (the Contras). The Afghanistan program, conducted in cooperation with Pakistan and Saudi Arabia and funneled through the CIA, provided billions of dollars of weapons and support to the mujahideen fighting Soviet occupation. The program was militarily effective — Soviet forces ultimately withdrew in 1988 — but the aftermath of the Afghan war created conditions that contributed to the rise of Taliban rule and, later, al-Qaeda.

The Iran-Contra affair involved a convoluted scheme in which administration officials secretly sold arms to Iran — in violation of an arms embargo and contradicting the administration's public refusal to negotiate with terrorists — and diverted the profits to the Nicaraguan Contras in violation of the Boland Amendment, which Congress had passed explicitly to prohibit executive branch funding for the Contras. When the scheme was revealed in late 1986, it threatened to engulf Reagan in a scandal of Watergate proportions. Reagan himself claimed not to have known of the diversion, though the documentary record remains contested. National Security Council staffer Oliver North, who had helped orchestrate the scheme, became a polarizing public figure. Reagan's approval ratings dropped sharply but eventually recovered; Congressional investigations produced findings of serious executive wrongdoing but did not lead to impeachment proceedings.

Reagan's relationship with Soviet leader Mikhail Gorbachev, who came to power in 1985, proved more complex and ultimately more consequential than Reagan's early Cold War bellicosity might have predicted. Gorbachev recognized that the Soviet Union's economic and political system was in deep crisis and undertook dramatic reform initiatives — glasnost (openness) and perestroika (restructuring) — that eventually escaped his control. Reagan and Gorbachev held a series of summits that produced the Intermediate-Range Nuclear Forces (INF) Treaty in 1987, the first agreement to actually eliminate entire categories of nuclear weapons rather than merely limiting them. How much credit Reagan deserves for the eventual collapse of the Soviet Union in 1991 — after Reagan had left office — is one of the most contested questions in the historical debate over his legacy.

Section 46.9

Political Realignment and Conservative Resurgence

The political transformation of the Reagan era extended well beyond any particular election or policy. It represented a fundamental realignment of the American political landscape — the kind of durable shift in voter coalitions, party identities, regional allegiances, and political assumptions that historians compare to the realignments produced by the Civil War era and the New Deal. Understanding this realignment requires examining who moved where in the political system, and why.

The most dramatic element of the realignment was the transformation of the South from solidly Democratic to solidly Republican. Before the 1960s, the South had been the most reliably Democratic region in the country — a product of the Civil War, Reconstruction, and the memory that Abraham Lincoln had been a Republican. The civil rights legislation of the 1960s broke this alignment by driving most white Southern conservatives out of the Democratic Party and into the Republican Party. Nixon's "Southern strategy" had begun capturing white Southern voters in 1968 and 1972. Reagan's 1980 campaign consolidated the shift. By the end of the 1980s, the eleven states of the former Confederacy, which had been the most reliable Democratic states in the nation for a century, were becoming the most reliable Republican states.

This geographic transformation had enormous consequences for both parties. The Democratic Party lost its Southern wing and became more uniformly liberal — more Northern, more urban, more secular, more racially diverse, and more committed to expanded federal programs. The Republican Party gained its Southern wing and became more uniformly conservative — more Southern, more rural, more religious, more committed to tax cuts and reduced government. The two parties became more ideologically coherent and more ideologically distant from each other — a sorting process that contributed to the polarization that defined American politics in subsequent decades.

The white working class was another crucial constituency in the realignment. The New Deal coalition had depended on the loyalty of white working-class and middle-class voters, particularly in the industrial Midwest. In the 1970s and 1980s, this loyalty frayed. Some erosion reflected racial conflict: as the Democratic Party became more identified with civil rights, affirmative action, and urban minority communities, many white working-class voters felt that the party was no longer speaking for them. Some reflected economic anxiety: as manufacturing employment declined and union density fell, the organizational ties that had connected working-class communities to the Democratic Party weakened. Some reflected cultural alienation: the social liberalism of the Democratic Party on abortion, feminism, gay rights, and school prayer felt distant from the values of working-class communities that were often more socially conservative.

Reagan's ability to communicate across class lines — to speak a language of patriotism, optimism, and respect for ordinary American working people that transcended ideological abstractions — was central to the coalition he assembled. He did not win white working-class voters by talking about supply-side economics; he won them by projecting strength, clarity, and a sense that America was a great country whose best days were ahead. The contrast with Carter's "malaise speech" could not have been starker.

The Democratic Party's response to defeat in 1980 and 1984 was initially uncertain and eventually pragmatic. The party nominated Walter Mondale in 1984, who was defeated in a historic landslide, carrying only his home state of Minnesota and the District of Columbia. The lesson drawn by many Democratic strategists was that the party needed to move toward the center — to shed the image of big-government liberalism that had become a liability. This reassessment eventually produced the Democratic Leadership Council, founded in 1985, and ultimately the presidential candidacy of Bill Clinton in 1992, who ran explicitly as a "New Democrat" and accepted many of the terms of the Reagan era's policy debate.

The Reagan era's effects on American political culture extended beyond electoral politics. The assumption that government was the problem rather than the solution — Reagan's formulation from his first inaugural address — entered mainstream political discourse in a way it had not been since before the New Deal. Even Democratic politicians began prefacing their policy proposals with acknowledgments that they were not advocating "big government." When Bill Clinton announced in his 1996 State of the Union address that "the era of big government is over," he was acknowledging the degree to which Reagan had permanently changed the political center of gravity.

Ronald Reagan's First Inaugural Address, January 20, 1981

Creator: Ronald Reagan · Date: January 20, 1981 · Type: Presidential inaugural address (public domain)

Context: Ronald Reagan delivered his first inaugural address on the steps of the Capitol in Washington, D.C. He was sixty-nine years old, the oldest person elected president to that point in American history. The hostages held in Iran for 444 days were released minutes before he took the oath of office — a timing that Reagan's supporters saw as symbolically significant. The address is one of the most consequential inaugural speeches in American history, articulating the central premises of Reagan's political philosophy in clear, accessible language that would define the era.

Reagan declared that in the present crisis, government was not the solution to the problem — government was the problem. He insisted that the federal government had grown beyond its bounds, taxing and regulating citizens and reducing the creative energy of the private sector. He promised to check and reverse the growth of government, to lighten the punishing burden of taxation, and to restore America's military strength. He spoke of ordinary Americans — the "heroes" of everyday life — rather than government programs or political leaders as the true source of American strength and national renewal.

Analysis: The address performed several important rhetorical and political functions simultaneously. By framing government as the problem rather than the solution, Reagan inverted the New Deal premise that active government was necessary for democratic fairness and economic stability. By celebrating ordinary Americans and their capacities rather than government programs, he spoke to a public that had been told for a decade that their problems were too complex for simple solutions. His optimism — genuine and carefully cultivated — provided emotional contrast with the anxious, uncertain atmosphere of the Carter years. The address also reveals the constructed nature of political rhetoric: Reagan spoke of "we the people" as the source of American greatness, but the policies his administration enacted primarily benefited business interests and high-income individuals. The gap between rhetorical populism and policy substance is itself a historical phenomenon worth examining.

Historical Debate

Was the Reagan Era a Conservative Revolution or a Limited Adjustment?

The question: Was the Reagan presidency a fundamental transformation of the American political and economic order, comparable to the New Deal in its scope and lasting consequences? Or was it a significant but ultimately limited adjustment that left the basic structure of American governance intact?

Interpretation A — The Reagan Revolution Was Real and Transformative: Many historians and political scientists argue that the Reagan era constituted a genuine revolution in American governance. Reagan's tax cuts fundamentally altered the distribution of the tax burden, shifting it away from high incomes. His deregulation agenda reduced the regulatory oversight governing financial markets, environmental protection, and consumer safety. His domestic spending cuts, while incomplete, established a new baseline for federal social provision that persisted under his successors. His appointment of conservative justices to the Supreme Court shaped constitutional interpretation for decades. And his rhetorical success in delegitimizing "big government" changed the terms of mainstream political debate in ways that even Democratic administrations felt compelled to accept — as evidenced by Clinton's own declaration that "the era of big government is over."
Interpretation B — Reagan's Radicalism Was Constrained by Political Reality: Other historians argue that Reagan's actual achievements fell substantially short of his stated ambitions. The federal government did not shrink during Reagan's presidency — total federal spending as a percentage of GDP was higher in 1988 than in 1980. Social Security and Medicare were largely protected, partly because their constituencies were too large and politically powerful to attack directly. The tax cuts were partially reversed by Reagan himself in 1982 when deficits soared. Deregulation was real but selective; many regulatory programs survived essentially intact. This interpretation emphasizes the gap between Reagan's rhetoric and his record, and argues that the durability of the New Deal framework demonstrates that it represented a genuine political settlement that no single presidency could overturn.

Evidence: Statistical data on federal spending, tax burden distributions, income inequality trends, regulatory enforcement records, judicial appointment records, and public opinion polls on government services all provide evidence relevant to this debate. The experiences of different communities — wealthy Americans, working-class Americans, African Americans, the poor — provide different lenses on what actually changed and what persisted.

Why it matters: How historians answer this question affects how they understand the contemporary United States. If the Reagan era was a true revolution, then the inequality, deregulation, and anti-government ideology of the following decades are its legacy. If it was a limited adjustment, then the continuities with the New Deal order are what deserve emphasis. The answer also shapes assessments of what political alternatives were possible and what choices particular actors made.

Student task: Using the evidence presented in this chapter, evaluate which interpretation is better supported. What additional evidence would you want to examine? Are there aspects of Reagan's legacy that fit one interpretation but not the other?

Multiple Perspectives

The Reagan Era Through Different Eyes

Working-class manufacturing workers: For the men and women who had built their lives around factory employment in the industrial Midwest, the Reagan era was a period of devastation. The combination of recession, deindustrialization, and the administration's hostility to labor unions dismantled the economic foundations of their communities. When Reagan fired the striking PATCO air traffic controllers in 1981 — summarily dismissing more than eleven thousand federal workers — he sent an unmistakable signal to employers that the federal government would not protect workers' rights to organize and strike. Corporate America responded with increasingly aggressive anti-union tactics. Real wages for production workers stagnated through the 1980s. Communities that had been economically stable for decades became sites of poverty, addiction, and despair.
Suburban professional families: For many suburban, college-educated professional families — voters who had been moving from the Democratic to the Republican column since the late 1960s — the Reagan years felt like a restoration. Inflation, which had eroded the real value of incomes and savings through the 1970s, was conquered. Interest rates eventually fell, making it possible to refinance mortgages and buy homes. The stock market began a historic bull run in 1982 that lasted, with interruptions, through 2000. Reagan's rhetoric of American greatness — his ability to make patriotism feel comfortable and natural again after Vietnam and Watergate — resonated deeply with voters who wanted to feel proud of their country.
African Americans: The Reagan era was a period of significant hardship and political marginalization for African American communities. The recession of 1981–1982 hit Black workers disproportionately hard, given their concentration in manufacturing sectors experiencing layoffs. Cuts to social programs — housing assistance, food stamps, Medicaid, job training — reduced support for communities with the fewest private resources. The administration's opposition to affirmative action and its reduction in civil rights enforcement signaled a retreat from the federal government's commitment to racial equality. Drug policy intensified mandatory minimum sentences that fell with particular severity on Black defendants, fueling the mass incarceration that would transform Black communities across the country.
The LGBTQ community and the AIDS crisis: For the lesbian, gay, bisexual, and transgender community, the Reagan era was marked not only by the social conservatism of the New Right but by a catastrophic public health emergency that the administration largely ignored. AIDS — Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndrome — was identified in 1981 and was devastating gay male communities, as well as intravenous drug users and others, by the mid-1980s. Reagan did not publicly address AIDS until 1987, by which point tens of thousands of Americans had died. The administration's failure to fund research, public education, and care — driven partly by conservative religious objections and partly by political neglect — constituted a moral and public health failure. ACT UP (the AIDS Coalition to Unleash Power), founded in 1987, became one of the most effective advocacy organizations in American history, forcing the federal government and pharmaceutical companies to accelerate research and treatment access through direct action and political pressure.
Constitutional Connection

Executive Power, War Powers, and the Reagan Presidency

The Reagan presidency raised important constitutional questions about the scope of executive power, particularly in foreign policy and national security. The Iran-Contra affair was the most dramatic instance, involving actions by executive branch officials that were not merely politically controversial but arguably criminal and unconstitutional.

The Boland Amendment, enacted by Congress in 1982 and strengthened in subsequent years, explicitly prohibited federal agencies from using funds to support the Nicaraguan Contras. National Security Council staff, reasoning that the amendment applied to executive departments but not to the NSC itself, proceeded to divert funds from the Iran arms sales to the Contras — a legal argument that the Tower Commission and congressional investigating committees found wholly unpersuasive. The episode raised enduring questions about the balance of power between Congress and the executive in foreign policy. The Constitution grants Congress the power of the purse — the authority to appropriate funds and restrict their use. When executive branch officials circumvent congressional restrictions by using alternative funding sources, they undermine the constitutional order.

The War Powers Resolution of 1973, enacted in response to the Vietnam War, had attempted to constrain presidential use of military force by requiring congressional notification and eventual approval. Reagan, like other presidents before and after him, largely ignored its requirements while publicly denying doing so. His deployment of forces in Lebanon, his intervention in Grenada, and his airstrikes against Libya all raised War Powers questions that Congress lacked the political will to press. These constitutional questions — about the scope of executive power, the role of Congress in war and foreign policy, and the accountability of intelligence agencies — did not originate with Reagan and were not resolved by his departure from office. They continue to define central debates in American constitutional law and democratic governance.

Cause and Effect

The Conservative Political Realignment

Long-Term Causes

The New Deal coalition's internal tensions — particularly between Northern liberals committed to civil rights and Southern conservatives committed to segregation — had been present since the 1930s. The growth of suburbanization and the postwar middle class created constituencies with economic interests increasingly at odds with labor and urban liberalism. The intellectual foundations of conservatism — free-market economics, anti-Communism, social traditionalism — had been carefully developed since the 1950s.

Medium-Term & Short-Term Causes

Civil rights legislation drove white Southern conservatives out of the Democratic Party. Social and cultural upheavals of the 1960s and 1970s generated a backlash among traditional voters. The failures of liberal governance in the 1970s — stagflation, Watergate, Carter's political ineffectiveness — discredited the governing philosophy of the Democratic mainstream. The Iran hostage crisis, the Soviet invasion of Afghanistan, and double-digit inflation in 1980 created decisive electoral conditions.

Event or Transformation

The 1980 presidential election produced a landslide victory for Ronald Reagan and a conservative Republican Senate. Reagan received over fifty percent of the popular vote and carried forty-four states. The following years saw the implementation of the most significant conservative policy agenda in American history.

Effects

Largest peacetime tax cut in history; dramatic increase in military spending; cuts to domestic social programs; the PATCO firing; conservative judicial appointments; deregulation of financial markets; a severe recession followed by strong recovery; widening income inequality that has continued for four decades; the eventual collapse of the Soviet Union; permanent transformation of both political parties.

Historical complexity: The conservative realignment cannot be reduced to any single cause. It was the product of long-term structural changes in the American economy and society, medium-term political failures of liberal governance, short-term electoral contingencies, and the specific skills and vision of Ronald Reagan himself. Historians who emphasize economic structure, cultural backlash, organizational capacity, or individual leadership will reach different assessments of which factors were most important.

Continuity and Change Over Time

The Federal Role in American Life, 1980–1989

What Changed

  • The terms of political debate shifted rightward; government became "the problem" rather than the solution in mainstream discourse
  • Top marginal tax rates fell from 70% to 28%; the distribution of the tax burden shifted toward middle incomes
  • Income inequality, stable or declining since World War II, turned sharply upward and has not reversed since
  • The composition of the federal judiciary shifted substantially in a more conservative direction
  • Union membership continued its decline; labor's political influence weakened further
  • The South shifted from solidly Democratic to solidly Republican, permanently transforming both parties
  • Evangelical Christians became a durable Republican constituency, reshaping the cultural politics of both parties

What Stayed the Same

  • The basic architecture of the New Deal state — Social Security, Medicare, Medicaid, public education, environmental regulation — survived largely intact
  • Total federal spending as a share of GDP did not decrease; military expansion offset domestic cuts
  • Public support for core government functions — Social Security, Medicare, public schools — remained high throughout the era
  • The fundamental constitutional structure of American government was not altered
  • The United States remained the dominant global military and economic power

Why the pattern matters: The Reagan era demonstrates that political rhetoric and policy reality can diverge substantially. Reagan promised smaller government and balanced budgets; deficits tripled and popular programs survived. Yet the rhetorical shift — normalizing the idea that government is the problem — changed political possibility in ways that outlasted any specific legislative achievement. Future Democratic presidents would feel compelled to accept the new political terrain, demonstrating how profoundly the era changed the terms of acceptable debate.

Everyday Life and Social History

Living Through the Reagan Era

For ordinary Americans, the Reagan era was experienced not as an ideological abstraction but as a set of concrete changes in daily life — some welcome, some devastating, many simply puzzling. The experience varied enormously depending on geography, class, race, employment sector, and a dozen other variables.

In the industrial Midwest, the recession of 1981–1982 arrived on top of deindustrialization that was already well underway. In Youngstown, Ohio — once one of the most productive steel cities in the world — the shutdowns of 1977 and subsequent years eliminated tens of thousands of jobs. By the early 1980s, unemployment in some Youngstown neighborhoods exceeded twenty-five percent. The men who had built their identities around physical labor, union membership, and the dignity of skilled factory work found themselves not merely unemployed but in many cases unemployable — their specific skills made obsolete by automation or offshore competition. Families that had been economically stable for decades found themselves dependent on food banks, unemployment insurance that ran out, and relatives who were themselves struggling.

In the Sun Belt, the same years felt entirely different. Cities like Houston, Phoenix, Atlanta, and Dallas were booming with defense contracts, real estate development, and new industry. Suburban subdivisions extended further into former desert and agricultural land. Shopping malls proliferated. Young professionals who moved to these cities in the early 1980s often experienced the decade as a period of opportunity, mobility, and rising standards of living. The contrast between Youngstown and Houston in 1983 was not merely economic; it was a contrast between two Americas moving in opposite directions and increasingly failing to understand each other.

Consumer culture in the 1980s took on a particular intensity. Wall Street, newly deregulated and energized by takeover deals and leveraged buyouts, became a cultural symbol. The phrase "Greed is good," from the 1987 film Wall Street, captured a real social atmosphere in which financial success was celebrated and the wealthy were publicly lionized in ways that had seemed somewhat vulgar in earlier decades. Television programs like Dallas and Dynasty depicted lives of luxury that served both as aspiration and escapism. The Walkman, the VCR, the personal computer, and the cable television subscription all changed how Americans spent their time, creating new industries and new consumer habits simultaneously.

For gay men in New York, San Francisco, Los Angeles, and other cities, the decade was shadowed by AIDS in ways that transformed community life with terrifying speed. Friends, lovers, roommates, and colleagues fell ill and died in rapid succession. The early years of the epidemic — before effective treatments and even before the HIV virus had been identified — were characterized by medical ignorance, social stigma, and political silence. The response of the gay community itself — organizing politically, funding research, developing support networks, demanding government action through organizations like ACT UP — represented one of the most remarkable instances of community mobilization in modern American history.

Chapter Summary

The 1970s and the Reagan era represent one of the most consequential periods in modern American political history — a period in which the political and economic framework built by the New Deal was fundamentally challenged, partially dismantled, and in important ways permanently altered. Understanding this transformation requires holding together multiple threads: economic crisis, political scandal, institutional failure, ideological challenge, demographic change, and the particular choices of specific historical actors.

The economic crises of the 1970s — stagflation, oil shocks, deindustrialization — were not the product of any single cause, and they were not simply the result of liberal policy failure, though that is how conservatives successfully narrated them. They reflected the end of the postwar economic order: the erosion of American industrial dominance, the rise of global competition, the fragility of oil-dependent industrial economies, and the limits of Keynesian economic management in the face of supply-side shocks. Carter's struggles reflected real constraints as well as real failures.

Watergate's damage to public trust in government was real and lasting. The revelation that a president and his top aides had committed criminal conspiracy from the highest office in the land produced a crisis of confidence that extended well beyond Nixon and the Republican Party. It fed a broader anti-government sentiment that conservatives were much better positioned to exploit than liberals, whose political program depended on citizens trusting government to act on their behalf.

The conservative movement that rose to power with Reagan in 1980 was the product of decades of intellectual preparation, organizational development, and political learning. It was not a simple reaction to the crises of the 1970s; it had been building since the 1950s, through the Goldwater campaign, through the growth of think tanks and media institutions, through the organizational efforts of direct mail pioneers and religious mobilizers. What the 1970s provided was the electoral opportunity to translate this accumulated capacity into national power.

The Reagan economic program produced real results — inflation was conquered, the economy recovered from recession — but also real costs. Tax cuts primarily benefited high-income households. Cuts to social programs fell primarily on the poor. Economic inequality began a decades-long rise. The promised fiscal discipline of supply-side economics did not materialize: deficits soared as tax cuts outpaced spending reductions. Deregulation of financial markets contributed to the savings and loan crisis and laid groundwork for future financial instability.

The Cold War strategy of the Reagan years accelerated American military spending and increased tensions with the Soviet Union in ways that created genuine risks of miscalculation. At the same time, Reagan's eventual willingness to engage with Mikhail Gorbachev produced arms control agreements and a diplomatic breakthrough that contributed to the conditions in which the Cold War ended peacefully. The relationship between Reagan's military pressure, his ideological challenge, and the internal Soviet dynamics that drove Gorbachev's reforms remains a subject of legitimate historical debate.

The political realignment of the Reagan era reshaped both parties in lasting ways. The Republican Party became the party of the Sun Belt, the suburbs, evangelical Christians, the business community, and cultural conservatism. The Democratic Party became more uniformly liberal, more urban, more racially diverse, and more secular — a coalition powerful enough to win presidential elections but with structural disadvantages in the geographic distribution of its supporters. The sorting of the parties into more ideologically distinct camps contributed to the polarization that has characterized American politics ever since.

For different Americans, the Reagan era meant different things. For suburban professionals who saw their investments grow and their taxes fall, it felt like prosperity and renewal. For steel workers in Youngstown or auto workers in Detroit who lost their jobs and watched their communities decline, it felt like abandonment. For African Americans dealing with the dual impact of economic dislocation and reduced civil rights enforcement, it felt like regression. For gay Americans living through the AIDS epidemic while their government looked away, it felt like moral catastrophe. History requires holding all of these experiences in view simultaneously, without reducing the era to any single narrative that obscures its complexity. The Reagan era did not complete a conservative revolution in the sense of permanently dismantling the New Deal state, but it did shift the center of political gravity and set in motion trends in inequality, judicial philosophy, and political polarization that have defined American life for the four decades since.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Stagflation
The simultaneous occurrence of economic stagnation (high unemployment, slow growth) and high inflation. Characterized the U.S. economy through much of the 1970s; discredited Keynesian economics.
Keynesian economics
Economic theory holding that government should manage the business cycle through fiscal policy — spending and taxation. Dominant framework of postwar policy; challenged by stagflation.
Supply-side economics
Theory holding that reducing taxes on businesses and high incomes stimulates investment and growth that benefits everyone. Central intellectual framework of Reagan economic policy.
Laffer Curve
Theoretical diagram suggesting that above a certain tax rate, cutting taxes increases revenue by stimulating growth. Provided intellectual justification for Reagan tax cuts.
Reaganomics
Popular term for Reagan's economic program: large tax cuts, domestic spending reductions, deregulation, and tight monetary policy.
Deregulation
Reduction or elimination of government regulations on business; major policy initiative of the Reagan administration with lasting consequences for financial markets.
Monetarism
Economic theory holding that controlling money supply growth is the key to controlling inflation. Influenced Federal Reserve policy under Paul Volcker.
OPEC oil embargo
1973 Arab oil producers' halt of petroleum sales to the United States; caused gasoline shortages and accelerated inflation throughout the economy.
Détente
Nixon and Kissinger's policy of reducing Cold War tensions through diplomacy and trade with the Soviet Union; Reagan rejected it in favor of confrontation.
New Right
The conservative political movement combining free-market economics, social conservatism, anti-communism, and cultural backlash; achieved national power with Reagan's election in 1980.
Deindustrialization
Decline of manufacturing as a share of American economy; caused factory closures, job losses, Rust Belt decline, and the weakening of organized labor.
Sun Belt
Rapidly growing states from Florida across the Gulf Coast through Texas, the Southwest, and into California; center of demographic shift and conservative politics.
Rust Belt
Former industrial regions of the Midwest and Northeast experiencing factory closures and population decline after deindustrialization.
Tax revolt
Late-1970s political movement demanding property and income tax reductions; most dramatically expressed in California's Proposition 13 (1978).
Moral Majority
Jerry Falwell's political organization (1979–1989) that mobilized conservative Christians as a Republican constituency; instrumental in Reagan's electoral victories.
Social conservatism
Political orientation prioritizing traditional family values, opposition to abortion and gay rights, and support for religious expression in public life.
Watergate
1972–1974 scandal in which Nixon authorized criminal conspiracy and cover-up; led to his resignation and damaged public trust in government for decades.
Iran hostage crisis
444-day captivity of 52 Americans in Iran (1979–1981); devastated Carter's presidency and inflamed American public opinion before Reagan's inauguration.
Strategic Defense Initiative (SDI)
Reagan's 1983 proposal for a space-based missile defense system; called "Star Wars" by critics; escalated Cold War arms debate.
Iran-Contra affair
Secret, illegal arms sales to Iran and diversion of profits to Nicaraguan Contras in violation of the Boland Amendment; most serious scandal of Reagan's second term.
Reagan Doctrine
Policy of supporting anti-Communist rebel movements worldwide; justified covert support for mujahideen in Afghanistan, Contras in Nicaragua, and others.
PATCO
Professional Air Traffic Controllers Organization; Reagan's 1981 firing of 11,000 striking PATCO workers signaled a new era of hostile labor-management relations.
Economic Recovery Tax Act (1981)
Legislation enacting Reagan's largest tax cuts; reduced top marginal rate from 70% to 50% initially and eventually to 28%; cornerstone of Reaganomics.
Deficit spending
Government spending beyond revenues; Reagan era produced record peacetime deficits despite anti-deficit rhetoric as military increases outpaced domestic cuts.

Major People and Places

Identification & Significance

Ronald Reagan (1911–2004)
Fortieth president (1981–1989). Former actor and California governor. Champion of supply-side economics, deregulation, and Cold War confrontation. Most consequential conservative political figure of the twentieth century. His presidency changed the terms of American political debate for decades.
Jimmy Carter (b. 1924)
Thirty-ninth president (1977–1981). Georgia governor and peanut farmer. Honest and intelligent but politically ineffective amid stagflation and the Iran crisis. His presidency became the negative reference point against which Reagan defined conservatism's promise.
Richard Nixon (1913–1994)
Thirty-seventh president (1969–1974). Orchestrated Watergate conspiracy and cover-up; first president to resign. His crimes produced generational distrust of government that shaped the political landscape Reagan exploited.
Paul Volcker (1927–2019)
Federal Reserve chairman (1979–1987). Raised interest rates dramatically to break inflation; caused severe recession of 1981–1982 but stabilized prices for decades. His monetarist approach became the new orthodoxy of central banking.
Jerry Falwell Sr. (1933–2007)
Baptist pastor, televangelist, and founder of the Moral Majority (1979). Led evangelical Christian mobilization into Republican politics; helped win Reagan roughly 80% of the white evangelical vote in 1980.
Phyllis Schlafly (1924–2016)
Conservative activist who led the successful campaign against the Equal Rights Amendment; became the defining figure of antifeminist social conservatism within the New Right.
Milton Friedman (1912–2006)
Economist at the University of Chicago. Articulated the case for free markets and monetarism; profoundly influenced Reagan administration economic policy and the broader intellectual environment of the era.
Barry Goldwater (1909–1998)
Arizona senator and 1964 Republican presidential candidate. His campaign energized the modern conservative movement despite electoral defeat; his loss educated a generation of conservative organizers who would achieve power in 1980.
Mikhail Gorbachev (b. 1931)
Soviet leader (1985–1991). His reform programs of glasnost and perestroika contributed to Soviet collapse; his negotiations with Reagan produced the INF Treaty and opened the path to the Cold War's end.
Oliver North (b. 1943)
National Security Council official who orchestrated the Iran-Contra scheme. His televised congressional testimony made him a polarizing public figure — a hero to some conservatives, a symbol of lawless executive power to critics.
Youngstown, Ohio
Steel city devastated by mill closures beginning 1977 when U.S. Steel shuttered major facilities. Became the most widely cited symbol of Rust Belt deindustrialization and the human cost of industrial collapse.
The Heritage Foundation
Conservative think tank established 1973. Produced the "Mandate for Leadership" policy blueprint extensively used by the Reagan administration. Became a model for the conservative policy infrastructure.

Chapter Reflection

The story of the 1970s and the Reagan era is, at one level, a story about a specific political and economic crisis — stagflation, Watergate, the oil shocks — and a specific political response: the rise of conservatism and the election of Ronald Reagan. But it is also, at a deeper level, a story about questions that have defined American democracy from its founding: Who benefits from economic growth? What obligations does government have to its citizens? How are the costs of economic change distributed? Whose values should guide public life? These questions did not originate with Reagan, and they were not settled when he left office. They are the questions that students of U.S. history are still living with today.

The conservative resurgence of the late 1970s and 1980s drew on real grievances and real failures. The economic crises of the 1970s were genuine, and liberal governance had not resolved them. Public trust in government had been damaged — not without reason — by Watergate, Vietnam, and a succession of institutional failures. Many Americans felt that the Democratic Party had drifted away from their values and their interests. These grievances were real, even when conservative solutions to them proved inadequate or harmful.

But the chapter also reveals how political narratives can simplify complex realities in ways that serve particular interests. Reaganomics promised that cutting taxes on wealthy individuals and corporations would produce growth that would benefit everyone. The evidence suggests that it produced growth in some sectors while widening inequality and failing to deliver the promised fiscal discipline. The narrative that government programs were the source of economic problems distracted from the structural causes of deindustrialization — automation, global competition, corporate choices about relocation — that no administration, liberal or conservative, fully addressed. The cultural politics of the era, which focused public anxiety on feminism, abortion, and gay rights, shifted attention from economic displacement and class inequality that the conservative economic agenda did little to alleviate.

What this chapter contributes to the larger story of United States history is an understanding of how political coalitions are built — how they depend on combinations of genuine interest and cultural mobilization, how they produce policy consequences that affect different communities differently, and how they change the terrain on which subsequent political struggles occur. The conservative coalition that Reagan assembled in 1980 did not emerge from nowhere. It was built by specific people, funded by specific donors, articulated by specific intellectuals, and organized by specific activists over decades. Its success — and the challenges it created for those it did not serve — is the product of historical choices, not inevitable social forces.

The Reagan era made the United States that followed it. The income inequality, the political polarization, the judicial conservatism, the weakened labor movement, the evangelical political engagement, and many other features of early twenty-first-century American life have roots in the choices made and the coalitions built between the mid-1970s and the end of the 1980s. Understanding those choices, who made them and why, who benefited and who bore the costs, is essential to understanding the country that students inherit. The United States that Ronald Reagan left in January 1989 was a different country from the one that Jimmy Carter had inherited from Gerald Ford in January 1977 — more unequal in its distribution of wealth, more conservative in its political center of gravity, more suspicious of its government's ambitions, more assertive on the world stage. It was also, in some respects, more prosperous and more confident than the anxious, crisis-ridden nation of the late 1970s. Both of these things were true simultaneously, and holding them together — without collapsing the era's complexity into either triumphalism or condemnation — is what good history requires.

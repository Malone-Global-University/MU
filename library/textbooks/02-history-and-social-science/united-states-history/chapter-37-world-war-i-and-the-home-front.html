Part VIII — Reform, War, and Modern America

World War I and the Home Front

United States History: Indigenous America, Republic, Conflict, and Change

Chapter Opening

"The world must be made safe for democracy. Its peace must be planted upon the tested foundations of political liberty."
— Woodrow Wilson, Address to Congress, April 2, 1917

On the morning of May 7, 1915, the British ocean liner Lusitania was sailing off the southern coast of Ireland when a German submarine fired a torpedo into her hull. The ship sank in eighteen minutes. Nearly 1,200 people died, including 128 American citizens. The news arrived in the United States with the force of a physical shock. Newspapers ran vivid accounts of survivors clinging to wreckage in the cold Atlantic, of lifeboats that never reached the water, of children lost in the chaos. Americans were outraged. But the nation did not go to war. President Woodrow Wilson, in a phrase that became both famous and mocked, declared that a man could be "too proud to fight." The United States, he insisted, must remain neutral.

That decision — to stay out of a war already consuming Europe — reflected something deep in American political culture. The United States had long prided itself on staying apart from the entangling alliances and dynastic quarrels of the Old World. George Washington's Farewell Address had warned against "permanent alliances." For more than a century, that counsel had shaped foreign policy. In 1914 and 1915, the war in Europe seemed exactly the kind of distant, complicated catastrophe that America was wise to avoid. It was a European war, fought over European empires, European rivalries, and European pride. Most Americans, including the many millions who had recently immigrated from Britain, Germany, Ireland, Austria-Hungary, Russia, and Italy, had complicated loyalties. They did not want to be asked to choose.

Yet within two years, the United States was fully at war. The transformation between 1914 and 1917 is one of the most consequential shifts in American history. It did not happen suddenly, and it did not happen because Americans simply changed their minds about the morality of war. It happened because of submarine warfare that threatened American lives and commerce, because of British propaganda that shaped how Americans understood the conflict, because of financial ties that bound American banks and businesses to Allied success, because of a diplomatic telegram that proposed a military alliance between Germany and Mexico against the United States, and because Woodrow Wilson came to believe — and persuaded much of the public — that American entry could reshape not just the war but the entire international order.

The consequences of that decision remade American life in ways that lasted long after the armistice. To fight a modern industrial war, the federal government mobilized the economy, regulated industry, raised an army through conscription, sold war bonds to finance the fighting, and launched the most ambitious propaganda campaign in American history. It also suppressed dissent with a ferocity that shocked civil libertarians, prosecuting Americans for criticizing the war, the draft, and the government. The Espionage Act of 1917 and the Sedition Act of 1918 sent hundreds of people to prison for speech that would be ordinary political commentary in peacetime. The Socialist leader Eugene V. Debs went to federal prison for a speech urging workers not to join what he called a capitalist war.

The war also accelerated some of the most important changes in American social history. African Americans by the hundreds of thousands left the South for northern cities in what historians call the Great Migration — not primarily because of the war, but because the war opened industrial jobs that had previously been closed to Black workers. The movement transformed Chicago, Detroit, Cleveland, Pittsburgh, and New York, and it changed the landscape of American racial politics for generations. Women entered the workforce in unprecedented numbers, took on new public roles, and pressed successfully for the constitutional amendment that gave them the right to vote. The Nineteenth Amendment, ratified in 1920, was in part a product of the argument that women who served the nation in wartime deserved full citizenship.

When the war ended in November 1918, Woodrow Wilson arrived in Europe as a hero. Crowds in Paris, London, and Rome cheered him as a savior. He had promised, in his Fourteen Points speech of January 1918, a new kind of international order — open diplomacy, self-determination for peoples, freedom of the seas, disarmament, and above all a League of Nations that would settle future disputes peacefully rather than through war. The Versailles peace conference of 1919 produced a treaty that fell far short of those promises. Germany was stripped of territory, burdened with massive reparations, and humiliated in ways that many observers warned would breed resentment and instability. Wilson accepted the compromises because he believed the League of Nations would correct them over time. He was wrong. The United States Senate refused to ratify the treaty, the League was weakened from birth, and the settlement planted the seeds of an even more destructive conflict two decades later.

This chapter examines the United States during the First World War — why it entered the conflict, how it mobilized at home and fought abroad, what happened to civil liberties, labor, race, and gender during the war years, and what the peace settlement meant for America and the world. The story of American involvement in World War I is not simply a military history. It is a story about democracy, power, propaganda, dissent, race, gender, and the question of what kind of nation the United States would become in the twentieth century.

Historical Context

The First World War grew from a network of tensions that had been building in Europe for decades. By 1914, the European great powers had organized themselves into two rival alliance systems: the Triple Alliance of Germany, Austria-Hungary, and Italy, and the Triple Entente of France, Russia, and Britain. These alliances were backed by enormous professional armies, elaborate war plans, and a naval arms race between Britain and Germany that had intensified national rivalries. Colonial competition in Africa and Asia added further friction. A web of secret treaties meant that a conflict between two nations could rapidly pull in all the others.

The spark came on June 28, 1914, when a Bosnian Serb nationalist named Gavrilo Princip assassinated Archduke Franz Ferdinand, the heir to the Austro-Hungarian throne, in Sarajevo. Austria-Hungary blamed Serbia and issued an ultimatum. Serbia's partial refusal triggered the alliance system. Austria-Hungary declared war on Serbia; Russia began mobilizing to protect Serbia; Germany declared war on Russia and then France; and Britain — bound by treaty to defend Belgian neutrality that Germany violated in executing its war plan — declared war on Germany. Within six weeks of Franz Ferdinand's assassination, most of Europe was at war.

The war that followed was unlike anything the world had seen. Industrial technology — machine guns, artillery, poison gas, barbed wire, airplanes, tanks, and submarines — produced a conflict of staggering destruction. On the Western Front, a line of trenches stretched from the Belgian coast to the Swiss border. The Battle of the Somme in 1916 cost the British Army 57,000 casualties on its first day alone. The Battle of Verdun, fought through most of 1916, killed or wounded nearly 700,000 soldiers from both sides. Empires that had stood for centuries — the Ottoman, Austro-Hungarian, Russian, and German — were crumbling under the pressure of industrial warfare and mass mobilization.

The United States in 1914 was a nation undergoing its own transformation. The Progressive Era had brought significant reforms: antitrust legislation, labor regulation, conservation, women's suffrage at the state level, the direct election of senators, the income tax, and the Federal Reserve. Woodrow Wilson, elected in 1912 as a reform Democrat, had continued and deepened the Progressive agenda. But the United States was also a nation with a complex relationship to Europe. Millions of Americans had been born in Germany, Ireland, Britain, Italy, Austria-Hungary, Russia, or Scandinavia. German Americans were among the largest ethnic communities in the nation. Irish Americans, many of them bitter about British rule over Ireland, had little sympathy for Britain's cause. Many socialists and labor activists saw the war as a conflict among empires that workers had no reason to join.

At the same time, the United States had important economic and cultural ties to Britain and France. American banks had lent enormous sums to the Allied powers. American farmers and manufacturers supplied food, weapons, and raw materials to the Allies. When Germany began using submarines to sink ships supplying Britain, it struck directly at American economic interests and American lives. The political context mattered too: Woodrow Wilson, shaped by Presbyterian Christianity and a belief in America's special mission in the world, would come to believe that American entry could transform not just the war but the entire international order.

Section 37.1

The United States and the World Before 1917

America's Global Position in 1914

By 1914, the United States was one of the most economically powerful nations on earth. Its industrial output rivaled or surpassed that of Germany and Britain. Its agricultural production fed much of the world. Its railroads, steel mills, oil fields, and financial institutions represented the most concentrated accumulation of industrial capital in history. The United States had also become an imperial power in its own right. Since the Spanish-American War of 1898, it controlled Puerto Rico, Guam, and the Philippines. It exercised strong influence over Cuba, Haiti, the Dominican Republic, and much of Central America. It had built the Panama Canal, opening in 1914, which reorganized global trade routes and demonstrated American engineering and geopolitical ambition.

Yet the United States defined itself, in its official ideology and in the beliefs of most of its citizens, as a non-European power that stood apart from the rivalries and entanglements of the Old World. Washington's Farewell Address had warned against "permanent alliances." The Monroe Doctrine of 1823 had declared the Western Hemisphere off-limits to further European colonization. Through most of the nineteenth century, these principles had served American interests well: the United States could expand across the continent, build its economy, and avoid the costs of European-style standing armies and global empire.

But by 1914, the United States was deeply embedded in the global economy. Its banks had lent money to European governments and corporations. Its farmers exported wheat, cotton, and corn to European markets. Its manufacturers supplied machinery, chemicals, and finished goods across the Atlantic. This economic integration meant that a war in Europe was never truly "Europe's problem." The war's disruption of trade, credit, and shipping would affect Americans directly — and the question of which side the United States would supply and support would shape both the war's outcome and America's place in the postwar world.

A Diverse and Divided Population

One of the most important realities shaping American policy in 1914 was the ethnic and political diversity of the American population. The period between 1880 and 1914 had seen the largest wave of immigration in American history: more than fifteen million people had arrived from southern and eastern Europe, as well as from China, Japan, and Mexico. Millions of German Americans lived in the Midwest, many in communities that maintained strong cultural and sentimental ties to Germany. Irish Americans, many bitter about British rule over Ireland, were often openly hostile to any American policy that aided Britain. Jewish Americans who had fled persecution in tsarist Russia had little sympathy for the Russian empire. Scandinavian Americans in Minnesota and Wisconsin were often committed pacifists.

This diversity was not merely a matter of sentiment. It was organized political pressure. German American organizations lobbied against any American alignment with the Allies. Irish American political networks in cities like New York, Boston, and Chicago were forces no politician could ignore. Labor unions, many of them led by immigrants or socialists, argued that the war was a conflict of capitalist empires in which workers had no stake. At the same time, the American cultural establishment — newspapers, universities, Protestant churches, and business leaders — leaned heavily toward Britain and France. British propaganda, produced by skilled professionals and delivered through American media channels, shaped how the war was understood by millions of Americans. Stories of German atrocities in Belgium — some real, some embellished — created a moral narrative in which Germany was the aggressor and the Allies were defending civilization.

Wilson's Neutrality and Its Contradictions

Woodrow Wilson declared American neutrality on August 4, 1914. He appealed to Americans to be "neutral in fact as well as in name" and "impartial in thought as well as in action." But American neutrality was never truly impartial. British naval power cut off Germany from American trade almost immediately. The Royal Navy mined the North Sea and enforced a blockade that prevented food, raw materials, and weapons from reaching Germany. The United States protested these violations of neutral rights — but did not break off trade with Britain.

Meanwhile, American banks and businesses grew deeply invested in Allied victory. By 1917, American banks had lent approximately $2.5 billion to Britain and France, compared to only $27 million to Germany. American factories were producing weapons, ammunition, and supplies for the Allies on a massive scale. A German victory would mean not only strategic defeat for the Allies but financial ruin for American lenders. Secretary of State William Jennings Bryan, who was genuinely committed to neutrality, resigned in 1915 in protest over Wilson's handling of the Lusitania crisis, which Bryan believed was pulling the nation toward war. His successor, Robert Lansing, was more amenable to the Allied cause.

The Progressive context mattered too. The Progressive Era had developed the tools — committees, agencies, expert administrators, public relations — that would be used to mobilize the nation for war. The war, in turn, would dramatically accelerate the growth of federal power and government reach into daily life, in ways that some Progressives celebrated and others feared. Wilson came to believe not just that the United States should enter the war, but that it should enter to transform the world — to end imperialism, to establish self-determination, to create a new international order based on law rather than power. This moral ambition would drive his Fourteen Points and his vision for the League of Nations.

Section 37.2

Neutrality and the Road to War

Submarine Warfare and the Lusitania

The central issue that pushed the United States toward war was German submarine warfare. Germany had entered the war with a smaller surface navy than Britain, which meant it could not challenge British control of the Atlantic by conventional means. Instead, Germany turned to submarines — U-booten — as a strategic weapon. In February 1915, Germany declared the waters around Britain a war zone and announced that enemy ships would be sunk without warning. Neutral ships were warned to avoid the zone because Germany could not guarantee their safety.

The sinking of the Lusitania on May 7, 1915 brought this policy into sharp and dramatic relief. The Lusitania was a British passenger liner carrying nearly 1,200 civilians — including 128 Americans — along with, as Germany claimed and evidence later confirmed, a cargo of war materials. A single torpedo sent her to the bottom in less than twenty minutes. The death toll shocked the American public. Wilson sent a series of sharp diplomatic notes demanding that Germany end the practice of sinking passenger ships without warning and provide reparations for American lives lost. Germany backed down — partially. After the sinking of the Arabic in August 1915, Germany issued the "Arabic Pledge," promising not to sink passenger liners without warning. After the sinking of the Sussex in March 1916, Germany issued the Sussex Pledge, promising not to sink merchant or passenger ships without warning. These pledges bought time, but Germany made clear it would resume unrestricted submarine warfare if the United States did not pressure Britain to lift its naval blockade.

The Election of 1916

The presidential election of 1916 took place against this backdrop of anxious neutrality. Wilson ran for reelection highlighting his Progressive achievements and using the slogan "He Kept Us Out of War." His Republican opponent, Charles Evans Hughes, was more hawkish but ambiguous. Wilson won narrowly, carrying several western states where Progressive and anti-war sentiment was strong. The election was so close that Hughes went to bed on election night believing he had won, only to learn the next morning that California had gone to Wilson by fewer than four thousand votes.

In 1916, most Americans did not want war. They respected Wilson's effort to keep the peace while defending neutral rights. But the conditions that had made neutrality possible were deteriorating.

The Breaking Point: Unrestricted Submarine Warfare and the Zimmermann Telegram

In January 1917, Germany made a fateful decision. German military leaders calculated that if they could starve Britain into surrender by cutting off its Atlantic supply lines, they could win the war before American intervention could be effective. They knew unrestricted submarine warfare would bring the United States into the conflict, but they gambled they could knock Britain out first. On February 1, 1917, Germany announced the resumption of unrestricted submarine warfare, in direct violation of the Sussex Pledge.

Wilson responded by breaking off diplomatic relations with Germany. He also asked Congress for authority to arm American merchant ships. A group of anti-war senators, led by Robert La Follette of Wisconsin and George Norris of Nebraska, filibustered the measure to death in the final hours of the congressional session. Wilson was furious, calling them "a little group of willful men" who had rendered the government helpless. He armed the merchant ships by executive authority instead.

The final catalyst was the Zimmermann Telegram. In January 1917, German Foreign Secretary Arthur Zimmermann sent a coded telegram to the German minister in Mexico, instructing him to offer Mexico an alliance: if Mexico would join Germany in war against the United States, Germany would help Mexico recover Texas, New Mexico, and Arizona. British intelligence intercepted and decoded the telegram and passed it to the American government. When it was published on March 1, 1917, it caused a sensation. Even in the American Southwest, where anti-war sentiment had been strong, the telegram was read as a direct threat to American sovereignty and territorial integrity.

By March 1917, German submarines had sunk several American merchant ships with loss of American life. The Russian Revolution of February 1917 had overthrown the tsar, making the Allies more clearly a coalition of democracies — a framing Wilson could use more easily. On April 2, 1917, Wilson appeared before a joint session of Congress to ask for a declaration of war, declaring that "the world must be made safe for democracy." Congress voted for war on April 6, 1917, with strong but not unanimous support: six senators and fifty representatives voted against the declaration, including Jeannette Rankin of Montana, the first woman ever elected to Congress.

Section 37.3

Mobilization and the American Expeditionary Forces

Building an Army

When the United States declared war in April 1917, it had a tiny professional army of approximately 128,000 men — far too small to make a meaningful difference in a war that had already consumed millions of lives. Congress quickly passed the Selective Service Act of 1917, establishing a military draft. Registration was made mandatory for all men between twenty-one and thirty (later expanded to eighteen to forty-five). By the war's end, approximately 24 million men had registered, and about 2.8 million were drafted. Another 2 million volunteered. In total, approximately 4.7 million Americans served in the armed forces during World War I.

The draft was administered through local boards rather than federal bureaucrats, which helped defuse some potential resistance. Local boards had wide discretion to grant exemptions for hardship, essential occupation, or conscientious objection. The system functioned more smoothly than the Civil War draft, which had triggered violent riots in New York and other cities. Opposition to the draft existed nonetheless. The Socialist Party denounced it as "a militarist program." Some religious communities, including Mennonites, Quakers, and other historic peace churches, sought conscientious objector status. About 65,000 men claimed conscientious objector status, and approximately 4,000 were officially recognized as such.

The American Expeditionary Forces in France

General John J. Pershing commanded the American Expeditionary Forces (AEF) in France. Pershing insisted on keeping the AEF as an independent American force rather than feeding American troops into depleted Allied units as replacements, as French and British commanders urged. This political decision had military costs in the short run but preserved American independence and gave Pershing more control over how American soldiers were used.

American troops began arriving in France in significant numbers in 1917 but did not play a major combat role until the spring and summer of 1918. The AEF fought in a series of major engagements: Cantigny (May 1918), Belleau Wood (June 1918), Saint-Mihiel (September 1918), and the massive Meuse-Argonne Offensive (September–November 1918). The Meuse-Argonne was the largest American military operation in history to that point, involving 1.2 million American soldiers across a forty-mile front. American forces suffered approximately 117,000 combat deaths and 200,000 wounded. Influenza killed approximately 50,000 more American soldiers in Europe.

The arrival of fresh American troops in 1918 came at a critical moment. Germany had launched a series of massive spring offensives that nearly broke the Allied lines. By summer, exhausted German forces were facing not just the battered French and British armies but a seemingly inexhaustible supply of American soldiers. The German high command recognized by late summer that the war was lost. On November 11, 1918 — the eleventh hour of the eleventh day of the eleventh month — an armistice ended the fighting.

African American Soldiers

Approximately 380,000 African Americans served in the military during World War I, though they faced severe discrimination throughout. The military was rigidly segregated. Black soldiers were assigned to separate units, given inferior equipment, denied access to recreational facilities available to white soldiers, and often assigned to labor and supply duties rather than combat. Despite these conditions, African American units served with distinction. The 369th Infantry Regiment — the "Harlem Hellfighters" — spent more consecutive days in combat than any other American unit, 191 days, and was awarded the French Croix de Guerre for its valor. They were assigned to the French Army partly because French commanders were more willing to use Black troops in combat.

The experience of fighting for democracy abroad while being denied equality at home created a profound contradiction that African American leaders and thinkers would articulate with great power after the war. W.E.B. Du Bois had controversially urged Black Americans to support the war in a 1918 editorial titled "Close Ranks," arguing that wartime service would advance claims for full citizenship. The postwar violence of the Red Summer of 1919 gave painful evidence of how wrong that hope would prove.

Industrial Mobilization

Fighting the war required an unprecedented mobilization of the American economy. The federal government created a series of new agencies to coordinate war production. The War Industries Board, led by financier Bernard Baruch, coordinated industrial production, set prices, allocated raw materials, and directed factories toward war-related output. The Food Administration, led by future president Herbert Hoover, managed the production and distribution of food, encouraging Americans to observe "Wheatless Mondays" and "Meatless Tuesdays." The Fuel Administration managed coal and oil production. The Railroad Administration took control of the nation's railroads and ran them as a unified system.

These agencies represented an enormous expansion of federal economic power — one that would have been unthinkable before the war and that was largely dismantled after it. The experience of wartime coordination provided a model, however imperfect, for later efforts to mobilize the economy during the New Deal and World War II.

Historical Vocabulary
Unrestricted Submarine Warfare

Unrestricted submarine warfare was Germany's policy of attacking any ship — including neutral merchant vessels — found in declared war zones without giving warning or ensuring the safety of passengers and crew. This policy violated international law as it existed at the time, which required warships to warn merchant vessels before attacking and to rescue survivors.

Germany adopted the policy because submarines were far more effective as surprise weapons than as enforcers of traditional naval rules. A submarine that surfaced to give warning became vulnerable to attack. Germany justified the policy by arguing that Britain's blockade of Germany was itself a violation of international law that starved German civilians.

For the United States, unrestricted submarine warfare was the proximate cause of war — it directly threatened American lives and commerce in ways that could not be ignored without abandoning the principle of neutral rights. Germany's calculation that it could knock Britain out before the United States could mobilize proved tragically wrong.

Section 37.4

Propaganda and War Bonds

The Committee on Public Information

Winning a modern war required more than armies and weapons. It required mobilizing public opinion — convincing millions of ordinary Americans to support the war, buy war bonds, conserve food and fuel, accept the draft, and maintain morale through years of hardship. Woodrow Wilson created the Committee on Public Information (CPI) in April 1917. He appointed George Creel, a progressive journalist and Wilson loyalist, to lead it.

The CPI became the most extensive government propaganda apparatus in American history to that point. It produced tens of millions of pamphlets, posters, and leaflets explaining American war aims and vilifying Germany. It organized a network of approximately 75,000 speakers — the "Four-Minute Men" — who delivered short, patriotic speeches in movie theaters, churches, schools, and other public gatherings, reaching audiences of approximately 400 million people during the war. It produced films, slide shows, and newspaper features. It worked with advertising agencies to design posters that remain iconic: James Montgomery Flagg's "I Want You" image of Uncle Sam pointing directly at the viewer, and Howard Chandler Christy's images beckoning men to enlist.

The CPI's propaganda was not simply patriotic; it was often aggressively anti-German. German Americans were depicted as dangerous aliens harboring loyalty to the Kaiser. German culture — music, philosophy, language — was treated as inherently militarist. Cities renamed German-named streets; sauerkraut was renamed "liberty cabbage." This anti-German campaign encouraged vigilantism and violence. In one extreme case, a German-born man named Robert Prager was lynched in Collinsville, Illinois in April 1918 by a mob that suspected him of disloyalty. The men charged were acquitted and cheered by onlookers.

The CPI raised important questions about the relationship between democracy, propaganda, and public opinion that remain relevant today. A democratic government, in theory, depends on an informed and freely deliberating citizenry. Yet the CPI used sophisticated techniques — emotional appeals, fear, stereotyping, and censorship of unfavorable information — to shape public opinion rather than merely inform it. The line between public education and manipulation was blurry, and many critics both at the time and afterward argued that the CPI crossed it repeatedly. Edward Bernays, who worked for the CPI, went on to become the founder of modern public relations, explicitly drawing on his wartime experience to theorize the manipulation of public opinion in democratic societies.

Liberty Bonds and Wartime Finance

The federal government financed much of the war through the sale of Liberty Bonds — government debt securities that Americans could purchase, effectively lending money to the government for the war effort. The Treasury Department conducted four Liberty Loan campaigns between 1917 and 1918, and a fifth Victory Loan in 1919. The campaigns raised approximately $21 billion, roughly two-thirds of the war's total cost. The remainder was covered by tax increases, including a steeply graduated income tax made possible by the Sixteenth Amendment of 1913.

The Liberty Bond campaigns were public spectacles. Film stars including Charlie Chaplin, Mary Pickford, and Douglas Fairbanks appeared at bond rallies in major cities. Parades, concerts, and public events were organized around bond sales. Local communities set bond quotas, and those who failed to meet them risked social pressure, public shaming, or worse — being labeled insufficiently patriotic. The pressure to buy bonds was not merely social encouragement; it shaded into coercion in many communities, particularly for German Americans and others whose loyalty was already suspect.

Progressive critics like Senator Robert La Follette argued that the war should be financed primarily through taxation of wealth and corporate profits rather than borrowing, noting that wealthier Americans who bought bonds would be repaid with interest by future taxpayers. Congress did increase taxes significantly — the War Revenue Act of 1917 raised the top income tax rate to 67 percent and imposed an excess profits tax on corporations — but bonds remained the primary financing mechanism.

Section 37.5

Civil Liberties During Wartime

The Espionage Act and Sedition Act

The mobilization of public opinion for war required not only promotion of the war effort but suppression of dissent. Congress passed the Espionage Act in June 1917. The law made it a crime — punishable by up to twenty years in prison — to interfere with military recruitment or operations, to cause insubordination in the armed forces, or to obstruct the draft. It also gave the postmaster general broad authority to exclude from the mail any material deemed treasonous or subversive. This provision was used extensively to suppress anti-war newspapers, Socialist periodicals, and labor publications.

Congress went further in May 1918, passing the Sedition Act, which amended the Espionage Act to criminalize any speech, print, or publication that used "disloyal, profane, scurrilous, or abusive language" about the government, the Constitution, the military, or the war effort. Under these laws, it became a federal crime to argue that the war was wrong, to say the draft was unjust, or to criticize American foreign policy.

Prosecutions and Suppressions

The Espionage and Sedition Acts produced approximately 2,000 prosecutions and more than 1,000 convictions. The targets were overwhelmingly socialists, labor organizers, pacifists, and German Americans — people who had opposed the war for political or ideological reasons, not actual spies or saboteurs. Kate Richards O'Hare, a prominent Socialist, was sentenced to five years in prison for a speech opposing the draft. Victor Berger, a Socialist congressman from Wisconsin, was sentenced to twenty years in prison for anti-war newspaper editorials — a sentence later overturned.

The most famous case was that of Eugene V. Debs. In June 1918, Debs spoke to a crowd in Canton, Ohio, praising imprisoned anti-war activists and warning workers against a capitalist war. He was arrested, tried, and sentenced to ten years in prison. His response at sentencing became one of the most quoted statements in American political history: "While there is a lower class, I am in it, while there is a criminal element, I am of it, and while there is a soul in prison, I am not free." He ran for president again in 1920 from his prison cell and received nearly a million votes. Wilson refused to pardon him; President Harding commuted his sentence in 1921.

The Industrial Workers of the World (IWW), a radical labor union that had opposed the war and organized among the most exploited workers in mining, lumber, and agriculture, was effectively destroyed by wartime prosecution. In a single mass trial in Chicago, 101 IWW leaders were convicted of Espionage Act violations and sentenced to terms ranging from five days to twenty years. The combination of federal prosecution, employer hostility, and vigilante violence broke the IWW as a national organization.

The Courts and Civil Liberties

The federal courts provided almost no protection for civil liberties during the war. In Schenck v. United States (1919), the Supreme Court unanimously upheld the Espionage Act as applied to Charles Schenck, a Socialist Party official who had mailed leaflets to drafted men urging resistance to conscription. Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes, writing for the Court, articulated the "clear and present danger" test: speech could be restricted when it presented a clear and present danger of bringing about "the substantive evils that Congress has a right to prevent." Holmes used the famous analogy that the First Amendment would not protect a man who falsely shouted "Fire!" in a crowded theater.

Holmes had second thoughts. A few months later, in Abrams v. United States (1919), he dissented — along with Justice Louis Brandeis — when the Court upheld the conviction of Russian Jewish immigrants who had thrown leaflets from a building criticizing American intervention in Russia. Holmes argued that the "surreptitious publishing of a silly leaflet by an unknown man" posed no danger the government was entitled to suppress. The Abrams dissent, with its argument for a "free trade in ideas" and the "competition of the market," became a foundational text of American free speech jurisprudence, even as the majority upheld the conviction. The wartime suppression of civil liberties catalyzed the formation of the American Civil Liberties Union (ACLU) in 1920, created by Roger Baldwin and others as a direct response to wartime repression.

Constitutional Connection

The First Amendment and Wartime Dissent

The First Amendment to the Constitution states that "Congress shall make no law… abridging the freedom of speech, or of the press." Yet Congress did exactly that with the Espionage Act of 1917 and the Sedition Act of 1918, and the Supreme Court upheld those laws. The wartime cases — Schenck, Frohwerk, Debs, and Abrams — are foundational texts in the history of American constitutional law, not because they protected free speech, but because they failed to do so in ways that subsequent generations recognized as wrong.

The "clear and present danger" test articulated in Schenck was initially quite permissive of government restriction. Over time, the courts significantly strengthened First Amendment protections, requiring that speech pose a much more immediate and likely danger before it could be restricted. In Brandenburg v. Ohio (1969), the Supreme Court held that speech could only be restricted if directed to inciting imminent lawless action and likely to produce such action — a far higher standard than the World War I-era test.

The World War I civil liberties crisis illustrates a recurring constitutional tension: democratic governments that claim to protect freedom sometimes suppress it most aggressively in the name of protecting it. Students should consider why the courts failed to protect civil liberties in 1917–1918, what changed in subsequent decades, and what the limits of First Amendment protection remain today.

Section 37.6

The Great Migration and Wartime Labor

Origins and Causes of the Great Migration

The Great Migration — the large-scale movement of African Americans from the rural South to northern cities — is one of the most transformative demographic shifts in American history. Between approximately 1910 and 1970, approximately six million Black Americans left the South for northern and western cities. The first great phase of this migration, from roughly 1916 to 1930, was closely tied to World War I, though its roots ran much deeper.

The conditions that pushed African Americans out of the South had been building for decades. After the collapse of Reconstruction in the 1870s, Black southerners lived under a system of legal segregation (Jim Crow), systematic disfranchisement, debt peonage and sharecropping that kept many in effective economic bondage, and the ever-present threat of racial violence. Lynching was not an exceptional event in the South; it was a sustained system of terror that claimed thousands of Black lives between Reconstruction and World War II. In 1916 alone, more than 50 Black Americans were lynched. For many families, leaving the South was not primarily an economic calculation — it was a survival strategy.

The war created powerful new incentives to go north. European immigration, which had supplied northern industries with a large share of their unskilled labor, was sharply curtailed by the war. At the same time, war production was generating unprecedented demand for labor in steel mills, meatpacking plants, automobile factories, munitions works, and railroads. Northern employers who had previously refused to hire Black workers now actively recruited in the South. The Chicago Defender, the nation's most widely read Black newspaper, ran editorials urging Black southerners to come north and published accounts of wages and opportunities available in northern cities.

Between 1916 and 1920, approximately 500,000 African Americans moved from the South to northern cities. Chicago's Black population doubled. The Black population of Detroit increased by 600 percent. Pittsburgh, Cleveland, Philadelphia, and New York all saw dramatic growth in their Black communities. These migrants transformed American cities, American culture, American politics, and American religion.

Wartime Labor and the National War Labor Board

The demand for labor created by war production dramatically strengthened the position of workers — at least temporarily. The unemployment rate fell sharply as military mobilization created jobs and reduced the available labor supply. The federal government, through the National War Labor Board (created in 1918), mediated labor disputes and established some important principles: the right of workers to organize and bargain collectively, equal pay for women doing equal work, and an eight-hour workday in some industries. Samuel Gompers, the president of the American Federation of Labor (AFL), supported the war and cooperated with the government's mobilization, receiving in exchange some formal recognition and a seat at the table of wartime economic planning.

For African American workers, the wartime labor market was transformative but also bitterly limited. Black workers who moved north found wages far higher than they had earned in the South and access to better schools for their children. But they also faced discrimination in northern workplaces. Most industrial unions affiliated with the AFL excluded Black workers from membership, leaving them in weaker bargaining positions. Housing discrimination confined Black migrants to overcrowded and overpriced neighborhoods — the ghettos that would define urban America for generations. And white workers, fearing competition for jobs and neighborhoods, sometimes responded with hostility and violence.

Racial Violence and the Red Summer

The wartime optimism that some Black Americans felt — the sense that service to the nation in its hour of need would be rewarded with greater equality — was brutally shattered in the aftermath of the war. The summer of 1919, known as the Red Summer, saw racial violence erupt in more than three dozen American cities. In Chicago, a race riot that began when a Black teenager swimming in Lake Michigan drifted into the "white" section killed 38 people and injured more than 500. In Washington, D.C., white veterans attacked Black neighborhoods for days. In Elaine, Arkansas, a labor dispute became the pretext for a massacre of Black sharecroppers.

The Red Summer was not simply random violence. It was white resistance — often led by veterans who had returned from fighting for democracy abroad — to the social and economic changes that Black migration, Black assertiveness, and Black economic success had produced. The crisis shattered the hope that wartime service had earned Black equality, and it contributed to the radicalization of Black political thought in the 1920s — toward Marcus Garvey's Black nationalism, the Harlem Renaissance, and the long-building infrastructure of civil rights activism that would eventually produce the movements of the 1950s and 1960s.

Section 37.7

Women's Suffrage and the Nineteenth Amendment

Women's Roles During the War

American women's experience of World War I was multidimensional. On the home front, women took on jobs in factories, offices, transportation, and agriculture that had previously been considered men's work. Women drove streetcars and trolleys in cities across the country. They worked in munitions plants and steel mills. Approximately 25,000 women served with the military in non-combat capacities — as nurses, clerical workers, telephone operators (the "Hello Girls" of the Army Signal Corps), and in other support roles. Some served overseas in France.

Women also organized the home front in ways essential to the war effort. The Food Administration's conservation campaigns were implemented largely through women's domestic labor — women made dietary changes, preserved food, planted victory gardens, and organized community conservation efforts. The war gave women new visibility, new skills, and new claims to public recognition.

The Suffrage Movement and the War

The women's suffrage movement had been building for decades before the war. The National American Woman Suffrage Association (NAWSA), led by Carrie Chapman Catt, had pursued a state-by-state strategy that had won voting rights in several western states. The more radical National Woman's Party (NWP), led by Alice Paul, argued that state-by-state progress was too slow and demanded a federal constitutional amendment. The NWP used confrontational tactics — including picketing the White House and hunger strikes when jailed — that outraged many but kept the issue in public view.

The war created new political dynamics that the suffrage movement skillfully exploited. NAWSA officially supported the war and organized women's contributions to the home front. Catt argued that this service demonstrated women's fitness for full citizenship and created a political debt that Wilson and Congress were obligated to repay. The NWP took a more confrontational approach, continuing to picket the White House even after the declaration of war, holding banners that quoted Wilson's own words about democracy and freedom — a tactic he found infuriating. When picketers were arrested and imprisoned, and when imprisoned women went on hunger strikes and were force-fed, the resulting publicity embarrassed the administration and drew attention to the suffrage cause.

Wilson and the Nineteenth Amendment

Wilson's position on women's suffrage evolved slowly under this combined pressure. He had long considered suffrage a state rather than federal issue. But by 1918, he had come to support a constitutional amendment. He addressed the Senate in September 1918, urging passage, explicitly connecting it to the war: "We have made partners of the women in this war; shall we admit them only to a partnership of suffering and sacrifice and toil and not to a partnership of privilege and right?" The Senate nonetheless failed to pass the amendment that year.

The amendment finally passed Congress in June 1919, after the war had ended. The ratification battle was fierce, particularly in southern states, where opposition was tied to fears that Black women voting would challenge racial segregation and white supremacy. Tennessee became the decisive state, ratifying on August 18, 1920, by a margin of one vote — provided by twenty-four-year-old Harry Burn, who changed his vote after receiving a letter from his mother urging him to "be a good boy." The Nineteenth Amendment was certified on August 26, 1920, giving approximately twenty-six million American women the right to vote.

The achievement was profound and incomplete. The Nineteenth Amendment applied formally to all women, but Black women across the South were effectively disfranchised by the same combination of violence, economic coercion, and legal manipulation used against Black men. Indigenous women would not be fully enfranchised as citizens until 1924, and faced state-level barriers long after. Chinese American women remained ineligible for naturalization until 1943. The formal right to vote and the full exercise of that right were not the same thing.

Section 37.8

Wilson's Fourteen Points

The Speech and Its Context

On January 8, 1918, with the war still raging and Germany not yet defeated, Woodrow Wilson appeared before a joint session of Congress and delivered what became one of the most ambitious statements of American foreign policy in the nation's history. The Fourteen Points speech laid out Wilson's vision for what the peace settlement should achieve — not just ending the current war, but restructuring the entire international order to prevent future wars.

The speech was delivered at a specific strategic moment. Russia had just experienced its Bolshevik Revolution, and the new Soviet government was publishing secret Allied war treaties that revealed the cynical territorial deals Britain, France, and Italy had made among themselves. These revelations were politically damaging to the Allied cause. Wilson's Fourteen Points were partly a response — an American counter-offer of a more idealistic peace that could sustain Allied morale, appeal to German liberals who might press for a negotiated settlement, and counter Soviet propaganda with a democratic vision of the postwar world.

The Points Themselves

The Fourteen Points addressed both specific territorial issues and broader principles of international order. The specific points included: evacuation of German forces from Russia, restoration of Belgium, return of Alsace-Lorraine to France, adjustments to Italian borders along ethnic lines, autonomy for the peoples of Austria-Hungary, an independent Poland with access to the sea, and an independent Turkish state with freedom of the Straits.

The broader principles were in many ways more important. Wilson called for open diplomacy — no more secret treaties. He called for freedom of the seas for all nations, removal of trade barriers, and arms reduction. And in the Fourteenth Point, the capstone of the speech, he called for "a general association of nations… for the purpose of affording mutual guarantees of political independence and territorial integrity to great and small states alike" — what became the League of Nations.

The principle of self-determination ran through many of the specific points: the idea that peoples should be able to determine their own political fate, that borders should align with ethnic and linguistic communities rather than being imposed by imperial conquest. This was a genuinely radical idea in a world still largely organized by empires — Ottoman, British, French, German, and Austro-Hungarian. It inspired subject peoples across Asia and Africa as well as Europe, though Wilson had primarily European peoples in mind and showed little concern for the independence of peoples colonized by Britain and France.

Reception and Expectations

The Fourteen Points speech had enormous impact. In Europe, subject peoples under German and Austro-Hungarian rule heard in it a promise of liberation. In Germany, it helped persuade some civilian leaders that a peace based on Wilson's principles might be preferable to continued fighting. When Germany sought the armistice in October–November 1918, it did so explicitly on the basis of the Fourteen Points, expecting Wilson's principles to guide the peace settlement.

Among American allies, the response was more guarded. British and French leaders admired the speech's effectiveness as war propaganda, but they had their own war aims — territorial gains, colonial spoils, reparations, security guarantees — that did not always align with Wilsonian idealism. French Premier Georges Clemenceau reportedly quipped that God had given humanity the Ten Commandments, and mankind had broken them; now Wilson arrived with Fourteen Points. The Europeans had fought four years of catastrophic war and lost millions of men; they wanted a peace that punished Germany and protected themselves against future attack.

Section 37.9

Treaty of Versailles and Senate Rejection

The Paris Peace Conference

When the armistice ended the fighting on November 11, 1918, Wilson made the decision — unprecedented for a sitting American president — to travel to Paris personally and lead the American delegation at the peace conference. He arrived in Europe to extraordinary popular acclaim. Crowds in Paris, London, and Rome cheered him as a messiah who would remake the world. The enthusiasm was genuine and also put enormous political pressure on Wilson: he had raised hopes he might not be able to fulfill.

The Paris Peace Conference began in January 1919. The major decisions were made by the "Big Four": Wilson, British Prime Minister David Lloyd George, French Premier Georges Clemenceau, and Italian Prime Minister Vittorio Orlando. Germany and its allies were excluded from the negotiations — they would be presented with a finished treaty and required to sign it. This "dictated peace" violated the spirit of Wilson's promises about open diplomacy and self-determination.

The Treaty's Terms

The Treaty of Versailles required Germany to accept full moral and legal responsibility for causing the war — the notorious "war guilt clause" (Article 231). It stripped Germany of substantial territory: Alsace-Lorraine to France, the Polish Corridor to the new state of Poland, and colonies overseas redistributed to the Allies as "mandates." Germany's army was reduced to 100,000 men, its navy largely eliminated, and it was forbidden to build submarines or aircraft. Most consequentially, Germany was required to pay reparations — war damages eventually set at 132 billion gold marks.

The British economist John Maynard Keynes, who had served with the British delegation, resigned in protest and published The Economic Consequences of the Peace (1919), arguing that the reparations were economically ruinous, would destabilize Germany, and would ultimately produce another war. His analysis proved prescient, though economic historians continue to debate whether the terms were as severe as he claimed. What is clear is that the combination of territorial loss, military humiliation, forced admission of war guilt, and economic burden created deep resentment in Germany — resentment that would be exploited by Adolf Hitler and the Nazi Party in the following decade.

Wilson accepted these terms because he believed the League of Nations — which the treaty also established — would correct the peace's injustices over time. The League was Wilson's greatest achievement at Paris: an international organization with the power to settle disputes peacefully, apply collective economic and military pressure against aggressors, and review and revise the peace settlements if they proved unjust.

Senate Opposition and Wilson's Collapse

The treaty faced a divided reception when Wilson returned to the United States. Republicans, who had won control of the Senate in the 1918 midterm elections, were not enthusiastic about Wilson's League or his treaty. Some were "irreconcilables" — led by Senator William Borah of Idaho and Senator Hiram Johnson of California — who opposed any treaty that bound the United States to collective security obligations. A larger group were "reservationists" led by Senator Henry Cabot Lodge of Massachusetts, chairman of the Senate Foreign Relations Committee. Lodge had substantive concerns — particularly about Article X of the League Covenant, which committed member nations to protect each other's territorial integrity against external aggression. He worried this was an open-ended military commitment that bypassed Congress's constitutional authority to declare war. Lodge proposed attaching reservations to the treaty — conditions that would protect American sovereignty and congressional prerogatives.

Wilson refused to accept any significant reservations. In a decision that most historians consider a disastrous political miscalculation, he treated any modification of the treaty as unacceptable — even reservations that might have secured enough votes for ratification. He decided to take his case directly to the American people, embarking on a national speaking tour, delivering forty speeches in twenty-two days across the western United States, covering more than 8,000 miles by train. In Pueblo, Colorado, on September 25, 1919, he gave what was effectively his last major public address and collapsed. He was brought back to Washington, where on October 2, 1919, he suffered a severe stroke that partially paralyzed him and left him cognitively impaired for the remainder of his presidency.

The Senate voted on the treaty twice — in November 1919 and again in March 1920. Both times, it failed to achieve the two-thirds majority required for ratification. The treaty with Lodge's reservations got more votes than the unamended treaty in November, suggesting that Wilson's refusal to compromise was decisive. In March 1920, Wilson instructed Democratic senators to vote against the treaty unless it was passed without reservations; most obeyed, and the treaty failed again. The United States never joined the League of Nations.

Consequences of American Absence

The United States' refusal to join the League had profound consequences. The League survived without the United States but was permanently weakened. Without American participation and power, it lacked the authority and resources to enforce its decisions effectively. When crises came — Japan's invasion of Manchuria in 1931, Italy's invasion of Ethiopia in 1935, Germany's remilitarization and eventual aggression — the League proved unable to mount effective collective responses. The failure of the Versailles settlement and the weakness of the League contributed directly to the conditions that produced World War II.

Historical Debate

Was Wilson Right to Refuse Compromise on the Treaty?

The question: Woodrow Wilson refused to accept Lodge's reservations to the Treaty of Versailles, effectively killing American participation in the League of Nations. Was this decision justified, or was it a catastrophic political failure?

Interpretation A: Wilson was right to hold firm. Some historians argue that Wilson correctly identified the reservations — particularly Lodge's reservation on Article X — as gutting the core of the League's collective security mechanism. If the United States could unilaterally decide not to honor its League obligations, the League would be meaningless. Moreover, the reservations were driven partly by Lodge's personal animosity toward Wilson and partisan Republican desire to deny Wilson a diplomatic triumph. From this perspective, Wilson was defending a principle of genuine international commitment against small-minded political opposition.
Interpretation B: Wilson's refusal was a disastrous miscalculation. Most historians conclude that Wilson's refusal to compromise was, whatever its principled basis, a catastrophic political mistake. Lodge's reservations — which most of America's own allies would have accepted — might have secured Senate ratification. A League that included the United States, even with reservations, would have been far stronger than the League that actually existed. Wilson's insistence on complete victory, or nothing, produced nothing — and arguably contributed to the conditions that led to World War II.

Evidence: The evidence includes the Senate vote tallies — showing more votes for the treaty with reservations than without — the texts of the reservations themselves, and the subsequent history of the League without American participation.

Why it matters: This debate raises fundamental questions about the relationship between idealism and pragmatism in foreign policy, the role of personality and illness in historical outcomes, and the long-term consequences of American decisions in 1919–1920.

Student task: Based on the evidence described above, which interpretation do you find more persuasive? What additional evidence would help you decide? Is this a question where the evidence can produce a definitive answer, or does it ultimately depend on judgments about values and priorities?

Section 37.10

Chapter Review and World War I Home Front Checkpoint

The preceding nine sections have traced the arc of American involvement in World War I from the outbreak of the conflict in 1914 through the Senate's rejection of the peace treaty in 1919 and the ratification of the Nineteenth Amendment in 1920. Before moving to the Chapter Summary, this section reviews the central themes.

Neutrality's failure: American neutrality was always partial and ultimately untenable. Economic ties to the Allies, German submarine warfare that directly threatened American lives and commerce, and Wilson's own moral and strategic convictions all pushed toward intervention. The Zimmermann Telegram was a dramatic catalyst, but it accelerated a process already well advanced.

Mobilization's scale: The United States mobilized on an extraordinary scale in a short period. The draft registered twenty-four million men. New federal agencies coordinated the entire national economy. The CPI reached hundreds of millions of Americans with its propaganda. The AEF grew from 128,000 men to more than four million in the space of a year.

Democracy's contradictions: The wartime suppression of civil liberties was severe and lasting in its effects. The Espionage and Sedition Acts sent hundreds of Americans to prison for speech. The anti-German campaign destroyed a community's public life. The courts provided almost no protection. These events created the civil liberties movement and forced a serious reckoning with the meaning of free speech in a democracy.

Social change: The war accelerated the Great Migration, transformed women's economic roles, and contributed to passage of the Nineteenth Amendment. It changed the demographic composition of northern cities and the political landscape of American race relations. It showed what women could do in the public sphere and created the political conditions for formal suffrage.

Peace's failure: The Treaty of Versailles was a deeply flawed document — harsh on Germany, partial in its application of self-determination, and structured in ways that created dangerous instabilities. Wilson's failure to secure American ratification — the result of political stubbornness, partisan opposition, and personal illness — left the League without the power it needed to enforce peace. The consequences would be catastrophic.

Multiple Perspectives

World War I and Its Meanings for Different Americans

African Americans: For many Black Americans, World War I presented a profound and bitter contradiction. W.E.B. Du Bois and other leaders argued that service would prove Black fitness for full citizenship. Hundreds of thousands of Black men served, many with distinction. But they returned home to find segregation intact, civil rights denied, and the Red Summer's violence waiting for them. The war exposed the depth of American racial hypocrisy: a nation that claimed to fight for democracy abroad maintained a system of racial subordination at home. The Harlem Renaissance of the 1920s drew on the anger, creativity, and assertiveness of a generation shaped by this contradiction.
German Americans: For German Americans — approximately eight million people — the war was a catastrophe for their public cultural identity. Before 1917, German Americans were among the most successful and culturally vigorous immigrant communities in the nation. German-language theater, newspapers, churches, and civic organizations flourished. The war ended all of that. German Americans faced surveillance, harassment, violence, and intense pressure to prove their loyalty by denouncing their own heritage. German-language newspapers were shut down. German instruction was banned in public schools in many states. The community survived, but its public German identity largely did not.
Anti-war activists and socialists: For the Socialist Party, labor organizers, pacifists, and civil libertarians who opposed the war, it was a period of repression unlike anything most Americans had experienced. Hundreds went to prison. Newspapers were shut down. Meetings were raided. The IWW was effectively destroyed. The sense that a democratic government had turned the apparatus of the state against political opponents — on the basis of speech, not action — created a lasting suspicion of wartime patriotism and government power that shaped American left and labor politics for decades.
Women suffragists: For women's rights activists, the war produced a complex mix of opportunity and exploitation. Women entered the workforce in unprecedented numbers. Suffrage leaders successfully argued that women's service proved their fitness for full citizenship. The Nineteenth Amendment was ratified in 1920. But women workers faced discrimination, lower wages than men, and the expectation that they would return to domestic roles after the war. The war advanced women's formal political rights while leaving many aspects of gender inequality in place.
Farmers and rural communities: For many American farmers, the war brought initially high prices for agricultural goods — wheat, cotton, corn, and livestock all sold at a premium. Farmers expanded production, borrowed money to buy more land and equipment, and experienced what seemed like prosperity. But the postwar collapse of agricultural prices left many deeply in debt, beginning a farm crisis that would eventually contribute to the broader economic collapse of the late 1920s.
Map Study

The Geographic Dimensions of World War I and the Home Front

What to look for: A map of the United States during World War I would reveal several important geographic patterns. Military training camps were established across the country — more than thirty-two in all — concentrating in the South (where year-round training was feasible) and the West (where land was available). The industrial centers of the Northeast and Midwest — Chicago, Pittsburgh, Cleveland, Detroit, Philadelphia, and New York — were the heart of war production, drawing workers from across the country and fueling the Great Migration northward.

On the Western Front, look for the geography of trench warfare: the nearly static line from the Belgian coast to Switzerland, the positions of major battles where American troops fought (the Meuse-Argonne in northeastern France, Belleau Wood near Paris, Cantigny in Picardy). The dense woodland of the Argonne Forest made supply and movement difficult and gave defenders advantages that cost American forces dearly.

The geography of German submarine warfare shows the Atlantic shipping lanes as contested space: routes from American ports to Britain passed through waters Germany declared a war zone. The Zimmermann Telegram proposed drawing Mexico — on America's southern border — into the conflict, adding a geographic dimension that made the war seem immediately threatening to American territorial security.

Primary Source Window

Wilson's War Message to Congress (April 2, 1917)

Creator: President Woodrow Wilson · Date: April 2, 1917 · Type: Presidential address to Congress; public political speech

Context: On April 2, 1917, President Woodrow Wilson appeared before a joint session of Congress to request a declaration of war against Germany. The speech is one of the most consequential in American history, combining specific grievances about German submarine warfare with a sweeping vision of American purpose in the world.

Wilson told Congress that it was "a fearful thing to lead this great peaceful people into war, into the most disastrous and dismal of all wars." Yet he insisted that "the right is more precious than peace" and that the United States would fight for democracy, for the rights of small nations, and for a universal order of right by a concert of free peoples that would at last make the world safe and free. He dedicated "everything that we are and everything that we have" to this task.

Analysis: Wilson's speech accomplished several things simultaneously. It provided the specific legal and diplomatic justification for war — Germany's violation of neutral rights through unrestricted submarine warfare — while elevating the conflict to a moral crusade for democracy and freedom. This rhetorical move was politically powerful: it transformed a war over shipping lanes and economic interests into a war over universal values, making opposition more difficult and inspiring more enthusiastic support.

The speech's limits are also evident. Wilson declared that the world must be made safe for democracy, but said nothing about making America safe for democracy for all its citizens. African Americans listening could note that the democracy Wilson was fighting for abroad was systematically denied to them at home through segregation, disfranchisement, and racial violence. Women could note that the democracy Wilson championed internationally was still denied to them domestically.

Questions for students:

  1. What specific reasons does Wilson give for asking Congress to declare war? What broader purposes does he claim for American involvement?
  2. Who was Wilson's intended audience for this speech? How might different groups of Americans — German Americans, African Americans, women, socialists — have heard his words differently?
  3. What does the speech reveal about Wilson's understanding of American identity and purpose?
  4. What can historians learn from this speech that they cannot learn from looking only at the Senate vote authorizing war? What aspects of the decision does it illuminate, and what does it leave unexplained?
Cause and Effect

The United States Enters World War I

Long-Term Causes

European alliance systems divided the continent into rival armed camps; industrial technology made modern warfare uniquely destructive; American economic integration into the global economy created material interests in Allied victory; British propaganda shaped American understanding of the war; American cultural ties with Britain and France created sympathetic alignment.

Short-Term Causes

German unrestricted submarine warfare killed American citizens; the Lusitania sinking demonstrated mass civilian casualties; Germany's February 1917 resumption of unrestricted submarine warfare violated the Sussex Pledge; the Zimmermann Telegram threatened American territorial integrity; the Russian Revolution made the Allied coalition more clearly democratic.

The Event

On April 6, 1917, Congress authorized a declaration of war against Germany, with Wilson's signature. The United States entered the First World War with the Selective Service Act immediately following.

Long-Term Effects

American intervention contributed to Allied victory; Senate rejected Treaty of Versailles; League of Nations fatally weakened; Great Migration transformed northern cities; Nineteenth Amendment ratified; Espionage Act created the civil liberties movement; flawed peace settlement contributed to World War II.

Historical complexity: American entry into World War I cannot be explained by any single cause. It was the product of German strategic decisions, British propaganda, American economic interests, Wilson's moral vision, and specific diplomatic failures over three years. A different German policy on submarines might have maintained American neutrality long enough to change the war's outcome.

Continuity and Change Over Time

American Civil Liberties in Wartime: World War I to the Present

What Changed During World War I

  • Federal government prosecuted hundreds of citizens for speech opposing a war — not for spying or violence, but for expressing political opinions
  • CPI created a national propaganda apparatus that had not previously existed
  • Anti-German sentiment produced discrimination and violence against an ethnic community at a scale not seen before
  • Postal service actively censored newspapers and periodicals deemed subversive
  • Courts upheld these measures almost without exception, establishing the "clear and present danger" test

What Stayed the Same

  • The First Amendment remained in the Constitution, unchanged
  • The tradition of political dissent — at great personal cost — continued to exist
  • Some voices continued to speak against the war: La Follette in the Senate, Debs in public, Baldwin among civil libertarians
  • The tradition of principled dissent, though suppressed, was not extinguished

The Recurring Pattern

The World War I civil liberties crisis established a pattern that would recur: wartime government expansion, suppression of dissent, gradual recovery of civil liberties after the emergency, and eventual recognition that some wartime restrictions had gone too far. Understanding this pattern helps students recognize it in later periods — Japanese American incarceration in World War II, McCarthyism in the early Cold War, and post-September 11 restrictions — and evaluate the constitutional and democratic values at stake.

Everyday Life and Social History: The War in Small-Town America

The First World War touched the lives of ordinary Americans in ways large and small, familiar and strange. In small towns across the country — in Iowa, Georgia, Pennsylvania, and Oregon — the war announced itself first on bulletin boards at the post office and on the front pages of local newspapers: lists of men who had registered for the draft, then lists of men who had been called, then casualty lists that began arriving in 1917 and continued through 1918.

Families planted victory gardens in their back yards, encouraged by the Food Administration's posters and pamphlets. Women canned vegetables, observed Meatless Tuesdays, and replaced white wheat flour in their baking with corn flour or barley. Coal was rationed; families kept the thermostat lower and wore more layers indoors. Communities organized scrap metal drives, cloth drives, and Liberty Bond campaigns. Local newspapers ran columns from soldiers at the front — brief, censored accounts of life in France that mixed bravado with homesickness.

Young men went away to training camps in numbers that small towns had never before experienced. Some camps were far from home; a boy from rural Tennessee might find himself at Camp Upton in New York, or Camp Travis in Texas, surrounded by men from backgrounds and places utterly foreign to him. The training camps were also, notoriously, incubators for the influenza pandemic of 1918, which killed more people than the war's weapons. Soldiers died in their barracks in alarming numbers; the pandemic reached the civilian population and eventually killed approximately 675,000 Americans — more than ten times the nation's combat deaths.

In cities like Chicago and Detroit, the arrival of African American migrants from the South transformed neighborhoods and created new cultural life. Blues and jazz music, which had developed in New Orleans and the Mississippi Delta, traveled north with migrants and began reaching white audiences. The Harlem Renaissance of the 1920s would not have been possible without the demographic concentration of Black talent, ambition, and audience that the Great Migration created in northern cities.

Women's daily lives changed significantly on the home front. With men in the military, women took jobs as streetcar conductors, postal workers, bank tellers, and factory workers. For many women — particularly white, middle-class women — this was their first experience of paid employment outside the home. The sense of public contribution and expanded capability that wartime work provided was one source of the renewed energy behind the suffrage campaign. Churches played a major role as centers of bond drives, comfort kits for soldiers, and community support for military families. Some pacifist denominations — Quakers, Mennonites, Brethren — maintained anti-war positions at significant social cost to their communities.

The end of the war in November 1918 brought celebrations, parades, and church bells ringing across the country. And then came the difficult return: soldiers coming home with physical wounds, psychological trauma (then called "shell shock," later recognized as post-traumatic stress disorder), and expectations of a country transformed. African American veterans returned with a new assertiveness — a sense that their sacrifice had earned equality — and found instead the violence of the Red Summer. Women who had worked in factories during the war were expected, and often pressured, to return to domestic roles. The country moved on, unevenly and incompletely, from its brief and transformative immersion in world war.

Chapter Summary

The United States entered the First World War in April 1917 after nearly three years of contested neutrality. The decision was shaped by multiple converging forces: German submarine warfare that threatened American lives and commerce, economic ties that bound the United States to Allied success, the Zimmermann Telegram's revelation that Germany was attempting to recruit Mexico as an ally against the United States, and Woodrow Wilson's own moral vision of American leadership in creating a more democratic world order. The declaration of war was not inevitable — it required specific decisions by specific leaders at specific moments — but the structural conditions that made it likely had been building since 1914.

The American war effort was remarkable in its speed and scale. The Selective Service Act raised an army of nearly four million men in a matter of months. New federal agencies coordinated industrial production, food distribution, fuel consumption, and transportation in ways that dramatically expanded the reach of the federal government. The Committee on Public Information conducted the most ambitious propaganda campaign in American history, reaching hundreds of millions of Americans with pro-war messaging and anti-German imagery. The Liberty Bond campaigns raised billions of dollars from ordinary Americans, combining patriotic appeal with social pressure.

The American Expeditionary Forces arrived in France under General John Pershing and fought their most significant battles in 1918. The Meuse-Argonne Offensive of September–November 1918, involving 1.2 million American soldiers, was the largest American military operation in history to that point and contributed significantly to the final Allied advance that led to the German armistice of November 11, 1918. Approximately 117,000 American soldiers died in combat; influenza killed another 50,000 or more.

The war's effects on American society were profound and lasting. The Espionage Act of 1917 and the Sedition Act of 1918 suppressed political dissent with a severity not seen before or since in American history. Hundreds of Americans were imprisoned for speech — opposing the war, criticizing the draft, supporting socialism or labor organizing. The Supreme Court provided almost no protection, upholding the laws in a series of decisions that established the "clear and present danger" test for speech restrictions. The backlash against this repression helped create the American Civil Liberties Union and stimulated decades of legal and political work to strengthen free speech protections.

The Great Migration accelerated dramatically during the war years, as African Americans left the rural South for northern industrial cities where wartime production created unprecedented labor demand. The migration transformed Chicago, Detroit, Cleveland, Pittsburgh, and New York, building the urban Black communities that would produce the Harlem Renaissance, new forms of African American politics, and ultimately the organizational infrastructure of the civil rights movement. But the war's promise of democratic progress for Black Americans was betrayed in the postwar period. The Red Summer of 1919 — with racial violence in dozens of American cities — demonstrated that white America was not prepared to grant the equality that Black service had supposedly earned.

Women's roles changed significantly during the war. Women entered the workforce in unprecedented numbers, took on new public responsibilities, and participated in war mobilization as workers, organizers, and advocates. The suffrage movement exploited the political dynamics of wartime — the contradiction between fighting for democracy abroad while denying it to women at home — to secure the Nineteenth Amendment. Ratified in 1920, it gave women the formal right to vote, though Black and Indigenous women in much of the country remained disfranchised in practice for decades.

Wilson's Fourteen Points speech of January 1918 articulated an ambitious vision for the postwar world — open diplomacy, freedom of the seas, arms reduction, self-determination, and a League of Nations to settle future disputes peacefully. The Treaty of Versailles imposed harsh terms on Germany — territorial losses, military restrictions, reparations, and the war guilt clause — that fell far short of Wilsonian idealism, even as it created the League of Nations Wilson had envisioned. Wilson's attempt to win Senate ratification of the treaty failed, largely because of his own inflexibility in refusing to accept modifications proposed by the reservationist faction. The Senate rejected the treaty twice. The United States never joined the League of Nations, leaving that organization permanently weakened. Without American participation, the League proved unable to prevent the aggression of the 1930s. The failure of Versailles and the absence of American leadership in international institutions contributed directly to the conditions that produced World War II.

Key Terms

Neutrality
The policy of not taking sides in a conflict between other nations; what the United States officially maintained from 1914 to 1917, despite powerful economic and cultural ties to the Allies.
Unrestricted submarine warfare
Germany's policy of attacking any ship, including neutral vessels, in declared war zones without warning; its resumption in February 1917 was a primary cause of American entry into the war.
Lusitania
British ocean liner sunk by a German U-boat on May 7, 1915, killing nearly 1,200 people including 128 Americans; the sinking intensified American debate about neutrality.
Zimmermann Telegram
Coded German diplomatic message (January 1917) proposing a German-Mexican military alliance against the United States; its publication in March 1917 helped push American public opinion toward war.
Selective Service Act (1917)
Law establishing military conscription for World War I; registered approximately 24 million men, of whom about 2.8 million were drafted into service.
American Expeditionary Forces (AEF)
The approximately 2 million U.S. soldiers sent to fight in France under General John J. Pershing; fought major battles including the Meuse-Argonne Offensive of 1918.
Committee on Public Information (CPI)
Federal agency created in 1917 and led by George Creel to manage wartime propaganda and build public support for the war through posters, films, and speakers.
Four-Minute Men
Network of approximately 75,000 volunteer speakers organized by the CPI to deliver short pro-war speeches in public venues; reached an estimated 400 million audience members.
Liberty Bonds
Government bonds sold to the public to finance the war; buying them was presented as a patriotic duty, and social pressure made refusal difficult in many communities.
Espionage Act (1917)
Federal law making it illegal to interfere with military recruitment, cause insubordination in the military, or obstruct the draft; used extensively against anti-war speech and publications.
Sedition Act (1918)
Amendment to the Espionage Act criminalizing "disloyal, profane, scurrilous, or abusive language" about the government, flag, military, or war effort; sent hundreds to prison.
Schenck v. United States (1919)
Supreme Court case upholding the Espionage Act; Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes articulated the "clear and present danger" standard for restricting speech.
Clear and present danger
Legal standard from Schenck holding that speech could be restricted if it posed a clear and present danger of causing harm Congress could prevent; later significantly strengthened by the courts.
Great Migration
The large-scale movement of African Americans from the rural South to northern industrial cities, accelerating significantly during World War I and transforming American cities and culture.
Red Summer (1919)
Period of intense racial violence in 1919, including riots in dozens of American cities, when white mobs attacked Black communities in response to postwar Black assertiveness.
Fourteen Points
Wilson's January 1918 statement of American war aims and a vision for postwar international order, including open diplomacy, self-determination, and a League of Nations.
Self-determination
The principle that peoples should be able to determine their own political status and form of government; a central concept in Wilson's Fourteen Points, applied primarily to European peoples.
League of Nations
International organization proposed by Wilson and created by the Treaty of Versailles to settle future disputes peacefully; the United States Senate refused to ratify the treaty and the U.S. never joined.
Treaty of Versailles (1919)
Peace treaty ending World War I; imposed reparations, territorial losses, military restrictions, and the war guilt clause on Germany and established the League of Nations.
War guilt clause
Article 231 of the Treaty of Versailles requiring Germany to accept full responsibility for causing the war; deeply resented in Germany and exploited by later nationalists including Hitler.
Reparations
Payments required of Germany under the Treaty of Versailles to compensate Allied nations for war damages; eventually set at 132 billion gold marks, creating lasting economic and political instability.
Reservationists
Senate faction led by Henry Cabot Lodge that supported ratification of the Treaty of Versailles with conditions attached; Wilson's refusal to accept their conditions helped doom ratification.
Nineteenth Amendment (1920)
Constitutional amendment ratifying women's right to vote; ratified on August 26, 1920, giving approximately twenty-six million American women formal voting rights.

Major People and Places

Identification & Significance

Woodrow Wilson (1856–1924)
Twenty-eighth president; led the United States into World War I, articulated the Fourteen Points, fought for the League of Nations, and suffered a disabling stroke while campaigning for the treaty. His moralistic internationalism left a lasting imprint on American foreign policy.
Henry Cabot Lodge (1850–1924)
Republican senator from Massachusetts and Senate Foreign Relations Committee chairman; Wilson's chief opponent in the treaty fight; led the reservationist faction. His opposition was both principled and personal — the two men deeply disliked each other.
Eugene V. Debs (1855–1926)
Socialist leader sentenced to ten years in prison under the Espionage Act for opposing the war; ran for president from prison in 1920 and received nearly a million votes. His case became a landmark in the history of civil liberties and free speech.
George Creel (1876–1953)
Journalist who led the Committee on Public Information, Wilson's wartime propaganda agency; helped pioneer techniques of mass persuasion that influenced public relations and advertising for decades.
John J. Pershing (1860–1948)
Commander of the American Expeditionary Forces in France; insisted on maintaining the AEF as an independent force rather than integrating with depleted Allied units, a politically important but militarily costly decision.
Carrie Chapman Catt (1859–1947)
Leader of NAWSA; led the successful campaign for the Nineteenth Amendment by linking women's wartime service to the case for full citizenship and lobbying Wilson directly and persistently.
Alice Paul (1885–1977)
Leader of the National Woman's Party; used confrontational tactics including White House pickets and hunger strikes to pressure for the Nineteenth Amendment. Her methods were controversial but effective in keeping suffrage in public view.
W.E.B. Du Bois (1868–1963)
African American intellectual and editor of The Crisis; controversially supported Black participation in the war as a path toward equality, then documented the betrayal of that hope in the Red Summer and its aftermath.
Robert La Follette (1855–1925)
Progressive Republican senator from Wisconsin who voted against the declaration of war and remained a leading critic of wartime repression; his stance cost him politically but made him a hero to anti-war progressives.
Georges Clemenceau (1841–1929)
French Prime Minister and principal architect of the harsh terms in the Treaty of Versailles; prioritized French security and German punishment over Wilsonian idealism, reflecting France's catastrophic wartime losses.
Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr. (1841–1935)
Supreme Court Justice who wrote the majority opinion in Schenck (articulating "clear and present danger") and then dissented in Abrams, developing the "free trade in ideas" argument that became foundational for First Amendment law.
The Harlem Hellfighters (369th Infantry Regiment)
African American military unit from New York that served under French command; spent 191 consecutive days in combat — more than any other American unit — and earned the French Croix de Guerre, becoming a symbol of Black military service and pride.
The Western Front
The system of trenches stretching from the Belgian coast to the Swiss border where the most intensive fighting of World War I occurred; site of millions of casualties in nearly four years of largely static warfare.
Versailles, France
Site of the Palace of Versailles where the peace treaty ending World War I was signed on June 28, 1919 — five years to the day after Franz Ferdinand's assassination triggered the war.

Chapter Reflection

World War I occupies a complicated place in American historical memory. Unlike World War II, which Americans have generally remembered as a "good war" fought against unambiguous evil, World War I has been harder to assimilate. It began with causes most Americans found confusing — alliance systems, imperial rivalries, a Serbian assassination — and produced a peace that many Americans quickly recognized as unjust. It sent young men into the mud of French trenches for reasons that seemed, in retrospect, increasingly difficult to justify. The disillusionment of the 1920s — the sense that the idealism of Wilson's war had been betrayed at Versailles and at home — became one of the defining cultural notes of the interwar period, expressed in the literature of the "Lost Generation" and in the political withdrawal that historians call isolationism.

Yet the war's significance cannot be reduced to its failures. The American entry in 1917 was decisive for Allied victory. Wilson's Fourteen Points and the League of Nations, however imperfectly realized, introduced ideas — collective security, self-determination, open diplomacy, international law — that would shape the post–World War II international order. The United Nations, the International Monetary Fund, the World Bank, NATO, and the other institutions of the postwar world owe something to the Wilsonian vision, even if they were designed partly to correct the mistakes of Versailles. The American experience in World War I demonstrated that the United States could mobilize enormous resources rapidly and project military power across the Atlantic — a capacity that would prove decisive in World War II.

For American domestic history, the war's significance is equally complex. It expanded federal government power in ways that were largely temporary during the war but established precedents drawn on again during the New Deal and World War II. It demonstrated the power of mass media and organized propaganda to shape public opinion at a national scale. It suppressed civil liberties in ways that stimulated a lasting civil liberties movement. It accelerated the Great Migration and transformed American cities. It contributed to the passage of the Nineteenth Amendment. It shaped the landscape of American racial politics for generations.

The chapter's deepest lesson may be about the relationship between democratic ideals and democratic practice. Wilson proclaimed that the world must be made safe for democracy; his government simultaneously imprisoned Americans for exercising their democratic right to dissent. He promised a peace based on self-determination; the peace settlement denied self-determination to colonial peoples. He fought for the League of Nations; his own political rigidity helped defeat it. These contradictions — between the ideals America proclaimed and the realities America practiced — are not unique to World War I. They run through American history from its founding to the present. Understanding the gap between democratic promise and democratic reality, and understanding that the gap can be narrowed by sustained political effort, is one of the essential lessons that this chapter, and this textbook, seeks to teach.

Students who understand World War I will be better equipped to understand everything that came after it: the 1920s disillusionment, the Great Depression's political effects, the debates about intervention in the late 1930s, the design of postwar institutions after 1945, the Cold War's ideological dimensions, and the civil liberties tensions that recur whenever the nation faces a security crisis. The war was not simply an episode that began and ended. It was a turning point that reshaped the world the United States would inhabit for the rest of the twentieth century and beyond.

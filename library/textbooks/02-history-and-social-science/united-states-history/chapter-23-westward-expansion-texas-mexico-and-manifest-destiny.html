Westward Expansion, Texas, Mexico, and Manifest Destiny

United States History: Indigenous America, Republic, Conflict, and Change

Chapter Opening

"Our manifest destiny [is] to overspread the continent allotted by Providence for the free development of our yearly multiplying millions." — John L. O'Sullivan, 1845

In the summer of 1845, a New York newspaper editor named John L. O'Sullivan put a name to something many Americans already felt in their bones. Writing about the annexation of Texas, O'Sullivan declared that it was the United States' manifest destiny to overspread the continent allotted by Providence for the free development of the nation's yearly multiplying millions. The phrase spread quickly. Politicians adopted it. Newspapers reprinted it. Soldiers carried its logic into battle. For a broad swath of white Americans in the 1840s, continental expansion was not simply a policy choice or a military ambition. It was, they believed, a moral imperative written into the geography of North America itself — a God-given right and duty to extend the boundaries of the republic from the Atlantic to the Pacific Ocean.

But what appeared to many white Americans as destiny appeared to others as dispossession, conquest, and catastrophe. To the millions of Native people who inhabited the lands between the Missouri River and the Pacific Coast, westward expansion meant the violent seizure of homelands they had occupied and cultivated for centuries. To the Mexican citizens living in Texas, New Mexico, and California, it meant military invasion, political subordination, and the erasure of legal rights once guaranteed by treaty. To enslaved Black Americans and to free people of color watching from the eastern states, expansion raised an urgent and agonizing question: would the new territories be slave or free? And to the tens of thousands of Anglo-American settlers, California gold seekers, and emigrants who pushed west along wagon roads, expansion meant opportunity, danger, hardship, and the transformation of their own lives in ways few had fully anticipated.

The story of westward expansion in the 1840s and early 1850s is one of the most consequential periods in United States history. Within the span of roughly a decade, the United States acquired more than one million square miles of new territory — in Texas, in the Oregon country, and across the vast lands taken from Mexico at the end of the Mexican-American War. These acquisitions did not simply enlarge the map. They transformed the nation's economy, its demographic composition, its relationship to neighboring peoples and nations, and, most fatefully, its politics. Every new territory brought with it the burning question that the framers of the Constitution had refused to resolve definitively: would slavery expand, or would it be contained? That question, amplified by each new acquisition, would drive the United States steadily toward the catastrophic Civil War of the 1860s.

Understanding westward expansion requires more than tracing the movement of wagon trains and military columns across a map. It requires asking who set the terms of that expansion, who benefited, and who paid the price. It requires examining the ideology of Manifest Destiny not simply as a belief but as a historical construction — a way of framing conquest as providence, of transforming military aggression into righteous mission, and of erasing the claims of those who already lived on the land. It requires listening to voices that the dominant narrative of Manifest Destiny often tried to silence: the voices of Comanche leaders defending their territory, of Mexican ranchers dispossessed of their land grants, of Cherokee and Navajo peoples facing removal and violence, of enslaved people watching anxiously as new territories opened, of women who crossed the continent on foot and by wagon and built communities in unfamiliar places.

The geography of this chapter's story stretches from the cotton fields of east Texas to the pine forests of Oregon's Willamette Valley, from the gold-bearing streams of the Sierra Nevada to the pueblos of New Mexico, from the Great Plains to the Pacific coast. It spans a decade of dramatic military, diplomatic, and social transformation. By the time the chapter ends, the United States has grown from a nation hugging the eastern half of the continent to one that spans it entirely — a continental empire whose internal contradictions will define the decades to come.

Historians have long debated how to characterize this expansion. Was it the triumphant fulfillment of republican democracy, or an act of imperial aggression little different from the European colonialism Americans claimed to have left behind? Was Manifest Destiny a coherent ideology, or a convenient post-hoc justification for decisions driven by economic greed, political ambition, and racial arrogance? These are not merely academic questions. They go to the heart of what the United States was, what it became, and what it continues to struggle with in its relationship to Indigenous sovereignty, immigration, and the legacies of conquest.

This chapter traces the ideology of Manifest Destiny, the conflict over Texas, the settlement of Oregon, the Mexican-American War, the transformation of California by gold and migration, the dispossession of Native peoples across the West, and the way that every new territory reopened the deepening crisis over slavery. Together, these developments made the 1840s one of the most consequential and contested decades in the history of the United States.

  2. Trace the history of Anglo-American settlement in Texas, the Texas Revolution, and the diplomatic controversies surrounding Texas annexation.
  3. Analyze the causes, major campaigns, and consequences of the Mexican-American War, including its effects on Mexico, on Mexican Americans, and on debates over slavery in the United States.
  4. Describe the settlement of Oregon and the diplomatic resolution of the Oregon boundary dispute with Great Britain.
  5. Explain the causes and consequences of the California Gold Rush, including its effects on Native peoples, on California's demographic transformation, and on national politics.
  6. Identify the major Native nations of the West and trace how U.S. expansion disrupted, threatened, and dispossessed Indigenous communities across the region.
  7. Evaluate the significance of the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo, including its territorial provisions and its treatment of the rights of Mexican citizens in ceded lands.
  8. Connect the expansion of the 1840s to the growing sectional crisis over slavery, including the Wilmot Proviso, the concept of popular sovereignty, and the debates that led to the Compromise of 1850.
  9. Compare the experiences of different groups — Anglo-American settlers, Mexican citizens, Native peoples, enslaved people, and women — during the era of westward expansion.
  10. Assess how the legacy of conquest and expansion shaped American law, culture, demographics, and political conflict in the decades following the 1840s.

Historical Context

The westward expansion of the United States in the 1840s did not emerge in a vacuum. It built on decades of settler movement, Indigenous displacement, diplomatic maneuvering, and ideological development that stretched back to the founding of the republic and beyond. To understand the 1840s, students need to understand the forces that had been building for a generation.

By the early nineteenth century, the United States had already expanded dramatically westward from its original Atlantic seaboard. The Louisiana Purchase of 1803 had doubled the nation's territory, and the subsequent exploration of Lewis and Clark had brought back detailed knowledge of the trans-Mississippi West. The acquisition of Florida in 1819, the admission of new states from Kentucky and Tennessee through Illinois and Missouri, and the opening of overland trails to the Southwest had all pushed American settlement further west. But the 1840s represented a qualitative leap — a sudden, dramatic expansion that brought the United States to the Pacific coast and transformed the nation's sense of its own destiny.

Several long-term forces drove this expansion. The first was demographic. American population was growing rapidly, and land hunger drove thousands of families to seek cheap farmland beyond established settlement. The second was economic. The cotton economy of the Deep South was constantly seeking new fertile lands to replace exhausted soils; meanwhile, northern entrepreneurs imagined Pacific ports, transcontinental trade routes, and access to Asian markets. The third was ideological. Evangelical Protestant Christianity had long taught many white Americans that they were a chosen people, and by the 1840s this religious conviction had fused with a powerful national mythology of republican destiny. The fourth was political. The Democratic Party, dominant in the 1840s under Presidents John Tyler and James K. Polk, was aggressively expansionist. Polk, elected in 1844, ran explicitly on a platform of acquiring Texas and Oregon, and he pursued those goals relentlessly once in office.

But expansion did not occur in an empty landscape. The West was already inhabited by millions of people: Native nations with deep roots in specific places, ecological relationships, trade networks, and political systems; Spanish-speaking communities that had built ranching and agricultural economies under first Spanish and then Mexican governance; and mixed communities in borderlands regions where different peoples had long interacted, intermarried, and negotiated relationships. The expansion of the United States into these spaces required confronting, displacing, subordinating, or destroying these existing communities — a process that was violent, contested, and morally troubling even to many Americans at the time.

The international context also mattered enormously. Mexico, independent from Spain only since 1821, was a young and politically unstable republic struggling to consolidate its own governance over an enormous and diverse territory. Britain remained a rival power in North America, with competing claims to Oregon and strong commercial interests in Texas and California. The United States in the 1840s was asserting itself against both of these powers simultaneously — a bold and risky strategic gamble that shaped the decade's events at every turn.

Finally, the internal political context of the United States was defined by the deepening crisis over slavery. The Missouri Compromise of 1820 had drawn a geographic line between slave and free territory west of the Mississippi, but every new territorial acquisition potentially reopened that settlement. The Gag Rule controversy in Congress, the abolitionist movement's growing strength, the political conflicts over Texas (which would be a slave state if admitted), and the always-present economic interests of slaveholding planters in new western lands meant that expansion could never be a simple question of geography. It was always, also, a question about the future of slavery — and therefore about the future of the republic itself.

Section 23.1

Manifest Destiny and Expansionist Ideology

What Was Manifest Destiny?

When John L. O'Sullivan wrote in 1845 that it was the "manifest destiny" of the United States to spread across the continent, he was not inventing an idea so much as christening one that had been circulating for decades. The belief that the United States had a special, providential right to expand westward was deeply embedded in American culture long before it had a name. O'Sullivan's genius was to compress a complex web of religious conviction, racial ideology, nationalist ambition, and economic desire into a phrase that seemed to say everything all at once.

At its core, Manifest Destiny drew on three interlocking arguments. The first was religious. Many white Protestant Americans believed that God had chosen the United States as a special nation, a beacon of republican liberty and Christian civilization. The expansion of that nation westward was, in this view, part of a divine plan — the fulfillment of a providential mission rather than a human choice. This religious framing made expansion feel not only justified but obligatory. To fail to expand was to reject the purposes of God.

The second argument was racial. Manifest Destiny was inseparable from the racial ideology that ranked peoples hierarchically and placed Anglo-Saxons at the top. In the racial thinking of the mid-nineteenth century, white Americans of northern European descent were held to be uniquely capable of self-government, industry, and civilization. The lands of the West, in this view, were not inhabited by peoples with legitimate claims and functional societies; they were inhabited by "savage" races who were doomed to disappear before the advance of civilization. This argument was used to dismiss the claims of Native peoples, of Mexican citizens, and of anyone else who stood in the path of Anglo-American expansion. It was, in a fundamental sense, a justification for dispossession.

The third argument was republican-democratic. Many Americans genuinely believed that the expansion of the United States meant the expansion of republican government, free labor, and the rights of common white men. The West represented opportunity: cheap land, economic independence, the chance to build farms and communities free from the old hierarchies of the East. For ordinary settlers — the small farmers, the young couples, the families packed into wagons — the ideology of Manifest Destiny resonated because it gave a grand moral meaning to their personal ambitions. They were not just moving for land; they were advancing civilization itself.

The Racial Politics of Expansion

Historians have analyzed Manifest Destiny as a racial ideology because it was explicitly so. O'Sullivan and other expansionist writers routinely described Mexicans, Native peoples, and others as racially inferior and therefore incapable of making full use of the land or governing themselves effectively. This racial framing did crucial ideological work: it transformed the seizure of land from peoples who lived on it into something that could be described as natural, inevitable, and even beneficial.

Not everyone accepted these arguments. Critics of expansion challenged both the ethics and the logic of Manifest Destiny from the beginning. Henry David Thoreau protested the Mexican-American War and was briefly jailed for refusing to pay taxes in opposition to it. Abraham Lincoln, then an Illinois congressman, challenged President Polk to name the precise "spot" where American blood had first been shed in Mexican territory — a pointed challenge to the administration's claim that Mexico had started the war. Frederick Douglass and other abolitionists condemned expansion as a plot to extend slavery. Whig politicians questioned whether a republic could or should acquire territory by conquest. These voices were minority voices during the 1840s, but they remind us that Manifest Destiny was contested from the start, not a universal American belief.

The ideology of Manifest Destiny also had a gendered dimension. Expansion was often described in the language of male adventure, conquest, and virility. The West was portrayed as a wilderness waiting to be tamed by masculine enterprise. Women who crossed the continent in wagon trains were often depicted as passive supporters of male initiative, though their actual experiences of migration were far more complex, demanding, and consequential than this stereotype suggested.

Expansion and Economic Interest

Behind the soaring rhetoric of providential destiny, concrete economic interests drove expansion. Southern planters sought new lands for cotton cultivation as southern soils became exhausted. Northern merchants and financiers imagined Pacific ports that would open trade with Asia. Railroads promoters envisioned transcontinental lines. Land speculators bought up western lands in advance of settlement. The Mexican-American War in particular served the interests of the slaveholding South, which wanted new slave states to maintain political parity with the growing free-labor North.

These economic interests were not identical across all regions and groups. Western farmers wanted cheap land more than Pacific trade. New England merchants worried about the extension of slave-state power. Workers in northern cities feared that western territories would be dominated by slaveholder aristocracies that would exclude free labor. Manifest Destiny was not a single ideology with a single meaning; it was a contested and flexible set of arguments that different groups used to advance different interests.

Historical Vocabulary
Manifest Destiny

Manifest Destiny refers to the belief, widespread among white Americans in the mid-nineteenth century, that the United States was divinely destined to expand across the entire North American continent from the Atlantic to the Pacific Ocean. The phrase was coined by journalist John L. O'Sullivan in 1845, though the underlying idea had been present in American political culture for decades.

The term "manifest" suggests that this destiny was obvious, self-evident, and undeniable — not a matter of choice or debate but a simple recognition of historical fact. The term "destiny" suggests that expansion was inevitable, foreordained by God or by history. Together, the words constructed a framework that made American territorial expansion seem natural and righteous rather than the product of deliberate policy, military aggression, and racial ideology.

Historians have analyzed Manifest Destiny as a complex cultural phenomenon. It combined Protestant evangelical Christianity, racial ideology, republican nationalism, and economic ambition. By wrapping all of these motives in the language of divine destiny, Manifest Destiny helped to obscure the human choices, power relationships, and moral costs involved in expansion. Understanding Manifest Destiny requires seeing it not as a natural American instinct but as a historically constructed argument that served specific interests and justified specific harms.

Section 23.2

Texas, Mexico, and Settlement Conflict

Texas Under Spanish and Mexican Rule

Texas — a region the Spanish called Tejas — had been part of the Spanish colonial empire in North America for more than a century before Mexican independence. Spanish settlement had been thin and difficult; the region was dominated by powerful Indigenous nations, especially the Comanche, who controlled vast territories and raided Spanish settlements at will. When Mexico became independent in 1821, Texas was part of the new republic's Coahuila y Tejas state — a large, sparsely populated frontier region with a mixed population of Native peoples, long-settled Tejano communities, and a small number of recent settlers.

In the early 1820s, facing financial pressures and hoping to populate and develop Texas, the Mexican government made what would prove to be a fateful decision: it invited Anglo-American settlers to colonize the region under a system of land grants called empresario contracts. Empresarios — land agents — would recruit settlers, who would receive generous land grants in exchange for agreeing to become Mexican citizens, convert to Catholicism, and obey Mexican law.

Stephen F. Austin was the most successful of these empresarios. By the early 1830s, he had brought thousands of Anglo-American families to Texas — many of them from the slaveholding South, many bringing enslaved people with them despite Mexico's moves toward emancipation. By 1830, Anglo settlers outnumbered Tejano residents in many parts of Texas, and the demographic balance was shifting rapidly.

Growing Tensions

The growing Anglo-American population created inevitable tensions with Mexican governance. The settlers brought with them Anglo-American cultural assumptions about land, law, slavery, and government that differed sharply from Mexican practice and law. Mexico abolished slavery in 1829 (with some exemptions that temporarily applied to Texas), but Anglo settlers resisted Mexican emancipation efforts and continued to hold enslaved people in practice. When the Mexican government moved to limit further Anglo immigration to Texas and to tighten customs enforcement in 1830, Anglo settlers grew increasingly resentful.

General Antonio López de Santa Anna, who rose to dominate Mexican politics in the 1830s, moved toward a more centralized government — reducing the autonomy of the states, including Texas. Anglo settlers, accustomed to considerable self-governance, saw this as tyranny. Tejano Texans, who had their own grievances against Mexican centralism, sometimes allied with Anglo settlers, though their interests and experiences were distinct.

Tensions came to a head in 1835 and 1836. Anglo-Texan militias began fighting Mexican forces, and in November 1835 a provisional government declared Texas in a state of rebellion. On March 2, 1836, Texas declared its independence as the Republic of Texas.

The Texas Revolution and Its Multiple Meanings

The battles that followed the declaration of independence have become legendary in American and Texan memory, though that memory has often been selective. At the Alamo in San Antonio, a small garrison of Texan fighters and Anglo volunteers — perhaps 180–200 men, including the famous frontiersman Davy Crockett and the knife-fighter Jim Bowie — was besieged by Santa Anna's army of several thousand. After a thirteen-day siege, the Mexican army stormed the Alamo on March 6, 1836, killing all of the defenders. "Remember the Alamo" became the battle cry of the Texas revolution.

Several weeks later, at the Battle of San Jacinto, Sam Houston's Texan army surprised and routed Santa Anna's forces. Santa Anna was captured and, under duress, signed treaties recognizing Texan independence — treaties the Mexican government later repudiated as illegally signed under coercion.

The memory of the Texas Revolution has long emphasized Anglo-Texan heroism and Mexican tyranny. But historians have complicated this picture significantly. The Alamo defenders included Tejanos fighting for Texas; their stories have often been obscured. The revolution was closely connected to the defense of slavery, which Mexican law threatened to eliminate. Enslaved people who escaped to Mexico before and during the revolution were sometimes forcibly returned or re-enslaved afterward. The Tejano community was internally divided, and many Tejanos who had supported the revolution found themselves treated as second-class residents of the new republic.

The Republic of Texas, independent from 1836 to 1845, was a slaveholding republic from the beginning. It sought admission to the United States almost immediately after independence, but the admission of another large slave state was politically explosive in the U.S. Congress. Texas remained independent for nearly a decade, during which it struggled with chronic financial crises, conflicts with Mexico (which never officially recognized independence), and devastating warfare with the Comanche nation on its western frontier.

Section 23.3

Texas Independence and Annexation

The Question of Annexation

The annexation of Texas was one of the most politically contentious questions in American politics during the late 1830s and early 1840s. The problem was not geographical; few Americans disputed the desirability of adding Texas to the Union in principle. The problem was slavery. Texas would enter the Union as a slave state — or potentially multiple slave states, given its enormous size — and this prospect alarmed antislavery northerners and threatened to upset the careful sectional balance that had held the Union together since the Missouri Compromise.

President Martin Van Buren and his successor John Tyler both approached annexation carefully. Tyler, a Virginia slaveholder who had broken with the Whig Party, aggressively pursued Texas annexation in 1844 and negotiated an annexation treaty. But the Senate rejected it by a wide margin — with northern senators, both Whig and Democrat, refusing to accept what they saw as a massive expansion of slaveholder power.

The election of 1844 transformed the situation. The Democratic Party nominated James K. Polk of Tennessee on a platform that explicitly called for the "re-annexation of Texas" (using the dubious historical claim that Texas had been part of the Louisiana Purchase territory) and the "re-occupation of Oregon." Polk won narrowly over the Whig candidate Henry Clay, who had tried to straddle the Texas question and satisfied no one. President Tyler, reading the election results as a mandate for annexation, pushed through a joint resolution annexing Texas in February 1845 — just before leaving office. A joint resolution, unlike a treaty, required only a simple majority in both houses of Congress rather than a two-thirds Senate vote, and it passed.

The Mechanics of Annexation and Mexican Response

Texas was formally admitted to the Union as the twenty-eighth state in December 1845. Mexico, which had never recognized Texas independence, regarded the annexation as an act of aggression. The Mexican government immediately recalled its minister from Washington and warned that annexation would be treated as a declaration of war.

The annexation also left a crucial geographic question unresolved: where was the southern boundary of Texas? Texas claimed the Rio Grande as its southern border; Mexico insisted that Texas extended only to the Nueces River, about 150 miles to the north. This disputed strip of territory between the Nueces and the Rio Grande would become the trigger for the Mexican-American War.

Polk sent General Zachary Taylor with a force of U.S. regulars to the disputed zone in early 1846, stationing them on the north bank of the Rio Grande. From the Mexican perspective, American forces had invaded Mexican territory. From the American perspective, they were defending Texan soil. When Mexican cavalry crossed the Rio Grande and attacked a U.S. patrol in April 1846, Polk had the incident he needed. He sent a war message to Congress claiming that Mexico had "shed American blood upon American soil." Congress declared war on May 13, 1846.

Tejano Experiences and the Meanings of Annexation

For Tejanos — the Spanish-speaking Mexican citizens of Texas who had long inhabited the region — annexation to the United States was a deeply ambiguous event. Some Tejano leaders had supported the Texas Revolution, hoping for greater autonomy and economic opportunity. But annexation quickly revealed that Anglo-Texan dominance meant Tejano subordination. Land titles that Tejano families held under Spanish and Mexican law were challenged, contested, and often ultimately stolen through legal manipulation, fraud, and violence. Social and political life in Texas increasingly relegated Tejanos to second-class status. Racial violence against Tejano communities was common, and the Texas Rangers — the paramilitary force that enforced Anglo-Texan dominance — gained a brutal reputation for violence against Mexican and Tejano communities.

The history of Tejanos in Texas reminds students that the Texas Revolution and annexation were not simply a story of Anglo triumph over Mexican tyranny. They were also the beginning of a long process of Mexican and Tejano dispossession that would continue long after annexation and that shaped Texas demographics, economics, and race relations for generations.

Section 23.4

Oregon and the Pacific Northwest

The Oregon Country

While the Texas crisis was moving toward war, the United States was simultaneously pursuing the acquisition of the Pacific Northwest. The "Oregon Country" — a vast region encompassing present-day Oregon, Washington, Idaho, and parts of Canada — had been jointly occupied by the United States and Britain since an agreement in 1818 that allowed citizens of both nations to settle the region. By the 1840s, American settlers were beginning to pour into Oregon in significant numbers, and American politicians were demanding clear U.S. sovereignty over the region.

The Oregon Country was already home to diverse Indigenous nations — the Cayuse, Nez Perce, Yakama, Chinook, Tillamook, and others — who had built complex economies, trade networks, and diplomatic relationships long before European contact. They had interacted with Euro-American traders, missionaries, and Hudson's Bay Company employees for decades. But the mass migration of American settlers in the 1840s would transform these relationships rapidly and disastrously for Indigenous peoples.

The Willamette Valley of present-day Oregon was the primary destination for American settlers. Its mild climate, rich soil, and navigable river made it attractive for farming. Protestant missionaries, including Marcus and Narcissa Whitman, had established missions in the region in the late 1830s, and their reports about Oregon's agricultural potential fueled enthusiasm in the East. By 1843, wagon trains were rolling west on the Oregon Trail, and within a few years tens of thousands of Americans had made the two-thousand-mile overland journey.

"Fifty-Four Forty or Fight"

The 1844 Democratic platform had called for the "re-occupation of Oregon" and had used the slogan "Fifty-Four Forty or Fight" — a reference to the line of latitude at 54°40' North that Democrats argued should be the northern boundary of American Oregon, extending well into present-day British Columbia. This aggressive claim would have given the United States all of the Pacific Northwest, including the valuable harbor at present-day Vancouver.

Despite the belligerent rhetoric, President Polk quickly recognized that simultaneously confronting Mexico over Texas and Britain over Oregon was diplomatically imprudent. In June 1846, Polk negotiated the Oregon Treaty with Britain, extending the existing boundary of the 49th parallel to the Pacific coast. Britain retained Vancouver Island, and the United States gained what is now Oregon, Washington, Idaho, and portions of Montana and Wyoming. The settlement was a compromise — dropping the "Fifty-Four Forty" demand — but one that gave the United States the bulk of the territory it wanted, including the ports of Puget Sound.

The Oregon Treaty was reached peacefully, by negotiation rather than war. This contrasted sharply with the approach taken toward Mexico in the same period, a contrast that critics noted had a racial dimension: Britain was a predominantly white, Protestant nation with which war seemed genuinely dangerous; Mexico was a predominantly mestizo and Indigenous nation against which war seemed more manageable and conquerable.

Oregon Migration and Indigenous Peoples

The mass migration along the Oregon Trail had devastating consequences for the Native peoples of the region. Wagon trains disrupted bison herds and other game; settlers' cattle overgrazed prairie grasses; the trails spread disease into communities with little immunity. The Cayuse nation, whose territory centered on the Walla Walla Valley in present-day Washington and Oregon, suffered a devastating measles epidemic in 1847. When Marcus Whitman, a physician operating a nearby mission, was unable to save many Cayuse patients while his own white patients seemed to recover, suspicion and grief turned to violence. In November 1847, a group of Cayuse attacked the Whitman mission, killing Marcus, Narcissa, and eleven others in what became known as the Whitman Massacre.

The event shocked American settlers in Oregon and led to a military campaign against the Cayuse, further entrenching American military and political presence in the region. It also illustrates a pattern that would repeat across the West: Indigenous nations struggling to survive epidemic disease, mission pressure, and settler encroachment, and American settlers interpreting Indigenous resistance as savagery requiring military suppression.

Section 23.5

Mexican-American War

Causes and Controversies

The Mexican-American War began in May 1846 and ended in February 1848 — a conflict that the United States won decisively and that resulted in the largest territorial acquisition in American history since the Louisiana Purchase. But from the beginning, the war was bitterly controversial. Its opponents argued that it was an unjust war of aggression, launched by a slaveholding president to seize land for the expansion of slavery. Its supporters argued that it was a legitimate defense of American territory and a triumph of American arms and civilization.

The immediate trigger was the skirmish on the Rio Grande in April 1846 between Mexican cavalry and a U.S. patrol. Polk's war message to Congress claimed that Mexico had "shed American blood upon American soil." Congressman Abraham Lincoln immediately challenged this framing with his "Spot Resolutions," demanding that Polk identify the specific spot where blood had been shed and whether it was actually American soil or disputed territory. The challenge stung because it exposed the manipulation at the heart of the war's origins. The American forces had been deliberately stationed in the disputed zone between the Nueces and the Rio Grande precisely to provoke an incident that could justify war.

The war declaration passed Congress with large majorities — the House 174 to 14, the Senate 40 to 2. But opposition remained significant. The Whig Party, though it voted war appropriations to support troops in the field, consistently questioned the war's justification. Henry David Thoreau was imprisoned for refusing to pay taxes in protest. Frederick Douglass and William Lloyd Garrison condemned the war as a slaveholders' war. Ulysses S. Grant, who served as a junior officer in the war and later became president, wrote in his memoirs that he believed the Mexican-American War was among the most unjust wars ever waged by a stronger nation against a weaker one.

Military Campaigns

The war was fought on three major fronts. In the north, General Zachary Taylor commanded U.S. forces in a campaign to seize the northern Mexican provinces along the Rio Grande. Taylor won major battles at Palo Alto, Resaca de la Palma, Monterrey, and Buena Vista, establishing his military reputation and making him a national hero. But the northern campaign, though successful, did not force Mexico to surrender.

Simultaneously, the Army of the West under General Stephen Kearny marched from Kansas across the Santa Fe Trail and seized New Mexico and then moved on to California. Kearny's forces captured Santa Fe in August 1846 with little resistance and proclaimed New Mexico a U.S. territory. The move incorporated the substantial Mexican and Native populations of New Mexico into American jurisdiction overnight.

In California, the situation was complicated by the prior presence of a small U.S. naval squadron in Pacific waters and by the activities of John C. Frémont, a U.S. Army officer who was conducting an "exploring" expedition in the region when the war began. Anglo settlers in northern California, led partly by Frémont, launched the Bear Flag Revolt in June 1846, declaring California an independent republic. Within weeks, U.S. naval forces arrived and raised the American flag, incorporating California into U.S. control. Californios (Mexican citizens of California) offered some resistance in the south, but by January 1847 the American conquest of California was essentially complete.

The decisive campaign came when President Polk sent General Winfield Scott to execute an amphibious landing at Veracruz on Mexico's Gulf Coast and march overland to Mexico City. Scott landed approximately ten thousand men at Veracruz in March 1847 — the largest amphibious operation in U.S. history to that point — and began a campaign modeled on Hernán Cortés's sixteenth-century conquest. His forces defeated Mexican armies at Cerro Gordo, Contreras, and Churubusco and in September 1847 captured Mexico City itself. Among the young officers who served under Scott and Taylor were future Civil War generals on both sides: Ulysses S. Grant, William T. Sherman, George McClellan, Robert E. Lee, Stonewall Jackson, and many others.

Mexican Resistance

The Mexican-American War should not be seen only from the American side. Mexico mounted determined resistance despite being outgunned and financially strained. The Battle of Buena Vista in February 1847 was a close-run affair in which Santa Anna's forces came very close to defeating Taylor's army before being repulsed. The defense of Mexico City in September 1847 included the legendary resistance of Los Niños Héroes — the young military cadets who, according to Mexican tradition, died defending the Chapultepec military academy rather than surrender. Mexican civilians suffered enormously from the war, and Mexican historical memory of the conflict is profoundly different from American celebrations of military triumph.

The war was also deeply unpopular in parts of Mexico on grounds that resonate with American critics: it was seen as an imperialist land grab. The Mexican government struggled to organize effective resistance partly because of internal political divisions — Santa Anna himself was a controversial and divisive figure — and partly because the United States had overwhelming advantages in artillery, logistics, and military training.

Section 23.6

Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo

Negotiating the Treaty

By the fall of 1847, Mexico City had fallen and the Mexican government was desperate for peace. President Polk had sent Nicholas Trist, a State Department official, to negotiate a treaty. The negotiations were complicated by the capture of Mexico City, the collapse of the Mexican government, and Polk's own changing demands — at one point Polk recalled Trist, but Trist, recognizing that the peace terms available were the best achievable, stayed in Mexico and signed a treaty anyway.

The Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo was signed on February 2, 1848, and ratified by the U.S. Senate on March 10, 1848. By its terms, Mexico ceded to the United States the vast territory of what is today California, Nevada, Utah, most of Arizona and New Mexico, and parts of Colorado and Wyoming — approximately 529,000 square miles, representing roughly half of Mexico's pre-war territory. In exchange, the United States paid Mexico $15 million and agreed to assume up to $3.25 million in debts that American citizens held against Mexico. The United States also agreed to protect the civil and property rights of Mexican citizens who remained in the ceded territories, including their right to keep their land, their language, and their religion.

Some American politicians, particularly southern expansionists, wanted to take even more — potentially all of Mexico. The "All of Mexico" movement argued that the United States should annex the entire Mexican republic. But this proposal raised uncomfortable questions about what to do with Mexico's predominantly mestizo and Indigenous population, which most Anglo-Americans considered racially ineligible for U.S. citizenship. The treaty that was actually signed represented a compromise — enormous in territorial ambition, but stopping short of the most extreme expansion.

The Rights of Mexican Citizens and Their Violations

The treaty's promise to protect the rights of Mexican citizens in the ceded territories proved, in practice, to be hollow. Article VIII guaranteed that Mexicans who chose to remain in the ceded territories could become U.S. citizens within one year. Article IX promised that they would enjoy the rights of citizens of the United States. Article X guaranteed their land grants — a provision so important that the Senate deleted it during ratification, replacing it with a protocol that weakened land-grant protections significantly.

What followed in California, New Mexico, and Texas was a systematic erosion of the legal protections the treaty promised. Californio rancho families, who had held land grants of tens of thousands of acres under Spanish and Mexican law, were forced to defend their titles in American courts under the California Land Act of 1851 — a process so expensive, time-consuming, and legally treacherous that many families lost their lands even when their titles were ultimately confirmed. Legal fees, yearslong litigation, and manipulations by Anglo speculators and lawyers reduced many Californio families from landed wealth to poverty within a generation.

In New Mexico, similar processes played out over decades. Anglo ranchers and speculators challenged the validity of Spanish and Mexican land grants, and federal courts often sided with the challengers. In Texas, Tejano families who had supported the Texas Revolution found that Anglo-Texan society treated them as racially Mexican regardless of their prior alliances, and land dispossession continued systematically throughout the nineteenth century.

The Gadsden Purchase

The Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo did not completely settle the U.S.-Mexico boundary. In 1853, the United States negotiated the Gadsden Purchase, acquiring a small additional strip of territory in present-day southern Arizona and New Mexico for $10 million. The purchase was motivated primarily by the desire to secure a southern route for a transcontinental railroad, but it also incorporated additional Mexican communities and lands into the United States. It was the last major territorial acquisition in the contiguous United States and fixed the boundary between the two countries in roughly its current form.

A map of North America before 1845 shows the United States extending westward to roughly the modern states of Iowa, Missouri, Arkansas, and Louisiana along the Mississippi River, with the Republic of Texas as an independent nation to the south and west, British-American joint-occupied Oregon to the north, and Mexico extending from Texas south through Central America, including California, New Mexico, and much of the modern American Southwest.

Mexico has lost approximately half its pre-war territory.

What to Look For on This Map:

Students examining this transformation should notice several features. First, the sheer scale of the territorial change: in less than a decade, the United States more than doubled its territory through a combination of negotiation and conquest. Second, the geography of the ceded territories: California and the Pacific ports that American merchants and politicians had long coveted were now American. The overland route to the Pacific — across New Mexico and Arizona — was secured by the Gadsden Purchase. Third, the new international boundaries: the Rio Grande became the U.S.-Mexico border in Texas, and the 49th parallel became the U.S.-Canada border in the Pacific Northwest. These lines, drawn by treaty and war, cut across Indigenous territories, separated communities, and imposed new political realities on peoples who had not chosen to live under American governance.

Fourth, students should note what is not shown on political maps of this kind: the locations of Native nations and their territories, which cut across the new political boundaries and which the treaties largely ignored. A map of Indigenous nations and territories in 1848 would look very different from a map of state and territorial boundaries.

Section 23.7

California, Gold, and Migration

Discovery at Sutter's Mill

On January 24, 1848 — just nine days before the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo was signed — a carpenter named James Marshall noticed glinting flecks in the tailrace of a sawmill he was building for John Sutter on the American River in the Sierra Nevada foothills. He had found gold. Marshall and Sutter tried to keep the discovery secret, but word spread rapidly. By the spring and summer of 1848, Californians of all backgrounds were abandoning their regular occupations and rushing to the mining regions. When President Polk confirmed the gold discovery in his December 1848 message to Congress, the rush became a torrent.

In 1849, the California Gold Rush reached its peak. From around the world, approximately 80,000 people flooded into California, sailing around Cape Horn, crossing Panama, or traveling overland across the continent. They came from the eastern United States and the Deep South; from Mexico, Chile, and Peru; from China, Australia, and Europe. They became known collectively as the "forty-niners," and their arrival transformed California from a sparsely populated frontier region into a chaotic, cosmopolitan, and explosively growing society almost overnight. California's non-Native population, which had been perhaps 14,000 in 1848, grew to about 100,000 by the end of 1849 and to over 300,000 by 1855.

The Gold Fields

The gold fields of the Sierra Nevada foothills — the Mother Lode region — stretched for roughly 150 miles along the western slope of the Sierra. Early miners found gold in stream gravels using simple techniques: the gold pan, the rocker (a rocking box that separated gold from gravel), and the long tom (a longer sluice). In the first rush, individual miners could find significant amounts of gold relatively quickly, and stories of men striking it rich overnight fueled the frenzy.

But the easy pickings did not last. By the early 1850s, placer deposits — loose gold in stream beds — were largely exhausted, and mining required capital-intensive hard-rock mining techniques that only corporations and companies could afford. The individual prospector who had imagined making a fortune was increasingly replaced by wage-earning miners working for mining companies. The get-rich-quick dream that had drawn so many people became, for most, a story of hard labor, disappointment, and modest wages.

Those who made reliable fortunes were often not the miners themselves but the merchants who supplied them: the storekeepers, the tool sellers, the clothing manufacturers, the boarding house owners. Levi Strauss, who came to San Francisco in 1853, built a clothing business serving miners — eventually producing the denim work pants that bear his name to this day. The merchants, lawyers, and entrepreneurs who serviced the mining economy often prospered far more steadily than the miners whose labor extracted the gold.

California's Demographic Transformation

The Gold Rush made California one of the most diverse places in the world almost overnight. Miners came from Mexico, where thousands of Sonoran miners — experienced with placer mining techniques — were among the earliest and most skilled workers in the gold fields. Chinese immigrants, primarily from Guangdong province, began arriving in significant numbers in 1849–1850, drawn by stories of "Gold Mountain." Chilean, Peruvian, Hawaiian, French, German, Irish, and British miners all added to the extraordinary mix.

This diversity quickly produced conflict. Anglo-American miners resented competition and used law and violence to exclude rivals. California's Foreign Miners' Tax of 1850 imposed heavy fees specifically on non-Anglo miners — initially targeting Mexicans and Chileans, and later applied to Chinese miners as well. Violence against Chinese, Mexican, and Chilean miners was common and frequently unpunished. The racial hierarchy of the gold fields replicated and intensified racial hierarchies from the rest of the United States.

Black Americans who came to California found a complex situation. California was admitted to the Union as a free state in 1850, and Black Californians were not enslaved. But they faced pervasive discrimination, exclusion from many occupations, and legal restrictions on testimony and civil rights. Some found California a place of greater opportunity than the South or even the North; others found the racism as crushing as anywhere else.

The Destruction of California's Native Peoples

For California's Indigenous peoples, the Gold Rush was nothing short of catastrophe. Before 1848, California's Native population had already been severely reduced by the Spanish mission system, Mexican rancho labor exploitation, and epidemic disease. Estimates suggest perhaps 150,000 Native Californians survived to see the Gold Rush. Within twenty-five years of the Rush's beginning, that population had collapsed to roughly 30,000 — a demographic disaster driven by murder, starvation, displacement, epidemic disease, and the violent seizure of traditional lands and food sources.

The Gold Rush eliminated the ecological systems — salmon runs, acorn-producing oak groves, grasslands and game — that Native peoples depended on. It flooded their territories with settlers who treated them as obstacles to be removed or eliminated. The state of California paid militia companies that conducted military campaigns against Native communities, destroying villages and killing men, women, and children. The kidnapping and forced servitude of Native children was legal in California under an 1850 state law. Historians have described what happened to California's Native peoples during the Gold Rush era as a genocide — the deliberate destruction of peoples and cultures through a combination of violence, starvation, and legal oppression.

The fate of California's Native peoples represents the darkest chapter in the history of the Gold Rush and a stark demonstration of the human cost of Manifest Destiny when stripped of its ideological justifications and examined from the perspective of those who bore its violence.

Section 23.8

Native Nations and Western Expansion

The Diversity of the West

When Americans spoke of "the West" in the 1840s, they were referring to an enormous and diverse landscape inhabited by millions of people organized into scores of distinct nations and communities. These were not empty lands; they were lived-in places with complex economies, social systems, diplomatic traditions, and military capabilities. Understanding the westward expansion of the United States requires understanding the peoples who occupied the lands being claimed.

The Great Plains were dominated in the mid-nineteenth century by the Lakota Sioux, the Comanche, the Cheyenne, the Arapaho, and other nations that had built powerful equestrian cultures over the previous century. The introduction of the horse from Spanish settlements to the south had transformed Plains societies beginning in the seventeenth century, enabling more mobile bison hunting and more effective mounted warfare. By the 1840s, the Comanche nation in particular was at the height of its power, controlling a vast territory — the Comanchería — that encompassed much of the southern Plains and effectively blocked both American and Mexican expansion into that region for decades.

The Southwest was home to the Navajo (Diné), Apache, Pueblo peoples, and others who had inhabited the region for centuries. They had complex relationships with Spanish colonialism, Mexican governance, and one another — relationships that American conquest would disrupt and transform. The Pacific Coast had its own remarkable diversity: the salmon-fishing nations of the Northwest, the oak-acorn-gathering societies of California, the fishing and whaling communities of the coast.

American Expansion and Indigenous Displacement

American expansion in the 1840s affected Native nations differently depending on where they lived, how much military power they could deploy, and what diplomatic and economic relationships they could maintain with American settlers and officials. In some regions, the immediate impact of the Mexican-American War and the Gold Rush was decisive and devastating, as in California. In others, particularly the Great Plains, Indigenous nations remained powerful enough to limit American encroachment for another two or three decades.

The Comanche nation presents a particularly important case. In the 1840s, the Comanche were arguably the most powerful Indigenous military force on the continent. They controlled vast territories, raided Mexican settlements far into the interior of Mexico, traded extensively with Pueblo communities and Anglo-American merchants, and were perfectly capable of destroying Anglo-Texan settlements when they chose. The Republic of Texas and then the U.S. government negotiated repeatedly with Comanche leaders and were often forced to accept Comanche terms.

Comanche power would not be broken until the 1870s, when a combination of U.S. military strategy, the destruction of bison herds by commercial hunters, and epidemic disease finally overwhelmed even Comanche resilience. That story belongs to later chapters, but understanding that the Comanche and other Plains nations were not simply passive victims of inevitable expansion — that they were powerful actors who shaped the history of the West for decades — is essential for understanding the period.

Reservation Policy and Its Beginnings

As American settlement expanded in the 1840s and 1850s, the federal government began to articulate what would become reservation policy: the concentration of Native peoples onto defined, limited territories where they could be "managed" and where their lands could be opened to American settlement. The Fort Laramie Treaty of 1851 attempted to define territories for the major Plains nations in exchange for annual government payments and safe passage for settlers on the Oregon Trail. But the treaty was violated almost immediately as settler pressure pushed beyond the agreed boundaries.

The reservation system as it would develop more fully in the post–Civil War decades was rooted in the expansionist politics of the 1840s and 1850s. The core logic — that Native peoples must cede their lands, relocate to defined territories, and ultimately assimilate into American society — was already present in federal policy by the time of the Mexican-American War. What changed in later decades was the scale of military force brought to bear on nations that resisted this logic.

Section 23.9

Expansion and the Slavery Question

The Wilmot Proviso

On August 8, 1846, just three months after the start of the Mexican-American War, Congressman David Wilmot of Pennsylvania introduced an amendment to a military appropriations bill. The Wilmot Proviso, as it became known, proposed that slavery should be prohibited in any territory acquired from Mexico as a result of the war. It was a simple proposition with explosive consequences.

The Wilmot Proviso passed the House of Representatives, where northern free-state representatives had a numerical majority. It failed in the Senate, where southern states had proportionally greater representation and could block it. But the vote on the proviso transformed the politics of expansion. It revealed a sectional division that cut across party lines: almost every northern congressman, regardless of party, supported the proviso; almost every southern congressman, regardless of party, opposed it. The slavery question had broken through the normal lines of partisan politics.

Why did the Wilmot Proviso matter so much? The answer lay in the long-term political arithmetic of the Union. Since the Missouri Compromise of 1820, the principle that roughly equal numbers of slave states and free states would be admitted to the Union had maintained a fragile political balance — especially in the Senate, where each state had two votes regardless of population. If Mexico's ceded territories became free states, the North would gain permanent political supremacy; if they became slave states, the slave South would retain its power. The stakes could not have been higher.

Popular Sovereignty and Constitutional Debates

Southern politicians responded to the Wilmot Proviso with their own positions. Some argued that Congress had no constitutional authority to restrict slavery in territories at all — that the Constitution protected slave property everywhere in the national domain. Others, following Senator John C. Calhoun of South Carolina, argued that the territories were common property of all the states and that citizens of slave states had the same right to bring their property (including enslaved people) into the territories as citizens of free states.

A middle position, championed by Senator Lewis Cass of Michigan and later by Senator Stephen A. Douglas of Illinois, proposed popular sovereignty: letting the settlers of each territory decide the slavery question for themselves through their territorial legislature. This was appealing to some because it avoided a direct congressional decision, but it raised its own problems: When would the decision be made? Who would be allowed to vote? What would happen if a territory voted for slavery in its early years but then wanted to reverse that decision before statehood?

These competing positions — congressional exclusion of slavery (the Wilmot Proviso position), congressional protection of slavery (the southern position), and popular sovereignty (the Democratic middle ground) — would define the politics of the 1850s and drive the nation toward the Civil War.

The Compromise of 1850

By 1849–1850, the territorial acquisitions of the Mexican-American War had created an acute crisis. California, flooded with Gold Rush migrants, had drafted a state constitution banning slavery and applied for admission as a free state. New Mexico and Utah, with smaller populations, remained as organized territories. Texas was claiming that its borders extended far into what is now New Mexico. The Fugitive Slave Act of 1793 was widely evaded in the North, enraging southern slaveholders. And the slave trade in Washington, D.C. — conducted in the shadow of the Capitol — offended northern antislavery sentiment.

Senator Henry Clay of Kentucky, the aging "Great Compromiser" who had brokered the Missouri Compromise thirty years earlier, proposed a package of measures designed to resolve all of these issues simultaneously. After months of bitter debate — including a famous three-hour speech by the dying John C. Calhoun, who had to have it read for him, and a stirring defense of Union by Daniel Webster — the compromise was passed in the summer of 1850, largely through the parliamentary maneuvering of the young Illinois senator Stephen A. Douglas.

The Compromise of 1850 admitted California as a free state, organized New Mexico and Utah as territories with popular sovereignty, settled the Texas boundary dispute with a payment to Texas, abolished the slave trade (but not slavery itself) in Washington, D.C., and passed a new, much stronger Fugitive Slave Act requiring northerners to assist in the capture of escaped enslaved people. The compromise satisfied no one completely and deeply angered many. The Fugitive Slave Act, in particular, outraged northerners who had previously been indifferent to slavery; its requirement that they participate in the return of fugitives brought the reality of slavery home to communities far from the South.

The Compromise of 1850 bought the Union about four years of relative quiet. But the underlying tensions had not been resolved — they had been postponed. The question of whether slavery would expand into the West remained unanswered, and the next round of territorial crises — triggered by the Kansas-Nebraska Act of 1854 — would shatter the compromise and push the nation further toward disunion.

Historical Vocabulary
The Borderlands

Borderlands refers to geographic and cultural zones where different nations, peoples, languages, and cultural systems meet, overlap, and interact. Scholars have used the concept particularly to describe the regions of the U.S.-Mexico Southwest — Texas, New Mexico, Arizona, and California — where Spanish, Mexican, Indigenous, and Anglo-American societies had been layered and intertwined for centuries before the Mexican-American War.

Historian Herbert Eugene Bolton first developed the borderlands concept in the early twentieth century to challenge the view of American history as a story of Anglo settlement moving west from the Atlantic. Bolton argued that the Spanish and Mexican borderlands had been important historical spaces long before Anglo-Americans arrived. Historians since Bolton, particularly Gloria Anzaldúa and David Weber, have deepened this analysis by emphasizing the cultural complexity, hybridity, and creativity of borderland communities, as well as the violence and inequality that shaped them.

The borderlands concept helps students understand why the Mexican-American War and the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo were not simply military and diplomatic events. They were transformations of deeply complex societies — societies that did not disappear when a new flag was raised but continued to exist, contest, and negotiate their place in the new political order. The Spanish language, the Catholic Church, the land grant system, the rancho economy, and Indigenous traditions all survived the conquest and continued to shape the Southwest in ways that persist to this day.

Understanding the borderlands means understanding that the boundary between the United States and Mexico is not a natural or inevitable line but a historically produced one — and that communities on both sides of that line carry the legacies of conquest, migration, and cultural mixture that the borderlands have always generated.

Primary Source Window

John L. O'Sullivan, "Annexation" (1845)

Creator: John L. O'Sullivan · Date: 1845 · Type: Political essay / editorial

Context: John L. O'Sullivan (1813–1895) was a Democratic journalist and editor who co-founded the United States Magazine and Democratic Review in 1837. In July and August 1845, he published two essays making the case for the annexation of Texas and the expansion of the United States. It was in these essays that he used the phrase "manifest destiny," which quickly became the defining slogan of American expansionism.

O'Sullivan argued that opposition to Texas annexation was hypocritical and shortsighted. He dismissed European interference in American affairs and declared that it was the destiny of the United States to spread across the continent that Providence had given for the development of the great experiment of Liberty and self-government. He claimed that the Texas question was not about war or conquest but about the peaceful spread of the American population and its republican institutions, and that wherever Americans settled, they carried with them the seeds of liberty that would make those places flourish under republican government.

Analysis: O'Sullivan's essay is a foundational document in the history of American expansionism, and it repays careful reading. Several features stand out.

First, O'Sullivan frames expansion not as conquest but as natural growth — as organic as the spread of a healthy plant. He describes the nation as "overspreading" rather than conquering or seizing. This framing is ideologically significant: it makes expansion seem inevitable and natural rather than chosen and violent.

Second, O'Sullivan invokes Providence — the will of God — to justify expansion. This religious framing is characteristic of Manifest Destiny ideology and helps explain why it was so powerful: it made territorial aggression into a moral and spiritual duty.

Third, O'Sullivan says almost nothing about the peoples who already inhabit the lands he claims for American expansion. The Indigenous peoples, the Mexican citizens, the diverse communities of the West are invisible in his account — or, when mentioned at all, are dismissed as obstacles destined to give way to superior civilization.

Fourth, O'Sullivan assumes that "Americans" means white Anglo-Americans. His vision of liberty and self-government was explicitly racial in its application.

What the source does not show: O'Sullivan's essay cannot tell us how Mexican citizens, Native peoples, or enslaved people responded to or experienced expansion. It cannot tell us about the material interests — economic, political, slaveholding — that animated expansion alongside the ideological claims. And it cannot tell us about the Americans who disagreed with his arguments.

  1. What reasons does O'Sullivan give for why American expansion is justified? Which do you find most significant?

Historical Debate

Was Manifest Destiny Primarily Racial Ideology or Republican Idealism?

The question: Historians have long debated what Manifest Destiny actually was at its core: Was it primarily a racial ideology that used the language of liberty and Providence to justify the subjugation of non-white peoples? Or was it a genuine expression of republican idealism — a sincere belief that the spread of American democratic institutions would benefit everyone — that was entangled with but not reducible to racism?

Interpretation A — Manifest Destiny as Racial Ideology: Historians working in this tradition, including Reginald Horsman (whose 1981 book Race and Manifest Destiny is foundational), argue that the ideology of continental expansion was inseparable from racial thought. American expansionists, Horsman argues, consistently framed expansion in terms of Anglo-Saxon racial superiority. The belief that white Americans were uniquely suited to self-government, industry, and civilization — and that Mexicans, Native peoples, and others were racially inferior and therefore unfit to control their lands — was not incidental to Manifest Destiny but essential to it. This racial logic explained why conquest was justified, why the rights of the conquered could be ignored, and why the United States deserved what it was taking.
Interpretation B — Manifest Destiny as Complex Republican Ideology: Other historians, while acknowledging the racial dimensions of Manifest Destiny, argue that it was a more complex and contradictory ideology that included genuine republican idealism. In this view, many American expansionists sincerely believed that they were spreading liberty, democracy, and opportunity — and that the spread of republican institutions was genuinely good for humanity. They point to Americans who opposed the worst racial violence of expansion while still supporting territorial growth, to the real material opportunities that expansion offered to ordinary white settlers, and to the ways that democratic participation was extended (for white men) through territorial growth.

Evidence: Evidence supporting the racial ideology interpretation includes congressional debates, newspaper editorials, and military correspondence that explicitly invoke Anglo-Saxon superiority; state laws in California and other territories that denied civil rights to non-white residents; and the pattern of land dispossession that targeted precisely those groups — Mexicans, Native peoples, Chinese — whom the racial hierarchy ranked lowest. Evidence supporting the more complex interpretation includes the fact that some Mexican citizens were successfully incorporated into American political life (though usually those who could "pass" as white or who had sufficient wealth and social capital); that abolitionists who were deeply opposed to racism also sometimes supported territorial expansion; and that ordinary settlers' motivations were often more about land hunger and economic opportunity than racial ideology.

Why it matters: This debate matters because it shapes how we understand the relationship between American democratic ideals and American expansion. If Manifest Destiny was fundamentally a racial ideology, then the ideals of liberty and democracy it invoked were always hypocritical cover for conquest. If it was a complex ideology with genuine idealistic elements entangled with racial thinking, then the story is more about tragic contradiction — a republic that genuinely believed in liberty while practicing racial domination.

Multiple Perspectives

The Mexican-American War

From the perspective of American expansionists and the Polk administration: The war was justified, necessary, and ultimately beneficial. Mexico had shed American blood on American soil; the United States was defending its legitimate territory. The war would spread republican institutions to lands formerly misgoverned by an unstable Mexican republic. The territories gained — California's ports, New Mexico's trade routes, the vast lands of the Southwest — would complete the continental republic that Providence had intended. Zachary Taylor and Winfield Scott were heroes; American soldiers were brave champions of civilization.
From the perspective of Mexico: The war was an act of naked aggression by a more powerful neighbor. The United States had deliberately manufactured a border incident to justify a war it had been planning since Texas annexation. Mexico had never recognized Texan independence; the annexation of Texas was itself illegal. American forces invaded Mexican territory, occupied Mexican cities, and ultimately stripped Mexico of roughly half its national territory. The war was a humiliation and a trauma that Mexicans remembered for generations — and that shaped Mexican national identity and the Mexican government's long-standing wariness of its northern neighbor.
From the perspective of antislavery northerners: The war was a "slaveholders' war" — a conspiracy by the southern planter class to seize new territories for slavery's expansion. The Polk administration, dominated by southern slaveholders, had manufactured a pretext for war in order to acquire new slave states. The Wilmot Proviso was the antislavery response: a demand that whatever land was seized from Mexico should not become slave territory. For abolitionists and antislavery Whigs like Abraham Lincoln and Henry David Thoreau, the war was morally indefensible — a violation of the principles of liberty that the United States claimed to embody.
From the perspective of Mexicans living in the ceded territories: The end of the war meant the sudden transformation of their political and legal status. People who had been Mexican citizens one day were Americans the next — incorporated into a new polity without their consent, promised rights by treaty that would be systematically denied in practice. Californios who had held vast ranchos found their land titles challenged and their social status reduced. New Mexicans faced the imposition of American legal and political systems on communities organized around different traditions. The experience of incorporation was one of dispossession, subordination, and cultural erasure for many.
From the perspective of enslaved Black Americans: The Mexican-American War was observed with acute attention and deep anxiety. Every new territory acquired brought the question of slavery's expansion closer to resolution — or closer to catastrophe. Some enslaved people attempted to escape during the war, crossing into Mexico, where slavery was illegal. Others noted bitterly that American soldiers were fighting to extend the dominion of a republic that held them in bondage. The Wilmot Proviso debate, visible from afar, showed that the question of their fate was embedded in every political conflict over western expansion.
Constitutional Connection

Territorial Expansion, Congressional Power, and Slavery

The acquisitions of the Mexican-American War immediately raised constitutional questions that went to the heart of the American republic's structure: Did Congress have the power to prohibit slavery in territories acquired from Mexico? Did the Constitution protect slave property in the national territories? Was popular sovereignty — letting territories decide for themselves — constitutionally sound?

The Constitution itself was ambiguous on these questions. Article IV, Section 3, gives Congress the power to make "needful Rules and Regulations" respecting U.S. territories, which most legal scholars interpreted as granting broad congressional authority over territorial governance, including the power to prohibit slavery. The Missouri Compromise of 1820 had exercised this power, banning slavery north of 36°30' in Louisiana Purchase territory.

But southern politicians, especially John C. Calhoun, developed a constitutional argument that the territories were the "common property" of all the states, and that citizens of slave states had the constitutional right — under the Fifth Amendment's protection of property — to bring their property (including enslaved people) into any territory. Congressional prohibition of slavery in territories was therefore unconstitutional in this view.

This constitutional argument would become even more consequential after the Mexican-American War. In 1857, the Supreme Court attempted to resolve the dispute in Dred Scott v. Sandford, ruling that Congress had no power to prohibit slavery in the territories — a ruling that effectively invalidated the Missouri Compromise and overturned decades of congressional practice. The Dred Scott decision intensified rather than resolved the sectional crisis, and its constitutional logic was one of the direct causes of the Civil War.

The Mexican-American War, by forcing the question of slavery's expansion into new territories, thus accelerated the constitutional crisis over slavery that would destroy the republic's political equilibrium and lead to civil war. The territories acquired from Mexico were not simply geographic additions; they were constitutional crises waiting to unfold.

Cause and Effect

The Mexican-American War

Long-Term Causes

The longstanding U.S. desire for Mexico's northern territories, particularly California's Pacific ports; Anglo-American colonization of Texas, which created a settler population tied to the United States; the ideology of Manifest Destiny, which gave expansion a moral and religious framework; the political ambitions of southern slaveholders seeking new slave territory; and Mexico's political instability and financial weakness.

Short-Term Causes

Texas annexation in 1845, which Mexico viewed as aggression; the disputed Texas-Mexico boundary (Rio Grande versus Nueces River); Polk's deliberate deployment of U.S. forces into the disputed zone; and the Mexican cavalry's attack on a U.S. patrol in April 1846.

The War

The United States declared war in May 1846 and won decisively by September 1847, when Mexico City fell to General Winfield Scott's forces. Approximately 13,000 American soldiers died, mostly from disease; Mexican casualties were substantially higher.

Effects

The Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo (1848) transferred roughly 529,000 square miles to the United States; Mexico was left politically humiliated and financially strained; the Wilmot Proviso, the Compromise of 1850, and ultimately the Civil War were all shaped by the territorial crisis the war created.

Historical complexity: The Mexican-American War cannot be reduced to a single cause — not simply southern slaveholder aggression, not simply Manifest Destiny ideology, not simply Mexican intransigence, not simply the specific decisions of President Polk. It was the product of decades of expansionist pressure, economic interests, ideological convictions, political calculations, and contingent events.

Continuity and Change Over Time

U.S. Expansion and Indigenous Peoples

What Changed

The period of the 1840s brought dramatic changes in the relationship between the United States and Indigenous peoples. The Mexican-American War brought millions of acres of Native territory — in California, New Mexico, Arizona, and elsewhere — under U.S. jurisdiction overnight. The California Gold Rush introduced a tidal wave of settlers into lands that had previously seen relatively limited Anglo-American presence, with devastating consequences for Native Californians. Federal Indian policy began to articulate reservation policy — the confinement of Native peoples to defined territories — as an explicit goal. The scale of violence against Native peoples also intensified, with the near-genocidal destruction of California's Native population representing a qualitative escalation of the violence that had characterized earlier periods of expansion.

What Stayed the Same

Despite the enormous scale of change, several continuities persisted. The fundamental logic of Native dispossession — the claim that American expansion was inevitable, natural, and superior — had been present since the earliest colonial period. The pattern of treaty-making followed by treaty violation had characterized U.S.-Native relations since the founding. The use of racial ideology to deny the legitimacy of Native sovereignty was not new in the 1840s; it had deep roots in colonial and early national policy. Indigenous peoples' responses also showed continuity: Native nations continued to negotiate, resist, adapt, and survive — using whatever strategies were available to protect their communities.

Who Experienced Change Most Directly

California's Native peoples experienced the most sudden and devastating change — the collapse of their populations within a generation. Native nations in New Mexico and Arizona were incorporated into U.S. territorial jurisdiction, fundamentally altering the political framework they operated within. Nations on the Great Plains — the Comanche, Sioux, Cheyenne, and others — experienced less immediate change in the 1840s and 1850s, as they remained militarily powerful enough to limit American encroachment. Their most severe losses would come in the 1860s and 1870s.

Why the pattern matters: Understanding that the dispossession of Indigenous peoples in the West was neither instant nor inevitable — that it was the product of specific policies, specific acts of violence, and specific demographic pressures — helps students resist the "inevitable vanishing" narrative that Manifest Destiny ideology promoted. Native peoples were not fated to disappear; they were destroyed, dispossessed, and confined by specific human choices that could have been made differently.

Everyday Life and Social History: Women on the Overland Trails

The overland migration to Oregon and California is often told as a story of male adventure — trappers, mountain men, wagon masters, and prospectors pushing into the unknown. But women were central participants in every aspect of overland migration, and their experiences shed light on both the costs and the meanings of westward expansion in ways that the heroic male narrative obscures.

Between 1840 and 1860, approximately 250,000 to 300,000 people crossed the continent on the Oregon and California Trails. Women made up a substantial minority of this number, traveling as wives, mothers, daughters, and sisters in family groups. A few, like the mountain man Jim Bridger's Shoshone wife and other Indigenous and mixed-heritage women, guided and interpreted for crossing parties. Most Anglo-American women traveled as part of the domestic unit of the family — their labor essential, their voices often unrecorded.

Women who kept diaries and letters — and many did — described a journey that was simultaneously exhilarating and exhausting, full of beauty and full of dread. The trail demanded physical endurance that conventional gender ideology of the era associated only with men. Women walked beside wagons for miles when the going was rough, carried children, helped push wagons up steep grades, and worked throughout the crossing to maintain the domestic routines — cooking, washing, nursing the sick — that kept families alive. When cholera or other diseases struck (and they struck frequently on the crowded, contaminated trail), it was often women who did the nursing, the burying, and the grieving.

Many women recorded profound ambivalence about the migration. They were frequently not the decision-makers; the choice to go west was typically made by husbands or fathers. Some wrote of their grief at leaving communities, family networks, churches, and friends — the social world that gave meaning and support to women's lives in the East. Narcissa Whitman, one of the first Anglo-American women to cross the continent in 1836, wrote movingly of the loneliness and isolation of mission life in the Oregon wilderness. Other women adapted remarkably, finding in the challenge of frontier life new capacities and competencies that transformed their sense of themselves.

Race shaped women's overland experiences profoundly. Enslaved Black women who were taken west by slaveholding families had no choice in their migration and faced the particular terror of being brought into new territories where the legal status of slavery was contested. Native women in western communities faced the disruption of their own societies by the waves of settlers moving through. Mexican women in California and New Mexico found their communities transformed by conquest and their legal status changed overnight.

The overland trail experience also shaped American culture in lasting ways. The stories women told about crossing the continent — in diaries, letters, published memoirs, and eventually oral tradition — became part of the national mythology of westward expansion. But the actual experiences those stories preserved were far more complex, ambiguous, and costly than the triumphalist narrative of Manifest Destiny allowed for. Historians of women and gender have used these sources to reconstruct a more complete picture of what expansion actually meant for the millions of people who lived it.

Chapter Summary

Chapter 23 has examined westward expansion in the 1840s as a transformative and deeply contested process that reshaped the geography, demography, politics, and culture of the United States. The ideology of Manifest Destiny — the belief in the providential, racial, and republican destiny of the United States to span the continent — gave expansionism its moral and ideological framework, but that framework concealed the concrete interests and violent realities that drove expansion.

The history of Texas traced a trajectory from Mexican frontier province to Anglo-American settlement zone to independent slaveholding republic to U.S. state — a trajectory that enriched Anglo-American settlers while dispossessing Tejano families, maintaining and expanding the institution of slavery, and triggering a war with Mexico. The conflict over Texas was never simply about geography or nationalism; it was always also about slavery, race, and the contest between different visions of what the American republic should be.

The resolution of the Oregon question through diplomacy with Britain, contrasted with the military approach to Mexico, revealed the racial and strategic logic that shaped American expansion. The United States negotiated carefully with a powerful British empire; it went to war against a weaker Mexico. The Mexican-American War was controversial from the start, with critics — from Lincoln to Thoreau to Douglass — challenging its justification and its moral premises. But it was prosecuted successfully, and its result was the largest territorial acquisition in U.S. history since the Louisiana Purchase.

The Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo promised to protect the rights of Mexican citizens in the ceded territories. Those promises were systematically violated. Californio rancho families, Tejano landowners, and New Mexican communities all experienced systematic dispossession in the decades after annexation, as Anglo-American legal and political systems were deployed to strip them of the lands, rights, and status the treaty had promised to protect.

The California Gold Rush transformed the West in ways that even the war had not anticipated. The discovery of gold brought tens of thousands of migrants from around the world, creating one of the most diverse societies in American history — and one of the most violent and unequal. The dispossession and near-destruction of California's Native peoples during the Gold Rush era represents one of the darkest episodes in American history, a story of mass violence driven by settler demand for land, water, and gold.

Native nations across the West responded to expansion in diverse ways. Some, like the Comanche, remained powerful enough to limit American encroachment for decades. Others, like the Native peoples of California, were overwhelmed quickly by demographic and military forces they could not withstand. All faced a United States that denied the legitimacy of their sovereignty and that was prepared to use enormous violence to enforce its claims.

The territorial acquisitions of the 1840s did not resolve the political crisis over slavery — they intensified it. The Wilmot Proviso, the popular sovereignty debate, and the Compromise of 1850 were all attempts to manage the explosive question of whether slavery would expand into the new western territories. The Compromise of 1850 bought temporary peace but satisfied no one, and the underlying tensions would burst forth again with even greater force in the 1850s.

Women who participated in the overland migration experienced expansion differently from men — with less decision-making power, equal physical demand, and complex emotional responses that ranged from grief to exhilaration. Their accounts, preserved in diaries and letters, offer historians a window into the human texture of expansion that political and military histories alone cannot provide.

The decade of the 1840s left the United States with a continental empire — and with the deepening contradictions that empire embodied. A republic founded on the principles of liberty and self-government had expanded through military conquest, racial dispossession, and the maintenance and extension of slavery. How those contradictions would be managed — or not managed — would define American politics for the next two decades and ultimately precipitate the Civil War.

Key Terms

Manifest Destiny
The belief, widespread among white Americans in the mid-nineteenth century, that the United States was divinely ordained to expand across the North American continent from the Atlantic to the Pacific, combining religious, racial, and republican ideological elements.
Texas Revolution
The 1835–1836 rebellion of Anglo-American settlers and some Tejanos against Mexican authority, resulting in the creation of the independent Republic of Texas.
Republic of Texas
The independent slaveholding nation established after Texas declared independence from Mexico in 1836; it existed until annexation by the United States in 1845.
Empresario
Under Spanish and then Mexican law, a land agent who recruited settlers to colonize a specified area in exchange for land grants; brought large numbers of Anglo-Americans to Texas in the 1820s and 1830s.
Tejano/a
A person of Mexican or Spanish heritage born in Texas; Tejanos had complex experiences during and after the Texas Revolution and annexation.
Annexation
The formal incorporation of a territory or state into an existing nation; Texas was annexed by the United States in 1845 through a joint resolution of Congress.
Oregon Trail
The approximately two-thousand-mile overland route from Independence, Missouri to the Willamette Valley of Oregon, traveled by hundreds of thousands of American migrants between the 1840s and 1860s.
Oregon Treaty
The 1846 treaty between the United States and Britain that extended the boundary between the U.S. and British Canada at the 49th parallel to the Pacific coast.
Mexican-American War
The armed conflict between the United States and Mexico from 1846 to 1848, triggered by the annexation of Texas and the dispute over the Rio Grande boundary; resulted in the cession of vast territories from Mexico to the United States.
Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo
The February 1848 peace treaty ending the Mexican-American War; Mexico ceded approximately 529,000 square miles to the United States and received $15 million in payment; the treaty promised to protect the civil and property rights of Mexican citizens in the ceded territories.
Cession
Territory formally transferred from one nation to another, typically as part of a peace treaty.
Californio
A Spanish-speaking Mexican citizen of California who typically held large rancho land grants under Spanish and Mexican law; many Californios lost their lands after U.S. conquest despite treaty protections.
Rancho
A large land-grant estate in California and the Southwest, typically devoted to cattle ranching; the rancho system was disrupted by U.S. conquest and the California Land Act of 1851.
Bear Flag Republic
A short-lived (June–July 1846) insurgent republic declared in California by Anglo-American settlers just before U.S. naval forces arrived to claim California during the Mexican-American War.
Gold Rush
The mass migration to California following the January 1848 discovery of gold at Sutter's Mill; by 1849, tens of thousands of people from around the world had arrived in California seeking gold.
Forty-Niners
The migrants who came to California in 1849 seeking gold; included Americans from all regions, as well as Mexicans, Chileans, Chinese, Australians, and Europeans.
Sutter's Mill
The sawmill on the American River in California's Sierra Nevada foothills where gold was discovered on January 24, 1848; the starting point of the California Gold Rush.
Wilmot Proviso
A proposed congressional measure, introduced by Pennsylvania Congressman David Wilmot in 1846, that would have prohibited slavery in any territory acquired from Mexico; it passed the House but failed in the Senate.
Popular sovereignty
The political principle that the settlers of a territory should decide for themselves whether to permit or ban slavery through their territorial legislature; championed by Senator Stephen A. Douglas.
Compromise of 1850
A series of congressional measures designed to resolve the sectional crisis generated by the territories acquired from Mexico; admitted California as a free state, organized New Mexico and Utah as territories with popular sovereignty, and passed a stronger Fugitive Slave Act.
Fugitive Slave Act (1850)
A federal law requiring northerners to assist in the capture and return of escaped enslaved people; deeply controversial in the North, where it made the reality of slavery impossible to ignore.
Borderlands
Geographic and cultural zones where different nations, peoples, languages, and cultural systems meet and interact; particularly used to describe the U.S.-Mexico Southwest.
Comanchería
The vast territory across the southern Great Plains (present-day Texas, Oklahoma, Kansas, and New Mexico) controlled by the Comanche nation in the mid-nineteenth century.
Gadsden Purchase
The 1853 acquisition by the United States of a small strip of territory in present-day southern Arizona and New Mexico from Mexico for $10 million; motivated largely by the desire to secure a southern transcontinental railroad route.
Filibuster
An unauthorized military expedition into a foreign country; Anglo-American filibusters repeatedly invaded Texas and other Mexican territories in the early nineteenth century before formal annexation.
Sectional crisis
The growing political conflict between the North and the South over slavery, particularly over whether slavery would be permitted in new western territories.
Bolton thesis / Borderlands historiography
The historiographical tradition, beginning with Herbert Eugene Bolton, that treats Spanish and Mexican North America as historically significant in its own right, not merely as a prelude to Anglo settlement.
Foreign Miners' Tax
An 1850 California law imposing heavy fees on non-Anglo miners, used to exclude Mexican, Chilean, and Chinese miners from the gold fields.
"All of Mexico" movement
A faction of American expansionists who argued, unsuccessfully, that the United States should annex the entire Mexican republic rather than only its northern territories.
Spot Resolutions
Resolutions introduced by Congressman Abraham Lincoln in 1847 challenging President Polk to identify the exact spot where American blood had been shed, questioning the war's justification.
California Land Act of 1851
A federal law requiring Mexican land grant holders in California to prove the validity of their titles before a land commission; the costly process led many Californio families to lose their land.
Fort Laramie Treaty (1851)
An agreement attempting to define territories for major Plains nations in exchange for government payments and safe passage for settlers on the Oregon Trail; violated almost immediately by settler encroachment.
Dred Scott v. Sandford
The 1857 Supreme Court decision ruling that Congress had no power to prohibit slavery in the territories, invalidating the Missouri Compromise and intensifying the sectional crisis rooted in the 1840s territorial expansion.
Reservation policy
The federal policy, beginning to emerge in the 1840s and 1850s, of confining Native nations to defined territories to facilitate American settlement of the lands they formerly controlled.

Tracing the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo and Its Legacy

The Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo, signed on February 2, 1848, is one of the most important and least-studied documents in American legal and political history. It transferred half of Mexico's territory to the United States, but it also made specific promises — about the rights of Mexican citizens, about land grants, about language and religion — that were systematically violated in the decades that followed. This project asks you to research the treaty, its provisions, and its legacy, and to evaluate the gap between what the treaty promised and what the United States actually delivered.

  1. Read the Treaty. The Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo is a public domain document available in multiple archives and online collections, including the Yale Avalon Project and the Library of Congress. Read Articles VIII, IX, and X carefully. What specific rights did the treaty guarantee to Mexican citizens in the ceded territories?
  2. Research the California Land Act of 1851. The act required Mexican land grant holders to prove the validity of their grants before a land commission. Research how the commission operated, how many grants were confirmed, and how much land Californio families ultimately retained or lost. The Bancroft Library at the University of California, Berkeley holds extensive records related to this process.
  3. Research a specific case. Choose one Californio or Tejano family whose land was lost or contested after the Mexican-American War. The Bandini family, the Vallejo family, or the de la Guerra family of Santa Barbara are well-documented examples in California. Research their history before and after the conquest and describe what happened to their land and their social status.
  4. Research contemporary legal debates. The Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo is still cited in contemporary legal cases and political debates, particularly those involving land rights in New Mexico and the legal status of Indigenous communities whose land was also affected by the treaty. Research one contemporary case or debate in which the treaty has been invoked.
  5. Write and present. Write a research report of 1,500–2,500 words that (a) explains the treaty's key provisions, (b) documents how those provisions were violated or upheld in specific cases, (c) analyzes why the violations occurred, and (d) connects the historical violations to contemporary issues involving the U.S.-Mexico border region, land rights, or immigration policy. Where possible, use primary sources alongside secondary historical scholarship.

Library resources to use: The Yale Avalon Project's primary source collection, the Library of Congress's online primary source collection, and secondary works including David Weber's The Mexican Frontier, 1821–1846, Leonard Pitt's The Decline of the Californios, and David Montejano's Anglos and Mexicans in the Making of Texas, 1836–1986.

Chapter Reflection

Chapter 23 has followed the United States through one of its most dramatic decades of transformation: the acquisition of more than a million square miles of new territory through a combination of diplomatic negotiation and military conquest, the ideological justification of that expansion through the doctrine of Manifest Destiny, and the unleashing of a political crisis over slavery that would ultimately destroy the fragile compromises holding the republic together. Looking back across the chapter, several themes stand out as especially important for understanding not just the 1840s but the arc of U.S. history as a whole.

The first theme is the relationship between idealism and power. Manifest Destiny was a genuine belief for many Americans — not simply a cynical cover story, but a sincere conviction that the spread of American republican institutions was good for humanity. Yet those idealistic convictions were inseparable from racial hierarchy, the maintenance of slavery, and the violent denial of the rights and lives of those who stood in the path of expansion. The history of the 1840s demonstrates that idealism and violence, liberty and conquest, can coexist — and that understanding how they coexist is one of the central challenges of reading American history honestly.

The second theme is the persistence of those who resisted and survived. The narrative of Manifest Destiny constructed expansion as irresistible, natural, and inevitable. But the peoples who experienced it did not simply disappear or accept their subordination. Comanche leaders negotiated, resisted, and defended their territory for decades. Tejano families fought in court to protect their land grants. Mexican communities in New Mexico and California maintained their language, religion, and cultural practices through generations of Anglo-American pressure. California's surviving Native peoples worked to preserve their identities and would eventually, in the twentieth century, begin the long legal and political struggle to reclaim sovereignty and recognition. The history of resistance, adaptation, and survival is as much a part of the story of westward expansion as the history of conquest.

The third theme is the inseparability of expansion and the slavery crisis. Every territorial acquisition in the 1840s reopened the question that the Constitution had refused to resolve: would slavery expand, or would it be contained? The Wilmot Proviso, the popular sovereignty debate, the Compromise of 1850 — all were consequences of the territorial revolution of the 1840s. The Mexican-American War, designed in part by southern slaveholders to extend the domain of slave-based agriculture, instead accelerated the political crisis that would destroy the slaveholding republic. History rarely moves in straight lines, and the men who launched the war for expansion could not have predicted that it would ultimately contribute to the conditions that led to slavery's destruction.

Finally, the chapter asks students to think about the relationship between past and present in a region that continues to be shaped by the legacies of conquest. The U.S.-Mexico border that was drawn by the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo and the Gadsden Purchase continues to be one of the most politically fraught boundaries in the world. The land rights of Indigenous and Mexican-heritage communities in the Southwest remain contested. The cultural diversity of the borderlands — the Spanish language, the ranching economy, the Catholic Church, the blended foodways and music and traditions — reflects the layered history of a region that was Mexican before it was American, Indigenous before it was Mexican, and that has never been exclusively or simply any single thing. Understanding that history, in all its complexity, is one of the purposes a textbook like this one is designed to serve.

The United States that emerged from the 1840s was a continental empire — larger, more powerful, and more diverse than it had been at the beginning of the decade. It was also a republic more deeply divided, more politically volatile, and more morally compromised than before. The next chapters will trace how those divisions and contradictions played out in the crisis of the 1850s and the catastrophe of the Civil War — events whose seeds were planted, in part, in the expansionist decade this chapter has examined.

Part IV — Founding the Republic

The Early Republic and the First Party System

United States History: Indigenous America, Republic, Conflict, and Change

Chapter Opening

"The experiment of self-government was not merely a political question in 1789. It was a question about whether the human capacity for reason and virtue was sufficient to sustain republican institutions — and no one knew the answer."

In the spring of 1789, George Washington rode into New York City — the nation's first capital — through crowds of cheering citizens who lined the streets, hung from windows, and called out his name. Church bells rang. Ships in the harbor fired their guns in salute. The procession was jubilant, but Washington himself was grave. He understood what no celebration could fully obscure: that the United States of America was an experiment with no guarantee of success. Republics were rare in the Atlantic world. Monarchies surrounded the new nation on every side. The Constitution had been ratified only months before, and its provisions remained untested. No one knew whether a large, diverse, geographically sprawling nation could hold itself together under a representative government chosen by popular election. Washington knew that everything he did in the coming months and years — every precedent he set, every cabinet he appointed, every decision he made about ceremony and authority — would shape what the American presidency became. He was not merely the first president. He was creating the template for all who would follow.

What followed Washington's inauguration was one of the most consequential and contentious decades in American political history. The 1790s saw the formation of the first national government under the Constitution, the construction of a national financial system, the birth of the first American political parties, the eruption of armed domestic resistance to federal taxation, the challenge of remaining neutral as Europe descended into revolutionary war, the passage of the most severe restrictions on civil liberties in the young nation's history, and the first peaceful transfer of executive power between opposing political parties in the nation's history. Each of these developments shaped American political life in ways that reverberate to the present day.

This chapter is not simply a story about presidents and politics. It is the story of how ordinary Americans argued, organized, petitioned, marched, and voted to shape the new government. It is the story of farmers in western Pennsylvania who took up arms against a federal tax. It is the story of immigrants and political refugees who feared a government that could deport them for their opinions. It is the story of enslaved people who petitioned Congress, who escaped to freedom, and who watched as the Constitution's protections for slavery were given their first legal form. It is the story of Native nations who negotiated, resisted, and suffered as the new republic pressed westward. It is the story of women who shaped political culture through conversation, correspondence, domestic ritual, and moral suasion even as formal politics remained closed to them.

The defining argument of this chapter is that the early republic was shaped by a fundamental conflict about what kind of nation the United States would be. Alexander Hamilton wanted a powerful national government capable of directing a commercial and manufacturing economy, building alliances with Britain, and suppressing domestic disorder. Thomas Jefferson and James Madison wanted a republic of independent farmers, suspicious of concentrated power, committed to states' rights, and aligned with France's revolutionary experiment. These were not merely abstract philosophical differences. They were arguments about taxation, banking, war, sovereignty, and who would hold power. From their conflict emerged the first American political parties — the Federalists and the Democratic-Republicans — and with them, the tradition of organized partisan competition that continues to define American democracy.

The decade of the 1790s was also a time when the contradictions of the American founding were made vivid. A republic founded on principles of liberty and equality operated an economy that depended on enslaved labor. A government that proclaimed freedom of speech and press passed the Alien and Sedition Acts, which made it a crime to criticize the president. A nation that declared independence in the name of human rights simultaneously dispossessed Native peoples of their lands through military force and fraudulent treaties. These contradictions were not hidden from Americans of the time — they debated them fiercely, in newspapers, pamphlets, legislative halls, and church meetings. Understanding the early republic requires taking these contradictions seriously — not as curiosities but as the central tensions that defined what the United States was and what it would become.

The election of 1800, which this chapter concludes by examining, was one of the most dramatic in American history. The Federalists and Democratic-Republicans contested the presidency with extraordinary bitterness, exchanging charges of tyranny, treason, atheism, and aristocracy. The election ended in a tie in the Electoral College, was thrown into the House of Representatives, and required thirty-six ballots before Thomas Jefferson was named president. What happened next was remarkable: the Federalists, who held the presidency and controlled the military, peacefully surrendered power to their opponents. The peaceful transfer of power from one political party to another — what Jefferson called a "revolution" — established a precedent of democratic legitimacy that few nations in the world had managed to achieve. The early republic was fragile, contentious, and full of failure, but it also produced moments of genuine democratic achievement that shaped the future of self-government.

Students who engage with this chapter will find not a simple story of heroes and villains, but a story of brilliant, flawed, and visionary people constructing a new kind of government under conditions of extraordinary uncertainty. They will find that the founding generation's debates about power, liberty, and the scope of government have never been permanently resolved — they have only been transformed into the arguments of every subsequent American era. And they will find that the republic's promises of equality and democracy, proclaimed in its founding documents, were from the beginning promises that included some Americans and excluded others, and that the history of the United States is in large part the history of struggles to make those promises real for all.

Historical Context

When George Washington took the oath of office on April 30, 1789, the United States government consisted of little more than the Constitution itself. There was no army to speak of, no navy, no treasury system, no established courts, no diplomatic corps, and no administrative machinery to carry out the laws Congress would pass. Everything had to be built from the ground up, and every choice about how to build it carried political consequences that its makers could only partially foresee.

The context in which the new government operated was shaped by four overlapping realities. First, the legacy of revolution was still fresh. The men who gathered in Philadelphia, New York, and later Washington to govern the new republic had lived through the experience of fighting a monarchical empire they believed had violated their constitutional rights. They were deeply sensitive — sometimes pathologically so — to any sign that the new government might itself become tyrannical. Every exercise of federal authority was scrutinized for signs of overreach.

Second, the new nation was economically fragile. The Revolutionary War had left the federal government deeply in debt, both to foreign creditors (primarily France and the Netherlands) and to domestic creditors who had purchased war bonds and provided supplies for the Continental Army. The states were also in debt. The currency was unstable. Credit was scarce. Trade was disrupted. Some political leaders, particularly Hamilton, believed that the nation's economic weakness was its greatest vulnerability and that building a strong financial system was the most urgent task facing the new government.

Third, the Atlantic world was being transformed by the French Revolution, which began in 1789 — the same year the new American government took office. The French Revolution quickly became a global ideological conflict. In its early stages, many Americans celebrated it as confirmation that the American example was inspiring freedom around the world. But as the Revolution grew more radical — executing the king, beginning the Terror, waging revolutionary war against the monarchies of Europe — Americans divided sharply over what to make of it. These divisions about Europe mapped onto divisions about domestic politics in ways that sharpened the conflicts of the 1790s into something approaching a crisis.

Fourth, the borders of the new republic were contested. To the north, Britain retained military posts on American soil it had agreed to evacuate. To the south, Spain controlled Florida and the mouth of the Mississippi River. Native nations occupied much of the land west of the original states, with their own diplomatic relationships, military capabilities, and claims. The new government had to navigate all of these challenges while building itself from nothing and maintaining the confidence of its citizens.

Major People, Groups, and Places

First president of the United States. Virginia planter and Revolutionary War commander who served two terms and deliberately set constitutional precedents. His decision to step down after two terms established the tradition that lasted until 1940.

First secretary of the treasury. Born in the Caribbean, Hamilton was a self-made lawyer and financial visionary who advocated strong national government, a national bank, and a commercial economy. His financial program shaped the American state and generated the first great partisan conflict.

First secretary of state and third president. Virginia planter, author of the Declaration of Independence, and Hamilton's great adversary. James Madison (1751–1836)
Virginia congressman and Jefferson's political partner. Principal architect of the Constitution who became the leading organizer of the Democratic-Republican Party in Congress and co-authored the Virginia Resolutions against the Alien and Sedition Acts.
John Adams (1735–1826)
First vice president and second president. Massachusetts lawyer and diplomat whose presidency was defined by the Quasi-War, the XYZ Affair, and the Alien and Sedition Acts.

Section 16.1

Washington and the New Government

On the morning of April 30, 1789, George Washington placed his hand on a Bible at Federal Hall in New York City and swore to "faithfully execute the Office of President of the United States" and to "preserve, protect and defend the Constitution of the United States." He then delivered an inaugural address notable for its solemnity and its almost anxious humility. Washington spoke of his "incapacity as well as disinclination" for civil administration and his "fervent supplications" to "the Benign Parent of the Human Race" for guidance. Whether or not the self-deprecation was entirely sincere, the tone was deliberate. Washington understood that the manner in which he exercised power would determine whether the republic survived its first years.

The Constitution Washington had been elected to administer was a document of surprising brevity. It established the three branches of government, specified their basic powers and relationships, and left enormous questions unanswered. How large should the president's staff be? What ceremonies should attend the office? How should the president consult with Congress? What powers did the executive possess in foreign affairs? How should the nation's debts be managed? The Constitution did not answer these questions. Washington and Congress had to answer them through practice.

Washington's first major decisions concerned the organization of the executive branch. Congress created three executive departments — the Department of State, the Department of the Treasury, and the Department of War — along with the offices of attorney general and postmaster general. Washington chose his department heads carefully and with attention to regional balance. He appointed Thomas Jefferson of Virginia as secretary of state, Alexander Hamilton of New York as secretary of the treasury, Henry Knox of Massachusetts as secretary of war, and Edmund Randolph of Virginia as attorney general. These men formed what Washington called his "Cabinet" — a word not found in the Constitution but a concept that became a permanent feature of the American executive. Washington consulted them both individually and collectively, establishing the precedent of executive advisory councils that has persisted ever since.

The ceremonial dimension of the presidency occupied Washington from the beginning. He was acutely aware that in a republic, ceremony had to strike a delicate balance: impressive enough to command respect, restrained enough not to suggest monarchy. He rejected suggestions that he be addressed as "His Highness" or "His Excellency," insisting instead on "Mr. President." He held regular public receptions but conducted them with formal distance. He traveled with an elaborate coach and retinue but resisted the most extravagant courtly customs. These choices were contested. Some critics thought Washington's receptions were too regal; others thought the simplicity of the republican title "Mr. President" undermined necessary dignity.

Washington set a critical precedent in his management of the relationship between the executive and legislative branches. The Constitution gave the Senate the power to "advise and consent" on treaties, and Washington initially interpreted this literally: he appeared before the Senate in person to seek their advice before negotiating a treaty. The experiment was a failure. Senators were uncomfortable deliberating in the president's presence, Washington was uncomfortable waiting, and the session dissolved into confusion. Washington left in frustration, never appeared before the Senate for advice again, and established the precedent that presidents negotiate treaties first and submit them for Senate approval afterward.

Washington set another critical precedent when he left office. After two terms — eight years — he refused a third term, returning to his Virginia plantation. The Constitution imposed no term limit; Washington could theoretically have served for life. His voluntary retirement was widely celebrated as a republican virtue, an act of disinterested service that demonstrated the constitutional order's legitimacy. The two-term tradition became so powerful that it stood until Franklin Roosevelt's third-term election in 1940, and was eventually codified in the Twenty-Second Amendment (1951).

The Congress that worked alongside Washington was equally engaged in creating precedent. The First Congress passed the Judiciary Act of 1789, which fleshed out the federal court system the Constitution had only sketched. The act established the number of Supreme Court justices (initially six), created a system of federal circuit and district courts, and defined the Supreme Court's appellate jurisdiction. Congress also passed the Bill of Rights — ten amendments protecting individual liberties and reserving powers to the states — ratified in 1791. These amendments addressed the major objections Anti-Federalists had raised during the ratification debates: guaranteeing freedom of speech, press, religion, and assembly; protection from unreasonable searches; the right to trial by jury; and protection from cruel punishment.

Washington's presidency was not merely administrative. It was a performance of legitimate power in a nation that had just fought a revolution against illegitimate power. Every choice — about ceremony, about personnel, about the scope of executive authority — carried symbolic as well as practical weight. The fact that the republic survived its first decade was not inevitable. It required the choices of many people, Washington prominently among them, to make it work.

Section 16.2

Hamilton's Financial Plan

No figure in the early republic was more consequential, or more polarizing, than Alexander Hamilton. Born out of wedlock in the West Indies — a circumstance that would have permanently limited his prospects in the hierarchical societies of the Caribbean — Hamilton came to New York as a young man, fought brilliantly in the Revolution, studied law, married into the Schuyler family (one of New York's most powerful patrician clans), and emerged in the 1780s as one of the most forceful advocates for a strong national government. As Washington's secretary of the treasury, he produced a series of reports on public finance that laid out an ambitious vision for American economic development — and that generated the most intense political controversy of the era.

Hamilton's first report, submitted to Congress in January 1790, addressed the problem of the national debt. The United States owed approximately $54 million to foreign and domestic creditors — money borrowed to fight the Revolutionary War. Hamilton proposed that the federal government honor this debt in full, paying its face value to whoever held the bonds. This was controversial for several reasons. Many of the original bondholders — soldiers, farmers, merchants who had accepted bonds in lieu of payment — had sold them at deep discounts to speculators who expected the government to default. If Hamilton's plan was adopted, those speculators would profit enormously at the expense of the original creditors. Hamilton's response was essentially financial: credit required confidence, confidence required reliability, and reliability required honoring obligations in full, whoever held them.

The second element of Hamilton's debt plan was more politically explosive: he proposed that the federal government "assume" the remaining wartime debts of the individual states — approximately $25 million. This proposal split Congress along regional lines that would persist for decades. States that had already paid down much of their war debt (primarily in the South, especially Virginia) objected strenuously to being taxed to pay the debts of states that had not. Hamilton's assumption plan seemed to them a transfer of wealth from responsible states to irresponsible ones — and, more troublingly, a mechanism for increasing federal power at the expense of state autonomy. Madison led the opposition in the House.

The assumption plan passed in one of the first great political deals in American history. Jefferson, newly returned from France and serving as secretary of state, brokered a dinner meeting at which Hamilton and Madison agreed: Virginia would provide enough votes to pass assumption in exchange for Hamilton's agreement to support locating the new permanent national capital on the Potomac River, on the border between Virginia and Maryland. The "Compromise of 1790" produced both the assumption of state debts and the future District of Columbia.

Hamilton's second major proposal was a national bank. Chartered by Congress for twenty years in 1791, the Bank of the United States would serve as the government's fiscal agent, hold federal deposits, issue bank notes, and provide commercial credit. Modeled on the Bank of England, it would be jointly owned by the government and private investors. Washington asked both Hamilton and Jefferson to advise him on the bank's constitutionality before signing the charter.

Jefferson's argument against the bank became the foundation of what historians call "strict construction." The Constitution, he argued, granted Congress only those powers explicitly listed. Chartering a bank was not among them. The "necessary and proper" clause — which gave Congress power to make laws "necessary and proper" for carrying out its enumerated functions — did not, Jefferson contended, mean "convenient" or "useful." It meant truly essential. The bank was not essential; the government had managed its finances without one.

Hamilton's defense of the bank — sent to Washington in February 1791 — became the founding argument for "implied powers." The word "necessary," Hamilton argued, did not mean "absolutely indispensable." It meant what it commonly meant in everyday usage: "needful, requisite, incidental, useful, or conducive to." The "necessary and proper" clause was meant to enlarge, not restrict, Congress's powers. If the Constitution gave Congress the power to collect taxes and borrow money, it implied the power to create institutions for doing so. Washington accepted Hamilton's argument and signed the bank charter. The question of implied powers would recur throughout American constitutional history.

Hamilton's third major report, on manufactures, submitted in December 1791, proposed that the federal government actively promote American manufacturing through protective tariffs, subsidies, and infrastructure investment. Hamilton believed that a nation dependent on agriculture alone was permanently vulnerable. A diversified economy that combined agriculture, commerce, and manufacturing would be stronger, more self-sufficient, and better positioned to defend itself. The report was visionary in its scope — Hamilton was describing, decades before it began, the kind of industrial economy the United States would develop in the nineteenth century. But Congress rejected most of its specific proposals. Agrarian interests were too strong.

To fund the national debt and the new government's operations, Hamilton proposed an excise tax on distilled spirits — whiskey. The tax was a logical choice on financial grounds: distilling was widespread, whiskey was a commodity rather than a necessity, and excise taxes were administratively simpler than customs duties in the interior. But the whiskey tax fell with particular harshness on western farmers who used whiskey as currency and resented the federal government's reach into their economic lives. The resistance to this tax would produce the most serious domestic crisis of Washington's presidency.

Hamilton's financial program succeeded in its primary goals. The federal government assumed the war debts, restored public credit, established a functioning national bank, and created revenue streams sufficient to operate the government. The cost was the emergence of a fierce political opposition convinced that Hamilton's program benefited wealthy speculators and merchants at the expense of farmers and ordinary citizens, and that it was concentrating dangerous power in the federal government. From that opposition would grow the first American political party.

Historical Vocabulary
Implied Powers / Strict Construction

The debate over the Bank of the United States produced two enduring constitutional doctrines. Strict construction, argued by Jefferson, held that Congress possessed only those powers explicitly listed in the Constitution. Implied powers (also called loose construction), argued by Hamilton, held that the "necessary and proper" clause granted Congress the authority to create any institutions reasonably useful to carrying out its listed functions.

Washington's decision to accept Hamilton's argument and sign the bank charter established implied powers as a working constitutional principle. Chief Justice John Marshall would confirm and expand this doctrine in McCulloch v. Maryland (1819), when the Supreme Court unanimously ruled that Congress had the implied power to charter a bank and that states could not tax it. The debate between strict and loose construction has never been fully resolved; it resurfaces whenever Americans argue about the limits of federal authority.

Section 16.3

Jefferson, Hamilton, and Political Conflict

The conflict between Thomas Jefferson and Alexander Hamilton was one of the most consequential rivalries in American political history. Both men were brilliant, both were committed to the republic, and both served in Washington's cabinet. But their visions of what the republic should be were deeply incompatible, and their disagreements drove the formation of the first American party system.

Hamilton's vision was of a commercial republic — a nation in which property rights were secure, credit was abundant, manufacturing grew alongside agriculture, a professional military guarded national security, and the federal government exercised broad powers to promote economic development and maintain order. He was comfortable with inequality, believing that the energies of ambition and self-interest, channeled through markets and institutions, would generate national wealth. He admired the British constitutional system and favored close relations with Britain over France. He was skeptical of the French Revolution from early on, believing that a republic required stable institutions and that revolutionary enthusiasm without institutional restraint produced chaos.

Jefferson's vision was of an agrarian republic — a nation of independent farmer-citizens who owned their land, produced what they consumed, participated in local democratic institutions, and maintained the economic independence that sustained political freedom. He was deeply suspicious of concentrated economic power, whether in the form of banks, corporations, or federal institutions. He feared that Hamilton's program would create exactly the kind of dependent, corrupted, deferential society that he associated with the monarchies of Europe. He celebrated the French Revolution long after it became violent, believing that liberty was worth the price of some disorder.

These differences were not merely philosophical. They were rooted in the different social worlds the two men knew. Hamilton's America was the America of the Atlantic commercial cities — New York, Philadelphia, Boston — where merchants, lawyers, bankers, and manufacturers were building the financial infrastructure of a commercial economy. Jefferson's America was the agrarian South and West — the plantation districts of Virginia, the small farms of the backcountry — where land and labor, not credit and commerce, were the foundations of economic life. Their conflict was, in part, a conflict between these two Americas.

The specific trigger for their rupture within Washington's cabinet was Hamilton's bank proposal. Jefferson and Madison concluded that Hamilton's interpretation of the Constitution, if accepted, would give Congress effectively unlimited power. This was not, to Jefferson and Madison, an abstract constitutional concern. It was a threat to the social order of the agrarian South — to the ability of states to manage their own economies, regulate their own institutions (including the institution of slavery), and protect the interests of agricultural property against the interests of commercial and financial capital.

Jefferson and Madison began organizing political opposition to Hamilton's program through the press. They recruited the poet Philip Freneau to found the National Gazette in Philadelphia in 1791, which became the principal organ of Democratic-Republican opinion. Hamilton responded through the Gazette of the United States, edited by John Fenno. These newspapers waged political warfare with an intensity that shocked contemporaries. Personal attacks, accusations of treason, comparisons to Cromwell and Caesar — the political journalism of the 1790s made later American politics look restrained by comparison.

The tensions within Washington's cabinet eventually became unbearable. Jefferson resigned as secretary of state in December 1793. Hamilton resigned as secretary of the treasury in January 1795. Both departed to continue their political activities outside the cabinet, and Washington, who genuinely hated partisan conflict, spent the final years of his presidency watching the country divide into opposing camps that regarded each other with something approaching political hatred.

Washington's Farewell Address — published in September 1796, as he prepared to retire — addressed the dangers of "the spirit of Party" directly and memorably. Washington warned that party spirit "agitates the Community with ill-founded jealousies and false alarms, kindles the animosity of one part against another, foments occasionally riot and insurrection." He called political parties "potent engines, by which cunning, ambitious, and unprincipled men will be enabled to subvert the power of the People." Subsequent American history did nothing to make these warnings seem exaggerated. But they also proved impossible to heed. Political parties were not an accident; they were the natural consequence of genuine disagreements about power, policy, and the character of the republic.

Section 16.4

The First Party System

The political parties that formed in the 1790s were not the organized, institutionally sophisticated parties of the nineteenth and twentieth centuries. They lacked permanent headquarters, formal membership rolls, official platforms, or nominating conventions. What they had was a shared set of political principles, a network of newspapers and political leaders who communicated them, and the ability to coordinate electoral activity around those principles. That was enough to make them the central feature of American political life for more than a decade.

The Federalists — the party of Hamilton, Adams, and the advocates of strong national government — drew their core support from New England, where the culture of deference to established authority blended with the commercial interests of merchants and manufacturers who benefited from Hamilton's financial program. They also drew support from the urban professional classes of the Middle Atlantic states — lawyers, ministers, newspaper editors — who tended to associate order, stability, and deference to authority with good government. The Federalists were the party of the Atlantic seaboard, of commercial capitalism, of the educated establishment.

The Democratic-Republicans — the party of Jefferson and Madison — drew broader support. Their core was the agrarian South, where planters resented federal interference with their economic and social order (including slavery). But they also drew strong support from western farmers who distrusted banks, speculators, and distant federal authorities, and from urban artisans and mechanics who resented what they saw as an aristocracy of wealth being created by Hamiltonian finance. Many recent immigrants — particularly the Irish, who had their own reasons to hate the British government that Hamilton seemed to admire — aligned with the Democratic-Republicans.

The parties organized around local committees, political clubs, and above all newspapers. The 1790s saw an explosion of partisan journalism. Every major city had multiple competing newspapers aligned with competing parties, and the boundary between journalism and political activism was essentially nonexistent. Editors were organizers. Printers were partisans. Readers were participants in a raucous, often vicious public debate about the character of the republic.

Women participated in this political culture even though they could not vote. Elite women hosted the salons and dinner parties where political networks formed and political intelligence circulated. They corresponded with political figures and with each other about political affairs. They read newspapers and wrote letters. Abigail Adams was among the sharpest political observers of the era, and her correspondence with her husband constitutes one of the richest sources for understanding the politics of the 1790s. Women also organized public demonstrations of partisan sentiment — wearing party colors, attending political dinners, signing petitions — that blurred the line between formal political participation (which was closed to them) and informal political influence (which was not).

African Americans were almost entirely excluded from formal political participation. In most states, free Black men who met property requirements could theoretically vote, and some did. But intimidation, hostility, and legal restrictions steadily narrowed even this limited participation. Enslaved people had no formal political standing at all. Yet they were deeply affected by the politics of the 1790s, and they were political actors in their own right — through resistance, petition, escape, and in some cases armed rebellion. The Haitian Revolution — the massive slave uprising in Saint-Domingue that began in 1791 and eventually produced the first Black republic in the Western Hemisphere — reverberated through American political life, terrifying Southern slaveholders and inspiring enslaved people. Gabriel Prosser's planned rebellion in Virginia in 1800 demonstrated that enslaved people were watching the rhetoric of liberty and drawing their own conclusions.

The Federalists won the elections of 1788, 1792, and 1796. Washington ran unopposed in the first two, and John Adams narrowly won the third, defeating Jefferson by three electoral votes. But the Democratic-Republicans were building steadily, and the combination of Federalist overreach in the late 1790s and the energy of the Democratic-Republican organizational network would eventually produce the decisive electoral shift of 1800.

Historical Vocabulary
Political Party

A political party is an organized group of citizens who share broadly similar political views and work together to win elections, control government, and put their policies into effect. The framers of the Constitution generally opposed political parties, which they called "factions," because they feared that organized partisanship would put narrow interests above the public good. Yet parties emerged almost immediately under the new government, because they were the most effective way for like-minded citizens to coordinate their political activities.

The Federalists and Democratic-Republicans of the 1790s were the first American parties, and though both eventually dissolved, the tradition of organized partisan competition they established became permanent. All subsequent American political history has operated within the framework of two competing parties — the specific parties changing in name and coalition, but the basic structure of two-party competition remaining constant.

Section 16.5

The Whiskey Rebellion and Federal Authority

The Whiskey Rebellion of 1794 was the most serious domestic challenge to federal authority in the Washington administration and one of the most revealing episodes in early American political history. It began as a tax protest, escalated into open armed resistance, and ended with Washington personally leading an army to suppress it — the only time in American history that a sitting president has commanded troops in the field. The rebellion illuminated the tensions between federal authority and local autonomy that ran through all of early American politics.

The background was Hamilton's excise tax on distilled spirits, passed by Congress in 1791. The tax seemed straightforward on paper: a levy on whiskey production, graduated by proof, that Hamilton intended as a source of domestic revenue. In practice, it bore disproportionately on the farmers of western Pennsylvania, the Kentucky frontier, and the Carolina and Virginia backcountry, who had made whiskey production a central part of their economic lives. In cash-scarce economies where hard money was rare, whiskey served as a practical currency: it was compact, storable, tradeable, and universally valued. A tax on whiskey was not merely an inconvenience; it was an attack on the medium of economic exchange in communities where commercial agriculture was impossible.

The farmers of western Pennsylvania also had grievances beyond the whiskey tax itself. They resented that cases arising under the excise law were to be tried in federal district courts, requiring travel to Philadelphia at enormous cost in time and money. They believed the tax was designed to benefit eastern merchants and speculators at their expense. They felt ignored by a federal government that had failed to open the Mississippi River to American commerce, failed to eliminate British garrisons from the Northwest, and failed to provide effective protection against Native nations on the frontier. Their resistance to the whiskey tax was inseparable from a broader sense of political abandonment.

From 1791 to 1794, resistance in western Pennsylvania moved through several phases. Tax collectors were harassed, their papers destroyed, their persons threatened. Excise officers were tarred and feathered. Anti-tax meetings drew hundreds of participants. Organized "Democratic Societies" — local political clubs inspired partly by French revolutionary societies — coordinated opposition and spread a rhetoric of popular sovereignty that Federalists found deeply alarming. Hamilton, characteristically, saw in the Democratic Societies the hand of subversion, the American equivalent of the Jacobin clubs he associated with the worst of the French Revolution.

The crisis peaked in the summer of 1794 when a federal marshal attempted to serve papers on whiskey distillers who had refused to appear in court. An armed confrontation at the farm of a prominent local distiller left two men dead. Within days, a force of about five hundred armed men attacked the home of the regional excise supervisor, burning it to the ground. A mass meeting of perhaps seven thousand people considered marching on Pittsburgh. The rebellion seemed to be spreading.

Washington and Hamilton responded with overwhelming force. The president invoked the Militia Act of 1792, called up nearly thirteen thousand militia from four states, and personally rode at the head of the force for part of its march westward — the last time a sitting American president commanded troops in the field. By the time the army arrived in western Pennsylvania, the rebellion had already collapsed: the rebels had dispersed, their leaders had fled or been arrested, and the show of federal force met no armed resistance. Two men were eventually convicted of treason; both were pardoned by Washington.

The rebellion's suppression established several important precedents. It demonstrated that the federal government could enforce its laws against armed resistance. It showed that the Militia Act was a workable mechanism for mobilizing force without maintaining a large standing army. It also deepened political divisions: Federalists celebrated the restoration of order; Democratic-Republicans criticized what they saw as the use of military force to suppress legitimate political dissent. Jefferson privately questioned whether the rebellion had been as dangerous as Hamilton claimed, and suspected that Hamilton had used it to expand federal power beyond what necessity required.

The human dimensions of the Whiskey Rebellion deserve attention. The western Pennsylvania farmers who took up arms were not simply criminals or lawbreakers. They were communities with genuine grievances, operating in an economy that the federal government's policies had disadvantaged, living in a region that the federal government had consistently failed to protect. Their resistance was rooted in a tradition of popular sovereignty and direct action that went back through the Revolutionary era. They believed — with some justification — that the republic's promises of self-government and equitable taxation were not being kept. The federal government's response was constitutionally justified, but it was also a demonstration that the new republic's promises of equality and democracy had limits that were enforced at gunpoint.

Map Study

Western Pennsylvania and the Whiskey Rebellion, 1794

The geography of the Whiskey Rebellion is inseparable from the political conflict that produced it. Western Pennsylvania in the 1790s was separated from the eastern cities by the Allegheny Mountains — a formidable barrier that dramatically increased the cost of transporting bulk agricultural products eastward. What could be grown could not easily or profitably be sold in the East unless it was first converted into a more concentrated, transportable form. Whiskey was the logical solution: it could carry the value of many bushels of grain in a small, portable, durable container.

The Pittsburgh area — located at the confluence of the Allegheny and Monongahela Rivers, which together form the Ohio River — was the principal commercial center of the region. But the Ohio River, the natural highway westward, was also the gateway to the Mississippi, controlled by Spain at its mouth in New Orleans. Western farmers depended on river commerce for their economic survival, but their access to markets was controlled by a foreign power. The federal government's failure to resolve this situation was a major source of western grievance that was inseparable from resistance to the whiskey tax.

Note also how few roads connected the western settlements to the eastern cities. The National Road would not be built until the 1810s. Overland transport of goods was expensive, slow, and limited. The regions most affected by the whiskey tax were precisely those most disconnected from the eastern commercial economy that the tax was designed to serve.

Section 16.6

Foreign Policy and Neutrality

If domestic politics in the 1790s were contentious, foreign policy was an outright minefield. The French Revolution transformed the context of American diplomacy almost overnight. The 1778 Treaty of Alliance with France — the agreement that had brought French military and financial support during the American Revolution — was still technically in force. When France declared war on Britain in 1793, Americans had to decide whether they were bound by this alliance to support France against their former colonial master. The decision they made — neutrality — seemed obvious in retrospect, but it was not obvious at the time, and the way it was made, as well as the political conflicts it generated, shaped American foreign policy for generations.

Washington issued the Neutrality Proclamation in April 1793 without consulting Congress, declaring that the United States would "pursue a conduct friendly and impartial toward the belligerent Powers." The proclamation was sensible on its merits: the new nation was militarily weak, commercially dependent on Britain (which dominated American trade), financially fragile, and in no position to be drawn into a massive European war. But it was constitutionally controversial. Did the president have the power to declare neutrality unilaterally? The Constitution gave Congress the power to declare war; did it not implicitly require Congress to decide when the nation would not go to war? Hamilton argued for broad executive power in foreign affairs; Madison argued that the Constitution placed the decision with Congress. The debate was not resolved; it became a permanent feature of American constitutional argument.

The immediate crisis was the arrival of Edmond-Charles Genêt, the new French minister to the United States, who proceeded to commission American ships as French privateers and recruit American citizens for military expeditions against Spanish and British possessions — all in violation of American neutrality and without regard for American law. "Citizen Genêt" appealed directly to the American public over the heads of the government, confident that ordinary Americans' enthusiasm for the French Revolution would override Washington's caution. His confidence was misplaced. Washington demanded his recall, though he eventually gave Genêt asylum in the United States when the radical Jacobin government that had sent him took power in Paris and would certainly have guillotined him if he returned.

The Genêt affair strained U.S.-French relations and deepened domestic political conflict. Democratic-Republicans who admired the French Revolution and organized Democratic-Republican Societies to express their solidarity were accused by Federalists of being agents of foreign subversion. Washington, in remarks accompanying his Farewell Address, publicly condemned the Democratic Societies — an unusual step that suggested the president was aligning himself with the Federalist Party in his final months.

The challenge of managing relations with Britain was equally complex. Britain was at war with France and needed naval supremacy; it maintained this supremacy partly by seizing neutral ships suspected of trading with France and impressing their sailors into the Royal Navy — including sailors who claimed American citizenship. The practice was a direct violation of American sovereignty and a source of intense popular outrage. Britain also continued to occupy military posts in the American Northwest, in violation of the 1783 Treaty of Paris, and was suspected of providing military support to Native nations resisting American expansion.

Washington sent Chief Justice John Jay to London in 1794 to negotiate these disputes. The resulting Jay's Treaty, signed in November 1794, was deeply unpopular. Britain agreed to evacuate the western posts (something it had already agreed to do eleven years earlier) and to allow some American trade with the British West Indies under restrictive conditions. On the most urgent issues — impressment, compensation for seized ships, and the principles of neutral trade — Britain made no meaningful concessions. Jay's Treaty was not a good deal for the United States, but it had the signal advantage of avoiding war at a moment when war with Britain would have been catastrophic.

The treaty produced one of the most intense political explosions of the decade. Jay was burned in effigy across the country. Democratic-Republican newspapers called the treaty a betrayal of American sovereignty and a sellout to British aristocracy. The Senate ratified it by the minimum two-thirds margin, only after prolonged debate. Washington signed it with evident reluctance. Hamilton, defending the treaty in newspaper essays, was stoned by a crowd in New York. The Jay's Treaty controversy drew the clearest possible line between Federalists (who supported the treaty as the price of commercial stability) and Democratic-Republicans (who opposed it as a capitulation to British power).

The contrast with Pinckney's Treaty — signed with Spain in 1795 and ratified unanimously by the Senate — illuminated how different Jay's Treaty was. Pinckney's Treaty gave the United States free navigation of the Mississippi River, access to the port of New Orleans for American goods, Spanish recognition of the thirty-first parallel as the southern boundary, and a Spanish commitment to stop arming Native nations in the American South. It was, in short, exactly what western Americans had been demanding for years, and it was greeted with celebration. Together, Jay's and Pinckney's treaties resolved most of the outstanding post-Revolutionary boundary and commercial disputes.

Primary Source Window

Washington's Farewell Address (1796)

Creator: George Washington (written primarily by Alexander Hamilton)  ·  Date: September 1796  ·  Type: Published political address

Context: George Washington published his Farewell Address in September 1796, announcing his decision not to seek a third term and offering what he described as "the disinterested warnings of a parting friend." The address was never delivered as a speech; it was published in the American Daily Advertiser and reprinted widely. It was written primarily by Hamilton, based on earlier drafts by Madison and Washington himself.

Washington argued that the unity of the nation was its greatest strength, and that party spirit was its greatest domestic threat. He warned against "passionate attachments" to any foreign nation, because such attachments distorted judgment and created conflicts of interest. He called for commercial relations with all nations but political connections with none, stating that the "great rule of conduct" in foreign affairs was to extend commercial relations while having as little political connection as possible.

Analysis: Washington's address reflected his genuine beliefs but also served the Federalist political project. By warning against "permanent alliances," he was effectively arguing against the French alliance that Democratic-Republicans celebrated. By condemning party spirit, he was — despite his stated intention to stand above partisan conflict — taking a position that benefited the Federalists, who controlled the government. The address reveals both Washington's sincere concerns about the republic's fragility and the impossibility, even for Washington, of standing entirely above the partisan conflicts of the era.

Section 16.7

Alien and Sedition Acts

The Alien and Sedition Acts of 1798 were the most severe restriction on civil liberties in the history of the early republic, and they remain among the most controversial legislative episodes in American history. They were passed by the Federalist-controlled Congress during the political crisis generated by the XYZ Affair, justified as national security measures, and used primarily to suppress political opposition. They tested the meaning of the First Amendment almost as soon as it had been ratified, produced the first major states' rights constitutional arguments, and helped drive the Democratic-Republicans to their decisive electoral victory in 1800.

The XYZ Affair was the precipitating event. In 1797, President Adams sent three commissioners to Paris to negotiate with the new French government (the Directory), which had been seizing American merchant ships trading with Britain. Before negotiations could begin, three agents of the French foreign minister Talleyrand — designated X, Y, and Z in the documents Adams eventually published — demanded a bribe of $250,000 as a precondition for meeting with Talleyrand, and a loan of $12 million to France. The American commissioners refused, and the negotiations collapsed. When Adams reported the episode to Congress, it caused an explosion of anti-French sentiment. The slogan "Millions for defense but not one cent for tribute" encapsulated the public mood. Congress authorized the expansion of the navy, suspended the French alliance, and approved the creation of a new standing army. An undeclared naval war — the Quasi-War — broke out in the Caribbean.

In this atmosphere of crisis and fear, the Federalist-controlled Congress passed four related laws in 1798. The Naturalization Act extended the residency period for citizenship from five to fourteen years — a direct attack on immigrant communities that tended to vote Democratic-Republican. The Alien Friends Act gave the president power to deport non-citizens he deemed dangerous, without trial or appeal. The Alien Enemies Act authorized the president to deport citizens of enemy nations during wartime. The Sedition Act made it a crime to publish "false, scandalous, and malicious" writings against the government, Congress, or president with intent to defame them or bring them into contempt.

The Sedition Act was passed over Democratic-Republican protests that it directly violated the First Amendment's guarantee of freedom of the press. Federalists offered several defenses. They argued that the First Amendment protected freedom of the press from prior censorship but not from punishment after publication for falsehoods. They argued that the Sedition Act was actually more liberal than the common law of seditious libel, because it allowed truth as a defense. They argued that in a time of national crisis, the government had the right to protect itself from malicious attack.

Democratic-Republicans — and subsequent historical judgment — were unpersuaded. The Sedition Act was used to prosecute at least twenty-five newspaper editors and writers, virtually all of them Democratic-Republican. Matthew Lyon, a Vermont congressman, was convicted of sedition for a letter criticizing Adams's foreign policy and was sentenced to four months in prison — while still serving in Congress. Benjamin Bache, editor of the Aurora (the leading Democratic-Republican newspaper in Philadelphia) was arrested, though he died before trial. The pattern of prosecutions made the political purpose of the Act unmistakable: it was being used not to protect national security but to suppress political opposition.

Jefferson and Madison responded with the Virginia and Kentucky Resolutions of 1798–1799. These documents argued that the federal government had exceeded its constitutional authority in passing the Alien and Sedition Acts. The Constitution was, they argued, a compact among states, and the states retained the authority to judge whether the federal government had exceeded its delegated powers. States had the right — indeed the duty — to "interpose" their authority against unconstitutional federal laws, and in Jefferson's most extreme language, to "nullify" them.

The Virginia and Kentucky Resolutions advanced a theory of federalism — states as the ultimate judges of constitutional questions — that was deeply controversial and would become more so in subsequent decades. The doctrine of nullification that Jefferson sketched would eventually be invoked by South Carolina in the 1830s, by Southern states arguing for the right to maintain slavery in the 1850s, and again during the civil rights movement of the 1950s and 1960s. The resolutions were powerful weapons against the Alien and Sedition Acts, but they also created a constitutional tool that could be — and would be — used for purposes Jefferson had never intended.

The Alien and Sedition Acts expired in 1801. No one was ever deported under the Alien Friends Act, though many French residents left the country under its threat. The prosecutions under the Sedition Act ended. Jefferson pardoned all those convicted under it. The Supreme Court never ruled on the Acts' constitutionality, leaving the question of whether the First Amendment prohibited laws like the Sedition Act unresolved until the twentieth century.

Historical Debate

Were the Alien and Sedition Acts a Legitimate Exercise of Emergency Powers or an Unconstitutional Attack on Civil Liberties?

The question: The Alien and Sedition Acts were passed during a genuine international crisis (the Quasi-War with France) by a government that faced what it believed was a real threat of subversion. Were they a defensible exercise of war powers in an emergency, or were they an unconstitutional suppression of political opposition?

Interpretation A — National Security Justification: Some historians have argued that the Federalists were not entirely wrong about the threat environment. The Quasi-War was a real conflict; French agents were active in the United States; some Democratic-Republican newspapers were openly hostile to the government. The Sedition Act, whatever its abuses, was arguably more liberal than the common law of seditious libel it replaced (truth was a defense). The Naturalization Act addressed real concerns about the political loyalties of recent immigrants. Governments facing genuine threats have always sought to limit speech that could endanger national security, and the early republic was no different.
Interpretation B — Political Suppression: The dominant historical interpretation holds that the Alien and Sedition Acts were primarily instruments of political suppression dressed in national security language. The evidence is difficult to escape: virtually every prosecution under the Sedition Act targeted Democratic-Republican newspapers and politicians. The crisis they were designed to address (the Quasi-War) was resolved diplomatically in 1800 — suggesting it was never as severe as the Federalists claimed. The Acts were unprecedented in their scope and their direct conflict with the First Amendment's guarantee of press freedom. Jefferson's pardons of all Sedition Act convicts, and Congress's refusal to renew the Acts, represent a historical judgment that the Federalists had exceeded constitutional bounds.

Evidence: The pattern of prosecutions provides the most compelling evidence: every Sedition Act prosecution targeted a Democratic-Republican. The Federalist press was never prosecuted. The timing of the Acts — during an election year, at a moment when Federalist political fortunes were declining — also suggests political motivation rather than pure security concern.

Why it matters: The Alien and Sedition Acts raised questions about the limits of governmental authority over political speech that recur throughout American history. The Espionage Act of 1917, the Smith Act of 1940, and post-9/11 surveillance debates all echo the arguments first made in 1798. How governments balance security concerns against civil liberties remains one of the central problems of democratic politics.

Student task: Evaluate the evidence for each interpretation. What additional evidence would you need to determine whether the Federalists' primary motivation was security or political self-preservation? How should courts decide when emergency conditions justify restrictions on civil liberties?

Section 16.8

The Election of 1800

The election of 1800 was, by any measure, one of the most consequential in American history. It was the first election in which a genuine transfer of power between opposing political parties took place — in which the party that held the government was voted out and a new party took office, peacefully and within constitutional procedures. Jefferson called it "the revolution of 1800," and while that phrase was partly campaign rhetoric, it captured something real: the election tested whether the constitutional machinery of democratic self-government could function under conditions of genuine partisan conflict, and it passed the test, though barely.

The campaign was brutal. Federalists portrayed Jefferson as an atheist, a Jacobin, a friend of French radicalism, and a man whose election would bring the violence and chaos of the French Revolution to America. The Connecticut Courant warned that if Jefferson won, "murder, robbery, rape, adultery, and incest will openly be taught and practiced." Jefferson's relationship with Sally Hemings — an enslaved woman at Monticello whom DNA evidence and historical scholarship now confirm was the mother of several of his children — was raised by Federalist newspapers as evidence of his moral unfitness. Democratic-Republicans portrayed Adams as a would-be monarch, a tool of the British, and the author of the unconstitutional Alien and Sedition Acts.

The electoral machinery produced a constitutional crisis. Under the original Constitution, each elector cast two votes for president, without specifying which was for president and which for vice president. The candidate with the most votes became president; the runner-up became vice president. Jefferson and Aaron Burr, his chosen running mate, each received 73 electoral votes, tying for first place. Adams received 65. The tie threw the election into the House of Representatives, where each state delegation cast a single vote, with a majority of state delegations needed to elect a president.

The House voted thirty-five times without producing a majority for either Jefferson or Burr. Some Federalists — who despised Jefferson — considered throwing their support to the more politically flexible Burr. Hamilton, who loathed Jefferson but had a deeper contempt for Burr (whom he considered a dangerous opportunist with no political principles at all), lobbied Federalist congressmen to support Jefferson as the lesser evil. On the thirty-sixth ballot, a sufficient number of Federalists abstained to give Jefferson the majority of state delegations, and he was declared president.

The crisis revealed a clear flaw in the Electoral College's original design: by failing to distinguish between votes for president and vice president, it invited exactly the kind of tie that had paralyzed the government for weeks. The Twelfth Amendment (1804) corrected this by requiring electors to cast separate ballots for president and vice president.

More significant than the constitutional mechanics was the political significance of the transfer of power. The Federalists, who controlled the military, the courts, and the executive branch, surrendered power peacefully to their political opponents. This was not inevitable. Contemporary examples from around the world suggested that governments that lost elections might well refuse to leave office. The early American republic was surrounded by examples of political violence and coup: France had gone through multiple regime changes; Britain was at war; Latin American independence movements were challenging imperial authority. That the Federalists accepted their defeat and the Democratic-Republicans assumed power within constitutional norms was a genuine achievement of democratic governance.

Jefferson's inaugural address struck a deliberate note of reconciliation: "We are all Republicans, we are all Federalists." This was aspirational rather than descriptive — the partisan hatreds of the 1790s did not dissolve with Jefferson's election — but it articulated an ideal of national unity beneath partisan competition that was genuinely important. Jefferson was also honest about the stakes: he argued that free political competition is safer than suppression, and that error of opinion can be tolerated where reason is left free to combat it.

The election of 1800 did not end partisan conflict. Jefferson's administration immediately used its power to displace Federalists from government positions and replace them with Democratic-Republicans — establishing a patronage tradition that later hardened into the "spoils system." The Federalists, now permanently in opposition, consoled themselves with the judiciary: in the final weeks of Adams's administration, they rushed through the appointment of dozens of Federalist judges, including the appointment of John Marshall as Chief Justice of the Supreme Court. Marshall would dominate the Supreme Court for the next thirty-four years, shaping constitutional law in ways that outlasted the Federalist Party itself.

Multiple Perspectives

The Election of 1800: Who Experienced It and How

Federalists: For Federalist political leaders, merchants, and clergy, the election of 1800 was a catastrophe. They had built the financial system, the navy, the diplomatic relationships, and the administrative machinery of the national government; they feared that Jefferson and his party would dismantle these achievements in the name of agrarian ideology. Some Federalists predicted civil war; others emigrated to Canada; still others simply retired from politics in disgust.
Democratic-Republicans: For Jefferson's supporters — western farmers, Southern planters, urban artisans, recent immigrants — the election was a liberation. The Alien and Sedition Acts had demonstrated that the Federalists were willing to use federal power to silence political opposition. Jefferson's election promised a return to the principles of 1776: limited government, agrarian democracy, states' rights, and popular sovereignty.
Enslaved people: For the hundreds of thousands of enslaved people in the United States, the election had deeply ambiguous implications. Jefferson was a slaveholder who had never freed more than a handful of the hundreds of people he enslaved. His election strengthened the political power of the slaveholding South. At the same time, Jefferson's rhetoric of liberty was one that enslaved people could — and did — invoke in their own struggles. Gabriel Prosser, planning his rebellion in Virginia in the summer of 1800, reportedly invoked the principles of the Declaration of Independence.
Native nations: For Native peoples east of the Mississippi, the election of 1800 was a prelude to disaster. Jefferson's stated ideology of agrarian expansion — his belief that the great republic of farmer-citizens needed land, vast quantities of land — meant continued pressure on Native territories. Jefferson would eventually develop a coherent policy of Indian removal, believing that Native peoples could survive only by "civilizing" themselves through agriculture or by removing westward. The Democratic-Republican triumph of 1800 accelerated the political trends that would produce Jackson's removal policy three decades later.
Section 16.9

Political Precedents of the Early Republic

The 1790s were, above all, a decade of precedent-setting. The decisions made in those years — about the scope of executive power, the meaning of the Constitution, the role of political parties, the limits of free speech, the management of foreign policy, the relationship between federal and state authority — shaped American political development in ways that persisted for generations. Understanding these precedents is essential for understanding American politics in any subsequent era.

The Washington administration established that the executive branch would be organized around a cabinet of department heads, that the president would exercise substantial independent authority in foreign affairs, and that the dignity of the republic required ceremony without monarchy. Washington's refusal of a third term established the two-term tradition. His insistence on civilian control of the military — despite his unparalleled military prestige — reinforced the constitutional principle that generals served presidents, not the other way around.

Hamilton's financial program established that the federal government would interpret its economic powers broadly, that national credit was a fundamental requirement of national strength, and that a national bank was both constitutional and useful. It also established — in the political opposition it generated — that economic policy would be a permanent arena of partisan conflict, with different economic interests and ideological visions competing for control of government policy.

The formation of the first political parties established that organized partisan competition would be the normal mode of American democratic politics, despite the founders' theoretical opposition to parties. It also established patterns of party structure, partisan journalism, and electoral competition that shaped all subsequent American party development.

The Whiskey Rebellion established that the federal government could and would enforce its laws against armed resistance. But it also established that popular grievances about taxation and federal authority were legitimate political questions that parties would compete to address. The rebellion's memory made it politically dangerous to impose unpopular domestic taxes without careful attention to their distributional effects.

The foreign policy crises of the 1790s established neutrality as the default American posture in European conflicts, commercial relations as the foundation of American foreign engagement, and executive leadership as the normal mode of diplomatic management. Washington's Farewell Address articulated these principles in a form that shaped American foreign policy thinking for more than a century.

The Alien and Sedition Acts, and the political reaction they provoked, established that First Amendment rights of speech and press were politically powerful even when their legal protection was uncertain. The Virginia and Kentucky Resolutions established states' rights as a constitutional doctrine — one that would be invoked for very different purposes in subsequent decades, most damagingly in defense of slavery and segregation, but also, in its original formulation, as a protection of civil liberties against federal overreach.

The election of 1800 established the peaceful transfer of power as a practical reality and a normative expectation of American democracy. It also established that elections had consequences — that losing power was a real possibility that had to be accepted — and that the constitutional machinery for transferring power was workable under conditions of genuine partisan conflict. These precedents were not achievements of abstract principle. They were the outcomes of human choices, made under pressure, in circumstances of genuine uncertainty, by people who disagreed passionately about the meaning of the republic they were building.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Political Parties in America: From 1790s to the Present

What Changed

  • The 1790s saw the emergence of organized political parties for the first time in American history, transforming political competition from personal networks and factional alignments into formal partisan organizations.
  • Parties developed newspapers, political clubs, electoral coordination mechanisms, and national networks that made mass political participation possible.
  • The Federalists were eventually displaced by the Democratic-Republicans; the Democratic-Republicans split and were succeeded by the Whigs and Democrats; the Democrats and Republicans have dominated since the 1850s.
  • The franchise expanded dramatically from the 1790s through the twentieth century, extending voting rights to non-property-owning white men, then to African American men, then to women, then to people over eighteen.

What Stayed the Same

  • The two-party system has been a constant feature of American politics since the 1790s, despite the rise and fall of specific parties.
  • The fundamental arguments that generated the first parties — about the scope of federal power, the role of government in the economy, the balance between national and local authority — have never been permanently resolved.
  • Partisan journalism, in various technological forms, has been a constant feature of American political culture from the 1790s' newspaper wars to today's partisan media ecosystem.

Why this pattern matters: The party system that emerged in the 1790s created the framework within which all subsequent American political conflicts were fought. Understanding why parties formed, who they represented, and who they excluded is essential to understanding every subsequent chapter of American political history.

Section 16.10

Chapter Review and First Party System Checkpoint

This chapter has examined one of the most consequential decades in American political history. The years from 1789 to 1801 saw the construction of the institutional infrastructure of the national government, the formation of the first political parties, the assertion of federal authority against domestic resistance, the navigation of complex foreign policy challenges, the passage of laws that threatened core civil liberties, and the first peaceful democratic transfer of power between opposing political parties.

The central conflict of the era was between two visions of the republic: Hamilton's vision of a commercially active, financially sophisticated, nationally powerful state and Jefferson's vision of an agrarian republic with limited federal government. Neither vision was entirely right or entirely wrong. Hamilton was correct that national credit and financial institutions were essential to national strength. Jefferson was correct that concentrated economic and political power was dangerous to liberty. The conflict between these visions — between national power and local autonomy, between commercial development and agrarian independence, between those who trusted government and those who feared it — did not resolve in the 1790s. It continued, in different forms, through every subsequent era of American history.

The decade also revealed the incompleteness of the republic's promises. A nation founded on liberty operated one of the most brutal slave systems in the Atlantic world. A constitution that guaranteed freedom of press passed a law making political criticism a crime. A republic that proclaimed equal rights dispossessed Native peoples through military force and fraudulent treaties. A political system that claimed to represent all citizens excluded women, enslaved people, Native peoples, and many immigrants from meaningful participation. These contradictions were visible to people at the time — they generated protests, petitions, rebellions, and arguments — but they were not resolved. They became the contested inheritance that later generations would struggle with.

Everyday Life and Social History: Ordinary Americans in the 1790s

The great political conflicts of the 1790s were not fought only by presidents, secretaries, and senators. They were fought in taverns, on farms, in city workshops, at church meetings, and in the homes of ordinary Americans who had opinions about taxes, banks, France, and the meaning of the republic.

For a farmer in western Pennsylvania, the 1790s meant a whiskey tax he could not afford, a federal government that seemed to serve the interests of Eastern merchants, and a frontier that the government had failed to protect. When the tax collector came, many farmers simply refused to pay. When the federal army marched across the mountains, they dispersed — but the resentment that had driven them to resist did not disappear. It found expression in the Democratic-Republican Party and, eventually, in the ballot box.

For a merchant's family in Philadelphia or Boston, the 1790s meant a functioning national bank, reliable currency, restored public credit, and access to British trade goods. Hamilton's financial program made their economic lives more predictable and more prosperous. Jay's Treaty, despite its unpopularity, preserved commercial relations with Britain that kept their businesses viable. They were Federalists not because they were aristocrats, but because the Federalist program served their interests and matched their social vision.

For women — planter's wife in Virginia, merchant's wife in New York, farmer's wife in New England — the politics of the 1790s were both omnipresent and inaccessible. Women could not vote, could not hold office, and had no formal standing in the new political institutions. But they were intensely engaged with political life. They read newspapers, discussed politics with relatives and friends, hosted the social gatherings where political networks formed, and wrote letters that circulated political intelligence and shaped political opinion. Abigail Adams's famous letter asking her husband to "remember the ladies" was not simply a personal request; it was a political argument about the republic's promise of equality, and it was part of a broader conversation among politically engaged women who believed they had a stake in the republic's future even if the republic did not formally recognize it.

For a free Black man or woman in Philadelphia or Boston, the 1790s were a time of organized community building and urgent political engagement. Free Black communities in Northern cities established churches — particularly African Methodist Episcopal churches — schools, mutual aid societies, and fraternal organizations that constituted the institutional infrastructure of Black civic life. They petitioned Congress about slavery and the slave trade. They sheltered fugitives. The Free African Society of Philadelphia, founded in 1787 by Richard Allen and Absalom Jones, was one of the earliest examples of Black civic organization in the new republic. These communities were not passive; they claimed the republic's promises and demanded that the republic keep them.

For an enslaved person in Virginia or South Carolina, the 1790s brought both terror and hope. The Haitian Revolution — visible to enslaved Americans through rumor, newspaper accounts, and the testimony of refugees — demonstrated that enslaved people could fight for and win their freedom. Gabriel Prosser's planned rebellion in Virginia in 1800 was inspired, at least partly, by this example. The passage of the Fugitive Slave Act in 1793 — the first federal legislation codifying the Constitution's fugitive slave clause — demonstrated that the new government would enforce slavery's demands even in free states. The decade offered no simple narrative of progress or despair for enslaved people; it offered, rather, the complex reality of a republic that proclaimed liberty and enforced bondage simultaneously.

For Native peoples on the frontiers — in the Northwest Territory, in the Southeast — the 1790s brought the military power of the new republic pressing against their territories with a force that their diplomacy, alliances, and resistance could slow but not stop. The Battle of Fallen Timbers (1794), in which General Anthony Wayne's forces defeated a confederacy of Northwestern nations, and the subsequent Treaty of Greenville, which opened most of present-day Ohio to American settlement, was the defining military event of the decade for the Native peoples of the Northwest. In the Southeast, the Muscogee (Creek), Cherokee, Chickasaw, and Choctaw nations navigated a complex diplomatic environment — negotiating with the United States, Spain, and Britain, maintaining internal divisions about how best to resist or accommodate American expansion.

Cause and Effect

The Formation of Political Parties in the 1790s

Long-Term Causes

Genuine ideological disagreements among the founders about the proper scope of federal power and the role of commerce in a republic. Regional economic differences between the commercial North and the agrarian South generating different political interests. The absence of a tradition of legitimate political opposition, requiring Americans to invent the idea that organized opposition was compatible with loyalty.

Short-Term Causes

Hamilton's financial program — particularly assumption and the national bank — created concrete policy conflicts. The French Revolution polarized American opinion. The Whiskey Rebellion and the Alien and Sedition Acts gave opponents concrete grievances around which to organize. The partisan press created networks of communication and political identity that sustained party organization between elections.

Event

The formation of the Federalist and Democratic-Republican parties between approximately 1791 and 1800, culminating in the election of 1800 and the first peaceful transfer of power between opposing parties.

Long-Term Effects

The establishment of the two-party system as the norm of American politics. The precedent of peaceful democratic transfers of power. The doctrine of states' rights, developed in opposition to Federalist overreach, which would later be used to defend slavery. The constitutional arguments about implied powers and strict construction that have shaped American constitutional law ever since.

Historical complexity: The formation of parties cannot be reduced to a single cause. It required the convergence of genuine ideological disagreement, concrete policy conflict, regional economic difference, foreign policy crisis, and the entrepreneurial political activity of specific leaders — particularly Hamilton, Jefferson, and Madison — who decided to organize their supporters rather than seek accommodation with their opponents.

Chapter Summary

The early republic — the years between Washington's inauguration in 1789 and Jefferson's inauguration in 1801 — was one of the most consequential and turbulent periods in American political history. The new government built under the Constitution had to construct everything from the beginning: departments, courts, financial systems, diplomatic relations, and the norms and precedents that would govern future political life. The choices made in these years shaped the American state in ways that persisted for generations.

Alexander Hamilton's financial program was the defining domestic event of the Washington years. By assuming the Revolutionary War debts, chartering a national bank, and creating reliable sources of revenue, Hamilton built the financial infrastructure of a viable national government. His success also generated the most intense domestic political conflict of the era, driving Thomas Jefferson and James Madison to organize the Democratic-Republican Party in opposition. From this conflict emerged the first American party system, a partisan competition that set the template for all subsequent American electoral politics.

The first party system was not simply a conflict of personalities or abstract principles. It reflected genuine divisions about what kind of republic the United States should be: commercial or agrarian, nationally powerful or locally governed, aligned with Britain or France, protective of concentrated wealth or suspicious of it. These divisions mapped onto regional, economic, and social differences that would persist in various forms throughout American history.

The Whiskey Rebellion of 1794 tested federal authority against popular resistance and established that the government could enforce its laws by force when necessary. At the same time, the rebellion demonstrated that popular grievances about taxation and distant authority were genuine political forces that parties would have to address if they wanted to win elections. Washington's decision to lead the army personally was an extraordinary exercise of executive power — and a demonstration that the republic's promises of equality and democracy had limits that were enforced at gunpoint.

American foreign policy in the 1790s was shaped by the impossible task of remaining neutral in a world at war. Jay's Treaty preserved commercial relations with Britain at the cost of intense popular anger; Pinckney's Treaty secured navigation rights on the Mississippi at much lower political cost. Washington's Farewell Address articulated the principle of avoiding permanent foreign entanglements that shaped American foreign policy thinking for more than a century.

The Alien and Sedition Acts of 1798 were the low point of the Federalist decade — a set of laws that restricted immigration, threatened deportation of political opponents, and criminalized criticism of the government. The political reaction they generated — including Jefferson's and Madison's Kentucky and Virginia Resolutions — contributed directly to the Federalist defeat in 1800. The Acts also introduced into American constitutional discourse the doctrine of nullification that would be used for very different, and far more dangerous, purposes in subsequent decades.

The election of 1800 was among the most significant in American history. Jefferson's victory established the principle that an opposition party could win power through elections and that the party in power would peacefully surrender office. The constitutional crisis produced by the tie between Jefferson and Burr in the Electoral College demonstrated a structural flaw that the Twelfth Amendment would correct. Jefferson's inaugural address articulated the ideal of peaceful democratic competition that served as a model for democratic transfers of power.

Throughout all of this, the experiences of those excluded from formal political participation remind us of the incompleteness of the republic's promises. The political conflicts of the 1790s were fought in their absence, but they were fought about things that profoundly affected enslaved people, Native nations, women, and immigrants. The Haitian Revolution shaped American politics; Gabriel Prosser's rebellion revealed the political consciousness of enslaved Americans; Native diplomatic activity demonstrated that Indigenous peoples were political actors with their own strategies and their own stakes in the republic's development. Women's political engagement through correspondence, social networks, and community institutions demonstrated that formal exclusion from the vote did not mean political marginalization.

The 1790s ended with the republic intact, the constitutional order functioning, and a historic democratic transfer of power accomplished. These were genuine achievements, bought at real costs — of political conflict, of suppressed civil liberties, of violence against frontier communities, of broken promises to enslaved people and Native nations. The early republic bequeathed to subsequent generations both a model of democratic governance and a set of unresolved contradictions that would generate conflict for the next two centuries.

Key Terms

Assumption
Hamilton's proposal that the federal government take over the states' Revolutionary War debts; passed as part of the Compromise of 1790 in exchange for locating the national capital on the Potomac River.
Bank of the United States
The federally chartered national bank established in 1791. Proposed by Hamilton, contested by Jefferson on constitutional grounds, it provided financial services to the government and issued paper currency.
Cabinet
The group of executive department heads who advise the president. Washington established the practice of consulting them collectively, creating an institution not mentioned in the Constitution.
Democratic-Republican Party
The political party organized by Jefferson and Madison to oppose Hamilton's financial program and advocate for limited federal government, states' rights, and agrarian democracy.
Electoral College
The constitutional mechanism for electing the president. The original design produced the crisis of 1800 when Jefferson and Burr tied; corrected by the Twelfth Amendment.
Excise tax
A tax on the production or sale of a specific commodity within the country. Hamilton's excise on whiskey triggered the Whiskey Rebellion.
Farewell Address
Washington's 1796 published address warning against permanent foreign alliances and the spirit of partisan faction; shaped American foreign policy thinking for over a century.
Federalist Party
The political party that coalesced around Hamilton's financial program and strong national government; strongest in New England; lost the election of 1800 and declined thereafter.
Implied powers
The doctrine, argued by Hamilton, that the Constitution's "necessary and proper" clause gives Congress powers beyond those explicitly listed. The foundation of loose construction.
Jay's Treaty
The 1794 agreement with Britain settling post-Revolutionary disputes; deeply unpopular because it made few real concessions on impressment or neutral trade.
Kentucky and Virginia Resolutions
Documents drafted by Jefferson and Madison in 1798–1799 arguing that states could judge the constitutionality of federal laws and resist those they deemed unconstitutional.
Loose construction
The view that the Constitution grants Congress broad implied powers beyond those explicitly listed; associated with Hamilton and the Federalists.
Naturalization Act
The 1798 law that extended the residency requirement for citizenship from five to fourteen years, targeting immigrant communities that tended to support the Democratic-Republicans.
Neutrality Proclamation
Washington's 1793 declaration that the United States would remain neutral in the war between Britain and France; established executive authority over foreign policy.
Nullification
The doctrine, sketched in Jefferson's Kentucky Resolutions, that states could declare federal laws void within their borders if they judged them unconstitutional.
Pinckney's Treaty
The 1795 agreement with Spain that granted the United States navigation rights on the Mississippi River and access to New Orleans; more popular than Jay's Treaty.
Political party
An organized group that works to win elections and control government. The Federalists and Democratic-Republicans were the first American parties, establishing the tradition of organized partisan competition.
Precedent
An action or decision that establishes a pattern for future cases. Washington's presidency was particularly significant for establishing presidential precedents in a system with no prior traditions.
Quasi-War
The undeclared naval war between the United States and France (1798–1800) that followed the XYZ Affair and provided the justification for the Alien and Sedition Acts.
Sedition Act
The 1798 law making it a crime to publish "false, scandalous, and malicious" writings against the government or president; used primarily to prosecute Democratic-Republican newspaper editors.
Strict construction
The view, associated with Jefferson, that Congress possesses only those powers explicitly listed in the Constitution; used to argue against the Bank of the United States.
Twelfth Amendment
The 1804 constitutional amendment requiring electors to cast separate ballots for president and vice president, correcting the flaw revealed by the election of 1800.
Whiskey Rebellion
The 1794 armed uprising in western Pennsylvania against Hamilton's excise tax on distilled spirits; suppressed by a federal army personally led by Washington.
XYZ Affair
The 1797–1798 incident in which French agents demanded bribes from American diplomats; triggered anti-French sentiment, the Quasi-War, and the Alien and Sedition Acts.

Major People and Places

Identification & Significance

John Adams (1735–1826)
Second president of the United States. Massachusetts lawyer and diplomat whose presidency was defined by the Quasi-War, the XYZ Affair, and the Alien and Sedition Acts; he was defeated for re-election in 1800.
Aaron Burr (1756–1836)
New York politician who tied with Jefferson in the Electoral College in 1800, producing a constitutional crisis; later killed Hamilton in a duel (1804) and was tried for treason after an alleged scheme to separate western territories from the United States.
Citizen Genêt (Edmond-Charles Genêt) (1763–1834)
French minister to the United States whose aggressive recruitment of Americans for French military action violated American neutrality and embarrassed France's American supporters.
Alexander Hamilton (1755–1804)
First secretary of the treasury; architect of the national financial system; leader of the Federalist Party; killed by Aaron Burr in an 1804 duel.
John Jay (1745–1829)
First chief justice of the Supreme Court; negotiated Jay's Treaty with Britain in 1794; burned in effigy for the unpopular treaty.
Thomas Jefferson (1743–1826)
First secretary of state; third president; principal ideological opponent of Hamilton; author of the Kentucky Resolutions; leader of the Democratic-Republican Party.
Matthew Lyon (1749–1822)
Vermont congressman convicted under the Sedition Act for criticizing Adams's foreign policy; sentenced to four months in prison while still serving in Congress; re-elected from prison.
James Madison (1751–1836)
Virginia congressman; leader of the Democratic-Republican opposition in the House; author of the Virginia Resolutions; later served as fourth president.
John Marshall (1755–1835)
Appointed chief justice of the Supreme Court by Adams in 1801; dominated the Court for thirty-four years and shaped constitutional law through landmark decisions including Marbury v. Madison (1803) and McCulloch v. Maryland (1819).
Gabriel Prosser (c. 1776–1800)
Enslaved Virginia blacksmith who organized an elaborate rebellion in 1800 that was discovered before it could be executed; executed by the state of Virginia; his plans demonstrated the political consciousness of enslaved Americans.
George Washington (1732–1799)
First president; Revolutionary War commander; established fundamental precedents for the executive branch; set two-term limit by refusing a third term.
New York City
First capital of the United States; site of Washington's first inauguration in April 1789.
Philadelphia
Capital of the United States from 1790 to 1800; center of American politics, finance, and print culture in the 1790s; home of the major Federalist and Democratic-Republican newspapers.
Washington, D.C.
Permanent capital of the United States from 1800; located on the Potomac River as a compromise between Northern and Southern political interests; designed by Pierre Charles L'Enfant.
Western Pennsylvania
Center of the Whiskey Rebellion; illustrates the frontier grievances against federal taxation and the geographic dimensions of early American political conflict.

Chapter Reflection

The early republic stands at the intersection of the American founding's ideals and its contradictions. The decade of the 1790s did not resolve the tensions between liberty and slavery, between national power and local democracy, between the promise of equality and the reality of exclusion. Instead, it institutionalized them — created the political parties, constitutional doctrines, and governmental precedents that would carry those tensions forward into every subsequent era of American history. To understand why the Civil War happened, why Reconstruction failed, why the civil rights movement was necessary, and why debates about federal versus state power continue to the present day, students must understand what was built and what was left unresolved in the 1790s.

The first party system established patterns that would prove enduring. American politics has been characterized by two-party competition since the 1790s, with the parties reshaping themselves around new issues and constituencies in successive eras but always maintaining the basic structure of organized partisan competition. The arguments that divided Federalists from Democratic-Republicans — about the proper scope of federal power, the relationship between economic development and democratic equality, the relative weight of national and local authority — have never been permanently resolved. They recur, in different forms, in every era of American political history. Understanding where those arguments came from, and what was at stake in their first formulation, is essential to understanding what has come since.

The political precedents of the Washington era shaped the American presidency in ways that remain visible today. Executive authority over foreign policy, the cabinet as an advisory institution, the two-term tradition (now constitutionally required), civilian control of the military — all of these were established in the first years of constitutional government. When contemporary presidents invoke executive privilege, issue proclamations, or consult their cabinet, they are following precedents that Washington's administration created out of necessity in a system with no established traditions.

The Alien and Sedition Acts, and the reaction they provoked, established that civil liberties would be a permanent site of political conflict in the United States. The Virginia and Kentucky Resolutions introduced into American political discourse a doctrine of states' rights that, while originally designed to protect political dissent, would later be invoked to defend slavery and segregation. This dual legacy — the same constitutional argument serving both liberty and oppression at different historical moments — is one of the most important lessons the early republic offers. Constitutional principles do not have fixed political valences; they are tools that different political actors pick up and use for different purposes in different historical contexts. Students of American history must always ask not only what a constitutional argument says, but who is making it, for what purpose, and in whose interest.

The peaceful transfer of power in 1801 remains one of the genuine achievements of the early American republic. In a world where governments typically changed through force, the United States demonstrated that organized political competition, constitutional procedures, and the willingness of those who held power to surrender it when they lost elections could sustain democratic governance. This achievement was fragile, contested, and built on the exclusion of most of the people who lived in the republic. But it was real, and it established a norm of democratic legitimacy that has shaped American political culture — with all its contradictions and failures — ever since. The unfinished work of making that norm universal, of extending the republic's promises to those who were excluded at its founding, is the continuing story of American history.

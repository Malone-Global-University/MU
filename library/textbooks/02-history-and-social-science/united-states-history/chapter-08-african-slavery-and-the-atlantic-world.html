Part II — Contact, Colonization, and Atlantic Worlds

African Slavery and the Atlantic World

United States History: Indigenous America, Republic, Conflict, and Change

Chapter Opening

"The history of African slavery in the Atlantic world is also the history of African American creation — communities, cultures, and identities that were neither purely African nor purely American but distinctly and powerfully their own."

On a morning sometime in the 1720s, an enslaved man not yet a teenager stood on the deck of a slave ship and watched the West African coastline disappear behind the horizon. He had been seized from his village in what is now southeastern Nigeria, marched through forests and traded from hand to hand across hundreds of miles, and finally sold to European slave merchants at a coastal trading fort. He had never seen an ocean before. He did not know where he was going. Around him, hundreds of other captives — men, women, and children from dozens of different African communities — lay chained in the darkness below deck. Many were already sick. Some would not survive the crossing. The ocean ahead stretched without end.

What he experienced was not an isolated tragedy. It was one moment in one of the largest forced migrations in human history. Between roughly 1500 and 1900, approximately twelve to fifteen million Africans were captured, enslaved, and transported across the Atlantic Ocean to the Americas. Millions more died during capture, during the march to the coast, or during the ocean crossing itself. This system — the transatlantic slave trade — reshaped the history of three continents. It built the plantation economies of the Caribbean, South America, and North America. It enriched merchants, planters, shippers, and financiers in Europe and the Americas. And it created, out of immeasurable suffering and loss, the African American communities that would become one of the most enduring and transformative forces in United States history.

Chapter 8 examines the origins and development of African slavery in the Atlantic world. It begins before the slave trade, with the diverse and sophisticated societies of West and Central Africa that existed long before European contact. It traces how Portuguese and then other European merchants entered African trade networks, how slavery expanded from a limited institution into an industrial-scale system of racial bondage, and how millions of African captives were transported across the Atlantic in conditions of extraordinary violence and cruelty. It examines the plantation economies of the Caribbean and North America where enslaved Africans and their descendants labored under legal systems designed to deny their humanity. And it explores how enslaved people resisted, built families and communities, created religious and cultural lives, and laid the foundations of African American history.

This chapter matters enormously for understanding U.S. history. Slavery was not an accident or a marginal feature of American colonial life. It was central. The plantation economies of the South, the commercial economies of the North, the constitutional compromises of the founding era, and the political conflicts that would eventually produce the Civil War were all shaped by the institution of slavery and the labor of enslaved Africans and their descendants. The wealth generated by enslaved labor built cities, funded universities, financed banks, and powered the early American economy. The legal and social systems designed to maintain racial slavery created structures of racial inequality that would persist long after emancipation.

Understanding African slavery in the Atlantic world also requires historians to grapple with Africa itself. Too often, American history textbooks treat Africa as simply a source of enslaved labor, ignoring the rich histories of the societies from which enslaved people were taken. West and Central African communities in 1500 were not primitive or isolated. They included large kingdoms and city-states, sophisticated trading networks that stretched across the continent and into the Mediterranean world, diverse religious traditions, advanced agricultural and metallurgical technologies, and complex political institutions. The slave trade did not simply exploit a helpless continent. It disrupted, destabilized, and in some regions catastrophically damaged societies that had their own histories, achievements, and struggles.

Finally, this chapter examines how enslaved people responded to their situation. Resistance to slavery took many forms — armed rebellion, escape, daily acts of defiance, the preservation of language and cultural memory, the creation of religious community, and the building of families under impossible circumstances. Enslaved people were not passive. They were historical actors who shaped the world around them even as that world tried to reduce them to property. Their stories of survival, resistance, and community-building are essential not only for understanding slavery but for understanding the foundations of African American culture, politics, and identity.

The evidence historians use to study this period includes ship logs, plantation records, legal documents, newspaper advertisements for runaway enslaved people, the records of slave trading companies, travelers' accounts, missionary reports, African oral traditions, archaeological evidence, and the small but extraordinary body of narratives written by formerly enslaved people themselves. Together, they allow historians to reconstruct this history with growing specificity and depth.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Describe the major societies of West and Central Africa before the transatlantic slave trade, including their political organization, economic systems, trade networks, and cultural practices.
  2. Explain how Portuguese and other European merchants entered African trade networks and how earlier forms of African slavery differed from the racial chattel slavery that developed in the Atlantic world.
  3. Trace the growth of the transatlantic slave trade from its origins in the fifteenth century to its peak in the eighteenth century, identifying major trading regions, routes, and participants.
  4. Analyze the experience of the Middle Passage and explain its physical, psychological, and demographic consequences.
  5. Describe the structure of plantation economies in the Caribbean and North America and explain how racial slavery functioned as a labor system, legal system, and social order.
  6. Compare slavery as it developed in different parts of the Americas, including the Caribbean sugar islands, the Chesapeake tobacco colonies, and the Lower South rice and indigo regions.
  7. Identify and explain the major forms of resistance enslaved people used to oppose slavery, from armed rebellion to everyday acts of defiance and cultural survival.
  8. Describe how enslaved people maintained family relationships, religious practices, and cultural identities under conditions designed to destroy them.
  9. Assess the long-term consequences of the slave trade and plantation slavery for Africa, the Americas, and the Atlantic world.
  10. Connect the history of African slavery in the colonial period to later developments in U.S. history, including the expansion of slavery, the Civil War, and the long struggle for African American freedom and equality.

  2. How did the transatlantic slave trade change the societies of West and Central Africa?

Historical Context

To understand the transatlantic slave trade and African slavery in the Americas, students must understand several layers of historical context: the history of Africa before European contact; the nature of earlier slavery in Africa and the Mediterranean world; the specific conditions of European colonization in the Americas that made plantation slavery so attractive to colonial landowners; and the process by which racial categories became the legal and social foundation of a permanent hereditary system of bondage.

Africa in 1400 was not a single entity but an enormous continent of extraordinary diversity. Its roughly 30 million square kilometers contained desert, savanna, rainforest, river systems, coastal regions, and highland plateaus. Its peoples spoke hundreds of languages, practiced dozens of different religions, and organized themselves into everything from small nomadic bands to large centralized states. West Africa — the region most heavily involved in the early Atlantic trade — contained several large and powerful political entities, including the Mali Empire, the Songhai Empire, the kingdoms of the Akan peoples in what is now Ghana, the city-state confederacies of the Yoruba, the Benin kingdom, and the Wolof states of Senegambia. These were not simple or primitive societies. They had professional armies, sophisticated diplomatic traditions, long-distance trade networks, written legal and religious traditions (especially in Islamic communities), elaborate artistic traditions, and urban centers with populations of tens of thousands.

Slavery had existed in Africa, as in virtually every part of the ancient and medieval world, long before European contact. African slavery, however, differed in crucial ways from the racial chattel slavery that would develop in the Americas. In most African societies, enslaved people were not defined by their race. They were typically war captives, debt slaves, criminals, or people sold during famines. Enslaved people in many African societies could own property, marry, accumulate certain rights, and sometimes achieve freedom or elevated status over time. Their children were not automatically enslaved. Slavery in Africa was embedded in complex social and political systems, not a permanent hereditary status defined by the physical characteristics of an entire population.

European colonization of the Americas beginning in the late fifteenth century created the conditions for a fundamental transformation. Spanish and Portuguese colonizers in the Caribbean and South America initially attempted to exploit the labor of Indigenous peoples through the encomienda system. But epidemic disease devastated Indigenous populations with catastrophic speed, and many surviving Native peoples resisted or escaped into territories beyond colonial control. Colonizers increasingly turned to African enslaved labor to work their mines and plantations. Enslaved Africans could not as easily escape into unknown territory. They could be obtained in large numbers through the growing Atlantic trade. And European colonizers, armed with developing ideologies of racial hierarchy, found it possible to justify permanent bondage on the basis of African ancestry and dark skin. By the seventeenth century, the plantation complex — a system of large-scale agricultural production using enslaved labor for export crops — was the dominant economic institution of the tropical and subtropical Americas.

Section 8.1

Africa and the Atlantic World

The history of the transatlantic slave trade begins not in the Americas, not on the ocean, but in Africa. To understand why millions of Africans were enslaved and transported across the Atlantic, and to understand the communities from which they were torn, students must first understand Africa as a historical world in its own right — not merely as a backdrop for European activity.

By 1400, Africa was home to some of the most sophisticated political and commercial systems on the planet. The trans-Saharan trade routes that connected sub-Saharan Africa to North Africa and the Mediterranean had been operating for more than a thousand years. Gold from the mines of present-day Ghana and Mali, ivory from forest regions, and enslaved people captured in warfare moved northward across the Sahara in camel caravans. Salt, copper, cloth, and manufactured goods moved southward in return. The great empires of the western Sudan — Ghana, Mali, and Songhai — had grown wealthy as middlemen in this trade. The city of Timbuktu, located at the southern edge of the Sahara in present-day Mali, had by 1400 become one of the most important centers of Islamic scholarship in the world, with universities, libraries, and a population of perhaps twenty-five to forty thousand people.

The Atlantic coast of West Africa had its own distinct histories. In Senegambia, Wolof and Mandinka kingdoms had developed sophisticated states with hereditary aristocracies, professional armies, and long-distance commercial networks. Farther south, along the Gold Coast, Akan-speaking peoples had built powerful political entities enriched by their control of gold resources. The kingdoms of Asante, Fante, and Denkyira were skilled at both warfare and diplomacy. To the east, in present-day Nigeria, the Yoruba city-states governed large and prosperous populations. The kingdom of Benin, famous for its remarkable bronze and ivory artworks, had political institutions of great sophistication and trade connections reaching far into the interior.

In Central Africa, the Kongo Kingdom occupied a large territory south of the Congo River. At its height in the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, Kongo was one of the largest states in Africa, with a centralized government, provincial administration, and a population that may have reached several million. When Portuguese explorers made contact with Kongo in the 1480s, the two kingdoms briefly engaged in something resembling diplomatic exchange between equals — the Kongolese royal court adopted Catholic Christianity, sent ambassadors to Lisbon, and engaged in correspondence with the Pope. This early relationship rapidly deteriorated as Portugal's hunger for enslaved labor overwhelmed any pretense of diplomatic equality.

None of these African societies was without conflict, hierarchy, or inequality. All of them practiced forms of slavery — typically involving war captives, debtors, or people sold by their families during periods of famine. But African slavery in this period differed fundamentally from the racial chattel slavery that would develop in the Atlantic world. In most African societies, enslaved people were not marked by race. Their status could change over time, and in many cases their children were born free.

The key historical development that transformed African trade into the transatlantic slave trade was the arrival of Portuguese sailors along the West African coast in the mid-fifteenth century. Portugal in the early 1400s was a small kingdom on the western edge of Europe, lacking the agricultural wealth of France or the commercial networks of Italian city-states. Portuguese monarchs and merchants saw Atlantic exploration as a way to bypass expensive overland and Mediterranean trade routes and to access directly the gold, spices, and other resources of West Africa and eventually Asia. Beginning in the 1420s and 1430s, Portuguese sailors — using a new, more maneuverable ship design called the caravel — began systematically exploring the West African coast.

In 1441, a Portuguese ship captain brought back to Lisbon a small number of Africans who had been seized during a raid on the Mauritanian coast. In 1444, a larger group of approximately 240 people was publicly sold in the Portuguese city of Lagos — the first large slave auction in European history. These early seizures by raid quickly gave way to a more systematic form of trade. Rather than raiding directly, which proved dangerous and expensive, Portuguese merchants found it more efficient to trade for enslaved people with African rulers and merchants who had their own systems for capturing people through warfare.

Historical Vocabulary
The Atlantic World

The Atlantic world is a concept historians use to describe the interconnected system of peoples, economies, cultures, and political structures that developed around the Atlantic Ocean between roughly 1400 and 1800. Rather than studying Europe, Africa, and the Americas separately, the Atlantic world framework encourages historians to see them as parts of an integrated system shaped by trade, migration (both forced and voluntary), disease, warfare, and cultural exchange.

The Atlantic world was not a world of equals. European states had enormous power to set the terms of trade and to use violence to enforce their interests. But the Atlantic world was also shaped by African political choices, Indigenous resistance and adaptation, and the cultures and labor of enslaved people. For U.S. history, the Atlantic world framework is essential: it shows that colonial North America was not an isolated frontier experiment but a part of a vast global system from its very beginnings.

Section 8.2

West and Central African Societies

The societies of West and Central Africa that came into contact with the Atlantic slave trade were as varied as the landscapes they inhabited. Understanding their diversity is essential for understanding what the slave trade destroyed, disrupted, and ultimately transformed.

Senegambia — the region of present-day Senegal, the Gambia, Guinea-Bissau, and parts of Guinea — was one of the earliest and most heavily affected zones of Atlantic trade. The Wolof and Mandinka peoples had built states with complex social hierarchies that included free nobles, commoners, artisans, and already-existing enslaved people. Islam had spread through much of this region by 1400, creating a class of Muslim scholars and merchants who maintained connections to the wider Islamic world. The Senegambian coast offered Portuguese and later Dutch, English, and French traders relatively accessible anchorage and a population of African merchants willing to trade enslaved captives for European goods.

The Gold Coast was home to some of the most politically powerful states in West Africa. Akan-speaking peoples such as the Asante, Fante, and Denkyira had developed elaborate political systems based on matrilineal kinship — inheritance and succession passed through the mother's line — and skilled use of both military force and diplomacy. The Asante kingdom in particular would grow in power through the eighteenth century, partly by using European trade goods (especially guns) to expand its territory and partly by selling enslaved war captives to European traders. The relationship between the Asante and European slave traders was always a negotiation. The Asante were militarily and commercially formidable, and European merchants who hoped to trade on the Gold Coast did so on terms that African rulers influenced significantly.

Farther east, the Niger Delta region — home to Igbo, Efik, and Ibibio-speaking peoples — became increasingly important in the slave trade during the eighteenth century. The Igbo peoples of the interior lived in largely decentralized communities organized around village councils and age-grade systems rather than centralized kingdoms. Their decentralization made them vulnerable to slave raiding from both their neighbors and from traders working on behalf of coastal merchants.

The kingdom of Dahomey, located in present-day Benin, became heavily involved in the slave trade by the eighteenth century. Dahomey's army conducted systematic raids on neighboring peoples — Yoruba, Bariba, and others — capturing thousands of people per year for sale at the coastal port of Ouidah. Dahomey's rulers were not simply passive victims of European demand; they were active agents who used the slave trade to acquire firearms, wealth, and political power. But they also operated within a context shaped overwhelmingly by European commercial demand. The scale of what Dahomey did — and the suffering it caused — was inseparable from the massive and growing European market for enslaved labor.

Central Africa, particularly the region associated with the Kongo Kingdom and its neighbors, contributed enormously to the populations of the Americas through the slave trade. King Afonso I of Kongo, who ruled in the early sixteenth century and had converted to Christianity, wrote repeatedly to the King of Portugal complaining that the slave trade was depopulating his kingdom and corrupting his nobles. His letters — remarkable documents preserved in the Portuguese archives — reveal a sophisticated African ruler who understood exactly what was happening to his society and tried, ultimately without success, to stop it.

What united all of these diverse regions was the way European commercial demand, combined with existing African political institutions and conflicts, drew millions of people into the machinery of the slave trade. Warfare, drought, political instability, and debt all produced enslaved people who entered a commercial system with an apparently insatiable appetite. The result was demographic catastrophe in some regions: lost population, disrupted communities, destabilized political systems, and a culture of fear and violence that spread from coastal trading centers deep into the African interior.

Section 8.3

European Trade and the Growth of Slavery

The transformation of African trade into the transatlantic slave trade was driven by specific economic demands, political decisions, and the brutal logic of colonial plantation agriculture. Portuguese traders along the West African coast in the late fifteenth century initially sought gold, pepper, and ivory as much as enslaved people. What changed the scale and character of the trade was the development of sugar plantations in the Atlantic islands — Madeira, the Canary Islands, and São Tomé — in the second half of the fifteenth century, and then the opening of plantation agriculture in Brazil after 1500 and in the Caribbean after 1492.

Sugar was the crucial commodity. In the medieval Mediterranean world, sugar had been a luxury item available only to the wealthy. The Atlantic island and then American plantation systems made sugar production possible on a much larger scale. But sugar was extraordinarily labor-intensive. It required workers to plant, harvest, crush, and boil sugarcane through an almost year-round agricultural cycle that was grueling, hot, and dangerous. In the tropical heat of Brazil and the Caribbean, it also killed workers at fearsome rates — through disease, overwork, and injury from the boiling vats used to process the cane juice. The plantations of the Caribbean consumed human beings at a rate that required constant replacement of the enslaved labor force. The slave trade was not merely a way to create an initial labor force; it was the ongoing lifeline of a system that killed people faster than they could reproduce.

Portuguese traders established a system of coastal trading forts — called feitorias or factories — along the West African coast. The most important of these forts was Elmina, established on the Gold Coast in 1482. Elmina became one of the most important sites in the history of the slave trade — the place where millions of enslaved Africans from the interior passed before being loaded onto ships for the Atlantic crossing. The trade worked through a chain of exchange. European ships arrived at the African coast loaded with manufactured goods: iron bars, copper and brass objects, cloth from India and Europe, rum, firearms, and beads. These goods were traded to African merchants and political rulers for enslaved people. The enslaved people were held at coastal forts, sometimes for weeks or months, before being loaded onto ships for the crossing.

The growth of the slave trade was accompanied by the development of racial ideologies that justified it. Earlier European justifications for enslaving Africans had relied partly on religious arguments — Africans were non-Christian, or descended from the biblical Ham, who was said to have been cursed — and partly on simple economic interest. Over time, increasingly systematic arguments about racial hierarchy developed. European writers and philosophers constructed theories claiming that Africans were naturally suited to hard labor or naturally inferior. These ideas were not simply reflections of prior beliefs; they were created and elaborated specifically to justify a profitable trade in human beings.

The numbers grew relentlessly. In the sixteenth century, perhaps 250,000 Africans were transported across the Atlantic. In the seventeenth century, the number was approximately 1.3 million. In the eighteenth century — the peak of the trade — approximately 6 million Africans were transported. The total across the entire period reached approximately 12.5 million, of whom roughly 10.7 million survived the crossing to arrive in the Americas. The others — roughly 1.8 million people — died during the Middle Passage.

The English entered the slave trade seriously in the mid-seventeenth century, founding the Royal Adventurers Trading into Africa in 1660 and then the Royal African Company in 1672. After 1698, when the monopoly was broken and the trade opened to all English merchants, the volume of enslaved people transported in English ships grew dramatically. In the eighteenth century, English and then British merchants dominated the slave trade, with the ports of Bristol and Liverpool becoming major centers of the slaving industry.

Section 8.4

The Middle Passage

The Middle Passage — the ocean crossing from the West African coast to the Americas — was one of the most traumatic passages of mass human suffering in history. Historians who study it confront not only the documented horror of the crossing itself but the longer shadow of terror, loss, and psychological violence that surrounded it: the capture, the march to the coast, the imprisonment in coastal forts, the loading onto ships, the crossing, and the arrival into a new world of permanent bondage.

The loading of enslaved people onto ships was itself a process of dehumanization. People who had been held for weeks or months in the dungeons of coastal forts — often in chains, in darkness, with inadequate food and water, amid disease — were marched to the beach and loaded into small boats that carried them out to the waiting slave ships. The ships had been specially fitted to maximize the number of people they could carry. Below deck, in the cargo hold, wooden platforms had been constructed to allow enslaved people to be stacked in multiple layers, each person allotted a space roughly the size of a coffin. Men were typically kept in the lower hold, chained in pairs by ankle and wrist. Women and children were sometimes given slightly more freedom of movement but were more exposed to the sexual violence of sailors and officers.

The conditions in the hold were catastrophic. In tropical heat, with hundreds of bodies pressed together in a space with almost no ventilation, temperatures became extreme. The holds were almost impossible to clean adequately during the crossing. Dysentery — called "the bloody flux" by sailors — spread rapidly and killed both enslaved people and crew. Smallpox, measles, and other diseases also moved through the crowded hold. People who died were thrown overboard. People who were too sick to recover were sometimes also thrown overboard, an act of calculated brutality that reflected how completely their captors had stripped them of humanity.

The crossing typically took five to eight weeks. During the crossing, enslaved people were brought on deck in small groups for brief exercise — sometimes forced to dance at the end of a whip — and then returned to the hold. Their food was minimal: a twice-daily meal of boiled grain or beans, occasionally with a small amount of dried fish. The psychological dimensions of the Middle Passage were as terrible as the physical ones. Most people who were loaded onto slave ships had never seen an ocean. They had no framework for understanding where they were being taken, what would happen to them, or whether they would ever see their families again.

Many enslaved people believed — based on the behavior of their captors and the terrifying strangeness of their situation — that they were going to be killed and eaten. Some people refused to eat and had to be force-fed. Some attempted suicide by jumping overboard, which led slavers to install nets along the sides of ships. Some deaths may have been intentional acts of resistance by people who chose death over continued captivity.

Mortality on the Middle Passage varied by era, route, and circumstances. In the early period of the trade, mortality rates among enslaved people could reach 30 to 40 percent of those loaded. As the trade became more systematic and the ships faster and somewhat better provisioned, average mortality fell to something closer to 15 to 20 percent by the mid-eighteenth century. Crew mortality was also high — perhaps one in five sailors died on a slaving voyage — because the same diseases that killed enslaved people also killed the ships' crews.

The historical evidence for the Middle Passage comes from multiple sources. Ship logs recorded dates, positions, cargo loads, and death counts in the same columns where goods were listed. Accounts from sailors and ships' doctors described conditions. A small number of survivors later wrote memoirs. Historians have also drawn on the Slave Voyages database — a remarkable digital project that has assembled records from approximately 36,000 slaving voyages — to reconstruct the statistical scale and geography of the trade with unprecedented precision.

Primary Source Window

Olaudah Equiano, The Interesting Narrative of the Life of Olaudah Equiano, or Gustavus Vassa, the African (1789)

Creator: Olaudah Equiano  ·  Date: 1789  ·  Type: Memoir / published autobiography / abolitionist political document

Context: Olaudah Equiano was born in the Igbo-speaking region of present-day Nigeria around 1745. He was enslaved as a child, transported across the Atlantic, sold into plantation slavery, and eventually managed to purchase his freedom after years of labor and saving. In 1789 he published his memoir, which became one of the foundational texts of the abolitionist movement and remains one of the most important first-person accounts of the slave trade available to historians.

Equiano described his first sight of the slave ship as producing absolute terror. He had never seen white men before, and their strange appearance and the nets along the ship's sides convinced him that he was about to be killed. When he was lowered below deck, the stench and the cries of the people already chained there overcame him. He fainted. He ate nothing for days. He described the suffocating heat, the filth, the screaming of the sick and dying, and the incomprehensible misery of hundreds of people crammed into a space with no adequate air, light, or sanitation. He wrote that the shrieks of the women and the groans of the dying were so terrible that he wished for his own death.

Analysis: Equiano's narrative is extraordinary because it is one of the very few first-person accounts of the Middle Passage from the perspective of an enslaved person. It reveals the psychological as well as physical terror of the crossing. Its limits are also worth noting: Equiano wrote decades after the events he describes, in English (not his original language), and for an abolitionist audience he was trying to persuade. Recent historians have also raised questions — still debated — about whether some details of his account are constructed from other sources. These questions do not diminish the document's historical importance, but they remind students that even powerful first-person accounts must be read critically.

Questions for students:

  1. What specific conditions on the slave ship does Equiano's account describe? What does this reveal about how enslaved people were treated during the Middle Passage?
  2. Equiano wrote his memoir for a British abolitionist audience nearly four decades after the events he describes. How might the passage of time and his intended audience have shaped his account?

Section 8.5

Plantation Labor and Racial Slavery

When enslaved Africans arrived in the Americas, their passage from one horror to another was immediate. The process of transition into plantation slavery — called by planters and merchants "seasoning" — was itself a period of extraordinary mortality. Newly arrived Africans, already weakened by the Middle Passage, were exposed to unfamiliar diseases in a new climate, forced into brutal labor regimes, and subjected to a process of coercion designed to break resistance and establish compliance. In the Caribbean and lowland South America, roughly a quarter to a third of newly arrived enslaved Africans died within their first year.

Plantation labor was organized with relentless rationality around the demands of the export crop. In the sugar islands of the Caribbean — Jamaica, Barbados, Saint-Domingue, and others — this meant a year-round cycle of planting, harvesting, and processing that left almost no room for rest. Gangs of enslaved people were organized into three tiers: a first gang of the strongest adults who did the heaviest field work, a second gang of those slightly less fit, and a third gang of children. In grinding season, work could continue twenty-four hours a day in shifts. The boiling houses, where juice was reduced to sugar and molasses in a series of increasingly hot vats, were scenes of almost nightmarish labor: blazing heat, scalding liquid, exhausted workers maintaining a process that could not stop.

Racial slavery — the specific American form of chattel slavery based on hereditary racial status — developed through a series of legal decisions made in the colonial legislatures of the seventeenth century. In the early decades of Virginia and Maryland, the legal status of Africans was ambiguous. Some Africans who arrived in the 1620s, 1630s, and 1640s appeared in the records as servants, some completed terms of indenture and became free, and a small number even acquired land and servants of their own. The line between African servants and English indentured servants was not yet absolute.

Beginning in the 1640s and accelerating through the 1660s and 1670s, Virginia and Maryland legislators made a series of decisions that hardened the line between freedom and bondage along racial lines. In 1662, Virginia declared that the enslaved status of a child followed the condition of the mother — partus sequitur ventrem — rather than the condition of the father. This was decisive: it meant that the children of enslaved women were automatically enslaved regardless of who their father was. In 1667, Virginia declared that baptism into Christianity did not free an enslaved person. In 1705, Virginia consolidated its various slave laws into a comprehensive Slave Code that defined enslaved people as real estate — property attached to the land — and systematically stripped free Black people of most legal rights.

The punishments prescribed by slave codes were savage. Whipping was universal and could be administered for almost any offense. Other punishments included branding, mutilation, and death. Public executions were sometimes elaborate rituals of terror, designed to warn other enslaved people of the consequences of resistance. The systematic violence of plantation slavery was not incidental to the institution; it was its foundation. Slavery required constant coercion because enslaved people were not freely choosing to work for others' profit and would not do so without compulsion.

South Carolina's enslaved people brought expertise that European colonizers lacked. Enslaved Africans from the rice-growing regions of West Africa, particularly Senegambia and Sierra Leone, brought knowledge of how to manage tidal irrigation, how to process rice, and how to handle the specific challenges of subtropical wetland agriculture. South Carolina planters actively sought to purchase enslaved people from West African rice-growing regions precisely because of this knowledge — a fact that underscores that the skills and expertise of enslaved Africans were central, not incidental, to the development of American agricultural economies.

Historical Vocabulary
Chattel Slavery

Chattel slavery refers to a system in which enslaved people are legally defined as personal property — chattel — that can be bought, sold, leased, mortgaged, bequeathed, and transferred in the same way as livestock or furniture. The word "chattel" comes from the same Latin root as "cattle," reflecting the legal equation of enslaved people with domestic animals.

American racial chattel slavery, as codified in the slave codes of the colonial period, was distinctive in combining this property status with hereditary racial transmission: not only were enslaved people property, but all people of African descent — regardless of how many generations their family had been in America — could be held as property. This made American slavery uniquely rigid and permanent. The implications extended far beyond the plantation, shaping American law, politics, and social life for centuries after emancipation.

Section 8.6

Slavery in the Caribbean and North America

The experience of enslaved Africans and their descendants varied significantly depending on where in the Americas they were taken. The Caribbean sugar islands were the most lethal environment in the Americas for enslaved people. Jamaica, Barbados, Martinique, Guadeloupe, and Saint-Domingue all developed plantation economies driven by sugar and dependent on a constant stream of newly imported Africans because the enslaved population could not reproduce itself fast enough to replace those who died. In Jamaica in the eighteenth century, there were roughly ten enslaved people for every free person. This demographic reality made white planters constantly anxious about rebellion and drove them to extreme brutality as a means of control. It also meant that African cultural practices, languages, and religious traditions survived more intact in the Caribbean than in many parts of North America, simply because the enslaved population was continuously replenished with newly arrived Africans.

In the Chesapeake colonies of Virginia and Maryland, tobacco shaped the experience of enslaved people from the late seventeenth century onward. By 1700, Virginia had approximately sixteen thousand enslaved people; by 1750, that number had grown to approximately one hundred thousand. Unlike in the Caribbean, the enslaved population of the Chesapeake did reproduce itself — birth rates among enslaved women in Virginia exceeded the death rate, meaning that the population grew naturally as well as through continued importation. This created conditions in which enslaved communities became more stable over time, with multigenerational families, established community networks, and a population increasingly born in America rather than Africa.

South Carolina developed the most brutal plantation system in mainland North America. Rice cultivation required working in swampy, mosquito-infested lowcountry during seasons when malaria was rampant. South Carolina planters imported enslaved people in enormous numbers — by 1720, Black people outnumbered white people in the colony — and the planters' fear of rebellion was matched by the ferocity of the punishments they inflicted. South Carolina also developed the "task system" of labor organization, in which enslaved workers were assigned specific daily tasks and were allowed to use any time remaining after completing those tasks for their own purposes. This system, compared to the gang labor system used in sugar production, gave enslaved people somewhat more control over their time, and they used it to cultivate garden plots, trade in local markets, and develop economic resources.

The northern colonies — New England and the Middle Colonies — had fewer enslaved people than the South, but slavery was not absent from the North. New England merchants played a critical role in the slave trade, financing slaving voyages and profiting from the trade in plantation products. Newport, Rhode Island became a major center of the American slave trade. In cities like Boston, New York, and Philadelphia, enslaved people worked as house servants, skilled artisans, sailors, and dock workers. The numbers were smaller — by 1770, New England had perhaps 15,000 enslaved people compared to several hundred thousand in the South — but the institutions of Northern colonial life were entangled with slavery through commerce, finance, and consumption.

Map Study

The Geography of the Atlantic Slave Trade

A map of the transatlantic slave trade shows an intricate web of routes connecting the West African coast to the Americas. The major departure points along the African coast included Senegambia in the northwest, the Gold Coast (modern Ghana), the Bight of Benin (the coast of modern Benin and western Nigeria), the Bight of Biafra (the Niger Delta region), and the West Central African coast (modern Angola and the Democratic Republic of Congo). The major destinations included Brazil (which received approximately 4.8 million enslaved people — more than any other destination), the Caribbean islands (approximately 4.7 million), and mainland North America (approximately 400,000).

Students should notice that what became the United States received a much smaller fraction of the total transatlantic slave trade than the Caribbean or Brazil, yet by 1860 the enslaved population of the United States had grown to approximately four million — by far the largest enslaved population in the Americas — because the North American enslaved population reproduced itself while Caribbean and Brazilian populations did not.

Section 8.7

Resistance, Rebellion, and Survival

One of the most important historical questions about slavery is how enslaved people responded to their enslavement. For generations, some historians and popular writers portrayed enslaved people as primarily passive — as victims whose suffering was real but whose agency was limited. More recent historical scholarship has fundamentally revised this picture. Enslaved people resisted their bondage in many ways, ranging from armed rebellion to daily acts of defiance, escape, cultural preservation, and the quiet but profound assertion of their humanity through family and community.

Armed rebellion was the most dramatic form of resistance, and it terrified enslavers. The Stono Rebellion of September 1739, which took place along the Stono River south of Charleston, South Carolina, was one of the largest slave revolts in colonial North America. On a Sunday morning, a group of approximately twenty enslaved people — many of them recently arrived from the Kongo Kingdom and some with military experience — gathered at a store near the Stono River, seized weapons and gunpowder, killed the two storekeepers, and began marching south toward Spanish Florida, where the governor had promised freedom to enslaved people who escaped from British colonies. As they marched, they killed approximately twenty-five white people they encountered and burned several plantations. Their numbers grew to perhaps sixty or eighty before they were intercepted by colonial militia. Most of the rebels were killed; those captured were executed.

The Stono Rebellion had important consequences. South Carolina tightened its slave code dramatically, restricting the movement and assembly of enslaved people, prohibiting the teaching of literacy to enslaved people, and requiring a larger ratio of white overseers to enslaved workers. It also illuminated several features of slave resistance: the connection between newly arrived Africans and the likelihood of organized rebellion; the strategic importance of Spanish Florida as a destination; and the role that knowledge of geography, collective organization, and planning played in resistance.

Flight was another form of resistance. Enslaved people ran away from their enslavers in large numbers — individually, in pairs, and occasionally in groups. The advertisements for runaway enslaved people published in colonial and early national newspapers provide detailed information about the people who ran: their names, their physical descriptions, their skills, the languages they spoke, the people they might be trying to reach. Some had specific destinations: family members on other plantations, free Black communities in cities, Indigenous nations that had a history of accepting fugitives, or Spanish Florida.

Those who successfully escaped permanently formed maroon communities — free settlements in remote areas that maintained their independence against periodic military attacks. In Jamaica, the Maroon communities of the Blue Mountains and Cockpit Country fought two wars against British colonial forces and eventually negotiated treaties that recognized their autonomy. In colonial Florida, escaped enslaved people established the settlement of Fort Mose near St. Augustine — the first free Black settlement within what would become U.S. territory, established with Spanish authorization in 1738.

Daily resistance pervaded plantation life. Enslaved people worked slowly when unsupervised, feigned illness, broke tools, spoiled crops, set fires to buildings, poisoned livestock, and sabotaged production in dozens of ways. These acts were dangerous — discovery could mean severe punishment or death — but they were rational responses to a situation in which direct confrontation was almost always lethal. Slavery's defenders often pointed to this "laziness" or "carelessness" as evidence that African people were naturally inferior; historians recognize it as evidence of rational resistance by people who had every reason to minimize their productivity for their enslavers' benefit.

Historical Debate

Were Enslaved People Primarily Victims or Agents?

The question: How should historians understand the position of enslaved people in American history? Were they primarily victims of a system of overwhelming power, or should historians emphasize their agency, resistance, and capacity for action?

Interpretation A — Emphasis on victimhood and structural power: Some historians emphasize the absolute power of the slave system. Chattel slavery was a system of extraordinary violence backed by the full force of law, community norms, and military power. Enslaved people who resisted faced brutal punishment and death. Their families could be separated at their enslavers' will. In this interpretation, emphasizing "agency" risks understating the violence of slavery and can even seem to hold enslaved people responsible for their own oppression. The primary historical responsibility for slavery lies with enslavers, slave traders, legislators, and the wider societies that supported and benefited from the institution.
Interpretation B — Emphasis on agency and resistance: Other historians emphasize that enslaved people were historical actors who shaped their own lives within the limits imposed on them. They resisted in dozens of ways. They built families, communities, and cultural institutions. They negotiated, bargained, and maneuvered. They preserved African cultural traditions, created new ones, and left a profound imprint on American culture. In this interpretation, treating enslaved people solely as victims denies them the historical humanity they insisted on maintaining.

Evidence: Both interpretations are supported by substantial historical evidence. The violence and legal power of slavery are documented in slave codes, court records, plantation account books, and the accounts of enslaved survivors. The agency and resistance of enslaved people are documented in runaway advertisements, court testimony, and the rich oral and literary traditions that enslaved people created and preserved.

Why it matters: How historians answer this question affects how students understand the history of African American freedom struggles, the relationship between historical oppression and historical agency, and the long-term consequences of slavery for American society.

Student task: Using evidence from this chapter, evaluate both interpretations. Which seems better supported? What kinds of additional evidence would you want to see?

Section 8.8

Family, Culture, and Religion Under Slavery

Perhaps the most powerful evidence of enslaved people's humanity and resilience was the world they built within the constraints of bondage: family relationships, community ties, cultural practices, religious life, and forms of expression that could not entirely be suppressed even by the most brutal system of control.

Family was the emotional core of enslaved community life. Enslaved people formed marriages, raised children, maintained relationships with siblings and parents on other plantations, and created extended networks of kin that could span considerable distances. These family relationships existed without legal recognition — enslaved people could not legally marry, and their children were legally their enslavers' property — but they were real, meaningful, and central to enslaved people's sense of identity and belonging. The threat of family separation through sale was one of the most effective tools of plantation discipline. Fathers separated from their children, mothers sold away from their infants, spouses divided by sale between different plantations — these losses were recounted in the narratives of formerly enslaved people with a grief that had not diminished over years and decades.

Despite the constant threat of separation, enslaved people worked to maintain family ties with extraordinary determination. When enslaved people ran away, advertisements for their return often noted that they were likely heading to a specific family member. The naming practices of enslaved communities reveal the importance of family continuity: children were regularly named after parents, grandparents, and other relatives, maintaining visible connections across generations even when physical separation made regular contact impossible.

Cultural life in enslaved communities drew on both African traditions and the new American environment. African languages — or words and phrases from African languages — survived in many enslaved communities, particularly in regions with high rates of direct African importation. The Gullah and Geechee languages spoken by enslaved people in the Sea Islands of South Carolina and Georgia are remarkable examples: they preserve extensive vocabulary, grammatical structures, and cultural references from West and Central African languages. Music, dance, storytelling, and material culture also showed African survivals: the banjo developed from African stringed instruments; ring-shout ceremonies combined African religious practice with Christian content; the quilts made by enslaved women sometimes incorporated geometric patterns with deep roots in West African textile traditions.

Religion was perhaps the most complex dimension of enslaved cultural life. African spiritual traditions — involving relationships with ancestors, healing practices, divination, and spirit possession — came to the Americas embedded in the bodies and memories of enslaved people. In the Caribbean and Brazil, African religions blended with Catholicism to produce syncretic traditions such as Vodou (Haiti), Candomblé (Brazil), and Santería (Cuba), in which African spiritual figures were identified with Catholic saints.

In the British North American colonies, many enslaved people converted to Christianity — particularly after the evangelical revivals of the mid-eighteenth century made Christian faith accessible and emotionally resonant across racial lines. The Great Awakening of the 1740s, with its emphasis on direct religious experience, spiritual equality before God, and emotional worship, appealed powerfully to enslaved people. The biblical story of the Exodus — in which God led the enslaved Israelites out of Egypt and into freedom — became a central text for enslaved Christians, interpreted as a direct prophecy of their own liberation.

Enslaved Christianity was not simply the acceptance of their enslavers' religion. White preachers who told enslaved people that the Bible commanded obedience to masters were met with enslaved congregations who maintained their own religious meetings, their own preachers, and their own theological understandings. The "invisible institution" — the network of hidden religious meetings that enslaved people held away from white supervision — maintained a tradition of Black Christianity that combined African performance traditions with biblical themes of liberation, community, and divine justice.

Multiple Perspectives

The Stono Rebellion of 1739

From the perspective of the enslaved rebels: For the men who led the Stono Rebellion, the action was a reasoned choice made in specific historical circumstances. Many were from the Kongo Kingdom, where Christianity, military organization, and a tradition of collective action were deeply embedded in the culture. Spanish Florida offered a plausible destination. The timing — a Sunday morning — was deliberately chosen. For these men, rebellion was not irrational desperation but a strategic response to an impossible situation. Most of them understood they might die. They chose to act anyway.
From the perspective of white South Carolina planters: For plantation owners and colonial officials, the Stono Rebellion confirmed their deepest fears. The rebellion was answered with swift and brutal violence — the rebels were killed or captured and publicly executed. The colonial legislature immediately tightened the slave code. Planters argued that stricter control was necessary not only to prevent future rebellion but to protect their families and their economic investments.
From the perspective of Spanish colonial Florida: Spanish officials used the offer of freedom to British enslaved people partly as a diplomatic and military strategy — encouraging rebellion in British colonies was a way of weakening a rival power. Fort Mose, the free Black settlement established near St. Augustine, served both as a refuge for escaped enslaved people and as a military buffer between Spanish Florida and British South Carolina.

Historical note on balance: These three perspectives differ not only in their factual account but in their moral weight. The enslaved rebels were fighting for their freedom. The planter response was a violent defense of a system of human bondage. The historical task is to understand why each group acted as it did — not to conclude that all perspectives were equally right.

Section 8.9

The Formation of African American History

The history of African slavery in the Atlantic world is also the history of African American creation. Out of the horrific violence of the slave trade and plantation slavery emerged communities, cultures, and identities that were neither purely African nor purely American but distinctly and powerfully their own — the foundations of what would become one of the most significant and enduring cultural and political traditions in U.S. history.

This process of cultural creation — what historians call creolization — was not simple or smooth. Enslaved people arrived in the Americas speaking dozens of different African languages, practicing different religions, and carrying the cultural memories of different societies. They were deliberately separated from people who spoke the same language. They were subjected to a system designed to strip them of their names, their cultural practices, and their identities. That they succeeded in creating coherent communities, cultural traditions, and shared identities under these conditions is one of the most remarkable achievements in human history.

Language was one of the first dimensions of this creative process. In communities where enslaved people from many different African language groups were forced together, new creole languages developed — blended tongues that combined elements of different African languages with English, French, Portuguese, or Spanish, depending on the colonial context. Gullah and Geechee in the South Carolina and Georgia lowcountry, Haitian Creole, and Louisiana Creole French all emerged from this process.

Music was another dimension of cultural creation. The music that enslaved people made in North America drew on West and Central African musical traditions — polyphonic vocal harmonies, call-and-response structures, syncopated rhythms — and combined them with the instruments, tunes, and hymns of European American culture. This musical synthesis would eventually produce the blues, jazz, gospel, rock and roll, hip-hop, and many other distinctly American musical forms. The roots of American popular music lie, to a remarkable degree, in the cultural creativity of enslaved communities.

Food, too, became a site of cultural synthesis. Enslaved people grew their own food in garden plots, hunted and fished when possible, and introduced foods from West Africa — okra, black-eyed peas, field peas, watermelon — into the American diet. Enslaved cooks working in plantation kitchens shaped the cuisine that would eventually be called Southern cooking, blending African, Indigenous, and European foodways in ways that reflected the complex cultural interactions of the plantation world.

By the late colonial period, a community that called itself African American had begun to develop a collective identity grounded in shared experience rather than shared African origin. Free Black communities in Northern cities established independent churches, mutual aid societies, and schools. Formerly enslaved people like Phillis Wheatley (who published a volume of poetry in 1773) demonstrated publicly and brilliantly that the claim of African intellectual inferiority was false. Richard Allen, who founded the African Methodist Episcopal Church in Philadelphia in 1816, built an institution of enduring importance. These early Black institutions — church, school, mutual aid society — would become the organizational backbone of African American community life for the next two centuries.

The formation of African American history was also, from its earliest moments, the formation of a freedom struggle. Enslaved people who ran away were asserting their right to freedom. Rebels who took up arms were asserting it more violently. Black preachers who told their congregations that God would deliver them were asserting it spiritually. This freedom struggle — which would eventually produce the abolitionist movement, the Underground Railroad, the Civil War, Reconstruction, and the civil rights movement of the twentieth century — began in the holds of slave ships and on the first plantations of the colonial Americas.

Continuity and Change Over Time

African American Community and Culture from the Early Colonial Period to the Late Eighteenth Century

What Changed

  • The majority of enslaved people shifted from being African-born (carrying specific national identities — Igbo, Wolof, Akan, Kongo) to American-born, identifying as Black or African Americans
  • English replaced African languages as the primary tongue; creole languages developed
  • Specific African national religious practices were creolized into new syncretic American forms
  • Multigenerational community networks developed in regions where natural population growth occurred
  • Early free Black institutions — churches, mutual aid societies, schools — began to form in Northern cities

What Stayed the Same

  • The legal condition of chattel slavery — bondage, family separation, coercion, and violence — persisted across the entire period
  • The desire for freedom, expressed through daily resistance, escape, and rebellion, was constant
  • The centrality of family to enslaved community life, despite the constant threat of separation
  • The assertion of humanity and dignity against a system designed to deny both

Why this pattern matters: Understanding this arc of change and continuity helps students see that African American history did not begin in 1865 with emancipation. The cultural creativity, political consciousness, and community institutions that African Americans brought to the freedom struggles of later centuries were built over many generations, under conditions of extraordinary difficulty, in the period of slavery itself.

Section 8.10

Chapter Review

Everyday Life and Social History: A Day on a Chesapeake Tobacco Farm

On a medium-sized tobacco plantation in the Virginia Tidewater around 1740, an enslaved person we can call James might wake before dawn to the sound of a horn blown by the plantation's overseer. He and the other enslaved men and women who worked the fields — perhaps twenty to thirty in total — gathered in front of their quarters for assignment. The quarters were small cabins, typically shared by multiple people, with packed dirt floors, a fireplace for cooking and heat, and little furniture: a straw sleeping pallet, a wooden chest for any possessions.

The work of a typical day in tobacco season began with hoeing the rows between the tobacco plants to keep down weeds — hot, repetitive work in the Virginia summer heat. Tobacco required constant attention: inspecting leaves for hornworm larvae (which had to be picked off by hand), suckering (removing unwanted side shoots to concentrate the plant's energy), and eventually topping (cutting the flowering head of the plant to encourage leaf growth). The work was done under the supervision of an overseer, who might be white or, on some plantations, an enslaved "driver" — a fellow enslaved person given authority over a gang, a position that placed enormous social and moral pressure on the person who held it.

After the official workday, James might tend a small garden plot. On Saturday evenings, if his enslaver permitted, he might walk several miles to a neighboring plantation where his wife — enslaved by a different planter — lived with their children. On Sunday he might attend a religious meeting where an elderly enslaved man led prayers and scripture reading. Within this constrained and dangerous life, James maintained relationships that mattered deeply to him. He had skills. He had a community. He had a shared moral universe that insisted, against everything the law and the plantation system said, on his own humanity and worth.

Constitutional Connection: Slavery and the Foundations of American Law

The history of slavery in colonial North America connects directly to the constitutional history of the United States. When the framers of the Constitution gathered in Philadelphia in 1787, slavery was already deeply embedded in the legal and economic systems of every state. The Constitution contained several provisions that directly addressed slavery, though it carefully avoided using the word itself.

The Three-Fifths Compromise (Article I, Section 2) determined that enslaved people would count as three-fifths of a person for the purposes of congressional representation and direct taxation — giving slaveholding states greater representation in Congress than they would otherwise have had. The Slave Trade Clause (Article I, Section 9) prohibited Congress from abolishing the international slave trade before 1808. The Fugitive Slave Clause (Article IV, Section 2) required that escaped enslaved people who fled to free states be returned to their enslavers.

These constitutional provisions were not incidental to the founding of the United States; they were central to it. The Constitution was, in part, a document that protected slavery. This fact would be a source of agonizing debate for nineteenth-century abolitionists: was the Constitution a pro-slavery document that had to be repudiated, or a document whose libertarian principles could be turned against slavery? The answer to this question would shape American politics for the next century and beyond.

Cause and Effect

The Growth of the Transatlantic Slave Trade

Long-Term Causes

The existence of slavery as an institution in both African and European societies before Atlantic contact; Portuguese exploration of the West African coast; the development of sugar cultivation in the Atlantic islands; European colonization of the Americas; the catastrophic mortality of Indigenous peoples from epidemic disease; rising European demand for sugar, tobacco, and other plantation products.

Short-Term Causes

Specific decisions by European colonial powers to charter slave-trading companies; decisions by African rulers to participate in the sale of enslaved captives; the rising price of plantation products in European markets; the declining availability of European indentured servants willing to immigrate to colonial labor markets in the late seventeenth century.

The Transformation

From a small-scale trade in the fifteenth century involving thousands of people per year, the slave trade grew to a massive industrial system in the eighteenth century involving tens of thousands per year, reshaping the demography, economy, and culture of three continents.

Long-Term Effects

Demographic catastrophe in some regions of West and Central Africa; creation of African diasporic cultures of extraordinary richness; accumulation of wealth in Europe and North America that contributed to the capital available for the Industrial Revolution; racial ideologies that persisted long after abolition; political conflicts over slavery that shaped American history through the Civil War and beyond.

Historical complexity: The transatlantic slave trade cannot be reduced to a simple story of European villainy. It involved African participation (both coerced and opportunistic), Indigenous dispossession, and the economic decisions of millions of consumers of plantation products. At the same time, recognizing complexity does not mean distributing moral responsibility equally. The primary architects and beneficiaries of the system were European colonial powers and their American colonial descendants.

Chapter Summary

Chapter 8 has traced the origins and development of African slavery in the Atlantic world from the diverse and sophisticated societies of West and Central Africa through the horrors of the transatlantic slave trade, the plantation economies of the Americas, and the remarkable communities and cultures that enslaved people created in the face of extraordinary violence and oppression.

Africa before the transatlantic slave trade was not a single entity but a continent of extraordinary diversity. The great kingdoms of the western Sudan, the sophisticated states of the Gold Coast, the powerful Yoruba city-states, the vast Kongo Kingdom, and dozens of other political and cultural communities had their own histories, achievements, and struggles that must be understood on their own terms before being subsumed into the history of the Atlantic slave trade.

Portuguese exploration of the West African coast in the mid-fifteenth century began a process of commercial engagement that was transformed, over the following century, into the machinery of the transatlantic slave trade. The specific catalyst for this transformation was the development of plantation agriculture — above all, sugar cultivation — in the Atlantic islands and then the Americas. The demand for labor on these plantations was insatiable, and the Atlantic slave trade grew to meet it over three centuries.

The Middle Passage was one of the most horrific passages of mass human suffering in history. Millions of Africans endured weeks at sea in conditions of extraordinary brutality, and roughly 1.8 million did not survive the crossing. Those who did survive arrived already weakened, disoriented, and separated from everything familiar, into a world of permanent bondage.

Racial chattel slavery as it developed in the American colonies was a distinctive and historically specific institution. Through a series of legal decisions in the seventeenth century, colonial legislatures converted the ambiguous status of the first Africans in Virginia into a comprehensive system of hereditary, race-based, permanent bondage. The slave codes that codified this system were enforced through constant violence and terror.

Enslaved people were not passive in the face of this system. They resisted through armed rebellion, escape, daily acts of defiance, and the active preservation of cultural life, family relationships, and spiritual traditions. The Stono Rebellion, the maroon communities of Jamaica and South Carolina, the hidden religious meetings of the "invisible institution," the cultural creativity of the Gullah and Geechee communities — all are evidence of people who maintained their humanity against extraordinary odds.

Family was the emotional core of enslaved community life, even as the threat of family separation through sale was one of the system's most powerful tools of control. Religion gave enslaved people a framework of hope and community. Music, language, food, and material culture all showed the creative synthesis that enslaved people achieved from diverse African traditions and their new American environment.

The formation of African American culture and identity in the colonial period was an act of creation under conditions of destruction. What emerged was not simply an imitation of either African or European culture but something new, resilient, and distinctly its own — the foundations of what would become one of the most significant and enduring cultural and political traditions in U.S. history. Understanding this history is not optional for students who want to understand the United States. It is foundational.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Transatlantic slave trade
The forced transportation of approximately twelve to fifteen million Africans to the Americas between roughly 1500 and 1900; one of the largest forced migrations in human history.
Chattel slavery
A form of slavery in which enslaved people are legally defined as personal property that can be bought, sold, inherited, and transferred in the same way as livestock or goods.
Racial slavery
Slavery based on racial identity, in which African ancestry determined permanent enslaved status and all descendants of enslaved women were themselves enslaved.
Middle Passage
The ocean crossing from West Africa to the Americas that enslaved people were forced to endure; the middle leg of the triangular trade route.
Plantation complex
The system of large-scale agricultural production using enslaved labor for export crops such as sugar, tobacco, rice, and indigo, which dominated the economy of the tropical and subtropical Americas.
Triangular trade
The commercial circuit linking Europe (manufactured goods), West Africa (enslaved people), and the Americas (plantation products).
Slave codes
Laws passed by colonial legislatures that defined enslaved people as property, restricted their rights and movements, and regulated the punishment of resistance.
Partus sequitur ventrem
Latin legal principle meaning "the condition of the child follows the condition of the mother"; the legal foundation of hereditary slavery in Virginia and other colonies from 1662.
Hereditary slavery
The principle that the enslaved status of a mother was automatically transmitted to all of her children, regardless of the father's status.
Creolization
The process by which diverse African peoples, languages, and cultural practices blended with each other and with European and Indigenous elements to produce new, distinctly American cultures.
Maroon
An escaped enslaved person who lived in a free community outside of plantation control, often in remote wilderness areas.
Seasoning
The brutal process by which newly arrived enslaved Africans were forced into the plantation labor regime; mortality during this period was very high.
Royal African Company
English company chartered in 1672 to conduct the slave trade; after its monopoly was broken in 1698, independent merchants dramatically expanded the English slave trade.
Encomienda
Spanish colonial labor system granting colonizers the right to the labor of Indigenous people in a particular area; its failure due to epidemic disease contributed to the shift to African enslaved labor.
Manumission
The formal legal release of an enslaved person from bondage.
Syncretism
The blending of different religious or cultural traditions; seen in the way enslaved Africans merged African spiritual practices with Christianity to produce new religious forms.
Gullah/Geechee
Creole language and culture developed by enslaved Africans and their descendants in the Sea Islands of South Carolina and Georgia, preserving significant West and Central African linguistic and cultural elements.
Invisible institution
The network of hidden religious meetings that enslaved people held away from white supervision, maintaining a tradition of Black Christianity centered on liberation and community.
African diaspora
The worldwide dispersion of people of African descent, produced primarily by the transatlantic slave trade.
Stono Rebellion
A 1739 slave uprising in South Carolina in which approximately sixty to eighty enslaved people marched toward Florida, killing white colonists and burning plantations before being suppressed by colonial militia.
Task system
A labor organization system used in South Carolina in which enslaved workers were assigned specific daily tasks and allowed to use remaining time for their own purposes.
Fort Mose
A free Black settlement established near St. Augustine, Florida, in 1738 with Spanish authorization; the first legally sanctioned free Black settlement in North America.

Major People and Places

Identification & Significance

Olaudah Equiano (c. 1745–1797)
Igbo-born formerly enslaved man who published his 1789 memoir, which became one of the most important abolitionist texts in history and remains a foundational primary source for the history of the Middle Passage.
King Afonso I of Kongo (ruled c. 1509–1542)
Kongolese ruler who converted to Christianity and wrote to the Portuguese crown protesting the devastating impact of the slave trade on his kingdom; his letters are remarkable documents of African political engagement.
Phillis Wheatley (c. 1753–1784)
Enslaved African woman brought to Boston as a child who became one of the first published African American poets; her 1773 volume demonstrated publicly that claims of African intellectual inferiority were false.
Richard Allen (1760–1831)
Formerly enslaved Methodist minister who founded the African Methodist Episcopal (AME) Church in Philadelphia in 1816; a central figure in early African American institutional development.
Elmina Castle (est. 1482)
Portuguese trading fort on the Gold Coast (modern Ghana); one of the most important transit points in the history of the transatlantic slave trade, through which millions of enslaved Africans passed before being shipped across the Atlantic.
Kingdom of Dahomey
West African kingdom (modern Benin) that became heavily involved in the slave trade in the eighteenth century, conducting systematic raids on neighboring peoples and selling captives at the coastal port of Ouidah.
Fort Mose
Free Black settlement near St. Augustine, Florida, established in 1738 with Spanish authorization; the first legally sanctioned free Black settlement in North America.
The Stono River (South Carolina)
Site of the 1739 Stono Rebellion; the waterway along which enslaved rebels marched toward Florida before being intercepted by colonial militia.
The Slave Voyages Database
A major digital historical project that has assembled records from approximately 36,000 slaving voyages, allowing historians to reconstruct the scale and geography of the transatlantic slave trade with unprecedented precision.
The Gullah/Geechee Nation
The people and culture of the Sea Islands of South Carolina and Georgia, whose language and cultural practices represent one of the most intact surviving connections to West and Central African traditions in North America.

Chapter Reflection

Chapter 8 has examined one of the most important and most difficult chapters in human history: the origins and development of the transatlantic slave trade and African chattel slavery in the Americas. Why does this chapter belong at the center of any serious study of U.S. history?

The most direct answer is that slavery was not peripheral to the development of the United States; it was foundational. The labor of enslaved Africans and their descendants built the agricultural economies of the colonial South, generated the capital that funded American commercial development, and shaped the social and political institutions of the new nation. The Constitution's compromises over slavery shaped American politics for the first century of the republic's existence. The conflict over slavery's expansion produced the sectional crisis that led to the Civil War. And the legal and social structures created to maintain slavery — racial ideology, legal discrimination, economic inequality rooted in generations of unpaid labor — left legacies that shaped American life long after emancipation.

The second reason this chapter matters is that it introduces students to the African American historical tradition — to the communities, cultures, and freedom struggles that enslaved people built under conditions of extraordinary oppression. African American history is not simply the history of victimhood. It is the history of cultural creation, community building, political consciousness, and persistent struggle for freedom and dignity that began in the hold of the first slave ship and has not ended. Understanding this history — its origins in the Atlantic slave trade, its development through generations of bondage, its expression in the cultural traditions of enslaved communities — is essential for understanding American history in its fullest sense.

The third reason is more broadly educational. The history of the transatlantic slave trade teaches students something important about how historical catastrophes happen: not through the actions of uniquely evil people, but through the choices of ordinary people and ordinary institutions in pursuit of profit, power, and security, armed with ideologies that told them their actions were justified. This is one of the most disturbing lessons of history: that ordinary people can participate in and perpetuate extraordinary evil. It is a lesson that remains relevant.

Finally, this chapter connects forward into the rest of U.S. history in ways that make it indispensable. The enslaved people whose lives are traced in Chapter 8 were the ancestors of the freedom fighters of later chapters. The racial ideologies whose origins are examined here shaped the politics of the Reconstruction era and the social structures challenged by the civil rights movement. The wealth generated by slave labor contributed to the economic development traced in the chapters on industrial capitalism. And the constitutional compromises made to accommodate slavery shaped the political conflicts that led to the Civil War. The United States cannot fully understand itself without understanding the history examined in this chapter. It is not comfortable history. But it is essential.

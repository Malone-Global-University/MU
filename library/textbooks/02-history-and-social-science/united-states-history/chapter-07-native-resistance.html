Part II — Contact, Colonization, and Atlantic Worlds

Native Resistance, Adaptation, and Survival

United States History: Indigenous America, Republic, Conflict, and Change

Chapter Opening

"Indigenous peoples were not passive subjects of colonial history. They were active agents who made choices, exercised power, suffered losses, and survived. Understanding their choices and their survival is not a supplement to the history of colonial America — it is the history of colonial America."

In the autumn of 1675, a Wampanoag leader named Metacom — known to English colonists as King Philip — watched the world his father Massasoit had navigated with such diplomatic care collapse into fire and blood. English settlements had expanded relentlessly across southern New England for more than half a century. Land that Wampanoag communities had managed, hunted, and farmed for generations had been purchased under agreements many Native leaders did not fully understand, or had been seized outright. Colonial courts now claimed jurisdiction over Indigenous people. English livestock destroyed Native crops. Missionaries pressed for Christian conversion. By 1675, Metacom understood that the space available for Wampanoag sovereignty — the political, cultural, and territorial independence his people had defended through diplomacy, negotiation, and patience — had nearly vanished. He made the decision to fight. The conflict that followed, which colonists called King Philip's War, became one of the most devastating in seventeenth-century North America, destroying dozens of English towns and killing thousands of people on both sides before Metacom was killed and his followers sold into Caribbean slavery or scattered across the continent.

King Philip's War was not an isolated explosion of violence. It was the product of decades of pressure, broken agreements, demographic collapse, economic entanglement, and the gradual erosion of Indigenous sovereignty — and it was one episode in a much longer story of Native resistance, adaptation, and survival across the entire continent. This chapter studies that story. It examines how Indigenous peoples responded to European colonization, not as passive victims of overwhelming forces, but as active decision-makers who resisted, negotiated, traded, relocated, fought, converted, adapted, and persisted under conditions they had not chosen and could not fully control.

The story of Native responses to colonization is not a simple narrative of defeat. Across North America, Indigenous communities made choices under conditions of extreme pressure. Some nations chose diplomacy over warfare, building alliances with one European empire to check another. Some communities adopted European trade goods, horses, and firearms while maintaining the political structures, spiritual practices, and community bonds that defined them as distinct peoples. Some fought wars of resistance that temporarily halted or reversed European expansion. Some survived epidemics that destroyed half or more of their populations by reorganizing communities, adopting refugees from other nations, and rebuilding social networks. Some communities converted to Christianity — at least outwardly — while sustaining Indigenous practices beneath the surface. Many did all of these things in different combinations at different times.

This complexity matters because the history of European colonization in North America has too often been told as the story of European expansion and Indigenous disappearance. That framing is both historically inaccurate and deeply misleading. Indigenous peoples did not disappear. They survived. They fought. They adapted. They maintained identities, cultures, kinship networks, spiritual traditions, and political claims across generations of colonial pressure. Understanding that survival — and the active choices it required — is essential to understanding not just the seventeenth century but the entire arc of U.S. history. The political struggles of Native nations in the twenty-first century are rooted in the same fundamental questions of sovereignty and survival that Metacom confronted in 1675.

The evidence historians use to study Indigenous resistance and adaptation is varied and challenging. European colonial documents — letters, court records, missionary reports, military dispatches — survive in large quantities, but they were written from the outside. They often misunderstood Indigenous motives, misrepresented Native cultures, or deliberately distorted events to justify colonial policies. Alongside these sources, historians rely on archaeological evidence, oral traditions, material culture, linguistic analysis, demographic records, and — increasingly — scholarly collaboration with Indigenous communities whose own historical memory preserves knowledge that written documents do not. Reading across these different kinds of evidence, and reading colonial documents against the grain, allows historians to reconstruct a richer picture of how Native peoples experienced and shaped the colonial world.

This chapter is organized around the major dimensions of Indigenous response to colonization: the effects of epidemic disease and how communities survived catastrophic population loss; the transformation of trade and its effects on Native economies and politics; the diplomatic strategies Native nations used to navigate the competition between European empires; the experience of Christian missionization and its complex effects on Indigenous communities; warfare and armed resistance; and the specific cases of King Philip's War and the Pueblo Revolt of 1680 — two of the most significant moments of armed Indigenous resistance in colonial North America. It concludes by examining how Indigenous persistence and adaptation carried Native peoples through the colonial era and into the centuries that followed.

The central argument of this chapter is straightforward: Indigenous peoples were not passive subjects of colonial history. They were active agents who made choices, exercised power, suffered losses, and survived. Understanding their choices and their survival is not a supplement to the history of colonial America — it is the history of colonial America.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain why Indigenous peoples had multiple and varied responses to European colonization rather than a single unified reaction.
  2. Describe the effects of epidemic disease on Native communities and analyze how communities survived catastrophic population loss.
  3. Trace how European trade goods changed Native economies, politics, and social relationships.
  4. Analyze the diplomatic strategies Indigenous nations used to navigate European imperial rivalries.
  5. Evaluate the goals and effects of European Christian missionary programs on Indigenous communities.
  6. Identify the major causes, events, and consequences of King Philip's War in southern New England.
  7. Identify the major causes, events, and consequences of the Pueblo Revolt of 1680 in the Southwest.
  8. Compare the strategies of armed resistance, diplomacy, and cultural adaptation as Indigenous responses to colonization.
  9. Assess the evidence historians use to reconstruct Indigenous experiences of colonization.
  10. Connect the themes of sovereignty, survival, and resistance in the colonial period to later struggles in U.S. history.
  11. Distinguish between the loss of political and territorial sovereignty and the persistence of Indigenous cultural identity, community, and historical memory.
  12. Contextualize the experiences of specific Native nations within the broader patterns of colonial North America.

Questions for Historical Inquiry

  2. How did epidemic disease change Native communities, and how did communities survive these changes?
  3. How did European trade goods transform Indigenous economies and political relationships?
  4. How did Indigenous nations use European imperial rivalries to their advantage, and how did those strategies change over time?
  5. What did Christian missionization mean for Indigenous peoples — religiously, politically, socially, and culturally?

  10. How do the patterns of resistance and adaptation in the colonial period continue to shape Indigenous history in later centuries?

Historical Context

To understand how Indigenous peoples responded to European colonization, students must first understand what colonization meant in practice. Europeans did not arrive in North America and immediately overwhelm Native nations with superior military force. For most of the seventeenth century — and in many regions well beyond — Indigenous peoples outnumbered European settlers, controlled most of the land, and held military and diplomatic advantages that colonial governments could not easily overcome. European expansion was contested, incomplete, and reversible in ways that later historical memory has obscured.

What made colonization so devastating was not military conquest alone. It was the combination of epidemic disease, which killed enormous proportions of Native populations before sustained military conflict began; the transformation of trade, which restructured Indigenous economies in ways that increased dependency on European goods; the competition between European empires, which gave some Native nations leverage but also drew them into conflicts not of their choosing; and the gradual, relentless pressure of settler expansion, which pushed Native communities from their lands, disrupted subsistence systems, and undermined the political structures that maintained sovereignty.

Before sustained contact with Europeans, North America was home to several hundred distinct Native nations, speaking hundreds of different languages and organized through an enormous variety of political systems, kinship structures, economic practices, and spiritual traditions. These communities were not isolated from each other. They were connected through extensive trade networks, diplomatic relationships, and patterns of migration that stretched across the continent. When Europeans arrived, they entered this existing world and had to navigate its politics, relationships, and power structures. For most of the seventeenth century, Native nations were not simply reacting to European power — they were incorporating Europeans into pre-existing systems of alliance, trade, and diplomacy, often on terms that Indigenous leaders defined.

The first decades of sustained contact in any region were often marked not by conflict but by trade. European demand for furs, hides, and other American goods gave Native producers real economic leverage. Indigenous hunters and traders knew the land, knew the trade routes, controlled the supply, and could choose which European partners to work with. This gave Native nations significant diplomatic power in the early contact period. The key question was whether that power could be maintained as European populations grew, as more land was settled, and as the fur trade gradually exhausted animal populations and the terms of trade shifted.

By the mid-seventeenth century, the pressures were intensifying across much of eastern North America. English, French, Dutch, and Spanish colonial populations were growing. Land was being transferred — through purchase, treaty, or seizure — at rates that alarmed Indigenous leaders. The fur trade had created new dependencies on European goods like metal tools, firearms, and cloth. European missionaries were pressing for religious conversion. Colonial courts were extending jurisdiction over Native peoples. These pressures did not appear everywhere at the same rate or in the same forms, but they defined the world within which Native peoples had to make choices.

Alliance of Mohawk, Oneida, Onondaga, Cayuga, and Seneca nations positioned between French and English colonial zones. Used that position to maintain diplomatic leverage throughout the colonial period.

Diverse communities of the Rio Grande valley speaking multiple languages but sharing architectural, ceremonial, and agricultural traditions. United to expel Spanish colonizers in 1680.

Segregated communities for Christianized Native Americans in Massachusetts, established by John Eliot beginning in 1651. Fourteen such towns existed before King Philip's War.

Section 7.1

Indigenous Choices in a Changing World

When European colonizers arrived on the eastern shores of North America, they did not arrive in an empty or passive world. They arrived in a world of established nations, complex diplomacies, ongoing rivalries, and long-tested strategies for managing relations with strangers. Native peoples did not experience European colonization as the overwhelming, inevitable force that later histories sometimes portrayed. They experienced it as a new set of challenges and opportunities that required responses — responses shaped by each nation's specific circumstances, political traditions, geographic position, demographic situation, and relationships with neighboring communities.

The first and most important point about Indigenous responses to colonization is that they were not uniform. There was no single "Native response" to European expansion. The Haudenosaunee Confederacy, sitting between English and French colonial spheres in the Northeast, used that position to play the two empires against each other for over a century, extracting trade goods, military support, and political recognition from both sides in exchange for alliance. The Catawba Nation of the Carolina Piedmont, devastated by epidemic disease and facing the collapse of older political structures, responded by drawing together the survivors of multiple different nations into a new, unified political community. The Pueblo peoples of the Rio Grande, subjected to Spanish labor demands and religious suppression for over eighty years, organized a secret, coordinated revolt that expelled the Spanish entirely for over a decade. Smaller coastal communities in New England, hemmed in by English settlement and losing access to traditional resources, sometimes sought survival through accommodation and Christianity even as their political independence eroded.

These different responses were not random. They reflected rational calculations — made under conditions of incomplete information and enormous pressure — about what strategies were most likely to preserve community survival, cultural integrity, and political autonomy in each specific situation. Understanding Indigenous choices means understanding the specific situations Native peoples faced, not judging their decisions by outcomes that could not have been foreseen.

One of the most important factors shaping Native choices was the balance of power. In regions where Indigenous nations remained militarily strong and demographically significant relative to European settlers, armed resistance was a viable option that could succeed. In regions where epidemic disease had devastated Native populations and European settlers had established numerical and military superiority, the costs of armed resistance were often prohibitive, and other strategies — accommodation, diplomacy, alliance — were more rational choices for community survival. Power was not static, however. The balance shifted over decades, and strategies that worked in one period might become untenable as conditions changed.

Geography also shaped Indigenous responses. Nations with access to multiple European trading partners — as the Haudenosaunee did, positioned between French Canada and the English colonies — had more diplomatic leverage than nations surrounded by a single colonial power. Nations controlling key trade routes or resources had leverage that purely agricultural communities might lack. Coastal nations, first exposed to European settlement, faced pressures that interior nations could observe and sometimes prepare for. Distance from European settlements did not provide permanent protection, but it provided time.

Internal political factors mattered too. Nations with strong confederacy structures that unified multiple communities — like the Haudenosaunee or the Pueblo peoples in 1680 — were better positioned to mount coordinated responses to colonial pressure than smaller, more isolated communities. But confederacy also had limits: colonial powers often deliberately tried to divide confederate nations by dealing separately with individual communities, making separate treaties, and offering different terms to different groups.

Finally, individual Indigenous leaders and communities had agency within these structural constraints. Diplomatic skill, military judgment, spiritual authority, and political acumen all shaped outcomes. Metacom's decision to go to war in 1675, and Popé's organizing of the Pueblo Revolt in 1680, were exercises of individual and communal judgment that changed the course of events. These were not inevitable or predictable outcomes of structural forces; they were choices.

Historical Vocabulary
Agency

Agency refers to the capacity of individuals or groups to make meaningful choices and act on them, even within structures of constraint and limitation. Historians emphasize Indigenous agency to counter older narratives that portrayed Native peoples purely as passive victims of European expansion. Recognizing agency does not mean ignoring power. Native peoples faced enormous pressures, enormous losses, and conditions of terrible inequality. But within those conditions, they made choices — and those choices mattered. Agency and oppression coexist. Understanding both is essential to historical accuracy.

Section 7.2

Disease, Population Loss, and Community Survival

The single most devastating consequence of European contact was epidemic disease. Before sustained contact with Europeans, the peoples of the Americas had no prior exposure to and therefore no biological immunity to many Old World diseases: smallpox, measles, typhus, influenza, bubonic plague, and others. When these diseases arrived — often traveling ahead of European settlers through Indigenous trade networks — the results were catastrophic. Historians estimate that Indigenous populations across the Americas declined by 50 to 90 percent in the first century or so after sustained contact, though estimates vary by region and are subject to ongoing scholarly debate. In some areas, the decline was even more severe.

The mechanisms of this demographic collapse were complex. Epidemic disease was the primary driver, but it interacted with other factors. Communities weakened by disease were more vulnerable to famine when they could not plant or harvest crops. Wars fought between communities already reduced by disease could be more devastating than they would otherwise have been. The disruption of social structures — the death of elders who held community knowledge, the loss of healers and leaders, the destruction of kinship networks — undermined the social fabric that allowed communities to respond to crisis.

The scale of population loss was almost impossible to process. In some communities, more than half of all people died within a few years. Elders who held oral traditions, ceremonial knowledge, diplomatic relationships, and practical expertise died before they could pass their knowledge to the next generation. Entire villages were emptied. In New England, English settlers who arrived in the 1620s encountered a landscape of abandoned village sites and cleared agricultural fields — the result of an epidemic that had swept through the region between 1616 and 1619, killing perhaps 90 percent of the coastal Wampanoag and Massachusett populations. Massasoit, the Wampanoag leader who negotiated with Plymouth Colony in 1621, was working from a position of demographic weakness that would have been unimaginable a decade earlier. His diplomatic engagement with the English reflected, in part, a calculation that alliance with Plymouth could provide security against neighboring nations who had survived the epidemic in greater numbers.

But Indigenous communities did not simply collapse. They survived — often through creative reorganization. One of the most important adaptive strategies was the adoption of survivors from other communities. As epidemics killed large proportions of some nations, surviving members sometimes merged with neighboring peoples, creating new composite communities. The Catawba Nation in the Carolina Piedmont is perhaps the most striking example: a new political community formed in the early eighteenth century from the survivors of the Catawba, Waxhaw, Sugaree, Keyauwee, and at least a dozen other nations, all devastated by disease and slave raiding. The Catawba Nation that emerged from this crisis was genuinely new — yet it preserved cultural practices, political forms, and community bonds that connected it to all of its predecessor peoples.

The Haudenosaunee Confederacy used a similar strategy rooted in their "mourning war" tradition. When community members died — from disease or from warfare — Haudenosaunee communities conducted raids to take captives who would be adopted into the community to replace the dead. This practice, which had deep roots in Haudenosaunee political culture long before European contact, took on new urgency as epidemic disease created enormous demographic pressure. The Beaver Wars of the mid-seventeenth century, in which the Haudenosaunee expanded violently into neighboring regions, were partly driven by this need for captives to replace epidemic losses. Thousands of captives from nations including the Huron (Wendat), Erie, and the Neutral Nation were incorporated into Haudenosaunee communities, sometimes constituting a majority of the population in some towns.

Communities also survived through the preservation of spiritual practices, oral traditions, kinship networks, and cultural knowledge. Even as populations declined catastrophically, the networks of meaning and identity that defined communities as distinct peoples survived. Songs, ceremonies, stories, languages, and social practices were carried through the crisis by survivors who understood their preservation as itself a form of resistance. The knowledge that communities maintained — about their territories, their histories, their relationships, their responsibilities — provided the foundation for eventual demographic recovery.

Multiple Perspectives

Epidemic Disease and Colonial Interpretation

From the perspective of English colonists: Many English colonists interpreted epidemic disease as evidence of divine favor — proof that God was clearing the land for the chosen people. Governor John Winthrop of Massachusetts wrote that smallpox "sweeps away" the Native population to "make room" for the English. This interpretation served as a theological justification for dispossession and absolved colonists of responsibility for the effects of contact.
From the perspective of Puritan missionaries: John Eliot and others sometimes saw epidemic death as evidence that Native peoples needed to convert to Christianity to secure both spiritual and physical salvation. This interpretation motivated missionary work but also used the crisis of epidemic death as leverage for religious pressure. The epidemic was not a reason to question English expansion but a reason to accelerate missionization.
From the perspective of Native peoples: Indigenous peoples experienced and interpreted epidemic disease through their own spiritual traditions. In many cosmologies, disease was connected to spiritual imbalance — to broken relationships, violated ceremonies, or the anger of powerful spiritual beings. Epidemics that killed healers and spiritual leaders were thus doubly devastating. Some Native peoples interpreted the epidemics as a sign that traditional practices were failing; others interpreted them as evidence that the newcomers possessed dangerous spiritual power that needed to be managed.
From the perspective of historians today: Modern historians recognize epidemic disease as one of the central mechanisms of colonial dispossession. Debate continues about whether disease spread was ever deliberately weaponized by colonial authorities — the Fort Pitt blanket incident of 1763 is the most documented case. What is not in dispute is that the demographic consequences were catastrophic, irreversible in the short term, and central to the political history of colonial North America.
Section 7.3

Trade Goods and New Economic Relationships

The fur trade was the engine of early colonial economics in the Northeast and Great Lakes regions, and it transformed Native societies in ways that went far beyond economics. European demand for beaver pelts — fashioned into felt hats enormously popular in European fashion markets — created a commercial relationship that gave Indigenous hunters and traders real economic power in the early colonial period. Native peoples knew the land, knew the animals, controlled access to the interior, and could choose which European partners to work with. This leverage shaped the early fur trade as a negotiated relationship rather than simple exploitation.

But the fur trade also created new dependencies. Metal tools — knives, axes, needles, kettles — were more efficient than their stone or ceramic equivalents, and once communities adopted them, returning to traditional technologies became increasingly difficult. Firearms required European powder and lead for ammunition. Wool cloth was warmer and more durable than many traditional garments. Over time, these goods shifted from luxury additions to necessities, and communities that needed them had to maintain trading relationships with European suppliers.

The fur trade also restructured political relationships among Native nations. Access to European trade goods became a source of political power — nations that controlled trade routes or had preferred relationships with European traders had advantages over those that did not. This created incentives for some nations to expand militarily to control trade corridors or to destroy competitors. The Beaver Wars, in which the Haudenosaunee Confederacy conducted massive campaigns against neighboring nations from the 1630s through the early eighteenth century, were partly driven by competition for beaver-rich territories as the fur trade exhausted animal populations in the Haudenosaunee homeland. The wars displaced or destroyed dozens of nations across the Great Lakes region, creating a large-scale population movement that reshaped the political geography of the entire continent's interior.

The introduction of horses, which spread northward from Spanish New Mexico beginning in the late seventeenth century, transformed the cultures of the Great Plains even more dramatically. By the early eighteenth century, nations including the Comanche, Lakota, and Cheyenne had adopted horses as central to their economies, political structures, and military power. Horse cultures were not simply old cultures with a new animal — they represented genuinely new social orders, with new patterns of gender relations, new military strategies, new political hierarchies, and new relationships to territory. The Comanche built an empire of extraordinary power on the southern Plains, controlling trade routes, demanding tribute from neighboring peoples, and effectively limiting Spanish, French, and later American expansion for over a century.

The introduction of firearms created similar transformations, particularly in the Northeast. Nations with access to firearms through trade had military advantages over those that did not, creating powerful incentives to maintain trading relationships even when those relationships involved significant compromises of political independence. The Haudenosaunee were among the first Native nations to acquire firearms through Dutch and then English trade, and this military advantage was one factor — alongside their confederacy structure and diplomatic skill — that allowed them to maintain their position against both European and Indigenous rivals throughout the seventeenth century.

Historical Vocabulary
Dependency

Economic dependency refers to a condition in which one party's economic survival depends on continued access to goods or resources controlled by another party. In colonial North America, many Indigenous communities developed dependencies on European trade goods — metal tools, firearms, cloth, ammunition — that did not exist before contact. Dependency did not eliminate Native agency; Native peoples continued to make active decisions about how to manage trade relationships. But it constrained choices and created vulnerabilities. When trade was cut off — as it sometimes was during warfare or political disputes — communities that had come to depend on European goods faced serious difficulties. Historians debate how quickly and how completely dependencies developed, and some scholars emphasize that trade relationships were more reciprocal and negotiated than the dependency framework implies.

Section 7.4

Diplomacy with European Empires

One of the most sophisticated and important Indigenous responses to European colonization was diplomacy — the use of negotiation, alliance, and strategic statecraft to manage relationships with European powers and protect Indigenous sovereignty. Native peoples were not novices at diplomacy when Europeans arrived. They had millennia of experience managing relationships between nations, negotiating alliances, conducting trade, making peace, and resolving disputes through formal protocols. They brought this expertise to bear in their relationships with European newcomers.

The Haudenosaunee Confederacy's diplomatic history in the colonial period is one of the most remarkable examples of sustained political skill in North American history. The Confederacy's geographical position — between the English colonies centered in New York and New England, and the French colonial sphere centered in Canada — gave it leverage that its leaders exploited brilliantly. For most of the seventeenth and early eighteenth centuries, the Haudenosaunee played French and English interests against each other, maintaining a posture of formal neutrality that extracted trade goods, diplomatic recognition, and military support from both empires in exchange for alliance.

The Haudenosaunee developed an elaborate diplomatic system, the "Covenant Chain," with the English colonies, particularly New York. This ceremonial diplomatic system, involving regular conferences, gift exchanges, speeches, and the renewing of alliance commitments, created a framework for managing political relationships that both sides found useful. For the English, the Covenant Chain provided a claim to Haudenosaunee alliance that helped counter French influence. For the Haudenosaunee, it provided access to English trade goods and recognition of their political authority over a broad region. The Confederacy used these diplomatic relationships to extend its own influence, sometimes claiming to speak for other Native nations in negotiations with European powers, even when those nations had not authorized such representation.

In the Southeast, the Creek Confederacy — a diverse alliance of communities speaking multiple languages — used its geographic position between English Carolina, Spanish Florida, and French Louisiana to maintain a "play-off system," balancing competing European powers against each other for over a century. Creek diplomats were skilled practitioners of what scholars call "strategic ambiguity," maintaining relationships with multiple European powers without fully committing to any single alliance. This strategy preserved Creek political independence longer than the approach of those nations that tied themselves too closely to a single European partner.

In the Southwest, Pueblo peoples used a different kind of diplomacy under Spanish colonial rule. Outwardly compliant with Spanish religious and political demands, many Pueblo communities maintained secret ceremonial practices, preserved traditional governance structures beneath the surface of Christian compliance, and maintained communication networks with other Pueblo communities and with non-Pueblo nations on the surrounding plains. This covert maintenance of cultural and political institutions was itself a form of diplomacy — managing a relationship with an imperial power that could not be openly defied without catastrophic consequences, while preserving the community foundations needed for eventual resistance.

Diplomacy had limits, of course. It worked most effectively when Native nations had resources, military strength, or geographic position that Europeans valued. As settler populations grew and Indigenous demographic and military power declined relative to colonial strength, the leverage that underpinned diplomatic strategies eroded. European powers also learned to use divide-and-conquer strategies — making separate agreements with individual nations within confederacies, creating factions within Indigenous political communities, and using trade leverage to divide potential alliances.

Section 7.5

Missionization and Religious Pressure

Christian missionization was a central feature of European colonial projects across North America, though its forms varied significantly between Spanish, French, and English colonial contexts. For Indigenous peoples, missionization represented not only religious pressure but a comprehensive attempt to transform social organization, political authority, labor patterns, gender roles, kinship structures, and cultural practices. Understanding the complexity of Indigenous responses to missionization requires understanding what missionaries actually demanded and what conversion actually meant in practice.

In Spanish colonial territories, Catholic missionization was organized through the Franciscan order and was inseparable from the political and economic structures of colonial administration. The missions in New Mexico, California, Texas, and Florida were not only religious institutions; they were labor systems. Pueblo peoples in New Mexico were required to provide labor to build mission churches, often using traditional forms of communal labor organization but directed by Spanish missionaries rather than Pueblo leaders. They were forbidden to practice their traditional ceremonies, their kiva ceremonies were raided, and their ritual objects — kachina masks, ceremonial objects, prayer sticks — were confiscated and burned. The suppression of Pueblo ceremonial life was not merely cultural interference; it was an assault on the entire basis of Pueblo social order, since ceremonial practices were deeply integrated with the management of water, crops, and community governance.

Pueblo responses to Spanish missionization were mixed. Some Pueblo individuals genuinely converted to Christianity, finding in it resources that made sense within their spiritual frameworks. Others maintained traditional practices in secret while performing compliance with Christian ritual demands. Still others maintained their traditional practices openly in communities where Spanish oversight was minimal. The pattern that emerged in most Pueblo communities was not straightforward conversion or straightforward resistance but a complex navigation of official demands and community needs — a navigation that preserved enough of the traditional ceremonial life to eventually support the coordinated revolt of 1680.

French Jesuit missionaries in the Great Lakes region and Canada worked under somewhat different conditions. The Jesuits, unlike the Franciscans in New Mexico, did not have a coercive labor system backing their missionary work. They relied on persuasion, learning Indigenous languages, living with Indigenous communities, and presenting Christianity in terms that made sense within existing spiritual frameworks. Jesuit missions still represented pressure, however — pressure to abandon polygamy, to restructure gender roles, to adopt Christian calendar observances, and to reorient community allegiances. And the Jesuit missions served French colonial interests by creating networks of allied, Christianized Native communities that provided intelligence, military support, and trade partners for New France.

English Protestant missions in New England operated through a different model: the "praying town." John Eliot, a Puritan minister, established a network of segregated communities for Christianized Native Americans in Massachusetts, beginning with Natick in 1651. By 1675, fourteen praying towns housed perhaps four thousand Native Christians. The praying towns required converts to abandon many traditional practices and to adopt English patterns of family organization, settled agriculture, and Puritan religious observance. They were deliberately segregated from both English settler society and traditional Native communities, creating a kind of social limbo for converts. Some Native peoples chose the praying towns as a survival strategy in a rapidly changing world; others chose them out of genuine religious conviction; still others were driven to them by the collapse of other options.

What is most important to understand is that missionization rarely produced the straightforward cultural transformation missionaries sought. Even communities that nominally converted to Christianity typically maintained elements of traditional spiritual practice, often blending Christian and Indigenous elements in ways that missionaries condemned as incomplete conversion but that served the needs of community members. This syncretism — the blending of different religious systems — was a form of creative adaptation that allowed communities to satisfy colonial demands while preserving elements of cultural identity.

Section 7.6

Warfare and Resistance

Armed resistance was one of the most dramatic — and costly — responses to European colonization. Indigenous peoples fought against European colonial expansion across the continent throughout the colonial period, often achieving significant military successes. Understanding this armed resistance requires understanding both its causes and its limits.

The causes of Indigenous armed resistance were almost always specific, immediate, and rooted in concrete grievances: land taken without adequate compensation, treaty promises violated, trade agreements broken, missionary interference with ceremonial life, colonial courts extending jurisdiction over Native peoples, colonial violence going unpunished, or the killing of Indigenous leaders. Wars did not begin simply because Europeans and Native peoples were inherently hostile. They began because specific actions by specific people violated the terms of existing relationships.

Indigenous military capacity in the seventeenth century was significant and should not be underestimated. Native warriors were often skilled fighters with deep knowledge of the terrain, superior mobility in forested or swampy environments, and the ability to sustain campaigns through local knowledge and subsistence strategies. Many Native nations had been fighting wars against each other for generations and had developed sophisticated military traditions — including both tactical excellence and the diplomatic knowledge of when to fight and when to negotiate.

But armed resistance against European colonialism also faced structural disadvantages that became more severe over time. European colonial populations were growing faster than Native populations could recover from epidemic losses. European nations could draw on metropolitan resources — troops, supplies, firearms — that Native nations could not match. European military technology, particularly artillery and the fortification of settlements, gave defensive advantages that were difficult to overcome. And Native confederacies, while sometimes extensive, were rarely able to maintain the unified front needed to prosecute a sustained military campaign across a large region.

The Pequot War of 1636–1638 illustrates both the potential and the limits of armed resistance in early New England. The Pequot nation, which controlled trade along the Connecticut River and had long been a powerful force in the region, came into conflict with English settlers over trade disputes, killings, and territorial pressure. When war broke out, the English allied with the Narragansett and Mohegan nations — Pequot rivals — and mounted a devastating campaign that culminated in the Mystic Massacre of May 1637, in which English and Native allied forces surrounded and burned a Pequot village, killing four to seven hundred people, mostly women, children, and elderly. The survivors were hunted down and either killed or sold into Caribbean slavery. The Pequot nation, as a political entity, was effectively destroyed. The Treaty of Hartford in 1638 formally dissolved the Pequot nation, prohibited the use of the name "Pequot," and divided surviving Pequots among the English's Native allies.

The Pequot War had two important consequences. First, it demonstrated the lethal potential of English-Native coalitions, in which English firepower and resources combined with Native military skill and territorial knowledge to produce devastating results against a common enemy. Second, it established a pattern in which English colonists used military force not merely to defeat enemies but to destroy them as political entities — a pattern that would be repeated throughout the colonial period and beyond.

Section 7.7

King Philip's War and Regional Conflict

King Philip's War, which began in June 1675 and lasted until August 1676, was the most devastating conflict in seventeenth-century New England, proportionately one of the deadliest wars in American history, and a turning point in the political history of Indigenous New England. Understanding it requires understanding the long accumulation of pressures that preceded it.

By 1675, English settlement had transformed southern New England in ways that left diminishing room for Wampanoag sovereignty. The English population had grown to perhaps sixty thousand in Massachusetts, Plymouth, Connecticut, and Rhode Island — far outnumbering the Native populations of the region, which had never fully recovered from the epidemic of 1616–1619. English farms, livestock, and settlements had spread across former Wampanoag and Narragansett territories. English courts had extended criminal jurisdiction over Native peoples, including capital jurisdiction — the authority to execute. In 1675, three Wampanoag men were tried and executed by a Plymouth Colony court for the murder of John Sassamon, a Christian Wampanoag man who had reportedly warned Plymouth officials of Metacom's plans for war. For Metacom and many other Wampanoag leaders, the execution of Wampanoag men by an English court was an intolerable violation of sovereignty — a declaration, in effect, that the Wampanoag were subject to English law.

The war that began in June 1675 was not a spontaneous explosion. It was the result of Metacom's long preparation and the alignment of numerous grievances and strategic calculations across multiple Native nations. In addition to the Wampanoag, the Nipmuc and eventually the Narragansett joined the conflict. The Narragansett, who had initially remained neutral, were attacked by colonial forces in December 1675 in what became known as the Great Swamp Massacre, when English and allied Native soldiers attacked a Narragansett winter encampment, killing several hundred people and destroying their food stores. The attack drove the Narragansett, who might otherwise have stayed out of the conflict, into active alliance with Metacom.

The early months of the war went badly for the English. Native forces attacked and burned dozens of English towns. By the spring of 1676, the war had reached within twenty miles of Boston. English forces suffered significant military reverses, and colonial governments struggled to organize effective responses. The war revealed that English settlers were far more vulnerable than they had imagined, and that the Native nations of southern New England, despite their demographic losses, remained formidable military adversaries.

The tide turned in the summer of 1676, for several reasons. Supplies for Native forces ran short as the war disrupted planting and hunting. Disease spread through Native encampments. The English recruited substantial numbers of allied Native warriors — particularly from the Mohegan nation and from Christian Indian communities — who provided crucial intelligence and military support. Mohawk warriors, part of the Haudenosaunee Confederacy allied to the English through the Covenant Chain, raided Metacom's forces from the north, cutting off potential refuge. By July and August 1676, the coalition was collapsing. Metacom was killed by a Native soldier fighting for the English on August 12, 1676. His body was dismembered; his head was displayed on a pole in Plymouth for twenty-five years.

The consequences of King Philip's War were devastating for Indigenous New England. Thousands of Wampanoag, Narragansett, and Nipmuc people were killed — the proportional death rate was extraordinarily high. Hundreds more were sold into Caribbean slavery. Metacom's wife and son were among those sold. Surviving community members scattered — some sought refuge with northern Abenaki communities, some merged with Mohawk communities, some remained in New England in greatly diminished circumstances. The war accelerated English expansion, eliminated the major political barrier to settlement of the interior of New England, and established patterns of coercive colonial authority over surviving Native communities that would persist for generations. Yet the Wampanoag did not disappear. The Mashpee Wampanoag and other Wampanoag communities survived and maintained their identities through the colonial period and into the present.

Primary Source Window

Metacom's Grievances, as Recorded by John Easton (1675)

Creator: John Easton, Quaker colonist of Rhode Island · Date: June 1675 · Type: Colonial account of an interview with Wampanoag leaders

Context: Before the outbreak of King Philip's War, Metacom (Philip) was interviewed by Rhode Island colonist John Easton, who was attempting to prevent the war through negotiation. Easton was a Quaker sympathetic to Native grievances and genuinely hoped to mediate the conflict. He recorded the grievances Metacom and other Wampanoag leaders expressed during their meeting.

Easton's account records that Metacom and other Wampanoag leaders described a series of specific grievances. English settlers allowed their cattle and horses to damage Native crops without compensation. When Wampanoag people killed English livestock to protect their crops, they were prosecuted in English courts. English courts had extended criminal jurisdiction over Wampanoag people in cases involving English people, but Wampanoag people could not obtain justice in English courts for injuries done to them by English people. When Wampanoag people sold land, they were later told the sale included more than they had understood. Missionaries had divided Wampanoag communities by creating a separate Christian faction that answered to English authority. And the recent execution of three Wampanoag men by Plymouth Colony was, Metacom argued, an act that violated Wampanoag sovereignty and left his people with no peaceful recourse.

Analysis: Easton's account is an invaluable source because it records Indigenous perspectives on the causes of the war with unusual directness. Easton was motivated to understand and present Wampanoag grievances fairly, which makes his account more sympathetic than most colonial documentation of Native perspectives. However, it remains a colonial account — filtered through Easton's understanding of what was said. It does not capture Wampanoag voices directly, and it represents the perspective of Metacom and his councilors at a specific moment. The account reveals that the war was not, from the Wampanoag perspective, a war of aggression; it was the culmination of decades of specific, concrete grievances about land, law, jurisdiction, and sovereignty.

Section 7.8

Pueblo Revolt and Indigenous Victory

While King Philip's War ended in defeat for the Wampanoag coalition in New England, a different Indigenous rebellion in the Southwest produced a strikingly different outcome — at least in the short term. The Pueblo Revolt of 1680 was the most successful large-scale Indigenous rebellion in colonial North American history, driving Spanish colonizers from New Mexico for twelve years and fundamentally reshaping the terms of Spanish colonial rule when it was eventually reestablished.

The Pueblo peoples of the Rio Grande valley and surrounding regions had been under Spanish colonial rule since Juan de Oñate's conquest of New Mexico in 1598. Spanish colonization imposed a crushing system of tributary labor and religious suppression. Under the encomienda system, Pueblo people were required to provide labor — building churches, farming for Spanish landowners, serving in Spanish households — in addition to their own agricultural work. Franciscan missionaries, whose missions were central to the colonial economy as well as the religious project, demanded labor for church construction and prohibited traditional ceremonial practices. The suppression of Pueblo religious life was an assault on the agricultural and political systems that Pueblo life depended on, since ceremonial practices were deeply integrated with the management of water, crops, and community governance.

By the 1670s, a combination of drought, Apache raids, epidemic disease, and the intensification of Spanish labor demands and religious suppression had created a crisis in Pueblo communities. In 1675, Governor Juan Francisco de Treviño arrested forty-seven Pueblo religious leaders, charging them with sorcery. Three were hanged and one committed suicide before the sentence could be carried out; the rest were publicly whipped and enslaved. This event, which Pueblo people experienced as a profound assault on their spiritual authority, proved to be one of the immediate triggers of the revolt that followed.

Among those arrested and whipped in 1675 was a man from Ohkay Owingeh Pueblo named Popé. Released after Pueblo warriors threatened to withdraw all labor from Santa Fe, Popé dedicated himself to organizing the revolt. He moved to Taos Pueblo, away from the center of Spanish power, and spent years building the conspiracy. The organizational challenge was enormous: the Pueblo peoples spoke at least six mutually unintelligible languages, had no single political authority, and had to coordinate secretly in communities where Spanish missionaries and soldiers might detect any signs of planning. The fact that Popé was able to organize nineteen separate Pueblo communities — stretching over hundreds of miles — and maintain the secrecy of the conspiracy for years is a remarkable testament to the depth of shared grievance and the sophistication of his organizational skill.

The revolt was set for August 11, 1680. When the conspiracy was betrayed by two young men from Tesuque Pueblo, Popé moved the date up by a day, sending runners with knotted cords to Pueblo communities — each knot represented a day, and when the cord was untied, the day had come. On the morning of August 10, Pueblo warriors attacked Spanish settlers, missionaries, and soldiers across New Mexico simultaneously. Over the following days, some four hundred Spanish colonists were killed, including twenty-one of the thirty-three Franciscan missionaries in the province. The remaining survivors — perhaps twenty-five hundred people — fled south to El Paso del Norte. Governor Antonio de Otermín attempted a counterattack but was driven back. The Spanish were gone.

For twelve years, New Mexico was free of Spanish colonial rule. Popé and other Pueblo leaders worked to restore traditional governance and ceremonial life. Missions were destroyed or converted to traditional uses. Christian baptisms were symbolically reversed through washing in rivers. Kachina ceremonies and other traditional practices were openly resumed. But the post-revolt period was also difficult. Drought continued. Apache and Navajo raids intensified in the absence of Spanish military protection. Political disagreements among Pueblo communities created instability. Popé himself became increasingly authoritarian.

When the Spanish returned in 1692, under Governor Diego de Vargas, they retook New Mexico without the scale of violence that had accompanied the original conquest. And critically, the terms of Spanish colonial rule were changed. The encomienda system was not fully reimposed. Franciscan missionaries moderated their demands and no longer raided kiva ceremonies with the same aggression. Pueblo labor requirements were reduced. The Spanish, in effect, acknowledged that the old terms of colonial rule had been untenable. The Pueblo peoples retained far greater autonomy over their internal governance and ceremonial life than they had possessed before 1680. The revolt had permanently changed the relationship between the Spanish and the Pueblo peoples — a genuine and lasting Indigenous political victory.

Historical Debate

Was the Pueblo Revolt Primarily a Religious Rebellion?

The question: What were the primary motivations behind the Pueblo Revolt of 1680? Was it primarily a religious rebellion against Christian missionization, or were political, economic, and social factors equally or more important?

Interpretation A — The revolt as religious rebellion: Many early accounts and some historians have emphasized the religious dimensions of the revolt. Popé's leadership, his invocations of traditional spiritual beings, his message that the Pueblo peoples needed to return to their traditional religious practices, and his reversal of Christian baptisms all suggest that the revolt was, at its core, a religious movement. On this reading, the primary motivation was the suppression of Pueblo ceremonial life by Franciscan missionaries — the confiscation and burning of ritual objects, the prohibition of kachina dances, the whipping of religious leaders.
Interpretation B — The revolt as political and economic resistance: Other historians emphasize that the revolt cannot be understood without the labor demands, tribute extraction, and political dispossession of Spanish colonialism. The encomienda system extracted enormous labor from Pueblo communities. The combination of Spanish demands with drought and Apache raiding created genuine material crisis. The fact that the revolt united communities speaking six different languages suggests that motivations went beyond any single issue, encompassing the full range of colonial exploitation.

Evidence: Both interpretations have significant evidentiary support. Popé's rhetoric emphasized religious restoration, and the immediate actions after the revolt — washing away baptisms, destroying churches, resuming ceremonies — suggest genuine religious motivation. But the same accounts also record Pueblo people describing economic grievances, labor demands, and political oppression as causes of the conflict.

Why it matters: Most contemporary historians see the distinction as somewhat artificial. In Pueblo life, religious practice, political authority, and economic activity were deeply integrated. The suppression of ceremonial life was also an assault on political authority and agricultural management. Understanding the revolt requires understanding this integration rather than isolating one dimension from the others.

Section 7.9

Adaptation, Persistence, and Sovereignty

King Philip's War and the Pueblo Revolt represent the most dramatic moments of armed resistance in seventeenth-century North America, but they represent only part of the story of Indigenous survival during the colonial period. Across the continent, Native peoples survived through strategies of adaptation, cultural persistence, and the defense of sovereignty that did not always involve armed conflict. Understanding these strategies is essential to understanding how Indigenous peoples carried their communities, cultures, and political claims through the colonial era and into the centuries that followed.

One of the most important adaptive strategies was the preservation of core cultural practices even under colonial pressure. Languages were maintained. Oral traditions — stories, histories, ceremonial knowledge, genealogies — were passed from generation to generation. Kinship structures and clan systems, which organized social life, community governance, and obligation networks, continued to function even as the external political environment changed. In many communities, traditional spiritual practices were maintained in private or in modified forms that satisfied colonial observers while preserving essential content. The Pueblo peoples are among the most striking examples: despite nearly a century of Spanish missionization before the revolt, and over three centuries of colonial and then American rule afterward, Pueblo ceremonial traditions survived and continue to be practiced today.

Political adaptation was equally important. As the structures of colonial government extended over Indigenous communities, Native peoples developed strategies for working within those structures while maintaining as much autonomy as possible. Tribal councils continued to function even as colonial authorities claimed the right to oversee or approve their decisions. Native leaders learned colonial legal systems and used them — petitioning colonial courts for redress of grievances, asserting treaty rights in legal proceedings, challenging colonial encroachments through the institutional channels available. This legal engagement was often unsuccessful in the short term, but it preserved the political habit of sovereignty — the insistence on the right to govern their own affairs — that would eventually become the foundation for more successful legal and political struggles.

The Catawba Nation in the Carolina Piedmont demonstrates creative adaptation in a context of extreme demographic pressure. By the early eighteenth century, epidemic disease and slave raiding had devastated the nations of the Carolina Piedmont. The Catawba Nation that emerged in the first decades of the eighteenth century was a new political entity, formed from the survivors of the Catawba, Waxhaw, Sugaree, Keyauwee, Cheraw, and other peoples. What made the Catawba successful as a new nation was their ability to create a shared political identity while honoring the distinct traditions of their constituent peoples, and their skilled diplomacy with English Carolina — positioning themselves as essential allies and buffers against enemy Native nations and potential slave raiders.

The persistence of sovereignty claims — the insistence that Indigenous nations retained the right to govern themselves, control their territories, and determine their own futures — was perhaps the most important form of continuity across the colonial period. Even in communities that had been devastated by disease, dispossessed of land, and placed under colonial political authority, the claim to sovereignty did not disappear. It survived in oral traditions, in the maintenance of ceremonial life, in the continued function of traditional governance structures, and in the memory of treaties and agreements that had recognized Indigenous political authority. These sovereignty claims, preserved through the colonial period, became the foundation for the political and legal struggles of Native nations in the nineteenth, twentieth, and twenty-first centuries.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Indigenous Sovereignty from the Colonial Period to the Present

What Changed

  • The formal political sovereignty of many Indigenous nations — their ability to govern territories without colonial interference — was severely eroded during the colonial period.
  • European colonial governments claimed jurisdiction over Indigenous peoples, controlled trade, extended criminal law over Native territories, and gradually transferred lands to colonial ownership.
  • Native diplomatic relationships with multiple European empires were replaced by a single dominating colonial power, eliminating leverage from the play-off system.
  • Demographic collapse changed the balance of power fundamentally in many regions.

What Stayed the Same

  • Indigenous peoples maintained the cultural, social, and spiritual foundations of community identity through the colonial period.
  • Languages, kinship structures, ceremonial practices, oral traditions, and political habits of self-governance continued.
  • The claim to sovereignty — the belief that Native nations retained inherent rights to self-government — persisted in community memory and was eventually asserted in legal and political struggles.
  • The deep connection between Indigenous peoples and specific territories persisted as both a spiritual and a political claim.

Why the pattern matters: The distinction between the erosion of formal political sovereignty and the persistence of cultural identity and sovereignty claims is crucial for understanding later Indigenous history. The political and legal struggles of Native nations in the nineteenth century, the twentieth century, and the twenty-first century all rest on the unbroken continuity of sovereignty claims that survived even the most devastating periods of colonial history.

Chapter Summary

Chapter 7 has examined Indigenous responses to European colonization — one of the most complex and important topics in early American history. The central argument of the chapter is that Native peoples were not passive subjects of colonial history but active agents who made choices, exercised power, suffered devastating losses, and ultimately survived under conditions of extraordinary pressure.

The most devastating consequence of European contact was epidemic disease, which killed fifty to ninety percent of many Native populations in the decades following sustained contact. Yet Indigenous communities survived this catastrophe through creative strategies: adopting refugees from other nations, reorganizing communities, preserving cultural knowledge and spiritual practices, and rebuilding social networks from the survivors of devastation. The demographic collapse did not erase Indigenous peoples; it forced them to adapt.

The fur trade transformed Native economies and political relationships profoundly. European trade goods — metal tools, firearms, cloth, and ammunition — became necessities in communities that had adopted them. This created dependencies that constrained Indigenous choices and could be leveraged by colonial powers. At the same time, the early fur trade gave Native producers real economic leverage, and the spread of horses and firearms from European trade reshaped the political and military landscape of the continent's interior in ways that sometimes increased Indigenous power.

Diplomacy was one of the most important and sophisticated Indigenous responses to colonization. Native nations across the continent used their geographic positions, their resources, and their political skills to navigate the competition between European empires, extract trade goods and military support from rival colonizers, and maintain as much sovereignty as conditions allowed. The Haudenosaunee Confederacy's strategy between the English and French is the most extensively documented example of this approach, but similar diplomatic sophistication characterized Indigenous responses across the continent.

Christian missionization represented a comprehensive attempt to transform Indigenous societies, not merely to change religious beliefs. Indigenous responses ranged from genuine conversion to strategic compliance to covert maintenance of traditional practices — and most communities did all of these things in different combinations, creating syncretic forms that satisfied colonial observers while preserving elements of cultural identity.

Armed resistance, the most dramatic form of Indigenous response, took many forms across the continent. King Philip's War in New England ended in defeat for the Wampanoag coalition, with devastating consequences for Indigenous New England. The Pueblo Revolt in the Southwest succeeded in expelling the Spanish from New Mexico for twelve years and permanently changed the terms of colonial rule when the Spanish returned — a genuinely significant Indigenous military and political victory.

Through all of these responses — diplomacy, trade, adaptation, missionization, resistance, and cultural persistence — Indigenous peoples maintained the foundations of community identity: languages, kinship structures, spiritual traditions, oral histories, and the claim to sovereignty that asserted their right to govern themselves. These foundations carried Indigenous peoples through the colonial period and provided the basis for the political and legal struggles of later centuries. The history of Native resistance and adaptation in the colonial period is not a story of simple heroism or inevitable defeat. It is a story of complex choices made by intelligent, resilient people in conditions of terrible pressure — and of the persistence that allowed those choices to matter across generations.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Sovereignty
The inherent authority of a people or nation to govern themselves, make decisions about their territory, and determine their own political future. Central to Indigenous history from the colonial period to the present.
Epidemic disease
Highly contagious infectious disease spreading rapidly through populations. European diseases including smallpox, measles, typhus, and influenza killed large proportions of Native populations with no prior immunity.
Demographic collapse
Severe and rapid population decline. Many Indigenous nations lost 50–90% of their people within a century of sustained European contact.
Fur trade
Commercial exchange of animal furs — particularly beaver pelts — between Indigenous hunters and European traders. Dominated the economy of the Northeast and Great Lakes and transformed Indigenous political relationships.
Encomienda
Spanish colonial labor grant giving an individual the right to extract labor and tribute from a specified group of Indigenous people. Central to Spanish colonialism in New Mexico.
Missionization
The process by which European missionaries attempted to convert Indigenous peoples to Christianity, involving not only religious change but comprehensive social, political, and cultural transformation.
Praying towns
Segregated communities for Christianized Native Americans in Massachusetts, established by John Eliot beginning in 1651. Fourteen existed before King Philip's War.
Covenant Chain
The ceremonial diplomatic system maintaining the alliance between the Haudenosaunee Confederacy and the English colonies, especially New York. Involved regular conferences, speeches, and gift exchanges.
Play-off system
The diplomatic strategy used by Indigenous nations of maintaining relationships with multiple competing European empires to preserve political independence by playing rivals against each other.
Syncretism
The blending of different religious or cultural traditions. Indigenous peoples across North America created syncretic forms combining elements of Christianity with traditional beliefs and practices.
Dependency
The condition in which one party's economic survival depends on access to goods or resources controlled by another. Many Indigenous communities developed dependencies on European trade goods.
Mourning war
Haudenosaunee practice of raiding neighboring peoples to take captives who would be adopted into the community to replace members lost to disease or war. Intensified dramatically as epidemic losses mounted.
Beaver Wars
Series of conflicts from the 1630s through the early 1700s in which the Haudenosaunee Confederacy expanded to control fur trade routes, displacing many Great Lakes nations.
Mystic Massacre (1637)
Attack by English and Narragansett/Mohegan forces on a Pequot village, killing several hundred people. Effectively destroyed the Pequot nation as a political entity.
Great Swamp Massacre (1675)
Colonial attack on a Narragansett winter encampment during King Philip's War, killing several hundred people. Drove the previously neutral Narragansett into alliance with Metacom.
King Philip's War (1675–1676)
Armed conflict between Metacom's Wampanoag-led coalition and English colonies in New England. Proportionately one of the deadliest wars in American history; ended Wampanoag political power in the region.
Pueblo Revolt (1680)
Coordinated uprising of Pueblo peoples against Spanish colonial rule in New Mexico, organized by Popé. Expelled the Spanish for twelve years — the most successful Indigenous rebellion in colonial North American history.
Agency
The capacity of individuals or groups to make meaningful choices and act on them, even within structures of constraint and limitation. Essential concept for understanding Indigenous history.
Accommodation
Adjusting behavior, practices, or political relationships to survive under colonial conditions without fully accepting colonial authority or surrendering core aspects of identity.
Assimilation
The process by which one cultural group adopts the practices, values, and identity of another, often under conditions of coercion. Missionaries and later colonial officials pursued Indigenous assimilation as a policy.

Northern Tiwa-speaking community in northern New Mexico; the location where Popé organized the Pueblo Revolt while in exile from Ohkay Owingeh.
Catawba Nation
New political community formed in the early eighteenth century from the survivors of multiple epidemic-devastated nations of the Carolina Piedmont. A striking example of creative Indigenous community rebuilding.
Santa Fe
Capital of Spanish New Mexico, established in 1610; from which Spanish Governor Otermín and approximately twenty-five hundred colonists fled during the Pueblo Revolt.
Plymouth Colony
English colony established in 1620 in Wampanoag territory; the primary English party in the original diplomatic relationship with Massasoit.
Creek Confederacy
Diverse alliance of communities in the Southeast speaking multiple languages; used the play-off system between English, Spanish, and French colonial powers to preserve political independence for over a century.

MGU Library Research Project

Building an Indigenous Resistance and Adaptation Research Profile

Choose one Indigenous nation, leader, or community from the colonial period (approximately 1600–1750) that appears in this chapter or in the broader history of the era. Create a research profile that examines how that nation, leader, or community responded to European colonization, using multiple types of sources.

  1. Select a subject. Options include: the Wampanoag Nation and Metacom; the Pueblo peoples and Popé; the Haudenosaunee Confederacy and the Covenant Chain; the Narragansett Nation; the Pequot Nation before and after 1637; the Catawba Nation and its formation; the Creek Confederacy's play-off system; the Christian Indians of the praying towns; or another nation you research independently.
  2. Find sources. Use your library to locate at least three sources. At minimum, try to find: one secondary source (a book, article, or scholarly chapter by a historian); one primary source (a treaty, court record, missionary account, colonial diary, or government report); and one source produced by or in collaboration with an Indigenous community (a tribal history, a tribal government website, an oral history, or a work by an Indigenous scholar).
  3. Analyze your sources. For each source, consider: Who produced it? When and where? For what purpose? What perspective does it present? What might it leave out?
  4. Construct your profile. Your research profile should address: the historical background of your subject; the specific colonial pressures the nation or community faced; the strategies the community used to respond; key leaders, events, or decisions; the outcomes — what changed, what was preserved, what was lost; and how the colonial-period experience connects to later history.
  5. Reflect on evidence. In a final paragraph, reflect on the limits of the sources you used. Whose perspectives are represented? Whose are missing? How would your profile look different if different sources were available?

Library resources to use: Tribal history collections, colonial archive databases, the MGU History Library's reference materials on Indigenous history, sovereignty, and resistance, and the Primary Sources in U.S. History collection.

Chapter Reflection

Chapter 7 has asked students to understand Indigenous responses to European colonization in all their complexity — not as a story of inevitable defeat, but as a history of active choices, creative adaptation, devastating losses, and remarkable persistence. This history matters for reasons that extend far beyond the seventeenth century.

The first reason it matters is historical accuracy. For too long, the history of European colonization was told almost entirely from the perspective of European colonizers, and Indigenous peoples appeared primarily as obstacles to settlement, casualties of disease, or defeated enemies in wars. This framing was not only unfair to Native peoples; it was historically inaccurate. Indigenous peoples shaped colonial history profoundly — through their diplomacy, their military power, their labor, their trade, their resistance, and their survival. A history of colonial North America that leaves out Indigenous agency and perspective is not simply incomplete; it is wrong.

The second reason it matters is that the history of colonization is not over. The political, legal, and social questions raised by the colonial period — about sovereignty, about land rights, about the recognition of Indigenous political authority, about the relationship between Native nations and the United States government — remain live and contested. Contemporary disputes about tribal sovereignty, treaty rights, sacred site protection, and Indigenous self-determination are direct descendants of the colonial-era conflicts this chapter has examined. Understanding where those disputes came from — understanding the historical creation of the legal and political conditions that Native peoples navigate today — requires understanding the colonial period.

The third reason it matters is what it teaches about historical complexity more broadly. The history of Indigenous responses to colonization resists simple narratives. It was not a story of heroes and villains, of simple victims and simple oppressors, of inevitable outcomes and predetermined results. It was a story of intelligent people making difficult choices under conditions of terrible constraint, with consequences that were never fully predictable. That complexity — and the habit of thinking carefully about it — is exactly what historical study is meant to develop.

The experiences of Metacom and Massasoit, of Popé and the Pueblo peoples, of the Haudenosaunee diplomats and the Catawba survivors — these are not peripheral stories in the history of the United States. They are central to understanding what the United States is, how it came to be, and what obligations and conflicts its history has created. They are part of the larger story this textbook tells: a story of a nation shaped by many peoples, many conflicts, many failures, and many forms of resilience that have never been fully resolved.

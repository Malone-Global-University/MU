Articles of Confederation and the Problem of Union

United States History: Indigenous America, Republic, Conflict, and Change

Chapter Opening

"In the summer of 1783, a group of angry soldiers marched on Philadelphia — veterans who had fought for American independence and now only wanted to be paid. Congress could not deliver. It had no power to tax."

In the summer of 1783, a group of angry soldiers marched on Philadelphia. They were veterans of the Continental Army, men who had fought through years of grueling war for American independence, endured hunger at Valley Forge, and survived the bloodiest battles of the Revolution. Now they wanted to be paid. The Congress of the United States, assembled in the Pennsylvania State House under the Articles of Confederation, owed these soldiers back pay and bounties promised them in exchange for their service. Congress could not deliver. It had no power to tax. It could only ask the states for money, and the states repeatedly refused or delayed. When the soldiers surrounded the building, demanding justice, Congress found itself helpless. The delegates fled Philadelphia and reassembled in Princeton, New Jersey — humiliated, powerless, and unable to protect even themselves.

This scene captured the central problem of government under the Articles of Confederation. Americans had just won independence from what they considered a tyrannical centralized empire, and they were determined not to create one of their own. The Articles, drafted during the Revolution and ratified in 1781, deliberately created a weak national government. Power was lodged in the individual states. The national Congress could declare war, sign treaties, and send diplomats abroad, but it could not tax citizens, regulate commerce, or enforce its own laws. It depended entirely on the voluntary cooperation of thirteen sovereign states that frequently disagreed, competed, and ignored national authority.

The years from 1781 to 1789, sometimes called the "Critical Period" by historians, were years of political experiment and recurring crisis. They were not simply years of failure, as older textbook narratives suggested. The Confederation Congress managed significant achievements, including the negotiation of the Treaty of Paris in 1783 and the passage of the Northwest Ordinance in 1787, which established a framework for governing the vast western territory that Britain had ceded. But the structural weaknesses of the Confederation grew harder to ignore as problems mounted: war debts unpaid, trade disrupted, currency unstable, Native nations resisting expansion, and states quarreling over commerce and borders.

The people who experienced the Confederation era were not simply waiting for a better constitution. Ordinary farmers, artisans, debtors, creditors, merchants, veterans, enslaved people, free Black communities, and Native nations all navigated the uncertain political landscape of the new republic in different ways. For many poor farmers, the Confederation's weakness was not the most urgent problem: crushing debt, high taxes levied by state governments, and the threat of losing land to creditors pressed on daily life far more than abstract questions of federal power. For merchants and creditors who needed stable currency, predictable commerce, and enforceable contracts, the Confederation's inability to regulate trade or stabilize money was a genuine crisis. For Indigenous nations in the Ohio Valley and beyond, the Confederation was threatening regardless of its weakness, because American settlers were pouring across the Appalachians and the national government could neither restrain them nor make enforceable peace.

The debate over the Confederation and calls for reform was, at its heart, a debate about what kind of republic the United States would become. Who would hold power — the states or a national government? Who would bear the costs of the Revolution — ordinary taxpayers or the wealthy bondholders? How would the West be settled, governed, and incorporated? How would Native sovereignty be handled? These were not only technical questions of constitutional design. They were contests over economic interest, political power, class, race, and the meaning of the Revolution itself.

This chapter examines the Articles of Confederation — why they were created, how they worked, what they accomplished, what they failed to do, and how the crises they could not resolve pushed political leaders toward the Constitutional Convention of 1787. Understanding the Confederation era is essential for understanding why the Constitution was written the way it was, what problems its framers were trying to solve, and what older forms of republican government they were trying to improve upon. The Confederation is not simply a failed experiment that Americans wisely abandoned. It was a genuine attempt to build a republic without tyranny — and the debates over its limits shaped American constitutional government for generations.

Historical Context

To understand the Articles of Confederation, students must understand the world from which they emerged. The American Revolution was not only a military conflict. It was a political and ideological movement built on deep suspicion of centralized power, arbitrary authority, and what colonial Americans called "tyranny." For nearly a decade before independence, colonial leaders had argued that the British Parliament had no right to tax them without their consent, that standing armies quartered in their homes were threats to liberty, and that a distant government exercising power over local affairs was a violation of their rights as Englishmen.

These ideas did not disappear when independence was declared. They shaped how Americans thought about government at every level. When political leaders drafted constitutions for their new state governments after 1776, they almost universally weakened executive power, strengthened legislatures, and built in restrictions on government authority. Governors in most states were stripped of powers they had held under royal charters. Legislatures gained enormous authority. Bills of rights declaring individual freedoms were attached to many state constitutions. These were not accidental choices. They reflected a generation's worth of argument about what made government legitimate and what made it dangerous.

The political philosophy of the era reinforced these lessons. The writings of English thinkers like John Locke and the classical republican tradition both warned that concentrated power corrupted those who held it. Montesquieu's influential work on the separation of powers argued that liberty could only survive where governmental authority was divided and each branch could check the others. American readers of these works, already primed by their colonial experience, drew the conclusion that the greatest danger to a republic was an overpowerful central government.

The international context also mattered enormously. The United States in the 1780s was a new and fragile nation surrounded by powerful European empires. Britain still occupied forts in the Northwest Territory in violation of the Treaty of Paris. Spain controlled the mouth of the Mississippi River, which was the essential outlet for goods produced by farmers and traders in the interior West. France was a wartime ally but a great power with its own interests. The young republic needed to negotiate, trade, and compete diplomatically with empires that did not take it seriously. A national government unable to enforce treaties or speak with a single voice in foreign affairs was a real liability.

The economic context was equally pressing. The Revolutionary War had been expensive, and the new United States was deeply in debt — to foreign creditors, to American merchants who had lent money to the government, and to the soldiers who had fought for it. The Continental currency issued during the war had inflated catastrophically, becoming nearly worthless by the war's end. "Not worth a Continental" became a common American expression for anything of no value. States issued their own currencies, which varied wildly in stability. Trade between states was complicated by competing tariffs and commercial policies. The economic disorder of the early 1780s fell hardest on those with the least margin — small farmers, artisans, debtors, and veterans who had come home from war to find their farms mortgaged and their savings eroded.

The social context included deep divisions that the Revolution had both exposed and created. The ideals of liberty and equality proclaimed in the Declaration of Independence did not disappear with independence. Enslaved people, free Black communities, women, and landless workers all heard those words and sometimes acted on them. Gradual emancipation began in several northern states during and after the Revolution. Free Black communities in Philadelphia, Boston, New York, and other cities organized churches, mutual aid societies, and political petitions. Women challenged their exclusion from civic life. Poor white men pushed for expanded voting rights. At the same time, southern states defended slavery with increasing fervor. Wealthy landowners and merchants across the new nation worried that democracy was going too far — that ordinary people were demanding too much. The Confederation era was shaped by this tension between the Revolution's expansive promises and the determination of many elites to contain them.

Section 14.1

Why the Articles of Confederation Were Created

The Articles of Confederation were not written in a moment of calm deliberation. They were drafted in 1776 and 1777, during the heat of a revolutionary war, by men who had just broken with what they considered a tyrannical imperial government. Every decision they made about the national government's powers was shaped by what they had experienced under British rule and by what they most feared about concentrated authority.

The colonial experience with British governance had taught American leaders several sharp lessons. The Stamp Act, the Townshend Acts, and the Coercive Acts of the 1760s and 1770s had demonstrated, in colonial eyes, how a powerful legislature far removed from those it governed could impose ruinous burdens without accountability. The presence of British troops in colonial towns, empowered to seize quarters in private homes, represented the use of standing armies against citizens. The powers of royal governors to dissolve assemblies, override local laws, and appoint judges whose salaries depended on royal favor had shown how executive power could be weaponized against representative government. When colonial pamphleteers and legislators wrote about "tyranny," they were describing specific experiences, not abstract fears.

The political philosophy of the era reinforced these lessons. The writings of English thinkers like John Locke and the classical republican tradition both warned that concentrated power corrupted those who held it. Montesquieu's influential work on the separation of powers argued that liberty could only survive where governmental authority was divided and each branch could check the others. American readers of these works, already primed by their colonial experience, drew the conclusion that the greatest danger to a republic was an overpowerful central government.

When the Continental Congress appointed a committee in June 1776 to draft a plan for national union, these ideas dominated the discussion. John Dickinson of Pennsylvania drafted an early version that would have given the national government more significant powers, including control over western lands. But other delegates resisted — particularly from the southern states, which were jealous of their sovereignty, protective of their power over slavery and labor, and unwilling to cede control over their vast western land claims to a national government. Smaller states worried about being dominated by larger ones. The debate dragged on for over a year.

The result — the Articles of Confederation — reflected a deliberate choice, not a failure of imagination. The men who drafted and debated the Articles knew they were creating a weak central government. That was largely the point. The Articles created what its framers called "a firm league of friendship" among "sovereign, free and independent" states. Each state retained its sovereignty, freedom, and independence. The national Congress was given specific, enumerated powers — the power to declare war, make peace, send and receive ambassadors, manage relations with Native nations, coin money (but not control state-issued money), and run the postal service. What Congress was explicitly denied was equally significant: it could not tax citizens, could not regulate commerce between the states, and had no independent executive authority to enforce its own resolutions.

These were not oversights. They were choices, and they reflected the political culture of the moment. The framers of the Articles believed, genuinely and with historical experience behind them, that concentrating these powers in a central government would recreate the very tyranny they had just fought to escape. They placed their faith instead in the states — in the idea that governments closer to the people, more directly accountable to local communities, would better protect liberty. Whether that faith was justified would become one of the central questions of the 1780s.

The Articles also revealed the enormous compromises required to hold the thirteen states together. The question of western lands created the most serious early obstacle to ratification. States like Virginia and Massachusetts claimed vast western territories based on their colonial charters. States without such claims — Maryland, Delaware, New Jersey, Rhode Island — feared that the land-rich states would become dominant in any national union. Maryland refused to ratify the Articles until the large states agreed to cede their western land claims to the national government. Virginia finally agreed to do so in 1781, clearing the way for Maryland's ratification and the formal beginning of the Confederation government — in the final year of the Revolutionary War.

The process of drafting and ratifying the Articles took five years, from 1776 to 1781. During that entire period, the Continental Congress governed without any formal legal authority — relying on emergency powers, state cooperation, and the urgency of war. That it managed to function at all was remarkable. That the Articles it produced would prove inadequate to peacetime governance was a lesson still to be learned.

Section 14.2

Structure of the Confederation Government

The government created by the Articles of Confederation was unlike any that had existed before in modern history — not a monarchy, not a confederation of the familiar European type, and not yet what would become the American federal system. It was a genuinely experimental form of government, designed to hold together thirteen sovereign states without subordinating them to any external authority.

At the center of the Confederation government was the Congress — a single chamber, unicameral body in which each state, regardless of size, cast one vote. This was a crucial decision. In a democracy based on population, larger states like Virginia and Massachusetts would dominate. Under the Articles, tiny Delaware had exactly the same vote as populous Virginia. This reflected the founding principle of the Confederation: it was a union of states, not a government of the people directly. The states sent between two and seven delegates to Congress, but the delegation voted as a bloc, casting the state's single vote. If a delegation was split, the state's vote was lost.

Congress met in various cities — Philadelphia, Baltimore, Lancaster, York, Princeton, Annapolis, Trenton, New York — reflecting the unsettled nature of the new nation and the practical difficulties of governing without a permanent capital. Delegates rotated in and out; attendance was frequently poor. The Articles required nine states to be present and voting for major legislation — to declare war, make treaties, borrow money, or regulate the army. This supermajority requirement, designed to prevent any majority from running roughshod over the minority, often meant that necessary actions simply could not be taken because enough delegates could not be assembled.

The Confederation had no president and no executive branch in any meaningful sense. Congress appointed committees to manage executive functions — a Committee of Foreign Affairs, a Board of War, a Board of Treasury — but these bodies had no independent authority and depended on Congress for direction and resources. The absence of a single executive authority meant that decisions were slow, often paralyzed by committee disagreement, and impossible to implement effectively without state cooperation.

The Confederation had no national court system. There was no federal judiciary to interpret laws, settle disputes between states, or enforce treaties. If two states quarreled over a border — and they did — there was no neutral arbiter. If a debtor refused to pay a creditor across a state line — and they did — there was no federal court to adjudicate. The Articles provided for a mechanism to resolve interstate disputes, but it was cumbersome and rarely used. The absence of a judiciary meant that national law, to the extent it existed, could not be systematically enforced.

The Confederation's financial structure was its most immediately paralyzing weakness. Congress had no power to tax. It could only "requisition" — that is, formally request — money from the states, allocating the amount each state owed based on the value of its land. States were supposed to collect taxes from their citizens and forward the proceeds to the national treasury. In practice, this almost never happened adequately. States had their own financial needs, their own political pressures, and no compulsion to prioritize national requisitions. In some years, the total received by the national treasury from all thirteen states was a small fraction of what had been requested. Congress had no remedy. It could not compel payment. It could embarrass states by publishing their delinquency — and it did — but embarrassment was not revenue.

Congress also had the power to coin money and set the value of coins, but it lacked the resources to mint significant quantities, and it had no power to regulate the paper currencies issued by the individual states. Thirteen states issued their own paper money, often in large quantities, leading to inflation, currency instability, and disputes about which currency would be accepted where. Merchants complained that they could not know, from state to state, what money was worth. Creditors refused to accept paper money issued by distant states. The currency chaos was a constant impediment to commerce and a source of deep economic anxiety.

One power the Confederation exercised more effectively was the management of foreign policy. Congress negotiated the Treaty of Paris in 1783, which formally ended the Revolutionary War and secured British recognition of American independence. The treaty was a genuine diplomatic achievement, negotiated by Benjamin Franklin, John Adams, and John Jay with considerable skill. It secured for the United States all British territory east of the Mississippi, south of the Great Lakes, and north of Spanish Florida — an enormous cession that more than doubled the territory Americans called their own. But the Confederation lacked the power to enforce the treaty's terms on either side. Britain refused to evacuate its frontier forts, citing American failure to pay prewar debts owed to British creditors — a failure the Confederation could not remedy because it could not compel the states to honor the treaty's provisions.

Section 14.3

Weaknesses of the Articles

Students of the Articles of Confederation often learn its weaknesses as a list: no power to tax, no power to regulate commerce, no executive, no judiciary, amendment requires unanimous consent. These are real and important structural problems, but they must be understood as symptoms of a deeper political reality rather than simply design errors.

The inability to tax was not an accident; it was the expression of a revolution fought partly against taxation without representation. Americans had just spent years arguing that their colonial legislatures, not a distant Parliament, had the sole right to tax them. Granting the national Congress taxing power over citizens — citizens who had never voted for those congressional delegates — seemed to reproduce the very problem that had caused the Revolution. The framers of the Articles were not naive. They knew that government needed revenue. They believed that government closest to the people should collect it, and that the national government should live on what the states chose to give it.

The problem was that this theory did not match political reality. States had immediate local pressures — war debts, constituents demanding tax relief, frontier defense costs — that made it easy to ignore national requisitions and politically difficult to honor them. No state delegation went home to its legislature and won applause by raising taxes to send to Philadelphia. The result was chronic underfunding of national functions.

The inability to regulate interstate commerce was similarly grounded in the states' determination to protect their own economic interests. States imposed tariffs on goods from other states, competed for trade, and issued economic regulations that conflicted with neighbors. New York taxed New Jersey goods crossing its harbor. Virginia and Maryland quarreled over navigation of the Potomac River. Connecticut merchants chafed under New York's port regulations. The commercial chaos was real and growing. American merchants who needed to trade across state lines — and most did — found the patchwork of state regulations crippling.

The amendment process made reform nearly impossible. Amending the Articles required the unanimous consent of all thirteen states. Every proposed reform was hostage to the most resistant state. When Congress sought the power to collect a five-percent impost — a tariff on imported goods that would have provided stable national revenue — the proposal was blocked twice by single states: first Rhode Island in 1781, then New York in 1786. Rhode Island's legislature, reflecting the interests of its merchant community, simply said no, and under the Articles that was enough. A single state could hold the entire national government's financial reform hostage indefinitely.

The lack of a national executive created permanent ambiguity about who, if anyone, was in charge of implementing national policy. During the war, this was managed through urgency and Washington's personal authority as commander. In peacetime, the absence was crippling. Congressional committees tried to manage the post office, relations with Native nations, the army, and foreign diplomacy simultaneously, often failing at all of them through the inherent limitations of government by committee.

The lack of a federal judiciary meant that the national government could not adjudicate disputes between states or enforce its own treaty obligations. Interstate conflicts over borders, debts, commercial rights, and navigation had no systematic resolution mechanism. Treaty violations — by both the United States and Britain — could not be adjudicated. The legal infrastructure of a functioning national government simply did not exist.

These weaknesses, taken together, meant that the Confederation was not so much a government as a compact — an agreement among sovereign states to cooperate on certain matters when they chose to. The choice was always theirs. In good times, with strong shared interests, such a compact might work. In the crises of the 1780s, it broke down.

Section 14.4

War Debt, Taxes, and Economic Problems

The economic crisis of the Confederation period was not simply a problem of government structure. It was a crisis of people's lives — of soldiers who had not been paid, farmers who could not pay their debts, merchants who could not do business across state lines, and veterans who had come home from war to find their promised bounties worth little and their land threatened.

The Revolutionary War had cost an enormous amount of money, and most of it had been borrowed. Congress had issued paper money — Continentals — in such quantities that the currency inflated to near worthlessness. States had issued their own paper money. Congress had borrowed from France, the Netherlands, and Spain. It had issued loan certificates to domestic creditors — merchants, wealthy patriots, and later speculators who bought the certificates at steep discounts from desperate veterans who needed cash. By the end of the war, the national government owed tens of millions of dollars in principal and interest, with no reliable revenue stream to service the debt.

The states also carried heavy debt burdens from the war. Most states responded by levying taxes — often significant ones — to pay off their war debts and meet current expenses. The irony was sharp: ordinary Americans had just fought a war against taxation without representation, and now their own state governments were levying taxes that fell heavily on farmers and artisans who had little cash. Property taxes, poll taxes, and excise taxes were collected in specie — gold or silver coin — which was scarce. Farmers who had produced goods for market found that market prices had collapsed after the wartime demand evaporated. They had goods but not coin, and the tax collectors wanted coin.

This was the economic trap that caught tens of thousands of ordinary Americans in the 1780s: they had fought the Revolution, often at great personal cost; they had come home to farms already mortgaged; they faced taxes they could not pay in currency they could not obtain; and when they could not pay, state courts moved against them — seizing property, imposing liens, threatening imprisonment for debt. The elegant mechanisms of property law and commercial credit worked efficiently against those who had borrowed during the war and could not repay after it.

The wealthy experienced the postwar economic world differently. Those who had money to invest in government securities — the domestic debt issued during the war — stood to gain enormously if the national government could eventually pay full value. Merchants and creditors who could operate across state lines faced commercial chaos, but they had the capital to weather disruption better than farmers. The economic crisis of the 1780s was not experienced equally. It fell hardest on those with the least margin.

Congress attempted to improve the national financial situation throughout the 1780s, but its structural weaknesses defeated most efforts. The impost proposal — a five-percent tax on imported goods — would have given Congress a reliable revenue source. It failed twice. Without revenue, Congress could not pay the interest on the national debt, let alone the principal. Foreign creditors grew nervous. American credit abroad deteriorated. The prospect of the United States defaulting on its obligations — to France, to Dutch banking houses, to domestic bondholders — was not abstract. It was a real and gathering danger.

The economic problems of the Confederation period also had a racial dimension. Enslaved people worked under the economic systems of the southern states with no recourse to any government — national or state. Free Black communities in the North, though they had gained some legal standing, faced economic discrimination, restricted access to credit, and legal systems that rarely protected their interests. The economic crisis fell on them too, compounded by racial exclusion. When the free labor market contracted and competition for work intensified, free Black workers in northern cities often found themselves pushed out of skilled trades and into the most precarious economic positions.

Section 14.5

Western Lands and the Northwest Ordinance

The West — the vast territory stretching from the Appalachian Mountains to the Mississippi River and from the Ohio River to the Great Lakes — was simultaneously the greatest asset and the greatest problem of the Confederation government. It was an asset because the land was immensely valuable and could, in principle, be sold to generate national revenue and provide farms for American settlers. It was a problem because it was already inhabited — by Indigenous nations who had not agreed to sell it, by French settlers in old river towns, by British traders who continued operating from frontier posts, and by American squatters who crossed the Ohio before any survey or sale had been organized.

The first major step was the Land Ordinance of 1785. The ordinance established the township-and-range system of land survey that became the template for nearly all subsequent American land distribution west of the original states. The country was to be divided into townships six miles square, each township further divided into thirty-six sections of one square mile (640 acres) each. Sections would be sold at public auction for a minimum of one dollar per acre, in minimum purchases of 640 acres — a requirement that favored land speculators and wealthy buyers rather than small farmers, since few frontier settlers had $640 in cash. Section 16 of each township was reserved for the support of public schools, an early statement of the principle that public education was a national interest.

The Land Ordinance of 1785 was a genuine achievement in land management. The township-and-range grid is still visible from the air across the Midwest, Great Plains, and West — the straight-line boundaries of counties, farms, and roads that trace back to this moment. It created an orderly, legible system for organizing land ownership that reduced the chaos of overlapping land grants and competing claims. But the ordinance said almost nothing about the people already living on the land it proposed to survey and sell. Indigenous nations had not been consulted. Their presence was treated as a temporary obstacle to be managed, not as a legitimate competing claim to the same territory. The surveys proceeded as if the land were empty. It was not.

The Northwest Ordinance of 1787 was even more significant than the Land Ordinance that preceded it. Passed in July 1787 while the Constitutional Convention was meeting in Philadelphia, it established the framework for governing the Northwest Territory — the region north of the Ohio River and east of the Mississippi. It is often considered one of the most important legislative achievements of the Confederation period.

The Northwest Ordinance addressed several urgent questions simultaneously. It created a three-stage path from territory to statehood. In the first stage, Congress would appoint a governor, a secretary, and three judges to govern the territory. In the second stage, when the free adult male population reached five thousand, a territorial legislature would be elected and a non-voting delegate sent to Congress. In the third stage, when the free white male population reached sixty thousand, the territory could apply for statehood on equal terms with the original states. The ordinance anticipated that between three and five states would eventually be carved out of the Northwest Territory — states that would eventually become Ohio, Indiana, Illinois, Michigan, and Wisconsin.

The ordinance also contained a bill of rights for territorial residents, guaranteeing freedom of religion, the right to trial by jury, the writ of habeas corpus, and proportional representation. These were significant civil liberty protections for an era when such rights were not universally guaranteed even in the existing states.

Most famously, the Northwest Ordinance contained an antislavery provision: there would be neither slavery nor involuntary servitude in the territory, except as punishment for crimes. This was the first time a national American government had prohibited slavery in any jurisdiction. The prohibition did not mean the Northwest Territory was a land of racial equality — Black residents faced discrimination in law and practice throughout the territorial period — but it did bar the formal institution of slavery from a large and growing part of the country. The long-term significance was enormous: the free states carved from the Northwest Territory would eventually become part of the political coalition opposing slavery's expansion in the 1840s and 1850s.

The ordinance's antislavery clause reflected a political bargain as much as an antislavery principle. Southern delegates to Congress supported the ordinance in part because they believed keeping slavery out of the Northwest would ensure that slavery expanded through the Southwest — through Kentucky, Tennessee, and eventually Alabama and Mississippi — without northern competition. The ordinance protected slavery's domain to the south while restricting it to the north. It was a compromise that preserved slavery nationally while limiting it geographically.

What the Northwest Ordinance did not do was obtain the genuine consent of the Indigenous nations who lived in the territory. The ordinance contained language promising that the utmost good faith would always be observed toward the Indians and that their lands and property would never be taken from them without their consent. These were words. American policy in the Northwest Territory in the years following the ordinance would demonstrate how empty those words were.

Section 14.6

Native Nations and Western Expansion

The Indigenous nations of the Ohio Valley and the Great Lakes region were not passive observers of the events of the Confederation period. They were active political communities with their own histories, alliances, diplomatic traditions, and strategies for survival in a rapidly changing world. Understanding the Confederation era requires understanding how Native nations experienced and responded to American expansion after the Revolution.

The Treaty of Paris of 1783 shocked many Indigenous communities who learned about it. Britain, in negotiating peace with the United States, had ceded to the Americans all territory east of the Mississippi — including the entire Northwest Territory — without meaningfully consulting the Native nations who lived there. Britain had been an Indigenous ally during the Revolution: many Native nations had fought alongside British forces, understanding the conflict as a contest between two powers over their land, and calculating that a British victory was more likely to preserve Indigenous sovereignty than an American one. The American victory and the subsequent treaty were a betrayal of those alliances.

The Shawnee, Delaware (Lenape), Miami, Ottawa, Chippewa, Potawatomi, Wyandot, and other nations of the Ohio Valley had not surrendered to the United States. They had not signed the Treaty of Paris. In their understanding, the British had given away land that was not theirs to give. The Ohio Valley was Indigenous territory, and they intended to keep it.

The Confederation Congress attempted to negotiate with Native nations in the Northwest through a series of treaties. In 1784, the Treaty of Fort Stanwix with the Six Nations of the Iroquois Confederacy pressed Iroquois representatives into ceding much of their territory in a deeply unequal negotiation. In 1785, the Treaty of Fort McIntosh pressed the Wyandot, Delaware, Chippewa, and Ottawa into ceding most of Ohio. In 1786, the Treaty of Fort Finney did the same with the Shawnee. These treaties were not agreements negotiated between equals. American negotiators often used coercion, threats, and alcohol; they negotiated with Native representatives who lacked authority from their communities; and they asserted conquest rights — the idea that because Britain had been defeated in war and the Indians had allied with Britain, the United States had the right to Indigenous lands by right of conquest.

Many Native communities simply rejected these treaties. The warriors and leaders most committed to resistance refused to be bound by agreements made under duress. The result was continuing conflict on the Ohio frontier. American settlers pushed into the territory north of the Ohio River, establishing cabins and clearings in violation of national policy and in the face of Indigenous resistance. Raids, counter-raids, and killings were constant. The Confederation government, with its meager military force and empty treasury, could do little to either protect settlers or restrain them.

Indigenous nations responded to this pressure by attempting to build broader alliances. Shawnee and Delaware leaders traveled extensively in the late 1780s, arguing for pan-Indian resistance — the idea that all Native nations needed to unite against American expansion or be destroyed one by one. The towns of the upper Maumee River became centers of an emerging resistance coalition that would challenge the United States more seriously in the early 1790s. The British, who remained in their frontier forts in violation of the Treaty of Paris, provided material support to Indigenous resistance, complicating American efforts to establish control of the Northwest.

For ordinary Indigenous families in the Ohio Valley, the Confederation period was one of profound anxiety and disruption. Traditional agricultural patterns, hunting territories, and seasonal movements were threatened by the constant pressure of American settlers and by the uncertainty of their political situation. Communities debated endlessly about how to respond: fight, negotiate, relocate, accommodate. There were no good choices — only less bad ones, and even those required constant vigilance and sacrifice.

The Northwest Ordinance's promise that Indigenous lands would never be taken without consent was, from the perspective of the Native nations of the region, already a hollow promise at the moment it was written. They had not consented to the surveys, the land sales, or the territorial government. The Confederation's land policy, whatever its merits in organizing American settlement, was built on the displacement of people who had not agreed to be displaced.

Historical Vocabulary
Confederation

A confederation is a political union in which the member states or units retain their sovereignty — their supreme governing authority — and delegate only limited, specific powers to a central governing body. Under a confederation, the central government exists to serve the member states and acts through them rather than governing citizens directly.

The Articles of Confederation embodied this principle explicitly. Each state was declared "sovereign, free and independent." The national Congress could pass resolutions, but it depended on the states to implement them. This stood in sharp contrast to the federal system created by the Constitution of 1787, in which the national government governs citizens directly and the Constitution itself (not the states) is the supreme law of the land.

The word "confederation" is still used in various contexts — Canada officially calls itself a confederation, though its actual constitutional structure is federal. Students should understand that the choice between confederation and federation is not simply technical. It reflects deep questions about sovereignty, democracy, and who should hold ultimate political authority.

Section 14.7

Shays' Rebellion and Political Fear

In the summer and fall of 1786, farmers in western Massachusetts began to organize against what they saw as crushing injustice. They were veterans of the Revolution, men who had fought for liberty, and they now faced property seizures, court judgments against them for unpaid debts, and the prospect of imprisonment — all because they could not pay taxes and debts in the specie (coin) that the state of Massachusetts demanded.

Massachusetts had adopted one of the most aggressive debt-repayment programs in the country. Its legislature, dominated by eastern merchants and creditors, imposed heavy property and poll taxes to pay off the state's war debt at full value — a policy that enriched bondholders and crushed farmers. Western farmers, who traded in agricultural goods rather than coin, found it nearly impossible to meet these demands. When they appeared before local courts to protest, the courts moved against them: attaching their land, seizing their livestock, and sometimes imprisoning them for debt. In the summer of 1786, county conventions of farmers began petitioning the legislature for relief — for paper money, for reduced taxes, for a temporary suspension of debt collection. The legislature refused.

When petitions failed, farmers turned to more direct action. Armed groups began appearing at county courthouses and preventing the courts from sitting — stopping the machinery of debt collection at its source. These actions spread across western and central Massachusetts through the fall of 1786. The movement's most prominent leader was Daniel Shays, a former Continental Army captain from Pelham who had never received his back pay. He was not the only leader, nor the most radical, but his name became attached to the uprising in contemporary accounts and has stayed there.

The Confederation government was powerless to respond. Congress had almost no money and almost no army. When Massachusetts appealed for national assistance, Congress managed to authorize the enlistment of some troops under the pretext of "Indian troubles" on the frontier — the real purpose was too politically explosive to state openly — but raised almost no money to pay them. The suppression of Shays' Rebellion fell to Massachusetts itself. Governor James Bowdoin organized a state army of 4,400 men, funded by emergency loans from Boston merchants. In January 1787, this force confronted the insurgents at the Springfield federal arsenal, which Shays and his allies had attempted to seize. Artillery fire killed four insurgents and scattered the rest. Shays fled to Vermont. The rebellion was over militarily within weeks.

But politically, Shays' Rebellion was just beginning to make its impact felt. For the wealthy merchant, lawyer, and planter class across the country — the people most likely to serve in Congress, correspond with national leaders, and shape national opinion — the uprising was a nightmare. It suggested that the democratic energy of the Revolution had no natural stopping point, that ordinary people emboldened by revolutionary rhetoric would challenge not only royal authority but the authority of American property and law. It suggested that state governments, pressured by popular majorities, might adopt inflationary paper money policies, reduce taxes on debtors, and refuse to protect creditor rights. And it demonstrated, viscerally, that the Confederation had no capacity to respond to internal disorder.

George Washington, in retirement at Mount Vernon, received reports of Shays' Rebellion from correspondents who were uniformly alarmed. James Madison, who had been studying the history of ancient and European confederacies and compiling a catalog of the failures of the Articles, saw Shays' Rebellion as confirmation of everything he had concluded. Alexander Hamilton, always the most urgent advocate for a powerful national government, was energized. Across the country, political correspondence in the months following Shays' Rebellion is full of the language of crisis — of a republic on the edge of dissolution, of democracy becoming mob rule, of the urgent need for constitutional reform.

Not everyone agreed. For many Americans, the real lesson of Shays' Rebellion was not that the national government needed more power, but that state governments needed to be more responsive to ordinary people. The Massachusetts farmers had genuine grievances, and the state legislature had been unreasonably rigid. Thomas Jefferson, writing from Paris where he was serving as American minister to France, observed that a little rebellion now and then was a good thing — that some popular unrest was the price of democracy and not the end of it. Jefferson's reaction was very much in the minority among the political class in 1787, but it represented a significant strand of American democratic thought.

What Shays' Rebellion definitively accomplished, politically, was to overcome the resistance to constitutional reform among those who had previously believed the Articles could be patched. After Shays' Rebellion, the call for a constitutional convention gained urgency. Most of the states agreed to send delegates to Philadelphia in May 1787. The Constitutional Convention was about to begin.

Section 14.8

Calls for Reform

The movement toward constitutional reform was not spontaneous. It grew from years of frustration, failed amendment attempts, economic crisis, and political humiliation — and it was pushed, organized, and intellectualized by a relatively small group of men who were convinced that the Confederation was doomed unless it was fundamentally restructured.

James Madison of Virginia was the intellectual center of the reform movement. Throughout the mid-1780s, Madison engaged in an extraordinary self-education in political theory and constitutional history. He read extensively about ancient Greek and Roman confederacies, about the Swiss and Dutch federations, about the theoretical writings of Enlightenment thinkers. He compiled a detailed catalogue of "the vices of the political system of the United States" — a systematic analysis of everything wrong with the Articles. His conclusion was that the Union needed a government that could act directly on individuals, not merely request compliance from states — a government with the power to tax, to regulate commerce, to enforce its laws through an executive and judiciary.

Alexander Hamilton had been urging similar reforms since the early 1780s, writing in letters and published essays about the dangers of a weak national government. Washington, through his private correspondence, conveyed his frustration with a government that could not even protect its own members and could not enforce the treaty he had fought to achieve. These men and others like them — nationalists who believed the survival of the republic depended on a stronger central government — worked to build the political consensus for a convention.

The Annapolis Convention of September 1786 was their opening move. Madison and Hamilton were both delegates. The Convention had been called to address commercial problems — the inability of the Confederation to regulate interstate and international trade — but only five states sent delegates, making it too small to accomplish anything formal. Instead, it issued a report, largely written by Hamilton, calling for a broader convention in Philadelphia in May 1787 to take into consideration the situation of the United States and devise provisions necessary to render the Federal Government adequate to the needs of the Union.

The state legislatures debated whether to send delegates to Philadelphia through the fall of 1786 and winter of 1787. Shays' Rebellion made the decision easier for many waverers. By the time the Convention opened in May 1787, twelve of the thirteen states had appointed delegates. Rhode Island refused to participate, reflecting its small-state, debtor-friendly politics and suspicion of the reform agenda.

Not everyone who supported the Convention agreed on what it should do. Some delegates hoped to patch the Articles — to add taxing power and commercial regulation while preserving the basic structure. Others, like Madison and Hamilton, came to Philadelphia prepared to replace the Articles entirely with a new framework of government. The tension between these factions would shape the Convention's debates. But the agreement to meet, to deliberate seriously, and to place the results before the states for ratification was itself a significant political achievement. Under the pressure of economic crisis and social conflict, the political system was generating the energy for its own reform.

Map Study

Western Territory and Indigenous Lands, 1783–1787

A map of the eastern United States in the mid-1780s would reveal several crucial geographic and political realities that shaped the Confederation era.

The Northwest Territory: The vast region north of the Ohio River, east of the Mississippi, and south of the Great Lakes — ceded by Britain in the Treaty of Paris — appears on such a map as American territory. But students should look carefully. The boundaries were legal constructions on paper. The land was occupied by Indigenous nations — Shawnee, Delaware, Miami, Ottawa, Chippewa, Wyandot, Potawatomi, and others — who had not agreed to be part of the United States.

British frontier forts: A careful map would mark British military posts — Detroit, Niagara, Oswego, and others — which Britain retained in violation of the Treaty of Paris. These forts sat within territory the treaty had assigned to the United States, giving Britain continued leverage over the fur trade and providing a supply point for Indigenous nations resisting American expansion.

Spanish Florida and the Mississippi: To the south and west, Spain controlled Florida and the mouth of the Mississippi River. For Americans in Kentucky, Tennessee, and the Ohio Valley, the Mississippi was the only practical outlet for goods. Spain could threaten to close it — and did in 1784 — creating enormous pressure on western settlers and revealing how dependent the interior was on access to markets controlled by foreign powers.

Settlement patterns: The map would show American settlement concentrated along the Atlantic coast and pushing into the interior of the Appalachians, with a scattering of settlements in Kentucky and Tennessee. The Ohio River marked a contested frontier rather than a settled border.

Section 14.9

The Confederation's Achievements and Limits

Older historical narratives of the Confederation era sometimes described it as an almost total failure — a "Critical Period" in which the new nation nearly collapsed before the Constitution rescued it. This view was developed most influentially by historian John Fiske in the 1880s and was long the standard textbook interpretation. More recent scholarship has complicated this picture significantly, and students should understand the Confederation both for what it failed to accomplish and for what it genuinely achieved.

The most significant achievement was the negotiation of the Treaty of Paris in 1783. The American commissioners — Benjamin Franklin, John Adams, and John Jay — secured an extraordinarily favorable peace. Britain recognized American independence unconditionally. It ceded all territory east of the Mississippi River. American fishermen retained access to the Grand Banks. These were remarkable gains for a country that had been an underfunded, undersupplied collection of colonial militias fighting the world's most powerful empire.

The Northwest Ordinance of 1787 was the Confederation's most consequential domestic achievement. It established the principle that territories could become states on equal footing with existing states — a crucial departure from the colonial model, in which territorial possessions remained subordinate to the mother country indefinitely. The ordinance's antislavery clause established the first federal prohibition on slavery's expansion. Its bill of rights provisions extended civil liberties to territorial residents. Its township school section established a national commitment to public education. These were genuine innovations that shaped American government for generations.

The Land Ordinance of 1785 created the surveying system that made organized settlement of the West possible. The township-and-range grid that still defines the landscape of much of the United States west of Ohio traces directly to this Confederation legislation. It was imperfect — it favored large buyers over small farmers, at least initially — but it was a genuine administrative achievement.

The Confederation also demonstrated, in a negative sense, what a national government needed in order to function. The years of the Articles showed conclusively that a government without the power to tax, regulate commerce, enforce its laws, or act directly on citizens could not sustain a republic under modern conditions. This demonstration was painful and expensive, but it was not without value. The framers of the Constitution knew exactly what problems they were trying to solve because the Confederation had made those problems unmistakably clear.

Where the Confederation failed most visibly was in the intersection of finance, commerce, and domestic order. It could not pay its debts. It could not stabilize the currency. It could not enforce treaty obligations. It could not suppress a domestic uprising without state action. It could not prevent states from creating commercial chaos through competing tariffs and regulations. These failures were real and consequential.

But students should be careful not to take the reformers' critique of the Confederation entirely at face value. The men who most urgently wanted a stronger national government were, disproportionately, men with property to protect, debts owed to them, and commercial interests that spanned state lines. Their concerns were legitimate, but they were not the only concerns in the republic. Many ordinary Americans — farmers, artisans, debtors — were worried about a different danger: that a stronger national government would be used to tax them into poverty, favor creditors over debtors, and concentrate power in the hands of an elite far removed from their daily lives. The Anti-Federalists who would oppose the Constitution were not simply obstructionists. They were people who had learned from colonial experience that powerful governments could become tyrannies.

The Confederation, ultimately, was a moment of democratic experimentation under severe pressure. It failed to solve the problems of a young republic facing economic crisis, military weakness, and social conflict. But it established important precedents — for peaceful territorial expansion, for public education, for limiting slavery's spread — that shaped the republic that replaced it.

The ordinance passed unanimously, suggesting broad political consensus on its major provisions.

First, it shows that Congress could act decisively and with lasting consequence when it had clear authority and political consensus. Second, the antislavery clause demonstrates that some national limits on slavery's expansion were politically possible even in the 1780s — though its passage was facilitated by a political calculation that keeping slavery out of the Northwest would benefit the South by channeling slavery's expansion southward. Third, the promise of fair treatment for Indigenous nations stands in sharp contrast to the actual policy pursued in the years following — a reminder that historians must distinguish between what governments declared and what they did.

Historical Debate

Was the Confederation a Failure?

The question: Historians and students have long debated whether the Articles of Confederation represented a fundamental failure of American governance or whether the standard "failure" narrative obscures the Confederation's real achievements and the legitimacy of its underlying democratic principles.

Interpretation A — The Confederation as Failure: The traditional interpretation, developed by historian John Fiske in the nineteenth century and widely repeated in twentieth-century textbooks, held that the Confederation was a dangerous failure that nearly allowed the new nation to collapse. In this view, the inability to tax, regulate commerce, enforce laws, or maintain a credible military left the United States unable to pay its debts, maintain diplomatic credibility, or prevent social disorder. Shays' Rebellion exposed the Confederation's powerlessness. The Constitution of 1787 rescued the republic from dangerous drift. Evidence for this view: Congressional correspondence throughout the 1780s is full of expressions of crisis. Washington's letters describe a government unable to function. The retreat from Philadelphia during the soldiers' mutiny of 1783 was a vivid symbol of national weakness. Foreign governments did not take the Confederation seriously.
Interpretation B — Revising the Failure Narrative: More recent scholarship, particularly the work of Merrill Jensen and later historians, has challenged the traditional narrative. In this view, the "Critical Period" interpretation was shaped by the very men who wanted to replace the Articles — the Federalists whose writings and correspondence form much of the historical record. These men had strong interests in emphasizing the Confederation's failures. The Confederation had genuine achievements: the Treaty of Paris, the Northwest Ordinance, the Land Ordinance. More importantly, the Articles reflected legitimate democratic principles — that government power should be decentralized, that states closest to the people should hold primary authority, and that ordinary people should be protected from distant, powerful national authority. The debtors and farmers who resisted state tax collection and supported Shays' Rebellion had real grievances. The move toward a stronger national government was, in part, a conservative reaction by elite creditors and merchants who wanted to use national power to protect their economic interests.

Evidence: Legislative records show the Confederation acting effectively on western lands and foreign policy. Historians analyzing the social composition of the reform movement note its disproportionate representation of wealthy merchants and creditors. Anti-Federalist writings articulate sophisticated arguments for decentralized government. The Northwest Ordinance was passed with unanimous support, suggesting the Confederation could function when it had consensus.

Why it matters: This debate concerns the fundamental question of what kind of government is most democratic. Was the Confederation's weakness a threat to republican government, or was the Constitution's centralization a threat to democratic government? This question has echoed throughout American history — in debates over states' rights, federal power, taxation, financial regulation, and the relationship between local communities and national authority.

Student task: Using the evidence presented in this chapter, evaluate which interpretation is better supported. What additional evidence — from financial records, congressional correspondence, or the writings of ordinary Americans rather than political elites — would help resolve this debate?

Multiple Perspectives

The Articles of Confederation: Different Experiences of the Same Crisis

Wealthy merchants and creditors: For merchants and creditors who conducted business across state lines, the Confederation's commercial chaos was a genuine and immediate problem. Competing state tariffs made interstate trade expensive and unpredictable. State paper money policies threatened the value of debts owed to them. The inability of the national government to enforce contracts across state lines made long-distance credit risky. These men were disproportionately represented in the reform movement and in the Constitutional Convention. They wanted a government strong enough to regulate commerce, stabilize currency, and enforce creditor rights.
Small farmers and debtors: For farmers in western Massachusetts, in the backcountry of Virginia, in rural New York and Pennsylvania, the Confederation period meant crushing debt, heavy state taxes, and the threat of losing land. Many had served in the Revolution, endured its hardships, and come home to find themselves deeper in debt than before. They were not enthusiastic about a stronger national government that might tax them more heavily or protect the creditors threatening their farms. They favored state paper money laws, debt relief legislation, and stay laws that prevented property seizures. Shays' Rebellion expressed their desperation; Anti-Federalism expressed their politics.
Enslaved people and free Black communities: For the approximately seven hundred thousand enslaved people in the United States in the 1780s, the Confederation period was a continuation of bondage, unaffected in any direct way by the debates over constitutional structure. The Revolution's ideals of liberty had created some opportunities — gradual emancipation in northern states, a few thousand manumissions in the South — but the institution of slavery was more deeply entrenched than before. Free Black communities in northern cities organized churches, mutual aid societies, and schools, building institutions for survival in a society that denied them full rights. Their voice in the constitutional debate was essentially none. They were subjects of American governance, not participants in designing it.
Indigenous nations: For the Shawnee, Miami, Delaware, and other nations of the Ohio Valley, the Confederation period was a period of severe pressure from American expansion regardless of whether the national government was weak or strong. Without a reliable central authority, there was no one to negotiate with who could actually restrain settlers. Indigenous leaders negotiated treaties with the Confederation Congress only to find that settlers ignored them. The Northwest Ordinance's promise of good faith toward the Indians was, from the Indigenous perspective, a document produced by people who were organizing the sale of Indigenous land without Indigenous consent.

Note: These perspectives should be understood in context of power. Wealthy merchants had enormous political influence; small farmers had some through state legislatures; enslaved people and Native nations had almost none in the formal constitutional debate. The history of who got to shape the Constitution reflects these power inequalities.

Constitutional Connection

The Articles and the Constitution: A Direct Line

The Articles of Confederation are the essential background for understanding the Constitution of 1787. The Constitution was not written in the abstract — it was written to solve specific problems that the Confederation had made visible, and every major provision of the Constitution can be connected to a specific failure of the Articles.

The Constitution's grant of taxing power directly to Congress (Article I, Section 8) was the answer to the Confederation's inability to requisition adequate funds. The power to regulate interstate and international commerce answered the commercial chaos of the 1780s. The creation of an independent executive (Article II) answered the Confederation's paralytic reliance on executive committees. The creation of a federal judiciary (Article III) answered the absence of any mechanism for enforcing national law or adjudicating interstate disputes. The Supremacy Clause (Article VI), declaring the Constitution and federal law supreme over state law, answered the states' habit of ignoring national authority. The amendment process in Article V, while still requiring supermajorities, was less demanding than the Articles' unanimity requirement.

The Tenth Amendment, added to the Constitution in the Bill of Rights in 1791, preserved a version of the states' rights principle from the Articles: powers not delegated to the United States by the Constitution are reserved to the States respectively, or to the people. This was a constitutional acknowledgment that the debate between national authority and state sovereignty — the central political contest of the Confederation era — was not fully resolved by the Constitution itself. That debate continued in arguments over the Bank of the United States in the 1790s, the nullification crisis of the 1830s, the secession crisis of 1861, civil rights conflicts of the twentieth century, and ongoing arguments about federal power today.

Cause and Effect

The Origins of the Constitutional Convention

Long-Term Causes

The Articles were designed to prevent centralized authority, reflecting colonial Americans' deep suspicion of powerful government. The Confederation's structural weaknesses — no taxing power, no commerce power, no executive, no judiciary — were built into the document. The Revolutionary War left the United States with large debts and no reliable way to pay. The commercial interests of merchants, creditors, and land speculators were not well served by the Confederation's fragmented economic environment.

Short-Term Causes

The failure of the impost amendment in 1781 and 1786 demonstrated that financial reform was impossible without structural constitutional change. Shays' Rebellion in 1786–1787 alarmed political leaders with evidence of social disorder and the Confederation's powerlessness. The Annapolis Convention of September 1786 formally proposed a constitutional convention. State legislatures, frightened by Shays' Rebellion, were more willing to send delegates to Philadelphia.

The Event

Twelve states sent delegates to the Constitutional Convention in Philadelphia in May 1787. Rather than revising the Articles, delegates drafted an entirely new constitution that gave Congress the power to tax and regulate commerce, created an independent executive and federal judiciary, made federal law supreme over state law, and established direct government over citizens rather than government through the states.

Effects

The Constitution was ratified by nine states by June 1788; the new federal government began in April 1789. Anti-Federalists secured a commitment to a Bill of Rights, ratified in 1791. The Constitution's protections of slavery embedded that institution more deeply in national law, creating a conflict that required a civil war to resolve. The expansion of federal power created new conflicts between federal and state authority that recurred throughout American history.

Historical complexity: The Constitutional Convention is sometimes presented as if the Confederation's failures made it inevitable and universally welcome. In reality, ratification was contested. Many Americans worried that the new Constitution gave the federal government too much power and protected the interests of creditors and property owners at the expense of ordinary people.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Federal Power and State Sovereignty, 1781 to the Present

What Changed

  • Under the Articles, states were sovereign; the national government existed at their sufferance
  • Under the Constitution, the national government was sovereign within its sphere; federal law was supreme
  • The government could now act directly on citizens, taxing them and enforcing law without state intermediaries
  • Merchants and creditors immediately experienced greater protection of their interests through the commerce and contract clauses

What Stayed the Same

  • The underlying tension between federal authority and state sovereignty did not disappear
  • The political culture of suspicion toward distant power — attachment to local self-governance — continued to shape American politics
  • The Constitution did not free enslaved people — it protected slavery more systematically than the Articles had
  • Women, Black Americans, and Indigenous peoples remained excluded from constitutional democracy
  • Indigenous dispossession continued and was actually organized more efficiently under the new government

Why the pattern matters: Constitutional structures can change while underlying social arrangements persist. The exclusion of women, Black Americans, and Native peoples from constitutional democracy was not a temporary oversight — it was a structural feature that required centuries of struggle to begin to address. The debate between national power and state sovereignty, the central political contest of the Confederation era, has never fully ended. It recurs in every generation as Americans argue about the proper scope of federal authority.

Everyday Life and Chapter Review Checkpoint

Everyday Life During the Confederation Period

The political and economic crises of the Confederation era were not abstract problems confined to congressional chambers and political correspondence. They affected the daily lives of ordinary people in immediate and often painful ways.

For a Massachusetts farmer in the mid-1780s, daily life was shaped by debt and anxiety about land. The farm that his family had worked might be mortgaged to a Boston merchant who had lent money during the war years. The state tax collector had come twice now, demanding silver coins that the farmer could not produce from goods that would not sell at wartime prices. His neighbor's property had been attached by the courts — the livestock driven off, the land judgment entered against him. The county courthouse was a place of fear, not justice. When news spread that other farmers were organizing to shut the courts, he understood why, and he went.

For a Philadelphia merchant in the same years, daily life was shaped by commercial unpredictability. The goods he imported from Britain faced state tariff schedules that changed without notice. His invoices from customers in Virginia were payable in Virginia currency that depreciated against Pennsylvania money. The national government had not paid the interest on his war bonds in two years. The Dutch bank he owed money to was asking pointed questions about American creditworthiness. He needed stable currency, enforceable contracts, and predictable commercial law — and the Confederation could provide none of them.

For an enslaved woman in Virginia, the Confederation era brought little change to the structure of her bondage. She had heard that some northern states were moving toward emancipation, and she knew that during the war, the British had promised freedom to those who escaped to their lines. Some did. She had not — her children were here, her community was here, and flight meant leaving everything she loved in the desperate gamble for freedom. The political debates among white men about the structure of the national government touched her life primarily through the labor demands it left unchanged and the legal structures it refused to challenge.

For a Shawnee family in the Ohio Valley, daily life in the mid-1780s was dominated by the constant pressure of American settlement. Hunters brought back reports of cabins and clearings appearing in territory that treaty lines were supposed to protect. The warriors debated endlessly: fight now, or wait, or try once more to negotiate. The elders remembered the last war, the losses, the promises made and broken. There was no path that did not involve sacrifice. Every choice was a choice between different kinds of danger.

These different daily experiences of the Confederation era were not separate stories. They were connected — by the economic system that bound creditor to debtor, merchant to farmer, enslaved labor to national prosperity; by the land system that made Indigenous displacement the condition of American settlement; by the political system that included some and excluded most. Understanding the Confederation means understanding all of these experiences together.

Chapter Summary

The Articles of Confederation represented the first attempt of the United States to govern itself under a formal constitution. Created during the Revolutionary War by men deeply suspicious of centralized power, the Articles deliberately created a weak national government that lodged sovereignty in the individual states. The Confederation Congress could request money from states, conduct diplomacy, and manage western lands, but it could not tax citizens, regulate commerce, or enforce its own laws. This structure reflected the political convictions of the revolutionary generation — but it proved inadequate to the challenges of governing a diverse and fragile republic in the years after independence.

The financial crisis of the Confederation period was its most immediately destructive challenge. With no ability to tax, the national government could not pay the debts incurred during the Revolutionary War. States issued paper money, imposed their own tariffs, and ignored national requisitions. These problems fell hardest on those with the least margin — small farmers, artisans, veterans — while creditors and merchants with enough capital to weather disruption worried about a different set of problems: instability, contract enforcement, and the unpredictability of a Confederation that could not speak with a single voice.

The western lands presented the Confederation with both its greatest opportunity and its most intractable challenge. The Land Ordinance of 1785 established the township-and-range survey system that organized American expansion for generations. The Northwest Ordinance of 1787 was the Confederation's most significant legislative achievement: it established a path from territorial status to statehood, guaranteed civil liberties to territorial residents, banned slavery north of the Ohio River, and promised — though failed to deliver — fair treatment of Indigenous nations. These were real and lasting achievements. At the same time, the land policy was built on the displacement of the Indigenous communities who lived in the Northwest Territory without their consent.

Native nations of the Ohio Valley — Shawnee, Delaware, Miami, Ottawa, and others — refused to accept American sovereignty over their homelands. They had not been parties to the Treaty of Paris and did not recognize British authority to cede Indigenous territory. Throughout the Confederation period, they organized resistance, sought pan-Indian alliances, and received support from the British, who retained their frontier forts in violation of treaty terms. The Confederation government could neither restrain American settlers nor offer Native nations meaningful diplomatic protection.

Shays' Rebellion in 1786–1787 was the most politically consequential domestic crisis of the Confederation period. Armed farmers in western Massachusetts, responding to crushing state tax policies and debt enforcement that threatened their land, shut down county courts and attempted to seize the federal arsenal at Springfield. The rebellion demonstrated that the Confederation government was powerless to respond to internal disorder. For political leaders already anxious about the Confederation's structural weaknesses, Shays' Rebellion provided the final urgency needed to build support for a constitutional convention.

The men who pushed most urgently for constitutional reform were not a disinterested group acting only for the common good. They were disproportionately merchants, creditors, land speculators, and large planters — people whose economic interests were damaged by the Confederation's weaknesses and who expected to benefit from a stronger national government. The Anti-Federalists who opposed ratification — often spokespeople for small farmers, debtors, and people skeptical of distant power — raised genuine concerns about concentrated authority that echoed the warnings of the revolutionary generation.

The Confederation was not simply a failure. It negotiated the Treaty of Paris, organized the Northwest Territory, established the survey system for western land, and demonstrated in concrete terms what structural problems a national government needed to solve. Its failures defined the agenda of the Constitutional Convention that replaced it. The Constitution of 1787 was shaped at every point by the experience of the Confederation that preceded it. What the Confederation period most clearly reveals is the difficulty of building a republic — and that the debates over power, representation, debt, democracy, and who the republic was for would continue through American history, changing form but never fully resolving.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Articles of Confederation
The first constitution of the United States, ratified 1781, which created a weak central government in which sovereignty was lodged primarily in the individual states. Congress under the Articles could not tax, regulate commerce, or enforce its own laws.
Confederation
A political union in which member states retain sovereignty and delegate only limited powers to a central government. Under a confederation, the central government acts through the states rather than governing citizens directly.
Sovereignty
Supreme political authority within a defined territory or community. The Articles declared each state "sovereign, free and independent."
Unicameral legislature
A lawmaking body with only one chamber. The Confederation Congress was unicameral, with each state casting one vote regardless of size.
Requisition
A formal request for money or resources. Under the Articles, Congress could only requisition funds from the states; it could not tax citizens directly.
Impost
A tax on imported goods; a proposed five-percent national impost was blocked by Rhode Island in 1781 and New York in 1786, demonstrating the paralysis of the Confederation's amendment process.
Land Ordinance of 1785
The Confederation law establishing the township-and-range survey system for the Northwest Territory, dividing land into townships of 36 square miles containing 36 one-square-mile sections. Section 16 of each township was reserved for public schools.
Northwest Ordinance
The landmark 1787 Confederation law organizing the Northwest Territory, establishing a three-stage path from territory to statehood, prohibiting slavery north of the Ohio River, and guaranteeing civil liberties to territorial residents.
Township-and-range system
The grid-based land survey method established by the Land Ordinance of 1785, which imposed a rectangular grid on the western landscape and remains visible in the straight-line boundaries of counties, roads, and farms across much of the United States.
Continental currency
Paper money issued by the Continental Congress during the Revolutionary War. Overprinting led to catastrophic inflation; the currency was nearly worthless by the end of the war.
Shays' Rebellion
The armed uprising of Massachusetts farmers in 1786–1787, led in part by Daniel Shays, against state tax collection and debt enforcement. It demonstrated the Confederation's powerlessness to maintain domestic order and energized the movement for constitutional reform.
Critical Period
The term used by some historians for the years between the Treaty of Paris (1783) and the ratification of the Constitution (1788), emphasizing the political and economic difficulties of the Confederation era. More recent scholars have questioned whether "crisis" is the most accurate description of the entire period.
Annapolis Convention
The September 1786 meeting of five states' delegates to address commercial problems. Delegates, including Madison and Hamilton, issued a call for a broader constitutional convention to meet in Philadelphia in 1787.
Treaty of Paris (1783)
The peace agreement ending the Revolutionary War, in which Britain recognized American independence and ceded all territory east of the Mississippi River, south of the Great Lakes, and north of Spanish Florida.
Federalism
The division of political power between a national government and state governments. The Articles of Confederation gave states primary sovereignty; the Constitution of 1787 created a different balance in which the national government had direct authority over citizens.
Republicanism
The political philosophy emphasizing government based on law, representation, civic virtue, and the common good. Both the Articles and the Constitution were expressions of republican principles, though they organized those principles very differently.
Anti-Federalism
The political movement that opposed ratification of the Constitution of 1787, arguing that it concentrated too much power in the national government, threatened states' rights and individual liberties, and served the interests of creditors and elites over ordinary people.
Manumission
The formal act of freeing an enslaved person. Several northern states adopted manumission statutes or gradual emancipation laws during and after the Revolution; the institution of slavery remained firmly entrenched in the South.
Quorum
The minimum number of members required for a legislative body to conduct business. The Confederation Congress required nine states for major legislation; achieving quorum was a persistent problem that frequently paralyzed national governance.
Territorial government
The administrative system established for a region before statehood. The Northwest Ordinance created a three-stage territorial government system that became the template for American expansion.

Major People and Places

Identification & Significance

Daniel Shays (1747–1825)
Massachusetts farmer and Revolutionary War veteran who became the most prominent leader of the 1786–1787 armed uprising. Shays, who had never received his military back pay, led insurgents in preventing courts from conducting debt proceedings. After the rebellion's suppression, he fled to Vermont and was eventually pardoned.
James Madison (1751–1836)
Virginia statesman, political theorist, and driving intellectual force behind the movement for constitutional reform. Madison's systematic analysis of the Confederation's weaknesses, his study of historical confederacies, and his leadership at both the Annapolis and Philadelphia conventions make him the most important figure in the transition from the Articles to the Constitution. Often called the "Father of the Constitution."
Alexander Hamilton (1755–1804)
New York statesman and former Continental Army officer who was one of the most urgent advocates for a stronger national government throughout the Confederation period. Hamilton organized the Annapolis Convention, was a key delegate at the Constitutional Convention, and later designed the financial system that solved the Confederation's debt problems.
George Washington (1732–1799)
Commander of the Continental Army and the most respected public figure in the new nation. Washington's frustration with the Confederation's inability to fund and support the Continental Army shaped his support for constitutional reform. His willingness to attend the Constitutional Convention gave it enormous credibility.
Thomas Jefferson (1743–1826)
Virginia statesman and drafter of the 1784 land ordinance. Serving as American minister to France during the Constitutional Convention, Jefferson viewed Shays' Rebellion with more equanimity than most of his contemporaries, seeing some popular unrest as a healthy sign in a democracy.
Benjamin Franklin (1706–1790)
Pennsylvania statesman and diplomat who helped negotiate the Treaty of Paris and served as a delegate to the Constitutional Convention at age eighty-one. Franklin's presence lent authority to the Convention's proceedings.
Nathan Dane (1752–1835)
Massachusetts delegate to the Confederation Congress who is credited with drafting much of the Northwest Ordinance of 1787, particularly its antislavery clause.
Shawnee Nation
One of the most prominent Indigenous nations in the Ohio Valley, whose homelands were directly threatened by American expansion after the Revolution. Shawnee leaders worked to organize pan-Indian resistance and maintained connections with the British in their frontier forts.
Delaware (Lenape) Nation
An Indigenous people whose homelands had already been disrupted by generations of conflict and who faced continued pressure from American settlement and treaty coercion during the Confederation period.
Northwest Territory
The land north of the Ohio River, east of the Mississippi, and south of the Great Lakes, ceded by Britain in the Treaty of Paris. Organized by the Northwest Ordinance of 1787, it was the site of ongoing conflict between American settlers and Indigenous nations and would eventually become Ohio, Indiana, Illinois, Michigan, and Wisconsin.
Springfield Arsenal
The federal arsenal at Springfield, Massachusetts, that Daniel Shays and his followers attempted to seize in January 1787. The arsenal attack marked the military climax of Shays' Rebellion and was repulsed by state troops using artillery.
Ohio River
The boundary between the United States and the Northwest Territory that American settlers were crossing in increasing numbers without legal authorization, creating conflict with Indigenous nations and demonstrating the Confederation's inability to control its own frontiers.

Chapter Reflection

The Articles of Confederation occupy an unusual place in American historical memory. They are often remembered primarily as what came before the Constitution — as the first draft, the failed experiment, the problem that 1787 solved. This framing, while not wrong, misses something important about what the Confederation era meant and what it reveals about American democracy.

The Confederation was a genuine experiment in a kind of republican government that Americans had reasons, grounded in real historical experience, to prefer: a government that lodged power close to the people, that prevented distant authority from taxing and commanding local communities, that built unity through voluntary cooperation rather than coercive power. That experiment ran into serious trouble — financial, diplomatic, commercial, social — and was replaced by a stronger framework. But the principles it embodied did not disappear. The suspicion of concentrated national power, the attachment to local self-governance, the fear that distant government would serve elites rather than ordinary people — these ideas and anxieties persisted through American history, surfacing in the Anti-Federalist writings of 1787–1788, in Jeffersonian opposition of the 1790s, in Jacksonian democracy, in states' rights arguments, in populist politics, and in debates over federal power that continue today. The Confederation was not simply an error corrected; it was a vision of American democracy that never fully disappeared.

The Confederation era also illuminates the exclusions and contradictions at the heart of the American founding. The republic proclaimed in 1776 that all men are created equal and endowed with unalienable rights, including liberty. The Confederation, like the Constitution that followed it, governed a republic in which hundreds of thousands of people were enslaved, in which women had no political voice, in which Indigenous nations were treated as obstacles to be managed rather than political communities with their own rights. The Northwest Ordinance proclaimed good faith toward Indigenous peoples and banned slavery north of the Ohio River — and American policy immediately set about violating both principles, because the settlement of the West required Indigenous land and because the political coalition that held the republic together required accommodating slaveholders. These contradictions were not accidents or oversights. They were structural features of the republic, and understanding them is essential for understanding why American history has been shaped so powerfully by conflicts over who truly belongs in the nation and who has a genuine claim on its promises.

The Confederation period shows students something that all of American history shows: that democracy is not a state achieved but a process contested. The men who designed the Articles were not building a perfect republic; they were trying to solve specific problems while managing real political pressures. The men who replaced the Articles with the Constitution were doing the same. In both cases, their solutions reflected their interests and their limitations as well as their ideals. Later generations would inherit their work and fight over it — over what it meant, whose interests it served, and how it should be changed. That is the nature of democratic self-governance, and it is the reason that understanding the founding period — not just its achievements, but its conflicts, its exclusions, and its contradictions — is essential for understanding everything that came after.

The Confederation era, finally, is a reminder that government matters. The absence of adequate national power to tax, regulate commerce, enforce treaties, and maintain order created real suffering — for creditors who could not collect, for farmers who could not sell, for soldiers who were not paid, and for a republic that could not be taken seriously by the powers surrounding it. The Constitutional Convention that replaced the Confederation was not a perfect solution, but it was a serious attempt to build institutions capable of governing an expanding, diverse, and contested nation. The debate it began about the proper scope of federal power and the rights of the states has never ended. Every generation of Americans has inherited it and added to it. That is the living legacy of the Confederation era — not a settled question answered in 1787, but an ongoing argument about what it means to govern a democratic republic.

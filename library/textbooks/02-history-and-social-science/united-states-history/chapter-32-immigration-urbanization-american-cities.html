Part VII — Industrialization, Immigration, and Empire
32

Immigration, Urbanization, and American Cities

United States History: Indigenous America, Republic, Conflict, and Change

Chapter Opening

"The era of mass immigration did not simply add population to the United States; it created the modern, multiethnic, urban America that would define the country through the twentieth century and beyond."

In the summer of 1903, a young woman named Pauline Newman arrived at Ellis Island in New York Harbor. She had traveled from a small town in what is now Lithuania, crossing the Atlantic Ocean in the steerage compartment of a steamship — the cheapest and most crowded section, where hundreds of migrants shared a dark, poorly ventilated space below the waterline. She was twelve years old, traveling with her mother and siblings, and she carried little more than a bundle of clothing and the address of a relative already settled on the Lower East Side of Manhattan. Within days of her arrival, she was working in a garment factory, stitching shirtwaists for fourteen hours a day, six days a week, for wages that barely covered rent in a two-room tenement apartment she shared with eight other people. She would go on to become an organizer for the International Ladies' Garment Workers' Union and a lifelong advocate for workers' rights. Her story — of immigration, labor, poverty, solidarity, and civic participation — was repeated millions of times across the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, in forms that shaped the United States more profoundly than almost any other force of the era.

Between roughly 1880 and 1924, the United States received one of the largest waves of voluntary migration in world history. More than twenty million people crossed the Atlantic or Pacific Oceans to reach American shores. They came from southern and eastern Europe — Italy, Poland, Russia, the Austro-Hungarian Empire, Greece, Romania, the Balkans — in numbers that dwarfed the earlier waves of Irish, German, and Scandinavian immigrants. They came from China, Japan, Korea, and the Philippines. They came from Mexico and Canada. They came from the Middle East and from India. They brought languages, religions, foodways, labor traditions, political ideas, and family structures that had no simple equivalent in the Anglo-Protestant culture that had dominated the United States since the colonial era. They arrived in a country undergoing rapid industrial transformation, and they became the labor force that built it.

The cities they entered were themselves being remade. In 1800, fewer than one in twenty Americans lived in a place that could be called a city. By 1920, more than half the population was urban. The United States had transformed from a predominantly rural, agricultural nation into a predominantly urban, industrial one — and immigration was both a cause and a consequence of that transformation. Immigrants filled the factories, dug the tunnels, built the bridges, laid the rail lines, staffed the sweatshops, operated the pushcarts, and crowded the tenements that defined American urban life at the turn of the twentieth century. Without their labor, the industrial economy that produced the wealth of the Gilded Age could not have functioned.

Yet the experience of immigration was never simply one of opportunity. It was also an experience of exclusion, exploitation, discrimination, cultural conflict, and legal restriction. Native-born Americans — themselves almost always the descendants of earlier immigrants — frequently viewed new arrivals with fear, contempt, or hostility. Nativist movements argued that immigrants from southern and eastern Europe were racially inferior, culturally incompatible, politically dangerous, or economically threatening. Chinese immigrants faced outright exclusion through federal law. Japanese immigrants were barred from naturalization and citizenship. Mexican workers were welcomed for their labor and expelled when the economy contracted. The history of immigration in this era is not a simple story of welcome and assimilation, but a contested story about who belonged in America, who had the right to work, who could become a citizen, and who could call the country home.

The cities immigrants helped build were extraordinary achievements and scenes of severe inequality at the same time. New York, Chicago, Philadelphia, Boston, Cleveland, Pittsburgh, Detroit, and dozens of other cities grew at astonishing speed, becoming centers of commerce, culture, politics, and social conflict. Architects designed skyscrapers that redefined the urban skyline. Engineers built elevated railways, subways, water systems, and sanitation networks. Political machines organized immigrant neighborhoods and delivered services in exchange for votes, creating forms of urban democracy that were simultaneously corrupt and inclusive. Reformers, social workers, journalists, and public health officials struggled to address poverty, disease, overcrowding, and exploitation in the teeming immigrant districts. Settlement houses became laboratories for social reform. Investigative journalists exposed unsafe food, dangerous tenements, and exploitative labor conditions. City governments debated how to provide parks, schools, libraries, and public health infrastructure to populations that had doubled or tripled within a generation.

This chapter examines immigration and urban growth in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. It asks how millions of people from different parts of the world came to live in American cities, what their lives were like, how they built communities, how they were treated by the society around them, and how their presence transformed the United States. It also examines the political and cultural conflicts that immigration produced — the rise of nativism, the passage of exclusion laws, the debates over assimilation and cultural identity — and asks why Americans so consistently struggled to reconcile their ideals about immigration with the reality of their responses to newcomers.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain the causes of mass immigration to the United States in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, including economic, political, and social factors in sending countries and the United States.
  2. Identify the major sending regions and national groups of the "new immigration" from southern and eastern Europe and contrast them with earlier immigration patterns.
  3. Trace the history of Chinese immigration and the Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882, analyzing the legal, racial, and political dimensions of exclusion.
  4. Describe the experiences of immigrants at Ellis Island and Angel Island, including the differences in how European and Asian immigrants were processed and treated.
  5. Analyze how immigrants built ethnic neighborhoods, community institutions, religious organizations, and mutual aid societies as strategies for survival and cultural preservation.
  6. Evaluate the living conditions in immigrant tenement districts, including overcrowding, disease, sanitation problems, and the public health responses they generated.
  7. Assess the role of political machines in immigrant neighborhoods, including both the services they provided and the corruption they generated.
  8. Explain the origins and work of the settlement house movement, including the contributions of Jane Addams and Hull House to urban reform.
  9. Identify the major arguments made by nativists against immigration and connect them to specific legislative restrictions, including the Chinese Exclusion Act and the Immigration Act of 1924.
  10. Compare the experiences of different immigrant groups — including Italian, Jewish, Polish, Chinese, and Mexican communities — in terms of labor, housing, discrimination, and civic life.
  11. Connect immigration and urbanization to the broader transformations of the Gilded Age and Progressive Era, including industrial capitalism, labor conflict, and reform.
  12. Contextualize debates over immigration restriction within longer American traditions of nativism, racial hierarchy, and contested definitions of citizenship.

Historical Context

The great wave of immigration that transformed the United States between 1880 and 1924 did not occur in isolation. It was the product of simultaneous and interconnected changes in Europe, Asia, Latin America, and the United States itself. Understanding why so many people came, and why they came when they did, requires looking both at the forces that pushed people out of their home countries and the forces that pulled them toward the United States.

In Europe, the late nineteenth century was a period of deep economic and social disruption. Agricultural economies across southern and eastern Europe were being destabilized by rising population, falling food prices caused by competition from American and Russian grain, declining availability of land, and the breakdown of traditional village economies under the pressure of early industrialization. In southern Italy, peasants who had farmed small plots for generations found themselves unable to survive on the declining returns from their land. In Russia and eastern Europe, Jews faced severe legal discrimination, restrictions on where they could live and work, and periodic waves of violent persecution known as pogroms. In Poland — partitioned among Russia, Prussia, and Austria — political repression combined with economic hardship to make emigration attractive. Across a vast swath of the European continent, young men and women were calculating that their chances were better elsewhere than at home.

The forces pulling immigrants toward the United States were equally powerful. American industries — steel, coal, textiles, meatpacking, garment manufacturing, construction, railroads — were expanding at enormous speed and required vast amounts of cheap labor. American employers actively recruited workers from Europe, sometimes sending agents to recruiting districts to promise wages and conditions that, while harsh by any comfortable standard, were genuinely better than what was available at home. Letters from immigrants who had already settled — passed through villages and read aloud in taverns and churches — spread word of the possibilities across the Atlantic. Steamship companies, which profited from carrying steerage passengers, advertised actively in European markets and made the voyage cheaper and more accessible than it had been for earlier generations.

The situation for Asian immigrants was dramatically different. Chinese workers had come to California in large numbers beginning with the Gold Rush of 1849 and had provided essential labor for the construction of the transcontinental railroad in the 1860s. But as the railroad was completed and the economy contracted in the 1870s, white workers attacked Chinese communities, lobbied for legal exclusion, and drove Chinese workers from mines, fishing grounds, and skilled trades. The result was the Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882 — the first and most sweeping racial restriction on immigration in American history.

American cities were already growing rapidly before the great wave of immigration arrived. But the scale and speed of urban growth in the final decades of the nineteenth century and the first decades of the twentieth was unprecedented. New York City grew from roughly one million people in 1860 to nearly seven million by 1910. Chicago, which barely existed in 1840, had nearly two million residents by 1900. Pittsburgh, Cleveland, Detroit, Buffalo, Boston, Philadelphia — all grew at rates that challenged every existing system of housing, sanitation, transportation, governance, and public order. The cities were building as fast as they could, but the infrastructure always lagged behind the population.

Section 32.1

Immigration and Urban Growth

Few developments shaped the United States more completely between 1880 and 1920 than the simultaneous arrival of millions of immigrants and the explosive growth of American cities. These two forces were not separate phenomena happening to coincide in the same decades. They were deeply interconnected: immigrants came because industrial cities needed workers, and cities grew because immigrants arrived to fill factories, construction sites, service industries, and neighborhoods. Together, immigration and urbanization transformed the United States from a predominantly rural, agrarian nation into a predominantly urban, industrial one within the span of a single generation.

The scale of the transformation was staggering. In 1880, the United States had a population of approximately fifty million people, most of them living in rural areas or small towns. By 1920, the population had nearly doubled to more than one hundred million, and for the first time in the nation's history, more Americans lived in urban areas than in rural ones. Fourteen American cities had populations of more than 300,000 people. New York City, with nearly seven million residents, was one of the largest cities in the world. Chicago, which had barely 30,000 residents in 1850, had 2.7 million by 1920. The physical geography of the United States had not changed, but the human geography had been remade almost beyond recognition.

The causes of urban growth were multiple and mutually reinforcing. Industrial capitalism required concentration — factories needed to be near sources of power, transportation, raw materials, and markets, and workers needed to be near factories. The railroad network built in the decades after the Civil War made it possible to move coal, iron ore, grain, and manufactured goods across continental distances at relatively low cost, and the rail hubs around which this movement organized — Chicago, Pittsburgh, Cleveland, Buffalo, St. Louis — became the sites of enormous industrial complexes. The telegraph and later the telephone made it possible to coordinate business activity across large distances. Electricity provided new forms of power that made large-scale manufacturing more efficient and also enabled the construction of elevators, electric streetcars, and other technologies that allowed cities to grow both taller and more spatially dispersed at the same time.

But industrialization alone does not explain the specific character of American urban growth in this era. What made American cities distinct — and distinctly challenging to govern, house, and manage — was the simultaneous arrival of millions of immigrants who came with very little capital, who took the lowest-paying and most dangerous jobs, and who crowded into housing that was inadequate for the numbers it was asked to hold. The immigrant city was not only an industrial city; it was a polyglot city, a city of dozens of languages, hundreds of cultural practices, competing religious institutions, and volatile political loyalties. Managing this complexity taxed every institution of urban government and produced conflicts that reshaped American democracy.

The relationship between immigration and urban growth was not passive or accidental. American employers actively sought immigrant labor and built systems for recruiting, transporting, and managing it. Steamship companies partnered with labor agents in Europe to fill their steerage compartments and profit from the passage. Railroad companies recruited workers from specific European regions, sometimes creating ethnic enclaves in particular industries and locations. Factory owners preferred immigrant workers in part because they were less likely to be members of established trade unions, more willing to accept lower wages in the immediate term, and in a weaker legal and political position to resist exploitation. The industrial economy and the immigrant labor supply reinforced each other in a relationship that was profitable for employers and genuinely life-improving for many workers even while it remained exploitative and dangerous by almost any objective standard.

The cities that immigrants entered were themselves in the midst of intense physical transformation. The decades between 1880 and 1920 saw the construction of some of the most ambitious infrastructure in human history: the Brooklyn Bridge (completed 1883), the Chicago elevated railway system, the New York City subway (opened 1904), the skyscrapers of the Loop and Lower Manhattan, the water systems and sewage treatment plants that made large urban populations survivable. Engineers, architects, city planners, and public health officials were inventing the modern city in real time, often barely ahead of the populations that required their work. The gap between what cities needed and what they had was the space in which urban poverty, disease, political corruption, and reform conflict all occurred.

Section 32.2

New Immigration from Europe

The immigrants who arrived in the United States between roughly 1880 and 1924 came from places that had contributed relatively few migrants in earlier decades. Historians often call this the era of the "new immigration" to distinguish it from the "old immigration" of the antebellum and immediate postwar decades, which had come predominantly from Britain, Ireland, Germany, and Scandinavia. The new immigrants came primarily from southern and eastern Europe: from Italy (especially the south and Sicily), from the Russian Empire (including its large Jewish population concentrated in the Pale of Settlement), from the Austro-Hungarian Empire (including Poles, Czechs, Slovaks, Croatians, Slovenians, and Ukrainians), from Greece, Romania, Bulgaria, Portugal, and from the Middle East (particularly Syria and Lebanon). The differences in national origin translated into differences in language, religion, cultural practice, and social organization that had profound consequences for how the new immigrants settled, organized, and were perceived.

Italian immigration exemplifies the "new immigration" in many of its key dimensions. Italian immigrants — the largest single national group in the new immigration — came overwhelmingly from the Mezzogiorno, the impoverished agricultural south of Italy and the island of Sicily. They were overwhelmingly Catholic, largely illiterate in Italian (and entirely unfamiliar with English), and experienced in agricultural labor rather than industrial work. Many came not with the intention of permanent settlement but with a strategy of temporary migration: work in the United States for several years, save money, and return to Italy to buy land or support a family. This "birds of passage" pattern meant that a significant portion of Italian immigrants — perhaps a third or more — did indeed return to Italy, making the Italian immigration experience genuinely transnational in character. Those who stayed built communities that were simultaneously deeply Italian and genuinely American.

Italian immigrants found work in a remarkably wide range of industries. In New York, they were heavily represented in construction — building the subways, bridges, tenements, and office buildings that the city's growth required. In Pennsylvania and West Virginia, they worked in coal mines. In California, they farmed and fished. In Chicago, they worked in packinghouses and steel mills. The padrone system — in which Italian-speaking labor contractors recruited, transported, and managed workers, taking a fee from their wages — was widespread in the early decades of Italian immigration, though it declined as immigrant communities became better established and workers developed independent labor organizations.

Jewish immigration from Russia and eastern Europe was different in its causes, its character, and its long-term consequences. Eastern European Jews did not come primarily for economic opportunity, though that was a factor; they came primarily as refugees from persecution. Anti-Jewish violence had a long history in eastern Europe, but it intensified dramatically in the 1880s after a wave of pogroms swept through Russia following the assassination of Tsar Alexander II. Russian authorities blamed Jews for social unrest and imposed increasingly severe legal restrictions: Jews were confined to the Pale of Settlement, barred from most professions and educational institutions, and subjected to quotas and discriminatory taxation. Pogroms recurred in waves — in 1881–1884, in 1903–1906, and again during the upheavals of World War I and the Russian Civil War. Each wave pushed more Jews toward emigration.

Unlike Italian immigrants, Jewish immigrants came predominantly with the intention of permanent settlement. They came in family units more often than as single young men. Many were skilled craftsmen — tailors, cobblers, carpenters, watchmakers — who brought expertise directly applicable to urban industrial labor. The garment industry in particular, centered in New York City, became both the economic anchor and the labor battleground of the eastern European Jewish immigrant community. Jewish immigrants also brought traditions of literacy, religious learning, and community organization that translated quickly into a rich institutional life: synagogues, Yiddish-language newspapers, labor unions, political clubs, literary societies, mutual aid organizations, and schools. The Forward (Forverts), a Yiddish-language socialist newspaper founded in 1897, became one of the most widely read newspapers in the United States, with a circulation of more than 200,000 at its peak.

Polish immigration, Slovak immigration, Croatian immigration, Greek immigration — each national group had its own specific pattern of causes, destinations, labor niches, community institutions, and relations with other groups. Poles came in large numbers to work in steel, coal, and meatpacking in industrial cities like Pittsburgh, Chicago, and Buffalo, and built dense communities organized around Catholic parish churches, which served simultaneously as religious institutions, social centers, schools, and markers of national identity in a foreign country. Greeks concentrated in certain service trades — restaurants, candy stores, shoeshine parlors — and developed tight commercial networks. Slovaks worked in Pennsylvania's steel mills and coal mines, building communities so dense that entire neighborhoods were organized by village of origin in Slovakia. The "new immigration" was not a single movement but a collection of parallel movements, each shaped by specific conditions in the sending country and in the particular American industries and communities where immigrants landed.

Historical Vocabulary
Chain Migration

Chain migration refers to the process by which earlier immigrants from a particular place attract and facilitate the migration of later immigrants from the same place. A young man from a village in southern Italy arrives in New York, finds work, and writes home with the address of the factory and the name of the boarding house keeper. His brother follows, then his cousin, then his neighbor. Over time, a specific block in Brooklyn or a particular floor in a Chicago tenement becomes home to people who knew each other in the old village.

Chain migration helps explain why immigrant communities were so precisely organized by place of origin, why certain national groups concentrated in specific industries and cities, and why the boundary between old country and new country was never as sharp in practice as it appeared on a map. It also shows that immigration was not a random or purely individual phenomenon but a social process embedded in networks of kinship, community, and information.

Section 32.3

Chinese Immigration and Exclusion

The history of Chinese immigration to the United States is inseparable from the history of racial exclusion — the deliberate use of federal law to bar an entire national group from entering and from becoming citizens. Understanding this history requires understanding both why Chinese immigrants came, what they contributed, and why the response to their presence took the extreme form of permanent legal exclusion.

Chinese immigrants had been coming to California since the Gold Rush of 1848–1849. Early arrivals came from Guangdong Province in southern China, drawn by the economic opportunities of the gold fields and the labor demands of a rapidly developing Pacific Coast economy. When the gold rush subsided, Chinese workers shifted to other forms of labor: building levees in the Sacramento-San Joaquin Delta, mining deeper deposits of gold and silver, fishing, operating laundries and restaurants, and — most significantly — providing the labor force for the construction of the Central Pacific Railroad, the western half of the transcontinental railroad completed in 1869. Approximately 12,000 to 20,000 Chinese workers, constituting the majority of the Central Pacific's labor force, laid track across the Sierra Nevada Mountains and the Nevada desert under extraordinarily dangerous conditions. They were paid less than their white counterparts, housed in segregated camps, and provided with no safety equipment for the blasting work that cost many their lives. Their contribution to one of the most ambitious infrastructure projects in American history was immense, and it was immediately and systematically erased from public memory.

As California's economy contracted in the 1870s, Chinese workers became targets of organized white working-class hostility. The argument was economic: Chinese workers, it was claimed, accepted lower wages and worse conditions than white workers, and therefore undercut white labor and drove down the general wage level. This argument contained a partial truth embedded in a larger distortion. Chinese workers did accept lower wages — but not because they had lower expectations or were inherently willing to be exploited; they accepted lower wages because they were legally barred from many occupations, excluded from most labor unions, and threatened with violence if they competed too directly with white workers. The conditions that produced Chinese wage competition were themselves the product of discrimination, not its cause.

Denis Kearney, an Irish immigrant who had become a prominent labor organizer in San Francisco, built the Workingmen's Party of California in the 1870s on a platform that combined genuine concerns about economic inequality and corporate power with virulent anti-Chinese racism. The party's slogan — "The Chinese must go" — captured a political climate in which demanding the exclusion of Chinese workers had become a mainstream position in California politics. By the late 1870s, anti-Chinese agitation had produced local ordinances restricting where Chinese residents could live, work, and conduct business. Violence against Chinese communities was common: Chinese neighborhoods were attacked, Chinese workers were driven from mines and fishing grounds, and in some cases entire Chinese communities were forced out of western towns through mob violence.

The pressure from California translated into federal action. The Chinese Exclusion Act, signed by President Chester Arthur in 1882, was a watershed in American immigration history. It prohibited Chinese laborers from entering the United States for ten years (a period later extended and eventually made permanent until 1943). It also prohibited Chinese immigrants already in the country from becoming naturalized citizens — a restriction that had no equivalent for any European immigrant group. Chinese people could not vote, could not own land in many states, and lived under a legal regime that denied them the most basic attributes of belonging.

The exclusion act did not end Chinese immigration entirely, but it changed its character dramatically. Some Chinese immigrants sought to enter by claiming exemption status — asserting that they were merchants or students rather than laborers. Others entered illegally, often through Canada or Mexico. Still others used a legal fiction known as "paper sons": following the 1906 San Francisco earthquake, which destroyed most immigration records, Chinese men could claim American-born parentage (and thus birthright citizenship) that could not be easily disproven, and they could bring relatives under these fictitious family identities. The paper son system created complex family histories of invented and real relationships that persisted across generations.

The Exclusion Act also set a precedent for the use of federal immigration law as an instrument of racial exclusion. The 1882 act was followed by a series of extensions and expansions: the Scott Act of 1888, the Geary Act of 1892 (which required all Chinese residents to register and carry identity papers at all times), and eventually a complete bar on Chinese immigration made permanent in 1902. The racial logic embedded in Chinese exclusion — the argument that certain groups were biologically or culturally incompatible with American democracy — would become the template for later immigration restrictions targeting other groups.

Section 32.4

Ellis Island and Angel Island

For most European immigrants arriving in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, the first encounter with American officialdom occurred at Ellis Island, the federal immigration processing station that opened in New York Harbor in 1892. Ellis Island was built to impose order on what had been a chaotic and corruption-prone system in which immigrants were processed at the pier by state immigration officials of varying competence and integrity. The new federal station centralized immigration processing, subjected arrivals to medical inspection, checked documentation, and began the process of determining who would be admitted and who would be turned away.

The experience of arrival at Ellis Island was both daunting and, for most immigrants, ultimately successful. Ships from Europe docked at Manhattan or Brooklyn piers, where first- and second-class passengers were processed onboard (their wealth and respectability assumed to guarantee their admissibility) and steerage passengers were transferred by ferry to Ellis Island. The great hall of Ellis Island's main building — a large, echoing space that could hold thousands of people simultaneously — was divided by metal railings into lanes that directed immigrants through the inspection process. Public health doctors examined immigrants as they walked past, using chalk to mark the coats of those who showed signs of physical or mental illness, infectious disease, or disability.

The questions at the legal inspection could be intimidating: Where are you going? Who is meeting you? How much money do you have? Do you have a job waiting? The last question was a trap: under the Contract Labor Law of 1885, immigrants who had been hired in advance by an American employer were legally barred. But immigrants who arrived without any employment prospects could also be denied entry as "likely to become a public charge." In practice, the vast majority of immigrants — approximately 98 percent — were admitted. But the two percent who were rejected, often after traveling weeks under terrible conditions and spending their life savings on passage, faced a devastating outcome.

Angel Island, the federal immigration station that operated in San Francisco Bay beginning in 1910, tells a very different story. While Ellis Island processed arrivals with relative speed and fairness for most European immigrants, Angel Island was designed primarily to enforce Chinese exclusion and to interrogate, detain, and often deport Asian immigrants. Where the average European immigrant spent a few hours at Ellis Island, Chinese immigrants at Angel Island could be detained for weeks, months, or even years while immigration officials investigated their claims to admission. The physical conditions at Angel Island were far worse than at Ellis Island: the detention barracks were overcrowded, poorly maintained, and essentially a prison. Immigrants were subject to intrusive physical examinations and endless rounds of questioning designed to find inconsistencies that would justify rejection.

Chinese immigrants at Angel Island responded to their imprisonment with poetry. Hundreds of poems were carved into the wooden walls of the detention barracks, written in classical Chinese verse forms, expressing grief, anger, homesickness, defiance, and determination. These poems — discovered in 1970 when the barracks were scheduled for demolition and saved by a park ranger who recognized their historical significance — are among the most remarkable primary sources of the immigration experience.

Japanese immigrants faced a different but related set of legal obstacles. The Gentleman's Agreement of 1907–1908 was a diplomatic arrangement between the United States and Japan in which the Japanese government agreed to limit the emigration of laborers to the United States in exchange for the United States government agreeing not to pass explicitly anti-Japanese immigration laws. The arrangement allowed Japanese women to continue immigrating as wives of men already in the United States, which led to the practice of "picture brides" — marriages arranged through the exchange of photographs, after which women emigrated to join husbands they had never met. The 1924 Immigration Act effectively ended Japanese immigration by barring all immigration from the "Asiatic barred zone."

Section 32.5

Ethnic Neighborhoods and Community Institutions

One of the most important facts about the era of mass immigration is that immigrants did not arrive as isolated individuals and scatter randomly across the American landscape. They arrived as members of networks — family networks, village networks, regional networks, religious networks — and they used those networks to concentrate in specific neighborhoods in specific cities, creating ethnic enclaves that were simultaneously communities of survival, centers of cultural life, and foundations for political participation. The ethnic neighborhood was not a sign of immigrants' failure to assimilate; it was an adaptive strategy that made survival in a hostile and unfamiliar environment possible.

The Lower East Side of Manhattan was perhaps the most famous immigrant neighborhood in American history, and it deserves examination in detail because it illustrates the institutional complexity of immigrant community life so completely. By 1900, the Lower East Side was one of the most densely populated places on earth, home to hundreds of thousands of eastern European Jewish immigrants crowded into a few square miles of narrow streets and multi-story tenement buildings. The physical conditions were crowded and often unsanitary, but the institutional life of the neighborhood was extraordinarily rich. There were hundreds of synagogues — not grand Reform temples but small Orthodox prayer houses (shtibls) organized by the immigrants' town or village of origin. There were Yiddish theaters offering entertainment ranging from melodrama to serious political drama. There were cafes and restaurants. There were labor union halls and political clubs. There were schools, both religious (yeshivot) and secular. There were newspapers. There were libraries. There were settlement houses. And there were the pushcarts that filled Hester Street and Orchard Street with food, clothing, and household goods, creating an informal economy that served both buyers and sellers who had little capital and no access to formal retail establishments.

The mutual aid society — a voluntary organization that members funded through regular dues and that provided benefits during illness, injury, or death — was one of the most important institutions of immigrant community life. Mutual aid societies took many forms: the Italian American società di mutuo soccorso, typically organized around a specific hometown or region in Italy; the Jewish landsmanshaft, organized around the town of origin in eastern Europe; the Polish fraternal benefit society, often linked to the Catholic Church; the Chinese tong, a form of mutual aid and commercial organization. Whatever their form, mutual aid societies served a population that had no access to government social services — no unemployment insurance, no workers' compensation, no public pension — and provided a basic social safety net through collective self-help.

The ethnic newspaper was another institution of central importance. By 1910, there were more than one thousand foreign-language newspapers published in the United States, in dozens of languages. These newspapers served multiple functions simultaneously: they provided immigrants with news from the old country and from the new, they helped immigrants navigate American institutions, they maintained cultural and linguistic connection for communities separated from their homelands, and they created a public sphere within immigrant communities where debates about labor, politics, culture, and community life could take place in a language that everyone could understand.

Religious institutions — Catholic parishes, Orthodox synagogues, Lutheran churches, Greek Orthodox churches, Buddhist temples — organized immigrant communities in ways that went far beyond the purely spiritual. In Italian and Polish communities, the Catholic parish was often the central social institution of the neighborhood: it provided schooling, organized social events, maintained the records of births, marriages, and deaths, and created a sense of bounded community within the anonymous city. Because the institutional Catholic Church in the United States was dominated by Irish American clergy, Italian and Polish immigrants frequently found themselves in conflict with church authorities over language, practice, and cultural autonomy. These conflicts sometimes led to the establishment of independent or "national" parishes that preserved the language and traditions of specific immigrant communities.

Multiple Perspectives

The Immigrant Neighborhood

From the perspective of recent immigrants: The ethnic neighborhood was home — a place where one could speak one's own language, buy familiar food, pray in a familiar manner, and find support from people who understood the pressures of displacement. It was a protective environment in which the skills and knowledge brought from the old country had value, and where the humiliations of the workplace or the street could be briefly set aside. For many immigrants, the neighborhood was a complete social environment that required relatively little navigation of the larger American society.
From the perspective of established American reformers: The immigrant neighborhood was a problem to be solved — a site of poverty, disease, political manipulation, and cultural backwardness that threatened American democratic norms. Progressive reformers like Jacob Riis and Jane Addams were genuinely concerned about the conditions of immigrant life, but their concern was often entangled with assumptions about the superiority of Anglo-Protestant American culture and the necessity of assimilation. The settlement house model embedded an asymmetry of power even as it served real needs.
From the perspective of nativists: The immigrant neighborhood was evidence of immigrants' refusal or inability to assimilate — a foreign enclave within American territory that threatened national unity, racial purity, and cultural coherence. Nativists saw the persistence of foreign languages, unfamiliar religions, and distinct cultural practices as proof that the new immigration was fundamentally different from and incompatible with American traditions. The ethnic neighborhood became a symbol of the alien presence they sought to restrict or eliminate.
Section 32.6

Tenements, Public Health, and Urban Poverty

The physical conditions in which most urban immigrants lived in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries were, by any standard, severe. The tenement — the multi-story, multi-unit apartment building that was the standard housing form of the immigrant city — was typically crowded, poorly built, inadequately ventilated, without running water or indoor plumbing, and plagued by fire hazards, vermin, and infectious disease.

The dumbbell tenement, so named because of the pinched central section visible from above that created the minimal airshaft required by New York City's Tenement House Act of 1879, became the characteristic housing form of the Lower East Side and similar immigrant districts. A standard dumbbell tenement was five to seven stories tall, on a lot twenty-five feet wide and ninety feet deep, and contained twenty to thirty apartments. The apartments were typically two or three rooms: a front room facing the street, a middle room with no window at all (lit by gas lamp even in the day), and a kitchen. A family of five or six persons in such an apartment was not unusual; apartments were frequently subdivided further, or rented out to boarders, to stretch the income that covered rent. Toilets — when they existed at all — were typically in the back yard or in the basement, shared by multiple families. Running water, when available, came from a single tap per floor shared by all apartments on that floor.

The consequences for public health were severe and predictable. Tuberculosis — the "white plague" — was endemic in tenement districts, spread by the combination of overcrowding, poor ventilation, and the physical stress of long working hours on workers already weakened by malnutrition. In the worst years, tuberculosis killed more New Yorkers than any other disease. Cholera and typhoid, spread through contaminated water and inadequate sanitation, swept through immigrant neighborhoods in epidemic waves. Infant mortality rates in tenement districts were shockingly high: in some Lower East Side blocks in the 1890s, more than one in five children born alive died before reaching their first birthday, primarily from gastrointestinal infections caused by contaminated milk and unsanitary conditions.

The man who brought these conditions to the attention of middle-class Americans most effectively was Jacob Riis, a Danish immigrant who had himself experienced poverty on arriving in New York in the 1870s and who later became a journalist and photographer. His 1890 book How the Other Half Lives combined text with photographs — produced using the newly invented technology of flash photography, which allowed images to be taken in the dark interiors of tenements — to show readers who had never entered a tenement what life there looked like. Riis's work was genuinely important: it shocked comfortable readers into awareness, helped build political support for housing reform, and contributed to the passage of the Tenement House Act of 1901, which established minimum standards for light, ventilation, and plumbing in new construction.

But Riis's work also had significant limitations. His portrayal of immigrant communities, while sympathetic in many respects, was shot through with ethnic and racial stereotypes. His photographs, while documentary in intent, posed subjects and selected scenes in ways that emphasized degradation and passivity, often stripping their subjects of agency and dignity. Riis saw the immigrant poor primarily as objects of reform rather than as actors in their own history — a limitation that reformers of the settlement house tradition would try, with incomplete success, to overcome.

Public health reform in immigrant cities involved multiple actors and multiple strategies. City and state health departments established infant milk stations that provided clean milk to reduce infant mortality. Public baths were built to provide bathing facilities that tenements lacked. Visiting nurses went door to door in tenement districts, educating mothers about hygiene practices that could reduce infant and childhood disease. Garbage collection, sewer construction, and water filtration — the basic sanitary infrastructure of the modern city — were gradually extended to immigrant neighborhoods, though always more slowly and less adequately than to middle-class districts.

Historical Vocabulary
Tenement House Reform

Tenement house reform refers to the Progressive Era movement to improve the physical conditions of urban housing for the poor through building codes, inspection, and regulation. New York City's Tenement House Act of 1901 established minimum standards for new tenement construction including requirements for windows in every room, running water, and indoor toilets.

The law did not immediately transform conditions in existing tenements, which were grandfathered in, but it gradually improved the stock of new housing available to immigrant families. Tenement house reform illustrates the Progressive approach to urban problems more broadly: a combination of investigative journalism, social science research, political advocacy, and regulatory legislation, all premised on the belief that the state had the power and responsibility to set minimum standards for private property in the public interest.

Section 32.7

Political Machines and City Services

In the chaotic, rapidly growing immigrant cities of the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, the political machine was a central institution of urban governance — and one of the most controversial. Political machines were urban organizations that maintained electoral power through an exchange relationship: they provided services, jobs, and favors to immigrant constituents in exchange for votes, and they used control of city government to distribute patronage that rewarded their supporters and sustained the organization. The most famous machine — New York City's Democratic organization known as Tammany Hall — has become synonymous in American memory with urban political corruption, but the history of machine politics is more complex than its reputation suggests.

For recent immigrants, the political machine provided services that no other institution offered and that government as formally constituted did not provide. When a recently arrived Italian family needed coal in winter and couldn't afford to pay for it, the ward boss could arrange delivery. When a Polish immigrant was arrested for selling from a pushcart without a license, the machine could intervene with the police or the courts. When a young Irish American wanted a job as a policeman, a fireman, or a city clerk, the machine could make it happen. When a Jewish family was being evicted by a landlord, the local precinct captain could talk to the landlord. These services were real. They came with an expectation of political loyalty, of votes delivered on election day. But they were also genuinely helpful to people who had no other recourse.

The machine also served as a mechanism for immigrant political incorporation. In a formal sense, naturalization and voting rights were available to European immigrants (though not to Chinese or other Asian immigrants barred from naturalization). But the formal mechanisms of American politics were not transparent or accessible to people who had arrived recently, spoke little English, and had no prior experience with American democratic institutions. The machine provided the informal infrastructure that connected immigrant residents to the formal political system: voter registration drives, election-day transportation, guidance on how to vote, and the sense of belonging to a political community that had tangible rewards.

Tammany Hall, the dominant machine in New York City for most of the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, built its power precisely on the immigrant communities of lower Manhattan and later the outer boroughs. Boss Richard Croker, who ran Tammany from 1886 to 1902, understood that the Irish-dominated machine needed to adapt to the arrival of millions of Italians and eastern European Jews if it was to maintain its electoral coalition. The machine extended patronage, political recognition, and organizational inclusion to Italian and Jewish leaders — though always within limits that preserved Irish dominance of the highest offices. The result was a political organization that was genuinely multiethnic by the standards of the time while also being organized around ethnic hierarchies that mirrored those of the larger society.

Machine politics was also genuinely corrupt. Machines systematically extracted money from businesses, contractors, and saloons seeking city licenses, permits, and contracts. They manipulated elections through ballot-box stuffing, repeat voting, and the purchase of votes. They protected illegal enterprises — gambling, prostitution, illegal alcohol — in exchange for financial contributions. The Tweed Ring, the most notorious Tammany corruption case, stole tens of millions of dollars from New York City in the late 1860s and early 1870s through inflated contracts and fraudulent payments.

The conflict between machine politics and reform politics was also a conflict between different definitions of democratic participation and different visions of American governance. Machine politicians saw politics as a practical exchange of concrete benefits for votes — a vernacular democracy rooted in neighborhood relationships and mutual obligation. Reformers saw politics as a matter of civic virtue and public interest, in which educated, disinterested citizens made rational decisions based on policy analysis rather than personal advantage. The reformers were often right about the corruption; the machine politicians were often right about the needs of the poor. The tension between these two versions of democracy has persisted in American political life long after the particular machines of the Gilded Age and Progressive Era were reformed or broken.

Section 32.8

Settlement Houses and Reform

The settlement house movement was one of the most important institutional innovations of the Progressive Era — a response to urban poverty, immigrant need, and middle-class women's search for meaningful public roles that created new forms of social knowledge and new approaches to social reform. Settlement houses were residential community centers established in poor urban neighborhoods, where educated volunteers and professionals actually lived and worked alongside — though never quite as part of — the immigrant communities they served. The movement was inspired by the example of Toynbee Hall in London, established in 1884, and it spread rapidly across the United States in the 1890s and early 1900s: by 1910, there were more than four hundred settlement houses in American cities.

Hull House, founded by Jane Addams and Ellen Gates Starr in Chicago's Near West Side in 1889, became the most famous and influential settlement house in the United States. The neighborhood surrounding Hull House was home to immigrants from Italy, Greece, Russia, Poland, Bohemia, and many other countries, many of them working in the nearby stockyards, garment factories, and manufacturing plants. Addams and her associates offered an extraordinary range of services: English-language classes, citizenship preparation, kindergartens for the children of working mothers, clubs for boys and girls, lectures and concerts, a labor museum, a cooperative kitchen, a gymnasium, a theater, and a library. They also conducted systematic social research, documented in the 1895 Hull House Maps and Papers, which mapped the nationalities, wages, and housing conditions of the neighborhood block by block and became a model for sociological investigation of urban poverty.

Jane Addams was a remarkable figure — one of the most important Americans of her generation. Born in 1860 in Cedarville, Illinois, educated at Rockford Female Seminary, and afflicted in her early adulthood with a sense of purposelessness she later described as a "snare of preparation" — the condition of educated women in a society that provided no adequate outlets for their talents — the settlement house movement offered both a purpose and a method. By living in immigrant neighborhoods, Addams believed, educated Americans could both serve those communities and learn from them, developing genuine understanding across the class and cultural divides that otherwise prevented any real social contact.

Hull House attracted an extraordinary community of women who went on to become major figures in American social reform. Florence Kelley, who lived at Hull House in the early 1890s, led investigations of sweatshop conditions in Chicago's garment industry and helped draft the Illinois Factory Act of 1893, which regulated working hours and conditions for women and children. She later became the general secretary of the National Consumers League and was one of the most effective labor reformers in American history. Julia Lathrop, another Hull House resident, became the first director of the U.S. Children's Bureau in 1912. Alice Hamilton pioneered the study of occupational disease and industrial toxicology. The settlement house was, among other things, a professional training ground for a generation of social reformers who largely lacked access to the formal institutional pathways through which men built professional careers.

The settlement house movement also had real limitations and contradictions. Addams and most settlement workers were genuinely committed to cross-cultural understanding and to the dignity of immigrant communities. But the relationship between the college-educated settlement worker and the immigrant community she served was inherently asymmetrical. Settlement workers brought assumptions about education, hygiene, family structure, and cultural practice that were rooted in middle-class Anglo-Protestant American norms, and their programs sometimes pressured immigrants to abandon cultural practices that carried deep significance for the communities in question. The Americanization programs that settlement houses often ran — English-language instruction, civics education, domestic science courses — were premised on the assumption that becoming American required setting aside being Italian or Polish or Jewish.

Addams herself was aware of these tensions and more thoughtful about them than most of her contemporaries. Her 1910 book Twenty Years at Hull House is a remarkable document of self-reflection, in which she acknowledges the ways in which settlement work could be paternalistic and the ways in which immigrants had much to teach the reformers who came to serve them. Her commitment to peace, her opposition to World War I, and her advocacy for civil liberties during the wartime suppression of dissent — positions that cost her enormous public support — reflected a consistent commitment to the proposition that democratic values applied to all people regardless of national origin.

Section 32.9

Nativism and American Identity

The era of mass immigration was also an era of intense nativist reaction — the organized effort by established Americans to restrict, exclude, or deport immigrants on the grounds that they were racially inferior, culturally incompatible, religiously threatening, or politically dangerous. Nativism was not new in American history; it had appeared in the anti-Catholic, anti-Irish hostility of the 1840s and 1850s, and in the anti-Chinese agitation of the 1870s. But the scale and character of the new immigration produced a new wave of nativist thought and action that was more systematic, more explicitly racial, and more politically powerful than anything that had preceded it.

The nativist argument in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries drew on several sources. One was economic: nativists argued that immigrant workers undercut wages, took jobs from native-born Americans, and imported standards of living so low that they made genuine economic progress for American workers impossible. This argument had a partial basis in the dynamics of labor market competition, but it systematically ignored the extent to which immigration also created economic growth — expanding the consumer market, providing labor for industries that could not have grown without it, and contributing entrepreneurially to new sectors of the economy.

A second source was cultural and religious: nativists argued that immigrants from Catholic, Jewish, Orthodox, and non-Christian traditions were fundamentally incompatible with the Protestant values that had shaped American democratic institutions. The argument that American democracy was a product of Anglo-Saxon Protestant culture — an explicitly racial claim — had a long history in American political thought, and it was revived and systematized in the late nineteenth century by intellectuals like Madison Grant, whose 1916 book The Passing of the Great Race argued that immigration from southern and eastern Europe was destroying the "Nordic race" that had built American civilization. Grant's work was pseudoscientific — it drew on discredited racial theories that conflated nationality, culture, and biology — but it was enormously influential, shaping the thinking of politicians and policymakers who pushed for immigration restriction.

A third source was political: nativists argued that immigrants brought radical political ideologies — anarchism, socialism, syndicalism — that threatened American democratic institutions. This argument gained traction after the assassination of President William McKinley in 1901 by Leon Czolgosz, the American-born son of Polish immigrants who had briefly associated with anarchist circles, and again after the Red Scare of 1919–1920, when the federal government deported hundreds of radical immigrants in a panic over Bolshevism following the Russian Revolution.

The legislative expression of nativism moved through several stages. The Immigration Act of 1882 established the first general federal criteria for immigration exclusion, barring convicts, lunatics, idiots, and those "likely to become a public charge." The Immigration Restriction League, founded in Boston in 1894 by Henry Cabot Lodge and other Harvard-educated reformers, lobbied for the literacy test — a requirement that immigrants demonstrate the ability to read in some language — as a device that would disproportionately restrict immigration from the regions of Europe with the lowest literacy rates while being facially neutral with respect to race. Congress passed literacy test legislation repeatedly; it was vetoed by Presidents Cleveland, Taft, and Wilson before finally becoming law in 1917.

The Dillingham Commission, a joint congressional committee that studied immigration from 1907 to 1911 and issued a forty-one volume report, gave scientific-seeming authority to nativist arguments by embedding racial categorizations into its analysis of immigration patterns and concluding that recent immigration from southern and eastern Europe was fundamentally different from and inferior to earlier immigration. The commission's work was methodologically flawed, but its political effect was significant, providing the intellectual framework for the quota legislation that followed.

The culmination of the restriction movement was the Immigration Act of 1924, also known as the Johnson-Reed Act or the National Origins Act. The act established a system of numerical quotas based on the national origin of the existing American population — a system deliberately designed to favor northern and western European immigration while drastically restricting immigration from southern and eastern Europe. Under the formula, immigration from Italy, Poland, Russia, and other major sending countries was cut by 80 to 90 percent from peak levels. The act also barred virtually all immigration from Asia by declaring persons "ineligible for citizenship" to be inadmissible. The system created by the 1924 act remained the basic framework of American immigration law until the Immigration and Nationality Act of 1965.

Historical Debate

Was Restriction Inevitable? Understanding the Immigration Act of 1924

The question: Why did the United States move from relative openness to immigration between 1880 and 1900 to the severely restrictive system established by the 1924 act? Was restriction a response to genuine social problems produced by mass immigration, or was it primarily a product of racial prejudice and elite manipulation?

Interpretation A — A Response to Genuine Social Tensions: Some historians emphasize the genuine social and economic tensions produced by mass immigration as drivers of restriction. The speed and scale of immigration between 1880 and 1920 did create real strains on housing, public health, and labor markets in receiving cities. The argument that some regulation of immigration was necessary for the management of social change — a Progressive argument, not merely a nativist one — had genuine adherents who were not simply racists. From this perspective, restriction was an overreaction to real problems, distorted by racial ideology, but not purely a product of prejudice.
Interpretation B — Primarily a Product of Racial Ideology: Other historians emphasize the racial and ideological character of restriction as its primary driver. The specific mechanisms of restriction — the national-origin quota system, the Asiatic barred zone, the pseudoscientific racial categories of the Dillingham Commission — cannot be explained primarily by social management concerns. They were designed to preserve the racial and cultural character of the American population as nativists defined it, and the evidence of racial motivation in the legislative debates is overwhelming.

Evidence: The legislative debates of the 1920s, the writings of restriction advocates like Madison Grant, the testimony before Congress, and the specific design of the quota formulas all support the second interpretation. But the persistence of Progressive reformers who supported restriction on social management grounds complicates any simple reading.

Why it matters: How we understand immigration restriction shapes how we evaluate American identity in the twentieth century and how we interpret contemporary immigration debates that often echo the arguments of the 1920s.

Primary Source Window

The Angel Island Poems (c. 1910–1940)

Creators: Chinese immigrants detained at Angel Island · Period: c. 1910–1940 · Type: Carved inscriptions; personal testimony; literary expression in classical Chinese verse forms

Context: Between 1910 and 1940, tens of thousands of Chinese immigrants were detained at the Angel Island Immigration Station in San Francisco Bay while their claims to admission were investigated by immigration authorities. Detention could last from days to months or even years. During their detention, immigrants carved poems into the wooden walls of the barracks. These poems were written in classical Chinese verse forms and expressed a range of emotions: grief, defiance, homesickness, anger, hope, and despair. When the Angel Island barracks were scheduled for demolition in 1970, a California State Parks employee noticed the carved writings and alerted authorities. The poems were photographed, translated, and published, becoming one of the most remarkable documents of the immigrant experience.

The poems describe the bitter experience of imprisonment — waiting indefinitely, facing interrogation designed to find grounds for deportation, confronting the gap between the promise of America and the reality of exclusion. One poem describes the poet having crossed a great ocean only to find himself imprisoned, his bravery and intelligence worthless in a place governed by unjust laws. Another invokes the humiliation of having one's papers examined and questioned, and vows that if the poet is admitted, he will work to repay the injustice done to him and his countrymen.

Analysis: The Angel Island poems are important as historical evidence in several ways. First, they document the direct experience of immigration restriction from the perspective of those subjected to it — perspectives systematically excluded from the official record of immigration proceedings. Second, they show that Chinese immigrants were not passive victims of exclusion but active agents who used cultural expression as a form of resistance and dignity-assertion. Third, they reveal the emotional and psychological cost of a legal system designed to presume guilt. The poems are also limited as evidence: they represent the experiences of those detained and waiting, not those who were summarily rejected, and they are written in a literary form that selected and shaped experience according to conventions of classical Chinese poetry.

Constitutional Connection
Immigration, Citizenship, and Equal Protection

Immigration law in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries raised fundamental questions about citizenship, equal protection, and the constitutional limits of federal power. In Chae Chan Ping v. United States (1889), the Supreme Court upheld the Chinese Exclusion Act and articulated the "plenary power doctrine" — the principle that Congress has virtually unlimited and essentially unreviewable authority over immigration. The Court reasoned that immigration policy was a matter of national sovereignty, and that federal courts had no authority to second-guess congressional decisions about who could enter the country.

United States v. Wong Kim Ark (1898) presented a different constitutional question: whether a person born in the United States to Chinese parents was a U.S. citizen under the Fourteenth Amendment's birthright citizenship clause. The Supreme Court held that yes, Wong Kim Ark was a citizen by birth, and that the Fourteenth Amendment's language was clear and unconditional. The decision established that birthright citizenship could not be denied on the basis of the parents' race or immigration status — a principle that has remained part of constitutional law ever since.

These two decisions together illustrate a fundamental tension in American constitutional law: the government has extraordinary power to restrict entry and exclude non-citizens on racial and national grounds, but those born in the United States or who have become citizens have strong constitutional protections. The line between the two categories — who is subject to plenary congressional power and who is protected by the Constitution — has been fiercely contested throughout American history and remains contested today.

Cause and Effect

The Chinese Exclusion Act and Its Consequences

Long-Term Causes

Racial hierarchy deeply embedded in American law and culture; the legal exclusion of non-white people from naturalized citizenship since 1790; the association of Chinese workers with coerced labor in the popular imagination.

Short-Term Causes

Economic depression in California in the 1870s producing labor market competition; organized political campaign of the Workingmen's Party; intense anti-Chinese violence including riots and the Rock Springs Massacre (1885).

The Event

Congress passed the Chinese Exclusion Act in 1882; the act barred Chinese laborers from entering the United States for ten years and denied Chinese immigrants the right to naturalized citizenship.

Long-Term Effects

Permanent exclusion (made permanent in 1902, repealed in 1943); the paper son system; the establishment of a precedent for racial immigration restriction applied to Japanese and other Asian immigrants; the plenary power doctrine; long-term gender imbalance in Chinese American communities.

Historical complexity: The exclusion act was also a product of racial ideology, political calculation, labor movement organization, and regional political dynamics specific to California. The workers who most loudly demanded Chinese exclusion were themselves immigrants — Irish Americans — whose own full acceptance into American whiteness was being consolidated in part through their opposition to Chinese inclusion.

Continuity and Change Over Time

American Identity and Immigration, 1880–1965

What Changed

  • The demographic composition of the United States changed profoundly; cities became genuinely multiethnic, multilingual, and multi-religious
  • Catholic and Jewish populations grew to constitute major proportions of the urban population
  • New cultural forms — jazz, Yiddish theater, Italian American cuisine, Polish Catholic parish culture — became part of the American cultural landscape
  • The labor movement was permanently shaped by immigrant workers and organizers
  • Urban political culture was transformed by machine politics and immigrant political participation
  • European immigrant groups were gradually incorporated into a broadly defined American identity over decades

What Stayed the Same

  • The racial hierarchy that placed people of European descent above people of color remained largely intact
  • Chinese and Japanese immigrants were excluded from citizenship, naturalization, and land ownership
  • African Americans, whose families had been in the United States for generations, were systematically excluded from the economic and political opportunities that European immigrants could eventually access
  • The definition of "American" expanded to include southern and eastern European immigrants but did not expand to include people of color in the same way or on the same timeline

Why this pattern matters: The selective expansion of American identity in this period is essential for understanding the twentieth century. The integration of European immigrant groups into the mainstream created a multiethnic white America that nevertheless maintained clear racial boundaries against people of color — shaping the opportunities and constraints of the entire century that followed.

Everyday Life and Social History

An Immigrant Day on the Lower East Side, c. 1905

To understand what immigration meant in human terms, consider a specific day in the life of a specific immigrant family. Sarah Abramowitz, twenty-eight, is awake before dawn. She lives with her husband Hyman, their three children, and Hyman's younger brother — six people — in a three-room apartment on Orchard Street. The apartment has no central heat; in winter, the kitchen stove provides the only warmth. There is no bathroom; the toilet is in the building's backyard, shared by all twelve families in the building. The single faucet in the kitchen provides the only water.

Hyman is a presser in a nearby garment factory, working ten hours a day, six days a week, for eight dollars a week. The family's rent takes three dollars, and food and coal consume most of the rest. The older children attend Public School 42 on Hester Street, where they receive instruction in English and Americanization programs alongside standard academic subjects. It is here that the children are learning to become Americans in a way that their parents, who arrived as adults, will only partially accomplish.

After the older children leave for school, Sarah prepares to do piece work: sewing buttons onto completed garments for a neighborhood contractor. She is paid by the piece — so her income depends entirely on how fast she can work while managing the household. At midday she takes the youngest child to Hester Street, where the pushcart market is in full operation, and buys a few carrots, a small piece of fish, and a loaf of bread for twenty cents. On Friday night, when the Sabbath begins, the candles are lit, the prayers are said, and for twenty-five hours the family observes the ritual rest that is, among other things, the most consistent experience of luxury and leisure in their week.

This life is hard, constrained, and often anxious. It is also genuinely different from the village life Sarah and Hyman left in Russia — more varied, more socially complex, with more potential mobility for the children even if not for themselves. Their children will eventually move to the Bronx, to Brooklyn, and ultimately to the suburbs, completing an immigrant trajectory that is both the story of individual families and the story of American social transformation.

Chapter Summary

The era of mass immigration between roughly 1880 and 1924 was one of the most transformative periods in American history, remaking the demographic, cultural, political, and economic character of the United States in ways that persist to the present day. More than twenty million people crossed the Atlantic and Pacific Oceans to reach American shores in these decades, drawn by the labor demands of an expanding industrial economy and pushed by poverty, persecution, and disruption in their home countries. They came primarily from southern and eastern Europe — from Italy, Russia, Poland, the Austro-Hungarian Empire, Greece, and dozens of other national and ethnic backgrounds — as well as from China, Japan, Mexico, and other parts of the world.

The distinction historians draw between the "old immigration" from northern and western Europe and the "new immigration" from southern and eastern Europe was partly real and partly constructed. The new immigrants did come from different national backgrounds, spoke different languages, practiced different religions, and had different cultural traditions than the northern European immigrants who had preceded them. But the idea that they were fundamentally different in character, intelligence, or capacity for democratic citizenship — the idea that drove nativist opposition to their arrival — was a product of racial ideology rather than evidence. In time, the descendants of Italian, Polish, Jewish, and Greek immigrants were incorporated into a broadly defined American identity, though the process took decades and was never automatic or complete.

The Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882 stands as the most significant exception to the pattern of eventual immigrant incorporation — the point at which the United States explicitly and legally defined one national group as permanently inadmissible on racial grounds. The act set a precedent for later restrictions on Japanese, South Asian, Filipino, and other Asian immigrants, and it encoded in federal law the racial hierarchy that distinguished between European immigrants who could eventually become citizens and Asian immigrants who could not. The Angel Island immigration station, where Chinese and other Asian immigrants were detained and interrogated in conditions far worse than those at Ellis Island, embodied the asymmetry between European and Asian immigrants in concrete physical form.

The cities that immigrants built and inhabited were simultaneously achievements of human organization and scenes of severe inequality and suffering. Tenement housing was crowded, dangerous, and disease-ridden. Working conditions in factories, mines, and construction sites were often lethal. The political machine, for all its corruption, provided a practical form of social support for communities that had no access to formal government services, and it connected immigrant communities to the democratic process even as it extracted loyalty and votes in return. Reformers — settlement house workers, investigative journalists, public health officials, labor organizers — worked to address the conditions of immigrant life with varying degrees of success and insight.

The community institutions that immigrants built in this era — mutual aid societies, ethnic newspapers, religious organizations, labor unions, political clubs — were not simply survival strategies. They were forms of social organization that generated cultural vitality, political power, economic cooperation, and civic participation. The Yiddish theater, the Italian mutual aid society, the Polish parish, the Chinese tong — all were responses to the challenges of immigration that produced genuine cultural achievements alongside practical forms of collective self-help. The labor movement in particular was deeply shaped by immigrant workers and organizers who brought traditions of working-class solidarity from Europe and forged them in the conditions of American industrial capitalism.

The nativist movement that culminated in the Immigration Act of 1924 drew on economic anxiety, racial pseudoscience, cultural defensiveness, and political calculation to construct a legal system that radically reduced immigration from southern and eastern Europe and effectively barred Asian immigration. The national-origin quota system was explicitly designed to preserve the racial composition of the American population as nativists understood it. The act remained the basic framework of American immigration law until 1965, when the Civil Rights era's commitment to non-discrimination produced the Immigration and Nationality Act that abolished national-origin quotas and opened the country to immigration from Asia, Latin America, Africa, and the Caribbean in unprecedented numbers.

The history of immigration and urbanization in this era is not a simple story of welcome and integration. It is a contested story about who counted as American, who had access to the opportunities that industrial capitalism was generating, who bore the costs of rapid urban growth, and who had the power to define the terms of belonging. European immigrants were eventually incorporated into American identity, though after generations of struggle and discrimination. Asian immigrants were excluded by law and force. Mexican immigrants were welcomed for their labor and expelled during economic contractions. African Americans were systematically excluded from the economic and political opportunities that European immigrants could eventually access. The era of mass immigration widened the definition of American in some respects and narrowed it in others.

Review Materials

Key Terms

New immigration
The wave of immigration from southern and eastern Europe (and parts of Asia) that dominated American immigration roughly between 1880 and 1924, distinct from earlier northern and western European sources.
Old immigration
The earlier wave of immigration primarily from northern and western Europe (Britain, Ireland, Germany, Scandinavia) that characterized immigration before 1880.
Chinese Exclusion Act (1882)
The first major racial restriction on American immigration, barring Chinese laborers from entering the United States and denying Chinese immigrants naturalization rights.
Ellis Island
The federal immigration processing station in New York Harbor where most European immigrants arrived between 1892 and 1954.
Angel Island
The federal immigration station in San Francisco Bay (1910–1940), characterized by long detention and aggressive interrogation of Asian immigrants, particularly Chinese.
Tenement
A multi-story urban apartment building, typically crowded, poorly maintained, and inadequately ventilated, that housed large numbers of low-income immigrant families.
Ethnic neighborhood
An urban residential area where a specific immigrant group concentrated, developing community institutions, businesses, religious organizations, and cultural life.
Mutual aid society
A voluntary organization formed by immigrants to provide insurance, burial assistance, medical help, and community support to its members through collective self-help.
Settlement house
A community center established in an immigrant neighborhood by educated middle-class reformers offering social services, education, and civic programs.
Political machine
An urban political organization that maintained power by exchanging patronage, services, and favors for votes; central to immigrant political incorporation in Gilded Age cities.
Nativism
The political belief that native-born Americans should be privileged over immigrants, often combined with racial, religious, and ethnic prejudice and the advocacy of immigration restriction.
Plenary power doctrine
The constitutional principle established in Chae Chan Ping v. United States (1889) that Congress has virtually unlimited authority over immigration policy, essentially unreviewable by courts.
Pogrom
An organized massacre or persecution of an ethnic or religious minority; specifically, the anti-Jewish violence that occurred in Russia and eastern Europe and drove large-scale Jewish emigration to the United States.
Padrone system
A labor contracting system in which an Italian-speaking middleman recruited and managed immigrant workers, often exploiting them through debt, deception, and control of housing and wages.
Chain migration
The process by which earlier immigrants from a particular place attract and facilitate the migration of later immigrants from the same place through social networks of family and community.
Americanization
The process or movement to assimilate immigrants into American culture through English-language instruction, civics education, and pressure to abandon traditional customs; a central aim of Progressive Era reform programs.
Sweatshop
A small manufacturing workshop, typically in a tenement, where workers labored for long hours under poor conditions for piece-rate wages with no legal protections.
National-origin quotas
Numerical limits on immigration based on a country's proportion of the existing American population; used after 1921 to restrict immigration from southern and eastern Europe.
Dillingham Commission
A joint congressional commission (1907–1911) that studied immigration and issued reports embedding racial categories in the policy discussion; its findings supported the restriction legislation of the 1920s.
Birthright citizenship
The principle, established by the Fourteenth Amendment and confirmed in United States v. Wong Kim Ark (1898), that all persons born on U.S. soil are citizens regardless of their parents' immigration status.
Immigration Act of 1924
Federal law (Johnson-Reed Act) establishing national-origin quotas that drastically reduced immigration from southern and eastern Europe and effectively barred Asian immigration; remained in effect until 1965.
Landsmanshaft
A Jewish mutual aid organization organized around immigrants from the same town or region in eastern Europe; a key institution of community cohesion and collective support.
Steerage
The lowest class of passenger accommodation on ocean liners; the cheapest passage, shared by hundreds of migrants in dark and poorly ventilated conditions below the waterline.

Identification & Significance

The densest immigrant neighborhood in American history; home primarily to eastern European Jews at peak settlement; center of Yiddish culture, labor organizing, and immigrant civic life.

Chapter Reflection

Chapter 32 has examined one of the most transformative periods in American history — the era of mass immigration and urban growth that reshaped the country between roughly 1880 and 1924. The history covered in this chapter matters not simply as a record of events that occurred in the past but as an explanation of how the United States became what it is: a multiethnic, urban, industrialized society marked simultaneously by extraordinary diversity and persistent racial hierarchy.

The immigrants who came in these decades — Italian, Jewish, Polish, Greek, Chinese, Japanese, Mexican, and dozens of other groups — built the country's industrial infrastructure, populated its cities, organized its labor movement, shaped its cultural life, and contributed to its political development in ways that are still visible today. They did this under conditions of severe hardship, discrimination, and legal vulnerability. The history of their communities — the mutual aid societies, the ethnic newspapers, the labor unions, the settlement houses, the religious organizations — is a history of collective human agency under difficult conditions: of people making lives and building institutions in circumstances they did not choose and could not fully control.

At the same time, this history is inseparable from the history of exclusion, restriction, and racial hierarchy that defined the limits of immigrant welcome. The Chinese Exclusion Act, the Angel Island detention barracks, the national-origin quota system, the systematic exclusion of Asian immigrants from naturalization and citizenship — these represent the United States at its worst, the betrayal of its stated ideals in the service of racial hierarchy and political fear. But the history also includes the extraordinary vitality and creativity of immigrant communities, the courage of people who left everything familiar to build new lives in an uncertain environment, the labor organizers who built unions under conditions of severe repression, and the reform advocates who challenged urban poverty at personal and professional cost.

This chapter connects to the broader themes of the United States History textbook in important ways. It shows how industrialization and immigration were inseparable processes in the transformation of the American economy and society. It shows how the Progressive Era's reform agenda was shaped by the conditions of the immigrant city, and how the settlement house movement and public health reform were responses to challenges that mass immigration made visible. It shows how the labor movement examined in Chapter 31 was built in large part by immigrant workers, and how the political conflicts of the Progressive Era (Chapter 36) were shaped by the presence of millions of new voters gradually incorporated into urban political machines and labor organizations.

The story of immigration is, ultimately, a story about who gets to define America — who belongs, on what terms, with what rights and what obligations. That question was asked with particular urgency in the era of mass immigration, and it was answered imperfectly and unjustly in ways that still carry consequences. The racial hierarchies that determined who was welcome in America in this era shaped the opportunities and constraints of the entire twentieth century. Understanding this history — with all its complexity, its humanity, and its injustice — is what this chapter, and this textbook, is for.

Part IV — Founding the Republic
13

Independence, Loyalists, Patriots, and the War for the Republic

United States History: Indigenous America, Republic, Conflict, and Change

Chapter Opening

"Independence was a beginning, not an end. The Revolution had destroyed one political order — British imperial rule over the mainland colonies — but it had not yet created a stable replacement."

In September 1783, Benjamin Franklin, John Adams, and John Jay signed their names to a document in Paris that formally ended the Revolutionary War between Great Britain and the United States of America. The Treaty of Paris recognized American independence, established borders from the Atlantic coast westward to the Mississippi River, and acknowledged that the former colonies were now a sovereign nation among the nations of the world. For the men who signed that treaty and for the Patriots who had fought and argued for independence since 1775, it was a moment of historic consequence. After eight years of war, political crisis, and uncertain fortune, the Revolution had succeeded.

Yet the Treaty of Paris settled far less than it appeared. Across the new United States, enormous questions remained unanswered. Who, exactly, belonged to this new republic? What did freedom mean for the roughly half million enslaved people who lived within its borders? What rights did women possess, and how would their place in the republic be defined? What would happen to the tens of thousands of American Loyalists who had remained loyal to Britain, many of whom now faced exile, property confiscation, and social humiliation? What obligations, if any, did the new United States have to the Native nations whose lands lay within — and far beyond — the boundaries the peace negotiators had drawn? These questions were not theoretical. They shaped the daily lives of millions of people across a vast and diverse continent.

Independence was a beginning, not an end. The Revolution had destroyed one political order — British imperial rule over the mainland colonies — but it had not yet created a stable replacement. In the years immediately following the Treaty of Paris, American leaders, communities, and ordinary people wrestled with the challenge of building republican governments from the ground up. State constitutions were drafted and debated. Religious establishments were questioned and, in many states, dismantled. Slavery, which the rhetoric of liberty had exposed as a profound contradiction, became the subject of gradual emancipation experiments in the North and of intensifying legal protection in the South. Economic disruption, debt, and uncertainty followed veterans home from the battlefield. And across the borderlands, Native nations who had not been invited to the Paris negotiations faced an aggressive new republic determined to expand into their territories.

This chapter studies the aftermath of the American Revolution — not just its military conclusion but the difficult, contested, often disappointing process of turning revolutionary ideals into governing institutions. The story is one of real achievements alongside sharp limits, of democratic expansion alongside violent exclusion, of high ideals alongside stubborn inequalities. The men who led the Revolution believed they were creating something new in the world: a republic built on popular consent, natural rights, and civic virtue. What they actually created was both more and less than that vision. The republic they founded was genuinely novel in some ways, yet it also preserved slavery, dispossessed Native peoples, restricted women's formal political participation, and concentrated power among property-owning white men.

To understand U.S. history, students must understand this founding moment not as a finished achievement but as a contested opening. The Revolution raised questions — about rights, freedom, equality, citizenship, and power — that Americans have argued over ever since. The contradictions embedded in the founding era did not disappear. They deepened, erupted into conflict, and eventually drove the nation toward a Civil War that would force a second revolution nearly a century later. Understanding the limits and achievements of the first revolution is essential for understanding everything that followed.

The evidence historians use to study this period is remarkably rich: diplomatic correspondence, state constitutional drafts, legislative debates, court records, newspapers, diaries, letters from ordinary soldiers and their families, petitions from enslaved people seeking freedom, memorials from Loyalists seeking compensation, and records from Native councils that tried to navigate the new political landscape. This chapter draws on that evidence to reconstruct a founding era that was not a golden age of unity but a period of profound struggle over who the republic would serve and who it would exclude.

Learning Objectives

  4. Describe the principles of republicanism and civic virtue and explain how they shaped the political culture of the new nation.

  6. Analyze the debates over religious freedom and disestablishment and explain the outcomes in different states.
  7. Evaluate the contradictions between revolutionary ideals of liberty and the persistence of slavery, tracing gradual emancipation movements in the North and the entrenchment of slavery in the South.
  8. Describe the situation of Native nations after the Treaty of Paris and explain how the new republic's territorial claims threatened Indigenous sovereignty.
  9. Assess the challenges of building stable republican government in the years after the Revolution.
  10. Connect the achievements and contradictions of the founding era to later developments in U.S. constitutional history and social reform.

Historical Context

To understand the aftermath of the Revolution, students must first understand the world the Revolution disrupted and what the war itself had done to American society. The thirteen colonies that declared independence in 1776 were not a unified community. They were thirteen distinct political units with different economies, social structures, religious cultures, and relationships to slavery. New England was dominated by small farming communities, Congregationalist churches, town meeting governance, and a maritime economy tied to fishing and trade. The Middle Colonies were ethnically and religiously diverse, with large Quaker, German Reformed, Dutch Reformed, Lutheran, and Anglican populations alongside growing commercial cities like Philadelphia and New York. The Chesapeake colonies of Virginia and Maryland were organized around tobacco agriculture and slave labor, with a planter class that exercised enormous political power. The Lower South — the Carolinas and Georgia — was even more deeply committed to plantation slavery, with an enslaved workforce that in some districts outnumbered white inhabitants.

The Revolutionary War itself, from 1775 to 1783, had been deeply disruptive. Roughly 200,000 men served in the Continental Army or state militias at some point during the war. Tens of thousands died — not primarily in battle, but from disease, exposure, and starvation. Disease killed far more soldiers than British muskets. The winter at Valley Forge in 1777–78 became the symbol of this suffering: Continental soldiers lacking adequate food, clothing, and shelter, dying at rates that alarmed their commanders. Veterans who survived returned home with damaged health, unpaid wages, and deep grievances against governments that had promised compensation and rarely delivered.

The war had also brought conflict directly into civilian communities. British forces occupied major cities including Boston, New York, Philadelphia, and Charleston at various points. In the South, particularly from 1778 onward after Britain shifted its strategy to focus on the Carolinas and Georgia, the war became a vicious civil conflict between Patriot and Loyalist neighbors who raided, burned, and killed each other's communities with a ferocity that made the southern theater look more like guerrilla warfare than conventional campaigns. Partisan violence left lasting scars on families and communities across Virginia, the Carolinas, and Georgia.

The war's economic disruption was severe. Continental currency, printed to finance the war, collapsed through runaway inflation. "Not worth a Continental" became a common phrase, describing paper money that could barely buy anything. Trade had been disrupted, debts had accumulated, and the machinery of commerce — credit networks, merchant connections, shipping routes — had been broken apart by years of conflict. Into this disrupted world, American leaders had to build something genuinely unprecedented: republican governments without kings. The challenge was immense, and most of the world in the 1780s was watching skeptically.

Section 13.1

The Treaty of Paris and Independence

The Negotiations

The war that had begun at Lexington and Concord in April 1775 effectively ended at Yorktown, Virginia, in October 1781, when General Charles Cornwallis surrendered his army of roughly 8,000 British soldiers to a combined Franco-American force under George Washington and the French commander Rochambeau. News of Yorktown reached London in November 1781 and shattered the British government's resolve to continue the war. The ministry of Lord North fell, replaced by a government willing to negotiate peace. For the next two years, American and British diplomats worked out the terms of a formal settlement.

The American peace commissioners — Benjamin Franklin, John Adams, and John Jay — were authorized by the Continental Congress to negotiate in concert with France, the United States' crucial wartime ally. France had entered the war in 1778 on the American side, and the French alliance had been indispensable: French money, troops, and naval power had made Yorktown possible. In practice, the American diplomats negotiated largely on their own. John Jay, distrustful of French intentions, feared that a peace brokered through France might sacrifice American territorial ambitions to satisfy Spanish demands. Acting on his own initiative, Jay opened direct negotiations with British representatives.

The result astonished many observers. The British government under Lord Shelburne proved willing to offer remarkably generous terms. Shelburne hoped that generous treatment of the United States would undercut the Franco-American alliance, restore commercial ties with the former colonies, and lay the groundwork for future friendship. The preliminary agreement reached in November 1782 — finalized as the Treaty of Paris in September 1783 — reflected this strategy.

The Terms

The Treaty of Paris recognized the United States as an independent nation and established boundaries stretching from the Atlantic coast westward to the Mississippi River and from the Great Lakes south to the thirty-first parallel. These were extraordinary territorial claims. The new United States on paper controlled a vast region that in reality it had not conquered and did not control — territories inhabited by dozens of Native nations, contested by Spain from the south and west and by British traders from the north.

Other treaty provisions addressed British debts, Loyalist property, and fishing rights. Article IV required that creditors on both sides meet no legal obstacles in recovering debts. Article V requested — but did not require — that Congress recommend to the states the restoration of confiscated Loyalist property. Article VI prohibited future confiscations and persecutions of Loyalists. And American fishermen received the right to fish in the rich waters off Newfoundland and Nova Scotia.

The debt and Loyalist provisions were almost immediately ignored. Most states had confiscated Loyalist property and had no intention of restoring it. Debts owed to British merchants were contested in American courts for years. The gap between the treaty's terms and the states' actual behavior became one of the persistent irritants of the 1780s, as Britain pointed to American violations to justify its own failure to withdraw from frontier posts it was obligated to evacuate.

What the Treaty Left Out

What the Treaty of Paris did not address was as important as what it did. Native nations were not parties to the negotiations and were not mentioned as sovereign peoples. The boundary settlements — which awarded vast territories to the United States — were negotiated over Indigenous heads. Peoples like the Haudenosaunee, the Shawnee, the Delaware, the Cherokee, the Creek, and dozens of other nations had fought throughout the Revolutionary War to defend their lands and sovereignty. The treaty treated their territories as British possessions to be given away, ignoring entirely the nations that actually controlled them.

The treaty also said nothing about slavery. The approximately 500,000 enslaved people within the new republic's borders — roughly one-fifth of the total population — had no voice in the settlement that determined the nation's future. The question of what independence meant for enslaved people was left entirely to the new states and to the contradictory impulses of the founding generation.

Section 13.2

The Costs of War

Human Costs

The Revolutionary War had been enormously costly in human terms. Historians estimate that between 25,000 and 70,000 Americans died as a result of the war — a range that reflects the difficulty of counting deaths from disease, starvation, and exposure, which killed far more soldiers than combat did. Out of a total colonial population of roughly 2.5 million, these losses were devastating. The proportion of Americans who died in the Revolution was higher relative to population than in any subsequent American war except the Civil War.

Continental soldiers suffered disproportionately. Men who served in the Continental Army — as opposed to short-term state militia — tended to be poorer, younger, and more vulnerable than the officers who led them. They were promised pay, land bounties, and pensions. In practice, pay arrived irregularly and in depreciated currency; land bounties were often slow to materialize; pensions were promises the new governments struggled to honor. Veterans returned home to find that their wartime service had not been rewarded as promised and that their economic situations had often deteriorated during their absence.

Disease had been the war's most lethal force. Smallpox swept through the Continental Army in the early years of the conflict until Washington made the controversial decision to inoculate his troops. Among civilian populations, the disruption of war spread smallpox, dysentery, and typhus. British prisoner-of-war ships anchored in New York Harbor became legendary death traps: thousands of American prisoners died aboard ships like the HMS Jersey, their remains later recovered from Brooklyn beaches.

Women who had maintained farms, businesses, and households during the war often found themselves in difficult circumstances afterward. Many had taken on economic roles that social convention normally reserved for men — managing debts, negotiating with merchants, coordinating labor — and discovered that the law gave them little formal recognition for this work. A woman whose husband had died in the war was often left with debts, competing claims on her household, and few legal protections.

Economic Disruption

The economic aftermath of the Revolution was severe. The Continental Congress had financed the war largely by printing money and borrowing, and the result was rampant inflation. "Not worth a Continental" became a common phrase, describing the near-worthless paper currency that had flooded the economy. Farmers who had accepted paper payment for food and supplies found themselves holding currency that could barely buy anything.

Trade disruption was equally serious. Before independence, American merchants had operated within the British imperial trading system, which gave them access to British markets, credit, and naval protection. Independence severed these ties. American ships could no longer legally trade with British colonies in the Caribbean — a devastating blow to the New England and Middle Atlantic commercial economies. New commercial relationships had to be built from scratch, a slow and painful process.

In Massachusetts, the situation became so desperate that farmers in the western part of the state, many of them veterans, organized resistance. In 1786 and 1787, a movement led by Daniel Shays — a former Continental Army captain — rose up to close the courts processing debt judgments. Shays' Rebellion was suppressed by state militia, but it alarmed leaders across the country who worried that the new republic was sliding toward anarchy. The rebellion accelerated the push for a stronger national government that led to the Constitutional Convention of 1787.

Regional Variations

The costs of war were not evenly distributed. The South, particularly the Carolinas and Georgia, had suffered the worst physical destruction. British forces had occupied Charleston, ravaged plantations, and presided over vicious partisan conflict between Loyalist and Patriot neighbors. The Low Country plantation economy had been disrupted by the flight of enslaved people — tens of thousands had escaped to British lines during the war — and by the physical destruction of plantations and infrastructure.

New England, while spared some of the worst fighting after the early years, suffered economically from the disruption of maritime trade. The fishing industry, the carrying trade, and connections with British West Indian markets had all been fractured. The Middle States had experienced the war's worst fighting in 1776 and 1777, when New York and New Jersey became primary battlegrounds. Philadelphia had been occupied by British forces for nearly a year.

Section 13.3

Loyalists and Exile

Who Were the Loyalists?

Not all Americans supported independence. Historians estimate that between 15 and 20 percent of the white colonial population were active Loyalists — people who remained loyal to the British Crown and actively opposed the Revolution. Another substantial portion was genuinely neutral, caught between two sides in a conflict they had not chosen. Among the enslaved population, loyalty to Britain was often more about freedom than ideology: Britain had offered freedom to enslaved people who escaped to British lines, making it a more appealing option than a revolution led by slaveholders.

Loyalists came from all backgrounds, classes, and regions, though their distribution was uneven. Anglican clergy, colonial officeholders, wealthy merchants with deep ties to British commercial networks, recent immigrants who still felt strong ties to Britain, and communities that had suffered from Patriot mob violence were disproportionately represented. In New York, which had the largest Loyalist population, Loyalism was strong in the city and in certain rural counties. In the backcountry of the Carolinas, communities that had been terrorized by Patriot planters sometimes embraced the British cause as a matter of protection rather than principle.

Some Loyalists took up arms. Loyalist military units — the Queen's Rangers, the British Legion, Butler's Rangers — fought alongside British regulars throughout the war. In the South, Loyalist partisans engaged in fierce fighting against Patriot neighbors in what amounted to a civil war within the Revolution. Others simply tried to keep their heads down, maintain their property, and wait for the outcome. For these quieter Loyalists, the end of the war brought not armed conflict but social and legal persecution.

Confiscation and Persecution

During and after the Revolution, states moved aggressively against Loyalists. Confiscation acts — passed by nearly every state — authorized the seizure of Loyalist property. Estates, farms, businesses, and personal possessions were seized and often sold to raise revenue or redistributed to Patriots. In New York alone, estates worth millions of dollars were confiscated. Large Loyalist landholdings in South Carolina, New York, and Georgia were broken up and sold.

Beyond property seizure, Loyalists faced social persecution. They were denied the right to vote, barred from practicing certain professions, stripped of civil rights in many states, and subjected to public humiliation, violence, and social ostracism. Neighbors who had known them for decades turned against them. Former business partners refused to deal with them. In some communities, Loyalists were tarred and feathered — a ritual humiliation that had been used against Stamp Act agents before the Revolution and was now turned against those deemed enemies of the new republic.

The Treaty of Paris had attempted to address this treatment. Article V called on Congress to recommend that states restore confiscated Loyalist property, and Article VI prohibited future confiscations. Congress dutifully sent the recommendations to the states. The states largely ignored them. The treatment of Loyalists became one of the most flagrant American violations of the treaty's terms, a source of lasting bitterness between the United States and Britain.

Exile

Faced with persecution, property loss, and social exclusion, between 60,000 and 80,000 Loyalists left the United States after independence — one of the largest forced migrations in North American history at that time. They went primarily to Canada (especially Nova Scotia and what would become New Brunswick), to Britain, and to the Caribbean.

Nova Scotia received roughly 30,000 Loyalist refugees between 1782 and 1784, a migration that fundamentally transformed the colony. The influx was so large that a new province, New Brunswick, was carved out to accommodate them. These refugees established new communities that carried with them distinct memories of loss, grievance, and exile. For generations, their descendants maintained identities built partly around the claim to have sacrificed everything for loyalty to the Crown.

Among the Loyalist refugees were thousands of Black Loyalists — formerly enslaved people who had escaped to British lines and had been promised freedom. The British honored this commitment to varying degrees, evacuating thousands from New York, Charleston, and Savannah. The Black Loyalists who went to Nova Scotia found a complicated freedom: they were nominally free but faced land discrimination, poverty, and racial exclusion. In 1792, approximately 1,200 Black Loyalists left Nova Scotia for Sierra Leone in West Africa, preferring to try their fortune in an African settlement to the hardships of Maritimes colonial life. Their journey represented both the failure of British promises and the extraordinary determination of people who had already risked everything for freedom.

The Loyalist Legacy

The Loyalist experience shaped the early republic in several important ways. The confiscation of Loyalist property transferred significant wealth — land, buildings, businesses — from one group of Americans to another. In some regions this redistribution broke up large estates and created opportunities for smaller property owners, an unintended democratizing effect. In others, it simply transferred wealth from Loyalist elites to Patriot elites.

The treatment of Loyalists also established troubling precedents about loyalty, dissent, and belonging. The revolutionary movement, which proclaimed the rights of man, proved willing to deny those rights to the roughly one in five Americans who had disagreed with independence. The Loyalist experience raises enduring questions about the limits of democratic tolerance — questions that would resurface in every subsequent era of political crisis in American history.

Historical Vocabulary
Republicanism

Republicanism was the central political philosophy of the American founding era. It referred not merely to a system of elected representatives, but to a broader set of ideas about citizenship, virtue, and the relationship between individuals and their communities.

At its core, republicanism held that legitimate government derived its authority from the consent of the people — not from a king's divine right or an aristocracy's inherited privilege. But it was not simply democracy. Most republican theorists believed that ordinary people were susceptible to passion and manipulation, and that pure direct democracy would degenerate into mob rule. The solution was to design governments that channeled popular will through representative institutions while building in protections against both tyranny from above and mob rule from below.

This required what republicans called civic virtue — the willingness of citizens to put the public good ahead of narrow self-interest. Republicanism was thus both radical and limiting: radical in its rejection of monarchy and hereditary privilege, limiting in its restriction of full citizenship to propertied white men deemed capable of civic virtue.

Section 13.4

Republicanism and Civic Virtue

The creation of republican government in the United States was not simply a matter of removing a king and holding elections. It required the development of a new political culture — new ideas about authority, citizenship, virtue, and the relationship between rulers and the ruled. The founding generation was deeply influenced by classical republican thought, drawing on examples from ancient Rome and Greece, and by English and European political philosophy, particularly the writings of John Locke, Montesquieu, and the civic republican tradition.

Central to republican thought was the concept of civic virtue: the idea that citizens must put the public good ahead of personal ambition. This was not merely a moral aspiration; it was considered a functional requirement of republican government. A republic was thought to be inherently fragile, vulnerable to the corrupting influence of wealth, faction, ambition, and luxury. Only a citizenry committed to the common good could sustain it. The concern with virtue shaped many of the founding generation's political anxieties — about standing armies, paper money, luxury goods, and the tendency of groups to pursue their own interests at the expense of the public good.

These anxieties were not merely theoretical. The 1780s seemed to confirm the worst fears. State legislatures were passing debtor relief laws that, in the eyes of creditors, amounted to attacks on property rights. Shays' Rebellion appeared to demonstrate that debtors would use violence to escape their obligations. Continental Army veterans were organizing to press for pensions that governments could not easily afford. To many republican leaders, the republic seemed in danger of being captured by narrow special interests or collapsing into chaos.

Washington and the Refusal of Power

One of the most consequential acts of civic virtue in the founding era was also one of the simplest: George Washington's resignation from command of the Continental Army in December 1783. After Yorktown, Washington was the most popular and powerful figure in America. Some had spoken openly of making him a king. He could, many believed, have claimed permanent political authority had he chosen to.

Instead, Washington appeared before Congress in Annapolis, Maryland, surrendered his commission, and retired to his Mount Vernon plantation. The gesture was deliberate and theatrical, shaped by his deep commitment to republican principles: military power must be subject to civilian authority, and leaders must be willing to step down. King George III, when told that Washington intended to resign and return to private life, reportedly said that if it were true, Washington would be "the greatest man in the world." Washington's voluntary surrender helped establish a crucial republican norm that would shape American political culture for centuries.

The Problem of Diversity

One of the most challenging aspects of building republican government was the sheer diversity of the new United States. The thirteen states were not a unified community sharing common traditions, economic interests, or social structures. The idea that such disparate people could be united in a single republic built on shared civic virtue was ambitious, and many contemporaries doubted it was possible.

The federalism that emerged — with significant power retained by individual states and a weaker central government under the Articles of Confederation — reflected this diversity. Most Americans in the 1780s believed republican government was most viable at the local and state level, where communities were more homogeneous and citizens could know their representatives personally. James Madison would directly challenge this view, arguing in Federalist No. 10 that a large republic was actually more stable because its diversity would prevent any single faction from dominating. But in the early 1780s, it remained an unproven idea in a republic that was visibly struggling.

Section 13.5

State Constitutions and Rights Declarations

The Constitutional Revolution in the States

Even before independence was formally declared, colonial leaders recognized that new governments needed to be created. With royal governors fleeing or being expelled and imperial institutions collapsing, the question of how to organize political authority became urgent. In May 1776, the Continental Congress recommended that the colonies establish new governments "under the authority of the people." What followed was an unprecedented experiment in democratic constitution-making.

Between 1776 and 1780, every state drafted and adopted a new constitution. These documents varied considerably, but they shared features that reflected the ideological commitments of the Revolution. Most significantly, they were written documents — not collections of unwritten customs and precedents as in Britain, but explicit statements of the principles, structures, and limits of government. The act of writing a constitution was itself a political statement: it meant that government was a human creation, established by deliberate choice, subject to deliberate revision.

Rights Declarations

Many state constitutions included explicit declarations of rights — lists of individual liberties that government was prohibited from violating. Virginia led the way. The Virginia Declaration of Rights, drafted primarily by George Mason and adopted in June 1776, declared that all men were by nature equally free and independent, possessed of certain inherent rights including life, liberty, property, and the pursuit of happiness and safety. It asserted popular sovereignty, protected freedom of the press, guaranteed jury trial, prohibited cruel and unusual punishment, and affirmed freedom of religion.

Massachusetts followed with a constitution in 1780, drafted largely by John Adams, that included a particularly influential declaration of rights and established the principle that the constitution itself must be ratified by the people in a special convention — not merely adopted by the legislature. This principle distinguished constitutions as higher law, above ordinary legislation, deriving authority directly from the sovereign people. When Anti-Federalists objected to the federal Constitution of 1787 on the grounds that it lacked a bill of rights, they were drawing on the precedent these state declarations had established. The federal Bill of Rights, adopted in 1791, was in many ways a compilation of rights already established in state documents.

Structures of Government

State constitutions varied in their institutional designs but shared common features. All created elected legislatures as the primary branch of government — reflecting the revolutionary conviction that the legislature, as the representative body, was closest to the people and most trustworthy. Most created relatively weak executive branches, reflecting colonists' deep suspicion of executive power developed through conflicts with royal governors.

The franchise — the right to vote — was typically restricted to white men who owned property above a certain minimum. This restriction was itself a departure from colonial practice in some areas, where broader property qualifications had sometimes allowed Catholics, Jews, women, and free Black men to vote. New Jersey was a notable exception: its 1776 constitution extended the vote to "all inhabitants" meeting property qualifications, which technically included women and free Black people. Women voted in New Jersey elections for several decades before the legislature explicitly disenfranchised them in 1807 — a reminder that the founding era's commitments to rights were contingent and reversible.

Section 13.6

Religious Freedom and Disestablishment

The Colonial Religious Landscape

Colonial America had not been a land of religious freedom in the modern sense. Most colonies had established churches — official religions supported by tax revenue and given legal privileges. In New England, the Congregationalist church was established in Massachusetts, Connecticut, and New Hampshire. In the southern colonies, the Anglican Church was the established religion. These establishments meant that all taxpayers, regardless of personal beliefs, contributed to the official church's support, and that dissenting denominations operated under legal disabilities.

The Revolution raised the question of religious establishment directly. If government derived its authority from the consent of the people, on what basis could it compel people to support a religion they did not profess? Revolutionary rhetoric about natural rights logically extended to religion. And practically, the Revolution required the cooperation of colonists from many different religious traditions — Presbyterians, Baptists, Methodists, Quakers, Catholics, Jews — creating political pressure to extend greater religious equality.

Disestablishment in Virginia

The most consequential battle over religious freedom in the founding era took place in Virginia. The struggle came to a head when Patrick Henry proposed a general assessment bill that would have required all Virginians to contribute to the support of some Christian church of their choice. Many considered this a moderate position. James Madison and Thomas Jefferson opposed it vigorously.

In 1785, Madison wrote his famous Memorial and Remonstrance Against Religious Assessments, arguing that the bill violated the natural right of religious freedom and that religion thrived better without government support. Madison circulated the memorial widely, collecting thousands of signatures, and the popular opposition he mobilized defeated Henry's bill. In January 1786, the Virginia legislature adopted Jefferson's Virginia Statute for Religious Freedom — drafted in 1779 but not yet adopted — which declared that no man should be compelled to support any religious worship, that no man should be burdened in body or goods on account of his religious opinions, and that all men should be free to profess their own religious views. Jefferson considered it one of the three greatest achievements of his life, alongside the Declaration of Independence and the founding of the University of Virginia.

Gradual and Uneven Change

Disestablishment was not immediate or uniform. Massachusetts maintained a Congregationalist establishment — technically reformed as a general Christian tax — until 1833, making it the last state to disestablish its official church. In the South, Anglican establishments collapsed quickly after independence because the Anglican Church was associated with Britain and many of its clergy had been Loyalists, and because Baptist and Presbyterian political pressure made establishment impossible to sustain.

The broader pattern was one of gradual, uneven movement toward religious pluralism. By the time of the First Amendment in 1791, complete disestablishment at the federal level had been achieved. The transformation of the religious landscape had profound social implications: the erosion of established churches created space for a competitive religious marketplace that would drive the explosion of evangelical Christianity — particularly Baptist and Methodist — in the early nineteenth century.

The statute reveals both the radical potential of the Enlightenment philosophy underpinning the Revolution and the sharp limits on who that philosophy was actually extended to.

Section 13.7

Slavery, Gradual Emancipation, and Contradiction

The Revolutionary Contradiction

No contradiction in the founding era was more glaring or more consequential than the coexistence of revolutionary rhetoric about natural rights and human liberty with the institution of slavery. The Declaration of Independence declared that all men were created equal, endowed with unalienable rights including life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness. The men who signed it knew — and many were deeply uncomfortable knowing — that this declaration sat in direct tension with their ownership of enslaved human beings.

The contradiction was not invisible to contemporaries. Enslaved people across the colonies heard the rhetoric of liberty and acted on it — running away in greater numbers, joining the British when offered freedom, and petitioning state legislatures for their own freedom. One 1777 petition submitted to the Massachusetts legislature by a group of enslaved men declared that "they have in Common with all other men a Natural and Unalienable Right to that freedom which the Grat Parent of the Unaverse hath Bestowed equalley on all mankinde." British critics of the Revolution pointed to the contradiction gleefully. The English writer Samuel Johnson asked memorably: "How is it that we hear the loudest yelps for liberty among the drivers of negroes?"

The founding generation's responses ranged from genuine moral discomfort to elaborate rationalizations. Thomas Jefferson wrote privately that slavery was a moral and political catastrophe, that it corrupted the enslaver as well as the enslaved, and that he trembled for his country knowing that God was just. He also owned over 600 enslaved people over his lifetime, freed only a handful, and crafted elaborate justifications for his failure to act on his stated convictions. Others — particularly in the Deep South — were moving by the 1780s toward an aggressive defense of slavery as a positive good, not merely a necessary evil.

Gradual Emancipation in the North

The North was not free of slavery in 1776 — enslaved people lived in every colony — but slavery was economically marginal in New England and the Middle Colonies compared to the South, making it easier for northern states to move toward emancipation. Pennsylvania led the way in 1780 with the Gradual Abolition Act — the first legislative abolition of slavery in American history. The law did not free any existing enslaved person. Instead, children born to enslaved mothers after March 1, 1780, would be free when they reached the age of 28. In the meantime, they remained bound as indentured servants. This meant slavery in Pennsylvania persisted for decades, but it was a beginning.

Massachusetts took a different path. A series of court cases in 1783 — most famously Quock Walker v. Jennison — effectively held that the state's declaration that "all men are born free and equal" had abolished slavery. Enslaved people who sued for freedom in Massachusetts courts won their cases, and within a few years slavery had effectively ceased to exist in that state. Rhode Island and Connecticut passed gradual emancipation laws in 1784. New York passed one in 1799, New Jersey in 1804.

Gradual emancipation was painfully slow by modern standards. It freed no one immediately and consigned the children of enslaved women to years of servitude. It was less a moral triumph than a grudging acknowledgment that slavery was inconsistent with revolutionary principles — and even that acknowledgment was made while carefully protecting the economic interests of enslavers.

The Strengthening of Slavery in the South

While the North moved gradually toward emancipation, the South moved in the opposite direction. The tobacco economy of the Chesapeake was struggling after the Revolution — soil exhaustion, market disruptions, and competition had made tobacco less profitable. Some Chesapeake planters began to manumit their enslaved workers, and Virginia passed a manumission law in 1782 that made it easier for individual enslavers to free their workers. The number of free Black people in Virginia grew substantially in the 1780s and 1790s.

But this movement toward manumission in the Chesapeake did not represent a fundamental challenge to slavery. It was driven by economic calculation, not moral commitment, and it was accompanied by increasing racial restrictions on free Black people — a pattern making clear that white Virginians distinguished sharply between the end of slavery and the extension of full citizenship to Black people. In the Deep South — South Carolina and Georgia — there was no comparable movement. South Carolina actually reopened the international slave trade after the Revolution, importing additional enslaved Africans to replace those lost during the war. The Constitutional Convention of 1787 reflected these regional differences in compromises that protected slavery from federal interference for decades to come.

Historical Vocabulary
Gradual Emancipation

Gradual emancipation was the process adopted by northern states in the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries to end slavery incrementally. Under these laws, existing enslaved people typically remained in bondage for the rest of their lives. Children born to enslaved mothers after a specified date were declared free, but were required to serve the enslaver of their mother as bound laborers — sometimes called "apprentices" — until adulthood.

The gradualism reflected several considerations. Northern enslavers had economic interests and political influence sufficient to prevent immediate emancipation. Legislators accepted gradual emancipation as a practical compromise, and the gradual approach reflected a widespread belief among white northerners that formerly enslaved people needed time to be "prepared" for freedom.

By modern standards it was painfully inadequate — leaving existing enslaved people in bondage for their entire lives. Its inadequacy helps explain why the abolitionist movement of the nineteenth century, impatient with half-measures, demanded immediate emancipation instead.

Section 13.8

Native Nations and the New Republic

Abandoned by Britain

Among the most devastating consequences of the Treaty of Paris for any group were those visited upon Native nations that had allied with Britain. The Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) Confederacy's experience was representative. The confederacy had been deeply divided by the Revolution: the Mohawk, Seneca, Onondaga, and Cayuga nations allied with Britain, while the Oneida and Tuscarora — partly influenced by missionary ties — sided with the Americans. Haudenosaunee warriors fought each other for the first time in the confederacy's history, an unprecedented rupture of the Great League of Peace.

The Mohawk leader Joseph Brant had led allied Mohawk and British forces in devastating raids on frontier settlements in New York and Pennsylvania throughout the war. In 1779, Washington responded with a massive punitive expedition under generals Sullivan and Clinton that systematically destroyed Haudenosaunee villages, orchards, and food stores across upstate New York. Thousands of Haudenosaunee people — including women, children, and the elderly — were left homeless and starving as winter approached.

The peace negotiations in Paris included no Native representatives. The boundary settlements treated Indigenous territories as prizes to be distributed by European powers without consultation or consent. When Mohawk and other Iroquois leaders learned that Britain had ceded their homelands in the treaty, their sense of betrayal was profound. The power they had fought to support had abandoned them without a word.

The American Approach

The new United States government approached its relationship with Native nations from a position of barely concealed aggression. American leaders understood that the nation lacked the military and financial resources to conquer the western territories immediately — the army was tiny, the treasury was empty. But they regarded the west as their by right of the peace treaty, and they were determined to take it.

A series of post-Revolution treaties in the mid-1780s attempted to formalize American control over lands in the Ohio Valley and Southeast. American negotiators frequently dealt with leaders who lacked authority to speak for their entire nation, created divisions within Native communities, applied economic and political pressure, and then presented the resulting documents as definitive settlements. These treaties were rejected by large segments of the Native nations involved as unauthorized and invalid. The result was not peace but accelerating conflict. The Northwest Territory remained a war zone throughout the late 1780s and into the 1790s, as Native nations organized military resistance. The resistance was ultimately crushed at the Battle of Fallen Timbers in 1794, but in the founding era, the outcome was far from certain.

The Northwest Ordinance

The most important legal framework the Confederation Congress created for the western territories was the Northwest Ordinance of 1787. This law organized the territory north of the Ohio River — present-day Ohio, Indiana, Illinois, Michigan, and Wisconsin — into a structure that would eventually produce new states equal in status to the original thirteen. It included a prohibition on slavery in the territory and language promising fair treatment to Native nations: "the utmost good faith shall always be observed towards the Indians" and their lands would "never be taken from them without their consent."

These promises were almost immediately betrayed. The United States government systematically violated them over the following decades through land grabs, fraudulent treaties, military conquest, and forced removal. The Northwest Ordinance's promise of good faith became one of the republic's most broken pledges — a pattern that would define the relationship between the United States and Indigenous nations for more than a century.

Section 13.9

Building a Republic After Revolution

The Challenge of Governance

Independence had been won. Now came the harder challenge: governing. The Articles of Confederation, which formally took effect in 1781, created a national government that deliberately minimized central power. Congress could not levy taxes, could only request funds from the states, and could not enforce its own laws. There was no national executive and no national court system. The Confederation's weaknesses became increasingly apparent in the postwar years. Without taxing power, Congress could not pay the national debt. Without enforcement power, Congress could not compel states to honor treaty provisions. Without a unified commercial policy, states competed against each other in ways that created economic chaos.

The states were governing themselves through their new constitutions, but with mixed results. State legislatures were responsive to popular pressure — sometimes to a degree that alarmed property owners and creditors who feared that majority rule would be used to attack contracts, redistribute property, and default on debts. Debtor relief laws were passed in several states. Rhode Island issued paper currency in such large quantities that creditors fled the state rather than be paid in nearly worthless paper. The experience reinforced fears that unchecked democratic legislatures were no more trustworthy than unchecked monarchs. Shays' Rebellion in 1786–87 was the most alarming symptom of a system in distress, and it accelerated the movement toward a stronger national government that produced the Constitutional Convention of 1787.

Political Culture and Participation

Despite the tensions and difficulties of the 1780s, the postwar years also saw genuine development of democratic political culture. Newspapers proliferated, political pamphlets circulated widely, and ordinary citizens engaged in political debate to a degree unprecedented in colonial society. Voluntary associations — political clubs, civic organizations, Masonic lodges, benevolent societies — spread through American towns and cities, creating networks of civic engagement.

The expansion of political participation among white men was real, even if it had sharp limits. Men who had served in the Revolution developed a sense of political entitlement — they had fought for the republic and expected to have a voice in it. The language of natural rights and popular sovereignty that the Revolution had deployed against Britain could not easily be withdrawn once independence was won. This culture of political argument — nurtured through the press, voluntary associations, and public debate — was one of the Revolution's most important and durable legacies.

Women and the Republic

The Revolution raised questions about women's place in the republic that it largely answered in ways that maintained their exclusion from formal political life. The concept of "republican motherhood" emerged in the postwar years: the idea that women's primary contribution to republican society was raising virtuous, civically engaged sons. This justified expanding women's education while simultaneously reinforcing their confinement to the domestic sphere by defining their public role entirely in relation to their children and husbands.

Some women pushed further. Abigail Adams, in her famous 1776 correspondence with her husband John, had urged him to "remember the ladies" and warned that women would "not hold ourselves bound by any laws in which we have no voice or representation." But her challenge remained private — a letter, not a political movement. Women's legal status under coverture — the common law doctrine that a married woman's legal identity was subsumed into her husband's — remained largely unchanged. The liberating logic of natural rights that the founders applied to men's relationship with government was not extended to women's relationship with their husbands or with the law.

Section 13.10

Chapter Review and Revolutionary Outcomes Checkpoint

This chapter has traced the complex consequences of the American Revolution — the treaty that formally ended the war, the costs of that war on soldiers and civilians, the fate of Loyalists who had opposed independence, the ideals and limits of republican government, the establishment of new constitutional frameworks in the states, the contested question of religious freedom, the profound contradiction of slavery in a republic founded on liberty, the devastating impact of independence on Native nations, and the challenges of building stable self-government from the ruins of colonial rule.

The central argument of the chapter is that the Revolution's end was not a resolution but a beginning — the opening of a new era of contested politics, social conflict, and unfinished business. The Treaty of Paris was a diplomatic achievement that left enormous questions unanswered. The state constitutions were experiments in republican government that revealed both the possibilities and the limits of the founding generation's vision. The contradictions of the founding era — over slavery, Native sovereignty, and the exclusion of women — established fault lines that would run through American history for generations.

Historical Debate

Was the American Revolution a Social Revolution?

The question: Historians have long debated whether the American Revolution was merely a political revolution — a transfer of authority from British imperial rule to American self-government — or a deeper social revolution that transformed the internal structure of American society. The question shapes how we understand the Revolution's achievements, its limits, and its long-term consequences.

Interpretation A — A Conservative Revolution: Some historians argue the Revolution was fundamentally conservative — primarily seeking to preserve existing liberties rather than transforming social structures. Its leaders were mostly existing colonial elites — planters, lawyers, merchants — who had no interest in overturning the social order that had made them powerful. The Revolution replaced British authority with American authority but left most social structures — slavery, property-based voting, gender hierarchies — largely intact. Evidence: the persistence and strengthening of slavery; the maintenance of property qualifications for voting; the continued exclusion of women from formal political life; the stability of wealth distribution before and after the Revolution.
Interpretation B — A Radical Social Transformation: Other historians argue the Revolution was profoundly radical in its long-term social consequences. The logic of natural rights and equality it unleashed could not be permanently contained. It gradually democratized American society, challenged hierarchy and deference, expanded the franchise for white men, created space for the antislavery movement, and eventually — through the long struggle of reform movements — forced the extension of rights to groups initially excluded. Evidence: the rapid collapse of deference to traditional social authority; the rise of democratic political culture; the growing antislavery movement; gradual emancipation in the North; the long-term democratizing trajectory of revolutionary ideology.

Why it matters: How historians interpret the Revolution shapes how they understand American democracy — whether the nation's founding committed it to an ever-expanding circle of rights, or whether it established a limited political order that later generations had to transform through new struggles.

Multiple Perspectives

Independence and Its Consequences for Different Groups

Patriot Elites: Men like Washington, Adams, and Madison had achieved the primary goal of their political lives. They faced enormous governing challenges — economic disruption, political instability, the difficulty of uniting a diverse nation — but faced them as leaders with legitimate authority. For them, the postwar years were a mixture of triumphant achievement and frustrating difficulty.
Ordinary Veterans: Soldiers who had served in the Continental Army returned home to find the republic often indifferent to their sacrifices. Promised pay and land bounties rarely materialized. Many veterans felt betrayed by the governments that had sent them to fight. Yet they carried a new sense of political entitlement — they had earned the right to a voice. This mixture of grievance and democratic expectation made veterans a politically significant and sometimes volatile force in the 1780s.
Loyalists: For tens of thousands who had remained loyal to Britain, the end of the war was a catastrophe. Their property was confiscated, their civil rights stripped, their social standing destroyed. Many faced a choice between public humiliation and exile. The Loyalist experience was the dark underside of the Revolution's victory — a reminder that democratic movements can be ruthlessly intolerant of political minorities.
Enslaved People: The Revolution's end brought different outcomes depending on location and circumstances. For those who had escaped to British lines and earned freedom, it meant emigration as Black Loyalists to Nova Scotia or the Caribbean. For the vast majority who remained enslaved in the South, independence changed little in daily life. In the North, gradual emancipation laws offered eventual freedom, but not immediately and not without years of continued servitude. For Black Americans as a group, the Revolution was an era of broken promises — its rhetoric of liberty never translating into freedom for those who needed it most.
Native Nations: For Indigenous peoples whose territories had been claimed in the Treaty of Paris, the end of the Revolution was the beginning of a more dangerous era. They had not been parties to the negotiations, had not consented to the territorial settlements, and now faced a land-hungry republic that regarded their homelands as prizes won by treaty. The founding era brought escalating conflict, further land loss, and the beginning of a long dispossession.
Women: The Revolution left women's formal status largely unchanged. State constitutions did not extend political rights to women; coverture continued to define married women's legal status; republican motherhood honored women's social importance while confining their civic role to the household. Individual women had expanded their capacities during the war, but these expansions were largely rolled back in peacetime. The revolution that had challenged the authority of kings did not challenge the authority of husbands.
Constitutional Connection

State Constitutions and the Foundations of American Constitutionalism

The state constitutions of the Revolutionary era were not merely temporary arrangements. They were the first sustained experiments in written constitutional government in the modern world, and they established principles that shaped the federal Constitution of 1787 and American constitutionalism for centuries.

Several principles pioneered in state constitutions became fundamental to American constitutional tradition. The idea that a constitution must be ratified by the people themselves — not merely adopted by a legislature — was established by the Massachusetts Constitution of 1780. This principle distinguished constitutions as higher law, above ordinary legislation, deriving authority directly from the sovereign people. The enumeration of individual rights in constitutional declarations — pioneered by Virginia in 1776 — established that certain liberties must be explicitly stated and protected from legislative interference. When Anti-Federalists objected to the federal Constitution of 1787 on the grounds that it lacked a bill of rights, they were drawing on the precedent set by state documents. The federal Bill of Rights, adopted in 1791, was in many ways a compilation of rights already established in state declarations.

The principle of separation of powers — dividing government into legislative, executive, and judicial branches with distinct functions — was also developed and tested in state constitutions before being applied at the national level. These experiments were imperfect: they excluded women, restricted the franchise to propertied white men, and failed to address slavery adequately. But they established the basic framework of American constitutionalism that would shape the federal Constitution and every constitutional debate that followed.

Cause and Effect

The Loyalist Crisis

Long-Term Causes

Genuine diversity of political opinion in colonial America; many colonists had deep attachments to the Crown, the Church of England, and British political traditions reflecting decades of cultural connection, economic ties, and family relationships with Britain.

Short-Term Causes

Escalating conflict after 1763 forced colonists to choose sides. Committees of safety, loyalty oaths, boycotts, and eventually armed conflict made neutrality impossible. Those who could not accept independence were defined as enemies of the Revolution.

The Event or Transformation

At independence, tens of thousands of Americans found their political identity defined as traitorous. States confiscated their property, stripped them of civil rights, and subjected them to persecution. Between 60,000 and 80,000 people left the United States permanently.

Effects

Massive redistribution of property; permanent disruption of families and economic networks; foundation of new Loyalist communities in Canada; lasting bitterness between the U.S. and Britain; troubling precedents about democratic tolerance of political minorities.

Historical complexity: The Loyalist crisis cannot be reduced to a simple story of Patriots triumphing over traitors. Many Loyalists held their views sincerely and suffered unjustly. Many Patriots' actions were opportunistic — a chance to seize property — as much as principled. The experience raises enduring questions about whether democratic communities can truly protect the rights of political minorities in times of crisis.

Continuity and Change Over Time

The Status of Enslaved People Before and After the Revolution

What Changed

  • Pennsylvania passed the Gradual Abolition Act (1780), the first legislative abolition of slavery in American history
  • Massachusetts courts effectively ended slavery in that state (1783)
  • Rhode Island and Connecticut passed gradual emancipation laws (1784)
  • The number of free Black people in the United States grew; free Black communities began building institutions in northern cities
  • The revolutionary rhetoric of natural rights gave new arguments and new legitimacy to antislavery activism
  • Virginia's manumission law (1782) made it easier for individual enslavers to free their workers

What Stayed the Same

  • For the roughly 90 percent of enslaved people living in the South, the Revolution changed nothing about their daily condition
  • South Carolina reopened the international slave trade after the Revolution
  • The Constitutional Convention of 1787 protected slavery from federal interference for at least two decades
  • Even in the North, free Black people faced systematic discrimination in employment, housing, education, and voting
  • The racial ideology underlying slavery was not challenged by the Revolution and was in some respects reinforced as a rationalization for the contradiction between liberty and bondage

Why this pattern matters: The pattern of change in the North and continuity in the South established a sectional divergence in slavery that would grow over the following decades. By 1800, the United States was already dividing into a North where slavery was gradually disappearing and a South where it was deeply entrenched and economically expanding — the fault lines that would eventually produce the Civil War.

Everyday Life and Social History

Veterans, Families, and the Postwar Economy

For the men who had served in the Continental Army, returning home was often a deeply ambivalent experience. They came back changed — by combat, by disease, by years away from home — to communities and families that had changed in their absence. Many returned with injuries that eighteenth-century medicine had no framework for understanding or treating. They had been promised pay, land bounties, and pensions; in practice, these promises were often unrealized. Continental Army certificates they had accepted in lieu of pay had depreciated sharply or been bought at steep discounts by speculators. Veterans felt, with considerable justification, that the republic they had fought for had abandoned them.

Women who had managed households, farms, and businesses during the war faced a complex transition when their husbands returned. For the years of conflict, many had necessarily made decisions — managing debts, directing labor, negotiating with merchants — that were legally their husbands' prerogative. The return of husbands meant the return of legal authority, and many women who had developed genuine competence and confidence during the war found themselves legally subordinated once again. At the same time, the patriotic rhetoric of the Revolution had reached women as well as men, creating a tension the concept of republican motherhood attempted to resolve by acknowledging women's importance while confining their formal civic role to the household.

For free Black people in northern cities, the postwar years brought both new possibilities and new restrictions. The growing free Black population — swelled by gradual emancipation laws — began building institutions in cities like Philadelphia, Boston, New York, and Providence. Absalom Jones and Richard Allen founded the Free African Society in Philadelphia in 1787, one of the first Black civic institutions in the United States and the seedbed from which the African Methodist Episcopal Church would eventually grow. These community-building efforts took place under conditions of persistent racial discrimination in employment, housing, and social life. Freedom was always conditional and precarious, but the foundations of Black civic life were being laid.

Chapter Summary

Chapter 13 has examined the consequences of American independence in the years from 1783 to approximately 1790 — a period of intense struggle to translate the ideals of the Revolution into the reality of republican governance. The Treaty of Paris of 1783, while a genuine diplomatic achievement, revealed fundamental tensions: between the territorial claims of the United States and the sovereignty of Native nations; between the promise of favorable treatment for Loyalists and the actual behavior of American states; between the vast domain claimed on paper and the limited power of the new government to control it in reality.

The costs of the Revolutionary War were enormous. Tens of thousands of Americans had died — most from disease rather than combat — and hundreds of thousands more had experienced disruption, displacement, economic hardship, and violence. Veterans returned home to find governments unable or unwilling to honor wartime promises. Women who had managed households during years of war returned to legal subordination. Communities ravaged by British occupation or partisan conflict struggled to rebuild. The postwar economic depression made the immediate postwar years a time of genuine hardship for many Americans, culminating in Shays' Rebellion, which alarmed political leaders across the country and accelerated the push toward constitutional reform.

The fate of Loyalists — between 60,000 and 80,000 Americans driven into permanent exile — revealed the underside of the Revolution's democratic triumph. States confiscated Loyalist property, stripped them of civil rights, and subjected them to persecution. The Loyalist exodus represented one of the largest forced migrations in North American history and established troubling precedents about the limits of democratic tolerance. The Black Loyalists who emigrated to Nova Scotia — formerly enslaved people who had earned freedom through service to Britain — carried with them the particular bitterness of people who had sought freedom only to find it constrained by racism in their new home as well.

The state constitutions of the revolutionary era were genuine achievements in democratic governance. They pioneered written fundamental law, popular ratification by specially elected conventions, explicit enumeration of individual rights, and the separation of powers. These innovations became the foundation of American constitutionalism and directly shaped the federal Constitution of 1787. Yet these constitutions also restricted the franchise to propertied white men, excluded women from formal political life, and failed to address slavery except in a handful of northern states.

The debates over religious freedom produced one of the Revolution's most consequential policy innovations. The Virginia Statute for Religious Freedom, adopted in 1786 after the defeat of Patrick Henry's general assessment bill, established the principle that government had no business compelling citizens to support any religion or abridging their religious beliefs. This principle became the foundation of the First Amendment's religion clauses and shaped the development of a pluralistic religious marketplace in the United States.

The question of slavery exposed the deepest contradiction of the founding era. Northern states moved toward gradual emancipation in the postwar years, creating the first legislative and judicial challenges to slavery in American history. But gradual emancipation was painfully slow, and in the South, slavery was being strengthened and entrenched. For enslaved people across the country, the Revolution's rhetoric of liberty was a promise that the founding generation largely refused to honor. The contradictions of the founding era in this respect would require a Civil War to begin to resolve.

For Native nations, independence was a catastrophe. The Treaty of Paris treated their territories as prizes to be distributed by European powers without consultation or consent. The Northwest Ordinance of 1787 promised good faith toward Native peoples and then almost immediately broke that promise. The founding era established a pattern of treaty-making, treaty-breaking, and forced dispossession that would define the relationship between the United States and Indigenous nations for more than a century.

The republic that emerged from the revolutionary era was a genuinely novel political creation — the first modern republic at scale, built on popular sovereignty and written constitutional law. It was also a republic with profound contradictions built into its foundations: slavery, the dispossession of Native peoples, the exclusion of women, and the restriction of full citizenship to property-owning white men. These contradictions were not accidental or peripheral. They were structural — embedded in the founding documents, the constitutional compromises, and the social assumptions of the founding generation. They would require further revolutions — the Civil War and Reconstruction, the suffrage movement, the civil rights movement — to begin to address.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Treaty of Paris (1783)
The formal peace agreement ending the Revolutionary War between the United States and Great Britain. Recognized American independence and established borders from the Atlantic to the Mississippi River, though Loyalist and debt provisions were widely violated.
Loyalists
Americans who remained loyal to the British Crown during the Revolution — estimated at 15–20 percent of the white colonial population. After independence, they faced property confiscation, loss of civil rights, social persecution, and, for many, permanent exile.
Black Loyalists
Formerly enslaved people who escaped to British lines during the Revolution after Britain offered freedom in exchange for service. Approximately 15,000–20,000 were evacuated from American ports at war's end, most going to Nova Scotia.
Confiscation acts
State laws authorizing the seizure of Loyalist property during and after the Revolution. Proceeds were often used to fund state governments or redistributed to Patriots. States largely ignored the Treaty of Paris's call to restore confiscated property.
Republicanism
The political philosophy central to the founding era, emphasizing government by elected representatives, popular sovereignty, individual rights, opposition to monarchy and aristocracy, and the importance of civic virtue for the survival of self-government.
Civic virtue
The conviction that citizens of a republic must prioritize the public good over personal ambition and private interest. Considered a functional requirement of self-government — a republic was thought to be fragile without a citizenry committed to the common good.
State constitutions
Written foundational documents drafted by the states during and after the Revolution, defining the structure and limits of government, establishing rights, and distributing power among branches of government. The first sustained experiments in written constitutional governance in the modern world.
Gradual emancipation
The process adopted by northern states of ending slavery incrementally by freeing children born to enslaved mothers after a certain date while leaving existing enslaved people in bondage. Pennsylvania (1780), Rhode Island and Connecticut (1784), New York (1799), and New Jersey (1804) all adopted such laws.
Virginia Statute for Religious Freedom (1786)
Law drafted by Jefferson, adopted by Virginia, prohibiting government from compelling citizens to support any religion or abridging their religious beliefs. Foundational document for the separation of church and state and an ancestor of the First Amendment's religion clauses.
Disestablishment
The formal separation of church and state — ending government support for official religions. The Revolutionary era saw rapid disestablishment in most states, though Massachusetts maintained a form of religious establishment until 1833.
Republican motherhood
Postrevolutionary ideology honoring women's importance as educators of the next generation of republican citizens while confining their formal civic role to the household. Used to justify women's education while limiting their formal political participation.
Coverture
The English common law doctrine, inherited by American states, that a married woman's legal identity was subsumed into her husband's. Married women under coverture could not own property, sign contracts, bring lawsuits, or control their own wages.
Northwest Ordinance (1787)
Law passed by the Continental Congress organizing the territory north of the Ohio River. It established a framework for eventual statehood, prohibited slavery in the territory, and promised good faith toward Native nations — promises almost immediately violated.
Indigenous sovereignty
The inherent right of Native nations to govern themselves and control their own territories. The Treaty of Paris's territorial settlements made no acknowledgment of Indigenous sovereignty, treating Native lands as prizes to be distributed by European powers.
Shays' Rebellion (1786–1787)
Armed uprising of western Massachusetts farmers, many of them Continental Army veterans, against debt judgments and property seizures. Suppressed by state militia, but it alarmed political leaders across the country and accelerated the push for a stronger national government.
Articles of Confederation
The first constitution of the United States, ratified in 1781, creating a national government with very limited powers — no taxing authority, no executive, no national judiciary. Its weaknesses drove the movement for constitutional reform that produced the federal Constitution of 1787.
Continental currency
Paper money issued by the Continental Congress to finance the Revolutionary War. By the war's end, it had depreciated so severely through inflation that "not worth a Continental" became a common idiom for worthlessness.
Manumission
The formal act by which an enslaver freed an enslaved person. Virginia's manumission law of 1782 made it easier for individual enslavers to free their workers; the number of free Black Virginians grew substantially as a result.
Franchise
The right to vote. In the founding era, typically restricted to white men who owned property above a minimum value. New Jersey briefly extended the vote to women and free Black people meeting property qualifications before disenfranchising them in 1807.
Popular sovereignty
The principle that the authority of government derives from the consent of the governed. A central premise of republican thought and of the Declaration of Independence; used to justify both revolution against Britain and the creation of new state and national governments.

Major People and Places

Identification & Significance

Benjamin Franklin (1706–1790)
Pennsylvania statesman, printer, scientist, and Founding Father. Chief American peace commissioner at Paris. Franklin's diplomatic skill and European reputation were crucial to securing favorable terms in the Treaty of Paris.
John Adams (1735–1826)
Massachusetts lawyer and diplomat. One of the three American peace commissioners. Adams drafted much of the Massachusetts Constitution of 1780, including its influential declaration of rights and the principle of popular ratification.
John Jay (1745–1829)
New York lawyer and diplomat. Third American peace commissioner. Jay's decision to negotiate directly with Britain contributed to the favorable peace terms. Later became the first Chief Justice of the United States Supreme Court.
George Washington (1732–1799)
Commander of the Continental Army and first President of the United States. His voluntary resignation from command in 1783 was a defining act of republican civic virtue, establishing a crucial norm of civilian supremacy over the military.
Thomas Jefferson (1743–1826)
Virginia planter, lawyer, and statesman. Author of the Declaration of Independence and drafter of the Virginia Statute for Religious Freedom. A central figure in founding-era debates who owned hundreds of enslaved people while writing eloquently against slavery.
James Madison (1751–1836)
Virginia statesman. Primary organizer of the campaign that defeated Patrick Henry's general assessment bill; author of the "Memorial and Remonstrance Against Religious Assessments." Later principal architect of the federal Constitution.
George Mason (1725–1792)
Virginia planter and statesman. Principal drafter of the Virginia Declaration of Rights (1776), the model for state rights declarations and the ancestor of the federal Bill of Rights.
Patrick Henry (1736–1799)
Virginia lawyer and orator; fierce defender of states' rights who proposed a general assessment for Christian churches. Organized influential opposition to the federal Constitution as too centralizing.
Joseph Brant / Thayendanegea (c. 1743–1807)
Mohawk military leader and diplomat who allied with Britain during the Revolution. After the war, worked to build an Indigenous confederacy to resist American expansion and negotiated land for Mohawk refugees in Upper Canada.
Daniel Shays (c. 1747–1825)
Massachusetts farmer and Continental Army veteran who led the armed uprising against debt courts in western Massachusetts in 1786–87 that bears his name. The rebellion became a symbol of the Confederation government's inadequacy.
Absalom Jones (1746–1818) and Richard Allen (1760–1831)
African American leaders in Philadelphia who founded the Free African Society in 1787. Jones became the first Black Episcopal priest in the United States; Allen founded the African Methodist Episcopal Church.
Free African Society (1787)
Mutual aid organization founded in Philadelphia providing support to free Black Philadelphians. One of the first African American civic institutions in the United States and the seedbed from which the AME Church would eventually grow.
Valley Forge, Pennsylvania
Site of the Continental Army's winter encampment of 1777–78. Symbol of the war's human suffering — approximately 2,000 soldiers died there, mostly from disease.
Nova Scotia
British colony (present-day Canada) that received roughly 30,000 Loyalist refugees after independence, transforming the colony so dramatically that a new province — New Brunswick — had to be created to accommodate them.
The Northwest Territory
Territory north of the Ohio River organized by the Northwest Ordinance of 1787 into a framework for eventual statehood. It included a slavery prohibition but remained a war zone as Native nations resisted American expansion throughout the 1780s and 1790s.

Chapter Reflection

Chapter 13 has laid bare the distance between the ideals the Revolution proclaimed and the realities it created. The founding of the United States was not a finished achievement but an ongoing struggle — one that generated remarkable political creativity alongside persistent injustice, that articulated universal principles of liberty and equality while building a republic on foundations of slavery, dispossession, and exclusion. The Treaty of Paris was a diplomatic triumph that left the hardest questions unanswered. The state constitutions were genuine innovations in democratic governance that nonetheless excluded the majority of the people who lived within their borders from full civic membership.

The founding era matters to U.S. history because it established the parameters within which subsequent struggles would be fought. The language of natural rights and popular sovereignty that the Revolution proclaimed — however incompletely honored in practice — gave future generations of Americans the rhetorical tools they needed to demand fuller inclusion. Abolitionists of the nineteenth century would point to the Declaration of Independence to argue against slavery. Suffragists would invoke the same document to argue for women's voting rights. Civil rights leaders of the twentieth century would appeal to the Constitution's promises of equal protection to challenge segregation and discrimination. The founding era's words proved more powerful than the founders themselves had intended or wanted.

The contradictions of the founding era also established the fault lines along which American history would fracture. The protection of slavery in the constitutional compromises of 1787 — built on the dynamics analyzed in this chapter — would eventually drive the nation toward a Civil War that cost more lives than any other conflict in American history. The dispossession of Native nations — begun aggressively in the years immediately after the Treaty of Paris — would continue for more than a century. The exclusion of women from formal political life would require the struggle of the suffrage movement and decades of feminist organizing to begin to overcome.

Understanding the founding era honestly — with its achievements and its failures, its genuine idealism and its structural injustices — is essential for understanding the United States as it has actually existed. The republic was not born perfect and then corrupted; it was born with contradictions built into its foundations, contradictions that subsequent generations have struggled, with varying success, to resolve. That ongoing struggle — to make the republic's promises more real, to extend its benefits more broadly, to close the gap between its stated ideals and its actual practices — is the central story of American history. Chapter 13 has shown where that story began. Chapter 14 will examine the next phase of that struggle: the attempt to build a stronger national government capable of addressing the republic's deepest weaknesses.

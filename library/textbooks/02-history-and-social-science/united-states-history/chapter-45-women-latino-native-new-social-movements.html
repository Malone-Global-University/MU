Women's Rights, Latino/a Activism, Native Activism, and New Social Movements

United States History: Indigenous America, Republic, Conflict, and Change

Chapter Opening

"A generation of young Americans who were not waiting for others to grant them recognition was taking to the streets, organizing communities, and demanding structural change."

In the summer of 1968, a group of Mexican American students walked out of East Los Angeles high schools to protest the unequal conditions of their education. They called it a "blowout." Their classrooms were overcrowded, their teachers often spoke no Spanish, their curriculum ignored their history, and the dropout rate at schools like Garfield High exceeded sixty percent. The students who walked out demanded bilingual education, more Latino teachers, and an end to punishment for speaking Spanish on school grounds. They were joined by parents, community activists, and members of the Brown Berets, a young Chicano organization modeled in spirit on the Black Panther Party. Los Angeles police arrested thirteen organizers on charges of conspiracy. The charges were eventually dismissed, but the walkouts had already signaled something important: a generation of young Americans who were not waiting for others to grant them recognition was taking to the streets, organizing communities, and demanding structural change.

The East L.A. blowouts were one moment in a vast, interconnected era of social movements that transformed the United States during the 1960s and 1970s. These movements drew inspiration from the Black freedom struggle examined in Chapter 43 and the antiwar protest culture examined in Chapter 44, but they also developed from their own distinct histories, grievances, and visions. Women's liberation challenged the assumption that gender was a natural limit on participation in public life. Chicano and Chicana activists built a movement that connected farmworker labor rights to community self-determination, education, and cultural pride. Puerto Rican activists in Chicago, New York, and other cities demanded recognition of colonial history and urban dignity. Native American activists confronted a federal policy history stretching back two centuries and insisted that Indigenous sovereignty was not a relic but a living reality. Asian American activists challenged the model minority myth and organized against discrimination. Gay and lesbian Americans, after generations of criminalization and social invisibility, began building a rights movement from the rubble of a police raid on a Manhattan bar. People with disabilities demanded that public life be made accessible to all. Environmentalists organized to demand that the natural world be protected against industrial destruction.

These were not separate stories happening in parallel. They were overlapping, intersecting, mutually influencing currents in American life. Activists in one movement often participated in others. Organizations borrowed tactics, rhetoric, and organizational structures from one another. Women within the civil rights movement and the antiwar movement who grew frustrated with their secondary roles helped spark the women's liberation movement. Chicana feminists challenged both Anglo feminist organizations that ignored race and Chicano nationalist organizations that marginalized women. Native American activists at Alcatraz Island in 1969 were watched closely by Chicano, Asian American, and Puerto Rican organizers who saw in the occupation a model for asserting rights through visible action.

These movements also shared a common historical context. The postwar liberal order had promised economic growth and social stability, but that promise rested on an unequal foundation. Women were expected to manage homes and children and were systematically excluded from equal pay, professional advancement, and legal personhood in credit, property, and reproductive decisions. Mexican Americans, Puerto Ricans, Native Americans, Asian Americans, and other communities faced discrimination in housing, employment, education, and law. Gay and lesbian Americans faced criminal prosecution, psychiatric pathologization, and police harassment. People with disabilities were institutionalized, excluded from schools, and ignored by public architecture. The social movements of this era arose because the gap between the nation's professed ideals and its actual practices had become intolerable to those who bore the weight of that gap.

The movements examined in this chapter did not end in complete victory. Some achieved landmark legislation. Some won court victories. Many transformed cultural life in lasting ways. But they also faced backlash, internal conflict, government surveillance, and the limits of an American political system that responded slowly and unevenly to demands from below. Understanding these movements — their origins, their tactics, their achievements, and their unfinished legacies — is essential for understanding the United States that emerged from the 1960s and 1970s and the debates over rights, recognition, inclusion, and power that continue today.

This chapter examines each major movement with attention to origins, key actors, major events and organizations, tactical choices, internal debates, legal and political achievements, cultural significance, and historical legacy. It also asks students to think about what these movements had in common, how they influenced one another, and what they reveal about the nature of social change in a democratic society.

Historical Context

To understand the social movements examined in this chapter, students must understand the world that produced them. Several long-term and short-term conditions shaped the activism of the 1960s and 1970s.

The postwar racial order. Reconstruction's failure had left African Americans in the South locked under Jim Crow, and the migration of millions of Black Americans to northern and western cities had produced new forms of urban segregation, employment discrimination, and political exclusion. Mexican Americans, Puerto Ricans, and Asian Americans faced comparable structures of discrimination, shaped by different histories but producing similar patterns of inequality. Native Americans had survived the allotment era, boarding schools, the Dust Bowl, and relocation programs that pushed them off reservations and into cities, yet found themselves largely invisible in national political life.

The Cold War and civil rights. As Chapter 43 established, the Black freedom struggle of the 1950s and 1960s had already demonstrated that nonviolent direct action could expose the contradictions of American democracy and force legislative change. The Civil Rights Act of 1964 and the Voting Rights Act of 1965 were landmark achievements. But for many activists, legal rights on paper did not automatically translate into economic justice, personal safety, or real political power. The limits of legislative gains pushed many activists in new directions.

The women's labor force and legal exclusion. By 1960, approximately forty percent of American women worked outside the home, but they were systematically excluded from many professions, paid less than men for comparable work, denied credit in their own names, subject to pregnancy discrimination, and protected by almost no laws addressing sexual harassment or domestic violence. The contradiction between women's growing economic participation and their formal legal subordination created conditions for organized protest.

The university as organizing space. Postwar higher education expansion brought millions of students, including many from communities that had never had significant access to college, into universities. Campus life created opportunities for organizing, networking, and exposure to political ideas. The student movements that developed — against the Vietnam War, for civil rights, for curriculum change — produced many of the activists and organizational frameworks of broader social movements.

The radicalization of 1968 and beyond. The assassinations of Martin Luther King Jr. and Robert Kennedy, urban uprisings following King's death, the police riot at the Democratic National Convention in Chicago, and the escalating Vietnam War produced a widespread sense among many activists that incremental reform was insufficient. The radicalization of this period was not uniform, but it set the context for the more confrontational tactics and structural critiques that characterized much activism in the late 1960s and 1970s.

Government surveillance and repression. The FBI's COINTELPRO program, which targeted civil rights organizations and eventually a wide range of left-wing and minority activist groups, shaped the context in which social movements operated. Infiltration, disinformation, harassment, and in some cases collaboration in the killing of activists created a climate of fear and internal suspicion that weakened many organizations. Understanding repression is essential to understanding why some movements developed as they did.

Section 45.1

The Era of New Social Movements

The years between the mid-1960s and the late 1970s witnessed an explosion of organized political activism that touched nearly every sector of American society. Historians have debated how to characterize this era. Some emphasize its continuity with earlier cycles of reform — the Progressive Era, the labor movement of the 1930s, the civil rights movement — arguing that American history has always been marked by recurring surges of popular mobilization. Others emphasize its distinctive qualities: the simultaneous emergence of multiple overlapping movements focused on identity, recognition, and structural power in ways that broke from earlier reform traditions.

What is not in dispute is the scale and variety of what happened. Between roughly 1965 and 1980, organized movements emerged or dramatically expanded on behalf of women, Mexican Americans, Puerto Ricans, Native Americans, Asian Americans, gay and lesbian people, people with disabilities, and the natural environment. Each of these movements had its own history, its own organizational forms, its own internal debates, and its own relationship to existing power structures. But they also shared important features that make it meaningful to study them as part of a common era.

Shared Conditions

All of these movements emerged from conditions of systemic exclusion. In each case, a group of Americans faced legal, economic, social, or cultural barriers that restricted their participation in public life. Those barriers were not accidents or individual failures — they were embedded in law, policy, institutional practice, and cultural assumption. The movements of this era demanded that those barriers be named, challenged, and dismantled.

The Influence of the Civil Rights Movement

The Black freedom struggle provided a model, a vocabulary, and in some cases direct organizational training for the movements that followed. The use of nonviolent direct action, the rhetoric of constitutional rights, the practice of mass demonstration, the willingness to court arrest, and the appeal to national conscience — all were techniques that had been developed and tested in the civil rights movement and that other movements adapted. At the same time, the limits of the civil rights model — particularly after the legislative breakthroughs of 1964 and 1965 did not end poverty, police violence, or economic exclusion — pushed many activists toward more confrontational and more structural critiques.

Organizational Innovation and Tactical Diversity

The movements of this era developed new organizational forms adapted to their specific needs and circumstances. Consciousness-raising groups became central to women's liberation. Community service combined with political confrontation characterized the Young Lords. Reservation-based organizing intersected with urban organizing in the American Indian Movement. Legal advocacy organizations like the NAACP Legal Defense Fund inspired parallel efforts in women's rights, disability rights, and immigrant rights. The interplay between grassroots organizing, legal litigation, cultural production, and legislative lobbying characterized the most effective movements.

These movements used a wide range of tactics: marches, sit-ins, boycotts, occupations, hunger strikes, lawsuits, lobbying, electoral campaigns, community service programs, cultural festivals, artistic production, and media campaigns. The choice of tactics reflected both strategic calculation and the specific circumstances of each movement. The farmworker boycott was a powerful economic tool because it mobilized consumers. The occupation of Alcatraz Island was a powerful symbolic action because it dramatized Indigenous claims in a way that attracted national media attention. The legal strategy used by disability rights advocates was powerful because it built on constitutional frameworks established by civil rights litigation.

Government Repression and Co-optation

Social movements in this era faced both repression and co-optation from the federal government. The FBI's COINTELPRO operations expanded in the late 1960s to include the Black Panther Party, AIM, Puerto Rican nationalist groups, women's liberation organizations, and others. Tactics included planting informants, spreading disinformation designed to provoke internal conflict, fabricating evidence, and in some cases facilitating or participating in direct violence against activists. At the same time, some movement demands were eventually incorporated into law and policy, a process that sometimes strengthened movements and sometimes absorbed and neutralized their energy.

Internal Debates and Cultural Production

Every movement examined in this chapter was also shaped by internal debates about goals, tactics, leadership, and the relationship between different forms of oppression. Should the women's movement focus on legal equality or on transforming gender relations more fundamentally? Should Chicano activists pursue integration or cultural separatism? Should AIM work within federal legal frameworks or outside them? Should gay rights advocates focus on legal reform or on building alternative community institutions? These debates reflected genuine disagreements about strategy and vision, and they were shaped by the intersecting identities of activists.

Alongside political organization, the social movements of this era generated extraordinary cultural production. Chicano muralists transformed barrio walls. Women's publishing collectives created new spaces for feminist writing. Native American literature experienced a renaissance. Gay and lesbian communities built bars, newspapers, bookstores, theater companies, and neighborhood institutions. This cultural production was itself political, creating community, asserting identity, and challenging dominant narratives.

The era of new social movements transformed American law, culture, and political life in ways that were not fully visible until decades later. The movements did not achieve all their goals, and many of their achievements were subsequently rolled back or contested. But they permanently expanded the range of what Americans consider legitimate political claims, permanently diversified the participants in American political life, and permanently changed the landscape of rights, recognition, and representation.

Section 45.2

Women's Liberation and Feminism

The women's movement of the 1960s and 1970s was not a single organization with a single ideology. It encompassed liberal feminists who worked through existing institutions for legal equality, radical feminists who argued that patriarchy required structural transformation, socialist feminists who connected gender oppression to capitalism, and Black feminists, Chicana feminists, and other feminists of color who challenged the whiteness and middle-class assumptions of mainstream feminist organizations while also challenging the gender blindness of movements organized primarily around race or class.

Roots of the Modern Women's Movement

Several developments in the late 1950s and early 1960s created the conditions for organized feminist activism. President Kennedy's Commission on the Status of Women, established in 1961 and chaired by Eleanor Roosevelt, produced a 1963 report documenting pervasive legal and workplace discrimination against women. The report revealed that women were paid significantly less than men for comparable work, were excluded from many professions, faced discrimination in educational opportunity, and were subject to legal disabilities in property, credit, and family law.

Betty Friedan's The Feminine Mystique, also published in 1963, identified what she called "the problem that has no name" — the sense of emptiness and unfulfillment experienced by educated, middle-class white women who had been told that marriage, suburban homemaking, and motherhood should be sufficient sources of meaning. Friedan's book resonated powerfully with her target audience. It was also, as Black feminists later pointed out, blind to the experiences of working-class women and women of color for whom paid labor was not a horizon of possibility but an economic necessity — and often a demeaning one. This tension between the concerns of white middle-class women and the concerns of women whose lives were also shaped by race, class, and immigration status would run through the women's movement for decades.

The Civil Rights Act of 1964 included a prohibition on sex discrimination in employment as Title VII, initially added by opponents of the bill who hoped the provision would kill the legislation. When the act passed, women activists immediately began pressing the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission to enforce the sex discrimination provisions seriously. The EEOC's initial resistance to doing so helped precipitate the founding of the National Organization for Women (NOW) in 1966, with Betty Friedan as its first president. NOW focused on legislative advocacy, legal action, and electoral politics — a liberal feminist approach that achieved important results but was also criticized by more radical activists as insufficiently transformative.

Women's Liberation and Consciousness-Raising

The more radical wing of the women's movement emerged partly from women's experiences within the civil rights movement, the antiwar movement, and the New Left. Women in these movements found themselves assigned to secondary roles — making coffee, typing newsletters, providing sexual availability — while men made the decisions and gave the speeches. Many concluded that male dominance was not only a feature of the broader society but of the left itself, and that a specifically feminist movement was necessary.

Women's liberation groups developed a practice called consciousness-raising: small groups of women meeting regularly to share personal experiences and analyze them as expressions of systematic social arrangements rather than individual failures or character flaws. The phrase "the personal is political" — one of the defining slogans of women's liberation — captured the insight that domestic violence, sexual coercion, unequal domestic labor, and limited personal freedom were not private problems to be managed individually but political conditions to be challenged collectively. Consciousness-raising spread rapidly in the late 1960s and early 1970s, creating a decentralized network of feminist organizing that reached women in cities and small towns across the country.

Radical Feminism and Its Arguments

Radical feminists argued that the fundamental problem was not legal inequality — which liberal feminism could address through legislation — but patriarchy itself: the system by which men dominated women in family life, sexuality, culture, language, and institutions. Shulamith Firestone's The Dialectic of Sex (1970) argued that women's biological role in reproduction was the root of their oppression. Kate Millett's Sexual Politics (1970) analyzed literature, psychology, and culture as mechanisms for enforcing gender hierarchy.

Radical feminists organized around specific issues that liberal feminist organizations had often avoided: rape, domestic violence, sexual harassment, and reproductive autonomy. The anti-rape movement of the early 1970s created the first rape crisis centers, challenged legal frameworks that made successful rape prosecution extremely difficult, and introduced the concept that rape was not primarily a crime of passion but an exercise of power. Battered women's shelters emerged from feminist organizing, creating the first systematic institutional response to domestic violence.

The ERA and Title IX

Two legislative battles defined the political women's movement of the early 1970s. The Equal Rights Amendment, a proposed constitutional amendment stating that equality of rights under the law shall not be denied on account of sex, passed Congress in 1972 with bipartisan support. Ratification required thirty-eight states; by 1977, thirty-five had ratified it, but the campaign then stalled. Conservative activist Phyllis Schlafly organized the STOP ERA campaign, arguing that the amendment would undermine protections for women in family law, expose women to military draft, and destroy traditional gender roles. The ERA ultimately failed to achieve ratification by the 1982 deadline.

Title IX of the Education Amendments of 1972 proved more durable. The law prohibited sex discrimination in any federally funded educational program. Its transformative effect on women's sports emerged over the following years as schools and universities were required to provide equitable athletic opportunities. By the 1980s and 1990s, Title IX had produced a revolution in women's athletic participation with effects visible in international competition, professional leagues, and everyday school life.

Roe v. Wade (1973), in which the Supreme Court held that the Constitution protected a woman's right to abortion during the first and second trimesters of pregnancy, became both the most significant legal victory and the most politically divisive achievement of the women's movement. The decision generated immediate organized opposition from religious conservatives and became the central organizing issue of the emerging antiabortion movement.

Black Feminism and the Critique of Mainstream Feminism

African American women had long been central to civil rights organizing, but their concerns were often subordinated to racial unity demands that treated gender as a secondary issue. The Combahee River Collective, a Boston-based Black feminist organization, issued a statement in 1977 that articulated what would later be called intersectional analysis: the argument that Black women's oppression could not be understood by simply adding race to the analysis of gender or gender to the analysis of race, but required a framework that addressed the specific experience of people who inhabited multiple marginalized identities simultaneously. bell hooks's Ain't I a Woman (1981) traced the erasure of Black women from both feminist and civil rights history.

Chicana feminists faced similar tensions. Within the Chicano nationalist movement, women were often expected to serve the movement by supporting male leaders, maintaining cultural traditions, and bearing children for the community. Chicana feminists challenged this subordination while also criticizing Anglo feminist organizations for failing to address the intersection of racism, immigration status, and poverty with gender discrimination.

Historical Vocabulary
Consciousness-Raising

Consciousness-raising was a feminist organizing practice developed in the late 1960s in which small groups of women met regularly to share personal experiences and analyze them as expressions of social and political conditions rather than individual circumstances. The practice drew on the civil rights movement's tradition of community organizing but was adapted specifically to address how women's personal lives were structured by patriarchal power.

The central insight of consciousness-raising was captured in the slogan "the personal is political." By sharing experiences of sexual coercion, domestic labor, professional discrimination, and bodily autonomy, women discovered that what they had understood as private problems were in fact common experiences produced by systematic social arrangements. This recognition was foundational to the women's liberation movement.

Consciousness-raising groups were deliberately small and structurally egalitarian, without formal leaders. They spread through women's friendships, college campuses, workplaces, and community organizations, creating a decentralized network of feminist organizing that reached women in communities far removed from formal movement organizations. Historians estimate that tens of thousands of such groups operated across the United States in the early 1970s, making this one of the most widely practiced forms of political organizing in American history.

Section 45.3

Chicano/a Movement and Farmworker Organizing

The Mexican American population of the United States in 1960 was concentrated primarily in the Southwest and California, with growing communities in midwestern cities like Chicago and Detroit. Mexican Americans faced pervasive discrimination in employment, housing, and education; school segregation in the Southwest had been challenged legally but had not been eliminated; and the political representation of Mexican Americans remained minimal despite their substantial population. By the early 1960s, a new generation was developing more assertive and confrontational approaches.

The Farmworker Movement

The organizing of California's agricultural workers was among the most dramatic and important developments of Mexican American political history. Farmworkers — the majority of whom were Mexican or Mexican American, with significant Filipino participation — occupied the most exploited position in the California economy. They worked for extremely low wages under dangerous conditions, without the legal protections that covered industrial workers under the National Labor Relations Act. Agricultural workers had been excluded from the act in 1935, a concession to southern Democrats who wanted to exclude Black laborers from labor law protections. Farmworkers were subject to the arbitrary power of growers and labor contractors, often housed in substandard labor camps, and exposed to pesticides whose health effects were poorly regulated.

Dolores Huerta and César Chávez co-founded the National Farm Workers Association in 1962 in Delano, California. Huerta, a former schoolteacher and community organizer, had already been active in the Community Service Organization and brought deep experience in door-to-door organizing and voter registration. Chávez brought a commitment to nonviolent organizing inspired by Gandhi and the civil rights movement, combined with deep knowledge of agricultural labor conditions from his own childhood as a farmworker. Together, they began the slow work of organizing a workforce that was constantly mobile, legally vulnerable, ethnically diverse, and employed by politically powerful growers.

In 1965, the National Farm Workers Association joined a strike begun by the Agricultural Workers Organizing Committee — a Filipino-led union — against Delano grape growers. The resulting Delano Grape Strike lasted five years. The union, which merged with the Filipino organization to form the United Farm Workers (UFW), combined strike action with a national consumer boycott of California grapes. The boycott was a stroke of strategic genius: because farmworkers had no legal right to organize under the NLRA, traditional union pressure had limited power. But the grape boycott mobilized millions of consumers across the country, particularly in major cities, who refused to purchase California grapes. Urban labor unions, churches, civil rights organizations, and college students supported the boycott, creating national pressure that complemented local strike action. The boycott ultimately succeeded in pressuring major grape growers to sign union contracts in 1970.

The role of Dolores Huerta in founding and building the UFW has been consistently undervalued in popular historical memory, which has tended to center Chávez as the singular figure of the farmworker movement. Huerta negotiated many of the union's major contracts, organized political lobbying, and was the primary architect of the political advocacy that produced the California Agricultural Labor Relations Act in 1975 — the first law in the United States guaranteeing farmworkers the right to organize and bargain collectively. Her phrase "Sí, se puede" ("Yes, we can") became a motto of the movement.

The Chicano Movement Beyond the Fields

The farmworker struggle was the most nationally visible expression of Chicano activism, but the movement had many other dimensions. In New Mexico, Reies López Tijerina organized the Alianza Federal de Mercedes to reclaim land grants that Mexican families had held under Spanish and Mexican law before the U.S. conquest in 1848, arguing that the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo had guaranteed those rights and that subsequent dispossession had been illegal. His movement reflected a distinctly New Mexican history of land loss and legal grievance.

In Denver, Rodolfo "Corky" Gonzales founded the Crusade for Justice in 1965, an organization that addressed urban Chicano concerns through community organizing, cultural work, and direct action. Gonzales authored I Am Joaquín (1967), an epic poem that became a touchstone of the Chicano cultural renaissance, asserting a hybrid identity rooted in Indigenous, Spanish, and Mexican heritage. The Crusade for Justice organized the First National Chicano Youth Liberation Conference in 1969, which produced "El Plan de Aztlán," a declaration of Chicano nationalism that envisioned the reclamation of the Southwest as a Chicano homeland.

The student movement produced MEChA (Movimiento Estudiantil Chicano de Aztlán), which organized walkouts, sit-ins, and demands for Chicano studies programs at high schools and universities across the Southwest. The East L.A. blowouts of 1968 were among the most dramatic expressions of Chicano student activism. Students demanded bilingual education, Chicano studies curricula, more Latino teachers and administrators, and an end to tracking systems that systematically channeled Mexican American students away from college preparatory courses.

The Raza Unida Party, founded in 1970 by José Ángel Gutiérrez in Crystal City, Texas, represented the electoral dimension of Chicano activism. Crystal City was a predominantly Mexican American community in south Texas where Anglo politicians had controlled local government. Raza Unida organized voter registration, ran candidates for local office, and won control of the school board and city council, then implemented bilingual education and other community-demanded programs.

The Brown Berets, founded in Los Angeles in 1967, combined community service with confrontational politics inspired partly by the Black Panther Party. The Berets organized free health clinics, documented police brutality, and participated in the 1970 Chicano Moratorium — the largest Mexican American demonstration against the Vietnam War — which drew twenty thousand marchers to East Los Angeles and ended in a police riot that killed three people, including journalist Rubén Salazar.

Chicana Feminism

The Chicano movement, like many nationalist movements, was marked by significant gender inequality. Male leaders and a machismo cultural tradition often relegated women to supporting roles. Chicana activists within the movement began organizing specifically as feminists, challenging both the gender hierarchy within Chicano organizations and the racial and class blindness of mainstream feminist organizations. The National Chicana Conference of 1971 produced resolutions on reproductive rights, equal employment, and educational opportunity that directly challenged the conservative gender politics of Chicano nationalism. Chicana feminists developed theoretical frameworks that anticipated the intersectional analysis of subsequent decades.

Section 45.4

Puerto Rican Activism and Urban Politics

Puerto Rico had been a U.S. territory since 1898, its residents U.S. citizens since 1917, but the island's political status remained ambiguous — neither a state with full democratic representation nor an independent nation with sovereignty. Puerto Rico's economy had been transformed by Operation Bootstrap, a U.S.-sponsored industrialization program begun in the 1940s that created manufacturing jobs and economic growth but also massive displacement from agricultural communities and massive migration to the U.S. mainland. By 1970, more than a million Puerto Ricans lived in New York City, with growing communities in Chicago, Hartford, Philadelphia, and other cities.

Puerto Ricans on the mainland occupied a position shaped by racial ambiguity and colonial history. They were U.S. citizens by birth, but they experienced discrimination in housing, employment, education, and law enforcement that paralleled what other communities of color faced. The urban renewal programs of the 1950s and 1960s had often deliberately displaced Puerto Rican communities to make way for highways, public housing projects, or development that served other populations — a form of displacement that Puerto Rican activists called by its real name.

The Young Lords

The Young Lords originated as a Chicago street gang in the 1960s and was transformed into a political organization in 1968 under the leadership of José "Cha-Cha" Jiménez, who had been radicalized while in prison by reading the writings of Malcolm X and Che Guevara. The transformed organization took inspiration from the Black Panther Party's model of community service combined with political confrontation. The Young Lords organized around community issues — garbage collection, lead paint poisoning, police brutality, housing conditions, tuberculosis testing — using direct action tactics that attracted media attention and built community support.

In 1969, the New York City chapter of the Young Lords was founded by college students who had been active in Columbia University's 1968 student strike and who were radicalized by Puerto Rican community conditions in East Harlem. The New York Young Lords quickly became one of the most visible Puerto Rican political organizations in the country. They occupied a Methodist church in East Harlem to demand space for a free breakfast program. They took over Lincoln Hospital in the South Bronx to protest inadequate health care, distributing health information and demanding community control of health facilities. They organized detox programs for heroin addicts, whom they understood as victims of social conditions rather than moral failures.

The Young Lords combined Puerto Rican nationalism — many members supported independence for Puerto Rico — with local community organizing and revolutionary rhetoric. Their newspaper, Palante ("Forward"), reached thousands of readers. Their thirteen-point platform addressed colonialism, capitalism, racism, sexism, and the rights of Puerto Rican people in terms that connected local organizing to international anti-colonial struggles. The Young Lords were among the first Puerto Rican organizations to explicitly include women's liberation in their platform, though the organization also struggled with gender inequality in its internal culture.

Puerto Rican Politics in New York

Community organizations like Aspira focused on educational opportunity for Puerto Rican youth and won a landmark 1974 consent decree requiring New York City to provide bilingual education to Spanish-speaking students. The Puerto Rican Legal Defense and Education Fund, founded in 1972 and modeled on the NAACP Legal Defense Fund, used litigation to challenge discrimination in employment, housing, and education.

Herman Badillo, a Bronx politician who had been raised in Puerto Rico, became the first Puerto Rican elected to a voting seat in Congress in 1970, representing the South Bronx. His election represented an important milestone but also illustrated the political system's capacity to incorporate individual representatives while leaving structural conditions largely unchanged.

The question of Puerto Rico's political status — statehood, independence, or continued commonwealth — was a persistent source of political division within Puerto Rican communities. The Fuerzas Armadas de Liberación Nacional (FALN), a clandestine independence organization, carried out bombings in New York and Chicago in the 1970s and 1980s as a form of armed struggle. Most Puerto Rican community organizations opposed the FALN's methods while sharing its critique of the colonial relationship between the United States and Puerto Rico.

Section 45.5

Native American Activism and Red Power

The Native American population of the United States in 1960 numbered approximately eight hundred thousand, distributed across hundreds of federally recognized tribes, reservation communities, and cities to which many had been relocated by the federal termination and relocation policies of the 1950s. Federal termination policy — the effort to end the formal legal status of tribes and assimilate Native Americans into mainstream society — had been a disaster for communities whose sovereignty, land base, and social organization depended on federal recognition and treaty rights. The relocation program produced large urban Native communities in Los Angeles, San Francisco, Chicago, Minneapolis, and other cities but left many relocatees cut off from their home communities and struggling with poverty, discrimination, and cultural dislocation.

Foundations of Red Power

The intellectual foundation for the Red Power movement was provided partly by Vine Deloria Jr.'s Custer Died for Your Sins (1969), a witty, incisive, and deeply argued critique of U.S. Indian policy, of anthropologists who studied Native people without regard for their interests, and of liberal allies whose help often reflected their own assumptions rather than Native needs. Deloria insisted on Native sovereignty as a living legal and political reality, not a historical relic, and challenged the assumption that assimilation was either inevitable or desirable.

The American Indian Movement

AIM was founded in Minneapolis in 1968 by Dennis Banks, Clyde Bellecourt, and other urban Native activists who were initially focused on police brutality against Native Americans in Minneapolis's "Indian ghetto." AIM organized street patrols that monitored police conduct in Native neighborhoods and provided legal assistance. The organization quickly grew and expanded its concerns to include education, economic development, treaty rights, and sovereignty. AIM established "survival schools" offering culturally rooted education, free clinics, and legal assistance for urban Native communities.

AIM drew heavily on the Black Panther Party's model of community service combined with political confrontation, adapted to the specific circumstances of Native communities. It also developed a powerful set of cultural symbols — the drum, the eagle feather, the AIM flag — that connected political organizing to Indigenous cultural identity.

The Alcatraz Occupation

In November 1969, a group of Native American students and activists, calling themselves "Indians of All Tribes," seized the abandoned federal prison on Alcatraz Island in San Francisco Bay. The occupiers made a deliberately ironic claim based on an 1868 Sioux treaty provision that gave Native people the right to claim unused federal property. Their proclamation offered to purchase the island for twenty-four dollars in glass beads and red cloth — the amount apocryphally paid for Manhattan — and proposed to establish a Native cultural center, university, and spiritual center on the island.

The occupation lasted nineteen months, from November 1969 to June 1971, making it one of the longest Native American occupations in the twentieth century. At its peak, several hundred people lived on the island. The occupation attracted national media attention, cultural figures, and political supporters, and it provided a template for subsequent Native American direct action. The occupiers were eventually removed by federal marshals, and no Native cultural center was built on Alcatraz — the island became a national park — but the occupation's political effects were significant.

Trail of Broken Treaties and the Wounded Knee Occupation

In 1972, AIM organized the "Trail of Broken Treaties," a caravan of Native American activists from the West Coast and Midwest that converged on Washington, D.C., just before the presidential election. The marchers brought a twenty-point position paper demanding restoration of treaty rights, tribal sovereignty, and Native land. When the Bureau of Indian Affairs failed to provide promised housing and meeting space, several hundred activists occupied the BIA headquarters building for seven days. The Nixon administration negotiated an end to the occupation but rejected the twenty-point platform.

The most dramatic and consequential AIM action was the occupation of Wounded Knee, South Dakota, in February 1973. Wounded Knee was the site of the 1890 massacre in which U.S. troops had killed between 250 and 300 Lakota men, women, and children — one of the most notorious acts of violence in the history of U.S.-Native relations. AIM activists, invited to Pine Ridge by Oglala Sioux traditional leaders who opposed the tribal chairman, occupied the small community on February 27, 1973. Federal marshals and FBI agents surrounded the occupation within days, and a 71-day armed standoff ensued. Two Native Americans were killed by federal fire, and a federal marshal was seriously wounded. The occupation focused enormous national and international attention on Native American conditions, particularly the issues of treaty rights and tribal self-determination.

The 1975 shootout on the Pine Ridge Reservation, in which two FBI agents and one Native American were killed, produced the conviction of AIM member Leonard Peltier on murder charges in a trial that Amnesty International and many others considered deeply unfair. Peltier remained imprisoned for decades, becoming a cause célèbre for Native rights activists.

Legislative Achievements

The Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act of 1975 shifted federal policy away from termination toward tribal self-determination, allowing tribes greater control over federally funded programs. The American Indian Religious Freedom Act of 1978 acknowledged the importance of protecting Native religious practices and sacred sites. The Indian Child Welfare Act of 1978 addressed the systematic removal of Native children from their families by state child welfare systems, establishing tribal jurisdiction over adoption and foster care decisions for Native children.

Historical Vocabulary
Sovereignty (Native American)

In the context of Native American history and politics, sovereignty refers to the inherent right of Indigenous nations to self-govern — to make and enforce their own laws, manage their own resources, and maintain their own political institutions and cultural practices without subordination to external authority. The legal concept of Native sovereignty in the United States is complex, rooted in treaties, federal statutes, and Supreme Court decisions dating to the early nineteenth century.

Chief Justice John Marshall's Supreme Court trilogy — Johnson v. M'Intosh (1823), Cherokee Nation v. Georgia (1831), and Worcester v. Georgia (1832) — established the framework of Native sovereignty that has persisted in contested form to the present. Marshall held that Native nations were "domestic dependent nations" — entities that retained internal sovereignty but were subject to the ultimate authority of the United States.

For activists in the Red Power movement, sovereignty was not merely a legal concept but a living political and cultural reality. The demand for sovereignty included the right to enforce treaties, govern reservation territory, control natural resources on tribal lands, maintain traditional cultural and religious practices, and educate children according to Indigenous values. The argument for sovereignty was an argument that Native peoples were not simply another minority group seeking inclusion in American society but nations with a prior and continuing legal standing that the United States was obligated to respect.

Section 45.6

Asian American Activism

The term "Asian American" was itself a political creation of the late 1960s. Before that period, Asian-origin communities in the United States — Chinese, Japanese, Filipino, Korean, South Asian — had been organized primarily within their own ethnic communities, often with significant internal divisions. The term was adopted by student activists at the University of California, Berkeley, and UCLA in 1968 as a deliberate act of coalition-building, modeled on the Black Power movement's political use of racial identity. The Asian American Political Alliance, founded in 1968, was among the first organizations to use the term and to develop a pan-Asian political identity.

The Model Minority Myth

The "model minority" narrative portrayed Asian Americans as a group that had achieved economic success and social stability through hard work, educational achievement, and cultural values that emphasized family discipline and deference to authority. Articles in popular magazines celebrated Asian Americans as evidence that racial discrimination could be overcome through individual effort, implying a contrast with African Americans and other communities of color who demanded structural change. Asian American activists explicitly rejected this narrative. They argued that it obscured the real poverty and discrimination faced by many Asian Americans, particularly working-class communities, new immigrants, and those whose educational credentials did not translate into comparable economic opportunity. They also argued that the model minority myth was a tool for dividing communities of color and deflecting demands for structural change.

Third World Liberation Front

The most dramatic expression of Asian American student activism was participation in the Third World Liberation Front (TWLF) strikes at San Francisco State College (1968–69) and the University of California, Berkeley (1969). The TWLF was a coalition of Asian American, African American, Latino, and Native American student organizations that demanded the establishment of ethnic studies programs — schools or departments that would teach the history, culture, and political experience of communities of color from those communities' own perspectives. The San Francisco State strike, which lasted five months, was the longest student strike in U.S. history. Both strikes eventually succeeded in establishing ethnic studies programs, which became models for similar programs at universities across the country.

Community Organizing and the International Hotel

Student activism was one dimension of Asian American politics; community organizing was another. The International Hotel struggle in San Francisco (1968–77), in which mostly elderly Filipino and Chinese working-class residents fought eviction by a developer who wanted to demolish their residential hotel, mobilized a broad coalition of Asian American students, community members, labor organizers, and supporters from other communities. The hotel's eventual demolition in 1977, over the objections of hundreds of protesters who formed a human chain around the building, was a defeat but also a formative experience for a generation of Asian American activists.

Japanese American Redress

One of the most significant long-term achievements connected to Asian American organizing was the movement for Japanese American redress. Japanese Americans who had been imprisoned without due process during World War II began organizing in the 1970s to demand a formal government apology and monetary compensation. The Congressional Commission on Wartime Relocation and Internment of Civilians, established in 1980, documented the constitutional violations of the incarceration and recommended redress. The Civil Liberties Act of 1988 provided a formal government apology and a payment of twenty thousand dollars to each surviving incarceration survivor — a landmark achievement that was both a practical remedy and a symbolic acknowledgment of historical injustice.

Section 45.7

Gay Rights and Stonewall

In 1969, homosexual acts between consenting adults were illegal in every state except Illinois. Police routinely raided gay and lesbian bars, arrested patrons for "disorderly conduct," and published their names in newspapers. Gay and lesbian people were barred from federal employment, denied security clearances, and subject to psychiatric classification as mentally disordered. Military service members suspected of homosexuality were dishonorably discharged. The social conditions of gay life before Stonewall were marked by a paradox: widespread invisibility in public life combined with concentrated visibility in particular urban spaces — bars, parks, bathhouses — that were subject to systematic police harassment.

The Stonewall Uprising

In the early morning hours of June 28, 1969, New York City police raided the Stonewall Inn, a gay bar in Greenwich Village. The Stonewall was not a particularly distinguished establishment — it was owned by organized crime, as were many gay bars that paid police for the privilege of operating. But the raid on the Stonewall triggered an unexpectedly fierce resistance. The patrons — a mix of gay men, lesbians, drag queens, and transgender people, many of them working class and people of color — refused to comply with the usual routine of passive arrest. They fought back, throwing bottles and coins and eventually overturning a parking meter to use as a battering ram. The uprising continued for several nights.

Stonewall provided a founding narrative, a symbol, and a date around which movement identity could coalesce. Within weeks of the uprising, the Gay Liberation Front had been founded in New York, adopting the militant language of Third World liberation movements and explicitly rejecting the assimilationist strategy of earlier homophile organizations. "Gay is good" — modeled on "Black is beautiful" — became a rallying slogan. The Gay Activists Alliance, founded shortly after, focused more specifically on gay rights as a political issue rather than on broader revolutionary transformation.

Coming Out as Political Act

One of the most powerful political tools developed by the early gay rights movement was the practice of "coming out" — publicly identifying as gay or lesbian as a political act. Gay liberation philosophy argued that the self-hatred, secrecy, and shame that gay and lesbian people had internalized under oppression were themselves forms of political damage that needed to be overcome through pride, visibility, and community. The first Gay Pride marches, held in New York, Los Angeles, San Francisco, and Chicago in June 1970, on the first anniversary of Stonewall, celebrated this politics of visibility. Gay Pride as an annual ritual — combining celebration and political demonstration — spread to cities across the country and eventually around the world.

Electoral Politics and Harvey Milk

The gay rights movement's political impact was visible in local electoral politics, particularly in San Francisco, where a large, organized, and geographically concentrated gay community developed significant political power. Harvey Milk, a camera shop owner who had moved to the Castro neighborhood, ran for San Francisco city supervisor in 1973 and 1975 before winning election in 1977, becoming one of the first openly gay elected officials in the United States. His assassination in November 1978, together with Mayor George Moscone, at the hands of fellow supervisor Dan White, and the subsequent lenient sentence given White, triggered the White Night riots in San Francisco — a demonstration of community grief and rage.

APA Declassification

The American Psychiatric Association's 1973 decision to remove homosexuality from its Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders followed years of organized pressure by gay activists who disrupted APA meetings, challenged the scientific basis of the classification, and built alliances with sympathetic psychiatrists. The declassification of homosexuality as a mental disorder undermined the legal, medical, and cultural framework that had been used to justify discrimination, forced treatment, and social control of gay and lesbian people.

Anita Bryant's 1977 campaign in Dade County, Florida, that successfully repealed a municipal anti-discrimination ordinance, triggered a national backlash and mobilization on both sides. Bryant's campaign was an early expression of the organized anti-gay-rights politics that would become central to conservative mobilization in subsequent decades.

Section 45.8

Disability Rights Movement

Approximately fifty million Americans in the 1970s had some form of disability — physical, sensory, cognitive, or psychiatric. The history of disability in the United States was a history of institutionalization, exclusion, and the cultural assumption that disability was a personal tragedy or a medical problem to be fixed rather than a form of human variation that society could accommodate. People with significant disabilities were routinely institutionalized, sometimes from childhood. Public schools routinely excluded students with disabilities. Public buildings, transportation systems, sidewalks, and communication technologies were designed without consideration of users who could not walk, see, hear, or read in standard ways.

Ed Roberts and the Independent Living Movement

Ed Roberts, a California resident who had been paralyzed by polio as a teenager and required an iron lung to breathe at night, applied to the University of California, Berkeley in 1962. The state vocational rehabilitation department initially refused to fund his attendance, saying he was "too severely disabled" to benefit from higher education. Roberts challenged this decision, won admission to Berkeley, and organized with other disabled students to create the Physically Disabled Students' Program. This became the model for the independent living movement.

The independent living movement challenged the medical model of disability, which treated disability as a problem requiring professional management, and proposed instead a civil rights model, which treated disability as a form of human difference requiring social accommodation. The argument was straightforward: the reason a person using a wheelchair could not enter a building was not the wheelchair — it was the stairs. Disability was produced not by individual bodies but by environments designed for a narrow range of bodies. The solution was not to fix disabled people but to design accessible environments.

The Center for Independent Living, founded in Berkeley in 1972 under Roberts's leadership, was the first organization of its kind: a community-based organization managed and staffed primarily by people with disabilities, providing peer counseling, information and referral, advocacy, and attendant care services. It became the model for hundreds of similar centers across the country.

Section 504 and the HEW Sit-In

Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973 prohibited discrimination on the basis of disability in any program receiving federal financial assistance. But it had minimal effect until the federal government issued implementing regulations. When the Carter administration continued to delay the regulations — under pressure from federal agencies and institutions that objected to the cost of compliance — disability rights activists staged a dramatic protest.

In San Francisco, more than a hundred people with disabilities occupied the federal Health, Education, and Welfare regional office for twenty-six days — the longest occupation of a federal building in U.S. history. Protesters had to manage the considerable logistical challenges of maintaining a sustained occupation while using wheelchairs, ventilators, and other assistive technology. Support from community organizations — including the Black Panther Party, which provided food — made the occupation possible. The San Francisco sit-in generated national media attention and ultimately succeeded: Secretary Califano signed the regulations in April 1977, giving Section 504 effective force.

Education for All Handicapped Children Act

The Education for All Handicapped Children Act of 1975, later renamed the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), required public schools to provide a "free appropriate public education" in the "least restrictive environment" for all students with disabilities. The law required individualized education plans (IEPs) for each student with a disability and created procedural rights for parents to challenge school decisions. The law transformed public education, ending the systematic exclusion of students with disabilities from public schools. Implementation was uneven and contested, but the principle that all children had a right to public education was firmly established.

Section 45.9

Environmental Movement and Earth Day

The modern environmental movement emerged from several converging streams. The conservation tradition, associated with figures like John Muir and the founding of the Sierra Club in 1892, had focused on preserving wilderness areas. The progressive-era conservation movement, associated with Gifford Pinchot and Theodore Roosevelt, had focused on the efficient management of natural resources for long-term human use. These traditions contributed to the institutional infrastructure of mid-twentieth-century environmental politics.

Rachel Carson and Silent Spring

The catalyst for the modern environmental movement is often identified as Rachel Carson's Silent Spring, published in 1962. Carson was a marine biologist and nature writer who had already published several widely read books about the ocean. Silent Spring documented the effects of pesticides — particularly the synthetic organochlorine DDT — on bird populations, ecosystems, and ultimately human health. The book argued that the massive use of pesticides was producing an ecological catastrophe, silencing the spring songbirds and contaminating the food chain from insects to birds to humans. Carson was subjected to a fierce personal and professional attack by the chemical industry, which challenged her scientific credentials and motivations. But Silent Spring reached a wide audience, catalyzed public concern about the relationship between industrial chemistry and ecological health, and ultimately contributed to the federal banning of DDT in 1972.

Earth Day and Legislative Achievements

The immediate organizational expression of environmental politics in 1970 was Earth Day, held on April 22 under the leadership of Senator Gaylord Nelson of Wisconsin and Denis Hayes, a Harvard law student who served as national coordinator. Earth Day 1970 was an extraordinary event: approximately twenty million Americans participated in teach-ins, demonstrations, clean-up events, and rallies across the country. Schools, colleges, churches, and civic organizations participated. The event demonstrated the breadth of environmental concern across political affiliations and social groups in a way that no previous environmental action had done.

Earth Day galvanized political action. In the months following the first Earth Day, Congress passed landmark environmental legislation. The Clean Air Act Amendments of 1970, the Clean Water Act of 1972, the Endangered Species Act of 1973, and the Safe Drinking Water Act of 1974 all followed in the Earth Day mobilization's wake, creating a comprehensive framework of federal environmental regulation that fundamentally changed the relationship between industrial production and environmental protection. The Environmental Protection Agency, established by Nixon in 1970, was a new federal agency dedicated specifically to environmental regulation.

Environmental Justice

The mainstream environmental movement of the early 1970s was primarily organized around and led by white middle-class Americans, and its priorities — wilderness preservation, endangered species, clean air and water in suburban and recreational contexts — reflected those communities' concerns. Communities of color and working-class communities bore disproportionate environmental burdens: toxic waste dumps were sited in low-income neighborhoods, lead paint and lead pipes poisoned children in urban housing, and exposure to pesticides and industrial chemicals affected farmworkers and factory workers far more than suburban hikers.

The environmental justice movement emerged from communities that experienced this disproportionate burden and challenged the mainstream environmental movement's indifference to environmental inequality. The United Farm Workers' campaign against pesticide exposure in the fields was an environmental justice cause as well as a labor cause. The 1982 protests in Warren County, North Carolina, against the siting of a PCB landfill in a predominantly Black community are often cited as a formative moment in the environmental justice movement, though the roots went much deeper. Robert Bullard's Dumping in Dixie (1990) later documented the systematic pattern by which hazardous waste facilities and toxic dumpsites were disproportionately located in Black communities across the South.

Section 45.10

Chapter Review and Social Movements Comparison

The social movements examined in this chapter shared several important features while also differing in significant ways. Understanding both the commonalities and the differences helps students analyze what made social movements effective, what limits they encountered, and what legacies they left.

All of the movements emerged from conditions of systematic exclusion. Whether the exclusion was legal, institutional, economic, or cultural, the movements arose because a gap existed between the nation's professed values of equality and democracy and the actual conditions of life for the people they organized. All of the movements developed new forms of collective identity — Chicano/a, Asian American, Red Power, gay pride, disability community — that were not pre-existing identities but were in significant measure created by the movements themselves. All produced cultural transformations that outlasted specific legislative campaigns. And all experienced the tension between insider and outsider strategies — between working within existing political and legal institutions and confronting those institutions from outside.

The movements differed in their relationship to existing legal frameworks. They differed in their political geography, in their internal unity, and in the specific combination of legal, cultural, economic, and political barriers they faced. These differences shaped different tactics and different political possibilities. Understanding both the commonalities and the differences is essential to understanding why some movements achieved more than others, why some of their achievements proved durable and some did not, and why organized opposition took the specific forms it did in the years that followed.

Social Movements and Urban Geography

A map of social movement activity in the 1960s and 1970s reveals important patterns about the geography of organizing. Social movements in this era were concentrated in several types of places. Major metropolitan areas — New York, Los Angeles, San Francisco, Chicago — were centers of movement activity because they concentrated populations, media, universities, political institutions, and existing community organizations. Southwestern states — California, Texas, New Mexico, Arizona, Colorado — were centers of Chicano/a organizing, reflecting the historical concentration of Mexican American populations in the states acquired from Mexico in 1848. Reservation communities were centers of Native American activism, but the geography of AIM reflected the postwar relocation program's creation of large urban Native communities, and the tension between urban organizing and reservation organizing was a recurring source of tension within the Native rights movement.

College campuses were key organizing spaces across all movements, reflecting the expansion of higher education and the concentration of politically activated young people in campus settings. The geography of campus organizing extended from elite research universities to state colleges and community colleges. Cities were particularly important to gay and lesbian organizing because urban density and anonymity allowed communities to form that could not exist in smaller, more socially scrutinized environments.

"El Plan de Santa Bárbara" (1969)

Creator: Chicano/a student and faculty activists · Date: April 1969 · Type: Political manifesto

Context: In April 1969, Chicano/a college students and faculty gathered in Santa Barbara, California, to produce a political manifesto for the emerging Chicano student movement. The document called for the establishment of Chicano studies programs at universities and colleges and articulated a vision of education as a tool for community liberation rather than individual assimilation.

The Plan argued that Chicano students at universities had a responsibility to use their education in the service of the Chicano community, rather than simply achieving individual mobility. It called for establishment of Chicano studies programs that would teach history, culture, and political situation of Mexican Americans from a perspective grounded in the community's experience. It organized the student movement under the umbrella organization MEChA (Movimiento Estudiantil Chicano de Aztlán) and called for solidarity with farmworkers and other Chicano working-class communities.

The document is also a product of its moment — it uses the masculine generic in ways that Chicana feminists would soon challenge, and its nationalist framework would be contested by socialist and feminist currents within the broader movement.

Historical Debate

Were the Social Movements of the 1960s and 1970s a Success or a Failure?

The question: How should historians evaluate the social movements of the 1960s and 1970s? Did they achieve their goals? Were their achievements durable? How should we weigh legislative victories against unresolved inequalities and subsequent backlash?

Interpretation A — Substantial Achievement: One major interpretation emphasizes the movements' real and lasting achievements. The women's movement won Title IX, transformed professional opportunity for women, and established domestic violence and rape as matters of public concern. The farmworker movement established the first legal right to organize for agricultural workers in California. The disability rights movement ended the systematic exclusion of disabled children from public schools. The gay rights movement declassified homosexuality as a mental disorder and built community institutions that would eventually support the campaign for marriage equality. Native activism reversed the termination policy. By any reasonable standard, these were significant changes that improved the lives of millions of Americans.
Interpretation B — Limited and Contested Gains: A second interpretation, often articulated by activists from these movements themselves, emphasizes the gap between legislative achievement and social reality. Women achieved legal equality in formal terms but continued to earn less than men and remained underrepresented in political leadership. Farmworkers achieved a right to organize in California but remained excluded from NLRA coverage nationally, and the UFW declined significantly in the late 1970s. Native peoples continued to face the highest rates of poverty, suicide, and health disparities of any demographic group. The movements' legislative achievements were real but incomplete, and the social and economic inequalities that had motivated organizing remained largely in place.

Evidence: Both interpretations draw on different kinds of evidence. The first emphasizes legislative and judicial records, changes in employment statistics, and institutional transformations. The second emphasizes economic data on persistent inequality, qualitative accounts of lived experience, and the political backlash documented in subsequent chapters.

Why it matters: How historians evaluate the social movements of this era shapes how they understand the subsequent decades — including the conservative resurgence of the Reagan era, the culture wars of the 1990s, and contemporary debates about identity politics, affirmative action, reproductive rights, and LGBTQ equality.

Multiple Perspectives

The 1970 Chicano Moratorium

On August 29, 1970, approximately twenty thousand Mexican Americans marched through East Los Angeles to Laguna Park to protest the disproportionate combat deaths of Chicano soldiers in Vietnam. The Chicano Moratorium was the largest Mexican American anti-war demonstration in U.S. history, and it ended in a police riot that left three people dead, including journalist Rubén Salazar.

From the perspective of Chicano demonstrators: The march was a legitimate expression of the community's grief and anger over the fact that Chicanos, who comprised a small percentage of the U.S. population, were dying in Vietnam at rates significantly higher than their demographic share. The police riot was an expression of the same system of racial violence that the demonstrators had gathered to protest. The death of Rubén Salazar, who had covered Chicano community concerns for the Los Angeles Times, was widely believed by community members to have been deliberate.
From the perspective of Los Angeles law enforcement: The rally had been preceded by reports of property damage in the neighborhood, and sheriff's deputies were deployed to respond to what they characterized as a riot already in progress. From the official law enforcement perspective, the deputies used force to restore order in a situation that had become dangerous, and the use of tear gas and batons was a proportionate response.
From the perspective of anti-war activists outside the Chicano community: The Chicano Moratorium demonstrated the breadth of anti-Vietnam War sentiment and the specific way in which the war intersected with racial inequality. For many Anglo anti-war activists, the moratorium was evidence that the anti-war movement needed to address the connection between the war abroad and racism at home.

Historical complexity: The events of August 29, 1970, remain contested. Subsequent investigations raised serious questions about whether the sheriff's response was proportionate and whether Salazar's death was accidental. The moratorium and its violent aftermath radicalized a generation of Chicano activists and intensified community criticism of police violence.

Constitutional Connection

The Equal Protection Clause and New Social Movements

The Fourteenth Amendment's Equal Protection Clause — which prohibits states from denying "to any person within its jurisdiction the equal protection of the laws" — was the constitutional foundation of the civil rights movement's legal strategy. The social movements of the 1960s and 1970s each grappled, in different ways, with the possibilities and limits of equal protection as a constitutional framework.

Women's rights and equal protection. Ruth Bader Ginsburg, then a law professor and director of the ACLU Women's Rights Project, developed a legal strategy for extending equal protection to women that drew directly on the civil rights movement's litigation approach. Beginning in the early 1970s, Ginsburg brought a series of cases to the Supreme Court that challenged laws discriminating on the basis of sex. In Reed v. Reed (1971), the Court for the first time struck down a sex-discriminatory law under equal protection. However, the Court never treated sex as a "suspect classification" requiring the same rigorous scrutiny it applied to racial classifications — a limitation that meant sex discrimination laws faced a lower constitutional bar.

Gay rights and due process. The Supreme Court's 1986 decision in Bowers v. Hardwick, which upheld Georgia's criminal sodomy law, demonstrated the limits of constitutional protection for gay and lesbian rights in the 1970s and 1980s. That decision was not overturned until Lawrence v. Texas (2003). The constitutional protection of gay rights developed primarily through the 2000s and 2010s rather than the 1970s.

Disability rights and remedial legislation. The disability rights movement pursued constitutional change primarily through federal remedial legislation rather than through Equal Protection Clause litigation. This reflected the constitutional reality that disability was not treated as a protected class requiring strict scrutiny, and that legislative approaches were more promising. Section 504 and the Education for All Handicapped Children Act established disability rights through statute, creating legal protections more specific and enforceable than constitutional doctrine alone would have provided.

Cause and Effect

The Rise of New Social Movements

Long-Term Causes

Systematic legal, economic, and social exclusion of multiple groups; postwar expansion of higher education concentrating politically activated young people; the Black freedom struggle providing a model and vocabulary; the Cold War creating both opportunities and constraints; expansion of federal programs under New Deal and Great Society creating institutional frameworks movements could engage.

Short-Term Causes

Civil Rights Act and Voting Rights Act demonstrating what organized movements could achieve while revealing limits of formal equality; Vietnam War radicalizing a generation; assassination of Martin Luther King Jr. in 1968 catalyzing urban uprisings; the Stonewall uprising and Alcatraz occupation demonstrating the power of visible direct action; publication of influential texts catalyzing public awareness.

What Happened

Between the mid-1960s and late 1970s, organized movements emerged or dramatically expanded on behalf of women, Mexican Americans, Puerto Ricans, Native Americans, Asian Americans, gay and lesbian people, people with disabilities, and the natural environment, using diverse tactics — strikes, boycotts, occupations, litigation, elections, and cultural production.

Immediate and Long-Term Effects

Title IX (1972); Indian Self-Determination Act (1975); Education for All Handicapped Children Act (1975); EPA banning of DDT (1972); Section 504 regulations (1977). Long-term: permanently diversified the landscape of American democratic participation; generated organized conservative counter-movement; reshaped cultural assumptions about gender, race, disability, sexuality, and environment.

Historical complexity: The social movements of this era cannot be reduced to a single cause. They emerged from the intersection of long-term historical conditions, short-term precipitating events, organizational innovation, individual leadership, and contingent political circumstances.

Continuity and Change Over Time

The Politics of Identity from the 1960s to the Present

What Changed

  • The legal category of "protected class" expanded dramatically: sex, national origin, disability added to those protected under federal civil rights law
  • Ethnic studies, women's studies, and disability studies programs established at universities across the country
  • Gay and lesbian community institutions — bars, newspapers, bookstores, health clinics — created visible infrastructure in major cities
  • Legislative framework: Title IX, Section 504, Education for All Handicapped Children Act, Indian Self-Determination Act
  • Cultural assumptions about gender, sexuality, disability, and ethnic identity shifted significantly in mainstream American culture

What Stayed the Same

  • Fundamental structures of economic inequality were largely unchanged: women continued to earn less than men; communities of color remained overrepresented in poverty
  • Political institutions remained structured by existing distributions of power, constraining how much change was possible in any given moment
  • The capacity of organized money, entrenched interests, and structural inertia to resist social movement demands remained considerable
  • The most economically disadvantaged members of each community — working-class women, farmworkers, Native peoples on reservations, urban poor — benefited least from movement gains

Why the pattern matters: The uneven distribution of social movement benefits is a recurring pattern in American history. Legal change is easier to achieve than economic change, and legal protections in formal terms do not automatically translate into improved conditions in practice. The social movements of the 1960s and 1970s changed the law significantly, but the full realization of those legal changes — in practice, in enforcement, in cultural acceptance — remained unfinished business that continued to shape American politics for decades.

Everyday Life and Social History: Living Through the Movements

The social movements of the 1960s and 1970s were not only matters of legislative history and political organization. They were also lived experiences that changed everyday life in specific, material, and personal ways for millions of Americans.

Women's everyday lives. For a woman entering the workforce in 1965, the practical landscape was dramatically different from what her daughter would encounter in 1980. In 1965, banks routinely required a married woman to have her husband co-sign a loan application. Airlines could fire female flight attendants when they married or turned thirty-five. Law schools admitted small quotas of women, and many medical schools admitted none at all. Help-wanted advertising in newspapers was sex-segregated, with professional and technical positions listed under "Help Wanted — Male."

By the late 1970s, the Equal Credit Opportunity Act (1974) had prohibited sex discrimination in credit, meaning women could open bank accounts and obtain credit in their own names. Title IX had been in effect for years, transforming the athletic opportunities available to girls in public schools. Abortion was legal under Roe v. Wade, meaning women could end an unwanted pregnancy safely rather than seeking illegal procedures that killed or seriously injured thousands annually. These were not abstract legal changes — they were changes in the daily reality of women's lives.

At the same time, daily life remained shaped by deep inequalities. Women who experienced domestic violence often found that police responded slowly or not at all, prosecutors were reluctant to pursue cases, and shelters were few and underfunded. Women who combined wage work with domestic labor — the vast majority — found that the "second shift" of housework and childcare remained largely their responsibility regardless of their employment status.

Daily life in farmworker communities. For a farmworker family in California's Central Valley in 1965, daily life was shaped by extreme economic vulnerability, the absence of labor law protection, and the arbitrary power of growers and labor contractors. Children worked alongside parents during school vacations and often during the school year, helping families meet minimal subsistence needs. Housing was typically provided by growers and could be ended at will. The work itself involved exposure to pesticides whose health effects were poorly documented but visibly present in illness rates. The UFW's achievements — improved wages, the right to organize, the California Agricultural Labor Relations Act — materially improved conditions for farmworkers in the union's organized sectors.

Life in Native communities. For Native Americans living on reservations in the 1960s and 1970s, daily life was shaped by federal policies that had reduced the Native land base by approximately ninety percent since the nineteenth century, by poverty rates that far exceeded the national average, and by health systems that were chronically underfunded. For urban Native Americans, the challenges of poverty and discrimination were compounded by distance from community and cultural institutions. The changes of the Red Power era were real and significant, but their practical effects took years to develop and were unevenly realized.

Gay and lesbian community life. For gay and lesbian Americans in the 1970s, the development of community institutions — bars, bookstores, newspapers, health clinics, community centers — created a new kind of everyday life that had not previously been possible. The Castro neighborhood in San Francisco, Greenwich Village in New York, Boystown in Chicago, and similar neighborhoods in other major cities became spaces where gay and lesbian people could live openly, build community, and participate in political culture. This transformation of daily life — from enforced invisibility to visible community — was itself one of the most significant achievements of the era.

Chapter Summary

The social movements of the 1960s and 1970s were among the most consequential popular political phenomena in American history. They transformed law, culture, political identity, and everyday life in ways that — for all their incompleteness — permanently altered the landscape of American democracy.

The women's liberation movement challenged the systematic exclusion of women from professional life, legal personhood, and bodily autonomy. Through a combination of consciousness-raising, street activism, legal litigation, and legislative lobbying, it produced Title IX, the Equal Credit Opportunity Act, legal recognition of sexual harassment and domestic violence as social problems, and the constitutional protection of reproductive choice established in Roe v. Wade. The movement was internally diverse, contested by conservative counter-movements, and unfinished in its ambitions, but it fundamentally changed what was legally and culturally possible for American women.

The Chicano/a movement connected labor organizing — most visibly through the United Farm Workers' grape boycott — with student activism, community organizing, cultural renaissance, and electoral politics. The farmworker movement produced the first legal right to organize for agricultural workers in California. Student activism produced Chicano studies programs that transformed higher education. The Raza Unida Party demonstrated the possibility of Chicano electoral power at the local level. Chicana feminists challenged both Anglo feminist organizations and Chicano nationalist patriarchy, developing theoretical frameworks that anticipated the intersectional analysis of subsequent decades.

Puerto Rican activism in mainland cities addressed the conditions of a community shaped by colonial history, urban poverty, and the specific forms of discrimination faced by communities that were neither recent immigrants nor historically rooted in a particular U.S. region. The Young Lords demonstrated the power of community service combined with political confrontation. Aspira and the Puerto Rican Legal Defense and Education Fund pursued educational opportunity and legal protection. The question of Puerto Rico's political status remained unresolved and continued to shape Puerto Rican political identity.

Native American activism challenged the most fundamental aspect of the American political order: the relationship between the U.S. government and the sovereign Indigenous nations that had preceded it. AIM, the Alcatraz occupation, and the Wounded Knee standoff dramatized Native grievances in ways that forced national attention. The Indian Self-Determination Act, the Indian Child Welfare Act, and the American Indian Religious Freedom Act represented important legislative changes, but the fundamental conditions that animated the activism — poverty, health disparities, and incomplete sovereignty — remained largely in place.

Asian American activism challenged the model minority myth and developed a pan-ethnic political identity that had not previously existed. The TWLF strikes established ethnic studies as an academic field. The Japanese American redress campaign ultimately achieved a formal government apology and monetary compensation for survivors of wartime incarceration. Community organizing in urban Asian neighborhoods addressed housing, labor rights, and police brutality.

The gay rights movement, catalyzed by the Stonewall uprising, built on earlier homophile organization to develop a new politics of visibility, pride, and rights claims. The declassification of homosexuality as a mental disorder, the creation of municipal anti-discrimination ordinances, and the development of community institutions marked real advances. The assassination of Harvey Milk and the AIDS crisis of the following decade would test the movement's political organization and community cohesion in brutal ways.

The disability rights movement challenged both the medical model that pathologized disability and the social model that excluded disabled people from public life. The independent living movement, the HEW sit-in, Section 504, and the Education for All Handicapped Children Act established legal frameworks that would eventually culminate in the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990. The movement's insistence that disability was a form of human variation requiring social accommodation rather than individual cure was a fundamental challenge to prevailing assumptions.

The environmental movement mobilized millions of Americans across political affiliations to demand protection of the natural environment from industrial damage. Earth Day 1970 demonstrated the breadth of environmental concern. The Clean Air Act, the Clean Water Act, the Endangered Species Act, and the creation of the EPA established a framework of federal environmental regulation that substantially changed the relationship between industrial production and ecological health.

These movements collectively represent a wave of democratic pressure that expanded American democracy's scope and deepened its meaning — imperfectly, unevenly, and with many contradictions and conflicts. They also generated organized opposition that would shape American politics for decades. Understanding both the achievements and the limits, both the movements and the backlash, is essential for understanding the United States that emerged from the 1960s and 1970s.

Key Terms

Feminism
Political ideology and social movement arguing for equal rights and opportunities for women, based on the claim that gender should not determine legal status, economic opportunity, or social participation.
Women's liberation
The more radical wing of the 1970s women's movement, which argued that legal equality was insufficient and that patriarchy itself — as a system of power — required structural transformation.
Consciousness-raising
Feminist organizing practice in which small groups of women shared personal experiences to understand them as expressions of systematic social conditions rather than individual circumstances.
Title IX
Provision of the Education Amendments of 1972 prohibiting sex discrimination in federally funded educational programs; transformed women's access to sports and academic opportunity.
Equal Rights Amendment (ERA)
Proposed constitutional amendment prohibiting denial of rights on the basis of sex; passed Congress in 1972 but failed to achieve ratification by the necessary thirty-eight states.
Chicano/a
Term adopted by Mexican American activists as an assertion of political identity and cultural pride, often associated with Indigenous heritage and resistance to assimilation.
United Farm Workers (UFW)
Labor union co-founded by Dolores Huerta and César Chávez to organize California agricultural workers; used strikes, boycotts, and hunger strikes to win recognition.
Boycott
Organized refusal to purchase goods or services as a form of economic pressure; the UFW's grape and lettuce boycotts were landmark examples.
Brown Berets
Chicano activist organization founded in Los Angeles in 1967, combining community service with political confrontation partly inspired by the Black Panther Party.
Young Lords
Puerto Rican activist organization that combined community service with radical politics; active in Chicago and New York City from 1968 onward.
Red Power
Slogan and movement of the late 1960s and 1970s asserting Native American rights, sovereignty, and cultural identity.
American Indian Movement (AIM)
Organization founded in Minneapolis in 1968 to address police brutality against urban Native Americans; became the central organization of the Red Power movement.
Sovereignty (Native American)
The inherent right of Indigenous nations to self-govern, maintain political institutions, and manage resources, recognized in treaties and federal law but systematically violated.
Model minority myth
Stereotype portraying Asian Americans as uniformly successful and politically compliant; used to deflect demands for racial justice from communities of color.
Third World Liberation Front
Coalition of students of color at San Francisco State and UC Berkeley who struck for ethnic studies programs in 1968–69.
Stonewall uprising
Resistance by gay and lesbian patrons of the Stonewall Inn in New York City against a police raid on June 28, 1969; widely identified as the beginning of the modern gay rights movement.
Gay liberation
Political movement emerging from the Stonewall uprising, emphasizing visibility, pride, and structural challenges to the criminalization and pathologization of homosexuality.
Coming out
Act of publicly identifying as gay, lesbian, or bisexual; reframed by the gay liberation movement as a political act that challenged invisibility and enabled community.
Independent living movement
Strand of disability rights activism emphasizing disabled people's right to live in their communities rather than institutions and to direct their own lives.
Section 504
Provision of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973 prohibiting discrimination on the basis of disability in programs receiving federal funding; became effective after the 1977 HEW sit-in.
Education for All Handicapped Children Act
1975 federal law requiring public schools to provide appropriate education to all students with disabilities; later renamed the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act.
Earth Day
Annual day of environmental education and activism, first held April 22, 1970; approximately twenty million Americans participated in the inaugural event.
Intersectionality
Analytical framework for understanding how overlapping identities — race, gender, class, sexuality, disability — combine to shape individuals' experiences of power and oppression.
COINTELPRO
FBI counterintelligence program that surveilled, infiltrated, and disrupted civil rights, Black Power, and other dissident organizations from 1956 to 1971.
Liberal feminism
Strand of feminism focused on achieving legal equality through legislation and litigation within existing institutions; associated with organizations like NOW.
Radical feminism
Strand of feminism arguing that patriarchy is a fundamental system of power requiring structural transformation, not merely legal reform.

Chapter Reflection

Chapter 45 has examined one of the most remarkable periods of popular political mobilization in American history. The social movements of the 1960s and 1970s transformed law, culture, political identity, and everyday life in ways that were neither complete nor uniform but were nonetheless profound and durable. Understanding why these movements arose, how they organized, what they achieved, and where they fell short is essential to understanding the United States that emerged from this era.

The movements examined in this chapter did not arise from nowhere. They were products of long histories of exclusion — the exclusion of women from legal personhood and professional equality, of Mexican Americans from labor rights and educational equity, of Puerto Ricans from economic opportunity and political recognition, of Native peoples from sovereignty and cultural survival, of Asian Americans from political voice and social acceptance, of gay and lesbian Americans from legal protection and social visibility, of disabled people from public education and community participation, of natural environments from legal protection and political representation. These histories of exclusion stretched back decades and in some cases centuries, and the movements that addressed them were shaped by those deep roots.

The era of new social movements also demonstrates something important about how change happens in democratic societies. Formal legal rights and political representation are insufficient to produce genuine equality when social, economic, and cultural structures reinforce exclusion. The movements examined in this chapter understood this — which is why they focused not only on legislation but on cultural transformation, not only on courtroom victories but on community building, not only on formal equality but on the redistribution of power. The insight that social change requires work at multiple levels simultaneously — legal, cultural, economic, institutional — is one of the most important lessons this era offers to students of American history.

At the same time, this chapter should leave students with a clear understanding that social change is contested, contingent, and never finally secured. The achievements of the 1960s and 1970s were real, but they were also incomplete, unevenly distributed, and subject to backlash. As Chapters 46 and 47 will examine, the conservative resurgence of the late 1970s and 1980s was organized in significant part as a response to the social and cultural changes of this era. The culture wars that shaped American politics through the 1990s and beyond were direct continuations of the conflicts that emerged in this period. The questions that the movements of the 1960s and 1970s raised — about who belongs in American democracy, on what terms, with what protections, and at whose expense — remain the defining questions of American political life.

Finally, the social movements examined in this chapter demonstrate the capacity of ordinary people, organized collectively, to change the conditions of their lives and the laws that govern them. The farmworkers who walked the picket lines in Delano, the disabled activists who occupied the HEW building in San Francisco for twenty-six days, the gay and lesbian patrons who refused to comply with a police raid in Greenwich Village, the Native American students who seized Alcatraz Island — these were not powerful people in the conventional sense. They were people who faced systematic exclusion, who organized with others who shared their situation, and who made demands on a political system that preferred their silence. That they achieved what they did is a testament to the power of organized democratic pressure. That they did not achieve more is a testament to the depth of the inequalities they confronted and the strength of the forces arrayed against them. Both of those truths belong in the story of the United States: contested, changing, unequal, creative, violent, democratic, expansionist, reforming, and unfinished.

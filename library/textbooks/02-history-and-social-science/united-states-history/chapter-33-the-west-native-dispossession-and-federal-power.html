The West, Native Dispossession, and Federal Power

United States History: Indigenous America, Republic, Conflict, and Change

Chapter Opening

"The story of the American West after the Civil War is one of the most consequential and contested chapters in United States history — a single generation in which hundreds of Indigenous nations were dispossessed of lands they had inhabited for thousands of years."

In the autumn of 1868, a Cheyenne village camped along the Washita River in what is today western Oklahoma. The people there, led by Chief Black Kettle, had sought peace with the United States. Black Kettle himself had survived the Sand Creek Massacre four years earlier, when Colorado militiamen attacked a peaceful Cheyenne encampment and killed more than 150 men, women, and children. He had traveled to Washington, met with federal officials, signed treaties, and counseled his people to avoid further conflict. On the morning of November 27, 1868, Lieutenant Colonel George Armstrong Custer led the Seventh Cavalry in a dawn attack on Black Kettle's village, killing the chief, his wife, and dozens of others. Custer reported the engagement as a military victory. The army destroyed the village's winter food supply, slaughtered hundreds of horses, and took women and children captive. The attack at the Washita was not an exception in the post–Civil War West. It was a pattern.

The story of the American West after the Civil War is one of the most consequential and contested chapters in United States history. Between roughly 1865 and 1900, the federal government, railroad corporations, mining companies, cattle ranchers, settlers, and land speculators transformed a vast continental region that had been home to hundreds of Indigenous nations for thousands of years. This transformation was rapid, violent, and irreversible. In the span of a single generation, Native nations that had organized rich political, spiritual, and economic lives across the Great Plains, the Rocky Mountains, the Great Basin, the Pacific Coast, and the Southwest were dispossessed of most of their lands, confined to reservations, subjected to assimilation campaigns, and stripped of political sovereignty they had maintained for centuries.

The transformation of the West was not simply a matter of population movement and economic development. It was organized, funded, and directed by the federal government through an interlocking system of military force, legal policy, treaty-making, treaty violation, administrative power, and ideological justification. The men who built the transcontinental railroads, slaughtered the buffalo herds, fenced the open range, and homesteaded the prairies did not act in isolation. They acted within a framework of federal land grants, federal military protection, federal Indian policy, and federal law that made dispossession both systematic and sweeping.

At the same time, the post–Civil War West attracted migrants from across the United States and the world. Chinese workers built the transcontinental railroads under dangerous conditions and then faced legal exclusion. African American settlers homesteaded on the Plains. Mexican American communities found themselves increasingly marginalized in their own ancestral homelands. Immigrant miners from Ireland, Wales, Cornwall, and eastern Europe dug for silver and copper in conditions of extreme danger and poverty. Women homesteaders worked the land alongside men or ran farms alone. The West that emerged from this era was not a blank canvas. It was a contested space that had been made and remade by many peoples, all struggling for land, livelihood, and recognition.

The mythology of the American West — the frontier, the pioneer, the cowboy, the cavalry — became one of the most powerful stories Americans told about themselves. That mythology celebrated expansion, individual heroism, and national destiny while obscuring the violence, dispossession, exploitation, and environmental destruction on which the transformed West was built. Historians have spent decades recovering the stories that western mythology suppressed: the Indigenous communities shattered by conquest, the laborers who built the railroads without recognition, the women who made homesteads function, the environmental consequences of industrial-scale agriculture and mining.

The consequences of the post–Civil War West did not end in 1900. The reservation system, the Dawes Act, the boarding schools, the destruction of Native economies and land bases — all of these policies shaped Indigenous life well into the twentieth century and continue to shape it today. The environmental transformations of that era — the plowing of the Great Plains, the draining of aquifers, the destruction of forests, the depletion of wildlife — set conditions that would produce the Dust Bowl of the 1930s and the water crises of the twenty-first century. Understanding the post–Civil War West is essential for understanding modern America.

Historical Context

To understand the transformation of the West after the Civil War, students need to understand several interconnected developments that preceded and shaped the era.

The West Before the Civil War

Before 1865, the trans-Mississippi West was not empty or uninhabited. It was home to hundreds of Indigenous nations with distinct political systems, economic practices, spiritual traditions, and territorial claims. The Great Plains were dominated by powerful equestrian nations including the Lakota Sioux, Cheyenne, Arapaho, Comanche, Kiowa, and Crow, whose cultures had been transformed over the previous two centuries by the spread of the horse from Spanish settlements in the Southwest. These nations hunted vast buffalo herds, conducted extensive trade networks, made and broke alliances, and defended their territories against both rival nations and encroaching American settlers.

The Southwest and California had been part of Mexico until the Mexican-American War (1846–1848) and the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo, which transferred an enormous swath of territory to the United States and left tens of thousands of Mexican citizens — and many Indigenous communities — under U.S. sovereignty without their consent. The Pacific Northwest was contested among Indigenous nations, British interests, and American settlers.

Prewar Federal Indian Policy

Federal policy toward Indigenous nations before the Civil War had already established destructive patterns. The Indian Removal Act of 1830 forced the relocation of dozens of eastern nations to lands west of the Mississippi River. Federal treaty-making had produced hundreds of agreements that promised Native nations territorial security, annuities, and trading rights, but most of these treaties were violated within years of their signing as settler pressure and land hunger overrode federal commitments. By 1860, it was a well-established pattern: the federal government signed treaties, settlers violated them, Congress or the Army failed to enforce them, and further dispossession followed.

The Civil War and the West

The Civil War did not leave the West untouched. In 1862, Congress passed the Pacific Railway Act and the Homestead Act — two landmark pieces of legislation that set the terms for western development for the next generation. The Civil War also produced brutal violence against Indigenous peoples even while the main armies clashed in the East. In 1862, the Dakota War in Minnesota followed the failure of the federal government to deliver treaty-promised food to starving Dakota communities. In 1864, the Sand Creek Massacre in Colorado killed more than 150 peaceful Cheyenne and Arapaho.

The Buffalo Economy

For the nations of the Great Plains, the American bison was not simply a food source — it was the foundation of an entire way of life. Buffalo provided food, clothing, shelter, tools, fuel, and spiritual significance. The great herds that stretched across the Plains numbered in the tens of millions before the Civil War. The destruction of those herds — which accelerated dramatically after 1865 through commercial hunting for hides and tongues — was not simply an ecological disaster. It was a deliberate or at least consciously facilitated strategy for ending Plains Indian resistance. Army generals including Philip Sheridan openly encouraged the slaughter of the buffalo, recognizing that destroying the herds would destroy the economic independence of the Plains nations and force their confinement to reservations.

Section 33.1

The West After the Civil War

When the Civil War ended in April 1865, the United States turned enormous political and military attention toward the lands west of the Mississippi River. The reasons were economic, political, cultural, and ideological. The federal government had promised railroads across the continent, land to settlers, and the eventual organization of western territories into states. Industrialists saw western resources — timber, minerals, cattle, grain — as the raw materials of national prosperity. Politicians saw western settlement as a solution to social tensions and economic pressures in the East. Ideologists saw westward expansion as the fulfillment of American destiny.

What stood between these ambitions and their realization were the Indigenous nations of the West, whose communities occupied the lands that Americans wanted, whose resistance could be formidable, and whose political independence challenged the vision of a unified national territory under federal authority.

The federal government in 1865 faced a genuine dilemma about how to handle the "Indian problem," as officials called it. The old policy of removal — pushing Native nations westward to make room for settlement — had run out of room. There was no further "west" to remove people to. The choices were essentially three: recognize Native territorial sovereignty and limit white settlement, concentrate Native peoples on reduced reservations while opening most of the West to settlement, or pursue outright extermination. Federal policy chose the middle path, though in practice the reservation system often shaded into de facto extermination through starvation, disease, and violence.

The federal apparatus for western development was substantial. The Bureau of Indian Affairs, housed first in the War Department and later in the Interior Department, managed the reservation system through a network of Indian agents who distributed annuities, enforced federal rules, and managed — often corruptly — the daily lives of reservation communities. The U.S. Army, freshly seasoned by four years of industrial warfare, was redeployed to the West to protect settlers, guard railroad construction crews, enforce treaty boundaries when it suited policy, and conduct punitive campaigns against Native resistance. Congress passed land laws that facilitated rapid transfer of the public domain into private hands — homestead entries, railroad land grants, mining claims, and timber sales.

For the Indigenous nations of the Great Plains and the broader West, the years after 1865 brought catastrophic change at a pace that made organized political response extraordinarily difficult. Nations that had successfully maintained their territorial independence for centuries found themselves confronting not just settlers but the full institutional capacity of an industrializing nation-state. The federal government could mobilize thousands of soldiers, build roads and railroads, supply distant military posts, and sustain military campaigns across vast distances. It could also manipulate food supply by encouraging the destruction of the buffalo herds that Plains nations depended on for survival.

The West after the Civil War was also shaped by the experiences of non-Native people who were not wealthy or powerful. Homesteaders faced brutal conditions on the Great Plains — drought, grasshopper plagues, isolation, debt, and the psychological weight of distance from family and community. Railroad workers, many of them Chinese or Irish immigrants, labored in conditions of extreme danger for wages barely sufficient to survive. African American "Exodusters" fled the violent collapse of Reconstruction to homestead in Kansas. Mexican Americans in the former territories of the Southwest saw their land titles, political rights, and community standing eroded by Anglo American legal and economic power. The West was never a simple story of white pioneer triumph. It was a complex collision of peoples, interests, and ambitions that produced suffering and opportunity in unequal measure.

Section 33.2

Railroads, Mining, and Settlement

No single development transformed the post–Civil War West more rapidly or completely than the transcontinental railroad. The Pacific Railway Act of 1862 created two railroad corporations to build the line: the Union Pacific, which would build westward from Omaha, Nebraska, and the Central Pacific, which would build eastward from Sacramento, California. Congress provided extraordinary federal support: loans of government bonds worth $16,000 to $48,000 per mile of track depending on terrain, and land grants of ten square miles for every mile of track completed — alternating sections in a checkerboard pattern extending twenty miles on either side of the right-of-way. The Union Pacific and Central Pacific together received approximately 20 million acres of federal land under the original acts. Later railroads — the Northern Pacific, Southern Pacific, and Atchison, Topeka and Santa Fe — received tens of millions of acres more.

The construction itself was a feat of industrial organization that depended on the labor of workers who received little recognition and less reward. The Central Pacific relied heavily on Chinese immigrant workers — at peak employment, some 12,000 to 15,000 Chinese men, representing perhaps 80 to 90 percent of the Central Pacific workforce — who performed the most dangerous work of blasting through the Sierra Nevada Mountains. They used nitroglycerin and black powder to bore tunnels through solid granite, hanging in wicker baskets over cliff faces to set charges that could kill without warning. They were paid less than white workers for the same labor, housed in segregated camps, and excluded from the celebratory photographs taken when the line was completed. The Union Pacific relied on Irish immigrant workers and Civil War veterans, also performing dangerous labor in the face of summer heat, winter cold, and occasional violence.

The completion of the first transcontinental railroad at Promontory Summit, Utah, on May 10, 1869, was celebrated across the country. A golden spike joined the two lines. Telegraph messages carried the news to cities from Boston to San Francisco. The ceremony represented, in the minds of American leaders and the press, the triumph of civilization, commerce, and national unity. What it also represented — though this was not discussed at the celebration — was the decisive acceleration of Native dispossession. The railroad sliced through territories guaranteed to Native nations by treaties. It brought settlers in numbers that previous transportation could never have sustained. It brought hunters who killed the buffalo for hides with industrial efficiency. And it brought U.S. Army troops who could be rapidly deployed to suppress resistance.

Mining drove settlement in specific regions before and alongside the railroad. The California Gold Rush of 1849 had established the pattern: the discovery of precious metals produced explosive population growth, environmental destruction, and conflict with Indigenous peoples. In the decades after the Civil War, significant mineral discoveries extended across the West. Silver was found in the Comstock Lode in Nevada in 1859. Gold was discovered in the Black Hills of Dakota Territory in 1874 — in territory guaranteed to the Lakota Sioux by the Fort Laramie Treaty of 1868. Copper was mined on a massive scale in Montana and Arizona.

Mining towns rose and fell with the fortunes of their deposits. They attracted workers from across the globe: Cornish hard-rock miners, Chinese laborers, Mexican American and Chilean workers, Italian immigrants, and native-born Americans. They also attracted saloons, brothels, merchants, lawyers, and land speculators. When deposits ran out, towns were abandoned, leaving behind landscapes scarred by open pits, tailings piles, and mercury and arsenic contamination that persists to the present. Hydraulic mining in California — which directed powerful water jets against hillsides to expose gold-bearing gravel — produced enormous quantities of sediment that clogged rivers, destroyed farmland downstream, and filled San Francisco Bay.

Section 33.3

Cattle, Farming, and Homesteading

The Great Plains that stretched from the Missouri River to the Rocky Mountains presented both opportunity and obstacle to the settlers who arrived in growing numbers after the Civil War. The Homestead Act of 1862 promised 160 acres to any adult citizen or intended citizen who paid a filing fee of $18 and lived on and improved the land for five years. The act was conceived in the tradition of the Jeffersonian yeoman farmer — the ideal of independent smallholders sustaining democratic society through honest agricultural labor. In practice, the Homestead Act had significant limitations. On the semi-arid Great Plains, 160 acres was often insufficient for profitable farming. Many of the most productive lands had already been claimed by railroad grants, meaning the best land available to homesteaders was often the land the railroads had rejected or were selling at inflated prices.

The cattle industry was the first major American enterprise to exploit the postwar Plains on a large scale. Longhorn cattle that had multiplied in the brush country of Texas during the Civil War could be driven north to Kansas railheads and sold for prices far higher than what Texas markets offered. Between 1867 and 1885, an estimated 10 million cattle were driven northward along the Chisholm, Western, and Goodnight-Loving Trails in what became known as the Long Drive. The Long Drive created the iconic figure of the American cowboy — romanticized in dime novels and later in film as the quintessential western hero, but in reality a working-class laborer who faced dangerous conditions, earned modest wages, and included significant numbers of African American and Mexican American (vaquero) workers whose contributions were largely erased from the mythology.

African Americans, many of them former slaves or the children of slaves who had fled the violent collapse of Reconstruction in the South, constituted perhaps one quarter to one third of the cowboy workforce. They performed the same dangerous work as white cowboys but faced racial discrimination in pay, opportunity, and historical memory. Mexican American vaqueros, whose horsemanship and cattle-handling skills had been developed over generations in the Spanish and Mexican ranching traditions of the Southwest, taught Anglo ranchers much of what they knew about handling cattle. The myth of the cowboy as exclusively white erased both groups from the history of an industry they had built.

The invention of barbed wire in 1874 by Joseph Glidden began the slow enclosure of the open range as ranchers and farmers alike fenced their claims. The severe winter of 1886–1887, which killed millions of cattle across the northern Plains, ended the era of large-scale open-range ranching and accelerated the transition to fenced operations with hay-fed cattle. The ecological transformation wrought by homesteaders was equally profound. The plowing of the prairie — turning over the deep-rooted sod that had bound the soil through drought cycles for millennia — created highly productive wheat and corn fields in wet years but exposed bare soil to wind erosion in dry years. By the 1930s, the combination of drought and decades of intensive plowing would produce the Dust Bowl — the largest ecological disaster in American history, driven in significant part by farming practices developed during the homestead era.

Homesteaders who settled the Plains faced an extraordinary range of hardships. The absence of timber required creative solutions: sod houses, cut from the dense root-mat of prairie grass, became the characteristic dwelling. Women on homesteads carried the vast burden of domestic labor: cooking over open fires or in iron stoves, making and maintaining clothing, preserving food, managing gardens, caring for children and elderly family members, and frequently participating in field work as well. Yet homesteaders also created communities around churches, schools, county fairs, and mutual aid. Women's networks were essential to community life, providing labor exchange during harvests and sickness, organizing social events, and maintaining the informal social structures that held rural communities together.

Section 33.4

Native Nations and U.S. Expansion

The post–Civil War era brought the most sustained and coordinated assault on Native territorial sovereignty in the history of the United States. Federal policy makers lumped the enormous diversity of Indigenous nations into a single "Indian problem" and sought uniform solutions, which inevitably failed to account for the enormous differences among Native communities.

The Peace Policy, promoted by President Ulysses Grant beginning in 1869, attempted to reform the reservation system by placing it under the control of religious groups rather than political appointees. Grant divided the country's reservations among various Protestant denominations and the Catholic Church. The stated goal was to "civilize" and Christianize Native peoples while protecting them from the worst abuses of the patronage system. The Peace Policy was a failure on multiple fronts: it did not stop military campaigns, did not eliminate corruption, and was premised on the fundamental assumption that Native cultures were inferior and needed to be eliminated in favor of Euro-American Christian culture.

For the nations of the Great Plains, the critical development of the post–Civil War era was the negotiation of major treaties in 1867 and 1868. The Medicine Lodge Treaty of 1867 concentrated the Southern Plains nations — Cheyenne, Arapaho, Comanche, and Kiowa — on reservations in Indian Territory. The Fort Laramie Treaty of 1868 recognized Lakota Sioux control of a large territory including the Black Hills and guaranteed the Lakota access to the Powder River country in Montana as "unceded Indian territory." Both treaties were violated almost immediately. Most catastrophically, a federal military expedition led by Custer entered the Black Hills in 1874 and confirmed the presence of gold deposits, touching off a rush that the federal government failed to stop and then essentially facilitated by declaring treaty rights void when the Lakota refused to sell.

The federal government's willingness to violate its own treaty commitments was not incidental — it was fundamental. Treaties with Native nations were ratified by the Senate and carried the same legal weight as international treaties. Federal officials knew when they were violating treaties. They did it anyway because settler demand for land and resources consistently outweighed the government's commitment to treaty obligations. Congress itself recognized the unsustainability of the treaty system and in 1871 passed legislation declaring that the United States would no longer make treaties with Indian nations — a formal abandonment of the legal framework of negotiated agreement in favor of unilateral federal authority.

The Comanche Empire of the Southern Plains had dominated Texas and the surrounding region for more than a century before American expansion reached its fullest pressure. The Red River War of 1874–1875 ended Comanche independence on the southern Plains. Colonel Ranald Mackenzie's forces conducted relentless winter campaigns, destroying Comanche villages and food supplies. The Battle of Palo Duro Canyon in September 1874 was decisive: Mackenzie's forces destroyed the Comanche horse herd — some 1,400 horses — and all winter food stores. Without horses or food as winter approached, the surviving Comanche bands had no choice but to surrender.

Historical Vocabulary
Treaty

A treaty is a formal agreement between sovereign political entities. Under the U.S. Constitution, treaties negotiated by the executive branch and ratified by the Senate are the "supreme law of the land," taking precedence over state laws and ordinary congressional legislation.

Between 1778 and 1871, the United States government negotiated approximately 375 treaties with Native nations. These treaties were the primary mechanism through which the U.S. acquired Native land — through purchase, cession in exchange for annuities and services, or post-conflict settlement. Nearly every treaty made with Native nations was violated by the United States through settler encroachment, congressional legislation, executive reinterpretation, or outright military force.

In 1871, Congress ended treaty-making with Native nations through a rider to an appropriations bill. This did not abrogate existing treaties, which remain legally binding today, but it ended the formal process and made future agreements subject to ordinary congressional legislation. When the Lakota Sioux Nation presses legal claims regarding the Black Hills, or when Pacific Northwest tribes assert treaty fishing rights, they invoke legal agreements that remain part of the supreme law of the land.

Section 33.5

Treaty Violations and Military Conflict

The pattern of federal treaty violation and the military campaigns it precipitated repeated itself across the West between 1865 and 1890. Each episode followed a similar arc: treaty agreement, settler violation, federal inaction, Native resistance, military campaign, defeat, and further land loss. Understanding this pattern as systematic, rather than as a series of unrelated episodes, is essential.

Red Cloud's War (1866–1868) demonstrated that the United States Army could lose. The Bozeman Trail, which ran through the heart of Lakota and Cheyenne territory in Wyoming and Montana, was opened to gold seekers heading for mines in Montana. Red Cloud, an Oglala Lakota leader, organized a broad coalition of Lakota, Cheyenne, and Arapaho warriors who besieged the forts and cut off their supply lines. The campaign culminated in the Fetterman Fight of December 21, 1866, when a Lakota decoy force led a U.S. Army detachment of 80 soldiers into an ambush. No soldier survived. The Fort Laramie Treaty of 1868 that followed was a diplomatic victory for the Lakota: the United States agreed to abandon the Bozeman Trail forts, which Red Cloud insisted be destroyed before he would sign.

The Great Sioux War of 1876–1877 demonstrated the limits of even such a remarkable victory. The discovery of gold in the Black Hills brought thousands of miners onto Lakota land in violation of the Fort Laramie Treaty. The Army's strategy for the 1876 campaign was a three-pronged movement converging on the Powder River country where Sitting Bull, Crazy Horse, and thousands of Lakota and Cheyenne had gathered. The Battle of Little Bighorn on June 25–26, 1876, resulted when Custer attacked a Lakota and Cheyenne encampment far larger than his scouts had indicated. Custer divided his force and led one battalion in a direct charge against the village. He and all 210 men under his direct command were killed.

The Nez Perce War of 1877 produced one of the most remarkable military retreats in American history. The Nez Perce of eastern Oregon and Idaho had maintained peaceful relations with the United States for decades but were ordered onto a greatly reduced reservation. Chief Joseph and other leaders decided to flee to Canada rather than surrender. The Nez Perce War lasted four months and covered approximately 1,170 miles across Idaho, Wyoming, and Montana. The band of some 700 people — including women, children, and elderly — fought off Army pursuit through a series of engagements that demonstrated remarkable tactical skill. They were finally stopped just 40 miles from the Canadian border. Chief Joseph's surrender speech became one of the most cited statements of Indigenous resistance: he was tired of fighting, his chiefs were killed, his little children were freezing to death — from that day forward, he would fight no more forever.

The Apache Wars in the Southwest extended the era of armed resistance into the 1880s. Geronimo's final campaign in 1885–1886 involved a band of fewer than 40 Apache fighters who evaded 5,000 U.S. troops and thousands of Mexican soldiers for months in the mountains of Chihuahua. When Geronimo finally surrendered in September 1886, he and approximately 450 Apache — including scouts who had served the U.S. Army — were transported to prison in Florida, then to Alabama, and eventually to Fort Sill, Oklahoma. Geronimo died in 1909 having never been allowed to return to Arizona.

Section 33.6

Reservation Policy

The reservation system was the primary administrative mechanism through which the federal government managed Indigenous peoples after the Civil War. The territories set aside as reservations were frequently poor in resources compared to Native peoples' original homelands. The Great Sioux Reservation, established by the Fort Laramie Treaty of 1868, was eventually reduced through a series of agreements — some coerced, some fraudulent — from roughly 60 million acres to a much smaller fraction by 1890. Indian Territory in present-day Oklahoma, originally conceived as a permanent space for removed eastern nations, was invaded by settlers, carved up by railroads, and ultimately dissolved when Oklahoma became a state in 1907.

Life on reservations in the late nineteenth century was marked by poverty, dependency, and the systematic destruction of the cultural and economic systems that had sustained Native communities. The annuity system, by which the federal government was supposed to provide food, tools, clothing, and other goods promised in treaties, was chronically dysfunctional. Annuities were often delayed for months or years, reduced by Congress, stolen by corrupt agents, or delivered in forms that were useless or insulting. Reservation communities that had fed themselves through buffalo hunting, agricultural cultivation, or fishing found themselves dependent on government rations that were frequently insufficient. Hunger was a constant feature of reservation life.

The federal government's relationship with reservation communities was administered through the Bureau of Indian Affairs and its network of Indian agents. Indian agents were political appointments, often going to men who had no knowledge of Native languages, cultures, or needs and who had been rewarded for political loyalty rather than administrative competence. Many agents systematically defrauded their charges by receiving payment for goods never delivered, selling off reservation resources for personal profit, or manipulating the annuity rolls.

The Office of Indian Affairs attempted to reshape reservation life through regulations that criminalized traditional practices. The Indian Religious Crimes Code, adopted by the Bureau of Indian Affairs in 1883, prohibited traditional dances, healing ceremonies, giveaway ceremonies, and related practices, with offenders subject to punishment by withholding of rations. This represented a direct attack on the spiritual and cultural foundations of Native community life. Communities found ways to continue many ceremonies in secret, adapting practices to hide them from agency surveillance.

The reservation system as it functioned in the late nineteenth century was less a solution to the "Indian problem" than an ongoing mechanism of dispossession, control, and cultural destruction. Yet it also became the space within which Native communities maintained family ties, cultural traditions, political organization, and resistance to assimilation. Reservations were sites of enormous suffering and of remarkable endurance.

Historical Vocabulary
Sovereignty

Sovereignty refers to supreme political authority — the power to govern without external control. In the landmark case Worcester v. Georgia (1832), Chief Justice John Marshall described Native nations as "domestic dependent sovereigns" — recognizing their political authority and right to self-government while acknowledging their dependence on the federal government.

After the Civil War, federal policy consistently moved away from respecting even this limited sovereignty. The 1871 act ending treaty-making was one step. Courts began to uphold federal plenary power over Native affairs — the idea that Congress could do essentially anything it wanted with regard to Indian affairs, overriding both treaty commitments and prior court decisions. The Dawes Act and the allotment system further eroded tribal sovereignty by imposing individual land ownership in place of communal tenure.

The long struggle to restore and protect Native sovereignty did not end in the nineteenth century. The Indian Reorganization Act of 1934, the tribal self-determination movements of the 1960s and 1970s, and the Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act of 1975 all represented efforts to reverse the erosion of Indigenous sovereignty. Contemporary debates over tribal gaming rights, water rights, and federal trust responsibilities all turn on questions of sovereignty first contested in the post–Civil War era.

Section 33.7

Battle of Little Bighorn and Wounded Knee

The Battle of Little Bighorn on June 25–26, 1876, and the Wounded Knee Massacre on December 29, 1890, mark the beginning and end of the era of large-scale armed Indigenous resistance on the Great Plains. The Battle of Little Bighorn was the largest military defeat ever suffered by the United States Army at the hands of an Indigenous force. Custer chose to attack a massive Lakota and Cheyenne encampment — possibly the largest gathering of Plains Indians in the nineteenth century, with an estimated 7,000 to 10,000 people and perhaps 2,000 to 2,500 warriors — without waiting for reinforcing columns. The result was catastrophic: Custer and the five companies he led directly were killed to the last man.

The response in the East was explosive. "MASSACRE" screamed the headlines. Congress rapidly appropriated funds for more soldiers and authorized the Army to reassign military management of the Sioux agencies. The Army moved aggressively through the following winter, relentlessly attacking Lakota and Cheyenne villages. By the following spring, even the most resistant leaders had surrendered or fled. Custer himself became a cultural phenomenon. His "Last Stand" was painted, dramatized, novelized, and eventually filmed hundreds of times. The Custer myth became a central story of the American West — the doomed hero facing overwhelming savage forces. This myth required erasing the facts: that Custer attacked in violation of his orders, that the Lakota and Cheyenne were defending their legal treaty lands, and that the battle was a Native victory and an American defeat.

The Wounded Knee Massacre of December 29, 1890, brought the era of large-scale armed resistance to its end. By the late 1880s, the conditions on the Sioux reservations had grown desperate. The federal government had pressured the Lakota into a further land cession in 1889, opening millions of acres to settlement and reducing rations at the same time. The Ghost Dance religion, founded by Wovoka and spreading rapidly, was viewed with alarm by federal agents who misunderstood it as preparation for armed uprising. Federal Indian agents called for military intervention. Sitting Bull, seen by agents as a potential focus of resistance, was ordered arrested and killed during the attempt on December 15, 1890.

Several hundred of Sitting Bull's followers joined the band of Chief Big Foot (Spotted Elk), who was already moving toward Pine Ridge Reservation. The Seventh Cavalry intercepted Big Foot's band — some 700 people including women, children, and elderly — and escorted them to a camp at Wounded Knee Creek. Big Foot himself was ill with pneumonia. On the morning of December 29, soldiers attempted to disarm the group. A shot was fired. The soldiers opened fire with rifles and with four Hotchkiss mountain guns that fired explosive shells at nearly fifty per minute. When the firing stopped, between 250 and 300 Lakota were dead, including Big Foot and many women and children who had fled across the creek and were pursued and killed. Twenty-five soldiers died, most likely from friendly fire. The Army awarded twenty Medals of Honor to soldiers who participated — an award that many Native people and historians today regard as one of American history's greatest injustices. Wounded Knee was not a battle. It was a massacre of a sick, hungry, and largely unarmed community.

Section 33.8

Boarding Schools and Cultural Assimilation

If military conquest was the sword of federal Indian policy, cultural assimilation was the tool intended to complete the work of eliminating Indigenous peoples as distinct communities. The assimilation project reached its fullest expression in the network of federal Indian boarding schools that spread across the country after 1879, most infamously exemplified by the Carlisle Indian Industrial School in Pennsylvania.

The founding logic of the boarding school system was expressed in the motto of Richard Henry Pratt, the Army officer who founded Carlisle: "Kill the Indian, save the man." Pratt and reformers who shared his view believed — with apparent sincerity — that Native peoples could be rescued from what they viewed as a "savage" way of life and incorporated into American civilization through education. They further believed that this could only be accomplished by removing children from their families and communities, cutting off their access to Native languages and cultural practices, and immersing them in Euro-American language, religion, work, and social norms.

The boarding school system grew rapidly in the 1880s and 1890s. By 1900, the federal government operated 25 off-reservation boarding schools and supported many more reservation and day schools. Thousands of Native children, ranging in age from roughly 5 to 18, were enrolled — sometimes voluntarily with family consent, sometimes through coercive pressure, and sometimes through outright seizure. Children were stripped of their clothing and given school uniforms. Their hair was cut — an act of profound violation in many Native cultures, where hair cutting was associated with mourning and death. They were given English names and forbidden to speak their Native languages; children caught speaking their home languages were punished, often physically.

The curriculum at boarding schools was designed to produce workers who could be economically useful in the dominant society. Boys received training in trades — blacksmithing, carpentry, farming, shoemaking. Girls received training in domestic service — cooking, sewing, laundry, cleaning. Many students who completed their time at boarding schools returned to their reservations to find that their skills were of limited use in reservation economies, while their disrupted connection to their communities, languages, and cultures left them in painful in-between positions.

The physical conditions at many boarding schools were dangerous. Tuberculosis and other infectious diseases spread rapidly through crowded dormitories. Many schools had inadequate food, heating, and medical care. Children died at boarding schools at rates exceeding those of the general population; many died far from home and were buried in school cemeteries. At Carlisle alone, an estimated 186 children died and were buried on school grounds. The psychological damage was pervasive and multi-generational. Children punished for speaking their languages sometimes lost fluency, creating barriers between themselves and elders when they returned home. Yet Native children and families were not simply passive recipients of institutional violence. Children found ways to speak their languages in secret. They took from the educational experience what was useful — English literacy, knowledge of American law — while resisting cultural erasure in ways both visible and invisible.

In 2021 and 2022, Secretary of the Interior Deb Haaland — herself a member of the Laguna Pueblo Nation and the first Native American to serve as a cabinet secretary — authorized the Federal Indian Boarding School Initiative Investigative Report, which identified 408 schools and nearly 54 burial sites associated with the boarding school era and documented the systematic abuse and deaths of Native children under federal policy.

Section 33.9

Dawes Act, Allotment, and Land Loss

The Dawes Severalty Act of 1887 was the capstone of the federal assimilation project. If the boarding schools aimed to eliminate Indigenous identity through the transformation of individual children, the Dawes Act aimed to eliminate the tribal land base — the collective territory that sustained community life — by converting communal landholding into individual private property and opening the remaining "surplus" land to white settlement.

The act was sponsored by Senator Henry Dawes of Massachusetts, a reformer who genuinely believed he was acting in the best interests of Native peoples. Dawes and the reformers who supported allotment believed that private land ownership was essential to civilization — that men who owned their own land would have the incentive to improve it, become economically self-sufficient, and gradually assimilate into American society. The argument ignored the fact that many Indigenous nations had developed sophisticated systems of communal land management that had sustained their communities for centuries, and that the primary purpose of allotment — from the perspective of land-hungry settlers and railroad corporations — was to open Native lands to white acquisition.

The Dawes Act authorized the President to survey tribal lands and divide them into individual allotments: 160 acres for family heads, 80 acres for unmarried adults. Allotted individuals would become U.S. citizens and would hold their land in trust by the federal government for 25 years, after which they would receive full title and could sell it. Once allotments were made, the remaining land — the "surplus" — would be purchased from the tribe and opened to non-Indian settlement.

The results were catastrophic for Native land holdings. Before the Dawes Act, Native nations held approximately 138 million acres. By 1934, when the Indian Reorganization Act reversed the allotment policy, that total had been reduced to approximately 48 million acres — a loss of roughly 90 million acres in less than 50 years. The land was lost through multiple mechanisms: forced sales of "surplus" land, individual allottees selling their parcels through fraud or desperation, loss through tax sales when allottees could not pay property taxes, and speculators claiming land as fees. Every mechanism of the American land market was used to transfer Native-owned land into non-Native hands.

The allotment era left Native communities economically devastated, politically fragmented, and geographically scattered. Reservations that had been coherent community spaces became checkerboard patterns of Native allotments, sold allotments, and non-Indian-owned land — making it extremely difficult for tribal governments to function, provide services, or enforce tribal law. The "checker-boarding" of reservation land remains a significant legal and administrative challenge for tribal governments today. In 2009, the federal government settled a class action lawsuit — Cobell v. Salazar — for $3.4 billion to compensate Native individuals for federal mismanagement of allotment trust accounts, an acknowledgment of the corruption and incompetence that had accompanied the allotment system since its inception.

Historical Debate

Was the Dawes Act Primarily a Reform Measure or a Land Grab?

The question: How should historians understand the Dawes Act of 1887? Was it primarily motivated by genuine humanitarian concern for Native peoples, as its sponsors claimed, or was it fundamentally a mechanism for transferring Native land to non-Native owners?

Interpretation A — The Dawes Act as Misguided Reform: Some historians emphasize the genuine humanitarian motivations of the act's supporters. Senator Dawes and the "Friends of the Indian" believed that communal land ownership prevented Native peoples from developing individual initiative and economic self-sufficiency. They saw allotment as a path to legal equality and assimilation. Their belief in the inferiority of Native cultures was wrong and caused enormous harm, but their intentions, historians in this camp argue, should be understood as characteristic of their era's reformist thought rather than as cynical cover for theft.
Interpretation B — The Dawes Act as Organized Land Theft: Other historians, including many Native scholars, argue that the humanitarian framing of the Dawes Act was at best a rationalization and at worst deliberate cover for the largest transfer of Native land to non-Native ownership since the colonial era. The act was actively supported by railroad companies, land speculators, and settlers who stood to benefit directly from the opening of "surplus" lands. The "surplus" mechanism was built into the act from the beginning — meaning that opening Native land to white settlement was not an unintended consequence but a core purpose.

Evidence: Evidence supporting the reform interpretation includes the records of Lake Mohonk conferences and the genuine belief — expressed consistently — that individual land ownership was necessary for Native peoples to achieve legal equality. Evidence supporting the dispossession interpretation includes the correlation between allotment and land loss (90 million acres in 47 years), the active support of land-hungry interests for the legislation, the building of the "surplus" mechanism into the act, and the documented corruption of the allotment process.

Why it matters: How we interpret the Dawes Act shapes how we understand the relationship between stated humanitarian intentions and actual outcomes in federal Indian policy — and more broadly, whether well-intentioned reformers can produce catastrophic harm without being cynical.

Historical Vocabulary
Allotment

Allotment refers to the federal policy, formalized by the Dawes Severalty Act of 1887, of dividing communally held tribal land into individual parcels assigned to tribal members, with remaining "surplus" land opened to non-Indian settlement. Before the Dawes Act, Native nations held approximately 138 million acres. By 1934, that total had been reduced to approximately 48 million acres — a loss of 90 million acres.

The allotment policy was grounded in a particular ideology of land and civilization: that individual land ownership was a prerequisite for economic progress, and that communal land tenure kept Native peoples in a state of "savagery." In practice, allotment was a massive land transfer that served the interests of settlers, railroad corporations, and land speculators.

The long-term effects of allotment included not just land loss but the fragmentation of reservation geographies — the "checker-boarding" of Native and non-Native-owned parcels within reservation boundaries — that remains a significant obstacle to tribal governance and economic development today. The Indian Reorganization Act of 1934 ended allotment and authorized tribes to organize governments and acquire additional land, but could not undo the scale of loss.

Section 33.10

Chapter Review and Western Expansion Evidence Study

The transformation of the American West in the decades after the Civil War was one of the most consequential periods in United States history. Through the combined forces of railroad construction, commercial mining, cattle ranching, homestead farming, military conquest, reservation policy, and cultural assimilation programs, the federal government and its private sector partners fundamentally restructured an enormous region — displacing, confining, and attempting to assimilate hundreds of Indigenous nations in the process.

Throughout this transformation, Native peoples were not passive victims. They resisted through military action, diplomatic negotiation, legal challenge, spiritual revival, cultural persistence, and the patient maintenance of community bonds under conditions of extraordinary pressure. The resistance of figures like Sitting Bull, Crazy Horse, Red Cloud, Chief Joseph, and Geronimo demonstrated that Indigenous nations could inflict significant military defeats on the United States. The legal advocacy of Cherokee leaders, the diplomacy of Plains treaty negotiators, and the memoir-writing of survivors like Luther Standing Bear demonstrated that resistance took many forms beyond military combat.

The West that emerged from this era was also the product of the labor and experience of many non-Native peoples whose stories complicate the pioneer mythology. Chinese railroad workers built the western rail system under conditions of discrimination and danger. African American cowboys rode the Long Drives and homesteaded the Plains. Mexican American communities maintained their cultural identities in the face of Anglo-American legal and economic displacement. Immigrant miners from across Europe dug the silver and copper. Women homesteaders managed farms, raised families, and built communities in conditions of isolation and hardship. The West was never a simple or singular story.

Multiple Perspectives

Western Expansion After the Civil War

Lakota Sioux families on the Great Sioux Reservation: For Lakota people who lived through the 1870s and 1880s, the post–Civil War era was one of catastrophic loss. In the space of two decades, the great buffalo herds that had sustained their economy and culture were destroyed. The Black Hills, sacred as Paha Sapa, were seized in violation of the Fort Laramie Treaty. Military campaigns destroyed the political and military capacity of Lakota resistance. Leaders were killed. Reservation confinement brought hunger, disease, dependency, and the systematic suppression of language, religion, and cultural practice. Children were taken to boarding schools far from home.
A homesteader family in Kansas or Nebraska: For a German immigrant family that arrived in Kansas in the 1870s, the post–Civil War West offered both extraordinary hardship and genuine opportunity. They filed a homestead claim, built a soddie, broke the sod with a horse-drawn plow, and began the long struggle to make the land profitable. They suffered through drought years, grasshopper plagues, and debt. But they built a community — a church, a school, a county government. The land they worked was, from their perspective, legitimately theirs under American law; they knew little of the treaty system and thought of themselves as participants in the American dream.
A Chinese railroad worker on the Central Pacific: For a Chinese immigrant worker, the West was a place of dangerous labor, racial exclusion, and uncertain future. He performed the most dangerous tasks of tunnel blasting through the Sierra Nevada, was paid less than white workers for the same work, was housed in segregated camps, and was excluded from the celebratory photographs when the transcontinental line was completed. The Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882 would then formally close the door to Chinese immigration. His contribution to building the economic infrastructure of the American West was largely erased from national memory for more than a century.
A white settler in Texas or Oklahoma: For a white settler moving into former Comanche ranges or Indian Territory in the 1880s and 1890s, the West offered land that the federal government had made available and the railroad had made accessible. The Oklahoma Land Rush of 1889 was experienced by participants as adventure and opportunity. The profound injustice of what had made that land available — the dissolution of treaty rights and the coerced sale of Native land — was not part of their understanding.

What this comparison reveals: These perspectives do not all deserve equal moral weight. The Lakota perspective accurately reflects the consequences of federal policy: treaty violation, military conquest, and systematic dispossession. Understanding multiple perspectives does not mean treating all perspectives as equally valid — it means understanding how different people experienced the same historical forces differently, and why.

Constitutional Connection

Federal Power, Native Sovereignty, and the Constitution

The post–Civil War transformation of the West raised fundamental questions about the relationship between federal power, Native sovereignty, and constitutional principles. The Supreme Court's foundational trilogy of Indian law cases — Johnson v. M'Intosh (1823), Cherokee Nation v. Georgia (1831), and Worcester v. Georgia (1832) — had established that Native nations were "domestic dependent sovereigns" with the right to self-government within their territories.

The post–Civil War era systematically eroded these principles without formally overturning them. In United States v. Kagama (1886), the Supreme Court upheld federal criminal jurisdiction over crimes committed by Native peoples on reservations, reasoning that federal power derived from the practical reality of Native dependence — circular logic that allowed Congress to claim authority over Native peoples because Native peoples were vulnerable.

In Lone Wolf v. Hitchcock (1903), the Supreme Court upheld Congress's power to unilaterally abrogate treaty provisions and dispose of tribal lands, even in violation of the explicit terms of a ratified treaty. Legal scholars today consider Lone Wolf one of the most destructive Supreme Court decisions in the history of American Indian law. Contemporary federal Indian law remains shaped by the accumulated weight of nineteenth-century legislation and court decisions. Recent decades have seen some restoration of tribal jurisdiction and recognition of treaty rights — including fishing rights, water rights, and gaming rights — but these gains have been hard-won and remain contested.

Cause and Effect

The Destruction of the Buffalo Herds

Long-Term Causes

Industrial capacity of American commercial hunting combined with railroad access to formerly remote Plains regions; eastern tanneries' demand for buffalo hides for leather goods and industrial belts; Army generals' deliberate encouragement of hide-hunting as a strategy to destroy the subsistence base of Plains nations.

Short-Term Causes

Commercial demand for buffalo hides and tongues made hide hunting profitable; long-range rifles allowed a skilled hunter to kill dozens of animals daily; railroad access to the Plains interior made large-scale hide transport economically viable.

The Transformation

The estimated North American bison population, which stood at perhaps 30 to 60 million animals before European contact, was reduced to approximately 1,000 animals by 1889. The southern herd was essentially gone by 1880; the northern herd followed by 1884.

Long-Term Effects

Destruction of the subsistence base of every Plains Indian nation; forced reservation confinement through starvation; ecological disruption of the Plains grassland ecosystem; multi-generational cultural loss; twenty-first-century bison restoration on tribal lands as both ecological and cultural project.

Historical complexity: The destruction of the buffalo cannot be attributed to a single cause or set of actors. Commercial hunters made economic decisions. Railroad companies provided transportation. Eastern manufacturers created the market. Army generals encouraged the hunting. Congress declined to pass bison protection legislation. The destruction was a systemic outcome produced by many decisions across many institutions.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Native Life Before and After the Reservation Era

What Changed

  • The buffalo economy and seasonal migration patterns that had sustained Plains nations for centuries were destroyed within a generation
  • Freedom of movement replaced by reservation confinement on fixed, shrinking land bases
  • Traditional governance structures displaced or criminalized by the federal agency system
  • Ceremonies and spiritual practices suppressed by the Indian Religious Crimes Code
  • Children removed to boarding schools, disrupting intergenerational transmission of language and cultural knowledge
  • Approximately 90 million acres of tribal land transferred to non-Native ownership through allotment

What Stayed the Same

  • Languages survived, sometimes in reduced form, maintained deliberately by elders and communities
  • Extended family networks continued to provide mutual support and social structure
  • Core spiritual beliefs and practices continued, often in secret or transformed into survivable forms
  • Political identities of specific Native nations — Lakota, Cheyenne, Nez Perce, Apache — remained meaningful
  • The sense of collective identity, of belonging to a specific people with a specific history and relationship to the land, proved more durable than federal policy makers anticipated

Why the pattern matters: The persistence of Native cultural identity through the reservation era explains why Indigenous revitalization movements of the twentieth century were possible. The boarding school survivors who told their stories, the elders who maintained languages in secret, the communities that practiced ceremonies out of sight of agency surveillance — these acts of cultural persistence created the foundation for the political activism, legal advocacy, and cultural renewal that characterized the Native rights movement of the twentieth and twenty-first centuries.

Chapter Summary

The transformation of the American West between 1865 and 1900 was one of the most consequential and violent chapters in United States history. Driven by the combined forces of federal policy, corporate investment, and settler migration, a vast continental region was remade in the span of a single generation. The peoples who had called that region home for thousands of years were dispossessed, confined, and subjected to systematic attempts at cultural elimination.

The transcontinental railroads, completed between 1869 and the 1880s, were the central infrastructure of western transformation. They made large-scale settlement possible by connecting distant western regions to national markets, reducing transportation costs to levels that made commercial agriculture and ranching profitable, and bringing settlers in volumes that earlier transportation could never have sustained. The railroads were built with extraordinary federal subsidies — millions of acres of land grants and hundreds of millions of dollars in government bonds — and the labor of Chinese and Irish immigrant workers who received far less recognition than the corporate promoters who managed the projects.

The cattle industry and the homestead movement transformed the ecology and social landscape of the Great Plains. The Long Drive moved millions of Texas longhorns northward to Kansas railheads during the 1870s and 1880s, creating the mythological figure of the cowboy and the reality of a diverse multiracial workforce that included significant numbers of African American and Mexican American workers whose contributions western mythology erased. The Homestead Act brought millions of settlers to the Plains, where they broke prairie sod, built sod houses, and struggled against drought, debt, and distance. The plowing of the prairie — turning over deep-rooted sod that had bound the soil for millennia — would eventually contribute to the Dust Bowl of the 1930s.

For the Indigenous nations of the Great Plains and the broader West, the post–Civil War era brought catastrophic loss. The federal government signed major treaties in 1867 and 1868 that were violated almost immediately as settler pressure and resource discovery overrode federal commitments. The destruction of the buffalo herds, deliberately encouraged by Army commanders and commercially driven by eastern tanneries, eliminated the subsistence base of Plains nations within two decades. Military campaigns conducted through the 1860s, 1870s, and 1880s — using the strategy of winter warfare against civilian villages — broke the organized armed resistance of Lakota, Cheyenne, Comanche, Kiowa, Nez Perce, Apache, and other nations.

The reservation system that confined Native peoples was characterized by chronic underfunding, corruption, dependency, and the systematic suppression of cultural practice. The Ghost Dance — a peaceful spiritual movement that offered hope in conditions of despair — was met with military force and the Wounded Knee Massacre of 1890, which marked the end of the era of large-scale armed resistance. The boarding school system removed tens of thousands of Native children from their families and communities, forbade them to speak their languages, and subjected them to physical and psychological abuse in institutions designed to eliminate Native identity. Children died at boarding schools in large numbers.

The Dawes Severalty Act of 1887 completed the assault on the material foundation of Native community life. By breaking up communally held tribal land into individual allotments and opening "surplus" lands to non-Indian settlement, the act transferred approximately 90 million acres from Native to non-Native ownership in less than 50 years. Whatever the motivations of humanitarian reformers who supported allotment, the outcome was the most devastating land loss in Native American history since the colonial era.

The mythology of the American West — the frontier, the pioneer, the cowboy, the cavalry hero — was constructed simultaneously with the historical events it misrepresented. Dime novels, Wild West shows, landscape painting, and eventually film produced romantic images of western conquest that celebrated Anglo-American heroism, erased or vilified Indigenous peoples, and ignored the labor and experience of Chinese, African American, Mexican American, and immigrant workers who had built the West. This mythology served important ideological functions: it justified conquest through the language of civilization and destiny, and it produced a usable national story in which American expansion was heroic rather than violent and dispossessive.

The legacy of the post–Civil War West continues to shape American life. Reservation communities continue to face the consequences of that era's land loss, governance disruption, and cultural assault. The water rights, fishing rights, and mineral rights guaranteed by nineteenth-century treaties continue to be contested in federal courts. The boarding school era's effects on language loss, family structure, and cultural practice are addressed by contemporary Native communities engaged in revitalization efforts. Understanding this history is not optional for Americans who want to understand their country — it is foundational.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Allotment
Federal policy formalizing the Dawes Severalty Act (1887) of dividing tribal lands into individual parcels, with "surplus" land opened to non-Indian settlement; resulted in the loss of approximately 90 million acres of Native land by 1934.
American bison (buffalo)
The keystone species of the Great Plains ecosystem and the foundation of Plains Indian economies and cultures; reduced from tens of millions to near extinction by the 1880s through commercial hunting.
Annuity
Annual goods or payments promised to Native nations in exchange for land cessions under treaty agreements; chronic delays, reductions, and theft of annuity goods were major grievances on reservations.
Barbed wire
Fencing technology patented by Joseph Glidden in 1874 that enabled the enclosure of the open range and transformed the Great Plains cattle economy; ended the era of the Long Drive by the late 1880s.
Battle of Little Bighorn (1876)
Military engagement in which Lakota Sioux and Cheyenne warriors defeated and killed Custer and more than 200 soldiers of the Seventh Cavalry; the largest U.S. Army defeat at the hands of an Indigenous force in North American history.
Boarding school
Government- or church-run school where Native children were removed from their families and forbidden to speak their languages or practice their cultures; model was the Carlisle Indian Industrial School, founded 1879.
Bureau of Indian Affairs (BIA)
Federal agency responsible for administering the reservation system and managing the government's relationship with Native nations; frequently criticized for corruption, paternalism, and mismanagement.
Chinese Exclusion Act (1882)
Federal legislation prohibiting Chinese laborers from immigrating to the United States; the first major federal law restricting immigration on the basis of race and national origin.
Dawes Severalty Act (1887)
Federal legislation breaking up tribal lands into individual allotments; resulted in the transfer of 90 million acres from Native to non-Native ownership; reversed by the Indian Reorganization Act of 1934.
Exoduster
African American migrants who left the post-Reconstruction South for Kansas and other western states in the late 1870s seeking land and freedom from racial violence.
Ghost Dance
A spiritual movement founded by Wovoka around 1889, spreading among Plains nations; practitioners believed the dance would restore the dead, return the buffalo, and end American conquest; its suppression contributed to Wounded Knee.
Homestead Act (1862)
Federal legislation offering 160 acres of public land to citizens or intended citizens who paid a filing fee and lived on and improved the land for five years; attracted millions of settlers to the Great Plains.
Indian Religious Crimes Code (1883)
Bureau of Indian Affairs regulations criminalizing traditional Native dances, healing ceremonies, and related practices, with violations subject to loss of rations; a direct assault on Indigenous spiritual and cultural life.
Long Drive
The practice of driving cattle herds from Texas northward to railheads in Kansas for shipment to eastern markets; a central feature of the 1870s–1880s cattle industry, employing diverse workers including African Americans and Mexican Americans.
Open range
The unfenced grassland of the Great Plains grazed by cattle herds before the widespread adoption of barbed wire fencing; the era of the open range ended by the late 1880s.
Pacific Railway Act (1862)
Federal legislation creating the Union Pacific and Central Pacific railroad companies and providing land grants and bond subsidies for construction of the first transcontinental railroad.
Plenary power
The doctrine upheld by the Supreme Court giving Congress essentially unlimited authority over Native nations and Native affairs, not subject to the constraints of treaty obligations; established in cases including Lone Wolf v. Hitchcock (1903).
Red Cloud's War (1866–1868)
Military conflict in which Lakota Sioux forces besieged U.S. Army forts along the Bozeman Trail; resulted in the Fort Laramie Treaty of 1868 and the abandonment of the forts — one of the few instances in which the United States was forced by military defeat to concede treaty terms.
Reservation
A tract of land set aside by the federal government, typically through treaty or executive action, for the use of a specific Native nation; generally far smaller than original homelands and subject to extensive federal administrative control.
Sovereign / Sovereignty
Supreme political authority; Native nations were recognized by the Supreme Court as "domestic dependent sovereigns," but their sovereignty was systematically eroded by late nineteenth-century federal policy.
Treaty
A formal legal agreement between sovereign governments; the United States negotiated approximately 375 treaties with Native nations between 1778 and 1871; nearly every treaty was eventually violated.
Wounded Knee Massacre (1890)
The killing of approximately 250–300 Lakota Sioux men, women, and children by the Seventh Cavalry at Wounded Knee Creek on the Pine Ridge Reservation; marked the end of the era of armed Native resistance on the Plains.

Major People and Places

Identification & Significance

Battle of Little Bighorn site, Montana
Location of the June 25–26, 1876, battle in which Custer and 210 soldiers were killed by Lakota and Cheyenne forces; now a National Monument that commemorates both the soldiers and the Native warriors who fought there.
Big Foot (Spotted Elk, c. 1826–1890)
Miniconjou Lakota leader who led his band toward Pine Ridge Reservation in December 1890 and was killed at Wounded Knee; he was ill with pneumonia when the massacre occurred.
Black Hills (Paha Sapa), South Dakota
Sacred mountains guaranteed to the Lakota Sioux by the Fort Laramie Treaty of 1868; gold discovery in 1874 precipitated their illegal seizure and the Great Sioux War; the subject of ongoing legal claims by the Lakota Nation.
Carlisle Indian Industrial School, Pennsylvania
Founded in 1879 by Richard Henry Pratt; the model for the federal boarding school network; operated until 1918; site of at least 186 student deaths now documented by federal investigation.
Central Pacific Railroad
The western half of the first transcontinental railroad, built from Sacramento eastward primarily by Chinese immigrant workers; met the Union Pacific at Promontory Summit, Utah, May 10, 1869.
Chief Joseph (Hinmaton Yalaktit, 1840–1904)
Nez Perce leader who led 700 people on a 1,170-mile retreat before surrendering 40 miles from Canada in 1877; his surrender speech became a defining statement of Indigenous resistance.
Crazy Horse (Tashunka Witko, c. 1840–1877)
Oglala Lakota war leader who fought at Little Bighorn; surrendered 1877; killed at Fort Robinson while in Army custody — his death under Army supervision remains contested.
Geronimo (Goyaałé, 1829–1909)
Bedonkohe Apache leader who led resistance to U.S. and Mexican forces; surrendered 1886; transported to prison in Florida and Alabama before being confined to Fort Sill, Oklahoma, where he died in 1909.
Luther Standing Bear (Ota Kte, c. 1868–1939)
Oglala Lakota author and advocate; one of the first students at Carlisle Indian Industrial School; his memoirs provide essential first-person accounts of the boarding school experience and Lakota life.
Promontory Summit, Utah
Site where Union Pacific and Central Pacific railroads met on May 10, 1869, completing the first transcontinental railroad; the ceremony celebrated by political leaders erased the Chinese workers who built the line.
Red Cloud (Mahpiya Luta, 1822–1909)
Oglala Lakota leader who forced the United States to abandon Bozeman Trail forts; later served as a political advocate for Lakota rights at the Standing Rock and Pine Ridge agencies.
Sitting Bull (Tatanka Iyotake, c. 1831–1890)
Hunkpapa Lakota spiritual leader and political figure who led resistance to reservation confinement, fled to Canada after Little Bighorn, returned in 1881, and was killed by Indian police in 1890.
Standing Rock Reservation, North and South Dakota
Lakota Sioux reservation where Sitting Bull lived and died; site of significant political activism in the twentieth and twenty-first centuries, including the Dakota Access Pipeline protests of 2016.
Wounded Knee, South Dakota
Site of the December 29, 1890, massacre of approximately 250–300 Lakota by the Seventh Cavalry; also the site of an American Indian Movement occupation in 1973 that called national attention to the ongoing legacies of federal Indian policy.
Wovoka (c. 1856–1932)
Northern Paiute spiritual leader whose 1889 vision and teachings founded the Ghost Dance religion; the federal government's violent suppression of the movement led directly to the Wounded Knee Massacre.

Chapter Reflection

The transformation of the American West in the decades after the Civil War stands as one of the defining episodes of United States history — an era in which the nation's geographic expansion reached its continental limits, its industrial economy extended its extractive reach across an enormous region, and its federal government deployed an unprecedented array of legal, military, and administrative tools to dispossess and subjugate the Indigenous peoples who had inhabited that region for thousands of years. The story of the post–Civil War West is not one that can be told simply or comfortably. It is a story of genuine hardship and genuine achievement, of democratic aspiration and democratic failure, of violence and resilience, of destruction and persistence.

The chapter matters in U.S. history for several reasons that extend well beyond the specific events it covers. The reservation system, the allotment policy, and the assimilation programs of the late nineteenth century created conditions that have shaped Native American life for well over a century. The loss of 90 million acres of tribal land did not end with the Dawes Act; its effects persist in the poverty, limited economic infrastructure, and governance challenges that many reservation communities continue to face. The boarding school system's disruption of language transmission and family bonds created a multi-generational crisis in Indigenous communities that programs of cultural revitalization have worked — with significant success — to address. The checker-boarded land ownership patterns produced by allotment continue to complicate tribal governance and economic development. These are not ancient grievances — they are living inheritances of decisions made in the 1870s and 1880s.

The chapter also reveals something important about the relationship between federal power and constitutional principles in American history. The systematic violation of treaty commitments — documents that the Constitution designates as the supreme law of the land — demonstrates that constitutional principles have historically been applied selectively, protecting some people's rights while ignoring others'. The Supreme Court decisions of the late nineteenth century that established congressional plenary power over Native affairs created a legal framework used to justify extraordinary intrusions on Native sovereignty, property, and cultural practice. The ongoing legal battles over Native rights in the twenty-first century are, in significant part, battles over the legacy of these late nineteenth-century decisions.

The mythology of the West — the frontier, the pioneer, the cowboy, the cavalry hero — that was constructed simultaneously with the historical events it misrepresented has had lasting effects on American self-understanding. A mythology that celebrates westward expansion as the triumph of civilization over savagery makes it difficult to reckon honestly with what that expansion actually entailed: the systematic dispossession of peoples whose civilizations were as rich and complex as those of the settlers who displaced them, the exploitation of immigrant labor, the construction of racial hierarchies that benefited Anglo Americans at the expense of Chinese, Mexican, African American, and other communities. Dismantling this mythology is not a project of national self-flagellation — it is a project of historical accuracy, essential for understanding who the United States actually is and how it came to be that way.

Finally, this chapter asks students to think carefully about the relationship between historical distance and moral judgment. The people who built the transcontinental railroads, who homesteaded the Plains, who commanded military campaigns against Native peoples, who designed the boarding school system, who drafted the Dawes Act — most of them were operating within the assumptions of their era and did not understand themselves as villains. But the fact that they acted within historical context does not make the outcomes of their actions any less real or any less harmful. The historian's task is to understand the context without excusing the consequences, to recognize both the human complexity of historical actors and the concrete effects of their decisions on communities that experienced those decisions as loss, violence, and destruction. This chapter is, among other things, an exercise in holding both of those truths at once — which is precisely the kind of thinking that serious historical study requires.

Segregation, Race, and Regional Inequality

United States History: Indigenous America, Republic, Conflict, and Change

Chapter Opening

"The humblest white man is superior to the most distinguished colored man." — Southern Democratic Party platform, 1890s. Against this claim, four thousand documented lives were extinguished in lynchings between 1877 and 1950. Understanding both the system and its resisters is essential to understanding what the United States was.

In the late summer of 1892, a New Orleans shoemaker named Homer Plessy boarded a train, sat in the car reserved for white passengers, and waited to be arrested. Plessy was seven-eighths white by ancestry, but Louisiana law classified him as Black, and the state's Separate Car Act of 1890 required railroads to provide "equal but separate" accommodations for white and "colored" passengers. His arrest was deliberate — organized by the Comité des Citoyens, a New Orleans civil rights organization, as a carefully planned legal test of the law's constitutionality. The case worked its way through Louisiana courts, then to the U.S. Supreme Court, where in May 1896 the justices issued a ruling that would stand as the constitutional cornerstone of racial segregation in the United States for the next fifty-eight years.

The world that produced Plessy v. Ferguson was built deliberately, violently, and systematically in the decades after Reconstruction's collapse. When federal troops withdrew from the South in the late 1870s, white supremacist governments moved swiftly to restore racial hierarchy. They passed laws mandating the separation of Black and white people in railcars, schools, hospitals, parks, hotels, restaurants, barbershops, theaters, courtrooms, jails, and cemeteries. They stripped Black men of the right to vote through poll taxes, literacy tests, grandfather clauses, and white primaries. They used terror — particularly lynching — to enforce a racial order built on the memory of slavery and the determination that Black freedom would never mean Black equality. The phrase "Jim Crow," derived from a minstrel caricature, came to name this entire system of racial apartheid in the American South.

But the story of racial inequality after Reconstruction was not only a Southern story, and it was not only a Black and white story. The same decades that saw the construction of Jim Crow also saw Chinese immigrants barred from the country by federal law, Japanese immigrants denied citizenship and property ownership in the West, Mexican American communities stripped of land and political power in the borderlands, and Native peoples subjected to federal policy designed to erase their languages, cultures, and tribal sovereignty. Racial hierarchy in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries was national in scope, varied in its forms, and embedded in law, custom, economics, and everyday violence.

This chapter examines these interlocking systems of racial inequality in the period roughly from 1877 to the early twentieth century. It traces the construction of Jim Crow segregation in the South, the mechanisms of Black disfranchisement, the epidemic of racial terror known as lynching, and the Supreme Court's decision in Plessy v. Ferguson. It also examines how African Americans responded — through education, business, migration, legal challenge, journalism, and organized resistance — and how Black intellectuals debated the best strategies for survival and advancement in a violently racist society.

The chapter then turns to the experiences of Mexican American communities in the borderlands, the federal policy of Native dispossession and forced assimilation, and the legal exclusion of Asian immigrants from membership in the national community. These were not separate histories. They were connected expressions of a racial order that sorted people by ancestry, skin color, language, and culture into hierarchies of rights, opportunities, and dangers. The men and women who built Jim Crow, excluded Chinese laborers, seized Native lands, and subordinated Mexican Americans drew on similar ideas: that some peoples were inherently inferior, that racial mixture threatened civilization, that the law should encode racial difference, and that white supremacy was the natural order of American life.

Those ideas were contested fiercely and continuously by the communities they targeted. Ida B. Wells documented lynching through investigative journalism that reached international audiences. W. E. B. Du Bois argued that accommodation to racial injustice was a path to permanent subordination. Native elders maintained languages and ceremonies that federal policy tried to eradicate. Mexican American farmworkers organized labor actions under conditions of extreme vulnerability. Chinese immigrant communities built institutions of self-governance and cultural preservation under legal exclusion. Understanding both the systems of inequality and the resistance they generated is essential to understanding what the United States was, what it claimed to be, and why the gap between those two things mattered so profoundly.

The evidence historians use to study this period is varied and powerful: legal records, newspaper archives, census data, government reports, the writings of Black intellectuals, oral histories, photographs, court decisions, legislative records, Mexican American community newspapers, Indigenous memoirs, and Chinese immigrant letters. Together, these sources allow historians to reconstruct a period of profound injustice and equally profound human endurance.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain how Jim Crow laws were constructed after Reconstruction and what legal mechanisms enforced racial segregation in the South.
  2. Trace the methods used to disfranchise Black voters between the 1870s and early twentieth century, including poll taxes, literacy tests, grandfather clauses, and white primaries.
  3. Analyze the role of lynching as a system of racial terror and social control, and assess the efforts of anti-lynching activists like Ida B. Wells.
  4. Evaluate the Supreme Court's reasoning in Plessy v. Ferguson (1896) and explain how the decision institutionalized segregation under "separate but equal."
  5. Compare the educational and political strategies advocated by Booker T. Washington and W. E. B. Du Bois, and assess the strengths and limits of each approach.
  6. Describe how African Americans built institutions — churches, newspapers, schools, businesses, fraternal organizations — as forms of community sustenance and resistance within segregation.
  7. Explain how Mexican American communities in the borderlands experienced land loss, legal subordination, and cultural erasure after the U.S.-Mexican War.
  8. Identify the major federal policies — including the Chinese Exclusion Act, the Dawes Act, and boarding school policy — through which the government constructed racial hierarchy for Asian, Indigenous, and other non-white communities.
  9. Connect the construction of racial inequality in this period to earlier and later chapters in U.S. history, including the legacies of slavery and the foundations of the civil rights movement.
  10. Assess how different communities experienced, accommodated, and resisted racial systems, recognizing that subordinated peoples were agents as well as subjects of history.
  11. Contextualize regional inequality within the broader national story of race and power.
  12. Evaluate how historical memory of this period has shifted over time, including debates about monuments, curricula, and reparations.

Guiding Historical Questions

Questions for Historical Inquiry

  1. How did racial segregation become embedded in law, custom, and everyday life after Reconstruction ended?
  2. What mechanisms did states and localities use to strip Black men of the right to vote, and how effective were those mechanisms?
  3. Why was lynching so widespread, who was targeted, who carried it out, and what was its social function?
  4. What did the Supreme Court's decision in Plessy v. Ferguson mean for the legal status of Black Americans, and how did the Justices reason their way to that conclusion?
  5. How did African Americans resist, survive, and build community within a violently segregated society?
  6. What were the differences between Booker T. Washington's and W. E. B. Du Bois's strategies for Black advancement, and what were the strengths and limits of each?
  7. How did racial systems in the West, the borderlands, and the broader United States share common features with and differ from Jim Crow in the South?
  8. What does the history of this period reveal about the relationship between law, power, and racial hierarchy in the United States?

Historical Context

To understand the construction of racial segregation and inequality in the late nineteenth century, students must understand what came before. The Reconstruction era — roughly 1865 to 1877 — had been a period of profound transformation and profound conflict. Constitutional amendments abolished slavery (Thirteenth Amendment, 1865), guaranteed equal protection and citizenship to Black Americans (Fourteenth Amendment, 1868), and prohibited denial of the vote on the basis of race (Fifteenth Amendment, 1870). Black men voted in large numbers across the South, and hundreds of Black officeholders served in state legislatures, Congress, and local governments. Freedmen's Bureau schools extended literacy to hundreds of thousands of formerly enslaved people. Reconstruction governments built public school systems, reformed labor laws, and attempted to create a biracial democracy in the former Confederate states.

But Reconstruction faced ferocious resistance. White supremacist organizations — the Ku Klux Klan, the White League, the Red Shirts — used assassination, arson, rape, and mob violence to drive Black officeholders from power, suppress Black voters, and terrorize communities that claimed their new rights. Federal commitment to protecting Black rights in the South eroded through the 1870s. The disputed presidential election of 1876 produced the Compromise of 1877, which effectively ended federal military Reconstruction and allowed white Democrats, calling themselves "Redeemers," to take over Southern state governments.

The period covered in this chapter — roughly 1877 through the early 1900s — was therefore not a period of racial progress abruptly reversed. It was a period in which the reversal completed itself and was institutionalized. The racial hierarchy that had existed under slavery was reconstructed under new legal forms: segregation, disfranchisement, convict leasing, debt peonage, and systematic exclusion from economic opportunity. Nationally, the same decades also produced federal immigration restriction targeting Chinese immigrants (1882), systematic federal assimilation policy targeting Native peoples (1887), and legal subordination of Mexican American communities in the Southwest. Against all of this, African Americans, Mexican Americans, Native peoples, and Asian immigrants built communities, maintained cultures, challenged laws, organized protests, published newspapers, founded schools and churches, and refused — sometimes at enormous personal risk — to accept the racial order as permanent or just.

Section 34.1

Race and Power After Reconstruction

The end of Reconstruction did not happen all at once. Federal troops withdrew from the South gradually through the mid-1870s, and the precise mechanisms of Black disfranchisement and segregation were constructed state by state, law by law, over the two decades following 1877. But the direction was unmistakable. The brief, contested experiment in biracial democracy that Reconstruction had represented was being dismantled, and in its place a new racial order — more legalized, more geographically comprehensive, and in some respects more systematic than slavery itself — was being built.

The political economy of this reversal was grounded in the plantation South's fundamental social arrangements. White landowners needed cheap, controllable labor. Formerly enslaved people needed land, capital, and political protection to achieve genuine freedom. The Reconstruction promise had been that freed people would have access to land — the famous "forty acres and a mule" — and political rights that would allow them to defend their economic interests. When Reconstruction ended without land redistribution and with political rights stripped away, the conditions were set for a labor system that reproduced many fundamental features of slavery without its formal legal status.

Sharecropping was the dominant result. In the sharecropping system, Black families rented land from white landowners and paid their rent in the form of a share of the crop — typically half the harvest. In theory, a successful sharecropper could accumulate savings and eventually purchase land. In practice, the system was structured to prevent that. Landowners controlled the commissary stores where sharecroppers were required to purchase supplies, and they kept the accounts. At the end of each agricultural year, many sharecroppers found themselves told they owed the landlord money rather than being owed money themselves, trapping them in debt they could not legally leave — particularly since vagrancy and contract-evasion laws could imprison anyone who attempted to walk away from a labor agreement.

Convict leasing completed the coercive labor system. Southern states criminalized poverty and Black mobility through vagrancy laws, which made it a crime to be unemployed or to travel without a labor contract. Black men were arrested in large numbers for trivial offenses — walking on a road, speaking too loudly near a white woman, being unable to produce proof of employment. They were convicted in county courts with little meaningful legal representation, then sentenced to terms of labor. The state leased these prisoners to private businesses — mining companies, railroad contractors, lumber camps, phosphate operations — which used them as forced labor under conditions frequently more brutal than plantation slavery had been. Convict laborers could be and frequently were worked to death; the lessee had no long-term financial interest in their survival, unlike the slaveholder who had capital invested in an enslaved person's body.

The ideology justifying all of this drew on several intellectual traditions. Social Darwinism — the misapplication of Darwinian evolutionary theory to human societies — claimed that economic and racial hierarchies reflected natural selection, and that Black poverty and political subordination reflected inherent unfitness for free society. Scientific racism produced academic literature claiming to measure racial capacity through skull size and brain weight. These ideas were taught in universities, published in popular magazines, and cited in legal arguments. They were not simply prejudice. They were an organized intellectual framework for racial inequality, and they shaped policy, law, and public culture well into the twentieth century.

Cause and Effect

The Construction of Jim Crow After Reconstruction

Long-Term Causes

The fundamental entrenchment of white Southern social and economic power in land ownership; the failure of land redistribution during Reconstruction; the ideological legacy of racial slavery; the political interests of the Democratic Party in racial solidarity across class lines.

Short-Term Causes

The Compromise of 1877 and federal troop withdrawal; the Supreme Court's 1883 Civil Rights Cases gutting the 1875 Civil Rights Act; Mississippi's systematic disfranchisement model of 1890; the Plessy v. Ferguson decision of 1896.

The Development

A comprehensive system of racial apartheid, labor coercion, political exclusion, and terror was constructed through state legislation, federal court decisions, economic power, and extrajudicial violence between 1877 and the early 1900s.

Effects

Near-total Black political exclusion across the South; the entrenchment of economic inequality through sharecropping and convict leasing; the epidemic of lynching; the legal framework of segregation upheld until Brown v. Board (1954); the Great Migration northward as communities sought escape.

Historical complexity: Jim Crow was not the inevitable product of Southern culture. It was built by specific people with specific interests making specific choices, using law, violence, and ideology as tools. Its construction required sustained political effort across multiple decades.

Section 34.2

Jim Crow Laws and Segregation

The term "Jim Crow" originally referred to a minstrel character — a caricature of a shuffling, dim-witted Black man — popularized by a white performer named Thomas Dartmouth "Daddy" Rice in the 1830s. By the late nineteenth century, it named the entire system of racial separation that Southern legislatures were constructing, statute by statute, across virtually every domain of public and social life. Jim Crow was not a single law. It was a comprehensive architecture of racial apartheid enforced by law, custom, economic power, and violence.

The legal construction of Jim Crow began in earnest in the 1880s and accelerated through the 1890s and early 1900s. Tennessee segregated its railcars in 1881; Florida in 1887; Mississippi, Texas, and Louisiana followed in the 1890s. By the early twentieth century, Southern states had passed laws mandating racial separation in railroad cars, waiting rooms, streetcars, steamboats, hotels, restaurants, barbershops, theaters, parks, beaches, swimming pools, libraries, courtrooms, jails, hospitals, asylums, orphanages, and cemeteries. The specificity of the legislation was extraordinary. Some laws required separate Bibles for Black and white witnesses swearing oaths in court. Some required separate phone booths. Some mandated that textbooks used by Black students never be mixed with those used by white students.

Schools were the most consequential site of segregation. Public education had been one of Reconstruction's great achievements — the creation of public school systems in Southern states that had previously offered public schooling only to white children. After Reconstruction, those systems were maintained in segregated form with profoundly unequal resources. Black schools were more crowded, had shorter school years, paid teachers far less, and had fewer instructional materials. The gap between Black and white school funding was not incidental; it was deliberately maintained to ensure that Black children would receive less preparation for economic and civic life.

The logic of segregation rested on claims of racial difference and the alleged need to protect white people — and especially white women — from contact with Black people. The actual social function of segregation was not protection but subordination. By requiring Black people to use inferior facilities, to enter through back doors, to give way on sidewalks, to address white strangers with deference while being addressed by their first names, and to accept a thousand daily reminders of their assigned inferiority, Jim Crow embedded racial hierarchy into the texture of everyday life. It was a system of humiliation as much as physical separation.

The enforcement of Jim Crow depended on law, custom, and violence working together. Formal legal penalties were one mechanism. The threat of economic retaliation — losing a job, losing credit, being evicted from a rented farm — was another. And always, behind both, was the threat of violence. A Black man or woman who violated the customs of segregation — who sat in the wrong seat on a streetcar, who failed to show appropriate deference, who attempted to enter a space designated for whites — risked not only arrest but mob violence. The boundaries of Jim Crow were enforced by community terror as much as by law enforcement.

The geographical extent of Jim Crow extended beyond the former Confederate states, though it was most systematically developed there. Northern cities had de facto segregation — racial separation enforced not by law but by real estate practices, economic discrimination, private agreements, and social custom. Black Northerners faced restrictions in housing, employment, and social life that were no less real for lacking the formal statutory structure of Jim Crow.

Historical Vocabulary
De Jure and De Facto Segregation

De jure segregation means segregation enforced by law — statutes that explicitly require racial separation in specific spaces. The Jim Crow laws of the South were de jure segregation. De facto segregation means segregation that exists in practice without formal legal requirement — the product of economic discrimination, real estate practices, private agreements, and social custom. Northern cities had extensive de facto segregation in housing, schools (through residential segregation), and employment.

The distinction matters legally: de jure segregation could be directly challenged in court, while de facto segregation was more difficult to address through legal means. Both systems produced racially unequal outcomes and confined Black Americans and other minorities to inferior resources, opportunities, and social standing. When critics of the civil rights movement argued that racism was a Southern problem, the existence of de facto segregation throughout the North and West was a powerful refutation.

Section 34.3

Disfranchisement and Political Exclusion

The Fifteenth Amendment to the U.S. Constitution, ratified in 1870, prohibited denying the right to vote on account of race, color, or previous condition of servitude. For a brief period during Reconstruction, Black men in Southern states had exercised that right in significant numbers, electing Black officeholders and influencing the policies of state and local governments. By the mid-1870s, violence and intimidation had already begun suppressing Black voting. By the 1890s, Southern states had moved from extralegal violence to systematic legal mechanisms designed to eliminate Black political participation entirely while nominally complying with the Fifteenth Amendment's text.

The key innovation was to devise disfranchisement mechanisms that did not explicitly mention race — since the Fifteenth Amendment prohibited racial exclusion — but that operated in practice to exclude almost all Black voters while preserving the franchise for almost all white voters. Mississippi pioneered this approach in its constitutional convention of 1890, and the "Mississippi Plan" became a template widely copied across the South.

The poll tax required citizens to pay a fee — typically one or two dollars annually — before being allowed to vote. This fee had to be paid months in advance of the election, often in a cumulative structure requiring payment of back taxes for years of non-participation. For the majority of Black Southerners who worked in agriculture and earned little cash income, this fee was a significant barrier. White Democratic registrars applied the requirement strictly to Black applicants and more flexibly to white ones.

The literacy test required prospective voters to read and interpret a passage from the state constitution to the satisfaction of a registrar. The test was administered selectively and subjectively: white applicants received easy passages and lenient grading, while Black applicants — including educated professionals — received complex passages and were failed on arbitrary grounds. Some registrars required interpretation of the entire state constitution. The literacy test was not about literacy; it was about giving white registrars discretion to exclude Black applicants.

The grandfather clause attempted to insulate white voters from the disfranchising effects of poll taxes and literacy tests by providing exemptions for men who had voted before 1867 or whose ancestors had voted before 1867. Since Black men had not had the vote before 1867, the clause effectively offered exemptions only to white voters. The Supreme Court struck down grandfather clauses in 1915 in Guinn v. United States, but by then other disfranchisement mechanisms had already accomplished the same purpose.

The white primary was perhaps the most effective disfranchisement tool of all, particularly after the defeat of the Populist movement in the 1890s made the Democratic Party the only viable political force in most of the South. Southern states and Democratic Party organizations restricted primary participation to white voters, arguing that a political party was a private organization with the right to set its own membership rules. The Supreme Court did not definitively strike down the white primary until 1944 in Smith v. Allwright.

The consequences of disfranchisement were immediate and comprehensive. The number of Black registered voters in Louisiana fell from 130,334 in 1896 to 1,342 in 1904 — a reduction of more than 98 percent accomplished through legal mechanisms. Similar collapses occurred across the South. Black officeholders disappeared from Southern legislatures, county commissions, school boards, and city councils. Without the vote, Black communities had no leverage over public policy: they could not influence school funding allocations, road and infrastructure spending, public health programs, policing, or the application of vagrancy and labor laws.

Section 34.4

Lynching and Racial Terror

Between 1877 and 1950, more than four thousand Black people were killed by lynching in the United States. The actual number was almost certainly higher, since many killings went unrecorded or were reported as justified homicides or accidents. The geographic concentration was heavy in the South — Mississippi, Georgia, Louisiana, Texas, and Alabama accounted for the largest numbers — but lynching occurred across the country, including in the Midwest and West. It was not a primitive holdover from an earlier era. It was a modern system of racial terror, functioning alongside and in support of the legal systems of segregation and disfranchisement.

The charges that preceded lynchings were varied: murder, rape, assault, theft, debt, insulting a white person, competing economically with white businesses, testifying against a white person in court, or simply being at the wrong place at the wrong time during a period of racial tension. Many victims were entirely innocent of any charge. The accusation of rape of a white woman by a Black man was particularly common as a stated justification — so common that Ida B. Wells's investigation would reveal it to be largely false in the specific cases she documented, often a cover for the killing of Black men who had engaged in consensual relationships with white women or who had achieved economic success that white competitors resented.

Lynchings were frequently public events. They were announced in newspapers, and crowds gathered to watch. Photographs were taken and sold as postcards. Victims were often tortured before death — burned alive, mutilated, castrated — in rituals of extraordinary public violence that sent a message not only to the victim but to every Black person in the surrounding community: this is what happens to those who challenge the racial order. The terror functioned precisely because it was public, because it was brutal, and because it operated with the effective complicity of local law enforcement, which rarely intervened and almost never prosecuted perpetrators.

The federal government's response to lynching was minimal. Congress repeatedly considered anti-lynching legislation in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. Bills passed the House but were blocked in the Senate by Southern Democratic filibusters. Presidents, including Theodore Roosevelt, spoke against lynching rhetorically while declining to press seriously for federal legislation. The argument that lynching was a state and local matter consistently prevailed over the argument that the federal government had an obligation under the Fourteenth Amendment to protect the lives of its citizens.

Ida B. Wells was the most important early journalist and activist to document and challenge the lynching epidemic. Born enslaved in Mississippi in 1862, Wells became a teacher and then a journalist in Memphis, Tennessee. In 1892, three of her close friends — owners of a successful grocery business that competed with a white-owned store — were removed from jail and lynched by a white mob. Wells used her newspaper, the Memphis Free Speech, to investigate and publicize the facts of this and other lynchings. She documented that the rape justification was frequently fabricated and that many victims had been killed for economic competition or social assertion. After Wells published her exposés, a white mob destroyed the offices of the Memphis Free Speech and threatened her life. She fled to Chicago and continued her journalism, traveled to Great Britain generating significant international pressure, and published her landmark 1895 pamphlet A Red Record.

Primary Source Window

Ida B. Wells, A Red Record (1895)

Creator: Ida B. Wells  ·  Date: 1895  ·  Type: Political pamphlet and statistical investigative report

Context: Ida B. Wells published A Red Record as a documented statistical account of lynching in the United States during 1892, 1893, and 1894. Wells gathered data from newspaper reports across the country, compiling the names of victims, the charges against them, and the circumstances of their deaths. She addressed the pamphlet to "the student of American sociology" and to the conscience of a public that claimed to stand for law, order, and civilization.

Wells argued that three periods of racial violence had each used a different justification. After emancipation, white Southerners claimed Black men posed a general danger requiring suppression. When that argument was discredited, the "war on property" justification was offered — that Black men were criminals threatening order. Finally, the "rape myth" became the dominant justification, used to excuse mob violence against Black men who were killed not for assault but for economic competition, social assertiveness, or entirely fabricated charges. Wells challenged her readers to examine the actual record of documented lynchings and compare it with the stated justifications.

Analysis: A Red Record is a landmark primary source because it demonstrates systematic research applied to a subject that most white journalists and politicians refused to take seriously. Wells's method — collecting documented cases, identifying patterns, publicly naming both victims and perpetrators — was investigative journalism of a kind unusual for its era. The source reveals both the scale of the lynching epidemic and the determined effort by one journalist to force public reckoning. Its limits include reliance on newspaper reports that were themselves often incomplete or biased; Wells acknowledged her count was certainly an undercount. The source does not give voice to victims or their families directly, focusing instead on systemic analysis.

Questions for students:

  1. What argument did Wells make about the justifications for lynching, and what evidence did she use to support it?
  2. Why might Wells have addressed her pamphlet to "the student of American sociology" rather than to a more popular audience?
  3. What can historians learn from this source about the relationship between racial violence and legal systems in this period?
  4. What aspects of the lynching experience does A Red Record not capture, and what other kinds of sources would historians need to develop a fuller picture?
Section 34.5

Plessy v. Ferguson

The legal challenge organized by the Comité des Citoyens in New Orleans was calculated and carefully constructed. The organization arranged for Homer Plessy — whose light complexion made his racial classification under Louisiana law a matter of social convention rather than obvious physical appearance — to board the train and violate the Separate Car Act. They had arranged with the train conductor to have Plessy arrested rather than simply removed, ensuring a formal legal case would result.

The case moved through Louisiana courts, with the Comité challenging the law as a violation of the Thirteenth and Fourteenth Amendments. Judge John Howard Ferguson of the Criminal District Court of Orleans Parish ruled against Plessy, finding the state's regulation of railroads within Louisiana to be a valid exercise of state police power. Plessy appealed to the Louisiana Supreme Court, which also ruled against him, and then to the U.S. Supreme Court.

The Supreme Court's decision, issued on May 18, 1896, upheld Louisiana's Separate Car Act by a vote of 7 to 1. Justice Henry Brown wrote the majority opinion. Brown argued that the Fourteenth Amendment's guarantee of equal protection did not require social equality, only legal equality. He further argued that the legislature was entitled to enact laws reflecting the "established usages, customs, and traditions of the people" — effectively deferring to Southern racial custom as a legitimate basis for law. Most infamously, he argued that enforced separation did not stamp the "colored race with a badge of inferiority," and that if Black people perceived such a badge, it was not by reason of anything in the act "but solely because the colored race chooses to put that construction upon it."

This argument was a remarkable act of judicial evasion. It claimed that racial segregation was a neutral exercise of state power whose meaning was supplied by the misperception of its targets rather than by its actual design. It established the "separate but equal" doctrine: that racially separate facilities were constitutionally permissible as long as they were nominally equal — a requirement the court declined to define or enforce with any specificity.

The lone dissenter was Justice John Marshall Harlan of Kentucky, a former slaveholder who had become the court's most consistent defender of the Reconstruction amendments. Harlan's dissent was categorical. "Our Constitution is color-blind, and neither knows nor tolerates classes among citizens," he wrote. He argued that the majority's reasoning would "permit the seeds of race hate to be planted under the sanction of law," and predicted, correctly, that the decision would be used to justify a comprehensive system of racial subordination.

Plessy became the foundational legal precedent for American apartheid. Southern states extended segregation further, and federal courts applied the separate-but-equal doctrine across public life. The "equal" part of "separate but equal" was effectively ignored: courts rarely examined whether facilities were actually equal, and Southern governments never came close to providing equal resources to segregated Black institutions. Plessy would stand as constitutional law until the Supreme Court overturned it in Brown v. Board of Education in 1954 — fifty-eight years of judicial sanction for a system that denied millions of Americans the equal protection of the laws.

Constitutional Connection

The Fourteenth Amendment and Plessy v. Ferguson

The Fourteenth Amendment, ratified in 1868 as part of Reconstruction, guaranteed equal protection of the laws to all persons born or naturalized in the United States. It was written specifically to prevent Southern states from denying rights to formerly enslaved people. The Supreme Court's interpretation of the Fourteenth Amendment in Plessy v. Ferguson (1896) directly contradicted this original purpose.

The Plessy majority (1896): Ruled that racially separate facilities satisfied equal protection as long as they were nominally equal. The court deferred to Southern "custom and tradition" as a valid basis for law and claimed that segregation carried no "badge of inferiority." Seven justices agreed.
Justice Harlan's dissent (1896): Argued that the Constitution "is color-blind" and "neither knows nor tolerates classes among citizens." Harlan predicted the majority's reasoning would be used to justify comprehensive racial subordination — and was proved right within a decade.

The Plessy decision illustrates a central dynamic in constitutional history: the meaning of constitutional provisions is determined not only by their text but by the interpretations courts impose on them in specific historical contexts. The same Fourteenth Amendment used by the Plessy majority to uphold segregation was later used by the Warren Court in Brown v. Board of Education (1954) to strike it down — the text unchanged, the interpretation transformed by a different legal and political context.

Section 34.6

Black Education, Business, and Resistance

Within the system of segregation, African Americans built extraordinary institutions. This was not acquiescence to injustice — it was survival and resistance under conditions of severe constraint. The church, the school, the newspaper, the fraternal organization, the mutual aid society, the business association, and the civil rights organization were all tools with which Black communities maintained dignity, transmitted culture, protected their members, and sustained the possibility of a different future.

The Black church was the most foundational institution. Independent Black churches had developed before the Civil War in both the North and South, though under severe restrictions in the South. After emancipation, church membership exploded. Churches were the primary sites of community life — not only for worship but for education, political organizing, mutual aid, entertainment, and social solidarity. Major denominations, including the African Methodist Episcopal Church, the African Methodist Episcopal Zion Church, and the National Baptist Convention, provided organizational networks extending across regions. The church was the one institution that the white power structure in the South had difficulty directly controlling, which made it the foundation from which other forms of organizing could be built.

Black newspapers were another vital institution. Papers including the New York Age, the Cleveland Gazette, and the Indianapolis Freeman served Black communities in the late nineteenth century. The Chicago Defender, founded in 1905, would become the most nationally important Black newspaper, playing a crucial role in the Great Migration. These papers reported on racial violence, advocated for legal and political rights, covered Black cultural and social life, and provided a public sphere in which Black voices could speak to Black audiences without white editorial control. The existence of an independent Black press was itself a challenge to the segregationist order.

Black fraternal organizations — the Prince Hall Masons, the Elks, the Knights of Pythias, and many others — provided insurance, social solidarity, leadership development, and community identity. Black women's clubs organized through the National Association of Colored Women (founded 1896) and provided services including kindergartens, day nurseries, libraries, and settlements in Black neighborhoods that were excluded from public services available to white residents. These organizations performed work that municipal governments refused to provide to Black communities, filling a gap created by segregation with community-funded labor and mutual care.

Black business communities developed in urban centers across the country, particularly in the South and in Northern cities. Insurance companies, banks, newspapers, funeral homes, beauty supply businesses, and retail establishments served Black customers who were excluded from or humiliated in white-owned businesses. Durham, North Carolina; Richmond, Virginia; Atlanta, Georgia; and Tulsa, Oklahoma (in its Greenwood district, called "Black Wall Street") developed substantial Black business districts. These communities were simultaneously evidence of Black economic capacity and targets of white resentment: successful Black businesses were frequently destroyed by white mobs, and the Greenwood district of Tulsa was razed in the Tulsa Race Massacre of 1921.

Black higher education developed largely through institutions founded during and after Reconstruction — Howard University, Fisk University, Atlanta University, Spelman College, and dozens of others — as well as through the industrial education institutions associated with Booker T. Washington. These colleges trained the Black professional and intellectual class — physicians, lawyers, teachers, ministers, journalists, and public intellectuals — who would lead the civil rights movements of the twentieth century. That these institutions existed, that they produced graduates of extraordinary capability under conditions of extraordinary oppression, is a historical fact of great importance. It also illustrates the price of segregation: every one was built because Black students were barred from or severely limited in white-controlled higher education.

Continuity and Change Over Time

The Status of African Americans from Emancipation to the Civil Rights Act (1865–1965)

What Changed

  • Legal slavery abolished (Thirteenth Amendment, 1865)
  • Birthright citizenship established (Fourteenth Amendment, 1868)
  • Black men gained voting rights (Fifteenth Amendment, 1870)
  • African Americans established schools, churches, businesses, and community institutions under segregation
  • Segregated schools ruled unconstitutional (Brown v. Board of Education, 1954)
  • Civil Rights Act (1964) prohibited discrimination in public accommodations and employment
  • Voting Rights Act (1965) enforced Black voting rights

What Stayed the Same

  • Racial hierarchies organizing economic life persisted through sharecropping, debt peonage, and discriminatory lending
  • White political violence against Black communities continued from Reconstruction through twentieth-century lynching
  • Residential segregation — produced by law and custom — persisted in both North and South
  • Economic inequality between Black and white Americans, rooted in the history of slavery, persisted throughout the period

Why the pattern matters: The persistence of racial inequality despite formal legal change illustrates that legal change alone does not automatically transform social and economic conditions. It also illustrates why civil rights activists understood that the struggle for equality could not end with any single legal victory.

Section 34.7

Booker T. Washington, W. E. B. Du Bois, and Debate

The most consequential public debate about Black strategy and politics in this period was conducted between two extraordinary figures: Booker T. Washington and W. E. B. Du Bois. Their disagreement was not simply personal. It represented a genuine strategic argument about how Black Americans could best survive and advance in a society determined to subjugate them, and the choices they argued over were real choices with real consequences.

Booker T. Washington was born enslaved in Virginia in 1856. After emancipation, he worked his way through Hampton Institute in Virginia and was deeply influenced by its model of industrial and agricultural training — education that would prepare students for work rather than elite professional careers. In 1881, he founded Tuskegee Institute in rural Alabama, building it from almost nothing into a nationally prominent institution. Washington's strategic argument, articulated most famously in his Atlanta Exposition Address of 1895, was that Black progress should focus on economic self-improvement, vocational education, and accommodation to Southern racial customs. He urged Black Americans to build economic security in the South rather than seeking political agitation for rights, and he discouraged political confrontation, arguing that economic achievement would gradually earn respect. This speech was celebrated by white audiences North and South, and Washington quickly became the most prominent Black public figure in the country. He used his prominence and Northern philanthropist support to build what historians have called the "Tuskegee Machine": a network of patronage, press influence, and political connections. Significantly, Washington secretly financed legal challenges to disfranchisement and segregation even while publicly advocating accommodation — a dual strategy reflecting the genuine constraints of his position.

W. E. B. Du Bois was born free in Massachusetts in 1868, educated at Fisk University and Harvard (where he was the first African American to earn a Ph.D.), and at the University of Berlin. His scholarship — including The Philadelphia Negro (1899) and The Souls of Black Folk (1903) — established him as the leading Black intellectual of his generation. In The Souls of Black Folk, Du Bois argued that Washington's program, whatever its intentions, effectively asked Black Americans to accept political disfranchisement, civic inferiority, and reduction to a permanently subordinate economic role. Du Bois insisted on the right to vote, civic equality, and higher education for Black Americans as immediate demands. His concept of the "Talented Tenth" held that Black advancement required a leadership class educated in the liberal arts and capable of guiding the community through political, intellectual, and cultural engagement. He was not dismissive of vocational education but argued that a people confined to it would be permanently relegated to economic and social subordination.

Historical Debate

Was Booker T. Washington's Strategy of Accommodation Justified?

The question: Was Washington's decision to publicly accept segregation and disfranchisement while focusing on vocational education and economic advancement the best strategy available to Black Americans in the late nineteenth century, or did his accommodation undermine Black freedom and dignity?

Interpretation A — Washington's strategy was pragmatic and protective: Historians sympathetic to Washington argue that he operated under conditions of extreme danger and constraint. Open political agitation in the 1890s South could cost Black activists their lives. Washington's accommodation bought space — physical safety, educational development, economic opportunity — that a more confrontational approach might not have preserved. His secret funding of legal challenges to disfranchisement shows that his public accommodationism was a tactical posture rather than a sincere acceptance of racial subordination. Given the actual balance of power in the 1890s, his strategy was arguably the most realistic available.
Interpretation B — Washington's accommodation legitimized racial oppression: Historians sympathetic to Du Bois argue that Washington's strategy, regardless of intent, provided white supremacists with Black endorsement of the racial order being constructed. Washington's speeches were used by Southern politicians to argue that Black people accepted their subordinate status. His opposition to political agitation made it harder for other Black leaders to organize resistance. His control of Black institutional patronage through the Tuskegee Machine suppressed voices calling for full civil rights. Accommodation did not prevent the hardening of segregation; it may have facilitated it.

Evidence: Both interpretations draw on Washington's speeches, letters, and institutional decisions, as well as the historical record of what happened to Black political rights during the period of his dominance. Du Bois's writings provide the primary articulation of the critique. Recent scholarship examining Washington's private correspondence reveals a more complex and conflicted figure than either his public image or his critics' portrait.

Why it matters: The debate connects to ongoing arguments about how marginalized communities should navigate hostile power structures — whether working within the system or challenging it from outside is more likely to produce change. It also raises questions about who gets to define the interests of a community under oppression.

Section 34.8

Mexican American and Borderlands Communities

The racial systems of the late nineteenth century extended well beyond the Black-white binary that dominated Southern politics. In the borderlands of the Southwest — territory acquired through the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo in 1848 and the Gadsden Purchase of 1853 — Mexican American communities faced dispossession, legal subordination, and cultural erasure that paralleled in important ways the treatment of Black Southerners, though through different mechanisms and within different historical contexts.

The Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo had formally guaranteed that Mexicans who chose to remain in the ceded territory and become American citizens would retain their property rights and civil rights. In practice, these guarantees were largely honored in theory and systematically violated in fact. Mexican landholding families — many of whom had held their land for generations under Spanish and Mexican grant — found their titles challenged in American courts that imposed unfamiliar evidentiary standards and required expensive legal proceedings conducted in English. Lawyers charged high fees, courts ruled against Mexican claimants on technical grounds, and the process of defending land titles frequently consumed the wealth the land represented. By the end of the nineteenth century, vast landholdings had passed into Anglo-American ownership through a combination of legal proceedings, debt, fraud, and outright theft.

Racial classification of Mexican Americans was legally ambiguous and practically discriminatory. The Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo had granted citizenship, and the 1848 treaty theoretically made Mexican Americans white under American law. But social practice classified Mexican Americans as racially distinct from Anglo-Americans, and they faced discrimination in schools, courts, public accommodations, and social life that made their nominal whiteness largely meaningless in everyday experience. In Texas and California, Mexican American children were frequently placed in separate, inferior schools. In many communities, Mexicans were excluded from Anglo restaurants, theaters, and other public spaces.

Violence was also part of the borderlands racial order. Texas Rangers — the state law enforcement and paramilitary force — engaged in a long history of violence against Mexican Americans, including mass killings. The period between 1910 and 1920, during the upheavals of the Mexican Revolution, saw particularly intense violence along the Texas-Mexico border, including large-scale massacres of Mexican and Mexican American men by Rangers and Anglo mobs. These killings, which historians have only recently begun to systematically document and count, produced a terror that functioned similarly to lynching in the South — enforcing racial hierarchy through public violence.

Mexican American communities responded to subordination through community organization, cultural maintenance, labor organizing, and legal challenge. Mutual aid societies — mutualistas — provided economic support, community solidarity, and cultural preservation for working-class communities. Along the border, Spanish-language newspapers reported on community affairs, challenged discrimination, and maintained connections to Mexico and to Mexican American communities across the region. Labor organizing was another form of resistance: Mexican American agricultural workers participated in strikes and labor actions in California, Texas, and elsewhere in the early twentieth century, asserting their interests under conditions of extreme vulnerability. The farmworker movement that Cesar Chavez and Dolores Huerta would lead in the 1960s built on this long tradition of borderlands labor activism.

Section 34.9

Asian Exclusion and Native Policy

Asian Exclusion

Chinese immigration to the United States had been substantial since the Gold Rush era. Tens of thousands of Chinese men had worked in California's gold mines, in agricultural fields, in San Francisco factories, and most famously in the construction of the Central Pacific Railroad, where Chinese workers performed the most dangerous and difficult labor of building the transcontinental railroad across the Sierra Nevada. Despite this economic contribution, Chinese immigrants faced pervasive hostility from white workers and politicians who blamed them for depressing wages during economic downturns.

The hostility intensified in the 1870s as economic depression increased competition for jobs in California. Anti-Chinese violence — including arson, beatings, and massacre — occurred in communities across the West. In 1871, a mob in Los Angeles killed approximately nineteen Chinese residents in one of the largest mass lynchings in American history. In 1885, white coal miners in Rock Springs, Wyoming, killed at least twenty-eight Chinese miners and drove hundreds more from the town. In 1882, Congress passed and President Chester Arthur signed the Chinese Exclusion Act.

The Chinese Exclusion Act prohibited the immigration of Chinese laborers to the United States and barred Chinese immigrants already in the country from becoming naturalized citizens. It was the first federal law to restrict immigration on explicitly racial grounds, marking a fundamental shift in American immigration policy toward racially selective restriction. The act was renewed multiple times and eventually made permanent; it was not repealed until 1943, when China was a U.S. ally in World War II.

Chinese communities maintained their institutions — temples, mutual aid associations, clan organizations — under conditions of legal exclusion and social hostility. Japanese immigration accelerated in the late nineteenth century as Chinese exclusion reduced one source of Asian labor. Japanese immigrant communities developed in California, Hawaii, and along the West Coast. California passed alien land laws in 1913 and 1920 barring Japanese immigrants — as "aliens ineligible for citizenship" — from owning agricultural land. These laws were explicitly designed to destroy Japanese agricultural communities that had become economically successful through intensive labor and cooperative organization. The pattern of legal discrimination against Japanese immigrants would culminate in the Japanese American incarceration of World War II.

Native Policy: Allotment and Boarding Schools

Federal policy toward Native peoples in this period represented a different form of racial hierarchy — one dressed in the language of civilization, progress, and benevolent paternalism, but functioning as a systematic program of cultural destruction, land seizure, and forced assimilation.

The Dawes Act of 1887 — also called the General Allotment Act — authorized the federal government to divide communally held tribal lands into individual allotments, typically 160 acres, assigned to individual Native heads of household. After allotment, the "surplus" land not assigned to individual allotments was opened to white settlement. The architects of the Dawes Act believed, some sincerely, that individual land ownership was the foundation of civilization. They also understood — and many explicitly acknowledged — that the act would reduce Indian land holdings dramatically.

The consequences confirmed the critics. In 1887, Native peoples held approximately 138 million acres. By 1934, when Congress reversed allotment policy, they held 48 million acres — a loss of 90 million acres in less than fifty years. Much of this land was lost through the "surplus" provisions, but allottees also lost their individual parcels through fraud, manipulated sales, tax delinquency, and legal mechanisms that left Native landowners vulnerable to exploitation. In Oklahoma, where oil was discovered on allotted lands, the exploitation of Native allottees reached astonishing levels of corruption and violence.

The boarding school system was the cultural complement to allotment. Federal policy under Commissioner of Indian Affairs Richard Henry Pratt, founder of the Carlisle Indian Industrial School in Pennsylvania, aimed to destroy Native cultures by removing Native children from their families and communities and placing them in institutions where they were forbidden to speak their languages, practice their religions, wear traditional clothing, or maintain traditional social practices. Pratt's motto — "Kill the Indian, save the man" — expressed the policy's logic: that Native cultural identity itself was the problem to be eliminated, leaving the individual to be absorbed into white American society. Children were taken from their families — sometimes willingly, as families hoped education would provide survival skills, and often by force or through economic coercion. At Carlisle and at the more than one hundred government boarding schools that developed across the country, children were given English names, cut their hair, dressed in uniforms, and subjected to military-style discipline. Those who spoke their Native languages were punished.

Many children died at boarding schools from disease and neglect; cemeteries on school grounds held hundreds of children who never returned home. Survivors returned changed — sometimes unable to speak their Native languages fluently, estranged from their communities, and without the skills to navigate white society that the schools had promised. The intergenerational trauma of the boarding school system — the damage done to families, communities, languages, and cultures — has been extensively documented by historians and Indigenous scholars and continues to shape Native communities today.

Multiple Perspectives

The Racial Order of the Late Nineteenth Century

For Black Southerners under Jim Crow: Daily life was structured by the constant management of danger. Submitting to the customs of segregation — using the "colored" waiting room, giving way on sidewalks, accepting a thousand daily reminders of assigned inferiority — was a survival strategy, not an expression of acceptance. Building a church, a school, a business, or a newspaper was an act of resistance and community sustenance simultaneously. Du Bois's meditations in The Souls of Black Folk on the "double consciousness" of being both American and Black in a society that denied the two could coexist captured the psychological reality of this existence.
For white Southerners who maintained the racial order: Many poor and working-class white Southerners lived in economic conditions not dramatically different from those of their Black neighbors. What the racial order offered them was psychological benefit — the assurance that no matter how poor or excluded from power, they were at least white. The "wages of whiteness," as historian David Roediger has described them (drawing on Du Bois), were not primarily economic but psychological: a sense of status that substituted for the economic and political power that most poor white Southerners also lacked. Elite white Southerners benefited more materially from racial hierarchy — it provided cheap labor, prevented interracial labor organizing, and maintained social control.
For Chinese immigrants in the West: The experience of exclusion was both legal and intensely personal. Most Chinese immigrants in this era were men who had left families behind in China, permanently barred from citizenship, unable to bring wives and children to join them, and legally vulnerable in a society that treated them as permanently foreign. The Chinatowns that Chinese communities built were both communities of solidarity and cages of exclusion — places where Chinese immigrants could live outside the reach of white hostility but within geographic and legal confinement.
For Native peoples under allotment and boarding school policy: The experience was one of deliberate, systematic destruction of every institution through which cultural identity was transmitted — land, language, family structure, religious practice, governance. The survival of cultural cores — ceremony, kinship networks, oral tradition, spiritual life that federal policy tried to prohibit but could not erase — represents one of the most consequential acts of resistance in American history.

Map Study

The Geography of Segregation and Racial Inequality

On a map of the United States in the 1890s, several spatial patterns illuminate the geography of racial inequality. In the South, Jim Crow legislation was most elaborate in the former Confederate states. The concentration of Black population in the rural South — the legacy of slavery's plantation geography — meant that racial labor systems of sharecropping and debt peonage operated most intensively in the cotton belt stretching from eastern Texas through Mississippi and Alabama to South Carolina and Georgia. Urban centers like Atlanta, New Orleans, Memphis, and Richmond developed more complex patterns of Black community life alongside the structures of segregation.

In the West, exclusion of Chinese immigrants and legal harassment of Japanese communities concentrated Asian American life in specific zones — San Francisco's Chinatown, California's Central Valley, Pacific Coast fishing communities. Native reservation lands, systematically reduced by allotment, were scattered across the West in geographically marginal areas after more valuable land had been distributed to white settlers. The borderlands of Texas, New Mexico, Arizona, and California were the zone of Mexican American dispossession and community formation.

In the North, de facto segregation organized Black life in urban centers. Chicago's South Side, Harlem in New York, and segregated neighborhoods in Detroit and Philadelphia developed as the result of real estate discrimination, restrictive covenants, and white resistance to residential integration — patterns already visible in the 1890s and intensifying as the Great Migration began after 1910.

Chapter Summary

Chapter 34 has examined the construction and operation of racial hierarchy in the United States in the decades following Reconstruction — roughly from 1877 to the early twentieth century. This was not a period of stasis or gradual progress but of deliberate, systematic construction of racial subordination through law, economics, violence, and ideology. The racial order built in this period was national in scope, though its most elaborate legal expression — Jim Crow — was concentrated in the South.

The chapter began by establishing the political economy of racial hierarchy after Reconstruction. The sharecropping system that replaced slavery trapped Black agricultural workers in cycles of debt dependency. Convict leasing extended forced labor under criminal justice cover. The disfranchisement mechanisms developed through the Mississippi Plan — poll taxes, literacy tests, grandfather clauses, white primaries — systematically stripped Black men of the right to vote guaranteed by the Fifteenth Amendment, doing so through nominally race-neutral legal mechanisms that operated entirely on racial grounds in practice. By the early 1900s, Black political participation in the South had been reduced from a meaningful presence to a statistical nullity.

Jim Crow laws mandated racial separation across virtually every domain of public life. The separate facilities were never equal; the "equal" provision of the "separate but equal" doctrine established in Plessy v. Ferguson was both legally unenforceable as applied and practically ignored by the governments that administered segregated systems. Black schools received a fraction of the funding of white schools. The logic of segregation was not administrative efficiency but social subordination — the daily ritualization of Black inferiority.

Lynching functioned as the terror that enforced the entire system. The epidemic of racial killing — more than four thousand documented deaths between 1877 and 1950, concentrated in the South — was not random violence but organized, public, and largely unpunished. Ida B. Wells's investigative journalism revealed the systematic nature of lynching, challenged its stated justifications, and sustained an anti-lynching movement that eventually contributed to making lynching politically costly enough that its frequency declined.

The Supreme Court's decision in Plessy v. Ferguson (1896) provided constitutional sanction for segregation that would stand for fifty-eight years. Justice Brown's majority opinion's claim that segregation imposed no "badge of inferiority" except in the misperception of its targets was a legal fiction so transparent that Justice Harlan's lone dissent could expose it with simple argument. But legal fictions embedded in Supreme Court decisions become law, and the Plessy doctrine shaped every legal challenge to segregation that followed until Brown v. Board of Education (1954) finally overturned it.

African Americans responded to this oppression not with acceptance but with extraordinary creativity and determination. The church, the school, the newspaper, the fraternal organization, and the business community were all vehicles of community sustenance and resistance. The debate between Booker T. Washington and W. E. B. Du Bois represented a genuine strategic argument about the best available path forward — accommodation and economic self-improvement versus political demands and liberal education — conducted by two exceptionally capable people under conditions of severe constraint. The NAACP, founded in 1909, synthesized elements of both approaches through the strategy of legal challenge that would eventually produce the civil rights victories of the mid-twentieth century.

The chapter also examined racial systems beyond the Black-white binary. Mexican American communities in the borderlands faced land dispossession, labor exploitation, and social subordination that were systematic and violent, even if the legal mechanisms differed from Jim Crow. Chinese immigrants were excluded from the country by the Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882. Japanese immigrants faced alien land laws designed to destroy successful agricultural communities. Native peoples were subjected to allotment policy that reduced their land holdings by two-thirds in less than fifty years, and to boarding school policy designed to eradicate their languages, cultures, and identities by removing children from their families.

What connected all of these systems was an ideology of racial hierarchy — the belief that some peoples were inherently superior to others, that racial boundaries were natural and necessary, that the maintenance of white supremacy was a legitimate object of law and policy. This ideology shaped the laws and institutions of a society that simultaneously proclaimed itself committed to freedom, equality, and democracy. The gap between that proclamation and that reality was the central problem of the period — and the organizing problem that resistance movements, in various forms, would spend the following century attempting to resolve.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Jim Crow
System of racial segregation laws and customs constructed in the South after Reconstruction, mandating separation of Black and white people in public spaces. Named after a minstrel caricature.
Segregation
Forced separation of people by race in public accommodations, schools, transportation, and social spaces, enforced by law, custom, and violence.
Disfranchisement
Systematic removal of the right to vote through legal mechanisms including poll taxes, literacy tests, grandfather clauses, and white primaries. Applied particularly to Black men in the South after Reconstruction.
Poll tax
Fee required as a condition of voting; used to exclude poor Black voters who had little cash income; required months in advance and sometimes accumulated year over year.
Literacy test
Requirement that prospective voters demonstrate ability to read and interpret legal text; administered selectively by white registrars to exclude Black applicants while passing white ones.
Grandfather clause
Provision exempting from voting requirements men whose ancestors had voted before 1867; effectively limited to white voters since Black men had not had the vote before 1867.
White primary
Democratic Party primary elections in Southern states restricted to white participants; since Democrats dominated Southern general elections, exclusion from the primary meant effective exclusion from political power.
Lynching
Extrajudicial killing by mob, used primarily against Black Americans as a system of racial terror and social control; more than 4,000 documented cases between 1877 and 1950.
Convict leasing
System by which Southern states leased convicted prisoners to private businesses as forced labor; disproportionately applied to Black men arrested under racially biased vagrancy and labor laws.
Sharecropping
Agricultural labor system in which tenants paid rent in the form of crop shares; frequently structured to trap Black farmers in debt dependency.
Debt peonage
Binding workers to employment through accumulated debt; illegal under federal law but widely practiced as a mechanism of labor control in the South.
Separate but equal
Doctrine from Plessy v. Ferguson holding that racially separate facilities satisfied the Fourteenth Amendment's equal protection guarantee; used to justify comprehensive segregation until overturned in Brown v. Board of Education (1954).
De jure segregation
Racial separation enforced by law; characteristic of the Jim Crow South; directly challengeable in court.
De facto segregation
Racial separation existing in practice without formal legal mandate; characteristic of Northern cities, produced by real estate discrimination, economic exclusion, and social custom.
Social Darwinism
Misapplication of Darwinian evolutionary theory to human societies; used to argue that racial and economic hierarchies reflected natural selection and were both natural and just.
Scientific racism
Pseudo-scientific tradition claiming to identify racial differences in intellectual and moral capacity through physical measurement; used to provide academic justification for racial hierarchy.
Accommodation
Booker T. Washington's strategic acceptance of segregation while pursuing economic and educational advancement; contrasted with Du Bois's insistence on full political rights.
Talented Tenth
W. E. B. Du Bois's concept that Black advancement required a leadership class educated in the liberal arts and capable of guiding the community politically, intellectually, and culturally.
Mississippi Plan
System of disfranchisement mechanisms adopted in Mississippi's 1890 constitutional convention and copied across the South; included poll taxes, literacy tests, and residency requirements.
Dawes Act
Federal law of 1887 dividing communally held tribal lands into individual allotments; reduced Native land holdings from approximately 138 million to 48 million acres between 1887 and 1934.
Boarding school
Federal and missionary institution designed to assimilate Native children by removing them from their communities and forbidding Indigenous language and cultural practice; Carlisle Indian Industrial School was the most famous model.
Chinese Exclusion Act
Federal law of 1882 prohibiting immigration of Chinese laborers and barring Chinese immigrants from naturalization; first racially targeted immigration restriction in American law.
Alien land law
State legislation prohibiting aliens ineligible for citizenship from owning land; directed primarily against Japanese immigrants in California and other Western states.
Borderlands
Geographic and cultural zones of overlap between the United States and Mexico, particularly in Texas, New Mexico, Arizona, and California; site of Mexican American dispossession and community formation.
Mutualista
Mutual aid organization serving Mexican and Mexican American communities in the borderlands; provided economic assistance, cultural preservation, and community solidarity.

Born enslaved in Virginia; founded Tuskegee Institute (1881); articulated accommodation strategy in the Atlanta Exposition Address (1895); dominant Black public figure until his death; secretly funded legal challenges to disfranchisement while publicly advocating accommodation.
W. E. B. Du Bois (1868–1963)
Born free in Massachusetts; first African American to earn a Harvard Ph.D.; author of The Philadelphia Negro (1899) and The Souls of Black Folk (1903); founder of the Niagara Movement (1905) and co-founder of the NAACP (1909); argued for full political rights and higher education over accommodation.
Justice John Marshall Harlan (1833–1911)
Former Kentucky slaveholder who became the Supreme Court's most consistent defender of the Reconstruction amendments; sole dissenter in Plessy v. Ferguson; author of the opinion's famous line that "our Constitution is color-blind."
Justice Henry Brown (1836–1913)
Author of the Plessy v. Ferguson majority opinion; articulated the "separate but equal" doctrine and argued that segregation imposed no "badge of inferiority" on Black Americans.
Richard Henry Pratt (1840–1924)
Army officer; founder of Carlisle Indian Industrial School (1879); architect of the boarding school approach to Native assimilation; coined the phrase "Kill the Indian, save the man."
Comité des Citoyens
New Orleans civil rights organization, primarily composed of Creole of color members, that organized the legal challenge to Louisiana's Separate Car Act resulting in Plessy v. Ferguson.
Tuskegee Institute
School founded by Booker T. Washington in Alabama (1881); center of vocational and agricultural education; symbol of Washington's educational philosophy; later site of the Tuskegee Airmen in World War II.
Carlisle Indian Industrial School
Federal boarding school in Pennsylvania, founded 1879; model for the system of more than one hundred government Indian boarding schools; most closely associated with Richard Henry Pratt's assimilationist philosophy.
NAACP
National Association for the Advancement of Colored People, founded 1909 through collaboration of Du Bois and other Black activists with white progressives; pursued legal challenges to segregation and disfranchisement throughout the twentieth century, culminating in Brown v. Board of Education (1954).
Niagara Movement
Civil rights organization founded by Du Bois and allies in 1905; demanded full political rights, equal education, and an end to racial violence; forerunner and organizational predecessor of the NAACP.
Memphis, Tennessee
City where Ida B. Wells worked as a journalist; scene of the 1892 lynching of Wells's friends that launched her anti-lynching campaign; her newspaper, the Memphis Free Speech, was destroyed by a white mob in response to her exposés.
The Borderlands
Geographic and cultural zones of Texas, New Mexico, Arizona, and California where Mexican American communities maintained complex identities and faced land dispossession, labor exploitation, and legal subordination in the decades after the U.S.-Mexican War.

Documenting a Lynching, Researching a Community

In 2018, the Equal Justice Initiative opened the National Memorial for Peace and Justice in Montgomery, Alabama, and published Lynching in America: Confronting the Legacy of Racial Terror, documenting more than 4,000 racial terror lynchings between 1877 and 1950.

Everyday Life and Social History: Living Within Segregation

The history of Jim Crow is most often told through its legal structures, political architecture, and most dramatic episodes of violence. But segregation was also experienced in the texture of ordinary life — in the decisions made every morning about how to navigate a world designed to communicate one's inferiority and to punish any challenge to that message.

A Black woman in Memphis in 1900 who needed to take the streetcar to work knew that she would sit in a designated "colored" section or be subject to arrest. She knew that if she stopped in a restaurant for lunch, she would be turned away from any establishment serving white customers. She knew that her children attended a school with fewer books, shorter hours, less-trained teachers, and worse physical facilities than the school her white neighbors' children attended. She knew that if she or her husband were accused of any crime — or simply found without employment — they might be swept into the convict leasing system. Within these constraints, she and her neighbors built a life. The church was the center of community. The women's club provided services that the city withheld: kindergartens, libraries, health clinics. The fraternal organization provided insurance against emergencies. The Black newspaper provided information, advocacy, and a sense of community extending beyond the immediate neighborhood.

In the borderlands, a Mexican American farmworker family in the 1900s faced the loss of land their parents or grandparents had farmed under Mexican title — a loss accomplished through court proceedings conducted in an unfamiliar language, with fees they could not afford. They worked on land they had once owned, or land like it, for wages that reflected their subordinate position in the labor market. The mutualista provided community solidarity and practical support. Spanish-language newspapers maintained cultural connection and community voice. The Catholic parish, maintaining traditions of religious and social life that predated American sovereignty, provided community continuity even as economic conditions changed.

For a Native family in the early 1900s, the boarding school had taken a child. The family waited for letters that sometimes came and sometimes did not. The child returned changed — able to speak English, less fluent in the language of home, trained for work that did not exist in the community, wearing clothes that marked the transformation. The land had been allotted and the surplus sold. The community maintained what it could: ceremony, kinship networks, oral tradition, the spiritual life that federal policy tried to prohibit but could not erase. The survival of that cultural core under conditions designed to extinguish it represents one of the most consequential acts of resistance in American history.

These ordinary lives — conducted within systems of oppression, sustained by community solidarity and cultural inheritance, shaped by both the weight of racial hierarchy and the human capacity for dignity and connection — are the human substance of the history that legal and political narratives can only partially capture.

Chapter Reflection

Chapter 34 has examined some of the darkest chapters in the American story — the systematic construction of racial hierarchy through law, violence, and ideology in the decades following Reconstruction. Understanding this history is not simply an exercise in documenting injustice, though the documentation of injustice is itself historically important. It is essential to understanding how the United States arrived at the civil rights struggles of the twentieth century, how racial inequality became embedded in economic and social structures that persist into the present, and what it cost — in human lives, human potential, and human dignity — to construct and maintain a system that contradicted the nation's foundational claims about equality and freedom.

The racial systems examined in this chapter were not natural or inevitable. They were built by specific people making specific choices, using law, violence, economic power, and ideology as tools. The architects of disfranchisement knew what they were doing — they said so in their own words, in constitutional conventions and legislative debates that are preserved in historical records. The designers of convict leasing knew it was forced labor. The builders of the boarding school system knew they were removing children from their families. The promoters of Chinese exclusion knew they were targeting a specific racial group. The claim that these systems were merely neutral administration, or natural social arrangements, or the expression of the people's will, was a claim made to obscure the deliberate, purposeful construction of racial hierarchy. Historians' task is to see through that obscuring and understand what was actually being done and why.

The resistance that communities mounted against these systems is equally important to understand. Ida B. Wells's anti-lynching journalism, Du Bois's intellectual challenge to accommodation, the Black church's role as community anchor, the mutualistas' preservation of Mexican American community life, the Native elders who maintained languages and ceremonies despite boarding school policy, the Chinese merchant associations that navigated exclusion — all of these represent the human capacity for dignity, solidarity, and resistance under conditions of extreme oppression. The civil rights movements of the twentieth century did not arise from nothing. They arose from communities that had been building the intellectual, organizational, and moral foundations of resistance throughout the long decades of Jim Crow and its analogues across the country.

The connection between this history and later American history runs directly through the following chapters. The Great Migration — the movement of millions of Black Southerners northward beginning around 1910 — was a direct response to Jim Crow, transforming both the South it left and the Northern cities it entered. The NAACP built the legal strategy that culminated in Brown v. Board of Education in 1954. The Niagara Movement's insistence on full political rights foreshadowed the demands of the civil rights movement's high tide in the 1960s. The debates between Washington and Du Bois continued, in different forms, in debates about integration and Black Power throughout the civil rights era. And the racial wealth gap, educational achievement gap, housing segregation, and disparities in criminal justice that characterize American society in the twenty-first century have roots in the deliberate policies documented in this chapter.

Understanding that connection between past policy and present inequality is not an argument that nothing has changed — much has changed — but it is an argument that history matters, that the past shapes the present through specific mechanisms that can be identified and analyzed, and that honest historical reckoning is a prerequisite for addressing the legacy of what was built. The United States that emerged from Reconstruction's collapse was a society that had made specific choices about who would have rights, who would have land, who would have economic opportunity, and who would have the protection of the law. Understanding those choices — who made them, who benefited, who paid the price, and what they cost — is central to understanding what the United States was, what it became, and what it might yet be.

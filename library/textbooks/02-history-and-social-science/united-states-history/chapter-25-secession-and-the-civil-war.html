Part VI — Civil War and Reconstruction
25

Secession and the Civil War Begins

United States History: Indigenous America, Republic, Conflict, and Change

Chapter Opening

"No one was killed by enemy fire during the entire bombardment of Fort Sumter. The historical consequences were not modest."

On the morning of April 12, 1861, Confederate artillery batteries ringing Charleston Harbor, South Carolina, opened fire on Fort Sumter, a federal installation occupying a man-made island at the harbor's mouth. The bombardment lasted thirty-four hours. Union Major Robert Anderson, commanding a garrison of fewer than ninety soldiers, could not return effective fire. Short of food, low on ammunition, and watching sections of the fort burn around him, Anderson surrendered on April 14. No Union soldiers died in the exchange itself, though two men were killed during a salute ceremony as the fort's flag was lowered. The physical damage was modest. The historical consequences were not. The firing on Fort Sumter announced that the United States had entered a civil war, a conflict that would kill approximately 620,000 soldiers, wound hundreds of thousands more, destroy the institution of slavery, transform the federal government, remake the meaning of American citizenship, and leave a legacy still contested more than a century and a half later.

The Civil War did not begin at Fort Sumter, however. It began in decades of political conflict over slavery, westward expansion, the meaning of the Constitution, and whether the United States would remain one nation or divide into two. By the time Confederate guns opened fire on the harbor, seven southern states had already left the Union, formed a new government, elected a president, and drafted a constitution. The secession crisis had been building since the election of Abraham Lincoln in November 1860, and the deeper roots of that crisis stretched back to the Missouri Compromise of 1820, the annexation of Texas, the Mexican-American War, the Compromise of 1850, the Kansas-Nebraska Act, and the steady hardening of sectional identities that made compromise increasingly difficult and then impossible. Fort Sumter was a trigger, not a cause.

Understanding the beginning of the Civil War requires understanding both the decision to secede and the decision to resist secession. White southern leaders who chose to leave the Union believed they were protecting their constitutional rights, their economic system, their racial order, and their political future. They feared that Republican control of the federal government meant the eventual destruction of slavery, the institution that organized southern society, economy, and power. They did not believe Lincoln would immediately abolish slavery — he had said repeatedly that he had no constitutional authority to do so — but they feared the long-term consequences of Republican dominance. They also believed secession was legal, that states had joined the Union voluntarily and could leave it the same way. Abraham Lincoln and the Republican Party disagreed on every point. They held that secession was unconstitutional, that the Union was permanent, and that the rebellion had to be suppressed. Lincoln's decision to resupply Fort Sumter rather than abandon it forced a confrontation that neither side could then avoid.

The Confederacy that formed in early 1861 was not simply a collection of aggrieved states. It was a new nation-state, with its own constitution, its own president and vice president, its own army, and its own ideology. Confederate leaders were explicit about what they were defending. Confederate Vice President Alexander Stephens, in his famous "Cornerstone Speech" of March 1861, declared that the Confederate government rested on the principle that Black Americans were not the equals of white Americans and that the subordination of Black people to white people was the natural condition of society. The Confederate constitution protected slavery by name in ways the U.S. Constitution never had. Secession was not primarily about tariffs or abstract constitutional theory, though those arguments appeared in the political rhetoric. It was above all a defense of slavery and the racial hierarchy it sustained.

The Union that resisted secession was also not a simple entity. Northern opinion was divided. Many Northerners opposed slavery but were not abolitionists. Many cared more about the integrity of the Union than about the fate of enslaved people. Some Northerners, particularly in the border states and the lower Midwest, had economic and social ties to the South and little enthusiasm for war. Democrats in the North worried that Lincoln's policies would destroy states' rights and expand federal power dangerously. At the same time, a powerful antislavery movement had been building for decades, and many Northerners — Black and white alike — saw the war from its earliest days as a war against slavery, even before the Lincoln administration was willing to say so publicly.

The early months of the war were defined by uncertainty, improvisation, and the shock of discovering that neither side would win quickly. Politicians and generals on both sides expected the conflict to be short. They were wrong. The first major land battle of the war, at Bull Run in Virginia in July 1861, ended in Confederate victory and Union rout, sending panicked soldiers and civilian spectators fleeing back toward Washington. That defeat shattered the illusion of an easy Union victory and forced a reckoning with the scale of the conflict ahead. The war would not be a short campaign. It would be long, destructive, and transformative in ways that few Americans fully anticipated in the spring and summer of 1861.

This chapter examines the beginning of the Civil War: why southern states seceded, how the Confederacy formed, how Lincoln and the Union government responded, what resources and strategies each side brought to the conflict, how border states complicated both sides' calculations, how early battles revealed the war's true scale, how governments mobilized their societies, what civil liberties meant in wartime, and what the war's opening months cost in human lives and experience. The Civil War was the most catastrophic event in American history. Understanding how it began is essential to understanding why it happened and what it eventually meant.

Historical Context

The Civil War did not appear without warning. The decades between 1820 and 1861 were filled with escalating conflicts over slavery, territorial expansion, and the constitutional structure of the Union. Each political crisis was resolved temporarily by compromise, but each compromise left deeper grievances and narrowed the space for future negotiation.

The Missouri Compromise of 1820 had established a geographical line — 36°30′ north latitude — dividing future slave states from free states in the Louisiana Purchase territory. For a generation it provided a workable if fragile framework. The Mexican-American War of 1846–1848 destroyed that framework by adding vast new western territories whose status was immediately contested. The Compromise of 1850 attempted another resolution, admitting California as a free state while strengthening the Fugitive Slave Act and leaving the question of slavery in other new territories to "popular sovereignty." The Fugitive Slave Act infuriated Northerners who were now legally required to assist in the capture and return of escaped enslaved people, even in free states. Harriet Beecher Stowe published her antislavery novel in 1852, and it became one of the most widely read books in the country, intensifying antislavery sentiment in the North.

The Kansas-Nebraska Act of 1854 reopened the question of slavery in territory where it had been prohibited under the Missouri Compromise. Senator Stephen Douglas of Illinois pushed the legislation through Congress to facilitate a transcontinental railroad route, hoping that popular sovereignty would resolve the slavery question peacefully. Instead it triggered a bloody struggle in Kansas between proslavery and antislavery settlers that gave the territory the name "Bleeding Kansas" and further convinced many Americans that compromise was impossible. The Republican Party emerged from these conflicts in 1854, a new organization committed to preventing the expansion of slavery into the territories. It was explicitly a sectional party — it had almost no support in the South — which made its rapid rise even more alarming to southern leaders.

The Dred Scott decision of 1857, written by Chief Justice Roger Taney, declared that Congress had no authority to prohibit slavery in any territory, that enslaved people were property rather than citizens, and that Black Americans had no rights that white Americans were bound to respect. The decision delighted proslavery southerners and outraged Republicans and antislavery Northerners. John Brown's raid on the federal arsenal at Harpers Ferry, Virginia, in 1859, intended to spark a slave rebellion, ended in Brown's capture and execution but terrified white southerners who feared an abolitionist conspiracy to destroy their society from within. By 1860, the Democratic Party had split into northern and southern factions over slavery, and the Republican Party's Abraham Lincoln won the presidency in a four-way race with only northern electoral votes. He had not appeared on the ballot in most southern states.

For many southern leaders, Lincoln's election was the decisive moment. Republicans had built their party on opposition to slavery's expansion. If Republicans controlled the executive branch and could appoint federal judges and officials, the long-term trajectory was clear even if slavery's immediate abolition was not imminent. South Carolina began secession proceedings within weeks of Lincoln's election. By February 1, 1861 — before Lincoln had even been inaugurated — six more Deep South states had followed: Mississippi, Florida, Alabama, Georgia, Louisiana, and Texas. Together they sent delegates to Montgomery, Alabama, where they drafted a Confederate constitution and elected Jefferson Davis of Mississippi as provisional president.

Lincoln's task in March 1861 was to find a response to secession that would hold the remaining slave states in the Union, reassure northern Democrats that he was not a radical, maintain the constitutional principle that secession was illegal, and somehow avoid triggering a war that would alienate potential Unionist allies in the South. His First Inaugural Address reassured southerners that he had no intention and no constitutional authority to interfere with slavery where it already existed, but he was equally clear that the Union was permanent and that acts of force against the government would be treated as insurrection. The government would hold federal property in the South. It would enforce the laws. But Lincoln hoped to do this without firing the first shot.

Section 25.1

Secession and Confederate Formation

The Decision to Leave the Union

The secession of the southern states between December 1860 and June 1861 was not a spontaneous or irrational act. It was the product of decades of political conflict, deepening sectional identities, and a well-developed set of arguments about constitutional rights, racial order, and economic interest. Understanding why southern states seceded — and why they seceded when they did — requires taking seriously both the stated reasoning of those who chose secession and the historical context that made their reasoning persuasive to a majority of white southern voters.

The election of Abraham Lincoln in November 1860 was the precipitating event. Lincoln received no electoral votes from the South — he had not appeared on the ballot in most southern states. He won the presidency entirely on the strength of northern votes, carrying eighteen free states with 180 electoral votes. He received about 40 percent of the popular vote, winning a plurality in a four-way race. His victory demonstrated something that southern political leaders had feared for years: it was now possible for a sectional northern party to win the presidency without carrying a single southern state. The South's political leverage at the national level was eroding.

For radical proslavery leaders — called fire-eaters in the political vocabulary of the time — Lincoln's election confirmed that the moment for decisive action had arrived. South Carolina moved first. On December 20, 1860, a state convention voted unanimously to dissolve the state's connection to the United States. South Carolina's declaration of the causes of secession was explicit: it cited northern states' refusal to enforce the Fugitive Slave Act, the election of a man whose opinions were hostile to slavery, and the antislavery ideology of the Republican Party. Six more states followed in rapid succession through February 1. In each state, delegates representing plantation-belt counties most dependent on enslaved labor drove the process forward.

The Confederate Constitution and Government

On February 4, 1861, delegates from the seceded states gathered in Montgomery, Alabama, to form a new government. The Confederate constitution closely resembled the U.S. Constitution in structure while making several significant alterations. It explicitly protected slavery, forbidding any law denying or impairing the right of property in enslaved people. It limited the Confederate president to a single six-year term. And while it claimed to protect state sovereignty, it simultaneously forbade any Confederate state from abolishing slavery — a striking limitation on state power in the very domain the secessionists claimed to be defending.

Jefferson Davis of Mississippi was elected provisional president. Alexander Stephens of Georgia, elected provisional vice president, explained the Confederate government's foundations with unusual directness in his "Cornerstone Speech" delivered at Savannah on March 21, 1861. He argued that the United States' founders had erred in treating slavery as a temporary evil that would eventually disappear. The Confederate government, he declared, rested on the principle of racial inequality and the permanent subordination of Black people — that this was its cornerstone. Slavery, not abstract constitutional theory, was the Confederacy's defining foundation.

The Question of the Upper South

By the time of Lincoln's inauguration on March 4, 1861, the Deep South had seceded, but the Confederacy was still far from complete. The slaveholding states of the Upper South — Virginia, North Carolina, Tennessee, and Arkansas — had not yet left the Union. Upper South states had stronger Unionist traditions, more diversified economies, and fewer enslaved people as a proportion of population than the Deep South. Virginia's secession convention rejected secession as long as the federal government did not attempt to coerce the Confederate states.

This waiting position could not survive Fort Sumter. When Lincoln called for 75,000 troops to suppress the rebellion, the Upper South states faced a stark choice: contribute soldiers to suppress a rebellion led by fellow southerners, or join the Confederacy. Virginia seceded on April 17, 1861, just two days after Lincoln's call for troops. Arkansas, Tennessee, and North Carolina followed within weeks. The Confederacy now included eleven states with a population of roughly nine million — including approximately 3.5 million enslaved people — and the military talent of officers like Robert E. Lee.

Section 25.2

Fort Sumter and the Outbreak of War

The Problem of Federal Property

When southern states seceded, they took possession of most federal property within their borders. But a handful of coastal forts presented a different problem, because they could be resupplied and reinforced by sea. Fort Sumter in Charleston Harbor was the most symbolically important. It sat on a man-made island at the harbor's entrance, within cannon range of the South Carolina batteries that Confederate forces began constructing almost immediately after secession.

Major Robert Anderson commanded the small Fort Sumter garrison. A Kentucky-born officer whose sense of military duty was stronger than any sympathy for secession, he had moved his command from the more exposed Fort Moultrie to the island fort in late December 1860. The Confederate government demanded evacuation. He refused. By late March 1861, the garrison was running short of food.

Lincoln's Decision

The Fort Sumter question crystallized the central political dilemma of Lincoln's presidency. If he abandoned the fort, he would appear to accept secession and the legitimacy of Confederate authority. If he sent warships and troops to force resupply, he might trigger a war that would drive the Upper South out of the Union. Lincoln found a middle course: he would send unarmed supply ships carrying only food, not weapons or reinforcements, and notified South Carolina's governor of this intention.

The decision placed the Confederacy in an equally difficult position. If Confederate forces allowed the resupply, they would appear weak. If they opened fire to prevent it, they would bear responsibility for starting a war. President Davis and his cabinet chose to act. They ordered General Beauregard to demand Fort Sumter's immediate surrender before the supply ships arrived.

The Bombardment and Its Consequences

Anderson refused to surrender. At 4:30 on the morning of April 12, 1861, Confederate batteries opened fire. The bombardment lasted thirty-four hours. Fires broke out inside the fort. After thirty-four hours, with the fort in flames and ammunition nearly exhausted, Anderson agreed to surrender. No one was killed by enemy fire, though two men died in a gun accident during the flag-lowering ceremony.

The news electrified the country. In the North, the attack on a federal installation under the American flag transformed the political atmosphere almost instantly. Northern Democrats who had opposed any confrontation with the South rallied around the Union. Stephen Douglas, Lincoln's longtime political rival, announced his support. On April 15, Lincoln issued a proclamation calling for 75,000 state militia volunteers. Northern governors pledged enthusiastic support. The governors of Virginia, Arkansas, Tennessee, North Carolina, Kentucky, and Missouri refused.

Fort Sumter matters not only as the war's opening event but as a demonstration of how political decisions produce historical turning points. Neither Lincoln nor Davis wanted to appear as the aggressor. Lincoln's decision to send food rather than weapons gave him the political high ground: the Confederacy had fired on an unarmed relief expedition delivering food to hungry soldiers. Davis's decision to fire before the ships arrived reflected his judgment that allowing the resupply would damage Confederate credibility more than starting a war would. The result was a war that neither side had quite chosen and neither side was prepared to fight.

Historical Vocabulary
Secession

Secession is the formal act by which a political unit declares that it is withdrawing from a larger political body. Southern leaders in 1861 argued that secession was a constitutional right grounded in the compact theory of the Union: states had agreed to join the United States voluntarily and retained the sovereign right to leave it. This argument drew on states' rights traditions going back to the Kentucky and Virginia Resolutions of 1798.

Abraham Lincoln and most Republican leaders categorically rejected this argument. They held that the Constitution created a permanent national government, not a voluntary compact among sovereign states. The preamble to the Constitution declared that it was ordained by "the People of the United States," not by individual states. Once a state had ratified the Constitution, it had given up any right to withdraw unilaterally. Secession was therefore not a constitutional right but an act of rebellion.

This constitutional debate was genuinely contested in 1861, and serious lawyers and politicians held both positions. The Civil War did not resolve the debate through legal argument — it resolved it through military victory. The constitutional question was not formally addressed by the Supreme Court until Texas v. White (1869), in which the Court declared secession unconstitutional.

Section 25.3

Union and Confederate Goals

What the Confederacy Was Fighting For

Confederate leaders were generally clear about what they were fighting to achieve. The most fundamental Confederate war aim was independence — the right to govern themselves separately from the United States, under a constitution that permanently secured the institution of slavery. They did not expect to conquer the North. They expected to defend Confederate territory until the North grew war-weary, political opposition in the North forced a negotiated settlement, and European powers — particularly Britain and France, which depended on Confederate cotton — recognized the Confederacy and provided diplomatic or military support.

This strategy rested on a plausible but ultimately incorrect reading of northern politics and European interests. Confederate leaders assumed that Northerners lacked the will to sustain a long and costly war to force the South back into a Union it did not want. They underestimated the depth of northern commitment to the Union and the ability of the Lincoln administration to sustain public support through military progress. Confederate military strategy in the war's early phases was primarily defensive: hold Confederate territory, make Union advances as costly as possible, and wait for northern war-weariness to create political pressure for peace.

What the Union Was Fighting For

Lincoln's stated war aim in 1861 was straightforward: the preservation of the Union. He was not, in 1861, fighting a war to abolish slavery. His message to Congress in July 1861 made the Union's cause explicit: the war was being fought to demonstrate that democratic government was not inherently weak, that a republic could defend itself against internal rebellion, and that the Constitution was the supreme law of the land. This framing had important advantages: it unified northern opinion across a wide spectrum, drawing in Democrats who cared deeply about national unity but little about abolition, and it gave border-state Unionists a reason to support the federal government without feeling they were supporting an abolitionist crusade.

Among African Americans and abolitionists, however, the Union's stated war aims were never sufficient. Frederick Douglass argued from the war's first days that a war against the South was necessarily a war against slavery. Black men volunteered for military service in northern cities and were turned away because the government was not yet ready to acknowledge that Black freedom was a Union war aim. As the war progressed, the contradiction between fighting a slaveholders' rebellion while officially leaving slavery intact became increasingly untenable. By 1862, Lincoln would begin moving toward emancipation — not because his personal views had changed dramatically but because military and political realities demanded it.

Section 25.4

Resources, Geography, and Strategy

The Balance of Forces

The United States entered the Civil War with enormous advantages in population, industrial capacity, railroad infrastructure, and financial resources. The Union had approximately 22 million people compared to the Confederacy's approximately 9 million (of whom about 3.5 million were enslaved and would not be used as soldiers in the war's first phase). The Union possessed about 90 percent of the nation's manufacturing capacity, 70 percent of its railroad mileage, most of its banking and financial institutions, and a functioning navy.

These advantages were real but not immediately decisive. The Confederacy had important asymmetric advantages. Confederate forces were fighting a defensive war on their own territory, which meant Union forces had to advance into hostile terrain, extend long supply lines, and occupy vast distances while Confederate forces could fall back on interior lines. The sheer size of the Confederate states — roughly 750,000 square miles — made total conquest an immense challenge even for a numerically superior force.

Confederate officers also had, at least initially, an advantage in quality of leadership at the senior level. Many of the most capable graduates of West Point had come from the South, and a significant number — including Robert E. Lee, Thomas Jackson, Joseph Johnston, and James Longstreet — resigned their U.S. commissions to serve the Confederacy. The Union's officer corps in early 1861 was thin at the top: Winfield Scott was seventy-five years old and in poor health; many of the army's most capable younger officers had gone south.

The Anaconda Plan and Union Strategy

General-in-Chief Winfield Scott's strategic concept for defeating the Confederacy became known derisively as the "Anaconda Plan" — after the constricting snake — but it was in many respects prescient. Scott proposed a naval blockade of all Confederate ports to strangle Confederate trade, a campaign down the Mississippi River to split the Confederacy in two, and maintaining pressure everywhere to prevent Confederate concentration. He planned for a long war of attrition rather than a dramatic invasion that would win quickly.

This strategy was deeply unpopular with a public that expected a quick, decisive war. "On to Richmond!" was the rallying cry of northern newspapers in June and July 1861. The pressure for action before armies were ready led directly to the disaster at Bull Run. But in its broad outlines, the Anaconda Plan proved correct: the war was won through attrition, blockade, and control of interior waterways as much as through any single dramatic campaign. Confederate strategy in the East focused heavily on Virginia and the defense of Richmond, which became the Confederate capital in May 1861. The proximity of Richmond and Washington to each other meant that the eastern theater would be the most watched and most politically sensitive front throughout the war.

Map Study

The Geography of the Civil War, 1861

Understanding the Civil War's geography is essential to understanding its military history. A map of the United States in 1861 reveals several critical features that shaped strategy and operations on both sides.

The Mason-Dixon Line and the Ohio River formed the practical boundary between Union and Confederate territory in the East and the border states. Virginia — which would eventually split, with its western counties becoming West Virginia in 1863 — sat between Washington and Richmond, making it the primary eastern theater. The Mississippi River was the great strategic artery of the continent: Union control of the river would allow forces to split the Confederacy in two and move supplies with far greater efficiency than overland movement permitted. The Appalachian Mountain chain divided the eastern and western theaters, because movement across the mountains was slow and logistically difficult; the two halves of the war required different strategies and often different commanders.

  2. How does the Mississippi River's geography explain why western campaigns often focused on controlling river crossings, forts, and cities along its banks?
  3. Why would a Confederate state surrounded entirely by Union-controlled territory be more difficult to supply and defend than one bordering the Confederate heartland?
  4. How might the South's large geographic extent — an advantage for defensive warfare — also create logistical problems for Confederate armies trying to move and supply forces?
Section 25.5

Border States and Divided Loyalties

Why the Border States Mattered

Four slave states did not secede: Delaware, Maryland, Kentucky, and Missouri. Delaware was never seriously at risk of secession. The other three were far more contested and far more important. Maryland surrounded Washington on three sides; if Maryland seceded, the Union capital would be entirely surrounded by Confederate territory. Kentucky controlled the Ohio River crossings that gave access to the Confederate heartland. Missouri, if lost to the Confederacy, would threaten Illinois and Indiana. Lincoln understood their importance with extraordinary clarity: his willingness to delay emancipation and initially decline to accept Black soldiers reflected the strategic necessity of holding the border states.

Maryland

Maryland's situation was the most immediately dangerous. On April 19, 1861 — just days after Fort Sumter — a Massachusetts regiment passing through Baltimore was attacked by a pro-Confederate mob. Four soldiers and twelve civilians were killed. Bridges were burned, telegraph lines cut, and Washington was briefly isolated. Lincoln responded by authorizing the military arrest of suspected Confederate sympathizers, including members of the state legislature, and suspended the writ of habeas corpus in the corridor between Washington and Philadelphia. Chief Justice Roger Taney ruled in Ex parte Merryman (1861) that only Congress had authority to suspend habeas corpus and that Lincoln's action was unconstitutional. Lincoln effectively ignored the ruling. Maryland never seceded, and as federal forces occupied strategic points in the state, Confederate sympathizers lost their ability to threaten its Unionism.

Kentucky and Missouri

Kentucky declared formal neutrality in May 1861, refusing to provide soldiers to either side. Lincoln accepted this rather than force Kentucky to choose too quickly. Both Union and Confederate forces eventually entered Kentucky — Confederate forces first, in September 1861 — and the Confederate invasion ended Kentucky's neutrality. The state legislature voted to align Kentucky formally with the Union. A Confederate shadow government was organized and Kentucky was admitted to the Confederacy on paper, but Confederate forces never controlled most of the state. Kentucky's divided loyalties — strong Unionist traditions alongside significant enslaved populations and cultural ties to the South — resulted in a state where family members sometimes fought on opposite sides and guerrilla warfare was brutal and persistent.

Missouri was the most violently contested border state. Governor Claiborne Jackson was a Confederate sympathizer who hoped to take Missouri out of the Union. When Captain Nathaniel Lyon of the U.S. Army captured the pro-Confederate state militia in St. Louis in May 1861, he prevented seizure of the federal arsenal and preserved Union control of the state's largest city, but triggered weeks of street violence. By summer 1861, Missouri was in a state of irregular civil war — Union and Confederate forces fighting for control while guerrilla bands operated largely independently of either regular army. The guerrilla war in Missouri was exceptionally brutal and shaped the character of the western theater throughout the conflict.

Section 25.6

Early Battles and Military Expectations

Bull Run and the End of Illusions

In the early summer of 1861, northern opinion demanded action. The ninety-day enlistments of many volunteers were running out; if the war was to be won before the armies dissolved, it had to be won soon. Political pressure was irresistible: "On to Richmond!" was the rallying cry of northern newspapers. Lincoln ordered an advance on the Confederate forces gathered at Manassas Junction, Virginia, where the Orange and Alexandria Railroad crossed Bull Run creek.

General McDowell's plan was sound in conception: a flanking movement that would roll up the Confederate left. But the army was inexperienced, coordination was poor, and the march was slow and disorganized. On July 21, 1861, Union forces initially drove the Confederate left back and seemed on the verge of victory. Then Confederate reinforcements arrived — including Thomas Jackson's Virginia brigade, which held a critical position on Henry House Hill with such steadiness that a Confederate general reportedly called out, "There is Jackson standing like a stone wall. Rally behind the Virginians!" The nickname "Stonewall" Jackson was born.

The Confederate counterattack in the afternoon turned Union retreat into rout. Soldiers and officers fled in disorder toward Washington, mixed with civilian sightseers who had come to watch what they expected to be a Union victory. The sight of armed men, ambulances, and panicked civilians crowding the road toward the capital shocked the nation. The Union army was not destroyed — casualties on both sides were roughly 2,900 Union and 2,000 Confederate — but its collapse in the battle's final hours demonstrated that untrained volunteers could not be expected to fight effectively after months rather than years of preparation.

The Aftermath and the Western Theater

Lincoln replaced McDowell with George B. McClellan, a thirty-four-year-old West Point graduate with a distinguished record in the Mexican-American War. McClellan was a superb organizer who took the dispirited force around Washington and rebuilt it into the disciplined, well-supplied Army of the Potomac. Soldiers genuinely loved him. But McClellan's caution about committing forces to battle would become one of the Union's persistent frustrations throughout 1862. Both sides spent the winter of 1861–1862 rebuilding and reorganizing. Meanwhile, in Missouri, Confederate forces defeated a Union army at Wilson's Creek in August 1861 and killed General Nathaniel Lyon — the first Union general killed in the war — demonstrating that the western theater would be as fiercely contested as the eastern.

Primary Source Window

Confederate Vice President Alexander Stephens, "Cornerstone Speech," Savannah, Georgia, March 21, 1861

Creator: Alexander Stephens, Confederate Vice President  ·  Date: March 21, 1861  ·  Type: Political speech, reported in the Savannah Republican

Context: Alexander Stephens of Georgia was the Confederate vice president. He had opposed secession before Lincoln's election but embraced the Confederate cause once the decision was made. On March 21, 1861 — weeks before the war began at Fort Sumter — he delivered an address in Savannah explaining the Confederate government's foundations to a large public audience.

Stephens argued that the founders of the United States had erred in treating slavery as a temporary evil that would eventually disappear. He declared that the Confederate government rested instead on the permanent truth of racial inequality — that African Americans were not equal to white Americans, that the subordination of Black people to white people was the natural and normal condition of society, and that this principle was the "cornerstone" of the new Confederate republic. He identified slavery not as a flaw to be corrected but as the foundation on which Confederate civilization would prosper.

Analysis: The Cornerstone Speech is one of the most historically important documents of the Civil War era. It states clearly what Confederate leaders sometimes obscured in more diplomatic communications aimed at European audiences. When Confederate apologists later argued that the war was about "states' rights" or "tariffs" rather than slavery, the Cornerstone Speech stands as direct evidence that the Confederate vice president himself, in a formal public address, identified slavery and racial hierarchy as the Confederacy's defining purpose. The speech also reveals the Confederate leadership's willingness to reject the Declaration of Independence's assertion of human equality. Its limits as a source are equally important: it represents one leader's views at one moment and does not tell us what ordinary Confederate soldiers or civilians believed they were fighting for.

Questions for students:

  1. What does Stephens identify as the error of the United States' founding generation, and what does the Confederate constitution correct?
  2. Why might Stephens have been so explicit about racial slavery as the Confederate "cornerstone" when other Confederate leaders were sometimes less direct?
  3. What can historians learn about Confederate ideology from this speech?
  4. What can historians not learn from this speech alone — what questions does it leave unanswered about how ordinary Confederate soldiers or civilians understood the war's purpose?
Section 25.7

Leadership and Mobilization

Raising Armies

Both the Union and the Confederacy began the war with tiny professional military forces and had to build mass armies from scratch. The U.S. Army in early 1861 had about 16,000 soldiers scattered across frontier posts and coastal forts. The Confederate states had no national army at all. Both governments relied on volunteers in the war's first year, appealing to patriotism, state pride, community pressure, and the widespread expectation that the conflict would be brief. Volunteers responded in enormous numbers: northern states exceeded Lincoln's call for 75,000 troops within weeks, and southern states organized regiments that filled Confederate needs. Men volunteered in family groups and neighborhood groups, forming companies and regiments organized by state, with officers often elected by the men themselves.

This volunteer system had serious weaknesses. Enlistments were short — many signed up for ninety days or six months. Officers lacked training and authority over men who had elected them. Both governments would eventually move toward conscription — the Confederate Congress instituted a draft in April 1862, more than a year before the Union followed in 1863 — but in 1861 both sides were working with what they could recruit voluntarily.

Financing the War

Wars cost money in quantities that neither government had ever approached before. The federal government's peacetime budget in the late 1850s was roughly $60–70 million per year; the Civil War would cost the Union alone more than $3 billion over four years. The Union relied on a combination of bond sales, new taxes (including the first federal income tax, enacted in August 1861), and paper money. Congress authorized the issuance of "United States Notes" — paper money not backed by gold or silver — in 1862. These "greenbacks" circulated at varying discounts to gold throughout the war but provided essential liquidity for a government that could not collect taxes fast enough to pay its bills.

The Confederacy faced a harder financial problem. Its economy was largely agricultural, its banking system less developed, and its taxing authority limited by states' rights ideology. Confederate finances relied heavily on printing money — issuing hundreds of millions of dollars in paper currency — which fueled severe inflation that eroded the purchasing power of ordinary Confederate citizens and soldiers throughout the war. The Confederacy also could not borrow easily from foreign governments that declined to extend diplomatic recognition.

Industrial Mobilization

The Union's industrial advantage was decisive in the long run. Northern factories could produce rifles, cannon, ammunition, uniforms, tents, blankets, shoes, railroad equipment, and medicine in quantities the Confederacy could never match. The Tredegar Iron Works in Richmond was the Confederacy's most important heavy industrial facility, producing artillery and ironclad plates, but it could not substitute for the North's overwhelming industrial capacity. The Confederacy made remarkable efforts to develop manufacturing but was always fighting a production deficit that the blockade made worse. The war accelerated industrial development in the North, where government contracts created enormous demand for manufactured goods and incentivized the expansion of factories across the region.

Section 25.8

Civil Liberties and Wartime Government

Lincoln and Constitutional Authority

The Civil War posed unprecedented challenges to constitutional government. Lincoln took broad views of presidential authority in ways that alarmed both Democrats and civil libertarians in his own party. His suspension of habeas corpus, his declaration of a naval blockade without a congressional declaration of war, and his call for 75,000 volunteers without waiting for Congress to convene all exceeded what strict constructionists considered the president's peacetime authority. Lincoln justified these actions on the grounds that the Constitution could not be a suicide pact — that a president who waited for Congress to act while the rebellion succeeded would have preserved the letter of the Constitution while destroying its substance. Congress, once it convened in special session on July 4, 1861, retroactively authorized most of Lincoln's emergency measures. But the constitutional tension between executive power and individual rights that his measures created persisted throughout the war.

The most visible civil liberties conflict was the arrest of civilian Confederate sympathizers imprisoned without trial in military custody. Congressman Clement Vallandigham of Ohio, a Democratic leader who gave speeches arguing that the war was unnecessary and Lincoln's administration tyrannical, was arrested by military authorities in May 1863, tried before a military commission, and banished to Confederate territory. The episode illustrated the tension between free speech and military necessity that the war consistently raised.

The Confederacy and Internal Dissent

The Confederate government faced its own civil liberties challenges. Jefferson Davis suspended habeas corpus and declared martial law in several Confederate cities in 1862. The Confederate draft provoked fierce opposition, particularly in Appalachian regions with few enslaved people, where men felt little connection to the plantation economy's defense. East Tennessee, western North Carolina, northern Georgia, and northern Alabama all contained significant Unionist populations that resisted Confederate conscription and sometimes provided intelligence or shelter to Union forces. The states' rights ideology that had justified secession created its own ironic problem: state governors like Joseph Brown of Georgia and Zebulon Vance of North Carolina resisted Confederate central authority in ways that made the war effort less efficient, arguing that the Confederacy was overriding the very state sovereignty it claimed to be defending.

Constitutional Connection

War Powers, Emergency Authority, and Civil Liberties

The Civil War raised constitutional questions about presidential power, individual rights, and the relationship between military and civilian authority that the founders had not anticipated.

Habeas Corpus: The Constitution provides in Article I, Section 9, that the writ of habeas corpus shall not be suspended except in cases of rebellion or invasion when public safety requires it. The placement of this provision in Article I suggests that only Congress can suspend it. Lincoln suspended it by executive order; Chief Justice Taney ruled in Ex parte Merryman that Lincoln's action was unconstitutional; Lincoln effectively ignored the ruling. Congress retroactively authorized the suspension in 1863.
Presidential War Powers: Lincoln's Civil War precedents — blockading ports, expanding the army, suspending habeas corpus, and later issuing the Emancipation Proclamation as a war measure — shaped how all subsequent presidents have understood executive power in wartime. The tension between presidential emergency authority and constitutional limits has recurred in every major American conflict from World War I through the early twenty-first century.
Military Tribunals: Lincoln authorized military commissions to try civilians accused of disloyal activities. The Supreme Court eventually ruled in Ex parte Milligan (1866) that civilians could not be tried by military tribunals where civilian courts were functioning — but the ruling came too late to affect wartime practice, and the precedent set by military tribunals remained contested in later conflicts.
Section 25.9

The War's Early Human Cost

Casualties and Medicine

The Civil War's killing power was a product of three developments that had not been fully anticipated: the widespread use of the rifled musket, the continued use of tactical formations designed for smoothbore weapons, and the inadequacy of medical knowledge to treat battlefield wounds. The rifled musket could kill accurately at ranges of 400 yards or more, far exceeding the 100-yard accuracy of smoothbore weapons. But generals continued to order infantry assaults across open ground in close formation, because that was how infantry attacks had been conducted for a century. The result was carnage on a scale that American armies had never experienced.

Medical science in 1861 predated the germ theory of disease and antiseptic surgery. Surgeons operated without sterile instruments, often cutting off limbs without cleaning their tools between patients. Infection killed more men than bullets in every major campaign of the war. Typhoid fever, dysentery, and pneumonia swept through encampments wherever sanitation was poor. For every soldier killed in battle, roughly two died of disease. Both the Union and the Confederacy scrambled to build medical organizations capable of handling mass casualties. Women played critical roles on both sides: Dorothea Dix was appointed Union superintendent of army nurses and organized hundreds of women into nursing service; Clara Barton collected medical supplies and eventually worked directly on battlefields; in the Confederacy, women like Sally Tompkins ran private hospitals in Richmond.

The Human Experience of Mobilization

For the hundreds of thousands of men who enlisted in 1861 — on both sides — the transition from civilian life to military service was a profound personal transformation. Most had never traveled far from home or experienced organized violence. Camp life was defined by tedium punctuated by physical hardship: hours of drilling, guard duty in rain and cold, inadequate food, and rampant illness. Letters home reveal a consistent arc — initial excitement, followed by the tedium of camp, followed by the shock of battle, followed by a more complicated relationship with the experience that mixed pride in service with revulsion at violence and grief at loss.

Families at home faced their own transformation. Women managed farms and businesses, organized Soldiers' Aid Societies, and coped with the information problem of a war fought at enormous scale: news was unreliable, rumors constant, and the arrival of a telegram always a moment of dread as well as relief. The war was not an equal experience. Wealthy men could hire substitutes to serve in their place, while the burdens fell most heavily on those with fewest resources — poor white soldiers in both armies, enslaved people forced to labor for Confederate military construction, and Black Northerners denied the right to serve and the social recognition that service might have brought.

Section 25.10

Chapter Review and Secession Documents Study

This chapter has examined how the Civil War began — through the secession of southern states, the formation of the Confederate government, the bombardment of Fort Sumter, and the first months of military mobilization and combat. Several major themes emerge.

First, secession was above all a defense of slavery. The evidence from secession declarations, the Confederate constitution, and Confederate leaders' own statements is clear and consistent. The Confederacy was founded to protect and perpetuate the institution of racial slavery. This does not mean that every Confederate soldier was personally motivated by slavery or that other factors were irrelevant to individual motivations. It means that the political decision to secede was driven by the defense of slavery, and that the Confederate state was built to protect it.

Second, the war's beginning was shaped by choices made under conditions of enormous uncertainty. Neither Lincoln nor Davis knew how long the war would last or what it would cost. The war's beginning, like many historical turning points, resulted from decisions that seemed rational at the time but produced consequences far beyond what any of the decision-makers anticipated.

Third, the war affected people across society in ways that went far beyond the men who did the fighting. Enslaved people saw the war as an opportunity for freedom almost immediately. Women on both sides took on new roles. Governments reached into citizens' lives in unprecedented ways — taxing, drafting, censoring, imprisoning — as the scale of the conflict forced improvisation in every area of governance. Understanding the Civil War's beginning means understanding these broad social transformations, not only the military and political events that triggered them.

Historical Debate

Was Secession About Slavery or States' Rights?

The question: Historians and Americans more broadly have long debated the fundamental cause of southern secession. One interpretation holds that secession was primarily a defense of slavery and the racial order it sustained. Another holds that secession was fundamentally about constitutional principle — states' rights and resistance to federal overreach.

Interpretation A — Slavery as the Central Cause: The secession declarations of South Carolina, Mississippi, Georgia, and Texas explicitly and repeatedly cited the threat to slavery as their primary reason for leaving the Union. Confederate Vice President Stephens's Cornerstone Speech identified racial slavery as the Confederacy's foundation. Confederate leaders fought throughout the war to preserve slavery — and when the war's outcome made emancipation inevitable, the Confederate Congress blocked a proposal to free some enslaved men in exchange for military service on the grounds that it would destroy what the Confederacy was fighting to preserve.
Interpretation B — Constitutional Principle and Sectional Identity: A minority of historians, and many popular voices particularly in the American South, have argued that the war was about broader constitutional questions — the right of states to leave the Union, resistance to tariff policies that favored northern manufacturers, and resentment of northern political dominance. Most Confederate soldiers were not slaveholders and were fighting for their homes, communities, and states against what they experienced as northern invasion.

Assessment: The documentary evidence strongly favors the first interpretation. The secession declarations themselves are primary sources of remarkable directness. At the same time, the experience of individual Confederate soldiers was genuinely shaped by loyalty to family, community, and place rather than abstract ideology. Both things can be true: the leaders who chose secession were defending slavery, while many of the soldiers who fought for the Confederacy were motivated by other loyalties.

Why it matters: The "Lost Cause" interpretation — which holds that the Confederacy fought for honor, states' rights, and principle rather than slavery — shaped public monuments, textbooks, and political rhetoric for more than a century. Historical clarity about secession's causes is essential to understanding not only the Civil War but the long history of racial violence, political conflict, and contested memory that followed it.

Student task: Locate the secession declarations of at least two southern states (available through the Yale Avalon Project and many state historical society archives). What reasons do they give for secession? How do those reasons compare to later arguments about "states' rights" or "tariffs"?

Multiple Perspectives

How Different Groups Experienced the War's Beginning

Enslaved People: From the war's first weeks, enslaved people across the South understood that the conflict offered possibilities for freedom that had not existed before. Enslaved people near Union lines began escaping to federal encampments almost as soon as Union forces appeared in the South. General Benjamin Butler at Fort Monroe, Virginia, refused to return escaped enslaved people to their owners in May 1861, reasoning that they were "contraband of war" — enemy property that could be seized rather than returned. This legal fiction opened the door to freedom for thousands who reached Union lines and pointed toward the war's eventual transformation into a war for emancipation.
White Southern Women: Many white women in the Confederate South greeted secession with enthusiasm. They sewed Confederate flags, urged husbands and sons to enlist, and invested emotional energy in the Confederate cause. But the war also disrupted the domestic order those women had inhabited. As men left for military service, women took on management of plantations, farms, and businesses. They confronted the reality that enslaved people were not the contented dependents that proslavery ideology had depicted — escapes increased, labor discipline became harder to maintain, and the Confederate home front experienced a quiet revolution in labor relations that official Confederate ideology never acknowledged.
Northern Working-Class Men: For working-class men in northern cities — immigrants, artisans, factory workers, day laborers — the war offered a mix of appeals and anxieties. Patriotic enthusiasm was real: enlistment bonuses, regular pay, and the adventure of military service attracted men who faced uncertainty in the civilian labor market. But working-class men also worried that the war would be fought by poor men while wealthy men avoided service through substitute hiring, and that emancipation might send freed Black workers north to compete for their jobs. These tensions would explode in the New York City draft riots of 1863, but they were visible from the war's beginning in Democratic political rhetoric.
Free Black Northerners: For free Black Americans in the North, the Civil War was from its beginning a war about slavery and freedom, even when the Lincoln administration refused to say so. Frederick Douglass argued from the war's first days that a war against the slaveholders' rebellion was necessarily a war against slavery. Black men volunteered for military service in northern cities and were turned away. Douglass understood that Black men's service to the Union would create moral and political claims on the nation's justice that could not be ignored — and pressed relentlessly for that opportunity, even as the Lincoln administration refused to grant it in the war's opening year.

Everyday Life and Social History: The Soldier's World in 1861

For the hundreds of thousands of men who enlisted in the summer and fall of 1861 — North and South — military life was both an adventure and a shock. Most had never been far from home, never experienced organized violence, never lived in close quarters with thousands of strangers organized by hierarchical command. Camp life was defined by tedium punctuated by physical hardship. Soldiers drilled for hours each day, stood guard duty at night in rain and cold, wrote letters home and waited weeks for replies, played cards, argued, sang songs, and got sick at alarming rates — measles swept through every new regiment of men from rural communities with no previous exposure, and typhoid fever, dysentery, and respiratory illness followed wherever sanitation was inadequate.

Food was a constant preoccupation and frequent complaint. The standard Union ration included hardtack (a dense biscuit that was rock-hard when dry and frequently weevil-infested when wet), salt pork or beef, dried beans, coffee, and occasionally fresh vegetables. Confederate rations were equally uninspiring and increasingly inadequate as the war and blockade progressed. Soldiers supplemented their rations by foraging, buying from sutlers who followed the armies, and receiving packages from home.

The first experience of combat was often described by soldiers as a kind of detachment from reality — the noise, smoke, and confusion of a nineteenth-century battlefield defied the organized heroism of patriotic imagination. Men who expected to feel brave discovered that their bodies behaved independently of their courage: legs moved without conscious decision, ears rang with artillery fire, and friends disappeared suddenly in ways the mind struggled to process. Veterans of the war's first battles wrote home with changed language — more careful, more honest about fear and confusion, less confident about adventure. Their letters, hundreds of thousands of which survive in archives across the country, are among the most important primary sources for understanding what the war was actually like for the men who fought it.

Women on the home front — both North and South — improvised their way through their husbands' and fathers' absences. They managed farms that suddenly lacked adult male labor, organized Soldiers' Aid Societies that made bandages and knitted socks, and coped with a constant information problem: news was unreliable, rumors constant, and the arrival of a telegram from the War Department was always a moment of dread. The war imposed very different burdens on different Americans: wealthy men could hire substitutes, officers had better food and medical care than enlisted men, and the heaviest burdens fell on those with fewest resources — poor white soldiers in both armies, enslaved people forced to labor for Confederate military construction, and Black Northerners denied the right to serve.

Cause and Effect

The Origins and Consequences of the Civil War's Beginning

Long-Term Causes

Decades of political conflict over slavery's expansion into new territories; the development of a northern free-labor ideology holding slavery incompatible with republican government; the growth of proslavery ideology arguing slavery was a positive good; the collapse of the Whig Party and the rise of the purely sectional Republican Party; economic divergence between an industrializing North and the plantation South.

Short-Term Causes

The Kansas-Nebraska Act (1854); "Bleeding Kansas" (1856–1858); the Dred Scott decision (1857); John Brown's raid on Harpers Ferry (1859); the splitting of the Democratic Party; the election of Abraham Lincoln in November 1860 without a single southern electoral vote.

The Events

South Carolina's secession (December 1860); six more Deep South states follow; the Confederate States of America is formed (February 1861); Confederate batteries bombard Fort Sumter (April 12–14, 1861); Lincoln calls for 75,000 volunteers; the Upper South states secede; the Civil War is fully underway by summer 1861.

Immediate and Long-Term Effects

Approximately 620,000–750,000 soldiers killed; legal abolition of slavery (13th Amendment, 1865); constitutional transformation of citizenship (14th and 15th Amendments); Reconstruction and its violent overthrow; transformation of the federal government's role in American life; a contested Civil War memory that would shape American culture and politics for more than a century.

Historical complexity: The Civil War cannot be explained by any single cause. It was the product of decades of structural conflict, political failure, and escalating crisis — shaped at every stage by choices that individual historical actors might have made differently. Complexity does not mean equivalence: the evidence strongly supports the conclusion that slavery was the central issue driving secession.

Continuity and Change Over Time

American Government and Civil Liberties in Wartime

What Changed

  • The scale and power of the federal government expanded dramatically: the army grew from fewer than 17,000 to more than a million men, and federal spending increased by orders of magnitude.
  • The income tax, the national banking system, and the military draft all represented expansions of federal authority the antebellum government had not possessed.
  • Civil liberties were restricted in ways that had no peacetime precedent: habeas corpus suspended, military tribunals used for civilians, newspapers occasionally suppressed.
  • The war accelerated industrialization in the North and transformed women's public roles on both sides.

What Stayed the Same

  • The fundamental constitutional structure survived: the Constitution was not suspended, Congress continued to meet, and elections continued to be held.
  • The two-party system continued, with Democrats providing persistent opposition to Republican war policies.
  • Racial hierarchy in the North remained largely intact in 1861; the war's opening year did not transform northern racial attitudes or Black civil rights, even as it began to transform the situation of enslaved people in the South.

Why this pattern matters: The Civil War established precedents for executive power, federal authority, and the relationship between national emergency and civil liberties that have shaped American governance ever since. Every subsequent wartime administration has grappled with the tensions Lincoln faced, and Civil War precedents have been cited — by supporters and critics alike — in debates from World War I through the twenty-first century.

Chapter Summary

The Civil War's beginning is rooted in four decades of escalating political conflict over slavery, sectional identity, and the constitutional structure of the Union. The election of Abraham Lincoln in November 1860 — the first Republican president, elected without a single southern electoral vote — precipitated the crisis that fire-eating proslavery leaders had been anticipating for years. South Carolina seceded on December 20, 1860, and six more Deep South states followed within weeks. By February 1861, the Confederate States of America had been formed, Jefferson Davis had been elected provisional president, and a Confederate constitution explicitly protecting slavery had been ratified.

Lincoln's response to secession was to insist on its unconstitutionality while attempting to avoid actions that would drive additional states out of the Union. His decision to resupply Fort Sumter with food rather than weapons forced Jefferson Davis to choose between allowing the resupply — which would damage Confederate authority — and opening fire, which would make the Confederacy the aggressor. Davis chose to fire. The Confederate bombardment of Fort Sumter on April 12–14, 1861, and Anderson's subsequent surrender, transformed the political climate in the North overnight and triggered the mobilization of northern opinion that Lincoln's call for volunteers both reflected and amplified.

The Upper South states — Virginia, Arkansas, Tennessee, and North Carolina — seceded after Lincoln's call for volunteers placed them in an untenable political position. The Confederacy now comprised eleven states with a broad geographic extent, significant agricultural wealth, and a corps of experienced military officers who would prove capable in the war's early years. The Union retained enormous advantages in population, industry, finance, and naval power, but those advantages would take time to translate into military effectiveness.

The border states — Delaware, Maryland, Kentucky, and Missouri — became critical arenas of political and military contest. Lincoln's handling of Maryland was legally controversial but politically effective. Kentucky's eventual alignment with the Union was strategically crucial. Missouri descended into a brutal irregular war. Both governments mobilized their societies for war in ways that had no peacetime precedent: volunteer armies were raised, trained, and equipped; bond drives, new taxes, and paper money financed governments whose spending suddenly dwarfed anything in American history; industrial production was redirected toward military supply; and women took on new roles in nursing and home-front management.

The war's first major land battle, at Bull Run on July 21, 1861, ended in Confederate victory and Union rout, shattering the illusion of a short, easy war and forcing a reckoning with the scale of the conflict ahead. Enslaved people recognized the war's revolutionary potential and began escaping to Union lines in growing numbers, forcing both governments to confront questions about slavery and freedom that they had hoped to defer. The Civil War's beginning established the stakes and many of the dynamics that would persist through four years of conflict. It was the product of decades of political failure, ideological conflict, and constitutional contradiction. Understanding how it began is essential to understanding why it was fought, what it cost, and what it ultimately meant for the United States.

Key Terms

Secession
The formal withdrawal of a state from the Union, claimed as a constitutional right by eleven southern states in 1860–1861; Lincoln and Republicans argued it was unconstitutional rebellion.
Confederate States of America
The government formed by seceding southern states in February 1861 at Montgomery, Alabama; eventually included eleven states with Jefferson Davis as president.
Confederate constitution
The governing document of the Confederacy; similar to the U.S. Constitution but explicitly protecting slavery and limiting the president to a single six-year term.
Fire-eaters
Radical proslavery southern leaders who had advocated secession for years before 1860, including William Lowndes Yancey, Robert Barnwell Rhett, and Edmund Ruffin.
Fort Sumter
Federal fortification on an island in Charleston Harbor, South Carolina; Confederate bombardment on April 12–14, 1861, opened the Civil War.
Cornerstone Speech
Confederate Vice President Alexander Stephens's March 1861 address explicitly identifying racial slavery as the Confederate government's foundational principle.
Anaconda Plan
Union General Winfield Scott's strategic proposal to defeat the Confederacy through naval blockade and control of the Mississippi River rather than a single decisive campaign.
Border states
The four slave states that did not secede: Delaware, Maryland, Kentucky, and Missouri; their loyalty was critical to Union strategy.
Contraband
Term used by General Benjamin Butler in May 1861 for enslaved people who escaped to Union lines; treating them as "contraband of war" allowed Union forces to decline to return them to Confederate owners.
Habeas corpus
The constitutional right to appear before a court before being held in prison; Lincoln suspended it in some circumstances, which Chief Justice Taney ruled unconstitutional in Ex parte Merryman.
Ex parte Merryman
1861 circuit court ruling by Chief Justice Taney that Lincoln's suspension of habeas corpus was unconstitutional; Lincoln effectively ignored the ruling during the war emergency.
Army of the Potomac
The principal Union army in the eastern theater, organized by General George McClellan from the forces around Washington after the defeat at Bull Run.
First Battle of Bull Run
The first major land battle of the Civil War, July 21, 1861, near Manassas, Virginia; Confederate victory and Union rout ended expectations of a short war.
Thomas "Stonewall" Jackson
Confederate general who earned his nickname at Bull Run for the steadiness of his brigade under fire; became one of the Confederacy's most celebrated commanders.
George B. McClellan
Union general who organized the Army of the Potomac into an effective fighting force but was notoriously reluctant to commit his forces to offensive battle.
Blockade
Union naval strategy to prevent Confederate ports from receiving foreign supplies or exporting cotton; grew progressively more effective through the war.
Greenbacks
Paper money issued by the U.S. government to finance the war, not backed by gold or silver; circulated at varying discounts to gold throughout the conflict.
Conscription
Compulsory military service (draft); the Confederacy instituted a draft in April 1862, the Union in 1863.
Impressment
Government seizure of private property or compelled labor for military purposes; used extensively in the Confederacy to support military construction and logistics.
States' rights
The political doctrine holding that states possess fundamental sovereignty limiting federal authority; used to justify secession, though the Confederate constitution itself limited state sovereignty by forbidding the abolition of slavery.
Popular sovereignty
The principle that territorial settlers, rather than Congress, should decide whether to permit slavery; used in the Kansas-Nebraska Act but produced violent conflict rather than peaceful resolution.

Major People and Places

Identification & Significance

Abraham Lincoln (1809–1865)
Sixteenth president; determined to preserve the Union and resist secession. His political skill in managing the border states, northern Democrats, and the evolving war aims of the Union was central to eventual Union success.
Jefferson Davis (1808–1889)
Confederate president; West Point graduate and former U.S. Secretary of War; committed and serious leader who was sometimes inflexible in dealings with subordinates and state governors.
Alexander Stephens (1812–1883)
Confederate vice president from Georgia; his Cornerstone Speech explicitly identified racial slavery as the Confederacy's foundational principle, providing the most direct documentary evidence of Confederate ideology.
Major Robert Anderson (1805–1871)
Union commander of Fort Sumter; his decision to hold the fort despite impossible odds made him a Union hero after the bombardment.
P. G. T. Beauregard (1818–1893)
Confederate general who commanded the Fort Sumter bombardment and led Confederate forces to victory at Bull Run.
Irvin McDowell (1818–1885)
Union commander at Bull Run; ordered into action before his forces were adequately prepared; replaced by McClellan after the defeat.
Thomas "Stonewall" Jackson (1824–1863)
Confederate general who earned his nickname at Bull Run; became one of the war's most celebrated commanders before his death at Chancellorsville in 1863.
Winfield Scott (1786–1866)
Aging Union general-in-chief at the war's outset; architect of the Anaconda Plan; replaced by McClellan in November 1861.
George B. McClellan (1826–1885)
Union general who organized the Army of the Potomac; a superb administrator but persistently reluctant to advance; eventually replaced by Lincoln.
Frederick Douglass (c. 1818–1895)
Formerly enslaved abolitionist leader who from the war's first days argued that a war against the slaveholders' rebellion was necessarily a war against slavery; pressed relentlessly for Black men's right to serve in the Union army.
Fort Sumter, South Carolina
Federal fortification whose bombardment opened the war; its fall galvanized northern opinion and enabled Lincoln to call for volunteers.
Montgomery, Alabama
First Confederate capital; site of Jefferson Davis's inauguration and the Confederate constitutional convention in February 1861.
Richmond, Virginia
Confederate capital after Virginia's secession in April 1861; its proximity to Washington made Virginia the central eastern theater throughout the war.
Manassas (Bull Run), Virginia
Site of the first major land battle, July 21, 1861; Confederate victory ended expectations of a short war and prompted thorough reorganization of Union forces.
Tredegar Iron Works, Richmond
The most important heavy industrial facility in the Confederacy; produced artillery and ironclad plates but could not substitute for the North's far greater industrial capacity.

Analyzing Secession Documents

The secession declarations and conventions of the southern states in 1860–1861 are among the most important primary sources for understanding the Civil War's causes. This project asks you to investigate those documents directly rather than relying on summaries or secondary accounts.

  1. Locate the secession declarations of at least three states. Good choices are South Carolina (December 1860), Mississippi (January 1861), Georgia (January 1861), and Texas (February 1861). These documents are available through the Yale Avalon Project, the Miller Center at the University of Virginia, and state historical society archives.
  2. Read each document carefully and take notes on: what reasons each state gives for secession; what specific grievances are mentioned; how prominently slavery appears and what specific language is used; whether the document mentions states' rights, tariffs, or other non-slavery grievances, and in what context.
  3. Compare your findings across the three documents. Do the states give similar or different reasons? Are there patterns in how they frame the conflict? Does any state give equal weight to slavery and other causes, or does slavery dominate?
  4. Compare the secession declarations to the Cornerstone Speech summary in this chapter. Is Stephens's argument consistent with what the secession declarations say, or does it differ in important ways?
  5. Write a 3–5 page analytical essay answering the following question: Based on the secession documents you have read, what were the primary causes of southern secession? How does the evidence in these documents support or complicate historical arguments about slavery versus states' rights as the war's cause?

Extension: Research how secession declaration language was discussed or disputed after the war during the "Lost Cause" era of the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. How did Confederate veterans and their sympathizers characterize the secession documents, and how do those characterizations hold up against the documents themselves?

Chapter Reflection

The Civil War's beginning is often reduced, in popular memory, to two images: the bombardment of Fort Sumter and the first shots of the war, and the tragic drama of men like Robert E. Lee choosing between loyalties. Both images contain truth, but neither captures the full historical significance of what happened in 1860 and 1861. The Civil War was not simply a tragedy of divided loyalties or a constitutional dispute over states' rights. It was a violent confrontation over the future of slavery in a nation that had proclaimed human equality as its founding principle while enshrining human bondage in its laws.

The war's beginning matters to later U.S. history in ways that extend far beyond the military campaigns of 1861–1865. The constitutional precedents Lincoln set — for executive power in wartime, for the federal government's authority over rebellious states, for the relationship between national emergency and civil liberties — shaped American governance for generations. The conflict over slavery that secession forced into open warfare was resolved by Union victory and the Thirteenth Amendment, but the social, political, and economic consequences of slavery's destruction were not resolved by 1865 or by the Civil War amendments of 1865–1870. They would be contested through Reconstruction, the Jim Crow era, the civil rights movement of the twentieth century, and beyond.

This chapter also reveals something important about how historical change happens. The Civil War was not the product of a single cause or a single decision. It emerged from the interaction of economic systems, political failures, ideological conflicts, geographical divisions, and individual choices made under conditions of uncertainty. The people who made those choices — Lincoln, Davis, the fire-eaters, the Unionist moderates, the enslaved people who escaped to Union lines, the soldiers who enlisted in the summer of 1861 — did not know how the story would end. They made decisions based on their understanding of their situations, their values, and their calculations about the future. Some of those calculations were right; many were catastrophically wrong.

The United States that emerged from the Civil War was fundamentally different from the United States that entered it. The destruction of slavery, the assertion of federal supremacy over state rebellion, the transformation of citizenship through the Fourteenth Amendment, and the physical and demographic devastation of the war itself all changed the nation permanently. Understanding the war's beginning is the essential prerequisite for understanding what the war ultimately produced — and what it ultimately failed to complete. Students who understand how the war began — why men and women on both sides made the choices they did, what was at stake in those choices, and what the early months of conflict revealed about the nation's deepest contradictions — are better equipped to understand not only the war itself but the long, unfinished story of American democracy that the war's outcome reshaped.

The Civil War remains the central crisis of American history — the event that determined whether the nation would survive and what kind of nation it would be. Its beginning was not inevitable, but the forces that produced it were powerful and long-building. The chapters that follow — on emancipation, on the remaking of American citizenship through Reconstruction, and on the long aftermath of the war — build directly on the foundations laid in the extraordinary months between Lincoln's election and the guns at Bull Run.

The 1920s: Mass Culture, Migration, and Conflict

United States History: Indigenous America, Republic, Conflict, and Change

Chapter Opening

"The 1920s were a decade of collision — between old and new, rural and urban, native-born and immigrant, white and Black, modern and traditional, prosperous and impoverished."

In the summer of 1925, a twenty-three-year-old Black woman named Josephine Baker boarded a steamship in New York City and sailed for France. She had grown up in poverty in St. Louis, learned to dance in segregated theaters, and performed in the all-Black Broadway revues that had recently electrified New York audiences. In Paris, she became a sensation — celebrated as an artist and a star in a way that American racism made impossible at home. Baker's story captures something essential about the 1920s. The decade was a time of explosive creativity, new freedoms, mass culture, and genuine cultural change. It was also a time of brutal exclusion, racial terror, nativist backlash, and deepening inequality. The same nation that produced jazz and the Harlem Renaissance also produced the Ku Klux Klan's largest revival and the most restrictive immigration laws in American history.

The 1920s began in the aftermath of World War I and ended on the edge of economic catastrophe. Between 1920 and 1929, the United States underwent a transformation in how ordinary Americans lived, worked, communicated, entertained themselves, and understood their place in the modern world. Automobiles became affordable for middle-class families. Radio entered millions of homes. Motion pictures with sound arrived in 1927, creating a mass entertainment industry of staggering size. Advertising became a sophisticated art and industry, teaching Americans to desire goods they had never before imagined they needed. Cities grew rapidly, skyscrapers defined new skylines, and consumer spending drove an economic expansion that seemed, to many observers, limitless.

Yet the prosperity of the 1920s was uneven, fragile, and exclusionary. Farmers struggled throughout the decade as agricultural prices collapsed after the wartime boom ended. Industrial workers fought for wages and working conditions and often lost, as unions weakened and employers asserted control. Black Americans in the South lived under a system of legal segregation, economic exploitation, and racial violence. The Great Migration continued to bring hundreds of thousands of Black southerners to northern cities, where they rebuilt communities, created cultural institutions, and organized politically — but where they also encountered discrimination in housing, employment, and daily life. Immigrant communities faced rising nativism, legal restrictions, and demands for Americanization. Indigenous people remained subject to federal policies designed to eliminate their land bases and cultures.

The decade's cultural conflicts were as revealing as its cultural achievements. Prohibition — the constitutional ban on alcohol that took effect in January 1920 — became one of the most contentious experiments in American history. It reshaped the relationship between law, commerce, morality, and organized crime. The Red Scare that followed World War I led to mass arrests, deportations, and restrictions on civil liberties in the name of fighting radicalism and communism. The revival of the Ku Klux Klan attracted millions of members across the country — not only in the South, but in the Midwest, the West, and in cities and suburbs — targeting not only Black Americans but also Catholics, Jews, immigrants, and those seen as threatening to Protestant American identity. The Scopes Trial of 1925, in which a Tennessee teacher was prosecuted for teaching evolution, brought into sharp relief the conflict between religious fundamentalism and scientific modernism that ran through the decade.

The cultural creativity of the 1920s was real and lasting. The Harlem Renaissance produced writers, poets, musicians, and visual artists whose work transformed American culture and gave African Americans a powerful voice in public life. Jazz — born in New Orleans from African American musical traditions blending blues, ragtime, gospel, and improvisation — became the music of the decade, spreading from Black communities to white audiences across the country and around the world. Poets like Langston Hughes and Claude McKay, novelists like Nella Larsen and Zora Neale Hurston, painters like Aaron Douglas, and musicians like Louis Armstrong and Duke Ellington created bodies of work that remain among the most significant in American history.

The 1920s also reveal how historical change is uneven, contradictory, and contested. The decade is sometimes remembered as the Roaring Twenties — an era of prosperity, liberation, and fun. That image captures something real, particularly for white, urban, middle-class Americans who benefited most from the decade's economic growth and cultural change. But the same decade was marked by Klan rallies in Indiana, deportation raids in American cities, lynchings in the South, and the dispossession of Native peoples on western reservations. Understanding the 1920s requires holding these contradictions together rather than smoothing them into a simple story of progress or decline.

This chapter examines the 1920s as a decade of collision — between old and new, rural and urban, native-born and immigrant, white and Black, modern and traditional, prosperous and impoverished. It explores consumer culture, new media, cultural movements, political repression, social conflict, and economic instability. The 1920s set the stage for the Great Depression, the New Deal, World War II, and the social movements that followed. To understand what came next, students must understand what this decade made, broke, and left unresolved.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain how mass consumer culture transformed American life in the 1920s and identify which groups benefited most from economic growth.
  2. Trace the development of new communications and entertainment technologies — automobiles, radio, and motion pictures — and analyze their social and cultural effects.
  3. Describe the Harlem Renaissance and explain its significance for African American history and for American culture broadly.
  4. Analyze the causes, character, and consequences of the Red Scare and the restriction of civil liberties in the early 1920s.
  5. Explain the causes and effects of immigration restriction, including the Immigration Acts of 1921 and 1924, and connect them to broader patterns of nativism and racial ideology.
  6. Evaluate Prohibition as both a social reform movement and a political and legal experiment, identifying its causes, effects, and contradictions.
  7. Compare the perspectives of religious fundamentalists and scientific modernists in the cultural conflicts of the 1920s, including the Scopes Trial.
  8. Identify the hidden economic weaknesses of the 1920s and explain how they contributed to the collapse of 1929.
  9. Assess how the 1920s shaped later developments in American politics, culture, civil rights, and economics.
  10. Contextualize the experiences of African Americans, immigrants, farmers, workers, women, and Indigenous peoples within the decade's broader history.
  11. Distinguish between the cultural image of the 1920s and the more complex historical reality revealed by evidence.
  12. Connect the conflicts of the 1920s to longer patterns of American history involving race, religion, immigration, democracy, and inequality.

  2. How did new technologies — the automobile, radio, motion pictures — change American society, and were those changes equally available to all Americans?
  3. What did the Harlem Renaissance mean for African Americans, and how did it challenge prevailing ideas about race in the United States?
  4. Why did Americans restrict immigration so sharply in the early 1920s, and what does that restriction reveal about American ideas of race, belonging, and national identity?

  8. How did the conflict between religious fundamentalism and scientific modernism reflect deeper tensions in American society and politics?
  9. What were the underlying economic weaknesses of the 1920s, and how did the prosperity of the decade conceal them?
  10. How do historians reconcile the genuine cultural creativity and liberation of the 1920s with the decade's racism, exclusion, and repression?

Historical Context

The 1920s cannot be understood without knowing what preceded them. The decade opened in the shadow of World War I — a conflict that had killed tens of millions of people across Europe, toppled four empires, and left the survivors with a mixture of grief, disillusionment, and a desperate desire for normalcy. The United States had entered the war in 1917, and Americans had mobilized on an unprecedented scale — producing weapons and supplies, sending two million soldiers to France, and organizing the home front under federal direction. The war had been sold to the American public as a crusade for democracy, but the peace that followed — the Treaty of Versailles, the League of Nations debate, the Senate's refusal to ratify the treaty — left many Americans feeling that the idealism had been betrayed and the sacrifices wasted.

The postwar years brought serious social and economic disruption. Returning soldiers needed jobs in an economy transitioning away from wartime production. A severe recession in 1920 and 1921 produced unemployment and deflation. Labor strikes swept across the country as workers demanded higher wages and better conditions, and employers pushed back aggressively. The Red Scare of 1919–1920 led to mass arrests and deportations of immigrants and suspected radicals. Race riots erupted in dozens of American cities during the Red Summer of 1919, as white mobs attacked Black communities in Chicago, Washington, D.C., Tulsa, and elsewhere. The Ku Klux Klan, dormant since Reconstruction, was revived in 1915 and began its dramatic expansion in the early 1920s.

Against this background, the prosperity that emerged after 1922 felt to many Americans like a relief and a reward. The economy grew, unemployment fell, industrial production increased, and consumer goods became more widely available than ever before. Republicans controlled the White House throughout the decade — Warren Harding, Calvin Coolidge, and Herbert Hoover — and all three generally supported policies favorable to business: high tariffs, low taxes, and limited regulation.

Longer-term changes also shaped the decade. The United States had become an urban nation for the first time by 1920, with more than half the population living in cities and towns. The Great Migration — the movement of Black Americans from the rural South to northern and western cities — accelerated during World War I and continued through the 1920s. Women had won the constitutional right to vote in 1920 with the Nineteenth Amendment. These changes created both new possibilities and new anxieties, as older patterns of social authority and cultural identity came under pressure from multiple directions simultaneously.

Understanding the 1920s also requires attention to what was not changing, or changing only slowly. For farmers — still a large portion of the American population — the decade brought not prosperity but chronic hardship. For most industrial workers, wages rose only modestly while hours remained long. For Black Americans in the South, legal segregation, disfranchisement, and racial terror continued with little federal challenge. For Native peoples, federal policy continued the assault on tribal land, culture, and sovereignty begun in the nineteenth century.

Section 38.1

The Modern 1920s

The 1920s are sometimes called the Roaring Twenties, and the label contains real historical truth — the decade was, for many Americans, a time of dramatic change, new pleasures, and expanding possibilities. But the roar was not equally audible to everyone. The decade's modernity was uneven, contested, and built on foundations that would crack in 1929 with catastrophic force.

When Americans voted in November 1920, they chose Republican Warren G. Harding over Democrat James Cox in a landslide. Harding campaigned on a return to "normalcy" — a word he probably invented, and one that captured a genuine national mood. After the extraordinary disruptions of World War I — the mobilization, the propaganda, the government expansion, the wartime restrictions on speech and dissent — many Americans wanted stability, quieter government, and economic opportunity. Harding and his successor Calvin Coolidge delivered a version of that promise, at least for some Americans. Federal taxes were cut, business regulation was relaxed, and the economy expanded.

The decade's modernity showed itself most visibly in cities. By 1920, for the first time in American history, more Americans lived in urban areas than in rural ones. Cities were growing faster than ever, fueled by internal migration from rural areas, continued immigration from Europe, and the expansion of industries that employed factory workers, office workers, retail clerks, and service workers. Skyscrapers rose in Chicago, New York, and other major cities, defining new skylines and symbolizing the power of corporate capitalism. Department stores, movie theaters, restaurants, and commercial amusements transformed urban public life. Electricity lit streets, homes, and businesses in ways that extended the active day and created new rhythms of work and leisure.

Women's lives were changing in ways that attracted enormous public attention. The suffrage victory of 1920 was the most dramatic legal change, but cultural changes were equally visible in the cities. Younger women — particularly white, middle-class urban women — adopted shorter skirts and hair, used cosmetics more openly, went to dance halls and movie theaters, smoked cigarettes in public, and pushed against older codes of feminine decorum. The "flapper" image — a media creation as much as a social reality — became a symbol of the new woman: modern, independent, and provocatively visible in public space. Behind this imagery lay real changes in women's social lives, employment, and consumer behavior. Yet the feminist energy of the suffrage movement did not translate easily into a sustained women's political movement in the 1920s, and most legal and economic inequalities that suffragists had identified remained intact.

The decade's cultural changes also reflected the nation's ongoing racial and ethnic diversity. First- and second-generation immigrants from Italy, Poland, Russia, Hungary, Greece, and dozens of other countries lived in urban neighborhoods where their languages, churches, synagogues, food, and customs were still central to daily life. Black Americans, most recently arrived from the South, were building communities in Harlem, Chicago's South Side, Detroit, and other cities. The 1920s did not resolve any of these communities' struggles for dignity and belonging — indeed, the decade brought new assaults on immigrant and minority rights — but those communities created enduring institutions and cultural forms that would shape American life for generations.

The decade ended in financial catastrophe. The stock market crash of October 1929 did not cause the Great Depression by itself, but it marked the end of the 1920s prosperity and revealed the structural weaknesses that had been building throughout the decade. The 1920s cannot be understood only through its glamour and energy. It must be understood as a decade whose promise was real but limited, whose prosperity was genuine but fragile, and whose conflicts over race, religion, immigration, and power were no less central to its history than its jazz, its movies, and its consumer goods.

Section 38.2

Consumer Culture and Advertising

The 1920s transformed the United States into a mass consumer society in ways that would define American life for the rest of the twentieth century. Before World War I, most Americans — especially rural Americans — produced many of the goods they consumed at home and bought necessities at local stores. By the 1920s, the combination of mass production, falling prices, rising incomes for some workers, new credit arrangements, and aggressive advertising had created a new relationship between Americans and the goods they purchased.

The foundation of consumer culture was mass production. American factories, organized around the principles of Taylorism — the systematic division of labor into repetitive, efficient tasks — and the moving assembly line pioneered by Henry Ford's automobile plants, produced manufactured goods in quantities and at costs that had been impossible a generation earlier. Ford's Model T, introduced in 1908, became the iconic example. Through continuous refinement of the assembly line at his Highland Park plant in Detroit, Ford drove the price of the Model T down steadily — from over $800 in 1908 to under $300 by 1925 — making it affordable for middle-class families and even some working-class ones. The logic of mass production spread from automobiles to refrigerators, washing machines, vacuum cleaners, radios, processed foods, and dozens of other products.

Consumer credit transformed buying habits. Installment buying — purchasing goods through regular payments over months or years — allowed Americans to buy things they could not afford outright. By the mid-1920s, the majority of automobiles, and large fractions of other major consumer goods, were purchased on installment plans. The practice spread purchasing power into the future, but it also created financial fragility for consumers who lost their jobs or faced unexpected expenses.

Advertising became a sophisticated industry in the 1920s. Agencies developed techniques for reaching mass audiences through national magazines, newspapers, billboards, and the new medium of radio. Advertisers moved beyond simple product descriptions to appeals based on psychology, aspiration, anxiety, and desire. They told consumers that the right soap would make them socially accepted, the right automobile would make them admirable, the right cosmetics would make them beautiful and attractive. They invented problems — body odor, bad breath, social awkwardness — and sold solutions. The annual volume of advertising spending in the United States grew from roughly $1.5 billion in 1918 to over $3 billion by 1929.

The geographic reach of consumer culture was uneven. Urban Americans had access to department stores, chain stores, and the full range of consumer goods. Rural Americans, who still made up a large share of the population, were reached by the Sears Roebuck and Montgomery Ward catalogs and, increasingly, by national radio programming. Consumer culture also reinforced inequalities. The goods of the consumer economy were more accessible to white, middle-class Americans than to Black Americans, immigrants, or the rural poor. Chain stores sometimes refused to serve Black customers. Advertising almost universally presented white Americans as the imagined consumer. The prosperity that consumer culture both reflected and promoted was not distributed equally.

Historical Vocabulary
Consumer Culture

Consumer culture refers to a social and economic system in which the purchase and use of manufactured goods plays a central role in everyday life, social identity, and cultural meaning. In a consumer culture, people define themselves partly through what they buy and display. Advertising, credit, mass production, and retail distribution all sustain consumer culture by creating demand, making goods affordable, and distributing them widely.

The United States developed a particularly intense consumer culture in the 1920s, for reasons that included the maturation of mass production, the growth of advertising, the expansion of credit, rising middle-class incomes, and the development of new communications technologies that spread advertising and product information widely. Consumer culture did not eliminate social inequality — access to consumer goods remained unequal across lines of race, class, and geography — but it created new cultural aspirations that cut across older social boundaries in complex ways.

Section 38.3

Automobiles, Radio, and Movies

Three new technologies reshaped American life in the 1920s in ways that would prove enduring: the automobile, the radio, and the motion picture. Each represented a different kind of transformation — in how Americans moved through space, how they communicated and entertained themselves, and how they shared national culture. Together, they made the 1920s feel to many Americans like a pivot into a new era.

The Automobile

The automobile was already a familiar technology by 1920, but in the 1920s it became genuinely democratic — available to a large fraction of the American middle class and even some working-class families for the first time. In 1910, there were roughly 450,000 automobiles registered in the United States. By 1920, that number had grown to 8 million. By 1929, there were approximately 27 million cars on American roads — nearly one for every five Americans, a rate of automobile ownership that no other country would approach for decades.

The consequences were vast. The automobile liberated Americans from the fixed routes of railroads and streetcar lines, enabling suburban development beyond streetcar lines, supporting the growth of tourist travel and vacation culture, and connecting rural areas to towns and cities in new ways. Gas stations, automobile repair shops, motels, and roadside restaurants appeared along improved road networks. The automobile also transformed the economy: the industry itself employed hundreds of thousands of workers and consumed enormous quantities of steel, rubber, glass, and petroleum. The social effects were equally important and more contested. Automobiles gave young people mobility and privacy that had been harder to achieve before, contributing to changing norms around courtship and dating. They contributed to suburban segregation as Black Americans were often excluded from the new suburbs automobile mobility made possible.

Henry Ford, whose River Rouge plant near Detroit became one of the largest industrial complexes in the world, was the decade's most celebrated industrialist and one of its most troubling figures. Ford was a virulent antisemite, and his newspaper, the Dearborn Independent, published antisemitic conspiracy theories that were distributed widely and influenced Nazi propaganda in Germany. Adolf Hitler reportedly kept a portrait of Ford in his Munich office. Ford's complexities — mass production pioneer, union-buster, antisemite, American icon — make him one of the decade's most illuminating and unsettling figures.

Radio

Radio broadcasting became a mass medium with remarkable speed. The first commercial radio station, KDKA in Pittsburgh, began broadcasting in November 1920. Within three years, there were over five hundred stations. By 1929, roughly 10 million American homes had radios. The Radio Corporation of America (RCA) and the networks that emerged — NBC in 1926 and CBS in 1927 — created national programming that could reach millions of simultaneous listeners.

Radio's cultural effects were immediate and far-reaching. It brought entertainment — music, comedy, drama, sports — into American homes in real time. It created shared national experiences: a championship boxing match or a presidential address could be heard simultaneously by audiences from Maine to California. It accelerated the spread of jazz and popular music and helped create national advertising campaigns. Radio also had effects that were more ambivalent: national programming began to standardize American entertainment and speech, contributing to the erosion of regional accents and local cultural forms over time. Black musicians found that radio — like the rest of the entertainment industry — was often segregated and exclusionary. National network radio was predominantly white in both performers and imagined audience.

Motion Pictures

The major Hollywood studios — Paramount, Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer, Warner Brothers, Universal, and others — consolidated their control over production, distribution, and exhibition in the 1920s, creating an integrated industry of extraordinary economic power. The star system promoted individual performers as celebrities whose personal lives were as much a product as their films. Stars like Charlie Chaplin, Mary Pickford, Douglas Fairbanks, Rudolph Valentino, and Clara Bow became among the most famous people in the world.

The transition to sound, accomplished with The Jazz Singer in 1927, transformed the industry again. The film starred Al Jolson, a white Jewish performer who appeared in blackface in portions of the film — a combination that illustrated the complex racial dynamics of 1920s entertainment, in which Black cultural forms were often appropriated, distorted, and repackaged for white audiences. Sound film opened new possibilities for musical performance and narrative, and drove enormous investment in new equipment.

By the late 1920s, Americans bought roughly 100 million movie tickets per week — roughly five times the total population — suggesting that most urban Americans went to the movies several times a month. Critics worried that movies encouraged immorality, loosened sexual norms, and undermined religious authority. Industry leaders, anxious to avoid government censorship, created the Motion Picture Producers and Distributors of America under Will Hays in 1922 — an early attempt at self-regulation that foreshadowed the more restrictive Production Code of the 1930s.

Section 38.4

Harlem Renaissance and Black Cultural Life

The Harlem Renaissance was one of the most significant cultural movements in American history. Between roughly 1919 and the early 1930s, a remarkable concentration of African American writers, poets, novelists, playwrights, visual artists, musicians, intellectuals, and political thinkers produced work in New York City's Harlem neighborhood that transformed American culture, challenged white supremacist ideas about Black intellectual and artistic capacity, and gave voice to the experiences and aspirations of African Americans in ways that would reverberate for decades.

The Making of Harlem

Harlem's transformation into the capital of Black America was a product of the Great Migration. Between 1910 and 1930, roughly 1.6 million Black Americans left the South, moving primarily to cities in the North and Midwest. They were leaving behind the violence, humiliation, and poverty of Jim Crow — the system of legal segregation, disfranchisement, and racial terror that governed Black life in the South. In the years before World War I, a real estate bust in Harlem left many buildings vacant, and a few Black real estate agents and churches acquired properties and opened them to Black tenants. Black institutions followed — churches, newspapers, fraternal organizations, political clubs, restaurants, theaters, and businesses. By the early 1920s, Harlem was home to hundreds of thousands of Black Americans from across the United States and the Caribbean, creating an extraordinary diversity of origins, experiences, and perspectives.

The New Negro

The concept of the "New Negro" — articulated most influentially by philosopher and critic Alain Locke in his 1925 anthology of that name — expressed the self-understanding of this generation. The New Negro rejected the accommodationist stance associated with Booker T. Washington, who had counseled Black Americans to focus on vocational education while avoiding direct confrontation with white supremacy. The New Negro demanded equality, celebrated Black culture and history, and insisted on the full humanity and creative capacity of African Americans. The Harlem Renaissance was the artistic expression of this political and cultural stance.

Literature and Poetry

Langston Hughes became the most widely celebrated poet of the Harlem Renaissance. Born in Joplin, Missouri in 1902, Hughes developed a poetic style that drew on the rhythms of jazz and the imagery of Black urban life. His 1926 essay "The Negro Artist and the Racial Mountain" articulated a manifesto: Black artists should draw on distinctly Black forms — jazz, blues, folk tradition, vernacular speech — rather than imitating white European models. His poems combined accessibility with political urgency, reaching audiences across class lines in ways that more formally experimental poetry could not.

Claude McKay, born in Jamaica and radicalized by his encounters with American racism, brought an international perspective and a militant voice to the Renaissance. His 1919 poem "If We Must Die," written in response to the race riots of the Red Summer, called for Black resistance and dignity with a power that echoed through the coming decades — Winston Churchill would later quote it in an address to the British Parliament during World War II. Zora Neale Hurston, trained as an anthropologist under Franz Boas, traveled through the South collecting Black folklore and vernacular speech, celebrating a folk culture that many middle-class Black intellectuals found embarrassing. Nella Larsen's novels Quicksand (1928) and Passing (1929) explored the psychology of mixed-race women navigating racial identity and social constraint — bringing to the Renaissance an attention to gender and sexuality that some of its more prominent male voices overlooked.

Music: Jazz and the Blues

Jazz had originated in New Orleans in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, emerging from the distinctive African American musical culture of that city. The music was characterized by improvisation, syncopation, and a collective, interactive quality. By the early 1920s, jazz had spread from New Orleans to Chicago, New York, and across the country, carried by recordings and the migration of Black musicians northward.

Louis Armstrong was the decade's most technically brilliant jazz musician. Born in New Orleans in 1901, he developed a trumpet technique and improvisational approach that set new standards. His recordings with the Hot Five and Hot Seven ensembles between 1925 and 1928 are among the most important in the history of American music. Bessie Smith was the most celebrated blues singer of the decade, recording for Columbia Records and reaching enormous audiences. She gave voice to the experiences of Black working-class women — loss, love, hardship, resilience — with a directness and power that made her recordings objects of intense emotional identification. Duke Ellington led the house band at the Cotton Club in Harlem from 1927. The Cotton Club was racially segregated — its Black performers entertained exclusively white audiences — exemplifying the complicated racial economics of 1920s entertainment.

Marcus Garvey and the UNIA

The most mass-based Black political movement of the 1920s was the Universal Negro Improvement Association (UNIA), led by Marcus Garvey. Born in Jamaica in 1887, Garvey founded the UNIA in 1914 and came to New York in 1916, where he rapidly built a movement of extraordinary size. At its peak, the UNIA claimed millions of members across the United States, the Caribbean, Central America, and Africa. Garvey taught that Black people had a glorious African history, that racism would never allow full equality in white-dominated nations, and that Black people should build their own economic institutions and ultimately return to Africa to build a free Black nation. His program of economic self-help included the Black Star Line shipping company.

Garvey's movement attracted passionate supporters among working-class Black Americans who found in his message of Black pride and Black power a compelling response to daily racism. He drew more ambivalent or hostile responses from established Black leaders — W. E. B. Du Bois and the NAACP were critical of his nationalism and skeptical of his business ventures. The Bureau of Investigation, under J. Edgar Hoover, surveilled Garvey's organization extensively and eventually prosecuted him for mail fraud. Garvey was convicted in 1923, imprisoned, and deported to Jamaica in 1927. His movement declined after his removal, but Garveyism left a lasting influence on Black nationalist thought and organizing.

Primary Source Window

Langston Hughes, "I, Too" (1926)

Creator: Langston Hughes  ·  Date: 1926  ·  Type: Published poem / literary and political statement

Context: Langston Hughes published "I, Too" in his 1926 collection The Weary Blues, one of the landmark publications of the Harlem Renaissance. Hughes was twenty-four years old and already one of the most celebrated young voices of Black American literary life.

The poem speaks in the first person as an unnamed Black man who is sent to eat in the kitchen when company comes — an image of racial exclusion and segregation governing Black life even in the North. The speaker is not broken by this exclusion. He grows strong, he laughs, he eats well, and he looks forward to the day when he will sit at the table openly and the company will see his beauty and be ashamed of their past treatment. The poem's final assertion — "I, too, am America" — claims Black belonging in the American nation against those who tried to make Black Americans invisible.

Analysis: The poem operates on multiple levels simultaneously. On its surface, it describes a domestic scene of racial exclusion. But the imagery of the kitchen and the table carries the weight of American racial history: the exclusion of Black Americans from full civic and social life, from education, from political participation, from equal treatment before the law. The speaker's insistence on his own strength, beauty, and future belonging is a statement of political as well as personal dignity. Hughes's choice of simple, accessible language was itself a political act — he believed that great Black poetry should reach working-class Black readers, not only educated elites.

Section 38.5

Red Scare and Political Repression

The years immediately following World War I brought a wave of political repression that shaped American civil liberties and political culture for the entire decade and beyond. The Red Scare of 1919–1920 — named for its focus on the perceived threat of communist and anarchist radicalism — led to mass arrests, deportations, surveillance, and restrictions on speech and association that revealed the fragility of constitutional protections when political fear was intense.

The Russian Revolution of 1917, which brought the Bolsheviks to power and established the world's first communist government, alarmed American political leaders, employers, and much of the middle class. American labor unrest in the immediate postwar period — including a general strike in Seattle, a nationwide steel strike, a Boston police strike, and dozens of other conflicts — seemed to some observers to be harbingers of revolutionary upheaval. A series of mail bombs sent to prominent political figures in spring 1919, and a subsequent bombing that damaged the home of Attorney General A. Mitchell Palmer, intensified the panic.

Palmer appointed the young J. Edgar Hoover to head the Bureau of Investigation's newly created General Intelligence Division. In November 1919 and January 1920, the Palmer Raids arrested thousands of suspected radicals across the country, often without warrants. Many of those arrested were immigrants, particularly Eastern European Jews, Russians, and Finns associated with socialist and anarchist organizations. Hundreds were eventually deported, including the anarchist activist Emma Goldman, who was sent to Russia in December 1919.

The Palmer Raids violated basic constitutional protections on a large scale. Arrests were made without warrants, suspects were held without charges for extended periods, and the right to counsel was denied. Assistant Secretary of Labor Louis Post reviewed the cases and found that the vast majority lacked legal basis; he canceled thousands of deportation orders, drawing fury from Palmer and Congress. Post's resistance showed that due process had defenders within the government, but also how easily administrative power could be wielded against constitutional rights when political fear was high. The Red Scare subsided in 1920 as Palmer's predictions of a revolutionary uprising proved wrong and his presidential campaign failed.

The Red Scare overlapped with a broader wave of nativist, racial, and religious hostility that found its most organized expression in the revived Ku Klux Klan. The revived Klan of the 1920s targeted not only Black Americans but also Catholics, Jews, and immigrants, marketing itself as a defender of Protestant American values. Its greatest strength was not in the deep South but in states like Indiana, Ohio, Oregon, and Colorado, and in cities and suburbs as well as rural areas. At its peak, the Klan had between 3 and 6 million members and was politically powerful in several states. Its methods included parades, rallies, community pressure, economic boycotts, and violence — floggings, cross burnings, and murders. The Klan's decline came rapidly after the conviction of Indiana Grand Dragon D. C. Stephenson in 1925 for murder, and his subsequent revelations of Klan corruption. But the underlying anxieties that had driven Klan membership did not disappear.

Historical Debate

Was the Red Scare a Legitimate Response to Real Threats, or a Political Panic?

The question: The Red Scare involved the federal government in widespread violations of civil liberties in the name of national security. Did the actual threat of radical revolution justify these measures, or was the Red Scare primarily political repression driven by fear, nativism, and hostility to labor organizing?

Interpretation A — A Response to Genuine Dangers: The bombings of 1919 were real; the Bolshevik government in Russia was actively calling for world revolution; and some American radical organizations were affiliated with the Communist International. In a period of widespread labor unrest and genuine social disruption, political leaders had reasonable grounds for concern about radical violence. The measures taken were excessive in many cases, but the underlying concern was not imaginary.
Interpretation B — Political Panic and Repression: The Red Scare was primarily driven by anxiety about immigration, labor organizing, and social change rather than by genuine evidence of revolutionary threat. The vast majority of those arrested in the Palmer Raids had no involvement in violence or conspiracy. The targets of repression were disproportionately immigrants and labor organizers who posed an economic and political challenge to employers and elites rather than a genuine threat to the constitutional order. The Red Scare used the language of national security to suppress legitimate political dissent and weaken the labor movement.

Evidence: Evidence for Interpretation A includes the actual bombings, the existence of communist and anarchist organizations with international connections, and the revolutionary rhetoric of some radical leaders. Evidence for Interpretation B includes Louis Post's legal analysis finding that most deportation cases lacked legal basis, the fact that most arrested had no connection to violent activity, and the subsequent failure of any revolutionary uprising to materialize.

Why it matters: This debate recurs whenever governments claim emergency powers in the name of national security — in World War II internment, Cold War McCarthyism, and post–September 11 surveillance and detention policies. Understanding the Red Scare helps students evaluate how governments balance security and civil liberties, and what happens when that balance tilts toward repression.

Section 38.6

Immigration Restriction and Nativism

One of the most consequential legislative developments of the 1920s was the dramatic restriction of immigration, achieved through laws passed in 1921 and 1924 that would shape the demographic composition of the United States for four decades. These laws drew on decades of nativist argument, racial science, and political organizing, and they reflected the anxieties of a significant portion of the American population about the pace and character of demographic change.

Mass immigration from Southern and Eastern Europe had been transforming American cities since the 1880s. By 1910, millions of Italians, Russians, Poles, Hungarians, Greeks, and Jews had settled in American cities and built communities. Nativist arguments against the new immigrants drew on several sources. Some were explicitly racial — pseudo-scientific ideas about hierarchies of European "races," popularized by writers like Madison Grant in The Passing of the Great Race (1916), claimed that immigrants from Southern and Eastern Europe were racially inferior to "Nordic" peoples. Some were cultural and religious — the new immigrants were predominantly Catholic or Jewish, and Protestant nativists feared the consequences for American religious identity. Some were political — the association of Eastern European immigrants with radicalism fueled fears that immigration was importing dangerous ideas.

The Emergency Quota Act of 1921 established numerical limits on immigration for the first time, restricting arrivals from each European country to three percent of that nationality's population in the 1910 census. This sharply reduced immigration from Southern and Eastern Europe while maintaining higher levels from Northern and Western Europe. The more comprehensive Immigration Act of 1924 reduced quotas further and established the national origins quota system, which would govern American immigration policy until 1965. Under this system, total immigration from Europe was capped at about 150,000 per year, with quotas heavily weighted toward Britain, Germany, and Scandinavia. The 1924 law also completely barred immigration from most Asian countries by excluding all "aliens ineligible for citizenship" — a legal category used since 1790 to exclude Asians from naturalization.

The immigration restriction laws had profound and lasting effects. The flow of European immigration slowed dramatically — from over one million in the early 1920s to around 150,000 per year after 1929. Jewish refugees fleeing Nazi Germany in the 1930s would find the quota system an insurmountable barrier, as the German quota was too small to accommodate the number seeking entry. Asian American communities were frozen in size and subjected to ongoing legal discrimination. Within the United States, the restriction laws cast a shadow of suspicion over immigrant communities and intensified assimilation pressures on second-generation Americans. The message Congress sent was clear: certain peoples were not the right kind of Americans.

Section 38.7

Prohibition and Organized Crime

Prohibition was one of the most ambitious social experiments in American history and one of the most consequential. The Eighteenth Amendment, ratified in January 1919 and taking effect in January 1920, prohibited the manufacture, sale, and transportation of intoxicating liquors. The Volstead Act, passed by Congress over President Wilson's veto in October 1919, provided the legal machinery for enforcement. For thirteen years, until the Twenty-First Amendment repealed Prohibition in 1933, the production and sale of alcohol was constitutionally banned in the United States.

The campaign for Prohibition had a long history, rooted in Protestant religious morality, the temperance movement, and genuine concern about the social costs of alcohol. Alcohol abuse was a real and serious problem in industrial America. The Women's Christian Temperance Union (WCTU), founded in 1874, and the Anti-Saloon League, established in 1895, had built powerful political organizations dedicated to restriction. The coalition that supported Prohibition was broad and ideologically mixed, including Protestant evangelical churches, Progressive reformers, employers who wanted sober workers, suffragists and women reformers who associated alcohol with domestic violence, and nativists who associated the saloon with immigrant and Catholic communities.

Prohibition's advocates expected the law to transform American social life. Instead, it produced a complex and contradictory reality. Alcohol consumption did decline in the early years — significantly so, according to health statistics showing falls in liver disease and alcohol-related hospital admissions. But millions of Americans simply did not accept the law's legitimacy, and the demand for alcohol created enormous economic incentives for illegal supply. Bootlegging became a massive industry. Home production of beer and wine was widespread. Illegal distilleries operated across rural and urban areas. Smuggling supplied high-quality spirits to those who could afford them. Speakeasies — illegal bars — proliferated in cities. New York City had an estimated thirty thousand speakeasies by the late 1920s.

The human costs of bootleg alcohol were real. In the absence of regulation, bootleg liquor quality was unreliable; some batches contained methanol or other toxic substances that caused blindness, paralysis, and death. The federal government directed that industrial alcohol be "denatured" — made poisonous — to prevent its diversion to drinking, and thousands of Americans died from consuming denatured industrial alcohol during Prohibition.

The most significant unintended consequence of Prohibition was the dramatic expansion of organized crime. Criminal enterprises found in the bootlegging trade an opportunity of a different order of magnitude. Al Capone, who took control of the Chicago bootlegging and crime operation in the mid-1920s, became the decade's most famous criminal. At his peak, Capone's organization earned an estimated $60 million annually. The St. Valentine's Day Massacre of 1929, in which members of Capone's organization killed seven members of a rival gang, dramatized the violence that organized crime's competition produced and drew national attention to the failure of Prohibition enforcement.

Prohibition also had cultural effects beyond its relationship to organized crime. The speakeasy culture that emerged was a genuine innovation: illegal bars sometimes mixed social classes and races in ways that respectable pre-Prohibition establishments had not permitted. Black musicians found that speakeasies and jazz clubs created unusual economic opportunities in environments that were sometimes less rigidly segregated than mainstream public establishments. The repeal of Prohibition in 1933 came partly under the pressures of the Depression — legalizing alcohol would create jobs and tax revenue — but also reflected genuine enforcement failure and broad urban unpopularity. Prohibition left lasting effects: the criminal organizations it had built continued operating after repeal, diversifying into other illegal enterprises.

Section 38.8

Religion, Science, and the Scopes Trial

One of the decade's most dramatic and revealing conflicts took place in a small courtroom in Dayton, Tennessee, in the summer of 1925. The Scopes Trial — formally State v. Scopes — brought into confrontation two visions of American culture, knowledge, and authority that had been building toward conflict for decades. It is remembered as a debate about evolution, but it was also about modernity, democracy, expertise, religion, and the question of who had the right to define what American education should teach.

Christian fundamentalism as a formal movement emerged in the early twentieth century in response to the challenges that modern biblical scholarship, science, and secular philosophy posed to traditional Protestant belief. Fundamentalism, named for a series of pamphlets called The Fundamentals published between 1910 and 1915, held that certain core Christian doctrines were not negotiable or subject to reinterpretation: the literal truth of the Bible, the virgin birth of Christ, the bodily resurrection, the second coming, and the substitutionary atonement. Against these doctrines, fundamentalists set what they saw as the errors of "modernism" — the attempt to reconcile Christian faith with Darwinian evolution, historical criticism of the Bible, and secular philosophy.

Tennessee's Butler Act, passed in March 1925, made it a misdemeanor to teach in a state-supported school any theory denying the divine creation of man as taught in the Bible. The American Civil Liberties Union, eager to challenge the law, advertised for a volunteer to test it. In Dayton, local citizens persuaded a young teacher named John T. Scopes to serve as the test defendant. William Jennings Bryan — three-time Democratic presidential candidate and celebrated voice of Protestant populism — joined the prosecution. Clarence Darrow — the nation's most celebrated defense attorney — came to defend Scopes. The national press, including H. L. Mencken of the Baltimore Sun, covered the proceedings with fascination and ridicule directed primarily at the small-town religious conservatives.

The trial's most dramatic moment came when Darrow called Bryan himself to the stand as an expert on the Bible, and subjected him to a withering cross-examination that highlighted the difficulties of literal biblical interpretation. Bryan stumbled on questions about the age of the earth and the nature of the days of creation. His performance was widely reported as a humiliation. Scopes was found guilty and fined one hundred dollars. The conviction was later overturned on a technicality, meaning the constitutional issues were never fully resolved. Bryan died five days after the trial ended.

The cultural legacy of the Scopes Trial was more complicated than popular memory suggests. Anti-evolution laws remained on the books in Tennessee and other states for decades. Textbook publishers, fearing controversy, often soft-pedaled or eliminated evolution from widely used textbooks for years after the trial. The conflict between religious conservatives and scientific modernity in American public education was not resolved in 1925; it continued to resurface in debates over curriculum throughout the rest of the century and beyond. The trial also illuminated something important about democratic authority: Bryan argued that the majority of taxpayers in a democratic state had the right to determine the content of public education; Darrow argued that scientific truth was not subject to majority vote. Both arguments captured something real about American democracy, and neither was entirely satisfying.

Constitutional Connection

Prohibition, the Scopes Trial, and the Limits of Law

The 1920s raised important constitutional questions about the relationship between law, majority will, and individual rights. Prohibition represented an attempt to use constitutional amendment to enforce Protestant moral standards on the entire population, including millions of Americans who did not share those standards. Its failure demonstrated that law cannot easily change behavior if the underlying social consensus is absent, and it raised questions about the limits of majoritarian lawmaking when it comes to personal behavior.

The Scopes Trial raised different constitutional questions. The Butler Act represented a majoritarian decision about public education — elected legislators had decided that evolution should not be taught. The defense argued this violated the constitutional separation of church and state, though the trial did not produce a definitive ruling. The Supreme Court would not rule on the teaching of evolution in public schools until Epperson v. Arkansas in 1968, when it struck down an anti-evolution statute under the First Amendment's Establishment Clause.

Both controversies illuminate how constitutional rights function as checks on majoritarian power. The Bill of Rights and the Fourteenth Amendment protect individual rights against government action — including democratically enacted laws. The tension between majority will and constitutional rights is a permanent feature of American governance, and the 1920s provided some of its most vivid illustrations.

Section 38.9

Prosperity, Inequality, and Hidden Weaknesses

The prosperity of the 1920s was real. Between 1922 and 1929, the American economy grew substantially. Manufacturing output increased. Real wages rose for many industrial workers, though not for all. The middle class expanded, and middle-class living standards improved. Unemployment was low. Corporate profits were high, and the stock market registered the enthusiasm of investors in a sustained bull market. But the prosperity concealed structural weaknesses that would make the collapse of 1929 catastrophic rather than merely severe.

Agricultural Depression

American farmers did not participate in the 1920s prosperity. After the wartime boom — when European demand for American food was enormous and agricultural prices high — the return of European agriculture after the war collapsed the export market. Prices for wheat, corn, cotton, and other commodities fell sharply in 1920 and 1921 and never fully recovered during the decade. Farmers who had borrowed to buy land and equipment during the boom found themselves with debt loads they could not service at the new, lower prices. Farm foreclosures were widespread throughout the 1920s. Rural banks tied to agricultural communities failed at high rates throughout the decade — a prelude to the broader banking crisis of the early 1930s. Congress passed farm relief legislation twice, and President Coolidge vetoed it twice, holding firmly to a philosophy of minimal government intervention.

Labor and Wages

The wages of industrial workers rose during the 1920s, but not as fast as productivity. The gap between what workers produced and what they were paid meant that purchasing power did not grow as fast as productive capacity. American factories were producing more goods than American consumers could buy. The gap was covered in the short term by consumer credit and foreign lending, but these were temporary solutions. Unions declined further during the 1920s as employers pursued a strategy called the "American Plan" — maintaining workplaces without union contracts — using a combination of welfare capitalism (providing workers with benefits like pensions and recreation facilities), company unions (employer-controlled alternatives to independent unions), and outright repression. Union membership fell from about 5 million in 1920 to about 3.4 million by 1929.

Financial Speculation

The stock market boom of the late 1920s was driven partly by genuine economic growth and partly by speculation disconnected from underlying value. The practice of buying on margin — purchasing stocks by borrowing a large fraction of the purchase price — allowed investors to control large amounts of stock with small amounts of capital. When prices rose, the returns were spectacular; when prices fell, the losses were equally spectacular. Investment trusts — holding companies that held portfolios of stocks and sold shares to investors — created complex financial structures in which leverage was applied at multiple levels, amplifying both gains and losses. Regulatory oversight of financial markets was minimal; the Securities and Exchange Commission did not exist until 1934.

Wealth Inequality

The prosperity of the 1920s was distributed very unequally. By the end of the decade, the top one percent of American households received roughly 23 percent of all income — a concentration of wealth not seen again until the early twenty-first century. An economy in which the wealthy own most assets and the majority of consumers are purchasing on credit is fragile. When the crisis came — when the stock market collapsed, banks failed, and credit contracted — the purchasing power that had been keeping the consumer economy running evaporated, and the downward spiral was difficult to arrest.

The Stock Market Crash of 1929

The stock market began to wobble in September 1929 and fell dramatically in the last week of October. On October 24 — "Black Thursday" — panic selling sent prices plunging, though intervention by major banks temporarily stabilized the market. On October 29 — "Black Tuesday" — the market fell again in the largest single-day loss in its history to that point. The crash wiped out billions of dollars of wealth on paper. Investors who had bought on margin faced ruinous margin calls. Banks that had lent to stock speculators found their loan portfolios suddenly worthless. The crash did not, by itself, cause the Great Depression — the Depression resulted from the complex interaction of the crash with the underlying structural weaknesses described above — but it was the event that triggered the collapse and revealed how fragile the prosperity of the 1920s had been.

Cause and Effect

The Collapse of 1929

Long-Term Causes

Chronic agricultural depression throughout the 1920s; structural imbalance between production and purchasing power; growing wealth inequality concentrating income at the top while wage growth lagged productivity; weakness of international trade and finance created by World War I debt burdens and high tariffs.

Short-Term Causes

Speculative stock market boom of the late 1920s; widespread practice of buying on margin creating leverage across the financial system; overleveraged banks that had lent heavily to speculators and strained consumers; Federal Reserve policy choices that may have accelerated the slowdown.

The Events

Stock market crash of October 1929 (Black Thursday, October 24; Black Tuesday, October 29). Wave of bank failures beginning in 1930. Credit contraction. Business investment collapsed. Unemployment began to rise sharply.

Long-Term Consequences

The Great Depression — the worst economic crisis in American history. The New Deal transformation of federal government's role. The regulatory architecture — SEC, FDIC, Social Security — that would shape American economic life for the rest of the century. Political consequences in Europe contributing to the rise of fascism.

Historical complexity: The crash of 1929 cannot be attributed to any single cause. It resulted from the convergence of long-term structural weaknesses, short-term financial instability, policy failures, and international economic fragility. Historians continue to debate the relative weight of these factors and the role of policy decisions in deepening and prolonging the Depression.

Continuity and Change Over Time

American Identity and Cultural Conflict: What the 1920s Changed and What It Didn't

What Changed

  • The scale and character of mass consumer culture transformed dramatically, reaching middle-class Americans in new ways
  • New technologies — automobile, radio, motion pictures — reached mass audiences and changed daily life
  • The demographic geography of Black America changed as the Great Migration accelerated
  • Women's legal status changed with the Nineteenth Amendment ratification in 1920
  • The legal framework for immigration changed fundamentally with the 1921 and 1924 restriction acts
  • The constitutional status of alcohol changed twice — with Prohibition and then its repeal

What Stayed the Same

  • The fundamental structure of racial inequality: legal segregation in the South was intact; racial discrimination in housing and employment was widespread in the North
  • The underlying distribution of economic power between capital and labor changed only modestly
  • The gender order, though pressed by suffrage and the flapper image, remained substantially intact in law and practice
  • Federal indifference to Black political rights and violence against Black Americans

Why this pattern matters: The uneven distribution of the 1920s' changes — the ways in which prosperity, freedom, and modernity were available to some Americans and not others — helps explain both the cultural conflicts of the decade and the political conflicts that followed. The Great Depression would reveal and deepen these inequalities, making them the central political issue of the 1930s.

Everyday Life and Social History

Leisure, Entertainment, and Daily Experience in the 1920s

For middle-class urban Americans of the 1920s, daily life changed in tangible ways. The automobile gave families mobility that the streetcar and the railroad had not offered. A Sunday drive, a summer vacation to a lake or mountain resort, a trip to an amusement park or roadside restaurant — these became available to millions of Americans who could afford a car and the gasoline to run it.

The radio created new domestic rhythms. Evening programming — comedy shows, musical performances, news broadcasts, sports — gathered families around the set in ways that television would later replicate. Radio created national audiences for specific performers and programs, accelerating the development of national popular culture. By the late 1920s, Americans bought roughly 100 million movie tickets per week — suggesting that most urban Americans went to the movies several times a month. The movie theater was air-conditioned in a world before home air conditioning, a glamorous escape, and a shared community experience.

For Black Americans in Harlem and other northern urban communities, the cultural scene offered genuine pleasures and creative spaces, even as discrimination shaped housing and employment. Dance halls, churches, barbershops, beauty salons, and community organizations provided social life. The newspapers — the Chicago Defender, the New York Amsterdam News, the Pittsburgh Courier — offered news, culture, and political commentary to audiences that the white press ignored or stereotyped.

For immigrant communities in ethnic neighborhoods, the 1920s brought both continuity with older ways of life and pressure toward Americanization. Neighborhood churches conducted services in Italian, Polish, Yiddish, or Greek. Foreign-language newspapers kept communities informed. The American-born children of immigrants were increasingly pulled toward English-language culture and American social life, creating generational tensions that were a common theme in immigrant literature of the period.

Chapter Summary

The 1920s were a decade of extraordinary cultural vitality and profound social conflict. The prosperity of the era was real for many Americans — particularly white, urban, middle-class Americans — and it produced genuine changes in how people lived, worked, entertained themselves, and understood their possibilities. New technologies, new consumer goods, new cultural forms, and new social freedoms marked the decade as a turning point in American life.

But the decade's modernity was contested at every level. The Harlem Renaissance produced landmark works of African American literature, music, and art that challenged white supremacist assumptions about Black intellectual and creative capacity and gave voice to the experiences of a people navigating racism, migration, and aspiration. Marcus Garvey's UNIA offered a different political vision, drawing millions to a message of Black nationalism and Pan-African dignity. Jazz and the blues crossed racial lines in complex ways, simultaneously demonstrating the creativity of Black American culture and exposing the exploitation through which white audiences and entrepreneurs often benefited from that culture.

The Red Scare of 1919–1920 revealed the fragility of civil liberties in a moment of political fear. The Palmer Raids targeted immigrants and labor organizers with mass arrests and deportations that violated constitutional protections on a large scale. The revival of the Ku Klux Klan demonstrated that racism, nativism, and religious bigotry could attract millions of adherents not only in the South but across the country. The immigration restriction laws of 1921 and 1924 encoded racial hierarchy and nativist ideology into federal law, closing the door to most immigration from Southern and Eastern Europe and barring nearly all Asian immigration.

Prohibition attempted to reshape American behavior through constitutional mandate and succeeded in some respects while failing dramatically in others. The law created an enormous black market, enriched organized crime, corrupted law enforcement, and demonstrated the limits of using law to change social behavior when the underlying social consensus is absent. The conflict between religious fundamentalism and scientific modernism found its most famous expression in the Scopes Trial of 1925, which brought the evolution controversy to national attention but left the underlying conflict unresolved.

The prosperity of the 1920s concealed structural weaknesses that would make the Depression catastrophic. Agricultural distress persisted throughout the decade. Labor's share of productivity gains remained limited. Financial speculation created dangerous leverage across the banking system. Wealth inequality was extreme. When the stock market collapsed in October 1929, these underlying weaknesses became visible, and the era of apparent prosperity gave way to the worst economic crisis in American history.

Understanding the 1920s requires holding these contradictions together: the genuine cultural creativity and the genuine repression; the real economic growth and the structural fragility; the new social freedoms and the enforced exclusions; the mass entertainment and the mass anxiety. The decade was neither simply a golden age nor simply a time of reaction and hatred. It was a decade in which the United States was changing rapidly, unevenly, and with consequences that would unfold through the decades that followed.

A term used by Black intellectuals of the 1920s to describe a generation that rejected accommodation and demanded equality, dignity, and creative expression; associated with the Harlem Renaissance.
Palmer Raids
Federal raids in 1919–1920 directed by Attorney General A. Mitchell Palmer against suspected radicals, labor organizers, and immigrants; mass arrests and deportations that violated constitutional due process protections.
Prohibition
The constitutional ban on the manufacture, sale, and transportation of intoxicating liquors, in effect from January 1920 to December 1933 under the Eighteenth Amendment.
Red Scare
A period of intense fear of radicalism, communism, and anarchism; the first Red Scare of 1919–1920 produced the Palmer Raids and a wave of political repression targeting immigrants and labor organizers.
Scopes Trial
The 1925 Tennessee trial of schoolteacher John Scopes for teaching evolution; became a national symbol of the conflict between religious fundamentalism and scientific modernism.
Speakeasy
An illegal bar operating during Prohibition; became a significant feature of urban social life in the 1920s, sometimes mixing social classes in ways that legal establishments did not permit.
Universal Negro Improvement Association (UNIA)
Mass organization founded by Marcus Garvey promoting Black nationalism, Pan-African unity, and economic self-sufficiency; attracted millions of members in the early 1920s.
Volstead Act
The federal law passed in 1919 providing the legal machinery for enforcing Prohibition; named for Representative Andrew Volstead of Minnesota.
Welfare capitalism
A strategy used by some employers in the 1920s of providing workers with benefits — pensions, recreation facilities, profit-sharing — in order to discourage independent union organizing.

Major People and Places

Identification & Significance

Louis Armstrong (1901–1971)
New Orleans-born trumpet player and vocalist; one of the defining figures of jazz; his recordings with the Hot Five and Hot Seven (1925–1928) set new standards for improvisational creativity and artistic virtuosity.
Josephine Baker (1906–1975)
St. Louis-born dancer and performer who became a celebrity in Paris; her career abroad illustrated the racial barriers that made equal artistic recognition impossible for Black performers in the United States.
Al Capone (1899–1947)
Chicago crime boss; the most famous figure of organized crime during Prohibition; his organization earned millions annually from bootlegging and gambling and demonstrated the failure of Prohibition enforcement.
Clarence Darrow (1857–1938)
Defense attorney in the Scopes Trial; famous for his skepticism of religious orthodoxy; subjected William Jennings Bryan to famous cross-examination challenging literal biblical interpretation.
Alain Locke (1885–1954)
Howard University professor and critic; edited The New Negro (1925), the defining anthology of the Harlem Renaissance; articulated the philosophy of the New Negro movement for a national audience.
W. E. B. Du Bois (1868–1963)
Sociologist, historian, and civil rights leader; co-founder of the NAACP; editor of The Crisis; critical of both Washington's accommodationism and Garvey's nationalism; a dominant intellectual voice of the era.
Duke Ellington (1899–1974)
Pianist and bandleader; led the house band at the Cotton Club from 1927; composed sophisticated jazz works of lasting importance; a defining figure of the Harlem Renaissance's musical dimension.
Henry Ford (1863–1947)
Automobile manufacturer; pioneered mass production through the assembly line; made cars affordable for middle-class Americans; also promoted virulent antisemitism through his Dearborn Independent newspaper.
Marcus Garvey (1887–1940)
Jamaican-born Black nationalist leader; founded the UNIA; promoted Black pride, Pan-Africanism, and economic self-sufficiency; convicted of mail fraud in 1923 and deported to Jamaica in 1927.
Langston Hughes (1902–1967)
Poet and central figure of the Harlem Renaissance; wrote accessible, jazz-inflected verse capturing Black urban life and political aspiration; his 1926 essay "The Negro Artist and the Racial Mountain" articulated a manifesto for Black artistic identity.
Zora Neale Hurston (1891–1960)
Anthropologist, folklorist, and writer; collected Black southern folk traditions; celebrated Black vernacular culture at a time when many Black intellectuals distanced themselves from folk forms.
Nella Larsen (1891–1964)
Novelist; wrote Quicksand and Passing, exploring the psychology of mixed-race women navigating racial identity and social constraint; brought attention to gender and sexuality within the Harlem Renaissance.
Claude McKay (1890–1948)
Jamaican-born poet and novelist; wrote militant poetry of defiance, including "If We Must Die" (1919); his novel Home to Harlem (1928) depicted Black working-class urban life with naturalistic frankness.
Bessie Smith (1894–1937)
Blues singer; one of the most celebrated and commercially successful musicians of the 1920s; gave voice to Black working-class experience with a directness and power that made her recordings objects of intense emotional identification.
Harlem, New York City
Neighborhood in upper Manhattan; became the cultural capital of Black America during the 1920s through the Great Migration and the concentration of Black cultural institutions; center of the Harlem Renaissance.
Chicago
Major industrial city; destination of the Great Migration; center of jazz, blues, and Black political life; site of the 1919 race riot; home of Al Capone's crime organization; symbol of both migration opportunity and northern racism.
Dayton, Tennessee
Site of the Scopes Trial in 1925; became a national symbol of the conflict between fundamentalism and modernism; demonstrated how local communities could become stages for national cultural battles.

Discussion Questions

  1. The 1920s produced both the Harlem Renaissance and the revived Ku Klux Klan, both women's suffrage and continued legal and economic inequality for women. How do you think about decades that contain such extreme contradictions? What framework helps you hold both realities in view?
  2. William Jennings Bryan argued at the Scopes Trial that the majority of a state's taxpayers should be able to determine what is taught in public schools. Clarence Darrow argued that scientific truth is not subject to democratic vote. Which argument do you find more compelling, and why?
  3. The immigration restriction laws of 1921 and 1924 were supported by a broad coalition that included Progressive reformers, labor advocates, racial nativists, and Protestant evangelicals. Does the diversity of supporters make the laws more or less understandable? How do you evaluate laws that emerge from mixed motivations?
  4. Historians debate whether Prohibition was primarily a failure or a more complicated experiment with some genuine effects. What criteria should historians use to evaluate the success or failure of a large-scale social policy?
  5. Marcus Garvey and W. E. B. Du Bois disagreed sharply about the best strategy for Black Americans in the 1920s. What are the strengths and limitations of each approach, and how do their disagreements illuminate the choices facing Black Americans in that era?
  6. Consumer culture in the 1920s promised individual fulfillment through the purchase of goods. What are the limits of that promise? Does consumer culture address the social and political problems of inequality and exclusion, or does it obscure them?
  7. The stock market crash of 1929 ended the decade's prosperity, but structural weaknesses had been present throughout the decade. What does this suggest about how we should evaluate periods of apparent prosperity? What warning signs might have been visible to contemporaries?

Tracing the 1920s in Your Community

The cultural conflicts and changes of the 1920s were not only national phenomena — they played out in specific places, affecting specific communities in distinctive ways. This project asks you to research how the 1920s affected a community of your choice, drawing on local historical sources.

  1. Choose a community. You might choose your own city or town, a city your family has ties to, or a community you have studied. You might also focus on a specific community within a larger city — an immigrant neighborhood, a Black community, a farm community, or a small town.
  2. Identify sources. Useful sources may include local newspapers from the 1920s (many available through public libraries and digital archives), local historical society collections, city directories and census records from 1920 and 1930, oral history collections, local church or synagogue records, city council minutes, and photographs from the period.
  3. Investigate specific questions. Choose at least three of the following to investigate: Did the community experience immigration from Southern or Eastern Europe? Was there a significant Black community, and did the Great Migration bring new residents? How was Prohibition enforced (or not) in the community? Was there Ku Klux Klan activity in the area? How did new technologies arrive and change local life? What was the community's economic situation during the decade? Were there specific conflicts or debates that reflected the broader cultural conflicts of the era?
  4. Synthesize your findings. Write a 3–5 page report describing what you found. Connect local history to the national patterns discussed in this chapter. Where did your community's experience match the national narrative? Where did it differ?
  5. Share your findings. Present your research to the class or to the MGU Library. Consider whether your local sources could be used to create a small exhibit, a digital timeline, or a community history guide for future students.

Library resources to use: Local history collections, newspaper archives (including ProQuest Historical Newspapers), census records (available through Ancestry Library Edition), historical society resources, and the MGU History Library's reference materials on the 1920s, immigration history, and African American history.

Chapter Reflection

The 1920s occupy a complicated place in American historical memory. The decade is remembered fondly by some as a time of prosperity, creativity, and liberation — the Jazz Age, the Roaring Twenties, the age of Fitzgerald and Hemingway and Louis Armstrong. It is remembered critically by others as a time of Klan revival, immigration restriction, and racial violence. Both memories capture something real. The task of the historian is not to choose between these memories but to understand how both were possible at the same time — and why.

This chapter has argued that the 1920s were a decade of collision. The collision between mass consumer culture and the economic precarity of millions of Americans who could not fully participate in it. The collision between the creative explosion of the Harlem Renaissance and the racial terror of the Klan and the persistence of Jim Crow. The collision between the aspirations of immigrant communities and the nativist hostility that culminated in the immigration restriction laws. The collision between the modernist culture of the cities and the religious fundamentalism of rural and small-town America. These collisions were not accidental — they reflected deep and enduring divisions in American society over race, class, religion, gender, and national identity that have recurred in different forms throughout American history.

The Harlem Renaissance deserves to be understood not as a cultural curiosity of the 1920s but as a major chapter in American cultural history. The writers, poets, musicians, and artists of the Renaissance produced work that had been impossible before — not because Black Americans lacked the talent or intelligence, which they had always possessed, but because the conditions of the Great Migration had concentrated creative talent and audience in ways that generated a collective cultural flowering. Their work challenged the foundational lie of white supremacy with evidence so powerful and so beautiful that it could not be honestly dismissed. The Harlem Renaissance is part of the foundation of American literature, music, and visual art, whether or not this is always acknowledged.

The political and legal developments of the decade — the Red Scare, immigration restriction, Prohibition, the Klan revival — illuminate how fear and anxiety drive political behavior. Americans in the 1920s faced rapid and disorienting change: demographic change driven by immigration and the Great Migration, economic change driven by industrialization and the market revolution, cultural change driven by new technologies and new ideas. The political responses to this change — restriction, repression, and in some cases outright violence — reflected the powerful human tendency to defend familiar identities and social structures against perceived threats. Understanding that tendency historically does not require endorsing it; it requires seeing clearly how it shaped events that had real consequences for millions of people.

Finally, the economic history of the 1920s offers a lesson in the limits of surface prosperity. The decade appeared prosperous to most observers, and in significant ways it was. But the prosperity concealed structural weaknesses that would make the coming crisis catastrophic. The lesson is that surface appearances require scrutiny, that prosperity distributed unequally is fragile prosperity, and that the health of an economy depends on conditions — the purchasing power of working people, the stability of the financial system, the resilience of agriculture — that are not always visible in the headline statistics. The 1920s shaped what came after in ways that make them essential to understanding the rest of the twentieth century. The Great Depression and the New Deal were direct consequences of the decade's economic structure. The civil rights movement drew on the organizational infrastructure and cultural confidence that the Harlem Renaissance and the Great Migration had built. To understand the United States after 1930, you must understand the 1920s — not as prologue or background, but as a decade whose choices, conflicts, and creations had lasting consequences.

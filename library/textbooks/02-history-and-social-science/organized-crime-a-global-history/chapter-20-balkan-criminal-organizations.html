Balkan Criminal Organizations

Organized Crime: A Global History

Chapter Opening

"The collapse of Yugoslavia did not merely redraw borders. It opened a corridor through the heart of Europe through which guns, drugs, and desperate human beings flowed for a generation."

In the spring of 1999, NATO aircraft bombed bridges over the Danube River in Serbia, hoping to cripple the military infrastructure of Slobodan Milošević's government during the Kosovo War. Within weeks, criminal entrepreneurs had organized flotillas of small barges to ferry contraband across the broken river, routing cigarettes, fuel, and weapons around the shattered crossings with a speed and efficiency that astonished Western intelligence analysts. The same networks that carried humanitarian goods also carried heroin. The same middlemen who brokered ceasefires also brokered arms deals. The same ethnic militias that patrolled villages also taxed smuggling convoys at improvised checkpoints. War and organized crime, in the Balkans of the 1990s, were not merely parallel phenomena. They were deeply, structurally entangled.

The Balkan Peninsula, occupying the southeastern corner of Europe where the continent tapers toward the Aegean and the Adriatic, has served for centuries as a crossroads between East and West, between the Ottoman and Austro-Hungarian empires, between the Communist bloc and the capitalist West. That geographic position, which has made the region a site of extraordinary cultural richness and relentless external interference, also made it a natural corridor for illicit trade. Long before the word "Balkan" entered the vocabulary of Western strategic analysis, merchants, smugglers, and armed escorts were moving goods and people across the mountain passes and river valleys of what is today Serbia, Albania, North Macedonia, Kosovo, Montenegro, Bosnia and Herzegovina, Croatia, Bulgaria, and Romania.

The particular form of organized crime that emerged from the Balkans in the final decade of the twentieth century was shaped by a specific and catastrophic historical conjuncture: the simultaneous collapse of Communist governance across the region, the violent disintegration of Yugoslavia into successor states, and the sudden exposure of isolated, impoverished populations to the demand signals of Western European consumer markets for drugs, cheap labor, and sex. The result was not simply a crime wave. It was the rapid construction of transnational criminal enterprises of remarkable sophistication, adaptability, and geographic reach, built by actors who had learned, in the wars of Yugoslav succession, that violence was a viable business tool and that state authority was negotiable.

Balkan criminal organizations are not monolithic. They do not share a single origin, a common ethnic identity, a single organizational structure, or a unified leadership. Albanian criminal networks, rooted partly in the traditional clan-based social code known as the Kanun and partly in the specific conditions of post-Communist Albania and the Kosovo conflict, are distinct in their culture and organization from Serbian criminal organizations, which evolved from the black-market networks of the Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia and from the paramilitary units that fought in the wars of the 1990s. Bulgarian criminal organizations, shaped by the particular relationship between Bulgaria's security apparatus and the criminal underworld during the Communist period, operate according to their own logic. Romanian organized crime, energized by EU accession and the freedom of movement that accompanied it, has developed its own specializations and geographic footprint. Yet all of these organizations operate in overlapping spaces, often cooperate on specific criminal ventures, and share a common capacity for geographic mobility that distinguishes them from older, more territorially rooted crime groups.

This chapter examines Balkan criminal organizations through four central themes identified in the chapter outline: their role in smuggling networks, their centrality to arms trafficking during and after the Yugoslav wars, their dominant position in European human trafficking, and their dramatic expansion across Western Europe and beyond in the post-Cold War period. Each of these themes connects to broader questions about the relationship between state collapse, criminal enterprise, violence, and governance that run throughout this textbook. The Balkan case is not merely a regional curiosity. It is one of the most instructive examples in modern history of how political chaos creates the conditions for criminal power to grow, and of how criminal networks, once established, can outlast and outlive the conflicts that created them.

Understanding Balkan organized crime also requires understanding the limits of easy categorization. The same individuals, networks, and organizations that appear in criminal justice databases as narcotics traffickers or human smugglers also appear in diplomatic records as political brokers, in business registries as legitimate entrepreneurs, and in military histories as paramilitary commanders. The Balkans produced, in the 1990s and 2000s, a class of actors who moved with remarkable fluency across the boundaries between state, crime, business, and war, and whose careers illustrate, with unusual clarity, the theoretical frameworks for understanding organized crime examined in earlier chapters of this textbook.

Finally, the Balkan case illustrates the consequences of organized crime for ordinary people who did not choose to participate in it. The women trafficked from Moldova and Romania into Western European brothels, the Albanian teenagers whose villages were depopulated by emigration organized by criminal smugglers, the Bosnian families who found weapons flooding their communities long after the formal end of the war — these are the human costs of the criminal enterprises this chapter examines. Understanding those costs, alongside the structures and methods of the organizations that imposed them, is essential to a complete picture of what Balkan organized crime has meant and continues to mean.

Historical Context

The Balkan Peninsula has been a crossroads of empires, religions, and civilizations for millennia. The term "Balkan," derived from a Turkish word for mountain, reflects the region's long incorporation into the Ottoman Empire, which ruled much of the peninsula from the late fourteenth century until the Balkan Wars of 1912 and 1913 finally expelled Ottoman authority from most of southeastern Europe. Under Ottoman rule, the Balkans developed complex systems of informal governance, local autonomy, and extralegal exchange that would leave lasting marks on the region's political culture. The practice of operating outside or around formal state authority, of negotiating with power rather than submitting to it, and of maintaining loyalty to family, clan, or ethnic community over loyalty to the state all have deep roots in Balkan social history.

The period between the two World Wars saw the Balkan states, including Yugoslavia (formed from the ruins of the Austro-Hungarian Empire), Romania, Bulgaria, Albania, and Greece, struggle with poverty, ethnic conflict, authoritarian governance, and foreign interference. The experience of World War Two was particularly devastating. Yugoslavia alone lost over a million people to the combined effects of Nazi occupation, Italian occupation, domestic civil war between Communist Partisans and the fascist Ustasha regime, and ethnic massacres. The scars of that period, including the massacres of Serbs by Ustasha forces, of Jews and Roma by both occupiers, and of Croatian civilians by Partisan forces, were never fully processed and would resurface with terrible force in the 1990s.

After 1945, the Communist regimes that took power across the Balkans — the Yugoslav federation under Josip Broz Tito, the Romanian People's Republic under Nicolae Ceaușescu, the People's Republic of Bulgaria, and the uniquely isolated People's Socialist Republic of Albania under Enver Hoxha — imposed state monopolies on economic life while simultaneously generating extensive black markets. Communist shortages of consumer goods created persistent demand for unofficial channels of supply. Party officials and security service officers who controlled access to scarce resources became, in effect, criminal entrepreneurs operating within the shell of the state. The security services of these regimes, including Yugoslavia's UDBA and Romania's Securitate, maintained relationships with criminal networks that could be exploited for intelligence, currency smuggling, or the movement of sensitive materials. When Communist rule collapsed between 1989 and 1992, these relationships did not disappear. They transformed.

Yugoslavia's disintegration was the defining event of Balkan organized crime's modern history. The Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia, a loose federation of six republics and two autonomous provinces held together by Tito's authority and by the balancing of ethnic tensions through federal structures, began to fracture following Tito's death in 1980. Economic decline, the rise of ethnic nationalist movements in Serbia, Croatia, and Slovenia, and the failure of federal institutions to manage mounting tensions produced a sequence of wars: the Ten-Day War in Slovenia in 1991, the Croatian War of Independence from 1991 to 1995, the Bosnian War from 1992 to 1995, the Kosovo War in 1998 to 1999, and the brief insurgency in North Macedonia in 2001. These conflicts killed approximately 130,000 people, displaced millions more, destroyed economic infrastructure across the region, and generated exactly the conditions of institutional collapse, armed mobilization, and ungoverned space in which organized crime thrives.

Albania's trajectory was separate from but parallel to Yugoslavia's collapse. The People's Socialist Republic of Albania under Enver Hoxha had been the most isolated and repressive state in Communist Europe, sealing its borders, banning religion, and constructing over 700,000 concrete bunkers across the country in preparation for an invasion that never came. When the Communist regime collapsed in 1991 and 1992, Albania had virtually no infrastructure for a market economy, no functioning civil institutions, and no experience of the rule of law. The resulting chaos reached its nadir in 1997, when the collapse of a network of fraudulent pyramid investment schemes wiped out the savings of much of the Albanian population, triggering a popular uprising in which armories across the country were looted and an estimated 600,000 to 700,000 weapons entered circulation. Those weapons, and the social networks mobilized to move them, were the foundation of Albanian organized crime's later international reach.

The geographic position of the Balkans, connecting the heroin-producing poppy fields of Afghanistan and the opium laboratories of Turkey to the consumer markets of Western Europe, gave the region's criminal networks a structural advantage that no amount of law enforcement pressure has fully overcome. The "Balkan route," running from Afghanistan through Iran, Turkey, Bulgaria or Romania, and then through the successor states of Yugoslavia into Western Europe, became in the 1990s the dominant pathway for European heroin supply. It remains, in modified form, one of the most important drug-trafficking corridors in the world today.

The opening of European borders through the Schengen Agreement, the progressive expansion of the European Union to include Bulgaria and Romania in 2007, and the establishment of various visa liberalization regimes for Western Balkan states added further complexity to the law enforcement challenge. Greater freedom of movement, which was designed to facilitate legitimate commerce, tourism, and labor migration, also facilitated the movement of criminal networks, their operatives, and their contraband. The same open borders that made Europe a more integrated economic space also made it a more integrated criminal space.

Section 20.1

Origins and Conditions: How Balkan Organized Crime Emerged

No criminal organization emerges from nothing. Every significant criminal enterprise is shaped by the specific historical, geographic, economic, and political conditions of its environment. The Balkan criminal organizations that became a defining feature of European crime in the 1990s and 2000s were products of a particular historical moment, a convergence of forces that created opportunities for criminal power on a scale the region had not previously seen.

The first of these forces was the legacy of Communist governance. Across the Balkans, Communist rule had created state monopolies on economic activity that generated, almost inevitably, parallel informal economies. When the state controlled the supply of consumer goods and that supply was chronically inadequate, black markets filled the gap. When the state controlled currency exchange at artificial rates, informal currency exchange flourished. When the state imposed restrictions on movement across international borders, smuggling networks developed to facilitate unofficial migration. By the time Communist regimes began to collapse in 1989 and 1991, these informal economies were mature, sophisticated, and staffed by people who had developed considerable expertise in operating outside legal structures. The transition to market economies did not eliminate these networks. It transformed them, allowing informal operators who had previously worked in the shadows of Communist states to compete openly, violently, and lucratively in the emerging market environment.

The second force was the particular character of post-Communist transitions in the Balkans, which differed significantly from transitions in Central European states like Poland, the Czech Republic, or Hungary. While Central European countries generally maintained functional state institutions and experienced relatively orderly transitions to democracy and market economies, the Balkan states faced more severe challenges. Yugoslavia's violent disintegration destroyed the institutional infrastructure of multiple republics simultaneously. Albania's extreme isolation left it without any of the institutional foundations that other Communist states, however imperfectly, possessed. Romania's transition was marked by the continued influence of former Communist officials and Securitate officers who used their knowledge of state structures and their networks of relationships to capture newly privatized assets and resist accountability. Bulgaria's transition featured similar patterns of former intelligence officers and Communist-era managers repositioning themselves as business elites and, in many cases, as organized crime figures. In each case, the weakness of emerging state institutions — underpaid judges, poorly equipped police forces, politically compromised prosecutors — made criminal actors difficult to prosecute and easy to corrupt.

The third force was geography. The Balkans sit at the intersection of the main overland routes connecting the Middle East and Central Asia to Western Europe. The heroin supply chain from Afghan poppy fields to European consumers runs through Iran, Turkey, Bulgaria and the Balkans, and then through Western Europe. This geographic reality meant that criminal networks capable of controlling transit routes through the Balkans could extract enormous rents from the global drug trade without themselves producing any narcotics. The value of geographic position, in criminal as in legitimate commerce, is enormous. Balkan criminal networks that managed transit corridors were, in effect, taxing one of the most valuable commodity flows in the world.

The fourth and most distinctive force was the experience of war. The Yugoslav wars of 1991 to 2001 were not simply military conflicts between states. They were complex, multi-sided struggles involving regular armies, ethnic militias, paramilitary units, criminal gangs, and warlord formations whose relationships to formal military command were often tenuous. In this environment, the line between soldier and criminal was frequently erased. Armed units that received official authorization to fight also engaged in looting, extortion, smuggling, and ethnic cleansing. War economies developed in which control of territory meant control of smuggling routes, and in which the funding of military operations depended on criminal revenue. Figures who began as political militants or nationalist volunteers emerged from the wars as experienced criminal entrepreneurs with established networks, weapons stockpiles, and a demonstrated willingness to use violence.

The Yugoslav Wars and the Criminal Entrepreneur

The career of Željko Ražnatović, universally known by his nom de guerre Arkan, illustrates the fusion of criminal and paramilitary careers in the Yugoslav context with particular clarity. Born in 1952, Ražnatović had already amassed a substantial criminal record across Western Europe — including armed robberies in Sweden, Belgium, the Netherlands, and West Germany — before returning to Yugoslavia in the 1980s. His international criminal connections brought him to the attention of the Yugoslav security service, which reportedly used him as an asset abroad. When Yugoslavia began to disintegrate, Ražnatović organized the Serb Volunteer Guard, known as the Tigers, which became one of the most feared paramilitary units of the Croatian and Bosnian wars. The Tigers were implicated in numerous atrocities, including the massacre of Croatian civilians at Vukovar and ethnic cleansing operations in Bosnia. They were also extensively documented as having looted the territories they passed through, with trucks of stolen goods following the military advance. Ražnatović himself parlayed his wartime reputation into a business empire that included a football club, a bank, and extensive real estate holdings. He was assassinated in a Belgrade hotel lobby in January 2000, reportedly in a criminal dispute rather than a political one, having become too powerful and too independent for the political establishment that had once patronized him.

Arkan was an extreme case, but not an isolated one. Across the Yugoslav successor states, the wars produced a generation of men who had learned that violence was a viable economic strategy, that state authority was negotiable, and that the connections built in wartime — to weapons suppliers, to political patrons, to diaspora funding networks — could be converted into postwar criminal enterprises. The wars also produced the physical infrastructure of later criminal operations: safe houses, transit routes, weapons caches, corrupt officials, and border crossing points that would continue to serve criminal purposes long after formal hostilities ended.

Historical Vocabulary
Transition Economy

A transition economy is an economy in the process of moving from a centrally planned Communist system to a market-based system. The transition period, which occurred across Eastern Europe and the former Soviet Union between roughly 1989 and the mid-2000s, was characterized by the privatization of state-owned enterprises, the liberalization of prices and trade, and the development of new legal and institutional frameworks for market activity.

In practice, transitions varied enormously in their speed, their fairness, and their outcomes. In some countries, the process was reasonably orderly and benefited broad sections of the population. In others, including several Balkan states, the transition was chaotic, corrupt, and captured by insiders with political connections, producing extreme inequality, institutional weakness, and conditions highly favorable to organized crime.

The transition economy concept is important for understanding Balkan organized crime because the specific failures of post-Communist transition, rather than anything inherent to Balkan cultures or peoples, go a long way toward explaining why criminal organizations emerged with such speed and power in the 1990s. Countries that managed their transitions more successfully experienced much lower levels of organized crime in the same period.

Section 20.2

Smuggling Networks: The Balkan Route and Its Operations

Smuggling is the oldest and most persistent form of organized crime, and the Balkans have been smuggling corridors for centuries. Under the Ottoman Empire, merchants who wished to avoid imperial customs duties developed elaborate routes through mountain passes and across rivers, using networks of local intermediaries, corrupt officials, and ethnic community ties to move goods from one market to another. Under the Communist regimes of the twentieth century, smuggling adapted to new restrictions, with currency, consumer goods, and eventually narcotics moving through channels that paralleled and undermined official trade. The post-Communist period did not create Balkan smuggling. It transformed and massively expanded it.

The most significant single smuggling operation in modern Balkan history is narcotics trafficking along the Balkan route. This route, in its classical form, began in the poppy fields of Afghanistan, which by the 2000s supplied more than ninety percent of the world's illicit opium. Raw opium or partially refined morphine base moved from Afghanistan into Iran, where some of it was processed further and some was transshipped directly. From Iran, the primary movement was into Turkey, where processing into heroin often occurred and where Turkish criminal organizations, particularly those with ethnic Kurdish or Turkish networks straddling the Iranian and European markets, played a central coordinating role. From Turkey, the main flow entered the Balkans through Bulgaria or through the former Yugoslav states, particularly along routes through North Macedonia, Kosovo, Albania, and Serbia, before entering Western European consumer markets through Italy, Austria, Hungary, or Germany.

The Balkan states' role in this supply chain was primarily that of transit and local distribution. Balkan criminal networks did not, in most cases, control production in Afghanistan or the primary processing infrastructure in Iran. What they controlled was the corridor. They maintained relationships with Turkish suppliers, managed the movement of heroin through their respective territories, extracted fees from the traffic, and developed retail distribution networks in Western European cities. The revenues from this transit role were enormous. Estimates of the annual value of heroin traffic through the Balkans in the peak years of the late 1990s and early 2000s ran to billions of dollars, even if only a fraction of that figure accrued to Balkan intermediaries.

Cigarette Smuggling and the Sanctions Economy

Alongside narcotics, cigarette smuggling became one of the defining criminal enterprises of the Yugoslav successor states in the 1990s. International sanctions imposed on the Federal Republic of Yugoslavia (Serbia and Montenegro) in response to the wars in Croatia and Bosnia created both desperate economic conditions and significant criminal opportunity. With the formal economy strangled and import channels restricted, black markets in cigarettes, fuel, and consumer goods flourished. The sanctions regime, intended to pressure Milošević's government, in practice enriched criminal networks that could navigate around it. Cigarettes produced in Western Europe or diverted from duty-free channels in Montenegro moved through Serbia and into the regional market at enormous profit margins. Some estimates suggest that the Montenegrin leadership under Milo Đukanović was directly involved in facilitating cigarette smuggling during this period, illustrating the degree to which criminal and political interests overlapped at the highest levels of post-Yugoslav governance.

The 1999 NATO bombing campaign created additional criminal opportunity by destroying bridge infrastructure over the Danube, disrupting legitimate river commerce while doing little to stop the criminal networks that quickly adapted to use smaller watercraft and alternative crossing points. Aid organizations and military peacekeepers noted with frustration that humanitarian supplies and criminal contraband often moved through the same channels, carried by the same intermediaries, and taxed at the same improvised checkpoints.

Fuel Smuggling and Structural Corruption

Fuel smuggling developed alongside cigarette smuggling as a major income source for criminal networks across the region. Large price differentials between fuel in different Balkan states, combined with the breakdown of border enforcement during the wars, created profitable arbitrage opportunities. Criminal networks imported cheap fuel from Serbia, where it was heavily subsidized, and resold it at market prices in neighboring states. In Kosovo, North Macedonia, and Bosnia, fuel smuggling was conducted on a scale large enough to represent a significant proportion of total fuel consumption, undermining legitimate distributors and depriving governments of substantial tax revenue. The networks built for fuel smuggling could be, and were, repurposed for the movement of other contraband goods.

The Smuggling Economy and Ordinary People

It is important to note that not everyone who participated in Balkan smuggling was a professional criminal. The economic collapse of the 1990s, in which formal employment evaporated, savings were destroyed by hyperinflation or fraudulent schemes, and legitimate business was impossible, pushed large numbers of ordinary people into informal and illegal economic activity simply to survive. Petty smuggling of cigarettes, consumer goods, and currency was normalized across much of the region. The same informal economy that sustained criminal entrepreneurs also kept households fed. This normalization of illegal economic activity had lasting effects on attitudes toward law and state authority in the affected societies, making the prosecution of more serious criminal activity more difficult and reducing the stigma associated with criminal careers.

Section 20.3

Arms Trafficking: War, Weapons, and Criminal Markets

The relationship between the Yugoslav wars and arms trafficking was circular and self-reinforcing. The wars created demand for weapons. Criminal networks supplied that demand. The revenues from weapons sales funded the continuation of the wars. And the institutional relationships, transit routes, and individual networks developed to move weapons during the wars became the infrastructure of broader criminal enterprise in the postwar period. Arms trafficking was not merely one criminal activity among many in the Yugoslav successor states. It was, in many cases, the founding enterprise of the criminal networks that later diversified into drugs, human trafficking, and financial crime.

The weapons that fueled the Yugoslav wars came from several sources. The massive stockpiles of the Yugoslav People's Army (JNA), distributed across the territory of the federation, were seized by the various successor states and their allied militias as the federation collapsed. Eastern European arms dealers, operating through networks of brokers and shell companies often registered in jurisdictions with minimal oversight, moved weapons from former Warsaw Pact stockpiles — particularly from Ukraine, Bulgaria, and Romania — into the conflict zone. Western governments and their allies provided weapons to various parties through both official and unofficial channels, with official embargo violations documented on multiple sides. And the specific Albanian events of 1997, when the looting of army arsenals placed an estimated 550,000 small arms and light weapons into circulation, created a reservoir of weaponry that continued to supply criminal and conflict markets for years.

The Albanian Arsenal and Its Consequences

The 1997 Albanian weapons looting deserves extended attention because its consequences were so far-reaching. When the pyramid scheme collapse triggered the uprising that briefly toppled the Albanian state, the response of military units was, in many cases, to open their armories to the public or simply to abandon them. Grenades, rifles, machine guns, rocket-propelled grenade launchers, and ammunition in enormous quantities flowed into Albanian society and, almost immediately, into the regional arms market. Criminal networks that already moved cigarettes, fuel, and people across the Adriatic into Italy or across the land borders into Greece and North Macedonia quickly added weapons to their product lines. The weapons spread across the region, appearing in subsequent years in the arsenals of the Kosovo Liberation Army, in criminal operations in Italy and elsewhere in Western Europe, and in conflicts as distant as West Africa.

The Albanian weapons looting also had a more specific political consequence that intersected with organized crime. The Kosovo Liberation Army, which began organized armed resistance to Serbian rule in Kosovo in 1996, acquired a significant portion of its early weapons from the Albanian arsenals. The KLA's fundraising network in the Albanian diaspora in Western Europe and North America — communities formed by decades of Albanian emigration — created financial channels that ran from expatriate communities to arms procurement networks in Albania and Kosovo. These same financial channels also moved heroin revenues, leading to documented entanglements between KLA financing and drug trafficking. This does not mean the KLA was a drug organization, nor that all or most of its members had criminal connections. It means that in the specific conditions of the late 1990s, the line between political financing and criminal revenue was difficult to maintain and was, in some cases, not maintained.

Postwar Arms Flows and Criminal Diversification

The end of formal hostilities in Bosnia in 1995 and in Kosovo in 1999 did not end arms trafficking in the region. Weapons that had been introduced during the wars remained in circulation. Criminal networks that had developed the expertise and relationships to move weapons during the conflicts continued to operate those networks in the postwar period, adapting to the changed environment. SFOR and KFOR peacekeepers documented extensive arms markets operating in postwar Bosnia and Kosovo, with weapons available at surprisingly low prices given their recent scarcity. Law enforcement efforts to collect and destroy weapons yielded significant quantities but were estimated to reach only a fraction of the total in circulation.

The postwar Balkan arms market also developed a regional export function. Weapons originating from Yugoslav-era stockpiles or from the Albanian arsenals moved westward into criminal markets in Western Europe, contributing to the arming of criminal networks in Italy, Scandinavia, and the United Kingdom. They moved southward into conflict zones in the Middle East and North Africa. They moved into Sub-Saharan Africa, where they appeared in multiple conflicts over the following decade. The Balkans became, in effect, one of the world's major secondary markets for small arms and light weapons, with criminal networks serving as both distributors and exporters.

The arms trafficking networks of the Balkans also illustrated a structural feature of transnational organized crime that appears repeatedly in this textbook: the tendency for criminal organizations to diversify across product lines using the same basic infrastructure. A network capable of moving weapons can also move drugs. A network capable of moving drugs can also move people. The relationships, the transit routes, the corrupt border officials, the safe houses, and the financial channels are, to a significant degree, interchangeable across different contraband commodities. This fungibility of criminal infrastructure is one reason why Balkan organized crime, once established, proved so resilient and so difficult to dismantle through enforcement actions focused on individual criminal activities.

Historical Vocabulary
Diaspora Network

A diaspora network is a web of social, economic, and cultural connections maintained among members of an ethnic or national community dispersed across multiple countries. Diasporas form through emigration driven by economic need, political persecution, war, or other pressures, and they maintain connections to both their host societies and their communities of origin.

Diaspora networks serve many legitimate purposes: they send remittances to families at home, support cultural institutions, provide job placement and housing assistance for newly arriving immigrants, and lobby for political causes affecting their homelands. But they also provide the organizational infrastructure that criminal networks can exploit. The same community ties that allow a legitimate Albanian immigrant organization to raise funds for a cultural center can be mobilized, in a criminal context, to recruit couriers, launder money, or apply social pressure on reluctant participants.

Albanian diaspora communities in Italy, Switzerland, Germany, the United Kingdom, and Scandinavia were particularly important to the expansion of Albanian organized crime in the 1990s and 2000s. These communities, formed by decades of emigration, provided local knowledge, established residency, social networks, and, in some cases, complicit or coerced participants in criminal operations. Understanding diaspora networks is essential to understanding how Balkan criminal organizations were able to expand so rapidly across Western Europe without requiring the kind of territorial base that older crime groups, like the Sicilian Mafia, depended on.

Section 20.4

Human Trafficking: Exploitation, Routes, and Victims

Of all the criminal enterprises associated with Balkan organized crime, human trafficking is arguably the most disturbing in its human consequences and the most revealing in its social and economic dimensions. The trafficking of human beings for sexual exploitation and forced labor became one of the dominant activities of Balkan criminal networks in the 1990s, and the Balkans became the source, transit, and destination region for a substantial portion of the human beings trafficked into Western Europe during this period. Understanding this phenomenon requires understanding both the supply-side conditions that made large numbers of people vulnerable to traffickers and the demand-side conditions that created markets for trafficked labor and sex.

The primary source countries for trafficking victims moving through and from the Balkans in the 1990s and early 2000s were Moldova, Romania, Ukraine, and Bulgaria, with significant numbers also coming from Albania itself. The victims were overwhelmingly women and girls, the majority of them young, most of them from impoverished rural or peri-urban communities with limited economic opportunities. The collapse of Communist economies had destroyed the social safety nets, employment opportunities, and institutional protections that, however inadequate, had provided some degree of security to women and families in the Communist period. The transition years brought mass unemployment, the withdrawal of state services, rising inequality, and the spread of a consumer culture whose images of Western prosperity reached Balkan audiences through newly available television, advertising, and emigrant remittances, while the means to achieve that prosperity through legitimate work remained extremely limited.

Recruitment and Deception

The recruitment methods documented by researchers, law enforcement, and NGOs across the region followed consistent patterns. Traffickers, who might be strangers, acquaintances, neighbors, or even relatives of the victim, approached young women with offers of legitimate employment abroad as waitresses, cleaners, hotel staff, or domestic workers. The offers were plausible: legal emigration for work was happening on a large scale, and the prospect of earning Western wages was genuinely attractive in communities where local earnings were negligible. Some victims were deceived entirely, believing they were taking a legitimate job offer. Others understood they were entering a legally ambiguous situation but did not anticipate the conditions of total control and sexual exploitation that awaited them. In some cases, families were involved in accepting payments from recruiters in exchange for their daughters, in the belief that the daughters would have opportunities abroad — a devastating outcome of both poverty and a social context in which some forms of female labor were undervalued.

Once recruited, victims were moved across borders, often through multiple transit countries, with their documents taken from them as a control mechanism. In the destination country — typically Italy, Greece, Germany, Austria, the Netherlands, the United Kingdom, or one of the Scandinavian countries — they were sold to brothel operators, placed in debt bondage for the supposed cost of their transport and accommodation, and subjected to violence or the threat of violence to enforce compliance. The debt bondage mechanism was particularly effective as a control tool because it provided an appearance of contractual obligation while ensuring that victims could never realistically accumulate enough to repay what was owed, since the debt was manipulated by the trafficker rather than calculated on any legitimate basis.

Albanian and Serbian Criminal Networks in Human Trafficking

Albanian criminal networks became the dominant actors in human trafficking through the Western Balkans in the late 1990s. Their geographic position, controlling Albania's Adriatic coast, the transit routes through Kosovo and North Macedonia, and established diaspora communities in Italy, provided structural advantages. Italian law enforcement documented extensive Albanian trafficking operations in which women, primarily from Moldova, Romania, and Ukraine, were brought by speedboat across the Adriatic from Albania to the Italian coast, transferred to inland holding locations, and distributed to brothel operators across Italy. The speedboat runs across the Adriatic, covering roughly 70 kilometers of open sea, were hazardous — a number of boats sank in high seas, drowning their occupants — but they were enormously profitable, with individual women sold for prices that law enforcement estimated at between $1,500 and $5,000, subsequently extracted many times over from the exploitation of their labor.

Serbian criminal networks operating out of Belgrade and Vojvodina also participated in human trafficking, primarily moving victims from Romania, Moldova, and Bulgaria through Serbia and into Western European destinations via the established heroin transit routes. The shared infrastructure between narcotics and human trafficking operations was documented by law enforcement in multiple countries: the same safe houses, the same corrupt border officials, the same transport networks served both commodity flows.

The Human Cost and Institutional Response

Quantifying the human cost of Balkan human trafficking is difficult because the activity was clandestine and many victims did not report to authorities, either from fear of deportation, fear of traffickers, distrust of police, or a combination of these factors. International Organization for Migration estimates from the early 2000s suggested that tens of thousands of women were trafficked through the Balkans annually at the peak of operations. Individual survivor accounts collected by NGOs documented experiences of rape, beatings, starvation, isolation, and psychological abuse that left lasting trauma.

The institutional response to trafficking in the 1990s was slow, inadequate, and in some cases actively harmful to victims. Law enforcement in destination countries often treated trafficking victims as illegal immigrants or prostitutes subject to deportation rather than as crime victims entitled to protection. Some police forces in the Balkans were themselves corrupted by trafficking networks. International peacekeeping missions in Bosnia and Kosovo were implicated in creating demand for trafficked sex workers, with documented cases of SFOR and KFOR personnel patronizing establishments staffed by trafficking victims. The presence of a large, relatively affluent international community in postwar Kosovo and Bosnia in fact increased demand for trafficking in those environments, a disturbing illustration of the relationship between peacekeeping and the criminal economies that accompany it.

From the early 2000s, international attention to human trafficking increased substantially, driven by a combination of NGO advocacy, media coverage, and the passage of the US Trafficking Victims Protection Act in 2000, which created a framework for evaluating countries' anti-trafficking records. Balkan states came under sustained pressure to strengthen anti-trafficking laws, improve victim protection, and prosecute traffickers more vigorously. Progress was uneven, and the trafficking of persons through and from the region has continued in modified forms. The primary source countries have shifted somewhat as conditions have changed, but the underlying structural vulnerabilities — poverty, limited female economic opportunity, corruption, and demand in destination countries — have not been eliminated.

Multiple Perspectives

Human Trafficking in the Balkans: Contrasting Experiences and Interpretations

From the perspective of trafficking survivors: Women who were trafficked and subsequently assisted by NGOs consistently described experiences of deception, violence, isolation, and exploitation that constituted severe human rights violations. Many described their initial vulnerability as rooted in economic desperation and lack of legitimate opportunity, and their victimization as made possible by the failure of states to protect them. Many also described the additional trauma of being treated as criminals rather than victims by law enforcement when they sought help.
From the perspective of law enforcement: Police and prosecutors in destination countries described human trafficking as a complex investigative challenge. Victims were often unwilling or unable to testify against traffickers due to fear or trauma. Criminal networks were sophisticated in their use of document fraud, debt bondage, and psychological control. Cross-border coordination between police forces was hampered by jurisdictional gaps, language barriers, and varying legal standards for what constituted trafficking. Some police described the difficulty of distinguishing trafficking from consensual migration for sex work, a distinction that legal frameworks often failed to address clearly.
From the perspective of Balkan government officials: Officials in source and transit countries often downplayed the scale of trafficking or attributed it primarily to foreign demand rather than domestic criminal enterprise. Some officials, particularly in the early period, denied that their countries were significant source countries or transit zones, viewing such claims as damaging to national reputation. Others acknowledged the problem but cited resource constraints, institutional weakness, and the difficulty of international coordination as barriers to effective response.
From the perspective of human rights and anti-trafficking organizations: NGOs and international human rights bodies emphasized that human trafficking was not merely a law enforcement problem but a symptom of structural conditions including poverty, gender inequality, corruption, and demand for cheap or unpaid sexual and domestic labor in destination countries. These organizations argued that enforcement approaches focused narrowly on prosecuting traffickers, without addressing the vulnerability of potential victims or the demand that sustained the market, would never be sufficient to eliminate trafficking.
Section 20.5

Post-Cold War Expansion: Balkan Networks Across Europe

The expansion of Balkan criminal networks across Western Europe in the 1990s and 2000s was one of the most significant developments in the history of European organized crime. Groups that had previously operated primarily within the Balkans, or in the limited diaspora communities that earlier waves of emigration had established, rapidly developed footprints in Italy, Germany, the United Kingdom, Switzerland, Austria, Scandinavia, Spain, and beyond. This expansion was neither sudden nor random. It followed the lines of existing social networks, exploited specific structural opportunities created by the opening of European borders, and was driven by the particular economic and political conditions of the post-Communist transition.

Albanian criminal networks were the most dramatic story of transnational expansion. In Italy, which Albanian migrants had been reaching in increasing numbers since the early 1990s, Albanian criminal groups initially operated as subordinate partners to Italian criminal organizations, providing services such as street-level drug distribution and the smuggling of fellow Albanians across the Adriatic. Over the course of the decade, the relationship shifted. Albanian networks established direct relationships with Turkish heroin suppliers, bypassing Italian intermediaries. They developed independent wholesale distribution capabilities. In some cities and regions, they became dominant suppliers of heroin to Italian retail markets. Italian law enforcement expressed consistent alarm at the rapidity of this shift, noting that Albanian criminal figures were willing to use extreme violence in market disputes and were difficult to penetrate through informants because of the intensity of clan-based loyalty.

The United Kingdom and Scandinavia

In the United Kingdom, Albanian criminal networks established significant presences in London, Birmingham, and other major cities from the late 1990s, again initially in secondary roles within existing criminal supply chains and subsequently as independent actors. British law enforcement noted the particular challenge posed by the speed of Albanian criminal expansion and the difficulty of developing intelligence within communities whose members had strong cultural incentives to avoid contact with state authorities. By the mid-2000s, Albanian networks were estimated to control a significant proportion of cannabis cultivation in the United Kingdom, having pioneered the development of indoor cannabis growing operations — a relatively low-risk, high-margin activity that required less exposure than street-level drug distribution.

In Scandinavia, Albanian and other Balkan criminal networks established themselves in the substantial asylum-seeker communities that had formed during the Yugoslav wars. Sweden, Norway, and Denmark, which had accepted significant numbers of refugees from Bosnia, Kosovo, and Albania, found that small numbers of individuals within those communities were involved in organized criminal activity, complicating the political management of both refugee policy and organized crime response. This created a politically sensitive dynamic in which criminal enforcement actions against Balkan criminal figures risked being perceived as targeting refugee communities as a whole.

Romanian and Bulgarian Networks in the EU

The accession of Romania and Bulgaria to the European Union in January 2007 marked a significant expansion of Balkan criminal capacity in Western Europe. Citizens of these two countries acquired the right to live and work in EU member states under the same conditions as other EU citizens, a right that was exercised by millions of legitimate workers and also by criminal actors who used the freedom of movement to embed themselves more deeply in Western European criminal markets. Romanian criminal networks, which had been building presences in Italy, Spain, France, and the United Kingdom since the 1990s through less regularized channels, now operated with the full right of residence of EU citizens, making deportation more difficult and community ties easier to maintain.

Romanian organized crime developed particular specializations in property crime, including organized shoplifting rings, ATM skimming operations, pickpocketing networks in tourist areas, and sophisticated financial fraud. These activities, while less dramatic than narcotics trafficking or arms dealing, generated substantial revenues with relatively low violence and attracted less law enforcement attention than more spectacular criminal activities. Romanian networks also became significant actors in labor exploitation, organizing the movement of fellow Romanian citizens into Western Europe under conditions of forced labor in agriculture, construction, and domestic service.

Bulgarian criminal organizations, shaped by the particular relationship between Bulgaria's state security apparatus (the DS) and organized crime during the Communist period, specialized in narcotics transit, contract violence, and financial crime. Former members of the DS and its successor bodies played significant roles in the development of Bulgarian criminal networks in the post-Communist period, using their knowledge of intelligence tradecraft, their international connections, and their relationships with political elites to create criminal enterprises with unusual sophistication. Bulgaria's position on the EU's external border with Turkey and the former Yugoslav states made it a critical node in the Balkan drug trafficking route.

Criminal Cooperation and Competition

The expansion of Balkan criminal networks across Western Europe did not proceed without conflict. Disputes over territory, market share, and criminal relationships produced violence in multiple countries. In Italy, conflicts between Albanian and Italian criminal groups produced assassinations that drew extensive law enforcement attention. In the United Kingdom and Scandinavia, disputes within Albanian communities between factions with different clan allegiances led to killings that demonstrated the transfer of Balkan clan conflicts to Western European settings.

At the same time, Balkan criminal networks demonstrated a consistent capacity for cooperation with non-Balkan criminal organizations when cooperation served mutual interests. Documented examples include cooperative narcotics arrangements between Albanian networks and British criminal groups, between Romanian networks and Moroccan networks in Spain, and between Serbian networks and Russian organized crime figures in financial crime. The flexibility of these collaborative arrangements, which formed and dissolved as criminal opportunities shifted, reflects a broader pattern in transnational organized crime: the preference for flexible, project-based criminal cooperation over rigid hierarchical structures, a theme explored in greater detail in later chapters of this textbook.

Section 20.6

Structure and Methods: How Balkan Criminal Organizations Operate

One of the most analytically interesting features of Balkan criminal organizations is their organizational structure, which differs significantly from the hierarchical models associated with the Sicilian Mafia, the American La Cosa Nostra, or even the Japanese Yakuza. Where those organizations tend toward relatively formalized hierarchies with clear chains of command, defined membership, and established procedures for internal governance, Balkan criminal networks tend to be flatter, more cellular, more flexible, and more dependent on personal and family relationships than on formal organizational structures.

This organizational character has several important implications. It makes Balkan criminal networks more resilient against law enforcement decapitation strategies: the arrest or killing of a leader does not necessarily disable the broader network because operations are distributed rather than centralized. It also makes them more difficult to penetrate through informants or undercover operations: the tight clan and family bonds that hold networks together create high barriers to outsiders and impose severe costs on members who cooperate with authorities. And it makes them adaptable to new criminal opportunities: without rigid organizational commitments or territorial monopolies to defend, Balkan criminal actors can shift activities and locations in response to changing enforcement environments or market conditions.

Albanian Clan Networks

Albanian criminal organization is most often described in terms of clan or fis (family grouping) loyalty. The Kanun, the traditional customary code of the Albanian highlands that regulated feuds, obligations, and honor, provides a cultural framework within which Albanian criminal networks operate, though scholars debate how directly it determines contemporary criminal behavior as opposed to providing a cultural idiom that criminal actors invoke selectively. What is clear is that Albanian criminal networks place extraordinary emphasis on blood ties and clan membership as the basis for trust, that they maintain strong internal discipline through social and familial pressure as well as the threat of violence, and that they have shown consistent willingness to use extreme violence against both criminal rivals and perceived betrayers.

Albanian criminal networks operating in Western Europe typically operate in cells, with individual cells maintaining limited knowledge of the broader network's structure. A cell operating a heroin distribution network in London, for example, might maintain direct contact only with its immediate supplier and its retail distributors, without detailed knowledge of where the supplier obtained the heroin or who financed the operation at a higher level. This cellular structure, familiar from analysis of terrorist organizations, limits the damage that any individual arrest can do to the broader network while maintaining operational security.

Serbian Criminal Networks and Their State Connections

Serbian criminal organization in the post-Yugoslav period was shaped by its particular relationship with the Serbian state. Under Slobodan Milošević, Serbian organized crime was not merely tolerated but in many cases actively patronized. Criminal figures who were useful to the regime — for currency smuggling that circumvented sanctions, for paramilitary operations that the state could not formally sanction, for the intimidation of political opponents — operated with a degree of state protection that made them, in effect, licensed criminals. The flip side of this arrangement was that criminal independence was limited: figures who became too powerful, too independent, or too embarrassing to the regime risked being eliminated.

After Milošević's fall in October 2000, this arrangement broke down, but the criminal networks themselves did not disappear. The Zemun Clan, which had operated as a quasi-official criminal organization under the Milošević regime, moved toward greater independence in the post-Milošević period, engaging in a series of assassinations that culminated in the murder of Prime Minister Zoran Djindjić in March 2003. Djindjić, who had been attempting to pursue reform of the security services and prosecution of organized crime figures, was shot by a sniper as he arrived at the government building in Belgrade, becoming the first Serbian head of government to be assassinated. The killing triggered a major state response, with thousands of suspected criminals arrested in the following months, and eventually resulted in the prosecution and conviction of numerous Zemun Clan members. But it also illustrated, with terrible clarity, the willingness of criminal networks to use political assassination when they perceived their interests to be sufficiently threatened.

Violence as a Business Tool

Violence, or more precisely the credible threat of violence, is a central tool of criminal enterprise, as Chapter 2 of this textbook explained in theoretical terms. Balkan criminal organizations have been notably willing to employ extreme violence in ways that distinguished them from some older criminal traditions. The willingness to assassinate public officials, to use torture as a debt collection method, and to engage in public killings that would draw law enforcement attention reflected a risk calculus shaped by the experience of the Yugoslav wars, in which extreme violence had proven effective and had gone largely unpunished. Criminal actors who had participated in wartime atrocities and suffered no legal consequences had reason to believe that violence against criminal rivals or even state officials would similarly go unpunished, at least in the specific institutional environment of post-Communist Balkan states.

This willingness to use violence also shaped the competitive dynamics of Balkan criminal markets. In markets where violence is employed freely and where state enforcement is weak, criminal actors face less constraint in imposing monopoly control over territory or activities. Conversely, extreme violence invites retaliation, attracts law enforcement attention, and can destabilize the informal cooperation arrangements on which criminal markets depend. Balkan criminal networks have navigated this tension with varying degrees of success, with some organizations achieving periods of dominance through violence and others losing ground to more disciplined competitors.

Primary Source Window

International Criminal Tribunal for the Former Yugoslavia (ICTY): Indictments and Testimonies, 1993–2017

Creator: International Criminal Tribunal for the Former Yugoslavia · Period Covered: 1991–2001 · Type: International legal documentation, witness testimony, prosecutorial records

Context: The International Criminal Tribunal for the Former Yugoslavia, established by the UN Security Council in 1993 and completing its work in 2017, generated an extraordinary archive of documentation about the wars of Yugoslav succession, including extensive testimony about the relationship between paramilitary forces, political authority, and criminal organization. The ICTY's records include testimony from participants and witnesses to events in Croatia, Bosnia-Herzegovina, and Kosovo that illuminate the intersection of war crime and organized crime in ways that few other sources can match.

Witness testimonies before the ICTY consistently described a pattern in which paramilitary units, nominally under military command, operated with substantial autonomy to loot, extort, and traffic in goods and persons. Witnesses described commanders who divided stolen property among unit members, who organized the movement of looted goods to markets outside the conflict zone, and who maintained relationships with criminal networks in Serbia, Montenegro, and abroad that persisted after the military operations concluded. Several testimonies described the recruitment of known criminals into paramilitary units specifically because their criminal networks and their demonstrated willingness to use violence made them valuable assets in operations that combined military objectives with criminal ones.

Analysis: The ICTY records represent an exceptional body of primary source evidence about the relationship between the Yugoslav wars and organized crime. However, they must be used with significant caution. The records were produced in an adversarial legal proceeding in which both prosecution and defense had strong incentives to shape testimony toward their legal objectives. Individual witnesses had varying degrees of reliability, varying motivations for their testimony, and varying levels of direct knowledge of the events they described. The ICTY records are therefore most valuable when multiple independent testimonies corroborate the same events, and when documentary evidence supports oral testimony.

Section 20.7

The State, Corruption, and Criminal Power

No examination of Balkan organized crime is complete without sustained attention to the relationship between criminal networks and state institutions. The theoretical framework established in Chapter 2 of this textbook emphasized that organized crime requires, at minimum, the tolerance of state authority and typically requires its active cooperation. In the Balkans of the 1990s and 2000s, this relationship went far beyond tolerance or passive corruption. In several cases, the line between state and criminal organization was so blurred as to become analytically meaningless.

The concept of state capture, introduced in discussions of post-Communist transition, refers to a situation in which criminal or oligarchic interests so thoroughly penetrate governmental institutions that the state's formal functions are redirected toward the private interests of those who have captured it rather than toward the public interest. State capture is different from ordinary corruption, in which individual officials take bribes in exchange for specific favors. In state capture, the entire institutional architecture, including the legislature, the judiciary, the prosecutor's office, the police, the customs authority, and the political parties, is colonized by criminal or oligarchic interests that use state power as a tool for private enrichment and impunity.

Several Balkan states experienced conditions approaching state capture at various points in the post-Communist period. In Serbia under Milošević, the security services maintained operational relationships with organized crime that served the regime's political and financial interests while providing criminal actors with protection from prosecution. In Montenegro, allegations of high-level political involvement in cigarette smuggling persisted for years. In Albania, the weakness of state institutions after 1991 meant that criminal networks operated with minimal institutional opposition, not because the state had been captured but because it had barely been constructed. In Bulgaria, the documented involvement of former Darzhavna Sigurnost (DS) officers in criminal networks reflected a particularly intimate form of state-crime entanglement in which the same individuals who had constituted the coercive apparatus of the Communist state became the leaders of criminal organizations in the post-Communist period.

Historical Vocabulary
State Capture

State capture describes a situation in which private interests — criminal organizations, economic oligarchs, or corrupt political factions — so thoroughly penetrate a state's institutions that the state's formal purposes are subordinated to those private interests. Rather than individuals bribing officials for specific favors (ordinary corruption), state capture involves the systematic colonization of state institutions: legislatures pass laws designed to protect criminal profits; courts dismiss cases against politically connected criminals; police forces enforce the interests of criminal patrons rather than the law.

State capture is particularly associated with post-Communist transitions in Eastern Europe and the former Soviet Union, where the simultaneous collapse of state authority and the rapid privatization of state assets created conditions in which insiders with political connections could acquire enormous wealth while undermining the institutional capacity of the state. The Balkans provided several significant examples of near-state capture during the 1990s and 2000s, with important consequences for the region's organized crime problem.

The distinction between state capture and a criminal state is analytically useful: in state capture, the formal apparatus of statehood is maintained and used by criminal interests, while in a fully criminal state, the formal distinction between state and criminal organization would largely disappear. The Balkan cases fall on a spectrum between these poles, with different countries at different times displaying varying degrees of criminal penetration of state institutions.

Corruption at the Border

The most practically important form of state-crime collaboration in the Balkans operated at the level of border control. The movement of narcotics, weapons, and human beings across the borders of the Yugoslav successor states required, at minimum, the cooperation of border guards who could be bribed to look the other way or actively facilitate passage. Law enforcement investigations in multiple countries documented extensive corruption of customs and border personnel, with payments ranging from individual cash bribes to systematic arrangements in which entire border posts were effectively leased to criminal networks for specific periods. In some cases, border guards themselves participated actively in criminal operations, using their official positions to facilitate smuggling while their presence on official duty provided a veneer of state legitimacy to the operation.

Judicial corruption was equally significant in limiting the accountability of criminal actors. In states where judges were underpaid, where political interference in judicial appointments was routine, and where the threat of violence against prosecutors or witnesses was credible and occasionally carried out, successful prosecution of significant organized crime figures was extremely difficult. High-profile cases were subject to procedural delays, evidence was lost or challenged, witnesses recanted or disappeared, and convictions were reversed on appeal. The cumulative effect was to create an environment in which criminal actors could operate with reasonable confidence that even if arrested, prosecution would be ineffective and punishment avoidable.

Reform Efforts and Their Limits

The post-2000 period saw significant efforts to reform state institutions across the Balkans, driven by both domestic pressures and the conditionality attached to EU accession processes. The prospect of EU membership — offering access to European markets, freedom of movement for citizens, structural funds for economic development, and the political legitimacy of the European club — created strong incentives for Balkan governments to demonstrate progress on anti-corruption and organized crime measures. EU accession conditionality required progress on judicial reform, police reform, witness protection, anti-money-laundering measures, and the prosecution of organized crime figures.

These reforms produced genuine improvements in some areas. Serbia's post-Djindjić crackdown resulted in significant prosecutions of Zemun Clan members and other organized crime figures. Croatia's accession process was accompanied by serious reform of judicial institutions. Albania strengthened its legal framework for anti-trafficking measures. Bulgaria and Romania, despite continuing concerns about corruption at the time of their 2007 accession, have made institutional progress in subsequent years, albeit unevenly.

But reforms also faced determined resistance from the interests they threatened, were often incomplete or unevenly implemented, and were complicated by the political difficulty of prosecuting figures with deep connections to ruling political parties. The assessment of progress in this area remains contested: European Commission monitoring reports have consistently noted both improvements and persistent gaps, while criminologists debate whether institutional reform is outpacing criminal adaptation or vice versa.

Historical Debate

Was Balkan Organized Crime a Product of Culture or of Circumstance?

The question: Analysts and policymakers have offered competing explanations for why organized crime became so powerful in the Balkans in the 1990s. One school of explanation emphasizes cultural factors, including the traditional Albanian Kanun, a supposed Balkan tolerance for extralegal authority, or the legacy of Ottoman governance. Another school emphasizes structural and circumstantial factors: Communist collapse, war, poverty, institutional weakness, and geographic position. The distinction matters because it shapes both our understanding of what happened and our expectations for whether and how it can change.

Interpretation A — Cultural Explanation: Some analysts have argued that Balkan organized crime reflects deep cultural traditions that predate the Communist period and survived it. In this view, the Albanian Kanun's emphasis on clan loyalty, blood obligation, and distrust of external authority creates a social environment in which criminal organization is culturally natural. More broadly, a supposed Balkan tradition of informal governance, of operating around rather than through formal state structures, is argued to predispose the region to organized crime. Advocates of this view point to the persistence of criminal networks even as formal institutional conditions improve, and to the transplantation of Balkan criminal culture to Western European settings.
Interpretation B — Structural and Circumstantial Explanation: Most criminologists and historians reject or significantly qualify the cultural explanation, arguing that it is both empirically weak and analytically misleading. They point out that organized crime has emerged with comparable or greater intensity in many non-Balkan environments when structural conditions of institutional collapse, poverty, and criminal opportunity were present — post-Soviet Russia, post-conflict West Africa, Mexico during the cartel expansion. They argue that the Kanun is invoked selectively and does not, in fact, straightforwardly determine criminal organization. And they emphasize that Balkan criminal power has declined in societies where institutional conditions have improved, suggesting that structural factors rather than fixed cultural essentials are the primary drivers.

Evidence: The structural argument is better supported by comparative evidence. Countries and regions of the Balkans that experienced less severe institutional breakdown — Slovenia, for example, and to a lesser extent Croatia in the later post-war period — developed significantly lower levels of organized crime than areas that experienced more severe breakdown. This variation within the region is difficult to explain through cultural arguments, which would predict relatively uniform outcomes across culturally similar societies.

Why it matters: If cultural factors are primary, then organized crime in the Balkans is a long-term, deeply rooted problem that institutional reform will have limited ability to address. If structural factors are primary, then improving institutions, reducing poverty, strengthening the rule of law, and integrating the region into broader European structures should gradually reduce criminal power. The policy implications of these two interpretations differ fundamentally.

Section 20.8

Law Enforcement Response: Challenges and Partial Successes

Combating Balkan organized crime has proven one of the most challenging law enforcement problems in modern European history. The difficulties are structural, jurisdictional, institutional, and political, and they have resisted straightforward solutions despite decades of effort and substantial international investment.

The most fundamental structural challenge is jurisdictional. Balkan criminal networks are explicitly designed to exploit the seams between national legal systems. A drug trafficking operation that sources heroin in Afghanistan, processes it in Turkey, transits it through Bulgaria and Serbia, distributes it in Italy, and launders the proceeds through shell companies in Cyprus and offshore accounts in the Caribbean is conducting criminal activity across at least eight or nine jurisdictions, each with its own legal standards, evidentiary rules, investigative authorities, and institutional capacity. A successful prosecution in one country may leave the broader network intact, with operations simply shifting to avoid the affected jurisdiction.

International law enforcement cooperation has been the primary institutional response to this challenge. Europol, established in 1995, provides a coordination and intelligence-sharing function for EU member states' police forces. Interpol facilitates cooperation with non-EU states. Joint Investigation Teams (JITs), which allow investigators from multiple countries to work together on specific cases under a common legal framework, have been used in major Balkan criminal cases with some success. The European Arrest Warrant, which simplified extradition between EU member states, has been a significant practical tool. US Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA) engagement with Balkan cases, including operations targeting Albanian heroin networks, has supplemented European law enforcement capacity.

Witness Protection and the Problem of Testimony

Witness testimony against organized crime figures requires that witnesses believe they can be protected from retaliation, a condition that has been difficult to guarantee in the Balkan context. High-profile cases in Serbia, Albania, and North Macedonia were repeatedly undermined by witnesses who recanted, disappeared, or were killed before they could testify. The murder of several key witnesses in organized crime prosecutions during the 2000s illustrated the limits of witness protection programs that remained inadequate relative to the resources and determination of the criminal networks they were supposed to protect witnesses against. In some cases, witnesses were relocated outside the region entirely, an expensive and disruptive solution that was not available for most potential witnesses.

Law enforcement has also faced the challenge that many of the most useful potential informants and witnesses are themselves deeply involved in criminal activity, creating legal and ethical complications for their use in prosecutions. The use of accomplice witnesses, who receive reduced sentences or immunity in exchange for testimony, is a standard tool in organized crime prosecution but is ethically and legally problematic and has been challenged in courts across the region. Different legal systems have handled this challenge with varying degrees of success, with Italy's extensive experience with pentiti (organized crime informants) providing a model that some Balkan states have partially adopted.

Asset Forfeiture and Financial Investigation

One of the most important tools developed for combating organized crime, including Balkan criminal networks, is asset forfeiture, the legal mechanism by which the proceeds of criminal activity can be confiscated by the state. Asset forfeiture attacks the fundamental economic logic of criminal enterprise: if the profits of crime can reliably be seized, the expected return on criminal investment declines. Several Balkan states, under pressure from the EU accession process and from international law enforcement partners, have developed asset forfeiture legislation and institutions in the 2000s and 2010s, with Serbia and Albania both establishing dedicated asset forfeiture agencies.

Financial investigation, tracing the flow of criminal proceeds through banking systems, shell companies, and real estate transactions, has proven both an important investigative tool and a persistent challenge. Money laundering through legitimate business was documented extensively across the Balkans, with criminal proceeds flowing into real estate, hospitality businesses, fuel distribution, and financial services. The same jurisdictional complexity that hampers drug trafficking prosecutions complicates financial investigations: criminal money is routinely moved through multiple jurisdictions, often with the assistance of professional intermediaries including lawyers, accountants, and financial advisers operating in jurisdictions with minimal oversight.

The Ongoing Challenge

After three decades of concerted international law enforcement effort, Balkan organized crime remains a significant feature of the European criminal landscape. Networks have adapted, diversified, and in some respects become more sophisticated in response to law enforcement pressure. Cannabis cultivation operations shifted from outdoor to indoor to evade detection. Financial fraud migrated to the internet. Human smuggling routes shifted in response to border enforcement patterns. The fundamental assessment of criminologists working in this area is that Balkan criminal networks have demonstrated sufficient adaptability, institutional connectivity, and geographic mobility to absorb sustained enforcement pressure without fundamental disablement.

At the same time, the law enforcement record is not entirely negative. Several major criminal figures have been successfully prosecuted. Networks have been disrupted, even if rarely destroyed. Victim identification and protection for trafficking survivors has improved substantially. Asset forfeiture has deprived criminal networks of some of their proceeds. And the gradual institutional improvement of Balkan states, however uneven, has reduced the most egregious forms of state-crime collusion. The honest assessment is that organized crime in the Balkans, like organized crime generally, is a problem of management rather than solution, a set of risks to be controlled and minimized rather than eliminated.

Cause and Effect

The Expansion of Balkan Organized Crime Across Western Europe

Long-Term Causes

Geographic position on the primary drug trafficking corridor between Central Asia and Western Europe; legacy of Communist black markets and security service-criminal connections; poverty and limited legitimate economic opportunity; cultural emphasis on extended family and clan loyalty as basis for trust; historic tradition of operating around formal state structures.

Short-Term Causes

Collapse of Communist regimes (1989–1992); disintegration of Yugoslavia and ten years of wars (1991–2001); Albanian pyramid scheme collapse and weapons looting (1997); Kosovo War and KLA diaspora fundraising (1998–1999); Schengen expansion and EU accession of Bulgaria and Romania (2007).

The Development

Rapid construction of transnational criminal networks operating in narcotics, arms, human trafficking, property crime, and financial fraud across Western Europe; displacement of some pre-existing criminal organizations in specific markets; establishment of Balkan criminal footprints from Italy and Scandinavia to the UK and Spain.

Effects

Tens of thousands of human trafficking victims exploited; significant heroin supply chains disrupted; dozens of contract killings across Western Europe; political destabilization of Balkan governments including assassination of a sitting prime minister; massive illicit capital flows undermining legitimate economies; sustained law enforcement response across multiple continents.

Historical complexity: Balkan criminal expansion cannot be reduced to any single cause. It was the product of a specific convergence of geographic position, institutional collapse, armed conflict, and economic desperation, in an environment where Western European demand for drugs and cheap labor created powerful market incentives. Understanding this complexity is essential to evaluating the effectiveness of different policy responses.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Balkan Organized Crime from the 1990s to the Present

What Changed

  • Major paramilitary-criminal hybrid figures of the Yugoslav war era are dead, imprisoned, or significantly diminished
  • Legal institutional frameworks across the Balkans have been substantially strengthened under EU conditionality
  • Asset forfeiture laws and dedicated financial investigation units have been established
  • International law enforcement coordination has improved significantly through Europol and joint investigation teams
  • Human trafficking victim identification and protection has improved
  • Balkan criminal networks have diversified into cybercrime, online fraud, and cryptocurrency, reducing dependence on physical smuggling infrastructure
  • Primary source countries for trafficking victims have shifted as conditions have changed in Moldova, Romania, and Bulgaria

What Stayed the Same

  • The Balkan route remains one of the primary heroin trafficking corridors into Western Europe
  • Clan-based loyalty and distrust of state authority continue to characterize Albanian criminal organization
  • Corruption of border officials, judges, and police continues, albeit at reduced levels in EU member states
  • The fundamental geographic advantage of the Balkans as a transit corridor has not changed
  • Underlying economic vulnerabilities — poverty, limited female opportunity, rural depopulation — that generate trafficking victims persist in parts of the region
  • Criminal networks continue to demonstrate the capacity to adapt to law enforcement pressure and relocate operations

Why the pattern matters: The persistence of Balkan organized crime despite significant institutional reform and law enforcement effort illustrates that criminal organizations can absorb considerable pressure when their structural foundations — geography, social networks, corruption, and market demand — remain in place. It also illustrates that some forms of criminal infrastructure are more durable than others: physical smuggling routes can be disrupted more easily than the social relationships and trust networks on which criminal enterprises are built.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has examined the rise, expansion, and contemporary significance of Balkan criminal organizations, tracing their development from the black markets of the Communist period through the catastrophic disruptions of the 1990s to their current status as major actors in European and global criminal markets. The story is not one of inevitable or culturally determined criminality but of specific historical conditions — Communist collapse, armed conflict, institutional weakness, poverty, and geographic opportunity — that converged to create exceptional conditions for the growth of criminal power.

The Balkan route for narcotics trafficking represents one of the most economically significant criminal corridors in the world, linking Afghan poppy production to Western European heroin markets through networks that are resilient, adaptive, and deeply embedded in the social fabric of the transit states. Balkan criminal organizations did not merely participate in this traffic; they actively managed and expanded it, establishing direct relationships with Turkish and Afghan suppliers and developing retail distribution capabilities across Western European consumer markets.

Arms trafficking was both a product of the Yugoslav wars and a cause of their continuation and aftermath. The weapons that flooded the Balkans through the disintegration of the Yugoslav People's Army, through Eastern European arms dealers, and most dramatically through the 1997 looting of Albanian arsenals, created a reservoir of military hardware that sustained criminal violence, fueled subsequent conflicts, and eventually spread to criminal markets in Western Europe and beyond. The networks built to move weapons during the wars were repurposed for the movement of drugs, people, and other contraband in the postwar period, illustrating the fungibility of criminal infrastructure across different product lines.

Human trafficking became one of the most devastating expressions of Balkan organized crime, exploiting the vulnerability of women and girls from Moldova, Romania, Ukraine, and Bulgaria who were deceived or coerced into situations of forced sexual exploitation in Western European and domestic markets. The Albanian criminal networks that dominated the Adriatic trafficking corridor developed a system of deception, debt bondage, document confiscation, and violence that made escape extremely difficult. The response of state institutions, from law enforcement in destination countries that treated trafficking victims as criminals rather than victims, to peacekeeping missions that created demand, was often inadequate or actively harmful.

The post-Cold War expansion of Balkan criminal networks across Western Europe was one of the defining developments of European organized crime in the late twentieth century. Albanian networks established themselves in Italy, the United Kingdom, Germany, and Scandinavia within a remarkably short period, displacing or subordinating pre-existing criminal organizations in specific markets and demonstrating a capacity for rapid geographic expansion that reflected both the organizational flexibility of clan-based networks and the structural opportunities created by open European borders and diaspora communities. Romanian and Bulgarian networks expanded similarly following EU accession in 2007, with their own specializations in property crime, labor exploitation, and financial fraud.

The relationship between Balkan organized crime and state institutions was not merely one of corruption but in several cases approached the condition of state capture: the systematic penetration of governmental institutions by criminal interests to the point where those institutions served criminal rather than public purposes. The careers of figures like Arkan, who operated as simultaneously a state asset, a paramilitary commander, and a criminal entrepreneur, illustrate the depth of this entanglement. The assassination of Serbian Prime Minister Zoran Djindjić in 2003 illustrated its most extreme consequences.

Law enforcement response has been sustained and partially effective but has not fundamentally dismantled Balkan criminal networks. The structural advantages of those networks, including geographic position, social solidarity, institutional corruption, and market demand, have proven more durable than individual criminal enterprises. The most important policy lesson of the Balkan experience may be that organized crime cannot be addressed purely through enforcement: reducing the structural vulnerabilities that generate criminal power, including poverty, institutional weakness, and demand for illegal goods in destination markets, is essential to any long-term solution.

The Balkan case connects to the broader analytical themes of this textbook in important ways. It illustrates the relationship between state weakness and criminal power, the adaptability of criminal organizations to changing enforcement environments, the importance of geographic position in criminal markets, the human costs imposed by criminal enterprise on the most vulnerable members of affected societies, and the limitations of law enforcement as the sole or primary response to organized crime. These themes will recur in subsequent chapters examining drug cartels, cybercrime, and the challenges of twenty-first-century criminal governance.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Arms trafficking
The illegal trade in weapons, ammunition, and related materials across international borders, conducted in violation of national and international law. In the Balkans, arms trafficking was both a cause and a consequence of the Yugoslav wars.
Balkan route
The primary narcotics trafficking corridor connecting Afghan heroin production to Western European consumer markets, running through Iran, Turkey, the Balkans, and into Western Europe. One of the most important drug supply chains in the world.
Clan network
A criminal or social organization based primarily on extended family and kinship ties rather than formal membership or hierarchical authority. Albanian criminal networks are frequently described as clan-based, with loyalty to the fis (family grouping) providing the primary basis for trust and obligation.
Debt bondage
A form of coercion in which a person is required to work to repay a debt, with the terms of repayment controlled by the creditor in ways that make it impossible to actually discharge the debt. Widely used by human traffickers to control victims.
Diaspora network
The social, economic, and cultural connections maintained among members of an ethnic or national community dispersed across multiple countries. Diaspora networks provided Albanian criminal organizations with established contacts, local knowledge, and cover in Western European cities.
European Arrest Warrant
A legal mechanism adopted by EU member states that simplifies and accelerates the extradition of criminal suspects between member states, significantly improving law enforcement cooperation across borders.
Europol
The European Union's law enforcement intelligence agency, established in 1995, providing coordination, information sharing, and analytical support to national police forces of EU member states in combating organized crime and terrorism.
Human trafficking
The recruitment, transportation, transfer, harboring, or receipt of persons through force, fraud, or coercion for the purpose of exploitation, including sexual exploitation, forced labor, or other forms of servitude. Balkan criminal networks became dominant actors in European human trafficking in the 1990s.
Joint Investigation Team (JIT)
A law enforcement mechanism allowing investigators from different countries to work together on a specific criminal case under a common legal framework, pooling evidence, intelligence, and investigative resources.
Kanun
The traditional customary legal code of the Albanian highlands, governing feuds, hospitality, and clan honor. Often invoked as a cultural framework shaping Albanian criminal organization, though its direct influence on contemporary criminal behavior is debated by scholars.
Kleptocracy
A form of government or governance in which those in power exploit their positions for personal financial gain, typically through corruption, theft of public assets, or manipulation of state resources for private benefit.
Kosovo Liberation Army (KLA / UCK)
The Albanian nationalist armed organization that fought Serbian forces in Kosovo from 1996 to 1999, whose fundraising in the Albanian diaspora created documented entanglements between political financing and criminal revenue, particularly from heroin trafficking.
Paramilitary
An armed organization organized along military lines but not part of a country's formal military establishment. During the Yugoslav wars, paramilitary units operated alongside regular forces while maintaining independence from formal military command, enabling both military operations and criminal activities.
Post-Communist transition
The process by which former Communist states reorganized their political and economic systems following the collapse of Communist governance, typically involving democratization, privatization, and market liberalization. The specific character of this transition in the Balkans shaped the emergence of organized crime.
Protection economy
An economic system in which a dominant actor extracts payments from others in exchange for protection from violence — including, in many cases, protection from violence the extractor could itself apply. A central concept in understanding how criminal organizations generate revenue from territory.
Sanctions economy
The illicit economic system that develops in response to international economic sanctions, as actors find ways to circumvent restrictions on trade and financial transactions. Sanctions imposed on Yugoslavia in the 1990s generated an extensive criminal economy of smuggling and sanctions evasion.
Schengen Area
The zone of European countries that have abolished passport and other types of border control at their mutual borders, allowing free movement of people within the zone. Schengen expansion significantly affected the operational geography of Balkan criminal networks.
State capture
A situation in which private interests, including criminal organizations or economic oligarchs, so thoroughly penetrate state institutions that those institutions are redirected toward serving private rather than public purposes.
Transition economy
An economy in the process of moving from central planning to market organization. The specific failures of post-Communist transition in the Balkans — including weak institutions, insider privatization, and inadequate legal frameworks — created conditions highly favorable to organized crime.
Ungoverned space
Territory in which state authority is absent or ineffective, allowing non-state actors including criminal organizations to exercise control. The Yugoslav wars created extensive ungoverned spaces that criminal networks exploited.
War economy
The economic system that develops within a zone of armed conflict, characterized by the diversion of resources to military purposes, the breakdown of normal commercial activity, and the emergence of criminal enterprises that profit from the conditions of violence and institutional breakdown.
Witness protection
Programs designed to protect individuals who agree to testify against criminal organizations from retaliation, typically by relocating them under new identities. The adequacy of witness protection has been a persistent challenge in Balkan organized crime prosecutions.
Zemun Clan
A major Serbian criminal organization based in the Belgrade suburb of Zemun, responsible for numerous assassinations in the early 2000s including the murder of Prime Minister Zoran Djindjić, exemplifying the penetration of Serbian state institutions by organized crime.

Chapter Reflection

The history of Balkan criminal organizations in the post-Cold War period is, at its deepest level, a history of what happens when the institutional structures that regulate human social life collapse simultaneously and in multiple ways. The Communist states of southeastern Europe did not merely collapse politically. They collapsed institutionally: the courts, the police, the prosecutors, the regulatory agencies, the banking systems, and the civic organizations that together constitute the architecture of a functioning society all weakened or failed at the same moment. Into that vacuum rushed actors with resources, networks, and the willingness to use violence — and they built criminal enterprises that proved, in many cases, more durable than the institutions meant to contain them.

This chapter connects to the theoretical foundations laid in the opening chapters of this textbook. Chapter 2 argued that organized crime emerges where state authority is weak, where demand for illegal goods is strong, and where the expected returns from criminal enterprise exceed those available from legitimate activity. The Balkan case is an almost laboratory-pure illustration of these conditions: extreme institutional weakness, strong demand from Western European consumer markets, poverty that made criminal careers relatively attractive, and geographic position that provided structural advantage. Understanding this should produce not fatalism but clarity: if the conditions that generate criminal power are identifiable, they are also, in principle, addressable.

The human costs of Balkan organized crime are the most important element of its history and the most important test of any analysis that claims to take the subject seriously. The women trafficked across the Adriatic in speedboats and sold to brothel operators in Italian cities were not statistics. They were persons with families, aspirations, and vulnerabilities that criminal networks exploited for profit. The communities from which they came were not simply labor pools for criminal entrepreneurs. They were places where poverty, gender inequality, and institutional failure combined to make exploitation possible. The policy failures that left these women to be treated as criminals rather than victims by destination country law enforcement were not inevitable. They were choices, and they had consequences that a serious engagement with this history requires acknowledging.

Looking forward, the Balkan case offers several enduring lessons for the study of organized crime generally. Criminal organizations are more resilient than enforcement pressure can fully account for when their structural foundations remain intact. The adaptability of criminal networks, including their capacity to shift products, routes, and organizational forms in response to enforcement, is a permanent feature of the phenomenon rather than an occasional aberration. International law enforcement cooperation, however improved, faces fundamental jurisdictional limitations that criminal networks do not. And the most effective long-term responses to organized crime are those that address the structural conditions, including poverty, institutional weakness, corruption, and demand, rather than those that focus exclusively on arresting individual criminal actors.

The Balkans of the 1990s and 2000s will not be repeated precisely. But the conditions that produced Balkan organized crime — state collapse, armed conflict, economic desperation, and geographic opportunity — are present in multiple regions of the world today, and the criminal networks that emerge from them will raise the same analytical challenges and impose the same human costs on the same vulnerable populations that the Balkan case so vividly documented. The history examined in this chapter is not merely historical. It is a living warning about what organized crime looks like when the conditions for its growth are allowed to persist, and about what it takes — in institutional strength, in political will, and in international cooperation — to contain it.

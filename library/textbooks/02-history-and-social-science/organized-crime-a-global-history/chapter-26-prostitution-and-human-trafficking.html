Prostitution and Human Trafficking

Organized Crime: A Global History

Chapter Opening

"Human trafficking is not a crime of the shadows. It operates in plain sight, sustained by economic desperation, structural inequality, and the organized criminal networks that have learned to profit from both."

In the autumn of 1902, investigators working for the International Bureau for the Suppression of the White Slave Traffic gathered in Paris to compare notes on a problem that had been alarming reformers, governments, and newspapers across Europe and North America for more than a decade. Women, they reported, were being recruited from villages in Poland, Ukraine, Hungary, and the Austro-Hungarian Empire under false pretenses of employment as domestic servants, factory workers, or waitresses. Once transported across borders, often by organized networks that coordinated passage, forged documents, and reception in destination cities, the women found themselves in brothels from Buenos Aires to Alexandria to New York, indebted for their passage and unable to leave. The men who ran these networks operated across multiple countries, communicated through coded telegrams, and bribed police and immigration officials with the same calm efficiency that a legitimate shipping firm might use to move cargo. What reformers called the "white slave trade" was, in every functional sense, an organized criminal enterprise.

The moral panic surrounding white slavery in the early twentieth century was shaped by racial anxieties, class biases, and assumptions about the vulnerability of "respectable" women that historians have since carefully dissected and complicated. Not all women in commercial sex were coerced, and not all were white. The campaigns that arose in response to the problem were often as much about controlling female sexuality and regulating immigration as they were about protecting victims. Yet beneath the distortions of the moral panic lay a genuine and serious phenomenon, one that this chapter examines with the tools of historical and criminological analysis: the organized criminal exploitation of human beings through the sex trade and, more broadly, through forced labor.

Human trafficking, as scholars and international law define the term today, encompasses not only forced prostitution but the recruitment, transportation, and coercion of people into any form of labor through force, fraud, or deception. Sex trafficking is the most studied and most sensationalized subset of this larger phenomenon, but trafficking for domestic service, agriculture, construction, fishing, and manufacturing has affected millions of people across history and across the globe. Understanding trafficking as a criminal enterprise requires examining its structure, its markets, its methods of coercion, its victims, and the ways it has adapted to changing economic conditions, migration patterns, and law enforcement environments over more than a century of organized operation.

The history of organized prostitution and trafficking is inseparable from the history of gender inequality. In virtually every society across recorded history, commercial sex has been disproportionately performed by women and girls and purchased by men. The economic conditions that push women into commercial sex, whether through desperate poverty, lack of alternative employment, displacement by war or environmental disaster, or the direct coercion of traffickers, are not simply individual misfortunes. They are products of structural systems, including the subordination of women in labor markets, legal systems that denied women property rights and independent contracts, and social norms that excluded women from respectable economic life while simultaneously punishing them for engaging in unrespectable economic activity.

Organized crime's relationship to commercial sex has varied significantly across time and place. In some contexts, criminal networks have been the primary organizers and operators of brothels and trafficking networks. In others, they have functioned more as protectors, taxers, and enforcers for enterprises run by individual madams, pimps, or small operators. In still others, state-licensed or state-tolerated prostitution created regulated markets in which criminal networks played a secondary role, though coercion of workers remained common even within nominally legal frameworks. The chapter traces these varying configurations, examining how criminal organizations have profited from commercial sex and human trafficking across different historical periods and geographic contexts.

By the late twentieth and early twenty-first century, human trafficking had emerged as one of the most globally significant organized crime enterprises, generating tens of billions of dollars annually and affecting an estimated millions of victims worldwide. The collapse of the Soviet Union, the expansion of global migration, the growth of internet-based recruitment and advertisement, and the increasing integration of trafficking networks with other criminal enterprises, including narcotics, arms, and money laundering, transformed trafficking from a regional phenomenon into a genuinely global one. At the same time, an unprecedented international legal and policy response, anchored by the United Nations Palermo Protocol of 2000, created new frameworks for prosecution, victim protection, and international cooperation that, however imperfect in implementation, represented a significant shift in how states and international institutions understood and addressed the problem.

This chapter does not treat trafficking as primarily a sensational or exceptional crime. It treats it as an organized criminal industry, one that follows recognizable economic logic, operates through identifiable organizational structures, adapts to law enforcement pressure and market conditions, and persists because it generates substantial profit from the exploitation of human vulnerability. Understanding it as such, rather than as simply a moral outrage, is essential to understanding both its history and the challenges involved in combating it.

Historical Context

Commercial sex, the exchange of sexual services for money or other material consideration, has existed in virtually every human society for which historians have written records. The ancient civilizations of Mesopotamia, Greece, Rome, and China all documented the existence of prostitution in urban settings, and in many of these contexts, some degree of institutional organization, whether through temple structures, licensed brothels, or regulated streetwalking districts, accompanied individual commercial transactions. What distinguishes the organized crime dimensions of this chapter's subject matter from this long general history is not the existence of commercial sex itself, but the emergence of structured criminal enterprises that controlled, coerced, and profited systematically from the sexual exploitation of others, operating across regional and eventually international networks with the organizational sophistication of a business enterprise.

The conditions that made large-scale organized trafficking possible emerged most powerfully in the context of nineteenth-century industrialization, urbanization, and mass migration. As millions of people moved from rural areas to rapidly growing cities, and as European migration to the Americas and other regions reached extraordinary volumes, the combination of uprooted populations, economic desperation, legal vulnerability, and weak urban policing created an environment in which organized exploitation could thrive. Women who arrived in cities without social networks, without knowledge of local laws and institutions, without financial resources, and, in many cases, without legal immigration status were profoundly vulnerable to recruiters who offered promises of employment and security.

The regulatory environment of commercial sex in the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries varied enormously by country and city. Some jurisdictions maintained state-licensed brothel systems, known as regulated prostitution or the regulationist system, modeled on the French model developed under Napoleon in the early nineteenth century. In these systems, registered prostitutes were required to submit to regular medical inspections, were prohibited from practicing outside designated areas, and were subject to police surveillance. The regulationist system did not eliminate criminal involvement in prostitution, but it did create a relatively visible and institutionalized commercial sex market in which the state took a formal administrative role.

Other jurisdictions moved toward abolitionism, the policy of suppressing prostitution through criminal penalties, either on sellers, buyers, or both. Britain's Contagious Diseases Acts of the 1860s, which required women suspected of prostitution to submit to involuntary medical examinations, provoked a fierce feminist and civil liberties backlash led by Josephine Butler and others, and were eventually repealed in 1886. The repeal campaign, which united early feminists, social purity reformers, and civil libertarians in unexpected coalitions, helped establish a framework for thinking about prostitution as a problem of exploitation rather than solely a problem of public health or moral disorder, a framework that would shape reform discourse for decades.

By the early twentieth century, the international dimensions of organized trafficking had become sufficiently visible to prompt the first sustained international policy responses. The International Agreement for the Suppression of the White Slave Traffic in 1904, the International Convention for the Suppression of the White Slave Traffic in 1910, and subsequent League of Nations conventions in the 1920s and 1930s represented early, if imperfect, attempts to address trafficking as a problem requiring cross-border state cooperation. These early efforts were limited by the racial and moral assumptions of their era, by the weakness of international enforcement mechanisms, and by the sharp disagreements among participating states over the relationship between regulation, abolition, and trafficking. They nevertheless established precedents in international law and institutional design that shaped later responses.

The second half of the twentieth century brought dramatic changes to both the structure of trafficking and the international response to it. The decolonization of Asia and Africa, the Cold War realignment of global economies, the development of mass tourism, the migration flows generated by conflict and economic inequality, and eventually the collapse of the Soviet bloc all reshaped trafficking patterns, creating new source regions, new destination markets, and new organizational configurations. The rise of global criminal networks capable of operating across multiple continents, coordinating with local operators, and exploiting the weaknesses of underfunded and sometimes corrupt law enforcement agencies in transitional and developing states transformed trafficking from a regional concern into a global organized crime phenomenon of the first order.

The definitional framework for understanding trafficking also evolved significantly. The 2000 United Nations Protocol to Prevent, Suppress and Punish Trafficking in Persons, Especially Women and Children, commonly known as the Palermo Protocol, provided the first comprehensive internationally agreed definition of human trafficking, encompassing recruitment, transportation, transfer, harboring, or receipt of persons by means of threat, force, coercion, abduction, fraud, or deception, for the purpose of exploitation. This definition was significant because it was victim-centered, because it encompassed labor as well as sex trafficking, and because it explicitly addressed the consent question by specifying that a victim's consent is irrelevant when any of the specified means of coercion have been used. Understanding this evolving definitional and legal landscape is essential context for analyzing the historical and contemporary dimensions of organized trafficking.

Historical Origins: Commercial Sex and Early Organization

Commercial sex, in its many historical forms, long predates the organized criminal networks that came to control much of it in the modern era. Ancient Mesopotamian sources document women attached to temple complexes in roles that included ritual sex, though the precise nature and degree of commercial exchange in these settings remains debated among scholars of the ancient Near East. Greek and Roman sources describe a stratified commercial sex market in which enslaved women occupied the lowest tier, working in taverns and cheaply priced houses near the docks, while more expensive and relatively autonomous courtesans, known as hetairai in Greece and meretrix in Rome, could achieve significant economic independence and, in some cases, cultural influence. The Roman state collected taxes on prostitution, treating it as a legitimate economic activity, while also imposing regulatory distinctions that separated prostitutes legally and socially from respectable matrons.

What is significant in this ancient history is not simply the existence of commercial sex but two structural features that recur across centuries: first, the heavy concentration of women in commercial sex who were there not by free choice but because of slavery, poverty, or social exclusion that made other economic options unavailable or impossible; and second, the emergence of third-party organizers who profited from the commercial sex labor of others. The ancient world knew the figure who profited from managing and renting the labor of women in commercial sex well enough to name and regulate it: the leno in Rome, whose legal status was formally degraded alongside the women whose labor generated his income, was recognizable as the functional ancestor of the modern pimp or trafficker, a third party extracting surplus from commercial sex work without performing it directly.

Medieval European cities developed commercial sex markets that were simultaneously regulated by municipal authorities, tolerated by the Church as a "necessary evil" that directed male desire away from more dangerous forms of sin, and organized by local economic networks. Municipal brothels, or lupanaria, operated in many European cities under license from civic authorities from roughly the thirteenth through the sixteenth centuries. The women who worked in these establishments were typically from the poorest social strata, often migrants from rural areas to cities, widows, abandoned wives, or women expelled from religious institutions. They occupied a legally ambiguous position, being simultaneously protected from some forms of violence by their status within a licensed institution and subject to comprehensive social stigma, restriction of movement and dress, and exploitation by the managers who ran the houses.

The Reformation and Counter-Reformation of the sixteenth century brought the closure of many municipal brothels across Europe, as religious reformers attacked the tolerance of prostitution as civic corruption. This did not eliminate commercial sex but drove it into less visible and less regulated forms, dispersed among taverns, bathhouses, and street solicitation, and increasingly controlled by informal criminal networks rather than civic licensing. The pattern of religious or moral campaigns driving commercial sex from licensed visibility into criminal informality, while doing little to eliminate the underlying market demand, would repeat itself with remarkable consistency across subsequent centuries and in very different political and cultural contexts.

Early modern European colonialism created new dimensions of sexual exploitation that connected commercial sex to the structures of racial slavery and imperial power. Enslaved women in the colonial Americas were sexually exploited by enslaving men as a matter of legal right as well as power. Plantation systems incorporated the sexual availability of enslaved women as an assumed dimension of enslavers' authority, and the children of such unions, who were enslaved by the condition of their mothers, represented a significant component of natural population increase in slave societies. While this form of exploitation differed from organized commercial prostitution in that it did not primarily involve monetary exchange, it represented a systematic commodification of women's sexual labor by organized economic and legal institutions, specifically slavery and colonial law, that informed the structures of later organized trafficking.

Port cities from the sixteenth century onward developed commercial sex markets oriented in part toward the large floating population of sailors, soldiers, merchants, and other transient male workers that imperial trade generated. In cities such as Amsterdam, London, Lisbon, Seville, and later Singapore, Calcutta, Shanghai, and Hong Kong, commercial sex districts emerged as recognized features of port geography, providing services to men who passed through and generating revenue for the organizers of those districts. These port districts were among the earliest settings in which something resembling modern organized criminal management of commercial sex appeared, with local power brokers controlling access to women, fixing prices, managing relationships with port authorities, and recruiting women from rural hinterlands or other regions through agents and intermediaries.

The nineteenth century brought dramatic intensification of all these processes. Industrial urbanization drew millions of workers to cities that were completely unprepared to house, employ, or provide social services to them. Women who came to cities in search of employment found a severely constricted labor market. Domestic service and piece work in the garment and textile industries were among the few options available, and these were notoriously exploitative. When work disappeared or became impossible to find, or when economic disasters, crop failures, or personal misfortunes destroyed whatever small stability working women had achieved, commercial sex appeared to many as one of the only available survival strategies. The phrase "the oldest profession" obscures more than it reveals: commercial sex was often not a profession of choice but a last resort for women whose other options had been taken from them by structural economic conditions that severely constrained women's participation in respectable economic life.

The Regulationist System and Its Critics

The regulationist system, formalized in France after the Napoleonic legal reforms of the early nineteenth century and exported through French cultural influence to much of Europe and the colonial world, represented the dominant official approach to managing commercial sex through most of the nineteenth century. Under this system, women practicing prostitution were required to register with the police, submit to regular medical examinations intended to control venereal disease, and confine their activities to licensed brothels or designated public zones. Unregistered women practicing prostitution were subject to arrest and forced registration. The system gave police and municipal authorities extensive coercive power over women in commercial sex while doing almost nothing to address the social and economic conditions that drove women into it or to protect them from violence and exploitation by clients and managers.

The regulationist system was a bureaucratic mechanism for managing commercial sex as a public health and public order problem, not for eliminating exploitation. Feminist critics, led most famously by Josephine Butler in Britain and echoed by reformers across Europe, argued that regulation institutionalized exploitation, treated women as sanitized instruments for male pleasure, ignored the violence and coercion that characterized much of commercial sex even within licensed houses, and placed the legal machinery of the state on the side of men who exploited women rather than women who needed protection. Butler's campaign against Britain's Contagious Diseases Acts, launched in the late 1860s, articulated a critique of regulationism that remains fundamentally recognizable in contemporary feminist debates about prostitution policy: that treating prostitution as a necessary and regulatable commercial service, rather than as a form of violence against women or a symptom of gender inequality, was morally unacceptable and practically harmful to the women involved.

The late nineteenth century also brought the "white slavery" moral panic, a transatlantic wave of concern about the alleged organized recruitment of innocent women into involuntary prostitution that combined genuine evidence of organized trafficking with racial anxieties about immigration, moral panic about urban life, and assumptions about female sexual innocence that bore little relationship to the complex realities of commercial sex. The figure of the innocent girl seduced or abducted into a brothel by a sinister foreign agent, typically depicted as Jewish or Mediterranean in American accounts, was a staple of reform literature, fiction, and journalism of the era. This figure carried more ideological than empirical weight, but the underlying concern about organized cross-border networks was not fabricated. Investigators from the International Bureau for the Suppression of the White Slave Traffic documented real networks that recruited women from Eastern Europe to brothels in Argentina, Brazil, Turkey, Egypt, and elsewhere, using a combination of fraudulent employment offers, romantic deception, and outright coercion.

Historical Vocabulary
Human Trafficking

Human trafficking, as defined by the 2000 UN Palermo Protocol, is the recruitment, transportation, transfer, harboring, or receipt of persons by means of threat or use of force, coercion, abduction, fraud, deception, or abuse of power, for the purpose of exploitation. Exploitation includes, at minimum, sexual exploitation, forced labor, slavery or servitude, and the removal of organs.

This definition is significant in several ways. First, it is victim-centered: the Protocol specifies that a victim's consent to being trafficked is irrelevant when any of the specified means of coercion have been used. Second, it encompasses labor trafficking as well as sex trafficking, recognizing that forced labor in agriculture, domestic service, construction, and manufacturing affects more people globally than sex trafficking. Third, it does not require crossing an international border, recognizing that domestic trafficking, the exploitation of people within their own country, is equally serious.

Students should be careful to distinguish trafficking from smuggling. Smuggling involves the facilitated illegal movement of people across borders, typically with the consent of the person being moved and for a fee. Trafficking involves exploitation that may or may not involve international movement. A person can be trafficked without ever leaving their country of birth, and a person can be smuggled across a border without being trafficked. The two often overlap, but they are legally and analytically distinct.

Section 26.2

Organized Exploitation: The Business of Controlled Commercial Sex

The transformation of commercial sex from a dispersed informal market into a systematically organized criminal enterprise occurred unevenly across different societies and time periods, but the general trajectory shows criminal networks developing progressively more sophisticated mechanisms of control, recruitment, and profit extraction over the course of the late nineteenth and twentieth centuries. Understanding this transformation requires examining both the organizational structures that criminal networks developed to manage commercial sex enterprises and the methods of coercion and control they used to prevent the women working in those enterprises from leaving or seeking help.

In nineteenth and early twentieth-century American cities, commercial sex was organized through a relatively decentralized system of brothels operated by madams, women who ran houses and managed the women working within them, in commercial sex districts that were often formally or informally tolerated by municipal authorities and political machines. The Storyville district in New Orleans, established by municipal ordinance in 1897 and operating until federal pressure forced its closure in 1917, was one of the most formally institutionalized of these commercial sex districts. In cities such as New York, Chicago, San Francisco, and New Orleans, the tolerance of commercial sex districts was part of a broader system of machine politics in which criminal entrepreneurs, police officials, and political machines shared in the revenues of vice industries. Madams paid regular fees to police and political organizations for the right to operate; politicians relied on vice revenues to fund their machines; police enriched themselves through a system of organized graft.

The organized crime syndicates that emerged from the Prohibition era of the 1920s increasingly moved into commercial sex as one of multiple revenue streams alongside bootlegging, gambling, and loan sharking. The concentration of criminal power in the hands of organized syndicates with the capacity for violence gave them leverage over brothel operators and independent pimps that they used to establish tribute systems, in which independent operators paid a percentage of their revenues to the dominant criminal organization in exchange for protection from interference. In some cities, criminal organizations moved beyond tribute collection to direct ownership and operation of commercial sex venues, eliminating the independent madam as a middle layer and capturing more of the revenue stream directly.

The post-Prohibition era brought significant changes to the organization of commercial sex in the United States and elsewhere. The aggressive law enforcement campaigns against brothels that accompanied World War II, driven in part by military concerns about venereal disease among soldiers, largely eliminated the traditional brothel district model in American cities. Commercial sex moved toward more dispersed forms, including streetwalking and later indoor venues such as massage parlors, escort agencies, and apartment-based operations that were harder for police to target. This dispersal changed the organizational structure of criminal involvement, making direct ownership and operation of brothels less common and shifting criminal activity toward control of individual women through pimps and coercive personal relationships.

Methods of Control: Debt Bondage, Isolation, and Violence

The mechanisms through which trafficking networks controlled the women they exploited were not simply brute physical force, though violence and its threat were always present as ultimate guarantors of control. Traffickers developed a sophisticated toolkit of psychological, economic, and social mechanisms that created dependencies and barriers to escape that were in many cases more durable and effective than physical confinement. Understanding these mechanisms is essential to understanding why trafficking victims often do not seek help, do not cooperate with law enforcement, and may actively resist identification as victims, a pattern that has frustrated law enforcement and victim services agencies across many different legal environments.

Debt bondage was, and remains, the most widespread and structurally significant mechanism of control in trafficking operations. The system worked, and continues to work, through the creation of a debt that the victim is told she owes to the trafficker for services rendered, typically including transportation, housing, food, and recruitment fees. These debts were, in most cases, entirely fabricated or grossly inflated, and they were structured to be impossible to eliminate: interest accrued on the debt, fines were levied for infractions of house rules, and the amount earned by the victim through her labor was credited against the debt at rates set entirely by the trafficker. A woman who had been told she owed a fixed sum for her passage from Eastern Europe to a Western European city might find, upon arrival, that the sum had doubled and that a complex system of deductions for rent, food, clothing, and penalties meant that she could never earn her way free. The legal fiction of the debt gave the system a surface appearance of commercial legitimacy while functioning as pure coercion.

Document confiscation was another near-universal feature of trafficking control. Upon arrival at their destination, victims frequently found their passports, identity documents, and travel papers confiscated by their traffickers on the pretext of "safekeeping." Without documents, victims could not travel independently, could not prove their legal status to authorities, and faced the threat of immediate deportation if they contacted police, a threat particularly powerful in contexts where victims lacked legal immigration status. The rational calculation of a victim who knows that approaching police is likely to result in her own arrest and deportation, rather than in help and protection, is a rational response to the actual legal environment in many destination countries, and it has been one of the most significant structural barriers to victim identification and assistance.

Isolation was systematically maintained by keeping victims geographically disoriented, linguistically isolated, and socially separated from anyone outside the trafficking network who might help them. Women trafficked across linguistic and cultural boundaries found themselves in cities where they did not speak the language, did not know the geography, did not know what services existed for them, and had been told by their traffickers that police in the destination country were corrupt and violent. Traffickers cultivated dependence by controlling all information about the outside world and positioning themselves as the only reliable mediators between the victim and her environment.

Violence, or the credible threat of violence, underlaid the entire system as a guarantor of last resort. Victims who attempted to escape were beaten as a lesson to themselves and others. Threats were extended to family members in the victim's home country, whose addresses and identities traffickers often knew from the recruitment process. The reputational threat, the warning that the victim's family would be told what she had been doing or that photographs would be sent to her community, was a particularly powerful form of coercion in cultural contexts where family honor was closely bound to female sexual reputation. Traffickers calculated, usually accurately, that many victims would endure continued exploitation rather than face the social consequences of disclosure.

Historical Vocabulary
Debt Bondage

Debt bondage is a system of control in which a person's freedom of movement or labor is conditioned on repaying a real or fabricated debt, typically one structured so that repayment is practically impossible. It is recognized under international law as a form of slavery and is prohibited by the 1956 UN Supplementary Convention on the Abolition of Slavery.

In the trafficking context, debt bondage typically arises from transportation costs, recruitment fees, housing, and other "expenses" that traffickers claim victims owe them. The debt is usually fabricated, grossly inflated, or subject to conditions that prevent repayment. Income earned by the victim is credited against the debt at rates entirely controlled by the trafficker, while fines and charges ensure the debt remains perpetually unresolved. The psychological power of the debt is reinforced by cultural norms around obligation and by the victims' awareness that they have no independent recourse to any authority that might adjudicate the debt fairly.

Debt bondage is not unique to sex trafficking. It is also a primary control mechanism in labor trafficking in agriculture, domestic service, construction, and manufacturing. The International Labour Organization estimates that debt bondage affects tens of millions of people globally across multiple sectors of the forced labor economy.

Organizational Structures of Criminal Networks

The organizational structures through which criminal networks managed large-scale commercial sex operations varied considerably by region, period, and the scale of operations involved. At the smallest scale, individual pimps operated informally, controlling one or a small number of women through personal domination, romantic manipulation, and violence. At the other extreme, sophisticated transnational trafficking networks employed recruiters in source countries, transporters who managed the movement of victims across borders, reception handlers who received victims at destinations, and managers who ran the brothels or other venues where victims were exploited, all coordinated through phone and later internet communication and connected by networks of financial transaction.

The Japanese Yakuza provides one of the earliest and most well-documented examples of a major organized crime structure that systematically integrated commercial sex into its criminal portfolio. From the late nineteenth century onward, Yakuza organizations were involved in managing and taxing commercial sex venues in Japan, and from the mid-twentieth century, their involvement expanded to include the trafficking of women from Southeast Asia, the Philippines, Thailand, and later Eastern Europe and Latin America into Japan's substantial commercial sex industry. Yakuza-affiliated networks developed recruitment operations in source countries, managed the debt bondage system that controlled imported women, and maintained relationships with venue operators across Japan. The sophistication of these operations, which included coordination with local criminal organizations in source countries, management of legal front companies, and relationships with corrupt officials, illustrated the degree to which major organized crime structures had integrated trafficking as a systematic business operation by the latter decades of the twentieth century.

Similar patterns of major criminal organization involvement in trafficking appeared across the globe. In Western Europe, criminal networks from Albania and other parts of the Western Balkans developed particularly significant operations following the collapse of communism and the conflicts of the 1990s, trafficking women from Eastern Europe and the former Soviet Union into commercial sex venues across Italy, Germany, the Netherlands, and the United Kingdom. The Albanian criminal networks that dominated trafficking into Italy in the 1990s and early 2000s were characterized by clan-based organization, intensive use of violence and terror as control mechanisms, and rapid adaptive response to law enforcement pressure. When police operations disrupted their operations in one country, networks relocated to adjacent markets with minimal operational pause.

Russian and Eastern European organized crime groups, empowered by the economic chaos and institutional collapse that followed the Soviet Union's disintegration, became major players in global trafficking from the 1990s onward. The combination of desperate poverty in former Soviet states, the availability of educated and skilled women who suddenly found themselves economically marginalized, weak law enforcement, and the connections between criminal networks and the new political elites of post-Soviet states created ideal conditions for trafficking operations. Women from Ukraine, Moldova, Belarus, and Russia were trafficked into Western Europe, the Middle East, Israel, Southeast Asia, and the United States in networks that, at their most sophisticated, operated with the logistical efficiency of a legitimate import-export business.

Section 26.3

International Trafficking Networks: Routes, Regions, and Transformations

The geography of human trafficking is not fixed. It shifts in response to changes in economic conditions, migration patterns, conflict and displacement, border enforcement, and the adaptive strategies of trafficking networks themselves. Mapping the major trafficking routes that have operated across the twentieth and twenty-first centuries reveals both consistent structural patterns, the movement of people from poorer to wealthier regions, from zones of conflict to zones of relative stability, from countries with weak protective institutions to those with stronger demand and higher prices, and significant historical variation as specific routes open, close, or shift in response to changing conditions.

The transatlantic trafficking routes that first attracted international attention in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries primarily moved women from Eastern and Southern Europe to the Americas. Argentina, Brazil, and Cuba were major destination markets for women trafficked from Poland, Ukraine, Romania, and Hungary, as well as from Spain, Portugal, and Italy. The Zwi Migdal society, a Buenos Aires-based criminal organization founded by Eastern European Jewish immigrants in the late nineteenth century, became one of the most extensively documented early examples of an organized transnational trafficking network, with recruitment offices in Eastern Europe, transportation arrangements through major shipping routes, reception networks in Buenos Aires, and management of brothels serving the large immigrant male population of the Argentine capital. The organization operated openly for decades, maintaining official records and even, according to some historical accounts, a cemetery, before a combination of internal whistleblowing and police action led to a major crackdown in 1930.

The post-World War II period brought significant restructuring of global trafficking routes, driven by the transformation of the global economy, decolonization, the Cold War, and the rapid development of mass air travel and tourism. Southeast Asia emerged as a major trafficking hub from the 1960s onward, driven initially by the enormous demand generated by the American military presence in Vietnam, Thailand, and the Philippines. The rest and recreation programs for American troops created demand for commercial sex that was met by the rapid development of organized commercial sex industries in Bangkok, Angeles City in the Philippines, and other locations near military bases. When American troops withdrew from Southeast Asia in the mid-1970s, the infrastructure of commercial sex that had been created to serve them did not disappear. It was redirected toward international tourism, and sex tourism, the practice of traveling internationally specifically to purchase sexual services, became a significant and organized economic sector in Thailand, the Philippines, Cambodia, and other Southeast Asian countries.

The sex tourism industry of Southeast Asia was not simply a collection of individual entrepreneurial decisions. It was structured by organized networks that linked international travel agencies, hotels, transportation companies, and entertainment venues to the commercial sex operators who managed and profited from the women and children working in the industry. The involvement of organized crime in this industry varied: in some markets, local criminal networks controlled access to venues and taxed operators; in others, the industry was managed through more diffuse networks of corruption involving police, local officials, and tourism industry actors. What remained consistent was the exploitation of women and children who, due to poverty, lack of education, family pressure, debt, or outright trafficking, found themselves performing commercial sex for the benefit of organizers who captured most of the economic value their labor generated.

The collapse of the Soviet Union in 1991 inaugurated a period that would prove to be one of the most significant turning points in the history of modern trafficking. The sudden dismantling of the Soviet economic system plunged hundreds of millions of people into poverty and economic uncertainty. Social safety nets collapsed. Unemployment exploded, particularly among educated women whose previously assured positions in the Soviet economy disappeared overnight. Criminal networks that had operated in the Soviet underground economy, known as the tolkachi system, rapidly reorganized themselves to take advantage of the new conditions, and among the most profitable opportunities they identified was the trafficking of women to the wealthy markets of Western Europe, the Middle East, Israel, and Japan.

The scale of trafficking from the former Soviet states in the 1990s was extraordinary. Ukraine and Moldova became the two most significant source countries relative to their populations, with estimates suggesting that hundreds of thousands of women were trafficked from these countries over the course of the decade. The recruitment mechanisms were varied: advertising in newspapers for domestic service, waitress, or model positions abroad; romantic manipulation by local men who recruited girlfriends and then sold them to traffickers; and, increasingly, direct approach by recruiters who had developed a reputation in economically desperate communities for placing women in legitimate work abroad and who could therefore approach new recruits with the apparent credential of having helped others successfully. The normalcy of labor migration from post-Soviet states made it extremely difficult for potential victims to distinguish legitimate employment offers from trafficking operations.

African and Latin American Trafficking Networks

West Africa, and Nigeria in particular, developed as a significant source region for trafficking into Western Europe from the 1980s onward, with flows intensifying dramatically in the 1990s and 2000s. Nigerian criminal networks, operating through the Edo State networks centered in Benin City, developed sophisticated transnational operations that trafficked women into commercial sex venues across Italy, Spain, the Netherlands, and the United Kingdom. These networks were distinctive in their use of traditional religious rituals, known as juju or voodoo oaths, to bind victims to obligations of repayment and secrecy. Women who had undergone these rituals were placed under what traffickers and victims understood to be a binding supernatural obligation that, combined with the conventional mechanisms of debt bondage and threats to family members, created particularly powerful forms of control that proved resistant to conventional intervention strategies.

In Latin America, the trafficking picture was shaped by the combination of high economic inequality, significant internal and cross-border migration, organized crime networks with deep roots in drug trafficking, and in some countries, the additional displacement generated by conflict. Colombia, Mexico, and Brazil were significant both as source countries for women trafficked to richer markets within Latin America and to Europe and North America, and as destination countries for women trafficked within the region. The overlap between drug trafficking organizations and sex trafficking networks was significant in several countries, with criminal organizations that had developed transportation, corruption, and money laundering infrastructure for drug trafficking finding it relatively easy to add human trafficking as an additional revenue stream using the same organizational assets.

The Internet and Digital Transformation of Trafficking

The emergence of the internet and, subsequently, social media platforms fundamentally transformed the operational landscape of human trafficking in ways that both enhanced the capabilities of trafficking networks and created new challenges for law enforcement and victim identification. The transformation began in the mid-1990s and accelerated dramatically through the 2000s and 2010s, reshaping both the recruitment side of trafficking operations and the advertisement and sale of commercial sex services at destination.

Online recruitment, using social media platforms, dating applications, fake job advertisement websites, and messaging platforms, allowed traffickers to reach potential victims far more efficiently and cheaply than traditional in-person recruitment. A trafficker operating from a single computer could simultaneously maintain dozens of online personas, each presenting a different offer: a modeling agency, an employment placement service, a romantic suitor, a legitimate domestic service employer. The anonymity and reach of online recruitment allowed traffickers to target victims across geographical distances that would have made traditional in-person recruitment impractical, and to rapidly abandon identities that attracted suspicion while creating new ones at negligible cost.

At the destination end, online platforms transformed the advertisement and sale of commercial sex. Websites that aggregated escort and massage advertisements allowed commercial sex to move from street solicitation and visible brothels into a dispersed, indoor, online-facilitated market that was far harder for law enforcement to monitor and suppress. The rise and fall of specific platforms, including Craigslist's adult services section and Backpage, became major episodes in the struggle between online commercial sex advertising and law enforcement, illustrating both the genuine law enforcement challenges posed by online trafficking and the risks that aggressive platform regulation posed for consensual adult sex workers who relied on these platforms for safety screening and income.

Section 26.4

The Business of Trafficking: Economics, Corruption, and Scale

Human trafficking is, fundamentally, a business, and understanding it analytically requires applying the same tools of economic analysis that illuminate other illegal markets. Trafficking networks emerge and persist because they generate substantial profit, and they will continue to operate as long as the profit potential exceeds the expected costs of operation, which in most contexts means the costs of bribery, enforcement avoidance, and the occasional penalty when operations are disrupted. Understanding the economics of trafficking, including the sources of demand, the cost structure of supply, the role of corruption in reducing enforcement costs, and the profit margins that make the industry attractive to criminal investment, is essential context for evaluating both why trafficking persists and what kinds of interventions are most likely to be effective.

The International Labour Organization has estimated the annual profits generated by forced labor globally, including but not limited to sex trafficking, at over one hundred and fifty billion dollars, making it one of the most profitable criminal industries in the world. The sex trafficking subset of this total generates disproportionate profits relative to the number of people it exploits, because the price of commercial sex in wealthy destination markets is many times higher than the average price of other forms of forced labor. A woman trafficked into commercial sex in Western Europe might generate tens of thousands of euros in revenue for her exploiters annually, while a person trafficked into agricultural labor might generate a fraction of that amount. This differential profitability is one reason why sex trafficking has historically attracted disproportionate criminal investment relative to its share of total trafficking victims.

The cost structure of trafficking operations is dominated by recruitment, transportation, maintenance, enforcement, and bribery costs. Recruitment costs vary enormously depending on the method used: romantic manipulation that turns a girlfriend into a trafficking victim costs the trafficker little beyond time and emotional manipulation, while paying professional recruiters in source countries is more expensive but scales more easily. Transportation costs include the physical movement of victims across borders, the cost of forged or procured documents, and the fees paid to guides, vehicle operators, and border officials. Bribery is a pervasive and significant cost component: corruption of police, immigration officials, brothel inspectors, and local politicians is not merely incidental to trafficking operations but is in many contexts a systematically budgeted operating expense without which operations could not continue.

The relationship between trafficking and state corruption operates at multiple levels. At the most local level, individual police officers or immigration inspectors may accept payments to overlook specific operations, to provide advance warning of raids, or to release victims who have been arrested before they can provide information to investigators. At higher levels, organized crime networks with significant trafficking operations may cultivate relationships with senior police or security officials, prosecutors, or politicians who provide protection for the network as a whole in exchange for regular payments or other benefits. In the most extreme cases, state capture by criminal networks, in which criminal organizations have sufficient political and financial power to influence appointments to key law enforcement positions, to shape prosecutorial policy, and to control the information environment in which oversight of law enforcement occurs, can essentially neutralize the formal state apparatus as a check on trafficking operations.

Trafficking and Related Criminal Enterprises

Human trafficking rarely operates in isolation from other criminal enterprises. The organizational infrastructure required for trafficking, including transportation networks, document fraud, money laundering, and bribery, overlaps extensively with the infrastructure of drug trafficking, weapons smuggling, and other organized crime activities. In many contexts, established criminal organizations that had developed this infrastructure for drug trafficking found it relatively easy and profitable to add human trafficking as an additional line of business using the same organizational assets, relationships, and expertise.

The overlap between drug trafficking and human trafficking is particularly significant in Latin American and some Asian contexts. Mexican cartels, including the Sinaloa Cartel, the Gulf Cartel, and the Jalisco New Generation Cartel, have all been documented as having significant involvement in human trafficking alongside their dominant drug trafficking operations. The transportation corridors used to move drugs across the U.S.-Mexico border are also used to move trafficking victims, and the cartels' control of border crossing points gives them both a logistical advantage in trafficking operations and an incentive to tax or control the trafficking activities of others using their territory.

Money laundering is an essential dimension of trafficking as a criminal business. The cash revenues generated by trafficking operations must be moved through the financial system to become usable assets, and the mechanisms used for laundering trafficking proceeds, including real estate investment, cash-intensive businesses, and offshore accounts, are broadly similar to those used to launder proceeds from drug trafficking and other organized crime activities. The financial investigation of trafficking networks has become an increasingly important law enforcement tool, one that can identify the full scope of criminal organizations and provide evidence for prosecution that is more durable than victim testimony, which is often unavailable due to fear and trauma.

Section 26.5

Victims and Survival: Experiences, Agency, and the Limits of Simple Stories

One of the most significant challenges in studying human trafficking historically and analytically is navigating between two inadequate frameworks that have dominated popular understanding of the subject. The first framework presents trafficking victims as passive, innocent, ignorant victims who have been deceived or abducted by external criminals and who would immediately seek help if the opportunity arose. The second framework presents everyone in commercial sex as an autonomous agent making free economic choices who is harmed only by the criminalization of those choices. Both frameworks contain elements of truth and significant distortions, and the historical and empirical evidence on trafficking resists reduction to either one.

The reality of trafficking victimization is characterized by a spectrum of circumstances, coercion levels, and forms of agency that do not fit neatly into the binary of "innocent victim" and "willing participant." Some people enter commercial sex through direct, unambiguous coercion, being kidnapped, drugged, imprisoned, and forced to perform sex acts under threat of violence. This is real and it happens, but it represents one end of a spectrum, not the entirety of the experience of trafficking. Many more people enter commercial sex through deceptive recruitment that begins with an apparent offer of legitimate employment, so that the moment of definitive coercion is not at the beginning but in the middle, when the person has already left home, crossed borders, and finds herself in circumstances where the cost of exit is very high.

Others enter commercial sex with some awareness of what work will be involved but in conditions of economic desperation that make the alternative of staying at home worse than the risks of migration into the sex industry. A woman in a village in Moldova who knows that the employment being offered is probably in commercial sex, but who also knows that her children are going hungry and that there are no other employment options within reach, is making a choice, but it is a choice so constrained by poverty and lack of alternatives that the language of free choice captures very little of what is actually happening. The coercion in her situation is structural, not individual, produced by the economic conditions of her country and the labor market exclusion of women like her, rather than by a specific trafficker who has done something to her directly.

Once inside a trafficking situation, the exercise of agency by victims is constrained but not entirely absent. Victims develop strategies of survival, accommodation, and in some cases resistance that reflect rational calculation under conditions of severe constraint. Some victims build relationships with more sympathetic clients or venue staff that they leverage for information or limited protection. Some gradually acquire information about their environment that enables eventual escape. Some become trusted by traffickers and are given expanded freedom of movement that they eventually use to leave. Others accommodate themselves to their situation indefinitely, not because they have chosen it but because the alternatives available to them, including police contact that will result in deportation, return to a community that will stigmatize them, and loss of the income that they are at least partially accessing, appear worse than continued exploitation.

The category of "survivor" has become central to contemporary anti-trafficking advocacy, and survivor voices have been increasingly important in shaping policy, legal frameworks, and public understanding of trafficking. This development is positive in many respects, as it centers the experiences and knowledge of people who have actually experienced trafficking in debates that have too often been dominated by outsiders. However, the category of survivor also raises difficult questions about representation: whose experiences are centered, which survivors are considered credible, and how the diversity of trafficking experiences, across different types of trafficking, different national contexts, different degrees of coercion, and different post-trafficking circumstances, is incorporated into advocacy and policy. The single story of the trafficked innocent, rescued and restored, is emotionally powerful but analytically reductive.

Historical Vocabulary
Consent and Coercion

The concepts of consent and coercion are central to debates about trafficking, but they are more complex in practice than they appear in theory. In legal terms, the Palermo Protocol specifies that a victim's consent to being trafficked is irrelevant when any form of coercion, including fraud and deception, has been used. This is an important legal principle that prevents traffickers from avoiding prosecution by claiming that their victims initially agreed to travel or to work in commercial sex.

However, the concept of consent operates on a spectrum, and many trafficking situations involve what scholars call "constrained consent," in which people make decisions that in some technical sense reflect their preferences, but under conditions so constrained by poverty, lack of alternatives, and structural inequality that the language of free consent captures little of the moral reality. When poverty means that no genuine alternative exists to commercial sex or migration into dangerous employment, the presence of formal consent, even fully informed consent, does not eliminate the exploitation that the Palermo Protocol is designed to address.

Coercion, similarly, operates through multiple mechanisms, not only through physical force. Psychological manipulation, emotional dependency, debt bondage, document confiscation, threats to family members, and the structural coercion of poverty can all produce compliance without physical violence. Understanding trafficking analytically requires recognizing these multiple dimensions of coercion rather than restricting the concept to its most physically obvious forms.

Labor Trafficking and the Broader Exploitation Economy

While sex trafficking attracts disproportionate attention in public discourse, policy debates, and scholarly literature, forced labor in non-sexual economic sectors affects far more people globally. The International Labour Organization estimates that approximately seventy percent of all forced labor victims are exploited in agriculture, domestic service, construction, manufacturing, and fishing, with only about fifteen to twenty percent in commercial sex. Understanding trafficking comprehensively requires taking labor trafficking as seriously as sex trafficking, even when it attracts less emotional and media attention.

The fishing industry provides a particularly striking example of labor trafficking at the margins of global supply chains. Reports from the Thai fishing industry beginning in the early 2010s documented migrant workers, primarily from Myanmar, Cambodia, and Laos, who were sold to fishing vessel captains, confined on boats at sea for months or years without pay, fed inadequately, subjected to physical abuse, and unable to leave. The remoteness of the fishing grounds, the absence of labor inspection on the open sea, the undocumented status of most workers, and the market pressure for cheap seafood in global markets all contributed to conditions of forced labor that persisted for years before investigative journalism and NGO reporting brought it to wider attention.

Domestic worker trafficking, while less visible than either sex trafficking or the extreme cases of fishing industry abuse, affects millions of people globally, predominantly women and girls from Southeast Asia, South Asia, and Africa who are recruited to work as household servants in wealthier countries, particularly in the Gulf states and wealthy households in other regions. The conditions of domestic worker trafficking exploit a near-universal gap in labor protections: in most countries, domestic workers are excluded from the labor laws that protect other workers, having been left out of minimum wage legislation, working hours protections, and the right to organize, by a combination of historical discrimination, class prejudice, and the classification of household work as private rather than economic. This structural exclusion creates vulnerability that traffickers exploit.

Section 26.6

Law, Policy, and Response: Approaches to Combating Trafficking

The legal and policy response to human trafficking and organized prostitution is one of the most contested areas in contemporary criminal justice and human rights policy. The fundamental disagreements among policymakers, advocates, scholars, and survivors about the best approach to commercial sex and trafficking reflect genuine empirical uncertainty, sharply differing values, and deeply contested questions about the relationship between individual autonomy, economic coercion, and the state's appropriate role in regulating adult sexual conduct. This section examines the major policy frameworks that have been proposed and implemented, assesses the available evidence about their effects, and situates the policy debate within the broader history of trafficking response.

The four main policy frameworks for commercial sex that have been debated and implemented in various countries are: full criminalization, under which both the buying and selling of sex are illegal; partial criminalization or the Nordic Model, under which buying sex is illegal but selling is decriminalized; full decriminalization, under which all commercial sex between consenting adults is legal and subject to standard labor and business regulation; and legalization with regulation, under which commercial sex is legal but subject to specific regulatory requirements including licensing, mandatory health testing, and designated zones. Each of these frameworks has different implications for the prevalence and organization of trafficking, though the empirical evidence about these effects is more contested than advocates on any side typically acknowledge.

The Nordic Model, first adopted in Sweden in 1999 and subsequently in Norway (2009), Iceland (2009), Canada (2014), Northern Ireland (2015), France (2016), and Ireland (2017), represents the most significant policy innovation in the approach to commercial sex in recent decades. The model's logic is that commercial sex is inherently exploitative because it is driven by economic inequality and gender power imbalances that mean true consent is rarely possible, and that the appropriate policy response is therefore to reduce demand by criminalizing buyers while decriminalizing sellers, who are understood as victims of a gendered system of exploitation rather than as criminal actors. The Swedish government has reported significant reductions in street prostitution following the law's implementation, though this is disputed by critics who argue that street prostitution became less visible rather than less prevalent, with commercial sex moving indoors and online in ways that make it harder to measure and potentially more dangerous for sex workers.

The decriminalization approach, advocated by most international health and human rights organizations including WHO, UNAIDS, Amnesty International, and Human Rights Watch, argues that criminalizing any aspect of consensual adult commercial sex makes sex workers less safe by driving the industry underground, preventing sex workers from reporting violence and theft to police, discouraging regular health care access, and giving exploiters leverage over workers who cannot appeal to law enforcement without risking prosecution. New Zealand, which fully decriminalized adult commercial sex in 2003, is frequently cited as a model by decriminalization advocates, who point to evidence of improved safety and health outcomes for sex workers and continued ability to prosecute trafficking and exploitation of minors. Critics of decriminalization, including many feminist scholars and abolitionists, argue that it normalizes the purchase of sex, potentially increases demand, and creates conditions under which trafficking for sex is harder to distinguish from consensual commercial sex.

The Palermo Protocol and International Implementation

The 2000 United Nations Protocol to Prevent, Suppress and Punish Trafficking in Persons, Especially Women and Children, supplementing the United Nations Convention Against Transnational Organized Crime, represented a watershed moment in international legal responses to trafficking. Adopted by the UN General Assembly on November 15, 2000, and entering into force on December 25, 2003, the Protocol has been ratified by over 170 countries, making it near-universal in its formal adoption. The Protocol's three-pronged framework of prevention, protection, and prosecution, often summarized as the "3Ps," established the template for national anti-trafficking legislation worldwide and created international standards for victim identification, protection, and assistance.

The translation of these international legal commitments into effective national practice has been highly uneven. Many countries that have formally ratified the Protocol and adopted national anti-trafficking legislation have made minimal investment in the victim identification, social services, and witness protection infrastructure that effective implementation requires. Prosecution rates for trafficking remain low in most countries relative to the estimated prevalence of the crime. Victim identification is notoriously poor, with many trafficking victims being processed as illegal immigrants or criminal actors rather than identified as victims and offered protection. The funding and capacity of victim services organizations, including shelters, legal assistance, and long-term support, are grossly inadequate relative to the scale of the problem in most countries.

The United States Trafficking Victims Protection Act of 2000, which created the annual Trafficking in Persons (TIP) Report issued by the U.S. State Department, introduced a unilateral tier-ranking system in which countries are assessed on their efforts to combat trafficking and can face diplomatic and economic consequences for failure to meet minimum standards. This system has been both praised for creating international pressure on source, transit, and destination countries to take trafficking seriously, and criticized for politicizing trafficking rankings in ways that reflect U.S. foreign policy priorities rather than objective assessment of anti-trafficking performance.

Historical Debate

Does Criminalization of the Purchase of Sex (the Nordic Model) Reduce Trafficking?

The question: The Nordic Model, which criminalizes the purchase but not the sale of sex, has been adopted by a growing number of countries on the argument that reducing demand for commercial sex will reduce trafficking by shrinking the market that traffickers exploit. Critics argue that it harms consensual sex workers while doing little to reduce trafficking. What does the evidence show?

Position A — The Nordic Model Reduces Trafficking: Proponents argue that demand reduction is the most effective anti-trafficking strategy because trafficking exists to supply a market, and shrinking that market reduces the economic incentive for trafficking. Sweden's official reports document significant reductions in visible street prostitution and surveys of police, social services, and sex workers suggest reduced prevalence overall. Cross-national comparisons by researchers including Cho, Dreher, and Neumayer found that countries with legalized prostitution had higher reported trafficking inflows, suggesting that legal prostitution markets attract trafficking to supply them.
Position B — The Nordic Model Harms Sex Workers Without Reducing Trafficking: Critics, including most international health and human rights organizations, argue that criminalization of buyers drives commercial sex underground, making it more dangerous for consensual adult sex workers who lose the ability to screen clients, work in visible locations where help is accessible, and report violence to police without risking deportation or prosecution of partners. Decriminalization advocates point to New Zealand's experience with full decriminalization, which has not resulted in measurable increases in trafficking, and argue that the Swedish reductions in street prostitution reflect displacement rather than genuine reduction. The cross-national comparison cited by Nordic Model proponents has been challenged on methodological grounds.

Evidence: Rigorous evidence on this debate is limited by the methodological challenges of measuring clandestine markets, the difficulty of distinguishing trafficking from consensual commercial sex, and the absence of controlled comparison cases. Most existing studies have significant methodological limitations, and advocates on both sides tend to cite the evidence selectively.

Why it matters: The policy debate over criminalization, decriminalization, and trafficking is not merely academic. It determines the legal environment in which millions of people work, the likelihood that trafficking victims will be identified and helped, and the effectiveness of anti-trafficking law enforcement. Getting the policy framework wrong has real costs for real people.

Multiple Perspectives

Who Experiences Human Trafficking, and How?

From the perspective of trafficking survivors: Experiences are diverse and do not fit a single narrative. Some survivors describe immediate, violent coercion from which they sought escape at the first possible opportunity. Others describe gradual entrapment through romantic manipulation and debt bondage that made resistance feel impossible for years. Many describe complex emotional relationships with their exploiters that took time to identify as abusive. Most describe the indifference or active hostility of police and immigration authorities as a barrier to accessing help, and many describe being processed as criminals rather than victims when they did encounter law enforcement.
From the perspective of law enforcement: Trafficking investigations are extraordinarily difficult. Victims are reluctant to testify, either because of genuine trauma, fear of retaliation against themselves or family members, continued loyalty to traffickers in the case of romantic manipulation, distrust of police, or fear of deportation. Building cases that do not depend on victim testimony requires sophisticated financial and electronic investigation that strains the budgets of most agencies. Trafficking networks adapt rapidly to law enforcement tactics, relocating operations when pressure increases and restructuring organizational hierarchies to insulate leadership from prosecution.
From the perspective of consensual adult sex workers: Many people who sell sexual services as adults, who are not trafficked or coerced, insist that conflating all commercial sex with trafficking erases their agency, denies them legal recognition and labor protections, and results in policies, particularly criminalization of buyers, that make their work more dangerous by driving it underground. Organizations representing sex workers in many countries oppose the Nordic Model and support decriminalization as a framework that protects their safety and rights without treating all commercial sex as inherently exploitative.
From the perspective of destination country governments: Trafficking creates complex policy challenges that cut across immigration enforcement, law enforcement, public health, and human rights obligations. The imperative to control irregular immigration frequently conflicts with the obligation to identify and protect trafficking victims, particularly when victims lack legal status. The political pressure to appear tough on immigration can push enforcement responses that harm trafficking victims while doing little to prosecute traffickers. Managing these tensions requires institutional capacity and political will that are unequally distributed across destination countries.
Cause and Effect

The Emergence of Large-Scale Post-Soviet Trafficking, 1991–2005

Long-Term Causes

Structural gender inequality in Soviet and post-Soviet labor markets; absence of civil society organizations capable of providing social support; lack of tradition of independent law enforcement; deep integration of organized crime into political and economic structures from the Soviet era's underground economy.

Short-Term Causes

Collapse of the Soviet Union (1991) and resulting economic catastrophe; hyperinflation eliminating savings; mass unemployment particularly affecting women; collapse of social welfare institutions; rise of violent criminal oligarchs in newly privatized economies; opening of borders to international travel; weak law enforcement in transitional states.

The Development

Explosive growth of trafficking from Ukraine, Moldova, Russia, Belarus, and other former Soviet states to Western Europe, the Middle East, Israel, Japan, and other wealthy markets; emergence of sophisticated transnational criminal networks controlling trafficking operations across multiple countries; hundreds of thousands of victims trafficked over a fifteen-year period.

Effects

Restructuring of European trafficking markets; increased international law enforcement attention to trafficking; acceleration of international legal development culminating in the Palermo Protocol (2000); lasting damage to hundreds of thousands of individual victims; establishment of criminal networks and corruption relationships that persist decades later.

Historical complexity: The post-Soviet trafficking surge was not simply a consequence of the Soviet Union's collapse. It was the product of the intersection of structural gender inequality, economic catastrophe, institutional weakness, and organized criminal capacity that had been building across decades of Soviet history. The specific form it took was shaped by the geography of criminal networks, the demand conditions in receiving countries, and the particular vulnerabilities of post-Soviet women in the economic transition period.

Continuity and Change Over Time

The Organization of Commercial Sex Exploitation from the Nineteenth Century to the Present

What Changed

  • The geographic scale of trafficking operations expanded from regional to genuinely global
  • Transportation technology, particularly air travel, dramatically reduced the cost and time of moving victims across international distances
  • The internet transformed recruitment, advertisement, and the operational geography of commercial sex
  • International legal frameworks developed from weak and limited agreements to near-universal instruments with defined state obligations
  • The definitional framework expanded from "white slavery" (a racially and gender-limited conception) to comprehensive trafficking definitions encompassing labor as well as sex and all genders
  • Victim-centered approaches, while imperfectly implemented, became the stated norm of international policy
  • Major new source regions emerged as economic disruption, conflict, and displacement created new vulnerability pools

What Stayed the Same

  • The fundamental structural driver: economic desperation, gender inequality, and lack of alternative options for the most vulnerable people
  • The core mechanisms of control: debt bondage, document confiscation, threats, isolation, and violence
  • The role of corruption in neutralizing law enforcement
  • The gap between the formal legal commitments of states and the actual resources invested in prosecution and victim protection
  • The pattern of trafficking flows moving from poorer regions and populations toward wealthier markets
  • The disproportionate burden borne by women, girls, and marginalized groups
  • The adaptation of trafficking networks to enforcement pressure by changing routes, methods, and organizational structures

Why the pattern matters: The persistence of the structural drivers of trafficking, across more than a century of legal development, international cooperation, and enforcement activity, suggests that law enforcement responses alone are insufficient. Addressing trafficking at its roots requires addressing the economic inequality, gender inequality, and institutional weakness that create vulnerability, not only the criminal networks that exploit it.

Primary Source Window

League of Nations Advisory Committee on Traffic in Women and Children: Investigation Reports, 1927

Creator: League of Nations Advisory Committee on Traffic in Women and Children · Date: 1927 · Type: International governmental investigative report

Context: In the mid-1920s, the League of Nations commissioned a systematic investigation into the actual nature and scale of international trafficking networks, rather than relying on the moral reform discourse that had dominated earlier discussions. The investigation, conducted by a team that included women investigators working undercover in commercial sex districts across Europe, North and South America, and other regions, produced one of the most empirically detailed pictures of organized trafficking operations available from this period. The reports documented recruitment methods, transportation networks, reception arrangements, control mechanisms, and the organizational structures of criminal networks with a clinical attention to operational detail that was quite different from the sensationalized "white slave" narratives that had dominated public discourse.

The investigating team reported that the most common recruitment method they documented was not physical abduction but what they described as a combination of fraudulent employment offers and romantic or social manipulation. Women were often recruited by individuals already known to them, or by agents who presented credible-seeming offers of domestic service or entertainment employment abroad. The teams found that many recruiters operated as apparently legitimate employment agencies, and that the same individuals appeared in records in multiple countries as both legitimate employers and traffickers.

Analysis: The League of Nations investigation is significant as an early attempt at evidence-based, empirical research into trafficking operations rather than moral-reform advocacy. Its findings, that trafficking operated primarily through fraud and deception rather than abduction, and that the victims were typically women in economically desperate circumstances rather than the "innocent girls" of popular imagination, challenged the dominant narrative in ways that were important but also, in the political climate of the 1920s, difficult to act upon. The reports also documented the role of state corruption and regulatory arbitrage, the tendency of trafficking networks to operate most intensively in jurisdictions with the weakest enforcement, in ways that remain relevant to contemporary understanding.

Limits: The investigation was conducted under significant constraints. The investigators worked in a limited number of countries and were unable to gain access to the most closed and violent segments of the industry. The report reflected the assumptions of its era, including assumptions about race, respectability, and female agency, that a contemporary reader must recognize and account for. The quantitative estimates in the report were necessarily speculative given the absence of systematic data collection infrastructure.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has traced the history of organized prostitution and human trafficking from ancient commercial sex markets through the contemporary global trafficking economy, examining the criminal organizations that have profited from sexual exploitation, the methods of control they have used, the legal and policy frameworks that have developed in response, and the structural conditions that have sustained the industry across radical changes in technology, geography, and law.

Commercial sex has existed in virtually every society for which historians have written records, but the emergence of large-scale, systematically organized criminal networks that recruited, transported, and controlled women across regional and eventually international distances is a modern phenomenon, rooted in the conditions of nineteenth-century industrialization, urbanization, and mass migration. The regulationist system of state-licensed brothels, which dominated official approaches to commercial sex in much of Europe and its colonies through the nineteenth century, institutionalized exploitation without effectively controlling it. The feminist campaigns led by Josephine Butler and others, who argued that regulation made the state complicit in exploitation rather than limiting it, articulated a critique that reframed the debate around questions of gender equality and victims' rights rather than public health and moral order.

The late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries saw the first organized international responses to trafficking, driven by the moral panic around "white slavery" that combined genuine evidence of organized cross-border networks with racial anxieties and assumptions about female innocence that distorted as much as they revealed. The International Agreement of 1904 and the subsequent League of Nations investigations and conventions established principles of international cooperation and empirical research that, despite their limitations, laid the groundwork for the more comprehensive international legal framework that would follow in the late twentieth century.

The major organized crime structures that emerged in the twentieth century, including the American organized crime syndicates of the Prohibition era, the Japanese Yakuza, Russian and Eastern European criminal networks, the Albanian trafficking organizations, and the West African networks associated with Nigerian criminal organizations, each developed their own organizational approaches to managing commercial sex as a criminal enterprise. What united them was the use of debt bondage, document confiscation, psychological manipulation, and violence to maintain control over victims who would otherwise leave, the cultivation of corruption among law enforcement and officials to neutralize enforcement, and the adaptation of organizational structures and operational methods in response to law enforcement pressure and changing market conditions.

The collapse of the Soviet Union in 1991 created conditions for one of the most dramatic expansions of trafficking in modern history, as economic catastrophe, institutional weakness, and the opening of borders combined with the organizational capacity of post-Soviet criminal networks to produce large-scale transnational trafficking operations that reshaped the European and global trafficking landscape for decades. At the same time, the development of sex tourism in Southeast Asia, the intensification of West African trafficking into Europe, and the growth of domestic trafficking within many countries demonstrated that the post-Soviet surge was part of a broader pattern of expansion driven by economic inequality, globalization, and the organizational capacity of criminal networks to exploit both.

The United Nations Palermo Protocol of 2000 provided the first comprehensive international legal definition of human trafficking, establishing victim-centered principles that recognized the irrelevance of consent when coercion has been used and encompassing both sex and labor trafficking. Despite near-universal formal ratification, implementation of the Protocol's obligations has been highly uneven, with prosecution rates remaining low, victim identification grossly inadequate, and social service infrastructure for survivors drastically underfunded in most countries. The TIP Report system established by the United States has created international accountability pressure but has also been criticized for politicization.

The policy debate over the appropriate legal framework for commercial sex, between full criminalization, the Nordic Model of buyer criminalization, full decriminalization, and legalization with regulation, reflects genuine empirical uncertainty and deep disagreements about the relationship between commercial sex and trafficking, between individual autonomy and structural coercion, and between the interests of trafficking victims and consensual adult sex workers. The evidence about the effects of different policy frameworks is contested, and honest assessment requires acknowledging both what the evidence shows and the significant limitations of available research.

The digital transformation of trafficking, through internet recruitment, online advertisement, and social media manipulation, has created both new operational capabilities for trafficking networks and new challenges for law enforcement and victim identification. The integration of trafficking with other organized crime enterprises, particularly drug trafficking, and the use of money laundering to obscure and protect trafficking proceeds, have made trafficking increasingly integrated into the broader organized crime economy rather than a standalone criminal industry.

Throughout all these transformations, the fundamental structural drivers of trafficking have remained consistent: economic inequality, gender inequality, the vulnerability created by poverty and lack of alternatives, the corruption that allows criminal networks to neutralize law enforcement, and the demand, from buyers of commercial sex, employers of forced labor, and consumers of products produced by forced labor, that makes trafficking economically rational for criminal entrepreneurs. Understanding these structural drivers, rather than focusing solely on the criminal networks that exploit them, is essential to understanding both why trafficking persists and what kinds of interventions have the most potential to reduce it.

This chapter has also insisted on the complexity of the human experience of trafficking, resisting both the narrative of the passive innocent victim and the narrative of the freely choosing economic actor. The reality of trafficking victimization spans a spectrum from unambiguous coercion to structurally constrained choice, and effective policy responses must be calibrated to that complexity rather than to the simplified narratives that dominate popular discourse. The voices and experiences of survivors, in all their diversity, must be central to how policies are designed, implemented, and evaluated.

The history of organized exploitation examined in this chapter connects to the broader themes of this textbook: the relationship between market demand, criminal supply, and state response; the role of economic inequality in creating the conditions that organized crime exploits; the adaptive capacity of criminal networks in the face of law enforcement pressure; and the limits of prohibition and criminalization as strategies for eliminating markets rooted in deep structural conditions. Trafficking is not a problem that has emerged in isolation from broader social, economic, and political conditions. It is a product of those conditions, and understanding it historically is essential to addressing it effectively.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Abolitionism (prostitution policy)
The policy position that prostitution should be eliminated through criminal penalties on buyers, sellers, or both; distinct from the nineteenth-century anti-slavery abolitionism.
Coercion
The use of force, threats, psychological manipulation, economic pressure, or other means to compel behavior; a central concept in defining trafficking and distinguishing it from consensual commercial sex.
Debt bondage
A system of control in which a victim's freedom is conditioned on repaying a real or fabricated debt, structured to be practically impossible to eliminate; a primary control mechanism in trafficking operations globally.
Decriminalization
The removal of criminal penalties from the sale and/or purchase of sex; distinct from legalization, which involves specific regulatory frameworks.
Demand reduction
Anti-trafficking strategy focused on reducing the market for commercial sex by criminalizing or stigmatizing buyers; the theoretical foundation of the Nordic Model.
Destination country
A country that is a primary market for trafficking victims; typically characterized by higher income levels and significant demand for commercial sex or cheap labor.
Document confiscation
The practice of traffickers taking victims' passports and identity documents to prevent independent travel, leave, or contact with authorities.
Forced labor
Work performed under coercion, including debt bondage, threats, and restrictions on freedom of movement; encompasses labor trafficking across multiple economic sectors.
Human trafficking
The recruitment, transportation, transfer, harboring, or receipt of persons by means of force, fraud, coercion, or deception for the purpose of exploitation, as defined by the Palermo Protocol.
Labor trafficking
Trafficking for the purpose of forced labor in non-sexual economic sectors including agriculture, domestic service, construction, manufacturing, and fishing.
Legalization
A regulatory framework under which commercial sex is legal but subject to specific requirements such as licensing, mandatory health testing, and geographic zoning.
Nordic Model (Swedish Model)
Policy framework that decriminalizes the sale of sex while criminalizing the purchase; first adopted in Sweden in 1999; subsequently adopted by several other countries.
Palermo Protocol
The UN Protocol to Prevent, Suppress and Punish Trafficking in Persons (2000); the foundational international legal instrument defining trafficking and establishing state obligations.
Procurer / Pimp
A person who manages and profits from the commercial sex work of others; historically a central figure in the organization of commercial sex exploitation.
Recruitment fraud
The use of false promises of legitimate employment or other deception to recruit trafficking victims; one of the most common trafficking recruitment methods globally.
Regulationism
The system of state-licensed and regulated brothels, modeled on the Napoleonic French system, that dominated official approaches to commercial sex in much of Europe and its colonies in the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries.
Sex tourism
Travel specifically for the purpose of purchasing commercial sex in destination countries, often from lower-income regions; a significant organized sector linked to trafficking in Southeast Asia and other regions.
Sex trafficking
Trafficking for the purpose of commercial sexual exploitation; a subset of human trafficking that receives disproportionate policy and media attention relative to its share of total trafficking victims.
Source country
A country from which a significant number of trafficking victims originate; typically characterized by economic hardship, institutional weakness, or conflict-related displacement.
Survivor-centered approach
Policy and advocacy framework that centers the experiences, voices, and priorities of people who have experienced trafficking in designing and evaluating anti-trafficking responses.
Trafficking network
An organized criminal structure involving recruiters, transporters, document forgers, border facilitators, reception handlers, and venue managers who coordinate to recruit, move, and exploit trafficking victims.
Transit country
A country through which trafficking victims pass en route from source to destination; may itself be a destination for some victims while functioning as a transit point for others.
White slavery
The term used in late nineteenth and early twentieth-century reform discourse to describe organized trafficking of women into forced prostitution; reflects racial and moral assumptions of the era but also genuine evidence of organized cross-border networks.
Zwi Migdal
A major early transnational trafficking organization, founded by Eastern European Jewish immigrants in Buenos Aires, that operated recruitment and brothel networks from Eastern Europe to Argentina in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries.

Major People and Places

Identification & Significance

Josephine Butler (1828–1906)
British feminist reformer who led the successful campaign to repeal Britain's Contagious Diseases Acts in 1886; articulated the framework that prostitution represents exploitation of women by men and the state; pioneered international anti-trafficking advocacy and helped establish the moral framework that continues to inform much feminist anti-trafficking thinking.
Buenos Aires, Argentina
Major destination city for late nineteenth and early twentieth-century transatlantic trafficking; home of the Zwi Migdal society, one of the most extensively documented early transnational trafficking organizations; its large immigrant population and developing commercial sex industry made it a primary market for Eastern European trafficking networks.
Benin City, Edo State, Nigeria
Center of West African trafficking networks that developed significant operations trafficking women into commercial sex venues across Europe from the 1980s onward; networks from this region were distinctive in their use of traditional religious rituals (juju oaths) as a control mechanism alongside conventional debt bondage.
Thailand and Southeast Asia
Major destination region for sex tourism that developed from the 1960s onward, initially linked to U.S. military presence during the Vietnam War; subsequently became a primary international sex tourism destination and a significant trafficking hub within the Greater Mekong Subregion.
Moldova and Ukraine
Two of the most significant source countries for post-Soviet trafficking, relative to their populations; the combination of economic devastation following the Soviet collapse, weak protective institutions, and proximity to Western European markets made women from these countries disproportionately vulnerable to trafficking networks from the 1990s onward.
New Zealand
First country to fully decriminalize adult commercial sex (Prostitution Reform Act, 2003); frequently cited in policy debates as an evidence base for the effects of full decriminalization; subsequent research has examined outcomes for sex workers' safety, health access, and reported prevalence of trafficking.
Sweden
First country to adopt the Nordic Model (1999), criminalizing the purchase of sex while decriminalizing its sale; the Swedish approach has been the most influential national policy innovation in the global prostitution policy debate of the past three decades.
United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime (UNODC)
Primary UN body responsible for monitoring global trafficking trends and supporting national implementation of the Palermo Protocol; publishes the Global Report on Trafficking in Persons, the most comprehensive regular assessment of global trafficking patterns and national responses.
International Labour Organization (ILO)
UN specialized agency that monitors forced labor globally; estimates that forced labor in agriculture, domestic service, construction, and manufacturing affects far more people than sex trafficking; essential corrective to the overemphasis on sex trafficking in policy and media discourse.
The Mann Act (White Slave Traffic Act, 1910)
U.S. federal law prohibiting transportation of women across state lines for "immoral purposes"; shaped American law enforcement approach to trafficking for decades; also notoriously used to prosecute consensual adult relationships and to target prominent Black men including boxer Jack Johnson.

Chapter Reflection

Human trafficking and organized prostitution occupy an uncomfortable place in the study of organized crime, because they connect the analytical framework of criminal enterprise, with its attention to organizational structure, economic logic, and law enforcement response, to some of the most intimate and brutal forms of human exploitation. The temptation, in studying trafficking, is to retreat into either emotional outrage that forecloses analysis or clinical detachment that erases the humanity of victims. This chapter has tried to do neither, treating trafficking as an organized criminal industry while insisting that the human experience of exploitation and the structural conditions that create vulnerability are not merely context for the criminal analysis but are at the center of what understanding trafficking actually requires.

The history traced in this chapter reveals several recurring patterns that connect to the broader themes of this textbook. Like all successful criminal enterprises, trafficking operates where market demand, organizational capacity, and the absence of effective enforcement intersect. Like all organized crime, it adapts to law enforcement pressure with remarkable agility, shifting routes, organizational structures, and methods when particular approaches become too costly. Like all organized crime in the modern era, it has integrated digital technology in ways that expand its operational reach while creating new vulnerabilities that law enforcement is only beginning to learn to exploit. And like all organized crime, it is sustained by the corruption of the officials and institutions that should constrain it, demonstrating once again that the boundary between criminal networks and the state apparatus is more permeable than formal legal frameworks suggest.

But trafficking is also distinctive in ways that matter analytically. Its primary victims are not the public in general, whose safety is disrupted by drug markets or extortion rackets, but specific people, predominantly women, girls, and marginalized populations, whose vulnerability is not incidental to the crime but constitutive of it. Trafficking is a crime that specifically targets people whom structural conditions have made most difficult to protect, whose access to the institutions of the state that should protect them is most constrained, and whose voices and experiences have historically been most excluded from the policy debates that determine how the crime is defined and addressed. Understanding trafficking thus requires understanding not only the criminal networks that perpetrate it, but the structural conditions, including gender inequality, economic inequality, and institutional weakness, that make it possible.

The policy debates examined in this chapter, around the Nordic Model, decriminalization, and the Palermo Protocol framework, represent genuine disagreements about difficult questions that do not have easy answers. The honest conclusion of the available evidence is that these debates are not resolved by the evidence we currently have, and that confident claims on all sides should be viewed with appropriate skepticism. What the evidence does support is the consistent finding that the gap between formal legal commitment and actual implementation, in investigation, prosecution, victim identification, and services, is one of the most significant failures in the global anti-trafficking response. Laws on paper, however well-crafted, do not protect people if they are not implemented with adequate resources, political will, and institutional capacity. This is perhaps the most important lesson this chapter offers for understanding both the persistence of organized trafficking and the conditions under which it might be more effectively addressed.

The themes of this chapter, exploitation, vulnerability, structural inequality, institutional corruption, and the limits of law enforcement as a strategy for addressing organized crime rooted in deep social and economic conditions, connect directly to the questions that will occupy the concluding chapters of this textbook. Why does organized crime persist, despite a century of increasingly sophisticated enforcement? What is the relationship between organized crime and the social and economic conditions that generate it? And what kinds of responses, beyond law enforcement, are necessary to address criminal industries that are sustained by demand, enabled by inequality, and protected by corruption? These are questions to which human trafficking offers some of the clearest and most sobering answers available in the history of organized crime.

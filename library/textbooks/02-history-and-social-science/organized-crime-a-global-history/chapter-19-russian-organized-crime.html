Russian Organized Crime

Organized Crime: A Global History

Chapter Opening

"In Russia, organized crime did not emerge in spite of the state. In many crucial respects, it emerged from within the state, drawing on structures of power, corruption, and secrecy that the Soviet system itself had built over seven decades."

In the winter of 1991, as the Soviet Union dissolved and fifteen successor states struggled into uncertain existence, a question hung over the wreckage of one of the twentieth century's great empires: who would inherit not only the territory, the weapons, and the institutions of the old order, but also its underground economy, its informal power networks, and its vast criminal underworld? The answer that emerged over the following decade was more complicated and more troubling than almost anyone had predicted. Russia did not simply produce organized crime as a side effect of its chaotic transition to capitalism. In significant ways, organized crime produced the Russia that emerged from that transition, shaping its economy, its politics, its culture, and its relationship with the rest of the world in ways that continue to unfold today.

Russian organized crime is one of the most consequential and least well understood phenomena in modern global history. It is consequential because Russian criminal networks, born in the wreckage of the Soviet state, grew within a decade into some of the most powerful and far-reaching criminal organizations on earth, operating across dozens of countries, penetrating financial systems on multiple continents, corrupting governments, trafficking in people and weapons and drugs, and generating revenues that dwarfed those of many national economies. It is poorly understood because it does not fit neatly into familiar categories. Russian organized crime is not simply crime in Russia. It is a global phenomenon with Russian roots and international reach. It is not simply the product of poverty or inequality, though both played a role. It is, in significant part, the product of a specific historical moment, the collapse of an empire, and of the particular structures of power, corruption, and informal economy that the Soviet system had created over seventy years.

The story of Russian organized crime begins not in 1991 but decades earlier, in the labor camps and prisons of the Stalinist Gulag, where a criminal subculture called the vory v zakone, or "thieves in law," developed its own codes, hierarchies, rituals, and values in deliberate opposition to Soviet authority. These men created a parallel social world within the most brutal institutions of the Soviet state, governed by an elaborate code of honor that rejected any cooperation with Soviet power, and they carried that world with them when they were released. The vory were not the only source of Soviet-era organized crime, however. The Soviet black market, the tenevaya ekonomika or "shadow economy," was not a marginal phenomenon but a structural feature of Soviet life, essential to the functioning of an economy that was chronically unable to supply what its citizens needed through official channels. By the 1970s and 1980s, Soviet citizens at every level of society, from factory workers to Communist Party officials, participated in informal economic networks that blurred the line between the legal and the illegal in ways that would prove fateful when the system collapsed.

The Soviet Union's collapse in 1991 created what social scientists would later call a "criminogenic environment," conditions so favorable to the growth of organized crime that it would have been surprising if criminal organizations had not flourished. The state had lost its monopoly on violence. Legal institutions were too weak and too corrupt to enforce property rights. A vast privatization process transferred enormous public assets into private hands at prices that bore no relationship to market value, creating instant fortunes for those with the right connections and the right willingness to use force. The police and security services, demoralized and underpaid, were for sale. And millions of citizens who had spent their lives in an economy governed by informal networks suddenly found those networks were the most reliable source of protection, credit, employment, and social order they had.

Out of this chaos emerged figures who would become notorious around the world: the bratva, or criminal brotherhoods; the oligarchs who built empires of wealth through insider privatization and criminal coercion; the avtoritety, or criminal authorities, who combined the traditional culture of the vory with the new opportunities of global capitalism; and, eventually, intelligence and security service veterans who moved between the worlds of state power and criminal enterprise with a fluidity that made the distinction between the two difficult to draw. The Russian state that emerged from the 1990s was not merely a state that tolerated organized crime. It was, in important respects, a state in which the boundary between government, business, and organized crime had been so thoroughly blurred that criminal methods had become a normalized feature of economic and political life.

This chapter traces that history from its Soviet-era origins through the chaos of the 1990s transition and into the twenty-first century, when Russian organized crime had evolved into a sophisticated global enterprise with operations spanning Europe, the Americas, Asia, and the Middle East. It examines the social structures, economic strategies, and political connections that made Russian criminal organizations so powerful, the international operations that carried Russian crime far beyond Russia's borders, and the difficult questions that this history raises about the relationship between state power, economic transformation, and criminal enterprise. It also asks what makes Russian organized crime distinctive, and what it shares with criminal organizations in other times and places, because answering that question is essential to understanding not just Russia but the broader global phenomenon of which Russian organized crime is now an integral part.

The stakes of this history extend well beyond criminology. The rise of Russian organized crime is inseparable from the rise of the Russian state under Vladimir Putin, from the corruption that has corroded democratic institutions across Eastern Europe and beyond, from the money-laundering operations that have passed through Western banks and real estate markets, and from the networks of influence and coercion that have shaped global politics in ways that are still being reckoned with. Understanding Russian organized crime is not simply an exercise in historical curiosity. It is essential background for understanding some of the most pressing political and security challenges of the contemporary world.

Learning Objectives

  2. Describe the origins, structure, and code of the vory v zakone within the Soviet Gulag system.

  4. Trace the specific mechanisms by which the Soviet collapse and the subsequent privatization process created extraordinary opportunities for criminal enrichment.
  5. Distinguish between the various types of criminal actors who emerged in post-Soviet Russia, including traditional vory, new-generation bratva, criminal oligarchs, and security service operatives.
  6. Assess how the relationship between organized crime and the Russian state evolved from the chaotic 1990s through the consolidation of Putinism.
  7. Identify the major international operations of Russian organized crime, including drug trafficking, arms dealing, human trafficking, cybercrime, and money laundering.
  8. Evaluate the debate among historians and criminologists about whether the Russian state should be understood as controlling organized crime, being penetrated by it, or being in some sense fused with it.

  10. Connect the history of Russian organized crime to broader themes of state failure, economic inequality, corruption, and globalization.

Questions for Historical Inquiry

  2. What was the vory v zakone, and why did the prison culture of the Gulag produce a criminal subculture with such distinctive characteristics and such lasting influence?
  3. In what ways was the Soviet shadow economy not a criminal phenomenon but a structural feature of the system, and how did that distinction shape what happened when the system collapsed?
  4. What specific features of the post-Soviet transition, privatization, institutional collapse, and the loss of state monopoly on violence, made it so favorable to criminal enterprise?

  7. How did Russian criminal networks extend their operations internationally, and what does their global reach reveal about the relationship between organized crime and globalization?

Historical Context

To understand Russian organized crime, students must begin with a clear picture of the society that produced it: the Union of Soviet Socialist Republics, a state that governed approximately one sixth of the earth's land surface, controlled one of the world's two largest arsenals of nuclear weapons, and maintained rigid political control over a population of nearly three hundred million people for more than seven decades. The Soviet Union was not a simple dictatorship of the straightforward authoritarian type. It was a system of extraordinary complexity, in which formal legal structures coexisted with informal power networks, official ideology coexisted with widely shared private cynicism, and the proclaimed mechanisms of governance coexisted with the actual mechanisms of getting things done, which often bore little resemblance to official descriptions.

The Soviet economic system was a command economy: the state owned virtually all productive assets, set prices and production targets through a central planning apparatus, and theoretically directed all economic activity from the center. In practice, this system was chronically inefficient. Central planners could not gather and process the information necessary to coordinate a modern industrial economy. Production targets were routinely missed. Consumer goods were scarce and often of poor quality. Supply chains broke down. Enterprises were unable to obtain the inputs they needed through official channels. The result was a massive and permanent gap between what the official economy promised and what it actually delivered, a gap that was filled not by market mechanisms but by informal networks of exchange, barter, bribery, and theft.

These informal networks were not a corruption of the Soviet system. They were, in an important sense, the Soviet system's real operating mechanism. Factory managers who could not fulfill their production quotas through official supply channels did so by trading favors with managers at other enterprises, paying bribes to obtain scarce materials, diverting state resources for private exchange, or simply falsifying their reports. Party officials used their positions to secure privileges for themselves and their families: access to special stores, better housing, vacation facilities, and medical care. Ordinary citizens relied on networks of personal connection, known as blat, to navigate a system in which official rules were insufficient to meet basic needs. Getting a child into a good school, obtaining a scarce apartment, accessing medical treatment, or repairing a car required knowing the right people and being willing to exchange favors, cash, or goods. Participation in these informal networks was not experienced as crime. It was experienced as the way things worked.

Within this broad informal economy, a more specifically criminal sector also flourished. The Soviet black market, known as the tenevaya ekonomika or shadow economy, encompassed everything from street-level speculation in scarce consumer goods to large-scale theft of state property, currency exchange at illegal rates, bribery networks within the party-state apparatus, and the activities of criminal entrepreneurs who had built significant underground enterprises. By the 1970s, some analysts estimated that the shadow economy accounted for a substantial fraction of actual Soviet economic activity, though the secretive nature of the system made precise measurement impossible. What was clear was that the shadow economy was not merely tolerated by Soviet officials; it was actively sustained by them, because it helped paper over the failures of the official economy and provided officials themselves with opportunities for enrichment.

Within the Soviet penal system, meanwhile, a distinct criminal subculture had been developing since the early Stalinist period. The Gulag, the vast archipelago of labor camps that stretched across the Soviet Union's most remote and inhospitable regions, held millions of prisoners at its peak in the late 1940s and early 1950s. Within the camps, a hierarchy of criminal prisoners, who called themselves the vory v zakone or "thieves in law," developed an elaborate subculture with its own values, rituals, hierarchy, and code of conduct. The vory code, known as the Vorovskoy zakon or thieves' code, prohibited any cooperation with Soviet authority, required members to renounce family ties and conventional social roles, mandated redistribution of criminal proceeds through a common fund called the obshchak, and bound members through a system of tattoos and initiation rituals that marked them as members of a distinct social world existing in permanent opposition to the Soviet state.

The vory were not the founders of Russian organized crime in any simple sense. But they established a cultural template, a set of values and practices around which criminal identity in the Soviet Union and its successor states would organize itself for decades. Their experience in the camps, where they survived and maintained solidarity in conditions of extreme violence, gave them a social capital that would prove enormously useful in the chaotic environment of the post-Soviet transition.

Against this background, the events of 1991 were not simply the end of a political regime. They were the sudden removal of the organizational structure that had, for all its failures and corruptions, provided the framework within which Soviet society functioned. When that framework collapsed, the informal networks and criminal organizations that had operated within and alongside it did not disappear. They adapted, expanded, and, in many cases, emerged into the light as the most effective power brokers in the resulting vacuum.

Section 19.1

Soviet-Era Black Markets

The story of Russian organized crime cannot begin in 1991, or even in the Gorbachev years of the late 1980s. Its roots run deep into the structures and culture of the Soviet system itself, and understanding those roots is essential to understanding why the collapse of Soviet power produced criminal organizations of such extraordinary scale and sophistication. The Soviet Union did not simply fail to prevent organized crime. In significant ways, it created the social and economic conditions in which organized crime was virtually inevitable.

The Command Economy and Its Informal Counterpart

The Soviet economic system was based on the principle of central planning. The state owned all significant productive assets, from factories and collective farms to transport networks and retail outlets. A vast bureaucracy of planning agencies, most centrally the State Planning Committee known as Gosplan, set production targets, allocated resources, and theoretically coordinated all economic activity across a country spanning eleven time zones and encompassing an enormous range of industries, climates, and populations. The fundamental premise of the system was that rational, coordinated central direction of the economy would be more efficient and more equitable than the anarchic mechanisms of the market.

In practice, the command economy generated chronic shortages, poor quality, and misallocation of resources on a massive scale. Central planners could not process the information needed to coordinate a modern industrial economy. Production targets, set in terms of quantity rather than quality, created perverse incentives: a factory producing nails measured by weight would produce a few very heavy nails rather than many small useful ones. Supply chains broke down because each enterprise in the production chain depended on others fulfilling their plans, and when one failed, the failure cascaded through the system. Consumer goods, always lower priority than heavy industry in Soviet planning, were perpetually scarce, often of poor quality, and distributed through channels that favored party members and urban populations over ordinary workers and rural communities.

This chronic gap between official supply and actual demand created the structural foundation for a vast informal economy. At the most basic level, the informal economy consisted of citizens doing what they needed to survive: growing food on private plots that the state officially tolerated, trading favors with neighbors and colleagues, obtaining scarce goods through personal connections rather than official channels. These activities shaded into what economists would call the black market: the exchange of goods and services at prices the state did not set, often involving goods obtained by theft from state enterprises or through unauthorized production.

The concept of blat was central to Soviet informal economic life. Blat referred to the use of personal connections, informal influence, and reciprocal favor exchange to obtain goods and services that were unavailable through official channels. Having good blat meant knowing the right people: the store manager who could put aside scarce items before they reached the official queue, the official who could accelerate an application for an apartment or a travel permit, the doctor who could arrange access to better medical care, the factory manager who could provide building materials for a private renovation. Blat was not simply corruption in the conventional sense, though it shaded into corruption at the higher levels. It was the social technology by which Soviet citizens navigated a system that was structurally unable to meet their needs through official mechanisms.

Above the level of individual blat relationships, more organized forms of underground economic activity also flourished. The Soviet black market encompassed currency exchange, in which citizens traded rubles for hard currency at rates far more favorable than the official rate, enabling the purchase of imported goods or the accumulation of internationally usable wealth. It encompassed trade in scarce consumer goods, from Western blue jeans and recorded music in the 1970s to electronics and luxury items later. It encompassed the large-scale theft of state property, from materials pilfered from construction sites to entire truckloads of goods diverted from official distribution channels. And it encompassed what the Soviets called tsekhoviki, literally "workshop people": underground entrepreneurs who operated private production facilities in parallel to official enterprises, using state materials and sometimes state equipment to produce goods for unofficial markets.

The Thieves in Law: A Criminal Society Within Soviet Society

Within the Soviet penal system, a distinct and remarkably durable criminal subculture was taking shape during the Stalinist period. The Gulag, the network of labor camps that held millions of political prisoners, common criminals, and a vast category of people caught between those definitions, was one of the most brutal institutions of the twentieth century. Prisoners in the camps faced forced labor in extreme conditions, inadequate food and clothing, violent treatment by guards, and the constant threat of violence from other prisoners. In this environment, social organization was a matter of survival.

The vory v zakone, or "thieves in law," were the criminal aristocracy of the Gulag. They were not simply strong or violent men, though they were often both. They were men who had been formally inducted into a criminal brotherhood through a ritual of acceptance by existing members, who had demonstrated their commitment to a strict code of conduct, and who had usually demonstrated their criminal credentials through multiple convictions and extensive prison experience. The vorovskoy zakon, or thieves' law, was the code that defined membership in this brotherhood and distinguished genuine vory from ordinary criminals.

The thieves' code was built around a set of principles that were, in their own terms, coherent and demanding. A genuine vor could not cooperate with Soviet authority in any way: no informing to prison officials, no military service, no legitimate employment in Soviet institutions. A vor must support other members of the brotherhood: contributing to the obshchak, the communal fund used to support imprisoned members and bribe officials; providing assistance and shelter to members in need; and resolving disputes within the brotherhood through internal councils rather than by appealing to state authorities. A vor renounced conventional family ties: marriage, acknowledgment of children, and permanent attachment to a place were all prohibited, the theory being that such attachments created vulnerabilities that the state could exploit. A vor lived only by crime, refusing any form of legitimate work as a betrayal of the code.

This code, rigorously enforced through violence within the camps, created a social world that functioned as a parallel institution to the Soviet state, with its own hierarchy, its own dispute resolution mechanisms, its own welfare system, and its own rituals of membership and status. The extensive tattoo culture of the vory served as a readable record of a man's criminal career: specific designs indicated the crimes for which he had been convicted, the camps he had served in, his rank in the criminal hierarchy, and his adherence to or violations of the code. In a society where official records were controlled by the state, the body became a credential document.

The relationship between the vory and the Soviet state was not simply one of opposition, however. The camp authorities frequently relied on the vory hierarchy to maintain order among the prisoner population, because the alternative, constant direct confrontation with organized criminal resistance, was more costly than allowing the vory to exercise their own authority. This created a tacit and uncomfortable coexistence: the state needed the vory to help manage the camps, while the vory code prohibited any formal cooperation with the state. The management of this tension required constant negotiation and created a culture of strategic ambiguity about the line between cooperation and resistance that would prove very useful in the post-Soviet world.

A major rupture in the vory world came during and after World War II. The catastrophic German invasion of 1941 led the Soviet state to offer prisoners the option of military service in exchange for reduced sentences or early release. Many vory accepted this offer, a decision that, by the strict logic of the thieves' code, constituted treason: they had cooperated with the state and taken up its weapons. When these men returned to the camps after the war, a violent conflict erupted between those who had served in the military, who were called suki or "bitches" by their opponents, and the traditional vory who had refused to serve. The "Suki Wars" of the late 1940s and early 1950s were extraordinarily violent, killing thousands of prisoners and reshaping the criminal hierarchy. The conflict ultimately produced a fragmented criminal world in which the rigid traditional code coexisted with more pragmatic approaches, a tension that would persist into the post-Soviet period.

The Brezhnev Era: Institutionalized Corruption

If the Stalinist period produced the foundational culture of Soviet criminality, the Brezhnev era, from 1964 to 1982, produced its institutionalization. Under Leonid Brezhnev, the Soviet leadership largely abandoned the brutal mass terror of the Stalinist period in favor of a system of rule based on stability, patronage, and the accommodation of corruption at every level of the party-state apparatus. The result was a system that scholars have called "developed socialism" but that Soviet citizens often described with the sardonic phrase vzyatochestvo, bribery, as the real operating principle.

Corruption under Brezhnev was not simply a matter of individual dishonesty. It was a structural feature of the system. Officials at every level used their positions to extract benefits, in the form of cash, goods, services, or influence, from those over whom they had authority. In return, they provided protection, access, and favorable treatment. This created elaborate hierarchies of corruption that mirrored the official hierarchy of the party-state: local officials paid tribute to regional officials, who paid tribute to republican officials, who paid tribute to the central apparatus. At the top, the Brezhnev family itself was widely known to engage in corrupt enrichment, a fact that the state's own security apparatus documented but declined to act upon.

The most spectacular example of Brezhnev-era corruption was the so-called Cotton Affair, in which officials in the Soviet republic of Uzbekistan systematically falsified production figures for cotton, the republic's major cash crop, for years while receiving payment from Moscow for cotton that had never been grown. When investigators finally exposed the fraud in the early 1980s, they found that it implicated thousands of officials at every level of the Uzbek party-state and extended into the central apparatus in Moscow. The Cotton Affair was unusual only in its scale; the practices it revealed were common throughout the Soviet system.

The Brezhnev era also saw the consolidation of what scholars have called "criminal capitalism" within the Soviet economic system. The tsekhoviki, underground entrepreneurs who operated private production operations within official enterprises, became more organized and more deeply integrated into corrupt networks of protection provided by party officials and police. A successful tsekhovik needed a party patron who could protect him from prosecution, a police contact who could warn him of inspections, access to state materials that could be diverted to private production, and a distribution network for selling his goods outside official channels. All of these required corruption, and all of them required something that closely resembled organized crime: a sustained criminal enterprise with multiple participants operating under a system of shared rules and mutual protection.

Historical Vocabulary
Vor v Zakone (Thief in Law)

The term vor v zakone, literally "thief in law," referred to a formally inducted member of the criminal aristocracy of the Soviet Gulag and its successor criminal world. The phrase is paradoxical: "in law" did not mean within the state's law but rather within the law of the thieves' own code, the vorovskoy zakon. A vor was someone who lived by the code, which required renouncing any cooperation with the state, supporting fellow members through the communal fund, resolving disputes internally, and living exclusively by crime.

The status of vor v zakone was granted through a formal process of nomination and acceptance by existing members and was marked by specific tattoos. At the height of the Soviet period, there were believed to be a few hundred genuine vory v zakone within the Soviet criminal world. In the post-Soviet period, the institution underwent significant change: the code was diluted, the numbers of claimed vory expanded dramatically, and the Georgian vory in particular, who had maintained the traditions most strictly, became especially prominent and internationally active. By the 2000s, criminal leaders of Georgian origin, operating across Russia and internationally, were among the most feared and powerful figures in the post-Soviet criminal world.

Gorbachev's Reforms and the Opening of Criminal Opportunity

When Mikhail Gorbachev came to power in 1985 and began his programs of glasnost (openness) and perestroika (restructuring), his intention was to reform the Soviet system from within, correcting its inefficiencies while preserving its fundamental character. The reforms had unintended consequences that proved far more consequential than their intended ones, and among the most significant of those consequences was the creation of new and unprecedented opportunities for criminal enrichment.

The 1987 Law on Cooperatives was particularly important. It allowed private businesses, organized as cooperatives, to operate legally for the first time since the early Soviet period. The law was intended to stimulate the economy by allowing private initiative in areas the state economy served poorly. In practice, cooperatives quickly became instruments for laundering black market money, for converting state assets into private wealth, and for building the business infrastructure that criminal entrepreneurs would use in the post-Soviet period. Criminal groups used cooperative structures to move money from the underground economy into legitimate business channels, to establish legal identities for enterprises that had previously operated entirely in the shadows, and to build relationships with corrupt officials who could provide protection and regulatory favor.

The partial liberalization of the Gorbachev period also created new criminal opportunities in the form of joint ventures with foreign companies, legal currency exchange operations, and expanded commodity trade. Criminal entrepreneurs with access to state assets, particularly raw materials such as oil, metals, and timber, could sell these at controlled domestic prices and sell them for hard currency abroad at world market prices, capturing the enormous profit differential. This practice, known as commodity arbitrage, became one of the most important mechanisms by which criminal wealth was accumulated in the late Soviet period, and it laid the groundwork for the much larger privatization-based enrichment that would follow the Soviet collapse.

Section 19.2

Collapse of the Soviet Union

The dissolution of the Soviet Union in December 1991 was one of the most consequential geopolitical events of the twentieth century. For the organized crime story, it created conditions that scholars have compared to the most fertile environments for criminal enterprise in history: more disruptive than the aftermath of American Prohibition, more prolonged than the chaos of post-war Italy that gave rise to the modern Mafia, and covering a geographic space far larger than any previous theater of criminal expansion. Understanding why the Soviet collapse was so favorable to organized crime requires examining its specific features, not just the general proposition that disorder breeds crime.

The Criminogenic Environment: Why the Collapse Was Different

The concept of a "criminogenic environment" refers to social and institutional conditions that are specifically favorable to the growth of organized crime, not simply because they create poverty or disorder, but because they create the particular combination of opportunity, impunity, and demand that organized criminal enterprises require. The post-Soviet space in the early 1990s was arguably the most criminogenic environment in modern history, and several specific features explain why.

First, the Soviet collapse eliminated the state's monopoly on violence without replacing it with anything. In a well-functioning state, the government's monopoly on the legitimate use of force provides the framework within which property rights can be enforced, contracts can be honored, and economic life can proceed. When the Soviet security apparatus, the KGB and its various subordinate agencies, collapsed or was reorganized, and when the Interior Ministry police became demoralized, underpaid, and effectively for sale, no institution remained capable of reliably enforcing even basic legal norms. In this environment, private coercion became the dominant mechanism for enforcing contracts and protecting property, and organized criminal groups were ideally positioned to provide exactly that service.

The concept of krysha, literally "roof," captured this dynamic precisely. In the post-Soviet criminal economy, every business needed a krysha: a protector, whether a criminal organization, a corrupt police unit, or a connected political figure, who could provide security against other predators, resolve disputes with business partners, and protect the enterprise from extortion by competing protection rackets. The krysha system was not simply extortion in the straightforward sense, though it often involved extortion. It was a market in protection services that criminal organizations supplied and that businesses genuinely demanded, because without protection of some kind, survival in the chaotic economic environment of the 1990s was very difficult.

Second, the privatization process that transferred Soviet state assets into private hands was conducted in conditions that systematically favored those who combined political connections with criminal methods. The theoretical basis of Russian privatization, under the direction of economists such as Yegor Gaidar and Anatoly Chubais and with advice from Western economic consultants, was the rapid conversion of state enterprises into privately owned companies through a voucher system that would, in theory, give ordinary citizens a share in the national wealth. In practice, the privatization process was captured by insiders: members of the old nomenklatura who used their positions to buy enterprises at heavily discounted prices before the market could establish real values, managers of state enterprises who used their control over assets and information to transfer value to themselves and their allies, and criminal entrepreneurs who used violence and corruption to seize opportunities that legal mechanisms alone would not have provided.

The result was what many scholars have called "primitive accumulation by criminal means," a process by which enormous wealth was transferred from public to private hands through mechanisms that, in a functioning legal system, would have been recognized as theft, fraud, and extortion on a massive scale. The speed of this process was staggering. In the space of a few years, individuals who had had no significant personal wealth under the Soviet system accumulated fortunes that made them among the richest people on earth, not by creating new value but by capturing existing value through a combination of political connection, legal manipulation, and criminal coercion.

The Loans-for-Shares Scandal and the Rise of the Oligarchs

The most notorious episode of this process was the loans-for-shares scheme of 1995 and 1996, which transferred control of some of Russia's most valuable industrial assets, including major oil companies and metals producers, to a small group of politically connected financiers at prices far below any reasonable market valuation. The scheme worked as follows: private banks, controlled by financial entrepreneurs who had already accumulated capital through the informal mechanisms of the late Soviet and early transition period, lent money to the cash-strapped Russian government. In return, the government pledged shares in major state enterprises as collateral. When the government predictably failed to repay the loans, the banks acquired the shares, becoming controlling shareholders in enterprises worth many times the value of the loans.

The beneficiaries of this scheme, figures such as Mikhail Khodorkovsky, who gained control of the oil company Yukos; Vladimir Potanin, who acquired the metals giant Norilsk Nickel; and Boris Berezovsky and Roman Abramovich, who obtained interests in the oil company Sibneft, became known as the "oligarchs," a term that combined the Greek root for rule by the few with a connotation of illegitimate wealth and political manipulation. The oligarchs were not simply the product of criminal enterprise in the narrow sense: the loans-for-shares scheme was conducted through formally legal mechanisms. But it was widely understood, both within Russia and internationally, to represent a systematic manipulation of the legal framework for private enrichment at public expense, enabled by political connections that amounted to corruption at the highest levels of the Russian state.

The oligarchs' political influence in the Yeltsin years was extensive. Some became closely connected to the Kremlin, providing financial support for political campaigns and using their control of major media outlets to shape public opinion. Others built private security forces that resembled small armies, employing former KGB and military officers and operating with a degree of autonomy from state authority that blurred the line between legitimate business security and criminal organization. The political economy of the Yeltsin period was characterized by what scholars have called "state capture": the phenomenon in which private interests, whether criminal organizations or nominally legitimate businesses, gain sufficient influence over state institutions to redirect those institutions toward their own ends.

Violence as a Business Tool: The Contract Killing Era

The most visible manifestation of organized crime in the Russian transition period was the explosion of contract killings, known in Russian as zakaznye ubiystva. In the mid-1990s, Moscow and other major Russian cities experienced murder rates that in some analyses rivaled the most violent cities in the world. The targets of this violence were not primarily ordinary citizens but rather businessmen, bankers, criminal competitors, journalists, and officials who had become obstacles to someone's economic interests. Between 1992 and 1997, Russia experienced what is estimated to be between fifteen hundred and five thousand contract murders per year, though the secretive nature of the phenomenon and the weakness of law enforcement in this period make precise figures impossible.

These murders were business transactions. In the post-Soviet criminal economy, violence was a tool of competition and dispute resolution in a world where legal mechanisms were unavailable or unreliable. When a business dispute could not be resolved through negotiation, or when one party had enough resources to pay for a more definitive solution, contract killing was an available option. The killing of a competitor, a creditor, or a business partner who had become inconvenient was a predictable risk of doing business in 1990s Russia in ways that had no parallel in economies with functioning legal institutions. Criminal organizations profited both by providing this service and by using it to eliminate competitors and enforce their own interests.

The contract killing era also produced an informal judicial system that operated in parallel to, and often more effectively than, the formal court system. Criminal avtoritety, or authorities, served as arbiters of disputes within the criminal world and, increasingly, in the broader business world as well. When two businesses had a dispute they could not resolve through official channels, they might bring it before a criminal authority, who would hear both sides, render a judgment, and, if necessary, enforce it through coercive means. The fees for this service, and the ability to profit from the leverage it provided, made conflict resolution one of the most lucrative services that organized crime provided to the Russian economy in the 1990s.

Historical Vocabulary
Oligarch

In the context of post-Soviet Russia, an oligarch refers to an individual who accumulated extraordinary wealth during the chaotic privatization process of the 1990s through a combination of political connections, insider information, legal manipulation, and, in some cases, criminal coercion. The term derives from the Greek concept of oligarchy, rule by the few, and implies that these individuals exercised not just economic but political power disproportionate to their numbers.

The Russian oligarchs of the 1990s were not a homogeneous group. Some, such as Mikhail Khodorkovsky, moved over time toward more conventional business practices and eventually toward political opposition. Others, such as Boris Berezovsky, remained deeply intertwined with political manipulation and eventually fled Russia. Still others accommodated themselves to the new order under Putin, accepting reduced political autonomy in exchange for protection of their economic interests. The common thread was that their wealth originated in a process that was, at best, legally dubious and that depended on political connections that would have been unavailable to ordinary citizens. The use of the term "oligarch" in Russia itself became contested: defenders of the privatization process argued that the oligarchs were simply the successful entrepreneurs that a market economy required, while critics argued that their wealth represented theft from the public on a historic scale.

Criminal Organizations of the Transition Period

The organized criminal groups that flourished in the transition period were diverse in their origins, structures, and methods. Some were rooted in the traditional vory culture of the Soviet criminal world. Others were new formations that drew on the vory tradition for prestige and cultural legitimacy while departing from the strict code in practice. Still others were essentially criminal enterprises organized by former athletes, security service veterans, or ethnic communities that used their own cultural networks as organizational frameworks.

The Solntsevskaya Bratva, or Solntsevo Brotherhood, became one of the most powerful and internationally connected organizations of the transition period. Based originally in the Solntsevo district on the southwestern outskirts of Moscow, the organization was built in the late 1980s by Sergei Mikhailov, known as "Mikhas," and his associates. Unlike the traditional vory, the Solntsevo group was willing to cooperate with both state officials and legitimate businesses, using a network of shell companies and offshore accounts to conduct financial operations that blended criminal proceeds with legal business income. By the mid-1990s, the Solntsevo organization had operations across Russia, Europe, and beyond, and Mikhailov himself was spending considerable time in Western Europe.

In St. Petersburg, criminal organizations known as the Tambovskaya and Malyshevskaya gangs established control over key economic sectors, including port facilities, the fuel trade, and banking. These groups were particularly significant because St. Petersburg was the political base of Vladimir Putin, and the relationships between these criminal organizations and Putin's circle of associates from his KGB service days have been extensively studied and debated by researchers attempting to understand the origins of Putinism. While direct proof of personal corruption is difficult to establish, the broader pattern of relationships between the Petersburg criminal world and the Petersburg political world that Putin represented is well documented.

Ethnic criminal organizations also played an important role in the post-Soviet criminal landscape. Chechen organized crime groups, which had established themselves in Moscow during the Soviet period, became particularly prominent in the early 1990s, partly because of the reputations for violence that Chechen fighters had earned in various conflicts and partly because of networks of community solidarity that gave them organizational advantages. Georgian criminal networks, often built around traditional vory leadership, extended their influence across Russia and increasingly into Western Europe. Armenian, Azerbaijani, and Central Asian criminal groups operated in their own niches within the broader criminal economy.

Historical Vocabulary
Nomenklatura

The nomenklatura referred to the list of positions within the Soviet party-state that required approval by the Communist Party for appointment, and, by extension, to the class of individuals who held those positions. The nomenklatura comprised the political, administrative, and managerial elite of Soviet society, approximately two to three million people at the system's peak, who enjoyed significant privileges in return for their loyalty to the party.

When the Soviet Union collapsed, members of the nomenklatura were uniquely positioned to profit from the transition. They had insider knowledge of state assets and their real values, they had the political connections necessary to navigate the privatization process, they often had access to the foreign currency accounts and contacts that enabled capital flight, and they had the administrative skills and institutional relationships that made them valuable to new private enterprises. The widespread conversion of nomenklatura position into post-Soviet economic power was a defining feature of the transition period, and it meant that the social elite of the post-Soviet system overlapped substantially with the social elite of the Soviet system, despite the apparent revolutionary character of the change. This helps explain why the transition to capitalism in Russia did not produce the property-owning middle class that Western advisers anticipated, but instead produced a small number of extremely wealthy individuals with intimate connections to state power.

Cause and Effect

The Rise of Russian Organized Crime, 1985–2000

Long-Term Causes

Seven decades of a command economy that generated chronic shortages and made informal exchange structurally necessary; the development of vory v zakone culture in the Gulag as a parallel social institution; institutionalized corruption during the Brezhnev era; the accumulation of underground entrepreneurial experience and capital through the shadow economy.

Short-Term Causes

Gorbachev's partial liberalization creating legal forms usable for money laundering; the Soviet collapse eliminating state monopoly on violence; rapid privatization at discounted prices controlled by insiders; demoralization and corruption of police and security forces; the 1998 financial crisis destroying savings and driving capital offshore.

The Development

Emergence of powerful criminal organizations with control over significant sectors of the Russian economy; the blurring of lines between organized crime, legitimate business, and state power; expansion of Russian criminal networks internationally; institutionalization of corruption at every level of public life.

Effects

Weakening of democratic institutions and the rule of law; massive capital flight that impoverished Russia while enriching criminal and political elites; creation of global Russian criminal networks; consolidation of power under Putin partly justified by the need to restore order; long-term damage to Russia's institutional development and international reputation.

Historical complexity: The rise of Russian organized crime was not simply a product of the Soviet collapse. It was the product of specific features of Soviet society, specific decisions made during the transition, and specific international conditions, including the availability of offshore financial systems, that could absorb criminal proceeds. No single factor explains the outcome.

Section 19.3

Criminal Oligarchs

By the mid-1990s, Russia had produced a class of extremely wealthy individuals whose fortunes had been accumulated through methods that blurred the boundary between business and crime in ways that made conventional distinctions difficult to apply. These figures, the so-called oligarchs of the Yeltsin era, were not simply criminals in the conventional sense: they operated through legally registered companies, they employed lawyers and accountants, they cultivated relationships with Western business partners and financial institutions, and some of them became genuinely significant entrepreneurs in the conventional sense. But the origins of their wealth, and the methods by which they protected and expanded it, were deeply intertwined with criminal practice, political corruption, and the coercive power of private security forces.

Boris Berezovsky and the Fusion of Power and Crime

Among the most prominent and controversial figures of the Yeltsin period was Boris Berezovsky, a mathematician-turned-businessman whose career illustrated the peculiar fusion of political influence, criminal methods, and legitimate business that characterized the Russian oligarchic class at its height. Berezovsky built his initial fortune in the early 1990s through the automobile business, using his access to the official Soviet automobile distribution system to accumulate capital during the transition period. He then moved into media, acquiring a controlling interest in the television network ORT, which gave him enormous political influence. He expanded into oil, obtaining interests in the company Sibneft through the loans-for-shares scheme. And he built deep personal connections to the Yeltsin family and the inner circle of the Kremlin.

Berezovsky's methods were frequently associated with criminal practice. His business career was marked by violence, including a car bombing in 1994 that killed his driver and nearly killed him, and the subsequent murder of the journalist Paul Klebnikov, among others whose deaths were connected, with varying degrees of evidence, to his business conflicts. He was also associated with the mysterious figure of the Chechen conflict, raising questions about whether his business interests in the region were entangled with the criminal economies of war. And his relationship with Vladimir Putin, whom he helped bring to national prominence, then supported for the presidency before having a catastrophic falling-out with him, illustrated the dangerous volatility of the relationship between oligarchic power and state authority in the Yeltsin era.

When Putin consolidated power after 2000 and made clear that the oligarchs would be required to choose between political subordination and elimination, Berezovsky chose confrontation and fled to London. He spent his remaining years funding opposition activities against Putin from exile and making a series of increasingly extravagant claims about plots against him. He died in Ascot in 2013 in circumstances that a British inquest could not definitively classify as suicide or murder, a death that was either the final act of a man destroyed by his own political mistakes or an assassination carried out with the sophistication expected of the state services he had helped create. The ambiguity was appropriate: Berezovsky's entire career had been defined by the impossibility of drawing clean lines between legitimate power, criminal enterprise, and state violence.

Semyon Mogilevich: The Brainy Don

If Berezovsky represented the fusion of politics and crime at the level of oligarchic power, Semyon Mogilevich represented organized crime of a more classic but far more sophisticated variety. Born in Kiev in 1946, Mogilevich was known in law enforcement circles as "the brainy don," a title that reflected both his criminal stature and his unusually sophisticated approach to criminal enterprise. Where most organized crime figures relied primarily on violence and political protection, Mogilevich built criminal enterprises of extraordinary financial complexity, using a global network of shell companies, offshore accounts, and legitimate business fronts to conduct operations across dozens of countries and dozens of criminal markets.

Mogilevich's criminal career spanned multiple continents and multiple decades. In the early post-Soviet period, he was associated with schemes to defraud Jewish emigrants leaving the Soviet Union, taking their assets in exchange for false promises of assistance. He moved into arms dealing, drug trafficking, and financial crime on a large scale. He became associated with the Bank of New York scandal of 1999, in which an estimated seven billion dollars of Russian money, some of it believed to be of criminal origin, was moved through accounts at the bank in ways that violated money laundering regulations. He built a Hungarian company called YBM Magnex that was listed on the Toronto and Philadelphia stock exchanges before collapsing amid accusations of fraud. And he maintained close relationships with both traditional vory figures and with political and security service contacts within Russia and across Eastern Europe.

Mogilevich was placed on the FBI's Ten Most Wanted list in 2009, one of the very few financial criminals to achieve that distinction. He was arrested in Moscow in 2008 on tax evasion charges, a characteristically Russian use of technically legal charges to manage a figure whose activities had become inconvenient, but was released in 2009 and has since lived openly in Moscow, apparently protected by connections that make his arrest and extradition politically impossible. His case illustrates one of the defining features of high-level Russian organized crime: the degree to which powerful criminal figures operate with state tolerance or protection, making conventional law enforcement approaches essentially ineffective.

The Putin Era: Managing Crime or Criminalizing Management?

The election of Vladimir Putin as president in 2000, following his appointment as prime minister by Boris Yeltsin in 1999, fundamentally altered the relationship between the Russian state and organized crime. Putin, a former KGB officer from St. Petersburg, came to power promising to restore order to a country that had experienced a decade of economic chaos, political instability, and rampant criminality. His early actions as president did indeed reduce the most visible manifestations of criminal disorder: the contract killing epidemic subsided, the most politically independent oligarchs were either subordinated or driven into exile, and the state reasserted its authority over significant sectors of the economy.

What Putin created, however, was not simply a state that had defeated organized crime. It was, as many scholars have argued, a state that had reorganized the relationship between crime and power, replacing the chaotic competition of the Yeltsin years with a more structured system in which criminal enterprise was tolerated, managed, and in some respects directed by state actors, in exchange for a share of the proceeds and for political loyalty. The concept of the "criminal state" or the "mafia state," advanced by scholars including Karen Dawisha and Misha Glenny, suggests that under Putin, the distinction between state and criminal organization became so blurred that applying either term alone was inadequate to describe the reality.

The evidence for this characterization is substantial, though contested. The enormous personal wealth that Putin and his close associates are believed to have accumulated through their control of state enterprises, despite official salaries that could not account for it, suggests corruption on a grand scale. The use of state institutions, including the FSB, the tax authorities, and the courts, to destroy political opponents and business rivals while protecting favored allies, has the functional characteristics of a protection racket operating at the state level. The tolerance extended to criminal organizations that operate internationally while remaining politically loyal, and the use of criminal networks for operations that serve state interests, including money laundering, election interference, and the management of foreign influence operations, suggests a relationship between the Russian state and organized crime that is not simply one of predator and prey but of something more like partnership.

The arrest of Mikhail Khodorkovsky in 2003 illustrated the new logic clearly. Khodorkovsky, the head of the Yukos oil company and briefly Russia's richest man, had accumulated his wealth through the same insider privatization mechanisms as the other oligarchs. But he had also begun funding opposition political parties and civil society organizations, and he had become publicly identified with demands for greater transparency and rule of law in Russian business. His arrest on fraud and tax evasion charges, his decade-long imprisonment, and the forced sale of Yukos assets to state-connected companies at below-market prices was understood by almost all observers as a political act: a demonstration that independent economic power, disconnected from Kremlin patronage, would not be tolerated. The message was reinforced by the fact that other oligarchs with comparable records of fraudulent enrichment faced no such prosecution, as long as they maintained political loyalty.

The Siloviki and the Security-Business Nexus

A distinctive feature of the Putin era was the emergence of the siloviki, a term derived from the Russian word for force, sila, referring to veterans of the security and military services who had moved into positions of political and economic power. Putin himself was a former KGB officer, and he surrounded himself with former colleagues from the security services who occupied key positions in the government, in state-owned enterprises, and in nominally private companies with close state connections. The siloviki brought with them the organizational culture of the security services: compartmentalization, the use of information as a weapon, tolerance for extra-legal methods, and the assumption that the distinction between legal and illegal was less important than the distinction between loyal and disloyal.

The siloviki were not simply a replacement for the criminal oligarchs of the Yeltsin era. They were, in many respects, a fusion of state authority and criminal method. Their access to state power gave them the ability to use intelligence resources, legal mechanisms, and the threat of prosecution as instruments of economic competition, enabling them to accumulate wealth and eliminate competitors through methods that were formally legal but functionally criminal. At the same time, they maintained relationships with organized criminal networks that could be used for operations that required deniability: money laundering, coercive pressure on foreign actors, and influence operations that needed to be conducted through channels that could not be directly traced back to the Russian state.

Testimony Before the U.S. Senate, Permanent Subcommittee on Investigations: "Russian Organized Crime in the United States" (1996)

Creator: U.S. Senate Permanent Subcommittee on Investigations · Date: May 1996 · Type: Congressional hearing record, government report

Context: By the mid-1990s, American law enforcement and intelligence agencies had become increasingly alarmed by the penetration of Russian organized crime into the United States. The FBI, the DEA, and the State Department all testified before the Senate subcommittee, along with law enforcement officials from other countries and academic experts. The hearings produced a substantial record of Russian criminal activities in the United States and helped justify expanded law enforcement cooperation with Russian authorities, though those efforts would prove complicated by the corruption of Russian institutions themselves.

Testimony from the hearings described how Russian organized crime figures had established operations in multiple American cities, particularly in communities with large Russian émigré populations such as Brighton Beach in Brooklyn, New York. Witnesses described fuel tax fraud schemes in which criminal networks had defrauded federal and state governments of hundreds of millions of dollars by exploiting the tax structure on gasoline; healthcare fraud operations that billed Medicare and Medicaid for services never rendered; and money laundering operations that moved criminal proceeds through a network of shell companies, offshore accounts, and real estate transactions.

Analysis: Congressional hearing records are valuable primary sources for several reasons. They document the state of official knowledge at a specific moment, revealing what law enforcement agencies understood and what they were still missing. They capture the range of interpretations being offered by different experts and officials. And they reflect the political context in which policy decisions were being made, because the framing of criminal threats in congressional testimony is always shaped by the political interests of those testifying and those conducting the hearings. This particular set of hearings is significant because it represented one of the earliest major efforts by the American government to systematically understand and respond to the Russian organized crime phenomenon, at a time when many American officials were still inclined to see Russia primarily as a partner in post-Cold War transition rather than as a source of criminal threat.

Section 19.4

International Operations

Russian organized crime did not remain confined to Russia. From the earliest years of the post-Soviet transition, Russian criminal networks extended their operations across borders with a speed and geographic range that surprised Western law enforcement agencies and created complex new challenges for international crime control. By the early 2000s, Russian organized crime had established significant operations in virtually every major region of the world, using the tools of global capitalism, including offshore financial systems, electronic money transfers, and legitimate corporate structures, to build criminal enterprises of extraordinary geographic reach and financial sophistication.

The Émigré Criminal Communities

Russian criminal expansion into Western countries built initially on foundations provided by earlier waves of Soviet émigré settlement. The largest and most criminally significant of these communities was concentrated in Brighton Beach, a neighborhood in Brooklyn, New York, that had received large numbers of Soviet Jewish emigrants in the 1970s and 1980s under immigration policies that granted them refugee status. Within this community, figures such as Marat Balagula built criminal operations that extended from fuel tax fraud, which exploited American tax collection mechanisms to generate enormous profits, to automobile theft, extortion, and drug trafficking.

Balagula's career illustrated the distinctive character of early Russian-American organized crime. Unlike Italian-American Mafia figures, who operated within a relatively stable organizational structure with clearly defined territories and protocols, Russian criminal figures in America tended to operate in more fluid, opportunistic networks that could rapidly adapt to new criminal opportunities and that drew on connections across the émigré community, back to Russia and Eastern Europe, and into the mainstream American business and financial world. Balagula himself was an early example of the type: a well-educated man who combined criminal entrepreneurship with a veneer of legitimate business activity, who maintained connections to the broader criminal world while operating through structures that were superficially difficult to distinguish from legitimate enterprise.

Fuel tax fraud became the signature crime of Russian-American organized crime in the 1980s and early 1990s. The scheme exploited a structural feature of American fuel taxation: because federal and state fuel taxes were collected from distributors rather than at the point of sale, a chain of shell companies could arrange for fuel to pass through a series of intermediate entities before reaching consumers, with one entity in the chain, the "burn company," responsible for remitting the taxes but structured specifically to fail to do so before disappearing. Russian criminal networks pioneered this fraud on a massive scale, defrauding federal and state governments of hundreds of millions of dollars, and used the proceeds to expand into other criminal activities and to launder money through legitimate business investments.

Money Laundering Through Western Financial Systems

The most consequential of Russian organized crime's international operations, in terms of its long-term impact on Western economies and institutions, was the systematic use of Western banking and financial systems to launder the proceeds of Russian criminal activity and to conduct the capital flight that transferred enormous Russian wealth into foreign accounts and assets. The scale of this operation was staggering. Estimates of the capital that left Russia in the 1990s vary widely, but most serious analyses suggest that hundreds of billions of dollars left the country during the decade following the Soviet collapse, a significant portion of it through mechanisms that involved money laundering.

The Bank of New York scandal of 1999 brought this phenomenon to international attention. An investigation by American authorities revealed that approximately seven billion dollars had been moved through Bank of New York accounts in ways that violated money laundering reporting requirements. The money's origins were diverse: some of it came from legitimate Russian businesses seeking to move funds offshore to avoid taxation and instability, some from corrupt officials seeking to protect illicitly accumulated wealth, and some from criminal organizations seeking to launder proceeds from drug trafficking, extortion, and other illegal activities. The scandal implicated not only the Russian individuals who had conducted the transactions but also employees of the bank itself, raising questions about the degree to which Western financial institutions were willing partners in Russian money laundering rather than passive victims of it.

The mechanisms of Russian money laundering evolved rapidly as Western regulators responded to each new technique. In the early transition period, direct wire transfers to offshore accounts in jurisdictions with weak anti-money-laundering frameworks, including Cyprus, Latvia, and the Isle of Man, were common. As regulations tightened, more sophisticated techniques emerged: "mirror trades," in which Russian clients would simultaneously buy and sell the same securities through different jurisdictions, effectively converting rubles to dollars while obscuring the transaction's origins; the use of complex chains of shell companies that made it impossible to trace the beneficial owner of funds; and the investment of criminal proceeds in real estate in London, Monaco, Miami, and other high-value markets, where the purchase of property through offshore corporate structures provided a legal veneer for criminal wealth.

The real estate dimension of Russian money laundering proved particularly significant and particularly difficult for Western governments to address. Major property purchases in London's prime real estate market, in luxury developments in Manhattan, in Florida condominiums, and in other high-value markets across the Western world were made through corporate structures that concealed the identities of their ultimate owners. Investigations by journalists and researchers, as well as government inquiries conducted particularly in the United Kingdom, revealed that significant quantities of Russian money of uncertain, and in many cases clearly criminal, origin had been invested in Western real estate, with the knowledge or at least the willful ignorance of the lawyers, real estate agents, and financial intermediaries who facilitated the transactions.

Drug Trafficking, Arms Dealing, and Human Trafficking

Beyond money laundering and financial crime, Russian organized crime networks became significant players in the global trade in drugs, weapons, and people. The geographic position of Russia and the former Soviet states, spanning the space between major drug-producing regions and major drug-consuming markets, created natural opportunities for involvement in the heroin trade. Afghan heroin, which dominates the European market, travels through Central Asia and Russia in quantities that make Russian criminal networks unavoidable intermediaries in the supply chain. The Balkan drug route, which carries a substantial portion of Afghan heroin into Western Europe, involves criminal networks from across the former Soviet space as well as from the Balkan countries examined in Chapter 20 of this textbook.

Arms trafficking was another area in which the collapse of the Soviet Union created extraordinary criminal opportunities. The Soviet military had maintained enormous stockpiles of weapons across a territory spanning eleven time zones, and the chaos of the transition period left these stockpiles in the custody of individuals and institutions whose loyalty, motivation, and capacity for honest custodianship were severely compromised by the surrounding economic and political disorder. Small arms, ammunition, anti-aircraft missiles, explosives, and even larger weapons systems found their way into criminal markets across the world, with Russian and former Soviet criminal networks serving as brokers and suppliers. The most notorious figure in this trade was Viktor Bout, a former Soviet military officer who built a global arms trafficking network using aircraft registered in multiple countries to deliver weapons to conflict zones in Africa, Asia, and elsewhere, before his arrest and extradition to the United States in 2010.

Human trafficking was a third major area of Russian criminal international operations. The collapse of the Soviet Union created millions of economically desperate people in the former Soviet states, many of them young women with limited economic opportunities at home and vulnerable to the promises and deceptions of traffickers. Russian and Ukrainian criminal networks built extensive operations trafficking women into the sex trade across Europe, the Middle East, and beyond, recruiting victims through false promises of legitimate employment, transporting them across borders through networks of corrupt officials and criminal contacts, and controlling them through debt bondage, violence, and the confiscation of identity documents. The scale of this traffic, while difficult to measure precisely, was sufficient to make former Soviet women a significant presence in the sex industries of multiple European countries by the late 1990s.

Cybercrime: The Next Frontier

As the internet developed and expanded in the late 1990s and 2000s, Russian criminal networks were among the earliest and most sophisticated adopters of cyber methods for criminal purposes. Russia's unusually high concentration of technical talent, the product of the Soviet emphasis on mathematics and engineering education, combined with the limited legitimate economic opportunities available to that talent in the chaotic post-Soviet economy, created ideal conditions for the development of criminal hacking culture. The early Russian cybercrime scene was characterized by brilliant technical innovation in service of criminal ends: credit card fraud, the theft of financial account credentials, the creation and sale of malware, and the operation of botnets that could be used for denial-of-service attacks or spam campaigns.

By the 2000s and 2010s, Russian cybercriminal organizations had evolved into sophisticated enterprises with professional structures, division of labor, and global customer bases. Groups such as the Russian Business Network, a criminal hosting provider that provided infrastructure for a wide range of malicious online activities, and later ransomware gangs such as Fancy Bear's criminal associates (distinct from the state-linked hacking groups sharing similar names) operated at a scale and sophistication that exceeded any other national criminal tradition's cyber capabilities. The relationships between these criminal cyber organizations and Russian state intelligence agencies were murky but clearly significant: state intelligence services recruited criminal hackers for operations when they needed deniable technical capabilities, tolerated criminal cyber operations that did not target Russian interests, and in some cases provided protection for criminal hackers who cooperated with state requests.

Historical Debate

Is Russia a "Mafia State"? The Debate Over State-Crime Relations Under Putin

The question: Since Vladimir Putin came to power in 2000, scholars, journalists, and policy makers have debated the nature of the relationship between the Russian state and organized crime. Some argue that Putin effectively subdued organized crime and restored state authority. Others argue that he did not eliminate the fusion of crime and power but reorganized it in ways that strengthened the state's control over criminal enterprise. A third position, advanced most forcefully by scholars such as Karen Dawisha, holds that the Russian state under Putin is itself best understood as a criminal enterprise, or "mafia state," in which the distinction between the state and organized crime has effectively collapsed.

Interpretation A — Putin Restored Order: Proponents of this view point to the measurable decline in contract killings, the subordination or exile of the most politically independent oligarchs, and the reassertion of state authority over key economic sectors as evidence that Putin genuinely reduced the criminality of the Russian political economy. On this reading, the chaos of the 1990s was the product of state weakness, and Putin's consolidation of authority represented a restoration of legitimate governance, however authoritarian its methods. Advocates of this position sometimes note that ordinary Russians did experience improved physical security and economic conditions during the early Putin years, suggesting that something recognizable as governance was occurring.
Interpretation B — Managed Crime, Not Eliminated Crime: A second interpretation holds that Putin did not defeat organized crime but rather restructured its relationship with the state. On this reading, criminal entrepreneurs who accepted political subordination and shared their proceeds with the right patrons were allowed to continue operating, while those who sought political independence or failed to share appropriately were destroyed. The result was not a state that had overcome criminal enterprise but a state in which criminal enterprise was managed from the top, with criminal methods normalized as instruments of state policy and state officials enriched through criminal means.
Interpretation C — The Mafia State: The most radical interpretation, advanced by Karen Dawisha in her book Putin's Kleptocracy and by investigative journalists such as Misha Glenny and Luke Harding, holds that the Russian state under Putin is itself best understood as an organized criminal enterprise that has captured the legal forms of a state. On this reading, the goal of the Putin system is not governance in any conventional sense but the extraction of wealth from the Russian economy and its transfer to a small group of individuals connected to Putin, using state institutions as instruments of coercion and protection. The distinction between the state and organized crime, on this reading, does not exist in any meaningful way.

Evidence: All three interpretations draw on real evidence. The decline in visible criminal violence under Putin supports the first. The fate of oligarchs who maintained loyalty while those who challenged were destroyed supports the second. The documented wealth of Putin and his close associates, far exceeding their official incomes, and the use of state institutions against political opponents while protecting criminal allies supports the third. The debate cannot be resolved without access to information that the Russian state closely guards.

Why it matters: How we characterize the Russian state has major implications for policy. If Russia is simply a state with an organized crime problem, conventional diplomatic and law enforcement cooperation may be the right response. If Russia is a state that manages crime for its own benefit, different approaches are required. If Russia is itself a criminal organization wearing the garb of a state, the implications for international relations, sanctions policy, and democratic security are far more serious.

Multiple Perspectives

The 1990s Transition: How Different Groups Experienced the Rise of Criminal Power

From the perspective of ordinary Russian citizens: The 1990s were a period of devastating economic insecurity. Savings were wiped out by hyperinflation. Wages went unpaid for months. Social services collapsed. Crime, including violent crime against ordinary people, rose sharply. For many Russians, the criminal organizations and oligarchs who emerged from the transition period were associated with the theft of public wealth and the destruction of the modest security that Soviet life had provided. The nostalgia for Soviet stability that became a significant factor in Russian politics after 2000 reflected, in part, genuine suffering during the transition period.
From the perspective of Western governments and advisers: In the early 1990s, Western governments and the Western economists who advised the Russian government were largely optimistic about the transition. They believed that rapid privatization, even if imperfect, was preferable to slow privatization, because it would create a class of private owners with interests in defending property rights and stable institutions. The scale of criminal capture of the privatization process, and the degree to which the resulting property rights were themselves based on criminal methods, was largely underestimated. Western financial institutions that processed Russian criminal money were sometimes willfully ignorant and sometimes genuinely deceived.
From the perspective of Russian criminal entrepreneurs: For those positioned to exploit the transition, the 1990s were an extraordinary opportunity. The ability to convert political connections and the willingness to use violence into economic wealth, in a context where the rules were being made up as the system went along, created fortunes on a scale that would have been impossible in any stable economic environment. Many of the individuals who benefited from this process did not understand themselves as criminals: they understood themselves as entrepreneurs succeeding in a difficult environment through skill and initiative, using methods that were admittedly unconventional but that the system itself had made necessary.
From the perspective of law enforcement in Western countries: Western police and intelligence services confronted a phenomenon unlike anything in their previous experience. Russian criminal organizations did not conform to the organizational models derived from the study of Italian-American organized crime or Latin American drug cartels. They were more fluid, more financially sophisticated, more willing to operate through legitimate corporate structures, and more deeply entangled with state institutions than Western law enforcement frameworks were designed to address. The jurisdictional complexity of operations spanning dozens of countries, combined with the corruption of Russian law enforcement partners, made cooperative investigation extraordinarily difficult.
Constitutional Connection

Rule of Law, Property Rights, and the Conditions for Criminal Power

The rise of Russian organized crime offers a case study in what happens when the institutional foundations of a rule-of-law system are absent or severely weakened. Democratic theory, as developed in the Western constitutional tradition and as embedded in international human rights norms, holds that certain institutional conditions are necessary for both economic freedom and personal security: an independent judiciary capable of enforcing contracts and protecting property rights; a police and prosecutorial system that enforces laws without regard to the political or economic status of the parties; freedom of the press to investigate and expose corruption; and political pluralism that allows citizens to organize against abuses of power.

The post-Soviet Russian experience illustrates what happens when these conditions are absent. Without an independent judiciary, property rights were enforced through violence and political connection rather than law. Without an honest police force, criminal organizations could operate with impunity as long as they maintained the right relationships. Without a free press, corruption was shielded from public scrutiny. And without genuine political pluralism, there was no mechanism by which citizens could organize to demand better governance. The constitutional lesson of the Russian case is not simply that organized crime exploits weak institutions, which is true everywhere, but that the specific combination of formal state structures, informal criminal networks, and corrupted legal institutions that emerged in Russia created something qualitatively different from ordinary organized crime: a system in which criminal methods had become normalized as the operating logic of public life.

For students of constitutional development, the Russian case also raises questions about the relationship between economic transition and democratic consolidation. The assumption, widespread among Western advisers in the early 1990s, that market capitalism and democratic governance were natural companions that would develop together, was tested and, in the Russian case, falsified. The creation of a capitalist economy through criminal methods produced a class of economic actors with strong interests in preventing the development of the rule of law and democratic accountability, because those developments would threaten the illegitimate foundations of their wealth and power.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Russian Organized Crime from the Soviet Period to the Present

What Changed

  • The Soviet state's monopoly on violence and official economic activity gave way to a pluralism of coercive power that included criminal organizations competing with each other and with the state
  • Criminal entrepreneurs moved from operating entirely in the shadows to operating openly, sometimes in positions of recognized public status
  • Russian criminal networks expanded from domestic operations to global enterprises with operations across dozens of countries
  • The tools of criminal enterprise evolved from primarily physical coercion to increasingly sophisticated financial manipulation and, eventually, cybercrime
  • The scale of criminal wealth increased from the modest accumulations possible in the Soviet shadow economy to fortunes comparable to those of the world's wealthiest individuals
  • Russian criminal organizations developed international reputations and relationships with criminal organizations in other countries

What Stayed the Same

  • The fundamental reliance on political protection, whether from Soviet party officials or post-Soviet state actors, as the essential condition for criminal operation
  • The use of violence as a tool for resolving disputes and enforcing agreements in the absence of reliable legal mechanisms
  • The deep interpenetration of official and unofficial power, with the same individuals often operating simultaneously in both spheres
  • The culture of the vory, though adapted and modified, continued to provide cultural reference points and organizational frameworks for Russian criminal identity
  • The structural conditions that made informal exchange necessary, including corruption, bureaucratic unpredictability, and selective enforcement of law, persisted under different political arrangements
  • The impunity of well-connected criminal actors, whether protected by Communist Party membership or by connections to the FSB and the Kremlin

Why the pattern matters: The persistence of corruption and the fusion of state and criminal power across the dramatic political transition of 1991 illustrates that political change alone, even revolutionary political change, does not automatically transform the informal power structures and cultural norms that sustain criminal enterprise. Addressing organized crime requires more than changing the formal political system; it requires changing the informal practices and relationships that organized crime depends upon.

Everyday Life and Social History

Living in the Criminal Economy: Ordinary Russians in the 1990s

The rise of Russian organized crime in the 1990s was not simply an event in the lives of oligarchs and criminal bosses. It shaped the daily experience of ordinary Russian citizens in immediate and often devastating ways. Understanding those experiences is essential to understanding both what organized crime meant in human terms and why it proved so persistent.

For many Russians, the most immediate manifestation of the criminal economy was the experience of the workplace and the non-payment of wages. As enterprises struggled and the formal economy contracted, millions of workers went months without receiving their wages. Some were paid in goods, receiving the products their factories produced rather than money. Others were simply owed wages that were never paid, while managers and owners extracted what value remained from the enterprise. The informal economy became, for many people, not a supplement to regular income but the primary source of survival: growing food in urban gardens and dacha plots, trading goods in street markets, performing services in the informal economy, and relying on the networks of mutual support that had always characterized Soviet social life.

The street market, or rynok, became one of the defining institutions of 1990s Russia. In cities across the country, open-air markets appeared where people could buy and sell almost anything: food, clothing, consumer electronics, tools, books, medicines, and goods whose provenance was often unclear. These markets were typically controlled by one or another criminal organization, which collected fees from vendors, provided protection from other criminal groups, and resolved disputes. For vendors, the choice between paying protection money and facing violence was not really a choice at all. For customers, the markets provided access to goods that the formal retail economy could not supply. The criminal organizations that controlled the markets were, from the perspective of the people who used them, providing a real service, even if the methods they used to maintain their position were violent and predatory.

Housing was another dimension of ordinary life deeply shaped by criminal power in the 1990s. The Soviet system had provided housing through a bureaucratic allocation system that, for all its inefficiencies, had given most urban families stable accommodation. When the housing market was privatized and ordinary citizens received vouchers entitling them to purchase their apartments, criminal entrepreneurs used a variety of fraudulent methods to extract housing from vulnerable people: targeting elderly residents who could be deceived into signing over their apartments, using corrupt notaries and registration officials to forge property documents, and employing violence or threats to compel residents to vacate properties that were then sold for profit. The crime of "apartment raiding," reiderstovo in Russian, became a significant phenomenon in 1990s and 2000s Russia, and its victims were disproportionately the elderly, the poorly educated, and those without the social connections needed to obtain protection.

For young men in particular, the criminal economy offered a parallel opportunity structure. Legitimate employment opportunities contracted sharply during the transition, while criminal organizations offered income, status, and a form of belonging that the disoriented post-Soviet society could not provide through conventional channels. The recruitment of young men into criminal organizations, often beginning with sports clubs and neighborhood networks that provided legitimate community before gradually drawing members into criminal activity, was a significant social phenomenon of the 1990s, and it created a generational cohort of men socialized into the values and practices of criminal enterprise at a formative stage.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has traced the history of Russian organized crime from its Soviet-era origins through the chaotic 1990s and into the twenty-first century, revealing a story that is at once distinctively Russian and broadly illustrative of larger patterns in the relationship between organized crime, state power, and economic transformation.

The Soviet Union did not simply tolerate organized crime. It created the conditions for it. Seven decades of command economy produced chronic shortages that made informal exchange structurally necessary, creating extensive networks of unofficial economic activity that blurred the line between the legal and the illegal in ways that would prove consequential when the system collapsed. The vory v zakone culture that developed within the Gulag created a criminal subculture with its own social institutions, value systems, and organizational structures, providing both a cultural template and a practical model for post-Soviet criminal organization. The institutionalized corruption of the Brezhnev era deepened the interpenetration of official and unofficial power, training an entire generation of Soviet officials in the use of their positions for private enrichment.

The collapse of the Soviet Union in 1991 created what this chapter has called a "criminogenic environment," conditions specifically favorable to criminal enterprise on an extraordinary scale. The elimination of the state's monopoly on violence, the rapid privatization process that transferred enormous public assets to private hands at prices far below market value, the demoralization and corruption of law enforcement, and the availability of offshore financial systems that could absorb criminal proceeds all combined to produce one of the most dramatic expansions of criminal power in modern history. Within a decade, criminal organizations had seized control of significant sectors of the Russian economy, established protection rackets across the business landscape, eliminated competitors through contract killings, and built the organizational foundations for international operations.

The relationship between organized crime and the Russian state was never simple opposition. From the earliest years of the transition, criminal organizations and state actors were intertwined in relationships of mutual benefit, shared corruption, and overlapping personnel. The oligarchs of the Yeltsin era accumulated their wealth through mechanisms that were simultaneously formally legal and functionally criminal, made possible by their political connections and their willingness to use coercive methods. Figures such as Semyon Mogilevich operated at the intersection of criminal enterprise and intelligence service connections in ways that made the application of conventional categories of crime and state difficult.

The Putin era did not end the relationship between the Russian state and organized crime. It restructured it, replacing the chaotic competition of the 1990s with a more organized system in which criminal enterprise was tolerated and managed by state actors in exchange for political loyalty and a share of the proceeds. Whether this system should be understood as a strong state that has brought criminal enterprise under control or as a criminal enterprise that has assumed the forms of statehood remains one of the most important and most contested questions in the study of contemporary Russia.

Russian criminal networks extended their operations internationally with a speed and geographic range that transformed organized crime from a Russian domestic problem into a global challenge. Money laundering through Western banking and financial systems, real estate purchases in major Western cities, drug trafficking from Central Asia through Russia to European markets, arms dealing to conflict zones across the world, human trafficking from the former Soviet states into the sex industries of Western countries, and cybercrime against targets on multiple continents all became significant features of Russian criminal international operations. The global reach of Russian organized crime made it not simply a problem for Russian law enforcement but a challenge for the international system as a whole.

Several broader themes emerge from this history. First, the distinctiveness of the Russian case, the specific combination of Soviet institutional legacy, the particular character of the post-Soviet transition, and the specific features of Putin's consolidation of power, should not obscure the general patterns that Russian organized crime shares with criminal enterprises in other settings. The reliance on political protection, the use of violence in the absence of reliable legal mechanisms, the exploitation of economic transition periods, and the adaptation to new technologies and new markets are all features that Russian organized crime shares with the Sicilian Mafia, the Colombian cartels, and criminal organizations in many other settings.

Second, the Russian case powerfully illustrates the thesis that organized crime is not simply a product of poverty or social deprivation, though both can contribute. It is fundamentally a product of institutional conditions: the absence of reliable legal mechanisms for enforcing contracts and protecting property; the corruption of the officials who are supposed to enforce those mechanisms; the weakness of civil society institutions that might otherwise constrain criminal power; and the availability of markets, legal and illegal, that criminal organizations can exploit. Where those conditions exist, organized crime tends to flourish, regardless of the specific cultural or national context.

Third, the history of Russian organized crime is inseparable from the broader history of globalization. Russian criminal networks became global enterprises because the same processes of financial integration, trade liberalization, and digital connectivity that created the global economy also created opportunities for criminal exploitation at a global scale. Understanding Russian organized crime therefore requires understanding it not just as a Russian phenomenon but as one expression of the relationship between globalization and criminal enterprise that is a defining feature of the contemporary world.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Vory v Zakone
The "thieves in law," formally inducted members of the Soviet and post-Soviet criminal elite, defined by adherence to a strict code forbidding cooperation with the state.
Vorovskoy Zakon
The "thieves' code," the elaborate set of rules governing conduct within the vory v zakone brotherhood, including prohibitions on cooperating with state authority.
Bratva
Russian criminal "brotherhood," used to describe the criminal organizations of the post-Soviet period that often departed from the strict vory code while drawing on its cultural prestige.
Gulag
The Soviet system of forced labor camps, site of the development of the vory criminal subculture and a defining institution of Stalinist repression.
Tenevaya Ekonomika
The "shadow economy," the extensive informal and illegal economic activity that supplemented and in many ways sustained the Soviet command economy.
Blat
The use of personal connections and reciprocal favor exchange to obtain goods and services unavailable through official Soviet channels; the essential social technology of everyday Soviet economic life.
Obshchak
The communal criminal fund maintained by vory networks, used to support imprisoned members, bribe officials, and finance criminal operations.
Nomenklatura
The Soviet party elite whose control over official positions gave them unique advantages in profiting from the privatization process.
Perestroika
Gorbachev's program of economic "restructuring," which unintentionally created new opportunities for criminal entrepreneurs through partial liberalization of the economy.
Glasnost
Gorbachev's policy of political "openness," which reduced some forms of state control while contributing to the destabilization of Soviet institutions.
Krysha
Literally "roof," the protection service provided to businesses by criminal organizations or corrupt officials in the post-Soviet period, essential to operation in an environment without reliable legal protection.
Zakaznye Ubiystva
Contract killings that peaked in Russia in the mid-1990s as a mechanism for resolving business disputes and eliminating competitors in the absence of reliable legal mechanisms.
Oligarch
An individual who accumulated extraordinary wealth during the post-Soviet privatization process through political connections, insider information, and criminal coercion.
Loans-for-Shares
The 1995–1996 scheme by which private banks lent money to the Russian government in exchange for shares in major state enterprises, transferring control of enormous assets to a small group of insiders at below-market prices.
Shock Therapy
The rapid transition to market capitalism advocated by Western advisers, involving rapid privatization and price liberalization, which created the conditions for criminal capture of the privatization process.
Siloviki
Veterans of the Soviet and Russian security and military services who moved into positions of political and economic power under Putin, bringing the organizational culture of the security apparatus with them.
Capital Flight
The transfer of wealth out of Russia through legal and illegal means, accelerated by the financial crisis of 1998 and channeled through a variety of offshore financial mechanisms.
Mirror Trade
A money laundering technique in which the same securities are simultaneously bought and sold in different currencies through different jurisdictions, effectively converting criminal proceeds from one currency to another while obscuring the transaction's origins.
State Capture
The phenomenon in which private interests gain sufficient influence over state institutions to redirect those institutions toward their own ends; a defining feature of both the Yeltsin and Putin eras in different forms.
Avtoritety
Criminal "authorities," the senior figures in post-Soviet criminal organizations who combined the cultural prestige of the vory tradition with the practical methods of the new criminal economy.
Tsekhoviki
Soviet-era underground entrepreneurs who operated private production facilities within official enterprises, accumulating capital that would later fuel post-Soviet criminal enterprise.
Asset Stripping
The extraction of value from a formerly state-owned enterprise through sale of assets, diversion of cash flows, and other methods that benefit insiders at the expense of the enterprise and its nominal owners.
Criminal State
An analytical concept applied to the Russian state under Putin by scholars who argue that the distinction between state authority and criminal enterprise has effectively collapsed in Russia.

Chapter Reflection

The history of Russian organized crime is, in the end, a history about institutions, about what happens when the informal rules and power structures that actually govern social life diverge catastrophically from the formal rules and institutions that are supposed to do so. The Soviet Union spent seventy years building an informal economy of connections, corruption, and unofficial exchange that supplemented and sustained an official economy that was structurally unable to meet people's needs. When the official structures collapsed, those informal networks did not collapse with them. They adapted, expanded, and in many cases emerged as the dominant organizing forces in a society that had lost its formal institutional framework. The lesson is not that informal economies are inherently criminal, but that when a society's informal networks are built on corruption and coercion rather than trust and reciprocity, the transition from formal to informal governance produces criminal power rather than social resilience.

The Russian case also illustrates the inadequacy of the assumption, widely held in the early 1990s, that markets and democracy would naturally develop together in post-Soviet societies. The creation of capitalism through criminal methods produced a class of economic actors with powerful interests in preventing the development of the institutions, independent courts, free press, genuine political competition, honest law enforcement, that would threaten the illegitimate foundations of their wealth. Organized crime and criminal oligarchic power did not simply operate in the absence of institutions; they actively worked to prevent institutions from developing. The path from the Soviet system to a genuine rule-of-law state, if such a path exists for Russia, is therefore blocked not just by the legacy of Soviet institutions but by the active resistance of interests that criminal transition created.

The global reach of Russian organized crime connects this chapter to the broader themes of this textbook. Organized crime is not simply a local or national phenomenon. It is a global institution that exploits the opportunities created by economic integration, financial globalization, and the gaps between national legal systems. Russian criminal networks were not simply more criminal than other criminal organizations; they were more skillful at exploiting the specific opportunities that the global financial system offered, and they were supported, in ways that other criminal organizations were not, by the resources and protection of a major state. The result was a form of organized crime that was genuinely novel in its scale, its sophistication, and its political entanglements, and that created challenges for which existing frameworks of law enforcement and international cooperation were inadequate.

For students of the broader history of organized crime, the Russian case raises questions that extend far beyond Russia. It asks us to think about the conditions under which criminal organizations can capture states rather than simply operating within them. It asks us to think about the relationship between economic transition and criminal power, and about whether the specific pattern that emerged in Russia was inevitable or was the product of specific choices that might have been made differently. And it asks us to think about the role of Western institutions, banks, law firms, real estate markets, and governments, in enabling and sustaining the criminal wealth that the post-Soviet transition produced. The history of Russian organized crime is not simply the history of Russian criminals. It is also the history of the international environment that made their operations possible, a history in which Western societies were participants as well as victims.

As this textbook moves forward to examine other aspects of organized crime in the contemporary world, including drug cartels, cybercrime networks, and the global infrastructure of money laundering, the Russian case provides essential context. It demonstrates that organized crime is not a static phenomenon but a dynamic institution that evolves alongside the states, economies, and technologies that shape its environment. It demonstrates that the relationship between criminal organizations and state power is never simple: sometimes crime exploits weak states, sometimes strong states use criminal methods, and sometimes the distinction between the two becomes so blurred that maintaining it requires more analytical care than conventional categories can provide. And it demonstrates that understanding organized crime requires understanding the broader social, economic, and political conditions that make it possible, because those conditions are always and everywhere the deeper story.

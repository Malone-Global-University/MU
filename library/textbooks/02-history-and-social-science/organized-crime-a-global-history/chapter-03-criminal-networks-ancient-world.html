Part II — The Origins of Organized Crime

Criminal Networks in the Ancient World

Organized Crime: A Global History

Chapter Opening

"The pirates held the entire sea and made it unnavigable. They terrorized the coasts of Italy and plundered the temples and sanctuaries. Their power had grown to such a degree that it was no longer possible to have any maritime commerce."

— Plutarch, Life of Pompey, describing Mediterranean piracy in the first century BCE

In the summer of 75 BCE, a young Roman nobleman named Julius Caesar was sailing from Rome toward the island of Rhodes, where he planned to study rhetoric. Somewhere in the Aegean Sea, pirates intercepted his vessel and seized him. The pirates set their ransom demand at twenty talents of silver, a substantial sum. Caesar, according to later accounts, laughed at the figure and told his captors they had clearly no idea who he was, because he was worth at least fifty. He dispatched his companions to raise the money, spent thirty-eight days as a captive, played games with his jailers, wrote poetry, and periodically reminded his hosts that once he was freed he would return and have them all crucified. When the ransom arrived and he was released, Caesar did exactly that. He organized a small fleet, tracked down the pirates who had held him, and had them crucified on a beach along the Anatolian coast.

The story is memorable, and its details may have been embellished over generations of retelling. But the situation it describes was entirely real. By the first century BCE, pirates operating out of bases along the Cilician coast of modern Turkey had built what contemporaries described as a vast criminal organization, complete with fleets of hundreds of ships, fortified harbors, communication networks spanning the entire Mediterranean basin, and commercial relationships with merchants, slave dealers, and even Roman politicians willing to buy stolen goods and trafficked human beings. The pirates were not simply thieves at sea. They were, in the language of this textbook, a criminal enterprise, organized for profit, operating across political boundaries, and making deliberate use of violence, bribery, and systemic evasion of state authority.

They were also not unique in the ancient world. Criminal networks, organized for profit and operating outside or alongside state authority, appear in the earliest records of complex human societies. The merchant cities of Bronze Age Mesopotamia recorded crimes against commercial property and the theft of goods in transit. Egyptian papyri describe networks of tomb robbers who plundered royal burial sites over multiple generations, apparently with the knowledge and connivance of local officials who shared the proceeds. Greek city-states passed laws against smugglers who evaded customs duties, against protection racketeers who preyed on merchants at market days and festivals, and against gangs who monopolized the sale of stolen slaves. Roman legal texts, court speeches, and historians' accounts document an elaborate world of organized theft, fraud, illegal banking, contract killing, and the systematic corruption of officials and judges.

This chapter examines criminal networks in the ancient world, focusing primarily on the Mediterranean civilizations of Greece and Rome, with attention to relevant parallels in the ancient Near East, Egypt, and the wider world of long-distance trade. Its purpose is not to catalog every crime committed in antiquity, which would be impossible, but to identify and analyze the organized, institutional dimensions of ancient criminal activity, those that involved coordination among multiple actors, systematic exploitation of markets and trade routes, deliberate corruption of official authority, and sustained operation over time. These are the dimensions that connect ancient criminal networks to the criminal enterprises examined throughout this textbook.

The chapter also addresses a question central to the broader themes of this textbook: what conditions produce organized crime? Ancient evidence suggests several answers that will recur across every subsequent period of history. Organized crime flourished where states were weak or their authority was fragmented across competing jurisdictions. It flourished where profitable legal markets created equally profitable opportunities for illegal alternatives, whether smuggling to evade taxes, selling stolen goods to eager buyers, or offering protection to merchants whom the state could not adequately defend. It flourished where inequalities of wealth and power created both the demand for illegal services and a large supply of people with reason to operate outside the law. And it flourished, almost invariably, where officials could be bribed, where legal distinctions between legitimate and illegitimate commerce were blurred, and where the boundaries between state power and criminal enterprise were less clear than they might appear.

Understanding ancient criminal networks also requires confronting what we do not and cannot know. The evidence is fragmentary, distorted by the perspectives of elite authors who wrote about crime from a position of power, and shaped by the legal and moral frameworks of societies very different from our own. Ancient peoples did not use the term "organized crime," and the legal categories they applied to criminal behavior often differ substantially from modern legal concepts. A Roman senator who accepted stolen goods from a pirate contact operated in a gray zone that Romans themselves recognized as legally and morally ambiguous. A merchant who smuggled goods to avoid customs duties might be prosecuted or might be quietly ignored, depending on the political climate and the connections of the parties involved. The historian must read ancient evidence carefully, recognizing both what it reveals about organized criminal activity and what it conceals or distorts.

Despite these limitations, the picture that emerges is striking. The ancient world had criminal networks that were, in their organizational sophistication, their exploitation of markets, their use of violence and corruption, and their complex relationship with state authority, recognizably similar in important ways to the organized criminal enterprises of later centuries. The specific forms changed, the scale changed, the technology changed, but the fundamental dynamics, the exploitation of prohibition, the commodification of protection, the corruption of officials, the organization of violence for profit, were already present in the ancient world, embedded in the very structures of complex commercial society. This chapter traces those dynamics across piracy, smuggling, banditry, protection rackets, and official corruption, while examining what ancient evidence can and cannot tell us about the origins of organized crime as a recurring feature of human society.

  4. Analyze the phenomenon of banditry in the ancient world, distinguishing between opportunistic individual crime and organized bandit kingdoms with political and economic dimensions.

Historical Context

The ancient Mediterranean world of the first millennium BCE and the early centuries of the Common Era was one of the most commercially dynamic environments in premodern history. Greek city-states, Phoenician trading colonies, Carthaginian commercial networks, Hellenistic kingdoms, and ultimately the Roman Empire all participated in a vast system of long-distance trade that connected the tin mines of Britain to the spice routes of Arabia, the grain fields of Egypt to the luxury goods workshops of the eastern Mediterranean. Ships crossed open water carrying grain, wine, olive oil, timber, metals, textiles, pottery, and hundreds of other commodities. Overland caravans moved across Anatolia, Syria, and North Africa. Merchants operated across political boundaries, maintaining commercial relationships that transcended the wars and political upheavals of individual states.

This commercial dynamism was also the foundation of ancient organized crime. Every profitable trade route was also a potential target for piracy, banditry, and extortion. Every state that taxed commerce created an incentive to evade those taxes through smuggling. Every merchant who accumulated wealth in a poorly policed environment faced pressure to pay for protection, whether from the state, from private strongmen, or from criminal organizations that offered security in exchange for tribute. Every official entrusted with enforcing commercial regulations faced the temptation of accepting bribes to look the other way.

The political geography of the ancient Mediterranean compounded these dynamics. For most of antiquity, the Mediterranean basin was divided among dozens of competing political entities, from the great empires of Persia, Macedonia, and Rome to small city-states, tribal confederacies, and semi-autonomous kingdoms. This fragmentation of political authority created numerous jurisdictional gaps that criminal operators could exploit. A pirate fleet based in territory beyond the reach of any particular state could operate with relative impunity. Goods smuggled across borders between competing states were difficult to trace. Bandits who operated in the borderlands between rival kingdoms could evade the forces of either. Even within large empires like Rome, the difficulty of communication and enforcement across vast distances meant that local criminal operators frequently enjoyed practical immunity from central authority.

The social structure of ancient Mediterranean societies also mattered. Slavery was fundamental to ancient economies, and the trade in human beings, both licit and illicit, created vast opportunities for criminal entrepreneurs. Wars produced enormous numbers of prisoners who could be enslaved and sold. Piracy produced captured civilians who could be trafficked to slave markets. The demand for slaves was enormous and consistent, and the legal status of slaves, as property rather than persons, meant that their abduction and sale generated relatively little legal scrutiny compared to the theft of free persons. The slave trade and the piracy that fed it were so intertwined that ancient writers sometimes treated them as synonymous.

Economic inequality within ancient societies also shaped the criminal landscape. In the cities and countryside of Greece, Rome, and the Hellenistic kingdoms, enormous disparities of wealth created populations of desperately poor people with little to lose by engaging in criminal activity, as well as populations of wealthy elites with both the resources to purchase criminal services and the political connections to avoid prosecution. The impoverished freemen of Roman cities, the landless agricultural laborers of the Greek countryside, the displaced veterans of Hellenistic wars, all constituted potential recruits for criminal enterprises. The wealthiest men in Roman society, meanwhile, were sometimes directly implicated in criminal networks as investors, customers, and political protectors.

Historians reconstructing ancient criminal networks face significant evidentiary challenges. Most of what we know comes from literary sources, primarily the works of Greek and Roman authors who were members of the educated elite and who wrote about crime and criminals from a perspective shaped by their class position, their moral assumptions, and their literary purposes. Cicero's court speeches discuss criminal networks in the service of rhetorical advocacy, not historical documentation. Plutarch's biographies highlight dramatic episodes of piracy and banditry to illuminate the characters of great men. Strabo's geographical writings describe criminal regions as curiosities or dangers for travelers, not as phenomena to be analyzed systematically. Each of these sources tells us something valuable, but each also distorts in its own way. Legal texts, inscriptions, papyri, and archaeological evidence can supplement and sometimes correct the literary record, but the overall picture remains partial and incomplete.

With these limitations in mind, this chapter traces the major forms of organized criminal activity in the ancient world, focusing on the Mediterranean basin while drawing where appropriate on evidence from the ancient Near East, Egypt, and the wider world of Eurasian trade. The goal is not to claim that ancient criminal networks were identical to modern organized crime, which would be anachronistic, but to identify the organizational features, the exploitation of markets, the use of violence and corruption, and the relationship with state authority, that make ancient criminal networks recognizable predecessors of the criminal enterprises examined throughout this textbook.

Major People, Groups, and Places

Reference — People, Groups & Places

Cilician Pirates
A loose confederation of pirate fleets operating primarily from bases along the rugged coast of Cilicia in southern Anatolia (modern Turkey) from roughly the second century BCE until Pompey's campaign in 67 BCE. Their operations extended across the entire Mediterranean and encompassed piracy, slave trafficking, and protection rackets at a scale that disrupted Roman grain supplies and prompted emergency legislative action.
Gnaeus Pompeius (Pompey the Great)
Roman military commander who received extraordinary authority over the entire Mediterranean in 67 BCE specifically to suppress the Cilician pirates. His rapid and methodical campaign, which essentially cleared the sea of organized piracy within three months, provides one of history's earliest documented examples of a state mobilizing overwhelming resources against a transnational criminal network.
Gaius Verres
Roman magistrate who served as governor of Sicily from 73 to 71 BCE and who was prosecuted by Cicero for organizing a systematic criminal enterprise that looted art objects, extorted merchants, and corrupted local officials. The Verrine case is one of the most detailed surviving documents of how official corruption functioned as organized crime in the ancient world.
Marcus Tullius Cicero
Roman orator, lawyer, and statesman (106–43 BCE) whose court speeches and letters provide extensive evidence about criminal networks, corruption, and the relationship between crime and politics in the late Roman Republic. His prosecution of Verres and his writings about piracy are major primary sources for this chapter.
Julius Caesar
Roman statesman (100–44 BCE) whose kidnapping by Cilician pirates in 75 BCE provides a famous illustration of the scale and audacity of ancient maritime criminal networks. Caesar's later political career intersected with questions of piracy, corruption, and the relationship between criminal enterprise and state power.
The Delos Slave Market
The Aegean island of Delos functioned as the largest slave market in the ancient Mediterranean, reportedly capable of processing ten thousand slaves per day at its peak. It became a critical node in the criminal supply chain that connected pirate raids with commercial slave buyers, illustrating how legal markets for goods (slaves) created demand for illegally obtained supply (raid victims).
Strabo
Greek geographer (c. 64 BCE–24 CE) whose Geography describes criminal regions, bandit strongholds, and pirate coasts across the ancient world. His geographical perspective provides evidence about the spatial organization of ancient criminal networks that complements the legal and biographical sources.
Appian of Alexandria
Greek historian (c. 95–165 CE) whose accounts of Pompey's campaign against the pirates provide important evidence about the organizational structure and political dimensions of Cilician piracy.
The Cilician Coast
The rugged, heavily forested coastline of southern Anatolia (modern Turkey's Mediterranean coast), dotted with natural harbors and difficult of access by land. Its geography made it an ideal base for pirate operations that the fragmenting Hellenistic kingdoms were unable or unwilling to suppress.
Rhodes
The island city-state of Rhodes maintained a powerful navy that suppressed piracy in the eastern Aegean for much of the Hellenistic period. Its decline as a naval power in the second century BCE, following Roman political pressure, contributed directly to the expansion of Cilician piracy by removing the only effective maritime police force in the region.
Tomb Robbers of Deir el-Medina
Archaeological and papyrological evidence from ancient Egypt reveals that the workers who built the royal tombs in the Valley of the Kings organized systematic plundering operations across multiple generations, apparently with the knowledge and protection of local officials. Their networks represent one of the earliest documented examples of organized criminal activity exploiting insider knowledge and official corruption.

Section 3.1

Ancient Piracy: Crime at Sea

Piracy is among the oldest forms of organized criminal enterprise, and the Mediterranean Sea was its most sustained ancient theater. From the earliest Bronze Age trading voyages to the twilight of the Roman Empire, organized predation at sea was a constant feature of Mediterranean commercial life, sometimes suppressed, sometimes tolerated, and sometimes so powerful that it threatened the stability of states themselves. Understanding ancient piracy requires grasping both its organizational dimensions, the features that made it something more than random violence, and its relationship to the broader political and commercial structures of the ancient world.

The Origins of Maritime Crime

The earliest historical records of Mediterranean trade already contain evidence of maritime predation. Bronze Age texts from Ugarit, a major trading city on the Syrian coast, describe raids by sea-borne attackers in the late second millennium BCE. Egyptian records from the same period refer to the "Sea Peoples," groups of maritime raiders who struck the Egyptian coast and the Levantine trading cities with sufficient force and organization to be treated as a geopolitical threat rather than merely a criminal nuisance. The precise identity of the Sea Peoples remains debated among historians, and they may have been as much migrants and mercenaries as pirates, but their activities illustrate the ancient reality that control of the sea was never guaranteed and that maritime commerce always existed under the shadow of predation.

The Homeric epics, composed sometime in the eighth or seventh century BCE but reflecting a much older heroic tradition, treat sea raiding as a normal and even honorable activity for powerful men. In the Odyssey, Odysseus casually mentions having raided a town in Thrace on his way home from Troy, and heroes across the epics treat the seizure of goods and people at sea as a legitimate if risky enterprise. This is not simply literary fantasy. Greek historians such as Thucydides, writing in the fifth century BCE, recognized that in an earlier period the distinction between piracy and legitimate commerce was largely one of power, not morality. Those who could defend themselves traded; those who could not were raided. Thucydides argued that the poverty and insecurity of early Greek society made piracy a normal and honorable occupation before states became strong enough to suppress it.

The Greek term for piracy, peirateia, originally carried a more neutral connotation than its modern equivalent. Ancient Mediterranean societies distinguished between raids carried out against enemies, which could be sanctioned by political authority and classified as legitimate warfare or privateering, and raids against neutrals or allies, which were classified as criminal. But this distinction was difficult to maintain in practice, because the identities of friend and enemy shifted constantly with the political fortunes of competing city-states and kingdoms. A Rhodian merchant who was robbed at sea might find that the perpetrators were Cretan raiders who could claim political sanction from a city-state technically at war with Rhodes. The line between piracy and naval warfare was systematically blurred, a phenomenon that would recur throughout the history of maritime crime.

The Organizational Structure of Ancient Piracy

What made ancient piracy, at its most developed, something more than opportunistic predation was its organizational structure. The Cilician pirates of the first century BCE, who represent the fullest documented example of ancient pirate organization, are worth examining in some detail, because their operations illustrate the degree to which ancient maritime crime could achieve the characteristics of a genuine criminal enterprise.

The Cilician coast offered ideal conditions for organized piracy. The mountains of the Taurus range behind the coast made the region difficult to control by land. The numerous small bays and coves provided natural harbors for pirate fleets. The timber of the coastal forests supplied raw material for shipbuilding. And the political situation of the late Hellenistic period, in which the once-powerful Seleucid kingdom was in terminal decline and Rome had not yet established effective control over the eastern Mediterranean, left the region in a jurisdictional vacuum. No state had both the interest and the capacity to suppress the criminal networks that established themselves in this environment.

Contemporary accounts, particularly those of Plutarch and Appian, describe the Cilician pirate operation in terms that emphasize its organizational sophistication. The pirates maintained permanent bases with fortified harbors, storage facilities for goods and captives, and facilities for repairing and building ships. They operated fleets of hundreds of vessels, ranging from small fast galleys for interception to larger cargo-carrying ships for transporting slaves and goods. They maintained lookout systems to identify targets and warn of threats. They negotiated collectively with potential victims and with the merchants who bought their stolen goods. They maintained intelligence networks in the major ports of the Mediterranean, receiving information about valuable shipments and wealthy travelers who might be worth kidnapping for ransom.

The pirates also developed an elaborate financial infrastructure. Ransoming captured individuals was a major revenue stream, and the process required intermediaries, negotiators, and contacts in the cities where ransoms were collected. Selling captured persons as slaves required connections to slave markets, most importantly the enormous slave entrepôt on the island of Delos. Selling stolen cargoes required merchants willing to purchase goods of doubtful origin, which in turn required networks of distributors and buyers in the major commercial centers of the Mediterranean. These commercial relationships extended into the highest levels of Roman society. Plutarch explicitly states that many Roman merchants enriched themselves by trading with the pirates, and that some Roman officials were complicit in the pirate networks as customers or political protectors.

The scale of the operation was extraordinary. Plutarch claims that the Cilician pirates controlled over a thousand ships at their peak and had attacked and plundered more than four hundred cities. These figures are probably exaggerated, as ancient numerical claims almost always are, but they reflect a genuine reality of massive, sustained predation. The pirates disrupted Rome's grain supply, which depended heavily on shipments from Sicily, Sardinia, and Egypt. They raided the Italian coast within sight of the city of Rome itself, reportedly kidnapping two Roman praetors and their entourages from a road near Ostia, Rome's port. Their depredations became severe enough that the Roman government eventually felt compelled to take emergency action, an event discussed in detail in Section 3.1's account of Pompey's campaign.

Historical Vocabulary
Piracy

Piracy refers to robbery or violent predation committed at sea or from a maritime base. In legal terms, classical Roman law defined pirates (piratae) as hostes humani generis, "enemies of all mankind," a phrase that meant they had no legal standing and could be killed or punished by anyone who caught them without the need for a trial or formal legal process. This legal concept anticipated the modern principle of universal jurisdiction over pirates.

The distinction between piracy and privateering was crucial in the ancient world, as it would remain through the early modern period and beyond. A privateer was a private ship operator commissioned by a state to attack enemy shipping, effectively a licensed pirate. When states needed naval capacity they could not build themselves, or when they wished to harm an enemy without technically committing an act of war, sponsoring privateers was an attractive option. The Cilician pirates benefited at various points from relationships with Hellenistic kings and even Roman political figures who found their services useful, giving their operations a quasi-legitimate character that complicated suppression.

Modern historians disagree about how firmly to apply the label "piracy" to ancient maritime predation. Some argue that the concept is anachronistic applied to periods before the development of modern international maritime law. Others argue that ancient sources clearly distinguish between legitimate naval activity and criminal predation, and that this distinction reflects a genuine social reality even if the legal framework differed from modern norms. For the purposes of this textbook, piracy is defined as organized maritime predation primarily motivated by profit and operating outside the sanction of any recognized political authority.

The Political Economy of Ancient Piracy

Ancient piracy was not simply a problem of criminal behavior. It was embedded in the political economy of the Mediterranean world in ways that made it simultaneously profitable for many parties, destructive for many others, and genuinely difficult to suppress. Understanding this political economy is essential for understanding why piracy persisted for so long and on such a large scale.

The most important commercial dimension of ancient piracy was the slave trade. Slaves were the most valuable portable commodity in the ancient world, and the demand for them was enormous and consistent. Every large agricultural estate in Italy, every urban workshop in Athens or Corinth, every Roman household of any pretension needed slaves, and the supply of legally obtained slaves from warfare and voluntary self-sale was never quite sufficient to meet demand. Piracy filled the gap. A single successful raid on a coastal village or merchant vessel could produce dozens of captives who could be sold for handsome prices at Delos or one of the other major slave markets. The profit margins were high enough to attract sustained criminal investment, and the slave buyers, many of them entirely respectable Roman citizens and Greek merchants, provided both the market and, by their willingness to purchase, the practical legitimacy that kept the trade alive.

This relationship between piracy and the legal slave trade illustrates a pattern that will recur throughout this textbook: organized crime does not exist in isolation from legitimate commerce. It is embedded in it, supplying illegal goods and services to markets that legal commerce cannot or will not fully satisfy. The Roman senators who bought slaves from Delian merchants who bought them from Cilician pirates were participating in a supply chain that blurred the boundaries between criminal and legitimate activity at every step. The final buyer did not need to ask where his new farm workers came from. The merchant who sold them had no legal obligation to investigate. The pirate who had raided the village from which they came operated in a jurisdictional space that no state had yet claimed to police.

There were also political dimensions to the toleration of piracy that further complicated suppression. In the complex diplomatic world of the late Hellenistic period, various states found it useful to maintain relationships with pirate fleets that could harass their enemies' shipping while maintaining plausible deniability. The kings of Pontus, a kingdom in northern Anatolia, at various points either sponsored or tolerated Cilician piracy because it weakened their rivals. Individual Roman politicians found it useful to have relationships with pirate networks that could supply cheap slaves for their estates, funnel illicit profits into their campaign finances, or provide military muscle in the violent politics of the late Republic. The convergence of criminal profit with political interest was not incidental to the survival of Cilician piracy. It was foundational to it.

Pompey's Campaign and the Limits of State Response

The Roman response to the Cilician pirates in 67 BCE was one of the most decisive suppressions of a criminal network in ancient history, and it deserves careful examination both for what it achieved and for what it reveals about the relationship between state power and organized crime.

By the mid-first century BCE, Cilician piracy had become intolerable for Rome. The pirates had disrupted the grain supply to the point of causing food shortages in the city. They had raided the Appian Way, the main road connecting Rome to its southern coast. They had kidnapped Roman citizens of high rank, including the incident with Julius Caesar described in the chapter opening. Public pressure for action was intense. The Roman Senate responded by passing the Lex Gabinia in 67 BCE, which granted the general Gnaeus Pompeius, known to history as Pompey the Great, an extraordinary military command over the entire Mediterranean and all the land within fifty miles of the coast, for a period of three years.

The powers granted to Pompey were unprecedented in Roman law, and they tell us something important about what it took to suppress a geographically dispersed criminal network. Pompey received authority over all Roman provincial governors in the affected area, eliminating the jurisdictional fragmentation that had allowed pirates to operate in the gaps between competing authorities. He received an enormous fleet, reportedly five hundred ships, and an army of one hundred twenty thousand men. He received the financial resources to sustain this force. And he received unified command that allowed him to coordinate a systematic campaign across the entire Mediterranean simultaneously, rather than pursuing pirates in one area while they simply moved to another.

The campaign itself lasted only three months. Pompey divided the Mediterranean into thirteen sectors, assigned a fleet to each, and systematically swept them from west to east, driving the pirates into their eastern strongholds where his main force could engage them. The pirates, unable to evade, negotiate, or relocate, were defeated in a series of engagements, their strongholds were reduced, and their leaders were captured or surrendered. Pompey's settlement of the defeated pirates was notable for its relative leniency: rather than executing most of them, he resettled them in depopulated areas of Anatolia and Greece, attempting to convert them into settled farmers. Ancient sources report that this resettlement was surprisingly successful.

Pompey's campaign illustrates several principles about the suppression of organized criminal networks that will recur throughout this textbook. Effective suppression required resources commensurate with the scale of the problem. It required unified authority that could overcome jurisdictional fragmentation. It required systematic, coordinated action rather than piecemeal response. And it required addressing not just the criminal operators themselves but the social and economic conditions that had produced them, which is what Pompey's resettlement policy attempted, however imperfectly.

The campaign also illustrates the limits of state response. The conditions that had produced Cilician piracy, jurisdictional gaps, profitable slave markets, political tolerance, weak maritime policing, were not permanently eliminated by Pompey's military success. As Roman political order deteriorated during the civil wars of the following decades, piracy revived. It was never again as dramatic or as large-scale as the Cilician peak, but it persisted throughout the Roman period as a chronic low-level problem, contained but never eliminated. The criminal network had been disrupted, its leadership destroyed, its infrastructure dismantled. But the underlying conditions that made maritime predation attractive, a profitable slave market, jurisdictional gaps, willing buyers for stolen goods, had not been fundamentally altered. They would generate new criminal networks whenever political conditions created the necessary space.

Section 3.2

Smuggling in Greece and Rome: The Original Black Markets

Where states tax trade, merchants find ways to evade taxation. This principle, which Chapter 2 identified as one of the fundamental dynamics underlying organized crime, was as true in the ancient world as in any later period. The Greek city-states and the Roman Empire both levied substantial taxes and duties on commerce, both regulated what goods could be traded and where, and both found themselves in constant struggle against merchants, middlemen, and criminal networks that profited from circumventing those regulations. Understanding ancient smuggling requires understanding both the commercial regulations that created black market opportunities and the criminal networks that exploited them.

Athenian Commercial Regulation and Its Discontents

Athens in the fifth and fourth centuries BCE developed one of the most sophisticated commercial regulatory systems in the ancient world, and with it, one of the most documented early histories of commercial fraud and smuggling. The Athenian commercial system was built around the Piraeus, the city's great harbor, which served as the primary entrepôt for trade throughout the Aegean. All goods entering or leaving the Piraeus were subject to a two-percent customs duty, a relatively modest levy by later standards but significant enough to incentivize evasion when multiplied across large commercial transactions.

The most valuable regulated commodity in Athens was grain. Attica's agricultural land was insufficient to feed Athens's large population, making the city chronically dependent on imported grain, particularly from the Black Sea colonies and later from Egypt. The Athenian state recognized this dependency as a strategic vulnerability and responded with an elaborate system of grain trade regulations. Grain imported to Athens was required to be sold in Athens, not re-exported for profit elsewhere. Athenian citizens were prohibited from lending money to fund grain shipments to any destination other than the Piraeus. Officials known as sitophylakes (grain guardians) were charged with monitoring the grain market and preventing price manipulation and diversion of supply.

These regulations created systematic incentives for evasion. Merchants who brought grain to Athens and sold it could earn a reliable profit from a captive market. Merchants who instead diverted grain to other cities where prices might be higher, or who manipulated supply to drive up prices, could earn much larger profits, at the cost of violating Athenian law. Athenian court records, preserved in the speeches of fourth-century orators like Demosthenes and Lysias, document numerous prosecutions of grain merchants accused of exactly these offenses, diverting grain, creating artificial shortages, and operating rings to fix prices. These prosecutions reveal a world of organized commercial fraud in which multiple merchants cooperated to manipulate the grain market, sharing information about prices, coordinating their shipments, and apparently bribing officials to overlook violations.

The silver trade also generated organized criminal activity. Athens's silver mines at Laurium were among the most productive in the ancient world, and Athenian silver coinage, the famous "owls" with their image of the goddess Athena, served as the reserve currency of the ancient Mediterranean. The export of unminted silver was controlled, and attempts to debase or counterfeit Athenian coinage were treated as serious crimes. Yet the profit potential was substantial enough to attract systematic criminal effort. Athenian law required the presence of public slave officials at the Piraeus specifically to test incoming silver coins and detect counterfeits, suggesting that the problem was chronic enough to require institutionalized response.

Roman Tax Evasion and Smuggling Networks

The Roman Empire created the most extensive commercial regulatory system in the ancient world, and with it the most extensive opportunities for systematic tax evasion and smuggling. Roman customs duties were levied at the boundaries of provinces, at major ports, and at a series of internal toll stations maintained by tax-farming companies known as publicani. The rates varied by province and commodity, ranging from a two-and-a-half percent duty at some provincial borders to higher rates on luxury goods and specific commodities. The total fiscal burden on commerce was not catastrophically high by later historical standards, but it was consistent and comprehensive enough to make evasion profitable for anyone moving substantial quantities of goods.

The publicani system, under which the right to collect taxes in a given area was auctioned to private companies, created a particularly complex environment for commercial crime. The tax farmers who won these auctions needed to collect enough revenue to cover their investment and produce a profit, which gave them both the incentive and the practical authority to be aggressive in their assessments. Merchants who felt they were being overtaxed had little recourse, since the publicani operated with substantial legal protection. But the same system that encouraged aggressive collection also created opportunities for collusion: a merchant who bribed a publicanus official could have his goods assessed at a lower value, saving money on duties. The publicani companies were essentially private corporations with delegated state authority, and the line between legitimate business practice and extortion or fraud was genuinely ambiguous in many of their operations.

The trade in luxury goods, which the Roman Empire regulated with particular attention, generated some of the most sophisticated smuggling operations documented in ancient sources. Roman law imposed restrictions and heavy duties on a variety of luxury imports, particularly silk from China, spices and aromatics from Arabia and India, and various gemstones and precious materials from the east. These goods were enormously valuable in Roman markets, and the duties levied on them were correspondingly high. The incentive to smuggle them past customs posts was substantial. Strabo and other geographical writers describe smuggling routes through the deserts of Arabia and the mountains of Persia that bypassed official Roman customs points, operated by merchants and middlemen who had developed expertise in avoiding state inspection.

The organization of these smuggling operations could be sophisticated. Moving luxury goods from India or China to Rome required overland and maritime transport across thousands of miles, through multiple political jurisdictions, involving dozens of intermediaries. Some of these intermediaries were honest merchants who simply found it more profitable to avoid Roman duties on the final leg of the journey. Others were more systematically criminal, maintaining established routes, bribed officials, and networks of contacts that allowed them to move contraband goods consistently and at scale. The difference between an honest merchant who occasionally avoided a customs post and a criminal network that systematically smuggled goods as a business was, in practice, more one of scale and consistency than of kind.

Historical Vocabulary
Contraband

Contraband refers to goods that are prohibited by law or that are subject to legal restrictions on their trade. In the ancient world, contraband typically fell into several categories: goods subject to customs duties that were being transported without paying those duties (tax evasion); goods prohibited from export or import by treaty or law; goods that were inherently illegal to trade, such as persons illegally enslaved; and goods produced by illegal means, such as items stolen from temples or tombs.

The concept of contraband requires a state capable of defining what is prohibited and at least attempting to enforce that prohibition. In the ancient world, this meant that contraband was primarily a phenomenon of complex, commercially active states like Athens and Rome that had developed both commercial regulations and the administrative apparatus, however imperfect, to enforce them. In areas beyond effective state control, the concept of contraband had little practical meaning, since there was no authority to define or enforce the prohibition.

This points to a fundamental dynamic of organized crime that Chapter 2 introduced and this chapter illustrates empirically: criminal enterprise requires both a state capable of prohibiting activity and a market that continues to demand it despite the prohibition. Where states are too weak to prohibit effectively, there is no black market because everything is accessible through normal channels. Where states are strong enough to prohibit but not strong enough to suppress demand, black markets emerge to supply what prohibition has made expensive or unavailable. The ancient evidence suggests this dynamic was operating as early as the classical period of Athenian commerce.

Organized Fraud and Commercial Crime

Beyond smuggling, ancient commercial networks produced sophisticated forms of organized fraud that share features with modern white-collar organized crime. Roman legal texts devote substantial attention to various forms of commercial deception, and the fact that specific provisions were necessary suggests that the problems were systematic enough to require legislative response.

One of the most documented forms of ancient commercial fraud involved the manipulation of weights and measures. Markets throughout the ancient world depended on accurate measurement of goods sold by weight or volume, and the temptation to use false weights, misrepresent quantities, or otherwise defraud buyers was pervasive. Ancient states responded by establishing official standards, appointing market inspectors, and prescribing penalties for fraud. But archaeological evidence from ancient marketplaces shows that merchants routinely possessed multiple sets of weights, calibrated to their advantage for buying versus selling, suggesting that systematic fraud in weights and measures was a normal part of commercial life rather than an occasional aberration.

Currency fraud was another area of systematic organized criminal activity. Ancient coinage was vulnerable to debasement, in which the silver or gold content of coins was reduced while maintaining their nominal value, and to outright counterfeiting. Debasement was often practiced by states themselves as a fiscal expedient, but private counterfeiters also operated at scale. Roman legal texts prescribe severe penalties for coin clipping (shaving metal from the edges of coins), forging coins, and accepting forged coins knowingly. The prescriptions for penalties against those who knowingly accepted false coin suggest that networks of people passing fraudulent currency were a recognized phenomenon, not just isolated incidents of individual fraud.

Perhaps most sophisticated was the practice of what Roman legal texts called falsum, falsification, which covered a range of documentary and commercial frauds including forging contracts, falsifying wills, and misrepresenting the legal status of goods or persons. The Roman courts heard numerous cases of commercial fraud that involved networks of actors who cooperated to defraud buyers, creditors, or the state. The speeches of Cicero, in particular, document cases in which fraud was not the opportunistic act of a single criminal but the coordinated activity of multiple parties who shared the proceeds and covered for one another. These cases anticipate the organizational structure of modern financial crime in ways that are striking even across the gulf of two millennia.

Section 3.3

Bandit Kingdoms: When Crime Becomes Power

The most politically ambiguous form of ancient organized crime was banditry. At its most basic, banditry was simple robbery on land, the terrestrial equivalent of piracy. A band of armed men preyed on travelers, merchants, and isolated settlements, taking by force what they could not acquire legitimately. This is crime in its most elemental form, and it appears in the historical record of virtually every ancient society. But banditry in the ancient world could become something considerably more complex, developing political dimensions, territorial control, and social organization that made the distinction between criminal gang and proto-state genuinely difficult to draw.

The Geography of Ancient Banditry

Like piracy, ancient banditry was shaped by geography. Bandit operations concentrated in the zones that geographers and ancient writers recognized as outside effective state control: the mountain ranges that divided the Mediterranean littoral from the interior, the desert fringes where settled agriculture gave way to nomadism, the frontier zones between competing empires, and the densely forested uplands that offered cover and escape from pursuing forces. Strabo's Geography is peppered with references to bandit-infested regions, the mountains of Isauria in Anatolia, the interior of Sardinia, the hill country of southern Italy, the ranges of the Balkans, each described as a zone where travelers risked attack and where effective state authority was minimal.

The persistence of these bandit zones reflects something important about ancient state capacity. Ancient empires, however impressive their military and administrative achievements, were fundamentally incapable of exercising detailed control over difficult terrain far from their centers of power. The Roman Empire, for all its sophistication, governed a vast territory with an administrative apparatus and a military force that, by modern standards, was relatively small. The legions controlled the major roads and the settled plains. In the mountains and deserts, local strongmen, whether bandits, tribal chieftains, or some combination of both, exercised the effective authority that Rome claimed but could not consistently enforce.

This gap between claimed and actual sovereignty was the political space in which banditry became something more than crime. Where a strong state was unable to police its own territory, the people who lived in or passed through that territory needed protection from someone. If the state could not provide it, private strongmen would. And the line between a strongman who provided genuine protection in exchange for tribute and a bandit who collected tribute under threat of violence was, in practice, thin to non-existent. Ancient writers recognized this ambiguity. Strabo describes the highland peoples of Isauria in terms that blur the categories of bandit, mercenary, and local militia, because in the actual conditions of the region, these were not distinct roles.

Social Banditry and Its Limits

Modern historians, drawing on the influential work of the historian Eric Hobsbawm, have used the concept of "social banditry" to describe a form of organized outlawry in which bandits, while undeniably criminal from the perspective of state law, maintain a degree of popular support within their communities by positioning themselves as defenders of the poor against the rich and powerful. Hobsbawm's most famous example was the English legendary outlaw Robin Hood, who stole from the rich to give to the poor. He argued that similar figures appear in many agrarian societies as a response to poverty, exploitation, and the perceived injustice of legal systems that protected the wealthy at the expense of the poor.

The concept has been applied with varying degrees of success to ancient banditry. Ancient literary sources do contain some material that could be read in the Hobsbawm mold. Diodorus Siculus describes the Sicilian slave leader Eunus, who led a major slave revolt in the second century BCE, in terms that give him almost legendary heroic qualities among the oppressed. Various Greek and Roman texts reference local strongmen who were celebrated by their own communities even while being condemned by official sources as criminals. And there is evidence that some ancient bandit leaders cultivated popular legitimacy by acting as local protectors and redistributors, at least within their own communities.

Historians have, however, subjected the concept of social banditry to substantial critique. Hobsbawm's model assumes that bandits are primarily victims of systemic injustice who turn to crime as a form of resistance, and that their violence is primarily directed against those who exploit them. The actual evidence from ancient sources, fragmentary as it is, often tells a different story: ancient bandits preyed primarily on the vulnerable travelers and merchants who happened to cross their paths, not selectively on the wealthy and powerful. The romantic image of the noble bandit may reflect the literary interests of ancient authors more than the actual behavior of ancient criminal networks. The evidence is too fragmentary and too filtered through literary conventions to support confident generalizations about the social character of ancient banditry.

From Bandit to Warlord: The Spectrum of Organized Violence

At the upper end of the organizational spectrum, ancient banditry shaded into something that contemporaries sometimes called a kingdom and we might call a warlord state. In regions where state authority was absent or contested, successful bandit leaders could expand their operations from predation to protection, collecting tribute from settled populations in exchange for not attacking them and, sometimes, for defending them against rivals. This transition from bandit to local strongman to proto-state authority was a recurring pattern in ancient history, and it illustrates how the boundary between criminal enterprise and political authority was not fixed but negotiated and contested.

The Bosporan kingdom, centered on the straits connecting the Black Sea to the Mediterranean, provides one of the clearest ancient examples of a political entity that straddled the boundary between state and criminal network. The Bosporan kings controlled the grain trade from the Black Sea to the Mediterranean and extracted tribute from the ships that passed through their waters. Whether this was taxation or piracy depended entirely on one's relationship with the Bosporan rulers: those who paid were protected; those who refused were plundered. The Greek cities of the Black Sea coast maintained generally cooperative relationships with the Bosporan kings, recognizing that the alternative to paying tribute was exposing themselves to raids from the steppe peoples to the north whom the Bosporan rulers also controlled. This three-way arrangement, Greek merchants, Bosporan rulers, and steppe raiders, looks from one angle like legitimate interstate commerce and taxation and from another like an organized protection racket operating on a regional scale.

Similar dynamics operated in the deserts of Arabia and Syria, where tribal confederacies controlled the overland routes connecting the Mediterranean world to the Persian Gulf and the trade routes of the east. The Nabataean kingdom, centered on the city of Petra in modern Jordan, grew wealthy by controlling access to the spice routes from Arabia and taxing the caravans that passed through its territory. The Nabataean kings maintained armies, built elaborate architecture, and participated in diplomatic relations with Rome, and from this perspective they were a legitimate state. But their economic foundation was control of a trade route by force, and the fees they extracted from merchants who wished to pass safely through their territory were functionally indistinguishable from the tribute demanded by a protection racket. The political legitimacy of the collection mechanism, royal decree versus criminal threat, was different; the economic mechanism, pay or suffer consequences, was the same.

This ambiguity between state power and criminal power in the management of trade routes is one of the most significant themes in the ancient history of organized crime. It suggests that the distinction between taxation and extortion, between a legitimate state that regulates commerce and a criminal network that preys on it, was not a fixed or natural boundary but a political achievement that required constant assertion and defense. Where state authority was strong enough to monopolize the legitimate use of force, the boundary was relatively clear. Where authority was contested, fragmented, or weak, criminal and political enterprise occupied the same space and often the same personnel.

Section 3.4

Protection Rackets on Ancient Trade Routes

The sale of protection, the offer of safety from violence in exchange for payment, is one of the oldest and most persistent features of organized criminal activity. Chapter 2 of this textbook introduced "protection as a commodity" as a fundamental concept in understanding organized crime, and the ancient world provides the earliest documented examples of how this commodity was produced, marketed, and sold. Protection rackets in the ancient world operated at multiple scales, from local markets and harbor towns to the great overland trade routes that connected the Mediterranean world to Asia.

Market Day Extortion and Urban Protection

At the most local level, ancient markets and commercial gatherings were sites of systematic extortion by those who could mobilize violence or the threat of violence. The periodic markets (nundinae) of Roman Italy, which brought merchants, farmers, and buyers together at regular intervals, were vulnerable to extortion by local strongmen who could credibly threaten disruption. Market inspectors and civic authorities were supposed to maintain order, but in practice their capacity to do so depended on the willingness of local elites to support enforcement, which in turn depended on whether those elites had interests in the market's smooth operation or in its disruption.

The harbors of the ancient Mediterranean were particularly fertile ground for protection operations. Harbor districts in major port cities, including Piraeus in Athens, Ostia in Rome, and the commercial ports of Alexandria, Carthage, and Corinth, concentrated enormous wealth in a small physical space, bringing together merchants with valuable cargoes, sailors with ready cash, and the various service providers, dock workers, warehousemen, agents, and brokers, who facilitated maritime trade. This concentration of mobile wealth in a spatially defined area, accessible to violence and poorly policed, was an ideal environment for systematic extortion.

Roman legal texts and court speeches provide glimpses of harbor extortion that are too consistent to be mere literary invention. Cicero's descriptions of Sicilian harbor towns under Verres's governorship describe a systematic operation in which merchants paid not just the official customs duty but a series of additional fees extracted by officials and their agents, fees that had no legal basis but that were enforced by the practical ability of the official to delay, confiscate, or otherwise harm the merchant's commercial interests. This is a protection racket operating through the machinery of official authority, and it illustrates a point emphasized in Chapter 2: the relationship between state power and criminal protection is not simply one of opposition but one of potential cooperation and symbiosis.

Historical Vocabulary
Protection Racket

A protection racket is a form of extortion in which a criminal enterprise offers protection from violence or other harm in exchange for payment, while implicitly or explicitly threatening to inflict the harm itself if payment is refused. The defining feature is that the criminal organization is simultaneously the source of the threat and the seller of the protection against it.

The protection racket is one of the most analytically important concepts in the study of organized crime because it illuminates the relationship between criminal enterprise and the legitimate function of the state. Providing protection from violence is one of the primary functions of states, which claim a monopoly on the legitimate use of force precisely in order to prevent private actors from offering protection on a commercial basis. Where states fail to provide effective protection, private actors, whether criminal organizations or local strongmen, fill the vacuum. The economics of this substitution are straightforward: merchants and individuals who are not protected by the state will pay for protection from whoever can credibly offer it, and those who can provide protection can extract payment from those who need it.

In the ancient world, this dynamic operated across a wide range of contexts, from local market extortion to the systematic control of overland trade routes by tribal confederacies. The fact that we can identify it operating in these very different contexts, separated by centuries and thousands of miles, suggests that it reflects a fundamental feature of the relationship between commerce, violence, and political authority rather than a culturally specific phenomenon.

The Long-Distance Trade Routes: Safety for Sale

The great overland trade routes that connected the Mediterranean world to Persia, India, and China were among the most complex systems of organized protection in the ancient world. These routes crossed territories controlled by many different political authorities, none of which could provide effective security for the entire journey. The merchants who used them developed a variety of mechanisms for managing the risks of long-distance travel, and some of these mechanisms were functionally indistinguishable from paying protection to criminal organizations.

The most common form of protection on overland routes was the hired escort, a group of armed men who accompanied a caravan in exchange for payment. Legitimate escort services were provided by professional soldiers, tribal groups with territorial knowledge, and city-state officials. But the distinction between a legitimate armed escort and a gang of bandits collecting protection money was, in practice, often determined by the balance of power along any given stretch of road. A well-organized caravan with enough armed guards and enough political connections might be able to negotiate the journey with minimal extortion. A smaller caravan with fewer resources might find that the escort it could afford was itself extracting tribute from it while nominally protecting it from others.

The Silk Road, the network of trade routes connecting China to the Mediterranean world, which became increasingly important from the second century BCE onward, generated particularly elaborate protection systems. The Parthian Empire, which controlled the central section of the Silk Road across Persia and Mesopotamia, built its commercial prosperity partly on the revenues it extracted from merchants crossing its territory. These revenues were officially taxes, and the Parthian state provided genuine services in exchange, including road infrastructure, caravanserais (rest stops and warehouses), and military patrols that suppressed opportunistic banditry along the routes. But the rates of taxation were set unilaterally by the Parthian rulers, enforced by their military power, and could be renegotiated only by states with sufficient military leverage to compel better terms. From the perspective of a Roman merchant crossing Parthian territory, the difference between a tax and a protection payment was largely semantic.

Religious Sanctuaries and Commercial Protection

One of the most distinctive features of ancient protection systems was the role of religious sanctuaries in providing commercial security. Throughout the Greek and Roman world, major religious sanctuaries, temples dedicated to gods such as Apollo at Delphi, Artemis at Ephesus, and Zeus at Olympia, served as neutral commercial zones where merchants from competing city-states and kingdoms could trade safely because the divine authority of the sanctuary provided protection that human political authority could not reliably guarantee. The great religious festivals associated with these sanctuaries, the Olympic Games, the Pythian Games at Delphi, the Isthmian Games at Corinth, were simultaneously religious celebrations and commercial fairs, and the sanctity that protected the religious activity also protected the commercial transactions that took place alongside it.

This system of divine protection had important commercial implications. The major sanctuaries accumulated enormous wealth as the beneficiaries of religious gifts and, in some cases, as commercial institutions in their own right. Temple treasuries became early banking institutions, holding deposits and making loans at interest. The religious neutrality of the sanctuaries made them trusted intermediaries in commercial disputes. And the political connections of the major sanctuaries, which were patronized by the most powerful states and rulers of the ancient world, gave them a degree of political protection that purely commercial institutions lacked.

But this system could also be exploited criminally. Sanctuaries were targets for theft precisely because of the wealth they accumulated. Ancient history is full of accounts of the plundering of temple treasuries, whether by foreign conquerors, domestic tyrants, or criminal networks. And the commercial activities of sanctuaries, while generally conducted on legitimate terms, could shade into activities that modern observers would classify as criminal. The Delian sanctuary of Apollo, before the island became primarily famous as a slave market, was a major commercial and banking center whose operations occupied a gray zone between religious charity, commercial banking, and political money management.

Section 3.5

Corruption Among Officials: Crime Within the State

If piracy was organized crime operating outside the state and banditry was organized crime operating in the spaces between states, official corruption was organized crime operating within the state itself. It is, in some respects, the most insidious form of ancient criminal organization, because it subverted the very apparatus that was supposed to control crime. And of all the forms of ancient organized criminal activity, it is the best documented, because it left traces in the legal and literary records that states and their educated members created.

The Structure of Ancient Corruption

Ancient corruption took many forms, but several recurrent patterns are well documented in both Greek and Roman sources. The most straightforward was the simple bribe: a payment made to an official in exchange for a favorable decision, whether in a court of law, an administrative proceeding, or a commercial transaction. Ancient sources are frank about the prevalence of bribery in ways that suggest it was understood as a normal feature of official life rather than an exceptional deviation from expected behavior.

More complex, and more relevant to the analysis of organized crime, was the systematic corruption of provincial administration. The Roman Empire was governed through a system of appointed provincial governors who wielded enormous authority over the territories under their control, including authority over the courts, the military, taxation, and commercial regulation. These governors served fixed terms of typically one to three years, and their accountability to the central government, while real in principle, was limited in practice by the difficulties of communication and the political connections of powerful men in Roman society. The combination of large discretionary power, limited oversight, and fixed short terms created a chronic incentive for governors to use their authority for personal enrichment during their time in office.

The prosecution of Gaius Verres by Cicero in 70 BCE provides the most detailed surviving account of how a Roman provincial governor could organize what amounted to a criminal enterprise within the apparatus of legitimate administration. Verres served as governor of Sicily from 73 to 71 BCE, and Cicero's prosecution speeches, known as the Verrine orations, describe his administration in terms that would be entirely recognizable as organized crime by any modern standard. Verres allegedly used his position to systematically loot the art treasures of Sicilian temples, extort merchants at the island's ports, manipulate the legal system to dispossess wealthy Sicilians of their property, and take a percentage of every commercial transaction that required official approval.

The Verres Case: Organized Crime in Official Dress

The details of the Verres case are worth examining carefully, because they illustrate how the machinery of state administration could be converted into a criminal enterprise by a sufficiently corrupt and powerful official. According to Cicero's account, Verres did not simply accept bribes when offered; he organized a systematic operation to extract maximum profit from every dimension of his official authority.

In the area of art theft, Verres maintained a team of agents, including a specialist in Greek art named Tlepolermus and a painter named Hiero, whose function was to identify valuable artworks in Sicilian temples, private homes, and public buildings. Once identified, these objects were acquired by a combination of purchase under duress, outright confiscation, and theft under cover of official requisition. Verres's agents cultivated relationships with local officials and temple priests who provided access to religious treasuries and advance warning of inspections. The network of agents, informants, and facilitators that Verres maintained constituted a genuine criminal organization operating within the official structure of provincial government.

In commercial affairs, Verres allegedly established what amounted to a parallel system of unofficial taxation at Sicilian ports. In addition to the official customs duties, merchants were required to pay a variety of additional fees to Verres's officials, with the understanding that refusal would result in delays, inspections, confiscations, or other official harassment that would cost more than the payment demanded. The rates of these unofficial levies were negotiated case by case, depending on the wealth of the merchant and the value of the cargo, and the proceeds were split among Verres, his official staff, and local collaborators who facilitated the operation.

Cicero's prosecution of Verres succeeded, and Verres went into exile rather than face conviction. But the Verrine orations reveal something important about the systemic dimensions of ancient official corruption: Verres was not unique. Cicero's arguments depend partly on showing that what Verres did was worse than what was normally done, which implicitly acknowledges that what was normally done was already substantially corrupt. The provincial governorship was a recognized opportunity for enrichment, and the political and social networks of Roman aristocratic society were organized partly around the expectation that men who held powerful positions would use them for personal gain and would share those gains with their political allies and clients. The prosecution of Verres was not a routine event; it was a political statement, made possible by the particular alignments of Roman political factions at a specific moment, and it did not fundamentally alter the systemic corruption of provincial administration.

Context: Marcus Tullius Cicero (106–43 BCE) was one of Rome's most celebrated orators and lawyers. When the Sicilians asked him to prosecute the former governor Gaius Verres for extortion and theft, Cicero undertook an extensive investigation of Sicily, gathering evidence from hundreds of witnesses and examining official records. He delivered his opening prosecution speech in 70 BCE; Verres chose exile rather than face the full case. Cicero published the remaining speeches as literary documents, and they survive as one of the most detailed accounts of organized corruption in the ancient world.

In Cicero's account, Verres maintained a sophisticated network of agents to identify and acquire valuable artworks from Sicilian temples and private collections. The governor's method combined official authority with criminal threat: he would request an artwork as a gift, establish an absurdly low purchase price, or simply order its confiscation as official property. Temple priests who resisted faced prosecution or physical intimidation. Private owners who refused to sell faced the same harassment that Verres could deploy against any Sicilian subject as provincial governor. Cicero described how entire temple treasuries were systematically stripped, with Verres's agents cataloguing items of value and returning later to collect them, while local officials who might have resisted were either suborned with a share of the proceeds or intimidated into silence.

Analysis: The Verrine orations are an extraordinary source for ancient organized crime precisely because they describe a criminal operation from the inside: Cicero had access to documents, witnesses, and official records that most historical sources lack. But they also require careful reading as a source. Cicero was an advocate, not a historian, and his purpose was to persuade a jury to convict his client's enemy. He almost certainly exaggerated the scale and systematicity of Verres's crimes for rhetorical effect. He also had his own political interests in the outcome of the case. The picture he paints of Verres as a uniquely monstrous criminal is likely somewhat distorted; the reality was probably that Verres was significantly more corrupt than average but operating within a system that tolerated substantial levels of corruption as normal. Reading Cicero against other sources, including inscriptions, papyri, and other legal texts, allows historians to reconstruct a more balanced picture.

Tax Farming and Systemic Corruption

Beyond the individual corruption of provincial governors, the Roman tax-farming system created structural conditions for organized corruption at a systemic level. The publicani, the tax-farming companies that purchased the right to collect revenues in specific provinces, were themselves powerful corporate actors with interests that did not always align with either the Roman state or the provincial populations they taxed. Their operations blurred the boundary between legitimate commercial activity and criminal extortion in ways that were recognized and debated by Romans themselves.

The tax-farming system worked as follows: Roman magistrates would auction the right to collect taxes in a given province for a fixed period, typically five years. The company or individual that won the auction paid the purchase price upfront to the Roman treasury and then sought to recover that investment plus profit by collecting taxes from the provincial population. The gap between what the tax farmer paid Rome and what he could extract from the provincials was the source of profit, and the wider that gap, the greater the incentive to use the discretionary authority of tax assessment aggressively. Companies that over-assessed taxpayers, demanded payments that had no legal basis, or threatened legal action against those who resisted were participating in what was functionally extortion, even if their actions were technically authorized by their tax-farming contract.

The publicani companies also created complex conflicts of interest with the provincial governors who were supposed to oversee them. Governors who cooperated with the tax farmers, overlooking their excesses in exchange for a share of the proceeds or for political support in Rome, were engaging in a form of organized corruption that combined official authority with corporate criminal enterprise. Governors who attempted to protect provincial populations from tax-farmer excesses faced powerful enemies with connections to influential Romans. The system created structural incentives for corruption at multiple levels simultaneously, making it genuinely difficult for honest officials to resist and genuinely difficult for the central government to monitor or correct.

Judicial Corruption and Criminal Networks

Ancient legal systems were vulnerable to corruption at every level, and the corruption of courts and judges was one of the most documented forms of organized criminal activity in both Greek and Roman sources. The problem was structural as well as individual: ancient courts lacked many of the procedural safeguards that modern legal systems use to reduce the influence of money and personal relationships on judicial outcomes, and the intimate connection between legal proceedings and political competition in both Athens and Rome meant that powerful men had strong incentives to interfere in legal processes.

Athenian courts, which used large citizen juries to decide cases, were vulnerable to a form of corruption that ancient sources call jury bribery. The practice is documented in numerous sources, including the speeches of orators who routinely accused their opponents of attempting to bribe jurors, and the laws of Athens, which prescribed severe penalties for both giving and receiving bribes in judicial proceedings. The sheer frequency of these accusations suggests that jury bribery was not merely a rhetorical device but a real practice, and the organizational challenges of bribing a substantial portion of a jury of hundreds or thousands of citizens required coordination among multiple actors that qualifies as organized criminal activity.

In Rome, the corruption of juries in the courts that tried cases of provincial extortion, the very courts that were supposed to hold men like Verres accountable, was so well documented that it became a major political issue of the late Republic. The composition of these juries, which shifted between senators, equestrians, and tribunes at various points in the late Republic's political upheavals, was debated partly in terms of which social group was least susceptible to corruption. The fact that this debate took place at all is evidence that jury corruption was not a minor problem but a systemic feature of the Roman judicial system, one that powerfully protected well-connected criminals from legal accountability.

Historical Debate

Was Ancient Piracy a Criminal Enterprise or a Political Institution?

The question: Modern historians of the ancient world disagree significantly about how to characterize ancient piracy, particularly the large-scale piracy of the Hellenistic period and the Cilician operations of the first century BCE. Was it primarily a criminal enterprise analogous to modern organized crime, or was it a political institution with recognized if unofficial status in the international system of the ancient Mediterranean?

Interpretation A — Piracy as Criminal Enterprise: One school of thought, drawing on ancient legal categories and the analysis of piracy's economic functions, argues that large-scale ancient piracy was indeed a form of organized crime in any meaningful sense of the term. The Cilician pirates, in this view, operated a genuine criminal enterprise with the defining features identified in Chapter 1: they were organized for profit, they used systematic violence, they corrupted officials and maintained political connections, they operated specialized markets for their criminal output (the slave trade), and they exploited jurisdictional gaps to evade state authority. The fact that ancient legal categories labeled them hostes humani generis, enemies of all mankind, without legal standing, reflects a genuine ancient recognition of the qualitative difference between piracy and legitimate political violence. Scholars including Philip de Souza have emphasized the economic rationality of ancient piracy, its systematic exploitation of markets and trade routes, as evidence that it functioned as organized crime rather than as a semi-legitimate political institution.
Interpretation B — Piracy as Political Institution: A competing school of thought, associated with scholars such as Vincent Gabrielsen, argues that modern categories of crime are anachronistic when applied to ancient piracy, and that ancient sea raiding existed on a continuous spectrum with legitimate naval activity, privateering, and interstate warfare. In this view, the Cilician pirates were not clearly distinguishable from other sea powers: they had political relationships with states, they maintained territorial bases, they negotiated diplomatically, and their activities were sometimes formally sanctioned by Hellenistic rulers who found their services useful. The line between the Cilician pirates and, say, the Rhodian navy, which also extracted tribute from maritime commerce and used force against those who refused to pay, was a matter of political legitimacy rather than organizational structure or moral character. This interpretation suggests that applying modern criminal categories to ancient piracy distorts the historical reality.

Evidence: Both interpretations have substantial ancient evidence to draw on. The legal status of pirates as hostes humani generis supports the criminal interpretation. The political relationships of pirate leaders with Hellenistic kings and Roman politicians, and the blurring of piracy with legitimate naval activity, support the political institution interpretation. The slave trade connection is consistent with either view, since legal slave markets existed alongside the illegal seizure of free persons for enslavement.

Why it matters: How we categorize ancient piracy affects how we understand the origins of organized crime. If piracy was primarily a political institution that gradually became criminalized as states extended their authority, then organized crime as a category is a product of the development of state power rather than a pre-existing phenomenon that states struggled to suppress. If piracy was fundamentally criminal from its origins, then organized crime is a persistent feature of commercial society that predates and resists state authority.

Section 3.6

Everyday Life, Ordinary Crime, and the Ancient Criminal Underworld

The grand criminal enterprises of ancient piracy, long-distance smuggling, and provincial corruption were the most visible and most documented forms of organized crime in the ancient world, but they rested on a broader foundation of everyday criminal activity that shaped the experience of ordinary people. Understanding crime in the ancient world requires looking beyond the dramatic episodes recorded by elite authors to the more mundane patterns of theft, fraud, extortion, and violence that permeated daily life in ancient cities, villages, and marketplaces.

The Criminal Ecology of Ancient Cities

Ancient Mediterranean cities, particularly the great urban centers of the Hellenistic and Roman periods, were environments in which wealth and poverty existed in extremely close proximity. Rome at the height of the Empire may have had a population approaching a million people, the vast majority of whom lived in crowded apartment buildings (insulae) with minimal security, inadequate fire protection, and constant pressure from the difficulty of finding steady income in a premodern urban economy. These conditions created both the need for criminal activity among the poor and the opportunities for criminal exploitation of the poor by those with more power and resources.

The collegia, associations or guilds that organized Roman workers and craftsmen by trade, were one of the key institutions of urban social life, and they occupied an ambiguous space between legitimate mutual aid organization and criminal conspiracy. Legitimate collegia provided their members with burial insurance, social support, and professional networks. But the same organizational structure, a group of men who knew each other, trusted each other, and had reason to act collectively, could also support criminal activity. Roman authorities periodically suppressed collegia on the grounds that they were covers for political conspiracy or criminal enterprise, while at other times tolerating or even encouraging them as stabilizing social institutions. This ambivalence reflects the genuine dual character of these organizations.

The warehouses (horrea) that stored grain, oil, wine, and other commodities in Roman port cities were notorious sites of theft and pilferage. The volume of goods that passed through these facilities, and the large number of workers involved in loading, unloading, and storing them, created extensive opportunities for organized theft that was difficult for owners to detect and prosecute. Theft from warehouses almost certainly involved networks of workers who identified valuable cargoes, coordinated their pilferage, and sold the proceeds through networks of receivers who could move stolen goods without attracting attention. This kind of internal theft network, exploiting insider access to create a systematic criminal operation, anticipates the organized warehouse theft rings that would appear in later historical periods.

Evidence from Egyptian Papyri

The dry climate of Egypt has preserved thousands of papyrus documents from the Roman period that give historians an unusually detailed window into everyday life and ordinary crime. Egyptian papyri include official documents, private letters, petitions to authorities, court records, and business accounts that collectively provide evidence about crime at the level of ordinary people rather than just elite actors documented by literary sources.

Petitions to Roman officials in Egypt describe a wide range of criminal activity, including theft of livestock and agricultural tools, assault, fraud in commercial transactions, and disputes over property. Many of these petitions describe not isolated incidents but patterns of repeated victimization by specific individuals or groups, suggesting that some criminals operated systematically in ways that would constitute organized criminal activity by modern standards. A petition complaining that a local strongman has repeatedly stolen from the petitioner's grain storage, or that a group of neighbors has organized to divert irrigation water fraudulently, describes patterns of organized criminal behavior even if the scale is small.

The papyri also provide evidence about the social context of crime in provincial Egypt. Criminal activity appears to concentrate among populations under severe economic stress, particularly during periods of agricultural failure when taxes remained due even as harvests fell short. The relationship between economic precarity and criminal activity is visible in the papyrological record in ways that the literary sources, focused on elite crime and spectacular events, largely obscure. The ordinary criminal landscape of the ancient world was shaped more by economic desperation, exploitation, and structural inequality than by the organizational sophistication of the great criminal networks that historians have most studied.

Tomb Robbing as Organized Crime in Egypt

One of the most striking examples of organized criminal enterprise in the ancient world comes not from Rome or Greece but from Egypt, where archaeological and documentary evidence reveals that the workers who built the royal tombs in the Valley of the Kings organized systematic plundering operations that persisted across multiple generations. The evidence comes from papyri discovered at Deir el-Medina, the village on the west bank of the Nile where the tomb workers lived during the New Kingdom period (c. 1550–1070 BCE), which include administrative records, letters, and court proceedings related to tomb robberies.

The organizational features of the Egyptian tomb-robbing networks are remarkable. The perpetrators were primarily the workers and officials who had legitimate access to the tombs, giving them insider knowledge of the contents, the layout, and the security arrangements that outside criminals lacked. The operations were conducted in coordinated groups that included workers who dug the access tunnels, specialists who identified and removed valuable items, and distribution networks that sold the stolen goods. Local officials who were supposed to investigate thefts were sometimes themselves implicated in the operations, either as active participants or as recipients of bribes to minimize their investigations.

The court records from these investigations, some of which survive from the reign of Ramesses IX (c. 1129–1111 BCE), describe what amounts to a multi-generational criminal network operating within the official structure of the Egyptian state. Workers passed knowledge of profitable tomb locations and security weaknesses from parent to child. Networks of receivers converted gold, silver, and bronze tomb furnishings into commercial goods that could be sold without attracting official attention. The scale of the operations was substantial enough that the Egyptian government eventually launched major investigations, resulting in trials and severe punishments, but the evidence suggests that the investigations recovered only a fraction of what had been stolen.

The Egyptian tomb-robbing networks illustrate several principles that will recur throughout this textbook. They were enabled by insider access, the key advantage that distinguishes organized crime from random predation. They were sustained by the corruption of officials who were supposed to prevent or investigate criminal activity. They operated across multiple generations, developing organizational continuity that transcended any individual actor. And they exploited the gap between a state's claims of control and its actual capacity to enforce those claims, a gap that in the Egyptian case was literally carved into the rock of the Theban hills.

Multiple Perspectives

Smuggling in the Ancient World: Who Benefited and Who Was Harmed?

From the perspective of the Roman state: Smuggling represented both a fiscal loss and a challenge to state authority. Customs revenues were an important source of imperial income, used to fund the military, infrastructure, and administration that maintained Roman power. Officials who caught smugglers were enforcing not just commercial regulations but the fiscal foundations of empire. The state's perspective framed smuggling as simple theft from the public treasury, to be punished accordingly.
From the perspective of the smuggler: Ancient smugglers, like modern ones, saw themselves primarily as businesspeople exploiting market opportunities. In a world where customs duties could represent a substantial fraction of a commodity's value, the profit from successful smuggling was large enough to justify significant risk. Many smugglers probably did not think of themselves as criminals in any deep sense; they were simply finding more efficient ways to supply goods that markets demanded at prices buyers were willing to pay. The criminal label was applied by the state; from the smuggler's perspective, the state was simply extracting an unjustified toll on commerce.
From the perspective of the consumer who bought smuggled goods: The consumers who purchased smuggled goods, typically luxury items available at lower prices than the legally taxed versions, also occupied an ambiguous moral and legal position. A Roman aristocrat who bought exotic spices or fine silks from a merchant who had evaded customs duties was breaking the law in the technical sense that receiving contraband goods was illegal. But the practical social risk was minimal for anyone with sufficient social status and connections, and the economic benefit was real. From the consumer's perspective, the state's customs regime was an interference with ordinary commercial life, and evading it was a reasonable response.
From the perspective of the legitimate merchant who paid full duties: Honest merchants who paid their customs duties faced systematic commercial disadvantage compared to competitors who evaded them. If a smuggler could sell spices at a twenty percent lower price because he had avoided a twenty percent customs duty, the honest merchant either had to match that price at a loss or accept losing customers. This competitive pressure created incentives for previously honest merchants to also begin evading duties, a classic dynamic of what economists call adverse selection in illegal markets: when criminal behavior creates competitive advantages, it tends to spread even among those who would prefer to operate legally.
Cause and Effect

The Causes and Consequences of Cilician Piracy

Long-Term Causes

Political fragmentation of the Hellenistic world after Alexander's death; decline of Rhodes as the primary naval police force in the eastern Aegean; growth of the slave trade and its demand for fresh supply; jurisdictional vacuum in the Cilician coast region; tolerance of piracy by Hellenistic kings for political and commercial reasons; corruption of Roman officials and merchants who profited from pirate commerce.

Short-Term Triggers

Collapse of Seleucid authority in southern Anatolia; Rome's failure to replace Rhodian naval power after stripping Rhodes of its tax revenues in 168 BCE; specific political crises in Rome's grain supply that made the consequences of piracy catastrophically visible; kidnapping of high-status Roman citizens that made the problem politically impossible to ignore.

The Crisis and State Response

By 67 BCE, Cilician piracy had disrupted Rome's grain supply, raided the Italian coast, and kidnapped Roman officials. The Lex Gabinia granted Pompey extraordinary authority over the entire Mediterranean. His systematic campaign cleared the sea of organized piracy within three months. His resettlement of defeated pirates attempted to address underlying economic conditions.

Effects and Legacy

Short-term: dramatic reduction in Mediterranean piracy; stabilization of grain supplies; demonstration that coordinated state action could suppress large-scale criminal networks. Long-term: piracy revived during the civil wars when Pompey's unified command structure dissolved; the precedent of extraordinary emergency authority that Pompey's command set contributed to the constitutional crises of the late Republic; the resettlement model influenced later thinking about rehabilitative responses to organized crime.

Historical complexity: The Cilician piracy crisis cannot be explained by any single cause. It required the convergence of political fragmentation, economic demand, jurisdictional gaps, and official complicity to reach the scale that made it an existential threat to Roman commercial life. Pompey's military success suppressed the immediate manifestation but did not eliminate the underlying conditions, which is why piracy revived when political conditions again created the necessary space.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Official Corruption as Organized Crime: Ancient Rome to the Present

What Changed

  • The legal frameworks defining and prosecuting corruption became more elaborate and more clearly distinguished between legitimate official discretion and criminal corruption
  • Modern states developed audit institutions, separation of powers, free presses, and other mechanisms specifically designed to reduce opportunities for official corruption
  • The scale and complexity of modern economies created new forms of corruption involving financial systems, regulatory agencies, and corporate actors that have no direct ancient equivalent
  • International anti-corruption frameworks, including treaties and intergovernmental organizations, created accountability mechanisms that had no counterpart in the ancient world

What Stayed the Same

  • The fundamental dynamic: officials with discretionary authority over valuable decisions face temptation to sell those decisions to the highest bidder
  • The structural conditions that enable corruption: inadequate oversight, concentrated authority, limited accountability, and opportunities for enrichment that exceed official salaries
  • The relationship between corruption and organized crime: criminal enterprises that cannot operate without official tolerance or active assistance must corrupt officials to survive
  • The difficulty of prosecution: well-connected corrupt officials, like Verres, could be prosecuted only when political conditions aligned and sufficient political will existed to override the protection of powerful social networks

Why the pattern matters: The persistence of official corruption across two thousand years of human history, despite enormous changes in legal frameworks, political systems, and social norms, suggests that corruption is not simply a product of moral failure by individual officials. It reflects structural conditions, concentrated authority, inadequate oversight, and the rational calculation that illicit gains outweigh risks of punishment, that appear wherever power over valuable decisions is vested in individuals without sufficient accountability. Understanding this structural dimension is essential for understanding why anti-corruption efforts have been so consistently difficult throughout history.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has traced the major forms of organized criminal activity in the ancient world, focusing on the Mediterranean civilizations of Greece and Rome while drawing on evidence from Egypt and the wider ancient Near East. The evidence examined reveals that criminal networks, organized for profit, exploiting markets and trade routes, corrupting officials, and operating in the gaps between state jurisdictions, were a consistent feature of ancient commercial society, appearing wherever complex economies, long-distance trade, and politically fragmented landscapes created the conditions for their emergence.

Ancient piracy, the most dramatic and best-documented form of ancient organized criminal enterprise, reached its apex in the Cilician operations of the first century BCE. The Cilician pirates were not random criminals but sophisticated organizational actors who maintained permanent bases, operated extensive fleets, developed commercial relationships with slave markets and Roman merchants, and exploited the political fragmentation of the late Hellenistic period to establish operations of a scale that contemporaries found genuinely alarming. The fact that Rome required an emergency grant of extraordinary authority to suppress them speaks to the organizational resilience of the network, and the fact that piracy revived when that political will dissolved illustrates the limits of even successful state suppression.

Smuggling and commercial fraud in the Greek and Roman worlds illustrate the fundamental dynamic introduced in Chapter 2: where states tax and regulate commerce, black markets emerge to supply demand at lower cost. The Athenian grain market regulations, the Roman customs system, and the various prohibitions and duties that ancient states imposed on commercial activity all created profitable opportunities for those willing to evade them. The networks that exploited these opportunities ranged from individual merchants cutting corners on duty payments to sophisticated criminal organizations with established routes, bribed officials, and reliable distribution systems for contraband goods.

Ancient banditry demonstrated how organized criminal violence could, under the right political conditions, transition into something approaching political authority. In the borderlands and mountain zones where state power was thin or absent, bandit leaders who could credibly monopolize violence collected tribute, offered protection, and exercised a form of territorial control that blurred the boundary between criminal enterprise and proto-state institution. The Nabataean kingdom and the Bosporan rulers are examples of this transition at its most successful, in which what began as or resembled criminal protection rackets became recognized political entities with their own legitimacy and diplomatic relationships.

Protection rackets in ancient markets and trade routes illustrated the commodity character of security in environments where the state could not provide reliable protection. Merchants who needed to move goods across dangerous territories, whether the bandit-infested mountain roads of Anatolia or the pirate-threatened sea lanes of the Aegean, paid for protection from whoever could credibly offer it, whether that protection came from official sources or from private criminal organizations that were functionally indistinguishable from official ones in their methods, if not their legal status.

Official corruption represented the deepest structural vulnerability of ancient criminal control: the subversion of the state apparatus itself by criminal enterprise. The Verres case demonstrated how a corrupt provincial governor could convert the machinery of legitimate administration into an instrument of systematic looting and extortion. The tax-farming system created structural incentives for corruption at the level of the corporations entrusted with revenue collection. The corruption of juries in the courts that were supposed to hold criminal officials accountable created a self-protective system that was genuinely difficult to break from the inside.

The Egyptian evidence, particularly the tomb-robbing networks of the New Kingdom period and the papyrological record of everyday crime in Roman Egypt, extended the picture beyond the dramatic criminal enterprises documented in literary sources to reveal the broader criminal ecology of ancient society. Organized crime in the ancient world was not limited to the spectacular operations of pirate fleets and corrupt governors; it pervaded everyday commercial and social life in ways that affected ordinary people far more consistently, if less dramatically, than the episodes that captured ancient authors' attention.

Throughout this chapter, two recurring patterns stand out as particularly significant for the broader argument of this textbook. First, the conditions that produce organized crime are structural, not accidental: wherever profitable commerce exists alongside jurisdictional gaps, official corruption, and demand for goods or services that the law prohibits or taxes heavily, criminal networks emerge to exploit those conditions. Second, state responses to organized crime, however successful in the short term, do not eliminate those underlying structural conditions. Pompey suppressed the Cilician pirates; piracy returned when political conditions recreated the space for it. The Roman courts prosecuted Verres; provincial corruption continued. Understanding these structural dynamics, visible in their earliest forms in the ancient evidence examined here, is essential for understanding the history of organized crime in every subsequent period.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Banditry
Organized robbery and predation conducted on land, typically by armed groups operating in territories with weak or absent state authority. In the ancient world, banditry ranged from opportunistic robbery of travelers to territorial control by organized bands that functioned as proto-states.
Black market
A system of exchange for goods or services that are illegal, heavily taxed, or otherwise regulated by the state, operating outside legal channels and state oversight. Black markets emerge wherever the legal market fails to satisfy demand at acceptable prices.
Collegium (pl. collegia)
A Roman association or guild, typically organized by trade or neighborhood, which provided members with social support, burial insurance, and professional networks. Their organizational structure made them potentially useful for both legitimate mutual aid and criminal coordination.
Contraband
Goods that are prohibited by law or transported without paying required duties. In the ancient world, this included smuggled luxury goods, stolen property, and illegally enslaved persons being transported for sale.
Corruption
The abuse of entrusted authority for private gain. In the ancient world, corruption operated at every level of official life, from jury bribery in courts to systematic looting by provincial governors.
Criminal enterprise
An organized group of individuals coordinating their activities for the systematic pursuit of criminal profit over an extended period. Distinguished from opportunistic crime by its organizational structure, continuity, and systematic exploitation of markets and opportunities.
Customs duty
A tax levied on goods imported into or exported from a territory, imposed at official inspection points such as ports or border crossings. Ancient customs duties created systematic incentives for smuggling.
Extortion
The criminal practice of obtaining something, typically money, through force, threats, or the abuse of authority. Distinguished from robbery in that extortion typically involves a continuing relationship in which the victim pays to avoid ongoing harm.
Hostes humani generis
Latin: "enemies of all mankind." The Roman legal concept that classified pirates as outside the protection of any law or political community, subject to punishment or death by anyone who caught them. This concept anticipated modern principles of universal jurisdiction over certain international crimes.
Impunity
Exemption from punishment or freedom from the negative consequences of one's actions. Criminal networks in the ancient world achieved impunity through a combination of geographical inaccessibility, official corruption, political connections, and the practical limitations of ancient law enforcement.
Jurisdictional gap
A space, geographic or legal, in which no single authority has clear and effective control. Criminal networks in the ancient world systematically exploited jurisdictional gaps between competing states, between central and provincial authority, and between land-based and maritime policing.
Lex Gabinia
A Roman law passed in 67 BCE that granted Pompey extraordinary military and administrative authority over the entire Mediterranean and surrounding coastline for the purpose of suppressing piracy. Its passage over senatorial opposition illustrates how perceived criminal threats can drive exceptional expansions of state power.
Maritime crime
Criminal activity conducted at sea or using maritime resources, including piracy, slave trafficking via ship, and the movement of contraband by sea. Maritime crime in the ancient world was facilitated by the same features that made sea transport attractive for legitimate commerce: speed, carrying capacity, and the practical difficulty of policing open water.
Piracy
Organized robbery or violence committed at sea or from a maritime base. Legally distinguished in the ancient world from privateering and naval warfare by the absence of political sanction. The Cilician pirates of the first century BCE represent the most documented example of large-scale ancient piracy as organized criminal enterprise.
Privateering
Sea raiding conducted by private actors commissioned or licensed by a state, giving the activity a quasi-legitimate political character. The distinction between piracy and privateering was crucial in the ancient world and would remain important through the early modern period.
Protection racket
An extortion scheme in which a criminal enterprise offers protection from harm, typically harm the criminal enterprise itself could inflict, in exchange for regular payment. The protection racket commodifies security in environments where legitimate state protection is unavailable or unreliable.
Publicani
Roman private tax-farming companies that purchased the right to collect taxes in specific provinces and then sought to extract enough revenue from provincial populations to cover their investment and produce profit. Their operations occupied a legally authorized but morally ambiguous space between legitimate tax collection and criminal extortion.
Ransom
A payment demanded in exchange for the release of a captive. In the ancient world, ransom was a major revenue stream for pirates, who captured wealthy travelers and merchants and negotiated their release with intermediaries. The ransom economy required commercial networks for negotiation, payment, and release that extended into legitimate society.
Receiver (fence)
An individual or network that purchases stolen goods and resells them through legitimate or semi-legitimate channels. Receivers were essential to ancient criminal networks because they converted the proceeds of crime into usable wealth, and their existence in legitimate commercial society connected criminal enterprise to licit markets.
Sitophylakes
Athenian state officials charged with monitoring the grain market and preventing price manipulation, diversion of supply, and commercial fraud. Their existence reflects the Athenian state's recognition that commercial regulation required active enforcement.
Slave trafficking
The illegal or extra-legal seizure and sale of persons as slaves. In the ancient world, slave trafficking was deeply intertwined with piracy, banditry, and commercial networks that connected violent predators with legal slave markets that purchased their human cargo without investigating its origins.
Social banditry
A concept developed by the historian Eric Hobsbawm describing bandits who maintain popular support within their communities by positioning themselves as defenders of the poor against powerful exploiters. The concept is contested when applied to ancient banditry, where the evidence for the "Robin Hood" dimension of outlaw activity is fragmentary and often filtered through literary conventions.
State capacity
The ability of a state to implement its policies, enforce its laws, and control activity within its claimed territory. In the ancient world, limited state capacity, particularly in remote, mountainous, or maritime environments, was the primary structural condition enabling organized criminal networks to operate with impunity.

Major People, Groups, and Places

Identification & Significance

Cilician Pirates (c. 150–67 BCE)
A loose confederation of pirate fleets based on the Anatolian coast that reached sufficient scale and organizational sophistication to be treated by Rome as a strategic threat. Their suppression by Pompey is one of the earliest documented cases of a state mobilizing extraordinary resources to suppress a criminal network.
Gnaeus Pompeius (Pompey the Great, 106–48 BCE)
Roman general who received unprecedented emergency authority to suppress Mediterranean piracy in 67 BCE. His rapid and systematic campaign provides the most detailed ancient example of coordinated state action against a transnational criminal network.
Gaius Verres (c. 120–43 BCE)
Roman governor of Sicily (73–71 BCE) prosecuted by Cicero for organizing what amounted to a systematic criminal enterprise within the apparatus of provincial administration. The Verrine case is the most detailed surviving account of official corruption as organized crime in the ancient world.
Marcus Tullius Cicero (106–43 BCE)
Roman orator and lawyer whose court speeches, particularly the Verrine orations, provide the most detailed surviving evidence about organized criminal networks and official corruption in the late Roman Republic.
Julius Caesar (100–44 BCE)
Roman statesman whose kidnapping by Cilician pirates in 75 BCE provides a famous illustration of the reach and audacity of ancient maritime criminal networks, and whose subsequent revenge execution of his captors illustrates the capacity of powerful individuals to take extra-legal action when the state fails to protect them.
Delos (Aegean island)
The central Aegean island that served as the largest slave market in the ancient Mediterranean, capable according to ancient sources of processing enormous numbers of enslaved persons daily. Delos was the critical commercial node connecting pirate raids and bandit kidnappings to the legal slave-owning economy of Greece and Rome.
The Nabataean Kingdom (c. 400 BCE–106 CE)
An Arab kingdom centered on Petra in modern Jordan that controlled key overland trade routes from Arabia to the Mediterranean. The Nabataean taxation of caravans illustrates how control of trade routes by force could transition from criminal protection racket to recognized political authority.
Rhodes (Aegean island city-state)
A powerful maritime city-state that maintained the effective naval policing of the eastern Aegean from the third century BCE until Rome stripped it of its customs revenues in 168 BCE. The decline of Rhodian naval power directly contributed to the expansion of Cilician piracy, illustrating how removing a criminal control mechanism can produce worse outcomes than maintaining an imperfect one.
Deir el-Medina (Egypt, New Kingdom period)
The village housing the workers who built the royal tombs of the Valley of the Kings. Papyrological evidence from this community documents multi-generational organized tomb-robbing networks that exploited insider knowledge and official corruption, one of the earliest known examples of criminal enterprise exploiting access to privileged information.
Strabo (c. 64 BCE–24 CE)
Greek geographer whose Geography provides important evidence about criminal regions, bandit strongholds, and pirate coasts across the ancient world. His geographical perspective complements the legal and biographical sources by documenting the spatial organization of ancient criminal activity.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter has established that organized crime is not a modern invention. The criminal networks of the ancient Mediterranean, organized for profit, exploiting markets and trade routes, corrupting officials, and operating in the jurisdictional gaps between competing political authorities, were recognizable predecessors of the criminal enterprises that will be examined in subsequent chapters of this textbook. The organizational sophistication of the Cilician pirates, the systematic corruption of Roman provincial administration, the multi-generational tomb-robbing networks of ancient Egypt, and the protection rackets of ancient trade routes all demonstrate that the fundamental dynamics of organized crime were already operating in complex commercial societies more than two thousand years ago.

The chapter has also established the analytical framework that will guide subsequent examination of organized crime in later periods. Three recurring principles emerge from the ancient evidence with particular clarity. First, organized crime is structurally produced: it emerges wherever profitable commerce exists alongside jurisdictional gaps, official corruption, and demand for goods or services that the law restricts. These structural conditions, not individual moral failure, are the primary determinants of organized crime's emergence and persistence. Second, organized crime is embedded in legitimate markets rather than existing entirely separate from them: the slave trade that sustained ancient piracy, the grain markets that incentivized ancient smuggling, and the official legal systems that corrupt officials subverted were all fundamentally legitimate commercial and legal institutions. Criminal enterprise parasitized these legitimate structures rather than creating entirely separate criminal economies. Third, state responses to organized crime, however successful in the short term, are limited by the persistence of the underlying structural conditions that produced the criminal network in the first place: Pompey suppressed the Cilician pirates, but the conditions that had enabled them persisted, and piracy revived when political stability collapsed.

The ancient evidence also introduces several questions that this textbook will continue to explore throughout subsequent chapters. One is the question of the boundary between criminal enterprise and political authority: in the ancient world, the Nabataean kingdom, the Bosporan rulers, and the pirate confederacies of the Cilician coast all occupied ambiguous spaces between recognized political entities and criminal organizations. This ambiguity will recur in later chapters when we examine the relationship between modern organized crime and the states within which it operates. Another is the question of how to use evidence produced by the powerful to reconstruct the experience of those whom criminal enterprise most directly harmed: the kidnapped persons sold into slavery, the provincial merchants extorted by corrupt governors, the ordinary farmers preyed upon by bandits. The ancient record speaks mostly in the voice of the educated elite; recovering other voices requires historical imagination as well as historical evidence.

Perhaps most importantly, the ancient evidence reminds students that the questions raised by organized crime, about the relationship between markets and prohibition, between state authority and criminal enterprise, between official power and criminal corruption, are not technical or administrative questions. They are fundamental questions about the organization of power in human societies, about who benefits from existing legal structures and who is harmed by them, and about the conditions under which people turn to illegal means to satisfy legal desires or to resist unjust authority. These are questions that appear in the earliest complex societies and that remain urgently relevant in the twenty-first century. Understanding their ancient dimensions is the first step toward understanding why they have been so persistent and so difficult to resolve.

Narco-States and Corruption

Organized Crime: A Global History

Chapter Opening

"When a government cannot or will not protect its citizens from criminal violence, and when the same institutions meant to provide security instead profit from crime, the line between the state and the criminal organization has effectively disappeared."

In the summer of 2006, newly elected Mexican President Felipe Calderón made a decision that would define his presidency and reshape the geography of power across his country. Faced with surging cartel violence in the western state of Michoacán, he deployed thousands of federal soldiers to confront drug trafficking organizations that had grown so powerful they operated openly in the streets of major cities, taxed local businesses, recruited from the ranks of the unemployed, and in some communities provided the only functioning system of dispute resolution and social order that residents knew. What became known as Calderón's War on Drugs was framed as an assertion of legitimate state authority over ungoverned spaces, a declaration that the Mexican government would not surrender its sovereign monopoly on violence to criminal enterprises. What unfolded over the following decade was something far more complicated: a convulsive conflict that claimed more than 150,000 lives, displaced hundreds of thousands more, exposed the depth of cartel penetration into police forces, military units, judiciaries, and elected offices at every level of government, and raised a question that observers in Mexico and abroad found deeply uncomfortable to confront. Was the Mexican state fighting organized crime, or had parts of the Mexican state become so integrated with organized crime that distinguishing between them was no longer analytically possible?

This question, and the conditions that make it necessary to ask, is the subject of this chapter. The concept of the narco-state, like most concepts in the study of organized crime, resists a clean definition. No country formally declares itself a narco-state, no government openly announces that its sovereignty has been contracted to criminal enterprises, and few situations present themselves as pure examples of the phenomenon. What the concept captures, rather, is a spectrum of conditions in which drug trafficking organizations exercise sufficient influence over state institutions, political processes, economic structures, and territorial control that the distinctions between the criminal and the legitimate, between private criminal violence and state coercion, and between the illegal economy and the formal economy become dangerously blurred.

Understanding how narco-states emerge requires understanding the institutional conditions that make state capture by criminal organizations possible. Weak states, poorly paid and undertrained security forces, judiciaries with limited independence and inadequate resources, electoral systems that channel campaign financing through informal and opaque networks, and economies in which the drug trade generates wealth far exceeding what formal employment can offer, all create the structural vulnerabilities that drug trafficking organizations exploit. The narco-state is not simply the product of moral failure or individual corruption, though both are always present. It is the product of institutional design, historical legacies of unequal development, and the extraordinary financial power that profitable drug markets generate.

The global drug trade, as examined in previous chapters, generates hundreds of billions of dollars annually. Even a small fraction of that sum, deployed strategically through bribes, political donations, threats, and the selective provision of social services, is sufficient to distort the institutions of states whose entire annual budgets are dwarfed by cartel revenues. A police officer earning a monthly salary that would not cover a week's rent in the capital city faces a fundamentally different calculation when a cartel representative appears with an offer described by the stark binary that Mexican criminal organizations have long deployed: plata o plomo, silver or lead, the bribe or the bullet. Thousands of individual calculations of this kind, repeated across entire institutions and entire generations, produce systemic corruption that cannot be addressed by arresting individual officers or prosecuting individual judges. It requires rebuilding institutions from the foundation, in political environments where those with the power to demand reform often benefit from the existing corrupt arrangements.

This chapter examines state capture and corruption as structural phenomena, tracing how drug trafficking organizations penetrate, co-opt, and in extreme cases effectively replace state functions across a range of national and regional contexts. It explores how political influence operates at multiple levels, from the payment of local police commanders to the financing of presidential campaigns, from the bribery of customs agents to the corruption of national intelligence services. It examines how violence operates not only as a tool of intimidation against rivals and civilians but as a form of governance, establishing hierarchies of power and territorial control in spaces where the state is absent or complicit. And it traces the consequences for ordinary people living under conditions in which the institutions meant to protect them have been compromised or captured, in which violence is a constant feature of daily life, and in which the choices available to them about how to navigate relationships with criminal power are constrained, dangerous, and morally complex.

The cases examined in this chapter draw primarily from Latin America, where the intersection of the global drug trade with historically weak institutions, high economic inequality, and the political legacies of authoritarian rule has produced some of the most acute examples of narco-state conditions. But the chapter also examines comparable dynamics in other regions, from West Africa, where cocaine transshipment has distorted governance in Guinea-Bissau and other fragile states, to Central Asia, where opium trafficking has funded warlord militias that operate in parallel to and in competition with formal government structures. The narco-state is not a Latin American phenomenon or a problem specific to developing economies. It is a product of the interaction between profitable illegal markets and institutional weakness, and those conditions can exist, in varying degrees, anywhere.

The chapter concludes by examining the conditions under which narco-state dynamics can be reversed, the social movements, institutional reforms, international pressures, and political transitions that have, in some contexts, reduced criminal penetration of state institutions. These cases of partial success are as instructive as the cases of failure. They illustrate that narco-state conditions, however entrenched, are not permanent features of political landscapes, and that the institutional choices made by societies facing criminal penetration of the state have consequences that extend across generations. The study of narco-states is not simply an academic exercise in the sociology of deviance or the political science of institutional failure. It is a study of what happens to human communities when the institutions designed to provide security, justice, and governance are turned against those purposes, and what it takes to reclaim them.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Define the concept of a narco-state and explain why the term describes a spectrum of conditions rather than a binary category.
  2. Identify the institutional conditions, including weak governance, economic inequality, and inadequate rule of law, that make state capture by drug trafficking organizations possible.
  3. Explain the mechanism of plata o plomo and analyze how systematic coercion and financial incentives together produce institutional corruption at scale.
  4. Trace the multiple levels at which drug trafficking organizations exercise political influence, from local police to national legislatures and executive offices.
  5. Analyze how cartel violence functions not only as criminal predation but as a form of territorial governance in spaces of state absence or complicity.
  6. Compare narco-state conditions across different regional contexts, including Mexico, Colombia, Guatemala, Guinea-Bissau, and Afghanistan.
  7. Describe the experience of ordinary people, including farmers, police officers, journalists, judges, and community members, living under conditions of criminal governance.
  8. Evaluate competing interpretations of the War on Drugs, including debates about whether militarized enforcement reduces or intensifies narco-state conditions.
  9. Assess the role of international drug demand, particularly from wealthy consumer countries, in creating and sustaining the financial conditions that enable narco-state dynamics.
  10. Identify examples of institutional reform, social movement resistance, and political transition that have reduced criminal penetration of state institutions.
  11. Connect the study of narco-states to broader questions about the relationship between organized crime, governance, and political legitimacy.

Historical Context

The narco-state did not emerge from a vacuum. To understand how drug trafficking organizations came to exercise governance functions in parts of Mexico, Colombia, Guinea-Bissau, Afghanistan, and other countries, students must first understand the historical conditions, economic structures, and institutional legacies that created the vulnerabilities criminal organizations exploit.

In Latin America, the most intensely studied region for narco-state dynamics, the relevant historical context stretches back through the twentieth century and into the colonial period. Spanish colonial governance in the Americas established patterns of centralized, patron-client political organization in which access to state power and state resources depended on personal relationships with powerful figures rather than on impersonal rule-following institutions. The wars of independence in the early nineteenth century transferred political control from Spanish colonial administrations to local elites, but the institutional patterns of governance, the deep integration of economic and political power, the weakness of civil society relative to the state, and the subordination of formal law to informal networks of obligation and coercion, were largely preserved.

The twentieth century brought periods of democratic development and periods of authoritarian rule across Latin American countries, and in both cases the institutional weaknesses that facilitate criminal penetration of the state were often reproduced or deepened. Military regimes that governed large parts of the region during the 1960s, 1970s, and 1980s frequently engaged in their own forms of organized criminality, running extortion rackets, disappearing political opponents, and in some cases directly collaborating with drug traffickers in exchange for financial support and political intelligence. The return to formal democratic governance in many countries during the 1980s and 1990s did not automatically produce effective, accountable, and independent institutions. It frequently produced competitive electoral politics layered on top of unreformed security services, judiciaries, and administrative agencies that had been shaped by decades of authoritarian institutional culture.

The economic context matters as well. The structural adjustment programs of the 1980s and 1990s, imposed on heavily indebted Latin American governments by international financial institutions as conditions of loan restructuring, cut public spending on education, health, and social services, reduced the formal employment that provided alternatives to criminal labor markets, and in some regions coincided with the dramatic expansion of cocaine production and trafficking. The drug economy offered incomes, social status, and organizational belonging to populations for whom the formal economy offered little, and it generated revenues for trafficking organizations that dwarfed the budgets of the state institutions nominally tasked with controlling them.

In West Africa, the historical context includes the legacies of colonial governance that created formal state structures with limited capacity and legitimacy, the disruptions of post-independence politics including numerous coups and civil conflicts, and the geographic vulnerability of the region's long coastlines and underfunded port and customs authorities to exploitation by international trafficking networks. Guinea-Bissau, which emerged in the 2000s as perhaps the most extreme example of narco-state conditions outside Latin America, had been devastated by a civil war in the late 1990s, had no functioning judiciary, maintained a military whose officers earned almost no salary, and sat along cocaine trafficking routes connecting South American producers to European consumer markets. The institutional conditions for state capture were almost perfectly assembled before the first trafficking organization made its first bribe.

In Afghanistan, the production of opium poppies has a long history, but the narco-state dynamics associated with the modern Afghan state are products of more recent conditions: the destruction of state institutions during the Soviet-Afghan War of the 1980s, the civil war of the 1990s, the Taliban regime's complex and contradictory relationship with opium production, and the post-2001 reconstruction effort that built a formal state apparatus on a foundation of warlord militias, many of whose commanders were simultaneously drawing salaries as government officials and deriving incomes from taxing or protecting drug production in their territories. International development assistance designed to build legitimate governance in Afghanistan was, in numerous documented cases, captured by networks that were simultaneously embedded in the drug economy, illustrating the ways in which well-intentioned external interventions can be absorbed by the corruption dynamics they are intended to remedy.

This historical context does not excuse or normalize the corruption and violence associated with narco-state conditions, but it is essential for understanding them. Corruption on the scale required for state capture is not simply a product of individual moral failure, though individual choices matter enormously. It is a product of institutional environments in which corruption is the normal operating condition of governance, in which the financial power of criminal organizations vastly exceeds the resources available to those tasked with controlling them, and in which the enforcement of law against criminal networks requires the same institutions that those networks have compromised.

Historical Vocabulary
Narco-State

A narco-state is a term used to describe a country or significant portion of a country in which drug trafficking organizations have so penetrated the formal institutions of governance, security, and economic regulation that those institutions can no longer function independently of criminal influence. The term is contested: critics note that it can oversimplify complex situations in which some state institutions remain functional and legitimate while others are compromised, and that it can stigmatize entire countries based on the criminal behavior of particular institutions or officials.

Nevertheless, the concept captures a real and important phenomenon: the systematic, sustained, and multi-institutional penetration of state functions by drug trafficking organizations, to a degree that the distinction between the criminal and the legitimate becomes analytically blurred. Scholars often prefer to speak of narco-state conditions or dynamics rather than narco-states as fixed categories, recognizing that the phenomenon exists on a spectrum and that conditions can change over time in response to institutional reforms, political transitions, and shifts in criminal power.

In its most extreme form, a narco-state involves trafficking organizations that control territory, administer a form of justice, provide social services, and exercise de facto sovereignty in areas where the formal state is absent or complicit in criminal activity. In less extreme forms, it may describe a situation in which specific institutions, such as a particular police force, a customs agency, or a group of legislators, have been systematically corrupted, while other institutions remain functional.

Section 24.1

Defining the Narco-State: A Spectrum of Conditions

The term narco-state entered public and scholarly discourse in the 1980s and 1990s, initially as a polemical label applied to countries where drug trafficking had become visibly entangled with state institutions. Panama under Manuel Noriega, who was indicted on drug trafficking charges in 1988 while still serving as the country's de facto ruler, was an early example that crystallized popular understanding of the concept. Noriega's Panama was a state whose head of government was simultaneously a participant in the drug trade he was nominally required to suppress, whose military received payments from Colombian cocaine traffickers in exchange for allowing the use of Panamanian territory, banking, and flag services for trafficking operations. The formal apparatus of state, the uniforms, the offices, the official titles, was being used to facilitate criminal enterprise.

But Panama under Noriega was an extreme case, and the concept of the narco-state is most useful not when applied to such obviously extreme examples, but when used to analyze the far more common and far more complicated situations in which some parts of the state are compromised by drug trafficking connections while others function with relative integrity, in which corruption is widespread but not universal, in which criminal organizations exercise de facto control over some territories while the formal state maintains effective authority in others. These mixed or partial narco-state conditions are the norm rather than the exception in countries where drug trafficking has achieved significant scale, and they are analytically more challenging precisely because they defy the simple binary of state versus criminal organization.

Political scientists and criminologists have developed several analytical frameworks for understanding the relationship between criminal organizations and state institutions. The concept of state capture describes a situation in which private interests, criminal or otherwise, gain sufficient influence over state decision-making processes that those processes are redirected from public purposes toward private benefit. In the context of drug trafficking, state capture describes the situation in which trafficking organizations gain sufficient leverage over particular state institutions, whether through bribery, intimidation, personnel placement, or campaign financing, that those institutions become instruments of criminal interest rather than public interest.

The concept of criminal governance extends this analysis to territorial and social dimensions. Scholars including Diego Gambetta and Ioan Grillo have described how criminal organizations in territories where the state is absent or compromised frequently develop governance-like functions: providing dispute resolution, enforcing informal contracts, organizing community security against rival groups, levying informal taxes on economic activity, and in some cases providing social services including schools, clinics, and food distribution. These governance functions are not altruistic: they serve the organizational interests of criminal groups by creating stable environments for their economic operations and by generating social support or at least passive tolerance from resident populations. But they complicate the simple picture of criminal organizations as purely predatory actors and raise difficult questions about what governance means when its functions are performed by groups outside the formal state.

The geographic dimensions of narco-state conditions are also important for analysis. In virtually all cases of significant criminal governance, there is a spatial pattern in which criminal control or influence is strongest in peripheral regions, border areas, rural communities, and urban neighborhoods characterized by poverty and state absence, while it is weakest in capital cities and in spaces directly under the surveillance of international observers. This spatial pattern reflects both the strategic logic of criminal organizations, which prefer to operate in spaces where law enforcement presence is limited, and the structural limits of weak states, which concentrate their limited institutional capacity in centers of population and political power while leaving peripheral territories inadequately governed. The narco-state, understood spatially, is typically not a uniform phenomenon spread evenly across national territory, but a complex patchwork in which state authority and criminal authority exist in contested and shifting relationships across different geographic spaces.

Measuring Corruption: The Limits of Indicators

Scholars and international institutions have developed numerous indices and measures attempting to quantify corruption and institutional weakness, from Transparency International's Corruption Perceptions Index to the World Bank's Worldwide Governance Indicators. These measures provide useful comparative information, but they also have significant limitations that students of narco-state dynamics must understand. First, most indices measure perceptions of corruption rather than corruption itself, and perceptions can be shaped by media coverage, cultural factors, and political campaigns that do not accurately reflect the actual distribution of corrupt behavior. Second, indices typically aggregate information across entire countries, masking the significant variation in corruption levels across different institutions, regions, and levels of government within the same country. Third, indices capture a snapshot in time and may miss the dynamics of corruption, whether it is deepening, stabilizing, or declining, and what drives those changes.

For students of narco-states, the most important analytical task is not to rank countries on a corruption index but to understand the mechanisms by which criminal organizations penetrate specific institutions, the conditions that make those institutions vulnerable, and the processes by which corruption spreads from individual actors to systemic practice. These mechanisms and conditions are examined in detail in the sections that follow.

Section 24.2

State Capture: How Criminal Organizations Penetrate Institutions

The penetration of state institutions by drug trafficking organizations does not happen all at once, and it rarely happens through a dramatic seizure of power. It happens gradually, through processes that are individually unremarkable and often barely distinguishable from the ordinary workings of political patronage and informal governance that characterize many states around the world. Understanding state capture requires understanding its mechanisms at the level of individual transactions and institutional relationships, and then understanding how those individual transactions aggregate into systemic corruption.

The Mechanism of Bribery: Plata o Plomo

The most basic mechanism of institutional penetration is the bribe. In the context of drug trafficking, bribery typically begins at the points of enforcement closest to criminal operations: the police officer who works a neighborhood where drug sales occur, the customs inspector who processes shipments at a port of entry, the local judge who handles low-level drug cases. These individuals are offered financial payments in exchange for specific services: looking the other way, failing to file reports, allowing shipments to pass, reducing charges, or providing advance warning of enforcement operations. The offers are typically framed not as choices but as requirements: the trafficking organization needs the cooperation of the official in question, and it is prepared to obtain that cooperation either through payment or through threats of violence against the official or the official's family.

The Mexican phrase plata o plomo, silver or lead, captures the essential structure of this coercive offer with striking economy. The official is not simply being asked to accept a bribe; the official is being told that refusal carries a lethal cost. For a poorly paid police officer in a town where the cartel has already killed colleagues who refused similar offers, the rational calculation is terrifyingly clear. The payment is significant relative to the official's formal salary, often representing months or years of income, and the alternative is death or the death of family members. These are not the conditions under which individual moral courage can reasonably be expected to produce institutional integrity. They are the conditions under which corruption is structurally enforced.

The systemic effects of plata o plomo extend beyond the individual officials who receive bribes or submit to threats. In police forces and military units where some officers have been compromised, others face pressure to remain silent about the corruption of their colleagues, because reporting corruption to supervisors who may themselves be on cartel payrolls can result in lethal retaliation. Intelligence about enforcement operations is shared with cartel contacts by corrupted officers, meaning that operations planned against trafficking networks fail consistently, not because of incompetence but because of betrayal. The culture of impunity, in which corruption is visible, known, and unpunished, creates powerful incentives for new recruits and previously honest officers to conclude that institutional integrity is both futile and dangerous. Corruption becomes self-perpetuating, spreading through institutions not only through active recruitment by criminal organizations but through the passive resignation of those who recognize the costs of resistance.

Historical Vocabulary
State Capture

State capture refers to the process by which private interests, including criminal organizations, gain sufficient leverage over public institutions that those institutions are effectively redirected from their public mandates toward private benefit. In the context of drug trafficking, state capture describes the systematic penetration of law enforcement, judicial, military, legislative, and executive institutions by trafficking organizations to a degree that those institutions cannot independently enforce laws against the criminal groups that have compromised them.

State capture differs from ordinary corruption in scale and systematicity. Individual corruption involves individual officials accepting bribes for individual favors. State capture involves the systematic, sustained, multi-institutional penetration of governance by criminal interests, to a degree that alters the fundamental operating logic of affected institutions. A corrupted individual official can be arrested and replaced. A captured institution requires much more fundamental intervention, including vetting of personnel, restructuring of incentives, and often the external oversight of reformed recruitment and supervision processes.

The concept was developed initially by World Bank economists analyzing post-Soviet transition economies, where well-connected business oligarchs captured regulatory and legislative institutions to entrench their economic advantages. In the context of drug trafficking, the same dynamic operates with the added dimension of violence as a tool of enforcement, which makes state capture by criminal organizations both faster-moving and more difficult to reverse than the economic state capture that occurs in non-violent political economies.

Penetrating the Judiciary: The Corruption of Courts

For drug trafficking organizations, the corruption of police forces, while valuable, is insufficient if independent judiciaries can still convict arrested traffickers and imprison them. The corruption of the judiciary is therefore a necessary complement to the corruption of law enforcement, and it takes multiple forms. At the most direct level, judges presiding over drug cases may be bribed to acquit defendants, reduce charges, exclude evidence on procedural grounds, or impose minimal sentences. Where judges resist bribery, they face the alternative instrument of plata o plomo: threats against themselves or family members that may be backed by the demonstrated willingness of trafficking organizations to assassinate judicial officials. In Colombia, Mexico, Brazil, Honduras, and elsewhere, judges, prosecutors, and court officials who have handled significant trafficking cases have been murdered, and these murders function not only to eliminate specific individuals but to communicate to the entire judiciary the personal costs of conducting vigorous enforcement.

At a more systemic level, trafficking organizations may seek to influence the appointment processes that determine which individuals become judges, prosecutors, and court administrators. Where judicial appointments flow through political channels, and where those political channels are themselves influenced by cartel money, the judiciary can be shaped over time by the placement of officials whose loyalties are aligned with criminal interests, even before any specific case arises that requires direct corruption. This form of institutional penetration is particularly difficult to address because it does not require the commission of any obviously corrupt act at the time of appointment: the judge or prosecutor in question may have been appointed through entirely legal processes by political officials who themselves received cartel support without any explicit quid pro quo being recorded.

Penetrating the Military: A Special Challenge

Military forces present a particular challenge for the analysis of state capture, because they are simultaneously among the most powerful instruments that states can deploy against criminal organizations and among the institutions most targeted for infiltration by those organizations. The recruitment of military personnel by criminal organizations provides access to weapons, training, intelligence about enforcement operations, and in some cases the direct operational capability of military units. In Mexico, the Zetas cartel originated as a group of former Mexican Army special forces soldiers who had been recruited by the Gulf Cartel in the 1990s, bringing their military training, equipment connections, and tactical expertise into criminal service. The Zetas' subsequent emergence as an independent and extremely violent criminal organization is in part a story of what happens when military institutional capacity is transferred to criminal enterprise.

In Guatemala, similarly, the Kaibiles, an elite counterinsurgency unit whose members were trained in extreme violence during the civil war that ended in 1996, became a major recruitment pool for the Zetas and other criminal organizations seeking trained killers willing to perform the most brutal operational functions of cartel violence. The movement of military-trained personnel into criminal organizations blurs the distinction between state violence and criminal violence in ways that are particularly damaging to institutional legitimacy and public trust.

Section 24.3

Political Influence: From Local Elections to National Power

The penetration of political institutions by drug trafficking organizations operates at multiple levels simultaneously, from the financing of municipal candidates to the corruption of national intelligence services. Understanding this multi-level political influence is essential for understanding why narco-state conditions prove so difficult to address through political means: the very institutions that would need to lead reform efforts, legislatures, executives, intelligence agencies, are frequently among those that have been most thoroughly compromised.

Campaign Financing and Electoral Politics

In political systems where campaign financing is inadequately regulated and where candidates for office depend on raising significant private funds, drug trafficking organizations represent an extraordinarily wealthy potential source of political support. The injection of cartel money into electoral politics takes multiple forms. At the most direct level, trafficking organizations may make substantial cash contributions to particular candidates or parties in exchange for specific commitments: the appointment of particular individuals to law enforcement or judicial positions, the award of government contracts to cartel-affiliated businesses, or simply the guarantee that law enforcement in the relevant jurisdiction will not aggressively pursue the trafficking organization's operations. These contributions are typically not formal, reported donations; they flow through informal channels, including cash payments to campaign operatives, the provision of in-kind services, and the mobilization of community networks controlled by criminal organizations to deliver votes for favored candidates.

The political consequences of this financing are multi-dimensional. Candidates who accept cartel support become, in varying degrees, clients of the organizations that funded their campaigns, constrained in their capacity to take independent action against those organizations even if they might otherwise wish to do so. Politicians who refuse cartel support may find themselves facing well-financed opponents, negative campaigning drawn on information held by criminal intelligence networks, or outright violence. In extreme cases, criminal organizations have assassinated candidates who threaten their interests, as in Colombia in the 1980s and early 1990s, when the Medellín Cartel murdered presidential candidates, Supreme Court justices, and hundreds of police officers as instruments of political intimidation.

The relationship between trafficking organizations and political parties is rarely simple or stable. Cartels typically hedge their political investments, supporting multiple candidates in the same race, switching allegiances as electoral fortunes change, and maintaining relationships with officials across party lines. This investment strategy maximizes the likelihood that whoever wins power will owe some degree of obligation to the trafficking organization, while reducing the organization's vulnerability to the electoral defeat of any particular ally.

Intelligence Penetration: The Most Damaging Form

Of all the forms of institutional penetration, the corruption of intelligence services may be the most damaging to a state's capacity to address narco-state conditions, because it redirects the very instruments designed to understand and disrupt criminal networks toward protecting and serving those networks instead. When a national intelligence agency has been penetrated by cartel assets, the agency's information about law enforcement operations, surveillance capabilities, informant identities, and investigation targets becomes available to the criminal organizations it nominally monitors. Operations are compromised before they begin. Informants are identified and murdered. Investigative files are tampered with or destroyed. The intelligence apparatus of the state is turned into an instrument of criminal security.

In Mexico, the trial of former Secretary of Public Security Genaro García Luna in a United States federal court in 2023 produced evidence that illustrated the scale to which this penetration could reach. García Luna, who served as Mexico's top federal law enforcement official from 2006 to 2012, overseeing thousands of police officers and directing anti-drug operations during the most violent years of Calderón's drug war, was convicted of accepting millions of dollars in cartel bribes over the course of his career, including during his tenure as the official nominally responsible for fighting the cartels that paid him. Evidence presented at trial described meetings at which García Luna received briefcases of cash from Sinaloa Cartel representatives, and the systematic sharing of law enforcement intelligence with cartel contacts. The García Luna case, while extreme in its scope, illustrates the structural principle that the officials with the greatest access to enforcement capabilities are also the most valuable targets for criminal corruption, and that the power to fight organized crime is precisely the power that organized crime most needs to neutralize.

Section 24.4

Violence and Governance: Criminal Authority in Ungoverned Spaces

Drug trafficking organizations do not only seek to neutralize or corrupt state institutions. In spaces where the state is absent, ineffective, or has itself been captured, trafficking organizations frequently develop positive governance functions, establishing systems of order, dispute resolution, security provision, and social service that substitute for formal state authority. This phenomenon, described by scholars as criminal governance or parallel governance, challenges the simple picture of criminal organizations as purely predatory entities and raises profound questions about the nature of political authority and the conditions under which populations may come to prefer criminal governance to formal state authority.

The Logic of Criminal Governance

For a drug trafficking organization seeking to operate profitably, a stable and predictable local environment is an operational necessity. Drug production requires agricultural land, labor, and protection from rival organizations. Drug trafficking requires roads, ports, communication infrastructure, and the cooperation of local communities who might otherwise report cartel presence to authorities. Retail drug markets require stable territorial control in which distribution networks can operate consistently and in which customer relationships can be maintained over time. All of these operational requirements are served by a criminal organization's capacity to provide a measure of local order, to resolve disputes that might otherwise attract law enforcement attention, and to maintain the social relationships with local communities that provide intelligence, labor, and political cover.

The provision of criminal governance therefore follows a recognizable organizational logic: it serves the business interests of the trafficking organization while simultaneously creating forms of social dependency that make communities more difficult for rival organizations or state forces to mobilize against the controlling criminal group. Communities that receive social services from a cartel may be reluctant to cooperate with state enforcement operations that threaten those services. Communities that depend on cartel-provided security may fear the vacuum that would be created by cartel removal more than they fear cartel authority itself. This dynamic does not reflect some form of false consciousness on the part of community members; it reflects a rational assessment of actual institutional alternatives in contexts where the formal state has provided little protection and where its replacement by criminal governance has at least brought a measure of predictable order.

Violence as Communication

The violence associated with drug trafficking organizations is not simply a byproduct of criminal activity, an unfortunate externality of a profitable enterprise. It is, from the perspective of those who deploy it, a strategic instrument for communicating power, establishing hierarchy, enforcing compliance, deterring rivals, and demonstrating to state authorities the costs of aggressive enforcement. Understanding violence as strategic communication, rather than simply as criminal brutality, is essential for understanding why narco-state conditions are so difficult to address through law enforcement responses that treat violence as a problem to be suppressed rather than as a message to be read.

The spectacular forms of violence associated with Mexican cartels in particular, the public display of mutilated bodies, the narco-mantas or banners left at crime scenes with messages directed at rivals or authorities, the mass graves of victims, and the public executions filmed and circulated on social media, serve multiple communicative purposes simultaneously. They signal to rival organizations the capacity and willingness of the perpetrating group to use extreme violence against those who challenge its territorial authority. They signal to state forces the costs that aggressive enforcement will impose on officers and their families. They signal to local communities that resistance or cooperation with rival groups or state forces is not survivable. And they signal to political audiences, including international observers, the degree to which state authority has been hollowed out in the territories where this violence occurs.

The communicative function of cartel violence helps explain the particular targeting patterns that observers have noted. The murder of journalists in Mexico, which has made the country one of the most dangerous in the world for press freedom, is not primarily about suppressing any particular story, though cartel organizations certainly have an interest in limiting unfavorable coverage. It is about communicating to the entire journalism community that investigating cartel activity carries a lethal cost, establishing a chilling effect that suppresses inquiry far more broadly than the murder of any individual journalist could accomplish. Similarly, the targeting of human rights defenders, anti-corruption activists, and local political leaders who challenge cartel authority communicates to civil society organizations broadly that organized resistance to criminal power is dangerous, establishing the boundaries of permissible public life in territories under criminal influence.

Historical Vocabulary
Impunity

Impunity refers to the condition in which crimes, including crimes committed by state actors or criminal organizations in collaboration with state actors, are not investigated, prosecuted, or punished. In the context of narco-state conditions, impunity is both a symptom and a driver of criminal governance: it reflects the degree to which institutions responsible for accountability have been compromised, and it creates the conditions in which criminal violence can continue to escalate without consequence.

Mexico's impunity rate for violent crimes is frequently cited as among the highest in the world: various estimates suggest that more than 90 percent of serious crimes are never prosecuted, a figure that reflects not simply inadequate law enforcement capacity but the systematic penetration of justice institutions by criminal interests. When perpetrators of criminal violence know that they will not be held accountable, the deterrent function of law disappears and criminal violence becomes rationally attractive rather than rationally costly. Impunity, in this sense, is not simply a problem of institutional inadequacy; it is an institutional feature of narco-state conditions, actively maintained by the criminal organizations that benefit from it.

The concept of impunity is also relevant at the international level. When trafficking organizations launder money through financial systems, real estate markets, and corporate structures in wealthy countries where enforcement resources are concentrated on drug supply rather than drug finance, the financial infrastructure of narco-states is partially maintained by the impunity available in jurisdictions that nominally oppose drug trafficking but fail to prosecute its financial dimension with comparable vigor.

Paramilitaries and the Blurring of State and Criminal Violence

In a number of countries experiencing narco-state conditions, the formal boundary between state security forces and criminal organizations has been further blurred by the development of paramilitary organizations, armed groups that operate outside the formal legal structure of state military or police forces but that may be tacitly supported, funded, or equipped by state actors or political networks, and that perform functions that formal state forces are unwilling or unable to perform. In Colombia, the paramilitary organization known as the AUC, Autodefensas Unidas de Colombia, operated during the 1990s and 2000s as an ostensibly anti-guerrilla force with connections to both drug trafficking and elements of the Colombian state. The AUC was officially designated a terrorist organization by the United States and was responsible for numerous massacres of civilian populations, but evidence of its relationships with Colombian military officers, politicians, and economic elites complicated simple narratives about the boundaries between legitimate state violence and criminal violence.

In Mexico, the emergence of community self-defense organizations known as autodefensas in states like Michoacán and Guerrero in the early 2010s represented a different form of the state-criminal-community violence nexus. These organizations emerged in response to the failure of state security forces to protect communities from cartel violence and extortion, taking up arms to defend their communities when the formal state was unable or unwilling to do so. Some autodefensas maintained clear separation from cartel interests and were genuinely community-controlled; others were penetrated or co-opted by the cartels they nominally opposed, becoming in effect cartel security forces operating under the cover of community self-defense legitimacy. The Mexican government's attempts to formalize autodefensas into state-controlled rural police forces produced mixed results, with some units becoming valuable community-state security partnerships and others becoming mechanisms for cartel infiltration of formally structured security institutions.

Section 24.5

Case Studies in Narco-State Conditions

The phenomenon of narco-state conditions, while sharing structural features across contexts, manifests differently depending on the particular institutional histories, economic structures, geographic conditions, and political cultures of the societies in which it occurs. The following case studies examine several of the most intensively documented examples of narco-state dynamics, drawing out both common features and significant contextual differences.

Colombia: From Near Collapse to Partial Recovery

Colombia in the 1980s and early 1990s represented a case of acute narco-state challenge to state sovereignty. The Medellín Cartel under Pablo Escobar waged what amounted to a war against the Colombian state, assassinating a presidential candidate, a minister of justice, an attorney general, hundreds of police officers, and numerous judges and journalists. Escobar's stated demand was simple: the Colombian government should not extradite Colombian citizens to the United States for prosecution, because the U.S. judicial system was the only institution Escobar genuinely feared, operating as it did outside the reach of his domestic corruption network. The Colombian state's attempt to enforce extradition became the proximate cause of the most intense period of cartel violence in the country's history.

Colombia's partial institutional recovery from the peak of Medellín Cartel power is instructive for understanding what conditions can enable narco-state dynamics to be reduced. The elimination of Escobar in 1993 did not by itself reduce cartel power: the Cali Cartel, which had cooperated with the Colombian government against Escobar, subsequently became the dominant trafficking organization. The longer-term institutional improvements that reduced narco-state conditions in Colombia involved a combination of factors including significant U.S. financial and military support through Plan Colombia, beginning in 2000; internal political decisions that prioritized institutional reconstruction including judicial reform and the creation of protected witness programs for those willing to testify against trafficking networks; the negotiated demobilization of paramilitary organizations, imperfect and incomplete though it was; and gradual improvement in the economic conditions that made criminal labor markets attractive to poor young Colombians. None of these factors was sufficient alone, and Colombian narco-state conditions were reduced rather than eliminated. But the Colombian trajectory illustrates that narco-state dynamics are not irreversible.

Mexico: The Scale of Penetration

Mexico presents a case of narco-state conditions operating at extraordinary scale in a country with a population of approximately 130 million people, a formal economy that is among the largest in Latin America, and a democratic political system that has undergone significant formal institutional development since the end of one-party PRI dominance in 2000. The persistence and even intensification of narco-state conditions in Mexico during a period of formal democratic transition illustrates the degree to which democratic political reform does not automatically produce institutional reform in the security and judicial sectors most directly relevant to criminal governance.

The single-party system that governed Mexico under the PRI from 1929 to 2000 maintained a particular form of corruption management in which drug trafficking was tolerated in exchange for payments to the ruling party and a form of implicit regulation: cartels operated in designated territories, maintained acceptable levels of visible violence, and avoided direct challenges to state authority. This arrangement, sometimes described as a pax mafiosa, provided criminal organizations with stable operating conditions while providing the ruling party with a revenue stream and an informal intelligence network. The collapse of this arrangement with competitive democratization left a vacuum in which multiple cartels competed violently for territorial control without the stabilizing mechanism of single-party mediation, and in which the newly competitive political system became a target for cartel financing without the regularized corruption management that had characterized the PRI era.

The Ayotzinapa disappearances of September 2014 became the most emblematic single episode of Mexican narco-state conditions in the public consciousness of both Mexico and the world. Forty-three students from a rural teachers' college were abducted after a confrontation with local police in the town of Iguala, Guerrero. Subsequent investigations, including those conducted by an international independent panel, established that local police had handed the students over to the Guerreros Unidos criminal organization, which subsequently murdered them. The mayor of Iguala and his wife were implicated in ordering the action. The case illustrated not the corruption of peripheral, low-level officials, but the systematic integration of local government with criminal organization in a part of Mexico where the cartel was effectively the governing authority and local elected officials were effectively its employees.

Guinea-Bissau: The Extreme Case

Guinea-Bissau, a small West African nation of fewer than two million people that gained independence from Portugal in 1974, became in the 2000s and 2010s perhaps the most extreme case of narco-state conditions anywhere in the world outside active conflict zones. The country's military and political leadership was documented as directly participating in cocaine trafficking, not simply accepting bribes from traffickers but acting as active partners in transshipment operations that moved South American cocaine through Guinea-Bissau to Europe.

The conditions that made Guinea-Bissau so vulnerable to near-total state capture were the product of its particular post-independence history. The country experienced repeated military coups after independence, with no stable constitutional transfer of power occurring between the 1970s and the 2010s. The civil war of 1998 to 1999 destroyed much of the country's remaining institutional infrastructure. Military officers received nominal salaries that were often paid irregularly or not at all, creating a permanently monetarily desperate officer corps that had few alternatives to criminal cooperation. The judiciary had almost no capacity for independent investigation or prosecution. The country's long Atlantic coastline, with numerous islands and inlets, and its historically weak customs and port authority, made it geographically ideal for cocaine transshipment.

When South American cocaine trafficking organizations, primarily Venezuelan and Colombian networks in the first instance, began using West Africa as a transit hub for European-bound cocaine in the mid-2000s, Guinea-Bissau was the most vulnerable point of entry. Investigations by the United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime and by the United States Drug Enforcement Administration documented the direct involvement of senior military and political figures in facilitating cocaine shipments, including the head of the navy and a former prime minister. In 2009, the military chief of staff and the president were assassinated within hours of each other, in killings whose precise motivations remain disputed but that many observers linked to conflicts within the trafficking networks penetrating the country's institutions.

Afghanistan: War, Reconstruction, and the Drug Economy

Afghanistan's relationship with opium production and narco-state dynamics is distinctive in several ways. Unlike the Latin American and West African cases, which involve primarily the trafficking of drugs produced elsewhere, Afghanistan is the world's dominant producer of opium, the raw material for heroin, supplying the majority of the global heroin market. The drug economy is therefore not a foreign intrusion into Afghan governance but a deeply embedded feature of the Afghan rural economy, a major source of income for farmers in much of the country's south and west, and a major source of revenue for political and military actors across the political spectrum.

The Taliban, which governed most of Afghanistan from 1996 to 2001, had a complex and evolving relationship with opium production. In its early years, the Taliban taxed opium production as a source of revenue. In 2000, seeking international recognition and responding to Islamic prohibitions on drug use, the Taliban imposed a dramatic reduction in poppy cultivation that was one of the most successful forced eradication programs ever implemented, reducing opium production by more than 90 percent in a single year. After the collapse of Taliban governance following the U.S. invasion in late 2001, opium production surged to record levels, driven by the chaos of post-invasion conditions and the absence of any effective governance structure in major production areas.

The post-2001 Afghan state, built with enormous international financial support and staffed in significant part by former warlord commanders who had been integrated into the new government as the price of their cooperation with the U.S.-led coalition, faced an irresolvable tension at the heart of its institutional design. Many of the commanders who were simultaneously drawing salaries as government officials, serving as provincial governors or police chiefs or members of parliament, were also deriving significant incomes from taxing or protecting opium production in their territories. International assistance designed to build state capacity was flowing to officials whose personal economic interests were served by the continued weakness of the rule of law in opium-producing regions. The result was a state that formally opposed drug production while its officials substantially facilitated it, a classic narco-state condition at the level of individual institutional actors embedded within a formal governance structure.

Section 24.6

Everyday Life Under Narco Power: Ordinary People in Extraordinary Circumstances

The academic analysis of narco-states involves concepts, mechanisms, and structural frameworks that are essential for understanding the phenomenon at the level of institutions and power. But behind those frameworks are the lived experiences of millions of ordinary people, farmers and shopkeepers, police officers and teachers, mothers and journalists, who navigate daily life in conditions of criminal governance with limited choices and at constant risk. Understanding those experiences is not supplementary to the analysis of narco-state conditions; it is central to understanding what narco-states actually are and what is at stake in efforts to address them.

The Farmer's Dilemma: Coca and Poppy as Survival Crops

In the coca-growing regions of Colombia and Peru, and in the opium-growing regions of Afghanistan and Myanmar, a significant proportion of the rural population depends on illicit crop production for its economic survival. These are not typically people who have made ideological commitments to the drug trade or who are indifferent to the social consequences of drug consumption in distant consumer markets. They are, in the vast majority of cases, poor farmers who have concluded, on the basis of accurate economic calculation, that coca or poppy cultivation provides a more reliable income than any available legal crop alternative, given the market access limitations, infrastructure deficiencies, and price volatility that characterize legal agricultural markets in the remote regions where drug crops are typically grown.

The farmer's relationship with the trafficking organization that purchases their crop is typically one of dependency and vulnerability rather than partnership or solidarity. Trafficking organizations set purchase prices, impose production requirements, and may use violence or threats of violence against farmers who attempt to sell to rival organizations or to cooperate with eradication programs. Farmers who depend on coca or poppy income have little leverage to negotiate these terms. Eradication programs that destroy crops without providing viable economic alternatives leave farming families without income while doing little to reduce the overall supply of drugs to consumer markets, since production rapidly relocates to other regions or countries as eradicated farmers elsewhere continue or expand production.

The social geography of drug-crop farming communities illustrates a fundamental tension in narco-state conditions: the populations most immediately present in drug production areas are not the beneficiaries of the drug economy but among its most constrained victims, dependent on criminal organizations for their economic survival, subject to the violence that trafficking organizations deploy to maintain control, and targeted by state enforcement programs that treat them as criminals rather than as people trapped in an economic situation not of their making.

The Police Officer's Choice

The police officer in a municipality where a cartel operates faces choices that illuminate the structural conditions that produce systemic corruption more clearly than any abstract institutional analysis. In many parts of Mexico, municipal police officers earn salaries equivalent to a few hundred dollars per month. They work in departments where equipment, vehicles, and communications technology are inadequate. They receive limited training. They operate in communities where cartel representatives know who they are, where they live, and who their families are. When a cartel representative approaches a police officer with an offer of several thousand dollars in exchange for sharing information about planned operations, the calculation the officer faces is not simply between corruption and integrity. It is between cooperation and the plausible risk of death for themselves and their families.

In departments where multiple officers have already been compromised, the honest officer faces an additional problem: the officers who would normally serve as colleagues and sources of institutional support are themselves working for the cartel, and reporting the corruption of colleagues to supervisors who may themselves be on cartel payrolls can result in lethal retaliation. The isolation of honest officers within corrupt institutions is a structural feature of advanced narco-state conditions, not simply a personal failing of individuals who happen to work alongside corrupt colleagues.

The experience of honest police officers attempting to work in conditions of systemic cartel corruption has been extensively documented by journalists and researchers in Mexico. Those who have spoken publicly, often from the protection of locations outside Mexico or from protection programs, describe a world in which the norms of institutional loyalty have been effectively inverted: in which cooperating with cartel representatives is the norm and resisting cooperation is the deviant behavior that invites sanction. This inversion of institutional norms, in which corruption becomes the standard operating procedure and integrity becomes the dangerous exception, is perhaps the most corrosive long-term effect of narco-state conditions on state institutions.

Journalists, Activists, and the Costs of Witnessing

Mexico has consistently ranked among the most dangerous countries in the world for journalists, with dozens of reporters, editors, and media workers murdered in the two decades since Calderón's war on drugs accelerated cartel violence. The vast majority of these murders have never been prosecuted, reflecting both the institutional corruption that provides impunity for cartel violence and the specific corruption of law enforcement institutions in communities where journalists covering cartel activity are most vulnerable. The pattern of journalist murders in Mexico is not random: it concentrates in the states and municipalities where cartel territorial control is strongest, and it targets local and regional journalists who cover corruption, local crime, and the specific activities of the cartels operating in their communities, not primarily the international correspondents of major news organizations, who have the partial protection of institutional prominence and foreign embassies.

The murder of journalists represents only the most extreme point on a spectrum of pressure against civil society actors who attempt to document or resist narco-state conditions. Human rights defenders, anti-corruption activists, environmental advocates resisting cartel encroachment on natural resources, teachers working in communities under cartel control, and community leaders who attempt to organize resistance to cartel authority all face systematic threats, displacement, and in many cases murder. These attacks are not, from the cartel's perspective, simply about silencing particular individuals. They are about establishing the boundaries of permissible public life in territories under criminal influence, communicating to the entire civil society of an affected region that organized resistance to criminal authority is not survivable.

Felipe Calderón's Declaration of War, December 2006

Creator: Felipe Calderón, President of Mexico · Date: December 2006 · Type: Presidential address and military deployment order

Context: In December 2006, newly inaugurated Mexican President Felipe Calderón, whose electoral victory over Andrés Manuel López Obrador had been contested and who faced immediate questions of political legitimacy, deployed approximately 6,500 federal soldiers to the state of Michoacán to confront drug trafficking organizations that had virtually displaced state authority in parts of the state. The deployment was presented as an assertion of sovereignty and a demonstration of political will against criminal power. It initiated what became known as the Mexican Drug War, a period of extraordinary violence that claimed an estimated 150,000 to 200,000 lives between 2006 and 2021 and displaced hundreds of thousands more.

In his public statements accompanying the deployment, Calderón framed the military campaign as the reclaiming of territory and institutional authority from criminal organizations that had effectively usurped governance functions in parts of Mexico. He described the drug trafficking organizations as a threat not merely to public safety but to Mexican sovereignty itself, and argued that the state had a constitutional obligation to reassert its monopoly on legitimate violence in territories where criminal organizations had displaced state authority. The deployment, he suggested, was not a choice but a necessity, required by the constitutional duty of the state to protect its citizens.

Analysis: Calderón's framing of the military campaign deserves careful analysis because it encapsulates both the genuine challenge that narco-state conditions pose to state sovereignty and the significant risks associated with a militarized response to that challenge. His diagnosis of the problem, that criminal organizations had effectively captured territorial governance functions in parts of Mexico, was largely accurate. His prescription, military deployment, reflected a particular theory of how narco-state conditions could be addressed, through overwhelming force rather than institutional reform, that proved deeply problematic in execution. The subsequent decade produced overwhelming evidence that military force, in the absence of simultaneous institutional reform of police, judicial, and political institutions, not only failed to reduce narco-state conditions but in many cases intensified them, as competing cartels fragmented into more numerous and more violent organizations, as military operations generated civilian casualties that delegitimized the state in the communities they were intended to protect, and as the ongoing corruption of security institutions meant that military intelligence was routinely compromised by cartel penetration.

Perspective gap: Calderón's public statements represent the perspective of a national executive seeking to assert state authority. Missing from this framing are the perspectives of communities in Michoacán who lived under conditions of criminal governance, many of whom had more immediate and complex relationships with local cartel power than the simple victim-perpetrator binary that official rhetoric suggested; of the soldiers deployed to conduct military operations in civilian communities; and of the cartel members themselves, whose organizational responses to military pressure, including fragmentation, recruitment escalation, and intensified violence, shaped the subsequent trajectory of the drug war in ways that Calderón's initial framing did not anticipate.

Section 24.7

International Dimensions: Demand, Finance, and Complicity

Narco-state conditions in producer and transit countries are not simply internal governance failures. They are products of a global system in which the demand for illegal drugs in wealthy consumer countries, primarily but not exclusively in North America and Europe, generates the extraordinary revenues that drug trafficking organizations deploy to capture state institutions. The international dimensions of narco-state dynamics include not only the trafficking routes and financial flows that connect producer, transit, and consumer countries, but the political relationships, legal frameworks, development assistance programs, and financial regulatory environments of consumer-country governments that shape the conditions in which producer and transit-country narco-state dynamics operate.

Demand as Driver

The fundamental economic driver of narco-state conditions is consumer demand for illegal drugs in wealthy countries. The United States alone spends an estimated 150 billion dollars annually on illegal drugs, with cocaine, heroin, methamphetamine, and fentanyl accounting for the largest shares of that market. European drug markets are comparable in total value. These markets generate revenues that dwarf the gross domestic products of many producer and transit countries, and even a small fraction of those revenues, retained in the supply chain at the production, processing, and transit stages, is sufficient to overwhelm the financial capacity of the state institutions nominally tasked with controlling trafficking.

The responsibility of consumer countries for the narco-state conditions of producer and transit countries is a politically contested but analytically important dimension of the problem. Consumer-country governments that pursue drug supply reduction strategies, including eradication of production, interdiction of trafficking, and institution-building in supplier countries, while maintaining domestic drug policies that ensure continued demand for illegal drugs, are in effect attempting to address the symptoms of a market while preserving the demand conditions that sustain that market. Supply reduction efforts, in the absence of significant demand reduction, simply raise the price of drugs in consumer markets, which increases the revenue available to trafficking organizations and intensifies the economic incentives for corruption and violence in producer and transit countries.

Money and Finance: The Consumer-Country Dimension

The profits generated by drug trafficking must be laundered, that is, converted from the cash proceeds of illegal activity into assets that can be enjoyed, invested, or deployed without attracting the attention of financial authorities. The money laundering dimension of narco-state dynamics has a significant consumer-country component that is frequently underemphasized in discussions that focus on corruption in producer and transit countries.

Cartel money flows into consumer-country financial systems through real estate purchases, shell company formation, trade-based money laundering, cash deposits in financial institutions with inadequate anti-money laundering controls, and investment in legitimate businesses ranging from restaurants and retail stores to professional sports teams. The United States, the United Kingdom, Canada, and other wealthy countries have financial regulatory environments that, despite nominally stringent anti-money laundering requirements, have in practice allowed large volumes of drug trafficking proceeds to enter and circulate in legitimate financial systems. The purchases of luxury real estate in Miami, Los Angeles, New York, London, and other major markets by anonymous shell companies, many of which have been linked to drug trafficking proceeds in subsequent investigations, represent a form of consumer-country complicity in narco-state dynamics that receives far less attention than the corruption of police officers in Michoacán or customs officials in Guinea-Bissau.

The Role of U.S. Policy: Plan Colombia and the Mérida Initiative

The United States government has been a major external actor in efforts to address narco-state conditions in Latin American countries, providing financial assistance, military equipment, training, intelligence cooperation, and diplomatic pressure through programs including Plan Colombia (launched in 2000 and providing more than ten billion dollars in assistance over two decades) and the Mérida Initiative (launched in 2008 to provide security assistance to Mexico and Central America). These programs reflect a theory that narco-state conditions can be addressed primarily through security sector assistance that increases the capacity of partner-country security forces to suppress trafficking organizations and the corruption they generate.

The results of these programs have been mixed in ways that illuminate the limitations of capacity-building assistance when institutional corruption is systemic. Plan Colombia has been credited with significant improvements in Colombian security conditions, reduction in homicide rates, and partial stabilization of state authority in territory previously under guerrilla and cartel control. It has been criticized for funding military and police units that engaged in serious human rights abuses, for the displacement of coca cultivation rather than its reduction, and for failing to address the poverty conditions that make poor Colombian farmers dependent on coca production. The Mérida Initiative has been criticized for providing security assistance to Mexican institutions, including the police force headed by Genaro García Luna, whose corruption was documented precisely during the period in which U.S. assistance was flowing to his agency.

Historical Debate

Does Militarized Drug Enforcement Reduce or Intensify Narco-State Conditions?

The question: When governments face drug trafficking organizations that have deeply penetrated their institutions, should they respond primarily with military force aimed at suppressing trafficking organizations directly, or should they prioritize institutional reform of the security and judicial sectors? Or are these approaches complementary rather than competing? The debate goes to the heart of what narco-state conditions are and what can address them.

Interpretation A — Military Enforcement as Necessary Assertion of Sovereignty: Proponents of the militarized approach argue that narco-state conditions represent a fundamental challenge to state sovereignty that can only be addressed by the credible assertion of state military power. In this view, drug trafficking organizations have filled a vacuum of state authority with criminal governance, and restoring legitimate governance requires first displacing the criminal organizations that have usurped it. Military force, supplemented by intelligence cooperation with international partners and by police reform over a longer timeframe, is the necessary precondition for the institutional rebuilding that can sustain reduced narco-state conditions over time. Plan Colombia's partial successes are cited as evidence that security-focused approaches can work when adequately resourced and sustained.
Interpretation B — Militarization as Counterproductive: Critics of the militarized approach, including many Latin American scholars, civil society organizations, and some policymakers, argue that military enforcement of drug laws intensifies rather than reduces narco-state conditions in several ways. Military campaigns that fragment large trafficking organizations into multiple smaller, more violent competitors do not reduce the total volume of drug trafficking but do dramatically increase the violence associated with it. Military operations in civilian communities generate human rights abuses that delegitimize the state among precisely the populations whose cooperation is most needed for intelligence and community policing. And military responses to fundamentally economic problems, drug production and trafficking driven by poverty and consumer demand, address the symptom while leaving the structural conditions intact.

Evidence: Mexico's experience between 2006 and the present provides the most extensive evidence base for this debate, and it supports conclusions that challenge advocates of both positions. Calderón's military campaign did not reduce trafficking or narco-state conditions; violence increased dramatically and cartel territorial control expanded in many regions. But the alternative, tolerating or negotiating with cartel governance, also proved untenable for subsequent governments. Colombia's more positive trajectory suggests that a combination of military and institutional approaches can produce improvement, but Colombia's specific conditions, including significant U.S. financial support and a particular configuration of guerrilla, paramilitary, and cartel organizations, may not be directly replicable elsewhere.

Student task: Based on the case studies in this chapter, develop an argument about what combination of strategies would most effectively address narco-state conditions in a country like Mexico. What evidence supports your position? What counter-arguments must your position address?

Section 24.8

Resistance and Reform: Challenging Criminal Governance

The analysis of narco-state conditions as structural phenomena shaped by institutional history, economic inequality, and the extraordinary financial power of drug trafficking organizations can, if taken too far, produce a deterministic account in which criminal governance is inevitable and irreversible. The historical record does not support that conclusion. In multiple countries and at multiple levels, individuals, communities, civil society organizations, and reform-minded governments have successfully challenged and reduced narco-state conditions, sometimes at extraordinary personal cost, and sometimes with lasting institutional consequences. Understanding these cases of resistance and reform is as important as understanding the mechanisms of state capture.

Civil Society Resistance

Among the most remarkable forms of resistance to criminal governance are the civil society movements that have emerged in communities under cartel control to assert the right to public life, institutional accountability, and freedom from criminal extortion. In Mexico, the murder of Javier Sicilia's son in 2011 catalyzed the formation of the Movement for Peace with Justice and Dignity, which brought together victims of cartel violence and families of the disappeared in a national movement demanding accountability, institutional reform, and a reorientation of the government's approach to the drug war away from militarization and toward addressing the social and economic conditions that sustain criminal labor markets. The movement did not achieve all of its demands, but it created a national political space for discussing narco-state conditions that had previously been suppressed by both cartel intimidation and government reluctance to acknowledge the depth of institutional corruption.

In communities under direct cartel territorial control, resistance has taken more localized forms, including the indigenous community self-defense movements in Michoacán and Guerrero that organized to expel cartel organizations from their communities, the community radio stations that continued broadcasting in regions where mainstream media had been silenced by cartel intimidation, and the networks of human rights observers who documented cartel violence and state complicity despite constant personal risk. These forms of local resistance are fragile, vulnerable to cartel retaliation, and rarely capable of producing sustained structural change on their own. But they demonstrate that narco-state conditions do not produce universal submission, and that communities retain agency even in conditions of extreme criminal governance.

Institutional Reform: What Has Worked

Effective reduction of narco-state conditions at the institutional level has, where it has occurred, involved several common elements that cut across the specific national contexts in which it has taken place. First, the vetting and restructuring of law enforcement institutions, replacing compromised personnel, creating new institutions less embedded in existing corruption networks, and establishing oversight mechanisms with sufficient independence and resources to identify and sanction corrupt conduct. Second, judicial reform that increases the independence, resources, and accountability of courts and prosecutors, enabling effective prosecution of trafficking-related corruption cases including those involving senior officials. Third, financial reforms that increase transparency in campaign financing, government contracting, and public official asset disclosure, reducing the opacity that allows cartel financial penetration of political institutions to operate invisibly. Fourth, international cooperation, particularly with the United States, in intelligence sharing, asset forfeiture, and the prosecution of trafficking cases in jurisdictions beyond the reach of domestic cartel corruption networks.

These institutional reforms are not sufficient alone: they require sustained political will from leaders willing to bear the personal risks of challenging cartel power, the development of constituencies within civil society and political coalitions that can sustain reform pressure over time, and international support that goes beyond security assistance to include the economic development investment that can reduce the poverty conditions sustaining criminal labor markets. The Colombian case suggests that when these conditions are combined with adequate resources over a sufficient timeframe, significant improvement in narco-state conditions is achievable. The persistence of narco-state dynamics in Mexico, despite significant reform efforts, suggests that the depth of institutional penetration matters enormously, and that addressing narco-state conditions at the level of a federal system of 130 million people is a different order of challenge from addressing them in a smaller country with a particular configuration of criminal organizations.

Multiple Perspectives

Who Experiences Narco-State Conditions? Competing Perspectives

The Rural Farmer in a Drug-Producing Region: For a coca farmer in the Colombian department of Nariño or a poppy farmer in Afghanistan's Helmand Province, the drug economy is not an abstraction but a livelihood. Legal crop alternatives may be unavailable, unprofitable, or geographically inaccessible. The trafficking organization that purchases their crop is also the organization that provides the only security available in their community, the only source of dispute resolution, and sometimes the only source of credit. State eradication programs that destroy their crops without providing alternatives represent not law enforcement but economic devastation. Their experience of narco-state conditions is simultaneously victimization by criminal power and dependency on the criminal economy for basic survival.
The Urban Middle-Class Resident: For middle-class residents of cities like Culiacán, Medellín, or Monterrey, the experience of narco-state conditions involves a complex negotiation between the visible presence of cartel wealth in urban culture, the real risks of kidnapping and extortion that affect business activity, the unreliability of state security institutions that may be working for the cartels their clients are reporting crimes to, and the extraordinary spectacle of cartel violence that periodically erupts in neighborhoods adjacent to their own. Some middle-class residents benefit economically from cartel-generated demand for luxury goods, real estate, and services. Others have lost family members to cartel violence. Most navigate a daily calculation about what to know, what to say, and what to ignore that is itself a product of narco-state conditions.
The Law Enforcement Officer: As discussed earlier in this chapter, the experience of law enforcement personnel in narco-state conditions is shaped by the coercive power of plata o plomo, by the institutional culture of agencies where corruption is the norm, and by the genuine danger faced by officers who attempt to work with integrity. But the perspective of law enforcement is not monolithic: some officers have become important sources of institutional resistance to cartel penetration, at extraordinary personal risk. Others have become active participants in cartel operations, not merely passive recipients of bribes but active informants, enablers, and in some cases perpetrators of cartel violence. The range of individual responses to institutional corruption illustrates both the structural power of narco-state conditions and the space for individual agency within those conditions.
The Cartel Recruit: The young men, and sometimes women, who join cartel organizations are themselves products of the conditions that narco-state dynamics create and perpetuate. Research on cartel recruitment in Mexico consistently identifies poverty, family disruption, school dropout, limited formal employment opportunity, and the absence of meaningful state social services as the primary factors that make criminal recruitment attractive. For a young man in a Guerrero village where cartel presence is total and formal employment is nonexistent, the choice between cartel recruitment and economic marginalization is not presented by the literature on organized crime but by the concrete circumstances of daily life. Understanding cartel recruitment as a product of structural conditions does not excuse the violence that cartel members commit, but it is essential for understanding why recruitment continues despite enforcement efforts and why reducing narco-state conditions requires addressing the socioeconomic conditions that sustain criminal labor markets.
Cause and Effect

The Institutional Collapse of Governance: How Narco-States Develop

Long-Term Causes

Historical institutional weakness; legacies of authoritarian governance; high economic inequality; limited formal employment opportunities; inadequate civil service salaries; weak judicial independence; geographic vulnerabilities to trafficking (coastlines, border regions, mountain corridors); consumer-country demand for illegal drugs.

Immediate Triggers

Entry of highly profitable trafficking organization into region; systematic recruitment of law enforcement through plata o plomo; assassination of officials who resist; penetration of political campaign financing; cartel provision of social services creating community dependency; impunity for cartel violence establishing culture of submission.

The Process

Individual corruption spreads to institutional corruption; culture of impunity normalizes criminal conduct; honest officials are isolated, threatened, or killed; cartel intelligence penetration makes enforcement operations ineffective; parallel governance structures replace state functions in peripheral territories; criminal organizations effectively exercise sovereignty in contested zones.

Consequences

Dramatic escalation of violence against civilians, journalists, and civil society actors; displacement of hundreds of thousands; economic distortion as criminal revenue structures local economies; loss of state legitimacy among populations who receive better governance from criminal organizations than from the formal state; intergenerational normalization of criminal authority.

Historical complexity: Narco-state conditions do not develop uniformly or inevitably. They represent the intersection of structural vulnerabilities with the specific strategic choices of criminal organizations, and they develop at different rates and to different depths depending on the particular configuration of institutional strength, criminal organizational capacity, and external conditions in each case. Understanding this complexity is essential for identifying the points at which intervention is most likely to be effective.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Corruption and Criminal Governance: What Changes and What Persists

What Changes

  • The specific criminal organizations that exercise influence change as some are dismantled through enforcement or internal conflict and new ones emerge
  • The geographic distribution of criminal governance shifts as enforcement pressure moves cartel activity from one region or country to another
  • The methods of cartel violence and intimidation evolve, incorporating new communications technologies, new weapons, and new forms of spectacular public violence
  • International legal frameworks for addressing narco-state conditions have developed since the 1980s, including enhanced mutual legal assistance treaties and asset forfeiture mechanisms
  • In some countries, including Colombia and parts of Mexico, sustained institutional reform has reduced the depth of cartel institutional penetration in specific agencies over specific periods

What Stays the Same

  • Consumer demand for illegal drugs in wealthy countries has remained high throughout the period under study, sustaining the revenues that enable cartel institutional penetration
  • The structural economic conditions, poverty, inequality, limited formal employment, that sustain criminal labor market recruitment have persisted in most producing and transit countries
  • The fundamental mechanisms of institutional corruption, the bribe, the threat, the placement of cartel associates in enforcement positions, have remained consistent across decades and across national contexts
  • Impunity for cartel violence and cartel corruption has remained extraordinarily high in most affected countries throughout the period
  • The spatial pattern of narco-state conditions, strongest in peripheral regions and weakest in capital cities and internationally visible spaces, has remained consistent

Why the pattern matters: The persistence of the structural conditions that enable narco-state dynamics, despite decades of enforcement effort and billions of dollars of assistance, suggests that addressing those conditions requires more fundamental change in both consumer-country drug policy and producer and transit-country economic development than security-focused approaches alone can achieve. The changes that have occurred, where they have occurred, typically reflect shifts in the political conditions, civil society pressures, and international relationships that shape institutional reform capacity, rather than simply changes in enforcement intensity.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has examined the narco-state as a concept, a historical phenomenon, and a set of ongoing conditions that shape the lives of millions of people in countries across Latin America, West Africa, Central Asia, and other regions where drug trafficking organizations have achieved sufficient financial power and strategic capability to penetrate, co-opt, and in some cases effectively replace legitimate state institutions. The narco-state is not a binary category, a country either is or is not a narco-state, but a spectrum of conditions in which the distinction between the criminal and the legitimate, between private criminal violence and state coercion, and between the illegal economy and the formal economy becomes dangerously blurred.

The chapter began by situating narco-state conditions within their historical and institutional context. The structural vulnerabilities that make state capture by drug trafficking organizations possible, including weak institutions, inadequate civil service compensation, limited judicial independence, opaque political financing, and the geographic vulnerabilities of particular territories, are products of historical legacies that include colonial governance patterns, post-independence political instability, and the economic disruptions of the late twentieth century. These structural vulnerabilities do not make narco-state conditions inevitable, but they create the environments in which the extraordinary financial power of the drug trade can be most effectively deployed to corrupt institutional actors.

The mechanisms of state capture were examined at multiple levels and through multiple instruments. The bribe and the threat, encapsulated in the Mexican concept of plata o plomo, are the most basic tools of individual institutional penetration, operating at the level of police officers, customs agents, and local judges. At higher institutional levels, campaign financing, political party penetration, and the placement of cartel associates in sensitive positions represent more systemic forms of state capture. The corruption of intelligence services, illustrated most dramatically by the García Luna case in Mexico, represents perhaps the most damaging form of institutional penetration, redirecting the state's own surveillance and security capacities toward protecting the criminal networks they nominally monitor.

Violence was examined not only as a product of cartel conflict but as a strategic instrument of governance. The spectacular forms of violence associated with Mexican cartels communicate power to rivals, costs to state forces, and boundaries of permissible behavior to civil society actors in territories under criminal influence. The targeting of journalists, human rights defenders, and community leaders reflects a systematic strategy of suppressing civil society resistance to criminal governance, establishing a culture of impunity that extends beyond specific acts of violence to reshape the conditions of public life in affected communities.

Case studies in Colombia, Mexico, Guinea-Bissau, and Afghanistan illustrated both the common structural features of narco-state conditions and the significant contextual variation in how those conditions manifest. Colombia's partial institutional recovery from the acute crisis of the Medellín and Cali cartel period demonstrated that narco-state conditions are not irreversible, but the continued challenges facing Colombia and the persistence of conditions in Mexico suggest that institutional improvement is slow, fragile, and requires sustained commitment across multiple dimensions simultaneously. Guinea-Bissau's near-total state capture represented an extreme case facilitated by the particular combination of post-conflict institutional destruction, extreme economic vulnerability, and geographic convenience for transshipment. Afghanistan illustrated how institutional assistance can be captured by the very networks it is designed to reduce when those networks are embedded in the formal governance structures receiving assistance.

The international dimensions of narco-state conditions were examined through the lens of consumer demand, financial flows, and the political relationships between consumer, transit, and producer countries. Consumer-country demand for illegal drugs generates the revenues that enable cartel institutional penetration, and consumer-country financial systems provide the money-laundering infrastructure through which cartel profits are converted into legitimate assets. The political responsibility of consumer countries for the narco-state conditions of producer and transit countries is analytically significant and politically contested, raising fundamental questions about the coherence of drug policy frameworks that pursue supply reduction while maintaining the demand conditions that sustain supply.

The chapter concluded by examining resistance and reform, the civil society movements, institutional reform efforts, and international cooperation frameworks that have, in some contexts, reduced the depth of cartel institutional penetration. These cases of partial success illustrate that narco-state conditions, however entrenched, respond to sustained and multi-dimensional intervention, and that the institutional choices made by societies facing criminal governance have consequences that extend across generations. They also illustrate the limitations of approaches focused exclusively on security enforcement, and the necessity of addressing the economic, social, and political conditions that sustain both criminal labor market recruitment and the demand for illegal drugs in consumer countries.

The study of narco-states ultimately raises fundamental questions about the nature of political authority, institutional legitimacy, and the relationship between governance and violence that go beyond the specific topic of drug trafficking. When people receive more consistent governance from criminal organizations than from formal state institutions, the meaning of state legitimacy requires rethinking. When the institutions designed to enforce law against criminal networks are themselves instruments of those networks, the meaning of the rule of law requires defending with new urgency. And when the global demand for illegal substances generates revenues sufficient to distort the governance of entire nations, the responsibilities of consumer societies in maintaining those conditions require confronting with more honesty than they have typically received in public discourse about the war on drugs.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Narco-State
A country or significant portion of a country in which drug trafficking organizations have so penetrated formal governance institutions that those institutions cannot function independently of criminal influence.
State Capture
The process by which private interests, including criminal organizations, gain sufficient leverage over public institutions that those institutions are redirected from public purposes toward private benefit.
Plata o Plomo
Spanish phrase meaning "silver or lead," describing the coercive offer made by trafficking organizations to officials: accept a bribe or face lethal violence. Encapsulates the mechanism of institutional corruption under criminal coercion.
Criminal Governance
The provision of governance-like functions, including order maintenance, dispute resolution, and social services, by criminal organizations in territories where the formal state is absent or compromised.
Impunity
The condition in which crimes go uninvestigated, unprosecuted, and unpunished, reflecting the degree to which accountability institutions have been compromised by criminal or political power.
Parallel State
A criminal organization that exercises governance functions, including taxation, security provision, and dispute resolution, in parallel to or in replacement of formal state authority in particular territories.
De Facto Sovereignty
Effective control over a territory and its population, exercised by an actor, whether a state or a non-state organization, regardless of formal legal recognition of that control.
Narco-Corruption
The systematic corruption of state institutions, including law enforcement, judiciary, military, and political offices, by drug trafficking organizations through bribery, intimidation, and personnel penetration.
Fragile State
A state with limited capacity to fulfill core governance functions, including the provision of security, the administration of justice, and the delivery of basic services, leaving populations vulnerable to exploitation by non-state actors including criminal organizations.
Money Laundering
The process of converting the proceeds of illegal activity into assets that appear to derive from legitimate sources, enabling criminal organizations to enjoy and deploy their revenues without attracting law enforcement attention.
Autodefensas
Community self-defense organizations that emerged in parts of Mexico, particularly Michoacán and Guerrero, in response to cartel violence; some remained genuinely community-controlled while others were penetrated or co-opted by cartel interests.
Narco-Culture
Cultural expressions, including music, fashion, mythology, and social norms, that valorize or normalize the lifestyle and values associated with drug trafficking organizations; serves to normalize criminal governance for some populations.
Plan Colombia
A U.S.-backed assistance program launched in 2000 providing financial, military, and intelligence support to Colombia's efforts to address drug trafficking and the insurgent organizations that profited from it.
Mérida Initiative
A security assistance program launched in 2008 providing U.S. support to Mexico and Central American countries for institutional capacity building in security and justice sectors.
Narco-Terrorism
The use of terrorist tactics, including assassinations of political figures, bombings of public spaces, and mass violence against civilians, by drug trafficking organizations to coerce state actors and achieve political objectives.
Pax Mafiosa
An informal arrangement in which a dominant criminal organization and political authorities reach a mutual accommodation, with the criminal organization operating freely in exchange for maintaining a level of visible order and limiting challenges to state authority.
Transitional Justice
Legal and social processes for addressing human rights violations and atrocities committed during periods of conflict or authoritarian governance, including mechanisms for accountability, reparations, and institutional reform.
Civil Society
The sphere of organized social life outside the state and the market, including community organizations, professional associations, religious bodies, media, and advocacy groups; often the primary site of resistance to narco-state conditions.
Femicide
The murder of women and girls, often with impunity, in conditions of gender-based violence; a particularly acute problem in regions under narco-state conditions, where cartel violence and gender-based violence intersect with institutional impunity.
Enforcement Gap
The disparity between the legal prohibition of drug trafficking and the practical capacity of state institutions to enforce that prohibition, creating space in which trafficking can occur without consistent legal consequence.

Chapter Reflection

The narco-state represents one of the most serious challenges to political order, human rights, and the rule of law in the contemporary world. This chapter has examined that challenge not as an abstraction but as a concrete set of historical processes operating in specific countries, institutions, and communities. The conviction of Genaro García Luna in a New York federal courtroom, the disappearance of forty-three students in Guerrero, the assassination of a president and a military chief in Guinea-Bissau within hours of each other, and the documented direct participation of senior Afghan officials in protecting opium production while drawing salaries as government administrators are not simply dramatic news stories. They are data points in a pattern that reveals something fundamental about the relationship between profitable illegal markets, institutional vulnerability, and the meaning of the state as a guarantor of rights and security.

What makes this chapter's material particularly difficult to engage with honestly is its resistance to simple moral narratives. The corrupted police officer who accepts cartel money under threat of death is both a perpetrator of institutional betrayal and a victim of structural coercion. The rural farmer who grows coca or poppies is both a participant in the supply chain of a globally damaging drug trade and a person making rational survival calculations in the absence of viable alternatives. The civil society activist who risks death to document cartel corruption is both an inspiring exemplar of human courage and a person whose work is made necessary by the failure of institutions that should have made that courage unnecessary. Holding these multiple truths simultaneously, rather than reducing them to simpler narratives of heroism and villainy, is one of the most important intellectual demands that the study of narco-state conditions makes on serious students.

The chapter also challenges students to examine the complicity of wealthy consumer societies in the conditions that narco-states represent. The demand for cocaine, heroin, methamphetamine, and other illegal substances in North America and Europe generates the revenues that fund the corruption of institutions in countries thousands of miles away. The financial systems of wealthy countries provide the money-laundering infrastructure through which drug profits are converted into legitimate assets and reinvested in consumer-country real estate, business, and financial markets. The political decisions of consumer-country governments about drug enforcement priorities, including the emphasis on supply reduction over demand reduction, shape the structural conditions in which narco-state dynamics operate. These are not comfortable observations for students in wealthy consumer countries to sit with. But the study of organized crime at a global level requires confronting them.

Finally, the chapter offers reason for neither despair nor complacency. Narco-state conditions, however entrenched, are not permanent features of political landscapes. They respond to the sustained application of institutional reform, civil society pressure, political will, and international cooperation. The Colombian trajectory, imperfect and incomplete though it is, demonstrates that meaningful improvement is possible even from very acute conditions of criminal governance. The courage of journalists, human rights defenders, and community organizers who continue to work in conditions of extreme personal risk demonstrates that the human capacity for resistance to criminal power is not extinguished by the depth of its institutional penetration. And the conviction of Genaro García Luna in a U.S. federal courtroom demonstrates that even the most senior figures of institutional corruption are not beyond accountability when investigators, prosecutors, and judicial institutions maintain sufficient independence and international reach. The study of narco-states is ultimately a study of what humans and their institutions are capable of, in both their worst and their most courageous dimensions.

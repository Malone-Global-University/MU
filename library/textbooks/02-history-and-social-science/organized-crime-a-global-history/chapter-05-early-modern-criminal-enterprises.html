Part II — The Origins of Organized Crime
5

Early Modern Criminal Enterprises

Organized Crime: A Global History

Chapter Opening

"Between the legitimate merchant and the outright pirate, there was, in the early modern world, a vast and profitable middle ground, and the most successful men of the sea moved through it with remarkable ease."

In the winter of 1696, a vessel called the Adventure Galley sailed out of New York harbor, its captain holding a royal commission that authorized him to hunt pirates on behalf of the English crown. Captain William Kidd was, by all formal measures, an agent of legitimate authority, a privateer operating under license. Within three years, he had become one of the most wanted pirates in the Atlantic world, hanged in London in 1701 with his body left to rot in a gibbet over the Thames as a warning to others. The transition from licensed agent of the state to condemned criminal had required almost no change in Kidd's actual behavior. What changed was the political context around him, the diplomatic needs of his royal sponsors, and the willingness of powerful men in London and New York to shield him from the consequences of acts they had previously encouraged.

Kidd's story captures something essential about organized crime in the early modern period, roughly the era spanning from 1450 to 1750. This was an age of expanding European empires, explosive growth in oceanic trade, fierce competition among nation-states for commercial supremacy, and the construction of elaborate legal frameworks designed to channel wealth toward royal treasuries and licensed merchants. It was also, for precisely those reasons, an age of spectacular criminal enterprise. The same forces that built empires and created legal markets also created the conditions for black markets, smuggling networks, maritime predation, and what historians have come to call the "shadow economies" that ran parallel to the official structures of early modern commerce.

The early modern period differs from the medieval world examined in the previous chapter in several crucial ways. The scale of commerce expanded enormously, driven by oceanic navigation that connected Europe, Africa, the Americas, and Asia in sustained webs of exchange for the first time. Mercantilist states imposed elaborate systems of tariffs, monopolies, and trade restrictions that virtually guaranteed the emergence of black markets and smuggling as rational economic responses. Colonial empires created distant territories that were both difficult to police and enormously profitable to exploit, providing ideal conditions for criminal enterprises that could operate beyond the reach of metropolitan authority. And the state itself, as we shall see repeatedly in this chapter, was frequently not the enemy of organized crime but its sponsor, its protector, and sometimes its direct partner.

This chapter examines five interconnected dimensions of early modern criminal enterprise: the great smuggling empires that stretched across continents and oceans; the colonial black markets that developed within and alongside the plantation economies of the Americas; maritime crime in its many forms, from opportunistic piracy to organized sea-robbery; the contraband trades in specific commodities, including wool, silk, tea, tobacco, and sugar; and state-sponsored piracy, in which national governments openly or covertly licensed violence against foreign commerce as an instrument of foreign policy. These categories overlap and interpenetrate. The same ship might carry both licensed cargo and contraband. The same captain might hold a royal commission in one port and fence stolen goods in another. The same merchant might operate a legal import business while running a parallel smuggling operation through a network of corrupt customs officials.

Several larger themes run through all of these stories. First, the relationship between law and crime in the early modern world was fluid and contested in ways that challenge any simple distinction between legal and illegal commerce. What was a crime in one jurisdiction was often not a crime in another, and the same activity could shift between legality and criminality depending on which monarch sat on which throne, which alliance was currently in force, and which merchants had purchased the relevant monopoly licenses. Second, organized criminal enterprise in this period was deeply intertwined with state power, not as a challenge to it but as a complement to it. Piracy and privateering were tools of statecraft. Smuggling networks were sometimes tolerated and sometimes fostered by local officials who shared in the profits. Criminal organizations and legitimate governments were not simply adversaries; they were, in many cases, partners in the exploitation of global trade.

Third, the human costs of early modern criminal enterprise fell very unequally. At the top of these networks were wealthy merchants, aristocratic investors, and royal sponsors who profited enormously while insulating themselves from legal risk. At the bottom were sailors, dock workers, smugglers, and small traders who faced the gallows, the press gang, and prison while their betters walked free. And cutting across all of these networks was the vast and terrible machinery of the Atlantic slave trade, which was simultaneously the largest forced migration in human history, a cornerstone of early modern commerce, and, from the perspective of those who were enslaved, a form of organized predation on a scale that dwarfs any pirate fleet or smuggling ring.

Students who complete this chapter will understand not only the historical details of early modern criminal enterprise but the deeper analytical point that connects this era to the organized crime of later centuries: criminal markets do not arise in a vacuum. They are created and sustained by the same forces, legal restrictions, commercial incentives, political corruption, economic inequality, and the state's uneven capacity and willingness to enforce its own laws, that shape legal commerce. The early modern period is where these dynamics first achieved global scale, and understanding them is essential to understanding everything that follows in this textbook.

Historical Context

To understand early modern criminal enterprise, students must first understand the political and economic world within which it operated. The period from roughly 1450 to 1750 witnessed a transformation in the scale and organization of global commerce that had no precedent in human history. Portuguese navigators opened sea routes around Africa to South and East Asia. Columbus's 1492 voyage inaugurated sustained contact between the hemispheres. Spanish conquistadors established the first large European land empires in the Americas. Dutch, English, and French merchants and investors followed, building networks of trade, plantation agriculture, and colonial settlement that eventually reached every inhabited continent.

The dominant economic ideology governing these developments was mercantilism, a set of theories and policies holding that national wealth consisted primarily in the accumulation of precious metals, particularly gold and silver, and that trade should therefore be organized to maximize exports and minimize imports. Mercantilist states imposed elaborate systems of tariffs designed to make foreign goods more expensive, granted monopoly trading licenses to favored companies and merchants that restricted competition, and attempted to channel colonial trade exclusively through the mother country rather than allowing colonies to trade directly with foreign powers. England's Navigation Acts, first passed in the 1650s, required that goods imported to England or its colonies arrive on English ships and that colonial products flow through English ports. France, Spain, Portugal, and the Netherlands maintained similar restrictions.

These policies created powerful incentives for evasion. When a commodity like tobacco could be sold for twice the price in a foreign market that English law prohibited, or when a merchant could triple profits by importing tea directly from Asia rather than through the East India Company's licensed channels, the economic logic of smuggling became irresistible. The elaborate tariff structures of mercantilist states did not simply protect national wealth; they also, inevitably, created black markets for everything they taxed. Historians have estimated that in some English ports during the eighteenth century, smuggled goods constituted a substantial share, perhaps as much as a quarter or more, of all goods traded.

The expansion of oceanic trade also created new opportunities and new temptations for maritime violence. The sea lanes between Europe and the Americas, between Europe and Asia, and between Africa and the Americas carried enormous concentrations of movable wealth: silver from the mines of Mexico and Peru, spices from the Moluccas, silks from China and India, sugar from the Caribbean, tobacco from Virginia. Whoever could seize this wealth, whether through licensed privateering commission or simple piracy, could become enormously rich. The difficulty was political: the same state that might welcome attacks on foreign shipping during wartime would prosecute the same attacks as piracy during peacetime.

Colonial settings added additional complexity. European empires claimed sovereignty over vast territories that they could not effectively police. The Caribbean islands, the coastlines of North and South America, and the settlement outposts of West Africa were all far from metropolitan centers of authority, poorly served by courts and law enforcement, and populated by mobile populations of sailors, merchants, indentured servants, and enslaved people who had limited attachment to the legal structures of any particular empire. In such environments, the boundary between legal commerce and criminal enterprise was porous, and the enforcement of any boundary depended on the energy and honesty of officials who were themselves often participants in the shadow economies they were nominally supposed to suppress.

Finally, students should understand that the early modern world was one of chronic interstate warfare. England, France, Spain, Portugal, and the Netherlands were almost continuously at war with one or more of each other throughout this period, and wartime created licensed opportunities for violence against foreign commerce that peacetime treaties then attempted to abolish, usually unsuccessfully. The men who sailed as privateers under royal commission in wartime did not simply dissolve their organizations when peace was declared; they looked for ways to continue profitable activities, and piracy was the most obvious option. The rhythms of early modern interstate conflict thus had a direct and measurable effect on the rhythms of maritime crime.

Section 5.1

Smuggling Empires

In the spring of 1746, a gang of armed men rode through the village of Goudhurst in Kent, England, and threatened to burn it to the ground. The men were members of the Hawkhurst Gang, one of the most powerful smuggling organizations in eighteenth-century Britain, and they were delivering a warning to local villagers who had organized a defense company to resist them. The gang had been operating for years, running massive quantities of tea, brandy, tobacco, and other taxed commodities from the Continent into England through networks of boats, ponies, and corrupt officials, and they had no intention of being stopped by a village militia. When the villagers refused to back down and organized an armed defense, the confrontation ended in a pitched battle in which the smugglers were driven off. Within two years, the Hawkhurst Gang had been broken by a combination of military force, informers, and the brutal public executions of its leaders. But the trade they had served continued, because the economic incentives that created it had not changed.

The Hawkhurst Gang was an extreme example, but smuggling on a large and organized scale was one of the defining features of early modern European commerce. Understanding why requires understanding the fiscal and commercial policies of early modern states. Throughout the sixteenth, seventeenth, and eighteenth centuries, European governments relied heavily on customs duties, import tariffs, and excise taxes on specific commodities as their primary sources of revenue. The wars that punctuated this period, almost continuously, required enormous expenditures, and taxes on trade were among the most reliable means of raising money without confronting the political resistance that came from taxing land or persons directly.

The result was that enormous price differentials opened up between goods in their country of origin and the same goods in countries where they were heavily taxed. French brandy that cost a few shillings in Calais might sell for many times that price in London after customs duties were added. Dutch and Chinese tea, flowing through the East India Company's licensed channels, carried a tax burden that made it many times more expensive than the same tea smuggled in directly. English raw wool, which Parliament prohibited from being exported because of its value to the domestic textile industry, could be sold for a handsome profit across the Channel in France or the Low Countries. In each case, the gap between the taxed legal price and the untaxed smuggled price was large enough to support not only the profits of merchants and sailors but also the substantial costs of bribery, organization, and risk.

The Structure of Smuggling Networks

Early modern smuggling operations were not simple affairs of a single man rowing a small boat across the Channel. The most successful networks were elaborate commercial enterprises with a sophistication that rivals any legal trading company of the period. At the top were wealthy merchant investors, sometimes members of the gentry or even the lesser nobility, who provided the capital to purchase contraband goods on the Continent and arranged their transport. These investors were rarely prosecuted, because their names rarely appeared on any incriminating document and because their social status gave them connections to the very officials who might otherwise investigate them.

Below the investors were the operational organizers: men who coordinated the logistics of a smuggling run, maintained relationships with suppliers abroad and distributors at home, and managed the network of lookouts, ponies, boats, and corrupt officials on whom the operation depended. These men were genuinely entrepreneurial figures, solving complex supply-chain problems under conditions of legal risk. Below them were the physical workers of the trade: the boat crews who made the Channel crossing, often at night in small swift vessels; the batmen and tubmen who carried goods from the landing points inland; and the lookouts and signalmen who watched for revenue cutters and excise officers. At the bottom of the hierarchy were the retail distributors, the innkeepers, chandlers, and small traders who received the smuggled goods and sold them to the ultimate consumers.

Each level of this hierarchy had its own relationship to risk and reward. The investors who provided capital were the least exposed and the most profitable. The boat crews and carriers were the most exposed and the least well-compensated, though even they earned significantly more from a night's work carrying contraband than from legitimate employment. The entire network was held together by a combination of shared profit, community solidarity, and, when necessary, violence and intimidation. Communities in areas where smuggling was a major local industry, particularly in coastal regions of England, France, and the Low Countries, often developed norms of silence and mutual protection toward smugglers that closely parallel the omertà later associated with the Sicilian Mafia. Revenue officers who attempted to enforce the law in such communities could expect obstruction, harassment, and, in extreme cases, physical attack.

The Wool Trade and the Owlers

Among the oldest and most persistent of English smuggling enterprises was the export of raw wool. English wool had been the foundation of the country's medieval commercial wealth, and Parliament had long attempted to protect the domestic textile industry by restricting its export. Raw wool was far more valuable to England if it was processed into cloth domestically, and so a series of laws dating back to the medieval period made unauthorized export of raw wool a serious offense. Violators became known as "owlers," a term that may derive from their habit of operating at night, like owls, though its exact etymology is disputed.

Despite heavy penalties, wool smuggling was a major industry in the counties of Kent, Sussex, Essex, and Suffolk throughout the early modern period. The geography was favorable: the English Channel is narrow, particularly at the Strait of Dover, and the sheltered harbors and inlets of the southeastern English coast provided easy launching points for small vessels that could make the crossing to France or the Low Countries quickly. Continental buyers, particularly in Flanders and France, were eager purchasers of English wool for their own textile industries and paid prices well above what could be obtained legally. The trade continued with only partial interruptions from the fifteenth through the eighteenth centuries, sustained by the same structural incentives, an artificial price differential created by legal restriction, and the same organizational responses, networks of investors, intermediaries, carriers, and corrupt officials.

Alongside wool, a vast trade in silk ran in the opposite direction. French and Italian silk was heavily taxed on entry to England, both to protect English textile manufacturers and to generate revenue. Organized gangs of smugglers ran silk across the Channel in quantities that seriously undermined the legal trade. The Spitalfields silk weavers of London, who depended on legal trade restrictions for their economic survival, were some of the most vocal advocates for vigorous enforcement of the customs laws, well aware that the flood of cheaper smuggled silk threatened their livelihoods. The politics of early modern smuggling were thus not simply a conflict between criminal enterprise and the state; they were embedded in deeper conflicts between different economic interests that were themselves competing for the protection of law.

Historical Vocabulary
Mercantilism

Mercantilism was the dominant economic doctrine of early modern European states, holding that national wealth and power depended on accumulating precious metals and maintaining a favorable balance of trade, exporting more than was imported. Mercantilist policies included high tariffs on imported goods, monopoly trading licenses granted to favored companies, restrictions on colonial trade with foreign powers, and prohibitions on the export of raw materials that could be processed domestically.

For the history of organized crime, mercantilism is crucial because its restrictions created the price differentials that made smuggling profitable. Every tariff was, in effect, a subsidy for the smuggler. Every monopoly license that restricted legal competition opened space for illegal competition. Historians sometimes describe mercantilism as having "created" smuggling in the sense that the more elaborate the restrictions, the more elaborate and profitable the evasion of those restrictions became. Understanding this relationship between legal restriction and criminal enterprise is one of the foundational principles of the economics of organized crime, and it applies as much to modern drug prohibition as to eighteenth-century tea duties.

The Tea Smuggling Business

No commodity better illustrates the dynamics of early modern smuggling than tea. When the East India Company gained its monopoly on the import of tea to Britain, it used that monopoly to maintain high prices that generated substantial profits for the company and substantial tax revenue for the government. The result was that legal tea was expensive, and the incentive to smuggle cheaper tea from Continental Europe, where it had been imported from Asia at lower cost and without English duties, was enormous. By the early eighteenth century, smuggled tea was widely available throughout Britain at prices dramatically below the legal product, and the trade was conducted on a scale that was openly semi-public in many coastal communities.

Estimates of the volume of the tea-smuggling trade in eighteenth-century Britain have varied, but historians broadly agree that it was staggering. Some have argued that in the mid-eighteenth century, more tea entered Britain illegally than legally, meaning that the government's attempt to control and tax the tea trade had been so thoroughly subverted that the legal market had become the minority market. The smuggling networks that handled tea also handled other commodities, sharing infrastructure, personnel, and corrupt contacts, creating integrated criminal enterprises that prefigure the diversified criminal organizations of later centuries.

The tea-smuggling problem eventually forced a significant policy response. In 1784, the Commutation Act dramatically reduced the duty on tea, collapsing the price differential between legal and smuggled tea and thereby eliminating most of the economic incentive for the trade. The effect was almost immediate: legal tea imports surged, smuggling collapsed, and the government actually collected more revenue at the lower tax rate because so much more tea was now bought through legal channels. The episode is one of the clearest historical examples of what later economists would call the Laffer curve, the principle that above a certain level of taxation, revenue actually falls because of evasion, and the most effective way to combat black markets may be to reduce the taxes that create them.

Colonial Black Markets

In 1733, the British Parliament passed the Molasses Act, which imposed a substantial duty on molasses and sugar imported into the British North American colonies from the non-British Caribbean islands. The intent was clear: colonial merchants who were buying cheap molasses from the French, Dutch, and Spanish Caribbean islands to distill into rum were undermining the profits of British Caribbean planters, who lobbied successfully for protective legislation. The actual effect was equally clear: the duty was so high that it made the legal trade economically unviable, and colonial merchants simply continued to buy their molasses from the foreign islands, bribing customs officials rather than paying the duty. The Molasses Act of 1733 was a notable piece of legislation less for what it accomplished than for what it failed to accomplish, and its failure illustrated a principle that would recur throughout early modern colonial commerce: the gap between the law on the books and the law in practice was, in the colonies, often a chasm.

Colonial settings were, for structural reasons, ideal environments for black markets. The distance from metropolitan authority was the most obvious factor. A ship could leave Boston or Philadelphia, conduct trade that was technically illegal under the Navigation Acts, and return with little practical risk of enforcement as long as local officials were cooperative, and cooperative they very often were. Colonial governors, customs collectors, and naval officers were poorly paid, far from oversight, and surrounded by communities in which illegal trade was economically central and socially normalized. The incentive to accept bribes was strong, and the incentive to resist community pressure was weak. The result was a system of enforcement that was selective at best and largely theatrical at worst.

The Atlantic Trade in Enslaved People

The largest and most consequential criminal market operating within the colonial world was, by any measure, the trade in enslaved human beings. The Atlantic slave trade was not, in its origins, a criminal enterprise; it was, in most of the jurisdictions where it operated, a legal trade regulated by royal charters, company monopolies, and treaties. The Royal African Company held a monopoly on English participation in the trade from 1672 until 1698, and even after the trade was opened to all English merchants, it continued to operate within a legal framework that defined enslaved Africans as commercial property and afforded them no legal protection whatsoever. What makes the slave trade relevant to a history of organized crime is not that it was illegal in its own era but that it demonstrates, with brutal clarity, the principles of criminal enterprise operating at maximum scale: the commodification of human beings through organized violence, the corruption of officials, the use of monopoly power, and the generation of enormous wealth for investors at the cost of catastrophic harm to those at the bottom of the supply chain.

There were, however, dimensions of the slave trade that were technically illegal even within the frameworks of the time, and these illuminate the relationship between criminal enterprise and colonial commerce. The Royal African Company's monopoly was routinely violated by independent traders, known as interlopers, who conducted their own slaving voyages without company license. After the monopoly was formally ended in 1698, what remained illegal was trading in the enslaved people of nations with which England was at peace, trading in territories where treaties prohibited it, and trading under the flags of rival powers. All of these prohibitions were regularly violated, and the violations were organized with the same commercial sophistication as the legal trade.

The colonial plantations that enslaved people were forced to labor on were also nodes within broader black market networks. Enslaved people who ran away, the community of people known as maroons who established independent settlements in the interior of Jamaica, Suriname, Brazil, and elsewhere, created their own shadow economies through which they traded with coastal communities in ways that colonial authorities could not fully control. Free Black traders in Caribbean ports served as intermediaries between legal and illegal markets in ways that complicated the enforcement of both commercial and slave-related regulations. The economic lives of enslaved and free Black people in the colonial Atlantic world were shaped by, and in turn shaped, the broader black market economies that surrounded them.

Tobacco, Sugar, and the Colonial Contraband Economy

Beyond the slave trade, the major plantation commodities of the colonial Americas generated their own extensive black markets. Tobacco was among the most commercially significant. English law required that colonial tobacco be shipped to England before it could be re-exported to the Continent, a requirement that added cost and delay while enriching English merchants and generating customs revenue. Colonial planters and merchants chafed at the restriction and regularly found ways around it. Direct trade between the colonies and European ports on the Continent was conducted by ships that falsified their manifests, concealed their destinations, and relied on a network of corrupt port officials at both ends. The Dutch, who maintained a more liberal trade policy, were frequent intermediaries, and the Caribbean island of Sint Eustatius in the Dutch Antilles became a major entrepôt for colonial goods that were being smuggled around English trade restrictions.

Sugar and its derivatives, particularly molasses and rum, generated equally extensive illegal trade. The structure of the Caribbean economy in the early eighteenth century created a near-perfect black market situation. British Caribbean islands like Jamaica and Barbados produced sugar but had difficulty producing enough molasses for the rum trade that New England distillers needed. The French Caribbean islands, particularly Saint-Domingue, Martinique, and Guadeloupe, produced abundant molasses but faced British trade restrictions that prevented selling it to British colonies. The result was an enormous mutual incentive for illegal trade: New England merchants wanted cheap French molasses; French Caribbean planters wanted to sell it; and the Molasses Act of 1733 stood in the way, with only a network of corrupt customs officials between the two parties and the trade they wanted to conduct.

The colonial customs service was, in practice, one of the most corrupt institutions in the British imperial system. Customs collectors in colonial ports regularly accepted "gifts," a term used euphemistically for what amounted to systematic bribery, in exchange for turning a blind eye to manifests that understated cargo, ships that failed to report their true origins, and goods that should have paid duties but did not. The corruption was so normalized that it was barely considered corruption by those who practiced it; it was simply the way business was done. When the British government attempted to reform the customs service in the 1760s and enforce the trade laws more strictly through writs of assistance, general search warrants that allowed officials to search premises without specifying what they were looking for, the colonial reaction was one of furious resistance, and the writs became one of the grievances that contributed to the revolutionary movement. The colonial economy had been built, in significant part, on the evasion of British trade law, and enforcing that law more strictly threatened the economic foundation of colonial prosperity.

Historical Vocabulary
Contraband

Contraband refers to goods that are prohibited by law from being imported, exported, or traded, either because the goods themselves are illegal or because they are being traded in violation of licensing, monopoly, or tariff regulations. The term derives from the Italian contrabbando, meaning against the ban or proclamation.

In early modern commerce, contraband covered a wide range of situations. Raw wool was contraband when exported from England because of legislation designed to protect the domestic textile industry. Tea was contraband when imported to Britain outside the East India Company's licensed channels because of the monopoly laws. Colonial tobacco was contraband when shipped directly to European ports without going through England first because of the Navigation Acts. Molasses from French Caribbean islands was contraband when imported into British colonies without payment of the Molasses Act duty.

Understanding what was contraband in any given context requires understanding the specific legal framework of that time and place, because the category was defined by law, and law varied enormously across jurisdictions. What was legitimate trade in one port was criminal in another. This jurisdictional variability is one of the defining features of early modern criminal enterprise and has direct parallels in the way modern criminal organizations exploit legal differences between jurisdictions.

Section 5.3

Maritime Crime

On the morning of November 17, 1717, a well-armed vessel called the Concorde encountered a British slave ship in the waters off the island of Martinique. The encounter was brief and violent, ending with the slave ship surrendering its cargo of enslaved Africans, its provisions, and its arms to the attacking vessel. The captain of the Concorde was a Welsh sailor named Bartholomew Roberts, who would go on to capture an estimated four hundred or more vessels before being killed in battle with the Royal Navy in 1722. Roberts was, by any definition, a pirate, a maritime criminal engaged in robbery on the high seas for his own profit. He was also, by any measure, a remarkably well-organized and strategically sophisticated figure whose operations had more in common with a successful commercial enterprise than with simple robbery.

Maritime crime in the early modern period encompassed a wide range of activities, from the opportunistic piracy of small boats preying on coastal shipping to the large-scale organized operations of pirate fleets that ranged across entire ocean basins. Between these extremes lay a spectrum of activities including privateering, which was licensed by the state; buccaneering, which was semi-licensed or tolerated by some colonial governments; wrecking, which involved the deliberate luring or exploitation of shipwrecks to plunder their cargo; and the systematic theft of cargo from ships in harbor by organized gangs of dock workers and river pirates. All of these activities shared a common economic logic: the concentration of movable wealth on ships, in ports, and on sea lanes created targets of extraordinary value for those willing to use violence to take them.

The Organization of Piracy

Popular culture has tended to portray pirates as anarchic figures, operating outside all social order. The historical reality was considerably more structured. A pirate ship was a complex organization that required coordination, hierarchy, and shared rules of behavior to function effectively over extended periods at sea. Most pirate ships during the Golden Age operated under written articles, agreements that governed the distribution of plunder, the rules of discipline, the compensation for injuries received in battle, and the rights of crew members in decisions about targets and routes. These articles were remarkably egalitarian by the standards of the time: on most pirate ships, the crew elected their captain and could vote to remove him, shared plunder according to agreed scales rather than the rigid hierarchies of the merchant or naval service, and had rights of appeal against arbitrary punishment.

Historians have long debated whether this internal democracy of pirate ships reflected genuine egalitarian values or simply the practical necessities of organizing violent men in an environment without external enforcement. The answer is probably both. Men who chose piracy were often doing so precisely because the conditions on merchant or naval vessels were harsh, hierarchical, and poorly compensated. Many pirates had previously served in the Royal Navy or on merchant ships, and the relative freedom and equal distribution of prizes on a pirate vessel was a genuine improvement in their working conditions. The internal governance of pirate ships thus tells us something important about the social conditions that produced piracy, including the brutal disciplinary regimes of the legal maritime world and the desperate poverty of common sailors in a period when wages were low and abuse by officers was common.

Beyond the internal organization of individual ships, piracy during the Golden Age also involved external networks that allowed pirates to convert stolen goods into cash and to resupply their operations. Pirates needed places where they could sell their plunder, buy provisions and ammunition, and make repairs without being arrested. Port Royal, Jamaica, before its earthquake destruction in 1692, was one such place. Nassau, Bahamas, was another. The North American colonial ports, particularly those in the Carolinas and Rhode Island, provided markets for pirate goods in the early eighteenth century, and merchants in those ports were willing buyers who asked few questions about the provenance of the goods they purchased.

The existence of these networks meant that piracy was not simply a matter of men on ships; it was embedded in the broader commercial networks of colonial port towns. Merchants who fenced pirate goods, tavern keepers who housed pirates between voyages, suppliers who sold provisions to pirate ships, and corrupt officials who looked the other way were all participants in the piracy economy. When the British government eventually moved to suppress the Golden Age of Piracy in the 1720s, it was not enough to hunt pirate ships at sea; the suppression of piracy also required eliminating the shore-based networks that sustained it, a pattern of interdependence between maritime criminals and land-based commercial networks that would recur in organized crime throughout subsequent centuries.

Wrecking and River Piracy

Not all maritime crime took place on the open sea. In coastal communities throughout Britain, France, Spain, and the colonial world, the practice of wrecking, profiting from the cargo of ships that ran aground or were wrecked in storms, was endemic and organized. The law drew a distinction between legitimate salvage, assisting a distressed ship and receiving compensation, and deliberate plunder of wrecked vessels, which was a serious crime. In practice, the line between the two was regularly crossed, and there are persistent traditions, some well-documented and some almost certainly mythological, of coastal communities deliberately luring ships onto rocks with false lights. Even where deliberate wrecking was not practiced, the organized plunder of genuinely accidental wrecks was widespread, rapid, and remarkably effective at stripping vessels before revenue officers could arrive.

In the great rivers and harbors of early modern cities, particularly in London and Amsterdam, organized gangs conducted systematic theft from ships anchored in the river or docked at wharves. London's Thames was home to a remarkable variety of maritime criminals. "Light horsemen" were gangs of thieves who worked by night in small boats, bribing or intimidating watchmen and making off with cargo from anchored vessels. "Heavy horsemen" were dock workers who concealed stolen goods in specially designed clothing while unloading legitimate cargoes. "Mudlarks" were scavengers, often children, who retrieved goods dropped or thrown from ships and sold them through networks of receivers. The systematic study of Thames-based crime by the magistrate Patrick Colquhoun in his 1800 work on the commerce and police of the river revealed a staggering scale of organized theft that had developed over decades with minimal effective response from authorities.

Section 5.4

Contraband Trade

In 1747, a customs officer named William Galley and a customs informer named Daniel Chater were seized by members of the Hawkhurst Gang near Chichester in Sussex. Over several days of captivity, the two men were tortured and murdered by the gang, their bodies concealed in a well and a shallow grave. The murders provoked a major government response, and the trial and execution of the gang members who were eventually caught became something of a public spectacle, with the bodies of the executed men displayed in gibbets along the roads of Sussex as a warning to others. The Hawkhurst Gang case became, for contemporaries and for later historians, the most dramatic evidence of how far organized smuggling in eighteenth-century England had devolved into organized violent crime.

The Hawkhurst Gang's violence was extreme, but the underlying trade it served was the same one that most respectable people in coastal England participated in, at least as consumers. When a Sussex farmer bought a bottle of brandy from a neighbor who happened to have obtained it rather cheaply, or when a Kent housewife purchased Dutch gin from an informal seller who asked no questions about where it came from, they were participating in the contraband economy that the Hawkhurst Gang supplied. The violence of the criminals at the top of the supply chain was one dimension of an economy that involved, at the consumer end, the comfortable and the respectable as much as the desperate and the criminal. This structure, in which organized violent enterprise at the production and distribution level serves a mass market of otherwise law-abiding consumers, would recur throughout the history of organized crime, most notably in the American Prohibition era of the 1920s.

The Tobacco Trade and Fiscal Violence

The tobacco trade in France provides a particularly illuminating case study of how contraband markets could achieve enormous scale and generate extreme violence. France imposed heavy excise duties on tobacco through a system of tax-farming, in which private companies called fermes paid the crown for the right to collect tobacco taxes and kept a portion of what they collected as profit. The Farmers General who operated this system were among the most powerful financial figures in France, and they maintained their own police forces and inspection services to combat smuggling, which cut directly into their revenues.

The tobacco smugglers of France operated on a scale that rivaled the Farmers General in organizational sophistication. In border regions, particularly along the frontiers with Switzerland, the Italian states, and the Spanish Netherlands, organized gangs known as faux-sauniers (literally "false salt-men," because salt was also a major contraband commodity) ran large quantities of tobacco, sometimes with the protection of armed escorts. Confrontations between smugglers and the Farmers General's agents were often violent, and the borderlands of France in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries saw something approaching a low-level guerrilla war between smuggling organizations and the fiscal enforcement apparatus of the state. The scale of the trade was such that contemporaries estimated that the majority of tobacco consumed in France arrived through illegal channels, an assessment that, if accurate, would mean the smuggling economy had effectively displaced the legal one.

Salt Smuggling and the Gabelle

Salt was another commodity whose regulation created massive criminal markets across early modern Europe. France's notorious gabelle, the salt tax, varied dramatically by region, creating enormous price differentials between areas of high taxation and neighboring regions or provinces where the tax was lower or nonexistent. In areas of heavy gabelle taxation, the price of legal salt could be ten times what it cost in untaxed regions just across a provincial border. The result was a salt-smuggling trade of immense scale, organized by the same faux-sauniers who ran tobacco and employing similar networks of carriers, lookouts, and corrupt officials.

The gabelle's injustice was a major popular grievance in pre-revolutionary France, not only because the tax itself was heavy but because the system of enforcement was brutal and arbitrary. People convicted of major salt-smuggling offenses faced the galleys or death, while the Farmers General who enriched themselves on the tax enjoyed the protection of the crown. The contrast between the harsh punishment of small-scale smugglers and the immunity of the powerful men who profited from the system was not lost on contemporaries, and it fed directly into the revolutionary resentment that exploded in 1789. The gabelle was abolished early in the French Revolution, a decision that testified to how profoundly the contraband economy had discredited the fiscal system it had grown up around.

Primary Source Window

Daniel Defoe (attrib.), A General History of the Robberies and Murders of the Most Notorious Pyrates (1724)

Creator: Attributed to Daniel Defoe · Date: 1724 · Type: Popular history / criminal biography / political commentary

Context: Published just two years after the death of Bartholomew Roberts effectively ended the Golden Age of Piracy, this work compiled biographical accounts of the major pirates of the preceding decades. Its authorship remains debated by historians, though it has long been attributed to Daniel Defoe, author of Robinson Crusoe and a prolific journalist. The book was enormously popular and went through multiple editions, shaping the public image of pirates for centuries.

The account describes Bartholomew Roberts as a man of unusual ability and ambition, noting that he accepted election as captain of a pirate vessel only reluctantly, on the grounds that in an honest service there was insufficient reward for his abilities, while among pirates there was at least a chance of wealth and authority that merit alone could never produce within the rigid hierarchies of the merchant or naval service. The text presents piracy not merely as crime but as a rational response to the social conditions of the maritime world, a choice made by men who had weighed their options and concluded that the risks of the illegal life were preferable to the certainties of legal poverty and servitude.

Analysis: The General History is one of the most important primary sources for the Golden Age of Piracy and contains much information that cannot be verified through other sources. This makes it invaluable and problematic in equal measure. The author shaped his accounts to create compelling narratives, and it is often impossible to distinguish between what was actually said or done and what was invented or embellished for literary effect. The book's sympathetic presentation of piracy as a rational response to social injustice may reflect genuine contemporary attitudes, or it may reflect the author's own political and literary purposes, which included a critique of the inequality and corruption of the legal commercial world. Its popularity testifies to how resonant this framing was with readers at the time.

Section 5.5

State-Sponsored Piracy

In 1572, Francis Drake sailed with a small fleet to the coast of Panama, where he raided a Spanish mule train carrying silver from the Pacific coast mines of Peru to the Atlantic port of Nombre de Dios. The attack was technically an act of piracy: Drake held no royal commission authorizing the raid, England and Spain were at peace, and the silver he seized belonged to the king of Spain. It was also, in practice, an act that Queen Elizabeth I of England quietly approved, because Drake shared his plunder with her. When Drake returned to England with his prizes in 1573, he was not prosecuted; he was celebrated. When he circumnavigated the globe between 1577 and 1580, raiding Spanish ports and shipping throughout the Pacific and Atlantic, the queen invested in the voyage and received a substantial return on her investment from the plunder. She eventually knighted him on the deck of his ship, the Golden Hind, in 1581.

Drake was not an anomaly. He was the most famous example of a well-established pattern in which European states used licensed and unlicensed maritime predators as instruments of foreign policy, attacking the commerce and colonies of rival powers while maintaining a formal fiction of peace. The instrument through which this was done was the letter of marque, a royal document authorizing a private vessel to prey on the shipping of a specified enemy nation. During wartime, such letters were freely issued and made privateering an extension of naval warfare by commercial means. The difficulty was that during peacetime, the same men who had conducted legitimate privateering during wars found it difficult to return to the lower-paid and more hierarchical world of merchant shipping, and the appetite of states for their services had not entirely disappeared.

Historical Vocabulary
Privateering

Privateering was a form of state-licensed maritime predation in which private individuals or companies were authorized by a government to attack the shipping of specified enemy nations. The authorizing document was called a letter of marque and reprisal. Privateers kept a portion of what they captured as prize money, while the issuing government received a share and gained the strategic benefit of harassing enemy commerce without the cost of maintaining a large naval fleet.

The distinction between a privateer and a pirate was, in theory, clear: a privateer had a letter of marque from a recognized government; a pirate did not. In practice, the distinction was deeply contested. A letter of marque issued by England authorized attacks on Spanish ships, but from Spain's perspective, this was simply piracy under another name. Privateers whose commissions had expired or been revoked sometimes continued their activities, becoming pirates by definition while their behavior remained unchanged. And some figures, like William Kidd, moved between the two categories in circumstances that remain disputed by historians.

Privateering was formally abolished among the major European powers by the Declaration of Paris in 1856, though the United States did not sign the declaration and the practice has resurfaced in modified forms at various points in the twentieth and twenty-first centuries, including ongoing debates about whether certain cyber warfare activities might constitute a modern form of it.

The Corsairs of the Barbary Coast

Among the most formidable examples of organized maritime predation in the early modern period were the corsairs of the North African coast, particularly those operating from the ports of Algiers, Tunis, Tripoli, and Salé. The Barbary corsairs, as they were known in Europe, raided merchant shipping in the Mediterranean and sometimes beyond, taking cargo and enslaving crews and passengers for ransom or labor. They operated with the explicit sanction of the Ottoman Empire and its North African client states, which derived substantial revenue from the corsair trade and provided the organizational framework within which it operated.

The scale of the Barbary corsair trade was staggering. Over the roughly three centuries from the early sixteenth to the early nineteenth century, it has been estimated that corsairs from the North African states captured between one and one and a quarter million Europeans as enslaved people, a number that rivals the more familiar history of European enslavement of Africans in absolute terms, though not in the structural economic importance or duration of the institution. The corsairs were also economically sophisticated: they maintained elaborate networks for ransoming captives, had agents in European ports to handle negotiations, and developed insurance and credit instruments to finance their operations.

From the perspective of the North African states that sponsored them, the corsairs were not criminals but naval auxiliaries, providing both revenue and strategic leverage against the Christian maritime powers of Europe. From the perspective of their victims, they were organized pirates operating on a massive scale. From the perspective of European states, they presented a persistent diplomatic and military problem that no combination of tribute payments, punitive expeditions, and treaties was able to solve definitively until the French conquest of Algeria in 1830. The Barbary corsairs thus represent the clearest early modern example of what later generations would call state-sponsored terrorism, violence organized and licensed by a government as an instrument of foreign policy and economic extraction.

The Buccaneers and Colonial Toleration

In the Caribbean, state-sponsored maritime predation took a distinctive form in the buccaneers of the seventeenth century. The buccaneers originated as a mixed group of hunters, castaways, escaped servants, and runaway enslaved people who settled on the western coast of Hispaniola in the early seventeenth century, living by hunting the wild cattle and pigs that roamed the island and selling smoked meat and hides to passing ships. When the Spanish authorities attempted to drive them off, these men turned to the sea, preying initially on Spanish shipping with the tacit encouragement of the French and English colonial authorities who found them useful as a semi-official naval force directed against a common enemy.

The most famous of the buccaneers was Henry Morgan, a Welsh-born mariner who conducted a series of spectacular raids on Spanish Caribbean settlements in the late 1660s and early 1670s, including the sack of Portobelo in 1668 and the destruction of Panama City in 1671. Morgan operated under a commission from the governor of Jamaica that authorized him to attack Spanish possessions, though the precise limits of his authorization were contested. When the raid on Panama occurred after England and Spain had concluded a new peace treaty, Morgan and the governor who commissioned him were technically in violation of international agreements. Both were arrested and sent to England, but Morgan was treated more as a celebrity than a criminal, received at court, and eventually returned to Jamaica as deputy governor, effectively making him the crown's representative in the colony he had previously raided on behalf of.

Morgan's story illustrates the fundamental instability of the state-sponsored predation model. When it suited English strategic interests to use the buccaneers against Spain, their activities were tolerated and even encouraged. When the diplomatic situation changed and England needed a working relationship with Spain more than it needed Jamaica's buccaneer auxiliaries, the same activities became criminal and their practitioners became expendable. The state's definition of criminality was entirely contingent on its current foreign policy needs, and the men who had organized their lives around state-sponsored violence were given little notice when the state changed its mind.

Historical Debate

Were Golden Age Pirates Rebels Against Social Inequality, or Simply Criminals?

The question: Since the publication of Marcus Rediker's influential work Villains of All Nations (2004) and earlier works by Christopher Hill, historians have debated whether the pirates of the Golden Age should be understood primarily as social rebels who created an alternative to the brutal hierarchies of the legal maritime world, or whether the social rebel interpretation romanticizes men who were, fundamentally, violent criminals who victimized and murdered innocent people.

Interpretation A — The Social Rebel Interpretation: Scholars like Marcus Rediker argue that piracy in the Golden Age was a conscious rejection of the exploitative conditions of the legal maritime world. Common sailors on merchant and naval vessels faced brutal discipline, arbitrary punishment, low wages, and few rights. Pirates created alternative social structures, governed by elected captains, democratic articles, and equal shares of plunder, that represented a genuine, if violent, utopian experiment in equality. Many pirates explicitly articulated their rejection of "honest service" and the system of inequality it represented. Their violence was primarily directed at the property and persons of the wealthy, and they sometimes showed conspicuous mercy to common sailors who joined them willingly. In this reading, piracy belongs in the same history as other early modern resistance movements, including peasant rebellions and labor disputes, and the violence it employed was not categorically different from the violence of the states that suppressed it.
Interpretation B — The Criminal Enterprise Interpretation: Other historians have argued that the social rebel interpretation romanticizes men who were, ultimately, violent criminals motivated primarily by self-interest. Pirates killed people, enslaved captives, and conducted extortion. The democratic governance of pirate ships was a functional necessity for managing violent men without external enforcement, not evidence of principled egalitarianism. Many pirates came from relatively privileged backgrounds and chose piracy for profit rather than as a response to social oppression. And the victims of piracy were not exclusively the wealthy: common merchants, sailors, and fishermen suffered the same violence as the great trading companies. The internal democracy of pirate ships did not prevent the rape, torture, and murder of captives, and the social rebel framing can obscure the genuine suffering piracy caused.

Evidence and stakes: Both interpretations draw on the same limited body of historical evidence, primarily trial records, the accounts in Defoe's General History, and scattered merchant correspondence, and the same sources support contradictory readings depending on what questions the historian asks and which episodes receive emphasis. The debate is not simply historical; it connects to larger questions about whether criminal enterprise can be a form of social protest, a question that recurs throughout the history of organized crime in this textbook.

Multiple Perspectives

Who Benefited and Who Suffered from Early Modern Smuggling?

From the perspective of wealthy merchant investors: Smuggling was a highly profitable business opportunity, lower-risk than the sea itself because most financial backers were well-insulated from prosecution by their social status and the indirect nature of their involvement. Investors in smuggling networks could earn returns substantially higher than legitimate trade, and in many coastal communities, participation in the financing of smuggling operations was simply part of the portfolio of a prosperous merchant.
From the perspective of ordinary consumers: Smuggling meant lower prices on goods that were otherwise taxed beyond the reach of ordinary budgets. Tea, brandy, tobacco, and silk were all significantly cheaper through illegal channels than legal ones, and for consumers who did not ask too many questions, the moral calculus was simple: the tax that made legal goods expensive was widely considered unjust anyway. Popular sympathy for smugglers in coastal communities was genuine and deep, partly because smugglers were local figures whose operations provided economic benefits to many people.
From the perspective of customs officials and revenue officers: Confronting organized smuggling operations was dangerous and poorly rewarded. Revenue officers were outnumbered, outgunned, and operating in communities where they had no social support and where potential informers faced severe retaliation. The temptation to accept bribes was enormous, not only because the financial reward was real but because the alternative could be violence. Those officials who did pursue smugglers zealously risked their lives, and the murders of William Galley and Daniel Chater by the Hawkhurst Gang were not unique in the annals of eighteenth-century smuggling enforcement.
From the perspective of legitimate merchants and manufacturers: Smuggling was devastating to businesses that paid their taxes and followed the law. A silk weaver in Spitalfields who paid duties on legally imported raw materials could not compete with a smuggler who paid no duties at all. A tobacco merchant who worked within the Navigation Acts could be undercut by a competitor who did not. The consistent complaint of legitimate traders throughout the early modern period was that the failure to enforce the law created a race to the bottom in which honest merchants were driven out of business by dishonest ones.
From the perspective of enslaved people within colonial black markets: The black market economies of the colonial Atlantic world were experienced very differently by the enslaved people whose labor produced the commodities being traded. For the majority, the black market represented simply another dimension of an economic system that extracted their labor without compensation and denied them legal personhood. For a minority, particularly skilled urban slaves and free Black people in colonial port towns, the informal and semi-legal economies of colonial ports offered small spaces of economic agency within a system of pervasive oppression.
Constitutional Connection

From Smuggling to Revolution: Contraband Trade and the Origins of American Constitutional Rights

The history of colonial smuggling and the British government's attempts to suppress it connects directly to the constitutional principles enshrined in the American Bill of Rights and to the intellectual traditions of constitutional government more broadly. When the British government moved in the 1760s to reform the colonial customs service and enforce the Navigation Acts more strictly, the tools it used provoked fierce constitutional objections that helped ignite the American Revolution.

The writs of assistance, general search warrants that allowed customs officials to search any premises without specifying the target of the search or having to establish probable cause, were challenged in the Massachusetts courts in 1761 in a case argued by James Otis Jr. Otis's argument, that general warrants violated the fundamental rights of Englishmen to security in their homes and papers, was influential on a generation of colonial lawyers and politicians, including John Adams, who later recalled that the argument against writs of assistance was the "child independence" and the moment when "the child independence was born." The Fourth Amendment to the United States Constitution, which prohibits unreasonable searches and seizures and requires warrants to specify the place to be searched and the persons or things to be seized, was a direct response to exactly this kind of colonial grievance.

The Stamp Act of 1765 and the Townshend Acts of 1767, which imposed new taxes on colonial trade, provoked resistance partly because they threatened to subject violators to trial in admiralty courts without juries, a procedure that colonial lawyers argued violated the right to trial by jury guaranteed by English common law. The Sixth and Seventh Amendments to the Constitution, guaranteeing the right to jury trial in criminal and civil cases respectively, reflect this colonial experience. The economic and legal history of early modern smuggling and contraband trade thus runs directly into the constitutional history of the United States, illustrating how the practical experiences of illegal commerce can shape the formal structures of constitutional government.

Cause and Effect

The Rise and Fall of the Golden Age of Piracy

Long-Term Causes

The expansion of Atlantic trade creating enormous concentrations of movable maritime wealth; the mercantilist system that made trade profitable and therefore made attacking trade profitable; the brutal conditions of legal maritime service that made piracy attractive to common sailors; the weakness of colonial governance in the Caribbean that created safe harbors for pirate operations; the long tradition of state-sponsored privateering that trained generations of skilled maritime raiders.

Short-Term Causes

The end of the War of the Spanish Succession in 1713, which demobilized thousands of privateers whose skills were no longer wanted by the state but who had no other profession; the collapse of the Spanish treasure fleet on the Florida coast in 1715, which drew treasure hunters to the Caribbean; the weakness of Governor Eden of North Carolina, who tolerated Blackbeard's operations, and similar governance failures across the Caribbean.

The Suppression

The appointment of Woodes Rogers as governor of the Bahamas in 1718, backed by naval force that could offer pardons to those who surrendered and execution to those who did not; the sustained Royal Navy campaign against pirate vessels in the early 1720s; the elimination of shore-based networks, particularly the colonial merchants who fenced pirate goods; the execution of major pirate leaders whose deaths disrupted organizational networks.

Effects and Legacy

The near-elimination of organized piracy in the Atlantic world within a decade of the campaign's beginning; a dramatic reduction in insurance costs and an increase in the security and profitability of Atlantic trade; the displacement of maritime predation toward other regions, including the Indian Ocean and the Pacific; the creation of the cultural mythology of piracy, through popular accounts like Defoe's, that has shaped representations of pirates ever since; and, ultimately, the confirmation that organized criminal enterprise can be effectively suppressed when states commit sufficient resources and political will to the effort.

Historical complexity: The Golden Age of Piracy cannot be explained by any single factor. It emerged from the interaction of structural economic incentives, particular historical conjunctures, governance failures, and individual choices. Its suppression similarly required addressing multiple causes simultaneously, not simply deploying naval force.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Criminal Enterprise from the Medieval to the Early Modern World

What Changed

  • The geographic scale of criminal enterprise expanded from regional and continental to genuinely global, following the expansion of oceanic trade
  • The organizational sophistication of criminal networks increased, with more complex supply chains, more elaborate financial instruments, and more systematic corruption of officials
  • The relationship between criminal enterprise and the state became more explicit and more institutionalized, through formal mechanisms like letters of marque and the tax-farming systems that were themselves targets and beneficiaries of contraband trade
  • The commodities involved in major criminal markets shifted to reflect the new global economy, with tobacco, sugar, tea, silver, and enslaved people replacing the regional commodities of medieval criminal trade
  • The volume of criminal enterprise increased enormously, as the expansion of global trade created both larger targets and larger consumer markets for illegal goods

What Stayed the Same

  • The fundamental economic logic of criminal enterprise, exploiting the gap between the price of legal goods and the potential returns from illegal trade, remained unchanged
  • The dependence of criminal enterprise on corruption within the enforcement apparatus of the state continued from ancient times through the early modern period without fundamental change
  • The social structure of criminal networks, in which wealthy and well-connected investors at the top bore little legal risk while poor workers at the bottom bore most of it, replicated the social hierarchies of the legitimate world
  • The state's ambivalent relationship to criminal enterprise, alternately sponsoring, tolerating, and suppressing it depending on current political needs, remained as characteristic of the early modern period as of the ancient and medieval worlds
  • The concentration of criminal activity in geographic spaces where state power was weak, whether border regions, colonial hinterlands, or the high seas, continued to provide the structural setting for organized criminal enterprise

Why the pattern matters: Tracing continuity and change from the medieval to the early modern period reveals that the expansion in the scale and sophistication of criminal enterprise was driven primarily by the expansion in the scale and complexity of legitimate commerce and state power, not by autonomous developments within the criminal world. Criminal enterprise adapted to and exploited the same transformations in commerce and governance that were reshaping the legitimate world, confirming the principle that organized crime evolves in response to the same forces that shape the legal economy around it.

Everyday Life and Social History

The Smuggler's World: Life in the Shadow Economy

For the inhabitants of the coastal communities of Kent, Sussex, Brittany, and Cornwall, smuggling was not simply a criminal activity; it was a central part of the local economy and social fabric. In villages where fishing was uncertain and agricultural wages were low, the income from smuggling, whether as a boat crew member, a carrier, a lookout, or simply a householder who agreed to store contraband overnight, was a significant supplement to household budgets. Communities where smuggling was well-established developed distinctive norms: a general silence about the trade, a willingness to provide false accounts to revenue officers, and a tolerance of the noise and activity that accompanied landing operations at night.

Women played important roles in the smuggling economy that are easy to overlook. The retail distribution of smuggled goods in many communities was conducted through female networks of informal sellers and customers. Women who purchased small quantities of tea, brandy, or tobacco from local suppliers and sold them to neighbors provided the final link in the supply chain between the organized criminal networks and the ultimate consumer. Some women were active in more dangerous aspects of the trade, providing safe houses, information about revenue officer movements, and communication services between different parts of the network. The social history of smuggling, when examined closely, is as much a history of female economic agency within a constrained legal environment as it is a history of male maritime adventure.

For the sailors and carriers who did the physical work of smuggling, the life was one of real risk and modest reward. The men who rowed small boats across the Channel on dark nights, or who carried heavy loads of tea-chests through fields and lanes to inland distribution points, worked hard for their earnings and faced genuine danger both from the sea and from revenue officers who were, by the mid-eighteenth century, increasingly well-armed and willing to use force. Fatalities in confrontations between smuggling gangs and revenue patrols were not uncommon, and the legal penalties for conviction, transportation to penal colonies, the gallows, or service in the navy under the press gang, were severe. The common carrier in a smuggling network was, in the language of modern organized crime scholarship, the street-level worker who bore the greatest physical risk while receiving the smallest share of the profits.

The social legitimacy of smuggling in coastal communities meant that even honest people rarely saw themselves as facilitating crime when they bought from a local seller who happened to have obtained goods without paying the duty. The crown and its customs collectors were distant abstractions; the neighbor who sold cheap tea was a known and trusted person. The moral framework of rural communities in the eighteenth century did not align well with the fiscal imperatives of a mercantilist state, and the practical result was that smuggling operated in many communities more as a normal economic activity than as a criminal one, a perception the state and its revenue officers were constantly and only partly successfully attempting to change.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has examined early modern criminal enterprise across five interconnected dimensions: smuggling empires, colonial black markets, maritime crime, contraband trade, and state-sponsored piracy. Running through all of these topics is a set of recurring analytical themes that will continue to illuminate the history of organized crime in subsequent chapters.

The most fundamental theme is the relationship between legal restriction and criminal market creation. The mercantilist trade policies of early modern European states, with their elaborate systems of tariffs, monopolies, and trade restrictions, created price differentials that made smuggling and contraband trade economically rational for enormous numbers of people across the social spectrum, from wealthy merchant investors to common sailors and rural carriers. This relationship between law and black market is not unique to the early modern period; it is a structural feature of any economic system that attempts to prohibit or heavily restrict trade in goods for which demand exists. The lesson is not that all restrictions should be abandoned, but that policymakers should understand that the price of restriction is the creation of black markets, and that the design of policy should take this into account.

The second major theme is the ambivalence of the state toward criminal enterprise. Throughout this chapter, we have seen the state acting simultaneously as the opponent of criminal enterprise and as its sponsor, protector, and in some cases direct beneficiary. Elizabeth I knighted Francis Drake and shared in his plunder. Colonial governors commissioned buccaneers and shared in their prizes. Customs officials accepted bribes and looked the other way. Tax-farmers who ran the tobacco and salt monopolies in France were themselves participants in a legal system so corrupt that it was barely distinguishable from the criminal networks it was supposed to suppress. This ambivalence is not a corruption of state authority; it is a structural feature of the relationship between states and criminal enterprise throughout history, and it will recur in every subsequent chapter of this textbook.

The third theme is the class structure of criminal enterprise. In the early modern world as in all subsequent periods, the organization of criminal networks replicated and reflected the social hierarchies of the legitimate world. Wealthy investors who provided capital bore little legal risk and received the greatest rewards. Skilled organizers in the middle of the network bore moderate risk and received moderate rewards. Workers at the bottom of the hierarchy bore the greatest physical risk, received the smallest rewards, and faced the harshest penalties when caught. This hierarchical structure, in which the most powerful participants in criminal enterprise are the most insulated from its legal consequences, is one of the most consistent features of organized crime across all periods and regions.

The fourth theme is the geographic concentration of criminal enterprise in spaces of weak governance. The high seas, colonial hinterlands, border regions, and poorly policed coastal areas were the natural habitats of early modern criminal enterprise, just as they had been for criminal networks in the ancient and medieval worlds. The suppression of the Golden Age of Piracy required not only naval force but the extension of effective governance into the spaces, particularly the Bahamas under Woodes Rogers, where piracy had been able to establish semi-permanent bases. This lesson, that criminal enterprise can be suppressed when effective governance is extended into the spaces it occupies, is a pattern that recurs throughout the history of organized crime.

The fifth theme is the role of ordinary people as participants, consumers, and sometimes victims of criminal enterprise. The great criminal networks of the early modern period were not isolated from the legitimate world; they were embedded in it, sustained by the purchasing decisions of ordinary consumers who preferred cheap smuggled tea to expensive legal tea, by the silence of community members who valued neighborly solidarity over fiscal obedience, and by the moral frameworks of coastal and border communities that did not recognize the fiscal claims of distant states as morally compelling. Understanding organized crime requires understanding not only the criminal organizations themselves but the social and moral worlds in which they operated, and the complicity, willful or innocent, of the broader public that consumed what they produced.

Finally, this chapter has illustrated the importance of contingency in the history of criminal enterprise. The Golden Age of Piracy was not inevitable; it emerged from a specific conjunction of historical circumstances, the end of a major war, the governance failure in the Bahamas, the particular skills and ambitions of specific individuals, and the particular commercial geography of the Atlantic at a specific moment. Its suppression was equally contingent; it required the political will of the British government, the particular talents of Woodes Rogers as an administrator, and the naval resources that could be spared from other commitments at the right moment. The history of early modern criminal enterprise is a history not only of structural forces but of human choices made under particular circumstances, and understanding those choices is as important as understanding the forces that shaped them.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Barbary corsairs
Maritime predators operating from the North African ports of Algiers, Tunis, Tripoli, and Salé, licensed by the Ottoman Empire and its client states to raid Mediterranean and Atlantic shipping and take captives for ransom or enslaved labor.
Buccaneer
A maritime raider operating in the Caribbean in the seventeenth century, initially tolerated or semi-licensed by English and French colonial authorities as an unofficial naval auxiliary against Spanish power, later suppressed as piracy became politically inconvenient.
Commutation Act (1784)
British legislation that dramatically reduced the duty on tea, collapsing the price differential between legal and smuggled tea and effectively ending large-scale tea smuggling by eliminating its economic rationale.
Contraband
Goods prohibited from import, export, or trade either because they are themselves illegal or because they are being traded in violation of licensing, monopoly, or tariff regulations.
Corsair
A type of privateer or pirate, used particularly for the maritime raiders of the Mediterranean, both the Barbary corsairs of North Africa and the Christian corsairs of Malta and other European islands who raided Muslim shipping.
East India Company
English (later British) joint-stock company that held a royal monopoly on Asian trade from 1600, making it the primary institutional target of merchants and consumers who sought to bypass its monopoly through smuggling.
Faux-sauniers
French term literally meaning "false salt-men," used for the organized smugglers who ran contraband salt and tobacco across France's internal and external borders, often in armed groups that clashed violently with the Farmers General's enforcement agents.
Fence
A person or business that buys stolen or smuggled goods and resells them, providing the link between criminal networks and legitimate commercial markets. Fences were essential to the profitability of piracy, smuggling, and other forms of property crime.
Flota de Indias
The Spanish treasure fleet system that transported silver and gold from American colonies to Seville in convoys, the richest maritime target in the early modern world and the primary object of both piracy and state-sponsored raiding by England, France, and the Netherlands.
Gabelle
The French salt tax, notorious for its variation by region and the extreme price differentials it created, generating massive smuggling operations and serving as one of the major grievances of the French Revolution.
Golden Age of Piracy
The period of roughly 1680 to 1730, and particularly the decade from 1713 to 1722, during which organized piracy in the Atlantic reached its greatest extent, producing figures such as Bartholomew Roberts, Blackbeard, and Calico Jack.
Hawkhurst Gang
A violent organized smuggling gang active in Kent and Sussex in the 1730s and 1740s whose murder of two customs-related figures provoked major government response and illustrated the potential for smuggling networks to develop into organized violent crime.
Interlopers
Merchants who traded in commodities or regions where a monopoly company held exclusive rights, thereby technically committing criminal violations of the monopoly, even when their commercial activities were otherwise identical to those of the licensed traders.
Letter of Marque
A royal or governmental document authorizing a private vessel to attack the shipping and property of a specified enemy nation. Letters of marque were the legal instrument that distinguished privateering from piracy, though the distinction was often contested in practice.
Mercantilism
The dominant economic doctrine of early modern European states, holding that national wealth depended on accumulating precious metals and maintaining a favorable balance of trade, leading to elaborate systems of tariffs, monopolies, and trade restrictions that created the conditions for extensive black markets.
Molasses Act (1733)
British legislation that imposed a prohibitive duty on molasses imported into the American colonies from non-British Caribbean islands, intended to protect British Caribbean planters but so economically unviable that it was almost universally evaded through bribery of customs officials.
Navigation Acts
A series of English laws, beginning in 1651, that required goods imported to England or its colonies to arrive on English ships and that required colonial products to flow through English ports, creating the regulatory framework that generated colonial smuggling and eventually contributed to American revolutionary grievances.
Owlers
English wool smugglers, particularly active in the southeastern counties of Kent and Sussex, who exported raw wool to the Continent in violation of English law. The term may derive from their nocturnal operations.
Piracy
Maritime robbery conducted without authorization from any government, distinguished in law from privateering by the absence of a letter of marque, though in practice the distinction was political rather than behavioral.
Privateering
State-licensed maritime predation in which private vessels were authorized by governments to attack the shipping of specified enemy nations, providing naval capability at low cost to the state while generating prize income for the private operators.
Shadow economy
The totality of economic activity that takes place outside official legal channels, including both criminal activity and informal economic activities that are not necessarily criminal but are unrecorded and untaxed.
State-sponsored piracy
Maritime predation conducted with the authorization, encouragement, or tacit approval of a government as an instrument of foreign policy or economic warfare, blurring the distinction between piracy and privateering.
Tax-farming
The early modern practice of selling the right to collect specific taxes to private individuals or companies, who paid the government upfront and kept a portion of what they collected. Tax-farmers were both targets and perpetrators of corruption within early modern fiscal systems.
Wrecking
The practice of profiting from the cargo of ships that ran aground or were wrecked, ranging from legitimate salvage to deliberate luring of ships onto rocks. Wrecking was practiced throughout early modern coastal communities and was often organized and semi-systematic rather than opportunistic.
Writs of Assistance
General search warrants used by British customs officials in the American colonies that allowed searches of any premises without specifying the target or establishing probable cause. Their use provoked constitutional objections that contributed to the American Revolution and influenced the Fourth Amendment.

Major People and Places

Identification & Significance

Francis Drake (c. 1540–1596)
English sea captain who conducted state-sponsored raids on Spanish shipping and ports with backing from Queen Elizabeth I. Knighted for his global circumnavigation, he exemplifies the fluid boundary between state-sponsored raiding and piracy in Elizabethan England.
William Kidd (c. 1645–1701)
Scottish privateer who sailed under a royal commission and was subsequently tried and executed for piracy in circumstances that exposed the political nature of the piracy/privateering distinction and the willingness of powerful sponsors to abandon their agents when politically convenient.
Bartholomew Roberts (c. 1682–1722)
Welsh pirate considered the most successful of the Golden Age by the number of ships captured, estimated at four hundred or more. His death in battle with the Royal Navy in 1722 effectively ended the organized piracy of the Golden Age.
Edward Teach ("Blackbeard", c. 1680–1718)
The most iconic figure of the Golden Age of Piracy, whose deliberate cultivation of a terrifying persona illustrates how pirates used fear and reputation as organizational assets. Killed by a Royal Navy expedition in 1718 off the North Carolina coast.
Henry Morgan (c. 1635–1688)
Welsh-born buccaneer who conducted major raids on Spanish Caribbean settlements under a commission from the governor of Jamaica. His subsequent appointment as deputy governor of Jamaica illustrates the incorporation of former criminals into state authority.
Woodes Rogers (c. 1679–1732)
English privateer and colonial governor whose appointment to the Bahamas in 1718, backed by naval force, broke the pirate republic at Nassau and demonstrated that organized piracy could be effectively suppressed through combination of amnesty and enforcement.
Patrick Colquhoun (1745–1820)
Scottish merchant and London magistrate whose 1800 study of crime on the Thames provided the first systematic quantitative estimate of the scale of organized criminal enterprise in a major city, an early landmark in criminological research.
James Otis Jr. (1725–1783)
Massachusetts lawyer whose 1761 argument against writs of assistance connected the colonial experience of customs enforcement and smuggling to the constitutional principles of protection against arbitrary search that eventually found expression in the Fourth Amendment.
Port Royal, Jamaica
The main English Caribbean port of the seventeenth century, which served as the base for privateers and pirates and provided markets for plundered goods before its partial destruction by earthquake in 1692. Its history illustrates how colonial port economies were built around the intersection of legal and illegal maritime commerce.
Nassau, Bahamas
The center of piracy during the height of the Golden Age, housing hundreds of pirates and their supporting networks until Woodes Rogers's arrival in 1718 began its transformation into a legitimate colonial settlement. Nassau exemplifies the "pirate republic" model of criminal governance in ungoverned colonial spaces.
Sint Eustatius (Dutch Antilles)
A small Dutch Caribbean island that functioned as a major entrepôt for illegal trade between British colonies and French Caribbean islands, exploiting Dutch trade neutrality and liberal commercial policies to serve as a hub for goods that were contraband under British law.
The Hawkhurst Gang
The most violent and notorious of the eighteenth-century English smuggling organizations, operating primarily in Kent and Sussex, whose murder of a customs informer and his escort in 1747 provoked the government response that eventually destroyed it.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter has examined a period when criminal enterprise first achieved genuinely global scale, following the expansion of European oceanic commerce across all the world's inhabited regions. The smuggling empires, colonial black markets, piracy networks, contraband trades, and state-sponsored maritime predation of the early modern period were not simply larger versions of the criminal enterprises examined in the previous chapters on the ancient and medieval worlds. They were qualitatively different in their geographic reach, their organizational complexity, their integration with the legitimate commercial world, and their relationship to state power. They were also, in many cases, directly constitutive of the global commercial system that we still inhabit. The trade routes established by early modern smugglers and contraband merchants, the financial instruments developed to manage the risks of criminal enterprise, the corruption networks that linked criminal organizations to state officials, and the organizational models of pirate fleets and smuggling gangs all contributed to the institutional landscape within which later and more familiar forms of organized crime would develop.

Several lessons from this chapter deserve particular emphasis as students move forward in this textbook. First, the relationship between legal restriction and criminal market creation is not an accident of history; it is a structural feature of any society that attempts to control the flow of goods and services through law. Whether the restricted commodity is tea, tobacco, molasses, or modern narcotics, the economic logic is the same: restriction creates price differentials, price differentials create profit opportunities, and profit opportunities attract criminal enterprise. This does not mean that restrictions are wrong or that all restrictions should be abolished. It means that the costs of restriction, in terms of the criminal markets they generate and the corruption they invite, must be weighed honestly against the benefits, and that the design of policy should take seriously the organizational response of criminal enterprises to the incentives that restrictions create.

Second, the ambivalence of the state toward criminal enterprise that we have observed in this chapter is not a corruption of some purer relationship that existed elsewhere; it is a recurring and structural feature of the history of organized crime. States create the legal frameworks within which criminal markets operate. States sponsor criminal enterprise when it serves their strategic interests and suppress it when it does not. States employ criminals as agents of foreign policy and then discard them when the policy changes. And states are themselves penetrated by criminal influence at every level, from the customs official who accepts a bribe to the royal sponsor who shares in pirate plunder. Understanding organized crime requires understanding this relationship, not as an exception or an aberration, but as a central and recurring feature of how criminal enterprise and state power interact.

Finally, this chapter has illustrated the importance of attending to the human costs of criminal enterprise across the full range of those affected by it. The history of early modern criminal enterprise is not only the exciting story of pirates and smugglers; it is also the history of the common sailors who were murdered, the captives who were enslaved by Barbary corsairs, the customs officers who were killed by smuggling gangs, the enslaved Africans whose forced labor made the plantation economies that criminal black markets exploited, and the ordinary workers who carried contraband through dark fields for modest wages while wealthy investors collected the profits from a safe distance. These human costs must be held in view alongside the organizational cleverness and commercial sophistication that historians rightly admire in the criminal enterprises of this period. The story of organized crime is not only a story about the ingenuity of criminal entrepreneurs; it is also a story about who pays the price for that ingenuity, and that price is almost always paid most heavily by those with the least power to refuse.

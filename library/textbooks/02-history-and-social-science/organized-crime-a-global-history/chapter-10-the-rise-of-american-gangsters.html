Part IV — Organized Crime Comes to America

The Rise of American Gangsters

Organized Crime: A Global History

Chapter Opening

"The city is the natural habitat of the gangster, as it is the natural habitat of ambition, anonymity, and inequality — and organized crime is nothing more than ambition organized in the shadows."

On a warm summer evening in 1890, a visitor strolling through the Bowery district of lower Manhattan would have encountered a world of dense, competing energies. Pushcart vendors crowded the sidewalks. Saloons spilled noise and light into the street. Tenement buildings rose six and seven stories, their windows dark with soot, their fire escapes draped with laundry. Children played in alleys while adults haggled at stalls selling secondhand clothes, cheap food, and goods of uncertain origin. And threading through it all, invisible to the casual eye but structurally present, was a network of criminal organizations that had grown alongside the city itself: street gangs that controlled blocks and corners, gambling operators who ran games from back rooms of saloons and barbershops, vice entrepreneurs who managed prostitution networks extending from seedy lodging houses to more elaborate establishments, and ward bosses who translated criminal connections into political power and political power back into criminal protection. The American gangster was not born in the 1920s. He, and occasionally she, had been building the foundations of organized criminal enterprise in American cities for decades before Prohibition made the phenomenon impossible to ignore.

This chapter examines the formative era of American organized crime, the decades between roughly 1860 and 1920, when street gangs evolved from crude neighborhood protection rackets into complex enterprises with political connections, territorial claims, diversified criminal revenues, and management structures capable of surviving the death or arrest of individual leaders. It is a story inseparable from the larger history of American urbanization, immigration, economic inequality, and political corruption. Understanding why organized crime flourished in the American city during this period requires understanding the city itself: its extraordinary growth, its social stratification, the weakness of its formal institutions, the power of informal ones, and the ways in which the line between legitimate and illegitimate enterprise was constantly negotiated, blurred, and crossed.

The rise of the American gangster was not simply a story of individual moral failure or immigrant pathology, as many observers of the era claimed. It was a structural story, rooted in specific economic and political conditions that made criminal organization not only possible but, for many participants, rational. In neighborhoods where legitimate economic opportunity was sharply constrained by poverty, discrimination, and the brutal rhythms of industrial labor, criminal enterprise offered something that factory work did not: autonomy, income, and a species of social status. In a political system riddled with corruption, criminal organizations offered votes, muscle, and money in exchange for protection from law enforcement. In a society that simultaneously prohibited many forms of pleasure and desired them intensely, criminal entrepreneurs built markets that the respectable economy refused to supply.

The geography of this story was concentrated but not confined. New York City was the largest stage, with its Five Points district, its Bowery gangs, its Hell's Kitchen Irish crews, and its later Italian and Jewish criminal networks. Chicago was a close rival, with its South Side gambling houses, its first-ward saloon politics, and the notorious Levee vice district that operated under explicit political protection for years. New Orleans had its own criminal traditions shaped by its distinctive history as a French and Spanish colonial city, a major port, and a place where racial categories were more complex and more permeable than in most American cities. San Francisco, Boston, Philadelphia, St. Louis, and a dozen other industrial cities all had their own versions of this story, shaped by local ethnic compositions, political cultures, and economic conditions.

What united these different urban criminal worlds was a set of common structural features. Criminal organizations nested within larger social and ethnic communities, drawing on networks of trust, loyalty, and shared identity that formal institutions could not easily penetrate. They provided services, including gambling, alcohol, prostitution, and money-lending, that large segments of the urban population wanted and that law and morality had placed beyond the reach of legitimate commerce. They built relationships with political machines that converted criminal resources into political protection and political protection into criminal profits. And they managed their internal affairs through a combination of hierarchy, personal loyalty, and strategic violence that anticipated the more elaborate organizational structures that would emerge in the Prohibition era.

This chapter also insists on complexity in understanding who the gangsters were and how different groups experienced the criminal underworld. For some young men from immigrant families, gang membership was a transitional phase, a way of establishing status in a neighborhood before moving into legitimate work. For others, it was a career that lasted decades and produced real wealth, however precarious. For the communities in which gangs operated, the relationship was deeply ambivalent: gangs provided certain protections and social services while also extracting tribute, generating violence, and corrupting the political institutions that neighborhoods needed to function. For reformers, the existence of organized crime in American cities was evidence of a political and moral crisis that demanded immediate attention. And for the political machines that depended on criminal relationships to sustain their power, the underworld was simply part of the ecology of urban governance, neither celebrated nor eliminated but managed.

By the time the United States entered the Prohibition era in 1920, the foundations of American organized crime had been laid over more than half a century of urban growth, political corruption, and criminal entrepreneurship. Understanding those foundations is essential to understanding what Prohibition produced and why it produced it so quickly and so dramatically. The gangsters of the 1920s did not appear from nowhere. They were the inheritors and refiners of a tradition of criminal organization that had been developing in American cities since the middle of the nineteenth century. Chapter 10 tells the story of how that tradition was built.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain the structural conditions in late nineteenth-century American cities that created favorable environments for the growth of organized criminal enterprise.
  2. Trace the development of street gangs from neighborhood protection rackets to more sophisticated criminal organizations with political connections and diversified revenue streams.
  3. Describe the major forms of illegal economic activity that sustained criminal organizations in American cities, including gambling, prostitution, and illegal alcohol sales, before Prohibition.
  4. Analyze the relationship between criminal organizations and political machines, explaining how each benefited from the other and how this relationship shaped urban governance.
  5. Identify the major cities in which organized criminal enterprise flourished in this period and explain how local conditions shaped the character of criminal organization in each city.
  6. Compare the experiences of different ethnic and racial communities in relation to organized crime, examining how immigrant identity, racial exclusion, and community networks shaped criminal organization.
  7. Evaluate the perspectives of reformers, residents, politicians, and criminal entrepreneurs on the problem of vice and organized crime in American cities.
  8. Assess the role of corruption in enabling organized crime to function, examining how criminal organizations neutralized law enforcement and purchased political protection.
  9. Connect the formative era of American organized crime to the later developments of the Prohibition era, explaining what continuities and what changes the passage of the Eighteenth Amendment produced.
  10. Distinguish between the historical reality of criminal organizations in this period and the mythologized version that popular culture has transmitted, evaluating what evidence supports and challenges common assumptions.

Historical Context

The rise of organized crime in American cities cannot be understood apart from the extraordinary transformation that American society underwent in the decades following the Civil War. Between 1860 and 1920, the United States completed one of the most rapid industrializations in world history. The population of the country grew from roughly 31 million to more than 106 million. The urban population, meaning people living in cities and towns of more than 2,500 residents, increased from roughly 20 percent of the total to nearly 52 percent. Cities that had been modest commercial centers in 1860 became giant industrial metropolises by 1920. New York City's population grew from about 1.2 million in 1880 to nearly 5.6 million by 1920. Chicago, which barely existed as a city in 1840, had a population of 2.7 million by 1920. These cities grew not only in population but in physical extent, economic complexity, and social diversity, and the institutions of governance, law enforcement, and social order struggled, often unsuccessfully, to keep pace with the pace of change.

The engine of urban growth was immigration. Between 1880 and 1920, more than twenty million immigrants arrived in the United States, the vast majority from Southern and Eastern Europe. Italians, Jews from Russia and the Austro-Hungarian Empire, Poles, Czechs, Hungarians, Slovaks, Greeks, and many others poured into American cities in numbers that fundamentally reshaped the social geography of urban America. These immigrants settled overwhelmingly in specific neighborhoods, often organized along ethnic lines: the Lower East Side of Manhattan was heavily Jewish, certain sections of Brooklyn and the South Side of Chicago were Italian, particular neighborhoods in Boston were Irish, and so on. These ethnic enclaves provided crucial social support networks for newly arrived immigrants, but they also created conditions, including linguistic isolation, economic marginality, and distance from the formal institutions of American civic life, that criminal organizations could exploit and inhabit.

The economies of these neighborhoods were constrained and pressured in ways that formal economic statistics rarely captured. Immigrant workers were concentrated in the most dangerous, least well-paid, and least stable sectors of the industrial economy: garment factories, meatpacking plants, construction gangs, mining operations, and the casual labor markets of ports and railroad yards. Wages were low, hours were long, and employment was irregular. The industrial economy of this era offered most immigrant workers not a path to prosperity but a grinding struggle for survival. In such an environment, the appeal of criminal enterprise as an alternative route to income and status was real and rational, even if it was also dangerous and morally complex.

The political environment of American cities in this era was dominated by political machines: organizations built around ward-level patronage networks, ethnic loyalties, and the systematic exchange of votes for services. In New York, Tammany Hall had controlled Democratic Party politics for decades. In Chicago, similar organizations dominated local government. In Boston, Kansas City, Memphis, and elsewhere, machine politics shaped who got city contracts, who got jobs in city government, and who faced enforcement of the law and who did not. Political machines were not simply corrupt in the narrow sense. They served real functions in an era when formal social welfare institutions barely existed, providing coal in winter, food in emergencies, bail money when someone was arrested, and jobs in city government in exchange for votes and loyalty. But they also systematically tolerated vice and criminal enterprise in exchange for money and political support, creating the protective umbrella under which organized crime operated.

Law enforcement in this period was deeply inadequate to the challenge of controlling organized crime, partly because of institutional weakness and partly because of deliberate corruption. Urban police forces in the late nineteenth century were poorly trained, inadequately paid, politically appointed, and deeply embedded in the patronage systems of the machines they nominally served. Police officers expected to receive payments from gambling houses, saloons, and prostitution establishments in exchange for non-enforcement of the laws against these activities. Promotions within police departments were often purchased rather than earned. And the top leadership of urban police departments was frequently aligned with the political machines that tolerated or actively profited from the criminal enterprises the police were theoretically responsible for suppressing. In this environment, law enforcement against organized crime was not a realistic policy goal but a performance of civic virtue occasionally staged when public pressure demanded visible action.

All of these factors, the explosive growth of cities, the arrival of millions of immigrants with limited economic options, the dominance of machine politics, and the corruption of law enforcement, combined to create the specific historical conditions in which the American gangster emerged. This chapter examines how those conditions translated into the specific criminal organizations, enterprises, and relationships that shaped American urban life from the Civil War through the eve of Prohibition.

Section 10.1

Street Gangs and Urban Crime

The street gang is among the oldest and most persistent forms of collective criminal behavior in American urban history. Long before the term "organized crime" entered common usage, cities like New York, Chicago, and New Orleans harbored networks of young men organized around neighborhood territory, ethnic identity, and mutual protection, and willing to use violence to defend their interests and enforce their claims. Understanding the street gang as a historical institution, rather than simply as a social problem or a symptom of individual pathology, requires examining the specific social, economic, and spatial conditions that produced it.

The earliest major American street gangs emerged in the slum neighborhoods of New York City during the 1820s and 1830s. The Five Points district of lower Manhattan, named for the intersection of five streets near what is now Foley Square, was among the most densely populated and desperately poor urban spaces in the Western world during this period. Former swampland that had been filled and developed cheaply, Five Points flooded frequently, bred disease, and attracted the most marginal populations of the city: free Black New Yorkers, Irish immigrants fleeing famine and poverty, and the urban poor of every origin who could afford nothing better. In this environment, gangs emerged as instruments of neighborhood protection, economic exploitation, and collective identity. The Plug Uglies, the Dead Rabbits, the Roach Guards, and the Shirt Tails were among the most notorious of the antebellum Five Points gangs, and their conflicts drew the attention of newspaper reporters and social reformers who described them in terms mixing genuine horror with sensationalism.

These early gangs were relatively simple in their organization. Leadership was typically informal and contested, based on physical dominance and charisma rather than formal hierarchy. Revenue came from petty theft, pickpocketing, the robbery of individuals who could be isolated from protection, and from whatever tribute the gang could extract from local businesses. Political connections, though nascent, were already present: gang members provided the muscle for ward politics, turning out votes on election day and intimidating opposition voters, in exchange for protection from arrest and occasional cash payments from machine politicians who found them useful.

The evolution from these relatively crude early formations toward more sophisticated criminal organizations accelerated after the Civil War, driven by several interconnected forces. The extraordinary growth of cities created larger and more complex criminal markets. The arrival of millions of new immigrants created new pools of potential recruits and new ethnic networks that criminal organizations could inhabit. The maturation of political machines created more stable and more profitable relationships between criminal enterprise and political power. And the accumulation of experience, as successful criminal operators learned from observation what worked and what did not, gradually produced more sophisticated organizational forms.

The Eastman Gang and the Five Points Gang

By the early twentieth century, New York's criminal landscape was dominated by two major gang organizations whose histories illustrate both the persistence and the evolution of American street gangs. The Eastman Gang, led by Monk Eastman, and the Five Points Gang, led by Paul Kelly, were rivals who between them controlled much of the criminal territory of lower Manhattan for nearly a decade.

Monk Eastman, born Edward Osterman in Brooklyn around 1873, was a physically imposing figure, reportedly standing nearly six feet tall and weighing over two hundred pounds, who built his gang from the street level up through a combination of personal violence and organizational talent. The Eastman Gang at its peak numbered perhaps a thousand members, organized into a loose hierarchy with Eastman at the top, a tier of trusted lieutenants below him, and a large base of young men who could be called upon for specific tasks, from pickpocketing and robbery to providing muscle in labor disputes or election-day violence. The gang's primary territory was the Lower East Side, and Eastman maintained his position there partly through violence and partly through an arrangement with Tammany Hall whereby the gang provided votes and muscle in exchange for protection from serious law enforcement.

Paul Kelly, born Paolo Vaccarelli, represented a somewhat different model of criminal leadership. Italian-born and more cosmopolitan in his self-presentation than Eastman, Kelly cultivated a persona of respectability, operating through the New Paul Kelly Association, a social club that served as a front for the Five Points Gang's criminal activities. Kelly's gang controlled territory on the Lower East Side south of Houston Street and engaged in similar activities to Eastman's organization, but Kelly was more attentive to building stable political relationships and diversifying his organization's revenue streams beyond simple street violence. His Five Points Gang became a training ground for a generation of future criminal leaders: Johnny Torrio and the young Al Capone both moved through its orbit in the early years of the twentieth century.

The rivalry between the Eastman Gang and the Five Points Gang produced periodic violence that alarmed even the Tammany Hall politicians who depended on both organizations. In 1903, a major battle between the two gangs on Rivington Street resulted in multiple casualties and attracted enough public attention that Tammany felt compelled to arrange a summit between Eastman and Kelly, settling the territorial dispute and reducing the visibility of gang violence. This episode was instructive: it demonstrated both the extent to which political machines exercised a moderating influence over gang conflict, and the extent to which that influence was motivated by self-interest rather than any concern for public safety.

Hell's Kitchen and the Irish Gangs

While lower Manhattan's gangs drew the most attention from newspapers and reformers, other neighborhoods produced their own criminal organizations with distinct characters shaped by their specific demographics and circumstances. Hell's Kitchen, the working-class neighborhood on Manhattan's West Side between roughly Thirty-Fourth and Fifty-Ninth Streets and between Eighth Avenue and the Hudson River, was dominated by Irish gangs whose organization reflected the specific social position of the Irish in American urban life.

Irish immigrants had arrived in America in enormous numbers during the famine years of the 1840s and 1850s, and while many had moved into more stable working-class and lower-middle-class positions by the late nineteenth century, those in Hell's Kitchen were concentrated in the most precarious sectors of the economy: dock labor, cartage, construction work on the expanding transit system, and the casual labor market of the waterfront. Gang membership in this context was deeply intertwined with work: the same networks of loyalty and mutual aid that organized gang activity also organized access to employment, particularly on the docks, where gang leaders often functioned as labor contractors, distributing work to their followers and extracting tribute from employers in exchange.

The Hudson Dusters and the Gophers were among the most notable Hell's Kitchen gangs of the early twentieth century. The Gophers at their peak may have numbered over five hundred members and engaged in a wide range of criminal activities from waterfront theft and burglary to protection rackets targeting local businesses. Their relationship with politics was mediated through the Democratic Party's West Side clubs, which depended on gang members to deliver votes and manage elections in exchange for protection. The Hell's Kitchen gangs never achieved the same degree of political prominence as the Tammany Hall operations downtown, partly because the West Side's denser network of legitimate working-class organization, including labor unions and fraternal societies, provided some counterweight to gang influence.

Chicago's Gang Landscape

Chicago's street gang history followed a somewhat different trajectory than New York's, shaped by the city's distinctive geography, its more recent founding, and the particular ethnic compositions of its neighborhoods. Chicago had no equivalent to New York's Five Points, partly because it had been settled later and its slum conditions, while severe, had not had as long to develop. Instead, Chicago's most significant criminal organizations in the pre-Prohibition era were organized less around territorial street gangs and more around specific economic activities, particularly gambling and vice, in particular neighborhoods and under particular political patronage arrangements.

This does not mean that Chicago lacked street violence. The city's ethnic neighborhoods, which included large Irish, Italian, Polish, Jewish, and African American communities, each with their own internal hierarchies and external tensions, generated substantial gang activity. But in Chicago, the pathway from street-level gang activity to more sophisticated criminal organization was mediated more directly through specific political figures and specific vice enterprises than in New York, where the sheer scale and density of the criminal environment produced more autonomous gang structures.

Historical Vocabulary
Racket

A racket is a criminal enterprise that generates revenue through fraud, extortion, or the illegal provision of goods and services. The term has its origins in the late nineteenth century, when criminal organizations in American cities ran "rackety" affairs, events or gatherings that generated noise and confusion providing cover for theft or extortion.

In its most common historical usage, a racket refers to a protection scheme in which a criminal organization demands payment from a business, individual, or community in exchange for protection from harm, which the organization itself is implicitly or explicitly threatening. A saloon owner who pays a gang leader fifty dollars a month in exchange for a guarantee that his establishment will not be vandalized is participating in a classic protection racket. The payment is not voluntary in any meaningful sense, even if no explicit threat is articulated, because all parties understand the implicit threat behind the arrangement.

The term was later extended to describe any illegal business scheme, including gambling operations, bootlegging enterprises, and labor racketeering, in which criminal organization extracts money through coercion, fraud, or the monopolization of an illegal market. This broader usage became especially common in the Prohibition era and beyond.

Section 10.2

Gambling Operations

Of all the illegal enterprises that sustained American organized crime in the pre-Prohibition era, gambling was the most important, the most pervasive, and the most revealing about the nature of the relationship between criminal enterprise, political power, and public demand. Gambling was everywhere in late nineteenth-century American cities: in saloons and poolrooms, in barbershops and cigar stores, in elaborate private clubs accessible only to men of means, and in the street-level number games that gave the urban poor a daily encounter with the hope of sudden improvement in their circumstances. The diversity of gambling operations reflected the stratification of American urban society, with different games serving different classes and operating under different levels of criminal organization and political protection.

The simplest and most widespread form of urban gambling in this period was the policy game, also known as the numbers game or the lottery. Policy was a form of lottery in which players selected numbers from a large range, typically one to seventy-eight, and wagered small amounts, often just pennies, on their selections. Winning numbers were drawn daily, sometimes multiple times daily, and winners received payouts at odds that were substantially less than the true mathematical odds, providing the policy operators with a reliable profit margin. The game was accessible to everyone, regardless of how little money they had, and it attracted enormous participation in working-class and immigrant neighborhoods.

The policy game required organizational infrastructure to operate effectively. Policy wheels, the mechanisms by which numbers were drawn, needed to be operated and overseen. Collections of bets, typically gathered by "runners" who circulated through neighborhoods collecting slips and money, needed to be organized and the revenue accounted for. Payouts to winners needed to be made promptly enough to maintain player confidence. And the entire operation needed protection from police interference, which required the payment of regular bribes to precinct officers, district police captains, and in some cities to central police authorities or their political supervisors.

In New York, the policy game by the late nineteenth century had developed into a substantial industry, with multiple competing policy banks operating in different neighborhoods and under different political protection arrangements. John "Bet-a-Million" Gates and other prominent gambling figures of the era operated policy operations as explicit businesses, with hired employees, regular accounting, and systematic protection arrangements. The revenue from policy in major American cities was substantial: estimates of the total take from New York's policy operations in the 1890s ran to millions of dollars annually, with a significant portion flowing upward to political figures and law enforcement as protection payments.

The Gambling House

At the upper end of the gambling spectrum sat the gambling house, an establishment offering a variety of games, including faro, roulette, poker, and dice games, typically in more elaborate surroundings designed to attract middle-class and upper-class clientele. The great gambling houses of this era were significant establishments, some of them occupying elegant townhouses in respectable neighborhoods, employing dozens of workers, offering food, drink, and sometimes entertainment alongside their gaming operations, and attracting men of wealth and social position who desired the combination of excitement, exclusivity, and illegality that the gambling house provided.

The most celebrated gambling entrepreneur of the mid-nineteenth century was John Morrissey, whose career traced an arc from gang fighter to professional gambler to legitimate politician that illustrated the permeability of the boundary between criminal enterprise and respectable society in this era. Morrissey had been a prize fighter and a member of the Dead Rabbits gang in his youth, but he used the proceeds of his gambling operations to establish a racing track at Saratoga Springs, New York, that became the center of fashionable American horse racing. He was subsequently elected to Congress from New York and served two terms as a congressman before his death in 1878. His career was extreme in its particular combination of achievements, but its basic shape, criminally derived wealth converted into legitimate social status through strategic investment and political participation, anticipated a pattern that would become common in American organized crime.

Chicago's gambling industry in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries was organized around a small number of powerful figures who operated under explicit political protection from the machine politicians who controlled the relevant wards and precincts. Mont Tennes was the most powerful of these figures in the first two decades of the twentieth century. Tennes built a near-monopoly on poolroom betting on horse racing in Chicago through a combination of organizational skill and political connection, controlling the wire service that transmitted race results to poolrooms and using that control to extract tribute from gambling operations throughout the city. His organization anticipated the syndicate structures of the Prohibition era in its geographic reach, its vertical integration of different parts of the gambling business, and its systematic use of political protection as a business resource rather than as an occasional necessity.

Gambling and Social Life

Understanding gambling as a historical phenomenon in this period requires moving beyond the perspective of law enforcement and reformers, who saw it primarily as a source of social harm, to consider why gambling was so enormously popular in working-class and immigrant communities. For workers whose wages provided a meager and uncertain existence, the policy game offered the daily experience of hope: for a few cents, a worker could purchase a ticket on the possibility of a sudden windfall that might solve, however temporarily, the grinding problem of poverty. The irrationality of the gamble from an expected-value perspective is obvious, but expected-value calculations do not capture the psychological and social reality of what the policy game provided to its participants. It was a community activity as much as a financial one, a shared ritual of aspiration played out daily in neighborhood stores and on street corners that knit together the social fabric of communities in ways that observers focused on its economic exploitation often missed.

Gambling also intersected with the social institutions of immigrant and working-class neighborhoods in ways that made its elimination difficult even when reform movements succeeded in disrupting specific operations. Saloons, which were among the most important social institutions in working-class neighborhoods, typically hosted gambling of various kinds as part of their normal operation. The poolroom, ostensibly a venue for the game of billiards, was in practice often primarily a venue for off-track betting on horse races. These establishments were not marginal to community social life; they were central to it, serving functions of male socialization, political organizing, labor recruiting, and credit extension that had no ready legitimate substitutes. Reform campaigns that attacked gambling without attending to the social functions it served often found that communities resisted their efforts, not because communities approved of exploitation, but because the organizations being targeted provided real services alongside the extraction.

Historical Vocabulary
Political Machine

A political machine is a political organization built around systematic patronage, the exchange of jobs, contracts, and services for political loyalty and votes. Political machines dominated urban governance in the United States from roughly the 1850s through the early twentieth century, and in some cities much later.

Machines were not simply corrupt in the narrow sense of providing illegal benefits to individual politicians. They were complex organizations that served real functions in the ecology of urban governance, particularly in an era when formal social welfare institutions were minimal or nonexistent. Ward bosses provided coal, food, rent money, and bail to constituents in distress. They found jobs in city government for loyal followers. They served as intermediaries between newly arrived immigrants and the often-bewildering institutions of American urban life. In exchange, they expected votes, loyalty, and in many cases a portion of whatever financial benefits flowed from city contracts and political protection arrangements.

The relationship between political machines and criminal enterprise was not incidental but structural. Machines needed money to operate, and the protection of vice industries was a reliable source of revenue. Criminal enterprises needed protection from law enforcement to operate, and machines were uniquely positioned to provide it. The result was a symbiotic relationship in which each party provided the other with something essential.

Section 10.3

Vice Districts

The vice district, a geographically defined area of a city within which the sale of sex, alcohol, and gambling was tolerated or explicitly permitted by local political authorities, was one of the most distinctive institutional forms of American urban criminal enterprise in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. Vice districts existed in virtually every major American city during this period, under different names and with different formal statuses, but sharing a common functional logic: the concentration of socially disapproved activities in specific spaces, under the supervision of political and criminal authority, in ways that generated reliable revenue for those authorities while making the activities invisible to the middle-class neighborhoods that would otherwise find them offensive.

The logic of the vice district from the perspective of its political managers was straightforward. Vice was not going to disappear simply because it was illegal. The demand for the services provided in vice districts, alcohol consumption, commercial sex, and gambling, was too widespread, too deep, and too connected to fundamental aspects of male working-class and upper-class social life to be eliminated by prohibition. If vice could not be eliminated, it could be managed: concentrated in specific areas, controlled by specific operators who paid regular tribute to political and law enforcement authorities, and kept sufficiently out of sight that respectable citizens could maintain their own self-image of living in a moral community. The vice district was thus a political compromise between the reality of urban demand for prohibited activities and the formal moral commitments of American public life.

The Levee District, Chicago

Chicago's Levee district, located in the First Ward near the downtown business district, was the most extensively documented vice district in American history, partly because of the extraordinary detail in which muckraking journalists and reform investigators described it, and partly because the political figures who protected it were so brazen in their public celebration of the arrangement. The two First Ward aldermen who presided over the Levee for nearly two decades, John "Bathhouse John" Coughlin and Michael "Hinky Dink" Kenna, were genuinely colorful political figures who made no particular effort to conceal the nature of their arrangements with the vice operators they protected.

Coughlin, a former bathhouse attendant of limited intellectual attainment but considerable political instinct, and Kenna, a diminutive and shrewd saloonkeeper, had built their political power on the First Ward's distinctive social geography. The ward included not only the most concentrated vice operations in the city but also a large population of transient workers, seasonal laborers, and the urban poor who inhabited the cheap lodging houses, saloons, and mission dormitories of the area. Coughlin and Kenna controlled this population through a combination of services, including meals, lodging vouchers, and bail money in emergencies, and organization, including the notorious First Ward Ball, an annual fundraising event whose guest list mixed Chicago's political elite with its most prominent vice operators. The Ball was simultaneously a political fundraiser, a demonstration of machine power, and a celebration of the arrangement between politics and crime that made the Levee possible.

The Levee at its height contained several hundred establishments ranging from cheap dives to the elaborate Everleigh Club, a brothel of such elegance and reputation that it attracted visitors from across the country and became something of a perverse tourist attraction. Operated by sisters Ada and Minna Everleigh from 1900 to 1911, the Everleigh Club charged prices far above those of ordinary establishments, employed musicians and maintained a wine cellar, and attracted a wealthy clientele whose patronage gave the club a certain degree of protection from the police harassment that lower establishments experienced. The Everleighs paid regular tribute to the political and law enforcement authorities who permitted their operation, making their club one of the most profitable enterprises in the criminal economy of early-twentieth-century Chicago.

The Levee finally came to an end under pressure from the reform administration of Mayor Carter Harrison Jr., who ordered the closure of the district's brothels in 1912. The closure of the Levee was genuinely significant: it ended the era of openly operated, politically protected vice districts in Chicago. But it did not end the vice industries themselves. Prostitution, gambling, and illegal alcohol sales continued in Chicago after 1912, but they did so in more dispersed, more covert, and more organizationally sophisticated forms. The destruction of the Levee accelerated the professionalization of criminal enterprise in Chicago, forcing vice operators to develop more elaborate protection arrangements, more sophisticated organizational structures, and more geographically dispersed operations. In this sense, the reform movement's success in closing the Levee paradoxically contributed to the more dangerous organized crime that would emerge in the Prohibition era.

Storyville, New Orleans

New Orleans offered a distinctive model of vice district management in its Storyville district, established by a city ordinance in 1897 and named after Alderman Sidney Story, who had introduced the legislation. Unlike Chicago's Levee, which operated through informal political protection of technically illegal activities, Storyville was created by explicit legislation that delimited a specific geographic area within which prostitution would be tolerated, regulated, and taxed. The ordinance did not legalize prostitution outright but rather specified that the city would not enforce existing laws against it within the designated boundaries.

Storyville operated for twenty years, becoming internationally famous as a center of jazz music as well as commercial sex. The district's establishments ranged from ornate parlor houses catering to wealthy clients to cheap "cribs," small rooms in which individual women received clients for minimal amounts. The racial composition of Storyville was complex, reflecting New Orleans's distinctive history as a French and Spanish colonial city with a significant Creole population that occupied an ambiguous position in the city's racial hierarchy. Black and white women worked in Storyville, sometimes in the same establishments, in an arrangement that would have been impossible in most other American cities of the era. The district was also a significant venue for African American musicians, and several of the most important figures in early jazz history, including Jelly Roll Morton, performed in Storyville's establishments during the district's operation.

Storyville was closed in 1917 by order of the United States Secretary of the Navy, who had authority to prohibit prostitution near military installations, on the grounds that the nearby naval station at Algiers made the district a threat to military health and discipline. The closure of Storyville, like the closure of Chicago's Levee, dispersed vice activities rather than eliminating them, and it contributed to the migration of jazz musicians from New Orleans to Chicago and New York, where their music found new audiences and began its transformation into the central American art form it would become.

Historical Vocabulary
Vice District

A vice district is a geographically defined area of a city within which socially prohibited activities, typically prostitution, gambling, and the sale of alcohol, are tolerated by local authorities. Vice districts existed in virtually every major American city during the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, operating under varying degrees of formal and informal political protection.

The term "vice" reflects the moral framework of middle-class reformers, for whom gambling, prostitution, and excessive drinking were moral failures with social consequences. But vice districts also reflected the reality of demand: they existed because large numbers of people wanted the services they provided and were willing to pay for them. The concentration of these activities in specific spaces was a political solution to the problem of unenforceable prohibition, not a moral endorsement of the activities themselves.

Vice districts generated substantial revenue for the criminal and political organizations that managed them, through direct ownership of vice establishments, through tribute extracted from independent operators, and through the political donations that vice entrepreneurs made to the officials who protected them. When vice districts were closed by reform movements, the activities themselves typically continued in more dispersed and less visible forms, often under the management of more sophisticated criminal organizations.

Section 10.4

Political Machines and Criminal Enterprise

The relationship between political machines and criminal organizations was not a corruption of American democracy but a structural feature of American urban governance in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, built into the institutional fabric of city politics in ways that made it remarkably durable and resistant to reform. Understanding this relationship requires moving beyond the language of individual moral failure, which dominated reformers' accounts of machine corruption, to examine the structural logic that made the machine-crime symbiosis rational and functional for all parties involved.

Political machines needed three things to maintain their power: votes, money, and organizational capacity. Votes came primarily from working-class and immigrant communities whose members depended on machine services and whose loyalty the machine cultivated through systematic patronage. Money came from a variety of sources, including city contracts awarded to machine-connected firms, assessments on the salaries of city employees who owed their jobs to machine patronage, and the protection payments extracted from gambling houses, saloons, and brothels. Organizational capacity came from the ward and precinct structure through which the machine maintained contact with its constituents, delivering services, gathering information, and mobilizing voters.

Criminal organizations needed political machines primarily for protection: protection from law enforcement, protection from prosecution, and protection from the reform movements that periodically threatened to close their operations. A gambling house that paid regular tribute to the relevant precinct captain, district attorney's office, and political boss could operate with minimal interference. One that did not make these payments faced the constant threat of arrest, prosecution, and business disruption. The protection that political machines provided was thus not a luxury but a fundamental cost of doing business in the illegal economy.

Tammany Hall and New York's Criminal Ecology

No institution more fully embodied the machine-crime relationship than Tammany Hall, the Democratic Party organization that controlled New York City politics for much of the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. Under a succession of leaders, including William Marcy Tweed, John Kelly, Richard Croker, and Charles Murphy, Tammany developed what amounted to a franchise system for urban vice, in which political protection was available to gambling operators, saloon keepers, and brothel managers who paid the appropriate tribute and maintained the appropriate political loyalties.

The mechanics of Tammany's relationship with criminal enterprise were extensively documented by the Lexow Committee, which conducted hearings in 1894 and took testimony from police officers, gambling operators, and political figures about the specific arrangements through which protection was provided and payment collected. The testimony revealed a system of extraordinary organizational sophistication. Police captains paid substantial sums for their appointments and expected to recoup these investments through tribute collected from vice operators in their precincts. The amounts were not arbitrary but followed a systematic schedule: gambling houses paid one amount, saloons another, brothels another. The collected tribute moved upward through the police hierarchy, with portions retained at each level and the balance forwarded to the political figures to whom the police owed their appointments.

Richard Croker, who led Tammany from 1886 to 1902, was the most powerful figure in New York City politics during this period and one of the most brazen in his enrichment from political power. Croker entered Tammany as a young Irishman of modest means and retired to Ireland as a multimillionaire, his fortune accumulated through political protection arrangements, city contracts, and investments in real estate and business facilitated by his political connections. His career demonstrated that the machine-crime relationship was not simply about tolerating criminal enterprise at a distance but about the direct personal enrichment of political figures through the systematic taxation of vice.

The Tammany system was also a system of ethnic politics, and the ethnic dimension of machine power had direct consequences for the organization of criminal enterprise. As the composition of immigrant populations shifted from predominantly Irish to increasingly Italian and Jewish by the early twentieth century, Tammany adapted, incorporating new ethnic constituencies into its structure and creating new protection arrangements with the emerging Italian and Jewish criminal entrepreneurs who were building organizations in the city's immigrant neighborhoods. This ethnic flexibility was one of Tammany's sources of organizational resilience: it could accommodate new groups by extending the same protection arrangements to them that it had previously reserved for the Irish.

Chicago's First Ward

The political management of vice in Chicago differed from New York's primarily in being more territorially concentrated and more openly celebrated by its principal managers. The First Ward aldermen Coughlin and Kenna operated what amounted to a franchise on vice in their ward, collecting tribute from hundreds of establishments and using the revenue to maintain a political organization capable of delivering their ward's votes as a block to whichever mayoral or state candidate promised to leave their arrangements undisturbed.

The First Ward Ball, held annually from 1896 to 1908, was the most visible demonstration of this arrangement. Held in the Chicago Coliseum and attended by thousands of participants, the Ball mixed Chicago's political figures, including state legislators, city aldermen, and ward committeemen, with the city's most prominent gamblers, saloon keepers, and brothel operators. Admission was sold at prices that generated substantial revenue for the First Ward Democratic organization, and attendance by a vice operator was understood to be not entirely optional, a payment in social form of the tribute that was otherwise collected in cash. Reform newspapers covered the event with a combination of outrage and fascinated detail, and the Ball became a national symbol of the brazenness with which Chicago's political class conducted its relationship with criminal enterprise.

The durability of the Coughlin-Kenna arrangement demonstrated a characteristic that frustrated reformers consistently underestimated: the machine-crime relationship was not simply imposed from above on unwilling communities but was sustained by genuine constituency support from below. The First Ward included large numbers of working-class voters whose immediate economic interests were served by the machine's patronage operations, and who had little reason to support reform campaigns that might disrupt the services the machine provided. Reformers who imagined that exposing corruption would automatically generate popular opposition to the machine often found instead that immigrant and working-class voters preferred the corrupt machine that served them to the reform government that promised clean administration but typically cared little about their daily economic needs.

Section 10.5

Corruption in Growing Cities

The corruption that sustained organized crime in American cities was not simply the product of individual dishonesty but a systemic feature of urban governance built into the structure of law enforcement, municipal administration, and political organization. Understanding corruption as a system, rather than as a collection of individual moral failures, is essential to understanding why reform movements so often achieved only temporary results, and why the same patterns of criminal-political cooperation recurred in city after city across the country.

Urban police forces in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries operated within an institutional structure that almost inevitably produced corruption. Police appointments were controlled by political machines, which meant that police officers owed their jobs to political figures who were themselves deeply embedded in the protection arrangements that sustained vice. Officers who did their jobs by enforcing laws against gambling and prostitution were not simply disrupting criminal enterprises; they were disrupting the revenue streams that sustained the political organizations to which they owed their employment. Officers who collected protection payments and turned a blind eye to vice were not aberrations in the system; they were functioning precisely as the system expected them to function.

The Lexow Committee hearings of 1894 provided the most detailed documentation of how this system worked in New York. Officers testified about the practice of "buying" positions: a promotion to police captain cost, by the testimony of various witnesses, between ten thousand and fifteen thousand dollars in the early 1890s, a sum that represented several years' salary for a working officer. This investment was expected to be recouped through the tribute collected from vice operators in the captain's precinct. Officers who did not collect this tribute not only failed to profit personally but found themselves at a competitive disadvantage relative to their colleagues who did, because the tribute was expected by the political figures above them in the hierarchy and failure to deliver it could jeopardize the officer's position.

The Structure of Police Corruption

Police corruption in this period was not disorganized bribery but a systematic institutional arrangement with its own rules, hierarchies, and expectations. Each level of the police organization had its own relationship with vice, its own expected share of the protection revenue, and its own political connections. The officer on the beat collected small amounts from street-level operations, the precinct captain collected larger amounts from the gambling houses and brothels in his territory, and figures higher in the hierarchy received percentages of the aggregate collected in their jurisdictions.

This systematic character meant that corruption was self-reinforcing. An honest officer who refused to participate in the system faced not only economic disadvantage but active career sabotage from corrupt colleagues and supervisors who saw non-participants as threats to the arrangement. Conversely, officers who participated deeply in the system had strong incentives to advance within it and to protect it from external challenge. Police opposition to reform movements was thus not simply a matter of individual officers protecting their personal income but of an organizational culture and structure that had evolved to sustain the corruption on which many careers and fortunes depended.

The corruption of the courts added another layer of protection for criminal enterprise. Judges in many cities were elected on tickets controlled by political machines, which meant that prosecutions of vice operators who had political protection were unlikely to result in conviction even when they resulted in arrest. Bail was set at levels that permitted continued operation pending trial. Cases were scheduled and rescheduled until witnesses lost interest or moved away. Charges were reduced or dismissed for technical reasons that courts with different political orientations would not have found compelling. The result was a justice system that functioned as a mechanism for the management of criminal enterprise rather than its suppression, processing arrests in ways that demonstrated the formal existence of law enforcement while ensuring that the enterprises generating protection revenue continued to operate.

Reform Movements and Their Limits

The decades between 1890 and 1920 saw recurring waves of reform activity aimed at disrupting the machine-crime relationship in American cities. The Progressive Era, roughly 1900 to 1917, produced a particularly intense burst of reform energy, including campaigns against vice, efforts to restructure municipal government through civil service reform and proportional representation, and investigative journalism that exposed the details of machine corruption to national audiences. Some of these campaigns achieved genuine results, at least temporarily: corrupt machine bosses were occasionally defeated, vice districts were sometimes closed, and police forces were periodically reorganized under more honest leadership.

But the structural limits of Progressive Era reform were significant and instructive. Reform movements were typically led by middle-class and upper-middle-class Americans whose relationship to the working-class and immigrant communities most affected by crime was distant and often condescending. Reformers tended to diagnose the problem of urban crime in moral and ethnic terms, attributing vice and gang activity to the supposed deficiencies of immigrant cultures rather than to the structural conditions of poverty, inequality, and political exclusion that actually produced them. This misdiagnosis led to reform programs that focused on moral uplift, the provision of settlement houses, temperance education, and Americanization programs, rather than on addressing the economic conditions that made criminal enterprise attractive.

Reformers also consistently underestimated the genuine services that political machines provided to their constituencies and the strength of working-class and immigrant loyalty to machine politicians who had served them. A Tammany ward boss who had provided coal in winter, secured employment for neighborhood residents, and produced bail money in emergencies commanded a real claim on voter loyalty that an honest reform candidate who had done none of these things could not easily displace. The machine-crime relationship was sustained not only by corruption but by a genuine exchange of services for loyalty that gave the machine a popular base reform movements found difficult to erode.

The limits of reform in this period had a paradoxical consequence for organized crime. When reform movements succeeded in closing vice districts, disrupting police protection arrangements, or convicting prominent criminal figures, they did not eliminate criminal enterprise but forced it to adapt. Criminal organizations that had operated openly under political protection learned to operate more covertly and more sophisticatedly. Operations that had been concentrated in specific districts dispersed into residential neighborhoods. Relationships with political figures became more subtle and less visible. The criminal organization that survived a successful reform campaign was typically more resilient, more organizationally sophisticated, and harder to attack than the one that had existed before the reform. In this sense, the reform movements of the Progressive Era, by disrupting but not destroying criminal enterprise, contributed to its professionalization in ways that bore fruit in the Prohibition era.

Primary Source Window

Jacob Riis, How the Other Half Lives (1890)

Creator: Jacob Riis · Date: 1890 · Type: Journalistic social investigation and reform advocacy, illustrated with photographs and sketches

Context: Jacob Riis was a Danish-born journalist who worked as a police reporter for New York City newspapers and used his access to police records and his observations of the city's immigrant slums to document conditions that the middle-class public rarely saw. How the Other Half Lives was a landmark of muckraking journalism that used a combination of vivid prose description and some of the first documentary photographs of urban poverty to bring the conditions of New York's tenement districts to a national audience. The book was deeply influential in the Progressive Era reform movement.

Riis described the Mulberry Bend neighborhood, at the heart of the old Five Points district, as a place where families of six or eight people were crowded into single rooms, where children grew up amid conditions of filth, darkness, and chronic shortage, and where the streets at night were the domain of gangs whose violence was a predictable product of the desperation of their environment. He noted that the police were largely irrelevant to the actual social order of these streets, which was governed by informal hierarchies whose authority was based on physical force and neighborhood reputation rather than any recognition by formal institutions.

Analysis: Riis's account is extraordinarily valuable as a primary source documenting the physical and social conditions of late nineteenth-century immigrant neighborhoods, but it must be read critically. Riis was a reform advocate, and his descriptions were shaped by his desire to move middle-class readers to support reform, which meant emphasizing the most dramatic and disturbing aspects of tenement life. He also reflected the ethnic prejudices of his era, describing different immigrant groups in terms that mixed genuine sympathy with ethnic stereotyping. His descriptions of Chinese, Italian, and Jewish neighborhoods, in particular, often relied on ethnic generalizations that historians must recognize as reflecting his era's assumptions rather than objective observation. Riis also rarely gave voice to the residents of the neighborhoods he described, presenting them as objects of social investigation rather than as subjects with their own perspectives and agency.

Section 10.6

Everyday Life and the Criminal Underworld

The history of organized crime in American cities is not only a history of gang leaders, political bosses, and criminal entrepreneurs. It is also a history of the ordinary people who lived within and around the criminal economy: the runners who carried policy slips through immigrant neighborhoods, the bartenders who served drinks in saloons that hosted gambling operations, the women who worked in vice districts, the merchants who paid protection to avoid vandalism, and the residents of criminal-dominated neighborhoods who navigated the presence of gang organizations in their daily lives with a mixture of accommodation, avoidance, and occasional resistance.

For the young men who joined street gangs in immigrant neighborhoods, the decision to do so was rarely a simple choice between legitimate and criminal pathways. In most cases, the boundary between gang membership and ordinary neighborhood life was permeable and sometimes invisible. Gangs organized themselves around neighborhood social networks, and belonging to a gang in its lower levels often meant nothing more than hanging around with the same group of young men who had grown up together, shared school experiences, worked the same casual jobs, and attended the same church or synagogue. The escalation from neighborhood social grouping to criminal organization happened gradually, through specific opportunities and specific decisions, rather than through a single moment of crossing a clearly marked line.

The economic logic that drew young men into criminal activity was straightforward in an environment where legitimate employment was both scarce and brutal. A young man from an Italian or Jewish or Irish immigrant family in lower Manhattan in 1900 faced a labor market that offered, at best, factory work at wages of six or seven dollars a week for sixty or seventy hours of labor in dangerous, degrading conditions. The streets of his neighborhood offered, to those with the right connections and the right combination of nerve and judgment, substantially more: a runner for a policy operation might earn ten or fifteen dollars a week, enough to dress better than factory workers, to contribute meaningfully to the family economy, and to enjoy the social status that a little money and a lot of street presence could confer in a poor neighborhood.

Women and the Criminal Economy

The history of women in the criminal economy of this era is more difficult to recover because women were much less likely to appear in the court records, newspaper accounts, and police reports that form the primary documentary basis for the history of organized crime. Yet women were not absent from criminal enterprise; they occupied specific and often vulnerable positions within it.

The most visible and most discussed role of women in the criminal economy was as workers in vice districts. The women who worked in the brothels, lodging houses, and street-level prostitution operations of districts like the Levee and Storyville occupied positions of extreme vulnerability. They were typically paid a fraction of what their employers collected for their services, required to purchase food, clothing, and lodging from their employers at inflated prices, and subject to a variety of controls that limited their freedom of movement and their ability to leave their situations. The popular narrative of the era emphasized the figure of the innocent girl "lured" or "trafficked" into prostitution by criminal operators, and while trafficking did occur, the majority of women who worked in vice districts had entered more or less voluntarily, driven by economic desperation rather than physical compulsion. The distinction mattered less than reformers sometimes implied: desperation in an economy that offered women few alternatives to low-wage domestic or factory work was itself a form of coercion.

Women also appeared in the criminal economy as proprietors: madams who managed brothels, some of whom, like Ada and Minna Everleigh, achieved considerable wealth and a degree of power within the criminal world. Women ran policy operations in some neighborhoods, particularly in African American communities where the numbers game was a major industry. Women worked as fences for stolen goods, as organizers of shoplifting operations, and in various supportive roles within the organizational structures of criminal enterprises. Their contributions to criminal enterprise were consistently understated both in the contemporary accounts that shaped the historical record and in the subsequent histories that relied on those accounts.

African American Communities and the Criminal Economy

African American communities occupied a distinctive and particularly difficult position in the criminal economy of this era. In northern cities, Black communities were concentrated in specific neighborhoods by a combination of housing discrimination, economic exclusion, and the social dynamics of racial segregation, and criminal organizations operated within those communities in ways that both reflected and reinforced the broader structures of racial inequality in American society.

The policy game was especially important in African American communities in northern cities, where it operated as one of the few economic activities that could provide substantial income to Black entrepreneurs who were excluded from most legitimate business opportunities. Policy operators in Black neighborhoods built genuine organizational structures, employed runners and accountants and managers, and accumulated capital that was reinvested in community businesses, churches, and political organizations. The numbers banker was often a figure of real economic importance in Black neighborhoods, providing credit, employment, and political connections that the formal economy refused to extend to Black residents.

But African American criminal entrepreneurs operated under an additional layer of constraint and exploitation that white operators did not face. White criminal organizations that controlled particular criminal markets in the broader city frequently sought to extract tribute from or take over Black criminal operations, using both violence and political connections to impose their authority. Law enforcement, which was already corrupt and unreliable in its treatment of white criminal enterprises, was even more systematically hostile and exploitative in its approach to Black communities, where the police were an instrument of racial control as much as of criminal justice. The result was that Black criminal entrepreneurs faced all of the normal risks of operating in the illegal economy plus the additional risks of racial violence and racially discriminatory law enforcement.

Historical Debate

Were Political Machines Complicit in Organized Crime, or Were They Providing Necessary Services in a Broken System?

The question: Political machines that tolerated and profited from vice and organized crime have been condemned by reform historians as corrupt institutions that betrayed the public trust. But some historians have argued that machines also served genuine social functions, particularly for immigrant and working-class communities, and that the relationship between machines and criminal enterprise was more complex than simple corruption. How should historians assess the political machines of the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries?

Interpretation A — The Machine as Systematic Corruption: Reformers and many later historians argued that political machines were fundamentally corrupt institutions that enriched a small political class at the expense of the public. The machine-crime relationship, in this view, was not an accidental feature of urban politics but its defining characteristic: machines existed to extract wealth from the city, and the protection of vice was one of their primary revenue sources. The social services machines provided were tools for buying political loyalty, not expressions of genuine concern for constituent welfare. The costs of machine politics, including the perpetuation of poverty through corrupt economic arrangements, the degradation of law enforcement, and the normalization of criminal activity, far outweighed the benefits.
Interpretation B — The Machine as Functional Institution: Other historians, following a tradition associated with sociologist Robert Merton and later scholars, have argued that machines served genuine social functions that formal institutions failed to provide. In an era without welfare programs, employment protections, or effective immigrant assistance agencies, machines provided real services to communities that needed them. The corruption of machines, in this view, must be evaluated against the alternative: not some imagined ideal of clean government, but the actually existing reform governments that typically cared more about middle-class taxpayers than about immigrant workers. Some historians have also argued that for ethnic communities, the machine provided a route to political representation and upward mobility that formal institutions denied them.

Evidence: Both interpretations draw on substantial evidence. The Lexow Committee and other reform investigations documented systematic corruption in detail. But oral histories, community studies, and the records of machine-affiliated social organizations document the real services machines provided. The debate ultimately turns less on facts than on how to weigh the costs and benefits of a deeply imperfect system in a specific historical context.

Why it matters: The debate about machines shapes how historians understand the relationship between criminal enterprise and legitimate politics, the nature of corruption in democratic systems, and the question of what communities actually need from government versus what formal political theory says they should want.

Multiple Perspectives

The Criminal Underworld in American Cities: Four Perspectives

From the perspective of Progressive Era reformers: Middle-class reformers viewed organized crime and vice as evidence of moral failure and political corruption that threatened the foundations of democratic society. They saw immigrant communities as particularly vulnerable to criminal exploitation and believed that education, settlement houses, temperance campaigns, and honest government could eliminate the conditions producing crime. Their solutions tended to focus on closing vice establishments, reforming police departments, and educating immigrant communities about American civic values.
From the perspective of working-class and immigrant communities: For residents of the neighborhoods in which organized crime operated, the relationship with criminal organizations was deeply ambivalent. Gangs provided certain neighborhood services, including protection from outside violence, social solidarity, and sometimes economic support, while also demanding tribute from local businesses, generating violence, and corrupting the political institutions through which neighborhoods might otherwise have secured better services and representation. Many residents tolerated criminal organizations not out of approval but out of a realistic assessment of the alternatives available to them.
From the perspective of criminal entrepreneurs: Those who built criminal organizations in this period operated within a rational economic logic: they identified demands that legitimate markets refused to supply, organized to supply them, and managed the resulting enterprises with whatever combination of service, loyalty, and violence was necessary to maintain their position. For many criminal entrepreneurs, particularly those from immigrant communities with limited legitimate economic options, criminal enterprise was a genuine route to wealth, power, and social status. Many subsequently invested criminal profits in legitimate businesses, real estate, and political careers, blurring the boundary between criminal and legitimate enterprise.
From the perspective of machine politicians: Political figures who maintained relationships with criminal organizations typically did not understand themselves as corrupt in any simple sense. They operated within a system of reciprocal obligations that was simply the normal fabric of urban politics as they had always known it. Vice operators were constituents who needed services and paid for them; the service they needed was protection, and the payment was tribute that funded the political organization's operations. Machine politicians who provided this service were, from their own perspective, doing their jobs effectively in an environment that required pragmatic accommodation with the realities of urban vice.
Cause and Effect

The Rise of Organized Criminal Enterprise in American Cities

Long-Term Causes

Rapid industrialization concentrating millions of workers in cities with inadequate wages and poor conditions. Mass immigration creating large communities with limited economic options and political access. The institutional development of political machines that systematically exchanged political protection for criminal tribute. The persistence of popular demand for gambling, alcohol, and commercial sex that prohibition could not eliminate.

Short-Term and Immediate Factors

The specific ethnic composition of particular neighborhoods creating pools of recruits with shared identities and trust networks. Individual entrepreneurial figures who built organizations on specific criminal opportunities. The development of police corruption systems that made protection of vice enterprises reliable and predictable. Specific reform campaigns that, by closing openly operated vice districts, forced the professionalization and dispersal of criminal enterprise.

The Development

Street gangs evolve from crude territorial protection into more sophisticated organizations with political connections, diversified revenue streams, and territorial management structures. Gambling operations develop from informal neighborhood games into systematic enterprises with organizational infrastructure. Vice districts emerge as politically managed concentrations of illegal activity generating reliable tribute for machine politicians.

Effects and Consequences

The systematic corruption of urban law enforcement and political institutions. The creation of organizational templates and skilled criminal personnel that would be available for the Prohibition era. The normalization of the relationship between criminal enterprise and legitimate politics in urban America. The specific criminal education of figures like Johnny Torrio and Al Capone who would build the next generation of criminal organization.

Historical complexity: The rise of American organized crime cannot be explained by any single factor. It was the product of specific structural conditions, individual entrepreneurship, political arrangements, and public demand, all operating simultaneously within the specific context of rapid American urbanization. No single cause is sufficient, and the interaction between causes is as important as any individual cause in isolation.

American Organized Crime from the 1860s to the Eve of Prohibition

What Changed

  • Gang organization evolved from informal neighborhood groupings to structured enterprises with recognized hierarchy and defined territories
  • Criminal revenue sources diversified from simple theft and extortion to include organized gambling, vice management, and labor racketeering
  • Political relationships became more systematic and more contractual, with protection arrangements regularized and tribute amounts standardized
  • The ethnic composition of criminal organizations shifted as immigration patterns changed, from Irish-dominated gangs to include Italian, Jewish, and other ethnic criminal entrepreneurs
  • Reform campaigns forced criminal operations to become more sophisticated and less visible, accelerating their organizational development

What Stayed the Same

  • The fundamental relationship between criminal enterprise and political protection remained the essential organizing principle of American organized crime throughout the period
  • Gambling, vice, and alcohol remained the primary revenue sources for criminal organizations from the earliest gangs through the eve of Prohibition
  • The concentration of criminal organization in immigrant and working-class communities, where economic constraint made criminal entrepreneurship relatively attractive, persisted throughout the period
  • The systematic corruption of law enforcement remained the most important enabling condition for criminal organization throughout the period
  • The demand for services that organized crime supplied, gambling, commercial sex, and cheap alcohol, remained powerful and relatively constant regardless of their legal status

Why this pattern matters: The combination of organizational evolution and structural continuity in American organized crime meant that by 1920, when Prohibition created a vast new criminal market, organizations with decades of experience, established political relationships, and proven organizational templates were ready to exploit it. The Prohibition era did not create American organized crime; it dramatically expanded a phenomenon that had been developing for half a century.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has examined the formative era of American organized crime, tracing the development of criminal enterprise in American cities from the earliest street gangs of the mid-nineteenth century to the sophisticated organizations that stood ready to exploit the opportunities created by Prohibition in 1920. The story is not one of individual moral failure or immigrant pathology but of structural conditions that made criminal organization a rational and resilient response to specific features of American urban life.

The extraordinary growth of American cities in the decades following the Civil War created the basic conditions for the rise of organized crime. Millions of immigrants arrived in cities that could absorb their labor but could not offer them economic security, social mobility, or meaningful access to the political institutions that governed their lives. In these conditions, the criminal economy offered alternatives, income, autonomy, and status, that the legitimate economy withheld. Street gangs emerged from the social networks of immigrant neighborhoods, drawing on ties of shared identity and mutual obligation to build organizations capable of controlling territory, managing criminal enterprises, and providing social services that formal institutions did not supply.

The evolution of street gangs from crude neighborhood violence into more sophisticated criminal enterprises was driven by specific opportunities and specific relationships. Gambling was the central economic activity of the urban criminal economy in this period, ranging from the penny policy games of working-class neighborhoods to the elaborate gambling houses that served wealthy clientele. Vice districts concentrated prostitution and related activities under political management, generating tribute for the machine politicians who tolerated them. Political machines, the dominant form of urban governance in this era, were structurally dependent on criminal tribute and structurally positioned to provide criminal protection, creating a symbiotic relationship that proved remarkably durable.

The corruption of law enforcement was the essential enabling condition for organized criminal enterprise throughout this period. Urban police forces were politically appointed, inadequately paid, and embedded within the patronage systems of the machines they nominally served. Officers expected to profit from vice by collecting protection payments; supervisors expected their share; and the revenue flowed upward through a systematic hierarchy that made honest law enforcement a structural impossibility rather than a failure of individual character. The courts added another layer of protection, with elected judges beholden to the same machines that depended on criminal tribute.

New York City was the largest stage for these developments, with its Five Points gangs giving way to the more sophisticated organizations of the Eastman Gang and the Five Points Gang in the early twentieth century, both nested within the political protection provided by Tammany Hall. Chicago's criminal ecology was shaped by the First Ward machine of Coughlin and Kenna, who managed the Levee vice district as an explicit political franchise. New Orleans offered the distinctive experiment of Storyville, a formally delimited vice district that operated under explicit city ordinance for twenty years. Each city presented a variation on the same structural theme: criminal enterprise sustained by political protection, organized around the provision of goods and services in persistent demand, and generating revenue that flowed between criminal operators and political figures in a mutually sustaining system.

The experience of different groups within and around this criminal economy was anything but uniform. For the young men who built careers in criminal organization, the rewards could be real, but so were the risks of violence, arrest, and betrayal. For the women who worked in vice districts, the criminal economy typically meant exploitation and vulnerability rather than opportunity. For African American communities, criminal enterprise offered one of the few economic pathways available to Black entrepreneurs while also exposing those communities to exploitation by white criminal organizations and racially discriminatory law enforcement. For working-class and immigrant communities as a whole, the presence of criminal organizations in their neighborhoods was deeply ambivalent, providing certain services and protections while also generating violence and corruption.

Reform movements of the Progressive Era achieved genuine successes in exposing corruption, closing vice districts, and temporarily disrupting machine politics, but they consistently fell short of their larger goals because they misdiagnosed the conditions producing crime. By focusing on moral uplift and individual character rather than on the structural conditions of economic inequality and political exclusion, reformers failed to address the underlying reasons why criminal enterprise was attractive, and they underestimated the genuine services that machines and criminal organizations provided to the communities that sustained them.

The closure of vice districts in the years before Prohibition had a paradoxical consequence: it forced criminal organizations to become more sophisticated, more covert, and more resilient. The figures who would manage Prohibition-era criminal enterprise, including Johnny Torrio and a young Al Capone, served their apprenticeships in the pre-Prohibition criminal networks of New York and Chicago, learning the organizational techniques, political relationships, and management strategies that would allow them to build the criminal empires of the 1920s. When Prohibition created the largest expansion of illegal market opportunity in American history, criminal organizations with half a century of experience were ready to fill it.

The rise of American gangsters in the pre-Prohibition era is ultimately a story about the relationship between a society's formal commitments and its actual behaviors, between the official values that American cities proclaimed and the reality of how they were governed, between the moral categories that reformers and politicians used to describe vice and the human needs and desires that those categories failed to accommodate. Criminal enterprise flourished in American cities not because Americans were more criminal than other peoples but because specific institutional arrangements, structural inequalities, and political cultures created environments in which criminal organization was both possible and, for many participants, rational. Understanding those conditions is the essential first step toward understanding what came next.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Alderman
An elected member of a city council. In the era of machine politics, aldermen were often the key figures through which criminal tribute was collected and political protection arranged, controlling their wards as personal fiefdoms.
Black Hand
A method of extortion used primarily against Italian immigrants in which anonymous letters demanding money threatened violence if payment was not made. The Black Hand was not a unified organization but a technique adopted by many different criminals.
Boss
The leader of a political machine or a powerful ward organization. The "boss" model of urban politics gave enormous power to individual political figures who controlled patronage, contracts, and criminal protection in their jurisdictions.
Brothel
An establishment where prostitution is practiced. In the vice district era, brothels ranged from elaborate establishments catering to wealthy clientele to crude operations serving working-class customers, all paying tribute to political and criminal protectors.
Criminal entrepreneur
An individual who applies organizational and business skills to the development and management of illegal economic enterprises. The term emphasizes the rational, business-like aspects of criminal organization rather than treating crime as simply pathological behavior.
Ethnic enclave
A neighborhood defined by the concentration of a particular ethnic group, often formed by a combination of immigrant chain migration and residential discrimination. Ethnic enclaves provided social support for immigrants but also created conditions that criminal organizations could inhabit and exploit.
Fence
A person who buys stolen goods and resells them. Fences were essential intermediaries in the criminal economy of this era, converting the proceeds of theft into cash and providing a market for stolen merchandise.
Graft
The acquisition of money or advantage through dishonest or unfair means, particularly through the abuse of political office. In the machine era, graft was systemic rather than incidental, built into the normal operation of urban political organizations.
Gambling house
An establishment offering organized gambling, typically including card games, dice, and roulette, often in elaborate surroundings designed to attract middle-class and upper-class clientele. Gambling houses were significant enterprises requiring substantial capital investment and systematic political protection.
Muckraking
A style of investigative journalism popular in the Progressive Era that exposed corruption, social injustice, and the conditions of urban poverty through detailed documentation. Muckraking journalists like Jacob Riis played an important role in bringing the conditions of criminal-dominated neighborhoods to public attention.
Numbers running
The work of collecting bets for the numbers game (policy) from individual bettors in a neighborhood and delivering them to the central operation. Numbers runners were the foot soldiers of the illegal lottery industry, typically earning a commission on the bets they collected.
Patronage
The distribution of government jobs, contracts, and other benefits to political allies and supporters in exchange for their loyalty and electoral assistance. Patronage was the foundation of machine politics and the mechanism through which criminal organizations purchased political protection.
Policy game
A form of illegal lottery popular in working-class and immigrant neighborhoods in which players selected numbers and wagered small amounts on the daily draw. The policy game was one of the most widespread forms of illegal gambling in American cities and a major revenue source for criminal organizations.
Poolroom
Ostensibly a venue for the game of billiards, poolrooms in this era frequently operated as off-track betting parlors for horse racing, providing a location where bookmakers could accept bets and receive race results via telegraph wire services controlled by criminal syndicates.
Precinct captain
The commander of a police precinct and a key figure in the machine political system. Precinct captains were typically political appointees who managed the collection of protection payments from vice operators in their territories and served as intermediaries between criminal enterprise and political power.
Racket
A criminal enterprise that generates revenue through extortion, fraud, or the provision of illegal goods and services. The term originated in late nineteenth-century American slang and became the standard description for organized criminal enterprises of all kinds.
Reform movement
An organized effort to improve social conditions or government practices through advocacy, legislation, or political action. In the context of urban crime, reform movements of the Progressive Era attempted to disrupt the machine-crime relationship through exposure, electoral campaigns, and the closure of vice establishments.
Saloon
A working-class bar that functioned as a central social institution in immigrant and working-class neighborhoods, providing space for socialization, political organizing, labor recruitment, gambling, and in many cases, access to a range of criminal services.
Street gang
An organized group of young men claiming a specific territory and prepared to use violence to defend it and enforce their claims. Street gangs in the late nineteenth century ranged from informal neighborhood social groups to sophisticated criminal organizations with political connections and diversified revenue streams.
Syndicate
An association of criminal entrepreneurs combining their resources and coordinating their activities, typically to monopolize a particular criminal market or territory. The term was used to describe both local combinations of gambling operators and more ambitious multi-city or national criminal alliances.
Tenement
A large apartment building in an urban slum, typically overcrowded, poorly maintained, and inadequately serviced by light, air, and sanitation. Tenements were the primary housing form for immigrant workers in late nineteenth-century American cities and the physical environment within which street gangs and criminal organizations developed.
Territory
A defined geographic area claimed by a gang or criminal organization as its exclusive sphere of operation. Territorial claims were enforced through violence and maintained through relationships with political figures who recognized specific criminal organizations' control of their areas.
Vice
Activities prohibited by law or condemned by prevailing moral standards, typically including gambling, prostitution, and excessive drinking. The term "vice" reflects the moral framework of reformers and lawmakers; from the perspective of those who supplied and consumed these services, they were simply markets for desired goods.
Vice district
A geographically defined area of a city within which prostitution, gambling, and related activities were tolerated by local political authorities, typically under systematic tribute arrangements with criminal operators and the political figures who protected them.
Ward boss
A political operative controlling a ward, the basic geographic unit of urban machine politics. Ward bosses managed the patronage and service relationships that sustained machine loyalty at the neighborhood level and served as the primary interface between machine politics and criminal enterprise.

Major People and Places

Identification & Significance

Monk Eastman (Edward Osterman, c. 1873–1920)
Leader of the Eastman Gang, one of New York's two dominant criminal organizations in the early 1900s. Eastman's career illustrated both the possibilities and the limits of the gang model: he built a large, territorially dominant organization but was eventually imprisoned after a shooting incident and found his political protection withdrawn.
Paul Kelly (Paolo Vaccarelli, 1876–1936)
Leader of the Five Points Gang and a pioneering figure in Italian-American organized crime. Kelly's more sophisticated organizational style, his cultivation of legitimate cover through social clubs, and his attention to political relationships made the Five Points Gang a training ground for the next generation of criminal leadership.
John "Bathhouse John" Coughlin (1860–1938)
First Ward alderman in Chicago who, with his partner Michael Kenna, presided over the Levee vice district and managed Chicago's most brazen example of machine-crime symbiosis. His annual First Ward Ball was a celebrated symbol of the open relationship between Chicago politics and criminal enterprise.
Michael "Hinky Dink" Kenna (1858–1946)
Co-manager with Coughlin of Chicago's First Ward political organization and the Levee district. Kenna's organizational skills complemented Coughlin's public visibility and the two together created one of the most durable examples of ward-level criminal-political management in American history.
Richard Croker (1843–1922)
The "Boss" of Tammany Hall from 1886 to 1902, who built the machine's protection arrangements into a systematic revenue stream and retired as a multimillionaire. His career epitomized the wealth that machine politicians could accumulate through criminal protection arrangements.
Jacob Riis (1849–1914)
Journalist and reformer whose documentation of conditions in New York's immigrant slums brought the social ecology of criminal neighborhoods to national attention. His work, though shaped by the biases of his era, remains an essential primary source for understanding the physical environment of late nineteenth-century urban crime.
John Morrissey (1831–1878)
Gang member turned gambling entrepreneur turned congressman, whose career illustrated the pathways from criminal enterprise to legitimate social status available to skilled and lucky criminal entrepreneurs. His development of the Saratoga racing track represented the conversion of criminally derived wealth into legitimate business.
Mont Tennes (1873–1941)
The dominant figure in Chicago's gambling industry in the early twentieth century, who built a near-monopoly on poolroom betting through control of race wire services and systematic political protection arrangements. His organization anticipated the syndicate structures of the Prohibition era.
Ada Everleigh (1864–1960) and Minna Everleigh (1866–1948)
Sisters who operated the Everleigh Club, Chicago's most celebrated brothel, from 1900 to 1911. The Everleigh Club was an unusually elegant and profitable vice establishment whose owners accumulated substantial wealth before its forced closure by a reform mayor.
The Five Points (New York City)
The most notorious slum district in American history, located in lower Manhattan, where some of the earliest and most influential American street gangs emerged. The Five Points gave its name to one of the most powerful gang organizations of the early twentieth century.
Storyville (New Orleans)
A legally delimited vice district in New Orleans operating from 1897 to 1917, unique in American history as an explicitly legislated zone of tolerated prostitution. Its operation and eventual closure under military pressure illustrated both the political logic of vice district management and the external forces that could disrupt it.
The Lexow Committee
A New York State Senate committee that conducted landmark hearings in 1894, producing the first systematic documentation of police corruption and its relationship to criminal enterprise in an American city. The Lexow hearings set a template for subsequent legislative investigations of organized crime.
Tammany Hall
The Democratic Party political machine that controlled New York City politics through much of the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. Tammany's systematic protection arrangements with vice operators created the model of machine-crime symbiosis that organized crime scholars have analyzed ever since.
Hell's Kitchen (New York City)
A working-class neighborhood on Manhattan's West Side that was home to some of New York's most violent Irish gangs through the early twentieth century. Hell's Kitchen's criminal economy was embedded in the waterfront labor market and the social networks of Irish immigrant communities.

Chapter Reflection

Chapter 10 has told the story of how organized criminal enterprise became a durable feature of American urban life in the decades between the Civil War and Prohibition, embedded in the institutional fabric of machine politics, immigrant neighborhood culture, and the illegal economies that served persistent public demand. This history matters not as a colorful sideshow to the main narrative of American development but as a central story about the relationship between formal institutions and actual social practices, between the laws that American governments enacted and the behaviors that American populations engaged in, and between the ideals of democratic governance and the realities of urban political economy.

The patterns established in the pre-Prohibition era, the symbiosis of criminal enterprise and political power, the corruption of law enforcement as a structural rather than individual phenomenon, the resilience of criminal organization in the face of reform pressure, and the social embeddedness of illegal markets in the communities that sustained them, proved remarkably persistent. They did not disappear with Prohibition; they were amplified by it. They did not disappear when Prohibition was repealed; they adapted to new circumstances. Understanding the structural foundations laid in the decades before 1920 is essential to understanding everything that followed, from the Capone era of the 1920s through the Mafia's national organization in the 1930s and 1940s to the racketeering prosecutions of the 1980s and beyond.

This chapter also raises larger questions about the nature of American democracy and the relationship between formal political institutions and the actual exercise of power. The machine-crime relationship that sustained urban organized crime in this era was not an aberration in American democratic practice but a systematic feature of it, built into the institutional structure of urban governance in ways that proved extraordinarily durable. Understanding why it was so durable requires understanding not only the corruption and self-interest of individual political figures but the structural conditions, the weakness of formal welfare institutions, the exclusion of immigrant communities from legitimate political representation, the impossibility of enforcing prohibition in the face of widespread demand, that made the machine-crime relationship functional for many of the people who participated in it.

The rise of American gangsters in the pre-Prohibition era is, in the end, a story about the gap between formal law and social reality, between what a society officially prohibits and what it actually demands, between the institutions it creates to govern behavior and the actual relationships that govern it. That gap, and the criminal enterprises that inhabit it, did not originate in 1920 with Prohibition, nor did it end when Prohibition was repealed in 1933. It persists wherever and whenever formal prohibition confronts persistent demand in an environment of weak enforcement and available political protection. The history examined in this chapter is not simply the history of a particular era; it is a recurring pattern that appears, in different forms, whenever the structural conditions that produced it are present. Chapter 11 will examine what happened when those conditions were combined with the largest single expansion of illegal market opportunity in American history: the Prohibition era.

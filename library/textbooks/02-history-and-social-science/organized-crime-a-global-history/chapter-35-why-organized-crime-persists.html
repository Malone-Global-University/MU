Why Organized Crime Persists

Organized Crime: A Global History

Chapter Opening

"Organized crime is not an anomaly in human society. It is a permanent feature of any social order that creates forbidden goods, unequal opportunities, and institutions too weak or too corrupt to resist the rewards that criminal enterprise can offer."

In the spring of 1951, Senator Estes Kefauver of Tennessee convened hearings of the Special Committee to Investigate Organized Crime in Interstate Commerce, and for the first time in American history, millions of citizens watched organized crime testimony broadcast on live television. What they saw astonished them. Witnesses described a national criminal syndicate stretching from New York to Miami to Chicago to Los Angeles, with tentacles reaching into city halls, police precincts, union halls, and legitimate businesses across the country. The testimony made one thing startlingly clear: organized crime was not a recent eruption, not a temporary problem that a well-aimed prosecution could extinguish. It had been growing, evolving, and embedding itself in American economic and political life for decades. The question the Kefauver hearings implicitly raised, but could not fully answer, was the same question scholars, policymakers, and citizens have been asking ever since: why does organized crime persist, across generations, across continents, across vastly different political systems and cultural contexts, seemingly immune to even the most determined efforts to suppress it?

This chapter addresses that central question. It does not ask whether individual criminal organizations have been damaged or destroyed by law enforcement, because of course many have been. American prosecutors using the Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act dismantled major Cosa Nostra families in the 1980s and 1990s. Italian prosecutors, at enormous personal risk, brought devastating cases against Sicilian Mafia leadership in the same era. Colombian authorities killed Pablo Escobar in 1993 and broke up the Medellín Cartel. Japanese authorities have pressed the Yakuza through increasingly strict legislation. These were real achievements. But none of them ended organized crime. New organizations filled vacuums left by old ones. Surviving networks reorganized, relocated, and adapted. The global volume of criminal enterprise, by virtually every available measure, has grown over the past century rather than declined.

Understanding why requires moving beyond the question of individual criminal organizations and their leaders, and asking instead about the structural conditions that make organized crime not merely possible but, in a certain sense, inevitable. The answer lies in four interlocking structural realities that this chapter examines in detail. The first is demand: as long as human beings want goods and services that governments prohibit, criminal markets will exist to supply them, because the profit available in serving forbidden demand is enormous precisely because the risk is high and legal competition is excluded. The second is inequality: organized crime recruits most heavily from populations that have been denied legitimate economic opportunity, and it offers rewards, social status, and a sense of belonging that legitimate institutions fail to provide to those at the bottom of deeply unequal societies. The third is institutional weakness: criminal organizations thrive wherever the formal institutions of government, law enforcement, and civil society are too underfunded, too incompetent, or too newly established to provide the security and dispute resolution that communities need. The fourth is corruption: even where formal institutions are formally strong, organized crime can neutralize them by purchasing the cooperation of officials, creating arrangements of mutual benefit that transform the state from a threat into a partner.

These four conditions do not operate in isolation. They reinforce each other in ways that can become self-sustaining. Criminal profits generated by serving forbidden demand fund the corruption of institutions, which weakens those institutions further, which expands the space available for criminal enterprise, which generates more profit, which funds more corruption. Inequality creates a supply of recruits willing to accept the risks of criminal enterprise, which strengthens criminal organizations, which further distort local economies through violence and extortion, which deepens inequality. The result is what scholars of organized crime have called a "criminal equilibrium," a stable, self-reproducing arrangement in which organized crime and the formal order coexist in a relationship of mutual dependence and mutual accommodation.

This chapter also examines why these structural conditions are so difficult to change. Demand for illegal goods is rooted in human desires, many of which have existed throughout recorded history and across every known culture, that do not respond reliably to prohibition or enforcement. Inequality is produced by the same economic and political systems that organized crime parasitizes, and addressing it requires political will and resources that are rarely available precisely where criminal power is strongest. Institutional weakness is partly a matter of resources, but it is also a matter of legitimacy: institutions that populations do not trust or identify with cannot perform the functions that would displace criminal alternatives. And corruption is self-protecting, because those who benefit from corrupt arrangements have both the motive and often the means to prevent the reforms that would end them.

None of this means that organized crime is invincible or that efforts to combat it are futile. The record of the past century shows clearly that specific organizations can be damaged, disrupted, or destroyed, and that the conditions sustaining criminal enterprise can be improved through thoughtful policy. But it does mean that combating organized crime effectively requires understanding its structural roots, not merely pursuing its visible manifestations. A society that arrests cartel leaders without addressing the poverty and institutional failure that make cartel recruitment easy is treating symptoms while the underlying disease progresses. A society that prosecutes corrupt officials without reforming the systems that make corruption profitable is playing a perpetual game of replacement. This chapter provides the analytical tools to think carefully about why organized crime persists, as a necessary foundation for thinking seriously about how it might be reduced.

The chapter draws on examples from across the history of organized crime documented in this textbook: the Sicilian Mafia and its roots in agrarian inequality and weak post-unification governance; the American experience of Prohibition and its demonstration that legal suppression of demand can create criminal supply chains of extraordinary profitability and violence; the transformation of Colombian and Mexican drug trafficking organizations from regional smugglers into multinational enterprises embedded in state structures; the post-Soviet explosion of Russian organized crime in conditions of institutional collapse and economic devastation; and the persistent resilience of criminal networks in cities across the world, from Naples to Lagos to Karachi to Chicago. Together, these cases illuminate the common structural logic that connects organized crime across its diverse historical and geographic forms, and explain why it has outlasted every previous effort to eliminate it.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain the four primary structural conditions, demand for illegal goods, economic inequality, weak institutions, and corruption, that cause organized crime to persist across historical periods and geographic contexts.
  2. Analyze how the prohibition of goods and services creates illegal markets and why those markets tend to be dominated by organized criminal enterprises rather than small independent operators.
  3. Describe the relationship between economic inequality and criminal recruitment, using specific historical cases to illustrate how marginalized populations have been drawn into criminal enterprise.
  4. Assess how weak or delegitimized state institutions create space for criminal organizations to provide alternative governance, dispute resolution, and protection services.
  5. Evaluate how corruption functions as both a tool criminal organizations use and a structural condition that enables their persistence, and distinguish between petty corruption and systemic state capture.
  6. Compare how different societies have attempted to address the structural conditions sustaining organized crime, and assess the relative effectiveness of supply-side versus demand-side approaches.
  7. Identify the concept of criminal equilibrium and explain how self-reinforcing feedback loops between criminal profit, corruption, and institutional weakness can stabilize criminal power.
  8. Trace the ways in which ordinary people, communities, and non-criminal institutions are affected by the persistence of organized crime in their societies.
  9. Contextualize contemporary organized crime within the longer historical patterns documented in this textbook, connecting specific contemporary phenomena to structural conditions that have existed across centuries.
  10. Distinguish between policies that address the surface manifestations of organized crime and policies that address its structural roots, and evaluate the implications of each approach.

Guiding Historical Questions

Questions for Historical Inquiry

  1. Why does organized crime persist across centuries, continents, and vastly different political and cultural contexts, even in the face of sustained law enforcement pressure?
  2. What is the relationship between legal prohibition and the growth of criminal markets? Does suppressing demand reduce criminal enterprise, or does it transform it?
  3. How does economic inequality create the conditions in which organized crime recruits, grows, and maintains its hold on communities?
  4. Why are some states and institutions more vulnerable to criminal penetration than others? What distinguishes weak from strong institutional environments from the perspective of organized crime?
  5. How does corruption function as a structural condition rather than merely a personal failing, and what makes corrupt arrangements so difficult to dismantle once established?
  6. What is the relationship between organized crime and ordinary communities? Are criminal organizations always destructive to the communities where they operate, or do they sometimes provide services that legitimate institutions fail to supply?
  7. When law enforcement destroys a criminal organization, why does another so often take its place? What does this pattern reveal about the structural roots of organized crime?
  8. What historical evidence is there that addressing structural conditions, reducing inequality, strengthening institutions, reducing the scope of prohibition, has reduced the power of organized crime?

Historical Context

Throughout the preceding chapters of this textbook, the history of organized crime has been told primarily as a story of specific organizations: the Sicilian Mafia and its Neapolitan and Calabrian counterparts; the American crime families that organized themselves under Lucky Luciano's Commission in the 1930s; the Japanese Yakuza; the Chinese Triads; the Soviet-era criminal networks that exploded into post-Soviet oligarchy; the Colombian and Mexican drug cartels; the Balkan trafficking networks; and the increasingly transnational cybercriminal enterprises of the twenty-first century. Each of these organizations has its own history, its own internal culture, its own relationship to the state and to the communities where it operates, and its own particular trajectory of rise, consolidation, crisis, and transformation.

But the persistence of organized crime across all of these diverse contexts, its apparent invulnerability to even the most sustained and resourced law enforcement efforts, suggests that the story of individual organizations, however important, is not the complete story. If organized crime were simply a matter of particularly dangerous individuals organizing themselves into criminal enterprises, then sufficiently determined law enforcement ought to be able to eliminate it, at least in relatively well-governed societies with well-funded police and prosecution services. The evidence of the past century suggests this is not so. Every time the American Mafia seemed to be on the ropes, whether from the Kefauver hearings of 1951, the McClellan hearings of the late 1950s, the first major RICO prosecutions of the 1980s, or the "Commission Trial" of 1986, organized crime adapted. Leadership changed, structures reorganized, and new arrangements emerged to fill the spaces that prosecution had temporarily disrupted.

This pattern of disruption and resilience is not unique to the American context. Italian authorities mounted extraordinary anti-Mafia campaigns in the 1980s and 1990s, prosecuted hundreds of Mafia members, and obtained testimony from key informants that penetrated deep into the organizational structure of the Cosa Nostra. The resulting convictions were genuine achievements of remarkable courage and legal skill. But the 'Ndrangheta of Calabria, which was less hierarchical and less visible than the Sicilian organization, continued to grow throughout the same period, and today is considered by many authorities to be the most powerful criminal organization in Italy and one of the most powerful in the world. When the Colombians broke up the Medellín and Cali cartels, trafficking did not stop. It reorganized into dozens of smaller, more flexible operations, some of which eventually merged into the powerful Mexican cartels that came to dominate the trade in the early twenty-first century. When American authorities achieved major successes against particular Yakuza syndicates, the overall criminal economy did not disappear; it shifted and adapted.

The historical record thus presents scholars and students with a compelling puzzle. Law enforcement has never lacked for dedication, courage, or legal tools. Yet organized crime, measured as a systemic phenomenon rather than as the fortunes of any particular organization, has not declined. Why? The answer that this chapter develops begins with a simple but powerful insight: organized crime does not persist because criminal leaders are particularly clever, though some are, and not because law enforcement is particularly weak, though sometimes it is. Organized crime persists because it performs functions, supplying forbidden goods, providing economic opportunity for the excluded, offering dispute resolution and protection where states fail to do so, generating the resources to corrupt those who might otherwise suppress it, that no society has yet eliminated the need for through other means.

This insight has important implications for how we think about organized crime as a social and historical phenomenon. It suggests that the standard "supply-side" approach to organized crime, which focuses on identifying, prosecuting, and imprisoning criminal leaders, while necessary and often important, cannot by itself eliminate organized crime if the structural conditions that generate it remain intact. It suggests that policies addressing demand, through decriminalization, harm reduction, or regulated markets for currently illegal goods, deserve serious analytical attention, even if they raise complex ethical and practical questions. It suggests that policies addressing inequality and institutional weakness, while not primarily designed to combat organized crime, may have more lasting effects on the criminal ecosystem than any prosecution strategy. And it suggests that corruption, which is simultaneously a tool criminal organizations use and a condition their persistence helps create, cannot be addressed effectively unless the structural incentives that make it attractive to officials are confronted directly.

This chapter does not advocate for any particular policy conclusion. The questions it raises, about legalization, about inequality, about institutional design, about the limits of law enforcement, are genuinely contested, and different analysts working with the same evidence have reached different conclusions. What this chapter aims to do is provide students with the analytical framework to engage those debates seriously, grounded in the historical evidence that this textbook has assembled, and equipped with the conceptual vocabulary to evaluate arguments and evidence about why organized crime persists and what, if anything, can be done about it.

Section 35.1

Demand for Illegal Goods and Services

The most fundamental reason organized crime persists is also the simplest: human beings want things that governments make illegal, and as long as that demand exists, someone will find it profitable to supply it. This observation sounds almost too obvious to be useful, but its implications are profound and are frequently ignored in policy debates about organized crime. The history documented in this textbook, from the bootleggers of Prohibition-era America to the narcotics traffickers of the contemporary global drug trade, is, at its root, a history of the relationship between legal prohibition and criminal supply.

When governments declare a good or service illegal, they do not eliminate the desire for it. What they accomplish, sometimes, is a reduction in the quantity demanded, as higher prices and the risks of prosecution deter some consumers. But they also accomplish something else: they transfer the supply of that good or service from the regulated legal economy to an unregulated criminal one. The consequences of this transfer are substantial and largely predictable. Because legal competitors are excluded, suppliers in criminal markets can charge prices far above the cost of production, generating profit margins unavailable in any legitimate industry. Because disputes cannot be settled through courts or regulatory agencies, they are settled through violence. Because participants cannot call the police when they are robbed or defrauded, they must provide their own security, which means building organizations capable of coercion. The result is precisely the organizational form that we call organized crime: a durable, hierarchical enterprise that combines economic activity with the capacity for violence, operating outside the legal framework that governs legitimate business.

The United States' experiment with alcohol Prohibition, examined in detail in Chapter 11, provides the clearest single demonstration of this dynamic in modern history. The Eighteenth Amendment and the Volstead Act did not stop Americans from drinking. They drove drinking underground, transferred the supply of alcohol to criminal networks, and generated profits that funded the organizational consolidation of American organized crime during a critical decade. Al Capone's Chicago operation, examined in Chapter 12, was not a mysterious eruption of criminal genius. It was a rational economic response to an extraordinary market opportunity created by government policy. When Prohibition ended in 1933, the bootlegging empires did not simply disappear, but they lost their most lucrative revenue source and had to diversify into gambling, loan sharking, labor racketeering, and other criminal markets. The lesson is not that Prohibition was a failure in all respects, it did reduce alcohol consumption to some degree, but that the secondary effects of the criminal supply chains it created were enormously costly and long-lasting.

The same dynamic operates in the global narcotics trade, which has provided the dominant context for organized crime since the mid-twentieth century. The global prohibition regime for narcotics, formalized through a series of United Nations conventions beginning in 1961, has not eliminated or even substantially reduced the demand for cocaine, heroin, cannabis, methamphetamine, or the dozens of other psychoactive substances that human beings across the world desire. What it has accomplished is the creation of a multi-hundred-billion-dollar annual criminal market that funds organizations of extraordinary scale and violence, from the Colombian cartels studied in Chapter 22 to the Mexican organizations examined in Chapter 23 to the transnational trafficking networks that deliver their products to consumers across Europe, Asia, and the Americas. The extraordinary violence generated by competition in these markets, the corruption of law enforcement and political institutions required to sustain them, and the organizational sophistication developed by the enterprises that dominate them are all, in a fundamental sense, products of prohibition rather than of the underlying demand for the substances themselves.

The Economics of Forbidden Markets

Understanding why criminal organizations, rather than small independent operators, tend to dominate illegal markets requires understanding the economics of those markets in some detail. In a legal market, many of the functions that create barriers to entry, establishing reliable supply chains, ensuring product quality, resolving disputes with suppliers and customers, protecting inventory from theft, are provided by the legal system. Contracts are enforceable in court. Property rights are protected by police. Disputes are resolved by arbitrators or judges. These institutional supports allow even small operators to function effectively in competitive markets.

In illegal markets, none of these supports are available. A drug dealer who is robbed of his inventory cannot call the police. A supplier who delivers inferior product cannot be sued for breach of contract. A buyer who is cheated has no legal recourse. The absence of legal institutional support creates a premium on size and organizational capacity. Large criminal organizations can enforce their own contracts through the credible threat of violence, protect their members and their assets through their own security apparatus, and maintain supply chain reliability through their own organizational discipline. Small independent operators, lacking these capacities, are vulnerable to predation from larger organizations and cannot provide the reliability that customers and suppliers prefer. The result is that illegal markets tend toward consolidation, dominated by organizations large enough to provide their own governance structures. This is why organized crime, rather than mere street-level dealing, tends to dominate major criminal markets.

This economic logic also explains why the destruction of a particular criminal organization rarely ends the market it served. When the Medellín Cartel was destroyed following Escobar's death in 1993, the demand for cocaine in the United States did not disappear. The market that the Medellín organization had served was immediately contested by surviving Colombian organizations, by the Cali Cartel, and increasingly by Mexican trafficking organizations that had been serving as logistics partners to the Colombians and now began to take over the supply chain. When authorities have succeeded in disrupting a major criminal organization, the result has almost never been the elimination of the criminal market; it has been reorganization of the criminal market around a new set of suppliers. Law enforcement scholars call this the "hydra effect" or the "balloon effect": squeeze the balloon in one place and it expands somewhere else. The market persists because the demand persists, and the demand persists because prohibition does not eliminate the desires that created it.

The Scope of Forbidden Demand

The range of goods and services that criminal markets supply is far broader than narcotics, though the drug trade dominates public discussion. As documented throughout this textbook, illegal markets have been organized around alcohol (during Prohibition), gambling (in most of the United States throughout much of the twentieth century), sexual services (prostitution is illegal or heavily restricted in most of the world), firearms (both in jurisdictions where gun ownership is prohibited and in cross-border trafficking from permissive to restrictive legal environments), counterfeit goods (examined in Chapter 27), human beings trafficked for labor and sexual exploitation (Chapter 26), pirated intellectual property, and many other goods and services.

Each of these markets has its own structure and history, but all share the common feature that the illegality of the underlying transaction creates a premium for the organized criminal networks that can supply it. Gambling provides a useful illustration. For most of the twentieth century, most forms of gambling were illegal in most American jurisdictions. The demand for gambling was evident and substantial: Americans bet on sports, played numbers games, patronized illegal casinos, and sought out bookmakers in every major city. Criminal organizations dominated the supply of these services, using the profits to fund other criminal enterprises and to corrupt the law enforcement officials who might otherwise have suppressed them. When states began legalizing gambling from the 1970s onward, beginning with New Jersey's Atlantic City casino legalization in 1976 and expanding dramatically with the spread of state lotteries, tribal casinos, and eventually sports betting legalization in the 2010s, the criminal share of the gambling market declined substantially, though it did not disappear entirely. This experience suggests that the relationship between the scope of prohibition and the scale of criminal enterprise is not merely theoretical; it is empirically observable and historically documented.

Historical Vocabulary
Black Market

A black market is an economic market for goods or services that are illegal, heavily regulated, or subject to price controls that make legal supply inadequate to meet demand. The term encompasses both the market itself and the networks of suppliers, distributors, and customers that constitute it. Black markets arise whenever the legal price of a good is set below the market-clearing price (through price controls), whenever a good is prohibited outright, or whenever taxes or regulations make legal supply so expensive or cumbersome that consumers prefer to obtain the good through unofficial channels.

Black markets are not simply informal: they are characterized by the absence of legal recourse, which in turn creates demand for the enforcement capacity that organized criminal enterprises can supply. The relationship between prohibition, black markets, and organized crime is one of the most consistently documented patterns in the modern history of criminal enterprise. Wherever governments have prohibited goods for which substantial demand exists, black markets have emerged, and those markets have tended to be dominated by organized criminal networks rather than small independent operators.

The scholarly study of black markets has expanded significantly since the 1980s, with economists, sociologists, and criminologists examining illegal markets using the same analytical tools applied to legal markets. This research has produced important insights: that black market prices reflect risk premiums above the true cost of supply; that quality uncertainty (since there is no product liability in illegal markets) creates distinctive hazards for consumers; and that the violence associated with black markets is a predictable consequence of the absence of legal dispute resolution, not an incidental feature of the personalities of criminal participants.

The policy implications of the demand-side analysis of organized crime are contested but important. If demand is the fundamental driver of criminal markets, then strategies that focus exclusively on supply, arresting producers, seizing shipments, prosecuting distributors, face an inherent limitation: they raise the cost and risk of supply, which raises prices, which in turn attracts new entrants willing to accept the higher risk in exchange for the higher reward. This does not mean that supply-side enforcement is without value: disruption imposes real costs on criminal organizations and may suppress the scale of criminal activity at the margin. But it does suggest that supply-side strategies alone cannot resolve the underlying structural problem, which is that prohibited goods are demanded by millions of people who will seek them out from whoever can provide them most reliably, at acceptable cost and risk.

Demand-side strategies, by contrast, seek to reduce the desire for prohibited goods through prevention, education, and treatment, or to transfer criminal markets to the legal economy through decriminalization or legalization. The evidence on prevention and education is mixed: drug education programs have historically shown limited effectiveness in reducing drug use, though some approaches have shown more promise than others. Treatment programs for addiction have demonstrated more consistent evidence of effectiveness in reducing demand from the most dependent users. Decriminalization and legalization have shown clear evidence of reducing criminal market revenue in specific contexts, most notably in Portugal's 2001 decriminalization of personal drug use, which produced significant declines in drug-related crime and incarceration without producing dramatic increases in drug use. But decriminalization and legalization raise their own complexities, including questions about what goods can be safely commercialized, how regulated markets should be structured, and what effects legal availability has on consumption and related harms.

Section 35.2

Economic Inequality and Criminal Recruitment

The second structural condition sustaining organized crime is economic inequality. Criminal organizations do not recruit from the entire population with equal success. They recruit most heavily, most consistently, and most durably from populations that have been denied access to legitimate economic opportunity, that experience the formal economy as a system rigged against them, and that lack the educational credentials, social connections, and cultural capital required to succeed through legal channels. Understanding this pattern requires no idealization of criminal enterprise and no minimization of the violence and exploitation it inflicts. It requires only an honest recognition of the economic logic that drives criminal recruitment in deeply unequal societies.

The historical evidence for this pattern is extensive and spans every context examined in this textbook. The origins of the Sicilian Mafia, examined in Chapter 6, cannot be separated from the catastrophic economic conditions of post-unification Sicily, where an agrarian economy dominated by large landowners (the latifondisti) left the rural poor with virtually no legitimate economic options and where the new Italian state was both too weak and too alien to provide reliable security or dispute resolution. The early Mafia recruited from precisely this population, offering young men from poor rural families an economic alternative that legitimate Sicily could not match. The Camorra of Naples, examined in Chapter 7, similarly drew on the vast population of the urban poor in one of Europe's most economically marginal cities. The Black Hand extortion gangs and early criminal societies of American immigrant communities, documented in Chapter 9, recruited from Italian, Irish, Jewish, and other immigrant populations who arrived in American cities with few resources, limited English, and restricted access to the legitimate economy, whether through discrimination, credential exclusion, or simply the poverty of their starting position.

The same pattern recurs in every subsequent era. The young men who staffed the bootlegging operations of the Prohibition era were predominantly drawn from poor immigrant neighborhoods in Chicago, New York, and other industrial cities, neighborhoods where legitimate economic opportunity was scarce and where the rewards available in criminal enterprise were genuinely extraordinary by comparison with available legal alternatives. The gang members who populated street-level drug dealing networks in American cities from the 1970s onward were overwhelmingly from poor, racially segregated neighborhoods where deindustrialization had eliminated the manufacturing jobs that had previously provided economic stability, and where the drug trade represented one of the few available paths to income, status, and a sense of belonging.

Inequality as a Structural Condition

The relationship between inequality and criminal recruitment is not simply a matter of absolute poverty. Highly impoverished societies without significant criminal organization exist, and many relatively prosperous societies have extensive organized crime. What appears to matter most is not the absolute level of poverty but the combination of two factors: the gap between the opportunities available to the most excluded populations and those available to the most included, and the perceived illegitimacy of the mechanisms that produce that gap. When young people in poor communities observe that the legal economy offers them not simply modest rewards but actively degrading conditions, while the criminal economy offers not simply higher income but also status, respect, belonging, and a sense of agency, the choice to enter criminal enterprise is not irrational. It is a response to the actual opportunity structure they face.

This analysis does not excuse the violence and harm that criminal organizations inflict on their own communities. Many of the most severe victims of organized crime are the poor and marginalized communities from which criminal organizations recruit. Drug addiction devastates the neighborhoods where drug markets operate. Extortion impoverishes the small businesses that criminal organizations victimize. Violence between criminal competitors kills and maims residents who have no involvement in criminal enterprise. The communities that organized crime most thoroughly exploits are rarely the wealthy communities of the powerful; they are the poor communities of the already-excluded. This is one of the deepest moral contradictions in the sociology of organized crime: that criminal organizations that present themselves as alternatives to an excluding order tend to be most destructive to the very communities they claim to represent.

But this contradiction does not eliminate the structural logic of criminal recruitment. Until legitimate economic opportunities are genuinely available, until discrimination is addressed, until the credentials and connections required for economic success are more widely distributed, until the experience of the formal economy shifts from one of exclusion and degradation to one of genuine participation and reward, criminal organizations will continue to find a ready supply of recruits willing to accept the risks of criminal enterprise in exchange for its rewards. Law enforcement that focuses on prosecuting individual recruits without addressing the inequality that makes recruitment easy is like trying to empty a swimming pool with a teaspoon while the faucet runs full.

Criminal Organizations as Alternative Economies

In some contexts, organized crime does not merely recruit from the excluded. It creates a parallel economic structure that partially substitutes for the legitimate economy in communities where that economy has failed or never existed. The favelas of Rio de Janeiro and other Brazilian cities have been extensively studied as examples of this phenomenon: in neighborhoods where the state provides minimal services, where the formal economy offers little, and where police presence is either absent or experienced as predatory rather than protective, drug trafficking organizations have provided employment, income, and sometimes even basic social services, creating a form of alternative governance that coexists, uneasily and often violently, with the formal state. Similar patterns have been documented in poor urban neighborhoods in Mexico, in parts of sub-Saharan Africa, in post-conflict zones across the Middle East and Central Asia, and in historically marginalized communities in the United States, Italy, and elsewhere.

The concept of organized crime as a provider of "alternative governance" is analytically important and morally complex. It is important because it explains why criminal organizations can maintain genuine popular support in some communities, at least partially: if the Mafia fixes a local dispute that the courts cannot reach, if the cartel builds a football pitch that the municipality cannot fund, if the street gang provides protection that the police cannot or will not offer, then the community's relationship with the criminal organization is not simply one of victimization. It involves elements of exchange, accommodation, and even dependence. Understanding this complexity is not a form of sympathy for criminal violence; it is a necessary condition for understanding why criminal power in some communities is so difficult to dislodge even with sustained law enforcement effort.

It is also morally complex, because the "services" provided by criminal organizations are provided at an extremely high price: in violence, in exploitation, in the suppression of legitimate political activity, and in the perpetuation of the conditions of exclusion and marginalization that make the community dependent on criminal provision in the first place. Criminal organizations that provide alternative governance are not charities. They are enterprises that extract profit from their communities while performing just enough of the functions of governance to maintain their legitimacy and their power. The communities they "serve" are simultaneously their primary victims, a reality that policy approaches focused only on law enforcement tend to obscure.

Section 35.3

Weak Institutions and the Vacuum of Governance

The third structural condition sustaining organized crime is institutional weakness. Criminal organizations flourish most powerfully in the spaces where formal institutions of government, law, and civil society are absent, new, underfunded, delegitimized, or simply unable to perform the basic functions that populations require. These spaces are not anomalies in the modern world; they are common, persistent features of many societies, and they appear not only in the poorest countries but in the poorest neighborhoods of wealthy ones.

The concept of institutional weakness, as it relates to organized crime, encompasses several distinct but related phenomena. The first and most dramatic is what scholars call a "vacuum of governance": a situation in which state authority is effectively absent in a given territory, leaving no functioning mechanism for law enforcement, dispute resolution, or the protection of property rights. Such vacuums arise in conflict and post-conflict zones, in failed or failing states, in remote areas where central government authority has never been effectively established, and in urban neighborhoods that formal authorities have effectively abandoned. In these vacuums, criminal organizations, or organizations that begin as proto-political actors and evolve into criminal enterprises, step in to provide the governance functions that the state cannot or will not supply. The Mafia's origins in post-unification Sicily, where the new Italian state lacked the capacity to project effective authority into the rural interior, represent a classic historical case. The rise of drug trafficking organizations in Mexico's northern states, where formal government authority has been intermittent, contested, and heavily corrupted, represents a contemporary one.

The second phenomenon is what might be called institutional delegitimization: a situation in which formal institutions exist and may even be relatively well-funded, but have lost the trust and legitimacy of the populations they nominally serve. Police departments that are experienced by minority communities as instruments of repression rather than protection; courts that are seen as accessible only to those with money or connections; regulatory agencies captured by the industries they nominally regulate: these institutions exist on paper and have formal authority, but they cannot perform the governance functions that build the social trust that makes communities resilient against criminal penetration. In communities where the police are feared rather than trusted, people do not call the police when they witness crimes. In communities where courts are inaccessible or corrupt, people seek other mechanisms of dispute resolution, including those that criminal organizations provide. In communities where regulatory authority is captured, legitimate competition is disadvantaged relative to criminal enterprise, which does not need to comply with regulations it has purchased the right to ignore.

The Post-Soviet Case

The most dramatic contemporary illustration of how institutional collapse creates space for organized crime is provided by the post-Soviet experience documented in Chapter 19. The Soviet Union had maintained, for all its brutality and dysfunction, an effective system of repression that kept criminal enterprise relatively constrained. Black markets existed and were often tolerated or exploited by Soviet officials, but large-scale organized crime of the kind familiar in Western Europe and the United States was absent from the Soviet landscape. When the Soviet Union collapsed in 1991, it took with it not merely the political system but the entire institutional framework within which economic activity had been organized: property rights, contract enforcement, banking regulation, law enforcement coherence, and the basic governance infrastructure that makes commercial life possible.

Into this institutional vacuum erupted criminal enterprise on a scale that stunned observers. The privatization of Soviet state enterprises, conducted with minimal legal framework and maximal speed, became a vehicle for criminal accumulation by the well-connected. Private "protection" businesses, many of them staffed by veterans of Soviet military and security services, filled the vacuum left by the collapse of state law enforcement, providing the enforcement of contracts and the security of property that the state could no longer reliably supply. These protection businesses were, in most essential respects, organized crime operating under quasi-legitimate cover. By the mid-1990s, Russian organized crime had established itself as a major feature not only of the Russian economy but of criminal markets across Europe and beyond, carrying into the globalized world the organizational capacity and connections accumulated during the chaotic transition years.

The Russian case illustrates a pattern visible in multiple other post-communist or post-conflict transitions: when states collapse or are dramatically weakened, criminal organizations move quickly to occupy the governance vacuum, and once established, they are extremely difficult to dislodge. The organizations that occupy a governance vacuum accumulate economic resources, social relationships, and political connections that give them a powerful stake in preventing the reconsolidation of effective state authority. Criminal actors who benefit from institutional weakness have both the motive and often the means to prevent institutional strengthening. This is one reason why state-building in post-conflict and transitional societies is so difficult, and why the criminal patterns established during periods of state weakness can outlast the weakness that created them by decades.

Institutions and Trust

Scholars of organized crime and political development have increasingly emphasized the role of trust, or the lack of it, in explaining why institutional weakness and criminal power tend to reinforce each other. Institutions gain the capacity to perform governance functions not merely by having legal authority or adequate resources, but by earning the trust of the populations they serve. Trust is built through consistent, fair, non-discriminatory, and effective performance of institutional functions over time. It is destroyed, sometimes rapidly and sometimes irreversibly, by corruption, abuse of power, discriminatory enforcement, or simple incompetence.

Where institutional trust is low, the informal arrangements that criminal organizations can provide, including enforcement of agreements, provision of security, mediation of disputes, and supply of goods that formal institutions will not provide, become more attractive relative to their formal alternatives. The problem is self-reinforcing: low institutional trust leads to greater reliance on informal and criminal arrangements, which reduces the pressure to build effective institutions, which allows institutional quality to remain low, which perpetuates low trust. Breaking this cycle is one of the most difficult challenges in political development, and it is rarely accomplished through law enforcement alone. The institutional reforms required to build trust, consistent anti-corruption enforcement, professional training of police and judiciary, transparent regulation, equitable service provision, are expensive, time-consuming, and politically difficult precisely in the contexts where they are most needed.

Geography Study

Mapping Criminal Opportunity: Weak States, Inequality, and Organized Crime

Scholars and law enforcement agencies have identified a series of geographic and institutional patterns that correlate with the presence and power of organized crime. Students examining a world map in conjunction with this chapter should be alert to the following spatial relationships:

Border zones and jurisdictional gaps: Organized crime consistently exploits the gaps between different legal jurisdictions. International borders, particularly between countries with very different levels of enforcement capacity or very different legal treatments of specific goods, are primary locations for smuggling operations. The U.S.-Mexico border is the most heavily studied example, but similar dynamics operate along the borders of the European Union with non-EU neighbors, at maritime borders where customs enforcement is intermittent, and along the porous borders of many African states. Criminal organizations that can operate fluidly across jurisdictions gain a structural advantage over law enforcement agencies constrained to single jurisdictions.

Post-conflict and transitional zones: Areas recovering from armed conflict, or undergoing rapid political transition, display predictably elevated levels of organized crime. The Balkans after the Yugoslav wars, Afghanistan after successive conflicts, Central American countries affected by Cold War violence, and the post-Soviet space all experienced dramatic expansions of organized criminal activity in the wake of political disruption that damaged or destroyed state capacity.

Urban marginalization: Within wealthy countries, the spatial concentration of poverty in specific urban neighborhoods has been consistently associated with higher levels of criminal organization, particularly in the drug trade. The segregation of poor, minority communities in American cities, produced by decades of discriminatory policy, created precisely the conditions, economic exclusion, institutional delegitimization, concentrated disadvantage, in which criminal markets thrive.

Resource extraction zones: Areas in which valuable natural resources, including not only narcotics crops but also timber, minerals, and other commodities, are extracted in environments with weak regulatory authority are consistently associated with organized crime. The cocaine-producing regions of Colombia and Peru, the timber-smuggling routes of Southeast Asia, and the mineral extraction zones of the Democratic Republic of Congo all illustrate this pattern.

Historical Vocabulary
State Capture

State capture refers to a condition in which private interests, including but not limited to criminal organizations, have so thoroughly penetrated and subverted state institutions that the state effectively serves those private interests rather than the public. State capture represents the extreme end of a spectrum of corruption: rather than individual officials being bribed to perform specific favors, the state itself becomes an instrument of private accumulation and protection, systematically redirecting law enforcement, regulation, prosecution, and public resources toward the benefit of the capturing interests.

The concept was developed in the context of post-Soviet transitions, where rapid privatization and weak governance created conditions in which well-connected individuals could acquire state assets at minimal cost and use political connections to protect those acquisitions from subsequent legal challenge. But state capture is not uniquely post-Soviet: historians have documented analogous conditions in narco-states like Pablo Escobar's Colombia, in Prohibition-era Chicago where Al Capone famously owned much of the city's political infrastructure, and in contemporary contexts from Honduras to Guinea-Bissau where drug trafficking organizations have effectively controlled key government functions.

State capture is analytically distinct from petty corruption, in which individual officials take bribes in exchange for specific favors while the overall institutional framework continues to function. Under state capture, the institutional framework itself has been redirected, making reform extraordinarily difficult because the very officials who would need to implement reform are beneficiaries of the existing captured arrangement.

Section 35.4

Corruption and the Neutralization of the State

The fourth structural condition sustaining organized crime is corruption, which deserves treatment both as a tool criminal organizations use to protect themselves and as a structural condition that shapes the environment in which they operate. Corruption is simultaneously a product of organized crime, because criminal profits provide the funds to corrupt officials, and a condition that enables it, because corrupt officials provide the protection from prosecution that allows criminal enterprises to function with impunity. This circularity is one of the deepest reasons why organized crime is so difficult to eradicate: the very institutions that might suppress it have been compromised by the wealth it generates.

The relationship between organized crime and corruption appears in every era and every context documented in this textbook. The Tammany Hall political machine in late-nineteenth-century New York, examined in Chapter 10, provided systematic protection for gambling, prostitution, and saloon operations in exchange for political support and financial payments, creating an arrangement that was simultaneously political and criminal. The bootlegging empires of the Prohibition era could not have operated without the cooperation of corrupt police officers, judges, and politicians who were paid to look the other way or actively facilitate criminal enterprise. The American Mafia at its peak, documented in Chapter 15, maintained relationships with labor unions, political figures, and law enforcement that gave it a degree of institutional protection that made direct prosecution extremely difficult. The Colombian cartels of the 1980s and 1990s, examined in Chapter 22, corrupted not individual officials but entire institutions: police forces, military units, judicial bodies, and legislative assemblies, creating a condition of near-total impunity in which Escobar could operate with the brazenness that eventually made his organization too visible to be sustained.

The Spectrum of Corruption

Corruption exists on a spectrum, and understanding its different forms is important for analyzing its relationship to organized crime. At one end of the spectrum is petty corruption: the individual police officer who accepts a bribe to ignore a minor violation, the customs inspector who waves through a shipment in exchange for a payment, the licensing official who expedites an application for a fee. Petty corruption is individually modest in scale but can be systemically significant: if every police officer on a given beat accepts small payments from local criminal operators, the cumulative effect is a zone of de facto impunity that enables criminal enterprise even without any high-level coordination.

At the other end of the spectrum is systemic or institutional corruption: the condition in which corrupt arrangements have become so normalized and so widely distributed across an institution that honest individual officers are the exception rather than the rule, and where the informal norms governing behavior within the institution actively reward corruption and punish non-compliance. In a systemically corrupt police force, the officer who refuses to participate in corrupt arrangements may face ostracism, harassment, or active retaliation from colleagues who have a stake in maintaining the corrupt system. In a systemically corrupt judiciary, the judge who rules against a criminal organization may face threats, intimidation, or worse, as demonstrated by the assassinations of Giovanni Falcone and Paolo Borsellino in Italy in 1992, the murders of numerous Mexican judges and prosecutors, and the killings of anti-corruption officials across the developing world.

Between petty corruption and systemic corruption lies what scholars have called "grand corruption": the corruption of senior officials, ministers, legislators, and political leaders who have the power not merely to ignore specific violations but to shape the legal and institutional environment in ways that systematically benefit criminal organizations. Grand corruption may involve direct payments to political leaders, but it may also involve more subtle arrangements: the appointment of sympathetic officials to key enforcement positions, the manipulation of legislation to create loopholes that criminal operations can exploit, the direction of public contracts to businesses with criminal connections, or the use of political influence to obstruct investigations and prosecutions. Grand corruption is typically harder to document and prosecute than petty corruption, because it operates at levels of power where individuals have the resources and connections to protect themselves from legal scrutiny.

Why Corruption Is So Difficult to Address

The persistent difficulty of addressing corruption in the context of organized crime is a product of several reinforcing factors. First, corruption is self-protecting: officials who accept criminal money have a powerful personal interest in preventing the investigations, prosecutions, and institutional reforms that would expose and end their corrupt arrangements. They use the power their positions give them to obstruct, delay, and deflect accountability. In contexts where organized crime has penetrated deeply into political institutions, the officials with the most power to pursue anti-corruption reforms are precisely the officials most likely to be participants in corrupt arrangements, creating a profound collective action problem.

Second, corruption creates shared interests between criminal organizations and their protectors that can be deeply difficult to disentangle. When a police commander is paid to ensure that criminal operators in his precinct are not prosecuted, his personal economic interest, his security, perhaps his family's wellbeing in a context where refusal might bring violent retaliation, align with the interests of the criminal organization he protects. This alignment of interests is not merely venal; it can create genuine bonds of mutual dependence and even loyalty that resist disruption even when prosecutorial pressure increases. Witnesses to corruption who contemplate cooperation with investigators face not only the standard risks of testifying against criminals but the additional risk that the investigators themselves may be compromised, and the additional deterrence of knowing that the officials who would prosecute a corrupt arrangement may themselves be part of it.

Third, the wages of corruption can be so much higher than the wages of honest service that even officials who are not structurally corrupt can be individually tempted in contexts where criminal wealth is abundant and enforcement is weak. A police officer in a Mexican border city earning a modest official salary who is offered a monthly supplement from a cartel that exceeds his annual income faces a choice that is not merely venal but is, in the context of his family's economic vulnerability, genuinely difficult. Anti-corruption strategies that rely solely on deterrence, threatening corrupt officials with prosecution, without addressing the wage structures and institutional cultures that make corruption attractive, address the symptom rather than the cause.

Primary Source Window

Testimony Before the Kefauver Committee, 1951

Source: U.S. Senate Special Committee to Investigate Organized Crime in Interstate Commerce (Kefauver Committee) · Date: 1950–1951 · Type: Congressional hearing testimony, government record

Context: Between May 1950 and May 1951, Senator Estes Kefauver of Tennessee led a Senate committee that conducted hearings on organized crime in cities across the United States, eventually calling over 600 witnesses. The hearings were among the first to be televised nationally, and the public was riveted by testimony from both criminal figures and the law enforcement and political officials who had worked with or around them. The committee's final report concluded that a nationwide criminal syndicate existed and that it had achieved deep penetration of legitimate business, law enforcement, and political institutions.

A particularly striking theme in witness testimony across cities was the frequency with which witnesses described arrangements between local criminal operators and law enforcement as normal, expected, and long-standing. Witnesses from multiple cities described systems in which criminal operators paid fixed monthly fees to precinct captains and beat officers, in exchange for which their operations were protected from prosecution. Several witnesses described the practice as so well established that new criminal operators, on entering a particular territory, were informed of the "rates" by established criminal figures who had already negotiated the arrangements. The committee concluded that in several major American cities, organized crime had become, in effect, a parallel governance structure that operated alongside and in intimate relationship with formal government.

Analysis: The Kefauver Committee testimony is a rich source for understanding the depth of corruption that organized crime had achieved by the mid-twentieth century. Its value as evidence is significant: unlike criminal informants, who have personal interests in their testimony, committee witnesses included law enforcement officers, prosecutors, politicians, and journalists, and the committee's lawyers were able to corroborate much of what witnesses said through documentary evidence. Its limitations are also significant: the hearings focused heavily on Italian American organized crime and may have reinforced ethnic stereotypes that distorted subsequent law enforcement focus. The television coverage also created a public spectacle that served political purposes for participants, and some witnesses may have exaggerated or simplified their accounts for effect. The hearings documented a real phenomenon, but the picture they presented was, like all primary sources, partial and shaped by the perspectives and purposes of those involved.

International Dimensions of Corruption

In the contemporary globalized world, the corruption associated with organized crime has taken on significant international dimensions. Criminal organizations that operate across borders require not merely the corruption of officials in their home countries but the corruption or at least tolerance of officials in the countries through which they route their operations and to which they export their products. The Mexican cartels, for example, require not only the cooperation of Mexican officials but access to American financial systems for money laundering, tolerance from American border officials, and the cooperation of officials in the Central American countries through which supply chains pass. The post-Soviet criminal organizations that penetrated European banking systems in the 1990s required not active corruption of European bankers but at minimum a regulatory environment permissive enough that suspicious transactions could pass without meaningful scrutiny.

The international dimensions of criminal corruption have produced growing interest in international anti-corruption frameworks: mutual legal assistance treaties, asset recovery mechanisms, financial transparency requirements, and coordinated law enforcement operations across borders. These frameworks have produced some successes, but they face inherent structural limitations. Sovereign states retain control over their own legal systems and can decline to cooperate with international requests. Offshore financial centers that profit from attracting criminal money have historically resisted transparency requirements. And the political will to pursue international anti-corruption cooperation is frequently limited by the interests of powerful actors, whether states that use criminal organizations as instruments of foreign policy or financial sectors that prefer not to look too closely at the origins of their deposits, that benefit from the status quo.

Section 35.5

Everyday Life Under the Shadow of Organized Crime

The structural analysis of why organized crime persists, demand, inequality, institutional weakness, and corruption, is essential for understanding the phenomenon at the systemic level. But understanding the persistence of organized crime also requires attending to the human experience of living within communities where criminal power is an everyday reality. Organized crime is not merely an abstract structural phenomenon; it is a social experience that shapes how ordinary people organize their lives, make their decisions, raise their children, conduct their businesses, and relate to the formal institutions of the state. The perspective of communities living under the shadow of organized criminal power is often missing from accounts that focus on criminal organizations and law enforcement agencies.

In communities where organized crime is deeply embedded, ordinary life involves a constant negotiation with criminal power that most residents of more fortunate communities never encounter. A small business owner in a neighborhood under criminal control is likely to pay some form of protection tribute, whether as an explicit demand from a criminal organization or as an informal expectation that is understood without being stated. The price of non-compliance is well known: vandalism, assault, the destruction of the business, or worse. This tribute is a tax on legitimate economic activity that disadvantages honest business and transfers wealth to criminal enterprises, reinforcing the economic inequality that makes criminal recruitment easy.

Residents of communities under criminal influence face decisions about participation, cooperation, and resistance that carry high stakes. Someone who witnesses a crime and considers reporting it to the police must weigh the potential retaliation from criminal networks against the potential failure of the police to protect them, a calculation made more complicated in communities where police are distrusted or where officers are known to have connections with local criminal figures. Parents in such communities must make decisions about how to steer their children away from criminal recruitment without the social and economic resources to provide alternatives that compete effectively with what the criminal economy offers. Community leaders who speak out against criminal power face threats and sometimes violence. The cumulative effect of these pressures is what scholars call a "culture of silence": not approval of organized crime, but a pragmatic accommodation to its power that makes community resistance difficult to organize and sustain.

Gender and Organized Crime

The impact of organized crime on ordinary life falls differently on different members of communities, and gender is among the most important dimensions of that differential experience. The criminal organizations documented in this textbook have been predominantly male enterprises: the leadership, the enforcement capacity, and the most visible forms of criminal activity have been disproportionately male. But this does not mean that women are absent from the story of organized crime. They appear as partners of criminal figures, as participants in criminal enterprise in specific roles, as victims of criminal exploitation, and as primary bearers of the consequences of criminal violence in their families and communities.

Women in families connected to criminal organizations often occupy positions of deep ambivalence: benefiting from criminal income while managing the risks and social costs of criminal association, bearing the primary burden of childcare and family management in environments shaped by criminal violence and the constant threat of imprisonment or death, and facing limited legitimate options of their own in communities defined by male criminal dominance. Research on Italian Mafia families, on the families of Mexican cartel members, and on the families of gang members in American cities has documented these patterns with some consistency, though the specific forms they take vary significantly by context.

Women also appear as direct victims of criminal exploitation, most visibly in human trafficking for sexual purposes, examined in Chapter 26, but also as victims of the protection rackets, extortion, and violence that criminal organizations inflict on communities more broadly. The feminization of poverty, the economic vulnerability of women in deeply unequal societies, makes women disproportionately susceptible to recruitment into criminal exploitation, particularly in contexts where legitimate economic options are limited and where criminal organizations present themselves as offering opportunity or security.

Historical Vocabulary
Social Capital

Social capital refers to the networks of relationships, norms of reciprocity and trust, and shared resources that enable people to cooperate effectively in pursuit of shared goals. The concept, developed by sociologists including Robert Putnam and Pierre Bourdieu, is relevant to the study of organized crime in several ways.

Criminal organizations rely on forms of social capital: the trust relationships between members of the same family or ethnic community, the norms of loyalty and reciprocity enforced by omertà or equivalent codes, the shared resources of information and connection that allow criminal enterprises to coordinate across distances. This criminal social capital is one reason why criminal organizations based on family or ethnic ties, like the 'Ndrangheta's clan structure or the family-based networks of Chinese Triads, tend to be particularly durable: the social capital that sustains them predates and will outlast any particular criminal enterprise, and is not easily targeted by law enforcement.

The relationship between organized crime and legitimate social capital in communities is complex. Criminal organizations can both draw on and destroy the social capital of the communities where they operate. They benefit from community trust, loyalty, and solidarity when those qualities protect members from law enforcement. But they also erode social capital by creating cultures of silence, fear, and distrust that undermine the cooperative relationships on which communities depend for legitimate governance and collective problem-solving. Communities with high criminal presence tend over time toward lower levels of generalized trust and weaker civic institutions, precisely the conditions that make them more vulnerable to continued criminal penetration.

Legitimate Business and Criminal Infiltration

The persistence of organized crime also shapes the experience of legitimate business in communities and industries where criminal penetration is significant. Throughout this textbook, recurring themes have been the capacity of criminal organizations to infiltrate legitimate business sectors: labor unions (examined in the context of the American Mafia's peak influence in Chapter 15), construction (where the Mafia's control of concrete and other materials gave it extraordinary leverage over New York's building industry through much of the mid-twentieth century), garbage collection, trucking, food distribution, and many other industries where criminal organizations could use their enforcement capacity to extort tribute, fix prices, or exclude legitimate competitors.

Criminal infiltration of legitimate business is damaging not merely to the direct victims, the businesses that pay extortion or are excluded from competition, but to the broader economy. It raises costs for consumers, reduces efficiency through the exclusion of legitimate competition, distorts investment patterns, and creates incentive structures that reward criminal connection over legitimate competence. In sectors where criminal infiltration is extensive, honest businesses face a competitive disadvantage relative to businesses that have accepted criminal partnership or protection, creating pressure toward broader criminal accommodation. The long-term economic effects of heavy criminal penetration of particular industries, studied most extensively in the Italian and American contexts, suggest that organized crime is a significant drag on legitimate economic development in the communities and sectors it penetrates.

Historical Debate

Does Legalization Reduce Organized Crime? The Debate Over Demand-Side Approaches

The question: If organized crime persists partly because prohibition creates profitable criminal markets, does legalizing or decriminalizing currently illegal goods and services represent a viable strategy for reducing criminal power? Scholars, policymakers, and advocates have debated this question intensely, particularly in the context of narcotics policy, with genuine disagreement about what the evidence shows.

Interpretation A — Legalization Reduces Criminal Power: Scholars and advocates in this camp argue that the historical evidence consistently shows that prohibition creates criminal markets, that criminal markets fund and empower criminal organizations, and that ending prohibition transfers market revenue from criminal to legal enterprises. They point to the end of alcohol Prohibition in 1933, which eliminated the bootlegging industry; to Portugal's 2001 decriminalization of drug use, which produced significant reductions in drug-related crime and incarceration without dramatic increases in drug use; and to the experience of cannabis legalization in several U.S. states and other jurisdictions, which has substantially reduced criminal market share for cannabis without apparent increases in overall cannabis use among adults. They argue that money spent on supply-side enforcement would be more effectively directed toward treatment and harm reduction, which address demand more directly.
Interpretation B — Legalization Is Insufficient or Counterproductive: Critics of demand-side approaches argue that legalization would not simply transfer markets from criminal to legal suppliers; it would expand markets by increasing availability and reducing social stigma, increasing overall harm even if criminal profit per unit declined. They note that legal markets for alcohol and tobacco, both of which remain heavily criminally infiltrated in some jurisdictions through tax evasion and counterfeit production, demonstrate that legalization does not automatically eliminate criminal involvement. They argue that organized crime is sufficiently diversified that eliminating revenue from one criminal market simply redirects criminal organizations toward others, and that the appropriate response to criminal power is better law enforcement and institutional strengthening, not accommodation to criminal markets.

Evidence: Both interpretations draw on available evidence, and the honest assessment is that the evidence is genuinely mixed and context-dependent. The post-Prohibition American experience and the Portuguese decriminalization results support demand-side arguments. The persistence of criminal involvement in legal alcohol markets (through tax evasion and distribution fraud) and the diversification of criminal organizations into new markets when old ones are disrupted support the skeptical view. Cannabis legalization data is still accumulating and its long-term implications for criminal markets are not yet fully clear.

Why it matters: The debate over legalization is not merely academic. It shapes drug policy in countries across the world, influences billions of dollars in law enforcement spending, determines the incarceration of millions of people, and has profound implications for the communities most affected by both criminal violence and law enforcement pressure. How policymakers think about the relationship between prohibition and criminal markets has direct consequences for human welfare on a large scale.

What additional evidence would be most useful for evaluating the debate? Is it possible that the answer differs by context, substance, or specific legal arrangement?

Multiple Perspectives

Who Experiences Organized Crime's Persistence, and How?

From the perspective of residents of communities with high criminal presence: For people living in neighborhoods where organized crime or criminal gangs exercise significant power, persistence is experienced as a daily constraint on freedom of movement, economic activity, and civic participation. The protection payments, the culture of silence, the constant negotiation between ordinary life and criminal power, and the presence of violence as a background condition of life constitute a form of structural victimization that is not fully captured by aggregate crime statistics. Many such residents do not experience criminal organizations primarily as sources of services or community support; they experience them primarily as sources of threat, taxation, and social restriction.
From the perspective of law enforcement professionals: Police officers, prosecutors, and investigators who work on organized crime cases frequently experience the persistence of criminal organization as a product of the structural conditions, political reluctance to address inequality and corruption, inadequate legal tools, resource limitations, and the jurisdictional constraints of national law enforcement in an international criminal environment, as much as of any failure of enforcement effort. Many law enforcement professionals are acutely aware that prosecuting individual criminals does not address the conditions that produce new ones, and are deeply frustrated by policy frameworks that ask them to address symptoms while structural causes remain intact.
From the perspective of scholars and researchers: Criminologists, sociologists, and historians studying organized crime generally emphasize structural conditions: the relationship between prohibition and criminal markets, the role of inequality in criminal recruitment, the significance of institutional weakness and corruption. From this perspective, the persistence of organized crime across centuries, continents, and cultural contexts is evidence of structural forces more powerful than any individual organization or law enforcement strategy, and the appropriate policy response involves addressing structural conditions rather than focusing primarily on prosecution of individual criminal actors.
From the perspective of policymakers and political leaders: Politicians and policymakers face incentive structures that often pull them toward visible, short-term enforcement actions, such as dramatic arrests and prosecutions of criminal leaders, that can be presented as evidence of decisive action. The structural interventions that scholars emphasize, addressing inequality, building institutional trust, reforming prohibition, are expensive, politically risky, slow to show results, and often unpopular with constituencies that prefer a law-and-order framing. The gap between what the scholarly evidence suggests is effective and what the political environment rewards is one reason why criminal policy so often focuses on supply-side enforcement despite its limited effectiveness at reducing criminal power over time.
Cause and Effect

The Prohibition Cycle: Why Criminal Markets Reproduce Themselves

Structural Conditions

Human demand for goods or services that governments declare illegal; economic inequality creating populations willing to accept criminal risk; institutional weakness limiting enforcement capacity; corruption enabling criminal protection.

Criminal Market Formation

Prohibition transfers supply of demanded goods from legal to criminal economy; high prices attract criminal entrepreneurs; violence replaces legal dispute resolution; large organizations emerge to manage supply chains and enforce arrangements.

Reinforcing Dynamics

Criminal profits fund corruption; corruption weakens enforcement; weakened enforcement enables expansion; expansion generates more profit; more profit funds more corruption. Simultaneously, criminal recruitment from excluded populations strengthens organizations while deepening community dependence.

Long-Term Effects

Criminal equilibrium: organized crime and formal institutions reach stable accommodation that is difficult to disrupt. Law enforcement disrupts individual organizations but structural conditions reproduce criminal enterprise. "Hydra effect" means that destroying one organization creates space for others.

Historical complexity: The persistence of organized crime is not the product of any single cause and cannot be addressed through any single intervention. It is the result of interlocking structural conditions that reinforce each other, requiring responses that address demand, inequality, institutional quality, and corruption simultaneously to be effective over the long term.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Organized Crime from Ancient Trade Routes to the Digital Age: What Changes and What Persists

What Has Changed

  • The specific goods and services at the center of criminal markets have shifted dramatically: from smuggled salt and silk in the ancient world, to untaxed alcohol in the Prohibition era, to narcotics in the modern period, to digital goods and ransomware in the contemporary era
  • The geographic scale of criminal organization has expanded dramatically, from local or regional operations to genuinely transnational enterprises operating across dozens of countries simultaneously
  • The technologies available to criminal organizations have transformed: digital communication, encrypted messaging, cryptocurrency, and the dark web have created new capacities for coordination and concealment
  • The legal and institutional tools available to law enforcement have grown significantly, including international cooperation frameworks, asset forfeiture laws, anti-money-laundering regulations, and prosecutorial tools like RICO that target organizations rather than individual acts
  • The academic and policy understanding of organized crime has deepened considerably, with rigorous scholarship producing analytical frameworks that did not exist a century ago

What Has Not Changed

  • The fundamental logic of criminal markets: prohibition creates profit, profit attracts criminal organization, criminal organization generates violence and corruption
  • The relationship between economic inequality and criminal recruitment: excluded populations have been the primary source of criminal labor across every historical period and geographic context examined in this textbook
  • The role of corruption as both a tool and a condition: criminal organizations in every era have used their profits to purchase protection from the officials who might suppress them
  • The capacity of criminal organizations to adapt: in every historical period, when one criminal market has been disrupted or reduced, surviving organizations have found new markets, new methods, or new organizational forms
  • The resilience of criminal enterprise in environments of institutional weakness: wherever formal governance has been absent, delegitimized, or corrupted, criminal organizations have filled the resulting vacuum

Why the pattern matters: The persistence of the structural conditions sustaining organized crime across the entire historical period examined in this textbook, despite dramatic technological, legal, and institutional changes, suggests that those conditions are more fundamental than any particular organizational form or criminal technology. Future changes in technology and law will produce new forms of criminal enterprise; but the underlying structural logic will remain operative until the structural conditions that generate it, forbidden demand, inequality, institutional weakness, and corruption, are addressed at their roots.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has addressed one of the central puzzles in the history and sociology of organized crime: why does it persist, across generations, across continents, across vastly different political systems and cultural contexts, seemingly impervious to even the most determined and sophisticated law enforcement efforts? The answer developed here is structural rather than organizational. Organized crime persists not because criminal leaders are uniquely clever or law enforcement uniquely incompetent, but because four interlocking structural conditions continuously reproduce the conditions for criminal enterprise: the demand for illegal goods and services, economic inequality and the exclusion of portions of the population from legitimate opportunity, the weakness or delegitimization of formal institutions, and corruption that neutralizes the state's capacity to suppress criminal power.

The first structural condition, demand, operates through the economics of forbidden markets. When governments prohibit goods and services for which substantial demand exists, they do not eliminate that demand; they transfer its supply to criminal networks. Because legal competition is excluded, criminal suppliers can earn extraordinary profit margins. Because legal dispute resolution is unavailable, criminal organizations must develop their own enforcement capacity. The result is precisely the organizational form, durable, hierarchical, coercive, and economically rational, that defines organized crime. The persistence of criminal markets after the disruption or destruction of individual criminal organizations demonstrates the fundamental logic: as long as demand is sufficient and supply is prohibited, someone will provide what consumers seek.

The second structural condition, inequality, explains why criminal organizations so consistently find a supply of recruits willing to accept the risks of criminal enterprise. Economic inequality, combined with the perception that the mechanisms producing it are illegitimate and the legitimate economy is inaccessible or degrading, makes criminal enterprise a rational response to the actual opportunity structure available to excluded populations. Criminal organizations embedded in poor communities can function as parallel economic structures, providing income, status, and belonging that legitimate institutions fail to offer. This does not mean criminal organizations are beneficial to the communities they exploit; rather, it means that addressing criminal recruitment requires addressing the inequality that makes it rational.

The third structural condition, institutional weakness, explains why criminal organizations can fill governance vacuums, providing dispute resolution, security, and social order in spaces where formal institutions are absent or delegitimized. The post-Soviet explosion of criminal enterprise following the collapse of the Soviet Union represents the most dramatic modern demonstration of this pattern, but the same dynamic is visible across dozens of contexts from post-Unification Sicily to contemporary favelas. Where states cannot or do not reliably provide security, criminal organizations that can provide it gain power that is extremely difficult to dislodge even when formal state authority subsequently improves.

The fourth structural condition, corruption, operates as both a tool criminal organizations use to protect themselves and a systemic condition that their persistence helps create. Criminal profits fund the corruption of law enforcement, judiciary, and political figures, which provides protection from prosecution, which enables the generation of more criminal profit, which funds more corruption. This self-reinforcing cycle can reach a condition of systemic corruption or state capture in which the formal institutions that might suppress criminal enterprise have been so thoroughly penetrated that reform requires dismantling and rebuilding the corrupt institutional framework itself, a profoundly difficult undertaking.

The chapter also examined the human experience of living in communities where organized crime is deeply embedded, emphasizing that the structural analysis of criminal persistence must be connected to the lived reality of ordinary people who navigate criminal power daily. The culture of silence, the tribute paid by small businesses, the constraints on civic life, the differential impact on women and children, and the infiltration of legitimate business all constitute the social texture of criminal power at the community level. Understanding this texture is essential for any policy approach that takes seriously the wellbeing of the communities most affected by organized crime.

The historical debate over demand-side versus supply-side approaches to organized crime reflects genuine uncertainty about the most effective policy responses. The evidence from Prohibition's end, from Portugal's decriminalization experience, and from the emerging data on cannabis legalization provides support for the proposition that reducing the scope of prohibition can reduce criminal market revenue. But the evidence also shows that criminal organizations are highly adaptive, and that eliminating one revenue stream tends to produce diversification into others. The most honest conclusion is that neither supply-side enforcement alone nor demand-side reform alone is likely to substantially reduce organized crime; effective long-term strategies require coordinated attention to all four structural conditions simultaneously.

The historical record documented throughout this textbook confirms the structural analysis of this chapter. Every era has produced criminal organizations adapted to its specific context, exploiting the specific combinations of forbidden demand, excluded populations, institutional weakness, and corrupt accommodation available to them. Every era has also produced law enforcement responses that have damaged or destroyed specific organizations, only to watch the underlying criminal markets reorganize around new structures. This does not mean the effort is futile; specific organizations cause specific harms, and reducing those harms matters even if the structural conditions remain. But it does mean that the goal of eliminating organized crime, as opposed to containing and reducing it, will remain beyond reach as long as the structural conditions that generate it remain intact.

The remaining chapters of this textbook examine organized crime in the twenty-first century and the recurring historical cycle that the full sweep of criminal history reveals. Together, they complete the argument that this chapter has made at the structural level: that organized crime is best understood not as an aberration from normal social order but as a persistent feature of social orders that create forbidden demand, tolerate deep inequality, permit institutional weakness, and fail to maintain the institutional integrity required to resist the corrupting power of criminal wealth.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Black market
An economic market for goods or services that are prohibited or so heavily regulated that supply through official channels is inadequate; characterized by criminal rather than legal enterprise and by the absence of legal recourse for participants.
Criminal adaptation
The capacity of criminal organizations to reorganize, relocate, diversify, or otherwise adjust their operations in response to law enforcement pressure, prohibition changes, or market shifts; one of the primary reasons why disrupting individual organizations does not eliminate criminal markets.
Criminal equilibrium
A stable, self-reproducing arrangement in which organized crime and formal institutions coexist in mutual accommodation, with criminal power embedded deeply enough in economic and political structures to resist disruption through normal law enforcement activity.
Criminal recruitment
The process by which criminal organizations attract members, typically drawing most heavily from populations excluded from legitimate economic opportunity by inequality, discrimination, or structural disadvantage.
Corruption
The abuse of entrusted power for private gain; in the context of organized crime, the use of criminal profits to purchase the tolerance or cooperation of law enforcement, judicial, and political officials.
Culture of silence
A pattern of non-reporting and non-cooperation with authorities in communities where criminal power is strong, produced not necessarily by approval of criminal activity but by rational fear of retaliation and distrust of official response.
Decriminalization
A policy approach that removes criminal penalties for the possession or use of a substance or activity while maintaining its illegal status for production and distribution; distinct from full legalization.
Demand-side policy
Approaches to reducing organized crime that focus on reducing the demand for the goods or services criminal markets supply, through prevention, treatment, decriminalization, or legalization.
Economic marginalization
The exclusion of specific populations from meaningful participation in the legitimate economy; a primary structural condition driving criminal recruitment.
Grand corruption
The corruption of senior officials, ministers, or legislators who have the power to shape the institutional environment in ways that systematically benefit criminal organizations, as distinct from petty corruption involving individual officers performing specific favors.
Harm reduction
A public health approach to drug use and other risky behaviors that focuses on minimizing adverse consequences rather than eliminating the behavior; often contrasted with abstinence-based or prohibition-based approaches.
Hydra effect
The pattern in which disrupting or eliminating a criminal organization creates space for new organizations to emerge in its place, analogous to cutting off one head of the mythical hydra only to have new heads grow; also called the "balloon effect."
Illicit demand
Consumer demand for goods or services whose production, sale, or use has been prohibited by law; the fundamental driver of criminal market formation.
Informal economy
Economic activity that takes place outside formal legal and regulatory frameworks; may include both benign unregistered activity and criminal enterprise, and is often particularly extensive in environments of poverty, weak institutions, and high regulatory burden.
Institutional delegitimization
A condition in which formal institutions retain legal authority but have lost the trust and legitimacy of the populations they nominally serve, reducing their effectiveness and creating space for informal criminal alternatives.
Institutional legitimacy
The degree to which populations accept and trust formal institutions as appropriate and fair; essential for institutions to perform governance functions effectively and critical to their ability to resist criminal penetration.
Narco-state
A state in which drug trafficking organizations have achieved such pervasive control of political and economic institutions that the state primarily serves the interests of those organizations rather than the public.
Parallel governance
The provision by criminal or informal organizations of functions ordinarily associated with the state, including security, dispute resolution, and social services, in environments where formal state provision is inadequate.
Pentiti
Italian term for criminal informants, particularly those who provided testimony about the Mafia in exchange for protection; the use of pentiti testimony was central to the anti-Mafia prosecutions of the 1980s and 1990s.
Petty corruption
Small-scale corruption involving individual officials accepting bribes for specific favors; distinct from grand corruption or state capture in scale but potentially significant in aggregate effect on criminal impunity.
Social capital
The networks of relationships, norms of reciprocity, and shared trust that enable collective action; criminal organizations exploit and often erode the social capital of the communities where they operate.
State capture
A condition in which private interests, including criminal organizations, have so thoroughly penetrated state institutions that the state effectively serves those interests rather than the public; the extreme end of the corruption spectrum.
Supply-side enforcement
Law enforcement approaches to organized crime that focus on disrupting the supply of illegal goods and services by targeting producers, distributors, and criminal organizations; contrasted with demand-side approaches.
Vacuum of governance
A situation in which effective state authority is absent in a given territory, creating space for criminal organizations to provide the governance functions that the state cannot or will not supply.
Weak state
A state lacking the capacity, resources, or legitimacy to effectively perform core governance functions including law enforcement, dispute resolution, and the protection of property rights; a primary condition enabling criminal organization and persistence.

Major People, Groups, and Concepts

Identification & Significance

Estes Kefauver (1903–1963)
U.S. Senator from Tennessee who led televised hearings on organized crime in 1950–1951 that documented the depth of criminal penetration of legitimate institutions across American cities. The hearings demonstrated that organized crime had achieved a form of parallel governance in multiple jurisdictions despite decades of law enforcement.
Giovanni Falcone (1939–1992) and Paolo Borsellino (1940–1992)
Italian anti-Mafia magistrates who developed the use of pentiti testimony and coordinated prosecutorial strategies that produced major convictions of Cosa Nostra leadership. Both were assassinated by the Mafia in 1992, demonstrating the lengths to which criminal organizations will go to protect themselves from institutional challenge.
Diego Gambetta
British-Italian sociologist whose 1993 analysis of the Sicilian Mafia as a "protection industry" providing private enforcement in an environment of low institutional trust became foundational for the scholarly understanding of organized crime as a response to institutional weakness.
The 'Ndrangheta
The Calabrian criminal organization that grew dramatically during the period of the most intense anti-Mafia campaigns against the Sicilian Cosa Nostra; its family-based structure and decentralized organization made it more resilient to prosecution than the more hierarchical Sicilian organization and provides a case study in criminal adaptation.
The Sinaloa Cartel
The dominant Mexican drug trafficking organization from the late 1990s onward; its rise illustrates how criminal organizations can achieve near-systemic corruption of state institutions, and its continued power despite the capture of its most famous leader, Joaquín "El Chapo" Guzmán, illustrates the organizational resilience produced by institutional depth.
Portugal's drug decriminalization (2001)
A major policy experiment in which Portugal decriminalized the personal use of all drugs and redirected resources to treatment and harm reduction; the results, including reductions in drug-related crime, HIV infection rates, and drug-related deaths without dramatic increases in drug use, have been extensively studied as evidence for demand-side approaches to criminal markets.
The Kefauver Committee Final Report (1951)
The document summarizing findings of the Senate investigation into organized crime; it concluded that a nationwide criminal syndicate existed with extensive penetration of legitimate business, law enforcement, and political institutions, and called for federal action to address organized crime as a systemic rather than local problem.
Hernando de Soto (b. 1941)
Peruvian economist whose influential work on informal economies documented how legal systems that exclude the poor from legitimate economic participation drive them toward extralegal arrangements; while not focused primarily on criminal enterprise, his analysis has been widely applied to understanding the economic conditions that sustain criminal recruitment.
Al Capone (1899–1947)
Chicago bootlegger and organized crime leader whose operations during Prohibition illustrated the structural relationship between legal prohibition, criminal market formation, and the corruption of public institutions; a central case study in the demonstration that criminal power is a product of structural conditions, not merely individual criminality.
The post-Soviet criminal oligarchs
Individuals who exploited the collapse of Soviet institutions and the rapid, poorly regulated privatization of the 1990s to accumulate vast wealth through criminal and semi-criminal means; their rise illustrates how institutional collapse creates criminal opportunity and how criminal power established during periods of weakness can be extremely difficult to dislodge afterward.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter has argued that organized crime is not an anomaly in modern social order but a persistent feature of any society that creates forbidden markets, tolerates deep inequality, permits institutional weakness, and fails to maintain the integrity of its governing institutions. This argument is, at its root, a structural one: it shifts analytical attention from the characteristics of individual criminal actors to the social, economic, and institutional conditions that make criminal enterprise rational, durable, and resistant to suppression. It is also, in an important sense, a historical argument: the persistence of organized crime across the many centuries, continents, and contexts examined in this textbook cannot be explained by reference to the particular characteristics of any criminal culture or any specific set of criminal leaders. It can only be explained by reference to structural conditions that recur across all of those diverse settings.

The implications of this structural analysis extend well beyond the academic study of criminal enterprise. They reach into the most fundamental questions of political economy: about the relationship between legal systems and the markets they seek to govern, about the consequences of inequality for the social fabric of communities and nations, about the conditions under which formal institutions earn or lose the legitimacy that makes governance possible, and about the corrupting effects of concentrated wealth, whether legitimate or criminal, on the political processes that are supposed to serve the common good. Organized crime is not merely a problem of policing; it is a symptom of deeper structural failures that policing alone cannot address. Understanding it requires the full toolkit of historical, sociological, economic, and political analysis that this textbook has been developing throughout its pages.

The history of organized crime also offers a sobering lesson about the limits of purely punitive approaches to complex social problems. Law enforcement is necessary, and the courage and dedication of the prosecutors, investigators, and officers who have worked to suppress criminal enterprise, often at great personal risk, deserve recognition and respect. But the historical record is unambiguous: punitive approaches that do not address the structural conditions sustaining criminal enterprise produce at best temporary reductions in the power of specific organizations, followed by the reconstitution of criminal power in new organizational forms. The communities most damaged by organized crime, the poor neighborhoods that bear the weight of both criminal exploitation and aggressive law enforcement, deserve more than this perpetual cycle of disruption and reconstitution. They deserve the structural investments, in economic opportunity, institutional quality, and anti-corruption reform, that might actually reduce the conditions from which criminal power grows.

As students move into the final chapters of this textbook, examining organized crime in the twenty-first century and the recurring historical cycle that the full sweep of criminal history reveals, the structural framework of this chapter should serve as an analytical foundation. The specific criminal organizations and markets of the contemporary period will differ from those of earlier eras in their technologies, their geographic reach, and their organizational forms. But the underlying structural logic, demand for what prohibition forbids, recruitment from those whom inequality excludes, power in the spaces where institutions fail, protection purchased with the wealth that criminal markets generate, will be immediately recognizable to any student who has absorbed the lessons of this chapter. That recognition is itself one of the most important things that the study of criminal history can offer: not merely knowledge of what happened, but understanding of why it happened, and why, unless something fundamental changes, it will happen again.

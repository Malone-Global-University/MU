Part V — The American Mafia

The Mafia at Its Peak

Organized Crime: A Global History

Chapter Opening

"The Mafia did not merely exist alongside the American economy in the postwar decades. In certain industries, in certain cities, it was the economy — the real authority behind the contracts, the unions, the concrete, and the cash."

In the early 1950s, a Brooklyn longshoreman who wanted to work the docks at the Port of New York did not simply report to a hiring hall and offer his labor. He reported to a shape-up, a daily assembly where hiring bosses handpicked workers for the day's shifts. Those hiring bosses, in turn, answered to waterfront racket bosses who collected kickbacks from wages, ran numbers gambling along the piers, lent money at usurious rates to men desperate to pay their rent, and divided the waterfront into territories under the authority of organized crime figures aligned with the five New York families. The union local that was supposed to protect the longshoreman's rights was controlled not by elected shop stewards accountable to members but by officials installed by criminal organizations who used union resources as a personal revenue stream and a tool of political leverage. A man who protested or organized independently risked not only losing his job but receiving a beating, or worse. The waterfront, celebrated in Elia Kazan's 1954 film On the Waterfront, was not an exceptional case. It was, in the postwar decades, a representative one.

Between roughly the mid-1940s and the early 1980s, the American Mafia, the network of Italian American organized crime families established in the reorganization engineered by Charles "Lucky" Luciano and his contemporaries in the early 1930s, operated at the height of its institutional power. The five families of New York, the Chicago Outfit, and allied organizations in Philadelphia, New England, New Orleans, Detroit, Cleveland, and a dozen other cities exercised a degree of economic and political influence that reached far beyond what earlier criminal organizations had achieved. They were not simply criminal enterprises in the narrow sense. They were, in many respects, shadow institutions that parallel-administered significant sectors of the American economy, particularly in construction, trucking, waste disposal, the garment industry, the waterfront, and the entertainment business, and that extracted tribute from those sectors with a regularity and sophistication that resembled taxation more than banditry.

This chapter examines the mechanisms, the scale, and the social and economic consequences of the American Mafia's postwar power. It focuses on four major sectors through which organized crime extracted wealth and exercised influence: labor racketeering, the construction industry, gambling operations, and loan sharking. It then examines how these revenue streams and the political connections they sustained translated into national influence, reaching into city halls, governor's offices, congressional committees, and, according to some investigators and historians, the highest levels of federal politics. It considers who benefited from this arrangement, who was harmed by it, and why it proved so durable for so long.

The story of the Mafia at its peak is not simply a story about criminals. It is a story about the vulnerabilities in democratic institutions, about the ways that weak labor law enforcement, fragmented policing, judicial corruption, and the enormous demand for certain illegal services created the conditions in which organized crime could function as a quasi-governmental force. It is also a story about the people who lived and worked in the shadow of that force: the construction worker who paid a corrupt steward for the right to work, the small businessman who borrowed from a loan shark because no legitimate bank would lend to him, the gambler who played the numbers because he could not afford legal recreation and the numbers banker gave him credit, the union member whose pension fund was quietly drained into criminal hands. These people were not passive victims of an alien force. They inhabited an economic world that the Mafia had shaped over decades, and their choices, however constrained, were part of the story too.

Historians have debated how to characterize the Mafia's postwar power. Some have emphasized its penetration of legitimate institutions, arguing that by the 1960s organized crime had become so deeply embedded in American economic life that disentangling it required not just law enforcement but structural reform. Others have emphasized the limits of Mafia power, noting that even at its height the organization was fractured by internal conflicts, rivalries among families, and the inherent instability of criminal enterprises built on personal loyalty rather than legal contracts. Both perspectives illuminate real dimensions of a complex historical phenomenon, and this chapter takes seriously the evidence on both sides.

Understanding the Mafia at its peak also requires understanding the period in which it flourished. The postwar decades, from roughly 1945 to the early 1970s, were years of rapid economic growth, surging construction and infrastructure investment, expanding union membership, and increasing prosperity for many working-class Americans. The Mafia's grip on certain industries meant that it extracted tribute from this prosperity at every level, skimming from union pension funds swollen by the postwar boom, from construction contracts funded by federal highway and housing programs, from gambling revenues that expanded with disposable income, and from legitimate businesses that found it cheaper to pay protection than to fight it. The Mafia's golden age was, in a real sense, a parasitic outgrowth of America's postwar golden age.

This chapter also traces the first serious cracks in that golden age, as federal investigators, congressional committees, and pioneering prosecutors began to develop the tools, the legal theories, and the political will needed to challenge organized crime's institutional power. The full story of the government's counteroffensive belongs to Chapter 16. But the seeds of the Mafia's eventual decline were already being planted during the years of its greatest strength, and understanding those seeds, the Kefauver Committee hearings, the early FBI surveillance programs, the debates over labor law reform, requires understanding the power those investigations were attempting to confront.

By the time the Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act became law in 1970 and federal prosecutors began developing the strategies that would eventually dismantle the major families in the 1980s, the Mafia had been exercising institutional power in American economic life for nearly four decades. That durability was not an accident. It was a product of deliberate institutional design, deep political connections, the complicity of some of the most powerful legitimate institutions in American society, and the willingness of enough ordinary people to make enough accommodations with criminal power that the entire system remained functional. Understanding how that system worked, at its height, is the task of this chapter.

Historical Context

The American Mafia's postwar ascendancy did not emerge from a vacuum. It was the product of decades of institutional development that the preceding chapters of this textbook have traced: the waves of Southern Italian immigration in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries that brought with them the criminal fraternal traditions of Sicily, Campania, and Calabria; the explosive profits of Prohibition-era bootlegging that provided the capital base for postwar criminal enterprises; the reorganization engineered by Lucky Luciano and his allies in the early 1930s that created the Commission and established the institutional framework of the American Mafia as a federated national organization; and the deepening penetration of legitimate economic sectors, particularly labor unions, that began in earnest during the 1930s and accelerated through the World War II years.

By 1945, the major organized crime families had survived Prohibition's end, the challenges of the Depression, and the disruptions of wartime, and they emerged into the postwar boom in a position of institutional strength that earlier generations of criminal entrepreneurs could scarcely have imagined. The five families of New York, the Gambino, Genovese, Lucchese, Colombo, and Bonanno organizations, divided the largest and wealthiest city in the United States among themselves with a precision that reflected decades of negotiated territorial arrangements. The Chicago Outfit, heir to the Capone organization, exercised comparable dominance over the Midwest's largest city and extended its influence across a network of subordinate operations in Las Vegas, Los Angeles, and other cities. The New England family, the Philadelphia family, the New Orleans family, and organizations in Detroit, Cleveland, Buffalo, and other cities completed a national network that, while not always in harmony, shared information, arranged cooperative ventures, settled territorial disputes through the Commission mechanism, and maintained the overall coherence of organized crime as a national institution.

The postwar economic context was crucial. The years between 1945 and the early 1970s saw the most sustained period of economic growth in American history. The GI Bill, the Federal Housing Administration's mortgage programs, and the Interstate Highway System funded massive construction expenditure. Union membership reached its postwar peak in the mid-1950s, with organized labor representing roughly a third of the American workforce and union pension funds accumulating assets that would eventually total hundreds of billions of dollars. The growing urban working class had money for entertainment and recreation, including gambling, and the persistence of illegal gambling prohibitions in most states meant that demand for those services could only be satisfied through criminal channels. All of these conditions created opportunities for an organized criminal network that was already well positioned to exploit them.

The political environment, too, was permissive. The FBI under J. Edgar Hoover famously and for decades denied the existence of organized crime as a national institution, preferring to focus on individual bank robbers and communist sympathizers than on the laborious, legally complex, and politically sensitive work of building cases against entrenched criminal families. Local police in cities where organized crime was most powerful were often deeply compromised, with individual officers and sometimes entire departments on criminal payrolls. Prosecutors who moved against organized crime figures faced witnesses who would not testify, juries that were susceptible to intimidation or bribery, and defendants who could afford the best defense attorneys available. The legal tools needed to prosecute criminal organizations as organizations, rather than simply prosecuting individual criminals for individual acts, would not exist until the RICO Act of 1970.

Students should also understand the demographic and urban context. The postwar decades were years of massive urban demographic change, as African American and Puerto Rican migration to Northern cities transformed neighborhoods that had previously been dominated by white ethnic working-class populations. The Mafia, rooted in Italian American communities, maintained its grip on certain neighborhoods and industries even as the underlying demographics shifted around it. The numbers racket, as this chapter will examine, was particularly important in this context: an operation that Italian American organized crime had pioneered found its most profitable markets in the growing Black and Puerto Rican communities of cities like New York, Chicago, and Detroit, creating a complex relationship between organized crime and communities of color that historians have analyzed from multiple perspectives.

Finally, students need to understand that the postwar Mafia operated in a world where its institutional presence was widely understood by residents of the communities it dominated, by politicians who accommodated it, by journalists who occasionally exposed it, and by federal and state officials who periodically investigated it. The Kefauver Committee hearings of 1950 and 1951, which were televised and watched by millions of Americans, made the existence and the institutional character of organized crime a matter of national public knowledge. What the hearings did not produce was an effective legal response, and understanding the gap between public awareness and effective prosecution is one of the central puzzles of this period in American organized crime history.

Section 15.1

Labor Racketeering: Unions as Criminal Instruments

Of all the mechanisms through which the American Mafia exercised its postwar economic power, none was more significant, more deeply institutionalized, or more damaging to American workers than labor racketeering. The term refers to the infiltration, domination, and criminal exploitation of labor unions, and it constituted the single most important institutional advantage that organized crime possessed in the postwar decades. Through control of key unions, organized crime families could extort employers, extract tribute from workers, manipulate pension and welfare funds, and exercise a degree of influence over entire industries that no strictly criminal enterprise operating outside legitimate institutional frameworks could have achieved.

The roots of organized crime's involvement in labor union affairs stretched back to the earlier decades of the twentieth century, when criminal figures had been recruited as strike breakers or, alternatively, as muscle to enforce union demands against reluctant employers. The violence and instability of early labor-management conflict created a demand for both kinds of services, and criminal entrepreneurs proved willing to supply them. Over time, some criminal figures moved from being service providers to labor organizations into positions of direct organizational control, installing allies in union leadership positions, manipulating elections, intimidating rank-and-file members who challenged their authority, and transforming unions from workers' protective institutions into revenue-generating instruments for their own benefit.

How Labor Racketeering Operated

The mechanisms of labor racketeering were several, and they operated simultaneously in most corrupted union environments. The most straightforward was direct embezzlement: racketeers who controlled union treasuries simply transferred funds to themselves and their associates, often through elaborate schemes involving fraudulent invoices, fictitious vendors, and payments to allies for services never rendered. More sophisticated was the manipulation of union welfare and pension funds, which by the 1950s and 1960s had grown to enormous size as union membership expanded and collective bargaining secured increasingly generous benefits for workers. These funds were theoretically governed by trustees bound by fiduciary duties to the membership, but in unions controlled by organized crime the trustees were installed by criminal organizations and the funds' assets were deployed to serve criminal interests rather than worker welfare.

The most notorious example was the Teamsters Central States Pension Fund, which by the early 1960s held assets of several hundred million dollars contributed by Teamsters members across the country as retirement security. Under the effective influence of organized crime allies installed in the union's leadership, the fund made a series of loans to developers of Las Vegas casinos at a time when no legitimate lending institution would finance gambling operations. These loans financed the construction and expansion of properties including the Stardust, the Fremont, the Desert Inn, and a succession of other casino hotels, and they came with arrangements that allowed organized crime figures to skim cash from casino revenues before those revenues were reported to tax authorities or casino shareholders. The Teamsters pension fund functioned, in effect, as organized crime's primary investment bank for the development of Las Vegas as a gambling city, and Teamsters members' retirement savings were the unwitting capital that made it possible.

Beyond embezzlement and fund manipulation, labor racketeering operated through the creation of "sweetheart contracts," agreements negotiated between union officials and employers that provided workers with wages and benefits below what genuine arm's-length collective bargaining would have achieved. In exchange, employers paid kickbacks to union officials or made contributions to funds controlled by organized crime. Workers received less than their labor was worth; employers paid less than genuine union scale required; and the difference flowed to criminal intermediaries who had brokered the arrangement. In industries where organized crime controlled all or most of the relevant union locals, non-participating employers could be subjected to work stoppages, vandalism, or the withdrawal of necessary labor precisely when it was most needed, making the sweetheart contract arrangement irresistible to businesses that could not afford the disruption of a genuinely adversarial union relationship.

"No-show jobs" and "ghost employees" represented another category of racket. Union officials and their associates would be carried on employer payrolls as employees who never actually appeared for work, with their salaries paid as a form of tribute to organized crime. In some cases the no-show employee was a fiction entirely; in others it was a genuine organized crime figure who held a nominal position at union scale wages while spending his working hours on criminal business. The construction trades, the waterfront, the trucking industry, the garment district, and the entertainment industry all generated documented examples of this practice at various points in the postwar period.

The "shape-up" system on the New York waterfront, which the Kefauver Committee documented in its hearings and which journalists and filmmakers brought to broad public attention in the early 1950s, illustrated how labor racketeering could transform the most basic mechanisms of employment into instruments of criminal tribute. Under the shape-up, longshoremen reported each morning to the piers in the hope of being selected for work. Hiring bosses, who held their positions under the sponsorship of organized crime figures controlling the waterfront, chose workers for the day's shifts. Men who paid kickbacks from their wages, who borrowed from loan sharks operating on the piers, who played the numbers with bookmakers under organized crime auspices, and who refrained from any challenge to the racket's operation were more likely to find work than those who did not. The shape-up was not just an inefficient hiring system; it was a mechanism for extracting tribute from workers at every step of their working lives.

The Teamsters and the Hospitality of Power

The International Brotherhood of Teamsters occupied a special place in the history of labor racketeering because of its size, its strategic importance to the American economy, and the depth and duration of its entanglement with organized crime. The Teamsters represented truck drivers, warehouse workers, and a vast range of other transportation and distribution workers, and because nearly every sector of the American economy depended on the movement of goods, the Teamsters' ability to shut down commerce gave the union extraordinary leverage with employers. That leverage was amplified, rather than moderated, when organized crime effectively co-opted the union's leadership.

Dave Beck, who served as Teamsters president before Jimmy Hoffa, was convicted of embezzling union funds. Under Hoffa, who took over the union's presidency in 1957 following a bitterly contested election that the McClellan Committee subsequently investigated, the relationship between the Teamsters and organized crime deepened rather than retreated. Hoffa was a genuinely complex figure: a man who had risen from a working-class background through actual labor organizing, who could speak credibly of his loyalty to the Teamsters rank and file, and who simultaneously maintained working relationships with organized crime figures across multiple cities that served his personal interests and those of his criminal partners more than the interests of the workers he represented. The McClellan Committee, with Robert Kennedy as its chief counsel, documented these relationships in extensive detail, and Hoffa's subsequent legal troubles, including his 1967 conviction on jury tampering and pension fraud charges, continued to generate headlines until his disappearance in 1975.

For ordinary Teamsters members, the organized crime entanglement produced genuinely mixed consequences. On one hand, Teamsters contracts, even those negotiated under racketeering conditions, often delivered wages and benefits substantially better than those available to non-union workers in comparable industries. The union's size and strategic position gave it genuine bargaining power that racketeering did not entirely neutralize. On the other hand, the diversion of pension fund assets into mob-controlled casino loans meant that when those loans eventually soured and pension funds suffered losses, it was ordinary Teamsters members, not the racketeers who had engineered the loans, who bore the financial consequences. The complexity of this situation, in which workers could simultaneously benefit from union power and be victimized by union corruption, is one reason why labor racketeering proved so difficult to combat: the union, even corrupted, provided real services to its members, and members were often reluctant to cooperate with investigations that threatened to destroy an institution on which they depended.

Historical Vocabulary
Labor Racketeering

Labor racketeering refers to the infiltration and criminal exploitation of labor unions and the labor-management relationship. It encompasses a range of criminal practices including embezzlement of union funds, manipulation of pension and welfare funds for criminal benefit, the extraction of kickbacks from employers in exchange for favorable contract terms ("sweetheart contracts"), the sale of "strike insurance" to employers willing to pay for labor peace, the use of threatened work stoppages as instruments of extortion, and the installation of organized crime figures or allies in union leadership positions through election fraud or intimidation.

Labor racketeering differs from ordinary theft or extortion in its institutional character: it exploits legitimate labor organizations, transforming institutions designed to protect workers' economic interests into instruments of criminal revenue extraction. Its victims include workers whose wages and benefits are reduced by sweetheart contracts, whose pension and welfare funds are depleted through fraud, and whose democratic voice within their own organizations is suppressed by intimidation; employers who must pay tribute to operate in racketeered industries; and consumers who ultimately bear the cost of inflated prices in industries where labor racketeering drives up the cost of doing legitimate business.

The term "racket" itself dates to the early twentieth century and originally referred to any criminal scheme that generated regular income through repeated illegal acts rather than a single crime. Labor racketeering was distinguished by its systemic, institutional character: it was not a one-time extortion but a permanent extraction machine embedded in legitimate organizational structures.

Section 15.2

Construction Rackets: Concrete, Steel, and Corruption

If labor racketeering gave organized crime its most widespread institutional reach into the American economy, the construction industry gave it perhaps its most systematically corrupt single sector. Construction was uniquely vulnerable to organized crime control for several structural reasons. It was an industry where labor was organized through craft unions, meaning that a criminal organization that controlled even a handful of key union locals, the cement masons, the ironworkers, the teamsters who hauled materials, the carpenters who framed structures, could shut down an entire project by withdrawing a single category of labor. It was an industry where work was project-based and competitive bidding theoretically determined which firms won contracts, making bid rigging relatively easy to arrange if key competitors could be persuaded or compelled to cooperate. It was an industry where the product, a building under construction, was inherently vulnerable to sabotage, slow-down, or the sudden unavailability of materials, giving criminal overseers multiple points of leverage over developers who needed projects completed on time and within budget. And it was an industry where the postwar construction boom, driven by federal highway spending, urban renewal programs, housing development, and commercial real estate expansion, generated enormous and rapidly growing revenue streams that organized crime was well positioned to exploit.

The Concrete Club and Bid Rigging

In New York City, the organized crime families' control of the construction industry was so thorough and so long-lasting that investigators eventually coined a specific term, the "Concrete Club," for the cartel-like arrangement through which organized crime controlled the supply and pouring of ready-mix concrete on major construction projects. The arrangement worked roughly as follows. The Gambino and Genovese families controlled the ready-mix concrete suppliers and the union locals whose members poured concrete on major building sites. Any developer building above a certain project size in New York was required, in practice, to use concrete suppliers approved by organized crime, pay a surcharge on concrete prices that flowed to organized crime, and hire workers through union channels controlled by organized crime. Developers who attempted to source concrete through non-approved channels, or who used non-union labor, found their projects delayed by work stoppages, their materials unexpectedly unavailable, their completed work vandalized, or their workers subjected to intimidation. The cost of resistance consistently exceeded the cost of compliance, and for decades most major developers in New York simply built the tribute into their project budgets as an ordinary cost of doing business.

The consequences for the cost of construction in New York and other organized crime-dominated cities were substantial. Studies conducted in the wake of major prosecutions in the 1980s and 1990s estimated that organized crime's grip on the New York construction industry added between fifteen and twenty percent to the cost of major construction projects in the city over the preceding decades. That premium was not paid by organized crime figures or by abstract corporate entities; it was paid by tenants in apartment buildings whose rents reflected inflated construction costs, by taxpayers whose government buildings were built on overpriced contracts, by homebuyers whose housing costs were elevated by a corrupted supply chain, and by legitimate construction firms squeezed out of a market they could not enter without paying tribute.

Bid rigging, the pre-arrangement of competitive contract bidding so that a pre-selected firm wins at an artificially inflated price, was a related mechanism that operated across multiple organized crime-controlled industries but was particularly prevalent in construction. The mechanics of bid rigging were straightforward: firms competing for a contract would coordinate in advance to ensure that all but one submitted artificially high bids, allowing the pre-selected winner to come in just below the inflated competition at a price still well above what genuine competition would have produced. The pre-selected winner then paid a portion of the excess profit to the organized crime figures who had arranged the scheme, and the losing firms were compensated for their cooperation with the promise of future winning bids. Bid rigging required collusion among supposedly competing firms, which in turn required either criminal coercion of the firms or, more commonly, criminal control of trade associations or regulatory processes that gave organized crime leverage over who could participate in the industry at all.

The Garment District and Other Industries

Beyond construction, organized crime's penetration of legitimate industries extended to the garment district, waste disposal, the food distribution industry, and the waterfront. In New York's garment district, criminal control of the trucking operations that moved finished goods from manufacturers to retail outlets gave organized crime leverage over an entire industry. Garment manufacturers who resisted tribute arrangements found their trucks hijacked, their goods damaged, and their deliveries delayed at the worst possible moments in the fashion cycle. The waste disposal industry in the New York metropolitan area was divided among organized crime-controlled haulers who parceled out accounts among themselves and enforced a prohibition on price competition that kept disposal costs artificially high for businesses and municipalities throughout the region for decades.

The fish market at Fulton Street in Manhattan, which supplied a significant portion of the city's seafood supply, was subject to organized crime control that dictated which buyers could enter the market, at what prices they could buy, and from which suppliers they were required to purchase. The Hunts Point wholesale produce market in the Bronx, which by the postwar period handled a substantial portion of the fresh produce entering the New York metropolitan area, was similarly subject to organized crime oversight. In each case, the mechanism was comparable: control of key union locals or trade associations, the ability to disrupt commerce at critical points, and the consequent ability to extract tribute from every participant in the market.

The scale of organized crime's penetration of legitimate industry was perhaps most dramatically illustrated by the assessment conducted for the President's Commission on Law Enforcement and Administration of Justice, which reported in 1967 that organized crime's annual revenues from all sources likely exceeded those of any corporation in the United States. While such estimates are inherently imprecise, the Commission's findings, which drew on years of FBI surveillance, informant reporting, and state and local law enforcement intelligence, made clear that organized crime had ceased to be a marginal criminal phenomenon and had become a major economic institution whose annual turnover and institutional complexity rivaled those of large legitimate corporations.

Section 15.3

Gambling Empires: From the Numbers to Las Vegas

Gambling was the oldest and most widespread revenue source for American organized crime, and in the postwar decades it remained the single largest generator of criminal income despite the emergence of labor racketeering and construction corruption as major additional revenue streams. Organized crime's gambling operations spanned an enormous range of activities, from the illegal daily lottery known as the numbers racket that operated in virtually every urban neighborhood in the country, to the wire rooms that took bets on horse races and sporting events, to the illegal casinos that in some cities operated with a brazenness that approached open defiance of law enforcement, to the legitimate Nevada casinos that organized crime penetrated through the combination of Teamsters pension loans and outright ownership.

Understanding organized crime's gambling operations requires understanding why gambling was illegal in most American jurisdictions during this period, and why that illegality created the conditions for criminal exploitation rather than eliminating gambling itself. The prohibition of gambling in most states reflected a mixture of religious objection, progressive-era reform impulses, and genuine concerns about the social consequences of unrestricted commercial gambling. But prohibition did not eliminate demand for gambling services; it simply drove that demand underground. Americans who wished to bet on horses, play policy numbers, wager on sporting events, or participate in casino games could not do so through legal channels in most jurisdictions, and organized crime provided the alternative. The parallel with alcohol Prohibition was direct and was noted by observers at the time: the criminalization of a widely desired commodity created a black market, and organized crime organized that market.

The Numbers Racket

The numbers racket, also known as policy gambling or the numbers game, was the most widespread form of organized crime gambling and the one that reached most deeply into the everyday economic life of urban working-class communities. The game was simple in concept: bettors chose a three-digit number from 000 to 999, paying a small amount, often as little as a penny or a nickel, for a chance to win if their number was drawn in the daily lottery. The winning number was typically derived from a publicly available source that could not be manipulated by the operators, such as the last three digits of the day's pari-mutuel totals at a local racetrack, or later from the closing numbers of various financial indices. Bets were collected daily by a network of runners who took numbers and money from bettors in neighborhoods, workplaces, barbershops, bars, and other community gathering points, then delivered the slips and cash to area collectors who passed them up to the numbers bank, the central operation that tabulated bets, calculated daily payouts, and provided the capital base that guaranteed payment to winners.

The numbers racket was profitable because the payout odds, typically 600 to 1 for a bet whose true probability was 999 to 1, gave the house a substantial mathematical advantage that, combined with the volume of small bets collected across a large urban population, generated consistently large revenues. A single numbers bank covering a substantial neighborhood could process tens of thousands of bets on a daily basis. The operation also had a social dimension that distinguished it from other forms of gambling: the runner who collected bets was typically a neighborhood figure known to his customers, credit was extended to regular bettors, and the numbers bank functioned as an informal financial institution for people who had limited access to legitimate banking services. For many participants, the daily numbers bet was not primarily a financial transaction but a social ritual that connected them to their neighborhood's informal economy and provided a daily moment of hope, however slim, for a windfall that could change their circumstances.

In the first decades of the twentieth century, numbers operations in African American and immigrant neighborhoods were often operated by independent Black entrepreneurs and community figures who kept most of the profits within the communities they served. By the postwar period, Italian American organized crime families had, in most major cities, either absorbed or displaced independent Black numbers operators, either through negotiated arrangements that left Black operators as franchisees of a Mafia-controlled network or through outright coercion that pushed them out. The process was not uniform across cities or across time, and historians have documented genuine complexity in the relationship between organized crime and Black numbers operators, including cases of partnership, of Black operators maintaining significant autonomy, and of violent displacement. But the general direction was toward organized crime consolidation of an industry that generated its most reliable revenues from communities of color while the profits flowed predominantly to white criminal organizations.

Bookmaking and Wire Rooms

Bookmaking, the taking of bets on horse races and sporting events, was another major revenue source that organized crime dominated in the postwar period. Unlike the numbers racket, which was primarily a retail operation serving individual bettors with small wagers, bookmaking operated at multiple levels: retail bookmakers who took individual bets from customers in bars, barbershops, and through telephone accounts; wholesale bookmakers who accepted layoff bets from retail operators who needed to reduce their exposure on particularly popular outcomes; and the national wire services that transmitted race results and betting information from tracks across the country to bookmakers in hundreds of cities simultaneously, allowing them to accept bets until the last possible moment and settle accounts with accurate results.

The wire service was a particularly important chokepoint that organized crime controlled during the Prohibition and immediate postwar era. The Continental Press Service, which monopolized the distribution of racing information to bookmakers across the country, was closely associated with organized crime interests, and access to the wire, the service that transmitted timely race results, was a precondition for running a serious bookmaking operation. The Kefauver Committee investigation devoted significant attention to the wire service as a mechanism of organized crime control over the national bookmaking industry, and the Committee's findings confirmed that organized crime's control of information distribution gave it leverage over thousands of independent bookmakers across the country, extracting tribute in exchange for access to a service they could not operate without.

Las Vegas: Criminal Capital of the Postwar West

No element of organized crime's postwar gambling operations has attracted more historical attention, or generated more cultural mythology, than its relationship to Las Vegas. Nevada had legalized casino gambling in 1931, and the small desert city had developed a modest gambling industry in the Depression and wartime years. But it was the postwar investment surge, much of it organized crime-connected, that transformed Las Vegas into a major entertainment destination and eventually into one of America's most visited cities.

The organized crime connection to Las Vegas operated through two primary channels. The first was direct investment and management, with criminal figures and their associates building, owning, and operating casino properties. Bugsy Siegel's Flamingo, opened in 1946 with financing from the national crime syndicate, was the pioneer, and though Siegel was murdered before his creation could prove its profitability, the Flamingo's eventual success demonstrated that a full-scale luxury casino hotel could be profitable in the Nevada desert. A succession of properties followed, many of them financed by organized crime or operated by criminal figures who held legitimate ownership stakes or simply skimmed revenue from the casino floor.

The second channel was the Teamsters Central States Pension Fund, which by the late 1950s and 1960s had become the primary legitimate financing source for Las Vegas casino development. Loans from the pension fund financed the construction or expansion of multiple properties, and the terms of those loans, arranged with the assistance of organized crime intermediaries who controlled the union officials making lending decisions, came with arrangements that allowed criminal figures to place allies in management positions and to skim cash from casino revenues. The skim, the practice of removing cash from casino revenue before it was counted and recorded, was documented by FBI surveillance of Las Vegas casinos that began in the early 1960s and eventually produced a series of prosecutions in the 1970s and 1980s.

The Las Vegas story illustrates one of the central dynamics of organized crime at its peak: the blurring of boundaries between criminal enterprise and legitimate business. The casinos were legal operations, licensed by Nevada authorities and visible to any visitor who walked through the door. The criminal activity they harbored, the skimming of revenue, the penetration of management by organized crime figures, the use of pension funds under criminal influence for investment, operated beneath the visible surface of a seemingly legitimate industry. This combination of surface legitimacy and underlying criminal exploitation was characteristic of organized crime at its institutional maturity, and it posed challenges for law enforcement that purely criminal enterprises did not.

Historical Vocabulary
The Numbers Racket

The numbers racket, also known as policy gambling or the numbers game, was a form of illegal daily lottery that operated in virtually every major American city during the first three-quarters of the twentieth century. Bettors chose a three-digit combination and paid a small sum, often as little as a penny, to a local runner who collected bets in the neighborhood. The winning combination was drawn from a publicly verifiable source, such as racetrack totals, to prevent operator manipulation. Winners received odds typically set at 500 to 1 or 600 to 1, compared to the true probability of 999 to 1, giving operators a consistent mathematical advantage across thousands of daily transactions.

The numbers racket was important in organized crime history for several reasons. It generated enormous revenues from small individual bets aggregated across large urban populations. It provided employment and income for hundreds of runners and collectors in each city where it operated. It functioned as an informal financial institution in communities underserved by legitimate banking. And it served as a foundation for organized crime's economic presence in urban working-class neighborhoods, because the runner who collected numbers bets was often also a conduit for loan shark credit, a source of information about neighborhood economic activity, and a point of contact between residents and the criminal organization that oversaw the operation.

The gradual legalization of state lotteries beginning in the 1960s and accelerating through the 1970s and 1980s undercut the numbers racket's market by offering a legal alternative, though historians have noted that state lotteries typically offered worse odds to players than the illegal numbers game had provided.

Section 15.4

Loan Sharking: The Economics of Illegal Credit

Loan sharking, the extension of credit at interest rates far exceeding legal limits, typically enforced through the threat or reality of violence, was one of organized crime's most consistently profitable activities and one of the most deeply embedded in the economic lives of communities where organized crime operated. Unlike gambling, which was a consumer service sought by individuals for recreation, or labor racketeering, which required the infiltration of legitimate institutions, loan sharking was a financial service that addressed a genuine economic need: the need for credit among individuals and small businesses that could not access it through legitimate channels.

The market for illegal credit was enormous and structurally persistent throughout the postwar period. Many working-class individuals had limited or no access to commercial bank lending; credit cards were not yet ubiquitous, and banks' underwriting standards, combined with discriminatory lending practices that systematically excluded Black and immigrant borrowers, meant that large segments of the urban population had nowhere to turn for emergency funds or business capital except to informal and often illegal credit sources. Small business owners, particularly in industries with volatile cash flows like restaurants, small retail establishments, and seasonal businesses, faced recurring needs for short-term credit that legitimate banks were unwilling to provide on acceptable terms. Gamblers who had run up losses with organized crime-controlled bookmakers or at illegal casinos were sometimes offered credit by the same organizations to continue playing, converting gambling customers into debt customers. And workers caught in financial emergencies, facing medical bills, rent arrears, or unexpected expenses, found loan sharks accessible in ways that formal financial institutions were not.

The Mechanics of the Juice Loan

The typical loan shark transaction, often referred to as a "juice loan" or "shy," operated on terms that would be immediately recognizable as usurious by any standard of legitimate finance. Interest rates were typically expressed as "points" per week, with a common rate of five points, meaning five percent of the outstanding principal per week. Expressed as an annual percentage rate, five points per week corresponds to an interest rate of over 260 percent annually, compared to legal consumer lending rates that, even including high-cost credit cards, rarely exceeded 30 percent during the postwar period. Some borrowers faced even higher rates, and the compounding of interest on missed payments could rapidly transform a manageable short-term loan into an unpayable accumulating obligation.

The enforcement of loan shark debt was the feature that distinguished it most sharply from legitimate lending and that has generated the most cultural mythology. In popular imagination and in many historical accounts, loan sharks routinely resorted to physical violence, beating or threatening borrowers who fell behind on payments. The reality was somewhat more complex. Outright physical violence was certainly used, particularly in cases where borrowers showed signs of fleeing or of reporting the arrangement to law enforcement. But systematic violence against borrowers was in many respects economically counterproductive: a borrower who had been hospitalized or killed could not repay his debt, and gratuitous violence attracted law enforcement attention. More commonly, loan shark enforcement operated through a combination of psychological intimidation, the credible threat of violence, social pressure in close-knit communities where the loan shark and the borrower had mutual connections, and the steady accumulation of debt through continued interest charges that kept the borrower perpetually obligated. The goal of most loan shark operations was not to punish defaulters but to keep borrowers paying interest indefinitely, transforming a short-term loan into a permanent revenue stream.

Loan Sharking as Economic Control

Beyond its direct revenue generation, loan sharking served as a mechanism of economic control that extended organized crime's reach into legitimate business. A small business owner who borrowed from a loan shark and found himself unable to repay the principal while interest accumulated faced a set of options that the loan shark could shape to organized crime's advantage. He might be required to hire particular workers from organized crime-controlled union locals, to purchase supplies from organized crime-connected vendors, to take on a silent partner whose investment represented the conversion of debt into equity, or in extreme cases to surrender an ownership stake in the business to settle the debt. Loan sharking thus functioned not merely as a financial product but as a mechanism for expanding organized crime's penetration of legitimate business enterprises, converting indebtedness into ownership or influence.

The connection between loan sharking and gambling was also structurally important. Organized crime-controlled gambling operations, whether bookmaking operations or illegal casino nights, created a natural customer base for loan shark services among gamblers who lost more than they could immediately cover. The same organization that took a bettor's wager could offer him credit when he ran short, and the interest on that credit could exceed the original gambling loss within weeks. In this way, gambling and loan sharking reinforced each other as revenue sources and kept customers bound to the same criminal organization across multiple product lines, an early form of what business analysts would later call cross-selling.

Historical Vocabulary
Loan Sharking

Loan sharking refers to the practice of lending money at interest rates that exceed legal limits, typically enforced through threats of violence or actual violence. The term "loan shark" dates to the late nineteenth century in American usage. In organized crime contexts, the practice is also known as "shylocking" or extending "juice loans," terms derived from the interest charges that accumulate like juice on the principal.

Loan shark operations in the postwar period typically charged weekly interest of two to five percent of outstanding principal, equivalent to annual rates of over 100 to 260 percent. The practice was economically significant not only as a revenue source but as a mechanism for extending organized crime's influence into legitimate businesses, since borrowers unable to repay principal faced pressure to provide criminal organizations with access to their businesses, hiring practices, supply chains, or ownership stakes.

The term vigorish (or "vig") referred to the interest charged on a loan shark loan, or alternatively to the bookmaker's commission on gambling bets. In both contexts, it described the regular payment extracted from customers that constituted the criminal organization's profit on each transaction. The vigorish was the engine of organized crime's consumer financial services, generating predictable recurring revenue from a large customer base with limited access to legitimate financial alternatives.

Section 15.5

National Influence: Politics, Law, and Entertainment

The revenues generated by labor racketeering, construction corruption, gambling, and loan sharking were not ends in themselves. They were the foundation for a broader exercise of influence that extended into political life, law enforcement, the legal system, and the entertainment industry, giving organized crime a degree of social and political power that transcended its strictly criminal activities and made it genuinely, if uncomfortably, part of the institutional landscape of postwar American life.

Political Corruption and Municipal Power

Organized crime's political influence operated at multiple levels and through multiple mechanisms. At the most basic level, criminal organizations in cities where they had been established for decades had developed relationships with the political machines that governed those cities, relationships that dated in some cases to the Prohibition era or earlier. In exchange for the toleration of gambling operations, the moderation of law enforcement attention, and access to municipal contracts, organized crime contributed to political campaigns, turned out votes in neighborhoods where it had organizational presence, and provided various forms of support to political allies. The relationship was not one of organized crime controlling politics but of mutual accommodation between two institutional systems that each had things the other needed.

In some cities and at some points in the postwar period, this mutual accommodation crossed into outright corruption. Police commanders who overlooked gambling operations in exchange for regular payments; judges whose bail and sentencing decisions were influenced by criminal payments or political pressure from organized crime allies; mayors and city councilmembers who steered municipal contracts to organized crime-connected firms; prosecutors who declined to pursue cases against protected criminal figures: all of these forms of corruption were documented in various cities during the postwar period, and the Kefauver and McClellan Committee hearings brought specific examples to national attention.

The Kefauver hearings, which were broadcast live on the newly ubiquitous medium of television in 1950 and 1951, are worth examining in some detail because they represent an important moment in the history of organized crime's national visibility and the public understanding of its political dimensions. Senator Estes Kefauver of Tennessee chaired a special Senate committee that traveled to fourteen cities, took testimony from hundreds of witnesses, and produced a report documenting organized crime as a national network with deep local political connections. The hearings drew enormous television audiences, sometimes rivaling those for major sports events, as millions of Americans watched bookmakers, mob associates, and occasionally reluctant local officials testify about criminal arrangements that, in many cases, city residents had long suspected but never seen confirmed in so public a forum.

The political consequences of the hearings were significant but limited. In several cities, reform candidates rode public outrage about corruption to electoral victories. Some notoriously corrupt officials were removed from office or declined to seek re-election. But the structural conditions that had made corruption possible, the combination of organized crime's wealth, its organizational capacity, and its ability to offer political actors things they wanted, did not change, and within a few years many of the reform administrations had given way to new arrangements that, while sometimes less openly corrupt than their predecessors, replicated the fundamental dynamic of accommodation between political power and criminal organization.

Law Enforcement Corruption and the FBI

Organized crime's ability to corrupt law enforcement was perhaps the most fundamental expression of its institutional power. A criminal organization that can reliably neutralize law enforcement can operate with a degree of security that makes it genuinely difficult to dislodge, since the very institutions charged with enforcing the law against it are converted into protectors. The corruption of police departments in cities where organized crime was entrenched was not universal, and many individual law enforcement officers maintained their integrity in difficult conditions. But systematic evidence of organized crime's ability to purchase protection at the departmental level, rather than simply bribing individual officers, was documented in city after city during the postwar period.

The Federal Bureau of Investigation's stance toward organized crime during the long tenure of Director J. Edgar Hoover represents one of the most discussed and debated questions in organized crime history. For decades, Hoover publicly denied the existence of a national organized crime organization, characterizing the Mafia as a journalistic myth while directing FBI resources toward bank robbers and communist infiltrators. The reasons for this denial have been extensively debated by historians. Some have emphasized Hoover's institutional preferences for cases that produced clear arrests, large press coverage, and limited legal risk, as opposed to the complex, slow-moving, legally uncertain work of organized crime investigation. Others have suggested that Hoover was himself compromised, that organized crime figures possessed information about his personal life that he could not afford to have publicized. Still others point to the genuine legal and jurisdictional obstacles that made organized crime investigation extremely difficult under existing law.

Whatever the reasons for Hoover's resistance, the Apalachin Conference of 1957 made his public position untenable. State police officers in upstate New York, investigating unusual traffic around a farm belonging to a local organized crime figure named Joseph Barbara, stumbled upon a gathering of more than sixty organized crime leaders from across the country. The attendees, who included the top figures of multiple crime families, fled the farm on foot when police arrived, and subsequent investigation established that the meeting had brought together organized crime leadership from across the United States for what appeared to be a national coordination session. The accidental discovery of Apalachin forced the FBI to acknowledge publicly that a national organized crime organization existed and to begin the process of building an intelligence capacity that would eventually produce the surveillance and informant programs that contributed to organized crime's prosecution in subsequent decades.

The Entertainment Industry and Cultural Penetration

Organized crime's relationship to the entertainment industry in the postwar period was extensive and complicated. The Mafia's involvement in entertainment ranged from the ownership and management of nightclubs and music venues, where organized crime figures were often genuine patrons of popular performance, to the control of jukeboxes and vending machine operations that generated revenue in bars and restaurants, to more complex relationships with recording labels, talent agencies, and booking operations that gave organized crime financial stakes in the careers of entertainers.

The jukebox business, seemingly trivial, was actually a significant organized crime revenue source and a mechanism of territorial control in the postwar period. Organized crime-controlled companies monopolized jukebox placement in bars, restaurants, and other establishments in many cities, charging placement fees and collecting revenue shares that made the jukebox network a profitable and self-reinforcing operation. Establishment owners who attempted to use jukeboxes from non-organized crime suppliers found their machines vandalized or their establishments subjected to other forms of pressure, making the organized crime jukebox monopoly as self-enforcing as its construction or gambling operations.

The relationship between organized crime and the music business was more complex and more contested historically. Some entertainers, particularly Italian American performers who rose to fame in the postwar decades, had personal and professional relationships with organized crime figures that ranged from genuine friendships based on shared cultural backgrounds to financial arrangements that gave criminal organizations stakes in performance careers or booking operations. The extent and significance of these relationships has been a subject of considerable historical and journalistic investigation, much of it more speculative than the documented record of organized crime's industrial activities. What can be said with confidence is that organized crime's presence in certain entertainment venues and in the nightclub business gave it a cultural visibility and social accessibility that pure industrial criminality would not have provided, and that this cultural presence contributed to the mythology of the Mafia as a glamorous as well as powerful institution.

The Valachi Hearings and National Awareness

The most dramatic public demonstration of organized crime's institutional character during the postwar period came not from a committee investigation of external witnesses but from the 1963 Senate testimony of Joseph Valachi, a low-ranking soldier in the Genovese family who had murdered a fellow prisoner in Atlanta's federal penitentiary in 1962, mistakenly believing the man was a Mafia hitman sent to kill him, and who subsequently agreed to cooperate with Justice Department investigators in exchange for protection. Valachi's testimony, delivered before the McClellan Committee and broadcast on television, provided the first detailed insider account of the American Mafia's internal structure, terminology, rituals of membership, rules of conduct, and organizational hierarchy.

Valachi introduced the term "Cosa Nostra," meaning "Our Thing" in Italian, as the name members used for the organization, contradicting Hoover's long-maintained position that no such organization existed. He described the ceremonies through which new members were inducted, the hierarchy of boss, underboss, capos, soldiers, and associates, the rule of silence that governed members' conduct toward law enforcement, the Commission as the governing body that adjudicated disputes among families, and the territorial arrangements that divided New York among the five families. The detail and internal consistency of his account, corroborated in many particulars by FBI intelligence gathered through surveillance, established for the first time in a public forum that the American Mafia was exactly the kind of sophisticated, nationally organized criminal institution that investigators and journalists had long suspected and that official denial had long obscured.

Valachi's testimony did not immediately transform law enforcement's capacity to prosecute organized crime; the legal tools needed for that transformation would not exist until RICO. But it profoundly altered public understanding of what organized crime was and how it operated, and it provided the political foundation for the legal and budgetary investments in organized crime enforcement that would eventually produce the prosecutions of the 1980s. It also entered American popular culture in ways that shaped how organized crime would be represented in fiction and film for generations.

Section 15.6

Everyday Life in the Shadow of the Mafia Economy

The history of organized crime at its peak has often been told from the top down: from the perspective of the bosses who ran the families, the investigators who pursued them, the politicians who accommodated or confronted them, and the journalists who reported on them. But the Mafia's postwar economic empire was sustained not only by its leadership but by millions of ordinary transactions between criminal organizations and the working-class communities they served, exploited, and controlled. Understanding those transactions from the perspective of the people who participated in them, as consumers of illegal services, as workers in corrupted industries, as business owners navigating an economy shaped by criminal power, is essential to a complete historical account of the period.

Workers in Corrupted Industries

For workers in industries dominated by organized crime-controlled unions, the experience of labor racketeering was genuinely ambiguous. On one hand, union membership, even in corrupted unions, typically provided wages and benefits substantially above what non-union workers in comparable industries received. The construction worker who paid tribute to a corrupt steward for the right to work might still earn twice what a non-union construction worker earned, and the pension and health benefits that came with union membership, even when those benefits were imperfectly managed, represented a level of security unavailable to most American workers outside the union sector.

On the other hand, the corruption had real costs that fell disproportionately on ordinary workers. Kickbacks from wages, however normalized, directly reduced workers' take-home pay. Sweetheart contracts that provided below-market wages and benefits, where they existed, reduced the real value of union membership. Pension fund mismanagement and outright theft reduced the retirement security of workers who had counted on those funds. And the suppression of democratic participation within union life, enforced through intimidation of members who challenged corrupt leadership, denied workers the basic right to govern their own organizations in their own interest.

The longshoremen of New York's waterfront provide perhaps the most extensively documented example of workers navigating organized crime's control of their industry. The shape-up system created daily economic anxiety, since men who needed to work could never be certain of employment regardless of their skills or their history of service. The kickback system extracted a regular percentage of wages for the right to work. Loan sharks operated on the piers, providing credit to men who needed it between work days but at terms that could trap borrowers in permanent debt. Numbers runners collected bets daily, providing entertainment and the dream of a windfall but also extracting money from workers who could ill afford to lose it. The waterfront was, in this sense, a self-contained criminal economy layered atop the legitimate economy of loading and unloading ships, with every layer of the legitimate operation generating revenue for the criminal structure that overlay it.

Small Business Owners and the Protection Economy

For small business owners in organized crime-dominated territories, the experience of criminal power was similarly complex. A small restaurant owner in a neighborhood where organized crime exercised territorial control might face demands for regular payments from a local organized crime associate, payments understood by both parties as "protection" money that paid for the right to operate without harassment. The alternative to paying was not necessarily violent confrontation; more often it was a series of escalating inconveniences, health inspection difficulties, noise complaints from neighbors, problems with suppliers, or the sudden unavailability of services needed to operate, that made resistance more costly than accommodation.

At the same time, some small business owners developed genuine, if asymmetrical, relationships with local organized crime figures that provided real benefits. In neighborhoods where the police were absent, indifferent, or corrupt, organized crime sometimes functioned as a de facto security provider, discouraging street crime and petty theft from businesses that paid tribute. This was, in essence, the protection racket in its most transparent form: paying for protection against threats that the protector could create or withhold. But for business owners who experienced street crime as a genuine threat and who saw organized crime control as preferable to uncontrolled disorder, the arrangement could seem, however coerced, genuinely preferable to the alternative.

Community Perceptions and the Mafia Myth

Popular perceptions of the Mafia within the communities where it operated were complex and contested, and they do not reduce to simple narratives of oppression or admiration. In Italian American communities, organized crime figures were simultaneously a source of community pride for some residents, a source of shame for others, and a pragmatic fact of life for most. The cultural mythology of the Mafia, which celebrated its hierarchy, loyalty, and code of honor while minimizing or romanticizing its violence and exploitation, was partly a product of community need, the desire to find something admirable in institutions associated with one's own ethnic group, and partly a product of the entertainment industry's systematic romanticization of criminal life.

In African American and Puerto Rican communities in Northern cities, the relationship to organized crime was shaped by the numbers racket's deep community roots and by the gradual displacement of Black numbers operators by Italian American organized crime. For many residents, the numbers runner was a neighborhood figure who extended credit, maintained relationships of personal accountability, and kept most of his operation's proceeds within the community. The displacement of such operators by Italian American organized crime, which extracted profits and returned them to criminal organizations based in other neighborhoods, was experienced as a form of economic colonialism by some community members and observers, and it generated resentment that periodically surfaced in organized crime history.

Geography Study

The Geography of Organized Crime's Postwar Industrial Empire

The American Mafia's postwar power was not evenly distributed across the United States. It was concentrated in specific cities and specific industries, and understanding its geography helps explain both how it operated and why it proved so durable. Students should be able to identify and analyze the following geographic patterns:

New York City was the center of the five-family system and the site of the Mafia's deepest penetration of legitimate industry. The waterfront, the construction trades, the garment district, the fish market, the waste disposal industry, and the trucking sector were all organized crime-influenced, and the geographic concentration of multiple industries in a single city made New York uniquely profitable criminal territory.

Chicago was the second major organized crime center, with the Outfit dominating the city's gambling operations, labor unions, and nightlife industry, and exercising influence through a network of suburban and downstate Illinois operations that extended the Outfit's reach well beyond the city itself.

Las Vegas emerged as a key node of organized crime's national network after World War II, a city whose legal gambling economy was penetrated by organized crime investment and whose casino revenues, skimmed before reporting, provided income to families from New York, Chicago, Kansas City, and elsewhere.

The industrial Northeast and Midwest contained the largest concentrations of organized crime activity outside New York and Chicago: Philadelphia, Boston, Providence, Detroit, Cleveland, Buffalo, and Pittsburgh each had established organized crime families with industry-specific penetration patterns shaped by local economic conditions.

The South and Southwest were much less deeply penetrated by the Commission-affiliated families, though individual organized crime figures operated gambling and other enterprises in New Orleans, Miami, and other cities.

Primary Source Window

The Valachi Senate Testimony (1963)

Creator: Joseph Valachi, Genovese family soldier  ·  Date: September–October 1963  ·  Type: Congressional testimony; oral primary source

Context: Joseph Valachi was born in New York City in 1904 and joined the Genovese family in the 1930s. He spent his career as a soldier, involved in gambling, narcotics, and other criminal enterprises. In 1962, serving time in the Atlanta Federal Penitentiary on a narcotics conviction, he believed that Vito Genovese had ordered his assassination. Valachi strangled a fellow prisoner he mistakenly believed to be the assassin. Facing murder charges and now convinced that organized crime had already marked him for death, he agreed to cooperate with Justice Department and FBI investigators. He testified before the McClellan Committee in September and October 1963 in nationally televised hearings that were watched by millions of Americans.

In his testimony, Valachi described in systematic detail the structure of the American Mafia, which he referred to throughout as "Cosa Nostra." He explained the induction ceremony, in which a new member was sponsored by an existing member, pricked his finger so blood was drawn, and held a burning paper image of a saint in his hands while swearing an oath of omertà, the code of silence. He described the organizational hierarchy: the boss at the top, the underboss beneath him, the capos who each led crews of soldiers, and the soldiers who were the rank-and-file members. He explained the distinction between members, who were always of Italian descent, and associates, who worked with the organization but were not formally inducted. He described the Commission as the governing body that mediated disputes and set general rules for the organization's national operations. He named individual organized crime leaders across multiple cities and described their roles and relationships with one another.

Analysis: Valachi's testimony was an extraordinary primary source for several reasons. It provided an insider account of an organization that had successfully maintained its secrecy for decades, corroborating in specific detail much of what FBI surveillance had captured but could not confirm through a single coherent witness. It was also, inevitably, a partial and perspectival account: Valachi was a low-level soldier who had limited knowledge of the organization's upper levels, whose memory of events decades past was imperfect, and who had clear incentives, including personal survival, to be as useful to his government handlers as possible. His account of glamorous meetings of crime bosses may have overstated the organizational coherence of what was in reality a looser and more fractious network than his testimony suggested.

The testimony was also profoundly consequential as a cultural document. It provided the template for virtually every subsequent journalistic and fictional account of the American Mafia, establishing the terminology, the organizational structure, and the internal culture that would be recognizable to any reader of Mafia memoirs or viewer of gangster films for the next half-century. Mario Puzo, whose novel The Godfather was published in 1969, drew on Valachi's published account, and through Puzo's novel and Francis Ford Coppola's films the Valachi version of organized crime became the dominant popular image of the Mafia for generations of Americans.

Historical Debate

Was the Postwar American Mafia a Powerful National Institution or an Overrated Criminal Network?

The question: Historians and criminologists have debated whether the American Mafia at its postwar peak is best understood as a coherent, powerful national institution, as the Valachi testimony and the 1967 President's Commission suggested, or whether the image of a unified, hierarchically organized national organization overstated the actual coherence and power of what was in reality a collection of local criminal organizations with overlapping interests and frequent internal conflicts.

Interpretation A — The Mafia as a Powerful National Institution: Historians and sociologists in the tradition of Donald Cressey's influential 1969 study Theft of the Nation have argued that the American Mafia represented a genuinely novel form of criminal organization: a federated national network with consistent internal rules, a dispute-resolution mechanism (the Commission), and sufficient institutional coherence to exercise sustained influence over major sectors of the American economy. On this view, the penetration of labor unions, the construction industry, gambling, and loan sharking was not the work of loosely affiliated individual criminals but the product of an organization capable of coordinating activity across cities, settling territorial disputes, enforcing codes of conduct, and presenting a unified front to law enforcement and political authorities. The scale and duration of the Mafia's postwar power, on this reading, can only be explained by genuine institutional coherence.
Interpretation B — The Mafia as a More Fragile and Fragmented Network: Other scholars, including criminologist Joseph Albini and sociologist Diego Gambetta in different ways, have questioned whether the Mafia was ever as coherent as the Valachi-Cressey model suggested. On this view, what appeared to outside observers as a unified organization was actually a collection of personal networks built on patron-client relationships and shifting alliances, with the Commission functioning less as a governing body and more as an occasional mediation forum for disputes that could not be resolved bilaterally. The Mafia's internal conflicts, which were frequent and sometimes violent throughout the postwar period, and the persistent failure of families to coordinate on issues of common interest, suggest an organization whose apparent coherence was partly a product of the mythology it cultivated and partly a product of outsiders' tendency to perceive organization where there was actually improvisation and contingency.

Evidence: The institutional interpretation draws on FBI surveillance records, organized crime records seized in various investigations, court testimony from prosecuted cases, and the documented scale and duration of organized crime's penetration of specific industries. The more skeptical interpretation draws on evidence of frequent internal conflicts, the inability of the Commission to prevent family wars, and the variability of organized crime's power across different cities and industries.

Why it matters: How historians answer this question affects how they understand both the nature of organized crime as an institution and the appropriate responses to it. If the Mafia was genuinely a powerful national organization, then disrupting it required the kind of organizational-level prosecution that RICO eventually made possible. If it was primarily a set of personal networks, then prosecution of individual network nodes might be sufficient. The debate also connects to broader questions about how institutions function, how coherent they actually need to be to exercise significant power, and whether the mythology of an organization can itself become a source of real institutional strength.

Can elements of both interpretations be simultaneously true, with the Mafia more coherent in some respects and more fragmented in others? What additional evidence would help resolve the debate?

Multiple Perspectives

Labor Racketeering: Whose Experience?

Organized crime's control of labor unions in the postwar period was experienced very differently by different groups. These perspectives do not cancel each other out; they illuminate the genuine complexity of a situation in which criminal exploitation and real economic benefit sometimes coexisted.

Rank-and-file union members: For many ordinary workers in organized crime-influenced unions, the union, however corrupted, remained an organization that had won them wages, benefits, and working conditions superior to what they would likely have obtained without union representation. A Teamsters driver in the 1960s earned substantially more than a non-union driver. A construction worker in New York, even one who paid kickbacks to a corrupt steward, earned union scale wages that supported a middle-class family life. The corruption was real and its costs were real, but for workers who compared their situation to non-union workers in comparable industries, the net assessment was often favorable to continued union membership, if not to the specific individuals running the union.
Reformers within corrupted unions: A different experience was had by union members who tried to challenge organized crime's control of their organizations. In documented cases across multiple unions and multiple cities, members who organized opposition slates, sought outside investigation of union finances, or attempted to contact law enforcement about union corruption were subjected to harassment, physical violence, loss of employment, and in some cases murder. The democratic governance of the union was not simply imperfect; it was actively suppressed, and the cost of trying to restore it could be severe. These reformers experienced organized crime's union control not as an ambiguous mixture of benefit and corruption but as a direct attack on their democratic rights and sometimes their physical safety.
Small employers in racketeered industries: For small employers in construction, trucking, the garment industry, and other organized crime-influenced sectors, the experience of racketeering was primarily one of added cost and constrained choice. A small construction contractor who wanted to build in New York had to use approved concrete suppliers, pay tribute through various mechanisms, hire through organized crime-influenced union channels, and budget for costs that had no counterpart in less corrupted markets. Large firms could absorb these costs more easily and sometimes developed cooperative relationships with organized crime that served their own interests in market control. Small firms that could not absorb the costs were often driven out of business or into permanent subordination to larger, better-connected competitors.
Pension fund beneficiaries: For the retired workers who depended on pension funds that organized crime had used as sources of investment capital and in some cases simply plundered, the consequences of racketeering were sometimes devastating. When Teamsters Central States Pension Fund loans to Las Vegas casinos soured and fund assets declined, it was retired drivers and warehouse workers, not the organized crime figures who had arranged the loans, who faced reduced pension payments. These retirees had contributed to the fund throughout their working lives in the expectation of retirement security; what they received was the financial residue of criminal exploitation of their savings.
Cause and Effect

Why Did the American Mafia Achieve Such Institutional Power in the Postwar Period?

Long-Term Causes

Decades of institutional development from Prohibition through the 1930s reorganization; deep roots in immigrant communities that provided membership pipelines and social cover; gradual penetration of labor unions beginning in the early twentieth century; the accumulation of capital through bootlegging that funded postwar diversification; the development of the Commission as a dispute-resolution mechanism that reduced destructive internal conflict.

Short-Term Causes

The postwar economic boom that expanded the industries, particularly construction, trucking, and gambling, in which organized crime had established positions; J. Edgar Hoover's prolonged refusal to direct FBI resources toward organized crime investigation; the inadequacy of existing law to prosecute organizations rather than individuals; the willingness of sufficient numbers of union officials, employers, and politicians to accommodate criminal power rather than confront it.

The Development at Peak Power (1950s–1970s)

Organized crime exercises sustained, institutionalized control over key sectors of multiple urban economies; pension fund exploitation funds Las Vegas development; construction corruption adds significant costs to major building projects; the numbers racket operates in virtually every major American city; loan sharking binds thousands of small businesses in debt relationships.

Effects and Consequences

Hundreds of millions of dollars extracted annually from legitimate economic activity; working-class communities bear disproportionate costs through higher consumer prices, reduced pension security, and economic exploitation; political corruption compromises democratic governance in multiple cities; cultural mythology of the Mafia shapes American popular culture for generations; accumulating evidence eventually produces RICO and the prosecutorial campaign of the 1980s.

Historical complexity: The American Mafia's postwar institutional power was not simply the product of criminal will or organizational genius. It was the product of specific historical conditions, including economic growth patterns, inadequate law enforcement tools, political accommodations, and the structural vulnerabilities of industries that depended on labor peace, that would change in the 1970s and 1980s in ways that made the organization far more vulnerable than it had been in its peak years.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Corruption and Criminal Power: From Prohibition to the Postwar Peak

What Changed

  • The primary revenue source shifted from alcohol bootlegging (Prohibition) to a diversified portfolio of labor racketeering, gambling, loan sharking, and legitimate business infiltration
  • The organizational structure became more institutionalized and geographically stable, with the Commission providing a dispute-resolution mechanism that reduced, though did not eliminate, destructive family wars
  • The scale of organized crime's penetration of legitimate economic institutions, particularly labor unions and the construction industry, vastly exceeded anything achievable during the earlier bootlegging era
  • Federal law enforcement's reluctant engagement with organized crime, forced by events like Apalachin and by the McClellan Committee, began the slow development of investigative capacity that would eventually produce RICO
  • Public awareness of organized crime's national institutional character was substantially greater by the 1960s than it had been in the Prohibition era

What Stayed the Same

  • The fundamental structure of organized crime as a federated system of territorial criminal organizations with shared rules and dispute-resolution mechanisms remained consistent from the early 1930s reorganization through the postwar peak
  • The relationship between organized crime and political power, based on mutual accommodation rather than either complete control or complete independence, remained structurally similar across decades
  • The exploitation of immigrant and working-class communities, who were simultaneously organized crime's primary workforce, its primary customer base, and its primary victims, continued throughout the period
  • The code of silence, omertà, as the organization's primary security mechanism, remained central to organized crime's ability to resist prosecution despite occasional defections
  • The concentration of organized crime's institutional power in the industrial Northeast and Midwest remained geographically stable throughout the period

Why this pattern matters: The combination of significant change in revenue sources and organizational scale with significant continuity in fundamental structure helps explain both why the postwar Mafia was more powerful than its Prohibition-era predecessor and why the tools that eventually disrupted it, particularly RICO's focus on criminal organizations as ongoing enterprises, needed to address the organizational continuity that ordinary criminal prosecution of individual acts could not reach.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has examined the American Mafia during the decades of its greatest institutional power, roughly from the end of World War II through the early 1970s. During this period, organized crime achieved a degree of economic and political influence that transcended ordinary criminality and made it, in certain industries and certain cities, a genuine shadow institution that parallel-administered significant sectors of the American economy.

Labor racketeering was the foundation of organized crime's postwar institutional power. By infiltrating and dominating key labor unions, particularly in transportation, construction, the waterfront, and the entertainment industry, organized crime transformed workers' protective organizations into instruments of criminal revenue extraction. Sweetheart contracts, embezzlement of union treasuries, manipulation of pension and welfare funds, the extraction of kickbacks from employers, and the suppression of democratic participation within union life all characterized the racketeered union. The Teamsters Central States Pension Fund provided the most dramatic example, with organized crime-influenced officials directing hundreds of millions of dollars in workers' retirement savings into loans for mob-connected Las Vegas casino development.

The construction industry was the sector most systematically corrupted by organized crime's institutional power. Through control of key union locals, the manipulation of competitive bidding processes, and the extraction of tribute from concrete suppliers, developers, and subcontractors, organized crime added substantial costs to major construction projects in New York and other cities for decades. The Concrete Club arrangement in New York illustrated how thoroughly organized crime could penetrate a single sector when it controlled the relevant labor and supply chain checkpoints. Similar mechanisms operated in the garment industry, waste disposal, the fish and produce markets, and other sectors of urban economies where organized crime had established territorial presence.

Gambling in its many forms remained organized crime's single largest revenue source throughout the postwar period. The numbers racket, the daily illegal lottery that operated in virtually every major American city, was the most widespread form of organized crime gambling and the one most deeply embedded in the everyday economic life of urban working-class communities, particularly communities of color. Bookmaking on horses and sporting events, illegal casino operations, and the organized crime-penetrated legal casinos of Las Vegas rounded out a gambling empire that generated revenues from millions of Americans who sought gambling services that legal channels could not provide.

Loan sharking, the extension of credit at usurious rates enforced through the threat or reality of violence, was both a major revenue source and a mechanism of economic control that extended organized crime's reach into legitimate business. The combination of genuine demand for credit among people and businesses with limited access to legitimate lending, and organized crime's willingness to supply that credit at enormously profitable rates, created a stable and self-reproducing market for illegal financial services. The conversion of unpayable loan shark debt into business ownership stakes or management influence gave organized crime a pathway into legitimate enterprises that extended well beyond the initial criminal transaction.

The national reach of organized crime's postwar influence extended beyond industrial sectors into political life, law enforcement, the legal system, and the entertainment industry. Municipal political corruption that accommodated criminal gambling and protected criminal enterprises was documented in multiple cities. Police and prosecutorial corruption enabled organized crime to operate with a degree of security that seriously hampered law enforcement attempts to disrupt it. The FBI's prolonged refusal to acknowledge organized crime's national institutional character, a stance that was not reversed until the discovery of the Apalachin Conference in 1957 made further denial impossible, represented one of the most consequential institutional failures in American law enforcement history.

The experiences of people who lived and worked in the shadow of organized crime's power were genuinely complex and resist reduction to simple narratives. Workers in organized crime-dominated unions received wages and benefits that typically exceeded those available to non-union workers, even as they were subjected to kickbacks, suppressed democratic voice, and pension fund exploitation. Small business owners paid tribute in exchange for a form of order that, however coercive, sometimes provided real protection against alternative forms of disorder. Numbers gamblers participated in an informal financial institution that met social and economic needs that legitimate institutions did not address. These complexities explain both why organized crime's power proved so durable and why many people who were simultaneously its beneficiaries and its victims were reluctant to cooperate with investigations that threatened to disrupt the arrangements on which they depended.

The seeds of the Mafia's eventual institutional decline were planted during the very years of its greatest strength. The Kefauver and McClellan Committee hearings created public awareness and political momentum that eventually translated into legislative action. The FBI's post-Apalachin engagement with organized crime investigation, however reluctant and belated, began building the intelligence capacity and the informant networks that would prove essential in the prosecutions of the 1980s. Joseph Valachi's 1963 testimony provided the first public insider account of organized crime's structure and terminology, shifting the political environment in ways that facilitated RICO's passage in 1970. The full story of the government's counteroffensive, and of organized crime's eventual institutional decline under the pressure of RICO prosecution, belongs to Chapter 16. But understanding the mechanisms and the scale of the power that government investigators were attempting to dismantle is essential preparation for understanding why that task proved so difficult and, eventually, how it was accomplished.

The American Mafia at its peak was neither the omnipotent criminal government of popular mythology nor a simple collection of street criminals who happened to know each other. It was something more interesting and more historically significant: an institutional network that had learned, over decades, to embed itself in legitimate economic and political institutions in ways that made it genuinely difficult to remove without damaging those institutions in the process. Understanding how it achieved that position is the central contribution of this chapter to the broader history of organized crime as a global phenomenon that changes alongside the states, economies, and institutions within which it operates.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Labor Racketeering
The infiltration and criminal exploitation of labor unions, including embezzlement, pension fund manipulation, sweetheart contracts, and the suppression of democratic union governance.
Sweetheart Contract
A collective bargaining agreement negotiated between corrupt union officials and employers that provides workers with wages and benefits below what genuine arm's-length bargaining would produce, in exchange for employer payments to union officials or their criminal sponsors.
Kickback
A payment made to a person in a position of influence or authority in exchange for favorable treatment; in labor racketeering, typically payments from employers to union officials in exchange for labor peace or favorable contract terms, or from workers to hiring bosses in exchange for employment.
No-Show Job
A position on an employer's payroll for which no work is actually performed; a mechanism of tribute in which organized crime figures or their associates receive wages as a form of payment for criminal toleration.
Shape-Up
A hiring system used on the New York waterfront in which longshoremen reported daily to the pier and were chosen for work by hiring bosses, creating conditions that organized crime exploited to extract kickbacks and other tribute from workers dependent on daily employment selection.
Bid Rigging
The pre-arrangement of competitive contract bidding so that a pre-selected firm wins at an artificially inflated price, with other competitors submitting even higher bids in exchange for the promise of future winning bids; prevalent in organized crime-controlled construction markets.
Concrete Club
The term used by investigators and prosecutors to describe organized crime's cartel-like control of the ready-mix concrete supply and installation in New York City's major construction projects during the postwar decades.
Numbers Racket
An illegal daily lottery in which bettors chose three-digit combinations and paid small sums to neighborhood runners, with winnings paid at odds below the true probability; one of organized crime's most widespread and profitable gambling operations.
Policy Banker
The central operator of a numbers gambling enterprise who provided the capital base that guaranteed payment to winners, tabulated daily bets, and oversaw the network of runners and collectors who gathered wagers in the neighborhoods.
Bookmaking
The business of taking bets on horse races and sporting events at predetermined odds; organized crime dominated bookmaking operations in most major American cities through control of betting lines, wire services, and physical betting establishments.
Wire Room
A facility where illegal bets on horse races and sporting events were accepted by telephone, staffed by clerks who recorded bets and paid out winnings; a key infrastructure element of organized crime's bookmaking operations.
Loan Sharking
The extension of credit at interest rates far exceeding legal limits, enforced through threats of violence or actual violence; also known as shylocking or providing "juice loans." A major organized crime revenue source and mechanism of business control.
Vigorish (Vig)
The interest charged on a loan shark loan, or the bookmaker's percentage on each bet; the regular payment extracted from customers that constituted organized crime's profit from consumer financial and gambling services.
Juice Loan
A colloquial term for a loan shark loan, emphasizing the interest (juice) that accumulated on the principal and constituted the criminal lender's revenue; the term captured the way interest could expand to dwarf the original loan amount.
Pension Fund
A pooled investment vehicle in which workers and employers contribute funds to provide retirement income for union members; organized crime-influenced union officials used pension funds, particularly the Teamsters Central States Pension Fund, as sources of investment capital for mob-connected enterprises and targets of outright embezzlement.
Skimming
The practice of removing cash from casino revenue or other business income before it was officially counted and recorded, allowing revenue to be concealed from tax authorities and shareholders; a central mechanism of organized crime's Las Vegas operations.
Tribute
Regular payments made to organized crime in exchange for the right to operate in a territory or industry; distinguished from one-time extortion by its regularity and its quasi-institutional character as a condition of doing business in organized crime-controlled markets.
Protection Racket
A criminal enterprise in which payments are demanded in exchange for protection against harm that the criminal organization itself threatens or could create; the foundational mechanism through which organized crime extracted revenue from businesses in its territories.
Commission
The governing body of the American Mafia, established in the early 1930s reorganization, that brought together the bosses of major organized crime families to mediate territorial disputes and set rules for the organization's national operations.
Cosa Nostra
Italian for "Our Thing"; the term that organized crime members used for their organization, as reported by Joseph Valachi in his 1963 congressional testimony; became the standard reference term in law enforcement and journalism.
Omertà
The code of silence that governed organized crime members' conduct toward law enforcement and outsiders; a fundamental institutional rule that enabled the organization to resist prosecution by preventing members from testifying against each other.
Capo
A captain in organized crime hierarchy who commanded a crew of soldiers and reported to the family's boss and underboss; responsible for managing the crew's criminal activities and delivering a share of revenues to the family leadership.
Soldier
A formally inducted member of an organized crime family, the lowest rank of full membership; responsible for conducting criminal activities under the direction of a capo, paying tribute upward through the hierarchy, and maintaining the code of silence.
Associate
A person who works with or for an organized crime family without being formally inducted as a member; associates performed criminal work for the family and paid a share of their earnings upward but lacked the protection and status of full membership.
Apalachin Conference
A meeting of more than sixty organized crime leaders at a farm in Apalachin, New York, in 1957, accidentally discovered by state police; forced the FBI to acknowledge the existence of a national organized crime organization and initiated a new era of federal organized crime investigation.
RICO Act
The Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act, passed in 1970; created statutory tools for prosecuting criminal organizations as ongoing enterprises rather than simply prosecuting individual criminals for individual acts; became the primary instrument of organized crime prosecution in the 1980s and 1990s.

Major People, Groups, and Institutions

Identification & Significance

Carlo Gambino (1902–1976)
Boss of the Gambino family from 1957 to his death; the dominant figure in New York organized crime during the late postwar period; known for extreme caution, strategic political alliances, and control of construction industry rackets through the family's influence in key union locals.
Vito Genovese (1897–1969)
Boss of the Genovese family; exercised broad influence in labor racketeering, gambling, and narcotics; his rivalry with other family leaders illustrated the internal tensions that coexisted with organized crime's institutional coherence.
Jimmy Hoffa (1913–1975)
President of the International Brotherhood of Teamsters from 1957; facilitated organized crime's use of the Teamsters Central States Pension Fund as investment capital for mob-connected Las Vegas casino development; convicted of jury tampering and pension fraud in 1967; disappeared in 1975, presumed murdered.
Joseph Valachi (1904–1971)
Genovese family soldier and the first member of the American Mafia to testify publicly about its internal structure; his 1963 Senate testimony introduced the term "Cosa Nostra" and provided the foundational public account of organized crime's hierarchy, rituals, and operations.
Sam Giancana (1908–1975)
Boss of the Chicago Outfit; exercised influence in Las Vegas, the entertainment industry, and, controversially, in political connections that have been the subject of ongoing historical investigation; murdered in 1975.
Senator Estes Kefauver (1903–1963)
Democratic senator from Tennessee who chaired the Senate Special Committee to Investigate Crime in Interstate Commerce; the 1950–51 Kefauver hearings, televised nationally, were the first major public investigation of organized crime's national scope.
Robert F. Kennedy (1925–1968)
Chief counsel to the McClellan Committee; led the Senate investigation into labor racketeering; subsequently served as Attorney General and made organized crime prosecution a federal priority; developed a deep personal antagonism with Jimmy Hoffa.
Tony "Ducks" Corallo (1913–2000)
Boss of the Lucchese family; controlled extensive labor union influence in the New York construction trades; his 1986 RICO conviction in the Commission Case was one of the landmark organized crime prosecutions of the postwar period's closing years.
International Brotherhood of Teamsters
America's largest private-sector union; deeply infiltrated by organized crime from the 1950s onward; its Central States Pension Fund was used as a source of hundreds of millions of dollars in loans for mob-connected Las Vegas casino development.
International Longshoremen's Association (ILA)
Waterfront union dominated by organized crime on the New York docks; the shape-up system it administered was one of the most thoroughly documented examples of organized crime's control of a labor institution for criminal exploitation of workers.
Teamsters Central States Pension Fund
The pension fund for Teamsters members in the central states; under organized crime influence became the primary legitimate financing vehicle for Las Vegas casino development; subsequent losses from bad loans reduced the retirement security of Teamsters members.
President's Commission on Law Enforcement (1967)
Federal commission whose report formally documented organized crime as a major national institution and provided the political foundation for the legislative developments, including RICO, that followed.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter has traced the American Mafia during the decades of its greatest institutional power, from the postwar boom of the late 1940s through the early 1970s when the legal tools necessary to mount an effective response were finally assembled. What this period reveals, above all, is that organized crime at its institutional maturity was not primarily a story about criminals. It was a story about institutions: about how legitimate institutions, trade unions, construction firms, pension funds, nightclubs, casinos, and city governments, can be penetrated, corrupted, and converted from their legitimate purposes into instruments of criminal exploitation without losing their outward appearance of legitimacy. The longshoremen still unloaded the ships. The cement masons still poured the concrete. The Teamsters drivers still delivered the goods. But the institutional structures that governed each of these activities had been reshaped, at critical points, by criminal power that extracted tribute from every layer of the legitimate operation.

This understanding matters for the history of organized crime as a global phenomenon because it suggests that the most durable and consequential forms of criminal power are not those that operate in open defiance of legitimate institutions but those that penetrate and use legitimate institutions for criminal ends. The Sicilian Mafia that Chapter 6 examined had pioneered a form of organized crime that derived its power from its ability to function as an alternative governance structure in a context where legitimate governance was weak. The American Mafia of the postwar period refined that model in a very different environment, one where legitimate governance existed but where specific institutional vulnerabilities, in labor law enforcement, in federal policing priorities, in the structure of urban political machines, created spaces in which criminal organization could embed itself. The lesson is not that strong institutions automatically prevent organized crime, but that organized crime's power is always partly a reflection of the specific weaknesses, vulnerabilities, and accommodations of the legitimate institutional environment in which it operates.

This chapter also raises questions about accountability and responsibility that extend beyond the criminal figures at the center of the story. The workers who accepted kickbacks as a condition of employment, the employers who paid tribute as a cost of doing business, the politicians who accepted campaign contributions and looked the other way, the FBI that for decades declined to investigate organized crime as a national phenomenon: all of these actors made choices that enabled and sustained organized crime's institutional power. None of them were simply passive victims of an irresistible criminal force. They were participants, however coerced or however willing, in a set of institutional arrangements that generated criminal power at the expense of the workers, consumers, and taxpayers who ultimately bore the costs. Understanding those choices, the pressures that shaped them, the alternatives that were available, and the conditions that eventually made different choices possible, is part of what makes this history genuinely illuminating rather than simply dramatic.

The Mafia at its peak was eventually dismantled, as Chapter 16 will examine, through a combination of new legal tools, sustained investigative effort, informants who broke the code of silence, and structural changes in the industries and communities where organized crime had been strongest. But the mechanisms it had developed, the penetration of legitimate institutions, the extraction of tribute from captured markets, the use of violence as a credible enforcement threat held largely in reserve, did not disappear with the specific organizations that had pioneered them. They were available to be learned, adapted, and deployed by the successor criminal organizations, Russian, Mexican, Chinese, Eastern European, that inherited the institutional landscape the American Mafia left behind. Understanding how those mechanisms worked, at their historical peak, is essential preparation for understanding the organized crime history that follows.

Chapter 33 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every political community is built on some idea of who belongs together and why. Some communities define themselves by shared laws and institutions. Others define themselves by shared history, language, religion, or culture. Still others define themselves by a sense of common struggle against those who hold power or who are perceived as hostile outsiders. The political movements explored in this chapter — nationalism, populism, and identity politics — all draw on the powerful human desire to belong, to be recognized, and to be represented by leaders and institutions that speak for people like oneself. Understanding these movements is not optional for students of government. They are among the most consequential forces shaping elections, institutions, conflicts, and international relations in the twenty-first century.

Nationalism is the belief that a people sharing a common identity — whether defined by language, culture, ethnicity, history, religion, or territory — forms a natural political community that deserves its own government. Nationalism has motivated the creation of new states, the dissolution of empires, the unification of divided peoples, and devastating wars. Populism is a political style and logic that frames politics as a conflict between a pure, virtuous people and a corrupt, self-serving elite. Identity politics refers to political organizing and advocacy organized around shared social identities. Each phenomenon is distinct; all three frequently overlap in the real world of politics.

The goal of this chapter is not to condemn or celebrate nationalism, populism, or identity politics in the abstract, but to explain what each means, how each operates in political systems, what problems each tries to solve, and what dangers each can create when it operates without institutional checks or civic moderation. Students should approach these topics with the same analytical care they bring to any serious question in political science: explain the system first, explain the debate second, explain the tradeoffs third.

Opening Scenario

In 2016, voters in the United Kingdom chose, in a referendum, to leave the European Union. The campaign turned on competing claims about national sovereignty, democratic control, immigration, cultural identity, and economic belonging. Supporters of "Leave" argued that the British people deserved to make their own laws without Brussels. Supporters of "Remain" argued that international cooperation served British interests and protected rights. Both sides used the language of the people, of democracy, and of belonging. The Brexit vote illustrates how nationalism, populism, and identity-based politics converge in a single democratic event — and why students of government need precise tools to understand each one.

Nationalism: Nations, Identity, and Self-Government

Section 33.1

Nationalism Defined

Nationalism is one of the most powerful and most misunderstood forces in modern politics. Before examining its varieties and consequences, students must understand what nationalism actually claims. At its core, nationalism is the political belief that people who share a common identity — a nation — deserve to govern themselves and that political authority should be aligned with national community. Nationalists hold that the nation is the natural and proper unit of political life, and that governments derive their legitimacy from the nation they serve.

The concept of the nation is itself contested. A nation is not simply a state or a government. Nations are communities defined by shared characteristics, which may include language, culture, history, religion, territory, ancestry, or some combination of these. What makes a group a nation rather than merely a population is partly objective — shared characteristics — and partly subjective — a belief among the group's members that they form a distinct community with a shared destiny. As the political philosopher Ernest Renan argued in the nineteenth century, a nation is a daily plebiscite: it exists as long as its members choose to regard themselves as part of it.

This definition immediately reveals that nations are not natural facts but social and political constructions. People decide — through collective memory, cultural practice, political mobilization, education, and storytelling — that they belong together as a nation. This does not mean nations are arbitrary or unimportant. Constructed identities can be deeply felt and historically powerful. The fact that a national identity was built by poets, educators, politicians, and propagandists over centuries does not make it less real to those who hold it. It does mean, however, that national identities can change, expand, contract, and be deliberately shaped for political purposes.

Modern nationalism emerged primarily in Europe in the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, intertwined with the French Revolution, the Romantic movement, and the collapse of dynastic empires. Before nationalism, most people owed primary loyalty to rulers, religions, local communities, or extended families — not to abstract national communities. Nationalism created new forms of mass political loyalty. It contributed to the unification of Germany and Italy in the nineteenth century, the dissolution of the Ottoman and Habsburg empires in the early twentieth century, and the wave of decolonization that created dozens of new states in the mid-twentieth century. It also contributed to catastrophic wars and mass atrocities, including the two World Wars and the genocides of the twentieth century.

The relationship between nationalism and the modern state is fundamental. The nation-state — a political unit in which political boundaries ideally correspond to cultural or ethnic communities — became the dominant model of political organization after the French Revolution. Before nationalism, empires routinely governed diverse peoples without expecting them to share an identity. Nationalism challenged the legitimacy of empire and polyethnic rule, declaring that each people deserved its own state. This principle — national self-determination — became central to international law and political practice after World War I, when President Woodrow Wilson made it a cornerstone of the post-war settlement. National self-determination, however, created new problems even as it solved old ones: many territories contained mixed populations, and drawing borders to match national groups was often impossible without displacing, excluding, or subjugating minorities.

Students should understand nationalism as a political force that can support democratic self-government and can threaten it. A democratic nation-state in which citizens identify with their shared institutions can be a stable, effective political community. A nationalist movement that defines the nation narrowly and treats minorities as outsiders or enemies can undermine democratic pluralism and produce persecution, ethnic conflict, or worse. The form nationalism takes — who it includes, who it excludes, and how it relates to institutions and rights — determines whether it strengthens or weakens political life.

Key Distinction: Nation vs. State

A state is a political and legal entity with a government, territory, population, and sovereignty. A nation is a community defined by shared identity. Many states contain multiple nations; some nations lack states. The United Kingdom contains English, Scottish, Welsh, and Northern Irish national identities within a single state. The Kurdish people are a nation spread across multiple states without a state of their own. These distinctions are essential for understanding nationalist politics.

Section 33.2

Civic and Ethnic Nationalism

The most important distinction within nationalism is between civic nationalism and ethnic nationalism. Understanding this distinction helps students analyze real political movements with precision and avoid the error of treating all forms of nationalism as equivalent.

Civic nationalism defines the nation in terms of shared political commitments rather than shared ancestry or culture. A civic nationalist holds that the nation is composed of all who accept its laws, institutions, values, and membership. In principle, anyone who participates in the political community and accepts its civic norms can belong to the nation, regardless of race, religion, language, or ancestral origin. The nation is a voluntary political community united by common institutions and shared commitment to civic ideals.

The French Republican tradition of nationalism is often cited as a model of civic nationalism. The French Revolution declared that sovereignty resided in the nation — defined as the people who participated in the republic — rather than in the king or in any particular ethnic group. In principle, French national identity was open to all who accepted the republic's civic values. The United States offers another example often analyzed as civic nationalism. American national identity, at its most expansive, has been defined by commitment to constitutional principles — liberty, equality, democratic government, and the rule of law — rather than by ethnicity or religion. "E pluribus unum" — out of many, one — expresses the idea that people of diverse origins can form a single national community through shared commitment to civic institutions.

Ethnic nationalism defines the nation in terms of descent, blood, language, religion, or culture. An ethnic nationalist holds that the nation is composed of people who share ancestral heritage or cultural characteristics that cannot be simply adopted by outsiders. Ethnic nationalism tends to view national membership as something you are born into, not something you choose. It often regards immigrants, minorities, or people of different descent as fundamentally outside the nation even if they hold legal citizenship.

Ethnic nationalism became politically influential in Central and Eastern Europe in the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, where national movements sought independence from multiethnic empires on the basis of distinct linguistic or cultural identities. The German Romantic tradition of nationalism, associated with thinkers like Johann Gottfried Herder, emphasized the unique spirit of a people expressed through language, folklore, and culture. This tradition placed authentic national character in organic cultural communities rather than in abstract political principles. At its extreme, ethnic nationalism defines outsiders as threats to national purity and justifies their exclusion, persecution, or elimination. The Nazi ideology of Aryan racial nationalism is the most catastrophic example of this logic in history.

In practice, most nations combine civic and ethnic elements. Even formally civic nations have often defined citizenship in culturally or racially exclusive ways in practice. The United States declared civic principles of equality while denying citizenship and rights to enslaved African Americans and many other groups. The gap between civic ideals and ethnic practice is often wide and politically significant. Contemporary nationalist movements frequently combine civic rhetoric with ethnic sentiment. Students should be attentive to both the formal claims nationalist movements make and the political practices and exclusions they produce.

Civic Vocabulary
National Self-Determination

National self-determination is the principle that peoples or nations have the right to govern themselves — to determine their own political status and form of government — without domination by foreign powers or external rulers.

The principle gained international prominence after World War I, when President Woodrow Wilson and other leaders invoked it to justify the dissolution of the Ottoman, Austro-Hungarian, and German empires and the creation of new states in Central and Eastern Europe. The principle was incorporated into the United Nations Charter in 1945, which declared respect for the self-determination of peoples as a foundational purpose of the international system.

Self-determination matters in government because it provides the foundational justification for why national communities deserve political independence. It explains why colonial rule came to be regarded as illegitimate and why independence movements succeeded in dismantling European empires after World War II. However, self-determination creates deeply difficult problems: when a group claims the right to secede, it directly conflicts with an existing state's claim to sovereignty and territorial integrity. Who counts as a people? Must a group already control territory? What about minorities within a new state who did not want independence? International law provides only partial guidance, and political outcomes depend heavily on power, recognition, and negotiation.

Section 33.3

Populism and "The People"

Populism is one of the most contested concepts in contemporary political science. Used carefully, populism refers to a specific political logic or style that frames politics as a conflict between two morally opposed groups: the pure, virtuous people on one side and the corrupt, self-serving elite on the other. The populist leader claims to speak directly for the real people and to represent their will against the established interests, institutions, and elites that have betrayed them. This logic resonates in societies where people believe that political institutions serve the wealthy, the connected, or the culturally dominant rather than ordinary citizens.

Populism is not a policy platform or a fixed ideology. It is better understood as a political logic or style that can be attached to many different substantive agendas. There is left-wing populism that defines the people as the economically exploited working class and the elite as wealthy capitalists, bankers, and corporations. There is right-wing populism that defines the people as the culturally authentic majority — often defined by religion, ethnicity, or tradition — and the elite as culturally alien cosmopolitans, bureaucrats, academics, and media figures who look down on ordinary people. The common thread across these variants is the antagonistic framing of politics as a conflict between the virtuous people and the corrupt elite.

Because populism presents the will of the people as unified and morally authoritative, it can be hostile to institutional complexity. Constitutions, independent courts, free press, bureaucratic procedures, international agreements, and minority rights can all be framed by populists as obstacles to the people's will — devices invented by elites to frustrate genuine popular governance. This hostility to institutional checks is what makes populism potentially dangerous for democratic systems. If the leader truly represents the pure people, who dares to limit the leader? Institutions that check executive power can be reframed as enemies of democracy rather than protections of it.

At the same time, populism has sometimes been a force for democratic expansion. The original American Populist movement of the 1890s sought to give ordinary farmers and workers more political power against railroad monopolies, banks, and established political parties. Latin American populists in the mid-twentieth century sometimes expanded social rights and civic participation even while concentrating executive power. The political impact of populism depends on what institutional conditions surround it and whether populist leaders respect constitutional limits after gaining power.

Why does populism emerge when and where it does? Research suggests several contributing conditions. Economic inequality and displacement create populations who feel left behind and suspicious of elites who profited from the same economic changes that harmed ordinary people. Political exclusion and unresponsiveness create voters who feel that established parties serve wealthy donors and organized interests rather than average citizens. Cultural anxiety and rapid social change create people who feel that their values, traditions, and ways of life are being dismissed or mocked by cosmopolitan elites. Distrust of institutions — government, media, courts, experts — creates openings for leaders who claim to bypass corrupted systems and speak directly to the people. Populism is a symptom as much as a cause: when it emerges powerfully, it signals that a significant portion of the population feels excluded, ignored, or dismissed.

Section 33.4

Elites, Institutions, and Anti-Establishment Politics

A central claim of populism — that elites have captured government and betrayed the people — raises important analytical questions. Who are the elites that populists target? What do they actually do? Are the claims populists make about elites accurate, exaggerated, or false? And how should democratic institutions respond to anti-establishment political challenges?

The concept of political elites refers to the relatively small number of people who exercise disproportionate power over political decisions. Political scientists have long studied how power concentrates in the hands of small groups even in formally democratic societies. This observation — that even democratic systems can have powerful, partially self-selecting elite groups — is a legitimate subject of political analysis. Populists simplify and dramatize this observation. They present elites as not merely powerful but as fundamentally corrupt, conspiratorial, and contemptuous of ordinary people. In reality, elites are rarely unified. Business elites compete with each other. Political elites are divided by party, ideology, region, and interest. Bureaucratic elites often genuinely serve public functions. The populist image of the elite as a monolithic, conspiratorial class is a simplification that can tip into conspiracy thinking.

Democratic institutions face a genuine dilemma when confronted with powerful anti-establishment movements. On one hand, dismissing or suppressing legitimate popular grievances can intensify distrust and delegitimize the very institutions that need to maintain public confidence. On the other hand, accommodating every populist claim can mean weakening the institutional checks — independent courts, free press, civil service rules, electoral integrity — that protect democratic governance from abuse. The most robust response to populism in democratic theory is not merely to oppose it rhetorically but to address the conditions that make it attractive: making democratic institutions more responsive and accountable, reducing corruption and conflicts of interest, and ensuring that economic growth and social protection reach ordinary citizens.

How the System Works
How Populist Leaders Consolidate Power
  • Step 1 Electoral victory through populist mobilization. A leader wins election by framing the contest as the people versus corrupt elites. Voters who feel economically marginalized, culturally threatened, or politically ignored provide the coalition. Promises of direct representation and dramatic change generate enthusiasm and high turnout among previously disengaged citizens.
  • Step 2 Delegitimizing opposition institutions. After winning, the leader characterizes opposing institutions — courts, free press, civil service, independent agencies — as tools of the elite rather than legitimate parts of the democratic system. This framing prepares the public to accept institutional attacks as popular victories over entrenched power rather than as democratic erosion.
  • Step 3 Attacking independent institutions. The leader moves to reduce the independence of courts by packing them, reducing their jurisdiction, or ignoring their rulings; undermine free press by revoking licenses, pursuing defamation suits, using state media, or encouraging hostility toward journalists; and reduce the independence of civil service or election administration by replacing professional administrators with political loyalists.
  • Step 4 Changing the rules. The leader pursues constitutional amendments, new electoral rules, or emergency powers that increase executive authority and reduce institutional checks. These changes are typically presented as democratic reform — correcting the imbalances created by the corrupt elite — rather than as concentrations of power.
  • Step 5 Consolidation. With weaker courts, reduced press freedom, and more compliant institutions, the leader faces fewer effective checks on continued power. Opposition finds it increasingly difficult to organize, compete, and win. Elections may continue but become less free and fair. The system may remain nominally democratic while losing the substance of democratic accountability.

Complications and exceptions: Not all populist leaders follow this script to its conclusion. Many govern within constitutional limits, lose elections, and accept defeat. Democratic resilience depends on the strength of existing institutions, the behavior of elites across sectors, the vitality of civil society, and the vigilance of citizens. Strong courts, an independent press, active civil society organizations, and allied democratic parties can resist and sometimes reverse populist institutional attacks. History shows that the outcome is never determined solely by the leader's intentions — it depends on the contest between institutional resilience and populist pressure.

Section 33.5

Identity, Representation, and Recognition

Identity politics refers to political organizing and advocacy organized around shared social identities. The identities involved are diverse: race, ethnicity, gender, religion, sexual orientation, disability, language, class, national origin, and more. Identity politics is not simply interest-group politics, though the two overlap. Interest groups pursue concrete policy goals — lower taxes, higher wages, environmental regulation. Identity-based politics often also pursues recognition — the demand that a group's existence, dignity, experience, and value be acknowledged by the political system and by fellow citizens.

The political philosopher Charles Taylor, whose work on "the politics of recognition" became widely influential, argued that recognition is a genuine and important human need. People need not only material resources and legal rights but also public affirmation of who they are. When a group's identity is denied, demeaned, or rendered invisible by political institutions and public culture, its members suffer a real injury beyond simple material deprivation. The demand for recognition — that LGBTQ+ people exist and deserve equal dignity, that Indigenous peoples have distinct histories and rights, that women's experiences are politically relevant — is a legitimate political claim.

Identity-based political organizing has a long history in democratic societies. The abolitionist movement organized around the collective experience and moral claims of enslaved people. The women's suffrage movement organized around the shared status of women excluded from political participation. The civil rights movement organized around the shared experience of racial discrimination. The labor movement organized around shared working-class economic status. Indigenous rights movements organized around shared histories of colonial dispossession. In each case, political organizing around a shared identity produced important expansions of democratic rights and civic inclusion.

Critics of contemporary identity politics raise several concerns. Some argue that organizing politics around particular identities fragments the common civic life that democratic solidarity requires. Others argue that identity politics can freeze social categories into rigid political identities rather than allowing for individual complexity and social evolution. Still others worry that competitive claims over whose identity-based grievances are most legitimate can become a substitute for genuine political analysis of power and material conditions.

Defenders of identity politics respond that the critique often comes from groups whose identities have historically been treated as the neutral, universal default rather than as particular identities at all. When straight, white, male, Christian perspectives are treated as simply "the American" or "the human" perspective, other identities appear as special pleading. Making visible the particularity of all identities — including dominant ones — is part of honest democratic politics. They also point out that refusing to name racial or gender discrimination does not produce equality; it allows discrimination to continue unmarked and unchallenged.

The civil rights movement is instructive: it organized around the particular identity and experience of African Americans while simultaneously appealing to universal American civic ideals of equality and constitutional rights. It did not choose between particular identity and universal principle; it used both in the service of democratic expansion. The question of how democratic societies balance particular identity claims and universal civic norms is one of the most important and unresolved questions of contemporary democratic politics.

Comparative Lens
Ethnic and Religious Diversity in Democratic Systems

Democratic societies have developed very different approaches to managing ethnic, linguistic, and religious diversity. Comparing these approaches illustrates both the range of possible institutional responses and the tradeoffs each involves.

Assimilation Models

Countries including France and the early United States have emphasized the integration of diverse populations into a single national culture and civic identity. The state does not formally recognize distinct ethnic or cultural groups in law; instead, it treats all citizens as individual rights-holders with equal status. Religious and ethnic identity is treated as a private matter. The assimilation model maintains a unified civic identity and equality before the law. Its limitation is that "assimilation" in practice has often meant pressure on minorities to adopt the dominant culture. The French model has faced significant tensions over the treatment of Muslim citizens and questions about religious expression in public space.

Multiculturalism Models

Countries including Canada and Australia formally recognize and accommodate cultural diversity. Canada's Multiculturalism Act of 1988 declared that cultural diversity is a fundamental characteristic of Canadian society and committed the government to supporting it. Multiculturalism can sustain stronger civic inclusion of diverse communities by affirming their identities. Critics argue it can create parallel communities that do not develop sufficient shared civic bonds, or make it difficult to challenge practices within minority communities that conflict with liberal democratic values.

Consociational Models

Countries like Switzerland, Belgium, and Northern Ireland have built power-sharing arrangements directly into political institutions. Switzerland's federal system and coalition government ensure that German, French, Italian, and Romansh speakers all have voice in national politics. In Northern Ireland, the Good Friday Agreement required power-sharing between unionist and nationalist parties. Consociational arrangements ensure that major communities feel represented, which can prevent violent conflict in deeply divided societies. Their weakness is that they can entrench community divisions and create political deadlock when communities cannot agree.

No single model works for all societies. The best approach depends on the nature of the diversity involved, the depth of historical grievances, the strength of civic traditions, and the willingness of political leaders to build bridges across communities.

Section 33.6

Nationalism and Democracy

The relationship between nationalism and democracy is not fixed. Nationalism has been both a friend and an enemy of democratic governance, and it remains a contested force within contemporary democratic systems.

Nationalism and democracy have historically reinforced each other in important ways. Democratic theory holds that the people are sovereign — that legitimate government derives from the will and consent of the governed. But who are the people? National community has historically provided the answer. The nation defines the demos — the collective self-governing body — that democratic institutions represent. Without some shared sense of belonging and political community, democratic governance becomes difficult. Nationalism, by providing that sense of shared community, can serve as a foundation for democratic solidarity.

Many of history's great democratic expansions were also nationalist in character. The American Revolution, the French Revolution, and the independence movements of the twentieth century all combined democratic demands for self-government with nationalist demands for freedom from foreign or dynastic rule. Anticolonial nationalism was a democratic force in the sense that it asserted the right of subjugated peoples to govern themselves rather than be governed by distant imperial powers.

At the same time, nationalism has repeatedly threatened democratic pluralism and rights. When nationalism defines the nation narrowly — in ethnic, racial, or religious terms — it creates insiders and outsiders within the political community. Minorities who are defined as outside the nation can be denied political rights, cultural recognition, and legal protection. The logic of national purity can justify suppressing minority languages, religions, and identities. Even in democratic systems without extreme ethnic nationalism, nationalist politics can undermine democratic pluralism by framing political disagreement as national disloyalty. If those who criticize the government are painted as enemies of the nation — traitors, foreign agents, or defenders of alien interests — then democratic debate becomes difficult. Democracy requires that political opponents be regarded as legitimate participants in shared governance, not as enemies to be defeated and silenced.

Contemporary democracies face challenges from nationalism both from within their societies and at their borders. Within societies, nationalist movements pressure governments to prioritize particular ethnic or cultural definitions of national identity, sometimes at the expense of minorities, immigrants, and diverse communities. At borders, nationalism shapes debates about immigration, trade, international agreements, and multilateral institutions. When nationalist leaders reject international commitments in the name of national sovereignty and the people's will, the question of how democracies balance national self-government and international cooperation becomes one of the defining policy debates of the era.

Institution in Practice
The European Union and the Limits of Supranational Governance

The European Union represents one of history's most ambitious experiments in governing across national boundaries. Created gradually after World War II from agreements among European states seeking to prevent future conflicts and build economic prosperity, the EU has evolved into a complex supranational organization with its own institutions, laws, courts, currency, and parliament. Its development raises fundamental questions about the relationship between nationalism, democracy, and international governance.

The EU was born from the conviction that European nationalism — particularly the extreme nationalist ideologies that produced two devastating world wars — needed to be tamed through economic integration and shared governance. The European project has been remarkably successful in this limited sense: no military conflict has occurred between EU member states since the organization's founding.

Over time, however, the EU's expansion of authority generated significant nationalist backlash. Critics argued that EU governance transferred decision-making from democratically accountable national governments to technocratic bureaucrats and unelected institutions in Brussels and Frankfurt. The "democratic deficit" — the perception that EU institutions lacked sufficient democratic accountability to citizens — became a major point of contention across member states.

The UK's decision to leave the European Union in 2016 — Brexit — was the most dramatic expression of this nationalist backlash. Supporters of Brexit argued that parliamentary sovereignty and democratic self-government required that the UK make its own laws without being subject to EU rules or the European Court of Justice. Opponents argued that the EU enhanced British economic prosperity, security, and international influence.

The Brexit experience illustrates several broader lessons. First, supranational governance faces inherent legitimacy challenges in a world where citizens identify primarily with national communities. Second, the promise of national sovereignty can be politically powerful even when its practical benefits are uncertain. Third, the design of democratic institutions — what level of government makes which decisions — has profound implications for political legitimacy and public trust. The EU's architects underestimated how much democratic legitimacy depended on national rather than supranational institutions in the minds of many European citizens.

Section 33.7

Nationalism and Conflict

The connection between nationalism and political conflict is well-documented in history and political science. Understanding why nationalism so frequently produces conflict — and under what conditions it does not — is important for students of both government and international relations.

Nationalism generates conflict potential through several mechanisms. First, multiple national groups often claim the same territory. When Serbs, Croats, Bosniaks, Slovenes, Albanians, and other peoples all claimed national rights over territories that they shared or that overlapped, political resolution proved impossible without devastating war. The dissolution of Yugoslavia in the 1990s produced civil wars precisely because national claims to territory were both passionately held and mutually incompatible. Second, minorities within nation-states are vulnerable to nationalist majorities. When a nationalist government defines the nation in ethnic terms that exclude minorities, those minorities may face discrimination, cultural suppression, or violence. Minority groups may respond by organizing their own nationalist movements, demanding autonomy or independence, or seeking external protection. Third, nationalism enables elites to mobilize mass support for foreign policy conflicts by framing them as threats to the national community. Leaders who face domestic difficulties sometimes turn to nationalist themes — foreign threats, external enemies, national humiliation — to redirect public attention and generate solidarity.

Fourth, nationalism interacts with historical grievances in ways that can sustain conflicts across generations. Communities that have experienced conquest, displacement, cultural suppression, or mass atrocity develop strong collective memories of historical injustice. These memories become politically salient when nationalist leaders invoke them to explain present difficulties or justify political demands. Resolving conflicts rooted in historical grievance requires not only political negotiation but also processes of historical acknowledgment, reconciliation, and in some cases transitional justice.

Despite this conflict potential, nationalism does not inevitably produce violent conflict. Switzerland has maintained a stable democracy among German, French, Italian, and Romansh speakers for centuries. Canada has accommodated Quebec nationalism through federalism, official bilingualism, and democratic negotiation. Spain has developed extensive regional autonomy for Catalonia, the Basque Country, and other regions. These cases demonstrate that nationalist tensions can be managed through institutional design and political will. The conditions that make nationalist conflict less likely include democratic institutions that give minorities meaningful political voice, federal or regional arrangements that allow diverse communities to govern themselves, constitutional protections for linguistic, religious, and cultural rights, civic education that builds understanding across communities, and political leadership willing to pursue accommodation rather than exploit divisions for short-term political gain.

Primary Source Window
Woodrow Wilson's Fourteen Points (1918)
Context

On January 8, 1918, United States President Woodrow Wilson addressed Congress to outline American war aims and his vision for a post-war international order. The address — delivered while World War I was still being fought — proposed fourteen points for a peace settlement. Several points dealt directly with the principle of national self-determination and became highly influential in reshaping the map of Europe after the war's end.

Paraphrase and Key Themes

Wilson called for a general association of nations to provide mutual guarantees of political independence and territorial integrity. He argued that national groups living under foreign rule deserved the right to determine their own political status. Peoples like Poles, Czechs, Slovaks, Serbs, and others living within the Ottoman, Austro-Hungarian, and Russian empires, Wilson implied, should have the opportunity to form their own states or at minimum receive meaningful autonomy. Wilson also insisted on open diplomacy, freedom of navigation, arms reduction, and the removal of barriers to international trade.

Analysis

Wilson's Fourteen Points had enormous consequences. The peace settlements after World War I broke up the German, Austro-Hungarian, Ottoman, and Russian empires and created or recognized more than a dozen new states in Europe and the Middle East on the basis — imperfectly applied — of national self-determination. However, the application was deeply uneven. The principle was applied primarily to peoples emerging from defeated empires, not to peoples living under Allied colonial rule in Africa, Asia, and the Middle East. The borders drawn in Paris could not cleanly separate mixed populations, leaving many national minorities inside the "wrong" state. Germany's humiliation in the peace settlement created resentments that nationalist leaders later exploited. The peace settlement intended to implement national self-determination contained the seeds of the next world war. Wilson's Fourteen Points illustrate both the power and the limits of national self-determination as a political principle: enormous moral appeal as a claim against imperial domination, but impossible to apply consistently and fairly across all competing national claims.

Questions for Students
  1. What claim did Wilson make about national self-determination, and why was it powerful at that historical moment?
  2. What institutions did Wilson propose to maintain peace in the new national order?
  3. What tensions or contradictions appear in the application of self-determination as Wilson described it?
Section 33.8

Populism in Comparative Perspective

Populism appears across many different political systems, economic contexts, and historical periods. Comparing populist movements across countries helps students understand what populism is and is not, why it emerges, what it achieves, and how democratic institutions respond to it.

In Latin America, populism has a long and complex history. Figures like Juan Perón in Argentina, Hugo Chávez in Venezuela, Evo Morales in Bolivia, and Andrés Manuel López Obrador in Mexico have combined anti-elite rhetoric with social policy, resource nationalism, and — to varying degrees — concentration of executive power and weakening of institutional checks. Latin American left populism has often mobilized poor and working-class communities, expanded social programs, and challenged established oligarchies, while simultaneously concentrating power in the executive and reducing institutional independence. The Venezuelan case under Chávez and his successor Nicolás Maduro illustrates how populist governance can slide from democratic reform to authoritarian control when institutions lack the strength to constrain executive ambitions.

In Europe, populism has appeared more prominently on the right in the twenty-first century, though significant left-populist movements emerged in Greece (Syriza), Spain (Podemos), and elsewhere. Right-wing populist parties — including the National Rally in France, Alternative for Germany, Fidesz in Hungary, Law and Justice in Poland (until its 2023 electoral defeat), and others — have mobilized voters concerned about immigration, cultural change, EU governance, and economic dislocation. The Hungarian government under Viktor Orbán represents a case study in how a populist government can systematically weaken judicial independence, press freedom, and electoral fairness while maintaining the form of democratic elections.

In the United States, populist themes have appeared across the political spectrum and throughout American history. The Populist Party of the 1890s mobilized farmers against railroad monopolies and banks. The Tea Party movement of the 2010s expressed anti-establishment conservative energy that eventually fed into the 2016 presidential campaign of Donald Trump. Trump's election combined anti-elite rhetoric, immigration restriction, skepticism of multilateral institutions, and direct attacks on free press and judicial independence. Bernie Sanders in the Democratic primary campaigns of 2016 and 2020 offered a left-populist challenge to economic elites and the Democratic Party establishment.

In South and Southeast Asia, populist movements have emerged in India under the Bharatiya Janata Party and Narendra Modi, combining Hindu nationalist identity politics with populist anti-establishment themes; in the Philippines under Rodrigo Duterte, who combined anti-elite rhetoric with authoritarian methods; and in Thailand, where populist politicians mobilized rural communities against Bangkok-based elites.

Comparing these cases reveals several patterns. Populism tends to emerge during periods of economic stress, cultural anxiety, and institutional delegitimation. Populist leaders are more likely to consolidate power when existing institutions are already weak, when political culture lacks strong norms of institutional independence, and when opposition parties and civil society organizations are fragmented or unpopular. Populism is more likely to be constrained when courts are strong and credible, when press freedom is genuinely protected, when civil society is vibrant, and when democratic norms have deep roots across political elites.

Civic Debate
Does Populism Strengthen or Weaken Democracy?

The Issue

Populism appeals to democratic ideals — popular sovereignty, accountability, government by and for the people. Yet political scientists have documented how populist movements, once in power, frequently weaken the institutional checks that protect democratic governance. Is populism fundamentally democratizing or fundamentally antidemocratic?

Argument A — Populism Strengthens Democracy by Forcing Accountability

Populism emerges when established institutions have become unresponsive to ordinary citizens and too close to powerful interests. When mainstream parties ignore working-class economic concerns, fail to address legitimate anxieties about immigration and cultural change, or become embedded in networks of elite privilege, populism gives voice to the politically marginalized. Populist challenges force established institutions to become more responsive, reduce corruption, and reconnect with the citizens they are supposed to serve. Left-wing populism in Europe and Latin America has sometimes expanded social programs, reduced inequality, and incorporated previously excluded communities into political life. Dismissing populism as ignorance or demagoguery ignores legitimate democratic deficits.

Argument B — Populism Threatens Democracy by Attacking Its Institutional Foundations

Democratic governance requires not only popular elections but institutions that protect minority rights, check executive power, maintain free press, and enforce constitutional rules. Populist leaders who present themselves as the direct voice of the pure people systematically attack these institutions — courts that rule against them, media that criticizes them, civil servants who resist politicization. The populist logic, once fully acted upon, produces elected authoritarianism rather than better democracy. The cases of Hungary, Venezuela, and Turkey illustrate how populist governments, elected through free elections, can dismantle the institutional protections that make future free elections possible. A democracy that abandons its institutional checks in favor of unmediated popular will is not a stronger democracy; it is a fragile one.

Other Perspectives

Some political theorists argue that the populist logic itself — the claim to represent a unified people against a corrupt elite — is always simplistic and potentially dangerous, regardless of whether it operates on the left or the right. Others argue that institutional responses matter more than condemnation of populism in the abstract.

Tradeoffs

The case for engaging seriously with populism is that ignoring genuine democratic deficits strengthens populist appeals. The case for defending institutions against populism is that populist attacks on courts, press, and civil service weaken the infrastructure of democratic self-correction. Democratic systems need to reform institutions to be more accountable while simultaneously protecting the institutional guardrails that prevent any single leader from consolidating unchecked power. Both things can be true simultaneously.

Civic Reasoning Question: A politician wins a national election by 60 percent of the vote, claiming a clear popular mandate to reform the judiciary by replacing politically appointed judges with judges directly elected by citizens. Should the legislature support this reform? Consider what democratic values support the proposal and what democratic values it might threaten.

Rights and Responsibilities

Civic Membership, Minority Rights, and Democratic Belonging

Democratic political communities face recurring tensions between the rights of majorities and the rights of minorities. Democratic majoritarian logic holds that collective decisions should reflect the preferences of the majority of citizens — elections are decided by more votes rather than fewer, and popular sovereignty means that the people govern themselves. The legitimacy of democratic outcomes rests significantly on this principle.

But unlimited majority rule conflicts with the rights of minorities. If majorities can do whatever they choose, they can vote to discriminate against racial or religious minorities, restrict minority voting rights, suppress minority languages, or exclude minorities from public life. A democracy that does these things retains the form of majority rule while abandoning the substance of democratic equality. Constitutional rights, judicial review, federalism, and minority protections are mechanisms for protecting minorities from majority tyranny — for ensuring that democratic systems serve the rights and dignity of all members, not only the most numerous.

The responsibilities of citizenship in a diverse democratic community extend in several directions. Majorities have a responsibility to respect the rights, dignity, and civic belonging of minorities — to recognize them as full members of the political community rather than outsiders to be tolerated at the majority's discretion. Minorities have a responsibility to engage with shared civic institutions and to pursue their claims through legitimate political channels. And all citizens share a responsibility to maintain the civic institutions — free press, independent courts, honest elections, civil service integrity — that make democratic governance possible regardless of which group holds power at any given moment. Nationalism can either strengthen or undermine these responsibilities depending on how it defines the political community.

Civic Vocabulary
Nativism

Nativism is the political preference for established or native-born inhabitants of a country over more recent immigrants or minorities perceived as foreign. Nativism typically involves the claim that the existing population has prior claims on the country's resources, culture, institutions, and political identity, and that immigration — particularly by culturally or racially distinct groups — threatens the nation.

Nativism is not the same as nationalism, though they frequently overlap. Nationalism is a broad political ideology about the relationship between national identity and political authority. Nativism is a more specific attitude toward immigration and minority groups within an existing political community. Nativist political movements have appeared throughout American history — the Know-Nothing party of the 1850s targeted Catholic immigrants from Ireland and Germany; anti-Chinese immigration laws of the late nineteenth century were explicitly nativist; the immigration restriction acts of the 1920s aimed to preserve the ethnic composition of the American population. Nativist themes also appear in European politics in debates about Muslim immigration, refugee resettlement, and integration policy.

Nativism raises important civic questions: Who belongs to the political community and on what terms? What obligations do governments have to their existing populations and to those seeking to join? How should democratic societies balance cultural continuity and openness to new members? These are legitimate policy questions. But nativism becomes problematic when it defines existing minorities — even legal citizens of long residence — as permanent outsiders, or when it relies on racial or religious stereotypes to evaluate the worth of prospective members.

Policy Lab
Managing Linguistic and Cultural Diversity in Public Schools

Scenario

A school district in a large metropolitan area has experienced rapid demographic change over the past decade. Approximately 40 percent of students now come from homes where languages other than English are spoken, including Spanish, Somali, Hmong, and Arabic. The district faces decisions about how to educate students still acquiring English fluency, how to maintain cultural connections for diverse communities, and how to build a shared civic culture across its increasingly diverse student body.

Stakeholders

  • Students who are English Language Learners (ELLs), who need instructional support in acquiring English while not falling behind in academic subjects.
  • Students' families, who have varied views on how much their children's home languages and cultures should be incorporated into schooling.
  • Teachers, who need training, resources, and time to serve diverse student needs.
  • Long-term residents and families who value the school community's existing cultural character.
  • Civic leaders who seek to build social cohesion and civic participation across diverse communities.

Policy Options

  1. English immersion: Place ELL students in standard English-language classrooms with additional ESL support but no native-language instruction. Emphasize rapid English acquisition as the primary goal.
  2. Transitional bilingual education: Provide initial instruction in students' native languages while gradually transitioning to English. Native-language instruction is a bridge to English, not a long-term feature.
  3. Dual-language immersion: Create programs in which both English-speaking and non-English-speaking students learn together in both languages, with the goal of bilingualism for all students.
  4. Multilingual and multicultural curriculum: Integrate diverse cultural content and linguistic perspectives throughout the curriculum for all students to build mutual understanding and recognition.
  5. Community partnership model: Involve diverse families and community organizations in curriculum design, cultural programming, and school governance.

Evidence Needed

Research on the effectiveness of different instructional approaches for English language acquisition; evidence on whether heritage language maintenance supports or hinders English acquisition; community survey data on the preferences and concerns of diverse families; budget and resource requirements for each option.

Tradeoffs

English immersion maximizes speed of English acquisition but may leave ELL students struggling academically during the transition and may signal to families that their languages and cultures are unwelcome. Dual-language programs can benefit all students and build genuine respect across communities but require significant resources. Community partnership models can build legitimacy and trust but may produce inconsistent educational outcomes if communities disagree.

Student Task

Select one of the policy options above, or develop a hybrid approach that combines elements of multiple options. Defend your recommendation by explaining which values you are prioritizing, what evidence would support your approach, and how you would address the most important tradeoffs and objections.

Chapter 33 — Back Matter

Common Misunderstandings

Misunderstanding

Nationalism is always extreme and always leads to conflict or violence.

Correction: Nationalism exists on a wide spectrum. Many stable democratic societies sustain strong national identities without producing ethnic conflict or authoritarian politics. The critical variable is how the nation is defined — inclusively or exclusively — and whether nationalist movements respect constitutional rights, minority protections, and democratic institutions. Nationalism that is civic and inclusive can coexist with democratic pluralism. Extreme ethnic nationalism that excludes and dehumanizes minorities creates conditions for conflict and violence.

Misunderstanding

Populism is simply demagoguery or ignorance dressed up in democratic language.

Correction: While populism can produce or enable demagoguery, it frequently reflects genuine political grievances about economic inequality, institutional unresponsiveness, and political exclusion. Dismissing all populism as ignorance prevents understanding of why it emerges and what legitimate concerns it expresses. The task for democratic political analysis is to distinguish between legitimate grievances that populism articulates and the simplistic or dangerous political responses it sometimes proposes.

Misunderstanding

Identity politics is a recent invention of progressive movements with no historical precedent.

Correction: Political organizing around shared social identities has occurred throughout the history of democratic politics. Religious identity, national identity, ethnic identity, class identity, and other forms of collective belonging have always shaped political coalitions, movements, and parties. The contemporary debate often focuses on particular progressive movements while ignoring long-standing forms of identity-based organizing — religious right movements, white ethnic political machines, agrarian populism — that were not labeled "identity politics" in the same critical way.

Misunderstanding

If a country holds elections, it cannot be moving toward authoritarianism.

Correction: Political scientists studying democratic backsliding have documented many cases where elected governments systematically weaken the institutions that make elections genuinely free and fair — independent courts, free press, civil service integrity, honest election administration — while continuing to hold elections. Hungary, Venezuela, Turkey, and Nicaragua are examples of countries that retained elections while reducing genuine democratic competition. Elections are necessary but not sufficient for democracy.

Misunderstanding

Civic nationalism and ethnic nationalism are completely separate things, and a political movement is clearly one or the other.

Correction: In practice, most nationalist movements combine civic and ethnic elements to varying degrees, and the balance can shift over time. A movement may use civic language — constitutionalism, rule of law, democratic self-government — while simultaneously mobilizing ethnic or religious identities in ways that functionally exclude minorities. Students should analyze both the formal claims of nationalist movements and the practical political effects they produce on minority groups and democratic institutions.

Misunderstanding

National self-determination means any group that calls itself a nation has the right to its own independent state.

Correction: International law and political practice recognize self-determination as a principle but have not translated it into an absolute right to secession for all groups. The international system generally protects existing state territorial integrity except in extreme cases of colonial rule, foreign occupation, or systematic oppression. The right of self-determination is more often interpreted as a right to meaningful political participation and cultural autonomy within existing states rather than as an unlimited right to independent statehood.

Misunderstanding

Multiculturalism means that all cultural practices must be accepted by democratic governments without question.

Correction: Democratic multicultural policies aim to support cultural diversity and protect minority cultural identities while still maintaining democratic civic norms — including protection of individual rights, equality before the law, and protection against discrimination and violence. Multicultural policies do not require governments to accept practices that violate fundamental rights. The challenge is defining which civic norms must be universally upheld and which cultural variations can be accommodated — a genuine and ongoing political debate.

Misunderstanding

Populist movements only appear in economically struggling or politically underdeveloped countries.

Correction: Populist movements have emerged in some of the world's wealthiest and most institutionally developed democracies, including the United States, France, Germany, the United Kingdom, and the Netherlands. Economic anxiety, cultural change, institutional distrust, and political exclusion can generate populist sentiment even in prosperous, well-governed societies. Populism's appeal depends not only on absolute economic conditions but on relative inequality, perceived political responsiveness, and cultural change — factors present in many established democracies.

Chapter Summary

  • Nationalism Nationalism is the political belief that people who share a national identity form a natural political community that deserves self-government. Modern nationalism emerged in the late eighteenth and nineteenth centuries alongside democratic revolutions and the collapse of dynastic empires. It contributed to both the creation of democratic nation-states and devastating wars. Nations are not natural facts but social and political constructions shaped by collective memory, cultural practice, political mobilization, education, and storytelling.
  • Civic vs. Ethnic The most important distinction within nationalism is between civic nationalism, which defines the nation in terms of shared political commitments and is in principle open to all who participate, and ethnic nationalism, which defines the nation in terms of ancestry, culture, or religion and can exclude legal minorities from full civic membership. In practice, most nations combine civic and ethnic elements, and the gap between civic ideals and ethnic practice is often politically significant.
  • Self-Determination The principle of national self-determination — that peoples deserve to govern themselves — became central to international law after World War I. Its application has been contested and imperfect. Many minorities ended up inside the "wrong" national state, and new states created their own minority problems. The tension between national self-determination and the protection of minorities within states has never been fully resolved in international law or political practice.
  • Populism Defined Populism is a political logic that frames politics as a conflict between a virtuous people and a corrupt elite. It is not a fixed ideology but a style that appears across the left-right spectrum in many different political systems. Populism resonates in conditions of economic inequality, institutional unresponsiveness, and cultural anxiety. It can give political voice to genuinely excluded communities. It can also threaten democratic institutions when populist leaders attack courts, press freedom, civil service independence, and electoral integrity in the name of the people's will.
  • Institutional Effects The outcome when populism gains power depends on the strength of existing institutions, the vitality of civil society, and whether political leaders maintain commitment to democratic norms. Populist leaders who follow the consolidation pattern — delegitimizing opposition institutions, attacking independent bodies, changing rules to increase executive power — can produce democratic backsliding: erosion of judicial independence, press freedom, and electoral integrity while maintaining the electoral form of democratic governance.
  • Identity Politics Identity politics refers to political organizing and advocacy organized around shared social identities. It has deep historical roots in democratic societies, from abolitionism to women's suffrage to civil rights movements. Contemporary identity politics reflects both the continuing reality of identity-based discrimination and the political demand for recognition — not only material resources but public acknowledgment of the dignity and worth of diverse communities. Critics worry about fragmentation of democratic solidarity; defenders argue that universal civic principles cannot substitute for addressing specific forms of identity-based discrimination.
  • Comparative Diversity Democratic societies have developed different institutional approaches to managing diversity — assimilation models, multicultural models, consociational power-sharing arrangements — each with different strengths and weaknesses. No single model works for all societies or all forms of diversity. The best approaches combine meaningful civic inclusion of diverse communities with strong shared democratic institutions, effective minority rights protections, and political leadership committed to building rather than exploiting divisions.
  • Nationalism and Democracy Nationalism has complex and ambivalent relationships with democracy. It provides the sense of shared community that makes democratic solidarity possible and has supported democratic independence movements against colonial and imperial rule. But when nationalism defines the nation narrowly in ethnic or religious terms, it can produce discrimination against minorities, vilification of political opponents, and hostility to the institutions of democratic pluralism. The key variables are how the nation is defined, whether minorities are protected, and whether nationalist movements respect constitutional limits on power.
  • Institutional Design Institutional design matters enormously. Federal systems that give diverse communities meaningful self-governance, constitutional rights that protect minorities, independent courts that enforce those rights, free press that holds power accountable, and civic education that builds understanding across communities — these are the specific institutional features that determine whether diverse democratic societies can sustain themselves over time against the centrifugal forces of division, grievance, and manipulation.
  • The Core Lesson Nationalism addresses real human needs for belonging, dignity, and political community. Populism addresses real problems of institutional unresponsiveness and democratic deficit. Identity politics addresses real experiences of discrimination and exclusion. The question is not whether these forces exist or have any legitimate basis, but how democratic systems can channel them toward democratic purposes rather than against the institutions and values that make democratic governance possible for all members of the political community.

Key Terms

Anti-establishment politics
Political movements or candidates that position themselves against existing political institutions, parties, and power structures, regardless of where they fall on the left-right spectrum.
Civic nationalism
A form of nationalism that defines the nation in terms of shared political commitments and civic values rather than ethnic ancestry or culture, treating national membership as open to all who participate in the political community.
Consociationalism
A form of power-sharing democracy designed for deeply divided societies, in which major communities are guaranteed representation and a share of executive power in government institutions.
Cultural politics
Political conflicts centered on values, identities, lifestyles, and cultural recognition rather than solely on economic interests or institutional arrangements.
Demagoguery
Political rhetoric that appeals to popular emotions, fears, prejudices, and grievances without regard for truth, deliberation, or democratic norms, typically in order to gain or maintain power.
Democratic backsliding
The gradual erosion of democratic institutions, norms, and practices in a formally democratic country, typically through executive overreach, attacks on independent institutions, and reduction of civil liberties and electoral fairness.
Ethnic nationalism
A form of nationalism that defines the nation in terms of shared ancestry, ethnicity, culture, language, or religion, treating national membership as primarily inherited rather than chosen.
Identity politics
Political organizing and advocacy organized around shared social identities such as race, ethnicity, gender, religion, sexual orientation, disability, or language.
Majoritarianism
A political principle or tendency that holds that the will of the majority should prevail without significant constraints from minority rights or institutional checks.
Minority rights
Legal and constitutional protections for individuals and groups that are numerically or politically disadvantaged within a larger political community, including protections against discrimination, cultural suppression, and political exclusion.
Multiculturalism
A policy approach that formally recognizes and accommodates cultural diversity within a political community, supporting the maintenance of distinct cultural identities rather than requiring assimilation to a single dominant culture.
Nation
A community of people who regard themselves as sharing a common identity — which may include shared history, language, culture, ancestry, religion, or territory — and who seek or hold political self-governance.
National identity
The set of characteristics, values, histories, and cultural practices that members of a nation regard as defining their collective identity as a people and distinguishing them from others.
National self-determination
The principle that peoples or nations have the right to determine their own political status and form of government without external domination, as articulated in the United Nations Charter and postwar peace settlements.
Nationalism
The political belief that people who share a national identity form a natural political community that deserves self-government, and that political authority should be aligned with national community.
Nativism
The political preference for established or native-born inhabitants of a country over more recent immigrants or minorities perceived as foreign, involving claims of prior rights to national resources, culture, and political identity.
Pluralism
A condition or principle of political life in which diverse groups, interests, identities, and values coexist and exercise political voice within a shared democratic system.
Populism
A political logic or style that frames politics as a conflict between a virtuous, unified people and a corrupt, self-serving elite, with the populist leader claiming to speak directly for the real people against established interests and institutions.
Recognition
The political demand that a group's existence, dignity, and distinct identity be publicly acknowledged by political institutions and fellow citizens, not merely tolerated or ignored — a central concept in contemporary identity politics.
Scapegoating
The political practice of assigning blame for social, economic, or political problems to a particular group — typically a minority or outsider group — regardless of actual responsibility, often to simplify complex problems and mobilize popular anger.
Social movement
A sustained collective effort by people organized around shared goals, typically seeking social or political change through protest, advocacy, organizing, and public pressure on institutions and leaders.

Document Analysis Activity

Document Analysis: The Civil Rights Movement and the Politics of Recognition
Connecting identity politics, civic nationalism, and the demand for recognition
Document Type and Context

In August 1963, civil rights leader Martin Luther King Jr. delivered his "I Have a Dream" speech at the March on Washington for Jobs and Freedom, before approximately 250,000 people gathered at the Lincoln Memorial. The speech came during a period of intense civil rights activism marked by mass demonstrations, police violence against protesters, and legislative debate over what would become the Civil Rights Act of 1964. It is one of the most significant political speeches in American history and a landmark document for understanding the relationship between identity politics and civic nationalism.

Key Themes for Analysis

King's speech simultaneously invoked universal American civic ideals — the Declaration of Independence's promise that all men are created equal, the Constitution's guarantees of rights and liberty — and specific claims on behalf of African Americans as a distinct group who had been systematically denied those promised rights. The speech argued that Black Americans were presenting a check to the "bank of justice" that America had long refused to honor. It expressed both a particular identity claim — rooted in the specific experience of racial discrimination — and a universal civic aspiration shared across racial lines.

Questions for Analysis
  1. How does the speech combine identity-based political claims with appeals to universal civic principles? Is this combination contradictory, or can you explain how the two work together in the logic of the speech?
  2. Does the March on Washington represent identity politics as the term is used today? What similarities and differences do you see between mid-twentieth century civil rights organizing and contemporary forms of identity-based political advocacy?
  3. The speech appeals to what King calls the American Dream and the promises of the founding documents. How does this appeal function within the framework of civic nationalism? What version of the national community does it invoke?
  4. Compare the rhetoric of this civil rights speech with what you have learned about populist rhetoric. How are they similar — both invoke the language of "the people" and their unfulfilled promises — and how are they different in their political logic and institutional implications?
  5. How does the concept of recognition, as discussed in Section 33.5, help explain what the civil rights movement was demanding beyond specific policy changes?

Chapter Closing Reflection

Nationalism, populism, and identity politics are among the most visible forces shaping political life in the twenty-first century. They appear in elections, legislative debates, court cases, international conflicts, and everyday civic conversations. Students who do not understand these forces will struggle to make sense of the political world around them. Students who understand them analytically — who can trace their historical roots, explain their institutional effects, compare them across countries, and evaluate their claims against democratic principles — will be far better equipped to participate in civic life as informed and responsible citizens.

The deepest lesson of this chapter may be about the relationship between belonging and democracy. Human beings need to belong — to communities, to histories, to identities that give life meaning. Politics responds to and shapes these needs for belonging. Nationalism, populism, and identity politics are all, in different ways, political responses to the human desire to be recognized, to have one's community honored, to participate in a political world that sees and includes you. Democratic institutions do not erase these desires; they channel them into forms of collective self-governance that can accommodate diversity, protect rights, and make binding decisions that citizens accept as legitimate.

When political movements exploit belonging for exclusionary or authoritarian purposes — when nationalism becomes ethnic persecution, when populism becomes elected authoritarianism, when identity politics becomes a vehicle for demagoguery — they pervert rather than fulfill the democratic promise. The study of government shows that these perversions are not inevitable. They can be resisted by strong institutions, active civil society, political leaders who honor democratic norms, and citizens who understand both the appeal and the dangers of political movements that promise simple answers to complex questions about who belongs, who has betrayed the people, and who deserves to be recognized.

Students are encouraged to bring the concepts, distinctions, and questions introduced here to their reading of news, history, political argument, and civic life. Every election, every movement, every policy debate about immigration, cultural diversity, economic inequality, or national identity can be illuminated by careful thinking about what nationalism, populism, and identity politics mean — and what they can become when democratic institutions are either strong enough to channel them constructively or too weak to prevent their most destructive possibilities. The next chapter, on Religion, Politics, and Moral Authority, will extend this inquiry into another domain where personal conviction and public authority intersect in ways that democratic systems must constantly navigate.

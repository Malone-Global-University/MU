Government

This textbook examines the foundations, institutions, rights, ideologies, policies, and comparative systems of government. Students study power, authority, citizenship, constitutions, lawmaking, civil liberties, political participation, public policy, international relations, and the major structures that shape modern political life.

Overview

Government is the study of how societies organize authority, make collective decisions, protect rights, distribute resources, and resolve conflict. This textbook begins with the core concepts of power, legitimacy, citizenship, law, sovereignty, and constitutional order before moving through political institutions, civil liberties, civil rights, democratic participation, political ideologies, public policy, international relations, global governance, and comparative political systems.

Learning Outcomes

  • Explain core concepts of government, including power, authority, legitimacy, sovereignty, law, and citizenship.
  • Analyze how constitutions, separation of powers, federalism, courts, executives, legislatures, and bureaucracies structure political authority.
  • Evaluate the role of civil liberties, civil rights, human rights, criminal justice, and equal protection in democratic and constitutional systems.
  • Compare electoral systems, political parties, interest groups, public opinion, media, and social movements as channels of political participation.
  • Assess major political ideologies including liberalism, republicanism, conservatism, socialism, libertarianism, nationalism, populism, and religion-based political authority.
  • Understand how public policy, international relations, global governance, comparative institutions, and democratic backsliding shape contemporary political life.

Contemporary America: Polarization, Inequality, Rights, and Democracy

United States History: Indigenous America, Republic, Conflict, and Change

Chapter Opening

"The United States in the twenty-first century remained, as it had always been, a nation unfinished — shaped by ideals and contradictions, by progress and resistance, by the perpetual contest over what kind of nation it would become."

On the morning of September 11, 2001, millions of Americans watched in disbelief as two commercial airplanes, hijacked by members of the al-Qaeda terrorist network, struck the twin towers of the World Trade Center in New York City. A third plane hit the Pentagon outside Washington, D.C. A fourth, United Airlines Flight 93, crashed in a field in Shanksville, Pennsylvania, after passengers struggled with the hijackers to prevent another strike on a national landmark. Nearly three thousand people died. They were office workers, firefighters, police officers, restaurant employees, flight attendants, passengers, and first responders. They came from more than ninety countries. In minutes, the United States had experienced the deadliest terrorist attack in its history, and the nation would not be the same again.

The attacks of September 11, 2001, opened a new era in American life. The federal government launched two wars — in Afghanistan and Iraq — that would last for decades. Congress passed sweeping new surveillance and law enforcement powers through the USA PATRIOT Act. A new federal agency, the Department of Homeland Security, reorganized the government's approach to domestic security. Airports, borders, schools, and public buildings changed. The country entered a prolonged debate about security versus civil liberties, about who could be trusted and who was suspect. Muslim Americans, Arab Americans, South Asian Americans, and Sikh Americans faced discrimination, surveillance, and violence. The wars overseas would claim thousands of American lives and hundreds of thousands of lives in Afghanistan, Iraq, and across the Middle East.

Yet the twenty-first century was not only defined by terrorism and war. It was shaped by economic catastrophe: the Great Recession of 2008–2009, the worst financial collapse since the Great Depression, wiped out trillions of dollars in household wealth, cost millions of Americans their homes, and reshaped the economy for years. The recession accelerated economic inequality that had been growing for decades, creating a United States in which the very wealthy captured an ever-larger share of national income while working families struggled with stagnant wages, rising costs, and uncertain employment. Economic anxiety fueled political volatility on both the left and the right.

The twenty-first century also brought a transformation in American politics. Barack Obama's election in 2008 as the first African American president marked a historic moment. His presidency generated both hope and fierce opposition. After Obama, Donald Trump's election in 2016 broke with nearly every political norm, reshaping the Republican Party around economic nationalism, hostility to immigration, and attacks on democratic institutions. Trump's presidency culminated in the January 6, 2021, assault on the United States Capitol by a mob seeking to overturn the results of the 2020 election — an event with few parallels in American history. Joe Biden's election in 2020 restored a more conventional presidency but did not resolve the deep polarization dividing the country.

Running through all of these developments was an accelerating transformation in communications technology. Social media platforms — Facebook, Twitter, YouTube, TikTok, and others — changed how Americans received information, formed communities, and engaged in politics. The collapse of local newspapers, the fragmentation of the media landscape, and the rise of algorithmic content delivery created an environment in which misinformation spread rapidly and Americans could increasingly live within information ecosystems that confirmed their existing beliefs. Democracy required a shared factual foundation; social media made that foundation harder to maintain.

Rights and social movements shaped the period as powerfully as political leadership. Movements for racial justice, LGBTQ+ equality, women's rights, immigrant rights, disability rights, and Indigenous sovereignty all gained strength in the twenty-first century, producing legislative victories, cultural changes, and fierce political resistance. The Supreme Court became a central battleground. Its decisions on voting rights, abortion, gun regulations, affirmative action, and executive power reshaped the legal landscape, often reversing precedents that had stood for decades. The court's 2022 decision in Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization, which overturned Roe v. Wade and eliminated the federal constitutional right to abortion, stood as one of the most consequential and contested decisions in generations.

Climate change emerged as perhaps the defining long-term challenge of the century. Scientific evidence for human-caused global warming was overwhelming, but political polarization made sustained policy responses difficult. Extreme weather events — hurricanes, wildfires, droughts, and floods — increased in frequency and severity. The COVID-19 pandemic of 2020–2022 exposed the vulnerabilities of the public health system and the consequences of political division in the face of a shared health crisis, killing more than a million Americans and disrupting every dimension of life.

This chapter examines the twenty-first century as a period of transformation, crisis, and contest. It follows the events and trends that define contemporary America not to deliver a verdict on the era but to help students understand how the long arcs of U.S. history — inequality and democracy, expansion and rights, nativism and pluralism, war and reform — continued to shape the nation into the present. The United States in the twenty-first century remained, as it had always been, a nation unfinished.

Historical Context

To understand the twenty-first century, students must understand the decades that shaped it. The United States that entered 2001 was the world's dominant military and economic power. The Cold War had ended a decade earlier with the collapse of the Soviet Union. The 1990s had brought relative prosperity, a technology boom, a budget surplus, and a sense among many Americans that history had settled into a stable liberal order. Yet beneath the surface, deep forces were building that would define the new century.

Economic inequality had been rising since the 1970s. The shift from a manufacturing economy to a service and finance economy, the decline of unions, the policy choices of the Reagan and post-Reagan eras, globalization, and technological change had all widened the gap between wealthy Americans and working-class Americans. By 2000, the United States had higher levels of income inequality than any other wealthy democracy. The gains of economic growth flowed disproportionately to those at the top.

The political system had become increasingly polarized. The ideological sorting of the two parties — with conservatives moving into the Republican Party and liberals into the Democratic Party — had accelerated since the 1960s and 1970s. The bitter partisan battles of the 1990s, including the Republican-led impeachment of President Bill Clinton in 1998, signaled that partisan conflict would intensify. The disputed presidential election of 2000, in which the Supreme Court halted the Florida recount and handed the presidency to Republican George W. Bush over Democrat Al Gore, deepened the sense of a politically divided nation.

Internationally, al-Qaeda, a Sunni Islamist terrorist network founded by Osama bin Laden, had declared war on the United States and carried out attacks against U.S. targets through the 1990s, including the 1998 bombings of American embassies in Kenya and Tanzania and the 2000 bombing of the USS Cole. Efforts to disrupt al-Qaeda had not succeeded, and the network operated from Afghanistan under the protection of the Taliban government. The environmental and scientific communities had been issuing increasingly urgent warnings about climate change since the 1980s, yet political will to address it had not kept pace with the scientific evidence. Into this world came the September 11 attacks, which transformed American politics, foreign policy, domestic security, civil liberties, and international relations for decades.

Section 48.1

September 11 and the War on Terror

The morning of September 11, 2001, began with clear skies over the eastern United States. By mid-morning, it had become the most catastrophic day on American soil since the Civil War. Nineteen hijackers affiliated with the al-Qaeda terrorist network took control of four commercial airplanes. Two planes struck the North and South Towers of the World Trade Center in lower Manhattan, causing both towers to collapse within hours. A third plane struck the western face of the Pentagon, killing 184 people. United Airlines Flight 93 crashed in a field near Shanksville, Pennsylvania, after passengers and crew members attempted to retake the aircraft. In total, 2,977 people were killed, not counting the hijackers.

Al-Qaeda had struck at symbols of American financial and military power in the heart of American cities. The attacks were spectacular in their ambition and devastating in their execution. Television networks broadcast the events live as they unfolded, meaning that millions of Americans watched in real time as the towers burned and fell. The images seared themselves into the national consciousness. For a generation of Americans, September 11 became a defining event, the way Pearl Harbor had been for an earlier generation.

President George W. Bush addressed the nation that evening, promising that the United States would make no distinction between the terrorists who had carried out the attacks and those who harbored them. This statement became the basis of the Bush Doctrine, which held that the United States had the right to act militarily against states that supported or provided sanctuary to terrorist organizations. Within weeks, Congress authorized the use of military force against the perpetrators of the attacks, and the Bush administration demanded that the Taliban government of Afghanistan surrender Osama bin Laden and dismantle the al-Qaeda network. When the Taliban refused, the United States launched military operations in Afghanistan in early October 2001.

At home, the attacks produced a sweeping reorganization of government and a transformation in domestic security. Congress passed the USA PATRIOT Act in October 2001 with overwhelming bipartisan support. The law expanded the ability of law enforcement and intelligence agencies to conduct surveillance of suspected terrorists, loosened restrictions on sharing information between agencies, and broadened the definition of terrorism. Civil liberties advocates warned that the law gave the government dangerous powers to monitor citizens without adequate oversight. Congress also created the Department of Homeland Security in November 2002, consolidating twenty-two federal agencies into a single cabinet department — the largest reorganization of the federal government since 1947. The Transportation Security Administration took over airport security, creating the intrusive screening procedures that became a permanent feature of air travel.

The attacks had devastating consequences for Muslim Americans, Arab Americans, South Asian Americans, and Sikh Americans. Despite having no connection to terrorism — and despite the fact that many were themselves victims of September 11 — these communities faced a dramatic wave of discrimination, harassment, assault, and murder in the weeks after the attacks. Mosques were vandalized. Individuals were attacked on the street. The federal government detained hundreds of Muslim men without charge or trial in the months after the attacks. A new climate of suspicion surrounded communities that had previously been integrated parts of American life, raising lasting questions about the cost of the War on Terror to civil liberties and civil rights at home.

The legal architecture of the War on Terror tested democratic norms and international law. The Bush administration created a detention facility at Guantanamo Bay, Cuba, to hold suspected terrorists outside the reach of U.S. courts. Detainees were held without charge, without trial, and without access to lawyers for years. The administration argued that suspected terrorists captured in war were unlawful combatants not protected by the Geneva Conventions or the U.S. Constitution. The Supreme Court repeatedly pushed back: in Hamdi v. Rumsfeld (2004), the Court held that even enemy combatants who were U.S. citizens were entitled to due process. In Boumediene v. Bush (2008), the Court held that Guantanamo detainees had a right to habeas corpus — the right to challenge their detention in federal court.

The administration's counterterrorism program also included the use of "enhanced interrogation techniques" — including waterboarding, sleep deprivation, and stress positions — against detainees at secret CIA facilities around the world. Military officers, intelligence professionals, and legal scholars described these techniques as torture that violated U.S. law and international treaties. A Senate Intelligence Committee report released in 2014 concluded that the CIA's interrogation program was brutal, ineffective, and based on false claims about its usefulness. The debate over torture, detention, and the legal rights of terrorism suspects became one of the most bitter moral controversies of the era.

September 11 and the War on Terror represented a pivotal moment in American history. They reshaped the government, the economy — defense spending surged — civil liberties, international relations, and the daily experience of millions of Americans, particularly Muslim and immigrant communities who bore the burden of suspicion and surveillance that the attacks produced. They also launched military commitments that would cost trillions of dollars and thousands of lives over the following two decades.

Section 48.2

Afghanistan, Iraq, and Military Power

The wars that followed September 11 defined the opening decades of the twenty-first century for American military personnel and for the populations of Afghanistan and Iraq. These wars were the longest in American history, consuming trillions of dollars, killing thousands of American service members and hundreds of thousands of civilians, and producing results far different from what their architects had promised.

Afghanistan: The Long War

The initial U.S. military campaign in Afghanistan in late 2001 achieved its immediate tactical objectives rapidly. Working with the Northern Alliance, U.S. forces, airpower, and special operations troops dismantled Taliban control of the country within weeks. But the primary target — Osama bin Laden — escaped. In December 2001, bin Laden and al-Qaeda fighters were cornered in the mountain caves of Tora Bora near the Pakistani border. A decision to rely primarily on local Afghan forces rather than committing larger numbers of U.S. troops to seal the border allowed bin Laden to escape into Pakistan, where he would live for nearly a decade before being killed by U.S. Navy SEALs in Abbottabad in May 2011.

The United States built a new Afghan government around Hamid Karzai. But the new government faced enormous challenges: decades of war had devastated the country; the society was divided by ethnicity, region, and warlord loyalty; the Taliban regrouped in Pakistan; corruption was endemic; and the U.S. commitment to the sustained nation-building that stability would have required was limited. The Taliban insurgency grew through the mid-2000s. President Obama ordered a surge of thirty thousand additional troops in 2009, adopting a counterinsurgency strategy aimed at protecting the Afghan population. The surge produced temporary security gains in some areas, but the Taliban proved resilient, and the fundamental problem — a government that never achieved the legitimacy or capacity to stand on its own — was never resolved.

The war lasted twenty years, from 2001 to 2021. President Trump negotiated a deal with the Taliban in 2020 committing the United States to full withdrawal. President Biden proceeded with the withdrawal in August 2021. The result was rapid collapse: the Afghan government and military disintegrated within weeks as Taliban forces swept across the country, entering Kabul on August 15, 2021. The United States evacuated approximately 124,000 people in a massive airlift, but chaotic scenes at the Kabul airport played out on global television. The Taliban returned to power in a country they governed much as they had before — banning women from schools and public life, suppressing political opposition. More than 2,300 American service members died. The United States spent more than two trillion dollars on the war. The twenty-year effort had not produced the stable, democratic Afghanistan that had been promised.

Iraq: The Case for War and Its Consequences

The war in Iraq was different in character from the war in Afghanistan. It was a war of choice, not necessity — and its justification proved to be false. The Bush administration argued that Saddam Hussein's government in Iraq possessed weapons of mass destruction and had connections to al-Qaeda. Secretary of State Colin Powell made the case for war to the United Nations Security Council in February 2003 in a presentation that included satellite images, recorded intercepts, and detailed claims about Iraqi weapons programs. The presentation was later shown to rest on false and unreliable intelligence. Powell himself called it a "blot" on his record.

The United States invaded Iraq in March 2003, leading a coalition that included Britain but notably excluded France, Germany, and most major world powers. The initial military campaign was swift: Saddam Hussein's government collapsed within weeks. On May 1, 2003, President Bush appeared on an aircraft carrier under a banner reading "Mission Accomplished," declaring that major combat operations were over. But the war was far from over. U.S. forces found no weapons of mass destruction. The postwar administration made catastrophic decisions: dissolving the Iraqi army, dismissing Ba'ath Party members from government, and imposing economic policies that produced mass unemployment and resentment. A complex insurgency emerged within months, and the country descended into sectarian violence.

By 2006, the situation had deteriorated into what many described as civil war. President Bush responded in 2007 by ordering a surge of approximately thirty thousand additional troops under the command of General David Petraeus, accompanied by an alliance with Sunni tribes who turned against al-Qaeda in Iraq — the "Anbar Awakening." Violence decreased significantly in 2007 and 2008. But the underlying political problems remained unresolved. President Obama withdrew combat troops in 2011, in accordance with an agreement the Bush administration had negotiated with Iraq. A residual U.S. training presence remained, but the withdrawal contributed to the rise of the Islamic State, a radical Islamist organization that emerged from al-Qaeda in Iraq and seized large areas of Iraq and Syria in 2013 and 2014, committing mass atrocities. A new U.S. military campaign against ISIS, including airstrikes and special forces deployments, largely defeated the organization territorially by 2019, though it continued to carry out attacks.

The Iraq War cost the United States approximately 4,500 military deaths, tens of thousands of wounded veterans, and an estimated two trillion dollars in direct costs — with total long-term costs, including veterans' care, projected far higher. Iraq suffered far more severely: estimates of civilian deaths range from 100,000 to 600,000 or more, with millions displaced. The war destabilized the Middle East, strengthened Iranian influence in the region, and damaged American credibility worldwide. It left a profound debate about the use of military power, intelligence failures, executive authority, and the role of the United States in the world.

Historical Vocabulary
Counterinsurgency

Counterinsurgency refers to military, political, and economic strategies designed to defeat armed non-state groups — insurgents — who are fighting against a government or occupying force within a civilian population. Effective counterinsurgency requires not only military force but also efforts to win the support of the local population, address the grievances that fuel the insurgency, build capable local security forces, and create legitimate governance.

U.S. military doctrine in both Afghanistan and Iraq adopted counterinsurgency approaches, with mixed results. The difficulty of counterinsurgency reflects the reality that armed conflict rarely succeeds without addressing the political conditions that cause it. Military victory does not automatically produce political stability, and armies cannot easily substitute for functioning institutions of governance.

Section 48.3

Great Recession and Economic Insecurity

While U.S. forces fought in Afghanistan and Iraq, the American economy experienced a catastrophic collapse that would reshape politics for years. The Great Recession of 2007–2009 was the worst financial crisis the United States had experienced since the Great Depression of the 1930s. It destroyed trillions of dollars in household wealth, cost millions of Americans their jobs and homes, and exposed deep vulnerabilities in the financial system.

The Housing Bubble and Financial Crisis

The crisis had its roots in the housing market and in financial innovations that had spread throughout the banking system. Through the late 1990s and 2000s, banks and mortgage lenders dramatically expanded the availability of home loans — including "subprime" mortgages made to borrowers with limited ability to repay them. These mortgages were bundled into complex financial instruments — mortgage-backed securities and collateralized debt obligations — and sold to investors around the world. Rating agencies gave these instruments high credit ratings, understating the risk. Banks and financial institutions borrowed heavily to invest in these instruments, building up enormous leverage.

When housing prices began to fall in 2006 and 2007, borrowers who had taken out adjustable-rate mortgages found their payments rising while their homes were worth less than they owed — a condition called being "underwater." Defaults rose. The value of mortgage-backed securities collapsed. In September 2008, the crisis reached its acute phase: Lehman Brothers, one of the largest investment banks, filed for bankruptcy. The federal government then was forced to rescue the insurance giant AIG, which had sold massive amounts of financial insurance tied to mortgage-backed securities. Congress passed the Troubled Asset Relief Program (TARP) in October 2008, authorizing up to $700 billion to stabilize the financial system.

The financial panic spread quickly to the broader economy. Credit markets froze. Businesses could not borrow. Consumers stopped spending. The unemployment rate rose from around 5 percent before the crisis to 10 percent by October 2009 — the highest level since the early 1980s. More than eight million jobs were lost. Home values fell by trillions of dollars nationally. Millions of Americans lost their homes to foreclosure — approximately 3.8 million foreclosure filings were made in 2010 alone. Retirement savings evaporated as the stock market fell by more than 50 percent from its peak.

The Response and the Uneven Recovery

Barack Obama took office in January 2009 in the middle of the crisis. The American Recovery and Reinvestment Act, a stimulus package of approximately $787 billion, combined tax cuts, direct spending on infrastructure and state aid, and investments in renewable energy, education, and technology. The legislation passed without a single Republican vote in the House, signaling the partisan environment in which the recovery would take place. The Federal Reserve, under Chairman Ben Bernanke, adopted extraordinary measures including cutting interest rates to near zero and purchasing large quantities of bonds through "quantitative easing."

The recovery was painfully slow. The technical recession ended in June 2009, but unemployment remained above 8 percent for years. Many workers who found jobs took lower-paying positions. Wages stagnated. Young workers entering the labor market faced particularly difficult conditions. The pattern of recovery was deeply unequal: the stock market rebounded sharply, benefiting those with investments, while working-class households and communities dependent on manufacturing or construction recovered much more slowly, if at all.

The Great Recession had deep political consequences. The bailout of major financial institutions — seen by many Americans as rewarding the bankers who had caused the crisis while ordinary homeowners lost everything — generated anger across the political spectrum. The Tea Party movement, emerging in 2009, drew heavily on this anger and pushed the Republican Party in a more populist, anti-establishment direction. On the left, the Occupy Wall Street movement of 2011 focused national attention on economic inequality, popularizing the language of the "99 percent" versus the "1 percent." Both movements reflected a deep sense that the political and economic system was rigged in favor of the wealthy and powerful — a sense that would fuel political upheaval for years to come.

Section 48.4

Obama Era and Changing Political Coalitions

Barack Obama's election on November 4, 2008, was a historic event in a nation whose history had been so deeply shaped by race. The son of a Kenyan father and a white mother from Kansas, raised in Hawaii and Indonesia, educated at Columbia and Harvard Law School, Obama had served as a community organizer, state senator, and U.S. senator from Illinois before his presidential campaign. His campaign, built on a message of hope and change and powered by remarkable grassroots fundraising and digital organizing, drew unprecedented enthusiasm, particularly among young voters, African Americans, and college-educated professionals. Obama carried states that had voted Republican for decades, including Virginia, North Carolina, and Indiana, winning 365 electoral votes and the popular vote by 7.2 percentage points.

Healthcare Reform

Obama's first term was defined largely by the effort to pass healthcare reform. The United States was the only wealthy industrialized nation without universal health insurance coverage, with approximately 46 million Americans uninsured when Obama took office. The Affordable Care Act, passed in March 2010 after a year of bitter legislative struggle, created insurance exchanges where individuals could purchase plans with subsidies, expanded Medicaid to cover more low-income adults, required insurers to cover people with pre-existing conditions, allowed young adults to remain on parents' insurance until age 26, and was projected to extend coverage to more than 30 million previously uninsured Americans.

The ACA became intensely politically contested. Every Republican in Congress voted against it. Republican-controlled states challenged it in the courts. The Supreme Court, in National Federation of Independent Business v. Sebelius (2012), upheld the individual mandate as a constitutional exercise of Congress's taxing power, while ruling that the Medicaid expansion could not be mandatory for states. Many Republican-governed states initially opted out, leaving millions of low-income adults without coverage. The ACA became a symbol of partisan polarization, with Republicans vowing repeal and Democrats defending it as a landmark achievement.

Race and the Obama Presidency

The Obama presidency raised profound questions about race in America. His election was celebrated as evidence that the country had transcended its racial history. His own presidency, however, demonstrated that structural racism remained deeply embedded in American society. The killing of Trayvon Martin, an unarmed Black teenager, by George Zimmerman in Sanford, Florida, in 2012, and Zimmerman's acquittal in 2013, prompted Alicia Garza, Patrisse Cullors, and Opal Tometi to create the Black Lives Matter movement. The killing of Michael Brown in Ferguson, Missouri, in 2014 — and the subsequent protests, the militarized police response, and the grand jury decision not to indict the officer — brought racial injustice and policing to the center of national debate.

Obama was also attacked by a "birther" movement — which falsely claimed he was not born in the United States and therefore was not a legitimate president — that drew support from millions of Americans and was promoted most prominently by Donald Trump. Obama left office in January 2017 with an approval rating in the high fifties. He had presided over an economic recovery that, while slow and unequal, had brought unemployment from 10 percent to 4.7 percent, had extended health insurance to approximately 20 million previously uninsured Americans, and had ended U.S. combat operations in Iraq. But he had not resolved — and in some ways had deepened — the political polarization that would define the years that followed.

Section 48.5

Trump Era, Populism, and Polarization

Donald Trump's election in November 2016 was one of the most unexpected and consequential events in modern American political history. A real estate developer and television personality with no government experience, Trump had defeated sixteen experienced Republican primary candidates and then defeated Hillary Clinton, the Democratic nominee, former secretary of state, senator, and first lady. Trump won the Electoral College 306 to 232 while losing the popular vote by nearly three million votes — a result that immediately became a source of partisan division.

The Rise of Trump

Trump's rise was rooted in forces that had been building in American society for years. Economic anxiety — particularly among white working-class voters in economically distressed communities — was central. Deindustrialization had hollowed out manufacturing regions, opioid addiction had devastated rural communities, wages had stagnated for workers without college degrees, and the Great Recession had left lasting scars. These communities felt ignored by both political parties. Cultural anxiety was equally important: demographic change, the legalization of same-sex marriage in 2015, debates about transgender rights, and the perceived dismissal of traditional religious and cultural values by coastal elites contributed to a sense among some voters that their country was changing in ways they had no voice in shaping.

Trump's campaign was built on a form of populism that identified a corrupt elite — financial, political, and media — as the enemy of ordinary Americans. His slogan, "Make America Great Again," implied that the nation had been degraded and needed restoration. He promised to build a wall on the southern border, to ban Muslim immigration, to renegotiate trade deals, to withdraw from international agreements, and to "drain the swamp" of Washington corruption. His rhetoric was frequently inflammatory and contemptuous of democratic norms. He attacked journalists as "enemies of the people," called political opponents by insulting nicknames, and encouraged supporters to view opposition not merely as mistaken but as treasonous.

The Trump Presidency

Trump's presidency reshaped American governance and political culture in fundamental ways. He withdrew the United States from the Paris Climate Agreement, the Trans-Pacific Partnership, the Iran nuclear deal, and the World Health Organization. He imposed tariffs on goods from China, Canada, Mexico, and Europe, launching trade disputes that raised costs for consumers and producers. He cut corporate taxes through the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017. He appointed three conservative justices to the Supreme Court — Neil Gorsuch, Brett Kavanaugh, and Amy Coney Barrett — fundamentally shifting the court's ideological direction for a generation. His administration pursued aggressive immigration enforcement, separating migrant children from their parents at the southern border, sharply reducing refugee admissions, and attempting to end DACA. He was impeached twice by the House of Representatives: first in December 2019 for pressuring Ukraine's president to announce an investigation of Joe Biden in exchange for military aid; and second in January 2021 for incitement of insurrection. He was acquitted by the Senate both times.

January 6, 2021

The attack on the United States Capitol on January 6, 2021, was the most serious assault on the peaceful transfer of presidential power in American history. After losing the 2020 election to Joe Biden — a loss confirmed by state election officials, federal courts, and the Electoral College — Trump refused to concede and promoted the false claim that the election had been stolen through widespread fraud. Courts dismissed more than sixty lawsuits challenging the results. The Department of Justice found no evidence of fraud sufficient to change the outcome. Every state certified its results.

On January 6, as Congress met to certify the Electoral College results, thousands of Trump supporters gathered in Washington. At a rally near the White House, Trump repeated his false claims about the election and urged the crowd to march to the Capitol. A mob broke through police lines, smashed windows, and invaded the Capitol building. Members of Congress hid under desks or were evacuated through tunnels. Rioters searched for Vice President Mike Pence, whom Trump had publicly blamed for refusing to reject Biden's victory. Officers and rioters clashed. Seven people died in connection with the events of that day. Hundreds of rioters were later charged and convicted.

The attack was a profound test of American democratic institutions. Those institutions held: the Capitol was secured, Congress reconvened that night, the results were certified in the early morning hours of January 7, and Biden was inaugurated on January 20, 2021. But the attack revealed the depth of the threat that political violence and the rejection of election results posed to democracy, and it revealed the degree to which a significant portion of the Republican Party had aligned itself with Trump's refusal to accept democratic outcomes.

Historical Debate

Was January 6 a Unique Threat to American Democracy?

The question: Historians and political scientists have debated whether the January 6, 2021, attack represented a genuinely unprecedented threat to American democracy, or whether it was a serious but ultimately manageable crisis that democratic institutions successfully weathered.

Interpretation A — A Genuine Democratic Crisis: January 6 represents an unprecedented threat — the first time in U.S. history that a sitting president encouraged an attempt to prevent the peaceful transfer of power. The attack was not a spontaneous protest; it was the product of a sustained, coordinated effort to overturn a free and fair election. The willingness of a major political party's base to reject election results it dislikes, and the failure of most Republican elected officials to clearly condemn the attack, signals a deep corrosion in the democratic norms that make self-government possible. Scholars of democratic backsliding point to January 6 as evidence that the United States is experiencing a crisis of democratic erosion from within.
Interpretation B — Democratic Institutions Proved Resilient: Democratic institutions proved strong enough to withstand January 6. Courts rejected Trump's legal challenges. State officials, including Republican ones, certified accurate results. The Capitol was secured. The certification proceeded. Biden was inaugurated peacefully. Historians of American political violence point out that the United States has survived far more severe crises — the Civil War, political assassinations, race riots, the internment of Japanese Americans — and emerged with democratic institutions intact. From this view, January 6 was serious, but it does not necessarily mark a permanent turning point toward democratic collapse.

Evidence: Evidence for the first interpretation includes widespread Republican support for Trump's false claims, the refusal of most Republican officials to clearly condemn his role, and the passage in Republican-controlled states of election laws critics argued would make future elections easier to manipulate. Evidence for the second interpretation includes the functioning of courts and election officials, the certification of Biden's victory, the peaceful inauguration, and the subsequent conviction of hundreds of rioters.

Why it matters: How historians assess January 6 has implications for how citizens and policymakers understand current threats to democracy and what responses are appropriate. If it was an aberration that institutions contained, modest reforms may suffice. If it was evidence of deep democratic erosion, more fundamental changes may be needed.

Section 48.6

Biden Era and Institutional Conflict

Joe Biden, who had served as a senator from Delaware for decades and as vice president under Obama, entered the presidency at age 78 — the oldest person ever inaugurated as president. His campaign had been built primarily around the argument that Trump was an existential threat to American democracy and that Biden's experience, temperament, and decency made him the right candidate to restore normalcy. Biden won the popular vote by more than seven million votes, and his victory was confirmed by every state, certified by the Electoral College, and affirmed by more than sixty court decisions.

Biden's first year produced a substantial legislative record. The American Rescue Plan, passed in March 2021, provided $1.9 trillion in economic relief: direct payments to households, expanded unemployment benefits, support for schools and local governments, and funding for vaccine distribution. The relief package contributed to rapid economic recovery — unemployment fell sharply — but also contributed to inflation, which rose to levels not seen since the early 1980s. The Federal Reserve responded by raising interest rates sharply beginning in 2022, slowing the economy and raising borrowing costs for households and businesses.

In August 2021, Biden signed the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act — a bipartisan bill providing approximately $550 billion in new spending on roads, bridges, broadband, water systems, the electrical grid, and public transit. The Inflation Reduction Act, passed in August 2022, directed approximately $369 billion toward climate and energy investments — the largest climate policy action in U.S. history. The CHIPS and Science Act of 2022 provided over $50 billion to support domestic semiconductor manufacturing. Together, these represented a significant expansion of federal investment in the physical and technological infrastructure of the American economy.

Russia's invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 became a defining foreign policy challenge. Biden rallied NATO allies to support Ukraine with weapons, economic aid, and sanctions against Russia — widely seen as effective multilateralism and a restoration of the alliance-based foreign policy that Trump had eroded. The Israeli military campaign in Gaza that followed the Hamas attacks of October 7, 2023, created significant tension within Biden's coalition, particularly among younger voters and Muslim Americans who called for a ceasefire and criticized U.S. support for Israeli military operations.

In July 2024, Biden made the extraordinary decision to withdraw from the presidential race, citing concerns about his ability to campaign effectively. Vice President Kamala Harris became the Democratic nominee — the first woman and first person of South Asian descent nominated for president by a major party. Harris lost to Trump in November 2024, and Trump was inaugurated for a second term in January 2025, the first person since Grover Cleveland to serve non-consecutive presidential terms. The second Trump term brought renewed efforts to reshape the federal government, reduce immigration, roll back regulations, and assert expanded executive power.

Section 48.7

Social Media, Misinformation, and Public Debate

Few developments have transformed American political and social life in the twenty-first century as profoundly as the rise of social media. The platforms that emerged in the 2000s and 2010s — Facebook (2004), YouTube (2005), Twitter (2006), Instagram (2010), TikTok (2016 in China, 2018 in the United States) — created entirely new ways for people to communicate, organize, consume news, and engage with political life. Their effects have been profound, complex, and deeply contested.

The Transformation of Information

Social media arose in a context of deep structural change in the traditional news industry. The newspaper business, already challenged by the internet's disruption of classified advertising, collapsed in the 2000s and 2010s. Thousands of local newspapers closed. Newsrooms shrank dramatically. The number of newspaper journalists fell by more than 57 percent between 2008 and 2020. Communities lost local reporting on city councils, school boards, county courts, and state legislatures — the kind of watchdog journalism that had held local government accountable for generations.

Into this vacuum came social media. But social media platforms were built around fundamentally different incentives than traditional journalism. Their business models depended on advertising revenue tied to user engagement. The algorithms that determined what content users saw were optimized for engagement, not accuracy. And research consistently showed that emotionally provocative content — outrage, fear, disgust — generated more engagement than calm, informative content. A landmark 2018 study published in Science found that false news stories spread on Twitter six times faster than true ones, and reached far more people. This was not primarily the product of automated bots; it was the product of human sharing behavior. False stories were more novel, more emotionally engaging, and more likely to be shared than accurate, nuanced reporting.

Political Consequences

The political consequences of the social media information environment were significant. Partisan media ecosystems — in which Republicans and Democrats consumed largely non-overlapping sets of sources and lived in largely separate information worlds — became more entrenched. Conspiracy theories gained unprecedented reach and influence. The QAnon conspiracy theory reached millions of Americans through social media, with devastating effects on families and, in some cases, motivating violence. Anti-vaccine misinformation spread widely and contributed to vaccine hesitancy that cost lives during the COVID-19 pandemic. Election fraud conspiracy theories, amplified by Trump and his allies, convinced millions of Americans that the 2020 election had been stolen — a false belief with real democratic consequences including January 6.

Foreign interference through social media added another dimension. The U.S. intelligence community concluded that Russia had conducted an information warfare campaign in the 2016 election, using fake accounts and targeted advertising on social media to inflame political divisions and promote Trump's candidacy. Social media companies struggled to detect and remove foreign influence operations. When Elon Musk purchased Twitter in 2022 — renaming it X — and reinstated accounts that had been banned for policy violations, including Trump's account, the move was celebrated by conservatives who argued that the previous policies amounted to political censorship and condemned by liberals who argued that it removed important guardrails against harmful content. The debate illustrated the degree to which control of information infrastructure had become a political battleground.

Section 48.8

Racial Justice, Immigration, Gender, and Rights Debates

The twenty-first century was a period of intense struggle over rights — over who was included in the American community, who was protected by law, and how the constitutional commitments of the nation applied to previously excluded or marginalized groups. These struggles produced major gains and intense backlash, often simultaneously.

Racial Justice and Policing

The killing of unarmed Black Americans by police officers became a recurring crisis in the twenty-first century, catalyzing mass protests and a national reckoning with the history and present of racial inequality. The pattern was documented in videos captured on smartphones and surveillance cameras, giving the public a direct view of incidents that might previously have been invisible. George Floyd's death on May 25, 2020 — pinned under the knee of Minneapolis police officer Derek Chauvin for more than nine minutes while bystanders pleaded with officers to stop — sparked the largest racial justice protests in American history. Protests erupted in hundreds of cities across the United States and in dozens of countries around the world. The diverse, multiracial character of the protests was notable. Chauvin was convicted of murder in 2021 and sentenced to more than twenty years in prison — an unusually strong accountability outcome in a system that historically had rarely held officers criminally responsible for killing Black people.

The Movement for Black Lives pressed for systemic changes beyond policing: addressing housing segregation, educational inequality, wealth disparities, the criminal justice system, environmental racism, and other structural dimensions of racial inequality. The 1619 Project, a New York Times initiative arguing for centering slavery and its consequences in understanding American history, sparked national debate and became a target of legislation in Republican-controlled states that sought to restrict how race and American history were taught in schools.

Immigration

Immigration remained one of the most contested political issues of the twenty-first century. The approximately eleven million undocumented immigrants in the United States — many of them long-term residents with U.S.-citizen children and deep community roots — lived in legal limbo, subject to deportation and ineligible for most federal programs. The Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals (DACA) program, created by Obama in 2012, provided temporary protection and work authorization to approximately 800,000 young people who had been brought to the United States as children. The program was subject to ongoing legal challenges throughout the decade.

Migration at the southern border increased substantially through the late 2010s and early 2020s, driven by violence, poverty, climate change, and economic instability in Central America, South America, and other regions. The immigration system struggled to process large numbers of asylum seekers, and the legal backlog in immigration courts grew to more than three million cases. The crisis at the border became a persistent political issue, with Republicans arguing that the system required much stricter enforcement and Democrats arguing that root causes of migration needed to be addressed and that the asylum system needed to be strengthened and adequately funded.

LGBTQ+ Rights

The LGBTQ+ rights movement achieved remarkable gains in the twenty-first century. The Supreme Court's ruling in Lawrence v. Texas (2003) struck down state laws criminalizing same-sex sexual activity. The Court's decision in Obergefell v. Hodges (2015) held that the Constitution's guarantee of equal protection and due process required states to recognize same-sex marriages — a decision that was the product of decades of organizing, litigation, cultural change, and shifting public opinion. Polls showed that support for same-sex marriage had risen from around 27 percent in 1996 to more than 60 percent by 2015.

Transgender rights became a major site of political conflict in the 2010s and 2020s. Republican-controlled legislatures passed numerous laws restricting transgender people's access to medical care, sports, bathrooms, and legal documentation. These battles were fought in state legislatures, federal courts, schools, and hospitals, involving the most intimate questions of identity, medical judgment, parental authority, and civil rights. The Respect for Marriage Act, passed by Congress in December 2022, codified federal recognition of same-sex and interracial marriages — a response to concerns that the Supreme Court, after overturning Roe v. Wade, might reconsider other rights established through judicial interpretation rather than legislation.

Women's Rights and the Dobbs Decision

The Supreme Court's 2022 decision in Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization overturned Roe v. Wade (1973) and Planned Parenthood v. Casey (1992), eliminating the federal constitutional right to abortion. The decision returned the question to individual states, with immediate and dramatic consequences: more than a dozen states moved quickly to ban or sharply restrict abortion. In states with bans, women with wanted pregnancies that had gone wrong medically reported that providers, fearing criminal prosecution, were reluctant to perform procedures necessary to save their lives. Rape and incest survivors were forced to carry pregnancies to term in some states. The burden fell most heavily on low-income women who could not afford to travel to states where abortion remained legal.

The Dobbs decision provoked immediate political backlash. Voters in multiple states — including several with Republican majorities — approved ballot measures protecting abortion rights, signaling that the public was significantly more supportive of abortion access than the court's ruling suggested. The decision became a central mobilizing issue for Democrats in the 2022 midterm elections, contributing to Democratic performance that exceeded most predictions in what might otherwise have been a Republican wave year.

Multiple Perspectives

The Dobbs Decision and Abortion Rights

From the perspective of anti-abortion advocates and religious conservatives: The Dobbs decision was a long-awaited correction of a constitutional error. Roe v. Wade had imposed a policy that had no legitimate basis in the Constitution's text or history, overriding democratic decisions by state legislatures. The decision appropriately returned the question to elected representatives. For the tens of millions of Americans who believe life begins at conception, the reversal of Roe was a moral imperative, not merely a political preference.
From the perspective of reproductive rights advocates and many women: The Dobbs decision removed a fundamental right that women had relied on for fifty years, with immediate and severe consequences for health, safety, and autonomy. Medical providers in ban states, fearing criminal prosecution, hesitated to perform procedures necessary to save lives. The burden fell most heavily on low-income women who could not travel for care. The decision imposed the religious and moral views of a minority on the entire country, reversing decades of settled precedent.
From the perspective of legal scholars debating constitutional interpretation: The constitutional question was whether the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment protects rights not explicitly listed in the Constitution's text. The court's prior jurisprudence had recognized such "substantive due process" rights in areas from contraception to marriage. The Dobbs majority argued that such rights must be "deeply rooted in this Nation's history and tradition." Critics argued that the majority's historical analysis was selective and that the decision's logic could threaten other rights resting on similar constitutional foundations, including contraception and same-sex marriage.
Section 48.9

Climate, Public Health, and Economic Inequality

Three structural challenges confronted the United States in the twenty-first century that demanded sustained policy responses but that political polarization made extremely difficult to address: climate change, public health, and economic inequality. Each represented not a single crisis but a systemic condition with long-term consequences for the well-being of the American people and the planet.

Climate Change

The scientific evidence for human-caused climate change was overwhelming by the early twenty-first century. Average global temperatures had risen approximately 1.1 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels by the early 2020s. Arctic sea ice was declining. Sea levels were rising. Extreme weather events — including hurricanes, wildfires, droughts, floods, and heat waves — were increasing in frequency and severity. Hurricane Katrina in 2005 killed more than 1,800 people and caused catastrophic flooding in New Orleans. Superstorm Sandy in 2012 flooded lower Manhattan and devastated coastal communities in New York and New Jersey. Catastrophic wildfires burned millions of acres in California, Oregon, and Washington in the 2010s and 2020s. Texas experienced a catastrophic winter storm in February 2021 that killed hundreds and caused widespread power outages.

Climate change disproportionately affected low-income communities and communities of color — groups with fewer resources to prepare for or recover from disasters, and that were often located in more environmentally vulnerable areas. Indigenous nations faced particular threats: rising seas threatened coastal communities in Alaska; drought threatened agricultural communities in the Southwest; warming threatened the ecosystems on which Indigenous food systems and spiritual practices depended.

The United States took inconsistent policy steps. The Obama administration negotiated the Paris Climate Agreement in 2015, committing nations to reducing emissions with the goal of limiting warming to 1.5 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels. Trump withdrew the United States from the agreement in 2017; Biden rejoined it in 2021. The Biden administration's Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 represented the largest investment in climate policy in U.S. history, directing approximately $369 billion over a decade toward clean energy, electric vehicles, building efficiency, and climate resilience. But the scale of the investment, while historically significant, fell short of what many scientists argued was necessary to meet the goals of the Paris Agreement.

COVID-19 Pandemic

The COVID-19 pandemic, caused by the SARS-CoV-2 coronavirus, emerged in Wuhan, China, in late 2019 and spread globally in early 2020. The World Health Organization declared a pandemic in March 2020. Within weeks, most of the United States had shut down: schools closed, businesses shuttered, hospitals filled, and social life came to a halt. The federal government's response under the Trump administration was widely criticized as slow, disorganized, and characterized by mixed messages on public health measures.

The economic consequences were severe: the unemployment rate rose to nearly 15 percent in April 2020 before stimulus payments and other measures cushioned the blow. Lower-wage workers in service industries, food service, and retail bore the greatest burden. Higher-income workers who could work remotely were far better insulated. Vaccines developed at unprecedented speed through the Operation Warp Speed program, receiving emergency authorization in December 2020. The Biden administration orchestrated a massive vaccination campaign in 2021. But vaccine hesitancy — fed by misinformation on social media and strongly correlated with partisan identity — meant that tens of millions of Americans refused or delayed vaccination, contributing to continued transmission and preventable deaths.

The pandemic killed more than one million Americans by 2022 and exposed and exacerbated racial and economic health disparities. Black, Latino, and Native American communities experienced significantly higher rates of infection, hospitalization, and death, reflecting pre-existing inequalities in healthcare access, housing density, and the ability to work from home. The pandemic also accelerated existing trends — toward remote work, e-commerce, and the restructuring of urban commercial real estate — with lasting effects on how and where Americans worked and lived.

Economic Inequality

Economic inequality remained one of the defining challenges of the twenty-first century. By the 2020s, the United States had the highest level of income and wealth inequality among wealthy democracies. The wealthiest one percent of Americans held approximately 38 percent of all household wealth. The bottom fifty percent held approximately 2 percent. The causes were complex and debated — the decline of union membership, globalization, technological change, tax policies, and corporate consolidation all played roles. The consequences were felt across American life.

Life expectancy, which had been rising for most of the twentieth century, began declining among less-educated white Americans in the 2010s — driven by what researchers Anne Case and Angus Deaton called "deaths of despair": drug overdose, suicide, and alcoholism, concentrated in economically distressed communities. The opioid epidemic, which began with the overprescription of legal opioids and evolved into a crisis of heroin and fentanyl addiction, killed hundreds of thousands of Americans and devastated communities across the country. Unequal access to quality education, healthcare, housing, and economic opportunity reproduced inequality across generations, limiting social mobility in ways that contradicted the American ideal of merit-based advancement.

Cause and Effect

The COVID-19 Pandemic

Long-Term Causes

Decades of underinvestment in public health infrastructure; fragmented healthcare system lacking universal coverage; rising vaccine hesitancy fed by online misinformation; structural racial and economic health disparities; globalized travel and trade enabling rapid spread of novel pathogens.

Short-Term Causes

Emergence of SARS-CoV-2 in late 2019, likely through zoonotic transmission; limited initial testing capacity; delayed public health response; inadequate stockpiles of protective equipment; inconsistent and politicized federal messaging.

The Event

The COVID-19 pandemic killed more than one million Americans between 2020 and 2022. It caused the largest economic disruption since the Great Depression, closed schools for months, strained healthcare systems, and deepened political polarization over basic public health measures.

Effects

Massive short-term job losses and recession; rapid vaccine development and deployment; acceleration of remote work and e-commerce; lasting "Long COVID" health effects for millions; intensified partisan polarization over public health authority; transformation of education; continued debate over preparedness for future pandemics.

Historical complexity: The pandemic cannot be reduced to any single cause. The pathogen was necessary but not sufficient. Political choices, pre-existing health disparities, social media misinformation, and the specific vulnerabilities of the American healthcare and economic systems all shaped the outcomes — and might have produced significantly different results under different conditions.

Section 48.10

Final Review and Contemporary Democracy Reflection

Continuity and Change Over Time

American Democracy from the Founding to the Present

What Changed

  • The franchise expanded from propertied white men to all citizens regardless of race, gender, or language
  • The federal government grew from a small administrative apparatus to a massive institution touching nearly every aspect of American life
  • Communications technology transformed from pamphlets and town meetings to instantaneous global digital networks
  • The United States became the world's dominant military and economic power
  • The economy shifted from agricultural and artisanal production through industrial capitalism to a post-industrial service and information economy
  • Major social movements achieved legal recognition of rights for African Americans, women, LGBTQ+ individuals, and people with disabilities

What Stayed the Same

  • Persistent tensions between federal and state power; between liberty and equality; between democratic self-government and protection of individual rights from majorities
  • The tension between inclusion and exclusion — debates over who belongs in the American community — never fully resolved
  • Economic inequality, far from being a new problem, was a feature of American society from the colonial era onward
  • Structural racial inequality, despite significant changes in law and culture, persisted in outcomes across income, wealth, health, education, and criminal justice

Why this pattern matters: Understanding continuity and change helps resist two temptations: the temptation to see American history as inevitable progress, and the temptation to see it as permanent oppression with no room for meaningful change. Both are distortions. The history shows real progress alongside real regression — gains achieved, then threatened or reversed — and that combination is the essential character of the American democratic experiment.

Map Study

Contemporary America — Political Geography

A map of twenty-first-century political America reveals a striking geographic pattern. The densely populated urban centers of the coasts and Great Lakes — New York, Los Angeles, Chicago, Boston, San Francisco, Seattle, Atlanta, Houston, Miami — are strongly Democratic. The vast geographic interior — rural counties, small cities, and most of the South, Plains, and Mountain West — is strongly Republican. Suburbs, once reliably Republican, have shifted toward Democrats in many metropolitan areas, while rural areas have moved dramatically toward Republicans.

This geographic polarization has important structural implications. The Electoral College awards electoral votes by state regardless of population, amplifying rural votes relative to urban ones. A small number of "swing states" — including Pennsylvania, Michigan, Wisconsin, Arizona, Georgia, and Nevada — often decide presidential elections. The Senate, which gives each state two senators regardless of population, systematically overrepresents rural states. These structural features translate the geographic sorting of the parties into asymmetric political power with major consequences for which policies can be enacted nationally.

Constitutional Connection

The Living Constitution and Contemporary Rights Debates

The twenty-first century has been a period of intense constitutional conflict. Several major questions have dominated the era.

What rights does the Constitution protect? The conservative majority on the Supreme Court has moved from a "living Constitution" approach — which held that constitutional meaning should evolve with social conditions — toward originalism, which holds that the Constitution should be interpreted according to its original public meaning at the time of ratification. The Dobbs decision applied this approach to overturn Roe v. Wade.

What is the scope of federal regulatory authority? The Court's 2022 decision in West Virginia v. EPA applied the "major questions doctrine" to limit administrative agencies' ability to regulate without explicit congressional authorization — significantly constraining environmental, health, and financial regulators.

What protections do democratic elections require? The Court's decisions in Shelby County v. Holder (2013) and Brnovich v. Democratic National Committee (2021) significantly limited federal protections for minority voting rights at a time when many states were passing legislation that critics argued was designed to reduce minority participation.

What limits on presidential power does the Constitution impose? The Court's 2024 decision in Trump v. United States, recognizing significant presidential immunity from criminal prosecution for official acts, raised fundamental questions about accountability, the separation of powers, and the rule of law that will be debated for years.

These constitutional battles reflect the unresolved tensions at the heart of American democracy: between rights and power, between federal authority and state autonomy, between judicial interpretation and democratic self-governance, and between protection of minorities and majoritarian rule.

Chapter Summary

The twenty-first century has been defined by transformation, crisis, and the testing of democratic institutions and ideals in ways that reveal both the resilience and the vulnerabilities of the American democratic experiment.

The September 11 attacks and the wars that followed reshaped American foreign policy, domestic security, and civil liberties in ways that persisted long after the acute crisis had passed. The wars in Afghanistan and Iraq demonstrated the limits of military power as a tool of political transformation, and the costs — human, financial, and moral — were enormous. The War on Terror raised fundamental questions about civil liberties, surveillance, due process, and the treatment of Muslim and immigrant communities that remain unresolved.

The Great Recession of 2007–2009 accelerated economic inequality that had been building for decades, producing political volatility that continued to shake American politics years after the immediate crisis. The economic anxiety of working-class communities, left behind by globalization, deindustrialization, and policies that consistently favored capital over labor, became fuel for populist movements on both left and right.

Barack Obama's presidency represented a historic break in American politics — the election of the first African American president — while simultaneously demonstrating the persistence of racial inequality and the ferocity of partisan opposition. His major achievements, including the Affordable Care Act, were built in the face of unified Republican opposition and remained under challenge throughout his presidency and beyond.

Donald Trump's rise and presidency represented a fundamental challenge to the political norms and institutional constraints that had governed American democracy. His refusal to accept the results of the 2020 election, and the January 6 attack on the Capitol that grew from that refusal, tested American democratic institutions in ways with few historical precedents. Those institutions held — the election results were certified, Biden was inaugurated — but the damage to the norm of the peaceful transfer of power was significant and lasting.

Social media transformed the information environment of American democracy, fragmenting the shared factual foundation that self-government requires, accelerating polarization, and enabling the rapid spread of misinformation. The decline of local journalism removed a critical pillar of democratic accountability. The rise of algorithmic content delivery created incentives that systematically rewarded emotional provocation over factual accuracy.

Social movements for racial justice, LGBTQ+ equality, women's rights, immigrant rights, and other causes achieved significant gains — most dramatically in the legalization of same-sex marriage — while confronting sustained political resistance. The Supreme Court's rightward shift produced decisions that reversed decades of precedent in areas from abortion to voting rights to administrative regulation, generating both legal transformation and political backlash.

Climate change, the COVID-19 pandemic, and persistent economic inequality confronted American policymakers with structural challenges of the first order. The pandemic exposed the consequences of decades of underinvestment in public health infrastructure and the political difficulty of sustaining coherent responses to shared crises in a polarized environment. Climate change raised the stakes of political dysfunction to an existential level.

Through all of these developments, American democratic institutions — courts, elections, a free press, civil society organizations, state and local governments — showed both resilience and vulnerability. The institutions were strong enough to survive the crises of the early twenty-first century, but not strong enough to prevent those crises or to resolve the underlying tensions that produced them. The United States in the twenty-first century remained a nation deeply shaped by its history, and a nation whose future remained unwritten.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Terrorism
The use of violence against civilians to create fear and achieve political goals; al-Qaeda's September 11 attacks exemplified the new form of non-state terrorism that defined the early twenty-first century.
USA PATRIOT Act
Legislation passed in October 2001 that dramatically expanded domestic surveillance powers, intelligence-sharing, and the definition of terrorism; critics argued it threatened civil liberties.
War on Terror
The broad U.S. military, intelligence, diplomatic, and law enforcement campaign launched after September 11, 2001, to combat terrorist organizations and the states that harbored them.
Department of Homeland Security
Cabinet-level federal agency created in 2002 to coordinate domestic security; the largest federal government reorganization since 1947.
Counterinsurgency
Military, political, and economic strategies to defeat armed non-state groups fighting within a civilian population; required winning popular support as much as defeating armed opponents.
Great Recession
The severe economic downturn of 2007–2009 caused by collapse of the housing market and related financial instruments; characterized by widespread job loss, home foreclosure, and financial system instability.
Subprime mortgage
High-risk home loans made to borrowers with poor credit histories; bundled into securities that collapsed in value, triggering the financial crisis.
TARP
Troubled Asset Relief Program; 2008 legislation authorizing up to $700 billion for the Treasury to stabilize the financial system; a central element of the government response to the financial crisis.
Affordable Care Act (ACA)
2010 legislation expanding health insurance coverage through exchanges, Medicaid expansion, consumer protections, and subsidies; the most significant health policy change since Medicare.
Black Lives Matter
Social movement founded in 2013 focusing on racial justice, particularly police killings of Black Americans; grew into a major force for policy and cultural change.
Obergefell v. Hodges
2015 Supreme Court decision establishing the constitutional right to same-sex marriage in all fifty states; product of decades of legal organizing and shifting public opinion.
Polarization
The process by which the political parties become more ideologically distant from each other, reducing overlap and making compromise more difficult; intensified sharply in the twenty-first century.
Populism
A political approach framing politics as a struggle between ordinary people and a corrupt elite; appeared in both left and right versions in contemporary American politics.
Social media
Digital platforms enabling user-generated content sharing and social networking; transformed political communication, organizing, and the spread of information and misinformation.
Misinformation / Disinformation
False information; disinformation refers specifically to false information spread with intent to deceive; both spread rapidly on social media platforms.
Algorithmic content
Content curated by automated systems based on users' past engagement; tends to favor emotionally engaging content over accurate, nuanced information.
January 6
The January 6, 2021, attack on the U.S. Capitol by a mob seeking to prevent certification of the 2020 election results; unprecedented assault on the peaceful transfer of power.
Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization
2022 Supreme Court decision overturning Roe v. Wade and eliminating the federal constitutional right to abortion; returned abortion regulation to individual states.
DACA
Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals; 2012 executive policy providing temporary protection and work authorization to undocumented immigrants brought to the United States as children.
COVID-19
Coronavirus disease pandemic that began in 2020; killed over a million Americans; disrupted economy, education, and daily life; exposed public health vulnerabilities.
Paris Climate Agreement
2015 international accord committing signatory nations to reducing greenhouse gas emissions; the United States withdrew in 2017 and rejoined in 2021.
Inflation Reduction Act
2022 legislation directing approximately $369 billion toward climate and clean energy investments; the largest climate policy action in U.S. history.
Deaths of despair
Term coined by economists Anne Case and Angus Deaton for the rise in mortality among less-educated white Americans from drug overdose, suicide, and alcoholism in economically distressed communities.
Originalism
A theory of constitutional interpretation holding that the Constitution should be interpreted according to its original public meaning at the time of ratification; associated with the contemporary conservative legal movement and the Supreme Court majority from 2022 onward.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter has examined the United States in the twenty-first century — a period of transformation, crisis, and renewed conflict over the fundamental questions that have defined American history from the beginning. The story of these decades is not simply the story of presidents, elections, or wars, though all of those matter. It is the story of ordinary Americans living through extraordinary times: of soldiers and their families navigating the longest wars in American history; of workers losing jobs, homes, and futures in the Great Recession; of Black families living with the ongoing reality of racially motivated police violence; of immigrant communities living in fear of deportation; of young people inheriting a planet in ecological crisis and a political system apparently struggling to respond to it.

The twenty-first century has tested the durability of American democratic institutions in ways that would have seemed extreme to earlier generations. The January 6 attack on the Capitol, the refusal of a sitting president to accept election results, the rapid spread of conspiracy theories and misinformation, and escalating partisan conflict over the most basic features of governance represent stresses on the system that the founders never anticipated, even as they built a system designed to withstand the abuse of power they feared. The institutions held — but their holding was not inevitable, and it cannot be taken for granted in the future.

At the same time, this period has witnessed remarkable democratic energy. Mass movements for racial justice, environmental protection, LGBTQ+ equality, women's rights, and immigrant rights have demonstrated that American civil society retains an extraordinary capacity for mobilization. The legalization of same-sex marriage, the expansion of health insurance coverage, the passage of major climate legislation, the conviction of a police officer for the murder of George Floyd — all achieved against formidable opposition — show that democratic change remains possible even in an era of intense polarization. These achievements were won through the same combination of organizing, advocacy, litigation, electoral politics, and cultural change that drove every previous expansion of rights in American history.

The history of the United States, traced across forty-eight chapters of this textbook, is a long argument about what kind of nation the United States should be. That argument has never been resolved — it has only taken different forms in different eras. The founders argued about it in Philadelphia in 1787. Abolitionists and slaveholders argued about it in the antebellum decades. Union and Confederate soldiers fought about it on the battlefields of the Civil War. Suffragists, civil rights marchers, labor organizers, and immigrant communities have all joined that argument in their own ways. The argument continues today, in courtrooms and legislatures, in streets and on social media, in elections and in movements, in the daily choices of millions of Americans about how to live and what to demand.

The purpose of studying history is not to find simple lessons or easy answers. The past does not repeat itself — but it does rhyme, and understanding the patterns helps us navigate the present more wisely. A student who understands the history of how democracy has been extended and defended, how it has been undermined and recovered, how ordinary people have made extraordinary demands on their institutions and sometimes succeeded, is better equipped to be an informed and active citizen than one who lacks that understanding. History does not determine the future — but it illuminates it. The United States remains, as it has always been, a nation whose future is not written — a nation whose promise is perpetually contested, perpetually unfinished, and perpetually open to those willing to do the work of democratic citizenship. That work is yours to continue.

Chapter 40 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

On the morning of September 11, 2001, four commercial aircraft were hijacked by terrorists over the eastern United States. Within hours, thousands of people had been killed, the skyline of New York City had been permanently altered, and the United States government was activating emergency protocols that had not been used in living memory. In the days and weeks that followed, Congress passed sweeping legislation authorizing military force and expanding intelligence and surveillance authorities. The president issued executive orders creating new agencies and detention policies. Courts began examining whether wartime powers could be used to suspend normal legal protections for suspected terrorists. A generation of Americans grew up shaped by airport security lines, two major wars, and an ongoing public debate about how much freedom must be traded for safety.

That moment illustrates the central challenge of this chapter: how governments protect their citizens from violence, disorder, and external threats while remaining accountable to the people they serve and respectful of the rights they are supposed to uphold. Security is not simply a technical problem of military hardware or intelligence tradecraft. It is a deeply political problem that touches on constitutional authority, democratic accountability, civil liberties, international law, and the relationship between citizens and the state. Every decision a government makes about defense, policing, surveillance, emergency powers, or military force involves tradeoffs between competing values — safety and freedom, secrecy and transparency, speed and deliberation, executive authority and legislative oversight.

Governments throughout history have recognized security as a fundamental purpose. Without some protection from internal violence and external aggression, no other government function is possible. Commerce cannot occur if roads are controlled by bandits. Courts cannot function if armed factions ignore their rulings. Rights cannot be exercised if citizens live in constant fear of attack. The ancient political philosophers who reflected on the purpose of political life considered security — the protection of persons, property, and communities — among the most basic reasons that human beings form governments at all. This is why even the most liberty-oriented political traditions have accepted that governments must have some coercive capacity, some military force, some emergency authority.

At the same time, history has repeatedly demonstrated the dangers of governments that use security as a justification for unchecked power. Authoritarian regimes routinely invoke threats — foreign enemies, internal subversives, terrorists, criminals — to justify suspending rights, silencing dissent, imprisoning opponents, and eliminating independent institutions. Even democratic governments have sometimes expanded executive power, weakened oversight mechanisms, and restricted civil liberties in the name of security, with consequences that outlasted the original emergency. The internment of Japanese Americans during World War II, the surveillance of civil rights leaders by the FBI in the 1960s, the detention policies created after September 11, and the use of national security laws against journalists and dissidents in many countries around the world all illustrate how security powers, once granted, can be misused.

The challenge, then, is not simply whether governments should have security powers — they obviously must — but how those powers should be structured, limited, overseen, and held accountable. Democratic constitutions around the world have approached this challenge in different ways, creating systems of civilian control over the military, legislative authorization for war, judicial review of executive detention, independent oversight of intelligence agencies, and constitutional constraints on emergency declarations. These institutional arrangements are the subject of this chapter. Understanding how they work — and how they can fail — is essential for any student of government.

Opening Question

A government intelligence agency has developed a program that monitors the phone records of millions of citizens without individual warrants, arguing that mass data collection is the most effective way to detect terrorist plots before they succeed. Critics argue this is an unconstitutional invasion of privacy with no meaningful oversight. Defenders argue that the program has stopped real attacks and that citizens have nothing to fear if they have done nothing wrong. By the end of this chapter, you will have the conceptual and institutional tools to analyze this dispute — to ask what democratic accountability requires, what constitutional limits apply, and how security and civil liberties can be reconciled within the framework of law.

Section 40.1

Security as a Government Function

The idea that governments exist, at least in part, to protect their citizens from violence is among the oldest and most durable claims in political thought. The ancient philosopher Aristotle observed that human beings form political communities for the sake of the good life, but they come together initially for the sake of mere life itself — that is, for survival and protection. Thomas Hobbes, writing in seventeenth-century England during a period of civil war, went further, arguing that without a sovereign authority capable of imposing order and protecting persons from violence, human life would be "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short." Even political philosophers who disagreed sharply with Hobbes about how much power governments should hold agreed that some degree of coercive authority was necessary for the basic protection of persons and communities.

Modern political systems have inherited this understanding and built it into constitutional frameworks, statutory law, and institutional design. The United States Constitution, ratified in 1788, lists among its core purposes "to insure domestic Tranquility, provide for the common defence, promote the general Welfare, and secure the Blessings of Liberty." The European Union's founding documents describe the security of member states as a collective responsibility. The United Nations Charter, created after the devastation of World War II, commits member states to maintaining international peace and security as a primary organizational goal. Security is not merely one government function among many; in most political traditions, it is treated as a prerequisite for everything else that governments do.

What "Security" Means in Governance

Security as a concept in government refers to the protection of persons, communities, institutions, territory, and the state itself from violence, attack, subversion, or collapse. This general definition contains several distinct and sometimes overlapping domains. National security typically refers to the protection of the state and its people from external threats — foreign military attack, terrorism with foreign connections, hostile foreign intelligence operations, and threats to national sovereignty and territorial integrity. Domestic security or public safety refers to the protection of citizens from internal threats — crime, civil unrest, domestic terrorism, and the breakdown of public order. Homeland security, a term that became prominent after September 11, 2001, refers to the coordination of national and domestic security functions to protect the country from catastrophic attacks or disasters. Cybersecurity refers to the protection of digital infrastructure, communications systems, and data from unauthorized access, attack, or disruption. Economic security refers to the protection of national economies from disruption, whether through hostile state action, sanctions, trade manipulation, or infrastructure attacks.

These categories are analytically distinct but practically overlapping. A terrorist attack may have both domestic and foreign dimensions. A cyberattack on financial infrastructure may threaten both economic stability and national security. A pandemic may require both public health responses and emergency powers normally associated with security crises. Governments must therefore maintain institutional capacity across multiple security domains and coordinate among agencies that have traditionally operated in separate spheres.

The Security-Liberty Tension

The most persistent political challenge in security governance is the tension between security and liberty. Both are values that democratic systems treat as fundamental. Security enables citizens to exercise their rights and live their lives without fear of violent disruption. Liberty — freedom from government overreach, arbitrary arrest, warrantless surveillance, detention without trial, and the suppression of speech and conscience — is itself a core value that governments are supposed to protect. The problem is that the tools governments use to provide security can themselves threaten liberty.

Police forces that prevent crime may also conduct racial profiling or use excessive force. Intelligence agencies that monitor foreign threats may also spy on domestic political movements. Military forces that defend national territory may also be used to suppress internal dissent. Emergency powers that allow governments to respond quickly to crises may also allow executives to bypass democratic processes and constitutional limits. The expansion of government surveillance capabilities enabled by digital technology creates the capacity for monitoring that would have been unimaginable to earlier generations of constitutional designers.

This tension is not unique to any particular political system. Every democracy that has faced genuine security threats has had to make difficult choices about how much liberty to sacrifice in the name of safety. Those choices have been made differently at different times and in different places, and they have been disputed by citizens, challenged in courts, debated in legislatures, and revised as circumstances changed. Understanding the institutional frameworks through which these choices are made — and the mechanisms by which they are held accountable — is essential to civic literacy in a security-conscious age.

Categories of Government Security Activity

It is useful to distinguish among the major institutional domains through which governments exercise their security functions. Military forces are organized, trained, and equipped to defend national territory against external attack, project force beyond national borders when authorized, and deter potential adversaries from initiating conflict. Intelligence agencies collect, analyze, and report information about foreign governments, international organizations, non-state actors, and internal threats. Domestic law enforcement maintains public order, enforces criminal law, investigates offenses, and in many systems shares some counterterrorism and national security responsibilities with intelligence and military institutions. Emergency management agencies coordinate government responses to natural disasters, infrastructure failures, public health emergencies, and other crises that require extraordinary governmental action but may not involve military force or foreign threats.

Each of these institutional domains has its own legal framework, chain of command, oversight structure, and relationship to democratic accountability. The following sections examine them in turn, beginning with national defense and the concept of civil-military relations.

Section 40.2

National Defense and Military Power

National defense — the maintenance of military forces capable of protecting a country from foreign attack and advancing its security interests abroad — is one of the oldest and most resource-intensive functions of government. In virtually every country in the world, defense spending constitutes a major portion of the national budget, and the organization of military forces represents a significant exercise of governmental authority over a large number of people, large amounts of equipment, and decisions of life and death.

What Military Power Does

Military forces serve several distinct functions in the security strategies of modern states. The most fundamental is deterrence — the maintenance of military capability sufficient to persuade potential adversaries that the costs of attacking would outweigh the benefits. Deterrence theory, developed systematically during the Cold War when the United States and Soviet Union each maintained nuclear arsenals capable of destroying the other many times over, holds that credible military power can prevent conflict by making the prospect of war too costly for any rational actor. Deterrence operates through the combination of capability and will — an adversary must believe not only that the threatened state has the capacity to respond to an attack, but that it is prepared to use that capacity.

Beyond deterrence, military forces are used for actual combat operations when deterrence fails or when governments choose to use force to achieve foreign policy objectives. Modern military operations range from large-scale conventional warfare between organized armed forces to counterinsurgency — fighting irregular forces within a population — to special operations involving small elite units conducting targeted missions, to humanitarian assistance and disaster relief that deploys military logistical capabilities for civilian protection in crisis situations. Military forces also conduct what is sometimes called military diplomacy: joint exercises with allied forces, port visits and military exchanges, participation in international peacekeeping operations, and training assistance to partner countries.

Constitutional Authority Over Military Force

In democratic systems, the authority to create, fund, equip, organize, and deploy military forces is distributed among constitutional institutions in ways designed to prevent any single actor from controlling military power without accountability. The United States provides one of the most elaborately divided systems. The Constitution gives Congress the power to declare war, raise and support armies, maintain a navy, and regulate the armed forces. It gives the president the role of commander-in-chief of the armed forces and the executive responsibility for conducting military operations. This division creates a system in which Congress holds the power to authorize and fund military activity, while the president holds the power to direct military operations once authorized.

In practice, this division has been contested and has evolved significantly over the two and a half centuries of American constitutional history. The last formal declaration of war by Congress occurred during World War II. Since then, presidents have used military force in Korea, Vietnam, the Persian Gulf, Kosovo, Afghanistan, Iraq, Libya, Syria, and many other places — sometimes with explicit congressional authorization, sometimes with informal congressional approval, and sometimes on the executive's claimed authority alone. Congress passed the War Powers Resolution in 1973 in response to the Vietnam War, attempting to require presidents to notify Congress within forty-eight hours of committing forces to hostilities and to withdraw forces within sixty days absent congressional authorization. Presidents have consistently disputed the constitutionality of this requirement, and the resolution has rarely been enforced in its intended manner.

Parliamentary systems handle the relationship between legislative and executive authority over military force differently. In the United Kingdom, the authority to commit the armed forces to hostilities traditionally rested with the royal prerogative, exercised by the prime minister and cabinet, without requiring parliamentary authorization. Votes in Parliament before the Iraq War in 2003 and blocking intervention in Syria in 2013 established a political convention that the government would seek parliamentary approval for major military action, though this convention has no statutory or constitutional force. Germany's Basic Law requires parliamentary approval before German armed forces can be deployed abroad, a requirement that reflects Germany's particular historical experience with unchecked military power.

The Resource Demands of Defense

National defense is extraordinarily expensive. Modern military forces require not only personnel — soldiers, sailors, pilots, support staff, and civilian employees — but also capital equipment representing some of the most technologically sophisticated products in the world. Defense spending varies enormously across countries, reflecting differences in security environment, alliance membership, strategic objectives, and domestic political priorities. The United States has consistently maintained the highest defense budget in the world in absolute terms. Countries that are members of the North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO) have committed to spending at least two percent of their gross domestic product on defense, though many have fallen short of this target. Defense budgets are not simply the product of rational strategic calculation; they are also the product of political processes involving lobbying by defense contractors, the electoral interests of legislators whose districts contain military facilities, interservice rivalries, and ideological debates about the proper role of military force in foreign policy.

International Law and the Use of Force

Military force is not used in a legal vacuum. The UN Charter prohibits member states from using force against the territorial integrity or political independence of any other state, with two major exceptions: self-defense in response to an armed attack, and force authorized by the UN Security Council as a collective response to threats to international peace and security. International humanitarian law — embodied in the Geneva Conventions and their additional protocols — governs the conduct of armed conflict once it begins. These rules require parties to a conflict to distinguish between combatants and civilians, to protect non-combatants from attack, to treat prisoners of war humanely, and to limit the means and methods of warfare to those necessary to achieve military objectives. Violations of these rules constitute war crimes subject to prosecution by international and domestic courts.

Section 40.3

Civil-Military Relations

Among the most important institutional challenges in democratic governance is maintaining effective civilian control over military forces. This challenge — which political scientists refer to as the problem of civil-military relations — is fundamental to democratic theory: if military institutions are not subordinate to civilian democratic authority, then elected governments cannot actually govern, and the use of force becomes the prerogative of the armed forces rather than a tool of democratic policy.

Why Civilian Control Matters

The history of modern politics is filled with examples of military forces that have displaced civilian governments. Military coups — in which armed forces remove elected or civilian leaders and install military rulers — occurred repeatedly in Latin America, Africa, the Middle East, and Asia during the twentieth century. Even in established democracies, the relationship between military institutions and civilian authority has sometimes been tense, with senior military officers publicly disagreeing with civilian policy, selectively implementing orders, or seeking to influence political outcomes through unauthorized means.

The basic principle of civilian control holds that military forces should be subordinate to elected political leaders and should implement the policy decisions made by democratic governments, even when military officers disagree with those decisions. This principle is essential because the alternative — military institutions that act on their own judgment about what policy should be — is incompatible with democratic self-government. If armed forces can override civilian decisions, then elections and legislative deliberation do not determine policy; military preferences do.

Civilian control does not mean that military expertise is irrelevant or that civilian leaders should ignore military advice. On the contrary, effective civil-military relations involves robust communication between civilian policymakers and military professionals, with civilians making ultimate policy decisions informed by honest military assessments. The problem arises when military institutions seek to determine, rather than inform, policy decisions, or when they implement civilian decisions selectively based on their own institutional preferences.

Mechanisms of Civilian Control

Democratic systems have developed several institutional mechanisms to maintain civilian control of military forces. The most fundamental is the principle of the military chain of command, which runs from the commander-in-chief through civilian defense officials to uniformed military leadership. This chain ensures that military forces receive their orders from civilian authorities and that military leaders are accountable to civilian superiors. Legislative oversight of defense provides another layer: legislatures authorize defense spending, create legal frameworks for military organization, confirm senior military appointments, and investigate military conduct through hearings and inquiries. Civilian leadership of defense departments — with a civilian secretary or minister of defense overseeing the department — is a structural expression of civilian control. In the United States, law requires that the secretary of defense be a civilian, with a specified period required between military service and appointment. Professional military education that emphasizes values of civilian control and apolitical professionalism also contributes to a military culture that supports rather than challenges civilian governance.

The Challenge of Politicization

In recent decades, public debate in several democracies has raised concerns about the relationship between permanent military and intelligence institutions — sometimes called the "deep state" — and elected civilian governments. These concerns take two distinct forms that point in different directions. On one hand, there are concerns that career military officials may act to constrain or undermine the decisions of elected civilian leaders they disagree with, protecting institutional prerogatives against unwelcome policy changes. On the other hand, there are concerns that political leaders may seek to politicize military institutions — using them for partisan purposes, demanding personal loyalty rather than constitutional loyalty, or pressuring them to provide justifications for predetermined conclusions. Both concerns are legitimate, and both can undermine democratic governance. The goal of sound civil-military relations is a military that is apolitically professional — loyal to the constitution and laws, responsive to civilian direction, honest in its advice, and unwilling to be used for partisan purposes.

Comparative Civil-Military Relations

Different countries have handled civil-military relations in different ways. The United Kingdom has a long tradition of military subordination to the crown, exercised through parliamentary government, with senior officers maintaining a strongly apolitical professional culture. Germany, shaped by its experience of military independence and the catastrophic consequences of the Third Reich, built exceptionally strong civilian control mechanisms into the Basic Law and the structure of the Bundeswehr. Japan's postwar constitution renounced war as a sovereign right of the state, though subsequent governments have interpreted these provisions to allow a substantial Self-Defense Force. Countries emerging from military dictatorship — in Brazil, Chile, Argentina, and many others — have faced the challenge of establishing meaningful civilian control over armed forces that had previously exercised independent political authority.

Civic Vocabulary
Civil-Military Relations

Civil-military relations refers to the institutional, legal, cultural, and political relationship between a country's military forces and its civilian government. In democratic systems, civil-military relations ideally involves the subordination of military forces to elected civilian authority, with military institutions providing professional expertise and implementing civilian policy decisions, while civilian leaders make ultimate decisions about defense policy, military operations, and the use of force. The principle of civilian control holds that authority belongs to elected and appointed civilians, not to the armed forces themselves. Healthy civil-military relations require not only formal institutional structures but also a professional military culture that values political neutrality and constitutional loyalty. When civil-military relations break down — through military coups, the politicization of armed forces by civilian leaders, or military institutional resistance to civilian direction — democratic governance suffers.

Section 40.4

Intelligence and Secrecy

Governments have always sought information about their adversaries' capabilities, intentions, and activities. The intelligence function — systematically collecting, analyzing, and reporting information relevant to national security — is as old as organized conflict itself. What distinguishes modern intelligence from earlier forms of military and diplomatic reporting is the scale of dedicated institutional capacity, the sophistication of collection methods, and the degree to which democratic systems have had to grapple with the accountability challenges that secret institutions create.

What Intelligence Agencies Do

Modern intelligence agencies perform several distinct functions, often divided among specialized institutions within what analysts call the intelligence community. Collection involves gathering raw information from multiple sources. Human intelligence (HUMINT) is information gathered by human sources — case officers who recruit agents within foreign governments or organizations, and diplomatic reporting. Signals intelligence (SIGINT) is information gathered by intercepting communications — telephone calls, emails, satellite transmissions, and other electronic signals. Imagery intelligence (IMINT) is information gathered through aerial photography, satellite imagery, and other visual collection methods. Open-source intelligence (OSINT) is information gathered from publicly available sources — newspapers, academic publications, social media, government documents, and broadcasts. Analysis involves processing raw collected information into assessments that policymakers can use. Counterintelligence involves identifying, assessing, and neutralizing the intelligence activities of foreign governments directed against a country's own government, military, and citizens. Covert action involves secret activities intended to influence conditions in foreign countries in ways that are not attributed to the government conducting them.

The Accountability Problem

Intelligence agencies present distinctive accountability challenges for democratic systems. Effective intelligence depends on secrecy — sources and methods that become known to adversaries lose their value, and intelligence officers who are identified may face danger. Yet secrecy is fundamentally at odds with the transparency that democratic accountability requires. Citizens and their elected representatives cannot evaluate the performance, priorities, and conduct of institutions whose activities they are not allowed to know about.

Democratic governments have developed several mechanisms for overseeing intelligence agencies while preserving operational secrecy. Congressional intelligence committees receive classified briefings on intelligence activities, review proposed covert operations, and oversee intelligence spending. Members of these committees are themselves subject to secrecy requirements, meaning their oversight occurs in closed session. Inspector general offices within intelligence agencies have authority to investigate complaints, audit agency operations, and report findings to congressional oversight bodies. Courts provide a third form of oversight, though their access to classified information is limited. The Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court (FISC) in the United States reviews government applications for surveillance warrants targeting foreign intelligence subjects, providing a judicial check on surveillance activities that would otherwise be conducted entirely in executive secrecy.

The fundamental tension between intelligence secrecy and democratic oversight has no perfect resolution. Some degree of classified activity is genuinely necessary for effective national security intelligence. But history has repeatedly shown that absence of oversight enables abuse — from the FBI's counterintelligence program (COINTELPRO) that monitored and harassed civil rights leaders in the 1960s, to the NSA surveillance programs that collected data on millions of ordinary Americans without their knowledge in the early twenty-first century.

Intelligence and Civil Liberties

The tension between intelligence activities and civil liberties is one of the most persistently difficult issues in democratic security governance. Intelligence collection — particularly signals intelligence and communications surveillance — can easily extend from foreign targets to domestic residents, and from specific suspects to broad populations. The Fourth Amendment to the United States Constitution protects against unreasonable searches and seizures, but the application of this protection to electronic communications, digital data, and intelligence collection has been contested and evolving. Public debate about intelligence and civil liberties intensified after the revelation in 2013, through documents disclosed by former NSA contractor Edward Snowden, that the NSA was conducting broad surveillance of phone records, internet communications, and other data involving millions of Americans and foreign nationals who were not themselves targets of any investigation. The revelations prompted legislative responses, including the USA FREEDOM Act of 2015, which ended bulk collection of domestic telephone records, as well as ongoing judicial and public debate about the scope of intelligence collection authorities.

How the System Works
Congressional Intelligence Oversight

Congressional oversight of intelligence agencies in the United States operates through a combination of dedicated committees, classified briefings, and statutory requirements. Understanding how this oversight works — and where it faces limitations — helps illustrate the broader challenge of holding secret institutions accountable.

  • Step 1Committee Structure. The Senate Select Committee on Intelligence (SSCI) and the House Permanent Select Committee on Intelligence (HPSCI) are the primary congressional bodies responsible for intelligence oversight. Their members and staff hold top-secret security clearances.
  • Step 2Classified Briefings. Intelligence agency leaders regularly brief committee members on ongoing operations, significant intelligence assessments, and proposed or ongoing covert actions. These briefings occur in secure facilities and may not be publicly disclosed.
  • Step 3Notification Requirements. By law, the executive branch must notify the intelligence committees of significant intelligence activities. The notification requirement for covert actions — requiring the president to sign a presidential finding and notify the committees — is designed to ensure that secret operations have executive authorization and congressional awareness.
  • Step 4Budget Review. Congress must authorize and appropriate funding for intelligence activities. Because intelligence budgets are classified (though the overall total is now publicly disclosed), this review occurs in secret, limiting the ability of non-committee members or the public to evaluate spending priorities.
  • Step 5Investigation and Reporting. When committees identify concerns about intelligence activities, they may conduct formal investigations, request documents, interview officials, and produce reports. Some reports are eventually declassified and made public, as was the case with the Senate Intelligence Committee's investigation of CIA interrogation practices.

Limitations: Committee members cannot share classified information with colleagues, constituents, or the public, limiting the accountability this oversight can provide. Agencies have sometimes withheld information from committees or provided misleading briefings. The executive branch has consistently claimed broad authority to limit congressional access to classified information on national security grounds, creating ongoing tension about the scope of congressional oversight authority.

Section 40.5

Terrorism and Counterterrorism

Terrorism — the use of violence against civilians or non-combatants to create fear and compel political change — is one of the most persistent and difficult security challenges facing democratic governments. Unlike conventional military threats from foreign states, terrorist organizations are typically small, non-state actors who use asymmetric tactics — attacking vulnerable civilian targets rather than engaging military forces directly — to achieve political objectives that range from independence or regime change to ideological transformation or the undermining of confidence in government.

Defining Terrorism

Terrorism is a notoriously difficult concept to define precisely, and the difficulty is not merely semantic. Different definitions include or exclude different actors, different methods, and different political contexts, and the line between terrorism and other forms of political violence — guerrilla warfare, resistance movements, revolutionary violence — has been consistently contested. The observation that "one person's terrorist is another's freedom fighter" captures a real political problem: the same act of violence may be characterized as terrorism by those who oppose it and as justified resistance by those who support it. For purposes of government security policy, terrorism is typically defined in law as the use or threatened use of violence against civilians or civilian infrastructure to advance a political, religious, or ideological agenda through the creation of fear.

Why Terrorism Challenges Democratic Governance

Terrorism creates distinctive challenges for democratic governments that go beyond the physical threat it poses. First, terrorism is designed to provoke a response, and overresponse can serve the terrorist's objectives — curtailing civil liberties, targeting ethnic or religious communities disproportionately, or abandoning legal norms may alienate the communities essential to preventing future attacks. Second, terrorism blurs the domestic-international boundary that traditionally separated law enforcement from military operations. When a terrorist organization is based abroad, recruiting internationally, and coordinating attacks across national borders, neither domestic policing nor national defense alone is fully adequate. Third, terrorism creates pressure for intelligence and surveillance capabilities that may conflict with civil liberties. Fourth, terrorism involves inherently difficult tradeoffs between security effectiveness and constitutional norms — including pressure to detain suspects without trial, to use interrogation methods that might otherwise be prohibited, and to conduct surveillance without judicial authorization.

Counterterrorism Strategies and Tools

Democratic governments have used a range of tools in counterterrorism efforts, each with different implications for civil liberties, international law, and democratic governance. Law enforcement approaches treat terrorism primarily as criminal activity, investigating attacks, prosecuting perpetrators, and disrupting networks through criminal investigative methods that maintain normal legal frameworks — probable cause requirements, warrant procedures, due process protections, civilian trial. Military approaches treat terrorism as a security threat requiring military force — airstrikes, special operations raids, military detention, and in some cases large-scale military campaigns. After September 11, 2001, the United States pursued a military approach in Afghanistan, ultimately extending military operations across multiple countries. Intelligence approaches emphasize disruption of terrorist networks through intelligence collection, infiltration, financial monitoring, and covert action. Prevention and counter-radicalization approaches attempt to prevent individuals from becoming involved in terrorism in the first place, by addressing the social, economic, ideological, and psychological factors that make some people receptive to extremist recruitment. Each approach carries different tradeoffs between effectiveness, civil liberties, and constitutional norms.

Civil Liberties and Counterterrorism

The tension between counterterrorism effectiveness and civil liberties protection has been among the most significant policy debates in democratic countries since September 11, 2001. Major civil liberties concerns include surveillance and privacy, detention without trial, profiling and discrimination, and targeted killing. The detention of hundreds of individuals at Guantanamo Bay, Cuba, following the September 11 attacks — and the subsequent litigation about their legal status — generated important judicial decisions about the scope of executive detention authority. The use of drone strikes and other lethal operations to kill specific individuals deemed to be terrorist threats, sometimes including individuals who are citizens of the country conducting the operation, raises profound questions about due process, the geographic scope of military authority, and international law.

Comparative Lens
Counterterrorism Approaches: United States and United Kingdom

The United States and the United Kingdom, both of which experienced major terrorist attacks in the early twenty-first century, illustrate different approaches to counterterrorism within democratic constitutional frameworks.

The United States responded to the September 11, 2001 attacks with a broadly military framing. The Authorization for Use of Military Force (AUMF) passed by Congress nine days after the attacks authorized the president to use force against those responsible and those who harbored them, providing open-ended legal authority that subsequent administrations extended to affiliated organizations. The USA PATRIOT Act significantly expanded domestic surveillance authorities. Military detention at Guantanamo Bay became a major legal controversy, producing a series of Supreme Court decisions — including Rasul v. Bush (2004), Hamdi v. Rumsfeld (2004), and Boumediene v. Bush (2008) — addressing the rights of detainees. The American approach was largely executive-centered and military-framed, with Congress playing a relatively secondary role after the initial authorizations.

The United Kingdom responded to the 7/7 London bombings of 2005 within a more law-enforcement-centered framework, though with significant emergency legislation. The UK's counterterrorism approach was shaped by its earlier experience with Irish Republican Army terrorism over several decades, which had generally been treated as a law enforcement and intelligence matter rather than a military one. Parliamentary oversight of intelligence and counterterrorism is provided by the Intelligence and Security Committee, a statutory body with members drawn from Parliament. British courts, applying European Convention on Human Rights standards, have constrained some counterterrorism detention and surveillance measures, including indefinite detention of foreign nationals without trial.

Both systems face the same fundamental tradeoffs — between security and liberty, between intelligence effectiveness and legal constraint, between executive speed and legislative deliberation — but they have organized their institutional responses differently, reflecting differences in constitutional structure, legal tradition, and political culture.

Section 40.6

Cybersecurity and Critical Infrastructure

Among the most significant developments in national security in the early twenty-first century has been the emergence of cyberspace — the interconnected digital networks that support communications, commerce, infrastructure, and government operations — as a domain of strategic competition and conflict. Governments that once thought primarily about military forces, geography, and conventional weapons now must also think about the security of computer networks, power grids, financial systems, water treatment facilities, and the data that flows through billions of connected devices.

The Cybersecurity Challenge

Cybersecurity threats take several forms. Espionage involves the unauthorized access to government, military, or corporate computer systems to steal classified information, intellectual property, or sensitive data. Nation-state actors — particularly Russia, China, North Korea, and Iran — have conducted sophisticated espionage operations through cyber means, stealing classified documents, defense research, commercial trade secrets, and financial information from targets worldwide. Sabotage involves attacks intended to damage or destroy computer systems or the physical infrastructure that depends on them. The Stuxnet worm, discovered in 2010, was a sophisticated piece of malware that destroyed centrifuges at an Iranian nuclear facility — widely attributed to the United States and Israel — demonstrating that cyberattacks could produce physical damage to critical infrastructure. DisruptionInfluence operations use digital means — social media manipulation, disinformation campaigns, fake news websites — to shape public opinion, undermine confidence in democratic institutions, and interfere with electoral processes. Russian interference in the 2016 U.S. presidential election brought extensive public attention to this form of threat.

Critical Infrastructure Protection

Critical infrastructure refers to the physical and digital systems whose disruption would have severe consequences for national security, economic stability, public health, or public safety. The protection of critical infrastructure against cyberattack is complicated by the fact that most such infrastructure in many countries is owned and operated by private companies rather than government agencies. Power grids, telecommunications networks, financial systems, and many other sectors are managed by private firms that may have different risk tolerances and investment priorities from government, and may resist regulatory requirements as burdens on their operations. Governments have developed several approaches: regulatory requirements mandating minimum cybersecurity standards, information sharing programs facilitating the exchange of threat information between government and private sector, incident response capabilities helping companies recover more quickly when attacks succeed, and diplomatic engagement with other countries to establish norms of behavior in cyberspace.

Attribution and Response

One of the most distinctive challenges of cybersecurity governance is attribution — determining who is responsible for a particular cyberattack. Unlike conventional military attacks, which typically involve identifiable forces operating in physical space, cyberattacks can be conducted through anonymizing infrastructure, using tools and techniques that imitate other actors, or through proxies that obscure the relationship to state sponsors. The difficulty of attribution complicates the development of deterrence strategies for cyberspace. Democratic governments face additional challenges in responding to cyberattacks: executive authority to respond through offensive cyber operations raises questions about whether such responses require congressional authorization, how they are subject to oversight, and how they comply with international law. The use of offensive cyber capabilities involves decisions that may need to be made rapidly but that also have significant implications for international norms, diplomatic relationships, and the safety of civilian infrastructure around the world.

Institution in Practice
The Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA)

The Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) was established within the U.S. Department of Homeland Security in 2018, bringing together several existing offices with responsibilities for protecting government computer systems and critical infrastructure. CISA represents one model for how governments organize their cybersecurity responsibilities.

CISA operates across several functional areas. Its cybersecurity division works to defend federal civilian government networks from cyberattack, distributes threat information to government agencies, and coordinates responses when agencies are compromised. Its infrastructure security division works with private sector owners and operators of critical infrastructure to assess vulnerabilities, improve security practices, and plan for responses to attacks. Its emergency communications division supports secure communications for government and emergency services.

CISA works with a national network of regional offices that engage directly with private sector entities in critical infrastructure sectors, establishing relationships and sharing threat information before crises occur. CISA cannot compel most private sector entities to implement specific security measures or share information about attacks they experience; instead, it operates primarily through voluntary partnerships, information sharing, and the provision of technical assistance. This model illustrates a broader challenge in cybersecurity governance: the need to coordinate security activities across institutional boundaries — between government and private sector, between civilian and military agencies, between federal and state governments — in a domain where no single institution has comprehensive authority.

Section 40.7

Emergency Powers

The idea that governments may need to exercise extraordinary powers during genuine emergencies — war, natural disasters, pandemics, constitutional crises — is present in virtually every constitutional system in the world. Emergency powers are provisions, usually in constitutional documents or statutes, that authorize governments to take actions in times of crisis that would not be permitted under ordinary legal rules. The rationale is straightforward: some crises develop too quickly, or require responses too concentrated and rapid, for ordinary democratic procedures to manage effectively.

But emergency powers are also among the most potentially dangerous provisions in any constitutional order. History is filled with examples of governments that used genuine emergencies to seize powers they then refused to relinquish, or that manufactured or exaggerated emergencies to justify the suspension of normal constitutional constraints. The Weimar Republic's emergency provisions — particularly Article 48 of the Weimar Constitution, which authorized the president to govern by decree in emergencies — were used by Adolf Hitler's government to establish the legal framework of the Third Reich. More recently, governments in Hungary, the Philippines, and other countries have used emergency declarations related to the COVID-19 pandemic to concentrate executive authority and weaken parliamentary and judicial oversight.

What Emergency Powers Do

Emergency powers typically allow governments to do one or more of the following: suspend or modify normal legal rules, including rights protections, that would otherwise apply; concentrate decision-making authority in the executive branch, bypassing normal legislative procedures; commandeer private resources for public use; restrict movement, assembly, commerce, or other activities; deploy military forces in domestic roles normally reserved for civilian authorities; and impose curfews, detention, or other exceptional measures affecting individual liberty.

In the United States, emergency powers are distributed across constitutional provisions, statutes, and executive orders. The National Emergencies Act of 1976 established a framework for presidential declarations of national emergency, but it gave Congress only limited tools to terminate emergency declarations. As a result, emergency declarations have sometimes remained in effect for decades after the original crisis passed. In parliamentary systems, emergency powers are often subject to more direct and continuous legislative control. Germany's Basic Law allows expanded powers during a "state of tension" or "state of defense" but requires parliamentary authorization for these measures to remain in force. France's constitution includes exceptional power provisions in Article 16, limited to situations in which republican institutions are genuinely threatened. The UK's Civil Contingencies Act 2004 requires emergency regulations to be renewed by Parliament every thirty days.

Constitutional Constraints on Emergency Powers

Democratic constitutions have attempted to limit emergency powers through several mechanisms. Temporal limits require that emergency powers expire after a fixed period unless renewed, forcing democratic deliberation about whether the emergency and extraordinary measures are still warranted. Legislative authorization requirements require that emergency declarations or specific emergency measures be approved by the legislature, preventing executives from claiming emergency powers unilaterally. Judicial review allows courts to assess whether emergency declarations are genuine, whether measures are proportionate, and whether rights are being violated. Non-derogable rights — rights that cannot be suspended even in a genuine emergency — are recognized in international human rights law and many national constitutions. The International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights designates certain rights — including the prohibition on torture, the right to life, and freedom from arbitrary execution — as absolute and non-derogable even in emergencies. Sunset clauses make emergency legislation automatically expire at a specified date unless affirmatively renewed, placing the burden on the government to justify continuing extraordinary measures.

Martial Law

Among the most extreme forms of emergency power is martial law — the temporary substitution of military authority for civilian government, with military courts replacing civilian tribunals, military commanders exercising civilian governmental functions, and constitutional protections suspended. Martial law is constitutionally contemplated in some systems but is generally regarded as appropriate only in the most severe crises, such as foreign invasion, widespread civil war, or the complete breakdown of civilian governmental capacity. The United States has a limited constitutional basis for martial law through the Suspension Clause, which allows habeas corpus to be suspended during rebellion or invasion. Military forces are generally prohibited by the Posse Comitatus Act of 1878 from performing domestic law enforcement functions. Full martial law has been declared in limited circumstances in American history, including in Hawaii following the attack on Pearl Harbor in 1941.

Emergency Powers and Democratic Erosion

Political scientists and constitutional scholars have identified emergency powers as one of the primary mechanisms through which democratic systems can be gradually transformed into authoritarian ones. Rather than a dramatic coup, the erosion of democracy can occur through the steady accumulation of emergency authority — executive decrees that bypass legislatures, surveillance programs that suppress political opposition, detention authorities used against political rivals, media controls justified as necessary for national security. The warning signs of emergency power abuse include: emergency declarations that target political opponents rather than genuine threats; emergency measures that extend well beyond the scope of the original crisis; executive refusal to allow legislative or judicial review; use of emergency powers to restrict political speech, assembly, or electoral competition; and the indefinite renewal of emergency declarations without genuine reconsideration of their necessity. Defenders of constitutional democracy have argued that maintaining the constitutional framework even during genuine emergencies is itself a security objective — a government that abandons constitutional principles in the name of security may succeed in addressing the immediate threat but at the cost of the political order it was supposed to protect.

Civic Vocabulary
Habeas Corpus

Habeas corpus is a legal principle and court procedure requiring that a government justify the imprisonment of a person before a court. The Latin phrase means "you shall have the body" — a writ of habeas corpus commands that a prisoner be brought before a court so that the court can determine whether the detention is lawful. Habeas corpus is one of the foundational protections against arbitrary government detention. Without it, governments can imprison people indefinitely without charge or trial, and those imprisoned have no legal mechanism to challenge their detention. The United States Constitution protects habeas corpus in Article I, Section 9, providing that it "shall not be suspended, unless when in Cases of Rebellion or Invasion the public Safety may require it." The scope and availability of habeas corpus review for military detainees held outside the United States became a major legal controversy after September 11, 2001, ultimately producing Supreme Court decisions holding that federal courts retained jurisdiction to review such detentions.

Section 40.8

Security, Liberty, and Accountability

The relationship between security and liberty is not simply a tradeoff in which more of one necessarily means less of the other. This simple version of the tension is misleading because security itself requires respect for rights — a government that routinely violates civil liberties undermines the legitimacy that is essential for effective governance, alienates the communities whose cooperation is necessary for intelligence gathering and crime prevention, and potentially creates the very grievances that generate security threats. Nevertheless, genuine tensions do exist, and the institutional mechanisms through which democratic systems manage those tensions are among the most important features of constitutional design.

Why Accountability Matters for Security

Accountability — the ability of citizens, representatives, and courts to know what security institutions are doing and to hold them responsible for their conduct — is not merely a value in tension with security; it is itself a security-relevant value. Security institutions that are not accountable to external oversight have repeatedly demonstrated their tendency toward abuse, inefficiency, and institutional self-interest that conflicts with the public good.

The FBI's COINTELPRO program, which operated from 1956 to 1971, provides a compelling historical example. Operating entirely in secrecy, without meaningful congressional oversight or judicial review, FBI counterintelligence operations targeted civil rights leaders, anti-war activists, feminist organizations, and political parties on the basis of their ideology rather than any genuine security threat. The program used surveillance, infiltration, harassment, and disinformation to suppress political movements that were entirely lawful. It was only through congressional investigation — particularly the Church Committee investigations of 1975 and 1976 — and journalistic reporting that the program was exposed and reformed. The lesson is not that intelligence agencies should not investigate genuine threats — they must — but that secret institutions without external oversight will almost inevitably expand their activities beyond their legitimate mandate.

Forms of Accountability

Democratic systems have developed multiple overlapping mechanisms for holding security institutions accountable. Legislative oversight provides democratic accountability through the representatives of the people, though it faces challenges when oversight of classified activities is conducted in secret and may not translate into meaningful public accountability. Judicial review provides legal accountability through the application of constitutional and statutory limits by independent courts, though governments can claim that classified information prevents courts from fully evaluating their justifications, and courts have historically been more deferential on national security matters. Inspector general oversight provides internal accountability through officials with independence from their agencies and access to classified information. Public accountability and journalism provides an external form of accountability that operates largely outside official channels — the publication of the Pentagon Papers, the reporting on NSA surveillance programs, and numerous other investigative journalism projects have informed public debate and prompted institutional reforms that official oversight mechanisms did not achieve. Civil society and advocacy organizations monitor security institutions, litigate civil liberties challenges, and maintain public attention on security policy debates that might otherwise receive little scrutiny.

Rights in the Security Context

The protection of individual rights in the security context is among the most persistently difficult challenges in constitutional law. The right to due process — to fair legal procedures before the government deprives a person of life, liberty, or property — applies to detention, deportation, asset freezing, and other government actions affecting individuals because of alleged security threats. The right against unreasonable searches and seizures applies to government surveillance, searches of digital devices, monitoring of communications, and related investigative activities. The application of this right to digital data, communications metadata, and mass surveillance programs has required courts to apply eighteenth-century legal principles to circumstances the Constitution's authors could not have anticipated. Freedom of speech and association is particularly relevant to the security context because governments have historically used security justifications to suppress political speech and associations they found threatening or inconvenient. The prohibition on torture and cruel, inhuman, or degrading treatment is relevant to interrogation of terrorism suspects and the treatment of prisoners captured in military operations — the debate over "enhanced interrogation techniques" used by the CIA on terrorism suspects after September 11, 2001, raised fundamental questions about where constitutional and international legal prohibitions apply and whether claimed security benefits could justify prohibited treatment.

The Permanent Challenge

Security governance in democratic systems is not a problem to be solved but a challenge to be managed continuously. The threats that governments must address evolve — from conventional military attack, to nuclear deterrence, to terrorism, to cyber operations, to pandemic and climate-related security challenges. The institutional mechanisms of democratic accountability must evolve with them. The most important institutional question is not whether to balance security and liberty but how to organize the institutions, procedures, and oversight mechanisms through which democratic systems manage that balance. A government that is powerful enough to protect its citizens while remaining limited enough to protect their freedom is not a fixed destination but a continuous achievement of institutional design, political commitment, and civic engagement.

Boumediene v. Bush, 553 U.S. 723 (2008) — Justice Kennedy for the Court
Context

Following the September 11, 2001 terrorist attacks, the United States military captured hundreds of individuals in Afghanistan and elsewhere and transported them to the detention facility at Guantanamo Bay, Cuba. The executive branch argued that because Guantanamo was not formally U.S. territory, the detainees held there had no right to challenge their detention in U.S. federal courts. Congress passed the Military Commissions Act of 2006, explicitly stripping federal courts of jurisdiction to hear habeas corpus petitions from Guantanamo detainees. The Supreme Court, in Boumediene v. Bush, held by a five-to-four vote that this jurisdictional stripping was unconstitutional.

Paraphrase of Key Passage
The laws and Constitution are designed to survive, and remain in force, in extraordinary times. Liberty and security can be reconciled; and in our system they are reconciled within the framework of the law.
Analysis

This passage articulates several important constitutional principles. First, it rejects the idea that constitutional protections are suspended during emergencies — the Constitution is designed to survive extraordinary times, not to yield to them. Second, it frames liberty and security not as opposites but as values that can be reconciled within a legal framework. Third, it locates that reconciliation "within the framework of the law" — meaning through established legal processes, including judicial review, rather than through executive discretion unsupervised by courts.

The decision held that the constitutional guarantee of habeas corpus extends to non-citizen detainees held at Guantanamo because the government exercises functional jurisdiction and control over the facility even if it is not formally U.S. territory. Congress cannot strip courts of jurisdiction to hear habeas petitions without meeting the constitutional standard for suspension, which requires an actual rebellion or invasion threatening public safety.

Rights and Responsibilities
Citizens and National Security

The relationship between citizens and government in the domain of national security involves both rights and responsibilities that are sometimes in tension with each other.

Citizens have the right to be protected by their government from violence, attack, and serious threats to their safety. This is among the most basic purposes of political community, and governments that fail to provide this protection have failed a fundamental obligation.

Citizens also have the right to be free from arbitrary government interference with their lives, communications, associations, and political activity. Security cannot justify unlimited surveillance, indefinite detention without charge, the suppression of political dissent, or the targeting of communities based on ethnicity, religion, or political beliefs.

Citizens have civic responsibilities related to national security that include obeying the laws that govern security institutions, paying the taxes that fund defense and security programs, participating in the political processes that determine security policy, and holding security institutions accountable through political engagement, journalism, civic advocacy, and informed public opinion.

Whistleblowing and dissent present one of the most difficult questions at the intersection of citizen responsibility and national security. When government employees believe that security institutions are engaged in unlawful activity, they face a conflict between their legal obligation to protect classified information and their civic obligation to uphold constitutional law. The legal and political treatment of whistleblowers in national security contexts — some of whom have been prosecuted, others celebrated — reflects genuine disagreement about where these obligations lie.

Public engagement with security policy is a civic responsibility that is easy to neglect because security issues are often classified, technical, and conducted by specialized institutions operating largely outside public view. But democratic security policy requires an informed and engaged public — citizens who understand the basic institutional frameworks and who hold their elected representatives accountable for the security decisions made in their name.

Civic Debate
How Much Surveillance Is Too Much?
The Issue

Modern technology has dramatically expanded the capacity of governments to monitor the communications, movements, and activities of their citizens. Counterterrorism intelligence, criminal investigation, and national security operations all benefit from access to vast amounts of data. But these same capabilities raise profound questions about privacy, civil liberties, and the relationship between citizens and the state.

Argument A — Broad Surveillance Is Necessary for Effective Security

Proponents argue that modern terrorist threats, cybercriminal networks, and foreign intelligence operations are sophisticated and capable of causing catastrophic harm. Detecting and disrupting these threats requires intelligence capabilities that can identify patterns across vast amounts of communications and financial data, flagging potentially suspicious connections before they become active threats. Restricting surveillance capabilities creates gaps that adversaries will exploit, and those gaps can lead to attacks that claim innocent lives. The government's first responsibility is to protect its citizens, and meeting that responsibility in a world of asymmetric threats requires powerful surveillance tools.

Argument B — Broad Surveillance Is Incompatible with a Free Society

Critics argue that the capacity to monitor communications, track movements, and analyze the digital activities of millions of people is inherently incompatible with the civil liberties that democratic societies are supposed to protect. When people know or suspect that their communications are monitored, they self-censor — chilling the free expression essential to democratic deliberation. Surveillance capabilities have historically been used to target political opponents, civil rights leaders, journalists, and members of minority communities. And the concentration of so much information about citizens in government hands creates a power asymmetry that threatens democratic governance itself.

Other Perspectives

Some analysts argue that the effectiveness of broad surveillance for security purposes has been exaggerated — that successful counterterrorism more often depends on targeted intelligence and human sources than on mass data collection — and that surveillance programs should be evaluated rigorously for their actual effectiveness, not simply their theoretical potential. International perspectives add another dimension: surveillance programs operated by democratic countries may also target the citizens and governments of allied countries, raising questions about sovereignty and the norms governing state behavior in cyberspace.

Tradeoffs

The surveillance debate involves real tradeoffs. Effective counterterrorism does benefit from intelligence, and some intelligence requires monitoring activities. But the scope of surveillance, its targeting, its legal authorization, and its effectiveness relative to less intrusive alternatives all matter. Systems that maximize security at any cost to civil liberties may undermine the democratic values they are supposed to protect; systems that prohibit all government monitoring may fail to detect genuine threats. The challenge is to design surveillance authorities that are effective and accountable, proportionate to genuine threats, authorized by democratic processes, and subject to meaningful judicial and legislative review.

Policy Lab
Emergency Declaration Oversight After a Cyberattack
Scenario

A government has declared a national emergency in response to a significant cyberattack on the power grid, which disrupted electricity to millions of citizens and caused significant economic harm. The emergency declaration activates dozens of special statutory authorities, including provisions allowing the executive to direct private companies to prioritize certain activities, commandeer equipment, and restrict certain digital communications. After six months, the power grid has been restored, but the emergency declaration remains in effect. The executive branch argues that ongoing vulnerability to future attacks justifies maintaining emergency authorities. Critics argue that the emergency has passed and that continuing the declaration enables unjustified executive power expansion.

Stakeholders

The executive branch values the flexibility the emergency declaration provides. The legislature has concerns about the indefinite concentration of authority in the executive. The judiciary may be asked to determine whether the declaration remains legally justified. Private companies are affected by the executive's emergency authorities. Civil liberties organizations are concerned about surveillance and communication restrictions. Citizens care about both cybersecurity protection and constitutional norms. The media is covering the controversy and investigating the use of emergency authorities.

Policy Options
  1. Let the emergency declaration expire automatically and return to normal legal frameworks, relying on regular statutory authority to continue cybersecurity improvements.
  2. Require congressional reauthorization of specific emergency provisions every ninety days, maintaining legislative oversight while preserving needed authorities.
  3. Narrow the emergency declaration to only those specific authorities still needed for ongoing cybersecurity remediation, with sunset of remaining provisions within a specified period.
  4. Create a standing emergency oversight board — including legislative, judicial, and independent members — with authority to review and terminate specific emergency provisions that are no longer warranted.
  5. Maintain the emergency declaration indefinitely, with regular executive reporting to Congress on the use of emergency authorities.
Evidence Needed

Students would need to know the specific statutory authorities activated by the emergency declaration, their use since activation, evidence of ongoing versus resolved threats, the technical assessment of continued vulnerability, legal analysis of the scope of executive authority to maintain the declaration, and comparative analysis of how other countries have handled similar situations.

Tradeoffs
  • Options giving the executive more flexibility may provide better security outcomes if the threat remains genuine but may also enable the accumulation of executive power that is difficult to reverse.
  • Options imposing stronger legislative or judicial oversight may better protect constitutional norms but may slow government response if a new emergency arises.
  • Standing oversight bodies may provide a middle path but create new institutional complexity and potential for conflict among oversight actors.

Chapter 40 — Common Misunderstandings

Clearing Up Common Errors

Misunderstanding 1
"The president can do whatever is necessary to protect national security."
Correction
In democratic constitutional systems, executive power — including the president's authority as commander-in-chief — is limited by the constitution, statutes, and judicial review. Even in genuine national security emergencies, the executive cannot violate constitutional rights, ignore congressional limitations, or exempt itself from judicial review. The Supreme Court has repeatedly affirmed, even in wartime, that executive authority has constitutional limits. The scope of those limits is often disputed and litigated, but the principle of limited executive power applies even in security contexts.
Misunderstanding 2
"Military forces are subject only to military law, not civilian courts."
Correction
While military personnel are subject to the Uniform Code of Military Justice and military courts for many purposes, military forces and the executive institutions that direct them are still subject to constitutional limits, congressional oversight, and judicial review. The Supreme Court has jurisdiction to review military tribunal decisions in cases raising constitutional questions. Military forces operating domestically are also subject to various statutory constraints, including the Posse Comitatus Act, which limits their ability to perform civilian law enforcement functions.
Misunderstanding 3
"Intelligence agencies can only spy on foreigners, not Americans."
Correction
The legal framework governing intelligence surveillance of American citizens and foreign nationals is complex, and its boundaries have been extensively litigated and debated. Intelligence agencies have legal authorities to monitor Americans under various circumstances, including with Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court warrants, under specific criminal investigation authorities, and through the collection of data from foreign communications that happens to include American communications. The scope and legal basis of these authorities have been controversial, and reforms have been enacted to limit certain forms of collection, but the simple assumption that intelligence agencies are prohibited from monitoring Americans is incorrect.
Misunderstanding 4
"Emergency powers are only used in genuine emergencies."
Correction
The history of emergency powers in many countries demonstrates that emergency declarations are sometimes maintained long after the original crisis has passed, are sometimes used to address situations that could be handled through normal legal processes, and are sometimes invoked for political purposes unrelated to genuine emergency conditions. The United States has maintained numerous emergency declarations for years or decades after the events that originally justified them. Democratic systems have sought to address this problem through sunset clauses, renewal requirements, and oversight mechanisms, but the tendency toward emergency power accumulation remains a persistent institutional challenge.
Misunderstanding 5
"Surveillance of foreign communications does not affect Americans."
Correction
Modern digital communications routinely cross national borders — emails, text messages, social media posts, and phone calls may be routed through servers in foreign countries even when both parties are in the same country. Intelligence collection of foreign communications thus frequently includes communications involving Americans, creating constitutional questions about the application of Fourth Amendment protections. NSA surveillance programs disclosed in 2013 involved collection of communications data that included significant amounts of American content, even though the formal legal authority targeted foreign communications.
Misunderstanding 6
"Civil-military relations is not an issue in established democracies."
Correction
While established democracies with long histories of civilian control are generally more stable than newer democracies, civil-military relations is an ongoing institutional challenge rather than a problem solved once and for all. Senior military officers in established democracies have sometimes publicly disagreed with civilian policy or attempted to influence policy through unauthorized channels. Civilians have sometimes sought to politicize military institutions or use military authority for partisan purposes. The institutional mechanisms of civilian control require ongoing maintenance and attention, not merely historical establishment.
Misunderstanding 7
"Cybersecurity is a purely technical issue that governments should leave to technical experts."
Correction
While cybersecurity involves significant technical complexity, the decisions about how to organize cybersecurity institutions, what authorities to grant security agencies, how to balance offensive and defensive capabilities, how to regulate private sector security practices, and how to engage with other countries on cybersecurity norms are fundamentally political and policy decisions requiring democratic deliberation. Technical experts play a crucial role in informing these decisions, but the decisions themselves involve value choices — between security and privacy, between government authority and private autonomy, between international cooperation and national advantage — that are properly subject to democratic accountability.
Misunderstanding 8
"A country with a strong military is automatically safe."
Correction
Military strength is one component of national security but does not guarantee safety. Countries with powerful militaries can still experience terrorism, cyberattacks, internal instability, economic crises, natural disasters, and other security challenges that military force is not well-suited to address. Moreover, excessive emphasis on military solutions can divert resources from diplomacy, development, intelligence, and law enforcement approaches that may be more effective for certain types of threats. Security is multidimensional, and effective governance requires a portfolio of approaches rather than a singular focus on military capability.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 40.1Security is a foundational government function — among the most basic purposes for which political communities are formed and governments organized. Security encompasses national defense, domestic public safety, homeland security, cybersecurity, and economic security. The most persistent political challenge in security governance is the tension between security and liberty, and understanding the institutional frameworks through which democratic systems manage that tension is essential to civic literacy.
  • Sec 40.2National defense — maintaining military forces capable of deterring and responding to external threats — is the most resource-intensive security function of modern governments. Constitutional systems distribute authority over military forces among branches of government, with legislatures typically holding power over authorization and appropriation and executives holding operational command. International law, particularly the UN Charter and the Geneva Conventions, places significant constraints on when and how states may use military force, though the application of these frameworks to modern conflicts has been extensively contested.
  • Sec 40.3Civil-military relations — the institutional relationship between armed forces and civilian democratic government — is a persistent challenge in every political system that maintains significant military forces. The principle of civilian control holds that military forces should be subordinate to elected civilian authority and should implement civilian policy decisions rather than determine them. Maintaining civilian control requires not only formal institutional arrangements — chains of command, legislative oversight, civilian defense leadership — but also a professional military culture that values political neutrality and constitutional loyalty.
  • Sec 40.4Intelligence agencies present distinctive accountability challenges for democratic systems. Effective intelligence depends on secrecy that is fundamentally at odds with democratic transparency. Democratic governments have developed mechanisms for overseeing intelligence agencies while preserving operational secrecy — congressional committees, inspector general offices, specialized courts — but these mechanisms have significant limitations. History has repeatedly shown that absence of oversight enables abuse, from COINTELPRO in the 1960s to mass surveillance programs in the early twenty-first century.
  • Sec 40.5Terrorism creates particular challenges for democratic governance because it is designed to provoke overreaction, blurs domestic and international boundaries, creates pressure for surveillance and detention authorities that may conflict with civil liberties, and involves inherently difficult tradeoffs between security effectiveness and constitutional norms. Democratic systems have responded through combinations of law enforcement, military, and intelligence approaches, with different countries emphasizing different institutional frameworks reflecting their constitutional structures, legal traditions, and political cultures.
  • Sec 40.6Cybersecurity represents a rapidly growing security domain that presents distinctive challenges for government. Digital systems that support critical infrastructure, government operations, and economic activity are vulnerable to espionage, sabotage, disruption, and influence operations from state and non-state actors. The protection of critical infrastructure is complicated by the primarily private ownership of most critical systems. Attribution — determining who is responsible for cyberattacks — is technically difficult and complicates deterrence strategies. Governance of cybersecurity requires coordination across institutional boundaries that no existing institution is fully equipped to manage alone.
  • Sec 40.7Emergency powers — the authority that constitutional systems grant governments to take extraordinary action during genuine crises — are present in virtually every democratic constitutional order. The rationale for emergency powers is clear, but they are also among the most potentially dangerous constitutional provisions, as they can be used to justify the permanent suspension of normal constraints. Democratic constitutions have attempted to limit emergency powers through temporal limits, legislative authorization requirements, judicial review, non-derogable rights, and sunset clauses, with varying degrees of effectiveness. Political scientists have identified emergency powers as a primary mechanism through which democratic systems can be gradually transformed into authoritarian ones.
  • Sec 40.8Accountability — the ability of citizens, representatives, and courts to know what security institutions are doing and to hold them responsible for their conduct — is not merely a value in tension with security but is itself security-relevant. Security institutions without meaningful oversight have repeatedly demonstrated their tendency toward abuse and institutional self-interest. The mechanisms of security accountability — legislative oversight, judicial review, inspector general investigations, journalism, and civil society advocacy — are imperfect but essential to maintaining the democratic character of security governance. Rights protection in the security context is not merely a constraint on effective security but an expression of the values that democratic governments exist to protect.

The institutional, legal, cultural, and political relationship between a country's military forces and its civilian government, particularly the question of whether military forces are subordinate to civilian democratic authority.
Civilian control of the military
The principle that military forces should be subordinate to elected civilian authority and should implement civilian policy decisions rather than determining them.
Military chain of command
The hierarchical structure through which military orders flow from the commander-in-chief through civilian defense officials to uniformed military leadership and subordinate units.
Intelligence community
The collection of agencies within a government that contribute to national intelligence, including agencies responsible for human intelligence, signals intelligence, imagery intelligence, and analysis.
Covert operations
Secret activities conducted by intelligence agencies intended to influence conditions in foreign countries in ways that are not publicly attributed to the government conducting them.
Counterterrorism
The policies, strategies, tactics, and institutions through which governments seek to prevent, detect, disrupt, and respond to terrorism.
Cybersecurity
The protection of computer systems, networks, and digital data from unauthorized access, disruption, or damage.
Critical infrastructure
Physical and digital systems whose disruption would have severe consequences for national security, economic stability, public health, or public safety, including energy, water, transportation, and financial systems.
Emergency powers
Provisions in constitutional documents or statutes authorizing governments to take actions during times of crisis that would not be permitted under ordinary legal rules.
Martial law
The temporary substitution of military authority for civilian government, with military courts replacing civilian tribunals and military commanders exercising civilian governmental functions.
State of emergency
A formal declaration that a crisis exists justifying the government exercising extraordinary powers beyond those available under normal legal frameworks.
War powers
The constitutional and statutory authorities governing the executive's ability to use military force and the legislature's role in authorizing and limiting that use.
AUMF
Authorization for Use of Military Force — a congressional resolution authorizing the president to use military force in a specific context, short of a formal declaration of war.
Surveillance
The monitoring of individuals' communications, movements, associations, or activities by government authorities for intelligence or law enforcement purposes.
Habeas corpus
The legal principle and court procedure requiring that a government justify the imprisonment of a person before a court; a fundamental protection against arbitrary government detention.
Due process
The constitutional requirement that the government must follow fair procedures before depriving a person of life, liberty, or property, including in the context of security-related detention and prosecution.
Enemy combatant
A legal designation used in post-September 11 American law for persons captured in connection with armed conflict who are subject to military detention rather than civilian criminal prosecution; extensively litigated in federal courts.
Geneva Conventions
The series of international treaties governing the treatment of wounded and sick military personnel, prisoners of war, and civilians in armed conflict, establishing humanitarian standards for the conduct of war.
Deterrence
A strategy aimed at preventing adversaries from taking threatening actions — particularly military attack — by threatening unacceptable consequences, requiring both demonstrated capability and credible will to respond.
Posse Comitatus Act
A U.S. federal law enacted in 1878 prohibiting the use of federal military forces to perform domestic law enforcement functions without specific authorization by Congress or the Constitution.
Inspector general
An independent official within a government agency responsible for investigating complaints, auditing operations, and reporting findings to the agency head and to congressional oversight bodies.
Congressional oversight
The monitoring, investigation, and evaluation of executive branch activities by Congress, including through hearings, investigations, budget review, and the confirmation of senior officials.
Whistleblower
An individual who discloses information about alleged unlawful or improper activity within a government agency or other institution, often in violation of confidentiality obligations, in order to expose wrongdoing.
Non-derogable rights
Rights that cannot be suspended even during a genuine public emergency; recognized in international human rights law as absolute, including the prohibition on torture and freedom from arbitrary execution.
Counterintelligence
The activities directed at identifying, assessing, and neutralizing the intelligence operations of foreign governments and adversarial organizations directed against a country's government, military, and citizens.

The War Powers Resolution (1973) — Key Provisions
Document Type

Federal Statute — Congressional response to the Vietnam War's expansion without formal declaration of war.

Historical and Political Context

The War Powers Resolution was enacted by Congress in 1973, over President Nixon's veto, in response to the sustained use of military force in Southeast Asia without a formal declaration of war. The Resolution attempted to reassert legislative authority over military commitments by requiring presidents to consult with Congress before introducing forces into hostilities, to report to Congress within forty-eight hours of doing so, and to withdraw forces within sixty days absent congressional authorization. It has been applied to — and disputed in — every major military engagement since its passage.

Text (Paraphrase of Key Provisions)
The president shall consult with Congress before introducing United States armed forces into hostilities. The president shall report to Congress in writing within 48 hours of introducing forces. The use of forces shall terminate within 60 days absent a declaration of war, a specific statutory authorization, or an extension by Congress. Congress may direct the removal of forces at any time by concurrent resolution.
Questions for Analysis

  2. Presidents since 1973 have generally complied with reporting requirements while disputing the constitutionality of the sixty-day termination provision. What does this selective compliance reveal about the limitations of statutory constraints on executive power?
  3. The Resolution has never been successfully enforced to terminate a military engagement. Does this mean it has been a failure? What other effects might it have had even without direct enforcement?
  4. How does the War Powers Resolution connect to the chapter's broader themes about the distribution of security authority across branches of government and the challenge of maintaining congressional oversight of executive security actions?

Chapter 40 — Closing Reflection

Connecting Security to the Larger Study of Government

Security governance sits at the intersection of some of the most fundamental questions in democratic political life: How much power should governments hold? How can that power be held accountable? What tradeoffs between collective safety and individual freedom are acceptable, and who gets to decide? How do constitutional frameworks designed for ordinary times apply in extraordinary circumstances? These are not technical questions with technical answers; they are political questions that every generation of citizens in a democratic society must grapple with for itself.

The institutions examined in this chapter — military forces, intelligence agencies, emergency management bodies, cybersecurity agencies, counterterrorism programs — are not abstract organizational charts. They are staffed by real people, operating under real legal authorities, making real decisions that affect real lives. The oversight mechanisms through which democratic societies hold these institutions accountable are similarly real: congressional hearings, judicial decisions, inspector general investigations, journalistic reporting, and the engaged political participation of informed citizens. The quality of democratic security governance depends on the quality of these accountability mechanisms, and the quality of these accountability mechanisms depends on the capacity and willingness of citizens to take them seriously.

The most important analytical insight this chapter offers is that security and liberty are not simply opposites on a scale — that gaining more of one necessarily costs more of the other. Rather, they are values that can be advanced together through careful institutional design, or undermined together through institutional failure. A government that abandons constitutional principles in the name of security may succeed in suppressing an immediate threat while creating deeper insecurity through the erosion of legitimacy, public trust, and the rule of law that sustain democratic life. A government that maintains its constitutional principles even under genuine threat demonstrates that liberal democracy is not a fragile system that can only survive in comfortable times, but a resilient order capable of confronting danger without becoming what it opposes.

As you continue through this textbook into comparative government, international relations, and global governance, carry with you this chapter's central insight: the challenge of security governance is inseparable from the challenge of constitutional democracy itself. Every decision about how to protect citizens from violence is also a decision about what kind of political community those citizens inhabit — what values it upholds, what limits it respects, and what it is ultimately worth protecting.

Chapter 23 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every democracy faces a fundamental translation problem. Millions of individual citizens cast votes expressing their preferences, fears, hopes, and judgments about who should govern. Those votes must somehow be converted into a working government — a parliament with seats filled, a president elected, a city council constituted, a legislature empowered to make binding decisions on behalf of the whole community. The machinery that performs this translation is called an electoral system, and the choices embedded in that machinery are among the most consequential choices any democracy makes.

Electoral systems are not neutral. They shape which voices get heard, which parties survive, which candidates have a realistic chance of winning, how many citizens feel their vote had meaning, and how governments are ultimately formed and held accountable. A country that uses one method of converting votes into seats will produce reliably different political outcomes than a country with identical voter preferences but a different method. Scholars who study comparative government have documented these differences carefully over many decades, and the evidence is substantial: electoral rules matter enormously.

Consider two democracies, each holding a national legislative election. In Country A, the territory is divided into hundreds of single-member districts, and in each district the candidate with the most votes wins that seat — even if they received only thirty-five percent of the vote and the remaining sixty-five percent of voters preferred someone else. In Country B, voters cast ballots for political parties rather than individual candidates, and seats are distributed to parties in rough proportion to the share of votes each party received nationwide. The same distribution of voter preferences will produce dramatically different legislative compositions in these two countries.

Neither of these outcomes is simply "right" or "wrong." Each reflects genuine values in tension with one another. Single-member district systems tend to produce stable majority governments, clear accountability, and strong geographic representation. Proportional systems tend to produce legislatures that more accurately mirror the full range of voter opinion and reduce the share of voters whose ballots seem to produce no result. Each system makes tradeoffs, and understanding those tradeoffs is essential to understanding how democracies actually function.

This chapter explores electoral systems and representation in depth — how different rules for counting votes produce different political outcomes, why the design of electoral systems is itself a major political question, how representation is understood in different democratic traditions, and what problems each major type of system faces. It also addresses gerrymandering, the representation of minorities and smaller parties, and how electoral rules interact with party systems to shape the broader landscape of democratic politics.

Opening Question

Electoral systems are not technical curiosities for specialists. They are the rules that determine who gets power, who gets represented, and who gets heard. Every citizen who participates in democratic life is participating in a system shaped by these rules, whether they are aware of it or not. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to explain how those rules work — and why two democracies with identical voter preferences can end up with very different governments.

Section 23.1

What Electoral Systems Do

The Translation Problem in Democracy. Democratic government rests on the idea that political authority should reflect the will and consent of the governed. But translating the scattered preferences of millions of individuals into a functioning government requires procedures — rules for who can vote, what choices they can make, how votes are counted, and how the results determine who holds office. These rules constitute an electoral system.

At the most basic level, an electoral system must accomplish several things. It must transform votes cast by individual citizens into the allocation of political offices: legislative seats, executive positions, and sometimes judicial appointments. It must do so in a way that most participants regard as fair, transparent, and legitimate. And it must produce a government capable of exercising authority, making decisions, and being held accountable when the next election arrives.

These goals are not always fully compatible. An electoral system that maximizes proportional representation of diverse voter preferences may make it more difficult to form a stable majority government. A system that reliably produces stable majority governments may do so by giving disproportionately large power to whichever party receives the most votes, leaving large minorities of voters feeling unrepresented. Every electoral system involves choices among competing values.

Electoral systems also shape the strategic behavior of voters, candidates, and parties. When voters know that only the top vote-getter in a district wins a seat, they may choose to vote for the candidate they think can actually win rather than the candidate they most prefer — a behavior called strategic or tactical voting. When voters know that any party crossing a modest threshold of support will receive seats in proportion to its vote share, they may feel freer to vote sincerely for smaller parties they genuinely prefer. These behavioral effects compound over time into distinctive party systems, political cultures, and patterns of democratic competition.

Components of an Electoral System

An electoral system has several identifiable components, each of which can be varied independently to produce different effects.

ComponentWhat It DeterminesExample Variation
District magnitudeHow many seats are filled from a given constituencySingle-member vs. multi-member districts
Electoral formulaThe mathematical rule converting votes into seatsPlurality, majority, or proportional formula
Ballot structureHow voters express their preferencesOne vote for a candidate vs. ranked preferences
ThresholdMinimum vote share required for seat eligibilityGermany's 5% vs. Netherlands' ~0.67%

As district magnitude increases — meaning more seats are filled from a single constituency — proportional representation of minority preferences generally improves. Plurality formulas give the seat to whoever receives the most votes, without requiring any particular share of the total. Majority formulas require a winning threshold, often fifty percent plus one vote, which may require multiple rounds of voting. Proportional formulas distribute seats in proportion to vote shares, using various mathematical methods to handle remainders.

Thresholds are used in proportional systems to prevent the legislature from fragmenting into dozens of tiny parties. Germany requires parties to receive at least five percent of the national vote before receiving any seats. The Netherlands has a threshold so low that it effectively requires only enough votes to fill one seat. Higher thresholds produce more concentrated party systems; lower thresholds allow more diverse representation.

Why Design Matters

Single-member plurality systems tend to produce two dominant parties and stable majority governments, but often produce legislatures that do not proportionally reflect voter preferences. Proportional systems tend to produce more parties and more diverse legislatures, but often require coalition governments. Mixed systems try to capture advantages of both, with tradeoffs of their own.

Section 23.2

Single-Member Districts

A single-member district (SMD) system divides the voting population into geographic constituencies, each of which elects exactly one representative. The United States uses single-member districts for elections to the House of Representatives: the country is divided into 435 congressional districts, each of which elects one member. The United Kingdom uses single-member districts for elections to the House of Commons. Canada, India, and many former British colonies use similar systems.

The geographic character of single-member districts is one of their defining features. Each district represents a defined territory, and the representative elected from that district is understood to represent the people of that specific place — not just the voters who supported them, and not the national electorate as a whole. Because each district elects exactly one member, there is by definition one winner and everyone else loses. This binary outcome is the source of both the system's strengths and its most significant weaknesses.

Single-member districts create a direct, visible connection between a legislator and a specific community. Voters know who their representative is; they can contact that representative, attend town hall meetings, hold the representative accountable for votes taken in the legislature, and — at the next election — decide whether to return that representative to office. This accountability relationship reflects a longstanding idea in democratic theory: that communities, not just individuals or abstract ideological tendencies, deserve representation.

Civic Vocabulary
Electoral Disproportionality

Electoral disproportionality refers to the gap between the share of votes a party receives and the share of legislative seats it receives. A perfectly proportional system would give a party that receives thirty percent of votes exactly thirty percent of seats. Most real electoral systems produce some disproportionality; the question is how much.

Single-member plurality systems typically produce high disproportionality — the winning party often receives a much larger share of seats than votes, and smaller parties often receive a much smaller share. Disproportionality matters because it affects whether citizens feel their votes had meaning and whether the legislature accurately reflects the preferences of the electorate.

Section 23.3

Plurality and Majority Systems

Plurality Systems: First Past the Post. The most widely used variant of the single-member district system is the plurality system, commonly called "first past the post" (FPTP). In a plurality system, the candidate with the most votes in a district wins, regardless of what share of the total vote they received. There is no minimum threshold. If five candidates split the vote as thirty percent, twenty-eight percent, twenty-two percent, twelve percent, and eight percent, the first candidate wins with thirty percent — even though seventy percent of voters preferred someone else.

First past the post is used for elections to the United States House of Representatives, the British House of Commons, the Canadian House of Commons, the Indian Lok Sabha, and many other legislative bodies around the world. Its simplicity and the clear winners it produces are among its most frequently cited advantages.

The Problem of the "Manufactured Majority." One of the most significant critiques of first-past-the-post systems is their tendency to manufacture legislative majorities that do not reflect genuine majority support in the electorate. A party that receives forty-two percent of votes cast nationally may win sixty percent of legislative seats if its support is geographically distributed to win many districts by modest margins.

The United Kingdom provides a dramatic illustration. In the 2015 general election, the Conservative Party received approximately thirty-seven percent of votes but won fifty-one percent of seats in the House of Commons, securing an overall majority government. The United Kingdom Independence Party received nearly thirteen percent of votes nationally but won only one of 650 seats. The Scottish National Party received under five percent of national votes but won fifty-six seats — more than forty times as many as UKIP — because its support was geographically concentrated in Scotland. The electoral system, not the distribution of voter preferences alone, produced these outcomes.

This phenomenon is called a "manufactured majority" — a legislative majority assembled from a plurality of votes through the disproportionating effects of the electoral system. Critics argue that governments claiming to represent the will of the people when they were elected by a minority of voters are making a questionable claim. Defenders respond that manufactured majorities enable stable, accountable governance, which is itself a democratic value.

Majority Systems and the Two-Round System. Unlike plurality systems, majority systems require that the winning candidate actually receive a majority — typically more than fifty percent of votes cast. The most common solution to handling races where no candidate initially reaches that threshold is the two-round system, or runoff. In the first round, all candidates compete. If no candidate receives a majority, the two leading candidates advance to a second round, where a majority is guaranteed because voters must choose between exactly two options.

France uses a two-round system for its presidential and legislative elections. Presidential candidates who receive more than fifty percent in the first round win immediately; otherwise, the top two candidates contest a second round two weeks later. This system ensures that the eventual winner has the support of at least a plurality of voters in head-to-head competition.

Some systems require an absolute majority but handle the multi-candidate problem differently. Australia's system for electing the House of Representatives uses preferential voting — voters rank candidates in order of preference, and candidates are eliminated from the bottom up, with their votes redistributed according to voter rankings, until one candidate has an absolute majority. This produces a majority winner without requiring voters to return to the polls for a second round.

How the System Works
First Past the Post — From Votes to Seats
  • District boundaries are established. The country or state is divided into geographic districts, each of which will elect one representative. In the United States, congressional district boundaries are redrawn after each decennial census.
  • Candidates qualify and campaign. Candidates qualify for the ballot through party primaries, petition signature requirements, filing fees, or combinations of these.
  • Voters cast ballots. On election day, voters in each district cast one vote for one candidate.
  • Votes are counted within each district. Tabulation happens independently; the results in one district have no direct effect on results elsewhere.
  • The plurality winner is declared. The candidate with the most votes wins, regardless of total votes cast or share received. There is no minimum threshold.
  • Seat counts are aggregated nationally. The total number of seats won by each party across all districts determines the composition of the legislature.
  • Government formation follows. If one party has won a majority of seats, it typically forms the government. If no party has a majority, negotiation among parties is required.

Complications and exceptions: In some systems, candidates must win by an absolute majority rather than a plurality, triggering a second round if necessary. In others, primary elections precede general elections, adding a prior step. Independent candidates can win seats under FPTP, though this is uncommon. Special elections may be held to fill vacancies between regular election cycles.

Section 23.4

Proportional Representation

The Core Idea. Proportional representation (PR) systems start from a different premise than single-member district systems. Rather than asking "who wins each district," PR systems ask "how should the total distribution of voter preferences be reflected in the total composition of the legislature?" The goal is to ensure that a party receiving twenty percent of votes nationwide receives approximately twenty percent of legislative seats.

This approach reflects a particular conception of democratic representation: that a legislature should be a microcosm of the electorate, reflecting the full range of political preferences held by citizens, not just the preferences of geographic pluralities. Proportional representation is used in various forms by most democracies in continental Europe, as well as many democracies in Latin America, Africa, Asia, and elsewhere.

Party List Systems. The most common form of proportional representation is the party list system. Instead of voting for an individual candidate in a local district, voters cast a ballot for a political party. Each party prepares a ranked list of candidates before the election. After votes are counted, seats are distributed to parties in proportion to their vote shares, and each party fills its seats from the top of its candidate list.

Countries using party list systems include the Netherlands, Israel, Spain, Sweden, Norway, Denmark, Portugal, and many others. A potential problem with proportional representation is legislative fragmentation: if every small party that receives even a few percent of votes receives seats, the legislature could consist of dozens of tiny parties, making it nearly impossible to form a stable working majority. To address this, most PR systems establish an electoral threshold.

Germany's electoral threshold is five percent of the national vote (with an alternative threshold of three direct constituency wins). A party that receives four and a half percent of votes receives no seats at all, even though under pure proportionality it would be entitled to roughly twenty seats in a 440-member Bundestag. Higher thresholds — Turkey has historically used ten percent — can exclude substantial portions of the electorate from representation. Lower thresholds, like the Netherlands' effective threshold of just under one percent, allow very diverse legislatures but may make coalition formation more complex.

Mathematical Formulas for Seat Allocation. The actual mechanics of converting vote shares into seat allocations involves mathematical formulas that handle the unavoidable problem of rounding. If a party receives 23.7 percent of votes and there are 100 seats to fill, it cannot receive exactly 23.7 seats — it must receive either 23 or 24. Several different formulas are commonly used.

Student Note

These technical differences matter at the margins — they can affect whether a party receives one more or one fewer seat, which can in turn affect coalition negotiations and governmental composition. These mathematical rules are themselves political choices, not purely neutral technical solutions.

Civic Vocabulary
Proportional Representation

Proportional representation is a family of electoral systems in which seats in a legislature are distributed to parties in rough proportion to their share of the total votes cast. The defining principle is that a party's share of legislative seats should approximately match its share of voter support.

PR systems contrast with majoritarian systems, which award seats based on winning plurality or majority competitions in individual districts. The key tradeoff is between governmental stability (often stronger in majoritarian systems) and representational accuracy (generally better in proportional systems).

Section 23.5

Ranked-Choice and Preferential Voting

Plurality voting systems face a structural dilemma when more than two candidates compete for a single seat. If voters' preferences are split among multiple candidates, the winner may receive only a modest share of the total vote — sometimes less than a third — while the majority of voters preferred someone else. Voters who genuinely prefer a third-party candidate may feel compelled to vote for their "lesser of two evils" among the leading candidates, in order to prevent their least-preferred candidate from winning.

Ranked-choice voting (RCV) — also called instant-runoff voting, preferential voting, or the alternative vote — is designed to address these problems. Instead of casting a single vote for one candidate, voters rank the candidates in order of preference: first choice, second choice, third choice, and so on. This ballot structure allows voters to express their genuine preferences without fearing that voting sincerely for a minor candidate will "waste" their vote.

How the System Works
Ranked-Choice Voting — Counting the Ballots
  • Count first-choice votes. If any candidate has a majority of first-choice votes (more than fifty percent), that candidate wins immediately.
  • Eliminate the last-place candidate. If no candidate has a majority, the candidate with the fewest first-choice votes is eliminated.
  • Transfer eliminated ballots. All ballots that had listed the eliminated candidate as their first choice now transfer to those voters' second-choice candidates.
  • Recount and repeat. First-choice and transferred votes are recounted. If any remaining candidate now has a majority, that candidate wins. Otherwise, the process repeats.
  • Declare the majority winner. This continues — eliminating the last-place candidate and redistributing ballots — until one candidate has a majority of all active ballots.

The result is that the winner has demonstrated majority support, at least in the sense that more than half of voters who expressed a preference among the final two candidates preferred them. Voters who preferred eliminated candidates are not left without a voice; their votes transfer to their best remaining option.

Where Ranked-Choice Voting Is Used. Australia has used preferential voting for its House of Representatives elections since 1918, making it one of the most extensively tested applications of ranked-choice voting in the world. Third parties and independents can compete more vigorously, because voters who support them need not fear "wasting" their vote if the candidate is eliminated. The two major parties remain dominant, but preference flows from smaller parties and independents play a significant role in determining outcomes in competitive seats.

In the United States, ranked-choice voting has been adopted for some elections at the state and local level. Maine became the first state to use RCV for statewide federal elections, having adopted it through a citizen initiative in 2016. New York City adopted RCV for its primary elections in 2021. Alaska adopted a distinctive system in 2020 that combines a nonpartisan primary with ranked-choice voting in the general election. Several cities across the United States use RCV for local elections, including Minneapolis and San Francisco.

The Single Transferable Vote. A particularly influential variant of ranked-choice voting is the Single Transferable Vote (STV), which combines ranked ballots with multi-member districts to achieve proportional representation while maintaining a preference for individual candidates rather than party lists. Ireland uses STV for its Dáil elections; Scotland uses it for local government elections.

Under STV, each electoral district elects multiple representatives — typically three to seven. Voters rank all candidates. A quota is established, calculated as a fraction of the total votes cast in the district. Candidates who immediately reach the quota are elected, and their surplus votes transfer to second-ranked candidates. Candidates at the bottom are eliminated and their votes also transfer. This continues until all seats are filled. STV produces roughly proportional outcomes across a multi-member district while preserving the ability of voters to choose among individual candidates from the same party.

Section 23.6

Districting and Gerrymandering

In any electoral system that uses geographic districts, someone must draw the boundaries of those districts. This is not a purely technical task. District boundaries determine which neighborhoods, communities, and populations are grouped together for electoral purposes, which profoundly affects which candidates and parties are likely to win — and therefore which party is likely to control the resulting legislature.

In the United States, congressional and state legislative district lines are typically redrawn every ten years following the decennial census. The drawing of these lines is conducted differently in different states — by state legislatures, by independent or bipartisan commissions, or by hybrid bodies — and the process is almost always politically contested. A party that controls the redistricting process can draw lines that pack its opponents' voters into a few districts or crack them across multiple districts. These strategies can translate a modest statewide voting advantage into a substantial majority of legislative seats.

The practice of drawing district lines to advantage a particular party, group, or candidate is called gerrymandering. The term dates to 1812, when Massachusetts Governor Elbridge Gerry approved a state senate district whose contorted shape was compared to a salamander. A Boston newspaper combined his name with the animal to coin the term.

Modern gerrymandering takes two primary forms. Partisan gerrymandering draws district lines to maximize the seat share of one political party, regardless of the geographic distribution of its voters. Racial gerrymandering draws district lines with the intent of diluting or manipulating the voting power of racial or ethnic communities — generally prohibited under federal law, though the distinction between illegal racial dilution and permissible partisan drawing has been the subject of sustained legal controversy.

TechniqueHow It Works
PackingConcentrates opposing voters into as few districts as possible, so excess votes beyond what is needed to win are "wasted"
CrackingSplits opposing voters across multiple districts where they form a minority in each, so their votes never produce a winning plurality

The combination of packing and cracking can allow the party controlling redistricting to win a majority of legislative seats while receiving fewer total votes than the opposing party.

Legal and Institutional Responses. Courts have ruled that racial gerrymandering intended to dilute minority voting power violates the Equal Protection Clause and may also violate the Voting Rights Act. However, in Rucho v. Common Cause (2019), the Supreme Court ruled that federal courts cannot hear challenges to partisan gerrymandering, because such claims present political questions courts cannot resolve. State courts may still hear partisan gerrymandering challenges under state constitutional provisions.

Independent redistricting commissions have been adopted in several states — including California, Arizona, Colorado, and Michigan — as an alternative to legislative redistricting. Evidence suggests these commissions produce less extreme gerrymanders, though they do not eliminate all partisan effects. Mathematical standards, such as the "efficiency gap" measure, have been proposed to objectively assess the partisan fairness of maps by calculating the difference in wasted votes between the two major parties.

Malapportionment. A related problem is malapportionment — drawing districts that contain significantly different populations. If one congressional district contains 500,000 voters and an adjacent district contains 800,000, each district elects one representative, but the smaller district's voters have much greater per-capita influence. The Supreme Court addressed malapportionment in a series of cases in the 1960s, establishing the "one person, one vote" principle: districts within the same legislative body must be approximately equal in population. This requirement applies to congressional and state legislative districts but does not apply to the United States Senate, where every state receives two senators regardless of population.

Institution in Practice
Redistricting in the United States

Every ten years, following the census, the United States conducts one of the most politically significant administrative exercises in American democracy: the redistricting of congressional and state legislative boundaries. The process begins when the Census Bureau releases population data, based on which the apportionment of House seats among the fifty states is recalculated — states that have grown significantly gain seats; states that have declined or grown more slowly may lose seats.

Within each state, district boundaries must then be redrawn to reflect the new population distribution. In most states, this responsibility falls to the state legislature, which means whichever party controls the legislature controls the map — a powerful incentive for both parties to invest heavily in state legislative elections in years ending in zero, the year before redistricting.

The process involves sophisticated Geographic Information System (GIS) software and detailed voter registration and demographic data. Legislative mappers can model the partisan and demographic effects of virtually any proposed boundary with considerable precision before any votes are cast — far more precision than was available to Elbridge Gerry's contemporaries.

Legal requirements constrain the process: districts must be approximately equal in population, must comply with the Voting Rights Act's protections for minority communities, and in some states must meet state constitutional standards of compactness, contiguity, and preservation of existing political boundaries. Within these constraints, however, substantial room for partisan manipulation typically remains. After a map is adopted, it is subject to legal challenge — redistricting litigation is routine following every census, and the legal battles can take years to resolve.

Section 23.7

Representation of Minorities and Smaller Parties

Democratic theory has always wrestled with a fundamental tension: democracy is government by the majority, but justice and equal citizenship require that minorities not be systematically excluded from political representation. When we speak of "minorities" in this context, we mean not only racial and ethnic minorities, but also religious minorities, linguistic minorities, regional minorities, and political minorities. Electoral systems handle this problem in very different ways, and the choice of system has significant consequences for whether such communities are effectively represented in legislative bodies.

Political scientists distinguish between two different concepts of representation. Descriptive representation refers to the degree to which a legislature's membership resembles the broader population in demographic characteristics — race, gender, religion, age, occupation, and so on. Substantive representation refers to whether legislators actually advocate for the interests and concerns of various communities, regardless of those legislators' own demographic characteristics. These two concepts are related but distinct, and both are valuable. Research has found that descriptive representation often has important effects on substantive representation, and the presence of descriptively representative legislators can itself build trust and political engagement among communities that have historically been excluded.

How Single-Member Districts Affect Minority Representation. If a minority community constitutes, say, fifteen percent of the overall population but is dispersed relatively evenly across a large state, its members will be a small minority in virtually every district and may not elect any representatives who share their background. The minority community's votes are not "wasted" in the sense of electing losing candidates, but they may be absorbed into the winning totals of candidates chosen primarily by majority-group voters.

To address this problem, the United States has experimented since the 1980s with "majority-minority districts" — districts drawn so that racial or ethnic minority voters constitute a majority within that district. The Voting Rights Act of 1965 and its subsequent amendments and judicial interpretations have been the legal framework for this approach. The politics of majority-minority districts are complicated, however: concentrating minority voters in a few districts also "packs" them, in effect, and some scholars have argued that this may have mixed effects on substantive representation by reducing minority influence in surrounding districts.

How Proportional Representation Affects Minority Representation. In a party list system with low thresholds, a minority community that constitutes ten percent of the population can vote for a party that specifically represents their interests and expect to win approximately ten percent of seats. There is no need to concentrate geographically, no risk of packing or cracking, and no structural pressure to subsume minority preferences within a majority coalition. Research comparing democratic countries has consistently found that proportional representation systems elect higher proportions of women and ethnic minorities to legislatures than single-member district systems.

This does not mean PR systems automatically produce diverse and inclusive legislatures. Closed party lists concentrate power in party leaders, who may be just as prone to selecting candidates from dominant groups as any other leadership body. And PR systems that produce many parties may fragment minority communities across multiple parties rather than consolidating their political power. Electoral thresholds also pose a particular challenge for smaller parties that represent minority political communities: a five percent national threshold means that a party with four percent support — representing, perhaps, millions of voters — receives no legislative representation at all.

Comparative Lens
Representing Women — SMD Versus PR Systems

One of the most consistent findings in comparative electoral studies is that proportional representation systems elect substantially higher proportions of women to national legislatures than single-member district systems.

In single-member district systems, women have often faced structural disadvantages. Party gatekeepers selecting candidates for competitive districts have historically preferred candidates who fit traditional templates of electability. Women who run in SMD systems must win a single competitive district, which can require substantial campaign resources and local name recognition, and the "winner take all" nature of each race fully preserves any advantage held by incumbents, who are disproportionately male.

In proportional representation systems, parties constructing candidate lists have both the opportunity and, in many countries, the political incentive to present balanced slates. Many European parties with PR systems have adopted gender quotas or "zipper" arrangements — alternating male and female candidates on the list — that effectively guarantee significant female representation.

The data are striking. Nordic countries — Sweden, Norway, Finland, Iceland — consistently achieve forty to fifty percent female representation using proportional systems. New Zealand, which shifted from SMD to a mixed system in 1996, saw its female representation increase substantially in the years following the transition. The United Kingdom, United States, Canada, and India — all using single-member plurality systems — have achieved significantly lower proportions of women in their national legislatures than most comparable democracies using proportional systems.

This comparison does not suggest that electoral systems alone determine gender representation — cultural attitudes, party practices, candidate recruitment, and campaign finance all matter. But the electoral system creates structural conditions that either facilitate or impede the inclusion of historically underrepresented groups.

Section 23.8

How Electoral Rules Shape Politics

Duverger's Law. One of the most famous propositions in comparative electoral studies is known as Duverger's Law, named for French political scientist Maurice Duverger. Duverger observed, in his classic 1951 work, that single-member plurality systems tend to favor two-party systems, while proportional representation tends to favor multiparty systems. This proposition has been refined, debated, and qualified by subsequent scholarship, but its core insight has proven remarkably durable.

The logic operates at two levels. At the voter level, plurality voters face strategic incentives to consolidate behind one of the two leading candidates, since a vote for a candidate who cannot win may feel "wasted." Over time, this strategic voting dynamic strengthens the two leading parties and weakens smaller parties. At the elite level, political entrepreneurs face similar incentives: if there is no realistic prospect of winning seats without achieving a local plurality, the incentive is to join a major party rather than build a third party that will never win representation.

Proportional representation counteracts these dynamics. When votes are translated into seats in proportion to national vote share, small parties can win representation without winning any single geographic district, and political entrepreneurs can build new parties without first winning a plurality. The result is a more fragmented party system with more viable political actors.

Coalition Government Under Proportional Representation. Because proportional systems tend to produce multiparty legislatures where no single party holds a majority, government formation typically requires coalition negotiations among parties. Coalition governments are the norm in most European democracies — Germany, the Netherlands, Sweden, Italy, and Finland routinely form governments through post-election negotiations among multiple parties, and negotiations lasting several months are not unusual.

Electoral Systems and Policy Outcomes. Beyond party systems and government composition, electoral systems may affect the substance of public policy. A substantial body of research has found that proportional representation countries tend to have higher voter turnout, more generous welfare states, lower income inequality, more environmental protection legislation, and greater overall voter satisfaction with democracy. These findings are contested, however — countries with PR systems also differ from SMD countries in historical, cultural, and economic ways that may independently explain these policy differences. What does seem clear is that electoral systems have consequences that extend well beyond the mechanics of vote counting.

Primary Source Window
The Voting Rights Act of 1965 — Section 2
Context

The Voting Rights Act of 1965 is among the most consequential pieces of legislation in American political history. Enacted following the Civil Rights Movement's sustained campaign to end the systematic disenfranchisement of Black voters, particularly in the South, the Act established federal oversight of elections in jurisdictions with histories of discrimination and prohibited voting practices that denied or abridged the right to vote on account of race or color. Section 2, as amended in 1982, is the provision most directly relevant to electoral system design and representation.

Paraphrase of Section 2

Section 2 prohibits any voting qualification, standard, practice, or procedure that results in the denial or abridgement of the right to vote on account of race or color. A violation is established if, based on the totality of circumstances, the political process is not equally open to members of a racial or language minority group, such that they have less opportunity than other members of the electorate to participate in the political process and elect representatives of their choice.

Analysis

Section 2 is important for electoral system design because it imposes legal requirements on how district lines are drawn. Its "results test" — which does not require proof of discriminatory intent, only discriminatory effect — means that redistricting plans that dilute minority voting power can be challenged in federal court even if the people who drew the maps did not consciously intend to discriminate. The Supreme Court's 1986 decision in Thornburg v. Gingles established a three-part test for Section 2 violations involving minority community size, geographic compactness, political cohesion, and racial bloc voting. The Act has been repeatedly amended and has been the subject of landmark Supreme Court decisions affecting both its coverage formula (Shelby County v. Holder, 2013) and its interpretation (Brnovich v. Democratic National Committee, 2021).

Questions for Students

  2. How does the "results test" in Section 2 differ from a requirement to prove discriminatory intent, and why might Congress have chosen a results-based standard?
  3. What tension exists between majority-minority districts and the concern that such districts may reduce minority influence in surrounding areas?
Civic Debate
Should the United States Change Its Electoral System?
The issue: The United States uses single-member plurality districts for elections to the House of Representatives and most state legislatures. Critics argue the system produces extreme partisan gerrymandering, large numbers of "wasted" votes, underrepresentation of minorities and women, and entrenched two-party dominance. Defenders argue that stability, accountability, and the geographic bond between representatives and constituents are worth preserving.
Argument A — Reform the System

The current system produces systematically distorted outcomes: the party winning a majority of votes nationally has sometimes won fewer House seats than its opponent because of how district lines were drawn. Millions of voters in "safe" districts effectively lack representation. Reformers point to countries using proportional representation, which typically have higher turnout, more diverse legislatures, and policy outcomes reflecting a broader range of citizen preferences. Some advocate multi-member districts with ranked-choice voting to eliminate gerrymandering while producing both geographic and proportional representation.

Argument B — Preserve the System

Single-member districts produce clear, direct accountability: every voter has a specific representative they can contact, hold responsible, and vote against. The two-party system this produces forces major parties to build broad coalitions. Proportional systems with many parties may produce fragmented legislatures requiring governance by elites bargaining behind closed doors. Some defenders acknowledge the gerrymandering problem but argue it is better addressed through independent redistricting commissions than by abandoning the district system entirely.

Other PerspectivesSome analysts favor incremental reform — adopting ranked-choice voting within single-member districts — without changing the basic geographic structure. Others argue that the Senate, where every state receives two senators regardless of population, is a more serious democratic distortion than the House electoral system and should be the focus of reform efforts instead.
TradeoffsElectoral system reform is difficult precisely because the rules governing how elections work are themselves political — those who benefit from current rules have strong incentives to preserve them. Any reform involves genuine tradeoffs between stability and representation, between geographic accountability and proportional fairness, and between reform idealism and institutional continuity.
Civic reasoning question: If you were advising a state legislative committee considering changes to electoral rules, what change would you recommend, if any? What evidence would you want to consult, and what values would guide your recommendation?
Rights and Responsibilities

The right to vote — and the right to have that vote count meaningfully — is one of the foundational rights of democratic citizenship. In the United States, this right is protected by the Fifteenth, Nineteenth, Twenty-fourth, and Twenty-sixth Amendments, as well as by federal and state statutes including the Voting Rights Act. But the right to vote carries civic responsibilities as well. Democratic self-government depends on citizens who are informed about their choices and who understand the stakes of electoral decisions.

Electoral systems mediate between individual voters and collective political outcomes. Understanding how a particular system works — what your vote actually does, how it is counted, how it translates into representation — is a prerequisite for informed participation. Citizens who participate in redistricting public comment processes, who monitor election administration, who support independent redistricting commissions, and who advocate for fair representation are exercising civic responsibilities that go beyond casting a ballot on election day. The rules of democratic competition are themselves a public good that requires active attention to maintain.

Policy Lab
Reforming State Legislative Elections
Scenario

The state of Midland has a bicameral legislature. Elections to the lower house use single-member plurality districts — 100 districts, each electing one representative. In the most recent election, Party A received fifty-three percent of statewide votes but won sixty-eight seats. Party B received thirty-nine percent of votes but won only thirty seats. Party C received eight percent of votes and won two seats. Several advocacy organizations have filed lawsuits challenging the district map as a partisan gerrymander. A state legislative committee is considering electoral reform proposals.

Stakeholders
  • Voters whose preferred party received fewer seats than their vote share suggests
  • Candidates and party officials in both major parties
  • Members of Party C, which won far fewer seats than its vote share would produce under proportional representation
  • Racial and ethnic minority communities concerned about their representation
  • Election administrators concerned about implementation logistics
Policy Options
  1. Maintain the current system but establish an independent redistricting commission to draw district lines without direct partisan control.
  2. Adopt ranked-choice voting for all state house races, keeping single-member districts but replacing plurality rules with instant-runoff voting.
  3. Convert to multi-member districts with the Single Transferable Vote, grouping the 100 single-member districts into 20 five-member districts.
  4. Adopt a mixed-member proportional system, keeping 60 single-member districts while adding 40 seats distributed proportionally based on a party vote.
  5. Adopt a full closed party list proportional representation system, eliminating geographic districts entirely.
Evidence Needed

What do comparative studies show about outcomes under each system? How have other states or countries that adopted similar reforms fared? What do affected communities themselves prefer? What are the implementation costs and administrative challenges of each option?

Tradeoffs

The independent commission approach addresses gerrymandering without restructuring the system, but leaves underlying disproportionality intact. Ranked-choice voting improves majority legitimacy but does not alter seat-share distribution. Multi-member STV and mixed-member proportional systems produce more proportional outcomes but require adaptation to new rules. Full PR maximizes proportionality but eliminates geographic representation entirely.

Chapter 23 — Review

Common Misunderstandings

The candidate with the most votes always wins the election.

Correction: In plurality systems, the candidate with the most votes in a single district does win that seat. But in systems using absolute majority requirements, a candidate must receive more than fifty percent of votes; if no candidate does, a runoff is held. In ranked-choice voting, the candidate with the most first-preference votes does not necessarily win if no one has a majority — votes are redistributed through a counting process that can change the final outcome.

Proportional representation is always more democratic than other systems.

Correction: PR better reflects voter preferences in that vote shares more closely match seat shares. But "democratic" is a complex concept encompassing accountability, stability, governance capacity, and voter comprehension as well as representational accuracy. PR systems with many parties may make it harder to hold governments accountable and easier for backroom coalition negotiations to produce outcomes distant from voter expectations.

Gerrymandering only happens when district boundaries look bizarre or irregular.

Correction: While highly contorted district shapes can be evidence of gerrymandering, shape alone is not a reliable indicator. A compact, regularly shaped district can be carefully drawn to pack or crack opposing voters, while an irregularly shaped district may legitimately keep a geographically dispersed minority community together. Mathematical measures like the efficiency gap assess the electoral effects of a map rather than its visual appearance.

A party that wins more seats than another has more voter support.

Correction: In single-member district systems, especially gerrymandered ones, a party can win a majority of seats while receiving fewer total votes than its opponents. Because votes are counted district by district, seat totals do not reliably indicate the overall distribution of voter preferences.

Ranked-choice voting always elects the most popular candidate.

Correction: RCV elects the candidate with majority support among those who expressed a preference among the final two candidates — but this is not necessarily the "most popular" candidate in every sense. It is theoretically possible (though uncommon) for a candidate who would defeat every other candidate head-to-head to be eliminated early because they received few first-preference votes — the "Condorcet loser paradox."

Majority-minority districts always help minority communities.

Correction: Majority-minority districts increase the likelihood of electing a representative from that community, strengthening descriptive representation. However, concentrating minority voters in a few districts removes them from surrounding districts, potentially making those districts easier for the majority party to win and reducing the overall number of legislators who need to appeal to minority voters.

Electoral systems are chosen on the basis of democratic theory and good governance alone.

Correction: Electoral systems are adopted through political processes, and the parties and groups in power at the time of adoption typically choose rules they expect to favor their continued power. Electoral system design is itself a political act, shaped by power and interest as much as by democratic theory.

A high voter turnout means an electoral system is working well.

Correction: Turnout reflects many factors beyond electoral system design, including competitiveness, registration laws, cultural attitudes, and perceived stakes. High turnout in an unfair or rigged system would not indicate democratic health, and lower turnout in a well-functioning democracy may simply reflect contentment. Turnout is useful data, but it must be interpreted in context.

Chapter 23 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Electoral systems are the rules that transform individual votes into collective political outcomes. The most fundamental division is between single-member district systems and proportional representation systems, though many democracies use mixed systems combining elements of both.

  • Plurality systems — used in the United States, United Kingdom, Canada, and India — produce clear winners, strong geographic representation, and a tendency toward stable majority governments, but generate high disproportionality and large numbers of wasted votes, and entrench two-party dominance.

  • Majority systems, including the two-round system used in France and the preferential voting systems used in Australia and several American jurisdictions, require winners to demonstrate broader support than a strict plurality, addressing the concern that plurality winners may not represent majority preferences.

  • Proportional representation systems, used across most of continental Europe and many other democracies, distribute seats according to parties' vote shares through party lists, subject to electoral thresholds that prevent legislative fragmentation. PR systems produce more proportional outcomes and more inclusive representation, but tend to require coalition governments.

  • Ranked-choice voting addresses the structural problems of plurality voting in multi-candidate races by allowing voters to rank candidates and redistributing votes from eliminated candidates until a majority winner emerges. The Single Transferable Vote extends this logic to multi-member districts to achieve proportional outcomes.

  • The drawing of district boundaries in SMD systems is among the most politically consequential administrative processes in democratic governance. Gerrymandering — through packing and cracking — has been a persistent feature of American politics. Legal responses have had mixed results: courts address racial gerrymandering under the Voting Rights Act, but the Supreme Court ruled in 2019 that federal courts cannot adjudicate partisan gerrymandering claims.

  • Representation of minority communities is shaped fundamentally by electoral system design. Single-member districts tend to underrepresent geographically dispersed minorities; majority-minority districts can increase descriptive representation but may reduce broader influence. Proportional systems tend to elect higher proportions of women and ethnic minorities.

  • Electoral rules shape the behavior of voters, candidates, and parties. Duverger's Law captures the well-documented tendency of SMD plurality systems to produce two-party competition, while PR systems tend to produce multiparty competition and coalition government.

  • No electoral system is perfect. Every system involves genuine tradeoffs among representational accuracy, governmental stability, political accountability, geographic connection, minority inclusion, and voter comprehension.

  • The design of electoral systems is not a technical question separate from politics — it is a political question embedded in democratic life. The rules that determine how votes become representation are themselves the product of political choices, contested by people with different interests and visions of what democracy should accomplish.

Key Terms Review

electoral system
The set of rules governing how votes are cast, counted, and translated into political representation.
constituency / district
A defined geographic or population unit from which one or more representatives are elected.
single-member district (SMD)
An electoral district from which exactly one representative is elected.
plurality
A vote total greater than any other candidate's, without necessarily constituting a majority of all votes cast.
majority
A vote total exceeding fifty percent of all votes cast.
first-past-the-post (FPTP)
A plurality electoral system in which the candidate with the most votes in a district wins, regardless of total share received.
run-off election
A second round of voting held when no candidate achieves a required majority, typically contested between the top two candidates.
proportional representation (PR)
A family of electoral systems distributing legislative seats to parties in approximate proportion to their share of votes.
party list
A form of proportional representation in which voters cast ballots for parties, which fill seats from a ranked candidate list.
closed list
A party list system in which voters can only choose a party, not influence individual candidate rankings.
open list
A party list system in which voters can express a preference for a specific candidate within their chosen party.
threshold (electoral)
A minimum vote share a party must receive before becoming eligible for legislative seats.
mixed-member proportional (MMP)
An electoral system combining single-member district seats with proportional "top-up" seats.
ranked-choice voting (RCV)
A system in which voters rank candidates; last-place candidates are eliminated and their votes redistributed until a majority winner emerges.
preferential voting
A general term for electoral systems in which voters rank candidates in order of preference.
Single Transferable Vote (STV)
A form of ranked-choice voting used in multi-member districts to achieve proportional outcomes.
gerrymandering
Drawing electoral district boundaries to advantage a particular party, group, or candidate.
malapportionment
Drawing legislative districts with significantly unequal populations.
wasted votes
Votes cast for losing candidates, or for a winner in excess of what was needed to win, contributing no additional representation.
safe seat
A district where one party's advantage is so large the outcome is rarely competitive.
swing district / marginal seat
A district where neither major party holds a dominant advantage and the outcome is genuinely competitive.
majority-minority district
A district drawn so that racial or ethnic minority voters constitute a majority of the voting population.
descriptive representation
The degree to which a legislature's membership resembles the broader population demographically.
substantive representation
The degree to which legislators actually advocate for the interests of various communities.
Duverger's Law
The proposition that SMD plurality systems tend toward two-party competition, while PR systems tend toward multiparty competition.
coalition government
A government formed when two or more parties agree to govern together, common in multiparty PR systems.
electoral disproportionality
The gap between a party's share of votes and its share of legislative seats.
efficiency gap
A mathematical measure of partisan gerrymandering based on the difference in wasted votes between major parties.
strategic (tactical) voting
Casting a vote for a candidate one does not most prefer, in order to prevent a less-preferred candidate from winning.

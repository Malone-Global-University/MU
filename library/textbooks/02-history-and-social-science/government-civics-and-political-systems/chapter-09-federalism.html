Chapter 9 — Front Matter

Chapter Opening and Overview

In 1787, the delegates assembled in Philadelphia to revise the Articles of Confederation faced a question that no major modern republic had answered before: how do you hold a large, diverse country together without allowing a central government to swallow the independence of its constituent parts? The thirteen former colonies had just fought a war to escape centralized imperial rule. They were deeply suspicious of concentrated national power. And yet they had also watched the loose confederation of states under the Articles collapse into dysfunction — states imposing tariffs on each other, refusing to contribute to national defense, printing incompatible currencies, and squabbling over territory. The delegates needed a system that could govern a continent without recreating the tyranny they had overthrown. Their solution was federalism: a constitutional division of authority between a national government and state governments, each operating with its own legitimacy, its own powers, and its own sphere of authority.

The question of how to distribute power across territory is not unique to the United States. Every large political community must decide: should authority be concentrated at the center, or dispersed to regions, provinces, states, or localities? Should a national government have the power to override local decisions, or should local governments operate as independent power centers in their own right? Should different regions of a country be permitted to adopt different laws, different policies, different tax rates, different educational systems? Or should the country speak with a single voice, implementing uniform standards that apply from one border to the next? These are not merely technical questions. They are questions about the nature of political community, the meaning of democratic self-government, the management of diversity, and the practical capacity of institutions to solve public problems.

Different political systems have answered these questions in very different ways. Some countries — France, Japan, New Zealand — concentrate authority decisively at the national level, allowing local governments to exist primarily as administrative subdivisions of the center, not as independent political units with constitutionally protected powers. These are called unitary systems. Other countries — the United States, Germany, India, Canada, Australia, Brazil, Mexico, Switzerland — divide authority constitutionally between national and subnational governments, each of which derives its powers directly from the constitution rather than from the generosity of the other level. These are called federal systems. A third model — used by the original American confederation and by early modern European arrangements — places authority primarily with the member states and allows a common body to act only in limited, delegated areas. These are confederations.

Each model solves some problems while creating others. Federal systems protect regional diversity and prevent concentration of national power, but they can also produce inequality across regions, create legal confusion when state and national law conflict, and make uniform national policy difficult to achieve. Unitary systems allow for consistent national standards and efficient administration, but they can suppress regional distinctiveness and concentrate power in ways that may not reflect local needs or preferences. Confederations protect local autonomy almost absolutely, but they struggle to coordinate collective action, manage shared resources, and project unified authority in moments of crisis.

This chapter explains how political authority is distributed across territory. It introduces the three major models — federal, unitary, and confederal — in depth. It examines how powers are shared, reserved, and contested between levels of government. It explores how local and regional governments operate within these frameworks. It considers the practical advantages and serious problems that each model creates. And it surveys examples from around the world, showing how different countries have made different choices — and lived with different consequences — in designing the territorial structure of their governments. Understanding these structures is essential for understanding how governments actually work: not as abstract principles, but as daily systems of rules, institutions, authority, and accountability that shape the lives of citizens from the national capital to the most distant local community.

Section 9.1

Geographic Distribution of Power

Every government must answer a fundamental organizational question: where does authority reside? In the smallest political communities — a town, a tribe, a city-state — this question may have a simple answer. There is one governing body, one set of rules, one territory small enough that the same institution can attend to most public needs. But as political communities grow in size, population, and diversity, this simplicity becomes impossible to maintain. A government managing one square mile and a few thousand people can reasonably make most decisions in a single council chamber. A government managing hundreds of millions of people spread across thousands of miles, speaking different languages, practicing different religions, working in different economies, and holding different political traditions, faces a categorically different problem.

The geographic distribution of power is the way a political system answers the question of who governs, and at what level. At one extreme, a perfectly centralized system would concentrate all meaningful authority at the national level, with subnational units existing only as administrative branches of the center, carrying out instructions but generating no independent authority. At the other extreme, a perfectly decentralized system would place all meaningful authority at the local level, with a common body existing only to coordinate voluntary cooperation among otherwise independent units. Real political systems occupy positions along this spectrum, with most combining elements of both centralization and decentralization in complex ways.

Why does this question matter? Because the answer shapes nearly everything citizens experience as government. Where your children go to school and what they learn there, how your community polices its streets, what environmental protections apply to the land around your home, how much you pay in taxes and to whom, what health care or social services you receive — all of these depend enormously on whether the relevant decisions are made at the national level, the state or provincial level, or the local level. In the United States, a family moving from one state to another will find different school curriculum standards, different gun regulations, different tax rates, different public university tuition policies, different voter registration rules, and different criminal sentencing practices — not because the Constitution is silent on these areas, but because the constitutional structure leaves many of them to the states.

The geographic distribution of power also shapes accountability and democratic participation. When decisions are made closer to the citizens they affect, those citizens may have more direct access to the decision-makers — it is easier to attend a city council meeting than to lobby Congress. But when decisions are decentralized, they may also be more variable, more susceptible to local capture by narrow interests, and less capable of addressing problems that cross boundaries. A pollutant released into a river upstream in one state affects communities downstream in another; a local government cannot effectively regulate a problem that its territory only partially contains.

The Logic of Territorial Organization

Political scientists have identified several major reasons why societies distribute authority across territory in different ways.

Managing diversity. Large countries often contain populations with distinct regional identities, languages, religions, or economic interests. Federalism or regional autonomy can allow these communities to govern themselves in some respects while remaining part of a larger political unit. Switzerland, with its four official languages and strong cantonal traditions, has maintained national unity in part through a federal structure that gives significant authority to cantons. Spain, though formally a unitary state, has granted extensive autonomy to regions like Catalonia and the Basque Country in response to strong regional identities.

Protecting liberty. Some political theorists argue that dividing authority between national and subnational governments provides an additional check on tyranny. If the national government becomes oppressive, states or provinces with independent authority may resist. James Madison, writing in Federalist No. 51, argued that a compound republic — dividing power both among branches and between levels of government — provided double protection for individual rights. Whether this protection actually works depends on circumstances: regional governments can also be oppressive, and the protection of civil rights in the United States has sometimes required national intervention against states that violated them.

Improving governance. Subnational governments may be better positioned to understand and respond to local conditions. A county government overseeing rural roads, local school districts, and agricultural zoning may make better decisions about those matters than a distant national bureaucracy. Political economists sometimes argue that competition among subnational governments — where people and businesses can "vote with their feet" by moving to jurisdictions with preferred policies — creates incentives for efficient governance. Critics argue that this competition can also produce a race to the bottom on labor standards, environmental protection, or social services.

Historical and political necessity. Many federal systems emerged not from abstract theory but from historical necessity — from the need to unite previously independent units that would not have accepted full absorption into a centralized state. The United States, Canada, Switzerland, Australia, and Germany all became federal states in part because preexisting political units demanded retention of their powers as a condition of joining the union. The federal structure was, in these cases, the price of unity.

Section 9.2

Federal Systems

A federal system is a constitutional arrangement in which governing authority is divided between a national government and subnational governments — states, provinces, Länder, cantons, or other units — with each level deriving its powers from the constitution itself rather than from the other level. This last point is crucial and distinguishes federalism from mere decentralization. In a federal system, the subnational governments are not creatures of the national government, empowered only to the extent that the center permits. They are independent constitutional actors with their own sphere of authority, their own democratic legitimacy, and their own legal standing.

The Constitutional Foundation of Federalism

In a genuine federal system, the constitution is the source of authority for both levels of government. Neither the national government nor the state governments can simply abolish or absorb the other, because both derive their authority from the same constitutional source that stands above both. This means that the distribution of powers is not merely a political arrangement changeable by ordinary legislation — it requires constitutional amendment to fundamentally alter, and constitutional provisions protecting the subnational governments cannot be swept away by a legislative majority at the national level.

In the United States, this principle is expressed in several constitutional provisions. The Tenth Amendment states that powers not delegated to the national government by the Constitution, nor prohibited by it to the states, are reserved to the states respectively, or to the people. The Constitution also requires the federal government to protect states from invasion and domestic violence. The Supreme Court has jurisdiction to hear cases arising under the Constitution, including disputes about the division of authority between national and state governments.

In Germany, the federal structure is protected by an even more explicit constitutional entrenchment. The Basic Law (Germany's constitution) declares in Article 79 that the federal structure of Germany cannot be amended — even by constitutional amendment. This so-called "eternity clause" places the federal character of the German state beyond the reach of any legislative majority, however large. The Länder (German states) participate directly in national legislation through the Bundesrat, an upper house of the national parliament composed of representatives appointed by state governments, which must approve legislation affecting state authority.

The Development of American Federalism

The American system of federalism has changed enormously since the founding, evolving through political conflict, judicial interpretation, constitutional amendment, and social change. Understanding this evolution helps students see federalism not as a static formula but as a living constitutional arrangement continuously renegotiated through history.

The founding period (1787–1800) established the basic framework, but the meaning of that framework was immediately contested. Federalists favored a strong national government with broad implied powers. Anti-Federalists feared that the new Constitution would swallow state sovereignty. The early debates over the national bank, the scope of the commerce power, and the proper role of the federal judiciary reflected genuine disagreement about the constitutional design.

The antebellum period (1800–1861) saw repeated conflicts over the scope of national versus state power, culminating in the doctrine of nullification — the argument, advanced by John C. Calhoun and others, that states had the right to declare federal laws unconstitutional and refuse to enforce them within their borders. President Andrew Jackson forcefully rejected nullification during the Nullification Crisis of 1832–1833, and the Civil War ultimately settled by force what political argument had not settled in law: states could not simply exit the Union or refuse to accept national authority.

The Civil War and Reconstruction (1861–1877) transformed American federalism by significantly expanding national power and explicitly extending constitutional protections to individuals against state action. The Fourteenth Amendment (1868), which guaranteed equal protection and due process against state action and gave Congress power to enforce these guarantees, represented a fundamental rebalancing of national and state authority in the area of civil rights.

The New Deal era (1933–1945) produced the most dramatic expansion of national power in American history. Facing the Great Depression, President Franklin Roosevelt and Congress enacted sweeping federal programs regulating agriculture, labor, banking, securities, and social welfare. The Supreme Court initially resisted, then reversed course in 1937. After this "constitutional revolution," the Court accepted a broad interpretation of the commerce power that allowed federal regulation of virtually any economic activity with a substantial effect on interstate commerce.

The post–New Deal period (1945–present) has seen ongoing tension between national expansion and state resilience. In United States v. Lopez (1995), the Court struck down the Gun-Free School Zones Act as exceeding the commerce power, signaling renewed willingness to enforce limits on federal authority. The contemporary period continues to involve active litigation and political conflict over the proper boundaries of federal and state power.

Dual vs. Cooperative Federalism

Dual federalism ("layer cake") — national and state governments each operate in distinct, non-overlapping spheres. Associated with the early republic, when most domestic governance was left to the states.

Cooperative federalism ("marble cake") — national and state governments work together on shared programs, each contributing funding, administrative capacity, and regulatory authority. Describes much of modern American governance, from Medicaid to highway construction to environmental regulation.

Civic Vocabulary
Federalism

Federalism is a system of government in which authority is constitutionally divided between a national (or central) government and subnational governments (such as states, provinces, or cantons), with each level deriving its powers from the constitution rather than from the other level.

Federalism differs from mere decentralization — where a national government chooses to delegate some functions to regional administrators — because in a federal system, the subnational governments have constitutionally protected powers that the national government cannot simply revoke by ordinary legislation. Both levels of government have independent democratic legitimacy: citizens elect representatives at both levels, and both levels may tax, spend, make law, and enforce it within their respective spheres.

Why does federalism matter? Because it shapes the fundamental question of who governs. In a federal system, the answer is not simply "the national government" — it is a complex, constitutionally structured relationship in which national and state governments each have authority, compete for influence, cooperate on shared programs, and sometimes conflict over the boundaries of their respective powers. Citizens in federal systems live under multiple layers of law simultaneously, and understanding which level of government is responsible for which decisions is essential for democratic accountability.

Section 9.3

Unitary Systems

A unitary system is one in which constitutional authority is concentrated at the national level, with subnational governments existing primarily as administrative subdivisions of the center. In a unitary system, regional and local governments derive their authority from the national government — through statute, delegation, or administrative arrangement — rather than from a separate constitutional foundation. The national government may delegate significant administrative responsibilities to regional and local bodies, but it retains the legal authority to override, restructure, or abolish those bodies through ordinary legislation.

The Logic of Unitary Government

Unitary systems are far more common globally than federal systems. Of the world's approximately 195 sovereign states, the large majority operate under unitary constitutional arrangements. This prevalence reflects several practical and political realities.

First, unitary systems tend to be associated with smaller and more homogeneous countries. France, Sweden, New Zealand, Japan, South Korea, and the United Kingdom (in its traditional form) all have relatively unified national identities, relatively small territories by global standards, and histories that emphasized national rather than regional political identity. These conditions make a concentrated central government more politically feasible and culturally acceptable.

Second, unitary systems emerged in many cases from pre-existing centralized monarchies or empires. France's strongly centralized administrative state traces directly to the Bourbon monarchy and was reinforced by Napoleon's administrative reorganization. Japan's modern state, established after the Meiji Restoration of 1868, was deliberately constructed as a centralized national government capable of rapid modernization. These historical trajectories created institutions, political cultures, and administrative traditions that favored centralization.

Third, unitary systems offer certain practical advantages. They can establish uniform national standards more easily — ensuring that the quality of education, health care, environmental protection, or infrastructure does not vary dramatically from one region to another. They can coordinate national policy more efficiently, without requiring the complex negotiations between levels of government that federal systems entail. And they avoid some of the legal complexity that federal systems create, where businesses, individuals, and institutions must navigate multiple overlapping regulatory regimes.

How Unitary Systems Operate

Even in strongly unitary systems, governing authority is almost never exercised exclusively at the national level. The practical demands of administration require that someone close to the ground deliver services, enforce regulations, manage local infrastructure, and respond to local needs. Unitary systems meet this demand through administrative decentralization — delegating functions to regional offices of national ministries, or to locally elected councils — without creating constitutionally independent centers of authority.

In France, the traditional model was expressed through the system of prefects — nationally appointed officials who represented the central government in each of France's departments. The prefect coordinated national policy at the local level, supervised local administration, and could override local decisions that conflicted with national law. France has since undertaken significant reforms — particularly the decentralization laws of the early 1980s — that expanded the powers of elected regional and departmental councils. But even after these reforms, French regions and departments remain creatures of national statute rather than constitutional actors with independently protected powers.

In the United Kingdom, the traditional constitutional principle that Parliament is supreme — that it can legislate on any subject and cannot bind itself for the future — made Britain a strongly unitary state in constitutional theory. But the United Kingdom has undergone significant devolution since the late 1990s, transferring legislative authority to the Scottish Parliament, the Welsh Senedd, and the Northern Ireland Assembly. These devolved institutions have genuine legislative powers in significant areas, but their powers are derived from Westminster legislation that Parliament could — in theory — repeal. Whether devolution has effectively transformed Britain into a quasi-federal state is a matter of ongoing constitutional debate.

Decentralization vs. Devolution

Administrative decentralization occurs when a national government assigns the implementation of national policy to regional offices or local bodies that remain agents of the center — they carry out national policy rather than making independent policy of their own.

Devolution occurs when a national government transfers actual legislative or policy-making authority to subnational bodies that may exercise genuine political discretion — though this authority remains derived from national legislation rather than from a separate constitutional foundation.

Both forms can give regional or local bodies significant practical independence within a formally unitary system. The key constitutional distinction remains: in a unitary system, the national government retains ultimate authority to reclaim what it has delegated.

Section 9.4

Confederations and Loose Unions

A confederation is a political arrangement in which independent states or political units join together under a common authority for limited, specified purposes, while retaining their fundamental sovereignty. In a confederation, the member states — not the confederation itself — are the primary locus of political authority. The common body operates only with the consent of its member states, typically cannot act directly on individual citizens (only on the member states themselves), and cannot compel compliance from member states that choose to defy its decisions.

Historical Examples

The Articles of Confederation (1781–1789) established the first government of the United States after independence. Under the Articles, the thirteen states agreed to a "firm league of friendship" while retaining their sovereignty, freedom, and independence. The Congress of the Confederation could make decisions — on war and peace, diplomacy, and borrowing money — but it could not levy taxes directly on citizens, only on states, which could refuse to pay. It could not regulate interstate commerce. It could not compel individual citizens to obey its laws. Its decisions required approval by nine of thirteen states for most measures, and unanimous agreement to amend the Articles themselves. The result was a government chronically short of funds, unable to regulate commerce, unable to enforce its laws, and unable to compel state compliance. By 1787, leading political figures concluded that the confederal structure had failed and that a stronger central government was necessary — leading to the Constitutional Convention and the replacement of the Articles with the federal Constitution.

The European Union presents a modern example of an arrangement that incorporates significant confederal elements — member states retain their sovereignty, their citizenship systems, their governments, and their ultimate right to exit — alongside elements of supranational authority that go beyond a traditional confederation. EU law is supreme over member state law in areas where the EU has competence. The EU has its own legislature, executive, and court. Citizens of member states have European citizenship in addition to national citizenship. The tension between confederal (member state sovereignty) and federal (supranational authority) principles has defined European integration since its inception.

The Swiss Confederation illustrates how confederal terminology can outlast the political reality. Switzerland formally calls itself a "confederation" (Confederatio Helvetica). However, the modern Swiss political system functions as a genuine federation, with a national government, a constitutional division of powers, and constitutionally protected cantonal authority. The confederal label reflects historical origins — Switzerland began as a loose alliance of cantons — but the contemporary system is federal in substance.

Why Confederations Have Been Unstable

The historical record of confederal arrangements is one of recurring instability, with confederations typically either evolving into tighter federal unions or dissolving. The reasons for this instability are structural.

The collective action problem. When member states retain the right to defy confederal decisions, the confederation struggles to coordinate action on shared problems. Any member state that resists bearing its share of collective costs — military service, financial contributions, economic adjustment — imposes costs on others. Over time, cooperative spirit erodes as member states calculate whether they can free-ride on others' contributions.

The enforcement gap. A confederation that can only act on member states, not on individual citizens, cannot enforce its decisions against states that refuse to comply. It can negotiate, sanction, and appeal — but ultimately it cannot compel. This enforcement gap makes the confederation's authority dependent on ongoing voluntary compliance, which may not persist when member states' interests diverge sharply.

Crisis demands. External threats — war, economic collapse, pandemic — tend to demand rapid, coordinated, decisive action that confederal structures are poorly suited to provide. States facing a common emergency may accept temporary centralization, which may then become permanent as institutions created during the crisis outlast it.

The transition path. Political scientists have noted that confederations that survive tend to evolve into federations. The pressure of collective action problems, enforcement gaps, and crisis demands tends to generate political momentum for stronger central authority, and once the central authority gains the ability to act on individual citizens and to enforce its decisions, the confederation has effectively become a federation.

How the System Works
How Federalism Divides Authority: Environmental Regulation in the United States
Step 1 Constitutional foundation. The Constitution does not mention the environment explicitly. However, the Commerce Clause gives Congress power to regulate commerce among the states, and pollution flowing across state borders has been held to fall within this power. States retain their general police powers — authority to regulate for the health, safety, and welfare of their citizens — under the Tenth Amendment.
Step 2 Cooperative federalism structure. Congress enacted the Clean Air Act (1970) and Clean Water Act (1972), establishing national standards for air and water quality. Rather than creating a purely federal enforcement system, these statutes established a cooperative structure: the Environmental Protection Agency sets national minimum standards, but states may adopt and administer their own programs so long as those programs meet or exceed federal requirements.
Step 3 The supremacy structure. If a state's environmental laws are weaker than federal minimums, federal law preempts — the federal standard applies. If a state wants to be stricter than the federal minimum, it generally may be, subject to some limits. California has historically been permitted to set stricter automobile emission standards than federal law requires, under a special waiver provision, and other states may then choose to follow California's standards.
Step 4 Conflict and litigation. Disputes over the division of authority are resolved by federal courts, ultimately by the Supreme Court. Massachusetts v. EPA (2007) established that the EPA had authority to regulate greenhouse gas emissions under the Clean Air Act, resolving a dispute about the scope of federal environmental authority.
Step 5 Fiscal federalism. Federal environmental programs are partly funded through grants to states. States that accept federal funding may be required to meet federal administrative and programmatic conditions. This fiscal leverage — the power to attach conditions to federal money — is one of the most powerful tools the national government uses to shape state policy even in areas where its direct regulatory authority is limited.

This process is complicated by the fact that environmental problems do not respect state borders. A power plant in one state emitting air pollution may cause acid rain in neighboring states, but the source state's government has little incentive to bear the economic costs of stricter regulation primarily for the benefit of others. Interstate externalities like these create arguments for national standards — but they also create difficult questions about which level of government has the better capacity to assess and balance complex technical and economic tradeoffs.

Section 9.5

Shared, Reserved, and Concurrent Powers

The practical architecture of federalism requires more than a general principle of divided authority. It requires specific constitutional rules about which government has authority over which subjects, and what happens when the two conflict. This section examines the major categories of power in federal systems and how their boundaries are defined, disputed, and enforced.

Enumerated Powers and the Commerce Clause

The enumerated powers are those explicitly listed in the national constitution as belonging to the national government. The theory behind enumeration is that the national government is one of limited, specified powers: it may do what the constitution authorizes, and no more. This theory contrasts with the traditional understanding of state police powers, which are general and need not be specifically listed — states may legislate for the health, safety, morals, and welfare of their citizens in any area not forbidden by the constitution or preempted by federal law.

In the United States, the most consequential enumerated power is the Commerce Clause, granting Congress the power "to regulate Commerce with foreign Nations, and among the several States, and with the Indian Tribes." The scope of this power has been the central battleground of American constitutional law for most of the nation's history. Does "commerce among the states" include only the physical movement of goods across state lines? Or does it include the manufacture of goods that will eventually enter interstate commerce? Or any economic activity that, in the aggregate, has a substantial effect on interstate commerce? The Supreme Court has swung between broader and narrower interpretations at different points in history, with enormously significant consequences for the reach of federal regulation.

The Necessary and Proper Clause expands the reach of enumerated powers by authorizing Congress to make all laws necessary and proper for executing them. Chief Justice John Marshall's opinion in McCulloch v. Maryland (1819) gave this clause an expansive reading, holding that Congress is not limited to means that are absolutely indispensable to carrying out its enumerated powers, but may choose any means that are plainly adapted to legitimate ends and not prohibited by the Constitution. This reading has been foundational to the broad scope of federal power in the modern era.

The Supremacy Clause and Preemption

The Supremacy Clause is a provision in Article VI of the United States Constitution stating that the Constitution, federal laws made pursuant to it, and treaties made under its authority are the supreme law of the land, binding on state courts even if state law says something different. When national and state law conflict, national law preempts state law — that is, national law controls, and the inconsistent state law is void. The Supremacy Clause prevents states from undermining national policy in areas where the Constitution gives the national government authority, but it applies only where the national government has actually legislated and acted within its constitutional power.

Civic Vocabulary
Preemption

Preemption is the legal doctrine, derived from the Supremacy Clause of the United States Constitution, under which a federal law displaces (renders void) a conflicting state law. When Congress has validly regulated an area within its constitutional authority, and a state law conflicts with that federal regulation — or when Congress has so thoroughly occupied a regulatory field that no room remains for state law — the state law is preempted and cannot be enforced.

Preemption matters because it defines the practical boundary between national and state authority in areas of concurrent power. It determines which level of government ultimately controls regulatory outcomes when both have legislated on the same subject. Understanding preemption is essential for businesses, lawyers, governments, and citizens who need to know which set of rules actually applies to a particular activity or situation.

The doctrine is not unlimited. Courts have held that preemption of traditional state functions must be clear — Congress should be presumed not to have preempted state law in areas of traditional state authority unless it has expressed that intent clearly. This presumption against preemption protects the federalism structure by ensuring that Congress does not inadvertently sweep away state authority on subjects historically governed by state law.

Interstate Compacts and State Cooperation

One important tool for interstate coordination in a federal system is the interstate compact — a formal agreement between two or more states, which may require congressional approval depending on its subject and scope. Interstate compacts allow states to cooperate on shared problems that cross state lines without involving federal government management.

The Port Authority of New York and New Jersey is one of the most significant examples — an interstate compact created in 1921 to manage transportation infrastructure in the New York-New Jersey metropolitan area, including bridges, tunnels, ports, and airports. The Colorado River Compact (1922) allocated water rights among seven Western states that depend on the Colorado River. The Interstate Compact for Adult Offender Supervision coordinates the transfer of probationers and parolees between states. There are hundreds of active interstate compacts covering subjects from education to environmental protection to emergency management.

Interstate compacts illustrate how states can solve collective problems using the tools of federal structure without always requiring federal intervention. They also illustrate the complexity of federalism in practice: states are not simply subordinate administrative units of the national government, but active political actors capable of forming their own cooperative arrangements.

Section 9.6

Local Government and Regional Authority

The national–state relationship in federal systems is often discussed as the central axis of federal power distribution, but it is not the only axis. Every political system also includes a third tier of government — the local level. Cities, counties, towns, villages, townships, school districts, special districts, regional authorities, and metropolitan governments are all forms of local government, and they play an enormous role in the daily governance of most citizens.

The Constitutional Status of Local Government

In the United States, local governments — cities, counties, townships, school districts, and special districts — are generally considered creatures of state law, not independent constitutional actors. The states created local governments by statute; states can restructure, merge, or abolish local governments through ordinary legislation; and local governments derive their authority from state charters and delegation rather than from a separate constitutional foundation. This relationship is sometimes expressed through Dillon's Rule, a legal principle articulated by Judge John Dillon in the nineteenth century: local governments possess only those powers expressly granted by the state, necessarily implied by express grants, or essential to the purposes of the local government.

Home rule is a significant modification of Dillon's Rule, adopted by many states. Under home rule provisions, municipalities are granted broad authority to govern their own affairs without requiring specific state authorization for each action, as long as they do not act contrary to state or federal law. Home rule cities can adopt their own ordinances, set their own local tax rates (subject to state limits), regulate their own zoning and land use, and manage their own personnel — with much more latitude than Dillon's Rule would permit.

Fiscal Federalism

Fiscal federalism refers to the financial relationships among levels of government — how money is raised, transferred, and spent across the federal system. These financial relationships are enormously consequential because they determine not only how government programs are funded but also, through the conditions attached to financial transfers, which level of government effectively controls policy.

Categorical grants are federal grants designated for specific, narrow purposes — building airport runways, providing school lunches to low-income children, conducting job training for displaced workers. The receiving government must spend the money on the designated purpose and must often match a portion of the federal funding with its own resources. Categorical grants give the federal government significant control over state and local programs in return for financial support.

Block grants provide money for broader purposes — community development, social services, homeland security — with fewer restrictions on how it may be spent within the general category. Block grants give recipient governments more flexibility but are sometimes criticized for reducing federal accountability for how money is used.

Unfunded mandates are a significant source of intergovernmental tension. When the national government requires states or local governments to implement a program or meet a standard but does not provide adequate funding to cover the costs, states and localities bear the financial burden. The Americans with Disabilities Act, for example, required local governments to make public facilities accessible regardless of whether federal funding was provided. The Unfunded Mandates Reform Act of 1995 attempted to limit such requirements, requiring Congress to explicitly consider costs before imposing major new mandates on state and local governments.

Institution in Practice
American State Government

To understand how federalism operates in daily life, it helps to look closely at an American state government — not as an abstraction but as an actual institution with branches, budgets, employees, laws, and relationships to both the federal government above it and local governments below it.

Consider a mid-sized American state such as Ohio, Colorado, or Virginia. Each state has its own constitution — often more detailed than the federal Constitution — establishing the structure of state government. The state legislature passes statutes on subjects ranging from the definition of felonies to the regulation of insurance companies to the licensing of barbers. The governor administers the executive branch, managing a bureaucracy of state agencies that oversee education, health, transportation, environmental protection, corrections, public welfare, and many other functions. The state supreme court interprets the state constitution and state statutes, independently of the federal judicial system except where federal constitutional questions are raised.

Each state manages a significant budget. The largest categories of state spending are typically Medicaid (health care for low-income residents, co-funded by the federal government), K–12 education (often partly funded by state aid to local school districts), higher education, transportation, corrections, and human services. The state both administers its own programs and functions as an intermediary for federal programs — receiving federal grants, administering federal requirements, and reporting back to federal agencies.

The state's relationship with local governments involves both authority and financial interdependence. The state sets the framework for local government structure and authority. The state distributes school aid to local school districts — often more than half of a typical district's budget — and imposes educational standards that districts must meet. The state establishes minimum standards for county health departments, county courts, and county elections administration. The state may set limits on local property tax rates and restrict local governments' ability to regulate certain subjects; many states, for example, preempt local firearm regulations.

This institutional portrait illustrates that American state governments are genuine, consequential governing entities — not merely administrative subdivisions of the federal government or supervisors of local communities. They are independent constitutional actors with their own authority, their own democratic legitimacy, their own fiscal capacity, and their own complex relationships with both the national government and the local governments within their borders.

Section 9.7

Advantages and Problems of Federalism

Federalism is not a neutral technical arrangement. It has real consequences for governance, policy, democratic accountability, economic equality, and civil rights. Students of government should be able to assess both the genuine advantages federalism offers and the serious problems it creates. Neither the advantages nor the problems are absolute — they depend on context, design, political conditions, and the specific policy area under consideration.

Advantages of Federal Systems

Policy laboratories. One of the most frequently cited advantages of federalism is that it allows states to serve as "laboratories of democracy" — experimenting with policy innovations that, if successful, may be adopted more broadly. The phrase comes from Justice Louis Brandeis's dissent in New State Ice Co. v. Liebmann (1932): "It is one of the happy incidents of the federal system that a single courageous State may, if its citizens choose, serve as a laboratory; and try novel social and economic experiments without risk to the rest of the country." Many significant national policies began as state experiments. Workers' compensation laws were pioneered in state legislatures before becoming national policy. The Massachusetts health insurance mandate of 2006 informed the design of the Affordable Care Act's individual mandate. Marijuana legalization began at the state level before spreading to multiple states and influencing ongoing federal legislative debates.

Accommodation of diversity. Federalism allows politically and culturally diverse regions to govern themselves differently in many areas. States with different political preferences, economic structures, cultural traditions, and population distributions can adopt different policies suited to their circumstances — rather than being subjected to uniform national rules that may fit none of them especially well. A state with vast rural territory and a sparse population may need different transportation policies than a densely populated urban state.

Proximity to citizens. State and local governments may be more accessible to ordinary citizens than the national government. Attending a city council meeting, contacting a state legislator's office, or participating in a county school board election involves less distance — literally and figuratively — than engaging with Congress or federal agencies. This proximity may enhance democratic accountability and civic participation.

Checking national power. Federal systems create multiple power centers, and states may resist national overreach in ways that unitary systems cannot. States have successfully challenged federal programs in court, refused to cooperate with federal immigration enforcement through so-called "sanctuary" policies, declined to implement federal marijuana prohibition, and used political mobilization to pressure Congress to change federal policy.

Economic efficiency through competition. Some economists argue that competition among subnational governments — where businesses and residents can choose to locate in jurisdictions with preferred tax and regulatory environments — creates incentives for efficient governance. Jurisdictions that tax heavily and regulate extensively may lose residents and businesses to those with lighter burdens, creating pressure for governments to provide services efficiently.

Problems of Federal Systems

Interstate inequality. When states have significant authority over programs like education, Medicaid, public housing, and infrastructure, the quality and generosity of those programs varies dramatically across states. A child growing up in a wealthy, highly-spending state may receive much better public education, more generous health care coverage, and more adequate public infrastructure than a child in a poor, low-spending state — not because of their individual circumstances but because of where they happen to be born. Federalism's tolerance of policy variation becomes, in practice, tolerance of geographic inequality in access to public goods.

The race to the bottom. The same competitive pressure that may encourage efficiency can also generate a "race to the bottom" in which states cut environmental protections, labor standards, tax rates, and social benefits to attract businesses and avoid the departure of wealthy taxpayers. If states compete primarily by reducing regulations and taxes, the competitive equilibrium may produce lower standards than citizens in any individual state would actually prefer — because each state fears that if it maintains high standards, it will lose businesses to less regulated neighbors.

Obstacles to national standards. Federalism makes it more difficult to establish and enforce uniform national standards in areas where uniformity is important. Businesses operating across multiple states must navigate dozens of different regulatory regimes, which imposes compliance costs. National efforts to address problems that cross state lines — climate change, opioid addiction, financial market instability, public health emergencies — face added complexity in a federal system where authority is divided.

Historical obstruction of civil rights. Perhaps the most serious criticism of American federalism is its historical role in enabling racial oppression. Southern states used their constitutional authority over elections, criminal law, education, employment, and public accommodations to establish and maintain systems of racial segregation and disenfranchisement for nearly a century after the Civil War. The doctrine of states' rights was invoked repeatedly to resist federal efforts to enforce civil rights. The civil rights movement ultimately required national intervention — through the Civil Rights Act of 1964, the Voting Rights Act of 1965, and federal court enforcement — to overcome state-level obstruction.

Coordination failures. Federal systems can produce significant coordination problems — including the kinds of problems the Articles of Confederation demonstrated. When multiple states must agree to coordinate on shared problems, collective action failures can result. During public health emergencies, the absence of a single national authority over public health can mean that responses are inconsistent, resources are not optimally allocated, and policies conflict across state lines in ways that undermine effectiveness.

Complexity and diffused accountability. The multiplicity of governments in a federal system creates complexity that may confuse citizens, increase compliance costs for businesses, and diffuse accountability. When a problem exists — poor school performance, deteriorating infrastructure, inadequate public health — it may not be clear which level of government is responsible or whom citizens should hold accountable.

Civic Debate
Should States Be Permitted to Have Stricter Regulations Than the Federal Government?
The Issue

In a federal system, the question regularly arises whether states should be permitted to adopt regulations that are stricter than federal minimums — higher minimum wages, stricter environmental standards, stronger consumer protections, broader civil rights protections — or whether federal standards should function as both a floor and a ceiling, preempting state regulations that go further.

Argument A — States Should Exceed Federal Standards

Proponents of state regulatory autonomy argue that federal standards represent political compromises reflecting national averages rather than the preferences of particular states. A state whose citizens strongly value environmental protection should be permitted to adopt stronger protections than a federal minimum negotiated partly by states that prefer looser rules. State regulatory autonomy allows citizens in each state to choose the level of protection they prefer. Innovation in regulation — trying new approaches that might improve outcomes — also requires room to experiment, which preemption closes off. California's leadership on automobile emissions, for example, produced significant environmental benefits nationwide, but it required permission to exceed federal minimums.

Argument B — Federal Preemption Should Establish Uniform Standards

Proponents of national uniformity argue that allowing states to exceed federal standards fragments the regulatory environment in ways that harm national markets. A business operating in multiple states must comply with the most restrictive state's rules, even if it operates predominantly where less restrictive rules apply. Furthermore, allowing states to be stricter may simply push problems to neighboring states rather than solving them: if one state prohibits a polluting industry, the industry may relocate to a neighboring state with weaker rules, while the pollution flows back across state borders. National standards address the problem everywhere at once.

Tradeoffs

The core tradeoff is between the value of uniform, comprehensive national standards — which ensure consistency, reduce regulatory complexity, and prevent races to the bottom — and the value of state diversity and autonomy, which allows adaptation to local preferences, rewards policy innovation, and preserves the federal character of the political system. The right balance depends on the nature of the regulated subject: how much externality flows across state lines, how much genuine local variation in preferences exists, and how much innovation is possible and valuable.

Section 9.8

Comparative Examples of Territorial Government

Federal and unitary systems take different forms in different countries, shaped by distinctive histories, cultures, demographic compositions, constitutional choices, and political traditions. This section examines several major examples, showing how different countries have answered the question of territorial power distribution in ways that illuminate both common principles and the wide range of possible arrangements.

Germany: Cooperative Federalism with Strong State Participation

Germany's federal system is significantly different from the American model, reflecting a different constitutional philosophy and a different political history. The German Basic Law (enacted in 1949 under Allied occupation following World War II) established a federal system with sixteen Länder (states) and a national government. But the German distribution of legislative and executive authority differs notably from the American design.

In Germany, most major legislation — criminal law, civil law, commercial law, environmental law, social insurance — is made by the federal government. The Länder are primarily responsible for administering federal law rather than making competing state law of their own. This means that the division of authority in Germany is more about administration than about separate lawmaking domains: the federal government sets the rules, and the Länder implement them through their own administrative agencies.

The Länder participate directly in federal lawmaking through the Bundesrat, the second chamber of the national parliament, which is composed of delegates from the sixteen state governments. Unlike the United States Senate, which is composed of senators elected independently of state governments, the Bundesrat is composed of officials appointed and instructed by state governments — essentially a chamber of state ambassadors to the federal government. Legislation that affects state administrative authority requires Bundesrat approval, giving the states a direct institutional voice in national policymaking.

German fiscal federalism also differs from the American model. Rather than having separate federal and state tax systems with complex grants-in-aid, Germany uses a system of revenue sharing, in which major national taxes are collected and then divided between the federal and Länder governments according to constitutional formulas. Additionally, the Finanzausgleich (fiscal equalization system) redistributes revenue from wealthier Länder to poorer ones, reducing regional fiscal inequality. This system means that German citizens receive more uniform public services across Länder than American citizens receive across states.

India: Asymmetric Federalism in a Diverse Democracy

India's federal system is remarkable both for its scale — a federation of nearly 1.5 billion people, 28 states, and 8 union territories — and for its management of extraordinary linguistic, religious, cultural, and economic diversity. The Constitution of India (1950) establishes a federal system with a notably strong central government — Indian federalism is often described as "quasi-federal" or as "federalism with a unionist bias."

The Indian Constitution divides legislative authority between the Union (national) government and state governments through three lists: the Union List (subjects exclusively within Union authority, including defense, foreign affairs, banking, and railways), the State List (subjects primarily within state authority, including public order, agriculture, and local government), and the Concurrent List (subjects on which both may legislate, with Union law prevailing in case of conflict). In practice, the Union's authority is very strong: the central government can dissolve a state government and impose President's Rule (direct central administration) when constitutional governance has broken down in a state.

India also practices asymmetric federalism — different states have different relationships with the national government and different levels of autonomy. Certain northeastern states have special protections for their distinct cultures and traditions. Scheduled tribe communities in several states have land protections that deviate from the general property law framework. This asymmetry reflects the enormous diversity of the Indian union and the political negotiations through which different communities were incorporated into the national framework.

Canada: Decentralized Federalism with Strong Provinces

Canada's federal system, established by the Constitution Act of 1867, is among the most decentralized in the world. Canadian provinces have significant constitutional authority over education, health care, natural resources, property and civil rights, and many other major policy areas. The provinces have their own constitutional standing and substantial fiscal autonomy, with their own income taxes, sales taxes, and royalty systems.

Canadian federalism has been particularly shaped by the presence of Quebec — a province with a predominantly French-speaking, Catholic cultural tradition within an otherwise predominantly English-speaking, Protestant country. Quebec's distinct cultural identity has generated repeated demands for either greater provincial autonomy within Canada or outright independence (sovereignty). Two referenda on Quebec sovereignty — in 1980 and 1995 — resulted in narrow votes to remain in Canada. The management of Quebec's status has driven significant constitutional development, including the entrenchment of language rights and the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms (1982).

France: Decentralization Within a Unitary System

France is one of the world's most prominent examples of a unitary state — and also of a unitary state that has undertaken significant decentralization without abandoning its unitary constitutional character. The Jacobin tradition in French political culture has historically emphasized a strong, unified national state, with equal treatment of all citizens under universal national laws rather than differentiated regional arrangements. Beginning with the decentralization laws of the early 1980s under President François Mitterrand, France substantially expanded the powers of its regional and departmental councils. Regional governments now have significant authority over economic development, transportation planning, secondary schools, and vocational training. But these powers are exercised within frameworks established by national law, funded substantially through national transfers, and subject to national oversight.

France's experience illustrates that a unitary state can accommodate significant regional diversity and administrative decentralization while maintaining the constitutional principle that local authority is derived from, and bounded by, national authority. The French model has been influential in many other countries that have sought to decentralize administration without creating the legal complexities of a federal constitution.

Switzerland: Federalism in a Multilingual State

Switzerland presents one of the world's most instructive examples of federalism in a context of profound cultural and linguistic diversity. Swiss territory is home to four official languages — German, French, Italian, and Romansh — and the country's twenty-six cantons reflect different linguistic traditions, different religious histories, and different economic structures. Despite this diversity, Switzerland has maintained a stable federal democracy for more than 170 years.

Swiss cantons have very significant constitutional authority — over taxation, education, policing, health policy, and many other subjects — and Swiss political culture strongly emphasizes cantonal and local autonomy. The principle of subsidiarity — the idea that decisions should be made at the lowest level of government capable of effectively making them — is deeply embedded in Swiss political practice. Swiss direct democracy, including referendum and popular initiative mechanisms at both the cantonal and national levels, further reinforces the importance of citizen and community input in governance.

Switzerland's success in managing multilingual diversity within a federal structure has made it a frequent reference point in discussions of how federal arrangements can accommodate cultural differences. However, the Swiss case also illustrates important contextual conditions: Switzerland is a relatively small, wealthy, highly educated country with a long tradition of political cooperation and compromise. Its particular form of federalism may not be easily transplanted to very different contexts.

Comparative Lens
Federal Systems — United States, Germany, and India

Three of the world's largest democracies — the United States, Germany, and India — each operate federal systems, but their federal arrangements differ in significant and instructive ways.

Constitutional Foundation

In all three, the federal structure is constitutionally protected and cannot be altered by ordinary legislation. But the specific protections differ. The United States Constitution reserves unspecified powers to the states through the Tenth Amendment, though the scope of federal authority has expanded enormously through broad interpretation of enumerated powers. Germany's Basic Law includes an "eternity clause" that permanently protects the federal structure. India's constitution gives the central government substantially more authority to intervene in state affairs than either the American or German systems — including the power to dissolve state governments and impose central rule.

Legislative Division

The United States and India both have dual legislative systems in which both national and subnational governments can make law in overlapping areas. Germany primarily allocates legislation to the federal government while allocating administration to the Länder — a fundamentally different design that produces more legal uniformity but preserves state administrative influence through the Bundesrat.

Fiscal Structure

American federalism relies heavily on complex systems of categorical and block grants, with significant variation in fiscal capacity across states and limited equalization. German fiscal federalism uses revenue sharing and strong equalization, producing more uniform fiscal capacity across Länder. India uses a planning-based transfer system and a Finance Commission that periodically allocates national revenues across states.

Managing Diversity

All three federations manage significant diversity, but through different mechanisms. The United States has dealt with regional and racial diversity partly through forced national integration — especially the Civil War and the civil rights movement. Germany's federal structure has been a mechanism of post-war reconstruction and reunification. India's asymmetric federalism acknowledges that different states have different relationships with the national government based on their distinct circumstances. These comparisons illustrate that "federalism" is not a single model but a family of constitutional arrangements, each of which makes different choices about the division of legislative authority, fiscal power, administrative responsibility, and mechanisms for state participation in national governance.

Primary Source Window
The Tenth Amendment to the United States Constitution (1791)

Context

The Tenth Amendment was ratified in 1791 as part of the Bill of Rights. Its adoption was directly connected to concerns raised during the ratification debates over the original Constitution (1787–1789). Anti-Federalists feared that the national government would use its broad enumerated powers and the Necessary and Proper Clause to gradually absorb state authority, leaving the states as mere administrative subdivisions of the center. The Tenth Amendment was offered as a reassurance: it expressed the principle that the federal government was one of limited, specified powers, and that all remaining authority was reserved to the states or to the people.

The Text

"The powers not delegated to the United States by the Constitution, nor prohibited by it to the States, are reserved to the States respectively, or to the people."

Analysis

The Tenth Amendment is notable for what it establishes and for what it does not resolve. It establishes the principle of reserved state powers — the idea that the national government does not have a general authority to legislate on all subjects, but only those the Constitution delegates to it. It makes explicit that the residual authority of sovereignty belongs at the state (or popular) level, not at the national level.

What the Tenth Amendment does not do is specify which powers are reserved. It says that powers "not delegated to the United States" are reserved — but the question of what has been delegated is itself the central contested question of American constitutional law. The Tenth Amendment provides a principle but not a formula: its practical application depends entirely on how the enumerated powers are interpreted. When the Supreme Court reads the commerce power broadly, the scope of reserved state powers shrinks. When it reads the commerce power narrowly, the states' reserved domain expands.

The Tenth Amendment has had a varied career in constitutional law. Courts largely ignored it as an independent restraint on federal power through much of the twentieth century. More recently, the Supreme Court has given it greater weight in cases like New York v. United States (1992) and Printz v. United States (1997), holding that the federal government may not "commandeer" state legislatures or state executive officials to implement federal programs — reaffirming that the states are not simply instruments of the federal government but constitutional actors with independent authority.

Rights, Responsibilities, Policy Lab, and Common Misunderstandings

Rights and Responsibilities in a Federal System

Federalism has significant implications for both rights and responsibilities in civic life. In the original constitutional design, the Bill of Rights limited only the national government, not the states. As a result, states were free under the original design to restrict speech, establish official religions, and deny due process in ways the federal government was forbidden to do. The Fourteenth Amendment (1868) fundamentally altered this relationship by prohibiting states from depriving any person of life, liberty, or property without due process of law, or denying any person the equal protection of the laws. Through a process called incorporation, the Supreme Court has held that most provisions of the Bill of Rights apply to state governments as well as to the federal government.

The result is that today, the major individual rights protections of the federal Constitution apply equally against both national and state governments. But this uniformity operates alongside significant variation in state-level rights: state constitutions often contain their own rights provisions, some of which are more protective than federal constitutional minimums. State courts interpreting state constitutional rights can provide greater protection than the federal floor, and many have done so in areas including privacy, education funding, and criminal procedure.

Civic responsibilities in a federal system are distributed across multiple levels. As a citizen, you have responsibilities to your national government (paying federal taxes, obeying federal law, serving on federal juries when summoned), to your state government (paying state taxes, obeying state law, serving on state juries), and to your local government (complying with local ordinances, paying local taxes, and participating in local governance through voting and civic engagement). This multi-level structure of civic responsibility reflects the multi-level structure of civic authority — and effective democratic citizenship in a federal system requires engagement at multiple levels, not just voting in presidential elections.

Policy Lab
Designing a Federal Response to a Public Health Crisis

Scenario

A highly contagious respiratory illness has emerged and is spreading rapidly across the United States. The disease requires different responses depending on local conditions: dense urban areas face different transmission dynamics than rural areas; some regions have better hospital capacity than others; and some communities have particular vulnerability factors that require targeted protection. At the same time, the disease does not respect state borders, and inconsistent state responses may undermine national effectiveness. Federal officials, state governors, and local health authorities each believe they should have primary authority over the response.

Stakeholders

  • Federal government (including the President, Congress, the CDC, and HHS)
  • State governors and state public health departments
  • County and municipal health departments
  • Hospitals and health care systems
  • Employers and businesses (whose operations may be restricted)
  • Schools and universities
  • Residents and families (who must comply with public health orders)
  • Vulnerable populations (elderly, immunocompromised, frontline workers)
  • Civil liberties advocates (concerned about mandatory measures)

Policy Options

  • Option A — Federal command. The federal government declares a public health emergency and imposes uniform national standards for testing, mask requirements, quarantine protocols, and business restrictions. States must comply with federal directives.
  • Option B — State authority with federal support. States retain primary authority over their public health responses. The federal government provides resources (vaccines, testing supplies, financial assistance) and coordinates information sharing, but does not mandate state policies.
  • Option C — Cooperative federalism. The federal government establishes minimum national standards (a floor) but allows states to adopt stricter measures as conditions warrant. Federal resources are directed to states that comply with minimum standards; states with unique circumstances may apply for waivers.
  • Option D — Local authority. Local health departments — which have the most direct knowledge of local conditions — have primary authority, with state and federal government providing resources and technical assistance without mandating local policies.
  • Option E — Interstate compacts. Groups of adjacent states form interstate compacts to coordinate their responses, adopting compatible policies and sharing resources, without requiring uniform national policy.

Evidence Needed

  • Data on disease transmission rates and hospital capacity by region
  • Evidence about which public health interventions are most effective
  • Analysis of how state and local governments have responded to previous public health emergencies
  • Data on economic impacts of various restriction policies
  • Evidence about compliance rates under voluntary versus mandatory approaches
  • Analysis of constitutional authority under emergency powers provisions at federal and state levels

Tradeoffs

Federal command maximizes coordination and uniformity but may not account for local conditions and may face state resistance that reduces compliance. State authority preserves democratic accountability at the state level and allows for local adaptation but may produce inconsistent cross-border policies and coordination failures. Cooperative federalism attempts to balance uniformity and flexibility but requires complex intergovernmental negotiation. Local authority maximizes local adaptation but may fragment the response in ways that allow disease to cross local boundaries freely. Interstate compacts allow regional coordination but exclude states that choose not to participate.

Common Misunderstandings

Misunderstanding 1
"Federalism means the federal government runs everything."
In a federal system, the word "federal" refers to the entire constitutional arrangement of divided authority, not just to the national government. The national government is one part of the federal system; the state governments are another. The point of federalism is precisely that authority is divided — the national government does not simply run everything.
Misunderstanding 2
"States' rights means states can do whatever they want."
States have constitutionally protected authority over many subjects, but that authority is bounded by the national Constitution. States may not violate constitutional guarantees including rights protected by the Fourteenth Amendment, federal law that preempts state law under the Supremacy Clause, or explicit constitutional prohibitions on state action (such as the prohibition on states entering into treaties or coining money). Reserved powers describe what remains to the states after the national government's constitutional authority is accounted for — it does not establish unlimited state power.
Misunderstanding 3
"Confederations are stronger than federations."
Confederations are actually weaker than federations, not stronger. In a confederation, the common authority is limited and can act primarily on member states, not directly on citizens. Member states may defy confederal decisions. Federal systems are stronger because the national government can act directly on individuals, enforce its laws through national courts and law enforcement, and exercise authority in all areas the constitution delegates to it — regardless of whether individual states agree.
Misunderstanding 4
"Unitary systems are always centralized and undemocratic."
Many of the world's most stable and successful democracies operate under unitary constitutional systems — including France, Sweden, Norway, Finland, Japan, and New Zealand. A unitary system simply means that constitutional authority is concentrated at the national level, not that all governance is done by a single body in the national capital. Unitary systems can and do practice significant administrative decentralization and local self-governance; they simply do not give subnational governments constitutionally independent authority. Democratic quality depends on many factors beyond whether a country is formally federal or unitary.
Misunderstanding 5
"In a federal system, the federal government always wins."
While federal law is supreme over state law when they conflict, the national government does not simply override state governments in all respects. The national government is limited to its delegated powers; in areas outside those powers, states retain authority. Additionally, the Supreme Court has held that the federal government may not commandeer state officials to implement federal programs, may not use its spending power to impose conditions so coercive that states have no real choice, and must respect certain structural protections for states even within areas where it has authority.
Misunderstanding 6
"Local governments have independent authority guaranteed by the federal Constitution."
In the United States, local governments — cities, counties, school districts, and special districts — are generally creatures of state law, not of the federal Constitution. The federal Constitution addresses the relationship between the national government and the states; it says little about local governments. Local governments derive their authority from state constitutions, state statutes, and state delegations. The federal Constitution does not protect local governments from state authority the way it protects state governments from national authority.
Misunderstanding 7
"Federalism protects civil rights."
The relationship between federalism and civil rights is historically complex and cannot be characterized simply as protective. In the American experience, federalism has both protected and threatened civil rights. Southern states used their constitutional authority to establish systems of racial oppression that required national intervention to dismantle — and invoked states' rights as the justification for resisting that intervention. Whether federalism protects or threatens rights depends on whether states are more protective or less protective than the national government in particular circumstances. It is not safe to assume that decentralized authority is automatically more rights-protective than national authority.
Misunderstanding 8
"The United States is the only federal country."
Federal systems are found all over the world and include some of the largest and most populous nations on earth. Among the countries that operate federal or quasi-federal constitutional systems are Germany, India, Canada, Australia, Brazil, Mexico, Argentina, Switzerland, Belgium, Austria, Malaysia, Nigeria, and the United Arab Emirates, among others. Each of these countries has its own distinctive form of federalism, shaped by its particular history, demographic composition, constitutional design, and political culture.
Chapter 9 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Section 9.1 The geographic distribution of power addresses where governing authority resides and how it is organized across the space of a political community. This question shapes nearly everything citizens experience as government — from the curriculum in their schools to the regulations on their neighborhoods to the taxes they pay. Political scientists identify four main reasons societies distribute authority across territory: managing diversity, protecting liberty, improving governance, and historical and political necessity.
  • Section 9.2 Federal systems divide constitutional authority between national and subnational governments, with both levels deriving powers from the constitution. Powers are categorized as enumerated (explicitly listed for the national government), implied (necessary to carry out enumerated powers), reserved (remaining with states under the Tenth Amendment), and concurrent (exercised by both levels). American federalism has evolved dramatically from dual federalism in the founding era to cooperative federalism in the modern period, shaped by civil war, reconstruction, the New Deal, and ongoing constitutional litigation.
  • Section 9.3 Unitary systems concentrate constitutional authority at the national level, with subnational governments as administrative subdivisions rather than independent constitutional actors. Unitary systems are more common globally than federal systems and are associated with smaller, more homogeneous countries and centralized historical traditions. Many unitary systems practice significant administrative decentralization and devolution — but the key constitutional distinction is that devolved authority is derived from national law and can be reclaimed by national legislation.
  • Section 9.4 Confederations are the weakest form of political union — arrangements in which independent states cooperate for limited, specified purposes while retaining fundamental sovereignty. The confederation cannot act directly on citizens and cannot compel member state compliance. Historical confederations, including the American Articles of Confederation, have generally been unstable because of collective action problems, enforcement gaps, and the demands of crisis — either evolving toward tighter federal union or dissolving.
  • Section 9.5 The practical architecture of federalism requires specific rules about which government has authority over which subjects. The Commerce Clause has been the central battleground of American constitutional federalism. The Supremacy Clause and the doctrine of preemption determine what happens when federal and state law conflict. Interstate compacts allow states to cooperate on shared cross-border problems without federal management, illustrating that states are active political actors, not merely subordinate administrative units.
  • Section 9.6 Local governments — cities, counties, townships, school districts, and special districts — are generally creatures of state law in the United States, not independent constitutional actors. They derive authority from state charters and delegation under Dillon's Rule, with home rule provisions expanding local discretion in many states. Fiscal federalism — including categorical grants, block grants, and unfunded mandates — shapes which level of government effectively controls policy outcomes and how equitably public resources are distributed across regions.
  • Section 9.7 Federalism offers genuine advantages: policy laboratories for experimentation, accommodation of diversity, proximity of government to citizens, checking of national power, and potential efficiency through jurisdictional competition. But it also creates serious problems: regional inequality in public services, races to the bottom in regulation, obstacles to national standards, historical obstruction of civil rights, coordination failures, and complexity that diffuses accountability. Neither the advantages nor the problems are absolute; they depend on context, design, and the specific policy area under consideration.
  • Section 9.8 Comparative examples from Germany, India, Canada, France, and Switzerland illustrate that federalism is not a single model but a diverse family of constitutional arrangements, each making different choices about legislative division, fiscal structure, administrative responsibility, and mechanisms for subnational participation in national governance. Germany uses administrative federalism with strong state participation through the Bundesrat and fiscal equalization. India has a strong central government with asymmetric state arrangements. Canada's highly decentralized provinces include Quebec's distinctive status. France practices significant decentralization within a unitary framework. Switzerland manages four-language diversity through cantonal autonomy and direct democracy.
Chapter 9 — Key Terms and Review

Key Terms

Federalism
A constitutional system in which governing authority is divided between a national government and subnational governments (states, provinces, cantons), with each level deriving its powers from the constitution rather than from the other level.
Unitary system
A constitutional arrangement in which governing authority is concentrated at the national level, with subnational governments existing primarily as administrative subdivisions of the center rather than as independent constitutional actors.
Confederation
A political arrangement in which independent states cooperate for limited, specified purposes under a common authority while retaining fundamental sovereignty; distinguished from a federation by the confederal body's limited capacity to act directly on citizens and enforce decisions on member states.
Enumerated powers
Powers explicitly listed in the constitution as belonging to the national government; in the United States, found primarily in Article I, Section 8, including the powers to tax, regulate interstate commerce, coin money, and declare war.
Implied powers
Powers not explicitly stated in the constitution but reasonably necessary to carry out enumerated powers; in the American system, authorized by the Necessary and Proper Clause and given broad scope in McCulloch v. Maryland (1819).
Reserved powers
Powers not delegated to the national government and not prohibited to the states, retained at the state level under the Tenth Amendment; includes most traditional domestic governance such as education, family law, property law, and local policing.
Concurrent powers
Powers that both national and subnational governments may exercise, such as the powers to tax, borrow money, and establish courts; conflicts between national and state law in concurrent areas are resolved by the Supremacy Clause.
Supremacy Clause
Provision of Article VI of the United States Constitution declaring that the Constitution, federal laws made pursuant to it, and treaties are the supreme law of the land, binding on state courts even if state law says otherwise.
Preemption
The legal doctrine by which valid federal law displaces conflicting state law; may be express (explicitly stated), field-based (Congress occupied the entire field), or conflict-based (compliance with both is impossible or state law obstructs federal purposes).
Dual federalism
A model of federalism ("layer cake") in which national and state governments each operate in distinct, non-overlapping spheres; associated with the early American republic when most domestic governance was left to the states.
Cooperative federalism
A model of federalism ("marble cake") in which national and state governments work together on shared programs, each contributing funding, administrative capacity, and regulatory authority; describes much of modern American governance including Medicaid, highway construction, and environmental regulation.
Devolution
The transfer of actual legislative or policy-making authority from a national government to subnational governments; may occur within a unitary system without creating a federal constitutional structure — the authority remains derived from national law and can theoretically be reclaimed.
Fiscal federalism
The financial relationships among levels of government, including how revenue is raised, transferred, and spent across the federal system; determines which level of government effectively controls policy outcomes through the conditions attached to financial transfers.
Grants-in-aid
Transfers of money from the national government to state and local governments, typically attached to requirements about how money must be spent and what standards must be met; the primary mechanism of cooperative federalism linking federal funding to state program administration.
Unfunded mandates
Requirements imposed by a higher level of government on a lower level without adequate funding to cover the costs of compliance; a major source of intergovernmental tension because state and local governments bear the financial burden of compliance without receiving corresponding financial support.
Home rule
A grant of authority from a state government to municipalities allowing them to govern their own affairs without requiring specific state authorization for each action, within the limits of state law; a significant modification of Dillon's Rule in many states.
Dillon's Rule
A legal principle holding that local governments possess only those powers expressly granted by the state, necessarily implied by express grants, or essential to the declared purposes of the local government; expresses the constitutional subordination of local government to state authority.
Interstate compacts
Formal agreements between two or more states, which may require congressional approval, allowing states to cooperate on shared problems that cross state lines without involving federal government management; there are hundreds of active compacts covering transportation, environment, criminal justice, and other areas.
Asymmetric federalism
A form of federalism in which different subnational units have different relationships with the national government and different levels of autonomy; found in India, Spain, and the United Kingdom, where historical, cultural, or political circumstances have made uniform treatment impractical or undesirable.
Subsidiarity
The principle that decisions should be made at the lowest level of government capable of effectively addressing them; prominent in European Union law, Swiss political culture, and Catholic social teaching; argues for preserving local decision-making authority rather than automatically centralizing.

Document Analysis Activity

Document Analysis
The Supremacy Clause and the Tenth Amendment Compared

Documents: The Supremacy Clause, Article VI, Clause 2 of the United States Constitution (1788); and the Tenth Amendment to the United States Constitution (1791).

Context: These two constitutional provisions together define the basic structure of the relationship between national and state authority in the American federal system. The Supremacy Clause was included in the original Constitution ratified in 1788; it establishes that the national Constitution, federal laws, and treaties are the supreme law of the land. The Tenth Amendment was added in 1791 as part of the Bill of Rights; it reserves to the states all powers not delegated to the national government.

The Supremacy Clause: "This Constitution, and the Laws of the United States which shall be made in Pursuance thereof; and all Treaties made, or which shall be made, under the Authority of the United States, shall be the supreme Law of the Land; and the Judges in every State shall be bound thereby, any Thing in the Constitution or Laws of any State to the Contrary notwithstanding."

The Tenth Amendment: "The powers not delegated to the United States by the Constitution, nor prohibited by it to the States, are reserved to the States respectively, or to the people."

Questions for Analysis

  1. The Supremacy Clause says federal law is supreme when made "in Pursuance" of the Constitution. What does this limitation suggest about the scope of federal supremacy? Does it mean that any federal law automatically overrides any state law?
  2. The Tenth Amendment reserves powers "not delegated" to the federal government. But how do we know which powers have been delegated? What constitutional provisions, interpretive methods, or institutions determine the scope of delegation?
  3. These two provisions might appear to be in tension — one asserting federal supremacy, the other asserting state retention of authority. How do courts reconcile this tension? What principles govern when federal law controls and when state law controls?
  4. Consider a situation in which Congress passes a law requiring state governments to create and maintain a registry of certain business entities, funding the program only partially. Using the Supremacy Clause and the Tenth Amendment, analyze whether this law would be constitutionally permissible.
  5. How has the practical application of these provisions changed over American history? What historical forces — political, judicial, economic, and social — have shifted the balance between national supremacy and state reservation?

Connection to the chapter: These provisions embody the fundamental constitutional architecture of American federalism: federal supremacy within the scope of delegated powers, and state reservation of everything else. They illustrate why the central question of American constitutional federalism is not whether federal law is supreme, but what the scope of the delegation is — and why that question has produced continuous political and legal conflict from the founding to the present day.

Research Project
Mapping the Federal Structure of a Policy Area

Choose one of the following major domestic policy areas: public education, environmental protection, health care (Medicaid or public health), transportation infrastructure, criminal justice, or housing.

Research how authority over this policy area is actually distributed in the United States federal system. Your project should address the following questions:

Chapter 10: Constitutional Interpretation and Change

Chapter 9 has shown that the distribution of power across territory is not fixed at the moment a constitution is written but is continuously renegotiated through political conflict, judicial interpretation, and historical change.

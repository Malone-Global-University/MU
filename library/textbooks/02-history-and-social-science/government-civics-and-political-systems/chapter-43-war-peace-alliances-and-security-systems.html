Chapter 43 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

On a summer morning in 1914, the assassination of one man in a provincial city of the Austro-Hungarian Empire set into motion a chain of decisions, ultimatums, mobilizations, and declarations that within six weeks pulled nearly every major power in Europe into the most destructive war the world had yet seen. No single government planned World War I in its totality. No individual leader or cabinet foresaw that the conflict would last four years, claim tens of millions of lives, topple four empires, and redraw the political map of the world. What happened instead was the product of alliance commitments, security fears, miscalculations, failed diplomacy, domestic political pressures, and the logic of strategic competition carried to catastrophic conclusion. The war that began in the summer of 1914 has been studied ever since as one of history's most sobering lessons in what can happen when conflict prevention fails.

War is among the most consequential of all human political phenomena. It reorganizes societies, destroys institutions, creates new states, and shapes the legal and moral frameworks that govern international life for generations afterward. But war is not simply a natural disaster that arrives without political cause or political cure. Wars are made by decisions — decisions about alliances, interests, threats, honor, opportunity, and the calculation of risk. They are also prevented by decisions: about diplomacy, deterrence, negotiation, institutional design, and the willingness of governments to constrain their own power in the service of shared security. Understanding why conflicts occur, how they escalate or are contained, how they end, and how their recurrence is prevented is one of the central tasks of political science and international relations.

This chapter examines war and peace as political phenomena. It begins with the causes of armed conflict, exploring the factors that make violence between and within states more or less likely. It then examines security dilemmas — the structural dynamic by which one state's defensive preparations can provoke fear in another, triggering competitive cycles that neither side originally intended. The chapter explains how alliances and collective defense arrangements attempt to manage security through shared commitment, and how those same arrangements can both deter war and draw states into conflicts they did not seek. It then distinguishes interstate wars from civil wars, exploring the different political logics and humanitarian consequences of each. The chapter addresses peacekeeping and peacebuilding — the international efforts to halt violence after conflict begins and to reconstruct political life so that violence does not recur. It examines arms control and disarmament as strategies for reducing the means of war even when the conditions that cause it have not fully changed. It engages just war theory, one of the oldest traditions in political philosophy, which attempts to determine when war is morally permissible and how it must be conducted. Finally, it surveys the full range of strategies and institutions for preventing conflict before it starts.

Students who have read the preceding chapters of this part of the textbook will recognize many of the concepts that appear here. Chapter 41 introduced the international system as a world of sovereign states operating in conditions of anarchy — without a world government capable of enforcing rules between them. Chapter 42 examined diplomacy, international law, and the mechanisms states use to negotiate agreements and resolve disputes short of violence. This chapter builds directly on those foundations. War is what happens when diplomacy fails or is not attempted, when law is disregarded or cannot be enforced, when security competition spirals beyond control, or when governments choose violence as a means of advancing their interests. Peace is what results when those same factors are managed successfully — not perfectly, and not permanently, but well enough that organized armed conflict is avoided or ended.

Understanding war and peace is not simply a matter of studying military history or strategic theory. It is a civic question of the highest importance. Democratic citizens in countries that maintain armed forces, participate in alliances, contribute to peacekeeping operations, fund defense budgets, and elect leaders who make decisions about war and peace bear a responsibility to understand what is at stake in those decisions. Knowing why wars begin, what conditions make them likely, what institutions and agreements help prevent them, and what moral constraints apply to their conduct enables citizens to evaluate the claims their governments make, to hold leaders accountable, and to participate meaningfully in the most consequential choices that political communities face. The study of war and peace is, in this sense, a fundamental part of civic education.

Opening Question

Two neighboring states have a long-running border dispute. Each has recently increased its military spending, citing the other's buildup as the reason. Diplomatic communications have broken down. A minor border incident has just occurred, and both governments are under domestic pressure to respond firmly. By the end of this chapter, you will have the conceptual tools to analyze this situation — to understand the security dilemma at work, to identify what mechanisms might de-escalate the crisis, and to evaluate what moral and strategic considerations should guide each government's response.

Section 43.1

Causes of War

What Makes Armed Conflict More Likely?

War is the organized use of armed force by political actors — states, governments, movements, or factions — to achieve political objectives. It is distinguished from other forms of violence by its scale, its organization, and its explicitly political character. Wars are not simply large fights; they are instruments of political will, used when those who command organized force believe that violence will achieve objectives that cannot be obtained by other means. Understanding why wars begin requires examining the conditions, decisions, and dynamics that make this calculation appear attractive, necessary, or unavoidable to those who make it.

Political scientists have proposed many theories about the causes of war, and no single theory explains all conflicts. Wars break out for different reasons in different contexts. Nonetheless, several major categories of explanation have emerged from decades of careful study of historical and contemporary conflicts, and understanding these categories helps make sense of why some situations are more dangerous than others.

Structural causes refer to features of the international system — the distribution of power, the absence of a central authority, the anarchic conditions in which states must provide for their own security — that create persistent pressures toward conflict. The most important structural insight is that states operating in an anarchic international system cannot fully trust one another. Because there is no world government to enforce agreements, states must be prepared to defend themselves, which means that the very act of preparing for defense can be perceived as a threat by others. At a structural level, wars are also more likely during periods of rapid shifts in the balance of power — when a rising state challenges the position of an established dominant power, or when the collapse of an empire or state creates a vacuum of authority that competing forces rush to fill.

Political causes involve the decisions and interests of governments and leaders. Wars are often fought over territory, resources, political control, ideological dominance, or national honor. Governments sometimes calculate that war will serve their political interests — expanding their territory, securing access to resources, eliminating a security threat, suppressing internal opposition, or satisfying domestic audiences who demand assertive action. Some political scientists argue that certain regime types are more prone to initiating conflicts: leaders in authoritarian systems without strong domestic accountability may face fewer constraints on the decision to use force. The long-running democratic peace theory observes that established democracies rarely fight wars against other established democracies, suggesting that the structure of democratic governance — including elections, public opinion, free press, and institutional checks — shapes the conditions under which war is chosen.

Economic causes intersect with political ones. Competition over trade routes, natural resources, agricultural land, water, or economically valuable territory has been a persistent driver of conflict throughout history. Colonial empires were sustained in part by economic competition for the wealth of subject territories. Modern conflicts over oil-producing regions, mineral-rich lands, or access to waterways reflect continuing economic dimensions of security competition. At the same time, economic interdependence — the degree to which states' economies are connected through trade, investment, and supply chains — is sometimes argued to reduce the likelihood of war by raising the costs of conflict for both sides. This argument has limits, however: interdependence did not prevent World War I among European states that traded extensively with one another.

Psychological and perceptual causes refer to the ways in which leaders and governments understand their situations, their adversaries, and their options. Misperception is a well-documented cause of conflict: leaders who misread an adversary's intentions, overestimate their own strength, underestimate an opponent's resolve, or fail to understand how their actions will be interpreted can stumble into wars they did not intend. Cognitive biases, nationalist sentiment, historical grievance, elite ambition, and the dynamics of crisis escalation all affect how leaders process information and make decisions under pressure.

Domestic political causes are sometimes underestimated. Leaders facing economic crisis, political opposition, or declining popularity sometimes find that external conflict — or at least the rhetoric of external threat — serves to rally public support, distract attention from domestic problems, and strengthen their political position. This pattern, sometimes called diversionary war theory, has been documented in historical cases, though it is not a universal law. Domestic political competition can also push governments toward more aggressive foreign policies when political entrepreneurs mobilize publics around nationalist, ideological, or religious causes.

Finally, historical and cultural factors shape the long-run likelihood of conflict between particular pairs or groups of states. Repeated past conflicts leave legacies of distrust, grievance, and zero-sum thinking that make future conflicts more likely. Contested histories — competing narratives about past wrongs, territorial claims, or national humiliation — fuel political mobilization around conflict-prone demands. In some regions, patterns of recurring conflict become self-reinforcing as each generation inherits the fears, grievances, and military investments of the previous one.

None of these causes operates in isolation. Most wars result from the interaction of multiple factors: a structural environment that creates insecurity, a political dispute that cannot be resolved through negotiation, a domestic political context that makes accommodation difficult, a history of hostility that reduces trust, and a specific precipitating event that triggers a decision to use force. Understanding the multidimensional character of war's causes is essential for thinking seriously about its prevention.

Security Dilemmas and Deterrence

The Trap of Mutual Fear

One of the most important concepts in the study of international security is the security dilemma — a structural dynamic in which one state's efforts to increase its own security make other states feel less secure, prompting them to take steps of their own that further reduce the first state's sense of security, creating a cycle of competitive buildup that can end in arms races, crises, and war. The security dilemma does not require bad intentions, greed, or aggression. It is a problem that arises from the combination of anarchy — the absence of a central authority in the international system — and the inherently ambiguous character of military power.

Consider the basic logic. State A, uncertain about the intentions of State B, decides to increase its military capabilities as a precaution. State B observes this buildup and, unable to be certain whether it is purely defensive or signals offensive ambitions, responds by building up its own military. State A, seeing B's buildup, feels even less secure and increases its forces again. This cycle can continue even if neither state ever intended to attack the other. The problem is that military capabilities are often inherently ambiguous: a tank, a bomber, or a ballistic missile can be used for defense or offense, and the state acquiring it may know its own intentions, but its adversary cannot be certain.

The concept was elaborated by political scientist Robert Jervis in his influential analysis of cooperation under the security dilemma. Jervis emphasized that the severity of the dilemma depends on two key variables: the offense-defense balance and the ability to distinguish offensive from defensive postures. When offensive military technologies are more powerful than defensive ones, and when it is difficult to distinguish defensive from offensive capabilities, the security dilemma is most intense, and the risk of war is highest. When defensive technologies are advantaged and when states can demonstrate clearly that their preparations are defensive, the dilemma can be partially mitigated.

Civic Vocabulary
Security Dilemma

A structural condition in international politics in which one state's efforts to increase its own security — through military buildup, alliance formation, or other means — cause other states to feel less secure, prompting countermeasures that ultimately leave all parties more insecure than when the cycle began. The security dilemma does not require hostile intent; it emerges from the combination of anarchic international conditions and the inherent ambiguity of military power. Managing the security dilemma is one of the central challenges of international security policy. States attempt to do so through arms control agreements, diplomatic communication, confidence-building measures, and alliance arrangements that shift the strategic calculus without eliminating underlying competition.

Deterrence

One of the most consequential responses to the security dilemma in the modern era has been the strategy of deterrence. Deterrence is the attempt to prevent an adversary from taking an undesired action by threatening to impose costs that outweigh any possible benefit. In simple terms: I will do something so painful to you in response that you will not want to attack me in the first place. Deterrence works through the adversary's calculation, not through direct physical prevention. The goal is to change what the adversary believes will happen if it acts, so that it chooses not to act.

Deterrence has two main variants. General deterrence aims to prevent a broad range of aggressive actions by maintaining a general capacity and willingness to respond forcefully. Immediate deterrence applies to specific, concrete threats in crisis situations, where one party attempts to convince an adversary that a particular action will trigger a particular response. Both forms depend on three conditions being satisfied: the deterring state must have the capability to carry out its threat; it must have the credibility to convince the adversary that it will actually follow through; and communication must be effective enough for the adversary to understand the threat and adjust its behavior accordingly.

Nuclear deterrence is the most extreme application of this logic. During the Cold War, the United States and the Soviet Union each accumulated enormous nuclear arsenals, and the central strategic concept that emerged was mutual assured destruction, or MAD — the recognition that both sides had enough nuclear weapons to survive a first strike and still deliver a devastating retaliatory blow. In this situation, neither side could rationally launch a nuclear first strike, because doing so would guarantee its own destruction in return. The terrifying stability of MAD rested on the very monstrousness of nuclear weapons: their catastrophic destructive capacity made their use suicidal, and therefore their main function was to prevent rather than to wage war.

Extended deterrence refers to a state's commitment to deter not only attacks on itself but attacks on its allies. The United States extended its nuclear deterrent to Western Europe and to Japan and South Korea during the Cold War, committing to respond to attacks on allies with the full range of its military capabilities, including nuclear weapons. Extended deterrence is inherently more complex than self-deterrence: it requires the protecting state to be credible not just to itself but to both the ally and the adversary, and it requires the ally to trust that the protecting state will in fact risk its own security on their behalf.

Civic Vocabulary
Deterrence

A strategy designed to prevent an adversary from taking an undesired action by convincing it that the costs of that action will outweigh any possible benefit. Deterrence works through the adversary's calculations, not through physical prevention. For deterrence to succeed, the deterring party must have the capability to carry out its threat, the credibility to convince the adversary it will follow through, and the ability to communicate the threat clearly. Nuclear deterrence, which relies on the threat of catastrophic retaliation to prevent nuclear attack, has been a central feature of international security since the Cold War, but its logic is also applied more broadly to conventional military threats, economic sanctions, and other forms of coercive pressure.

Arms Races and Their Dangers

The competitive dynamic of the security dilemma frequently produces arms races — periods of rapid and mutual military buildup in which states match and attempt to exceed each other's capabilities. The naval arms race between Britain and Germany in the decade before World War I saw both powers compete for dominance in battleship construction, contributing to the mutual suspicion and competitive atmosphere that made the July Crisis of 1914 so difficult to manage. During the Cold War, the United States and the Soviet Union competed in nuclear warheads, delivery systems, missile defenses, and conventional military capabilities at enormous economic cost.

Arms races can be dangerous in several ways. They consume resources that could be used for other social purposes. They can increase the perceived stakes of conflict, making leaders more desperate and more willing to take risks. They can create "use it or lose it" pressures — situations in which a state's military commanders believe that their forces are most effective if used before an adversary can strike first, creating incentives for preemptive attack in a crisis. And they can undermine the confidence-building and communication that are essential for crisis management, replacing them with suspicion and competitive logic.

Arms races can also end without war, through negotiation, exhaustion, or one party's economic inability to continue competing. The Cold War arms race ended largely because the Soviet Union's economy could not sustain continued competition with the United States, contributing to the pressures that eventually led to Soviet reform and collapse. Understanding both the dangers and the possible exits from arms races is essential for thinking about arms control, examined in Section 43.6.

Alliances and Collective Defense

What Alliances Are and What They Do

An alliance is a formal or informal agreement between two or more states to cooperate for security purposes, typically including a commitment to assist one another in the event of attack. Alliances are one of the oldest tools of statecraft, used by states throughout recorded history to pool resources, deter adversaries, and manage the balance of power. They vary enormously in form: some are codified in detailed treaties with specific military obligations; others are looser understandings based on shared interests and historical relationships. Some alliances are bilateral, involving only two states; others are multilateral, binding many states together in a common security arrangement.

The basic logic of alliance formation is straightforward: by combining their military capabilities and presenting a united front, allied states can deter adversaries who might otherwise be tempted to attack any one of them alone. A state that would hesitate to challenge one strong adversary may calculate that it can defeat a weaker opponent; an alliance erases that calculation by making clear that an attack on one will bring the others into the conflict. This deterrent effect is one of the primary purposes of modern alliance arrangements.

Alliances serve other purposes as well. They provide frameworks for military coordination, standardization of equipment, joint training, intelligence sharing, and logistical cooperation. They create forums in which allied governments consult on security policy, reducing misunderstanding and enabling coordinated responses to common threats. They can also serve as instruments of assurance — signals to allies that they will not be abandoned, which can reduce their own incentives to acquire independent military capabilities, including nuclear weapons.

The North Atlantic Treaty Organization

The most important military alliance in the contemporary world is the North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO), founded in 1949 in response to Soviet expansionism in Europe following World War II. The alliance's core commitment is contained in Article 5 of the North Atlantic Treaty, which provides that an armed attack against one member is to be considered an attack against all, and that each member will take such action as it deems necessary to restore and maintain the security of the North Atlantic area, including the use of armed force.

NATO began with twelve members and has grown over decades to encompass thirty-two members, including most of Western Europe, several Central and Eastern European states that were previously part of the Soviet bloc, and North American members Canada and the United States. The alliance has an integrated military command structure headquartered in Belgium, maintains permanent multinational forces in several member states, and conducts regular exercises and training activities. The United States, as the alliance's most powerful member, has historically contributed the largest share of NATO's collective military capabilities and has stationed forces in Europe throughout the alliance's history.

NATO's Article 5 commitment is deliberately ambiguous in one respect: it says that each member will take "such action as it deems necessary" — which does not legally require any specific military response. This ambiguity reflects the reality that no democratic government can pre-commit absolutely to go to war on behalf of an ally under all conceivable circumstances. The political and constitutional constraints on governments make absolute, automatic commitments impossible. What NATO does instead is create a powerful political and institutional structure that makes it very costly for any member to abandon an ally in crisis: the alliance's credibility, and by extension the deterrence it provides, depends on the belief that its commitments will be honored.

How the System Works
Invoking a Collective Defense Commitment

The process by which a collective defense commitment is invoked — and how an alliance decides whether and how to respond — illustrates the complexity of modern security arrangements.

  • Step 1An armed attack occurs on a member state, or a situation arises that a member argues constitutes a threat requiring collective response. The affected member notifies the alliance through diplomatic and military channels, invoking its rights under the treaty.
  • Step 2The alliance's governing body — in NATO's case, the North Atlantic Council, where each member has one vote — convenes. Representatives of all members participate. The Council consults on whether the attack triggers the alliance commitment, what the facts of the situation are, and what range of responses is available.
  • Step 3NATO's Article 5 was formally invoked only once in the alliance's history — after the September 11, 2001, attacks on the United States. NATO determined that the attacks constituted an armed attack against a member and invoked the collective defense commitment, leading to allied participation in subsequent military operations in Afghanistan.
  • Step 4Alliance members discuss what military, political, economic, or other measures to take. Some members may contribute combat forces; others may provide support functions, logistics, intelligence, or financial contributions; still others may offer only political solidarity. NATO's consensus-based decision-making means that all members must agree.
  • Step 5Once a decision is reached, NATO's military command structure — led by the Supreme Allied Commander Europe (SACEUR) — coordinates the operational response, integrating national forces into a unified command and executing the alliance's decisions.

The process is complicated by the fact that member states have their own constitutional requirements for military action — some require parliamentary approval — and by the reality that states will assess the risks and costs of involvement differently depending on geography, interests, and domestic politics. The alliance's strength comes from its institutional depth and its record of cohesion, but cohesion is never automatic.

How Alliances Both Reduce and Increase Risk

The relationship between alliances and war is more complex than it might first appear. Alliances can reduce the risk of war by deterring potential aggressors who would otherwise be tempted to attack individual states. A state that faces the combined military power of an alliance is far less likely to initiate conflict than one facing a single weaker adversary. This deterrent effect was central to NATO's purpose during the Cold War: the goal was not to fight a war in Europe but to make the cost of any Soviet attack so high that it would never be attempted.

But alliances can also increase the risk of war under some conditions. First, alliances can create moral hazard — the possibility that a state protected by an alliance commitment will behave more recklessly than it would if facing threats alone, knowing that it can rely on allies to bail it out. If a small ally picks a fight with a neighbor, expecting its larger ally to intervene, the alliance arrangement has contributed to the conflict rather than preventing it. Second, alliances can transform a localized conflict into a general war. The July Crisis of 1914 shows how alliance commitments can cascade: Serbia's confrontation with Austria-Hungary triggered Russia's mobilization, which triggered Germany's, which triggered France's and then Britain's. A regional dispute became a world war in part because alliances made each state's participation nearly automatic. Third, alliances can provoke the very threats they are designed to deter by convincing adversaries that they face encirclement or hostile coalition, prompting counter-alliance formation and increasing regional tension.

Collective Security versus Collective Defense

It is important to distinguish between collective defense and collective security. Collective defense, as embodied in NATO and similar arrangements, means that a group of states agrees to defend each other against external threats. The commitment is to members of the alliance. Collective security, by contrast, is a broader arrangement in which a large group of states — ideally all states — agrees that aggression by any state against any other will be met by the collective response of the whole group. The United Nations Security Council is the primary institutional embodiment of collective security in the contemporary international system: in theory, when one state attacks another, the Security Council can authorize a collective response on behalf of the international community.

In practice, collective security through the United Nations has been difficult to achieve, because the five permanent members of the Security Council each hold a veto over Council decisions. When the aggressor is one of these states, or when the conflict involves their interests, the Security Council can be paralyzed. The gap between the theory of collective security and its practical application is one of the enduring challenges of international order.

Civil Wars and Interstate Wars

Two Different Kinds of Armed Conflict

Armed conflicts are not all alike. A war between two or more sovereign states — an interstate war — differs in important ways from a war fought within a single state among competing factions, governments, or movements — a civil war, or intrastate conflict. Understanding these differences matters for analyzing causes, dynamics, humanitarian consequences, and the possibilities for international response.

Interstate wars — conflicts between the armed forces of recognized sovereign states — were the dominant form of major war for most of the modern era. The Napoleonic Wars, World War I, World War II, the Korean War, and the Iran-Iraq War are examples of interstate conflicts in which state militaries fought one another across recognized international frontiers. Interstate wars have become significantly less common since the end of World War II, for reasons that may include the deterrent effects of nuclear weapons, the institutionalization of international norms against aggressive war, the growth of economic interdependence, and the consolidation of democratic governance in many regions.

Civil wars — armed conflicts within states — have become the dominant form of political violence in the contemporary period. Since 1945, the vast majority of armed conflicts have been intrastate rather than interstate. Civil wars vary enormously in their character: some are conflicts between a government and rebel movements challenging its authority; some are ethnic or communal conflicts between groups competing for political power or resources; some are revolutionary struggles by movements seeking to overthrow an existing order; some are fought by separatist movements seeking independence; and some combine several of these elements simultaneously.

Causes and Dynamics of Civil Wars

The causes of civil wars share some features with the causes of interstate wars — competition over resources, political power, and security — but also have distinctive characteristics. Civil wars often develop in contexts of state weakness or failure: when a government lacks the capacity to maintain order, provide services, and enforce its authority throughout its territory, competing armed groups can emerge to fill the vacuum. Political exclusion — the denial of access to political power or economic resources to particular groups — fuels grievance that can motivate armed opposition. Ethnic, religious, or communal identity can serve as a basis for organizing armed movements, especially when identity groups perceive threats to their security, status, or survival.

Economic factors are deeply implicated in civil wars. Access to valuable natural resources — oil, diamonds, timber, minerals — can both motivate conflict and provide the financial resources to sustain it. The phenomenon sometimes called "conflict resources" refers to situations in which armed groups finance themselves by controlling and exploiting natural resource production, giving them both motivation and capacity to continue fighting even when political settlement might otherwise be achievable. At the same time, poverty and economic desperation provide a pool of potential recruits for armed groups, lowering the costs of mobilization.

Civil wars tend to be long. Research has found that civil wars are on average much longer than interstate wars. They are also especially destructive of civilian populations. Modern civil wars frequently involve deliberate targeting of civilians, mass displacement, sexual violence, and the destruction of infrastructure, health systems, and economic life. The humanitarian consequences — measured in refugees, internally displaced persons, deaths, injuries, and destroyed communities — are among the most serious challenges facing the international community.

Comparative Lens
Interstate and Civil Wars in the Post-1945 Period

The pattern of armed conflict since the end of World War II shows a striking shift from interstate to intrastate violence. The major wars of the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries were predominantly fought between states. The world wars involved dozens of states in combat against one another. Since 1945, however, interstate wars have been relatively rare, while the wars with the highest death tolls have been civil wars.

The Korean War combined civil and interstate dimensions: it began as a conflict between two Korean governments but drew in the United States, China, and other participants. The Vietnam War was similarly complex — nominally a civil war but deeply shaped by Cold War great-power competition. The Rwandan genocide of 1994, the civil wars in Sierra Leone and Liberia in the 1990s, and more recently conflicts in Syria, Yemen, and South Sudan have all been primarily civil wars with devastating humanitarian consequences and significant international dimensions.

This shift reflects several factors. The international norm against aggressive interstate war — codified in the UN Charter — has had some restraining effect. Nuclear deterrence has made great-power wars extremely costly to initiate. Decolonization created many new states with weak institutions, ethnic divisions, and contested political authority, creating conditions ripe for internal conflict. And the ideological competition of the Cold War expressed itself partly through support for rival factions in civil wars from Angola to Afghanistan. Understanding this pattern helps explain why much of the international community's focus on conflict prevention, peacekeeping, and peacebuilding over recent decades has been directed at civil wars rather than classic interstate conflicts.

Section 43.5

Peacekeeping and Peacebuilding

The Challenge of Halting Violence and Restoring Order

When armed conflict erupts — whether between states or within them — the international community faces two related but distinct challenges. The first is stopping the fighting: halting the immediate violence, protecting civilians, and creating conditions in which a political process can begin. The second is building peace: addressing the underlying causes of the conflict, reconstructing political institutions, restoring economic life, and creating conditions in which violence is unlikely to recur. These two tasks are often referred to as peacekeeping and peacebuilding, though the distinction is not always sharp in practice.

Peacekeeping refers to the deployment of international personnel — typically military forces, police, and civilian experts — to a conflict zone to monitor ceasefires, separate combatants, protect civilians, support the delivery of humanitarian aid, and create a stable environment in which political negotiations can take place. Peacekeeping in its original form was developed by the United Nations in the 1950s and 1960s, and it was built on three classic principles: consent of the parties to the conflict, impartiality among the parties, and the use of force only in self-defense. These principles distinguished peacekeeping from war-fighting: peacekeepers were not supposed to take sides or impose outcomes; they were supposed to be neutral facilitators of a peace process that the parties themselves had chosen.

United Nations peacekeeping operations have been deployed in dozens of conflicts over more than seventy years, from Cyprus to Cambodia, from Mozambique to Liberia, from Lebanon to the Democratic Republic of Congo. Peacekeeping has had real successes — Mozambique and Namibia are often cited as cases in which UN missions helped manage transitions to peace and democracy — and significant failures, including the inability to prevent the Rwandan genocide in 1994 and the massacre at Srebrenica in Bosnia in 1995.

Civic Vocabulary
Peacekeeping and Peacebuilding

Peacekeeping is the deployment of international military, police, and civilian personnel to a conflict zone, with the consent of the parties, to monitor ceasefires, protect civilians, and create conditions for a political settlement. Traditional peacekeeping emphasizes impartiality and restraint in the use of force. Modern peacekeeping missions often include a broader mandate covering humanitarian protection, civilian administration, disarmament, electoral assistance, and rule of law support.

Peacebuilding is the set of activities aimed at addressing the underlying causes of conflict and creating durable conditions for peace after violence has ended or subsided. It includes political institution-building, reconciliation processes, economic reconstruction, security sector reform, transitional justice, and civil society development. It is a long-term enterprise that often extends for years or decades beyond the end of active fighting.

The Evolution of Peacekeeping

The original model of peacekeeping — impartial forces monitoring a ceasefire agreed to by the parties — has been supplemented and in some cases replaced by more ambitious and more complex forms of international intervention. The end of the Cold War opened space for broader international engagement in conflict situations, and the 1990s saw a wave of more ambitious peacekeeping mandates. In Somalia, Haiti, Bosnia, Cambodia, East Timor, and Sierra Leone, international forces were authorized not merely to monitor agreements but to protect civilians, support transitional governments, disarm combatants, organize elections, and rebuild state institutions.

These "multidimensional peacekeeping" operations involve large numbers of personnel from dozens of contributing countries, complex mandates covering military, police, and civilian functions, and extended timelines. They have produced mixed results. In East Timor (now Timor-Leste) and Sierra Leone, international missions contributed substantially to building peace. In Congo and South Sudan, large and expensive missions have operated for years without achieving stable peace, partly because the underlying political and economic conditions that drive conflict have not been resolved.

A further evolution is the concept of peace enforcement — the use of military force by international actors not merely to separate combatants who have agreed to a ceasefire, but to impose peace on parties who are still fighting. The NATO bombing campaign in Bosnia in 1995, which helped end the Bosnian War, and the NATO intervention in Libya in 2011 are examples of international military action aimed at protection of civilians and coercive pressure on warring parties. Peace enforcement is more coercive than traditional peacekeeping and more controversial, because it explicitly takes sides against those whose behavior threatens civilians or violates international standards.

The Responsibility to Protect

One of the most significant developments in international norms regarding conflict and intervention has been the emergence of the Responsibility to Protect, or R2P. Adopted at the 2005 World Summit of the United Nations, R2P holds that sovereignty is not simply a state's right to be free from outside interference; it is also a state's responsibility to protect its own population from genocide, war crimes, ethnic cleansing, and crimes against humanity. When a state fails to meet this responsibility — or when it is itself the perpetrator of mass atrocities — the international community has a responsibility to respond, up to and including coercive measures authorized by the UN Security Council.

R2P represents a significant evolution in the norms of international order, because it suggests that sovereignty is conditional rather than absolute. It has been used to justify international action in Libya in 2011 and has been invoked in debates about situations in Syria, Yemen, and other conflict zones. It remains controversial, however. Critics argue that R2P can be used by powerful states as a pretext for pursuing strategic interests under a humanitarian cover. The selective application of the norm — robust international response in some atrocity situations, inaction in others — raises questions about its consistency and integrity. And the fact that Security Council vetoes can block action against the behavior of great powers or their allies means that R2P can be invoked for some situations but not others.

Institution in Practice
The United Nations Department of Peace Operations

The United Nations Department of Peace Operations (DPO), formerly the Department of Peacekeeping Operations, is the primary institutional body responsible for planning, deploying, and managing UN peacekeeping missions worldwide. Headquartered in New York, DPO works with member states to design mission mandates, recruit and deploy military and police personnel, coordinate civilian experts, and report to the Security Council on mission progress.

DPO does not maintain its own standing army. UN peacekeeping forces are assembled from voluntary contributions by member states, which provide uniformed personnel — soldiers, military observers, and police — for deployment. As of the mid-2020s, the top contributors of peacekeeping personnel were countries from South and Southeast Asia and Africa, including Ethiopia, Bangladesh, Rwanda, Nepal, India, and Pakistan. These countries provide most of the operational "boots on the ground," while wealthier countries often contribute financial resources, logistics, training, and specialized capabilities.

Each peacekeeping mission operates under a mandate established by the Security Council, which defines the mission's purpose, geographic scope, authorized size, rules of engagement, and duration. Mandates are renewed periodically — typically every six to twelve months — giving the Security Council ongoing oversight. Missions are led by a Special Representative of the Secretary-General, with military operations led by a Force Commander and police operations by a Police Commissioner.

The realities of peacekeeping are far more complex than the institutional framework suggests. Peacekeepers operate in some of the most dangerous environments in the world, often with limited resources and ambiguous mandates. Contributing countries sometimes impose national caveats — restrictions on what their forces can be asked to do — that limit mission effectiveness. Political disagreements among Security Council members can delay or water down mandates. And mission success ultimately depends on the willingness of the parties to the conflict to cooperate with the peace process, which cannot be created by external actors alone.

Section 43.6

Arms Control and Disarmament

Managing the Means of War

The strategies examined in previous sections — deterrence, alliances, peacekeeping — all operate in the shadow of existing military capabilities. Another approach to international security focuses on limiting those capabilities themselves: arms control aims to regulate the numbers, types, and deployment of weapons through negotiated agreements; disarmament aims at the reduction or elimination of particular categories of weapons; and nonproliferation aims to prevent the spread of the most dangerous weapons to additional actors. Together, these strategies represent an attempt to reduce the means of war even when the political conditions that produce conflict cannot be fully eliminated.

Arms control does not assume that states will become friends or that their interests will perfectly align. It assumes something more modest: that states with adversarial relationships can nevertheless identify situations in which limiting certain weapons serves their mutual interests by reducing the risk of accidental war, preventing the expense of unnecessary buildup, and reducing the catastrophic potential of conflict. The classic insight of arms control theory is that competing states sometimes have shared interests in managing their competition, even when they cannot resolve the underlying political disagreements that divide them.

Nuclear Arms Control

The most consequential and most developed area of arms control has been nuclear weapons. The terrifying destructive power of nuclear weapons — and the recognition, especially after the Cuban Missile Crisis of 1962, that the superpowers had come perilously close to nuclear war — provided strong motivation for both the United States and the Soviet Union to negotiate limits on their nuclear competition.

The first major nuclear arms control agreement was the Partial Nuclear Test Ban Treaty of 1963, which prohibited nuclear weapons tests in the atmosphere, underwater, and in outer space — addressing both the arms race dimension of testing and growing public concern about radioactive fallout. The Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT) of 1968 was a landmark agreement that divided the world into nuclear-weapon states (the five permanent members of the UN Security Council) and non-nuclear-weapon states. Non-nuclear states agreed not to develop or acquire nuclear weapons; nuclear states agreed to work toward eventual disarmament and to provide non-nuclear states with access to peaceful nuclear technology.

The Strategic Arms Limitation Talks (SALT I, 1972, and SALT II, 1979) placed limits on the numbers of strategic nuclear weapons delivery systems. The Strategic Arms Reduction Treaties (START I, 1991, and New START, 2010) went further, requiring actual reductions in deployed warheads and delivery systems and establishing verification mechanisms — on-site inspections, satellite monitoring, and data exchanges — to ensure compliance. The INF Treaty of 1987, negotiated by President Ronald Reagan and General Secretary Mikhail Gorbachev, eliminated an entire class of nuclear weapons — intermediate-range missiles deployed in Europe. The United States withdrew from the INF Treaty in 2019, citing Russian violations, and it lapsed.

Nonproliferation and Its Limits

The NPT framework has been the primary mechanism for limiting the spread of nuclear weapons, but it has faced persistent challenges. India, Pakistan, and Israel never joined the NPT and developed nuclear weapons outside the treaty framework. North Korea joined the NPT but withdrew in 2003 and subsequently tested nuclear devices, establishing itself as a nuclear-armed state operating outside the nonproliferation regime. Iran has pursued nuclear capabilities that raised proliferation concerns, leading to the negotiation of the Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action (JCPOA) in 2015, which imposed limits on Iran's nuclear program in exchange for sanctions relief. The United States withdrew from the JCPOA in 2018, and Iran subsequently expanded its nuclear activities.

The nonproliferation regime reflects both the achievements and the limits of arms control. It has successfully limited the spread of nuclear weapons for decades — the world has fewer nuclear-armed states than many analysts predicted in the 1960s. But it cannot fully prevent determined states from pursuing nuclear capabilities, especially when they perceive existential security threats that nuclear weapons seem to address. The tension between the regime's demand that non-nuclear states remain so and the perception that nuclear weapons provide unique security guarantees to those who possess them is a persistent source of strain.

Section 43.7

Just War Theory and Laws of War

The Moral Question of Force

The question of when the use of military force is morally justified is among the oldest in political philosophy. It does not admit easy or simple answers. War involves killing on a large scale — the deliberate taking of human lives as an instrument of political will. It causes suffering on an enormous scale, affecting not just combatants but civilians, families, communities, and future generations. Yet the complete rejection of war under all circumstances — absolute pacifism — leaves states and peoples defenseless against aggression, genocide, and conquest. The challenge is to develop moral criteria that can distinguish justified from unjustified uses of force, and that can impose limits on the conduct of war even when it cannot be prevented.

Just war theory is the oldest and most developed tradition in Western political thought for addressing this challenge. Its roots lie in classical antiquity — in the writings of Cicero — and it was developed extensively by Christian theologians, especially Augustine of Hippo in the fifth century and Thomas Aquinas in the thirteenth century. The tradition has continued to evolve through the work of international lawyers, political philosophers, and moral theologians, and its core concepts have been incorporated into modern international humanitarian law — the legal framework that governs the conduct of armed conflict.

Just war theory is organized around two fundamental questions: When is it just to go to war? (the question of jus ad bellum, Latin for "justice toward war") and How must war be conducted justly? (the question of jus in bello, Latin for "justice in war"). These two questions are treated as analytically distinct in the tradition: a war can be just in its initiation but conducted unjustly; and a war that is unjust in its initiation still imposes obligations of just conduct on combatants.

Jus ad Bellum: When Is War Justified?

Classical just war theory identifies several criteria that must be met for the initiation of war to be morally justified.

Just cause. The war must be fought for a morally legitimate reason. The clearest just cause is self-defense against aggression — the right of a state or people to defend itself against armed attack. Just war theorists have debated whether other causes are also just: the defense of innocent third parties against attack, the enforcement of international law, the prevention of genocide or massive human rights violations, or the recovery of unjustly seized territory. The expansion of permissible just causes beyond self-defense is one of the most contested areas of contemporary just war thinking.

Right intention. The war must be fought with the right intention — genuinely aiming at peace and justice, not at conquest, enrichment, or revenge. Even a state with a just cause can act unjustly if it pursues the war for selfish or destructive purposes.

Proportionality. The benefits of going to war must be proportionate to the costs — including the harm done to combatants and civilians on both sides. A war fought to achieve a minor objective at the cost of massive destruction and suffering fails the proportionality test, even if it has a technically just cause.

Last resort. War must be a last resort — undertaken only when all reasonable peaceful alternatives, including diplomacy, negotiation, mediation, and sanctions, have been tried and failed or are clearly futile. This criterion is often difficult to apply in practice, because it is rarely obvious that all alternatives have been exhausted.

Legitimate authority. War must be authorized by a legitimate political authority — not simply by any person or group with the means to wage it. In the classical tradition, this meant the proper sovereign authority of the state. In the contemporary framework, there are debates about whether international authorization — by the UN Security Council, a regional organization, or some other body — is required for wars beyond self-defense.

Reasonable chance of success. Just war theory generally holds that a war with no reasonable prospect of success is not just, because it causes suffering without achieving the goal that might otherwise justify that suffering. This criterion is controversial, because it seems to deny the right of resistance to peoples facing overwhelming odds.

Jus in Bello: How Must War Be Conducted?

The jus in bello tradition governs the conduct of war, independent of its initiation. Two core principles govern jus in bello.

Discrimination (also called distinction). Combatants must distinguish between legitimate military targets — enemy combatants and military objects — and protected persons and objects — civilians and civilian property. Deliberate attacks on civilians are prohibited. This principle is fundamental to the laws of war: it is what separates warfare from massacre. In practice, applying the principle is often difficult, because modern warfare frequently takes place in urban areas where combatants and civilians are intermingled, and because some objects — roads, bridges, power plants — have both military and civilian uses.

Proportionality in conduct. Even when a military target is legitimate, the harm caused to civilians incidentally — "collateral damage" in the language of military planners — must be proportionate to the military advantage sought. An attack that would cause massive civilian casualties in order to destroy a minor military target violates proportionality, even if the target itself is legitimate. This principle is enormously difficult to apply with precision, and its practical application is subject to extensive debate.

Modern just war thinking has added a third category — sometimes called jus post bellum, justice after war — which concerns the obligations of states after conflict ends: how to make peace, restore order, hold those who committed atrocities accountable, and reconstruct post-conflict societies in ways that are fair and durable.

International Humanitarian Law

The principles of just war theory have been codified in international humanitarian law (IHL) — the body of international law that governs the conduct of armed conflict. The core instruments of IHL are the four Geneva Conventions of 1949 and their Additional Protocols, which establish protections for wounded and sick combatants, prisoners of war, and civilians in both international and non-international armed conflicts. The Conventions are among the most widely ratified treaties in international law, with virtually universal state participation.

The Geneva Conventions are not self-executing: their effectiveness depends on the willingness and ability of states and armed groups to comply with them, and on mechanisms of enforcement and accountability. War crimes — serious violations of IHL — are prosecutable under international criminal law, including by the International Criminal Court (ICC) and special tribunals established for specific conflicts. The ICC, established by the Rome Statute of 1998, has jurisdiction over genocide, crimes against humanity, war crimes, and the crime of aggression. Its record of prosecution has been incomplete and contested, but its existence represents an important assertion that those who commit the most serious violations of the laws of war can be held individually accountable.

Primary Source Window
The Geneva Conventions (1949)
Context

The four Geneva Conventions were negotiated and adopted by an international conference in Geneva, Switzerland, in 1949, in the aftermath of World War II. The conventions built on earlier humanitarian law traditions dating to the nineteenth century, but dramatically expanded their scope in response to the mass atrocities — deliberate targeting of civilians, mistreatment of prisoners of war, and systematic destruction of civilian populations — that characterized World War II. The conventions were sponsored by the International Committee of the Red Cross (ICRC) and have since been ratified by virtually all states in the world.

Key Provisions (Paraphrased)

The conventions require that wounded and sick armed forces in the field be collected and cared for without distinction. They mandate humane treatment of prisoners of war, including prohibition of torture and degrading treatment, and specify minimum standards of food, shelter, and medical care. They require protection of civilians in armed conflicts, prohibiting deliberate targeting of civilian populations, deportation, hostage-taking, collective punishment, and pillage. They establish the principle of distinction between combatants and civilians as a fundamental rule of armed conflict. Common Article 3 — applying to all four conventions — establishes minimum standards even for non-international armed conflicts, including civil wars.

Analysis

The Geneva Conventions represent a remarkable achievement in international law. In the immediate aftermath of the most destructive war in human history, states agreed to bind themselves to specific rules governing the conduct of armed conflict — rules that would apply to their own forces and protect their own citizens. The conventions reflect the just war tradition's principle of distinction and its insistence that even in war, certain protections must be maintained. They also reflect the practical recognition that states have a shared interest in establishing reciprocal protections: if all states commit to treating prisoners humanely and protecting civilians, all states' soldiers and citizens benefit.

Section 43.8

Preventing Conflict

The Challenge of Prevention

Everything examined so far in this chapter — the causes of war, the security dilemma, the management of alliances, the conduct of peacekeeping, the limitations of arms control, the criteria of just war — points toward a single encompassing challenge: preventing armed conflict from occurring in the first place. Conflict prevention is both easier and harder than conflict resolution. It is easier because it operates before the violence, the destruction, the entrenchment of hostilities, and the massive humanitarian costs that armed conflict generates. It is harder because it requires identifying and addressing dangerous situations before they reach crisis, which demands political foresight, sustained attention, and the willingness to invest resources in preventing harms that have not yet materialized and may never materialize with certainty.

Conflict prevention operates at multiple levels and through multiple mechanisms. It is useful to distinguish between structural prevention — addressing the underlying conditions that make conflict more likely — and operational prevention — managing specific crises before they escalate to violence. Both are necessary, and they reinforce each other.

Structural Conflict Prevention

Structural prevention addresses the long-term conditions that create risks of armed conflict: poverty and inequality, political exclusion, weak or corrupt governance, competition over scarce resources, ethnic or communal discrimination, historical grievances, and the absence of legitimate mechanisms for peaceful dispute resolution. The argument is that when states have functioning institutions that allow citizens to manage political competition peacefully, when economic conditions provide basic security and opportunity, and when minority groups are included in political life and protected in their rights, the risk of armed conflict is substantially lower.

Regional organizations play important roles in structural prevention by promoting democratic norms, economic integration, and institutional capacity in their member states. The European Union, which began as a project of economic integration designed partly to make war between France and Germany — which had fought three wars in seventy years — structurally impossible, is often cited as the most successful example of structural conflict prevention in history. By integrating the economies, institutions, and political identities of European states, the EU created conditions in which armed conflict among member states became essentially unthinkable. The experiences of West Africa, Southeast Asia, Southern Africa, and other regions with regional organizations suggest that similar, if less complete, effects can be achieved elsewhere when regional cooperation is sustained.

Operational Conflict Prevention

Operational prevention focuses on specific situations where conflict appears imminent. It employs a range of tools: diplomatic engagement, early warning systems, mediation, economic incentives and disincentives, confidence-building measures, preventive deployment of military or police forces, and international monitoring.

Early warning — the systematic monitoring of situations for signs of rising tension, political instability, or impending violence — is a foundation of operational prevention. The UN Secretary-General, regional organizations, NGOs like the International Crisis Group, academic researchers, and intelligence agencies all contribute to early warning. The challenge is not only detecting warning signs but ensuring that warnings translate into timely political action — a gap often called the "early warning/early action problem."

Mediation — the involvement of a third party to facilitate negotiation between conflicting parties — is among the most important tools of operational prevention. Effective mediators must have credibility with both sides, access to relevant information, the ability to propose creative solutions, and sometimes the leverage to provide inducements for agreement or disincentives for continued confrontation. Mediators can be individual senior diplomats, heads of government, UN officials, regional organization representatives, or respected civil society figures. Successful mediation has prevented or ended conflicts in numerous cases — Kenya in 2008 and Colombia's peace process with the FARC beginning in 2012 are notable examples.

Confidence-building measures (CBMs) are agreements between adversaries to share information, limit certain activities, or communicate in ways that reduce mutual suspicion and the risk of miscalculation. The Hot Line established between the United States and Soviet Union after the Cuban Missile Crisis, the notifications required before military exercises, and the protocols established in various arms control agreements are all confidence-building measures. They do not eliminate underlying political conflicts, but they reduce the risk that those conflicts will produce accidental or unintended escalation.

Economic tools — development aid, trade preferences, sanctions threats, and investment guarantees — can be used preventively to create incentives for peaceful behavior and disincentives for conflict. States that would lose access to lucrative trade relationships or international investment if they initiated conflicts have an economic stake in peace. Conversely, targeted sanctions against individuals and entities associated with conflict risks can raise the personal costs of conflict for the leaders who make the relevant decisions.

Rights and Responsibilities
Citizens and the Decision to Use Force

In democratic systems, the decision to go to war — or to commit forces to peacekeeping, enforcement, or alliance obligations — ultimately rests on authority delegated by citizens through their representatives. This creates important civic responsibilities.

Democratic constitutions typically require some form of legislative involvement in the decision to use armed force. In the United States, Congress has the constitutional authority to declare war and to appropriate the funds that any military operation requires. The War Powers Resolution of 1973 attempted to constrain presidential use of force by requiring congressional notification and, after sixty days, authorization. Similar requirements exist in Germany, Japan, and other democracies, where parliamentary authorization for military deployments is legally or constitutionally required.

Citizens bear responsibility for understanding the decisions their governments make about war and peace. This includes evaluating the claims governments make about threats and justifications for force; understanding the real costs of military action — in lives, resources, and the risk of escalation; assessing whether alternatives have been genuinely exhausted; and holding leaders accountable for the consequences of their decisions. The principles of just war theory provide a framework that citizens as well as governments can use to evaluate these claims: Is the cause genuinely just? Is force truly a last resort? Are the means proportionate to the objectives? Will the action make things better or worse? Civic engagement with questions of war and peace is not only a right but a responsibility in democratic systems. The power to commit a nation to armed conflict is among the most consequential powers government possesses.

Civic Debate
When Is Military Intervention Justified?
The Issue

The question of when, if ever, it is appropriate for one state or a group of states to intervene militarily in another state — whether to stop a civil war, prevent genocide, respond to massive human rights violations, or protect civilians from their own government — is one of the most contested in contemporary international politics. It sits at the intersection of sovereignty, human rights, international law, and the practical realities of military power.

Argument A — The Case for Intervention

The principle of sovereignty cannot be an absolute shield behind which governments commit atrocities against their own people. When a government engages in genocide, ethnic cleansing, or systematic mass murder, it has forfeited the moral claim to non-interference. The international community has both the right and the responsibility to protect innocent people, even when doing so requires military force. Waiting for the Security Council to authorize action is not always possible, given the veto power of permanent members. The failures of the international community to prevent genocide in Rwanda and Srebrenica in the 1990s demonstrate the terrible costs of inaction. The Responsibility to Protect provides a normative framework for collective action that should be strengthened, not abandoned.

Argument B — The Case for Restraint

Military intervention without clear legal authorization — particularly without UN Security Council approval — sets a dangerous precedent. If powerful states can intervene militarily whenever they judge that another government has committed sufficient abuses, the door is opened to selective enforcement, geopolitical manipulation, and the abuse of humanitarian justifications to pursue strategic interests. Interventions frequently cause more suffering than they prevent: they destabilize fragile states, create power vacuums, extend conflicts, and produce complex humanitarian emergencies that persist for years. The record of military interventions — in Iraq, Libya, and elsewhere — suggests that even well-intentioned interventions can have catastrophic unintended consequences. Respecting sovereignty, imperfect as it is, may be a more reliable protection for most people in most situations than creating a norm of intervention that powerful states can invoke selectively.

Other Perspectives

Some analysts argue for a middle path: humanitarian intervention should be permissible when a genuine emergency exists, when it has the broadest possible multilateral authorization, when it is limited to the minimum force necessary to stop the atrocities, and when it includes a credible plan for post-intervention stability. Others argue that non-military tools — sanctions, international prosecution, diplomatic pressure, support for civil society — are more effective and less dangerous than military intervention in most situations.

Tradeoffs

Each position protects something important and risks something important. Strong sovereignty norms protect all states from unwanted interference but can shield governments that commit atrocities. A robust intervention norm protects potential victims of state violence but can be abused by powerful states pursuing strategic interests under humanitarian cover. The tension between these values is genuine and unlikely to be fully resolved through any formula.

Chapter 43 — Common Misunderstandings

Clearing Up Common Errors

Misunderstanding 1
"War is caused by human nature — people are naturally violent, so conflict is inevitable."
Correction
While human beings are capable of violence, the evidence does not support the view that war is simply an expression of unchangeable human nature. Wars are political decisions made by governments and leaders under specific conditions. The variation in the frequency of war across different societies, historical periods, and political systems shows that structural, institutional, and political factors matter enormously. The dramatic decline in interstate wars since 1945, and the existence of large regions of the world where armed conflict is extremely rare, suggests that human beings are quite capable of organizing political life without war when conditions and institutions support peaceful competition.
Misunderstanding 2
"Deterrence guarantees peace — as long as both sides can destroy each other, neither will attack."
Correction
Deterrence reduces the probability of rational, deliberate attack, but it does not eliminate risk. Deterrence can fail if leaders miscalculate, if communications break down, if technical failures produce false alarms, or if a leader decides that the threat of retaliation is not credible. The Cuban Missile Crisis came closer to nuclear war than most people realize, partly because of misinformation and miscommunication. Deterrence is a strategy for reducing risk, not a guarantee of safety.
Misunderstanding 3
"Arms control treaties don't work because states will cheat."
Correction
Arms control treaties have often included robust verification mechanisms — including on-site inspections, satellite monitoring, and data exchanges — that make cheating detectable. States have generally complied with major arms control agreements more than they have violated them, because the benefits of mutual restraint often outweigh the gains from cheating. When violations have occurred, they typically do not remain secret for long. The history of nuclear arms control shows substantial achievements: there are far fewer nuclear weapons in the world today than at the height of the Cold War.
Misunderstanding 4
"Peacekeeping forces can impose peace on warring parties."
Correction
Traditional peacekeeping operations are not designed to impose peace by force; they are designed to support peace agreements already reached by the parties. When peacekeeping forces have been asked to operate in situations without a genuine peace to keep, they have frequently struggled or failed. Effective peacekeeping requires the consent and cooperation of the parties and cannot substitute for a political settlement that addresses the underlying conflict. Peace enforcement — using force to compel compliance — is a more coercive approach with its own limitations and risks.
Misunderstanding 5
"Just war theory says that some wars are good."
Correction
Just war theory does not argue that war is good; it argues that war is sometimes permissible as the lesser of evils. The tradition begins from the recognition that war is terrible and deeply regrettable, and that it can only be justified when its costs are outweighed by the harms it prevents or addresses. The purpose of just war criteria is to set a high bar for the use of force, not to celebrate war. A war can meet just war criteria and still be a tragedy.
Misunderstanding 6
"Alliances always prevent war."
Correction
Alliances can deter war by raising the costs of aggression, but they can also contribute to escalation by entangling states in conflicts they might otherwise avoid, creating bloc dynamics that make crises harder to manage, and giving smaller states incentives for reckless behavior. The experience of 1914 demonstrates how alliance commitments can transform a regional conflict into a world war. Alliances are tools of security management, not guarantees of peace.
Misunderstanding 7
"Civil wars are simply internal matters and don't affect international relations."
Correction
Civil wars routinely have major international dimensions. They generate refugees who cross borders, creating pressures on neighboring states. They attract external intervention by states pursuing their interests or those of ethnic or ideological kin. They can destabilize entire regions by spreading violence, weapons, and armed groups across borders. And they often give rise to international humanitarian crises that draw the attention and resources of the international community. The idea that civil wars are purely internal is not supported by historical evidence.
Misunderstanding 8
"Conflict prevention is just wishful thinking — wars happen when they're going to happen."
Correction
While conflict cannot always be prevented, the evidence shows that specific interventions — diplomatic engagement, mediation, development assistance, institutional reform, economic integration — have contributed to preventing conflicts that might otherwise have occurred. The transformation of Western Europe from a region of recurring catastrophic war to a region of stable peace is the most compelling example. Conflict prevention is difficult and imperfect, but it is not futile.
Chapter 43 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 43.1War and armed conflict are not simply natural phenomena; they are the products of decisions shaped by structural, political, economic, psychological, domestic, and historical factors. No single cause explains all conflicts, and most wars result from the interaction of multiple factors. Understanding the causes of war is the foundation for developing strategies to prevent it. The democratic peace theory, the role of misperception, and the logic of diversionary war all illuminate how different conditions and incentive structures affect the likelihood that leaders will choose armed force.
  • Sec 43.2The security dilemma is a structural dynamic in which one state's defensive preparations generate fear and countermeasures in other states, producing competitive cycles even without hostile intent. Deterrence — the attempt to prevent attack by threatening unacceptable costs — emerged as the central strategy for managing this dilemma, especially in the nuclear era. Mutual assured destruction rested on the terrifying logic that catastrophic retaliation made nuclear first strikes irrational. Extended deterrence complicates these calculations by requiring credibility not only to oneself but to allies and adversaries simultaneously. Arms races driven by the security dilemma consume resources, raise the stakes of conflict, and can create pressures for preemptive action in a crisis.
  • Sec 43.3Alliances pool military capabilities and deter potential aggressors by making attack too costly. NATO is the most developed contemporary alliance, built on Article 5's collective defense commitment. Alliances reduce risk through deterrence but can also increase risk through moral hazard, automatic escalation dynamics, and the bloc formations that make crises harder to manage. The distinction between collective defense — protecting alliance members — and collective security — the broader UN framework for responding to international aggression — reflects different ambitions and institutional designs with different strengths and limitations.
  • Sec 43.4Interstate wars have become significantly less common since 1945, while civil wars have become the dominant form of organized political violence. Civil wars have distinctive causes — state weakness, political exclusion, resource competition, communal identity — and distinctive dynamics, including longer duration, greater civilian targeting, and complex international dimensions. The post-1945 shift toward intrastate conflict has shaped the international community's focus on peacekeeping, peacebuilding, and humanitarian protection.
  • Sec 43.5Peacekeeping — deploying international personnel to monitor ceasefires and protect civilians — has evolved from traditional impartial operations into multidimensional missions covering military, police, and civilian functions. Peace enforcement, which uses coercive force to halt ongoing violence, is more ambitious and more controversial. The Responsibility to Protect asserts that sovereignty is conditional — states that fail to protect their populations from mass atrocities forfeit the norm of non-interference. Peacebuilding after conflict requires sustained attention to security sector reform, political institution-building, economic reconstruction, and reconciliation — a long-term enterprise that extends well beyond the end of active fighting.
  • Sec 43.6Arms control seeks to limit the means of war through negotiated agreements, building on the insight that competing states sometimes have shared interests in managing their rivalry. Nuclear arms control — including the NPT, START treaties, and other agreements — achieved substantial reductions from Cold War peaks while facing ongoing challenges from states outside the regime and from the erosion of treaties. The nonproliferation regime has limited the spread of nuclear weapons but cannot fully prevent determined states from pursuing them. Chemical and conventional arms control extends these principles to other categories of weapons.
  • Sec 43.7Just war theory provides the most developed philosophical framework for evaluating the moral dimensions of armed conflict. Its jus ad bellum criteria — just cause, right intention, proportionality, last resort, legitimate authority, and reasonable chance of success — set conditions for when initiating war is morally permissible. Its jus in bello principles — discrimination between combatants and civilians, and proportionality in the use of force — govern how war must be conducted. These principles have been codified in international humanitarian law, including the Geneva Conventions, and are enforced through international criminal tribunals and the ICC.
  • Sec 43.8Conflict prevention operates through structural measures — addressing the long-term conditions that make conflict more likely, including poverty, exclusion, and weak governance — and operational measures — managing specific crises through early warning, mediation, confidence-building measures, preventive deployment, and economic tools. Regional integration, exemplified by the European Union, is among the most powerful forms of structural prevention. Citizens in democratic states bear civic responsibilities to understand and engage with their governments' decisions about war and peace, applying the frameworks of just war theory and international law to evaluate the claims governments make about necessity, justification, and conduct.

Key Terms

Alliance
A formal or informal agreement between two or more states to cooperate for security purposes, typically including a commitment to assist one another in the event of attack.
Arms control
Negotiated agreements between states to regulate the numbers, types, deployment, or capabilities of weapons systems, aimed at reducing the risk and scale of armed conflict.
Arms race
A cycle of competitive military buildup in which two or more states respond to each other's military acquisitions by increasing their own, driven by mutual fear and the security dilemma.
Civil war
An armed conflict fought within a single state, typically between a government and rebel movements, or among competing factions, groups, or regions. Also called intrastate conflict.
Collective defense
An arrangement in which a group of states agrees to defend one another against external attack, as embodied in NATO's Article 5 commitment.
Collective security
A broad international arrangement in which a large group of states agrees that aggression by any member will be met by the collective response of the group, as embodied in the UN Security Council.
Confidence-building measures
Agreements between adversaries to share information, limit certain activities, or improve communication in ways that reduce mutual suspicion and the risk of miscalculation.
Deterrence
A strategy designed to prevent an adversary from taking an undesired action by convincing it that the costs of that action will outweigh any potential benefit.
Disarmament
The reduction or elimination of particular categories of weapons, as distinct from arms control, which regulates but does not necessarily eliminate them.
Extended deterrence
A state's commitment to deter not only attacks on itself but attacks on its allies, typically by pledging to respond with the full range of its military capabilities.
International humanitarian law
The body of international law — including the Geneva Conventions and their protocols — that governs the conduct of armed conflict, protecting wounded combatants, prisoners of war, and civilians.
Interstate war
Armed conflict between the organized military forces of two or more sovereign states, fought across recognized international frontiers.
Jus ad bellum
Latin for "justice toward war"; the branch of just war theory that addresses when the initiation of armed conflict is morally justified.
Jus in bello
Latin for "justice in war"; the branch of just war theory that addresses how armed conflict must be conducted, regardless of the justice of its initiation.
Just war theory
A tradition of political and moral philosophy rooted in classical and Christian thought that attempts to determine when the use of military force is morally permissible and how it must be conducted.
Laws of war
The body of legal rules, embodied principally in the Geneva Conventions and customary international law, governing the conduct of armed conflict. Also called international humanitarian law.
Mediation
The involvement of a third party to facilitate negotiation and assist conflicting parties in reaching a peaceful settlement, without the authority to impose an outcome.
Mutual assured destruction (MAD)
The Cold War strategic doctrine in which both the United States and the Soviet Union maintained sufficient nuclear forces to survive a first strike and deliver a devastating retaliatory blow, making deliberate nuclear war suicidal for both sides.
Nonproliferation
The effort to prevent the spread of nuclear weapons — and sometimes other weapons of mass destruction — to states or non-state actors that do not currently possess them.
Peace enforcement
The use of military force by international actors to impose peace on parties that have not agreed to a ceasefire or are violating one, going beyond traditional peacekeeping's principle of impartiality.
Peacebuilding
The set of long-term activities aimed at addressing the underlying causes of conflict and creating durable conditions for peace, including institution-building, reconciliation, economic reconstruction, and security sector reform.
Peacekeeping
The deployment of international military, police, and civilian personnel to a conflict zone, typically with the consent of the parties, to monitor ceasefires, protect civilians, and create conditions for a political settlement.
Responsibility to Protect (R2P)
A norm adopted at the 2005 UN World Summit holding that sovereignty entails a responsibility to protect a population from mass atrocities, and that when a state fails this responsibility, the international community has an obligation to respond.
Security dilemma
A structural dynamic in international politics in which one state's efforts to increase its own security cause other states to feel less secure, prompting countermeasures that ultimately leave all parties more insecure.
War
The organized use of armed force by political actors to achieve political objectives, characterized by its scale, organization, and explicitly political character.
War crimes
Serious violations of international humanitarian law — including deliberate targeting of civilians, torture of prisoners of war, and use of prohibited weapons — that are subject to individual criminal prosecution under international criminal law.

Article 5 of the North Atlantic Treaty (1949)
Document Type

Treaty Provision — International Security Commitment

Historical and Political Context

The North Atlantic Treaty was signed in Washington, D.C., on April 4, 1949, by the United States, Canada, and ten Western European nations, in response to the perceived threat of Soviet expansionism in Europe following World War II. It established the North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO) and remains the foundational document of the alliance. Article 5 is the treaty's most important provision, establishing the commitment to collective defense that has been the core of NATO's deterrent posture for more than seventy years. The treaty was negotiated in a context of deep anxiety about Soviet intentions, the recent experience of appeasement leading to World War II, and the determination of Western democracies to create a credible deterrent before, rather than after, another catastrophe.

Key Language (Paraphrased)
The parties agree that an armed attack against one or more of them in Europe or North America shall be considered an attack against them all, and that in response each party shall take such action as it deems necessary, including the use of armed force, to restore and maintain the security of the North Atlantic area.
Analysis Questions
  1. The provision states that each party "shall take such action as it deems necessary" — including but not limited to armed force. Why do you think the treaty uses this language rather than making an explicit, automatic commitment to use military force? What are the advantages and disadvantages of this formulation for deterrence and for alliance cohesion?
  2. Article 5 was invoked only once in NATO's history — after the September 11, 2001, attacks on the United States. Why might Article 5 have been so rarely invoked? What does this tell you about the relationship between legal commitments and political decisions in international relations?
  3. The provision covers attacks against NATO members "in Europe or North America." How does this geographic scope reflect the political context of the treaty's negotiation in 1949? How has the alliance's activities evolved beyond this geographic scope in subsequent decades?
  4. From the perspective of just war theory, how would you evaluate the use of Article 5 to justify military action? What criteria would need to be met for military action taken under Article 5 to be morally justified?
  5. How does Article 5's collective defense commitment differ from the collective security framework of the United Nations Security Council? What are the strengths and limitations of each approach?

Chapter 43 — Closing Reflection

Connecting the Chapter to the Larger Study of Government

The study of war, peace, alliances, and security systems returns us, at the end, to one of the most fundamental questions in political life: how can communities of human beings — divided by interests, identities, histories, and fears — organize themselves to resolve their conflicts without organized killing? No fully satisfying answer has been found. The international system remains one in which armed conflict is possible, in which the threat of catastrophic violence — including nuclear annihilation — shapes the behavior of states, and in which people in many parts of the world continue to experience the devastating consequences of war. This is not a record of failure alone. The relative decline in interstate wars since 1945, the development of international law and institutions, the achievements of arms control, the evolution of peacekeeping, and the growth of regional integration all represent genuine progress in the management of armed conflict — progress that has preserved the peace in many regions that might otherwise have experienced catastrophic war.

What this chapter reveals is that war and peace are not simply natural conditions that political communities find themselves in. They are the products of choices — choices about institutions, alliances, arms, diplomacy, and the willingness to invest in conflict prevention. The institutions examined in this chapter — international law, peacekeeping bodies, arms control regimes, alliance frameworks, mediation mechanisms — are not natural features of the international landscape. They were built through sustained political effort, negotiation, and commitment by generations of leaders and citizens who understood that organized violence was not inevitable and that collective institutions could reduce its likelihood. Their imperfect achievements should be valued and their ongoing work supported, not because any of them has eliminated war but because each of them has contributed, in some measure, to making war less likely.

Students studying war, peace, and security systems are engaging with questions that will shape their political world throughout their lives. The decisions their governments make about alliances, arms, deterrence, peacekeeping, and the use of force will affect not only the security of their own countries but the lives of people around the world. Just war theory reminds us that those decisions are not merely strategic but moral: that the use of organized violence always carries a heavy burden of justification that citizens as well as governments are responsible for weighing. Conflict prevention reminds us that the most important work in international security happens not on the battlefield but in the difficult, patient, often unglamorous efforts of diplomats, development workers, institution-builders, and civil society organizations who address the conditions that make war more likely before violence erupts. And the full record of international security efforts — achievements and failures alike — reminds us that the choices we make about how to organize international life are consequential. The alternatives to effective international institutions are not necessarily a peaceful world without institutions; they may be a more dangerous world in which the security dilemma operates with fewer constraints and fewer resources for prevention.

This chapter connects directly to the larger project of this textbook. Government is not only domestic; it operates in an international environment where authority is contested, law is often difficult to enforce, and the decisions of one state affect the security and lives of others. Understanding international security is therefore essential for understanding government itself — for grasping how states exercise power, manage threats, make commitments, and are held accountable. The principles of constitutional government, civic responsibility, rule of law, and democratic accountability that run through this textbook do not stop at national borders. They raise questions about how the international community can develop the institutions, norms, and practices that make a world of sovereign states somewhat more orderly, somewhat more just, and somewhat more peaceful than it would otherwise be. That is the ongoing work of international relations — and the study of it is an essential part of civic education.

Chapter 47 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

In January 2001, a crowd of several hundred thousand people gathered in Washington, D.C., to witness the inauguration of the forty-third President of the United States. Two months earlier, the presidential election had ended in a disputed count of votes in the state of Florida. A legal contest moved through multiple courts over thirty-six days before the United States Supreme Court issued a ruling that effectively halted the recount and confirmed the Republican candidate's narrow electoral victory. The Democratic candidate, who had won more votes nationally, conceded the election and urged his supporters to accept the outcome. The losing side was deeply unhappy. Many believed the outcome was unjust. Protests occurred. Criticism of the Supreme Court's decision was sharp and sustained. But the transfer of power happened on schedule. The institutions held. The democratic system, despite severe stress, reproduced itself.

That outcome is not automatic. It is not guaranteed by constitutions, laws, or good intentions alone. It depends on something that political scientists call democratic consolidation — a condition in which democracy has become the only game in town, accepted by all significant political actors as the only legitimate way to organize political competition and transfer power. A consolidated democracy is one in which losing an election does not mean the end of your political future, in which the rules of the game are accepted even by those who lose under them, and in which no actor — no matter how powerful, popular, or aggrieved — can successfully claim that extra-constitutional means of seizing power are justified.

The study of democracy has expanded dramatically since the late twentieth century, and for good reason. Between the 1970s and the early 2000s, the world witnessed a significant expansion in the number of countries that held regular competitive elections, protected civil liberties, and maintained independent courts and free media. Scholars called this period the "third wave of democratization." Countries in southern Europe, Latin America, Eastern Europe, sub-Saharan Africa, and parts of Asia transitioned from authoritarian rule to competitive electoral systems with varying degrees of civil liberties protection. The expansion of democracy was celebrated as evidence that democratic governance was both desirable and achievable across diverse cultural, historical, and economic contexts.

Yet democracy is not a permanent condition. It can erode. It can be dismantled gradually, through legal means, by leaders who win elections and then use the power of office to weaken the institutions that would constrain them. It can collapse through military coups or constitutional crises. It can hollow out — maintaining the forms of democratic competition while its substance drains away. Political scientists have given this process a name: democratic backsliding, sometimes called democratic erosion or democratic regression. Since roughly 2005, by most systematic measures, the overall level of democracy in the world has declined. Countries that seemed consolidated have experienced significant institutional deterioration. Long-standing democracies have shown symptoms of stress that experts found alarming. The question of how democracies die — and what can be done to prevent it — has moved from the margins of political science to the center of urgent public debate.

This chapter examines democracy seriously and carefully. It begins by asking what democracy actually requires — not as a slogan or a vague aspiration, but as a set of specific institutional conditions that can be measured, compared, and evaluated. It distinguishes between electoral democracy, which holds elections but may not protect civil liberties, and liberal democracy, which combines competitive elections with robust rights protections, independent courts, and limits on executive power. It examines the institutions and norms that make democracies function — not just the visible machinery of elections and legislatures, but the less visible habits, expectations, and informal rules without which the visible machinery cannot operate. It explores how democratic consolidation happens and what makes it durable, and it examines in detail the phenomenon of democratic backsliding — how democratic governments can erode from within, through the actions of elected leaders who use legal means to undermine the institutions that hold them accountable. The chapter concludes by examining what can be done to defend democratic systems when they come under stress.

Students who complete this chapter will understand democracy as a political system with specific requirements, not merely as a value or a hope. They will be prepared to analyze claims about democratic health or erosion with concrete criteria rather than partisan assumptions. They will understand why democracy is difficult to build, why it is possible to lose, and why its defense requires active, informed, and institutionally sophisticated citizenship.

Opening Question

A government wins a free and fair election with 55 percent of legislative seats. Over the following two years, it restructures the constitutional court, revokes broadcast licenses of critical television channels, passes a law requiring civil society organizations receiving foreign funding to register as "foreign agents," and redraws electoral district boundaries in ways that increase its projected seat share. Each measure is defended as legal reform. Opposition parties and international observers describe the cumulative effect as systematic democratic erosion. Is this democracy or authoritarianism? By the end of this chapter, you will have the analytical tools to answer that question — and to explain why that answer matters.

Section 47.1

What Democracy Requires

The Challenge of Definition

Democracy is one of the most widely invoked words in political life, and one of the most contested. Nearly every government in the world today calls itself democratic in some sense. Governments that hold no competitive elections describe themselves as "people's democracies." Governments that hold elections but imprison political opponents describe themselves as "guided democracies" or "sovereign democracies." Governments that are generally recognized as free and fair by international observers describe themselves simply as democracies. The word is applied so broadly that it risks losing analytical precision. Before we can study the health or decline of democracy, we need to know clearly what we mean by it.

At its most basic, the word democracy derives from the Greek words for "people" (demos) and "rule" or "power" (kratos). Democracy means, in its etymological root, rule by the people. But this phrase immediately raises further questions: Which people? Ruled how? Through what institutions? With what protections for those who disagree with the majority?

Different political thinkers and systems have answered these questions differently. Ancient Athens held assemblies in which (male, non-enslaved) citizens could speak and vote directly on public decisions. This direct democracy is impractical at the scale of modern states. Modern democracies are primarily representative democracies, in which citizens elect representatives who make decisions on their behalf. But the mechanics of representation — who can vote, how elections are conducted, what representatives can do, what limits constrain majority decision — vary enormously.

Minimalist and Robust Conceptions

Political scientists typically distinguish between what they call minimalist and more robust conceptions of democracy. A minimalist definition focuses on one essential feature: competitive elections in which citizens choose among real alternatives, and in which the losing side accepts the outcome and gives up power. The political theorist Joseph Schumpeter famously described democracy as a method for arriving at political decisions, in which individuals acquire the power to decide by means of a competitive struggle for the people's vote. On this view, what distinguishes democracy from other systems is not the wisdom of its decisions or the equality of its citizens but the mechanism of competitive elections for choosing leadership.

This minimalist definition has the virtue of clarity. It is relatively easy to measure: Does the country hold elections? Are there real alternatives on the ballot? Does the winner actually take power, and does the loser actually give it up? These questions can be answered with reasonable objectivity. But the minimalist definition has significant limitations. It can classify as democratic many systems that provide elections while gutting the conditions that make elections meaningful — preventing opposition candidates from running, controlling the media so voters receive only favorable information about the incumbent, using state resources to advantage the ruling party, or manipulating the counting of votes. The minimalist definition can mistake the form of democracy for its substance.

More robust definitions of democracy add requirements beyond competitive elections. They insist that meaningful democracy also requires protection of civil liberties that enable citizens to form opinions, organize politically, and communicate freely without fear of imprisonment or violence. They insist on the rule of law — that government officials are themselves bound by legal rules that are fairly applied and independently enforced. They insist on effective institutions — not just elections but functioning legislatures that make law, courts that adjudicate disputes impartially, and a civil society that provides forums for political organization and expression. And they insist on some form of protection for political minorities — so that being outvoted on a given issue does not mean the loss of fundamental rights or the permanent exclusion from political life.

The Procedural Core

Despite the debates between minimalist and more expansive definitions, there is substantial agreement among political scientists that a political system must meet several procedural requirements to be classified as democratic at all. These requirements form what we might call the procedural core of democracy.

First, the system must hold regular, competitive elections in which citizens choose among genuine alternatives. The elections must be held on a schedule that is not subject to arbitrary cancellation or indefinite postponement. There must be real alternatives — not just one approved candidate or one approved party. Citizens must be able to vote without coercion, and their votes must be honestly counted.

Second, universal or near-universal suffrage must extend the right to vote to virtually all adult citizens. Historically, many systems held competitive elections while restricting the vote to property owners, men, members of a dominant ethnic group, or other subsets of the population. Modern democracy requires that the franchise extend broadly, without restrictions based on race, gender, religion, or class.

Third, there must be effective protection of civil liberties that allow democratic competition to occur meaningfully. These include freedom of expression (so citizens can criticize the government without fear), freedom of the press (so independent journalism can inform the public), freedom of assembly (so citizens can organize politically), and freedom of association (so political parties, interest groups, and civil society organizations can form and operate). Without these freedoms, elections may occur but cannot be meaningfully competitive.

Fourth, the system must include civilian control of government and the exclusion of military intervention in politics. A system in which elected officials govern at the pleasure of military commanders — who can remove them if dissatisfied — is not genuinely democratic regardless of how elections are conducted.

These four elements constitute a threshold below which a system cannot be considered democratic. Systems that meet these requirements can be described as holding the procedural core of democracy — what political scientists call electoral democracy. But meeting this threshold is not the same as having a fully functioning, rights-protecting, accountable democratic system. For that, more is required.

Civic Vocabulary
Democracy

Democracy is a system of government in which political authority derives from the people, expressed through free and competitive elections, and in which those who hold power are accountable to the citizenry. The word comes from the Greek demos (people) and kratos (rule or power). In modern representative democracies, citizens do not govern directly but elect representatives who exercise governmental authority on their behalf. Democracy implies not only elections but also the civil liberties, legal protections, and institutional checks that make elections meaningful and ensure that the rights of all citizens — including those in the political minority — are protected. A system that holds elections without these additional protections may be an electoral democracy but is not a fully liberal democracy.

Section 47.2

Liberal and Electoral Democracy

The Distinction That Matters

One of the most important advances in the comparative study of democracy has been the careful distinction between two types of democratic system: electoral democracy and liberal democracy. This distinction, developed by scholars including Fareed Zakaria and systematized in the measurement projects of organizations like Freedom House and the V-Dem Institute, captures a crucial difference in the quality and character of democratic governance that simple electoral criteria miss.

An electoral democracy is one that holds regular, competitive elections with real opposition candidates, universal suffrage, and reasonably honest vote counts. These systems meet the procedural core described in Section 47.1. But they may not protect civil liberties robustly, may not maintain genuinely independent courts, may not subject the executive to effective legal accountability, and may not respect the rights of political minorities. Elections happen, and the results matter, but the space between elections is characterized by substantial executive power that operates without strong legal or institutional constraint.

A liberal democracy meets the requirements of electoral democracy and adds several additional institutional characteristics. It protects civil liberties — freedom of speech, press, assembly, religion, and privacy — systematically and effectively, not merely on paper. It maintains an independent judiciary that can rule against the government without political retaliation. It subjects the executive to meaningful legal constraints and to accountability through courts, legislatures, and independent oversight institutions. It respects the rights of political minorities — religious minorities, ethnic minorities, opposition voters, dissidents — not only the preferences of electoral majorities. And it maintains a free and independent media capable of informing citizens and scrutinizing governmental behavior.

Why the Distinction Matters in Practice

The distinction between electoral and liberal democracy has enormous practical importance. A country can hold elections that are technically competitive while simultaneously using the power of government to harass opposition parties, control or suppress critical media, stack courts with loyalists who will rule in favor of the government, use state resources to advantage the ruling party, and intimidate civil society organizations. Such a system may score reasonably well on narrow electoral criteria while scoring poorly on broader measures of democratic quality.

Fareed Zakaria argued in the late 1990s that the world was witnessing the rise of what he called illiberal democracies — systems that hold regular elections but that do not protect the civil liberties and constitutional limits that give democracy its substance. The existence of illiberal democracies demonstrated that elections alone are not sufficient to produce the rights-protective, accountable governance that the concept of democracy implies to most citizens in established democracies. Elections are necessary but not sufficient.

The Components of Liberal Democracy

The institutional components that distinguish liberal democracy from mere electoral democracy can be specified precisely. These components have been identified and measured by political scientists using large comparative datasets and form the basis for the indices that track democratic health globally.

Judicial independence means that courts — and especially the highest constitutional court — can rule against the government without facing political retaliation, personnel manipulation, or jurisdictional stripping. An independent judiciary serves as a fundamental constraint on executive power: it provides a forum in which any person or institution can challenge government action as illegal or unconstitutional and receive a fair hearing from judges who are not beholden to the government they are judging.

Protection of civil liberties means that rights to expression, press, assembly, religion, and privacy are protected effectively — not merely guaranteed on paper — for all citizens including those who criticize the government. The protection must be genuine, meaning that those who exercise these rights are not subject to legal harassment, criminal prosecution on pretextual charges, physical intimidation, or other forms of retaliation that would deter the exercise of rights even without formal prohibition.

Horizontal accountability means that institutions within the government — courts, legislatures, audit bodies, prosecutors, electoral commissions — can hold the executive accountable through legal and political means. This intra-governmental checking of executive power operates laterally across institutions rather than vertically from citizens to rulers. Strong horizontal accountability means that no branch or official operates above the law.

Vertical accountability means that citizens and civil society can hold the government accountable through elections, public pressure, journalism, and organized advocacy. Elections are the primary mechanism of vertical accountability, but a vigorous free press, active civil society organizations, and robust public debate reinforce electoral accountability between election cycles.

Civilian supremacy and subordination of security forces means that police, military, and intelligence agencies operate under civilian command and within legal boundaries. Security forces that operate independently of civilian oversight, that intimidate political actors, or that can be used against political opponents represent a fundamental threat to democratic governance.

Minority rights and political pluralism mean that the rights and political participation of minority groups — ethnic, religious, regional, political — are protected, so that majorities cannot use democratic procedures to systematically exclude or harm minorities. Liberal democracy is majoritarian in decision-making but not in its treatment of fundamental rights; the rights of the minority are not subject to majority vote.

Together, these components constitute the institutional architecture of liberal democracy. When they are all present and functioning, a political system can be described as a liberal democracy in good standing. When they are present in weakened or compromised form, the system is democratic but with significant deficiencies. When elections continue but these components are largely absent, the system may have drifted from electoral democracy into competitive authoritarianism — a concept explored further in Chapter 48.

Comparative Lens
Measuring Democracy — How Political Scientists Compare Democratic Quality

Comparing democratic systems requires reliable measurement, and political scientists have developed several sophisticated indices for this purpose. Each reflects somewhat different theoretical choices about what democracy requires.

Freedom House produces annual ratings of countries on political rights and civil liberties, assigning each country a score and a categorical rating of Free, Partly Free, or Not Free. Freedom House uses a combination of electoral criteria and civil liberties criteria, making it sensitive to the distinction between electoral and liberal democracy. Its ratings are widely used by policymakers and journalists as well as scholars.

The V-Dem (Varieties of Democracy) Institute produces the most comprehensive measurement of democracy currently available. V-Dem disaggregates democracy into multiple components — electoral, liberal, participatory, deliberative, and egalitarian — and measures each separately, producing hundreds of indicators based on expert codings of conditions in virtually every country from 1789 to the present. V-Dem data allow fine-grained analysis of trends within and across dimensions of democracy and are extensively used in academic research.

The Economist Intelligence Unit's Democracy Index classifies countries into full democracies, flawed democracies, hybrid regimes, and authoritarian regimes based on electoral process, civil liberties, government functioning, political participation, and political culture.

These different indices do not always agree on specific country classifications, reflecting both differences in measurement methodology and genuine uncertainty about borderline cases. But they converge on the broad patterns: there are a relatively small number of robustly liberal democracies; a larger number of electoral or flawed democracies; a significant number of hybrid regimes; and a substantial number of clearly authoritarian systems. Comparing trends across indices reveals that the overall level of democratic quality globally has declined since approximately 2005 — a finding that appears consistently across multiple measurement approaches.

Section 47.3

Democratic Institutions and Norms

Institutions Are Not Enough

A political system's democratic character is not determined by its formal institutions alone. Constitutions, laws, courts, legislatures, election commissions, and independent agencies can all be specified in founding documents — and yet fail to function as democratic institutions in practice if the political culture, informal understandings, and behavioral expectations of politicians, officials, and citizens do not support them.

This insight is one of the most important contributions of recent political science research on democratic health. Political scientists Steven Levitsky and Daniel Ziblatt emphasized that alongside formal institutions, democracies depend critically on what they call informal norms — unwritten but widely understood and broadly respected rules of political behavior that constrain what politicians actually do even when formal rules would permit them to do more.

Norms are not laws. They are not enforced through courts or police. They operate through social expectations, reputational sanctions, and the political culture that defines what is considered acceptable behavior. A politician who violates a norm does not go to prison — but may face significant political costs from peers, opponents, voters, and the press who consider the violation disqualifying or disrespectful of the political community's shared understanding of fair play.

Two Foundational Norms

Levitsky and Ziblatt identified two norms as particularly fundamental to democratic stability in established systems: mutual toleration and institutional forbearance.

Mutual toleration is the norm that political competitors accept each other as legitimate rivals. It means that parties and politicians on different sides of political competition regard their opponents as fellow citizens who are entitled to compete for power, even when they disagree profoundly about policy. Under this norm, a political party that loses an election does not conclude that the victors are enemies who must be destroyed, imprisoned, or prevented from governing. It treats political differences as ordinary disagreements among citizens, not existential conflicts between legitimate and illegitimate actors.

The breakdown of mutual toleration is often visible in the language political leaders use about opponents: when politicians describe opponents as criminals, traitors, existential threats, or alien forces that must be defeated rather than merely lost to in an election, they are signaling that they do not regard their opponents as legitimate participants in political life. This has serious consequences: if your opponent is not a legitimate rival but a threat to the nation, then normal democratic constraints — accepting election results, respecting legal limits on your power, allowing the opposition to organize and campaign freely — may seem like obstacles to necessary action rather than obligations to be honored.

Institutional forbearance is the norm that political actors refrain from using legal powers to their fullest technical extent when doing so would damage the health of democratic institutions. Many constitutional systems give political actors powers that could, if used aggressively, undermine the checks and balances the system depends on. A president might technically have the authority to fire independent prosecutors, appoint loyalists to oversight positions, or use emergency powers broadly. These actions might be legal — or at least not clearly illegal — and yet their aggressive use would hollow out the institutions the formal rules are supposed to sustain.

When the norm of institutional forbearance erodes — when the reasoning becomes "we can do this, and our opponents would do it if they could, so we will" — the political system enters a dynamic of escalating institutional aggression that can rapidly degrade democratic quality.

The Role of Legislatures, Courts, and Civil Society

Beyond the behavioral norms of politicians, democratic institutions include specific organizational structures that perform essential functions.

Legislatures are the primary lawmaking institutions of democratic systems. They represent citizens, deliberate on public policy, approve budgets, pass laws, and provide oversight of executive action. A functioning legislature serves as a check on executive power — not merely through the legal power to override vetoes or impeach officials, but through the ongoing work of scrutiny, questioning, investigation, and public debate. Legislatures that have been marginalized, packed with loyalists of the executive, or stripped of oversight capacity cannot perform the checking function that democratic accountability requires.

Courts serve multiple democratic functions. They adjudicate individual legal disputes impartially, under law rather than political instruction. They subject government action to legal review, ensuring that officials operate within the legal authority they have been granted. Constitutional courts or supreme courts with the power of judicial review can strike down laws or executive actions that violate constitutional protections. In performing these functions, courts serve as a fundamental institutional check on majority will — they can protect the rights of individuals and minorities even when electoral majorities support laws that harm them.

Civil society — the network of voluntary associations, interest groups, advocacy organizations, labor unions, professional associations, religious institutions, and other non-governmental organizations — is an essential component of democratic life. Civil society organizations provide forums for political organization and expression, advocate for citizens' interests in the policy process, monitor government behavior, and provide alternative channels of political participation beyond elections. A robust civil society is not merely a nice amenity of democratic systems; it is a structural component that sustains the information, organization, and political energy that democracy requires.

Free and independent media perform essential democratic functions that no government institution can fully substitute for. Independent journalism investigates and reports on government action, exposes corruption and abuse, provides citizens with the information they need to make electoral choices, and creates the public record through which government is held to account. When media independence is compromised — through government ownership, advertiser pressure, legal harassment of journalists, or the collapse of business models that make independent journalism financially viable — democratic accountability suffers regardless of what formal institutions remain in place.

Election administration bodies — the commissions, ministries, and agencies that organize, administer, and adjudicate elections — must be independent of the ruling party or incumbent government, adequately resourced, professionally staffed, and legally empowered to enforce election rules impartially. An election commission controlled by or subordinate to the incumbent government cannot provide the impartial administration on which fair competition depends.

How the System Works
How Democratic Accountability Operates

Understanding how democratic accountability actually functions requires seeing both the formal mechanisms and the informal ones that support them.

  • Step 1Vertical accountability through elections. At regular intervals, citizens vote on whether to return incumbents to power or replace them with challengers. Elections work as accountability mechanisms when they are genuinely competitive, when voters have access to accurate information about incumbent performance, when the rules prevent incumbents from suppressing opposition, and when the results are honestly counted and implemented.
  • Step 2Vertical accountability between elections. Between elections, vertical accountability operates through ongoing political pressures: public opinion, organized advocacy by interest groups and civil society organizations, investigative journalism, and the knowledge that current performance will affect the next election.
  • Step 3Horizontal accountability through independent institutions. Courts, audit institutions, inspectors general, ombudspersons, legislative oversight committees, and independent prosecutors provide ongoing checking of executive action — they can investigate alleged wrongdoing, issue legal rulings against government actors, and impose legal consequences for violations without waiting for the next election cycle.
  • Step 4Accountability through free media and civil society. Independent journalism investigates government behavior and brings findings to public attention. Civil society organizations monitor government programs, advocate for affected populations, and maintain public pressure on officials to honor their legal obligations and political commitments.
  • Step 5International accountability mechanisms. In some contexts, international observation of elections, treaty bodies that monitor human rights compliance, regional human rights courts, and the diplomatic pressure of allied democratic governments provide additional accountability mechanisms that supplement domestic institutions.
  • LimitsAccountability mechanisms can fail. Courts can be captured or intimidated. Legislatures can be dominated by a ruling party that protects rather than scrutinizes the executive. Media can be economically or politically pressured. Civil society organizations can be legally harassed or defunded. International mechanisms lack enforcement power. Understanding democratic health requires understanding not just which formal accountability mechanisms exist on paper but whether they are actually functioning effectively.
Section 47.4

Free Elections and Fair Competition

The Heart of Democratic Competition

Elections are the most visible expression of democratic self-governance. They are the mechanism through which citizens exercise collective choice about who will hold public authority. For elections to perform this function, they must be both free and fair — two distinct but related requirements that together define the conditions under which electoral competition can legitimately determine political outcomes.

Free elections are those in which citizens can vote without coercion, candidates can run without being subject to arbitrary legal barriers or physical intimidation, voters can form and express political opinions without fear of retaliation, and political parties can organize, campaign, and compete for votes. Freedom in elections means that no actor — including the incumbent government — can use intimidation, threats, violence, legal harassment, or other forms of pressure to prevent citizens from voting their conscience or candidates from running.

Fair elections are those in which the rules of competition are applied evenhandedly to all parties and candidates. Fairness requires impartial administration of election rules, equal access to the ballot, reasonable and non-discriminatory voter registration requirements, honest counting of votes, prompt and impartial resolution of electoral disputes, and equitable access to campaign opportunities including media exposure. Fair competition does not mean equal resources — some parties and candidates will always have more money, organizational capacity, and name recognition than others — but it does mean that the rules themselves do not advantage one party at the expense of others.

Electoral Integrity and Incumbent Advantage

One of the most important and difficult issues in democratic elections is the management of incumbent advantage. By definition, the ruling party or incumbent government controls the apparatus of the state during election periods. Even in well-functioning democracies, incumbent parties enjoy some advantages simply from being in power: visibility, the ability to announce popular policies, and the association with governmental achievements. The question is not whether any incumbent advantage exists — some always does — but whether that advantage is so extreme that it effectively eliminates the possibility of competitive opposition.

Electoral manipulation takes many forms. Some are crude and immediately obvious: outright ballot-box stuffing, buying votes with cash or material goods, physically preventing opposition voters from reaching polling stations, or simply announcing results that do not reflect actual counts. These forms of manipulation are relatively easy to detect through election observation, parallel vote tabulation, and statistical analysis of results.

Other forms of manipulation are subtler and harder to prove: drawing district boundaries to pack opposition voters into a small number of districts (gerrymandering), setting voter registration requirements that disproportionately affect opposition constituencies, requiring voter identification that opposition voters are less likely to possess, timing the election to disadvantage the opposition, using campaign finance rules or media regulations selectively, or prosecuting opposition candidates on criminal charges timed to remove them from the ballot. The subtler forms of manipulation are particularly consequential because they can occur within the forms of legal democratic competition while systematically undermining its fairness.

The Role of Election Administration

Independent election administration is essential to free and fair elections. When elections are administered by bodies controlled by or subordinate to the incumbent government, the conflict of interest is obvious and severe: the same authorities who benefit from the electoral outcome are in charge of the rules and procedures that determine it. Even where outright fraud does not occur, the lack of independence compromises public confidence in results and removes the institutional safeguard against partisan manipulation of election rules.

In established democracies, election administration is typically handled by independent commissions, nonpartisan civil servants, or bipartisan bodies with representation from all major parties. The independence of these bodies is not only instrumentally important — preventing actual manipulation — but symbolically important: it signals that the rules of electoral competition belong to the polity as a whole, not to any particular party or incumbent.

International election observation — the practice of sending teams of independent observers from other countries or international organizations to monitor elections — has become a standard feature of the global democracy landscape. International observers assess whether elections meet international standards for free and fair competition and issue public judgments about the overall quality of the electoral process. Observation can play a valuable role in deterring the most obvious forms of electoral fraud, providing impartial assessment of disputed results, and lending legitimacy to elections that observers find credible. But observation has significant limitations: observers cannot monitor every polling station simultaneously, and sophisticated manipulation may occur through legal means that are difficult to document or contest.

Section 47.5

Accountability and Transparency

Why Accountability Is Central to Democracy

Democratic accountability — the obligation of those who hold public power to answer for how they have exercised it — is not merely a desirable add-on to electoral competition. It is structural to what democracy means. Elections without accountability are insufficient because citizens cannot make informed electoral choices about incumbents whose behavior has been concealed from them. Laws without accountability are insufficient because officials who know they will not face consequences for violating legal obligations have weak incentives to comply. Rights without accountability are insufficient because rights that cannot be enforced when violated are effectively meaningless in practice.

Political scientists distinguish several dimensions of accountability that democratic systems must maintain. Answerability is the obligation of officials to explain and justify their decisions to oversight bodies, the public, and the media. Officials who cannot be required to explain their decisions — who operate in secrecy without any obligation of disclosure or justification — are not accountable even if they are technically subject to legal constraints. Answerability requires transparency: the availability of information about government decisions and their basis, made accessible to oversight institutions and the public.

Enforceability is the capacity of accountability institutions to impose real consequences on officials who violate legal obligations, abuse their authority, or engage in corruption. Without enforceability, accountability is merely formal — officials must answer questions but face no meaningful consequences for the answers they give. Enforceability requires that accountability institutions have the independence, resources, and legal authority to act against officials who violate their obligations, regardless of their political connection to the current government.

Scope is the range of officials and decisions subject to accountability. Accountability that extends only to minor officials while exempting the executive and the executive's inner circle is a weak and distorted form of accountability. Genuine democratic accountability applies to all officials, including the highest — the president or prime minister, senior ministers, the heads of security agencies — who may be the most important and the hardest to hold accountable.

Transparency as a Democratic Requirement

Transparency — the availability of government information to citizens, oversight institutions, and the media — is a prerequisite for meaningful accountability. Citizens cannot hold officials accountable for decisions they do not know about. Oversight institutions cannot investigate conduct that is systematically hidden from them. Journalists cannot report on government behavior they cannot access.

Transparency requirements in democratic systems typically include: freedom of information laws that give citizens and media the legal right to request and receive government records; requirements that government decisions be documented and that the documentation be publicly available; open meeting requirements for legislative and administrative bodies; disclosure requirements for campaign finance, lobbying, and government contracting; and financial disclosure requirements for officials that allow detection of corruption.

Corruption and Democratic Integrity

Corruption — the abuse of public authority for private gain — is one of the most pervasive threats to democratic accountability. Corruption takes many forms: bribery of officials to make favorable decisions, theft of public funds, nepotism in public appointments, manipulation of contracts to benefit connected parties, and the use of regulatory authority to extract payments from regulated businesses. When corruption is widespread and unpunished, it undermines democratic accountability in multiple ways: it diverts resources from public purposes, it distorts competition among citizens and businesses, it rewards loyalty to political patrons over competence, and it corrodes public trust in institutions.

Corruption is particularly damaging to democracy because it tends to be self-reinforcing. Officials who engage in corruption have strong incentives to protect themselves by controlling the institutions that could investigate them — appointing loyalists to anti-corruption agencies, influencing prosecutors, and capturing courts. As corruption entrenches itself in government, the institutional capacity to fight it erodes, creating a cycle that is very difficult to break without significant external pressure or internal political crisis.

Many democratic systems have created specialized anti-corruption institutions — independent prosecutors, anti-corruption commissions, financial intelligence units, audit bodies, and inspectors general. These institutions can be effective when they are genuinely independent, adequately resourced, legally empowered, and supported by a political culture that does not regard corruption as simply the normal cost of doing business in politics. But they are also targets: governments engaged in corruption have strong incentives to defund, reorganize, capture, or discredit the institutions investigating them.

Institution in Practice
The Role of the Constitutional Court

Constitutional courts perform one of the most important functions in democratic governance: they serve as institutional guardians of the constitutional limits on government power, deciding whether laws and executive actions conform to the constitution and, when they do not, striking them down.

In the German system, the Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) was designed after World War II with the explicit purpose of preventing the kind of constitutional collapse that had enabled the rise of National Socialism. The Court has jurisdiction to review the constitutionality of federal and state laws, adjudicate disputes between government organs, and protect fundamental rights. Its judges serve twelve-year non-renewable terms and are chosen through a process requiring supermajority approval in both houses of the legislature — designed to make the Court's composition impossible for any single party to control without substantial consensus. The Court has issued rulings that invalidated laws passed by large legislative majorities and has developed a substantial body of constitutional doctrine that shapes German politics.

In Hungary, by contrast, the Constitutional Court was systematically subordinated to the ruling Fidesz party after 2010. The government expanded the court from eleven to fifteen members, filling the new seats with party loyalists. It then used a two-thirds legislative majority to constitutionally override a major court ruling it disliked, and subsequently amended the constitution to strip the court of jurisdiction over budget-related matters — precisely the area where it had been most active in challenging government policy. Within a few years, the court that had once served as an effective constitutional check on government power had become an institution that reliably approved rather than constrained government action.

These two contrasting examples illustrate how the same institutional form — a constitutional court — can function very differently depending on whether it is genuinely independent or whether its independence has been compromised. Institutional form alone does not determine democratic outcomes; institutional independence and operational integrity are what matter.

Section 47.6

Democratic Consolidation

What Consolidation Means

Democratic consolidation is the process by which a democratic system becomes stable and self-sustaining — the condition in which democracy has become, in the memorable phrase of political scientists Juan Linz and Alfred Stepan, "the only game in town." A consolidated democracy is one in which no significant political actor seriously considers non-democratic alternatives to resolving political conflicts, in which all major actors accept democratic institutions as the legitimate framework for pursuing their interests, and in which the democratic system has demonstrated the resilience to survive electoral defeats, economic crises, political scandals, and other serious challenges without collapsing.

Consolidation is not the same as quality. A democracy can be consolidated — stable, resilient, accepted by all major actors — while still having significant deficiencies in its quality: electoral systems that produce unrepresentative outcomes, civil liberties protections that are incomplete, accountability mechanisms that are weak. Conversely, a democracy can be high in quality while remaining unconsolidated — facing genuine threats from actors who do not fully accept democratic rules. Consolidation describes the stability of the democratic system as such, not the virtue of its specific outcomes.

Indicators of Consolidation

Political scientists have identified several indicators that suggest a democracy is consolidated or moving toward consolidation.

Multiple peaceful transfers of power are perhaps the most important indicator. When a democracy has successfully transferred power from one party or coalition to another — particularly when the original governing party lost an election and stepped down willingly — and has done so multiple times, it demonstrates that the electoral mechanism is functioning and that all major actors accept its outcomes. The first peaceful transfer of power in a new democracy is significant. The second is more so, because it demonstrates that the initial transfer was not merely tolerated but that the pattern is established.

Acceptance of election results by losers is closely related. In a consolidated democracy, losing parties concede defeat, congratulate the winners, and begin preparing for the next electoral contest rather than disputing results, organizing extra-constitutional resistance, or seeking military intervention to reverse the outcome. The norm of concession is powerful precisely because it is voluntary — it represents the losing party's acknowledgment that the democratic process, even when it produces unfavorable results, is legitimate and must be respected.

Absence of serious anti-democratic actors in major positions of political influence. In consolidated democracies, parties or movements that openly reject democratic rules have been effectively marginalized. Civil society and media independence that operates robustly provides ongoing checks on government power and sources of political organization independent of the state. Elite consensus on democratic rules — agreement among political leaders, business elites, military officers, and other major power-holders that democratic institutions provide the legitimate framework for resolving political conflicts — deprives would-be authoritarians of the elite allies they typically need to consolidate power.

Conditions Associated With Consolidation

Economic development and wealth are positively associated with democratic consolidation. Wealthier societies tend to have larger middle classes, more literate populations, stronger civil society organizations, and more resources for building effective public institutions. History of democracy matters: countries with longer histories of democratic practice tend to consolidate democracy more successfully than countries where democratic institutions are entirely new. Strong rule of law traditions preceding democracy are associated with better democratic outcomes. Absence of severe ethnic or social polarization makes consolidation somewhat easier, though democracies have succeeded in deeply divided societies when institutional design accommodates diversity effectively. International context matters particularly: countries embedded in international environments that provide institutional support, economic incentives for democratic behavior, and political pressure against backsliding tend to consolidate democracy more successfully. The European Union's enlargement process — which conditioned membership on democratic governance — is often cited as a significant factor in democratic consolidation in Central and Eastern Europe after 1990.

Civic Vocabulary
Democratic Consolidation

Democratic consolidation refers to the process by which a democratic political system becomes stable, self-sustaining, and broadly accepted as the only legitimate framework for political competition and the resolution of conflicts over power. A consolidated democracy is one in which all significant political actors — parties, elites, military officers, civil society leaders — accept democratic institutions and rules as the only legitimate way to compete for power and resolve political disputes. In a consolidated democracy, losing an election is a political setback, not an existential threat; no major actor considers military intervention, constitutional suspension, or other extra-constitutional means of seizing or maintaining power as acceptable or viable alternatives to democratic competition. Consolidation describes the stability of the democratic system, not the quality of its specific decisions.

Democratic Backsliding

What Democratic Backsliding Is

Democratic backsliding — also called democratic erosion or autocratization — refers to a process by which a democratic system deteriorates in quality and moves toward authoritarianism, typically through incremental steps taken by legally or electorally legitimate actors rather than through a sudden coup or revolutionary seizure of power. Democratic backsliding is, in many ways, a more insidious threat to democracy than military coups precisely because it works through the forms of democratic legitimacy while hollowing out the substance of democratic governance.

The classic mode of democratic collapse in the twentieth century was the military coup: a general and his officers seized power by force, suspended the constitution, dissolved elected institutions, and established military rule. Military coups were dramatic, unmistakable, and obviously anti-democratic. They could be condemned unambiguously by democratic actors at home and abroad because they openly violated democratic norms and legal authority.

Democratic backsliding looks different. It typically begins with a leader who wins a legitimate election, often by large margins, on a platform that may include genuine popular grievances. This leader then, once in power, uses a combination of legal means, popular mobilization, institutional appointments, and selective prosecution to weaken the checks and balances that democratic systems depend on. Each individual step may be defensible on some legal or political ground: restructuring the judiciary to improve efficiency, enacting media regulations to ensure fairness, reforming civil society regulations to prevent foreign interference, changing electoral rules to ensure integrity. The cumulative effect of these individually defensible steps is the systematic erosion of the institutional checks that held the government accountable. By the time the erosion is clearly visible, the institutions that would have pushed back may already be too compromised to do so effectively.

Political scientist Nancy Bermeo, who has extensively studied forms of democratic erosion, categorizes democratic backsliding along a spectrum from overt forms (obvious authoritarian coups) to covert ones (incremental erosion through legal means). The most common contemporary form, she argues, is executive aggrandizement — the gradual accumulation of power by elected executives at the expense of the other institutions designed to constrain them.

The Mechanics of Backsliding

Democratic backsliding follows recognizable patterns across different countries and contexts. The comparative study of backsliding has identified a set of institutional tactics that are deployed repeatedly in countries experiencing democratic erosion.

Attacking the judiciary and courts. In virtually every case of significant democratic backsliding, early targets include the courts — particularly constitutional courts, supreme courts, and independent prosecutors who could challenge the government's actions or hold officials accountable for wrongdoing. The tactics used to neutralize courts include expanding the number of judges on a court and filling the new positions with loyalists (court-packing), changing appointment rules to give the executive more direct control over judicial selection, forcing the retirement of sitting judges through mandatory retirement age changes, restructuring or consolidating courts to eliminate troublesome jurisdictions, and stripping courts of jurisdiction over sensitive matters. The goal is to transform the judiciary from an independent checking institution into an institution that reliably approves and protects government action.

Electoral manipulation and changing the rules of competition. Governments engaged in backsliding typically change electoral rules in ways that advantage the ruling party. This may include redrawing district boundaries (gerrymandering), changing campaign finance rules to favor well-resourced incumbents, imposing voter registration requirements that disproportionately affect opposition constituencies, timing elections to disadvantage the opposition, preventing opposition candidates from appearing on ballots through selective enforcement of candidacy rules, or controlling election administration bodies to provide incumbent-friendly administration of electoral procedures.

Capturing or suppressing media. Independent media is an essential source of information for citizens evaluating incumbent performance and an essential vehicle for political opposition. Governments engaged in backsliding typically seek to control the information environment by acquiring media through government-aligned business interests, using regulatory power over broadcasting licenses to pressure critical outlets, applying tax investigations or other legal pressure to critical media owners, and using defamation or national security laws to prosecute journalists who report critically on the government.

Restricting and co-opting civil society. Governments engaged in backsliding frequently target civil society through legislation that restricts foreign funding of civil society organizations, imposes burdensome registration and reporting requirements, criminalizes certain forms of advocacy or protest, or uses tax and regulatory authorities to harass and defund organizations that are politically inconvenient.

Exploiting emergency powers. Democratic constitutions typically allow for expanded executive authority during genuine emergencies — wars, natural disasters, public health crises. Governments engaged in backsliding sometimes declare or prolong emergencies — real or exaggerated — to justify expanded executive authority that persists well beyond what the actual emergency warrants.

Politicizing security forces and state institutions. Democratic backsliding often involves the politicization of institutions that are supposed to be politically neutral: the civil service, the police, military intelligence, anti-corruption agencies, tax authorities, and prosecutors. When these institutions come under effective political control of the ruling party, they can be used to reward political allies, punish political opponents, and protect the government from accountability.

Using constitutional mechanisms to entrench power. Some governments engaged in backsliding have used their electoral success to change the constitution in ways that entrench their position and make future change more difficult. This may involve lengthening term limits or removing them entirely, changing the rules for amending the constitution to make future revision harder, or changing electoral systems in ways that advantage the ruling party in perpetuity. When these constitutional changes are enacted through procedures that formally comply with existing amendment rules, they present a particularly difficult challenge: they are arguably legal but are used specifically to prevent future democratic competition from producing different outcomes.

Case Studies in Democratic Backsliding

Comparative political science has documented democratic backsliding in multiple countries across different regions and time periods. A brief survey of several cases illustrates the common patterns described above.

Hungary is one of the most extensively studied cases of democratic backsliding in an established democratic system. Hungary became a democracy after the collapse of communism in 1989 and joined the European Union in 2004. In 2010, the Fidesz party led by Viktor Orbán won an election with a two-thirds supermajority in the parliament — enough to amend the constitution without any opposition support. Over the following years, the government used this majority to systematically restructure the Hungarian constitutional order: writing a new constitution without meaningful opposition input, expanding the Constitutional Court and filling new seats with loyalists, restructuring the judiciary to give the government more control over judicial appointments, changing media regulation to advantage government-aligned outlets, restructuring electoral district boundaries to maximize Fidesz's seat share from a given vote share, requiring civil society organizations receiving foreign funding to register as "foreign agents," and restricting academic freedom. By the mid-2010s, independent assessments described Hungary as an "illiberal democracy" or "competitive authoritarian" system rather than a liberal democracy. The government achieved all of these changes through formally legal means — legislation passed by democratic majorities — while maintaining the forms of electoral competition and parliamentary procedure.

Venezuela experienced a different but related pattern under Hugo Chávez and his successor Nicolás Maduro. Chávez won a legitimate presidential election in 1998 and moved quickly to restructure the constitution, calling a constituent assembly that wrote a new document that concentrated power in the executive. Over the following years, the government progressively weakened the independence of courts and the electoral system, used oil revenues to build a patronage network that sustained electoral support, and restricted media and civil society. After Chávez's death in 2013, Maduro continued and accelerated the erosion of democratic institutions as economic crisis reduced the government's ability to sustain support through material rewards. By the late 2010s, Venezuela was generally classified as a competitive authoritarian system in which elections occurred but the playing field was so heavily tilted toward the incumbents that meaningful competition was impossible.

India, the world's most populous democracy, experienced significant democratic backsliding indicators beginning around 2014 under the government of Narendra Modi and the Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP). International democracy indices documented increases in attacks on press freedom, harassment of civil society organizations critical of the government, communal tensions and violence against religious minorities (particularly Muslims), weakening of university autonomy, and changes in legal procedures affecting minority rights. India remained an electoral democracy with competitive elections in which the BJP could lose — as it did in several state elections and experienced setbacks in the 2024 national election — but its standing on measures of liberal democratic quality declined substantially from earlier periods.

Poland experienced patterns similar to Hungary after the Law and Justice party won parliamentary elections in 2015, including rapid changes to the constitutional tribunal, restructuring of the judiciary, changes to public broadcasting to increase political control, and restrictions on judicial independence through legislative action. Poland's situation became a major controversy within the European Union, which initiated rule-of-law proceedings against Poland for violations of EU legal standards, demonstrating how regional organizations can serve as external constraints on democratic backsliding — with limited but real effect.

These cases, drawn from different regions, different economic contexts, and different political histories, all display common patterns: the use of electoral majorities and legal mechanisms to weaken accountability institutions, the targeting of courts, media, and civil society as the primary institutional targets, and the incremental accumulation of executive power at the expense of checks and balances. They also demonstrate that democratic backsliding is not unique to poor or newly democratic countries but can occur in wealthy, EU-member states with decades of democratic experience.

Why Backsliding Happens: Structural and Political Causes

What explains the wave of democratic backsliding that political scientists have documented since approximately 2005? Scholars have offered several explanations, which are not mutually exclusive.

Economic inequality and grievance create political conditions in which anti-establishment, anti-elite appeals by leaders who promise to restore power to "the people" against corrupt elites gain significant electoral traction. Where established democratic parties have failed to address economic insecurity, globalization-driven dislocation, or the sense that ordinary citizens have been left behind while politically connected elites benefit, voters may be receptive to leaders who promise to break the existing institutional order rather than work within it.

Polarization and the breakdown of mutual toleration make democratic norms harder to sustain. When political competition becomes existential — when each side views the other as not merely wrong but as a fundamental threat to the nation — the norm of mutual toleration erodes. Politicians who believe their opponents represent an existential threat have weaker reasons to honor the constraints of democratic competition.

Institutional weakness — legislatures that do not effectively check the executive, courts that have not established strong traditions of independence, civil society organizations that are small and poorly resourced, media that is economically precarious — makes backsliding easier. Strong institutions resist executive aggrandizement more effectively than weak ones.

Elite complicity is often essential to backsliding. Leaders who undermine democracy typically cannot do so without the cooperation of economic elites, judicial appointments willing to rule in the government's favor, media owners willing to suppress critical coverage, civil servants willing to serve political masters rather than legal obligations, and legislative majorities willing to pass laws that concentrate power in the executive.

International permissiveness — the absence of effective international pressure against backsliding — matters. When the international environment is permissive of democratic erosion, when major powers are themselves either experiencing backsliding or are too interested in stable relationships with backsliding governments to apply pressure, the external constraints on erosion are weak.

Primary Source Window
The Gettysburg Address and Democratic Self-Conception — Abraham Lincoln (1863)
Context

On November 19, 1863, President Abraham Lincoln delivered a brief address at the dedication of the Soldiers' National Cemetery at Gettysburg, Pennsylvania, four months after a major Civil War battle. The United States was in the midst of its most severe constitutional crisis — a war over slavery, secession, and the meaning of democracy itself. Lincoln used the occasion not merely to honor the dead but to articulate a vision of what American democracy was, what it was fighting for, and what the stakes of its failure would be.

Paraphrase of Key Passage
Lincoln described the American republic as a nation founded on the proposition that all people are created equal, and framed the Civil War as a test of whether a nation built on that proposition — and on the principle of democratic self-government — could endure. He dedicated the cemetery as a renewed commitment to the cause the soldiers had died for: that democratic government "of the people, by the people, for the people" should not perish from the earth.
Analysis

Lincoln's address illustrates several important dimensions of democratic political thought. It connects democracy to a foundational principle — equality — not merely to a procedural mechanism, suggesting that elections alone are insufficient if they do not rest on deeper commitments to equal human dignity. It frames democracy as fragile and historically contingent — something that must be actively sustained rather than taken for granted. And it insists that the failure of American democracy would have implications beyond America — that representative self-government is an experiment watched by the world, whose success or failure carries meaning for the larger human project of self-governance.

The address also implicitly raises the tension between majority rule and minority rights that has been central to American constitutional debate since the founding: the Civil War was, in part, about whether a majority of white voters could maintain an institution — slavery — that denied basic rights to a minority of the population. Lincoln's framing of democracy as resting on a commitment to equality was a response to this tension, suggesting that democracy requires not only majority rule but a foundation of equal human dignity that majority will cannot override.

Defending Democratic Systems

Why Defense Is Necessary

Democratic systems do not defend themselves automatically. Their survival depends on active choices by politicians, citizens, civil society organizations, courts, media, and international actors to honor and enforce the norms and rules that democratic governance requires. A constitution cannot enforce itself. A norm of mutual toleration cannot persist if one side abandons it. Courts cannot remain independent if their members are intimidated or their decisions systematically ignored. The institutions of democracy are, in the end, sustained by the political choices of the people who operate and support them.

This insight carries a difficult implication: if democracy depends on choices that people could make differently, then its decline is always possible. No level of wealth, no length of democratic history, no strength of constitutional design fully guarantees democratic survival against sustained internal pressure from leaders and movements determined to undermine it. But the recognition that democratic defense requires active choices is also empowering: it means that citizens, institutions, and political actors are not passive observers of democratic fate but participants whose choices matter.

The Role of Political Parties and Leaders

Political parties are the primary vehicles of democratic competition, and the choices their leaders make are among the most consequential for democratic health. Levitsky and Ziblatt have argued that democratic breakdowns typically require the complicity of mainstream political parties — either their active support for authoritarian-leaning leaders or their failure to oppose such leaders effectively. Conversely, one of the most important defenses against democratic backsliding is the refusal of mainstream parties to mainstream, legitimate, or form alliances with leaders who show authoritarian tendencies.

The challenge for party leaders is real. A leader who appeals to deep popular grievances, who wins large electoral followings, and who may be ideologically close to the mainstream party on many issues can appear as a potential ally rather than a threat. The temptation to treat such a leader as a normal political partner — one who can be managed, moderated, or used for electoral advantage — is substantial. But the historical record of such alliances is not encouraging: parties that expected to control anti-democratic leaders typically found that the opposite occurred.

Opposition parties play a crucial role in democratic defense. Effective opposition — presenting credible policy alternatives, maintaining discipline in legislative chambers, refusing to grant quorums when votes would violate democratic norms, declining to legitimate extra-constitutional procedures through participation — is an essential check on executive overreach. Opposition parties that are fragmented, internally divided, poorly funded, or willing to cooperate with backsliding governments in exchange for policy concessions are weaker checks than unified, well-organized, principled opposition parties.

The Role of Courts and Independent Institutions

Courts, anti-corruption agencies, election commissions, and other independent oversight institutions are among the most important structural defenses against democratic backsliding. When courts strike down unconstitutional legislation, when anti-corruption agencies investigate powerful officials, when election commissions enforce campaign finance rules impartially, they are performing the checking functions that democratic accountability requires.

The defense of these institutions from political capture is therefore a critical democratic challenge. Several approaches can help protect institutional independence: designing appointment processes that require supermajority approval or bipartisan agreement; using long, non-renewable terms for institutional leaders to reduce the leverage that governments can exert over officials hoping for reappointment; creating statutory protections for institutional independence; and building international legal or reputational norms that define politically motivated interference with independent institutions as violations of democratic standards.

But these structural protections are ultimately only as strong as the political will to defend them. When courts are threatened with defunding, reorganization, or jurisdiction-stripping, their defense depends on whether the political community — including legislatures, media, civil society, other governments, and the public — is willing to mobilize in their defense. Courts that have built strong public legitimacy over time, that are seen as genuinely impartial rather than as partisan actors, are more likely to receive that political support than courts whose perceived fairness is disputed.

The Role of Civil Society and Citizens

Civil society organizations — advocacy groups, professional associations, labor unions, religious institutions, think tanks, community organizations — provide crucial resources for democratic defense. They organize citizens for political action. They maintain records of government behavior. They provide legal support to individuals and organizations that are targeted by government pressure. They create alternative information channels when mainstream media is captured or self-censoring. They sustain the organizational infrastructure of political opposition when formal party structures are weakened or suppressed.

Citizen mobilization — protest, petition, boycott, organized advocacy — has historically been important in defending democracy and reversing backsliding. Mass protest has helped defeat constitutional changes that would have entrenched authoritarian power, driven out governments that won elections through fraud, and signaled to international actors that populations will not passively accept democratic erosion.

Civic education — the knowledge and skills that citizens need to participate effectively in democratic life — is foundational to long-term democratic resilience. Citizens who understand how democratic institutions work, who can recognize the signs of institutional erosion, who know their rights and how to exercise them, and who regard democratic participation as a civic obligation are better equipped to defend democratic systems than citizens who are politically disengaged, ill-informed, or who lack civic skills and knowledge.

International Democratic Solidarity

International actors — other governments, international organizations, regional bodies — can contribute to the defense of democracy both positively (by providing resources, legitimacy, and institutional support to democratic actors) and negatively (by applying pressure on governments that engage in backsliding). The European Union's rule-of-law mechanisms — including the ability to suspend voting rights of member states that violate EU democratic standards and to condition EU funding on rule-of-law compliance — represent one of the most developed examples of international pressure on democratic backsliding.

The effectiveness of international democratic solidarity is limited by several factors: the interests of major powers in stable relationships with governments they know, even when those governments are backsliding; the norm of non-interference in domestic politics; and the capacity of backsliding governments to resist external pressure by framing it as illegitimate foreign interference in national sovereignty. International pressure is most effective when combined with strong domestic democratic opposition and when international actors are unified rather than divided in their response.

Civic Debate
Should International Organizations Sanction Countries That Undermine Democracy?
The Issue

When a government that is a member of an international organization or alliance systematically undermines its own democratic institutions — attacking judicial independence, suppressing free media, manipulating elections, restricting civil society — what, if anything, should international actors do? This question has become urgent as democratic backsliding has occurred within institutions like the European Union, whose members have accepted democratic standards as conditions of membership.

Argument A — International Organizations Should Apply Meaningful Sanctions

Democratic backsliding within international organizations damages the organization's credibility and sets precedents that encourage further erosion. When an organization that proclaims democratic values tolerates authoritarian-leaning behavior by its members without consequence, it signals that democratic standards are optional rather than binding. International organizations should use available tools — suspension of voting rights, withholding of funds, public censure, conditional access to markets — to impose real costs on governments that systematically undermine democratic institutions. Citizens of backsliding countries who are working to defend democratic institutions deserve the support of the international community they are formally part of.

Argument B — International Organizations Should Be Cautious About Sanctions

Sanctioning elected governments for their domestic political choices creates a precedent that is difficult to apply fairly and that can be used for political rather than principled purposes. Who decides when a government has crossed the line from controversial but legitimate reform to democratic backsliding? Powerful states within international organizations may use democracy criteria selectively — against governments they dislike on other grounds while ignoring similar behavior by governments they find politically convenient. Sanctions that are seen as interference in domestic politics may strengthen rather than weaken the political position of backsliding governments, which can frame sanctions as foreign attacks on national sovereignty.

Other Perspectives

Some argue that international pressure can be most effective when targeted at specific institutional actors — such as judicial or electoral officials who cooperate with backsliding — rather than applied as broad country sanctions that harm ordinary citizens. Others argue that regional organizations composed of democracies should establish clearer ex ante criteria for democratic standards that all members commit to, making enforcement a matter of upholding agreed obligations rather than external judgment.

Tradeoffs

International democratic solidarity involves real tradeoffs between effective defense of democratic norms and respect for state sovereignty, between principled action and the diplomatic relationships that make international cooperation possible, and between short-term disruption and long-term institutional resilience. These tradeoffs are not resolved by any formula and require ongoing political judgment.

Rights and Responsibilities: The Civic Obligations of Democratic Citizenship

Democracy does not merely grant citizens rights; it places obligations on them. The health of democratic institutions depends not only on formal structures and political elites but on citizens who take their democratic responsibilities seriously.

The most basic civic obligation in a democracy is political participation — engaging with the political process rather than withdrawing from it. This begins with voting but extends much further: staying informed about public affairs, engaging with issues in civil discourse, participating in community organizations and public meetings, contacting elected officials, participating in campaigns and advocacy organizations, and being willing to serve in public roles when called upon.

A closely related obligation is civic information literacy — the capacity to evaluate information critically, to distinguish reliable journalism from misinformation and propaganda, to understand how institutions work, and to resist manipulation by actors who use false information to undermine trust in democratic institutions. In an information environment characterized by the rapid spread of misinformation through social media, partisan media bubbles, and deliberate disinformation campaigns, the ability to evaluate information critically is not merely a personal virtue but a democratic necessity.

Respect for democratic norms and institutions — including the outcomes of fair elections and the decisions of independent courts even when one disagrees with them — is another civic obligation. Citizens who condition their acceptance of democratic outcomes on winning, who are willing to accept or support extra-constitutional action when their preferred side loses, or who regard the opposing party as so dangerous that normal democratic constraints should be abandoned undermine the norms of mutual toleration and institutional forbearance that democratic stability depends on.

Civil discourse and engagement across difference — the willingness to engage with citizens who hold different political views as fellow members of the political community rather than as enemies — is increasingly difficult in polarized political environments but essential to democratic health. Democracy requires not agreement but the capacity to disagree without delegitimizing the fundamental right of political opponents to compete for power.

Policy Lab
Responding to Signs of Democratic Backsliding
Scenario

The government of a democracy that has been classified as "free" for three decades wins a parliamentary election with 55 percent of legislative seats. Over the following two years, the government passes legislation that: (1) expands the constitutional court from nine to thirteen members and immediately fills the four new seats with loyalists; (2) creates a new government-controlled broadcasting regulator that revokes the licenses of several critical television channels on technical grounds; (3) passes a new civil society law requiring organizations that receive more than 5 percent of their funding from foreign sources to register as "foreign agents" and submit to enhanced monitoring; (4) changes the electoral district boundaries, increasing the government's projected seat share from the same vote share by approximately 8 percentage points. The government defends each measure as legal reform. Opposition parties, civil society organizations, and several international observer bodies describe the cumulative effect as systematic democratic erosion.

Stakeholders

Citizens of the country; opposition parties; civil society organizations; independent judiciary; independent media; international partner governments; regional organizations the country belongs to; investors and economic actors with interests in the country.

Policy Options
  1. Internal opposition mobilization: Opposition parties, civil society organizations, and citizens organize sustained legal challenges, public protests, campaign and advocacy activity, and international outreach to document and resist the government's actions through domestic political means.
  2. Constitutional legal challenges: Opposition parties and civil society organizations bring legal challenges to each government measure in domestic courts, seeking rulings that the measures violate constitutional provisions, and appeal internationally if domestic courts are compromised.
  3. Regional organization proceedings: The country's regional organization initiates formal rule-of-law proceedings, documents the government's measures against democratic standards, and threatens financial and membership consequences if the measures are not reversed.
  4. International diplomatic pressure: Partner governments issue formal statements of concern, raise issues in bilateral diplomatic meetings, and condition certain forms of cooperation on measurable improvements in democratic governance.
  5. Civil society international network: Domestic civil society organizations partner with international organizations and democracy-support networks to share documentation, training, and resources, building the capacity of domestic democratic actors to sustain opposition over a long period.
Evidence Needed

Detailed legal analysis of whether each measure violates constitutional provisions; assessment of judicial independence in the domestic court system; analysis of the regional organization's available mechanisms and political will to use them; public opinion data on citizens' views of democratic institutions; comparison with international standards for judicial independence, media regulation, civil society law, and electoral administration.

Tradeoffs
  • Internal opposition faces the challenge of operating against a government that increasingly controls the institutions through which opposition would normally operate.
  • International pressure faces the challenge of being dismissed as interference in domestic sovereignty.
  • Legal challenges face compromised courts.
  • Sustained civic mobilization requires resources and organization over long periods.
  • Combination strategies may be more effective than any single approach but require coordination across actors with different interests and tactics.

Chapter 47 — Common Misunderstandings

Clearing Up Common Errors

Misunderstanding 1
"Democracy just means that the majority gets what it wants."
Correction
Democratic systems do use majority rule for many decisions, but liberal democracy combines majority rule with protections for minority rights, civil liberties, and constitutional limits that the majority cannot override through ordinary legislation. The rights of political, ethnic, religious, and other minorities are not subject to majority vote in a liberal democracy — courts can strike down majority-enacted laws that violate constitutional protections. A system in which the majority could do literally anything it wished to minorities would be majoritarian, but not liberal democratic in the full sense.
Misunderstanding 2
"If elections are held, the country is democratic."
Correction
Holding elections is necessary but not sufficient for democracy. Many countries hold elections that are competitive in some formal sense while manipulating the conditions of competition — controlling media, using state resources for the incumbent, preventing opposition candidates from running, and manipulating results — to such an extent that the elections do not produce genuine democratic accountability. The concept of "electoral democracy" distinguishes systems that hold elections from "liberal democracy," which requires genuine civil liberties, rule of law, and accountability mechanisms alongside elections.
Misunderstanding 3
"Democratic backsliding means a military coup has occurred."
Correction
Democratic backsliding typically does not involve military coups. The most common contemporary form of democratic erosion is executive aggrandizement — the gradual accumulation of power by elected leaders through legal means, including court-packing, electoral rule changes, media restrictions, and civil society restrictions — that weakens accountability institutions without overtly suspending democratic forms. This more insidious form of backsliding is harder to recognize and oppose because each individual step may appear defensible while the cumulative effect is the systematic erosion of democratic governance.
Misunderstanding 4
"Once a country is a consolidated democracy, it will always remain democratic."
Correction
Consolidation does not guarantee permanent democracy. Even long-established democracies can experience significant democratic backsliding if informal norms erode, institutional independence is compromised, and political leaders abandon the restraints that democratic governance requires. Comparative political science has documented democratic erosion in countries with decades of democratic experience. Democratic consolidation reduces the risk of collapse, but it does not eliminate the need for ongoing commitment by citizens, institutions, and political actors to democratic norms and rules.
Misunderstanding 5
"A country cannot be a democracy if it has significant inequality or poverty."
Correction
While economic development and wealth are associated with democratic stability, poor and unequal societies can and do sustain democratic governance. India, one of the world's largest democracies, has maintained democratic institutions across many decades despite high levels of poverty and inequality. Economic conditions affect the quality and resilience of democracy, but they do not determine whether democracy is possible. Conversely, wealthy countries can experience democratic backsliding if institutional independence and political norms are compromised.
Misunderstanding 6
"Democratic norms are merely matters of political etiquette and do not really affect how governments behave."
Correction
Informal norms — particularly mutual toleration and institutional forbearance — are structural features of democratic systems that significantly affect how political actors behave and whether institutions function effectively. Norms constrain behavior through reputational sanctions, political costs, and the expectations that shape how officials, parties, and citizens respond to political actions. When norms erode — when politicians no longer treat opponents as legitimate rivals, or when political actors use their formal powers to their fullest extent without regard for institutional damage — the practical functioning of democratic institutions can deteriorate rapidly even without formal legal change.
Misunderstanding 7
"Strong leaders who win large electoral majorities are more democratic because they have a larger popular mandate."
Correction
Electoral mandate does not determine democratic quality. A leader who wins a large electoral majority and uses it to weaken courts, suppress opposition, and restrict civil society is not more democratic for having a large mandate — the mandate authorizes governing within the constitutional framework, not restructuring that framework to eliminate checks on executive power. Democratic governance requires that even leaders with large popular mandates operate within constitutional constraints and respect the institutions designed to hold them accountable.
Misunderstanding 8
"International pressure is ineffective at protecting democracy in other countries."
Correction
International pressure on countries experiencing democratic backsliding has mixed effectiveness — it is not reliably decisive, and it can sometimes backfire by strengthening nationalist sentiment against perceived foreign interference. But it is not uniformly ineffective either. EU rule-of-law proceedings have imposed real economic and reputational costs on Hungary and Poland. International election observation has deterred fraud and provided legitimacy to contested results. International support for civil society organizations has helped sustain democratic opposition in difficult environments. The effectiveness of international pressure depends heavily on context.
Chapter 47 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 47.1Democracy is a political system in which authority derives from the people, expressed through free and competitive elections and sustained by the institutions, norms, and civic culture that make elections meaningful. Minimalist definitions focus on elections; more robust definitions add civil liberties, rule of law, and institutional checks. The procedural core includes regular competitive elections, near-universal suffrage, protection of civil liberties, and civilian control of government. Systems that meet this threshold are electoral democracies but not necessarily liberal democracies.
  • Sec 47.2The distinction between electoral and liberal democracy is one of the most important in comparative politics. Liberal democracy adds judicial independence, protection of civil liberties, horizontal accountability, minority rights, and civilian supremacy to the requirements of electoral democracy. Illiberal democracies hold elections while compromising these additional requirements. Measuring democratic quality requires composite indices — Freedom House, V-Dem, and the Economist Democracy Index — that capture multiple dimensions beyond elections alone. Global democratic quality has declined since approximately 2005 by most measures.
  • Sec 47.3Democratic systems depend not only on formal institutions but on informal norms — particularly mutual toleration (accepting opponents as legitimate rivals) and institutional forbearance (refraining from using legal powers in ways that damage institutional checks) — that constrain political behavior without being enforceable through law. Legislatures, courts, civil society, free media, and election administration bodies each perform distinct functions essential to democratic accountability. The functioning of each depends on its independence from political capture by the governing party.
  • Sec 47.4Free elections allow citizens and candidates to participate without coercion; fair elections apply the rules of competition evenhandedly. Both requirements can be compromised by incumbent advantage, electoral manipulation, or biased election administration. International election observation can deter fraud and provide impartial assessment but cannot monitor all aspects of electoral competition, particularly legal forms of manipulation through gerrymandering, unequal media access, and selective enforcement of candidacy rules.
  • Sec 47.5Democratic accountability requires answerability (the obligation to explain decisions), enforceability (the capacity to impose consequences for violations), and scope (the range of officials subject to accountability including the highest). Transparency — the availability of government information through freedom of information laws, open meetings, and disclosure requirements — is a prerequisite for meaningful accountability. Corruption, the abuse of public authority for private gain, is self-reinforcing and targets accountability institutions to protect itself. Anti-corruption institutions require genuine independence to function effectively.
  • Sec 47.6Democratic consolidation refers to the condition in which democracy has become the only game in town — accepted by all significant political actors as the only legitimate framework for political competition and transfer of power. Indicators include multiple peaceful transfers of power, acceptance of election results by losers, absence of serious anti-democratic actors, strong civil society and media, and elite consensus on democratic rules. Economic development, democratic history, strong rule of law traditions, and favorable international context are associated with consolidation, though none is sufficient in isolation.
  • Sec 47.7Democratic backsliding — the gradual erosion of democratic quality through incremental steps taken by elected leaders — has become the dominant pattern of democratic decline in the contemporary world. Unlike military coups, backsliding works through the forms of democratic legitimacy while hollowing out institutional checks. Its most common mechanism is executive aggrandizement, deployed through court-packing, electoral manipulation, media capture, civil society restrictions, exploitation of emergency powers, and constitutional changes that entrench ruling parties. Cases in Hungary, Venezuela, India, and Poland display common patterns across different regions and economic contexts. Structural causes include economic inequality, polarization, institutional weakness, elite complicity, and international permissiveness.
  • Sec 47.8Defending democratic systems requires active choices by multiple actors: political parties that refuse to mainstream anti-democratic leaders and maintain effective opposition; courts and independent institutions that resist political capture; civil society organizations that mobilize citizens and maintain accountability; free media that investigates and reports on government behavior; citizens who participate, stay informed, and maintain commitment to democratic norms even when elections produce unfavorable outcomes; and international actors who apply meaningful pressure on governments that systematically undermine democratic standards. Democratic resilience is ultimately cultural as well as institutional — dependent on the norms, habits, and civic commitments of citizens and political actors who understand what democracy requires and commit themselves to sustaining it.

Key Terms

Autocratization
The process by which a political system becomes less democratic over time, moving toward authoritarianism through gradual or incremental change.
Civil liberties
Protections against government interference with individual freedoms, including speech, press, assembly, religion, and privacy, which are essential to meaningful democratic competition.
Competitive authoritarianism
A political system in which nominally democratic institutions — elections, legislatures, courts — exist but are systematically biased in favor of the incumbent to such an extent that genuine democratic competition is impossible.
Constitutional hardball
The use of legal or constitutional powers in ways that are technically within the rules but that damage the norms and institutional checks that democratic governance depends on.
Court-packing
The practice of expanding the size of a court and filling the new seats with political loyalists in order to change the court's effective independence and likely rulings; a common tactic in democratic backsliding.
Democratic backsliding
The gradual deterioration of democratic quality in a political system, typically through incremental steps taken by elected leaders rather than through outright military coups or revolutionary seizures of power.
Democratic consolidation
The condition in which democracy has become accepted by all significant political actors as the only legitimate framework for political competition and the transfer of power; a consolidated democracy is stable and self-sustaining.
Democratic erosion
A synonym for democratic backsliding; the incremental weakening of democratic institutions, norms, and practices by elected actors who use legal means to concentrate power and reduce accountability.
Democratic recession
A period or trend characterized by a global or regional decline in the number or quality of democratic systems; political scientists identified a democratic recession beginning approximately in 2005.
Electoral democracy
A political system that holds regular competitive elections with universal or near-universal suffrage and honest vote-counting, but that may not fully protect civil liberties, maintain judicial independence, or provide robust accountability mechanisms.
Electoral manipulation
The use of legal or illegal means to advantage the ruling party or incumbent in electoral competition, including gerrymandering, selective enforcement of candidacy rules, and control of election administration.
Executive aggrandizement
The gradual accumulation of power by elected executives at the expense of other institutions — courts, legislatures, civil society, media — through legal and quasi-legal means; identified as the most common contemporary form of democratic backsliding.
Free and fair elections
Elections that are free (conducted without coercion of voters or candidates) and fair (administered impartially, with equal rules applied to all parties and candidates).
Guardrails of democracy
The informal norms, institutional conventions, and political expectations that constrain how far political actors will push their formal legal powers, serving as protections against the abuse of legal authority for anti-democratic purposes.
Horizontal accountability
The capacity of institutions within the government — courts, legislatures, audit bodies, prosecutors — to check and hold accountable the executive and other government actors through legal and political means.
Illiberal democracy
A political system that holds competitive elections but does not protect civil liberties, maintain judicial independence, or provide effective accountability mechanisms; a term associated with Fareed Zakaria's analysis of emerging hybrid systems in the 1990s.
Incumbent advantage
The structural and political advantages that governments in power enjoy over challengers in electoral competition; illegitimate when it involves the direct use of state power for partisan electoral purposes.
Institutional forbearance
The informal norm of refraining from using legal powers to their fullest technical extent when doing so would damage the institutional fabric and checking functions of democratic governance.
Liberal democracy
A political system that combines regular competitive elections with robust protection of civil liberties, an independent judiciary, rule of law, horizontal accountability mechanisms, and respect for minority rights.
Media capture
The process by which governments or government-aligned interests gain control over previously independent media outlets, enabling the suppression of critical coverage.
Mutual toleration
The informal norm that political competitors accept each other as legitimate rivals entitled to compete for power; a foundational norm for democratic stability identified by Levitsky and Ziblatt.
Norm
An unwritten but broadly understood and respected rule of political behavior that constrains what politicians and officials do even when formal rules would permit them to do more; enforced through reputational and political sanctions rather than law.
Third wave of democratization
The global trend toward democratic governance that began in the mid-1970s in southern Europe and expanded through Latin America, Eastern Europe, sub-Saharan Africa, and parts of Asia, dramatically increasing the number of countries holding competitive elections.
Vertical accountability
The capacity of citizens, through elections and organized advocacy, to hold government officials accountable for their performance and decisions.

Chapter 47 — Closing Reflection

Connecting the Chapter to the Larger Study of Government

Democracy is neither natural nor inevitable. Human societies have organized political authority in many ways over recorded history — through monarchy, aristocracy, theocracy, military rule, and oligarchy, as well as through various forms of collective self-governance. The contemporary era, in which democratic governance has spread across a substantial portion of the world's countries, represents a remarkable historical achievement that required enormous effort, sacrifice, and institutional innovation to produce. It also represents an achievement that requires ongoing effort to maintain. Democracy is not a destination that societies arrive at and then inhabit permanently; it is a condition that must be actively sustained by the choices of citizens, politicians, judges, journalists, civil society leaders, and institutional actors who commit themselves, continuously and in the face of competing pressures, to honoring its requirements.

This chapter has introduced democracy as a political system with specific institutional requirements, not merely as a value or a slogan. It has distinguished between the forms of democracy that hold elections and the fuller forms that also protect civil liberties, maintain judicial independence, and provide robust accountability. It has explored how democratic norms — the informal rules that constrain political behavior beyond formal legal requirements — are as essential to democratic functioning as the formal institutions themselves, and how the erosion of those norms is both a warning sign and a mechanism of democratic decline. It has examined democratic consolidation as a condition of stability that reduces, without eliminating, the risk of collapse. And it has explored democratic backsliding — perhaps the defining political challenge of the contemporary era — as a process that works through legal means and that can advance quite far before it becomes unmistakably clear that a democratic system has been compromised.

The study of democratic backsliding is not cause for despair, but it is cause for seriousness. The cases examined in this chapter demonstrate that even wealthy countries with decades of democratic experience and membership in institutions that affirm democratic values can experience significant institutional erosion when political leaders commit themselves to accumulating power at the expense of the checks that constrain them. They also demonstrate that democratic deterioration is neither instant nor irreversible — it proceeds through recognizable stages that can be identified and resisted, and it has in some cases been reversed through sustained political effort by democratic actors who refused to accept the erosion as inevitable. The students who understand these dynamics, who can recognize the warning signs and the mechanisms of erosion, and who are prepared to act as democratic citizens rather than passive observers of political fate are among the most important resources that democracies possess.

As you move through the remainder of this textbook — and through the political life of your society — the concepts developed in this chapter should provide a framework for ongoing evaluation. Not every political controversy is evidence of democratic erosion. Not every institutional dispute is a crisis. But some developments do represent genuine threats to the conditions that make democratic governance possible, and distinguishing between ordinary political conflict and systemic democratic deterioration requires precisely the kind of civic knowledge, institutional literacy, and critical judgment that the study of government is designed to develop.

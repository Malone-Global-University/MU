Chapter 8 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

In the summer of 1974, the United States Supreme Court issued one of the most consequential rulings in American constitutional history. President Richard Nixon had been ordered by a special prosecutor to hand over audio recordings of his private White House conversations. Nixon refused, claiming that the separation of powers gave him an absolute right to protect confidential presidential communications. In a unanimous decision in United States v. Nixon, the Court disagreed. It ruled that while a general claim of executive privilege existed, it could not override the specific demands of the judicial process in a criminal investigation. Nixon complied. Shortly afterward, the recordings revealed evidence of his involvement in obstruction of justice, and he resigned from office—the only American president to do so voluntarily.

The Nixon crisis illustrated something that political theorists had argued for centuries: power, if not limited and checked by other power, tends to expand until it overwhelms the institutions meant to contain it. The framers of the American Constitution were acutely aware of this tendency. They had read the political philosophers who warned about concentrated authority. They had lived under a colonial government they considered tyrannical. And they had struggled through the inadequacies of the Articles of Confederation. From those experiences, they built a system designed to prevent any single person, office, or faction from controlling the whole of government—dividing power among three branches and constructing a web of rules requiring each branch to depend on and constrain the others.

This chapter examines the separation of powers and checks and balances in depth. It explains why power is divided, how the legislative, executive, and judicial branches are defined, how they check each other in practice, and how the American model compares to parliamentary systems in which powers are more fused. Understanding this design—its logic, its costs, and its limits—is essential for any serious student of government and civic life.

Opening Question

If a popular President refuses to comply with a court order, claiming that executing the law is the executive's job and that courts cannot compel a President, what happens? Who enforces the ruling? What stops the President? These questions go to the heart of what separation of powers actually means in practice—not just on paper, but when a real political conflict tests the limits of each branch's authority.

Section 8.1

Why Power Is Divided

The Fear of Concentrated Authority

The idea that governmental power should be divided among separate institutions did not emerge suddenly or from a single source. It developed over centuries of political experience, philosophical argument, and bitter observation of what governments do when no one can stop them. To understand why power is divided, it helps to begin with the problem that division is meant to solve: the danger of tyranny.

Tyranny, in political thought, does not simply mean cruelty or violence. It refers to the condition in which government acts without accountability to law, without constraint by independent institutions, and without meaningful recourse for those who are harmed. A tyrant may be generous or cruel, wise or foolish. What makes rule tyrannical is not the personality of the ruler but the structure of power—specifically, the absence of limits on what the ruler can do and the absence of institutions that can stop or correct the ruler's actions.

Political philosophers going back to ancient Greece observed that concentrated power tends to be abused, even by people who begin with good intentions. Aristotle noted that rulers who are not checked by law or representative bodies tend to govern for themselves rather than for the community. Medieval European thinkers struggled to find ways to constrain monarchs through law, church authority, and representative bodies like parliaments. By the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, the argument for formal constitutional limits on governmental power had become central to political theory.

The English political philosopher John Locke argued in the late seventeenth century that government derives its authority from the consent of the governed and that this authority is limited by natural rights no government may legitimately violate. Locke also argued that the power to make laws and the power to execute laws should be separated, because concentrating both in the same hands creates a dangerous temptation to use the law for the rulers' own benefit rather than the public good.

The French philosopher Montesquieu, writing in the mid-eighteenth century, developed this argument into one of the most influential frameworks in the history of constitutional design. In The Spirit of the Laws (1748), Montesquieu argued that political liberty requires that the three essential functions of government—making law, enforcing law, and judging cases under law—be exercised by three separate institutions. If the same person or body held all three powers, there could be no liberty, because the ruler could enact tyrannical laws, enforce them arbitrarily, and judge any challenge in favor of the ruler. Only by dividing these functions could citizens be protected from the abuse of authority.

The American Founding and Constitutional Design

When the delegates to the Philadelphia Convention gathered in 1787 to write a new constitution, they brought with them Montesquieu's framework, their experience with colonial and state government, and a deep anxiety about both concentrated power and weak government. The solution they chose divided governmental power among three distinct branches—Congress (legislative), the President (executive), and the federal courts (judicial)—while also creating mechanisms by which each branch could check and constrain the others.

The design reflected a sophisticated and somewhat pessimistic theory of human nature: that people in positions of power, no matter how virtuous, are tempted to expand that power, and that the best protection against such expansion is structural rather than moral. James Madison, one of the Constitution's principal architects, articulated this logic in Federalist No. 51 (1788). Madison wrote that if men were angels, no government would be necessary; and if angels governed men, no checks on government would be necessary. Since neither condition holds, a well-designed constitution must force ambition to counteract ambition. Each branch's institutional interests should be designed to resist encroachments by the others—not because officeholders are unusually virtuous, but because they have strong incentives to protect their own branch's power.

This insight is crucial for understanding separation of powers as a structural, not a moral, solution. The system is designed to function even when the people within it are self-interested, competitive, or partisan. It works not because officials are better than ordinary people, but because each official's interest in protecting their institutional position leads them to resist encroachments from other institutions.

Why Division Alone Is Not Enough

Simply labeling three separate branches and assigning them different functions would not, by itself, prevent the concentration of power. A strong executive could ignore the legislature's laws if no institution could stop it. A legislature could pass any law if no institution could strike it down. A judiciary filled with loyalists would fail to apply the law impartially. For separation of powers to work, there must be mechanisms by which each institution can actively restrain the others—that is, there must be checks and balances.

The concept of checks and balances accepts that power is not perfectly separated in practice. The Constitution gives the President a veto over legislation, gives the Senate the power to confirm or reject judicial nominees, and gives Congress the power to remove the President through impeachment. These overlapping powers force each branch into relationship with the others. No branch is truly independent; each depends on some cooperation or acquiescence from the others to exercise its functions. This is not a flaw—it is the design. As Madison put it in Federalist No. 47: the accumulation of all powers, legislative, executive, and judicial, in the same hands is the very definition of tyranny.

Section 8.2

Legislative Power

Legislative power is the power to make law. In a constitutional system, a legislature is the institution vested with primary authority to create, modify, and repeal the rules governing social and political life. Law shapes nearly every aspect of public and private conduct—what conduct is criminal, what rights individuals have, how public money is spent, what regulations businesses must follow, and how government itself is organized.

Most legislatures in constitutional democracies are composed of elected representatives. The rationale is democratic: those who make the laws governing everyone should themselves be accountable to the people who live under those laws. Elections are the mechanism that creates and enforces this accountability.

In a constitutional democracy, the legislature's lawmaking power is not absolute. Constitutions typically place certain subjects beyond legislative reach. A legislature cannot pass a law eliminating freedom of speech or abolishing the rights of the accused without amending the constitution—usually a far more demanding procedure than ordinary legislation. Legislatures are also constrained by the other branches: through the executive veto, judicial review, and constitutional requirements for supermajority votes on certain matters.

Legislatures in constitutional systems also exercise significant power over the executive branch through oversight—the authority to investigate how executive departments carry out the law, how public funds are spent, and whether officials are complying with statutory requirements. In the United States, congressional committees can hold hearings, demand documents, subpoena witnesses, and conduct investigations. Oversight is a critical check because the executive branch is far too large and complex for any legislature to monitor exhaustively through lawmaking alone.

Section 8.3

Executive Power

Executive power is the power to implement, administer, and enforce the law. Executives carry out the decisions made by the legislature; they manage the day-to-day operations of government; they represent the state in diplomatic relations; they command the military; they respond to emergencies; and they exercise discretion in how law is applied to particular circumstances. In most constitutional systems, the executive branch is by far the largest part of government, encompassing hundreds of agencies and bureaus staffed by millions of civil servants and political appointees.

The executive branch is also the fastest-moving and most action-oriented branch. While a legislature debates and deliberates, an executive can act. This capacity for rapid action is an asset in crises, military conflicts, and diplomatic negotiations. These same qualities—the ability to act quickly and without public deliberation—also make executive power the most dangerous form of governmental power from the perspective of constitutional constraints.

In a presidential system like the United States, the executive power is vested in a separately elected President who serves a fixed term and is not dependent on the legislature for tenure in office. The American President's constitutional powers include the ability to sign or veto legislation, command the armed forces as Commander in Chief, make treaties (subject to Senate ratification), nominate federal judges and executive officials (subject to Senate confirmation), grant pardons, and receive foreign ambassadors. Over time, Presidents have also accumulated significant informal powers through executive orders, executive agreements, and emergency declarations.

Important Distinction

The President's role as Commander in Chief does not confer unlimited military power. Congress holds the formal power to declare war and the appropriations power that funds military operations. The War Powers Resolution (1973) attempts to limit the duration of unauthorized military engagements. Courts can review whether specific military actions comply with domestic law. The constitutional structure limits what the executive can do with military power even in wartime.

The executive branch does not consist only of the President and a cabinet. It includes a vast network of departments (Treasury, Defense, State, Justice, Education, and many others), independent agencies (the Environmental Protection Agency, the Federal Reserve, the Securities and Exchange Commission), the armed forces, law enforcement agencies, and the career civil service that staffs all of these institutions. Most of the day-to-day work of government happens in this administrative apparatus, far from the direct attention of Congress or the courts. Understanding executive power requires understanding not just the Presidency but the entire administrative state—the system of agencies, regulations, and professional administrators that gives executive power its practical reach.

Section 8.4

Judicial Power

Judicial power is the power to interpret law and apply it to specific cases and controversies. Courts do not make law in the primary sense—that is the legislature's job. They do not carry out law in the operational sense—that is the executive's job. Instead, they resolve disputes by determining what the law requires in particular factual situations and by ensuring that the law is applied consistently, fairly, and in accordance with the constitution.

The judicial function is distinctive in several ways. Courts are reactive, not proactive: they can only act when a specific dispute is brought before them. Courts must justify their decisions by reasoned argument—by explaining how the law applies to the facts—rather than simply asserting authority. And courts are meant to be impartial: judges are supposed to apply the law as written rather than favor one party because of their power or political connections.

The independence of the judiciary—insulation of judges from direct political pressure—is considered essential to the proper functioning of courts. If judges feared losing their positions because they ruled against powerful political actors, they could not be trusted to apply the law impartially. Federal judges in the United States hold their positions during "good behavior," effectively meaning life tenure, and can be removed only through impeachment and conviction—a procedure that has never resulted in the removal of a Supreme Court justice.

Judicial Review

The most politically significant power exercised by courts in constitutional systems is judicial review: the power to strike down laws or executive actions that the court finds inconsistent with the constitution. Judicial review gives courts an enormous checking power over both the legislature and the executive. A law passed by Congress and signed by the President can be invalidated by a majority of Supreme Court justices who are not elected and who serve for life.

This power is not explicitly stated in the text of the American Constitution. It was established by the Supreme Court itself in Marbury v. Madison (1803), in which Chief Justice John Marshall argued that it is inherently the province and duty of the judicial branch to say what the law is, and that if ordinary law conflicts with constitutional law, the court must give effect to the constitution. This claim was controversial at the time and has remained a subject of debate ever since.

Judicial review raises fundamental questions about democratic theory. Is it consistent with democratic self-governance for unelected, life-tenured judges to override the choices of elected representatives? Defenders argue that democracy requires more than majority rule—it requires the protection of fundamental rights and constitutional structure, and courts are the most insulated institution from temporary majority pressures. Critics argue that judicial review amounts to rule by judges who impose their own preferences in the guise of constitutional interpretation. This debate has no settled answer. Most democracies have resolved it by adopting some form of judicial review while placing various institutional and procedural limits on it.

Courts as Infrastructure

The availability of courts as a check on government power is a central feature of rule-of-law systems. If government officials know their actions can be reviewed by independent courts, they have incentives to stay within their legal authority. If individuals know they can take grievances to an impartial tribunal, they have a means of seeking redress that does not require violence or political connections. Courts are, in this sense, an essential infrastructure of constitutional government—not merely a third branch, but an institution without which neither the separation of powers nor the protection of rights can function reliably.

Section 8.5

Checks and Balances

Checks and balances is the name for the system of institutional interdependencies that allow each branch to limit, monitor, and in some cases override the actions of the other branches. Division of power creates distinct institutions; checks and balances give those institutions the tools to maintain that division against encroachment. Without checks, separation would be nominal—one branch could simply ignore the others and accumulate all effective power.

Presidential Veto and Congressional Override

The presidential veto is one of the most direct and frequently invoked checks in the American system. When Congress passes a bill, it goes to the President for signature. The President can sign it into law, veto it by returning it to Congress with objections, or allow it to become law without signature (if the President does not act within ten days while Congress is in session). A vetoed bill can still become law if Congress overrides the veto—requiring a two-thirds vote in both the Senate and the House, a demanding threshold that means most vetoes are sustained.

The veto gives the President significant legislative influence, because Congress must often take the President's likely reaction into account when drafting legislation. The veto is a check of the executive on the legislature; the override is a check of the legislature on the executive. Together, they create a negotiating dynamic: the President and Congress must reach agreement, or the President must anticipate that Congress will override, or the President accepts political consequences for blocking legislation the public supports.

Advice and Consent

The Senate's power of advice and consent over executive appointments and treaties is another critical check. The President nominates federal judges, cabinet secretaries, ambassadors, and other high officials, but they cannot take office without Senate confirmation. The President can negotiate treaties with foreign governments, but they do not take effect unless approved by two-thirds of the Senate. These requirements force the executive and the legislature into ongoing negotiation about the composition of the government and its international commitments. A President whose nominees are consistently blocked by the Senate is effectively constrained from filling key positions.

Impeachment and Trial

The Constitution gives Congress the power to remove the President, Vice President, and all civil officers through the process of impeachment and trial. The House has the sole power to impeach—formally charging an officer with high crimes and misdemeanors. The Senate then holds a trial and must convict by a two-thirds vote to remove the officer from office. Three American presidents have been impeached by the House: Andrew Johnson (1868), Bill Clinton (1998), and Donald Trump (twice, in 2019 and 2021). None were convicted and removed. The rarity of successful impeachment reflects both the high constitutional threshold and the reality that the process is partly political—partisan alignment can make conviction difficult even when the underlying conduct is serious.

Appropriations Power

Congress's power of the purse—the authority to appropriate funds for all governmental activities—is perhaps its most fundamental check on the executive. No money can be legally spent without a congressional appropriation. The President cannot fund a war, create a new program, or maintain an existing agency without legislative authorization. This gives Congress enormous leverage over executive branch activities. When Congress and the President cannot agree on a budget, the result can be a government shutdown—a cessation of non-essential governmental functions until an agreement is reached.

Civic Vocabulary
Checks and Balances

Checks and balances refers to the system of institutional rules and powers by which each branch of government can limit, monitor, and in certain circumstances override the actions of the other branches. A check is a power that one branch holds over another—such as the presidential veto over legislation, the congressional power to override that veto, or the courts' power of judicial review. A balance refers to the overall structure in which these checks are roughly reciprocal, so that no branch can dominate the others indefinitely.

The concept is closely related to but distinct from separation of powers: separation divides governmental functions among different institutions, while checks and balances give those institutions tools to maintain that division through active mutual accountability. Together, they are designed to prevent any single person, office, or faction from accumulating all governmental authority.

Section 8.6

Separation of Powers in Practice

Constitutional design and actual political practice are never the same thing. The framers designed a system on paper; subsequent generations have operated, tested, stretched, bent, and sometimes violated that system under conditions the framers could not fully anticipate. Separation of powers in practice looks different from separation of powers as an abstract concept, because it operates in the context of political parties, mass democracy, national crises, technological change, and the enormous expansion of governmental functions since the late eighteenth century.

The Rise of Political Parties

One of the most significant departures from the framers' expectations has been the rise of political parties. The Constitution does not mention parties. The framers generally feared permanent factional alliances. But parties emerged almost immediately after ratification and have been central to American political life ever since. The existence of parties profoundly affects how separation of powers operates. When the President's party also controls both chambers of Congress, checking relationships tend to weaken—a party majority in Congress is less likely to investigate, obstruct, or override a President of the same party. When different parties control different branches (divided government), checking relationships tend to intensify, sometimes producing paralysis.

The Expansion of the Executive

Over the course of American history, the executive branch has substantially expanded its power, often pushing the formal boundaries of constitutional authority. Modern governance is enormously complex, requiring expertise and administrative capacity that neither Congress nor the courts possesses in anything like the degree that executive agencies do. National crises—wars, economic depressions, pandemics, natural disasters—create demands for rapid and decisive action that executive officials are better positioned to provide than deliberative legislative bodies.

Congress has sometimes accepted or even encouraged executive expansion by delegating broad legislative authority to executive agencies through statutes giving them wide discretion to make rules, set standards, and regulate industries. The administrative state—the vast network of executive agencies governing everything from air quality to securities markets to food safety—rests partly on this delegated authority. Critics argue that this delegation has undermined the constitutional balance by concentrating too much policy-making power in the hands of officials not directly accountable through elections. Defenders argue that modern governance requires administrative expertise that constitutional formalism cannot accommodate.

Judicial Independence Under Pressure

The independence of the federal judiciary is formally protected by life tenure and salary guarantees. But the courts operate in a political environment. Judicial nominations and confirmations are intensely political processes. Presidents carefully select nominees whose judicial philosophies they favor. Senate confirmation votes often divide along partisan lines. The composition of the Supreme Court has shifted significantly over time as nominations and confirmations have reflected the political environment in which they occurred.

Moreover, courts are aware of their own institutional limitations. The Supreme Court lacks the power of the purse (Congress) and the power of the sword (the executive). Courts depend on the executive to enforce their rulings and on the public to respect their authority. When courts issue rulings that powerful actors refuse to follow, the court's power is revealed as contingent on political and social compliance. The relationship between courts and the political branches is thus not a simple one-way check but an ongoing constitutional dialogue in which each institution shapes and responds to the others.

How the System Works
The Legislative Process as a Checking Mechanism

The process by which a bill becomes law in the United States illustrates how separation of powers operates as a practical checking mechanism. Each step is a potential veto point where legislation can be blocked, delayed, or significantly modified.

The Steps
  1. Introduction. A bill is introduced by a member of Congress and referred to a committee with jurisdiction over the subject.
  2. Committee action. The committee may hold hearings, mark up (amend) the bill, or decline to act. A bill not reported out of committee typically dies—giving committee chairs significant power to block legislation.
  3. Floor debate and vote. If reported, the bill goes to the full chamber. The Senate allows the filibuster—a procedure by which a minority of senators can block a vote unless sixty members vote for cloture to end debate.
  4. The other chamber. The bill must then pass the second chamber. If the second chamber amends the bill, the two chambers must reconcile their versions.
  5. Presidential action. The President can sign the bill into law, veto it, or allow it to become law without signature (if Congress is in session and no action is taken within ten days). If the President does not act and Congress adjourns within ten days, the result is a pocket veto.
  6. Veto override attempt. If vetoed, Congress may attempt an override requiring two-thirds of both chambers—a threshold rarely reached.
  7. Implementation. Executive agencies implement the law through regulations and enforcement. Congress monitors implementation through oversight, Inspector General reports, and the Government Accountability Office.
  8. Judicial review. Courts may interpret the law's meaning in specific cases and may strike down the law or certain applications as unconstitutional.

This process illustrates why major legislation in the United States is slow and frequently stalled. Requiring majorities at multiple stages means that significant legislative change requires unusually broad political consensus—a design feature that prevents hasty action but also makes rapid response to new problems difficult.

Section 8.7

Advantages and Disadvantages of Divided Power

The Case for Separation and Checks

The case for separating powers and institutionalizing checks and balances rests primarily on three arguments: protecting individual rights, preventing tyranny, and improving the quality of governance through deliberation.

Protection of rights. When all power rests in one institution, there is no independent body to protect individuals whose rights that institution violates. Separation of powers creates multiple institutional actors, at least some of which may be positioned to defend rights against governmental overreach. Independent courts can strike down legislation that violates constitutional rights. A legislature can investigate and expose executive abuses. An executive can nominate judges who will protect rights from legislative violations. None of these mechanisms is perfect, but their combined effect provides more layers of protection than any single institution could.

Prevention of tyranny. Concentrated power creates dangerous conditions for tyranny by removing the institutional constraints that might otherwise check authoritarian impulses. The history of the twentieth century is filled with examples of governments that collapsed into dictatorship partly because they lacked strong institutional checks. Separated powers and robust checking mechanisms do not guarantee against authoritarianism—determined authoritarian movements can capture multiple institutions—but they raise the cost and difficulty of democratic erosion by requiring that any would-be authoritarian overcome multiple institutional obstacles simultaneously.

Deliberation and policy quality. Requiring agreement among multiple institutions before law takes effect forces policy proposals to survive scrutiny from multiple vantage points. A bill that must pass both chambers of a bicameral legislature, survive a presidential veto, and withstand judicial challenge has been subjected to more sustained analysis and criticism than one enacted by a single institution. This process can improve legislation by identifying flaws, unintended consequences, and constitutional problems before the law takes effect.

The Costs of Divided Power

Gridlock and ineffectiveness. When different parties control different branches, or when intense partisan disagreement exists within a coalition, checking mechanisms can produce prolonged governmental paralysis. Major legislation may be blocked for years. Emergency responses may be delayed while institutional actors negotiate or litigate. Government shutdowns, debt ceiling standoffs, and legislative impasses are recurring features of American political life precisely because the constitutional system makes it easy to block action and hard to build the consensus needed for major policy change.

Diffuse accountability. When multiple institutions share responsibility for policy, it can be genuinely difficult for voters to identify who is responsible for a policy failure. If the economy performs poorly, is it the President's fault, Congress's fault, or the Federal Reserve's fault? Diffuse accountability can make democratic control less effective, because voters cannot easily punish or reward the right institutions for their performance.

Unequal veto power. The system of checks does not distribute power equally. Small groups can sometimes block actions supported by large majorities. The filibuster allows a minority of senators to prevent a vote. Committee chairs can kill legislation before it reaches the full chamber. Whether these features protect legitimate minority interests or allow privileged minorities to obstruct democratic majorities is a deeply contested question in American political debate.

Crisis response limitations. The slow, deliberative process of lawmaking is poorly suited to rapid responses to emergencies. Natural disasters, military crises, pandemics, and financial collapses may require immediate governmental action that the normal legislative process cannot provide quickly enough. This creates pressure for executive action outside normal constitutional channels—declarations of emergency, executive orders—which in turn generates concerns about executive overreach. The tension between emergency authority and constitutional constraint is a persistent and unresolved feature of constitutional governance.

Section 8.8

Comparing Separated and Fused Powers

The American model of separated powers is one of two major models for organizing constitutional democratic government. The other is the parliamentary model, in which executive and legislative powers are largely fused rather than separated. Most of the world's established democracies use some variant of the parliamentary model. The United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, Germany, India, Sweden, Japan, and many others have parliamentary systems in which the head of government (typically the Prime Minister) is selected by and accountable to the elected legislature. A third model—the semi-presidential system—combines elements of both, with a separately elected President who exercises significant executive authority alongside a Prime Minister accountable to the parliament. France is the most prominent example.

How Parliamentary Systems Work

In a parliamentary system, voters elect a legislature, and the legislature chooses the head of government from among its own members. The Prime Minister is typically the leader of the party or coalition of parties that controls a majority of seats in Parliament. The cabinet—senior ministers who collectively run the executive branch—is drawn from Parliament and is collectively accountable to it.

The most important structural feature of parliamentary systems is the confidence relationship: the government (executive) can only remain in office as long as it holds the confidence of Parliament. If Parliament passes a vote of no confidence, the government must resign and either a new government is formed or new elections are called. This mechanism ensures ongoing executive accountability to the legislature in a direct and immediate way that the fixed-term presidency does not provide.

Because the Prime Minister and cabinet are drawn from and accountable to Parliament, the separation between executive and legislative power in parliamentary systems is much less than in presidential systems. This fusion of powers has important implications. First, it tends to make government more efficient in converting electoral mandates into enacted policy, because a government commanding a parliamentary majority can typically pass its legislative agenda without the obstruction that characterizes divided government in presidential systems. Second, it makes the executive more directly and immediately accountable to the legislature, because a Prime Minister who loses legislative confidence can be removed between elections—a mechanism far more readily available than impeachment in presidential systems.

When governments command large, stable parliamentary majorities, however, checking mechanisms within the legislature may be weak—a majority party with strong discipline can push through legislation with limited scrutiny. Coalition governments—common in multiparty parliamentary systems—face different challenges: they may be unstable, falling apart when coalition partners disagree, or they may give disproportionate power to small parties whose support is necessary to maintain the coalition.

Semi-Presidential Systems

The semi-presidential system, exemplified by France under the Fifth Republic (established 1958), attempts to combine the legitimacy of a separately elected President with the parliamentary accountability of a Prime Minister dependent on the assembly. In the French system, the President is elected separately by voters and holds significant executive powers, particularly in defense and foreign policy. The Prime Minister is responsible to the National Assembly and must command its confidence for the government to function. This creates the interesting constitutional possibility of "cohabitation"—when the President and Prime Minister come from different political parties because the President's party does not control the National Assembly, requiring executive power to be effectively shared between two political adversaries. Russia, Finland, Portugal, and many post-communist Eastern European countries have also adopted semi-presidential forms, each with variations in how power is divided.

Comparative Lens
Presidential Systems versus Parliamentary Systems

The two dominant models for organizing constitutional democratic government represent different solutions to the same problem: how to make the executive powerful enough to govern while keeping it accountable to elected representatives and ultimately to the people.

Parliamentary systems offer more immediate executive accountability and more effective policymaking, but at the cost of weaker formal protections when a government controls a large parliamentary majority. Neither system is universally superior. Different countries have adopted different models based on their historical experiences, social conditions, party systems, and constitutional values.

Institution in Practice
The U.S. Senate's Advice and Consent Role in Judicial Appointments

Few institutional practices better illustrate the complexity of checks and balances in daily operation than the U.S. Senate's power of advice and consent over presidential nominations to the federal judiciary.

When a vacancy arises on the Supreme Court, the Constitution requires the President to nominate a replacement who must then be confirmed by a majority vote of the Senate. The process typically begins with the President's selection of a nominee—consulting legal advisors, interest groups, and political allies to identify candidates whose legal philosophy and political reliability seem promising. The nominee's past legal writings, judicial decisions, and public statements are scrutinized intensively.

The nominee then appears before the Senate Judiciary Committee for confirmation hearings lasting several days. Senators question the nominee about judicial philosophy, views on precedent, approaches to constitutional interpretation, and positions on specific legal issues. Nominees typically decline to give specific answers about how they would rule on future cases—a practice sometimes called the "Ginsburg Rule" after comments by then-nominee Ruth Bader Ginsburg in 1993 about the impropriety of pre-committing to specific outcomes.

The political dynamics of this process have intensified dramatically in recent decades. The ideological composition of the Supreme Court now affects major questions of public policy—abortion rights, gun control, campaign finance, voting rights, and many others—making confirmation fights more consequential. Party-line voting on nominations has become the norm. The Senate's handling of several nominations has itself become a source of political controversy, including the Republican-led Senate's refusal in 2016 to hold hearings on President Obama's nominee Merrick Garland, and the subsequent elimination of the filibuster for Supreme Court nominations in 2017.

This institutional practice illustrates several key features of checks and balances: the appointment power links the executive and legislative branches in a formal constitutional relationship; the Senate exercises genuine checking authority over judicial composition, but that authority is constrained by the President's exclusive right of nomination; political parties shape how checking operates in practice; and the stakes of the process—shaping the judiciary for decades—have made this checking relationship an arena of intense political competition.

Primary Source Window
Federalist No. 51 — James Madison (1788)
Context

The Federalist Papers are a collection of eighty-five essays written in 1787–1788 by Alexander Hamilton, James Madison, and John Jay, published under the pseudonym "Publius," to persuade New York citizens to ratify the proposed Constitution. Federalist No. 51, written by James Madison, addresses how the proposed constitutional design would maintain the separation of powers in practice. It remains one of the most influential statements of constitutional theory in American political history.

Paraphrase of the Core Argument
Madison argued that the great security against a gradual concentration of powers in any single branch consists in giving those who administer each branch the necessary constitutional means and personal motives to resist encroachments from the other branches. Ambition must be made to counteract ambition. The interest of the person must be connected with the constitutional rights of the place. It may be a reflection on human nature that such devices should be necessary to control government. But what is government itself but the greatest reflection on human nature? If men were angels, no government would be necessary. If angels were to govern men, neither external nor internal controls on government would be necessary. The great difficulty in framing a government to be administered by men over men lies in this: you must first enable the government to control the governed, and in the next place oblige it to control itself.
Analysis

This passage reveals the foundational logic of constitutional checking. Madison does not assume that officials will be virtuous. He assumes they will be ambitious and self-interested, just like other human beings. The constitutional system is designed not to produce virtue but to harness self-interest in service of institutional balance. Each branch has incentives to resist encroachments from others—not primarily because its members are devoted to constitutional principle, but because they have institutional and personal interests in protecting their branch's power.

This logic is both practical and somewhat pessimistic about human nature. It reflects the Enlightenment conviction that good institutional design can produce good outcomes even from imperfect human material. The question of whether this design has succeeded, and under what conditions it may fail, remains one of the central questions of American constitutional experience.

  3. What does Madison's argument assume about the relationship between institutional interests and human behavior? Do you think these assumptions are accurate?
Chapter 8 — Civic Features

Rights, Debate, and Policy

Rights and Responsibilities: Separation of Powers and Civic Life

The separation of powers and the system of checks and balances are often understood as mechanisms operating within the government, among branches and officials. But they also have profound implications for individual citizens and civic life.

Rights protection. The most direct connection between separated powers and individual rights is through the judiciary's checking function. When the government violates a constitutional right—searches a home without a warrant, silences a speaker without constitutional justification, denies a person due process—the courts stand as an institution where that violation can be challenged and potentially remedied. The existence of an independent judiciary, insulated from direct political pressure and empowered to review government action, creates a legal avenue for rights vindication that would not exist in a system where the executive or legislature could simply override judicial decisions.

Civic participation. Separation of powers affects how citizens participate in political life. Because different institutions are elected at different times and by different constituencies, civic engagement is not limited to a single election. Citizens can pressure Congress, petition the executive, support litigation before the courts, contact local officials, or participate in rulemaking processes before administrative agencies. The distribution of power across multiple institutions creates multiple points of access for civic engagement.

Accountability and information. The checking relationships among branches generate information that citizens can use to hold government accountable. Congressional oversight hearings produce public records of government conduct. Judicial opinions explain how the law applies to executive and legislative actions. Inspector general reports reveal bureaucratic failures. The requirement that each branch justify its actions to other branches—and ultimately to the public—creates a transparency that concentrated power would not provide.

Civic responsibilities. The system of separated powers also places responsibilities on citizens. A system designed to prevent tyranny through institutional checks requires citizens who understand those institutions, participate in elections, hold officials accountable, and resist the temptation to support concentrated power in a leader they like simply because institutional constraints feel frustrating. Constitutional structures that lack civic support can erode. The long-term functioning of checks and balances depends not only on institutional design but on the civic culture in which institutions operate.

Civic Debate
Should the Senate Filibuster Be Eliminated?
The Issue

The Senate filibuster is a parliamentary procedure by which a senator can extend debate indefinitely, preventing a vote on a bill unless sixty of the one hundred senators vote to invoke cloture (end debate). Since it effectively requires sixty votes to pass most legislation, the filibuster gives the minority party the power to block legislation supported by a simple majority. The filibuster is not in the Constitution; it is a Senate procedural rule that has evolved over time. The question of whether to eliminate or further reform the filibuster is one of the most contested procedural debates in contemporary American politics.

Argument A — Preserve the Filibuster

Defenders of the filibuster argue that it is a critical check on majority tyranny and a safeguard for minority rights. In a system designed to require supermajority consensus for major changes, the filibuster plays a valuable role by forcing the majority to accommodate minority concerns before passing legislation. It slows down potentially hasty or poorly crafted legislation and forces deliberation and compromise. It protects smaller states and minority factions from being steamrolled by temporary national majorities. It reflects the Senate's traditional role as a deliberative body, distinct from the more majoritarian House.

Argument B — Eliminate the Filibuster

Critics argue that the filibuster has evolved into a routine tool of obstruction that is fundamentally inconsistent with democratic governance. The Constitution specifies supermajority requirements only in a few explicit instances (treaty ratification, veto override, impeachment conviction, constitutional amendment); in all other cases, majority rule is the constitutional default. The filibuster effectively rewrites this default, imposing a supermajority requirement not through democratic deliberation but through Senate procedural rules. Critics also argue that the filibuster has been used historically to obstruct civil rights legislation and other majority-supported reforms, and that it currently enables a minority to block policies most Americans support.

Tradeoffs

Those who favor preserving the filibuster value minority protection, deliberation, and supermajority consensus, at the cost of potential legislative paralysis. Those who favor elimination value majority accountability and governmental effectiveness, at the cost of reduced minority protection and potentially more volatile policy swings as Senate control changes hands. Some reformers propose modifications short of complete elimination—requiring senators to actually speak continuously to maintain a filibuster (a "talking filibuster"), or limiting the number of filibusters per session.

Civic Reasoning Question Given the constitutional design's emphasis on majority rule tempered by minority rights, does the Senate filibuster protect constitutional values or undermine them? What evidence would you need to make a well-reasoned argument either way?
Policy Lab
Constitutional Stress and Executive Emergency Powers
Scenario

A major public health emergency has rapidly spread across multiple states, straining hospital systems and requiring immediate governmental response. The President has issued an executive order requiring mask mandates in all federal buildings and directing federal agencies to coordinate vaccine distribution with states. However, the President has also directed federal agencies to withhold certain emergency funding from states that do not adopt specific public health measures—a condition not explicitly authorized in the relevant statutes. Several states have challenged these conditions as exceeding executive authority. Congress is deadlocked on emergency legislation because the majority party is divided internally over the appropriate federal response.

Stakeholders
  • The President and executive branch (seeking rapid, coordinated national response)
  • Congress (divided; some members want to act, others want to preserve state authority or limit executive power)
  • State governments (some cooperating with federal conditions, others challenging them in court)
  • Federal courts (asked to determine whether the conditions are legally authorized)
  • Public health officials (prioritizing effectiveness of response)
  • Civil liberties advocates (concerned about executive overreach beyond legal authority)
  • Citizens (needing clear information, predictable rules, and effective governmental response)
Policy Options
  1. Allow the executive to act unilaterally on all emergency measures, deferring to executive expertise and urgency, with judicial review to follow.
  2. Require Congress to pass emergency legislation authorizing specific presidential powers before the executive acts, slowing the response but ensuring democratic authorization.
  3. Allow the executive to act within clearly established statutory authority while requiring judicial review of contested conditions before they take effect.
  4. Create a joint executive-congressional emergency committee with rapid decision-making authority, blending branches' expertise and accountability.
  5. Return the matter entirely to state governments, limiting the federal role to coordination and voluntary assistance.
Evidence Needed

What statutory authority exists for the executive's conditions? What has past emergency response practice established? What are the health implications of delay? What are the constitutional constraints on spending conditions? What do the courts say about similar conditions in previous cases?

Tradeoffs

Rapid unilateral executive action may be more effective but creates precedents for expanded executive authority. Requiring prior congressional authorization ensures democratic accountability but may slow responses to unacceptable levels. Court review provides legality checks but creates uncertainty during a crisis. Returning matters to states preserves federalism but may produce fragmented and uncoordinated responses.

Chapter 8 — Back Matter

Common Misunderstandings

Misunderstanding 1
"The three branches of government are completely separate and never overlap in their functions."
Correction: Separation of powers divides primary responsibilities among branches but does not create hermetically sealed institutional silos. The President vetoes legislation (an executive actor influencing the legislative function), the Senate confirms judicial nominees (a legislative body shaping the judiciary), and courts strike down laws (a judicial body limiting legislative action). These overlaps are features of the design. Checks and balances require that each branch have tools that reach into the functions of the others.
Misunderstanding 2
"Checks and balances mean that no single branch can ever act decisively."
Correction: Checks and balances constrain unilateral action and require consensus for major changes, but branches can and do act decisively within their defined authorities. The executive can respond to military crises, diplomatic events, and administrative matters without legislative approval for each action. Courts issue rulings that immediately affect parties before them. Congress can pass emergency legislation quickly when there is political will to do so. The system slows some types of action while leaving other types relatively unconstrained.
Misunderstanding 3
"The Senate filibuster is required by the Constitution."
Correction: The filibuster is a Senate procedural rule, not a constitutional requirement. The Constitution specifies supermajority requirements only in specific, limited situations (treaty ratification, veto override, conviction in impeachment trials, constitutional amendments, and a few others). In all other cases, the Constitution's default is majority rule. The filibuster has evolved through Senate practice and can be modified or eliminated by a simple majority vote of senators.
Misunderstanding 4
"Judicial review means courts can simply overrule anything Congress or the President does."
Correction: Judicial review allows courts to strike down governmental actions that are unconstitutional, but courts have limited tools to enforce their rulings and are constrained in several ways. Courts can only act on cases brought before them. Courts generally defer to Congress and the executive on questions of policy rather than constitutionality. The political branches have tools to respond to judicial rulings, including appointing new judges over time, passing revised legislation, and amending the Constitution. Judicial review is a powerful but institutionally constrained check, not an absolute power.
Misunderstanding 5
"Parliamentary systems have no checks on executive power."
Correction: Parliamentary systems check executive power differently from presidential systems but no less significantly. The confidence mechanism allows Parliament to remove a government that loses its support. Parliamentary committees conduct oversight of executive departments. Opposition parties challenge government actions through formal procedures including question time, censure motions, and committee hearings. Independent courts and ombudspersons review executive conduct. The key difference is that checking in parliamentary systems operates more through political accountability within and around Parliament than through formal inter-institutional constitutional mechanisms.
Misunderstanding 6
"Impeachment is the same as removing a President from office."
Correction: Impeachment is the process by which the House of Representatives formally charges the President or other official with high crimes and misdemeanors. Impeachment is not removal: it is the equivalent of an indictment in criminal law. Removal requires conviction by the Senate, which in the case of a President requires a two-thirds vote. Three American presidents have been impeached by the House; none were convicted and removed by the Senate.
Misunderstanding 7
"Separation of powers automatically prevents dictatorship."
Correction: Formal constitutional separation of powers is a significant obstacle to authoritarian consolidation of power, but it is not a guarantee. Democratic backsliding can occur even in systems with separated powers if political norms erode, if one party captures multiple institutions simultaneously, or if checks are politicized to the point of dysfunction. Constitutional structures require civic support, institutional norms, and political culture to function as designed. The history of the twentieth and twenty-first centuries includes examples of democracies with formal constitutional structures that nonetheless experienced democratic erosion or collapse.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 8.1Separation of powers developed from centuries of political philosophy and experience warning against concentrated authority. Montesquieu's framework, arguing that legislative, executive, and judicial functions must be exercised by separate institutions, profoundly influenced American constitutional design. The framers, particularly Madison, built a system that forced institutional ambition to counteract ambition rather than relying on the virtue of individual officeholders.
  • Sec 8.2Legislative power is the authority to make law, vested primarily in Congress. Most constitutional democracies organize their legislatures into either bicameral (two-chamber) or unicameral (single-chamber) forms. Legislatures are constrained by the executive veto, judicial review, and constitutional limits. They also exercise checking power through oversight, appropriations, and the confirmation of executive appointments.
  • Sec 8.3Executive power is the authority to implement, administer, and enforce the law. The American presidency has expanded substantially through history, reflecting the demands of modern governance and national crises. The executive branch includes not just the President but a vast network of agencies, departments, and professional administrators. Executive power is checked by the legislature through appropriations and oversight, and by the courts through judicial review of executive actions.
  • Sec 8.4Judicial power is the authority to interpret law and apply it to specific cases. Courts perform their most significant checking function through judicial review—striking down legislative and executive actions found to violate the constitution. This power was established in Marbury v. Madison (1803) and raises enduring questions about democratic legitimacy. The independence of the judiciary, protected by life tenure and salary guarantees, is considered essential for impartial legal interpretation.
  • Sec 8.5Checks and balances create a web of institutional interdependencies through which each branch can limit the others. Key mechanisms include the presidential veto and congressional override, the Senate's advice and consent power over appointments and treaties, the appropriations power of Congress, the impeachment and trial process, and judicial review of both legislative and executive actions. These overlapping powers force each branch into ongoing relationship with the others.
  • Sec 8.6Separation of powers in practice differs from separation of powers as an abstract concept. The rise of political parties has profoundly affected how checking operates—unified party control weakens inter-branch checking, while divided government intensifies it. The executive branch has expanded significantly over American history. Congressional tools like appropriations, reporting requirements, and the War Powers Resolution attempt to constrain executive expansion. Judicial independence, while formally protected, operates in a political environment shaped by the appointment process.
  • Sec 8.7The advantages of separated powers include protection of individual rights, prevention of tyranny, and promotion of deliberation and policy quality. The disadvantages include gridlock and governmental paralysis, diffuse accountability that makes it hard to assign responsibility for policy failures, unequal veto power that can allow minorities to block majority preferences, and limitations on crisis response. Whether these costs are worth the benefits is a question that has no universally agreed answer.
  • Sec 8.8The American model of separated powers is one of two major constitutional models. Parliamentary systems fuse executive and legislative power, with the Prime Minister and cabinet drawn from and accountable to the legislature through the confidence relationship. Semi-presidential systems combine a separately elected President with a legislature-accountable Prime Minister. Parliamentary systems tend toward more effective policymaking and more immediate executive accountability; presidential systems tend toward stronger formal checks but more potential for gridlock. Neither model is universally superior.

Key Terms

separation of powers
The constitutional principle that governmental authority should be divided among distinct legislative, executive, and judicial branches, each with its own primary functions.
checks and balances
The system of institutional rules and powers by which each branch of government can constrain, monitor, and in some circumstances override the actions of the other branches.
legislative power
The authority to make, modify, and repeal laws; constitutionally vested primarily in Congress in the American system.
executive power
The authority to implement, administer, and enforce the law; constitutionally vested in the President in the American system.
judicial power
The authority to interpret law and apply it to specific cases and controversies; constitutionally vested in the federal courts.
bicameralism
The organization of a legislature into two separate chambers—upper and lower—each with different membership, rules, and roles. The U.S. Congress has the Senate and the House of Representatives.
unicameralism
The organization of a legislature into a single chamber without an upper house. Examples include New Zealand, Sweden, and Denmark.
veto
The executive's power to refuse to sign legislation passed by the legislature, returning it to Congress with objections; in the American system, can be overridden by a two-thirds vote in both chambers.
override
The legislative power to enact legislation despite an executive veto; in the American system, requires two-thirds of both the Senate and the House of Representatives.
judicial review
The power of courts to strike down legislative and executive actions found to be inconsistent with the constitution. Established in the United States in Marbury v. Madison (1803).
advice and consent
The constitutional power of the Senate to confirm or reject presidential nominations to the federal judiciary, cabinet positions, and other high offices, and to ratify or reject treaties.
impeachment
The formal process by which the House of Representatives charges the President or other civil officer with high crimes and misdemeanors. Separate from removal, which requires conviction by a two-thirds Senate vote.
appropriations
The legislative power to authorize governmental spending. No money may be legally spent from the Treasury without a congressional appropriation.
executive privilege
The President's claimed authority to withhold certain confidential communications from other branches. Recognized by the Supreme Court in United States v. Nixon as a limited, not absolute, protection.
fused powers
A constitutional arrangement in which executive and legislative powers are combined in a single institution or closely linked, as in parliamentary systems where the Prime Minister and cabinet are drawn from and accountable to the legislature.
parliamentary system
A form of constitutional government in which the head of government (Prime Minister) is selected by and accountable to the legislature; the government must maintain the legislature's confidence to remain in office.
presidential system
A form of constitutional government in which the executive (President) is separately elected and serves a fixed term, independent of legislative confidence. Associated with the United States, Brazil, and Mexico.
semi-presidential system
A form of constitutional government combining a separately elected President with significant executive powers and a Prime Minister accountable to the legislature. France is the primary example.
divided government
A condition in which different political parties control different branches of government—most commonly, the presidency and one or both chambers of Congress.
oversight
The legislature's authority to investigate, monitor, and evaluate the implementation of laws and the conduct of executive branch departments and agencies.
independent judiciary
A judiciary whose members are insulated from direct political pressure, typically through life tenure or long fixed terms, salary protection, and limited grounds for removal.
constitutional gridlock
A condition in which institutional conflict among separated branches prevents action on major policy issues despite public demand or pressing need.
pocket veto
A form of presidential veto in which the President fails to sign a bill within ten days while Congress is adjourned, preventing the bill from becoming law without an active veto that Congress could override.
cloture
A Senate procedure requiring sixty votes to end a filibuster and bring a bill to a vote; effectively imposes a supermajority requirement for most Senate legislation.
filibuster
A Senate procedure by which a senator can extend debate indefinitely, preventing a vote on a bill; can be ended only by a cloture vote of sixty senators. It is a Senate rule, not a constitutional requirement.
confidence relationship
In parliamentary systems, the requirement that the government (Prime Minister and cabinet) maintain the support of a legislative majority to remain in office. The legislature can remove the government through a vote of no confidence.
cohabitation
In semi-presidential systems, the condition when the President and the Prime Minister come from different political parties, requiring executive power to be shared between political adversaries. Experienced in France during 1986–1988, 1993–1995, and 1997–2002.

Document Analysis Activity

Document Analysis
Article I, Section 7 and Article II, Section 2 — U.S. Constitution (1788)

Document type: Constitutional text — the foundational legal document of American government, drafted by the Philadelphia Convention in 1787 and ratified by the required nine states in 1788.

Historical and political context: The framers were designing a new governmental system after the inadequacies of the Articles of Confederation. They drew on Enlightenment political philosophy, English constitutional tradition, and their experience with state governments to create a system of divided powers and institutional checks. The specific provisions below encode the veto-override relationship and the appointments and treaty powers that are central to the chapter's analysis.

Key Provisions

Article I, Section 7 establishes the legislative process, including the requirement that revenue bills originate in the House, the process for presenting bills to the President, and the procedure for a presidential veto and congressional override. Every bill which shall have passed the House of Representatives and the Senate must, before becoming law, be presented to the President. If the President objects, the bill is returned to the originating chamber with objections, and if two-thirds of both chambers vote to pass it, it becomes law notwithstanding the President's objections.

Article II, Section 2 defines the President's executive powers, including the role of Commander in Chief of the armed forces, the authority to make treaties with Senate consent (two-thirds of senators concurring), and to nominate—and by and with the Advice and Consent of the Senate, appoint—judges of the Supreme Court, ambassadors, and other officers of the United States.

Questions for Analysis
  1. Article I, Section 7 requires that every bill be "presented to the President of the United States" before becoming law. Why does the constitutional text require presentation to the President? What checking function does this create, and what is the consequence of the veto procedure for the balance of power between Congress and the executive?
  2. Article II, Section 2 gives the President power to make treaties "by and with the Advice and Consent of the Senate... provided two thirds of the Senators present concur." Why does the Constitution require a two-thirds Senate majority for treaties when ordinary legislation requires only a simple majority? What does this tell you about the framers' views on the significance of international commitments?
  3. How do these two constitutional provisions work together to illustrate the broader principle of checks and balances? What ongoing relationship between the executive and legislative branches do they create?
  4. Article II, Section 2 also gives the President power to nominate judges "by and with the Advice and Consent of the Senate." In light of the chapter's discussion of judicial independence, why might the framers have required Senate consent for judicial appointments rather than allowing the President to appoint judges unilaterally? What risks does Senate consent both reduce and create?

Connection to the chapter: These constitutional provisions are the textual foundation for the veto, override, treaty ratification, appointments, and advice and consent mechanisms discussed throughout this chapter. Analyzing them helps students see how the abstract principles of checks and balances are operationalized in specific constitutional text—and how the choices made in Philadelphia in 1787 continue to structure American political conflict today.

Research Project
Comparative Constitutional Analysis: How Two Countries Divide and Check Power

Choose two countries from different constitutional traditions—for example, one presidential system (United States, Brazil, Mexico, or South Korea) and one parliamentary system (United Kingdom, Germany, Canada, India, or Japan)—and conduct a comparative analysis of how each country divides governmental power and uses institutional checks and balances.

Research Questions to Address

Constitutional structure: What are the major branches of government in each country? How are they organized? What are their primary constitutional powers?

Checking mechanisms: What formal constitutional mechanisms allow each branch to check the others? Which mechanisms are most significant in practice?

Executive-legislative relationship: How are the executive and legislature related? What happens when they disagree? Can one remove the other from office?

Judicial review: Do courts in each country have the power of judicial review? How is it exercised? What constraints exist on it?

Party system effects: How does the party system in each country affect how constitutional checking mechanisms operate in practice?

Comparison of tradeoffs: Based on your research, what tradeoffs does each constitutional design involve? Which system appears more effective at preventing governmental overreach? Which appears more effective at enabling decisive policy action?

Deliverable Options

Your final product might take the form of a comparative essay, a structured comparative chart accompanied by an analytical narrative, a presentation to the class, or a policy brief recommending elements of constitutional design for a hypothetical new democracy. Cite your sources using appropriate academic conventions, and draw explicitly on the concepts introduced in this chapter.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 9: Federalism, Unitary Systems, and Confederations

Chapter 8 examined how power is divided horizontally—among legislative, executive, and judicial branches operating at the same level of government. Chapter 9 turns to a different kind of power division: vertical, or territorial. Federal systems like the United States divide power between a national government and state or provincial governments. Unitary systems concentrate authority at the national level while allowing some administrative decentralization. Confederal arrangements distribute authority primarily to member units while reserving limited powers to a central body.

The questions you have developed in this chapter—about how to balance governmental effectiveness with accountability, how to prevent concentration of authority, how to represent diverse interests within a single constitutional system—all carry directly into the study of federalism. Chapter 9 will show you why territorial distribution of power creates its own checking mechanisms, its own tradeoffs, and its own constitutional conflicts, many of which mirror the horizontal conflicts between branches you have studied here.

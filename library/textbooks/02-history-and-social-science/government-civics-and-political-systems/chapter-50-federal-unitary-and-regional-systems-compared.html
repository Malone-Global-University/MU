Chapter 50 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

When the citizens of a country go to vote, they vote for officials at multiple levels — national legislators, regional governors or premiers, city council members, school board representatives. The layers of government that receive these votes are not accidental. They reflect some of the most consequential constitutional choices any political community can make: Where should authority reside? Which decisions should be made nationally, and which should be left to regions, provinces, states, or localities? How much variation across territory should a political system permit, and how much uniformity does national identity or administrative efficiency require? These questions do not have a single correct answer, and different political communities have answered them differently, producing an extraordinary variety of territorial arrangements across the world's political systems.

The study of how governments distribute authority across territory is one of the most practically important dimensions of comparative government. It shapes not only who makes which decisions, but how effectively those decisions are implemented, how well the government responds to local needs, how fairly it distributes resources, and how capable it is of managing diversity and conflict within its borders. A country that has chosen to centralize most authority in a national government operates very differently from one that has divided sovereignty between a national government and constitutionally protected states or provinces. A country that allows regions to legislate in different languages and maintain distinct legal traditions creates a different kind of public life than one that insists on uniform national standards from its capital.

This chapter examines the major types of territorial organization in comparative perspective. It introduces the concepts that political scientists use to describe and compare territorial arrangements — federalism, unitarism, devolution, regional autonomy, home rule, asymmetrical decentralization — and explains what each means, how each works in practice, and what tradeoffs each involves. The chapter draws on examples from countries as diverse as the United States, Canada, Germany, Australia, India, Brazil, Switzerland, the United Kingdom, France, Japan, Spain, Belgium, South Africa, Nigeria, Indonesia, and China. Each of these cases illustrates a different approach to the challenge of governing complex, diverse, and geographically extended political communities.

One of the central insights of this chapter is that territorial systems exist on a continuum rather than falling into neat categories. The real world of territorial government is more fluid, overlapping, and hybrid than any simple typology can capture. Many countries that are formally unitary have devolved substantial authority to regions. Many federal countries have, in practice, developed significant national dominance over subnational governments. The important analytical task is not to assign a country to a category, but to understand how its specific territorial arrangements shape political life in practice — who makes which decisions, at which level, through which processes, with what kinds of accountability, and with what consequences for citizens.

The chapter also addresses the relationship between territorial organization and social diversity. Many countries have designed their territorial systems with explicit attention to ethnic, linguistic, religious, or regional differences within their populations. Federal arrangements in countries like Canada, Switzerland, Belgium, and India reflect, in part, deliberate efforts to accommodate linguistic and cultural communities within a shared political framework. Understanding how territorial design addresses — or fails to address — social diversity is essential for any serious comparative study of government.

Students who complete this chapter will be equipped to analyze territorial arrangements systematically, compare federal and unitary systems on multiple dimensions, understand devolution and regional autonomy as intermediate arrangements, explain the fiscal and administrative dimensions of territorial government, and evaluate the tradeoffs involved in different territorial designs. This knowledge will serve as the foundation for Chapter 51, which applies comparative analysis to specific country case studies using a consistent template.

Section 50.1

Comparing Territorial Systems

Every constitutional system must make a fundamental choice about where authority resides. This choice operates along what political scientists call the vertical dimension of government — the distribution of power not across branches (legislative, executive, judicial) but across levels: national, regional, provincial, state, and local. The horizontal distribution of power, examined in earlier chapters, concerns separation of powers within a single level of government. The vertical distribution concerns how authority is shared, divided, or concentrated among multiple levels simultaneously.

The vertical distribution of governmental authority is not simply an administrative detail. It shapes everything from the uniformity of public services to the vibrancy of local democracy, from the efficiency of policy implementation to the capacity of national governments to manage crises. Countries that have concentrated authority at the national level experience different patterns of policy-making, political participation, and intergovernmental conflict than countries that have distributed authority across multiple constitutionally protected levels. Understanding these differences requires clear conceptual tools.

The most basic distinction is between centralization and decentralization. A centralized system concentrates decision-making authority at the national or central level. A decentralized system distributes authority — either constitutionally or administratively — to regional or local levels. But these terms describe endpoints on a continuum rather than categories into which every country neatly falls. In reality, virtually all modern political systems are mixed: they combine elements of centralization for some functions with elements of decentralization for others.

A crucial distinction within decentralization itself separates administrative decentralization from political decentralization. Administrative decentralization involves transferring the implementation of policies — the practical work of service delivery — to regional or local offices of the central government. These offices carry out national policy under national direction; they do not have independent authority to make law or set policy. Political decentralization, by contrast, transfers actual decision-making authority — the power to legislate, tax, and govern — to sub-national political bodies that have their own democratic mandate, their own sources of legitimacy, and their own constitutionally protected spheres of competence. This distinction matters enormously. A country that has administratively decentralized its education bureaucracy into regional offices is very different from one that has constitutionally empowered state governments to design their own education systems.

Another important concept is subsidiarity — the principle that decisions should be made at the lowest level of government capable of making them effectively. Subsidiarity is an organizing principle in the European Union, where it is used to allocate authority between the EU and member states, and it appears in the constitutional traditions of several federal countries. It reflects the intuition that local decisions made by people close to an issue are often better than distant decisions made by remote national authorities — but it also recognizes that some problems genuinely require national or supranational responses.

Territorial systems can also be categorized by the constitutional status of subnational governments. In some systems, regional and local governments exist at the pleasure of the national government — they may be created, reorganized, or abolished by national legislation. In other systems, subnational governments have constitutionally entrenched status: they cannot be abolished or stripped of their core powers by the national government acting alone. This distinction between constitutionally protected and constitutionally unprotected subnational authority is one of the most important differences between federal and unitary systems, though even here the picture is more complex than a simple binary suggests.

Political scientists have developed several concepts for describing territorial systems. The three most common are federalism (systems in which authority is constitutionally divided between national and subnational governments, both of which possess independent democratic legitimacy), unitarism (systems in which all authority is constitutionally concentrated in the national government, which may delegate authority to local units but retains the right to reclaim it), and devolution (the transfer of significant governing authority from a central government to regional or subnational governments, typically by statute rather than by constitutional entrenchment). These categories are useful starting points, but students should treat them as tools for analysis rather than rigid classifications. As subsequent sections will show, real territorial systems often blend characteristics associated with multiple categories.

Civic Vocabulary
Federalism

Federalism is a system of government in which authority is constitutionally divided between a national government and subnational governments (such as states, provinces, or cantons), each of which exercises independent authority within its own sphere. In a true federal system, both levels of government have their own democratic mandates, their own constitutional powers, and their own direct relationships with citizens. Neither level is legally subordinate to the other within its domain of authority. This distinguishes federalism from a unitary system, in which subnational governments derive their powers from the national government, and from a confederation, in which the national government derives its powers from the member states or units.

Section 50.2

Federal Countries

Federal systems are defined by the constitutional division of authority between a national government and a set of constituent subnational units — states, provinces, Länder, cantons, or similarly named entities — each of which possesses its own democratic legitimacy, its own sphere of competence, and constitutionally protected powers that the national government cannot unilaterally eliminate. This constitutional protection distinguishes federalism from mere administrative decentralization: in a federal system, the subnational units are not creatures of the national government that can be reorganized or abolished at will; they are autonomous political entities with guaranteed constitutional standing.

The allocation of powers in federal systems takes several characteristic forms. Reserved powers (sometimes called exclusive powers) are those assigned specifically to either the national or the subnational governments and not shared. In the United States, the Tenth Amendment reserves to the states or to the people all powers not delegated to the federal government by the Constitution. In practice, this has generated centuries of dispute about which powers are truly reserved and which are implied by broader national grants. Concurrent powers are those that both levels of government may exercise simultaneously — taxation is a classic example in virtually every federal system. Delegated powers are those specifically granted to the national government, typically enumerated in the constitution. The interplay among these categories is the central drama of intergovernmental relations in federal systems.

The United States, the world's oldest surviving federal system, established constitutional federalism in 1787 with a design that has evolved dramatically through constitutional amendment, judicial interpretation, and political practice. The original Constitution delegated specific powers to Congress — regulating commerce, coining money, declaring war — while reserving other matters to states. Over more than two centuries, the scope of federal power has expanded enormously, particularly through broad interpretations of the Commerce Clause and the Spending Power. American federalism today is characterized by extensive intergovernmental cooperation, joint programs (cooperative federalism), federal grants-in-aid to states, and persistent tensions between national standards and state autonomy across issues including education, health care, environmental regulation, and criminal justice.

Canada's federal system, established in 1867, adopted a design that deliberately inverted the American logic in some respects: while the American founders had sought to limit national power in favor of states, Canada's founders — influenced by concerns about the fragility of the American Union demonstrated by the Civil War — created a system in which the national government held stronger residual powers. Yet Canadian federalism has evolved in the direction of what scholars call "province-building": the provinces, particularly Quebec, Ontario, and Alberta, have asserted increasingly robust claims to autonomy. Canada's federalism is today among the most decentralized in the world on many measures, with provinces controlling health care, education, social policy, and natural resources in ways that produce significant variation in public services across the country. The asymmetry created by Quebec's distinctive status as a French-speaking civil law society within an otherwise English-speaking common law federation represents one of the most complex applications of federal principles anywhere.

Germany's federal system, established in the Basic Law of 1949, represents a distinctive model often called cooperative federalism or "administrative federalism." The German federal government (Bund) holds most legislative authority, including in areas like criminal law and commercial regulation. The states (Länder), however, hold primary responsibility for administering federal law — a division between law-making and law-implementing that differs significantly from the American model, where states make and administer their own laws. The German Bundesrat, the upper house of parliament, represents state governments in national legislation and must approve legislation that affects state competences, creating formal institutional integration between the two levels. German federalism is generally regarded as more cooperative and less conflictual than American federalism, in part because its constitutional design creates incentives for collaboration rather than competition.

Australia's federal system, established in 1901, features a constitutionally strong national government (Commonwealth) and six states. Australian federalism has been characterized by significant fiscal centralization: the Commonwealth collects the vast majority of tax revenue and redistributes it to states through grants, giving the national government substantial leverage over state policy even in areas of formal state responsibility. This pattern — sometimes called "vertical fiscal imbalance" — means that the practical distribution of power in Australian federalism is more centralized than the formal constitutional text suggests.

India's federal system, established by the Constitution of 1950, is one of the most complex in the world. It features a strong national government (Union) with an unusually broad set of emergency powers that can temporarily subordinate state governments to Union authority — a provision that has been used, controversially, dozens of times. India's constitution includes three lists allocating subjects between the Union, states, and both simultaneously. Indian federalism accommodates extraordinary diversity: 29 states and numerous union territories, with populations speaking hundreds of languages and practicing diverse religious traditions. The linguistic reorganization of states in the 1950s — redrawing state boundaries to correspond more closely to linguistic communities — represented a distinctive approach to accommodating territorial identity that has had lasting consequences for Indian politics.

Brazil and Mexico, the two largest federal systems in Latin America, both adopted federal constitutions in the nineteenth century, partly inspired by the American model. Brazilian federalism features 26 states and a federal district, with the 1988 constitution significantly strengthening state and municipal authority after decades of military centralization. Mexican federalism, formally established in 1917, was for most of the twentieth century overshadowed by the dominance of a single party (the PRI), which controlled national, state, and local governments simultaneously, creating de facto centralization within a formally federal structure. Democratic transition beginning in the 1990s has produced more genuine federalism in Mexico, with opposition parties governing many states and a more contested intergovernmental relationship.

Switzerland's federal system — one of the oldest in the world, with roots in the medieval confederation of Swiss cantons — features 26 cantons with substantial autonomy, their own constitutions, and significant fiscal independence. Swiss federalism is notable for the extent to which it accommodates four linguistic communities (German, French, Italian, and Romansh) within a single federal framework, making it one of the most successful examples of linguistic federalism anywhere. Switzerland's federal design is also notable for its emphasis on direct democracy: referendums and initiatives operate at both the cantonal and federal levels, giving citizens unusual direct participation in decisions at multiple governmental tiers.

How the System Works
Fiscal Federalism: Revenue, Transfers, and Intergovernmental Finance

One of the most consequential dimensions of any federal system is fiscal: how governments at different levels raise money, how revenue is allocated among them, and how transfers flow from richer to poorer units. Understanding fiscal federalism is essential because it often determines the real distribution of power regardless of formal constitutional arrangements.

  • Step 1Revenue Assignment: Federal systems must decide which governments have the authority to levy which taxes. Some taxes are assigned exclusively to the national government (customs duties in the US); others to subnational governments; many are concurrent. The pattern of revenue assignment profoundly shapes fiscal capacity at each level.
  • Step 2Expenditure Assignment: Similarly, systems must decide which level of government is responsible for which expenditures. Some functions — defense, monetary policy — are assigned to the national government everywhere. Others — education, local infrastructure — are often subnational. When revenue and expenditure assignments are misaligned, a vertical fiscal imbalance results.
  • Step 3Vertical Fiscal Imbalance: In many federal systems, the national government collects more revenue than it spends directly, while subnational governments spend more than they collect. This imbalance is addressed through intergovernmental transfers — grants, revenue-sharing, and equalization payments flowing from the national to subnational governments.
  • Step 4Equalization Transfers: Because some regions are richer than others, federal systems often include equalization mechanisms designed to ensure that all subnational governments can provide comparable levels of public services regardless of their tax base. Canada's equalization program, Germany's Länderfinanzausgleich, and Australia's Commonwealth Grants Commission are well-known examples.
  • Step 5Conditional Grants: National governments often attach conditions to grants they provide to subnational governments, requiring them to meet national standards as a condition of receiving funding. In the United States, federal grants for education, health care, and transportation typically carry such conditions, giving the national government significant policy leverage even in areas of formal state authority.
  • Step 6Fiscal Autonomy vs. Fiscal Dependence: The degree to which subnational governments can set their own tax rates and determine their own spending priorities is a major dimension of real federal autonomy. A state that depends on national transfers for most of its revenue has less real autonomy than one that can finance its activities independently — regardless of what the formal constitution says.

Fiscal federalism is thus a key mechanism through which the real balance of power in territorial systems is shaped. Constitutional text alone rarely tells the full story of where power actually resides in a territorial system.

Section 50.3

Unitary Countries

In a unitary system, constitutional sovereignty is concentrated in the national government. Regional and local governments exist and may exercise substantial authority, but they do so at the sufferance of the national government, which created them by statute and can in principle restructure, reorganize, or abolish them. There is no constitutionally entrenched division of sovereignty between national and subnational levels; all legitimate public authority flows, in principle, from the national center.

This formal description may suggest that unitary systems are highly centralized and that local governments in such systems are mere administrative extensions of the national capital. In practice, many unitary systems are significantly decentralized in their actual operations, with substantial authority delegated to regional and local governments that are active, democratically elected, and capable of making meaningful policy decisions. The key difference from a federal system is not necessarily how much authority local governments exercise in practice, but rather the constitutional source and the constitutional security of that authority. What a national parliament has granted through statute, it can take back; what a federal constitution has entrenched, it generally cannot.

France is the paradigmatic example of a unitary state, with a tradition of administrative centralization deeply rooted in the Napoleonic era. France is organized into regions, departments, and communes, all of which are governed in part by elected officials — but the national government has historically exercised strong oversight through the institution of the prefect, a national official stationed in each department to represent the central government and supervise local administration. Reforms beginning in 1982 under President François Mitterrand transferred significant authority from prefects to elected regional and departmental governments, a process called decentralization (décentralisation in French). France today is considerably less centralized in practice than it was in the Napoleonic period, though it remains formally unitary. The 26 regions of metropolitan and overseas France have elected councils with powers over economic development, education infrastructure, and regional planning, and they have independent budgets financed by grants and local taxes. Yet no region has the constitutional standing of a German Land or an American state.

Japan's unitary system features 47 prefectures governed by directly elected governors and prefectural assemblies, along with thousands of municipalities. Japanese local governments are responsible for delivering a wide range of public services — education, public health, social welfare, infrastructure — and they employ a substantial share of Japan's public sector workforce. Yet Japanese prefectures derive their authority from national legislation, not from a division of constitutional sovereignty. The national government can and does reshape the framework within which prefectural and municipal governments operate through legislation. Fiscal centralization is also significant: Japanese local governments depend heavily on transfers from the national government for much of their revenue.

New Zealand offers an interesting case of a small, highly centralized unitary state that has undergone significant administrative reform. With a population of around five million concentrated in a compact island nation, New Zealand has historically been one of the most centralized among developed democracies. Most public services are delivered by national agencies or their regional offices, with relatively limited genuine local autonomy compared to larger countries. New Zealand's experience illustrates how geography and size affect the appropriate design of territorial systems: a small, relatively homogeneous country may be able to deliver effective, uniform services through a centralized system in ways that would be impossible in a large, diverse country.

The Scandinavian countries — Norway, Sweden, Denmark, and Finland — present a particularly interesting model of unitary states with strong local governments. In these countries, municipalities and (in Sweden and Norway) counties or regions exercise substantial authority over services including primary health care, schools, social services, and local infrastructure. Local governments in Sweden raise their own income taxes, giving them genuine fiscal autonomy that many formally federal subnational governments envy. The Scandinavian model suggests that a unitary constitutional framework is entirely compatible with effective, democratically accountable, and genuinely autonomous local government — what matters is political culture, institutional design, and the practical distribution of authority, not merely the formal constitutional label.

South Korea's unitary system, while retaining formal constitutional supremacy at the national level, has undergone significant decentralization reforms since democratization in the late 1980s. Provinces and metropolitan cities are governed by directly elected governors and mayors who have expanded competencies in economic development, urban planning, and social services. The central government still dominates fiscal policy and sets national standards for education and other key services, but local governance has become more politically competitive and locally responsive than it was under the authoritarian period when central government control was pervasive.

The practical functioning of unitary systems depends heavily on how the national government uses its constitutional supremacy. A national government that routinely overrides local decisions, controls all major appointments, and allows no fiscal independence creates a very different local political environment than one that delegates broad authority, respects local discretion, and provides stable financing. The formal constitutional structure of unitarism sets the legal framework, but political culture, party systems, and administrative tradition determine how that framework is actually used.

Civic Vocabulary
Decentralization

Decentralization refers to the transfer of authority, responsibility, or resources from a central government to regional or local governments or organizations. Political scientists distinguish between administrative decentralization (moving the implementation of national policy closer to the people it affects, without transferring decision-making authority) and political decentralization (transferring actual governing authority — the power to legislate, tax, and make binding decisions — to subnational governments). A country can be administratively decentralized while remaining politically centralized. True political decentralization, particularly when combined with fiscal decentralization (giving subnational governments their own revenue sources), significantly reshapes the distribution of power in a territorial system.

Section 50.4

Devolution and Regional Autonomy

Between strict federalism and strict unitarism lies a range of intermediate arrangements through which formally unitary states — and some formally federal ones — grant significant governing authority to particular regions without fully converting to a federal constitutional design. The most important of these intermediate arrangements is devolution: the process by which a central government transfers legislative, executive, or administrative powers to regional governments through ordinary legislation rather than through constitutional entrenchment.

Devolution differs from federalism in a legally crucial way: devolved powers are delegated, not constitutionally guaranteed. A parliament that has devolved powers to a regional body can, in principle, reclaim those powers by passing new legislation. This legal reality does not necessarily mean that devolved arrangements are fragile — political realities may make recentralization practically impossible even when it is legally possible — but it does mean that devolved regional governments lack the constitutional armor that characterizes genuinely federal subnational governments.

The United Kingdom provides the most extensively studied contemporary example of devolution in a traditionally unitary state. For most of its modern history, the United Kingdom was governed from Westminster with very limited regional autonomy — though Scotland had retained its own legal system, church, and educational tradition since the Acts of Union in 1707. Beginning in 1998 under the Blair government, the UK devolved significant powers to elected parliaments and assemblies in Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland. The Scottish Parliament received the most extensive powers, including control over health, education, justice, housing, and some taxation — a degree of autonomy that approaches federal status in practice even though it remains legally revocable by Westminster. The Welsh Senedd and Northern Ireland Assembly have different, generally more limited, powers reflecting different political circumstances and different histories.

This asymmetrical pattern — in which different regions within the same country possess different levels of authority — is one of the most distinctive features of the UK's devolved arrangements. Scotland has more powers than Wales; Northern Ireland's arrangement is shaped by the unique circumstances of its peace process. The result is what political scientists call asymmetrical devolution: a territorial system in which regions are not treated uniformly but have different constitutional relationships with the center depending on historical, cultural, and political factors.

Spain's system of Autonomous Communities (comunidades autónomas) represents another major example of devolution in a formally unitary state, though Spain's arrangements are so extensive that many political scientists treat Spain as a quasi-federal system in practice. Created by the 1978 Constitution after the end of Franco's centralist dictatorship, Spain's 17 Autonomous Communities enjoy substantial self-governing powers, including in education, health, policing (in some cases), and economic development. The original Spanish design envisioned different speeds of autonomy — a "fast track" for historic nationalities like Catalonia, the Basque Country, and Galicia, which had previous experience of self-government, and a "slow track" for other regions. Over time, the arrangement has become more symmetrical, with all communities acquiring broadly comparable powers, but significant tensions persist — most dramatically with Catalonia, where a movement for independence has created a sustained constitutional crisis over the limits of regional self-determination within a unitary framework.

Italy's system of regions (regioni) has evolved significantly since the 1948 Constitution established them. Five regions were given special autonomous status from the outset — Sicily, Sardinia, Trentino-Alto Adige/Südtirol, Friuli-Venezia Giulia, and the Aosta Valley — because of their distinctive cultural, linguistic, or geographic circumstances. The remaining fifteen ordinary regions received their powers more gradually. Constitutional reforms in 2001 significantly strengthened regional competences and gave regional governments greater fiscal flexibility. Italian regionalism remains contested: the northern regions, which are economically more developed, have periodically sought greater autonomy — a movement reflected in the political platform of the Lega Nord (Northern League) — while persistent disparities between the prosperous north and the economically disadvantaged south (the Mezzogiorno) complicate the politics of territorial equity.

China's territorial system is formally unitary but incorporates several distinctive arrangements that do not fit easily into standard categories. The Special Administrative Regions of Hong Kong and Macau operate under a "one country, two systems" framework that grants them significant autonomy from mainland Chinese law — their own legal systems, currencies, and governance arrangements — for a defined period of fifty years following their return to China in 1997 and 1999 respectively. The five autonomous regions designated for ethnic minority populations (including Tibet and Xinjiang) carry the constitutional promise of significant self-governing powers, though in practice the central government has maintained strong control. China also has a system of special economic zones that enjoy regulatory and economic flexibility not available elsewhere. These varied arrangements illustrate how a formally unitary state can incorporate significant differentiation in its territorial governance without adopting federalism.

Indonesia's experience with decentralization since 1999 is one of the most dramatic examples of rapid territorial change in a large country. Under the Suharto dictatorship, Indonesia was highly centralized, with regional governments largely controlled by Jakarta. Following Suharto's fall in 1998 and the transition to democracy, Indonesia underwent sweeping decentralization: districts (kabupaten) and cities (kota) received extensive new powers in a "big bang" reform that transferred authority for most public services and substantial revenue to more than 500 local governments almost simultaneously. The result has been a significant redistribution of authority and resources, though uneven implementation has produced widely varying results across the archipelago. Special autonomy arrangements for Aceh and Papua add another layer of complexity.

Comparative Lens
Devolution in the United Kingdom and Spain: Two Models of Asymmetrical Regionalism

The United Kingdom and Spain both developed extensive devolution arrangements in the late twentieth century, following periods of centralized rule. Both chose asymmetrical approaches, giving different regions different powers. But the two cases differ in important ways that illuminate the range of territorial options available to unitary states.

In the United Kingdom, devolution was driven primarily by distinct national identities — Scottish, Welsh, and Northern Irish — each with different historical relationships to the English-dominated Union. The Scottish Parliament received the most powers, reflecting the strength of Scottish civil society, the experience of Scottish legal and educational distinctiveness, and the political pressure of Scottish nationalism. The Welsh Senedd received fewer initial powers, reflecting a different political situation. Northern Ireland's Assembly was shaped entirely by the peace process following three decades of political violence. The resulting arrangement is highly asymmetrical not because of any abstract constitutional theory but because of pragmatic responses to different political circumstances. The "English question" — whether England should have its own parliament or whether English matters should be decided only by English MPs — remains unresolved.

In Spain, the 1978 Constitution created a general framework for regional self-government without fully specifying which regions would receive which powers. The framers deliberately left the territorial model open-ended — sometimes called the "Estado de las Autonomías" or State of Autonomies — to allow regions to negotiate their powers with the central government through their Statutes of Autonomy. This produced an initial asymmetry between the "historic nationalities" (Catalonia, Basque Country, Galicia) and other regions, which over time was largely equalized as all communities acquired similar powers. The persistent exception is the Basque Country and Navarre, which retain distinctive fiscal arrangements (the "foral" system) under which they collect their own taxes and transfer agreed amounts to the central government — the reverse of the normal relationship.

Both cases illustrate a common pattern: devolution tends to be driven by political pressures from below — national or regional movements demanding self-government — rather than by abstract constitutional theory. And once established, devolved arrangements develop their own institutional momentum, constituencies, and political dynamics that make recentralization practically difficult even when it remains legally possible.

Section 50.5

Local Government in Comparative Perspective

The study of territorial systems often focuses on the relationship between national and regional governments — the federal-unitary dimension. But for many citizens, the most immediately felt level of government is local: the municipality, the city, the county, the district, the borough. Local government provides the services that people encounter most directly — garbage collection, road maintenance, public parks, local police, fire protection, libraries, schools (in some systems), and social services. Understanding how local government is organized and what authority it holds is essential to a complete picture of territorial governance.

The constitutional and legal position of local government varies significantly across systems. In federal countries, local governments are typically creatures of state or provincial law — they exist under authority delegated by the intermediate level of government, not by the national constitution. The United States Constitution does not mention cities, counties, or municipalities; their existence, powers, and structure are governed by state law, which varies considerably from state to state. This means that the legal position of a city like Houston (in a state that allows extensive home rule) can be very different from that of a similarly sized city in a state with more restrictive municipal legislation.

In unitary countries, local governments are typically creatures of national law. The structure of local government in France — its communes, departments, and intercommunal bodies — is established by national legislation and modified periodically through national reform. France has nearly 35,000 communes, many of them tiny rural municipalities whose governance consumes resources with limited capacity for meaningful self-government. Debates about whether to consolidate French municipalities into larger, more capable units have been politically contested for decades, as local identity and the mayoralty remain powerful institutions in French civic culture.

The question of home rule — the authority of cities and municipalities to govern their own affairs without interference from higher levels of government — is a significant issue in many systems. Home rule doctrine, developed in the United States, holds that cities and municipalities should have broad authority to regulate local affairs without requiring specific authorization from the state legislature for each action. Not all American states grant home rule to their municipalities; those that do create a meaningfully different local governance environment. In Germany, the Basic Law protects the self-governing rights of municipalities (Selbstverwaltungsrecht), giving local government constitutional status that American cities lack.

Metropolitan governance presents a particular challenge in territorial systems. Large metropolitan areas — cities and their surrounding suburbs and exurbs — often sprawl across multiple municipal, county, and sometimes even state or provincial boundaries, creating coordination problems for services like transportation, water supply, air quality, housing, and regional economic development. Different countries have developed different institutional responses to the metropolitan challenge. France created the Métropole de Lyon, a powerful metropolitan authority that replaced the department and many municipal functions for the Lyon urban area. The United Kingdom created the Greater London Authority in 2000 to provide coordinated governance for one of the world's largest metropolitan areas. The United States has generally struggled more with metropolitan coordination, relying on special-purpose districts, interlocal agreements, and voluntary regional associations rather than powerful metropolitan governments.

Local governments everywhere face a fundamental tension between the desire for local autonomy — the ability to make decisions reflecting local preferences and circumstances — and the need for resources sufficient to perform their functions. This tension is particularly acute for smaller municipalities, which may have strong preferences for local control but lack the fiscal capacity to fund the services their residents expect. Many countries address this through intergovernmental transfers, but this creates the dependency and conditionality discussed in the fiscal federalism section: local governments that depend on grants from higher-level governments have less real autonomy than their formal status suggests.

Local elections are a crucial dimension of local democratic accountability. In many countries, local elections are separate from national elections and take place on different schedules — a design intended to allow voters to evaluate local officials on local criteria rather than simply reflecting national partisan alignments. In practice, however, local elections are often heavily influenced by national political currents, especially in strong party systems where local officials are affiliated with national parties. Turnout in local elections is typically lower than in national elections in most countries, raising questions about the democratic legitimacy and accountability of local government even where it is formally well-designed.

Institution in Practice
Metropolitan Governance: The Greater London Authority

London is one of the world's largest cities, with a metropolitan population of over 9 million people in the Greater London area and more than 14 million in the wider metropolitan region. Governing a city of this scale and complexity is an enormous institutional challenge. For much of the twentieth century, London lacked effective metropolitan-wide government: the Greater London Council, created in 1965, was abolished by the Thatcher government in 1986, leaving London with no city-wide elected authority for fourteen years.

The Greater London Authority (GLA), created by the Greater Labour government under Tony Blair and established in 2000, represents one of the most significant institutional experiments in metropolitan governance in recent decades. The GLA has two elected components: a directly elected Mayor of London, with executive authority over strategic issues; and a 25-member London Assembly, which scrutinizes and holds the Mayor accountable. The Mayor has powers over transport (through Transport for London, which operates the iconic Underground, buses, and other transit), the Metropolitan Police (through the Mayor's Office for Policing and Crime), fire services, economic development, planning policy, and housing strategy. The 33 London boroughs — separately elected local governments within Greater London — retain responsibility for most day-to-day local services.

The GLA model illustrates several features of effective metropolitan governance. A directly elected mayor with real powers and a publicly visible mandate can provide strategic leadership across a fragmented metropolitan area. The combination of executive authority (the Mayor) and scrutiny (the Assembly) provides internal accountability. And the division between strategic metropolitan functions (handled by the GLA) and local service delivery (handled by the boroughs) allows metropolitan-scale coordination without eliminating local identity and democratic accountability.

The GLA also illustrates the challenges of metropolitan governance in a unitary state. The GLA's powers are entirely creatures of Westminster legislation; Parliament could in principle abolish it again, as it abolished the GLC. The Mayor's fiscal autonomy is limited: most GLA revenue comes from central government grants and from levies on the London boroughs, with only a small supplemental business rate under direct mayoral control. The relationship between the GLA, the borough governments, and the national government requires constant negotiation and coordination.

Section 50.6

Ethnic, Linguistic, and Regional Diversity

One of the most powerful forces shaping territorial constitutional design is the social diversity of political communities. Countries that contain multiple linguistic, ethnic, religious, or regional communities face a distinctive challenge: how to organize political authority in ways that allow diverse groups to participate in public life, maintain their distinct identities, and feel that the political system is genuinely theirs — without fragmenting the political community so thoroughly that effective national governance becomes impossible. Territorial organization is one of the principal tools through which political systems attempt to manage this challenge.

Multinational states — countries that contain multiple historically distinct national communities — face the most acute version of this challenge. Belgium is perhaps the most extensively studied contemporary case. A country of roughly 11 million people, Belgium contains two major linguistic communities (Dutch-speaking Flanders and French-speaking Wallonia) and the bilingual Brussels capital region, along with a small German-speaking community. Belgian politics since the 1960s has been organized almost entirely around linguistic and community lines: the major political parties are organized separately in each linguistic community, the federal government operates through complex power-sharing arrangements that require agreement between communities, and the state has been progressively transformed into what its constitution now describes as a federal state. Belgium's federalism is unusual in that it features two overlapping sets of entities — the three regions (Flanders, Wallonia, Brussels) based on territory and the three communities (Flemish, Francophone, German-speaking) based on population — with different competencies. This complex institutional architecture reflects the difficulty of accommodating two major national communities within a single state, and Belgium's periodic political crises — including governments that have taken over a year to form — reflect the genuine tensions the system must manage.

Switzerland's linguistic federalism provides a contrasting example of a more successful accommodation of multiple language communities within a federal framework. Switzerland's 26 cantons are largely unilingual in practice, though each canton determines its own official language(s), producing a mosaic of German-, French-, Italian-, and Romansh-speaking cantons that makes Switzerland genuinely multilingual at the national level while allowing relatively homogeneous linguistic communities at the cantonal level. This territorial sorting of linguistic communities — placing them in separate cantonal units with their own governments — differs from the Belgian model, in which linguistic communities overlap geographically, particularly in Brussels and in the communes around Brussels where the two communities compete for influence.

Canada's management of its French-English divide through federalism represents one of the world's longest-running experiments in accommodating a linguistic minority within a federal framework. Quebec — the province in which most French Canadians live — has used its provincial powers to build a distinctive French-speaking society in North America, with its own civil law tradition, language laws (requiring French as the language of business and public life), cultural institutions, and social policies. Canadian federalism provides the constitutional framework for this distinctiveness: Quebec's government has used its powers over education, health, social services, and culture to maintain and strengthen French-language society in ways that would be impossible in a more centralized state. Yet Canadian federalism has not fully resolved the tensions: Quebec nationalism remains a significant political force, and the question of Quebec's constitutional status — whether it is a "distinct society" deserving special recognition, a province like the others, or a potential independent nation — has generated recurring constitutional crises including two referendums on sovereignty in 1980 and 1995.

India's approach to linguistic diversity through federalism is both remarkable and distinctive. The reorganization of Indian states along linguistic lines in 1956, following the recommendations of the States Reorganisation Commission, fundamentally reshaped the map of India's federal system. States like Maharashtra (Marathi-speaking), Tamil Nadu (Tamil-speaking), Karnataka (Kannada-speaking), and West Bengal (Bengali-speaking) were created or consolidated to correspond to major linguistic communities. This linguistic reorganization has been credited with channeling linguistic competition into the institutional politics of state governments rather than into secessionist movements, and with making state governments genuinely responsive to the linguistic communities they serve. India also recognizes 22 official languages under the Eighth Schedule of the Constitution, and the linguistic plurality of its federal system is a major feature of its democratic diversity.

Indigenous self-government represents another dimension of territorial organization and diversity, one that has received increasing attention across many countries. In the United States, federally recognized tribes possess a form of sovereignty that exists alongside state and federal authority — a complex legal arrangement sometimes described as "domestic dependent nations." Canadian First Nations, Métis, and Inuit peoples have rights recognized in the Constitution Act of 1982 and have negotiated numerous self-government agreements that give them varying degrees of authority over their lands and communities. Australia's Indigenous peoples lack comparable constitutional recognition but have achieved some land rights and self-management arrangements. New Zealand's Treaty of Waitangi framework provides a foundation for Māori participation in governance. These arrangements reflect evolving recognition that indigenous peoples have prior claims to political autonomy that standard federal or unitary frameworks may not adequately accommodate.

Secession movements — demands by regions or communities to exit the political system and form independent states — represent the most extreme response to the failure of territorial arrangements to accommodate diversity. Catalonia's independence movement, Scotland's independence referendum in 2014 (which produced a 45% vote for independence) and subsequent pressures, Quebec's close sovereignty referendums, and Kosovo's declaration of independence from Serbia all illustrate how demands for self-determination can challenge the territorial integrity of existing states. Constitutional systems respond to these pressures differently: some provide legal mechanisms for regions to vote on independence (Canada's Clarity Act, which establishes the rules for how Quebec could pursue sovereignty); others deny any such right (Spain's position on Catalonia); still others are ambiguous. How political systems manage the boundary between legitimate demands for regional autonomy and challenges to state integrity is one of the most difficult questions in contemporary constitutional design.

Section 50.7

Centralization and Decentralization

The fundamental choice in territorial governance — how much authority to concentrate at the center versus how much to distribute to regions and localities — is not a choice that can be made once and forgotten. Political systems continually renegotiate the balance between centralization and decentralization in response to changing circumstances, shifting political pressures, new policy challenges, and evolving understandings of what effective governance requires. Understanding this ongoing negotiation requires a clear analysis of what centralization and decentralization each make easier and what each makes more difficult.

The case for centralization rests on several arguments. First, national coordination: some policy challenges — macroeconomic management, national defense, pandemic response, climate policy — require coordinated action across the entire national territory. Fragmented subnational responses to these challenges can produce collective action failures in which regions free-ride on each other's efforts or make decisions that impose costs on neighboring jurisdictions. A national government with authority over the entire territory can set uniform standards, coordinate implementation, and prevent the race-to-the-bottom dynamics that can emerge when jurisdictions compete to attract investment by lowering environmental, labor, or regulatory standards.

Second, centralization can promote equity. When public services are financed and delivered locally, the quality of services available to citizens depends heavily on the wealth of their jurisdiction. A poor rural county in a decentralized system may provide dramatically inferior education, health care, and infrastructure compared to a wealthy urban county. National standards and national financing can reduce this variation, ensuring more equal access to public services regardless of where citizens happen to live. This equity argument is one of the most powerful cases for national authority in systems where subnational fiscal capacity varies widely.

Third, centralization can enable economies of scale. Some governmental functions — military procurement, specialized medical research, national transportation networks, nuclear energy — benefit from scale and specialization that only national-level organization can efficiently provide. Fragmentation of these functions across many smaller jurisdictions can produce inefficiency, duplication, and gaps in provision.

The case for decentralization is equally substantial. First, local responsiveness: decentralized governments are better positioned to tailor public services and policies to the specific preferences, needs, and circumstances of their communities. A policy that works well in a dense urban area may be inappropriate for a sparse rural region. A unitary system that imposes uniform national standards may sacrifice responsiveness to local conditions in the pursuit of uniformity. Decentralization allows for what economist Charles Tiebout called "voting with your feet" — citizens choosing among different jurisdictions with different packages of taxes and services — and it allows policy variation to reflect genuine differences in preferences and circumstances.

Second, decentralization can promote democratic participation. When significant decisions are made locally, citizens have more opportunities to participate in and influence those decisions. Local government is more accessible than national government: it is easier to attend a city council meeting than a congressional hearing, easier to know and contact a local mayor than a national minister. Decentralization thus can enlarge the scope of meaningful democratic participation beyond the quadrennial or quinquennial national election.

Third, decentralization enables policy experimentation. When subnational governments have genuine authority to design and implement their own policies, different jurisdictions can try different approaches to common problems. Some experiments will succeed and spread; others will fail and be abandoned. This laboratory function of decentralized government — the idea of states or provinces as "laboratories of democracy" — is one of the most frequently cited benefits of federal systems. Universal health insurance, the initiative and referendum, and numerous social programs were pioneered at the state or provincial level in federal systems before being adopted nationally.

Fourth, decentralization can provide a structural check on national power. A system in which powerful regional governments exist as independent political actors provides a form of vertical accountability — a check on national government abuse — that purely horizontal separation of powers may not fully achieve. Governors, premiers, and regional presidents who have their own political mandates, their own fiscal resources, and their own institutional bases can resist national overreach in ways that centrally appointed local officials cannot.

The practical tradeoffs between centralization and decentralization are context-dependent. A large, diverse country with significant regional variation in culture, language, and economic circumstance has different needs than a small, relatively homogeneous country. A country recovering from a period of authoritarian centralization may need to decentralize to rebuild local democratic capacity. A country facing separatist challenges may need to decentralize to reduce the incentives for secession. A country managing a national fiscal crisis may need to centralize fiscal authority to avoid competitive austerity races among regions. No single balance is right for all circumstances, which is why territorial arrangements continue to evolve in virtually every political system in the world.

The concept of intergovernmental relations — the practical, day-to-day relationships among governments at different levels — is essential to understanding how territorial systems actually work. Even in federal systems with clear constitutional division of powers, the real business of governing requires constant interaction, negotiation, and coordination among governments. Federal agencies work with state agencies to implement joint programs. State governments lobby the federal government for grants, regulations, and appointments. Local governments work with both state and federal officials on infrastructure, housing, and public safety. This dense web of intergovernmental relationships — involving bureaucrats, elected officials, lobbyists, professional associations, and intergovernmental bodies — shapes policy outcomes in ways that no formal constitutional map can fully capture.

The Federalist Papers, No. 45 — James Madison (1788)
Context

Federalist No. 45, authored by James Madison, addresses concerns about whether the proposed Constitution would give the federal government too much power at the expense of the states. Written to persuade New Yorkers to ratify the Constitution, it provides a classic defense of the federal design and an explanation of how the new system was intended to allocate powers between the national and state governments.

Key Passage (Paraphrase)
The powers delegated by the proposed Constitution to the federal government are few and defined. Those which are to remain in the State governments are numerous and indefinite. The former will be exercised principally on external objects, as war, peace, negotiation, and foreign commerce. The powers reserved to the several States will extend to all the objects which, in the ordinary course of affairs, concern the lives, liberties, and properties of the people, and the internal order, improvement, and prosperity of the State.
Analysis

Madison's passage captures the original theory of American federalism: the national government was to have a limited set of specifically delegated powers concerned primarily with external affairs, while the states would retain broad residual authority over domestic life. The reality of American government has evolved dramatically from this vision — today's federal government is deeply involved in education, health care, environmental regulation, and countless other matters Madison would have assigned to the states. This gap between founding theory and political reality illustrates how territorial systems evolve through constitutional interpretation, political change, and the expanding scope of governmental responsibility over time.

Section 50.8

Choosing a Territorial Design

Having examined the major types of territorial organization — federal, unitary, devolved, regional, and hybrid — and having analyzed the tradeoffs between centralization and decentralization, we are now in a position to ask how political communities can evaluate and choose among territorial designs. This is not primarily a theoretical exercise; it is a practical question that constitutional designers, reform commissions, and political leaders face whenever a country writes or rewrites its fundamental law.

The first step in evaluating territorial designs is to identify the specific problems a country faces. A country with deep ethnic, linguistic, or regional divisions needs a territorial design that can accommodate diversity without fracturing national unity. A country with vast geographic extent and poor infrastructure needs a design that can make government accessible across territory. A country with extreme regional inequality needs a design that can redistribute resources without creating permanent dependency. A country emerging from authoritarian centralization needs a design that can rebuild local democratic capacity. None of these problems are solved by the same territorial arrangement.

National unity is a basic requirement for any territorial system. A design that allows regions to undermine national cohesion, free-ride on national public goods, or pursue policies that impose serious costs on other regions is unlikely to be politically sustainable. But national unity should not be confused with uniformity — many countries maintain strong national identities precisely because their territorial systems allow diverse communities to maintain their distinctiveness within a shared framework. Switzerland's multilingual federalism, India's linguistic state system, and Canada's accommodation of Quebec are all examples of territorial designs that have contributed to national unity by accommodating diversity rather than suppressing it.

Democratic representation requires that territorial design give citizens meaningful access to decision-making at levels close enough to be accessible. A system that concentrates all significant decisions in a distant national capital may formally be democratic but may in practice fail to give citizens genuine influence over the decisions that most affect their lives. Effective democratic representation across territory requires not just elections at multiple levels but genuine authority at multiple levels — something to vote about at each tier that actually matters.

Administrative efficiency considerations push toward some degree of centralization for functions with clear economies of scale or strong coordination requirements, and toward decentralization for functions where local knowledge and adaptation are most valuable. The challenge is identifying which functions belong in which category — a question that different political systems have answered differently and that the same political system may answer differently at different historical moments.

Fiscal management in a territorial system requires sustainable arrangements for revenue, expenditure, and transfers. Systems in which subnational governments have large expenditure responsibilities but inadequate own revenues create chronic dependence on central transfers and the political tensions that accompany that dependence. Systems in which subnational governments have extensive taxing powers but limited equalization mechanisms produce large service disparities tied to accidents of geographic wealth. Sustainable fiscal federalism requires matching revenue and expenditure responsibilities and providing equalization that is adequate but does not eliminate the incentive for fiscally responsible behavior.

Constitutional stability requires that territorial arrangements be broadly acceptable to the main political communities within the country and that procedures for adjusting them be available and legitimate. Constitutional arrangements that are perceived as unjust, that exclude significant communities from meaningful participation, or that cannot be adapted as circumstances change are likely to generate instability over time. The most durable federal and devolved arrangements are those that have been repeatedly adapted through legitimate processes rather than locked into forms that cannot evolve with political reality.

The comparative study of territorial systems teaches a fundamental lesson about constitutional design: there is no universally correct answer to the question of how authority should be distributed across territory. The appropriate territorial design depends on historical circumstances, geographic factors, demographic composition, institutional traditions, and the specific problems a political community faces. Federal systems have advantages in managing diversity and preserving liberty, but they also generate coordination challenges and can entrench regional inequalities. Unitary systems have advantages in national coordination and policy uniformity, but they can be unresponsive to local conditions and can accumulate problematic concentrations of power. Devolved and hybrid arrangements offer flexible intermediate options but may lack the constitutional stability of more clearly defined systems.

The most useful lesson from comparative analysis is not which type of territorial system is best, but how to evaluate any system systematically: What problems was it designed to address? Does it address them effectively? What are its principal weaknesses, and how have they been managed? How has it evolved over time, and what forces have driven that evolution? These questions, applied consistently across cases, allow students to engage with territorial systems analytically rather than to simply categorize them by label.

Chapter 51, the final chapter of this textbook, will apply these analytical tools — along with those developed throughout Part IX — to a set of specific country case studies. Each case will be examined using a consistent template that allows systematic comparison across constitutional design, institutional structure, regime type, party systems, electoral systems, rights frameworks, judicial arrangements, policy challenges, and international roles. The concepts and frameworks introduced in this chapter will be essential tools for that comparative analysis.

Civic Debate
Should States and Provinces Be Allowed to Opt Out of National Standards?
The Issue

In many federal systems, a persistent tension exists between national standards — uniform rules applied across the entire country — and regional autonomy — the right of subnational governments to set their own rules. This tension arises in education, health care, environmental regulation, social benefits, criminal justice, and numerous other policy areas. The debate concerns how much variation among subnational governments a federal system should allow and under what circumstances national standards should override regional choices.

Argument A — National Standards Protect Equal Citizenship

This position holds that citizens should have access to comparable levels of basic public services regardless of which state or province they happen to live in. Where a child is born should not determine the quality of the education they receive, the health care available to them, or the environmental quality they experience. National minimum standards ensure that states and provinces do not race to the bottom by cutting services to attract business or reduce tax burdens at the expense of residents who need public services most. National civil rights protections, for example, prevent states from discriminating against minority populations even when majorities in those states might prefer to do so. From this perspective, national standards are not threats to diversity but guarantees of equal citizenship across territory.

Argument B — Regional Variation Reflects Genuine Diversity

This position argues that the whole point of a federal system is to allow different communities to govern themselves differently according to their own values and preferences. Citizens in different regions genuinely differ in their preferences about the appropriate scope of public services, the balance between taxation and private provision, environmental regulation and economic development, and numerous other policy questions. Imposing national standards suppresses this legitimate diversity and concentrates too much power in a national government that may be distant from the communities it governs. If a state or province makes policy choices that its citizens disagree with, those citizens can vote to change those policies — or, in an extreme case, move to another jurisdiction. National standards that override state or provincial choices remove this democratic option without compensating for it.

Other Perspectives

Some argue that the opt-out question should be answered differently for different policy areas. For fundamental rights — freedom of speech, due process, equal protection — no regional opt-out should be allowed, because rights are not legitimately subject to majoritarian override at any level. For service provision — the quantity and design of public services — more variation may be appropriate. For economic regulation where regional differences can distort markets and create spillovers across jurisdictions, national coordination may be necessary even if not full uniformity. The challenge is developing principled criteria for deciding which policy areas fall into which category.

Tradeoffs

National standards reduce inequality and protect against races to the bottom, but they reduce the responsiveness of governance to local conditions and suppress legitimate diversity. Regional variation allows policy experimentation and democratic self-determination, but it can produce extreme inequality across jurisdictions and enable some communities to deny rights to minorities. The design of federal systems continuously negotiates this tension, and there is no formula that resolves it once and for all — it must be managed through ongoing political and constitutional processes.

School attendance rules? For each, what are the arguments for national standards and for state or provincial autonomy? What principles would you use to determine which decisions belong at which level?

Rights and Responsibilities in Territorial Systems

The vertical distribution of governmental authority has profound implications for rights and civic responsibilities. In federal systems, citizens simultaneously hold membership in multiple political communities — the nation and the state or province — and their rights may be defined differently at each level. This creates both opportunities and risks for individual rights protection.

One of the most important developments in American constitutional history was the nationalization of civil rights and civil liberties through the Fourteenth Amendment and subsequent Supreme Court interpretations. Before this development, the Bill of Rights bound only the federal government; states could and did restrict speech, establish religions, and deny equal protection in ways that the federal government could not. The incorporation doctrine — the principle that most guarantees of the Bill of Rights apply to state governments through the Fourteenth Amendment — transformed the relationship between national rights and state authority. Today, a state government cannot violate freedom of speech, deny due process, or impose cruel and unusual punishment any more than the federal government can.

This nationalization of rights reflects a tension inherent in federal systems: the same decentralization that allows local self-government to flourish can also allow local majorities to deny rights to minorities. The history of racial segregation in the American South illustrates this tension starkly: state governments used their broad authority over education, public accommodations, and electoral systems to deny the rights of African Americans in ways that the federal government eventually — through the Civil Rights Act of 1964 and Voting Rights Act of 1965 — overrode. Federal intervention to protect minority rights against state-level violations represents one of the most significant expansions of national authority in American history, and it illustrates why rights protection often pushes toward centralization even in otherwise decentralized systems.

The question of which rights are protected at which level — and what happens when national and subnational rights standards conflict — remains contested in virtually every federal system. In Canada, the Charter of Rights and Freedoms (1982) applies to all levels of government, but the "notwithstanding clause" allows Parliament and provincial legislatures to override certain Charter rights for renewable five-year periods — a provision that has been used (most notably by Quebec) and that remains controversial. In Germany, the Basic Law establishes a comprehensive rights framework that applies to all levels of government; the federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) is the ultimate arbiter of rights in the entire federal system.

Citizens in federal systems also have responsibilities that are distributed across levels. Paying taxes to multiple levels of government is one such responsibility — Americans pay federal, state, and often local income and property taxes simultaneously. Voting in multiple elections — national, regional, and local — is another. Understanding the division of responsibilities among levels of government, and knowing which government to contact about which problem, is a form of civic knowledge that citizens in federal systems must acquire. This is non-trivial: surveys routinely show that many citizens in federal systems cannot accurately describe which level of government is responsible for major policy areas.

A distinctive civic responsibility in federated systems is engagement at the subnational level. Because state and provincial governments control important policy areas — education, health care (in many systems), social services, criminal justice — and because subnational elections typically attract lower turnout than national elections, citizens who engage only with national politics are missing a significant portion of the governing activity that affects their lives. Civic education in federal systems should not only teach students about the national constitution but should develop their capacity to engage with state, provincial, and local governments as well.

Policy Lab
Designing a Territorial System for a New Pluralist Democracy
Scenario

A newly independent country called Verantia is drafting its first constitution. Verantia has a population of 25 million people spread across a territory roughly the size of France. It has four major regions, each with distinct cultural and linguistic identities. The northern region (Norland) is the most economically developed, with 40% of the national GDP, and has a strong regional identity based on a distinct language spoken by 35% of the population. The eastern region (Eastmark) has significant agricultural wealth and a conservative political culture. The western region (Westport) is heavily urbanized and economically diverse. The southern region (Souterra) is the poorest, with high unemployment and significant infrastructure needs. Verantia's constitution-makers must decide how to organize territorial government.

Stakeholders

Norland's political leaders advocate for federalism with strong regional governments and independent taxing authority. Eastmark prefers a unitary system that can maintain national standards. Westport wants strong metropolitan governance for its major cities. Souterra wants a robust national redistribution system to address its fiscal disadvantage. Minority ethnic communities in all regions seek guarantees of cultural and linguistic rights at whatever level of government is established.

Policy Options
  1. Full federalism: Four constituent states with constitutionally guaranteed powers, independent legislatures, and significant taxing authority. A national government with limited enumerated powers and a strong equalization program.
  2. Unitary state with devolution: A formally unitary state in which the national parliament devolves substantial powers to elected regional assemblies by statute, retaining the right to reclaim those powers but in practice delegating them broadly.
  3. Asymmetrical arrangement: Norland receives federal-style constitutional status because of its distinct language and identity; the other three regions receive substantial but more limited devolved powers.
  4. Regionalized unitary state: The national government delegates significant administrative authority to regional governments but retains legislative supremacy. Strong national standards apply everywhere; regional variation is limited to administrative implementation.
  5. Confederation: The four regions become near-sovereign units that delegate limited powers to a weak national government. The national government requires unanimous regional agreement for major decisions.
Evidence Needed
  • How deep are the linguistic and cultural divisions, and how likely is regional resentment to translate into secessionism if regional autonomy is denied?
  • What are the fiscal capacities of the four regions? How much equalization is needed to enable Souterra to provide comparable services?
  • What administrative capacity exists at the regional and local level? Can regional governments actually deliver services effectively?
  • What do citizens in each region most want from their government?
Tradeoffs
  • Full federalism may satisfy Norland's autonomy demands but may leave Souterra with inadequate resources unless equalization is generous.
  • Unitary devolution maintains flexibility but may not reassure Norland's population that its autonomy is secure.
  • Asymmetrical arrangements may generate resentment among regions that receive fewer powers.
  • A confederation may be too weak to address national challenges including fiscal equalization, defense, and market integration.

Chapter 50 — Common Misunderstandings

Clearing Up Common Errors

Misunderstanding 1
Federal systems always give more power to regions than unitary systems.
Correction
This is not always true. Many formally federal systems are, in practice, highly centralized — especially in fiscal terms. Australia is a federal country whose states depend heavily on Commonwealth grants and have limited independent revenue, giving the Commonwealth substantial policy leverage. Conversely, some formally unitary countries have granted so much authority to regional governments through devolution that they function more like federal states in practice. What matters is the practical distribution of authority, not the constitutional label alone.
Misunderstanding 2
Unitary governments are more efficient because they make decisions faster.
Correction
Efficiency in governance depends on many factors beyond constitutional structure. Federal systems can be efficient when intergovernmental coordination works well and when subnational governments have adequate capacity. Unitary systems can be inefficient when national bureaucracies are remote from the problems they are trying to solve, or when central governments are overloaded with decisions that local governments could handle better. The relationship between constitutional structure and administrative efficiency is complicated and context-dependent.
Misunderstanding 3
Devolution and federalism mean the same thing.
Correction
Devolution refers to the transfer of powers from a central government to regional governments by statute, within a formally unitary constitutional framework. The central government retains the constitutional power to reclaim what it has delegated. Federalism involves the constitutional entrenchment of subnational authority so that the national government cannot unilaterally eliminate the powers of subnational units. The UK is a unitary state that has devolved substantial powers to Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland — but Westminster retains the constitutional authority to override or reverse those arrangements. The United States is a federal state in which the powers of the states are constitutionally protected from unilateral national elimination.
Misunderstanding 4
All regions within a federal country have the same powers.
Correction
Asymmetrical federalism — in which different subnational units have different powers — is quite common. Canada's provinces formally have equal constitutional status, but Quebec has used its powers in distinctive ways that make it function differently from other provinces, and some asymmetrical arrangements (like the Senate seats allocated to provinces and territories) formally differ. Spain's Autonomous Communities formally have the same constitutional status but differ significantly in their historical powers and arrangements (notably the Basque Country and Navarre's fiscal arrangements). India's states vary in their relationships with the central government. Asymmetry often reflects historical, cultural, or political differences among regions that uniform arrangements cannot accommodate.
Misunderstanding 5
Local governments in federal systems have constitutional protection for their powers.
Correction
In most federal systems, the constitution protects the powers of intermediate subnational governments (states, provinces, Länder) but not necessarily those of local governments (cities, counties, municipalities). In the United States, cities and counties are creatures of state law and have only those powers that states grant them. The US Constitution does not mention cities. Some countries have extended constitutional protection to local governments — Germany's Basic Law protects municipal self-government — but this is not a universal feature of federal systems.
Misunderstanding 6
Federalism is the best system for managing ethnic and linguistic diversity.
Correction
Federalism can be an effective tool for managing diversity, but it is not the only tool and not always the best one. Its effectiveness depends heavily on how territorial boundaries correspond to social boundaries. If ethnic or linguistic communities are territorially concentrated, giving them regional governments with genuine authority can accommodate their distinctiveness effectively (Switzerland, India). But if communities are intermingled geographically — as in Belgium's Brussels region or in many post-colonial African states — territorial federalism may not adequately protect minority communities and may actually exacerbate ethnic tensions by creating ethno-territorial units that compete for resources and dominance. Non-territorial arrangements, including power-sharing at the national level (consociationalism), linguistic rights regimes, and cultural autonomy bodies, can sometimes manage diversity more effectively than territorial federalism alone.
Misunderstanding 7
Secession is always a democratic right in federal systems.
Correction
Most federal constitutions do not recognize a unilateral right of secession. The United States Constitution is silent on secession, and the Supreme Court and the outcome of the Civil War established that states cannot unilaterally leave the Union. Spain's Constitutional Court ruled that Catalonia's 2017 independence referendum was unconstitutional. Canada's Supreme Court, in the Quebec Secession Reference (1998), held that Quebec does not have a unilateral right to secede under either domestic or international law, but also that if a clear majority voted clearly for independence, the rest of Canada would be obligated to negotiate. Whether and how secession should be permitted is a deeply contested question in constitutional theory and international law, with no universally accepted answer.
Misunderstanding 8
Intergovernmental relations are simply about which level of government wins disputes.
Correction
Intergovernmental relations are not primarily adversarial. In practice, the day-to-day relationships among national, regional, and local governments involve extensive cooperation, joint programs, shared administration, and mutual adaptation. Even in systems with significant intergovernmental conflict, most governmental activity is not conflictual — it involves governments at different levels working together to deliver services, implement programs, and address public problems. The cooperative dimension of intergovernmental relations is often more consequential for policy outcomes than the conflictual dimension that tends to attract political attention.
Chapter 50 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 50.1Territorial organization — the vertical distribution of governmental authority across national, regional, and local levels — is a fundamental dimension of constitutional design. All political systems must decide how much authority to concentrate at the center and how much to distribute to subnational units. The key conceptual distinctions are between centralization and decentralization, between administrative and political decentralization, and among federal, unitary, devolved, and hybrid arrangements. These categories are tools for analysis rather than rigid classifications: real systems often blend elements of multiple types.
  • Sec 50.2Federal systems constitutionally divide authority between national governments and subnational units (states, provinces, Länder, cantons) each of which has independent democratic legitimacy and constitutionally protected powers. Different federal systems vary enormously in how they allocate powers: some (like Germany) emphasize cooperative federalism with Länder implementing federal law; others (like the United States) have historically emphasized dual sovereignty with distinct spheres. Fiscal federalism — the intergovernmental arrangements for revenue, expenditure, and transfers — shapes the real distribution of power regardless of what the formal constitutional text says.
  • Sec 50.3Unitary systems concentrate constitutional sovereignty in the national government, which may delegate authority to regional and local governments but retains the right to modify or reclaim those powers. Unitary systems vary enormously in how much they actually decentralize in practice. France has undergone significant administrative decentralization since the 1980s; Sweden's municipalities have substantial fiscal autonomy; Japan's prefectural governments provide extensive services. The constitutional label of unitarism describes the legal structure of authority, not necessarily the practical distribution of governing activity.
  • Sec 50.4Devolution occupies the middle ground between strict federalism and strict unitarism. When formally unitary states transfer significant governing authority to regional bodies through statute, they create devolved arrangements that can approach federalism in practice while remaining formally revocable. The United Kingdom's devolution to Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland and Spain's system of Autonomous Communities are major examples. Asymmetrical devolution — giving different regions different powers — reflects the reality that regions within one country often have different historical claims, cultural identities, and political pressures for autonomy.
  • Sec 50.5Local government — municipalities, cities, counties, and metropolitan authorities — is often the level of government that citizens encounter most directly. In federal systems, local governments are typically creatures of state or provincial law, lacking the constitutional protection of intermediate subnational units. In unitary systems, local governments exist under national statutory authority. The metropolitan governance challenge — coordinating services across fragmented urban jurisdictions — has produced innovative institutional arrangements including the Greater London Authority. Local elections provide important opportunities for democratic participation but typically attract lower turnout than national elections.
  • Sec 50.6Territorial organization is often shaped by the social diversity of political communities. Federal and devolved arrangements can accommodate linguistic, ethnic, and regional diversity by giving distinct communities their own governmental units within a shared national framework. Switzerland's linguistic cantons, Canada's accommodation of Quebec, India's linguistic states, and Belgium's community and regional federalism all illustrate this use of territorial organization to manage diversity. The limits of territorial accommodation — particularly in cases of geographic intermingling of communities and when minority communities seek independence rather than autonomy — represent ongoing challenges in the most diverse political systems.
  • Sec 50.7The choice between centralization and decentralization involves genuine tradeoffs. Centralization promotes national coordination, policy equity across territory, economies of scale, and unified national standards. Decentralization promotes local responsiveness, democratic participation, policy experimentation, and a structural check on national power. Neither the case for centralization nor the case for decentralization is universally decisive; the appropriate balance depends on the specific problems a political community faces, its geographic and demographic characteristics, its administrative capacity at different levels, and its political culture. Intergovernmental relations — the cooperative and conflictual working relationships among governments at different levels — are the practical arena in which territorial systems are managed day to day.
  • Sec 50.8Choosing a territorial design requires matching constitutional arrangements to the specific needs, circumstances, and problems of a political community. No territorial design is universally superior; each represents a different set of tradeoffs appropriate for different contexts. Evaluation criteria include national unity, democratic representation, administrative efficiency, fiscal management, local responsiveness, constitutional stability, policy innovation, and adaptability. The comparative study of territorial systems provides a rich toolkit of institutional options and analytical frameworks for understanding how different countries have solved — and continue to negotiate — the challenge of governing complex, diverse, and territorially extended political communities.

Key Terms

Administrative decentralization
The transfer of administrative tasks — service delivery, implementation — to regional or local offices without transferring genuine decision-making authority; distinct from political decentralization.
Asymmetrical federalism
A federal or devolved arrangement in which different subnational units possess different powers or have different constitutional relationships with the national government.
Centralization
The concentration of decision-making authority in a national or central government, with limited independent authority at regional or local levels.
Concurrent powers
Powers that both national and subnational governments may exercise simultaneously; taxation is a classic example in most federal systems.
Confederation
A loose union of largely sovereign states that delegate limited powers to a common authority; the common authority derives its legitimacy from the member states rather than directly from citizens.
Cooperative federalism
A model of federal practice in which national and subnational governments work together on joint programs and policy implementation rather than maintaining strictly separate spheres of authority.
Decentralization
The distribution of authority, resources, or responsibility from a central government to regional or local governments; may be administrative or political.
Devolution
The transfer of legislative, executive, or administrative powers from a central government to regional governments by statute, within a formally unitary constitutional framework where the transferred powers could in principle be reclaimed.
Dual federalism
A model of federalism in which national and subnational governments operate in largely separate spheres of authority, each supreme within its own domain; often contrasted with cooperative federalism.
Federalism
A constitutional system in which authority is divided between a national government and subnational governments (states, provinces, etc.), each of which has independent democratic legitimacy and constitutionally protected powers.
Fiscal federalism
The intergovernmental arrangements by which revenue is assigned, expenditures allocated, and transfers structured among national and subnational governments in a territorial system.
Home rule
The authority of cities or municipalities to govern their own affairs without requiring specific authorization from higher levels of government for each action; the scope of home rule varies significantly across jurisdictions.
Intergovernmental relations
The working relationships — cooperative, competitive, or conflictual — among governments at different levels within a territorial system; the practical arena in which territorial arrangements are managed.
Intergovernmental transfers
Payments from one level of government to another, including grants, revenue-sharing, and equalization payments; a crucial mechanism for addressing fiscal imbalances in territorial systems.
Local government
The governments closest to citizens — municipalities, cities, counties, districts — responsible for delivering many everyday public services; generally created by and subordinate to state, provincial, or national law.
Metropolitan authority
A government body with jurisdiction over a large urban area that spans multiple municipal boundaries, designed to coordinate services and policy for an integrated metropolitan region.
Multinational state
A state containing multiple historically distinct national communities, each with a sense of collective identity, which may or may not be territorially concentrated; multinational states face distinctive challenges of territorial accommodation.
Political decentralization
The transfer of actual governing authority — the power to legislate, tax, and make binding decisions — to subnational governments with their own democratic mandates; distinct from administrative decentralization.
Regional autonomy
The authority of a regional government to govern its territory with significant independence from the national government, which may be constitutionally guaranteed (federal) or statutorily created (devolved).
Reserved powers
Powers allocated specifically to either the national government or subnational governments and not shared; in the US, the Tenth Amendment reserves to states or the people all powers not delegated to the federal government.
Secession
The withdrawal of a subnational unit from a larger political system to form an independent state; most federal constitutions do not recognize a unilateral right of secession.
Shared sovereignty
The defining feature of federalism in which both national and subnational governments possess genuine, constitutionally protected authority over their respective spheres, neither being purely subordinate to the other.
Subsidiarity
The principle that decisions should be made at the lowest level of government capable of making them effectively; an organizing principle in European Union law and in the constitutional traditions of several federal countries.
Territorial integrity
The principle that the territory of a state should not be divided without consent; often invoked in opposition to secessionist movements and recognized in international law as a fundamental attribute of sovereignty.
Unitary system
A constitutional system in which sovereignty is concentrated in the national government, which may delegate authority to regional and local governments but retains the constitutional right to modify or reclaim those powers.
Vertical fiscal imbalance
The condition in which one level of government collects significantly more revenue than it spends directly, while another level spends more than it collects; addressed through intergovernmental transfers but creates dependency and leverage relationships.

Historical and Political Context

The Tenth Amendment was ratified in 1791 as part of the Bill of Rights, reflecting concerns from Anti-Federalists that the new national government might expand its authority beyond its delegated powers. It was intended to reinforce the principle that the federal government has only the powers the Constitution grants it. The Fourteenth Amendment was ratified in 1868 following the Civil War and is one of the most consequential amendments in American constitutional history. It transformed the relationship between federal and state authority by making the national government the guarantor of individual rights against state-level violation.

Chapter 50 — Closing Reflection

Connecting the Chapter to the Larger Study of Government

This chapter has traced the vertical dimension of constitutional design — the question of how governments distribute authority not across branches but across levels. From the shared sovereignty of federalism to the delegated authority of devolution to the concentrated supremacy of unitary systems, territorial arrangements reflect fundamental choices about how political communities can be governed effectively, democratically, and justly across space. These choices are never purely technical. They are deeply political, reflecting the historical experiences, social compositions, institutional traditions, and power relationships of the communities that make them.

One of the most important lessons of comparative territorial analysis is that constitutional labels are less telling than institutional practice. A country that calls itself federal may be more centralized in practice than one that calls itself unitary; a country with devolved arrangements may grant its regions more genuine authority than many formal federal states do. Understanding territorial systems requires going beyond constitutional texts to examine how authority is actually exercised, how fiscal resources flow, how intergovernmental relationships function in practice, and how citizens actually experience the multiple layers of government that govern them. This kind of institutional realism — understanding how systems actually work rather than how they are formally designed — is the hallmark of sophisticated comparative analysis.

The diversity of territorial arrangements across the world's political systems also teaches something important about the relationship between institutional design and political culture. No territorial arrangement is self-sustaining; each requires the ongoing cooperation of political actors — elected officials, bureaucrats, party leaders, civil society organizations, and ordinary citizens — who must be willing to work within the system, respect its rules, and maintain its institutional norms even when it would be advantageous in the short term to circumvent them. German cooperative federalism works not only because the Basic Law requires it but because German political culture, party systems, and administrative traditions support it. Canadian federalism accommodates Quebec not only because the constitution permits provincial autonomy but because successive Canadian governments have made political choices to accommodate rather than suppress Quebec's distinctiveness. Institutions shape behavior, but behavior also shapes institutions.

As students turn to Chapter 51 and its country case studies, they carry with them the frameworks, concepts, and analytical tools developed throughout this textbook: the theory of political authority and legitimacy, constitutional design and interpretation, the structure of executive and legislative institutions, the rights and liberties that limit government power, the electoral and participatory systems through which citizens engage, the ideological traditions that frame political debate, the public policies that governments produce, and the international systems within which states operate. The study of territorial systems completes this picture by showing how authority is distributed not only functionally across branches but spatially across territory — a dimension of constitutional design that shapes everything from the daily services citizens receive to the deepest questions of national identity, regional autonomy, and the meaning of political membership in diverse communities.

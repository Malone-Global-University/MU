Chapter 24 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

On a Tuesday evening in early November, millions of voters in a democratic country walk into polling places, scan their ballots, and mark a name or a party symbol next to their preferred candidate. Most of those voters have made their choice long before reaching the booth. They know the party the candidate belongs to, roughly what that party stands for, and how much they trust the people who lead it. A voter who has supported the same party for thirty years may not know the candidate's biography in any detail. But they know the party — its positions on taxes, its record on health care, its attitude toward immigration, its historical coalition of supporters, and the kind of judges it tends to appoint. That familiarity, built up over years of political experience, is one of the most powerful forces in democratic politics. It is the work of political parties.

Political parties are among the most important and misunderstood institutions in democratic life. They are not governments themselves, but they shape who governs. They are not laws, but they produce the lawmakers who write the laws. They are not the whole of public opinion, but they organize and channel public opinion into elections, legislatures, and policy debates. Citizens often express frustration with political parties — they seem rigid, self-interested, polarizing, and slow to respond to genuine public concerns. Yet in nearly every functioning democracy, political parties remain indispensable. No other institution has yet replaced them as the primary mechanism for selecting candidates, organizing legislative action, connecting citizens to government, and providing accountability across elections.

Understanding political parties is essential for any serious student of government. Parties do not merely reflect political divisions that already exist; they help create and sustain those divisions. They decide who runs for office, what policy proposals become serious contenders, how legislative majorities are assembled, and what governments do once they take power. A country's party system — whether it has two dominant parties, many competing parties, or one party that tolerates no rivals — shapes the entire character of its political life.

This chapter explains what political parties are and why they exist. It traces how parties form, develop platforms, recruit members, nominate candidates, and organize legislative coalitions. It explains the major types of party systems — two-party systems, multiparty systems, dominant-party systems, and one-party states — and what each means for democracy. It examines how parties select candidates through primaries, conventions, and other nomination procedures. And it explores how parties contribute to democratic accountability and how they can sometimes undermine it. Throughout, comparisons between different countries and systems will help students see that party politics takes very different forms around the world, and that no single model defines what a party system must look like.

The study of parties is also a study in democratic theory. If democracy means that citizens govern themselves through their representatives, then parties are the primary machinery through which that ideal becomes practice — imperfect, contested, and sometimes disappointing practice, but practice nonetheless. Students who understand parties understand one of the central mechanisms through which democratic authority is both organized and challenged.

Section 24.1

What Political Parties Do

The Basic Definition

A political party is an organized group that contests elections with the goal of placing its members in public office and using governmental power to advance its preferred policies. This definition has several important components worth examining individually.

First, parties are organized. Unlike a spontaneous crowd at a public meeting or a loosely connected network of like-minded citizens, a political party has institutional structure. It has members, officers, committees, rules for selecting candidates, mechanisms for raising and spending money, and procedures for developing positions on public issues. That organizational infrastructure is what allows a party to persist across elections, to coordinate the work of politicians in different offices and different levels of government, and to develop an identity that voters can recognize and rely upon over time.

Second, parties contest elections. This is what most clearly distinguishes parties from interest groups, advocacy organizations, and social movements. An interest group may lobby politicians, fund campaigns, endorse candidates, or mobilize voters, but it does not run candidates under its own banner. When a party runs a candidate, it is putting its collective reputation on the line. If the candidate wins and governs badly, voters can punish the party at the next election. This accountability mechanism — however imperfect — is central to what parties contribute to democracy.

Third, parties seek governmental power. They are not satisfied with influencing government from the outside; they want to be inside it, controlling offices, writing laws, appointing officials, and implementing policy. This ambition distinguishes parties from purely advocacy-oriented organizations and explains why parties are willing to make compromises that pure advocates sometimes refuse. Winning requires broad coalitions, and building broad coalitions requires accepting positions one might not prefer in isolation.

The Core Functions of Political Parties

Political scientists have identified several core functions that parties perform in democratic systems. Understanding these functions helps explain why parties exist and why they are so difficult to eliminate or replace, despite the frustration they often provoke.

Recruiting and nominating candidates. Parties identify, support, and formally nominate candidates for public office. Without this function, elections would feature hundreds of self-declared candidates with no clear organizational backing, making it very difficult for voters to compare choices or hold anyone accountable. By certifying candidates, parties provide voters with a shortcut — a signal about what the candidate believes, who supports them, and what they are likely to do in office.

Organizing voter mobilization. Parties work to register voters, contact supporters, explain issues, encourage turnout, and provide information about how and where to vote. These mobilization activities increase political participation and connect citizens who might otherwise remain passive to the ongoing work of democratic governance.

Developing platforms and policy positions. Parties aggregate the preferences of their members and supporters into policy programs that can guide legislative action. A platform is not a binding contract, but it provides a framework within which candidates and elected officials make decisions, and it gives voters something concrete to evaluate when deciding how to vote.

Organizing government. Once elected, party members coordinate legislative activity. Parties determine committee assignments, elect legislative leaders, schedule votes, negotiate with other parties, and maintain enough cohesion to pass legislation or block it. Without party organization, legislatures would be chaotic collections of individual actors with no capacity for collective decision-making.

Providing accountability and choice. By offering voters distinct choices — different candidates, different platforms, different records — parties make electoral accountability possible. When a government fails, voters can throw the governing party out and try the opposing party. This "throwing the rascals out" mechanism is crude but powerful. It gives governing parties an incentive to perform and gives voters a meaningful mechanism for expressing discontent.

Educating and informing the public. Parties engage in political communication, explaining their positions, attacking their opponents' records, and framing public problems in particular ways. While parties are not neutral sources of information, their competition for voter support generates significant civic information and debate.

What Parties Are Not

Understanding parties also requires distinguishing them from things they are sometimes confused with.

Parties are not governments. A governing party is in power, but the government includes civil servants, independent courts, regulatory agencies, and other institutions that the party does not control. When a party loses an election, it leaves office, but the government continues.

Parties are not the same as political ideologies. A party typically espouses an ideology — a set of beliefs about the proper role of government — but parties are institutions, not ideas. An ideology can be held by individuals regardless of any party affiliation, and parties frequently modify their ideological positions over time in response to political pressure.

Parties are not interest groups. Both seek to influence government, but parties do so primarily through elections and office-holding, while interest groups do so primarily through lobbying and pressure. Interest groups do not typically nominate candidates or accept the electoral accountability that parties accept when they run for office.

Parties are not social movements. Movements seek to change public opinion, culture, and policy through organizing and advocacy. They may eventually influence parties — as labor movements, civil rights movements, and environmental movements have done — but they do not themselves seek governmental power through elections.

Section 24.2

Party Platforms and Political Identity

What Is a Party Platform?

A party platform is the official statement of a political party's positions on major issues of public concern. It represents an attempt to synthesize the views of party members, activists, elected officials, and allied groups into a coherent governing program. Platforms are typically adopted at party conventions and revised before each major election cycle.

In practice, platforms serve several overlapping purposes. They communicate the party's priorities to voters. They provide guidance to the party's elected officials when they must make legislative decisions. They help recruit activists and donors who share the party's commitments. And they create a basis for political accountability — allowing voters, journalists, and political opponents to assess whether a party's behavior in government matches its stated positions before the election.

How Platforms Are Developed

The platform development process varies across countries and parties, but in many democratic systems it is an exercise in internal party negotiation. Party activists, interest group allies, elected officials at various levels, and ordinary members all participate to some degree. The result is often a compromise document reflecting the internal balance of power within the party rather than a perfectly coherent ideological statement.

In the United States, the major parties adopt platforms at their national conventions every four years. Platform committees receive testimony from various groups, debate proposed planks, and eventually produce a document for convention delegates to approve. In many parliamentary systems, parties develop election manifestos — similar documents that form the basis of their campaign and, if they win a legislative majority or lead a coalition, their governing program.

Civic Vocabulary
Party Platform

A party platform is the official written statement of a political party's positions on public issues, typically adopted at a party convention. The term "platform" is metaphorical: a party "stands on" its platform the way a speaker stands on a physical platform. Platforms address major policy areas — taxes, health care, foreign policy, education, immigration, and others — and communicate the party's priorities and commitments to voters.

Platforms matter for two reasons. First, they inform voters about what a party intends to do if it wins power. Second, they create a basis for accountability — journalists, opponents, and citizens can compare what a party promised with what it actually did in office. Research shows that governing parties follow through on significant portions of their platform commitments more often than the public tends to assume, particularly in parliamentary systems where parties must govern in order to implement their programs.

Catch-All Parties and Programmatic Parties

Political scientists distinguish between different types of parties based on how they develop their platforms and relate to voters.

A programmatic party develops a coherent platform, communicates it clearly to voters, and attempts to implement it when in power. Voters for a programmatic party make a choice based on policy content — they vote for the party because they agree with its program. Programmatic parties tend to develop in conditions where voters are politically informed, elections are genuinely competitive, and parties have clear ideological distinctions.

A catch-all party attempts to appeal to as broad a spectrum of voters as possible, deliberately softening its ideological edges to maximize electoral support. Rather than leading with a specific program, it emphasizes leadership, competence, national unity, or general values that can attract voters from across the ideological spectrum. Many of the major parties in wealthy, stable democracies have moved in catch-all directions over the decades as their traditional class or religious bases have become less dominant.

A clientelistic party does not primarily compete through platforms or programs. Instead, it builds voter loyalty through the exchange of personal favors, material benefits, jobs, and services — distributing patronage to supporters in exchange for votes. Clientelism is widespread in many countries and is a rational response to specific institutional conditions where parties find it more efficient to distribute targeted benefits than to offer collective programs.

Section 24.3

Two-Party Systems

Defining the Two-Party System

A two-party system is a party system in which two major parties dominate electoral competition, routinely dividing most votes and most seats between themselves, and in which a third party has little or no realistic prospect of winning national power. The United States is the most prominent example in the world today. The Democratic Party and the Republican Party have dominated American national politics for well over 150 years. Other parties exist — the Libertarian Party, the Green Party, various regional and single-issue parties — but they have not won a presidential election or controlled Congress since the Republican Party itself emerged as a major party in the 1850s.

The United Kingdom is another example, though with some qualification. The Conservative Party and the Labour Party have alternated in government throughout most of the twentieth century and into the twenty-first. However, the Liberal Democrats and, in more recent decades, the Scottish National Party and other regional parties have won significant numbers of seats and complicated the traditional two-party dynamic, particularly in the devolved parliaments of Scotland and Wales.

Why Two-Party Systems Develop: Duverger's Law

The most influential explanation for why some countries develop two-party systems is known as Duverger's Law, named for the French political scientist Maurice Duverger, who articulated it in the mid-twentieth century. Duverger observed that electoral systems using single-member districts and plurality voting — in which the candidate with the most votes wins the single seat, regardless of whether they have a majority — tend to produce two dominant parties over time.

The logic runs as follows: in a single-member district with plurality voting, only one candidate can win. If a voter prefers a third party that is unlikely to win, their vote produces no representation at all. Rational voters face a strong incentive to vote for whichever of the two leading candidates they find least objectionable, even if they would prefer a third option. This "wasted vote" logic gradually erodes third-party support over time. Candidates and donors similarly have incentives to concentrate on the two viable parties, because investment in a losing third party yields nothing. Over time, two parties tend to crowd out the alternatives.

Duverger's Law is not a perfect predictor — there are important exceptions, and the relationship between electoral rules and party systems is more complex than the simple law suggests. But it captures an important general tendency that helps explain why countries using single-member plurality systems tend to have fewer significant parties than countries using proportional representation.

Advantages of the Two-Party System

Clarity of choice. Voters face a relatively simple decision between two clearly defined alternatives. There is less complexity in determining what each party stands for, what coalition government might form, or who is responsible for policy failures.

Stable, single-party government. In a two-party system, one party typically wins a majority of seats and governs without needing to negotiate a coalition. This tends to produce more stable governments with clear lines of responsibility. When things go well, the governing party receives credit; when things go badly, voters know exactly whom to blame.

Governmental capacity. Governments with a legislative majority can more easily pass legislation, implement policy, and respond to emergencies without the complexity of coalition negotiations.

Disadvantages of the Two-Party System

Voter suppression and unrepresented views. Many voters have political views that do not fit neatly into the positions offered by two parties. Two-party systems leave these voters choosing the lesser of two evils rather than a genuine expression of their preferences.

Wasted votes. In a two-party plurality system, voters in "safe" districts — constituencies where one party reliably wins — have little meaningful choice regardless of which party they support, which reduces the incentive to participate.

Polarization. Two-party competition can encourage polarization, as each party seeks to mobilize its base rather than appeal to undecided voters. If elections are decided by turnout among committed partisans rather than persuasion of swing voters, parties have incentives to become more extreme rather than more moderate.

Barriers to new ideas. Two-party systems create significant structural barriers to new ideas, social movements, and emerging political forces. A movement that grows rapidly in influence may find it very difficult to achieve electoral representation unless it is absorbed into one of the two existing parties.

Section 24.4

Multiparty Systems

What Makes a System Multiparty?

A multiparty system is a party system in which three or more parties regularly win significant numbers of legislative seats and in which no single party can typically win a legislative majority on its own. Multiparty systems are far more common globally than two-party systems. The Netherlands, Germany, Italy, Sweden, Denmark, Israel, Belgium, the Czech Republic, New Zealand, and many other countries regularly feature elections in which four, five, six, or more parties win seats in the national legislature.

The diversity among multiparty systems is significant. Some have a single large party that regularly leads governments, supported by smaller coalition partners — this describes Germany for much of the postwar period. Others feature genuine multiparty fragmentation, with no party typically winning more than a quarter of the vote, requiring large and complex coalitions — this describes Israel, the Netherlands, and Italy at various points in their histories.

How Proportional Representation Encourages Multiparty Systems

The most important institutional driver of multiparty systems is proportional representation (PR). Under PR electoral systems, parties win seats roughly in proportion to the share of votes they receive. A party that wins 15 percent of the vote wins roughly 15 percent of the seats, and a party that wins 3 percent may still cross the threshold needed to win some representation.

Because no vote is "wasted" in a pure PR system, voters have less incentive to vote strategically for a large party they find acceptable over a small party they genuinely prefer. As a result, small parties survive and grow in PR systems in ways that are structurally much harder under single-member plurality rules. PR systems also encourage the survival of parties based on specific religious communities, regional identities, ethnic or linguistic groups, or narrow ideological traditions.

Coalition Government in Multiparty Systems

The most significant consequence of multiparty systems for democratic governance is the need for coalition government. When no single party wins a legislative majority, parties must negotiate agreements with other parties to form a government. These negotiations determine who becomes prime minister, how cabinet posts are distributed, and what policy priorities the coalition will pursue.

Coalition negotiations can be complex, time-consuming, and unpredictable. After a German federal election, for instance, it is common for weeks or months to pass while parties negotiate the terms of a coalition agreement — a detailed document specifying what the participating parties have agreed to support and avoid during the government's term. The Netherlands took 271 days to form a government after its 2021 election.

Once formed, coalition governments must maintain internal cohesion across parties with different priorities and voter bases. This requires ongoing negotiation, compromise, and management of internal disagreements. When a coalition breaks down — when one party withdraws from the agreement — the government may fall and new elections may be required.

Comparative Lens
United States versus Germany: Two Models of Party Competition
United States — Two-Party System

Single-member plurality rules. Democratic and Republican parties dominate at every level. Third parties occasionally win local offices or affect outcomes as spoilers, but do not govern nationally. Produces clear, unified governments — but voters with views crossing party lines must compromise or abstain. The two parties must each be enormous, diverse coalitions.

Germany — Multiparty System

Mixed electoral system (single-member districts plus PR). A 5 percent threshold prevents excessive fragmentation. Social Democrats, Christian Democrats, Greens, Free Democrats, Left Party, and Alternative for Germany have all won Bundestag seats. Governments are routinely coalitions. Broader political diversity represented — but coalition formation can take months.

The comparison reveals genuine tradeoffs. American elections produce clear, unified governments with strong mandates — when they work well. German elections produce governments representing broader political diversity and requiring compromise — when they work well. Neither system is simply better. Each reflects its country's electoral rules, constitutional structure, historical experience, and political culture. Students of comparative politics should approach these differences as problems to analyze, not as occasions to declare a winner.

Advantages and Disadvantages of Multiparty Systems

Advantages: Greater voter representation — more voters can cast ballots for parties that genuinely represent their views. Accommodation of diversity in countries with significant ethnic, linguistic, religious, or regional complexity. Consensual governance — coalition negotiations can produce more moderate, broadly supported policies. And a more accurate translation of public opinion into legislative composition.

Disadvantages: Complexity and instability in forming and maintaining coalition governments. Diffuse accountability — when a government is formed by three or four parties, it can be difficult for voters to assign responsibility for policy success or failure. Small-party leverage — a very small party holding the balance of power may extract disproportionate concessions. And slower decision-making when coalition negotiations delay urgent action.

Section 24.5

Dominant-Party and One-Party Systems

The Dominant-Party System

A dominant-party system is one in which competitive elections occur and other parties are legally permitted, but one party consistently wins and governs for long periods, often decades. Dominant-party systems differ from one-party states because opposition parties exist, contest elections, and win some seats — but they are unable to challenge the dominant party's control of government in practice.

Japan from the end of World War II until 1993 is a prominent example. The Liberal Democratic Party (LDP) governed Japan continuously for nearly four decades, occasionally interrupted by brief opposition governments. The LDP's dominance was sustained by its organizational strength in rural areas, its management of Japan's postwar economic development, a fragmented opposition, and an electoral system that initially favored the party's geographic distribution of support.

Mexico under the Institutional Revolutionary Party (PRI) is another frequently cited example. The PRI governed Mexico from 1929 until 2000, when Vicente Fox of the National Action Party won the presidency. During its long dominance, the PRI was sometimes described as a case of "perfect dictatorship" — it maintained the forms of competitive elections while using patronage, media control, and institutional advantages to prevent any genuine threat to its power.

South Africa under the African National Congress (ANC) since the end of apartheid in 1994 represents a more recent dominant-party system in a context where the party carries enormous historical legitimacy but where opposition voices have grown stronger over time. The ANC won large majorities in the early post-apartheid elections but has seen declining support in more recent contests, suggesting that even dominant parties are not immune to accountability over time.

One-Party States

A one-party state is a political system in which only a single political party is legally permitted to hold power. Opposition parties are banned, suppressed, or rendered meaningless. Elections may be held, but they offer no genuine choice. The one-party state is a form of authoritarian government.

China under the Chinese Communist Party (CCP) is the world's most prominent contemporary one-party state. The CCP has governed China since 1949. No other party is permitted to challenge its authority. Elections occur at the local level, but candidates must be approved by the party, and national leaders are selected through internal party processes rather than competitive elections. The CCP justifies its monopoly on power through a combination of claims: historical leadership of the revolution, economic development success, Marxist-Leninist ideology, and the argument that competitive party politics would produce instability.

Students should understand that one-party states are qualitatively different from dominant-party democracies. In a dominant-party system, opposition parties exist, compete, and occasionally win. In a one-party state, opposition is prohibited. The absence of genuine electoral competition is not merely a practical disadvantage but a fundamental feature of the system's design. Chapter 48 of this textbook examines authoritarian systems in much greater depth.

Civic Vocabulary
Dominant-Party System vs. One-Party State

A dominant-party system maintains competitive elections — other parties legally exist, contest seats, and occasionally win them — but one party consistently forms the government over long periods. Accountability through elections is weak but real: the dominant party can, in principle, lose. A one-party state prohibits genuine competition entirely. No meaningful opposition exists, and elections (if held at all) are rituals of affirmation rather than genuine choices. The distinction matters enormously for democratic theory, even though the line between them can grow blurry when a dominant party accumulates enough structural advantages to effectively suppress competition without formally banning it.

Section 24.6

Party Coalitions and Realignment

What Is a Party Coalition?

A political coalition, in the context of party politics, is the collection of social groups, geographic regions, demographic communities, and political movements that constitute a party's reliable base of support. A coalition is not simply a list of voters; it is a set of relationships between the party and the groups it represents and relies upon, built over time through policy commitments, symbolic recognition, organizational ties, and shared political identity.

In a democracy with competitive elections, winning requires assembling a coalition large enough to win a majority — or at least a plurality — of votes. Because no single demographic group, region, or interest typically constitutes a majority on its own, parties must bring together multiple groups with somewhat different priorities and interests. The management of these internal differences is one of the central ongoing challenges of party politics.

Coalition Building and Policy Tradeoffs

Because a coalition is a combination of groups with somewhat different priorities, party leadership must constantly make decisions about how to satisfy different parts of its base. A party's union members may favor trade protectionism that its business supporters oppose. A party's religious conservatives may hold social positions that its libertarian members resist. A party's environmental wing may push policies that its working-class industrial voters fear. Managing these tensions without alienating key groups is one of the most demanding aspects of party leadership.

These internal tensions explain much of the internal party conflict that citizens observe and sometimes find confusing. When politicians from the same party argue publicly about policy, they are often navigating disagreements within the party's coalition. When party leaders take seemingly inconsistent positions over time, they may be responding to shifts in the coalition's priorities or to the demands of appealing to new groups while retaining established ones.

Party Realignment

Party realignment is a major, durable shift in the composition of a party's coalition and, often, in the overall structure of party competition. Realignment occurs when significant groups of voters move from one party to another — not just in a single election, but in a lasting way that restructures the basic geography of party support.

The most studied realignment in American political history is the New Deal realignment of the 1930s. Franklin Roosevelt's New Deal programs shifted the existing coalition dramatically, bringing organized labor, African Americans (a shift accelerated during World War II), and many urban working-class voters into a stable Democratic coalition that persisted for decades.

The subsequent realignment of the South is equally significant. From the Civil War through the mid-twentieth century, the South was solidly Democratic — so reliably that it was called the "Solid South." The Democratic Party's support for civil rights legislation in the 1960s — particularly the Civil Rights Act of 1964 and the Voting Rights Act of 1965 — triggered a major partisan shift. Over the following decades, white Southern voters moved increasingly toward the Republican Party, and the South transformed into one of the most reliably Republican regions of the country.

Realignments do not happen overnight. They typically unfold over a decade or more as individual voters update their party identifications, as older voters who identified strongly with one party die and are replaced by younger voters with different attachments, and as parties themselves make strategic adjustments to appeal to new constituencies.

Coalition Instability and Dealignment

Not all shifts in party support constitute full realignments. Dealignment refers to a weakening of partisan identification across the population — a situation in which more voters identify as independent rather than partisan, split their tickets across parties, and make electoral choices based on candidates and issues rather than party loyalty. Some political scientists argue that many contemporary democracies are experiencing dealignment rather than realignment: voters have not decisively shifted from one party to another so much as they have become more loosely attached to parties in general.

This loosening of partisan identification has significant consequences for politics. It makes elections more volatile and less predictable, increases the importance of candidate quality and short-term issues, and makes it harder for parties to maintain stable governing coalitions. It may also encourage parties to focus more on media strategy and personality politics than on policy platforms, since the voters they most need to win are less likely to be moved by platform content than by candidate appeal.

Section 24.7

Primaries, Nominations, and Candidate Selection

Why Nomination Matters

Nomination is the process by which a political party selects the candidate who will represent it in a general election. The nomination process is, in an important sense, the first election — the internal competition within a party that determines which individual will carry the party's banner. Because parties set the menu of choices for general elections, whoever controls the nomination process shapes the range of options available to voters.

For most of democratic history, nominations were controlled by small groups of party insiders — professional politicians, organizational leaders, and influential supporters who negotiated over candidates in informal settings. In the United States, this produced the famous "smoke-filled room" — the backroom meetings at party conventions where deals were struck and candidates selected. In many other democracies, party leadership and parliamentary caucuses still play the primary role in determining who runs under the party label.

Types of Primary Elections

A primary election is a preliminary election in which voters choose among candidates seeking a party's nomination for a general election. Primary elections take several forms, each with different rules about who can participate.

A closed primary allows only registered members of the party to vote. If you are registered as a Democrat, you can vote in the Democratic primary but not the Republican primary, and vice versa. Closed primaries give more power to committed party members and activists, who tend to hold more ideologically intense views than the general electorate.

An open primary allows any registered voter to participate in either party's primary, regardless of their own partisan registration. A registered independent, or even a registered member of one party, can choose to vote in the other party's primary. Open primaries give more influence to moderate and independent voters, potentially producing nominees more appealing to the general electorate.

A semi-open or semi-closed primary allows registered party members to vote in their party's primary and may allow unaffiliated or independent voters to choose which party's primary to participate in, but prohibits voters registered in one party from voting in the other party's primary. Some states use a blanket or jungle primary in which all candidates from all parties appear on a single ballot and the top two vote-getters advance to a general election, regardless of party.

How the System Works
Presidential Nomination in the United States
  • Step 1 Candidates announce and begin campaigning. Candidates who seek the presidential nomination of a major party typically announce months or years before the first state contests. They begin fundraising, building staff organizations, visiting early-primary states, and seeking media attention and endorsements. The number of candidates in a competitive primary season can reach into the double digits.
  • Step 2 States hold primaries and caucuses. Beginning in January of a presidential election year, states hold primaries or caucuses to allocate delegates to the national convention. Iowa and New Hampshire have traditionally held the earliest contests, giving them outsized influence. Other states follow in a calendar that extends through June. Candidates who perform strongly in early contests tend to attract more media attention, endorsements, and donations, building momentum. Those who perform poorly often drop out.
  • Step 3 Delegates are allocated. Each state contest allocates delegates to the national convention according to complex rules. Democratic Party rules require proportional allocation. Republican Party rules vary by state and in some cases allow winner-take-all allocation. The total delegate count across all states determines who will be nominated.
  • Step 4 The national convention. In late summer, the party holds its national convention. Delegates vote to nominate the presidential candidate. In most recent cycles, a single candidate has clinched a delegate majority before the convention, making the convention's vote a formality. In contested conventions — where no candidate has clinched a majority — delegates negotiate and vote repeatedly until one candidate wins. The convention also formally adopts the party platform and nominates the vice-presidential candidate.
  • Step 5 The general election campaign. After nomination, the general election campaign begins. The nominees of the major parties face each other (and occasionally minor-party candidates) in a national campaign culminating in Election Day in November, and ultimately in the Electoral College vote that formally determines the winner.

Complications and tradeoffs: The nomination process has been criticized for giving disproportionate influence to small, unrepresentative early-primary states; for rewarding ideological intensity over general-election electability; for requiring massive fundraising that advantages wealthy candidates; and for producing a months-long spectacle that may not reliably identify the best nominees. Reformers have proposed various changes, including rotating the order of early states, replacing all primaries with a national primary, or returning more power to party leaders who can evaluate candidates more thoroughly. Each proposed reform has its own tradeoffs.

Nomination in Parliamentary Systems

In parliamentary systems, the candidate selection process works quite differently. Parties typically control candidate selection more directly, and voters have less formal influence over who runs under each party's label.

In the United Kingdom, for instance, candidates for parliamentary constituencies are formally selected by local party associations — the party members in each district who choose the person they want to stand for Parliament. The central party leadership may have varying degrees of influence over this process — sometimes vetting candidates or placing people on approved lists — but formal selection authority traditionally rests with local members.

In some countries, party leadership plays a more direct role. In closed party-list PR systems, where voters vote for a party rather than an individual candidate and seats are allocated proportionally, the order of names on the party's list is critical. The party leadership or an internal party body decides this order, effectively determining which candidates will win seats if the party does well. Candidates placed high on the list are virtually guaranteed seats; those placed low are unlikely to win. This gives party leadership considerable power to reward loyalty and punish dissent.

Section 24.8

Parties and Democratic Accountability

The Accountability Function

Democratic accountability — the ability of citizens to hold their government responsible for its actions and replace those who fail — is among the most important features that distinguish democratic from nondemocratic systems. Political parties are the primary instrument of democratic accountability in most electoral systems.

The logic of party accountability runs as follows: parties nominate candidates, those candidates campaign on platforms, voters choose between competing platforms and parties, the winning party governs, and voters evaluate the governing party's performance at the next election, rewarding success with continued power and punishing failure with removal from office. This "electoral accountability" mechanism gives governing parties a strong incentive to perform well and to respond to public opinion.

For this mechanism to work, certain conditions must be met. Voters must be able to identify which party is responsible for outcomes. Party platforms must be coherent enough that electoral victory implies some policy commitment. The governing party must actually attempt to implement its commitments. And the opposition must be a credible alternative capable of replacing the government if voters choose to remove it. When these conditions are met, party competition can provide powerful accountability.

Party Discipline and Legislative Cohesion

For parties to provide effective governmental accountability, they need a reasonable degree of internal discipline — the ability to coordinate the votes and behavior of their elected members. A party whose legislative members vote in conflicting ways provides no clear accountability signal to voters.

Party discipline varies significantly across systems. Parliamentary systems, in which the government depends on maintaining a legislative majority, tend to produce high party discipline: MPs who defect from the party line may be denied reappointment, stripped of committee positions, or face electoral consequences at the next election. In the United Kingdom, Canada, and Australia, parliamentary parties are generally highly disciplined, with individual MPs rarely voting against their party on important matters.

The United States features somewhat lower party discipline in historical perspective, though partisan voting in Congress has increased significantly in recent decades. Individual senators and representatives have traditionally maintained more independent positions, and party leaders have fewer formal mechanisms to compel compliance than parliamentary leaders. Nevertheless, modern American legislative parties vote together at very high rates on major partisan legislation.

Institution in Practice
The Party Whip System

In legislative bodies around the world, parties maintain internal discipline and coordination through an institution known as the whip system. The term "whip" comes from fox hunting, where a "whipper-in" was responsible for keeping the hounds together — and the metaphor captures something of the function, though modern whips operate through persuasion, information, and selective rewards rather than literal control.

In the United States Congress, each party in both the House and Senate maintains a whip organization. The majority whip and minority whip are among the most powerful party leaders in the chamber. Their primary responsibilities include counting votes — determining in advance how members intend to vote on upcoming legislation — and ensuring that party members who are committed to support or oppose a bill actually show up and vote accordingly. The whip office also communicates the legislative schedule to members, explains the party's position on upcoming votes, and flags which votes are considered most important to the party leadership.

In the United Kingdom's House of Commons, the whip system is more formalized and carries more direct consequences. Party whips issue weekly documents (also called "whips") to MPs indicating which votes are most important. A "three-line whip" is the strongest direction — MPs are expected to attend and vote with the party, and failure to do so is treated as a serious breach of party discipline. MPs who repeatedly defy the whip may lose committee positions, face challenges in their constituency reselection, or in extreme cases be expelled from the parliamentary party.

The whip system illustrates the tension between party discipline and individual representation. Party discipline is necessary for coherent governance — a party that cannot coordinate its members' votes cannot govern. But legislators are also individual representatives who were elected by specific constituencies with specific concerns, and those concerns may not always align with the party line. How individual legislators navigate this tension — when to follow the party and when to break ranks — is one of the ongoing dilemmas of representative government.

When Parties Weaken Accountability

Parties are not always reliable instruments of accountability. Several dynamics can weaken or distort the accountability function.

Safe seats and guaranteed majorities. In highly gerrymandered or naturally one-party districts, the governing party may face no meaningful electoral competition. Politicians in such districts are accountable to their primary electorate — which may be a small, ideologically intense minority — rather than to the broader public.

Long-term dominance and clientelism. When a single party dominates for decades, it accumulates structural advantages that make it increasingly difficult for opposition to compete on equal terms. Patronage networks, control of media and public institutions, and the entrenchment of party supporters throughout the bureaucracy can insulate a dominant party from the accountability mechanism.

Partisan polarization and negative partisanship. When voters are motivated primarily by hostility to the opposing party rather than by support for their own party's performance, accountability is distorted. If voters would support their party regardless of how poorly it governed, simply because the alternative is worse, the governing party faces little incentive to perform well.

Opaque party finances. When parties can raise and spend large amounts of money from undisclosed sources, parties may become more accountable to their major donors than to their voters.

Factionalism and incoherence. When parties are deeply internally divided, party accountability becomes murky. Voters cannot hold the party responsible for a coherent set of decisions if the party itself was unable to make coherent decisions.

Parties and the Health of Democracy

The relationship between political parties and democratic health is neither simply positive nor simply negative. Healthy parties — competitive, ideologically coherent enough to be accountable, organizationally robust enough to mobilize voters and train leaders, internally democratic enough to represent their members — are a significant asset to democratic governance. Unhealthy parties — internally corrupt, controlled by narrow factions, financed by opaque money, insulated from electoral accountability — can be a significant threat.

Political scientists who study democratic backsliding often point to the manipulation of party competition as an early indicator. When governing parties change electoral rules to favor themselves, restrict opposition parties' access to ballots or media, use state resources for partisan purposes, or undermine the independence of electoral commissions and courts, they are not merely using parties to govern; they are weaponizing parties against democracy itself. Understanding this distinction — between parties as instruments of democratic governance and parties as potential instruments of democratic erosion — is essential for students who seek to be informed citizens.

Primary Source Window
George Washington's Farewell Address (1796) — Warning Against Political Parties
Context

In September 1796, as he prepared to leave the presidency after two terms, George Washington published his Farewell Address — a document addressed to the American people laying out his reflections on the challenges facing the new republic. One of its most famous sections warned against the dangers of political parties, which Washington called "factions."

Paraphrase of Relevant Passage

Washington described the "spirit of party" as demanding constant vigilance. He argued that political parties would inevitably cultivate leaders who used parties to advance their own ambitions rather than the public good, would create unnecessary divisions among citizens, inflame passions against opposing groups, and provide opportunities for foreign powers to interfere in American politics by playing factions against one another. He acknowledged that the spirit of party might be unavoidable in popular governments, but counseled that wise citizens should work to diminish and restrain its effects rather than embrace and amplify them.

Analysis

Washington's warning reflects a view common among many of the founding generation — that organized political competition was a dangerous feature of popular government. What is notable in historical perspective is how quickly his warning was overtaken by events. Within years of his Farewell Address, the foundations of the first American party system had been laid, with Federalists and Democratic-Republicans competing vigorously. The founders' anti-party idealism collided with the practical realities of democratic politics: people who agree on some things and disagree on others will organize, and organized groups that seek political power become parties.

Questions for Students
  1. What specific dangers does Washington associate with political parties? Which of these concerns, if any, seem relevant to contemporary party politics?
  2. Washington wrote at a time before political parties existed in the United States. What institutions or mechanisms did he seem to think would organize democratic competition without parties?
  3. How does Washington's warning about parties connect to the concept of democratic accountability? Can accountability exist without partisan competition?
Civic Debate
Should Primary Elections Be Open or Closed?
The Issue

In systems that use primary elections to select party nominees, one of the most contested design questions is who should be allowed to vote. Should primaries be open to all voters regardless of party registration? Or should they be restricted to registered party members?

Argument A — In Favor of Open Primaries

Supporters of open primaries argue that they produce candidates more likely to win general elections and govern effectively. Because primary electorates in closed systems tend to be smaller, more partisan, and more ideologically intense than the general electorate, closed primaries may systematically produce nominees who are more extreme than the median voter. This, in turn, may contribute to polarization. Open primaries allow the moderate, independent voters who often determine general elections to shape the nomination as well, potentially producing more electable candidates with broader appeal. Advocates also argue that independent voters — whose numbers have grown significantly in many democracies — have a legitimate interest in participating in nomination decisions that effectively determine who governs.

Argument B — In Favor of Closed Primaries

Supporters of closed primaries argue that parties are private organizations with the legitimate right to determine their own membership and choose their own leaders. Requiring party registration as a condition of voting in a party's primary ensures that nominees are chosen by those who have made a genuine commitment to the party. Open primaries, critics argue, invite strategic manipulation: members of one party can vote in the opposing party's primary to choose the weakest or most extreme opponent. They also dilute the voice of committed party members, making parties less coherent and less responsive to their actual membership.

Tradeoffs

The open-versus-closed debate involves genuine tradeoffs between competing values: the internal self-governance of parties versus the broad civic participation of all voters; the coherence of party programs versus the responsiveness of nominees to general electorates; protection from strategic manipulation versus inclusion of independents. There is no empirical consensus on whether open or closed primaries produce more moderate candidates, since many factors beyond primary rules affect nominee ideological positioning.

Civic Reasoning Question: Given the tradeoffs described above, what type of primary election rules would you recommend, and why? What values does your recommendation prioritize, and what does it sacrifice? Are there other structural changes that might address the concerns you find most compelling?
Rights and Responsibilities
Civic Participation and Political Parties

The right to form parties. Freedom of political association — the right to form, join, and participate in political parties — is a fundamental democratic right. In healthy democracies, the government cannot ban parties simply because their views are unpopular or because they challenge those in power. The Supreme Court has recognized that political association is protected by the First Amendment in the United States, and similar protections exist in constitutional democracies around the world.

The responsibility to be an informed voter. Citizens in democracies have the civic responsibility to make electoral choices on some basis of information — to understand, at least roughly, what the parties they support stand for, what their records indicate, and what their stated commitments are. Informed voters who evaluate parties on their records and platforms create stronger accountability incentives than voters who support parties regardless of performance.

The responsibility to engage beyond voting. Party organizations depend on citizens who do more than vote every few years. Volunteers canvass neighborhoods, staff phone banks, attend precinct meetings, participate in platform development, and perform the organizational work that keeps parties functioning. Citizens who participate actively in party organizations shape those organizations from the inside — selecting candidates, developing platforms, and building the coalitions that determine what the party stands for.

The responsibility to remain civically independent. There is a tension in democratic citizenship between loyalty to a party and independence of judgment. Strong party loyalty makes democratic accountability possible — if voters abandoned parties at the slightest difficulty, the accountability mechanism would not work. But excessive tribalism — supporting a party's candidates and positions regardless of their actual performance or merit — surrenders independent judgment to group identity. Thoughtful democratic citizens maintain their own assessment of policy and performance even as they work with and support parties that generally represent their values.

Policy Lab
Reforming the Financing of Political Parties
Scenario

Political parties require financial resources to operate — to recruit candidates, develop platforms, train organizers, communicate with voters, conduct campaigns, and maintain offices between elections. In virtually every democracy, there is ongoing debate about how party financing should be structured, regulated, and disclosed. How a party raises its money affects who it is accountable to, which voices it hears most clearly, and whether it operates on a level playing field with other parties.

Stakeholders

Political parties themselves (which have varying interests based on their fundraising strengths), wealthy individual donors, corporations and business associations, labor unions, grassroots small donors, ordinary voters, media organizations, civic advocacy groups, election administrators, constitutional lawyers, and academic researchers studying democratic governance.

Policy Options
  1. Public financing of parties and campaigns. Provide government funding to parties that meet threshold requirements, reducing dependence on private donors. May be combined with spending limits and bans or caps on private contributions.
  2. Expanded disclosure requirements. Require rapid, comprehensive disclosure of all significant contributions to parties and affiliated groups, without limiting the amounts that can be raised or spent. Transparency allows voters to evaluate who is funding whom.
  3. Small-donor matching. Provide government matching funds for small donations, encouraging parties and candidates to build broad bases of small donors rather than depending on large contributions.
  4. Strict caps with enforcement. Impose strict limits on contributions to parties and candidates from any source, with strong enforcement mechanisms and meaningful penalties for violations.
  5. Status quo or deregulation. Argue that existing rules are adequate or that further regulation creates more problems than it solves, potentially restricting political speech.
Evidence Needed

Students examining this issue would need to consider: Does money translate directly into electoral outcomes, or is the relationship more complex? Do countries with public financing have more or less competitive elections? Do disclosure requirements change donor behavior? How do courts in different countries treat campaign finance regulation as a constitutional matter?

Tradeoffs

Each option involves tradeoffs. Public financing requires taxpayer support for parties voters may strongly dislike. Disclosure may deter some legitimate donors who fear retaliation. Spending limits may disadvantage challengers (who need more money to overcome incumbents' name recognition) or create incentives for evasion through nominally independent organizations. Small-donor matching is popular but does not eliminate large-donor influence entirely.

Student Task

Select one of the policy options above and write a brief defense of it, explaining what values it prioritizes, what tradeoffs you accept, and how you would respond to the strongest criticism of your chosen approach.

Chapter 24 — Back Matter

Common Misunderstandings

Misunderstanding 1
Political parties are the same as special interest groups.
Correction
Parties and interest groups are related but distinct. Interest groups focus on influencing policy on specific issues and do not typically nominate candidates or seek to win governmental power themselves. Parties contest elections, nominate candidates, seek to control government, and take positions across the full range of public policy. Interest groups often work with and through parties, but they are not the same institution.
Misunderstanding 2
In a two-party system, both parties represent the same interests and offer voters no real choice.
Correction
Two-party systems do narrow the range of official choices compared to multiparty systems. But the two parties in systems like the United States typically do represent meaningfully different coalitions, policy priorities, and governing philosophies. The differences between major parties on taxes, health care, immigration, environmental policy, judicial appointments, and international relations are substantial, even if the available choices do not satisfy every voter.
Misunderstanding 3
A party platform is a binding promise — politicians who don't follow their platform are lying.
Correction
Platforms are statements of intent and priority, not legally binding contracts. Governing parties face multiple obstacles to platform implementation: legislative resistance, constitutional limits, fiscal constraints, changed circumstances, coalition negotiations, and unanticipated events. Research shows that parties follow through on significant portions of their platforms in favorable conditions, but complete platform implementation is rare in any democratic system.
Misunderstanding 4
Third parties cannot succeed in the United States because the system is "rigged" against them.
Correction
Third parties in the United States face structural disadvantages rooted in electoral rules — particularly single-member districts with plurality voting — that make it very difficult to win seats even with substantial vote shares. This is not primarily the result of active manipulation, but of institutional design. Duverger's Law explains why these rules tend to produce two dominant parties. Electoral reforms like proportional representation, ranked-choice voting, or instant-runoff voting would reduce these barriers.
Misunderstanding 5
Political parties are responsible for "polarization" and were less divisive in the past.
Correction
Party competition has always involved conflict and disagreement; past eras were not uniformly more civil or cooperative. The mid-twentieth century United States had substantial cross-party cooperation in part because both major parties contained large internal factions spanning the ideological spectrum, but this also reflected the suppression of African Americans' political voice in the South and other forms of exclusion that reduced visible conflict. Contemporary polarization is real and measurable, but it has multiple causes — geographic sorting, media fragmentation, economic inequality, and institutional changes — and cannot be attributed solely to parties.
Misunderstanding 6
Voting for a third party is "throwing your vote away."
Correction
This claim reflects a specific electoral logic — that in a plurality system, a vote for a candidate unlikely to win does not contribute to electing anyone. This logic is real and explains why many voters engage in strategic voting. However, third-party votes can affect electoral outcomes as spoilers, can signal public demand for policy alternatives, can contribute to the long-term growth of alternative parties, and may, in some contexts, be the most honest expression of a voter's actual preferences. Whether to vote sincerely or strategically is a genuine choice involving real tradeoffs.
Misunderstanding 7
Party discipline is undemocratic because legislators should always vote their conscience.
Correction
Party discipline and democratic legitimacy are in genuine tension, but they are not simply opposed. Party discipline allows voters to hold parties accountable as collective entities, which is how electoral accountability generally works. A legislature full of independent actors who vote their conscience on every issue provides no party-level accountability signal to voters. The tension between following the party line and representing specific constituents or personal convictions is a real and legitimate challenge for individual legislators, but party discipline itself is not inherently undemocratic.
Misunderstanding 8
Dominant-party systems are basically the same as one-party states.
Correction
There is a meaningful difference between a system where one party consistently wins competitive elections — because it is genuinely popular, well-organized, and faces a fragmented opposition — and a system where competing parties are legally prohibited or actively suppressed. Dominant-party systems may raise concerns about accountability and fairness, and the line between them and soft authoritarianism can become blurry over time, but they are not equivalent to one-party states. Elections matter even when one party usually wins them, because the possibility of losing is real and constrains how the dominant party governs.

Chapter Summary

  • Core Definition A political party is an organized group that contests elections with the goal of placing its members in public office and using governmental power to advance preferred policies. Parties are distinct from governments, ideologies, interest groups, and social movements, each of which serves different functions in political life.
  • Core Functions Parties perform six essential functions in democratic systems: recruiting and nominating candidates, organizing voter mobilization, developing policy platforms, organizing government through legislative coordination, providing electoral accountability and choice, and educating and informing the public through political communication and competition.
  • Party Platforms Platforms are official statements of a party's policy positions, typically adopted at party conventions. They communicate priorities to voters, guide elected officials, recruit activists and donors, and create a basis for post-election accountability. Parties vary from programmatic (policy-driven) to catch-all (broad appeal) to clientelistic (patronage-based).
  • Two-Party Systems Two-party systems feature two dominant parties dividing most votes and seats between them. They tend to arise under single-member plurality electoral rules (explained by Duverger's Law). Advantages include clarity of choice and strong governmental capacity; disadvantages include underrepresentation of minority views, wasted votes, and potential polarization.
  • Multiparty Systems Multiparty systems feature three or more parties regularly winning significant seats, typically requiring coalition governments. They tend to arise under proportional representation electoral rules. Advantages include broader voter representation and accommodation of diverse communities; disadvantages include coalition complexity, diffuse accountability, and potential instability.
  • Dominant-Party & One-Party Dominant-party systems maintain competitive elections while one party consistently governs. One-party states prohibit meaningful competition entirely and represent authoritarian government. The distinction is fundamental to democratic theory, though dominant-party systems can blur the line when structural advantages approach suppression of genuine competition.
  • Coalitions & Realignment Party coalitions are the collections of social groups, geographic regions, and political communities forming a party's reliable base. Coalition building requires managing internal tensions among groups with different priorities. Realignment occurs when major, durable shifts restructure which groups support which parties — as in the New Deal realignment and the transformation of the American South.
  • Nomination Nomination — the process by which parties select candidates — is effectively a first election shaping the choices available in the general election. The United States uses primary elections (open, closed, or semi-open) and caucuses. Parliamentary systems typically give more control to party leaders, parliamentary caucuses, or local party associations. Nomination rules significantly affect who runs and what positions are viable.
  • Accountability Parties are the primary instrument of democratic accountability — voters can reward or punish governing parties at elections based on performance. For this to work, voters must be able to identify responsible parties, platforms must be coherent, governing parties must attempt implementation, and oppositions must be credible alternatives.
  • Party Discipline Party discipline — the coordination of legislative members' votes — is essential for coherent governance but creates tension with individual representatives' obligations to their constituents. The whip system institutionalizes this coordination in many legislative bodies, with greater formal authority in parliamentary than in presidential systems.
  • Weakened Accountability Several dynamics can weaken party accountability: safe seats with no meaningful competition, long-term dominance enabling structural entrenchment, extreme polarization transforming elections into identity battles rather than performance evaluations, opaque party financing shifting accountability toward donors, and factionalism preventing coherent governing programs.
  • Parties and Democracy Despite the frustration they provoke, parties remain the primary mechanism through which democratic governments are formed, held accountable, and replaced. They can also be weaponized against democracy when governing parties manipulate electoral rules, restrict opposition, or undermine independent institutions. Students who understand this distinction are better equipped to protect democratic self-governance.

Key Terms

Political party
An organized group that contests elections with the goal of placing members in public office and using governmental power to advance preferred policies.
Party platform
The official written statement of a political party's positions on major public issues, typically adopted at a party convention before an election.
Party system
The overall structure of competition among parties in a country, including the number of parties, their relative strength, and their patterns of interaction.
Two-party system
A party system dominated by two major parties, in which third parties have little or no realistic prospect of winning national power.
Multiparty system
A party system featuring three or more parties that regularly win significant numbers of legislative seats, typically requiring coalition governments.
Dominant-party system
A system in which competitive elections occur but one party consistently governs for long periods, accumulating structural advantages over competitors.
One-party state
A political system in which only one party is legally permitted, with no genuine electoral competition; a form of authoritarian government.
Party coalition
The collection of social groups, geographic regions, and political communities that constitute a party's reliable base of electoral support.
Party realignment
A major, durable shift in which groups support which parties, restructuring the basic map of party competition over elections and decades.
Primary election
A preliminary election in which voters choose among candidates seeking a party's nomination for a general election.
Open primary
A primary election in which any registered voter may participate, regardless of party registration.
Closed primary
A primary election in which only registered party members may vote in that party's contest.
Nomination
The formal process by which a party selects the candidate who will represent it in a general election.
Party convention
A formal gathering of party delegates that nominates candidates and adopts the party platform.
Party caucus
A gathering of party members or legislators to select candidates, make legislative decisions, or coordinate party strategy.
Programmatic party
A party that competes primarily by developing and communicating specific policy programs and attempting to implement them when in power.
Catch-all party
A party that seeks to appeal to as broad a spectrum of voters as possible by softening ideological edges and emphasizing competence and broad values.
Clientelism
A form of party-voter relationship based on the exchange of material benefits and personal favors for electoral support, rather than policy platforms.
Party discipline
The ability of a party to coordinate the votes and behavior of its legislative members, ensuring they act in a coherent manner.
Factionalism
The presence of internal groups within a party that disagree on strategy, ideology, or priorities, potentially undermining party cohesion.
Proportional representation
An electoral system in which parties win seats roughly in proportion to the share of votes they receive, encouraging multiparty competition.
Duverger's Law
The observation that electoral systems using single-member plurality districts tend to produce two dominant parties over time, because of the wasted-vote problem facing third parties.
Safe seat
A legislative district in which one party reliably wins election after election, reducing meaningful electoral competition.
Party identification
A voter's psychological attachment to a political party, one of the strongest predictors of voting behavior in political science research.
Ticket splitting
Voting for candidates from different parties in the same election, rather than supporting one party's candidates across all offices on the ballot.
Third party
A political party other than the two major parties in a two-party system; faces structural disadvantages in plurality electoral systems.
Coalition government
A government formed by two or more parties that have negotiated an agreement to share power and pursue agreed-upon policies together.
Party whip
A legislative officer responsible for maintaining party discipline, counting votes, and ensuring members vote according to party positions on important legislation.
Dealignment
A weakening of partisan identification across the electorate, with more voters identifying as independent and making choices based on candidates and issues rather than party loyalty.

  3. How do the platforms reflect the party coalitions of 1860? Based on the platform language, what groups of voters does each party seem to be speaking to?
  4. The 1860 election produced a major party realignment — the collapse of the old party system and the emergence of Republican-versus-Democrat competition that persisted for generations. What does the 1860 party split suggest about how party systems can break down under sufficient political stress?
  5. Compare the process of platform development in 1860 with how platforms are developed today. What has changed, and what remains similar?

Research or Extension Activity

Project: Comparative Party System Analysis

Select two countries that use different types of party systems — for example, one with a two-party system and one with a multiparty system, or one with a strong programmatic party tradition and one with significant clientelistic patterns. Using publicly available resources including government websites, academic political science databases, newspaper archives, and comparative democracy indexes, develop a comparative analysis of the two countries' party systems.

Your analysis should address: the number and identity of major parties; the electoral rules that shape party competition; typical coalition patterns and government stability; how parties select candidates; how parties are financed; how cohesive legislative parties are; and how international democracy indexes rate each country's democratic health. After gathering this information, write a comparative essay explaining what your two cases reveal about how party systems affect democratic governance. Identify at least two important similarities and two important differences between the systems, and draw at least one broader conclusion about what these cases suggest about parties and democracy.

Chapter Closing Reflection

Political parties are imperfect instruments for an imperfect project. Democracy — the idea that citizens should govern themselves through their representatives — requires institutions that can aggregate diverse preferences, select leaders, organize legislative action, and create accountability over time. Parties have proven, across enormously varied historical contexts, to be the most effective institutions available for performing these functions. They are not ideal institutions; they can be rigid, self-serving, polarizing, corrupt, and unresponsive to significant portions of the public. But they are real institutions, embedded in the practical work of democratic governance, and understanding them is essential to understanding how democracies actually function.

This chapter has presented parties from multiple angles: what they do, how they develop platforms and coalitions, how different party systems create different patterns of representation and accountability, how candidates are nominated, and how party discipline both enables coherent governance and creates tensions with individual representation. Throughout, the key analytical question has been: how do parties help or hinder the core democratic requirement that citizens can hold those who govern them accountable for how they exercise power?

That question is not settled. In some systems and some historical moments, parties perform their accountability functions effectively: voters have genuine choices, elections produce meaningful consequences, governing parties face real incentives to perform, and the peaceful transfer of power between competing parties is taken for granted. In other systems and moments, parties become instruments of entrenchment, polarization, or outright manipulation — vehicles for the accumulation of power rather than for its responsible exercise and democratic limitation.

Students who have worked through this chapter are better equipped to ask the right questions about party politics in any context: Who controls the nomination process, and whose preferences does it reflect? What do the competing platforms actually promise, and how does governing performance compare? What coalition has this party assembled, and whose interests is it most likely to serve? How is the party financed, and to whom does that financing make it accountable? These are not cynical questions — they are the questions of informed democratic citizens who take both the promise and the challenges of self-government seriously. The study of political parties, at its best, is preparation not merely for understanding politics but for participating in it with clarity, judgment, and responsibility.

Part I — Africa, Geography, and Human Origins
4

Early African Societies

Africa: A History of a Continent

Chapter Opening

"Long before the first cities rose along the Nile or the first caravans crossed the Sahara, African communities had already built lifeways of extraordinary sophistication, resilience, and creativity."

In the rock shelters of the Drakensberg Mountains in southern Africa, painted images survive that are thousands of years old: lines of eland antelope rendered with such precision that modern zoologists can identify the exact species and posture of each animal; human figures in postures of dance, ritual, and trance; handprints pressed against stone by people whose names we will never know. These paintings were not created by people living at the dawn of history in any simple sense. They were created by people living within fully developed societies — societies with religious systems, social rules, artistic traditions, and detailed knowledge of their environment built up over countless generations. To call such people "primitive" or "simple," as many nineteenth- and twentieth-century writers once did, is to fundamentally misunderstand what we now know about how human societies organized themselves before the spread of farming and herding across the continent.

This chapter studies the societies that developed across Africa during the long period before agriculture and livestock-keeping became the dominant way of life in most regions — though, as later chapters will show, that transition happened at dramatically different times in different parts of the continent, and in some regions did not happen at all until very recently, if ever. These communities are often labeled "hunter-gatherer" societies, though historians and anthropologists increasingly prefer the term "forager" societies, since the people involved gathered plants, dug roots, collected shellfish, trapped small game, and fished, in addition to hunting larger animals. Whatever term is used, these were not failed or incomplete societies waiting to become something else. They were complete societies in their own right, with their own logic, their own values, and their own remarkable record of adaptation and survival across every environment the African continent offered — from the Kalahari Desert to the Congo rainforest, from the Mediterranean coastlands to the highlands of the Cape.

Why does this history matter? First, because Africa is the place where the foraging way of life was practiced for the longest unbroken stretch of time anywhere on Earth — longer than anywhere else, since modern humans first evolved on the African continent, as the previous chapter explained. Second, because foraging societies were not static. They changed continuously: they developed new tools, new social arrangements, new relationships with their environments, and new forms of exchange with neighboring communities. Third, because the social patterns, technologies, and belief systems developed during this long period did not simply vanish when farming and herding spread. They persisted, adapted, blended with new economic systems, and in many places continued alongside agriculture and pastoralism for thousands of years — and in a few places, continue in modified forms today.

This chapter also matters because it challenges a deeply rooted and damaging set of assumptions in much older historical writing: the assumption that societies without large cities, written records, or centralized states were therefore "ahistorical" — without meaningful change over time, without internal complexity, and without anything worth studying except as a contrast to "civilization." Archaeological and anthropological research over the past several decades has overturned this assumption entirely. Forager societies developed sophisticated technologies suited to their environments, intricate kinship systems that organized cooperation and marriage across wide territories, religious and ritual traditions of considerable depth, and forms of ecological knowledge that allowed small communities to survive for tens of thousands of years in some of the most challenging environments on Earth, including deserts and dense rainforests that later, technologically different societies have sometimes found difficult to inhabit.

The evidence available to historians studying this period is different from the evidence available for later periods of written history. There are no royal inscriptions, no court chronicles, no diplomatic correspondence. Instead, historians rely heavily on archaeology: stone tools, the remains of campsites, traces of ancient hearths, fragments of bone that reveal diet and butchery practices, and, in some regions, spectacular rock art that records ceremonial life, animals, and human activity. Historians also draw on the study of living foraging communities, such as the San peoples of the Kalahari, the Hadza of Tanzania, and Central African forest-dwelling communities sometimes referred to collectively (though imprecisely) as "Pygmy" peoples — though such modern comparisons must be used carefully, since contemporary foraging communities have themselves changed enormously over centuries of contact with farming, herding, and state societies, and are not frozen relics of an ancient past.

Finally, this chapter matters because it lays crucial foundations for everything that follows in this textbook. The social structures, technological skills, religious concepts, and patterns of regional exchange developed during this long foraging period did not disappear when farming began. They shaped how African communities responded to agriculture, how they organized early village life, how kinship and gender roles developed in later societies, and how religious and artistic traditions evolved across thousands of years of subsequent African history. Understanding early African societies, in other words, is not a detour before the "real" history of Africa begins. It is the foundation on which everything else in African history was built.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain what a foraging or hunter-gatherer economy is and how it differs from agricultural and pastoral economies.
  2. Describe the major forms of social organization and kinship found in early African foraging societies.
  3. Identify the technologies — including stone tools, fire, shelter, and clothing — developed by early African societies and explain how these technologies were adapted to local environments.
  4. Compare how foraging communities adapted to different African environments, including deserts, forests, savannas, and coastlines.
  5. Analyze the role of ritual, belief, and rock art in the lives of early African communities.
  6. Trace the networks of exchange and communication that connected foraging communities across regions.
  7. Evaluate the evidence for gender roles and the division of labor in early African societies.
  8. Assess the significance of technological innovation during the African Stone Age, including the development of microlithic tool industries.
  9. Connect the social and technological foundations of early African societies to later developments in agriculture, pastoralism, and complex society.
  10. Interpret archaeological and rock art evidence as historical sources, including their strengths and limitations.
  11. Contextualize outdated "primitive society" assumptions within the history of anthropology and explain why modern historians reject them.

Guiding Historical Questions

Questions for Historical Inquiry

  1. What does it mean to live in a foraging economy, and how did such economies actually function across different African environments?
  2. How did early African communities organize themselves socially, and what role did kinship play in organizing cooperation, marriage, and resource sharing?
  3. What technologies did early African societies develop, and how were these technologies shaped by local environmental conditions?
  4. What can rock art, burials, and archaeological remains tell historians about belief systems and ritual life in early African societies — and what can they not tell us?

  6. What roles did men, women, and children play in the daily work of foraging societies, and how do historians know?

  8. How did the foundations laid during this period shape the agricultural, pastoral, and political developments that followed in later African history?

Historical Context

The previous chapter explained that early hominins and, later, anatomically modern humans evolved in Africa and gradually developed increasingly sophisticated tools, social behaviors, and methods of adapting to diverse environments. By roughly 200,000 to 300,000 years ago, Homo sapiens had emerged in Africa, and over the tens of thousands of years that followed, human populations spread across nearly every environment the continent offered. This chapter picks up that story by examining how these populations organized their daily lives, social relationships, and technologies during the long period historians and archaeologists often call the Later Stone Age — a period that, in different regions of Africa, stretched from roughly 50,000 years ago down to the spread of farming and herding, a transition that itself occurred at dramatically different times across the continent, beginning in some regions before 5000 BCE and continuing in others into the last few centuries.

It is essential for students to understand that "Stone Age" is a technological label, not a measure of social simplicity. Societies that relied on stone, bone, wood, and other organic materials for their tools — rather than metal — were nonetheless capable of extraordinary social, artistic, and technical sophistication. The label refers only to the dominant material used for cutting and shaping tools; it tells us nothing by itself about social organization, religious life, or intellectual capacity. Historians today are careful to avoid the older, now-discredited assumption that technological stages map directly onto stages of "civilization," an assumption that was frequently used in earlier centuries to justify the dispossession and domination of foraging peoples by societies that saw themselves as more advanced.

Geography mattered enormously to how foraging societies developed. Africa's environmental diversity — examined in detail in Chapter 3 — meant that foraging communities in the Kalahari Desert faced radically different challenges and opportunities than communities in the Congo rainforest, the Ethiopian highlands, the Mediterranean coastal zone, or the fertile river valleys of East Africa. Each environment offered a different combination of plant foods, game animals, water sources, and raw materials for tools and shelter, and foraging communities developed specialized knowledge — passed down across generations through oral instruction, demonstration, and practice — that allowed them to extract a stable and often abundant living from environments that might appear harsh or unproductive to outside observers unfamiliar with local conditions.

It is also important to understand that foraging was not a single uniform way of life practiced identically everywhere. Some foraging communities lived in small, highly mobile bands that moved frequently in search of seasonal food sources. Others, particularly in resource-rich environments such as the Nile Valley, the Great Lakes region, or certain coastal zones, were able to settle for longer periods in semi-permanent camps, exploiting abundant fish, shellfish, or plant resources without needing to move as often. This range of settlement patterns reminds us that foraging economies were flexible and adaptive systems, not a single fixed stage that all societies passed through identically before "advancing" to farming.

Finally, students should understand that the foraging way of life did not end uniformly or completely when farming began. In many regions, foraging and farming or herding communities coexisted for centuries, exchanging goods, ideas, and sometimes people through marriage and trade. In a small number of African regions — most famously parts of the Kalahari Desert inhabited by San communities, and parts of the Central African rainforest inhabited by various forest-dwelling communities — foraging remained a significant or dominant way of life into the nineteenth, twentieth, and even twenty-first centuries, shaped continuously by contact, trade, and sometimes conflict with surrounding farming and pastoral societies and, more recently, by colonial and postcolonial states.

Section 4.1

Hunter-Gatherer Lifeways

The term "hunter-gatherer" describes communities that obtained their food primarily through hunting wild animals, fishing, and gathering wild plants, roots, nuts, and insects, rather than through farming or herding domesticated animals. Across Africa, such communities developed an enormous range of subsistence strategies tailored to local conditions. In the Kalahari Desert, foraging communities relied heavily on deeply buried tubers and roots that retained moisture during dry seasons, supplemented by hunted game obtained through poison-tipped arrows and patient, highly skilled tracking. In the Congo rainforest, foraging communities developed detailed knowledge of forest plants, insect larvae, honey sources, and the seasonal movements of forest animals, along with specialized techniques for net hunting that required cooperation among many households. Along African coastlines and around inland lakes and rivers, foraging communities harvested fish, shellfish, and waterfowl, sometimes in such abundance that they could sustain larger and more settled populations than communities relying purely on terrestrial hunting.

What unified these diverse strategies was not a single technology or social structure but a shared economic logic: rather than producing food through planting and herding, foraging communities extracted food directly from naturally occurring resources, requiring deep, often highly specific knowledge of plant cycles, animal behavior, water sources, and seasonal change. This knowledge was not casual or incidental. It represented an accumulated body of empirical understanding, built and refined across countless generations and transmitted carefully from elders to children through direct instruction, storytelling, and practice.

Mobility was central to many, though not all, foraging economies. Because food resources in a given location were often seasonal and could be depleted if a group remained too long, many foraging bands moved periodically — sometimes every few weeks, sometimes several times a year — following the availability of water, plant foods, and game. This mobility shaped nearly every other aspect of foraging life: it limited the accumulation of heavy material possessions, encouraged portable rather than permanent shelter, and required social arrangements flexible enough to allow bands to split apart and recombine as resources and relationships demanded.

However, mobility was not universal or constant. In resource-rich environments — particularly around major rivers, lakes, and coastlines — foraging communities sometimes maintained semi-permanent or even permanent settlements, especially where fish, shellfish, or other abundant and reliable food sources reduced the need to move frequently. Archaeological evidence from sites along the Nile, the Great Lakes of East Africa, and certain southern African coastal areas reveals thick accumulations of shell, fish bone, and tool debris that suggest repeated or continuous occupation over long periods — challenging any simple assumption that all foraging societies were highly mobile.

Population density among foraging societies was generally low compared to later farming societies, since foraging economies generally supported fewer people per unit of land than agriculture eventually would. But "low density" should not be mistaken for "small populations" or "limited social complexity." Foraging bands maintained extensive social and kinship networks stretching across large territories, allowing for cooperation, intermarriage, information-sharing, and mutual support across distances of many dozens or even hundreds of kilometers — a pattern explored further in Section 4.6.

Section 4.2

Social Organization and Kinship

The basic social unit of most African foraging societies was the band: a small group, typically ranging from a few dozen to perhaps one or two hundred people, who camped, foraged, and made decisions together for periods of time before splitting into smaller groups or merging with other bands. Bands were not rigid, permanent institutions. Their membership shifted regularly as individuals and families moved between groups in response to disputes, marriage, the availability of resources, or simply personal preference. This fluidity was itself an important social strategy: it allowed bands to adjust their size to match locally available resources and provided individuals with an important social safety valve, since people who experienced conflict within one band could relocate to another where they had kinship ties.

Kinship — the web of relationships created through descent, marriage, and culturally recognized bonds — organized nearly every dimension of foraging social life. Kinship determined who an individual could marry, who they were obligated to share food with, who they could call upon for support in times of need, and who they would camp with at different points in the year. Many African foraging societies practiced rules of exogamy, meaning that individuals were expected or required to marry outside their own immediate kin group or band, which had the important social effect of creating marriage ties — and therefore obligations of mutual support — across different bands and territories.

Decision-making within bands tended to be highly egalitarian compared to later farming, herding, and state societies. Most African foraging societies that have been studied by anthropologists lacked formal chiefs or rulers with coercive authority over others. Instead, decisions were typically made through extended discussion among adult members of the band, with respected elders, skilled hunters, or individuals recognized for particular knowledge or ritual ability exercising influence through persuasion and reputation rather than command. This does not mean that foraging societies were without internal disagreement, competition, or social tension — anthropological and historical evidence shows clearly that they were not — but it does mean that social inequality based on inherited rank, formal political office, or accumulated wealth was generally much less pronounced than in later, larger-scale societies.

Sharing was a deeply embedded social value and practical necessity in most foraging societies. Because food resources — particularly meat from large game — were often unpredictable and could not easily be stored for long periods in warm climates, successful hunters were typically expected to share their catch widely across the band according to well-understood social rules, rather than keeping it for their own household alone. This practice, sometimes called the "meat-sharing" norm by anthropologists, served an important function: it spread risk across the community, since today's successful hunter might be tomorrow's unsuccessful one, and a system of mutual obligation ensured that everyone in the band had access to food even during periods of personal misfortune.

Section 4.3

Food, Shelter, and Technology

The technologies developed by early African foraging societies were carefully matched to the demands of mobility, available raw materials, and local environmental conditions. Stone remained the dominant material for cutting tools throughout most of this period, but African toolmakers developed increasingly refined and specialized stone-working techniques over tens of thousands of years. By the period archaeologists call the Later Stone Age, toolmakers across much of the continent were producing microliths — small, carefully shaped stone blades and points, often only a few centimeters long, that were set into wooden or bone shafts using plant resin or other adhesives to create composite tools such as arrows, spears, and knives.

Microlithic technology represented a significant technical advance over earlier, larger stone tools. Because microliths were small and could be produced relatively quickly from a single stone core, toolmakers could create a wide variety of specialized implements — different point shapes for different game, different blade edges for different cutting tasks — without needing to carry large quantities of raw stone. Damaged microliths could also be easily replaced within a composite tool without discarding the entire implement, making this technology efficient for highly mobile communities who needed to minimize the weight and bulk of what they carried.

Beyond stone, foraging communities used an enormous range of organic materials: wood for spear shafts, bows, and digging sticks; bone and horn for points, awls, and ornaments; plant fiber for cordage, nets, and baskets; animal hide for clothing, bags, and shelter coverings; and ostrich eggshell, widely used in southern Africa both as a water-storage container — since hollowed and sealed eggshells could hold water for transport across dry terrain — and as raw material for beads, some of the oldest deliberately manufactured ornaments known anywhere in the world.

Fire was a fundamental and ancient technology, used for cooking, warmth, light, protection from predators, and the hardening of certain tool materials. Evidence of controlled fire use appears at African archaeological sites stretching back hundreds of thousands of years, and by the period this chapter examines, fire management was a routine and essential part of daily and seasonal life, including in some regions the deliberate, controlled burning of vegetation to encourage the growth of preferred plant foods or to make hunting easier — an early form of environmental management that some archaeologists argue blurs the line between simple "foraging" and active landscape modification.

Shelter varied enormously according to environment, mobility, and season. In many regions, foraging bands constructed lightweight, easily assembled shelters from bent branches covered with grass, bark, or hide — structures that could be built within hours and left behind without major loss when the group moved on. In areas with greater seasonal stability or richer resources, communities sometimes built more substantial shelters intended to be reused across multiple seasons, and natural rock shelters and caves were used extensively wherever available, offering protection from weather and predators as well as serving, in many documented cases, as sites of long-term ritual and artistic activity, discussed further in Section 4.5.

Historical Vocabulary
Band Society

A band society is a small-scale social and economic group, typically composed of related and unrelated families numbering anywhere from a few dozen to a couple hundred people, who cooperate in foraging, share resources according to established social rules, and generally lack permanent, centralized political authority. Band societies are flexible in membership: individuals and families move between bands over time in response to marriage, resource availability, conflict, or personal choice.

Band society should not be confused with simplicity. Many band societies maintained sophisticated kinship systems, ritual traditions, ecological knowledge, and long-distance social networks. The absence of formal political hierarchy reflected a different — not a lesser — form of social organization, well suited to the demands of mobile foraging economies operating across variable and sometimes unpredictable environments.

Section 4.4

Adaptation to Local Environments

Africa's environmental diversity, described in Chapter 3, posed dramatically different challenges to foraging communities depending on where they lived, and the adaptive strategies developed in each region reveal a remarkable degree of ecological sophistication. In the Kalahari Desert, foraging communities such as the ancestors of today's San peoples developed an intimate, highly detailed knowledge of water-bearing plants and deeply buried tubers capable of sustaining a population through long dry seasons when surface water was scarce or absent. San trackers developed renowned skill in reading animal spoor — tracks, droppings, and other subtle environmental signs — allowing them to locate and pursue game across vast, seemingly featureless terrain with an accuracy that has astonished trained outside observers.

In the Congo Basin rainforest, by contrast, the challenge was not aridity but density: a thick forest canopy that limited large game and visibility while offering an enormous variety of plant foods, insects, and smaller animals dispersed across a complex three-dimensional environment. Forest-dwelling foraging communities developed specialized techniques such as cooperative net hunting, in which long nets woven from forest vines were used by multiple households working together to drive and capture forest antelope and other game — a technique requiring substantial social coordination and trust among the participating families.

Along coastlines and around major lakes and rivers, foraging communities developed specialized fishing technologies, including bone and later more elaborate fish hooks, fish traps woven from plant fiber, and harpoons tipped with carefully shaped bone or stone points. Coastal and riverine environments often allowed for unusually stable, even semi-permanent settlement, since fish and shellfish populations, unlike many terrestrial game animals, did not require extensive mobility to exploit reliably across seasons.

In highland and mountain environments, such as the Ethiopian highlands or the mountains of eastern and southern Africa, foraging communities adapted to colder temperatures, seasonal frost, and altitude-dependent variation in plant and animal life, often moving seasonally between higher and lower elevations to follow the availability of food resources — a pattern of vertical mobility distinct from the more horizontal seasonal movement common in lowland regions.

This environmental diversity meant that no single description of "African foraging life" can be fully accurate. Historians and archaeologists today emphasize regional variation and adaptive flexibility rather than treating foraging as a single uniform stage of human development. The technologies, diets, settlement patterns, and even social structures of foraging communities differed substantially according to the specific demands and opportunities of their local environments — a key lesson in how geography, examined more broadly in Chapter 3, shaped specific historical outcomes at the level of individual communities.

Section 4.5

Ritual Life and Belief Systems

The religious and ritual lives of early African foraging societies are among the most difficult aspects of this period for historians to reconstruct, since belief systems leave far less direct physical evidence than tools or shelters. Nonetheless, archaeological evidence — particularly rock art, deliberate burials, and certain categories of artifacts — provides historians with meaningful, if incomplete, windows into the symbolic and spiritual lives of these communities.

Some of the earliest known evidence of symbolic behavior anywhere in the human archaeological record comes from African sites. At Blombos Cave in South Africa, archaeologists have recovered pieces of ochre — a naturally occurring iron-oxide pigment — engraved with deliberate geometric patterns, dated to roughly 70,000 to 100,000 years ago, alongside shell beads that appear to have been deliberately perforated and strung as ornaments. These finds are widely regarded by archaeologists as some of the earliest evidence of symbolic thought and personal adornment in the human record anywhere in the world, suggesting that the capacity for symbolic expression — central to art, ritual, language, and shared belief — was present in African populations tens of thousands of years before comparable evidence appears elsewhere.

Rock art represents the richest single category of evidence for ritual life among African foraging societies, and southern Africa in particular preserves one of the largest concentrations of rock art anywhere in the world, with tens of thousands of painted and engraved sites across the Drakensberg Mountains and surrounding regions. Detailed anthropological research among San communities, combined with careful archaeological analysis, has allowed researchers to connect much of this art to specific ritual practices, particularly a trance dance in which specially recognized individuals — sometimes described by researchers as ritual specialists or shamans, though such outside terms should be used carefully — entered altered states of consciousness, often through extended rhythmic dancing, in order to access spiritual power, heal the sick, control rainfall, or mediate between the human community and the spirit world.

Many rock art images depict the eland, a large antelope species that held particular ritual and symbolic significance in San cosmology, frequently associated with potency, healing power, and the visionary experience of the trance dance itself. Other common images include therianthropes — figures combining human and animal characteristics — which researchers interpret as visual representations of the transformation experienced by ritual specialists during trance states, when practitioners were believed to take on animal characteristics or travel to the spirit world in animal form.

Burial practices provide another important category of evidence. Archaeological excavations across Africa have revealed deliberate burials, sometimes accompanied by ornaments, pigments, or grave goods, suggesting beliefs about an afterlife, the social significance of death, or specific obligations toward the deceased. The careful, deliberate nature of many of these burials — bodies positioned in particular postures, sometimes covered with ochre, sometimes accompanied by personal ornaments — indicates that death was understood as an event of considerable social and spiritual significance, requiring specific ritual responses rather than simple disposal.

Historians must exercise particular caution when interpreting ritual and belief evidence from this period. Unlike written sources, archaeological remains do not explain their own meaning; researchers must infer belief and practice from physical traces, often by drawing analogies with documented practices among more recent foraging communities — a method that carries real interpretive risks, since modern communities are not frozen survivals of the ancient past and their beliefs and practices have themselves changed substantially over time, including through contact with farming, herding, and colonial societies.

Section 4.6

Exchange and Community Networks

Although foraging bands were typically small, they were rarely socially or economically isolated. Across much of Africa, foraging communities maintained extensive networks of exchange, communication, and mutual obligation stretching well beyond the boundaries of any single band's immediate foraging territory. These networks served crucial social and economic functions, particularly in environments where resource availability could be unpredictable from year to year or season to season.

Among San communities of southern Africa, anthropologists have documented an elaborate gift-exchange system, often referred to by the term hxaro, in which individuals maintained long-term reciprocal exchange relationships with specific partners, sometimes living many days' travel away. Through hxaro and similar exchange systems, items such as ornaments, tools, and other valued objects circulated across wide territories, but the primary social function of these exchanges was not strictly economic. The exchanges created and reinforced durable social relationships — bonds of obligation and trust — that individuals and bands could call upon during difficult periods, such as drought or local resource scarcity, when a family might need to relocate temporarily to camp with exchange partners in another region.

These long-distance social networks also facilitated the spread of information: knowledge about water sources, the movement of game, the condition of plant resources in distant areas, and news of events affecting other bands could travel rapidly along kinship and exchange networks, allowing foraging communities to respond flexibly to changing conditions across a much wider territory than any single band could monitor directly through its own movements.

Exchange networks also extended, in various periods and regions, between foraging communities and neighboring societies practicing different economic strategies — herding communities, and later farming communities, as those economies spread across the continent (a process examined in detail in Chapter 5). Archaeological and historical evidence shows that foraging and farming or herding communities frequently exchanged goods, intermarried, and exchanged labor and knowledge over long periods, challenging older assumptions that foraging and farming societies existed in simple isolation from, or constant conflict with, one another. The relationship was often considerably more complex, involving cooperation, mutual dependence, and gradual, uneven processes of cultural exchange and economic change.

Section 4.7

Gender Roles and Labor

The division of labor by gender in early African foraging societies has been the subject of considerable research and revision among archaeologists and anthropologists. Earlier generations of researchers, working in the mid-twentieth century, often assumed a simple and rigid pattern: men hunted large game, providing the bulk of the band's protein and prestige, while women gathered plant foods, a supposedly secondary and lower-status activity. This model — sometimes called "Man the Hunter" — shaped popular understanding of foraging societies for decades, but more recent research has substantially complicated and, in important respects, overturned it.

Detailed studies of documented foraging communities, including extensive research among the Hadza of Tanzania and San communities of southern Africa, demonstrate that gathered plant foods — collected predominantly, though not exclusively, by women — frequently constituted the majority of the caloric intake of foraging bands, particularly in environments where large game was scarce, seasonal, or unpredictable. In other words, in many documented foraging economies, women's labor provided the more reliable and often more substantial share of the band's overall food supply, even though hunted meat carried significant social and ritual prestige and was often the focus of sharing obligations and ceremonial attention.

This research has important implications for how historians understand the social structure of foraging bands more broadly. If women's gathering labor was central — not peripheral — to the band's survival, then models of foraging society that treat men's hunting as the defining economic activity, with women's labor as merely supportive, significantly understate the economic and, by extension, the likely social significance of women's contributions. Archaeological evidence consistent with this revised understanding includes the wide distribution of digging sticks, gathering bags, and processing tools associated with plant food preparation across foraging sites throughout Africa.

At the same time, historians must be cautious about projecting any single, uniform gender division of labor across all African foraging societies in all periods and regions. Some documented communities show more flexible patterns, in which both men and women participated in various forms of gathering, and in which women occasionally participated in certain forms of hunting, particularly of smaller game, while in other communities the division between hunting and gathering tasks was more sharply defined by gender. Ritual and ceremonial roles also varied: some evidence suggests women played significant ritual roles in certain communities, including specific roles within trance dance ceremonies discussed in Section 4.5, while in other contexts ritual specialization appears to have been more strongly associated with men, or with both genders in different ceremonial capacities.

Children, too, contributed meaningfully to foraging economies from a relatively young age, participating in gathering activities, learning toolmaking and tracking skills through observation and supervised practice, and gradually taking on greater responsibility as they grew older — a pattern of learning through direct participation rather than formal, separated instruction that characterized education across most foraging societies.

Section 4.8

Innovation in the Stone Age

Far from representing a static technological baseline, the African Stone Age was a period of sustained and significant innovation, spanning hundreds of thousands of years and producing increasingly refined and specialized tool traditions. Archaeologists divide this long period into successive technological traditions — broadly, the Early, Middle, and Later Stone Age — each marked by characteristic advances in toolmaking technique, material use, and tool design, though the boundaries and timing of these traditions varied considerably across different regions of the continent.

The development of microlithic technology, discussed in Section 4.3, represents one of the most significant technical innovations of the Later Stone Age, but it was far from the only one. Toolmakers across Africa developed increasingly precise techniques for shaping stone through pressure flaking — carefully removing small flakes from a stone core using controlled pressure rather than direct percussion — allowing for finer, more standardized tool shapes. Evidence from several African sites indicates that toolmakers also developed heat-treatment techniques, deliberately heating certain stone materials before working them in order to improve their flaking properties, a sophisticated technical insight requiring careful experimentation and the accumulation of specialized knowledge across generations.

Beyond stone tools, this period saw the development of increasingly specialized bone and antler technology, including finely made awls, needles, and points used in the production of fitted clothing, containers, and other manufactured goods. The bow and arrow — a technology that allowed hunters to attack game from a greater distance and with greater accuracy and stealth than earlier thrown spears — appears in the African archaeological record at a relatively early date compared to other world regions, with some researchers arguing for evidence of bow-and-arrow technology, or at least the small stone points consistent with arrow tips, stretching back tens of thousands of years in parts of southern Africa.

Ornament production, discussed in Section 4.5, also represented an important and sustained area of technical and artistic development, involving the careful selection, shaping, and perforation of shell, bone, eggshell, and other materials. The sheer scale and consistency of ornament production at certain archaeological sites suggests not occasional or incidental activity but established craft traditions, requiring specific skills passed down through observation and practice.

This long record of technological refinement matters historically for several reasons. First, it demonstrates that foraging societies were dynamic, innovative communities actively solving practical problems and accumulating technical knowledge over very long periods, rather than remaining in an unchanging technological condition. Second, the regional variation in tool traditions across Africa — different microlith styles, different toolmaking techniques in different areas — provides historians and archaeologists with valuable evidence for tracing regional cultural traditions, patterns of contact and exchange, and, in some cases, population movement, discussed further in later chapters covering migration. Third, this innovative capacity laid essential technical foundations — including refined cutting tools, fiber technology, and detailed environmental knowledge — that later farming and herding societies would draw upon and adapt as new economic strategies spread across the continent.

Historical Vocabulary
Microlith

A microlith is a small, finely shaped stone tool, typically only a few centimeters in length, designed to be mounted into a wooden, bone, or other organic shaft using adhesives such as plant resin, creating a composite tool such as an arrow, spear point, or knife. Microlithic technology became widespread across much of Africa during the Later Stone Age and represented an important technical advance: it allowed toolmakers to produce a wide range of specialized implements efficiently, to repair damaged composite tools by replacing only the small stone component, and to minimize the weight and bulk of raw materials that highly mobile foraging communities needed to carry.

The study of microlith styles across different African regions helps archaeologists trace regional technical traditions, patterns of cultural contact, and, in some cases, evidence of population movement and interaction between different communities over time.

Historical Vocabulary
Foraging Economy

A foraging economy, sometimes called a hunter-gatherer economy, is an economic system in which a community obtains its food through hunting wild animals, fishing, and gathering wild plants, roots, insects, and other naturally occurring resources, rather than through the cultivation of domesticated crops or the herding of domesticated animals. Foraging economies require detailed environmental knowledge, often involve significant seasonal mobility, and historically supported lower population densities than agricultural economies, though population density and settlement patterns varied considerably depending on local resource abundance.

Historians emphasize that foraging economies were not a single uniform "stage" that all societies passed through identically before "advancing" to farming. Many foraging societies persisted for thousands of years alongside farming and herding societies, sometimes by choice, reflecting the practical and social advantages that foraging continued to offer in particular environments.

Section 4.9

The Foundations of Later Societies

The social structures, technologies, and belief systems developed by African foraging societies over tens of thousands of years did not disappear when farming and herding economies began spreading across the continent, a process examined in detail in Chapter 5. Instead, these foundations shaped, informed, and in many cases persisted alongside the new economic systems that gradually transformed African life in different regions at different times.

Kinship systems developed within foraging societies — including rules governing marriage, descent, and mutual obligation — provided social templates that later farming and pastoral communities adapted and elaborated as they developed more settled, often larger-scale forms of social organization. The basic principle that kinship obligations create networks of mutual support, reciprocity, and marriage exchange across community boundaries, refined over many generations within foraging societies, remained a foundational feature of African social organization across many subsequent centuries and societies, including in farming villages, pastoral communities, and even in some of the early states examined in later chapters of this textbook.

Technical knowledge accumulated during the Stone Age — including detailed environmental knowledge, fiber and textile technology, fire management, and tool production techniques — provided practical foundations that early farming communities drew upon directly. The careful observation of plant growth cycles, seasonal change, and animal behavior that foraging communities had refined over thousands of years gave early cultivators and herders crucial baseline ecological knowledge as they began experimenting with plant cultivation and animal management in various African regions.

Religious and ritual traditions, including beliefs about ancestral spirits, healing practices, divination, and sacred specialists capable of mediating between human and spiritual realms, also carried forward into many later African societies, discussed further in Chapter 26 on indigenous African religions. While farming and herding societies developed religious systems considerably more elaborate and varied than those documented for foraging societies — often incorporating beliefs tied specifically to agricultural fertility, livestock, ancestral land claims, and eventually sacred kingship — many underlying concepts, including the importance of ritual specialists, ancestral connection, and the use of trance or possession states to access spiritual power, show clear continuity with patterns first documented among foraging societies.

It is equally important to recognize that the transition toward farming and herding was neither sudden, universal, nor complete. In many African regions, foraging communities continued alongside farming and pastoral neighbors for centuries or millennia, sometimes adopting elements of agricultural technology selectively, sometimes maintaining foraging as a deliberate, sustained way of life even while living in close proximity to and active exchange with farming societies. This pattern of long-term coexistence, rather than simple replacement, is an important corrective to older historical narratives that treated farming as an inevitable "improvement" that foraging societies eagerly and rapidly adopted everywhere it became available.

Everyday Life and Social History

A Day in a Foraging Camp

Although no single description can capture the full diversity of African foraging life across thousands of years and dozens of environments, archaeological and ethnographic evidence allows historians to sketch a plausible composite picture of daily life in many southern African foraging camps during the Later Stone Age.

A camp might consist of several related and unrelated families, their shelters arranged loosely around a central area used for cooking, tool repair, and socializing. Mornings often began with planning: deciding, based on recent observations of animal tracks, plant ripening, or water conditions, where different members of the band would direct their efforts that day. Groups of women, often accompanied by children, might set out together to gather plant foods, digging roots and tubers with wooden digging sticks, collecting fruits, nuts, and edible insects, while sharing news, instructing children in plant identification, and maintaining the social bonds that structured camp life. Men, sometimes alone and sometimes in small groups, might track and pursue game over considerable distances, a process that could take an entire day or longer and did not guarantee success.

Tool repair and production occupied significant time: resharpening or replacing microlithic points, repairing hide containers, twisting plant fiber into cordage, or preparing hides for clothing or bedding. Evenings often centered on shared meals, in which successful hunts were distributed according to established sharing rules, and on storytelling, music, and, on certain occasions, the extended ritual dancing associated with trance practices discussed in Section 4.5. Children learned constantly through observation and participation, gradually taking on greater responsibility for gathering, toolmaking, and eventually hunting as they grew older.

This composite picture should not be mistaken for a description of any single, specific historical camp; it represents a synthesis of archaeological evidence and careful, cautious ethnographic comparison. It is offered to help students imagine the texture of daily life behind the abstractions of "foraging economy" and "band society" discussed elsewhere in this chapter.

Primary Source Window

San Rock Art of the Drakensberg Mountains

Creator: Ancestors of San communities of southern Africa · Date: Created over a span of several thousand years, with many sites dated to within the last two to three thousand years, though some traditions are considerably older · Type: Rock painting (visual / material source)

Context: Across the Drakensberg Mountains and surrounding regions of southern Africa, San communities created tens of thousands of rock paintings depicting animals, human figures, ceremonial scenes, and composite human-animal figures known to researchers as therianthropes. Twentieth- and twenty-first-century researchers, working closely with San ethnographic and linguistic evidence, have argued that much of this art relates closely to the trance dance ritual described in Section 4.5.

Typical scenes include lines of eland antelope rendered in fine detail, human figures shown in postures associated with dance — bent forward, arms extended backward — and figures combining human and animal features, sometimes interpreted as visual depictions of the transformation experienced by ritual specialists during trance.

Analysis: Rock art offers historians a rare direct window into the symbolic and ritual world of a foraging society that left no written record. The careful, repeated depiction of specific animals, particularly the eland, suggests sustained cultural and religious significance rather than incidental decoration. However, rock art interpretation carries real limits: researchers cannot ask the original painters what specific images meant in specific instances, and interpretations rely heavily on analogy with documented San beliefs and practices recorded by later observers, which themselves may not perfectly reflect older traditions. Different panels were likely painted by different individuals across long spans of time, for purposes that may have varied considerably even within a shared broad tradition.

  3. What can rock art tell historians about ritual and belief that stone tools and animal bones cannot?
  4. What might rock art fail to tell historians, even when carefully studied and analyzed?
Historical Debate

Were Foraging Societies "Simple," or Were They Sophisticated Systems Adapted to Their Circumstances?

The question: Older anthropological and historical writing frequently described foraging societies as "primitive," "simple," or "early stages" of human development, implying a linear progression toward farming, herding, cities, and states. More recent scholarship has challenged this framework directly. How should historians characterize the social and technological complexity of African foraging societies?

Interpretation A — Foraging as a Simpler Stage of Development: Some older scholarship, and some popular accounts even today, treat foraging societies as representing an earlier, less developed stage of human social organization — smaller populations, simpler technology, and less elaborate social and political structures than farming or state societies that followed. In this view, the eventual spread of agriculture represents social and technological "progress."
Interpretation B — Foraging as a Sophisticated, Adapted System: Most contemporary archaeologists and historians argue that foraging societies should be understood as sophisticated systems precisely adapted to their environmental and social circumstances, not as incomplete or inferior versions of later societies. Evidence of detailed ecological knowledge, technical innovation, extensive social networks, and rich ritual and artistic traditions demonstrates considerable complexity that simply took different forms than the complexity associated with farming or state societies. In this view, foraging persisted for so long in many regions not because foraging peoples failed to "progress," but because foraging remained a highly effective, sustainable strategy in many environments.

Evidence: Interpretation A draws on the historical fact that population density, settlement permanence, and certain forms of technological elaboration (such as metallurgy) generally increased with the spread of farming. Interpretation B draws on extensive archaeological and ethnographic evidence of technical innovation, complex kinship and exchange systems, rich symbolic and ritual traditions, and instances of foraging communities deliberately maintaining their way of life even in proximity to farming societies.

Why it matters: This debate has real historical consequences. The "simple stage" framework was historically used to justify the displacement, marginalization, and sometimes violent domination of foraging communities by societies that considered themselves more "advanced." Understanding foraging societies as sophisticated, adapted systems rather than incomplete stages has important implications for how historians — and contemporary societies — understand and treat foraging and former-foraging communities today.

Student task: Based on the evidence presented in this chapter, which interpretation do you find better supported? Is it possible that foraging societies were simultaneously sophisticated and, in certain narrow technical respects, less complex than later farming societies? Explain your reasoning.

Multiple Perspectives

Foraging Communities Encountering Farming and Herding Neighbors

From the perspective of established foraging communities: The arrival of farming or herding neighbors in a region could represent both opportunity and disruption. New trade partners offered access to goods such as metal tools, livestock products, or grain, but expanding farms and herds could also compete for land, water, and game, sometimes pressuring foraging communities to adapt their practices, relocate, or, in some cases, gradually adopt farming or herding themselves.
From the perspective of incoming farming or herding communities: Farmers and herders moving into new territories often depended on the detailed environmental knowledge of established foraging communities, including knowledge of water sources, soil conditions, and seasonal patterns, and frequently engaged in trade, intermarriage, and the exchange of labor and skills with foraging neighbors, rather than existing entirely separately from them.
From the perspective of historians studying these encounters today: Historians caution against assuming that contact between foraging and farming or herding communities was always either purely cooperative or purely conflictual. The evidence suggests a wide range of outcomes across different times and places — from sustained cooperation and intermarriage to competition over land and resources — that varied according to local environmental conditions, relative population sizes, and specific historical circumstances.
From the perspective of foraging communities that persisted into more recent centuries: Communities such as San and Central African forest-dwelling groups, who maintained foraging traditions long after farming and herding had become dominant elsewhere on the continent, experienced increasing pressure over time — including loss of land and access to traditional resources — particularly as farming and herding populations expanded and, in much later centuries, as colonial and postcolonial states imposed new political and economic systems across the continent.
Cause and Effect

Toward Food Production: Why Did Some Foraging Communities Begin to Adopt Farming and Herding?

Long-Term Causes

Accumulated environmental knowledge of plant growth cycles and animal behavior developed over tens of thousands of years of foraging; long-term climatic shifts at the end of the last Ice Age altering the distribution of wild plant and animal resources in some regions; gradually increasing population pressure in certain resource-rich zones.

Short-Term Causes

Localized droughts or resource shortages that made experimentation with plant tending or animal management more attractive; contact with neighboring communities already experimenting with cultivation or herding, encouraging the spread of new techniques and crops or livestock.

The Event or Transformation

In several African regions, beginning at different times in different places, foraging communities began deliberately tending wild plants, managing herds of wild or domesticated animals, and gradually increasing their reliance on these strategies relative to wild foraging — a slow, uneven, and far from universal transition examined fully in Chapter 5.

Effects

Gradual increases in population density in farming and herding regions; new forms of settlement, social organization, and eventually political complexity; continued coexistence of foraging communities alongside farming and herding societies in many regions for thousands of years.

Historical complexity: The shift toward farming and herding cannot be explained as a single inevitable "advance" caused by any one factor. It resulted from a complex combination of environmental change, accumulated knowledge, local pressures, and contact between communities, and it occurred unevenly across the continent — a pattern explored in full in the next chapter.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Foraging Communities from the Later Stone Age to the Nineteenth Century

What Changed

  • Tool technology became increasingly refined, including the spread of microlithic industries
  • Some foraging communities adopted elements of farming or herding, or shifted entirely to these economies
  • Population density and territory available to foraging communities shrank in many regions as farming and herding populations expanded
  • Trade and exchange relationships with farming, herding, and eventually state and colonial societies became increasingly significant for many remaining foraging communities

What Stayed the Same

  • Core foraging knowledge — plant identification, tracking, environmental reading — persisted within communities that maintained foraging as a primary strategy
  • Kinship-based social organization and reciprocal exchange relationships remained central to social life
  • Ritual traditions, including elements connected to trance practice in some southern African communities, showed significant continuity over long periods
  • Generally egalitarian, consensus-based decision-making persisted within many foraging bands even as surrounding societies developed more hierarchical political structures

Why the pattern matters: The combination of persistence and change in foraging communities over thousands of years demonstrates that historical continuity does not mean stagnation, and historical change does not mean complete replacement of older ways of life. Foraging communities adapted continuously while preserving core elements of their social organization and ecological knowledge across an extraordinarily long span of time.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has examined the foraging societies that developed across the African continent over tens of thousands of years, beginning well before the emergence of farming and herding economies and, in some regions, persisting alongside or even after those economies spread. The central argument of the chapter is that these societies were not simple, static, or incomplete stages of human development, but sophisticated, dynamic systems precisely adapted to the demands of their specific environments and capable of significant technological, social, and artistic innovation over very long periods of time.

The chapter has shown that foraging economies varied enormously across Africa's diverse environments — from the Kalahari Desert to the Congo rainforest, from coastal shellfish-gathering communities to highland communities practicing seasonal vertical mobility. In each case, foraging communities developed detailed, locally specific ecological knowledge that allowed them to extract stable and often abundant livelihoods from environments that might appear unproductive to outside observers.

Social organization within foraging bands tended to be flexible and generally egalitarian, organized around kinship ties that created obligations of marriage, sharing, and mutual support both within bands and across extensive regional networks. This kinship-based social structure, along with practices such as meat-sharing and long-distance exchange systems like hxaro, provided crucial mechanisms for managing risk and uncertainty in environments where resource availability could change unpredictably.

Technologically, the chapter has traced the development of increasingly refined stone tool industries, culminating in the spread of microlithic technology, alongside sophisticated use of bone, fiber, hide, and other organic materials. Evidence of deliberate ornamentation and engraved pigment stretching back roughly 70,000 to 100,000 years represents some of the earliest known symbolic behavior anywhere in the human record, underscoring Africa's centrality not only to human biological evolution but to the development of human culture, art, and symbolic thought.

Ritual and belief, though difficult to reconstruct directly, are documented through rich evidence including rock art, deliberate burial practices, and, through careful and cautious ethnographic analogy, connections to documented practices such as the San trance dance. This evidence reveals communities with sophisticated symbolic and spiritual lives extending back tens of thousands of years.

The chapter has also revised older, now largely discredited assumptions about gender roles in foraging societies, showing that women's gathering labor frequently constituted the majority of caloric intake in many foraging economies, challenging simplistic "Man the Hunter" models that treated hunting as the primary economic activity and gathering as merely supportive.

Importantly, the chapter has emphasized that the foundations established during this long period — kinship structures, technical knowledge, ecological understanding, and religious concepts — did not disappear with the spread of farming and herding. They persisted, adapted, and in many cases directly shaped the social and cultural patterns of later African societies, including the agricultural, pastoral, and eventually state societies examined in subsequent chapters of this textbook.

Finally, the chapter has stressed that the transition toward farming and herding, examined fully in Chapter 5, was neither sudden, universal, nor complete. Foraging communities continued alongside farming and herding neighbors for centuries or millennia in many regions, and in a small number of cases — most notably certain San communities of the Kalahari and forest-dwelling communities of Central Africa — foraging traditions persisted as a significant or dominant way of life into the modern era, shaped continuously by ongoing exchange, adaptation, and, in more recent centuries, by colonial and postcolonial political pressures.

Understanding early African societies on their own terms — rather than as a deficient preliminary stage before "real" history begins — is essential to the broader purposes of this textbook. It establishes Africa not only as the birthplace of humankind, as the previous chapter explained, but as the site of some of the longest continuous, most sophisticated, and most successful systems of human adaptation, innovation, and social organization in the entire human historical record.

These foundations — of kinship, technology, ecological knowledge, ritual, and exchange — set the stage directly for the developments examined in the next chapter: the gradual, uneven, and historically significant transition toward agriculture, pastoralism, and village life across different African regions.

Band society
A small-scale, flexible social and economic group of foraging families, generally lacking permanent centralized political authority.

The maximum population that a given environment can sustainably support given a particular subsistence strategy and available resources.

A tool constructed from multiple materials, such as a stone microlith mounted on a wooden or bone shaft.

The distribution of different tasks among different members of a society, often organized by gender or age in foraging communities.

A society with relatively little formal social or political hierarchy, in which decision-making is typically consensus-based.

The study of contemporary or historically documented societies to develop interpretive insights useful for understanding archaeological evidence from the past.
Exogamy
A marriage rule requiring or encouraging individuals to marry outside their own immediate kin group or band.
Forager economy
An economic system based on hunting, gathering, and fishing rather than farming or herding domesticated animals.
Hxaro
A documented San system of long-term reciprocal gift exchange that created and maintained social relationships across considerable distances.
Kinship
A culturally recognized system of relationships based on descent and marriage that organizes obligations of cooperation, sharing, and support.
Later Stone Age
The archaeological period, varying in dates by region, characterized by refined stone tool industries including microliths.
Material culture
The physical objects — tools, ornaments, shelters, and other items — created and used by a society, studied by archaeologists as evidence of past life.
Microlith
A small, finely shaped stone tool typically mounted into a composite tool such as an arrow or spear point.
Ochre
A naturally occurring iron-oxide pigment used by early African societies for symbolic engraving, body decoration, and other purposes.

A social norm of mutual exchange and obligation, central to sharing practices and long-distance exchange networks in foraging societies.

An individual recognized within a community as having particular ritual or spiritual ability, such as mediating trance states or healing.

Building a Foraging Societies Reference Profile

This project asks you to research one African foraging community — historical or contemporary — and build a detailed reference profile connecting its way of life to the concepts introduced in this chapter.

  1. Select a community. Choose one documented African foraging community, such as the San of the Kalahari, the Hadza of Tanzania, or a Central African forest-dwelling community such as the Baka, Mbuti, or Aka.
  2. Research subsistence strategy. Using library and academic resources, research how this community obtains food, including specific plant, animal, and other resources used, and seasonal patterns of mobility or settlement.
  3. Research social organization. Investigate kinship structures, marriage rules, decision-making practices, and exchange systems documented for this community.
  4. Research ritual and belief. Identify documented ritual practices, beliefs, or artistic traditions associated with this community, noting carefully which sources are used and how reliable or contested they are.
  5. Connect to historical change. Research how this community's way of life has changed over the past one to two centuries, including the effects of contact with farming, herding, colonial, or postcolonial states.
  6. Write your findings. In a 3–5 page essay or equivalent presentation, present your research and reflect on what this community's history reveals about the broader patterns discussed in this chapter, including the dangers of treating any single community as a simple "window" into the ancient past.

Library resources to use: Academic anthropology and archaeology databases, the MGU History Library's African ethnography collection, and peer-reviewed journal articles on African foraging societies.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter has asked students to set aside older, deeply rooted assumptions about "primitive" societies and to engage seriously with the sophistication, adaptability, and historical depth of African foraging communities. This is not merely an academic correction. It is an essential step toward understanding African history as a genuinely continuous, dynamic story extending back tens of thousands of years, rather than a story that only "begins" with farming, cities, or written records.

The foundations examined in this chapter — kinship systems built on reciprocity and exchange, technical knowledge refined over countless generations, religious and ritual traditions documented through rock art and burial evidence, and flexible, adaptive social organization — did not disappear when new economic strategies emerged. They shaped how African communities responded to the spread of agriculture and pastoralism, examined in the next chapter, and they persisted, in various forms, across thousands of subsequent years of African history.

This chapter also illustrates several of the broader historical thinking skills introduced in Chapter 1: the importance of evaluating evidence carefully, including its limits; the danger of imposing simplistic "stages of progress" narratives onto complex historical processes; the value of considering multiple perspectives, including the perspectives of foraging communities themselves rather than only the perspectives of outside observers; and the recognition that continuity and change coexist in nearly every historical period, including the very long period examined in this chapter.

As this textbook moves forward into the history of agriculture, kingdoms, trade networks, religious traditions, colonialism, and the contemporary continent, students should carry forward the central lesson of this chapter: that complexity, sophistication, and meaningful historical change are not the exclusive property of societies with cities, writing, or centralized states. Africa's foraging societies — diverse, adaptive, and remarkably enduring — remind us that history is made by all human communities, in every form of social organization, across every span of time.

Understanding early African societies on their own terms is therefore not a preliminary step before the "real" history of Africa begins. It is itself one of the deepest and most instructive chapters in the long, diverse, and unfinished story this textbook tells.

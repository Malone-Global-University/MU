Part VIII — Colonialism, Conquest, and Resistance
46

Exploration, Missionaries, and Imperial Ambitions

Africa: A History of a Continent

Chapter Opening

"I am prepared to go anywhere, provided it be forward." — David Livingstone, whose journeys through Central Africa became, for many Europeans, the emblem of an age that called itself exploration but that African societies experienced as intrusion, survey, and, ultimately, the opening move of conquest.

In March 1871, a group of porters, guides, and translators — most of them African, most of them unnamed in the European accounts that followed — guided an ailing Scottish missionary named David Livingstone into the town of Ujiji, on the eastern shore of Lake Tanganyika. When the American journalist Henry Morton Stanley found him there later that year and reportedly greeted him with the words "Dr. Livingstone, I presume," the encounter became one of the most retold stories of the nineteenth century. It appeared in newspapers, schoolbooks, paintings, and eventually films across Europe and the United States, told as a tale of heroic discovery in a supposedly unknown land. But Ujiji was not unknown. It was a flourishing town on a well-established trade route, populated by merchants, farmers, and travelers who had been moving people and goods across the interior of Africa for generations before any European set foot there. What Europeans called "discovery" was, from the perspective of the peoples of the region, simply the arrival of new and unfamiliar visitors into a world that Africans already knew intimately.

This chapter examines the decades before the formal colonial partition of Africa — roughly the 1850s through the 1880s — when European interest in the continent intensified dramatically and took new forms. Explorers mapped river systems and interior regions that had been largely unfamiliar to European geographers, even though these same regions were well known to the African societies who lived there and to Arab, Swahili, and other traders who had long moved through them. Christian missionaries established stations across the continent, seeking converts and, in the process, generating some of the most detailed — and most biased — written descriptions of African societies available to European readers. Merchants and mining companies sought new markets and raw materials. Scientific societies funded expeditions that combined geography, botany, ethnography, and often crude racial theorizing. And gradually, out of this mixture of curiosity, piety, profit, and prejudice, emerged the political and ideological groundwork for the conquest and partition that would follow in the 1880s and 1890s.

It would be a mistake to treat this period as simply a prelude to colonial rule, a set of preliminary events mechanically leading toward an inevitable outcome. Nothing about European conquest of Africa was predetermined. For centuries, European traders had operated along African coasts on terms largely set by African states and merchants, who controlled access to interior markets, set the prices of goods, and determined which foreigners could operate where. Disease, particularly malaria, had made the African interior extraordinarily dangerous for Europeans, killing explorers and missionaries in appalling numbers. Well into the 1870s, most of the African interior remained under African political control, and many African rulers dealt with European visitors as guests, subjects to be taxed, or potential allies rather than as conquerors to be feared. The story of this chapter, then, is not simply the story of Europeans steadily advancing into Africa. It is the story of a shifting balance of knowledge, technology, disease environment, and ambition that, by the 1880s, would tip decisively — though not permanently or completely — in Europe's favor.

Understanding why and how this shift occurred requires attention to several intertwined developments. New medical knowledge, especially the use of quinine to treat and prevent malaria, dramatically reduced European mortality in tropical Africa after mid-century. Steamships and eventually the Suez Canal, opened in 1869, shortened travel times and made sustained European presence in Africa more feasible. New weapons technology, particularly repeating rifles and eventually machine guns, gave European military forces an increasing advantage over African armies armed with older firearms or traditional weapons — though this advantage should not be exaggerated for the period covered in this chapter, since it became fully decisive only in the conquests of the 1880s and 1890s covered in the next chapter. Missionary societies, scientific geographical societies, and commercial firms built new institutional networks that funded expeditions, publicized African "discoveries" to European publics, and lobbied European governments for protection and support.

African societies, for their part, responded to these newcomers in enormously varied ways, shaped by their own political interests, religious traditions, economic calculations, and assessments of the relative power of the visitors. Some rulers welcomed missionaries as potential allies who might provide firearms, literacy, or diplomatic connections to distant powers. Others expelled them or restricted their movement. Some coastal and interior merchants embraced new commercial relationships eagerly, especially the expanding trade in palm oil, ivory, rubber, and other goods that replaced the declining Atlantic slave trade. Others viewed European commercial penetration with suspicion, recognizing — correctly, as it turned out — that expanding foreign economic influence often preceded expanding foreign political control.

This chapter also asks students to think carefully about sources and their limitations. Much of what is known about African societies during this period comes from European travel narratives, missionary reports, and geographical society publications — sources created by outsiders, often with specific religious, scientific, or commercial purposes, and frequently shaped by racial ideologies that portrayed Africans as backward, savage, or in need of European tutelage. These sources remain valuable, since they often contain detailed observations unavailable elsewhere, but they must be read critically, with constant attention to the perspective, purpose, and blind spots of the person who produced them. Where possible, this chapter draws attention to African oral traditions, archaeological evidence, and the internal records of African states that offer alternative windows into the same events.

By the end of this chapter, students should understand that the era of exploration and mission was not a neutral, purely scientific, or purely religious enterprise, separate from the imperial conquest that followed. It was deeply entangled with commercial ambition, racial ideology, and the accumulation of the very knowledge — of rivers, resources, populations, and political weaknesses — that European governments would later use to justify and carry out the conquest and partition of the continent. At the same time, the individuals involved, whether missionaries, explorers, African guides, translators, or rulers, were complicated historical actors whose motives, actions, and legacies deserve careful, unflinching examination rather than either uncritical celebration or simple condemnation.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain the political, economic, religious, and scientific motives that drove growing European interest in Africa during the nineteenth century.
  2. Trace the major European exploration expeditions into the African interior and evaluate their significance for African and European history.
  3. Analyze the role of Christian missionary societies in shaping European knowledge of, and attitudes toward, African societies.
  4. Describe the transition from the Atlantic slave trade to "legitimate commerce" in commodities such as palm oil, ivory, and rubber.
  5. Evaluate the ideologies — including racial theory, the "civilizing mission," and social Darwinism — that Europeans used to justify growing intervention in Africa.
  6. Compare the diverse responses of African rulers, merchants, and communities to European explorers, missionaries, and traders.
  7. Identify the technological and medical changes that made sustained European presence in tropical Africa more feasible after mid-century.
  8. Distinguish between early colonial contact and full colonial conquest, and explain why this period should not be treated as inevitable.
  9. Interpret primary sources, including missionary and explorer writings, with attention to perspective, purpose, and bias.
  10. Assess the historical debate over the relative importance of economic, religious, strategic, and ideological motives for European expansion in Africa.
  11. Contextualize Africa's nineteenth-century encounters with Europe within longer patterns of African-European trade and diplomacy.
  12. Connect the developments of this chapter to the Berlin Conference and the Scramble for Africa examined in the following chapter.

Guiding Historical Questions

Questions for Historical Inquiry

  1. Why did European interest in Africa intensify so dramatically in the middle decades of the nineteenth century?
  2. What made the interior of Africa so difficult for Europeans to access before this period, and what changed?
  3. What were African societies' own perspectives on the explorers, missionaries, and traders who arrived among them?
  4. How did missionary activity shape European knowledge of, and attitudes toward, Africa?
  5. What is meant by "legitimate commerce," and how did it transform African coastal and interior economies?
  6. What ideologies did Europeans use to justify growing involvement in African affairs, and how did these ideologies relate to earlier justifications for the slave trade?
  7. Why should this period not be understood as an inevitable prelude to colonial conquest?
  8. How reliable are European travel and missionary accounts as historical evidence, and what questions should historians ask of them?
  9. How did African rulers evaluate the relative power and intentions of European visitors, and how did their strategies vary?
  10. What connections exist between the exploration and mission era and the Scramble for Africa that followed?

Historical Context

To understand the exploration and mission era, students must recall the long history of contact between Africa and Europe that preceded it. European traders had operated along African coasts since the fifteenth century, when Portuguese ships first established trading posts along the West and East African coasts. For nearly four centuries, this contact was overwhelmingly coastal. European trading companies built forts and factories at points such as Elmina, Cape Coast, Whydah, Luanda, and Mozambique Island, but rarely penetrated far inland. African states and merchants controlled the terms of trade in the interior, supplying goods — including, for centuries, enslaved people — to coastal markets on their own terms and at prices they largely set. European knowledge of the African interior remained fragmentary, based mostly on secondhand reports from African and Arab traders rather than direct observation.

By the early nineteenth century, several forces converged to change this pattern. The British Parliament abolished the slave trade within the British Empire in 1807 and slavery itself in 1833, and the Royal Navy began patrolling West African waters to suppress the trade — a campaign discussed in more detail in the chapters on abolition. As the Atlantic slave trade declined, though it did not disappear, European commercial interest shifted toward other African commodities: palm oil for soap and industrial lubricants, ivory for piano keys and ornamental carving, and eventually rubber for the expanding industrial economies of Europe and North America. This shift toward what contemporaries called "legitimate commerce" created new economic incentives for Europeans to establish direct access to the sources of these goods in the African interior, rather than relying entirely on African and Afro-European coastal middlemen.

At the same time, European scientific and geographical societies — the Royal Geographical Society in London, the Société de Géographie in Paris, and similar institutions elsewhere — began funding expeditions explicitly designed to fill in the blank spaces on European maps of Africa. These societies combined genuine scientific curiosity about geography, botany, and ethnography with commercial and strategic interests, since accurate maps of rivers, resources, and populations were valuable to merchants and governments alike. European newspapers eagerly published accounts of explorers' journeys, creating celebrity status for figures such as Livingstone, Stanley, Richard Burton, John Hanning Speke, and Heinrich Barth, whose exploits were framed for European readers as triumphs of civilization, science, and Christian faith over a supposedly dark and unknown continent — a framing that obscured the fact that African societies already possessed detailed knowledge of their own geography and history.

Christian missionary activity, meanwhile, expanded dramatically during this period, driven by a wave of religious revival in Britain, continental Europe, and North America known as the evangelical awakening. Missionary societies such as the London Missionary Society, the Church Missionary Society, the Basel Mission, and various Catholic orders sent growing numbers of missionaries to Africa, often combining evangelism with efforts to suppress the slave trade, promote "legitimate commerce," and spread European education and medicine — a combination David Livingstone himself famously summarized as "Christianity, Commerce, and Civilization." Missionaries frequently served as the first Europeans to establish long-term residence in interior regions, and their letters, journals, and reports became crucial sources of European knowledge about African societies, even as that knowledge was filtered through missionary assumptions about African religion, culture, and social organization as inferior or in need of transformation.

Advances in tropical medicine, particularly the growing use of quinine as a prophylactic against malaria from the 1840s onward, dramatically reduced the mortality rate among Europeans traveling in tropical Africa, which had previously been so severe that West Africa was known among European sailors as "the white man's grave." Combined with steamship technology that made river travel faster and more reliable, and later the opening of the Suez Canal in 1869 that shortened the sea route to East Africa, these changes made sustained European penetration of the African interior far more physically feasible than it had been at the start of the century — one of the essential preconditions for the conquest that would follow.

Section 46.1

Europe's Growing Interest in Africa

Throughout most of the eighteenth century, European engagement with Africa was overwhelmingly commercial and overwhelmingly coastal. Chartered companies operated trading posts along the coasts of West and East Africa, exchanging manufactured goods for African commodities and, until abolition, for enslaved human beings. The interior of the continent remained, from a European standpoint, largely unmapped and unknown, its geography understood mainly through secondhand reports and speculation. This changed markedly over the course of the nineteenth century, as several distinct but overlapping European interests converged on Africa with unprecedented intensity.

The first was commercial. As the Atlantic slave trade declined following British and, later, other European abolition, merchants sought new sources of profit in African commodities: palm oil from the forests of West Africa, valuable for soap-making and machine lubrication in industrializing Europe; ivory, prized for piano keys, billiard balls, and ornamental carving; groundnuts, gum arabic, and eventually rubber. These goods could be obtained through coastal trade with African merchants, as had long been the pattern, but European traders increasingly sought direct access to the interior sources of supply, hoping to cut out African and Afro-European middlemen and capture greater profits for themselves.

The second was religious. A wave of evangelical revival that swept Britain, continental Europe, and North America in the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries generated enormous missionary energy, expressed through societies dedicated to spreading Christianity to what evangelicals viewed as the "unreached" populations of the world. Africa, alongside Asia and the Pacific, became a major target of this missionary enterprise. Missionary societies raised funds from congregations across Europe and North America, trained and dispatched missionaries, and published extensive accounts of missionary work that shaped European public perceptions of Africa for generations.

The third was scientific and intellectual. The nineteenth century was an era of intense European fascination with mapping, classifying, and cataloguing the natural world, expressed through the rise of geographical societies, natural history museums, and scientific expeditions. Finding the sources of great rivers — above all, the long-sought source of the Nile — became a matter of national and scientific prestige, generating public excitement, government and private funding, and fierce rivalries among explorers and the nations that sponsored them.

The fourth, increasingly important as the century progressed, was strategic and political. As European industrial economies expanded and international rivalries intensified, especially following German unification in 1871 and growing competition for overseas markets and resources, African territory itself became an object of state interest — not merely as a source of trade goods, but as territory to be claimed, secured, and administered. This strategic dimension would become fully dominant only in the 1880s, discussed in the following chapter, but its roots lay in the exploration, missionary, and commercial activity of the preceding decades, which supplied the geographical knowledge, the on-the-ground presence, and the public enthusiasm that made later territorial claims possible.

It is essential for students to recognize that these four strands — commercial, religious, scientific, and strategic — were not separate or contradictory but deeply intertwined in the minds and careers of the individuals involved. David Livingstone, perhaps the most famous figure of the era, saw missionary work, the promotion of legitimate commerce, and the suppression of the slave trade as a single unified project, famously summarized in his call for "Christianity, Commerce, and Civilization" to transform Africa together. This fusion of motives, sincere as it often was on an individual level, nonetheless produced a body of activity — mapping, treaty-making, publicizing African resources and populations to European audiences — that European governments and companies would later draw upon directly in justifying and organizing colonial conquest.

Section 46.2

Exploration and Mapping

European exploration of the African interior in the nineteenth century is often presented in popular memory as a story of solitary heroic Europeans venturing into blank, unknown territory. This picture is deeply misleading. Every major European expedition depended overwhelmingly on African knowledge, labor, and cooperation. Expeditions employed African guides who knew the terrain, African porters who carried supplies over enormous distances, African translators who negotiated with local rulers, and African hunters who provided food. Explorers routinely relied on maps and information supplied by African and Arab traders who had long traveled the routes the Europeans were "discovering." When Livingstone reached Victoria Falls on the Zambezi River in 1855 — a waterfall known to local Kololo and Tonga peoples for generations as Mosi-oa-Tunya, "the smoke that thunders" — he was not finding something unknown; he was the first European to see something Africans had known intimately for centuries.

Heinrich Barth's expeditions through the Sahel and Sahara between 1850 and 1855 stand out among European exploration accounts for their comparative depth and respect. A German scholar with strong linguistic training, Barth spent years traveling through Hausa city-states, Bornu, and Timbuktu, learning local languages, engaging seriously with African scholars and rulers, and producing detailed accounts of West African political systems, commerce, and history that remain valuable historical sources. Barth's accounts stand in some contrast to the more sensationalized and racially charged narratives that characterized much European exploration writing, though his work, too, was shaped by European assumptions and written for a European scientific audience.

The search for the source of the Nile became one of the most publicized scientific controversies of the era. Richard Burton and John Hanning Speke's joint expedition of 1857–1858 reached Lake Tanganyika, after which Speke, traveling separately, reached Lake Victoria, which he declared to be the source of the Nile. Burton disputed this claim, and the two men's public feud, conducted through lectures, books, and newspaper articles, became a sensation in Britain. Speke's claim was substantially vindicated by later expeditions, but the controversy illustrates how African geography became entangled with European scientific reputation, national prestige, and public spectacle — often with little reference to how African societies themselves understood the same landscapes and waterways.

David Livingstone's expeditions across southern and Central Africa between the 1840s and his death in 1873 combined missionary work, geographical exploration, and antislavery advocacy. His journeys took him across the Kalahari, along the Zambezi River, and eventually into the Congo Basin region, where he sought — mistakenly — to identify the sources of the Nile. Livingstone's detailed accounts of the East African and Central African slave trade, in which Arab, Swahili, and some African traders raided and transported captives toward the East African coast and the Indian Ocean world, became powerful antislavery propaganda in Britain, and his death in 1873 in present-day Zambia — his heart, according to tradition, buried by his African companions beneath a tree near where he died, with his body carried more than a thousand miles to the coast for burial in England — became one of the most mythologized episodes of the era.

The 1871 encounter between Livingstone and Henry Morton Stanley at Ujiji became a global media sensation, reported in newspapers across Europe and North America and immortalized in Stanley's own bestselling account. Stanley subsequently undertook his own major expedition between 1874 and 1877, tracing the full course of the Congo River from its upper reaches to the Atlantic. This expedition would prove to have consequences far beyond scientific discovery: the detailed geographical and commercial information Stanley gathered was subsequently used by King Leopold II of Belgium to stake a personal claim to the entire Congo Basin, a story explored in depth in Chapter 47.

Section 46.3

Missionaries and Conversion

Christian missionary activity expanded enormously across Africa during the nineteenth century, driven by the evangelical revival that swept Protestant Europe and North America and by renewed Catholic missionary energy on the continent. Missionary societies established stations along the coasts and, increasingly, in the interior, combining evangelism with education, medical care, agricultural instruction, and — frequently — efforts to promote European-style commerce as an alternative to the slave trade.

Missionaries were often the first Europeans to establish sustained, long-term residence among African communities, learning local languages, translating scripture and hymns, and producing detailed written accounts of African religion, social organization, and daily life. These accounts became crucial sources of European knowledge about Africa, widely read by European publics and used by scientific societies and government officials. But missionary knowledge was never neutral. Missionaries typically viewed African religious traditions as superstition to be replaced rather than belief systems to be understood on their own terms, and they frequently interpreted African social and political institutions through the lens of European Christian and Victorian assumptions about family, gender, and civilization.

African responses to missionary activity varied enormously. Some rulers welcomed missionaries as potential sources of firearms, diplomatic connections, literacy, and technical knowledge, and some individuals and communities embraced Christianity sincerely, adapting it to local circumstances and eventually producing distinctively African forms of Christian practice and leadership — a process examined further in Chapter 28. Other rulers restricted missionary activity carefully, permitting schools and medical work while limiting missionaries' ability to challenge existing political and religious authority. Still others expelled missionaries entirely, particularly where missionary teaching was seen as undermining political loyalty, social order, or existing religious practice. In the kingdom of Buganda, for example, King Mutesa I permitted both Christian missionaries and Muslim traders to operate at his court, using the rivalry between them, alongside relationships with Arab and European visitors, to strengthen his own diplomatic position and access to firearms and trade goods — a sophisticated strategy of managed engagement rather than passive acceptance.

Missionary stations also became sites of significant social transformation, sometimes intended and sometimes not. Mission schools produced a new generation of African Christians literate in European languages, some of whom would later become influential intermediaries, translators, clergy, and eventually critics of colonial rule itself. Mission stations frequently attracted people fleeing slavery, warfare, or difficult social circumstances, offering a degree of protection and a new community, though also demanding conformity to missionary rules and expectations. The complicated legacy of missionary activity — as simultaneously an agent of cultural disruption, a source of education and medical care, a precursor to colonial rule, and, for some Africans, a genuine source of new spiritual meaning and community — is a subject of ongoing historical debate, examined further later in this chapter.

Section 46.4

Science, Commerce, and Empire

The scientific, commercial, and religious activities described so far in this chapter did not operate in separate spheres. They were bound together by institutions — geographical societies, missionary societies, trading companies, and eventually colonial lobbying groups — that pooled information, funding, and public enthusiasm in ways that steadily built the foundation for later territorial claims. The Royal Geographical Society in Britain, along with similar bodies in France, Germany, Belgium, and Portugal, funded expeditions, published explorers' accounts, awarded prestigious medals, and lobbied governments to support further exploration and, eventually, territorial claims. Exploration was rarely purely disinterested science; it was consistently linked to commercial intelligence-gathering (which regions held valuable resources, which rivers were navigable, which rulers might be persuaded into treaties) and to national prestige in an era of intensifying European rivalry.

Commercial firms operating along African coasts, meanwhile, pushed steadily for expanded access to interior markets, sometimes financing exploration directly and frequently lobbying their home governments for protection, favorable trade terms, or outright political control over regions where they operated. In West Africa, palm oil traders on the Niger Delta pressed for British government support against African rulers and rival European traders alike. In Central Africa, King Leopold II of Belgium — acting not through the Belgian state but through his own personal fortune and a supposedly humanitarian and scientific organization, the International African Association, founded in 1876 — combined scientific rhetoric, commercial ambition, and eventually ruthless political maneuvering to build a personal claim over the vast Congo Basin, a process this chapter introduces and Chapter 47 examines in full detail.

The knowledge accumulated through decades of exploration, mission work, and commercial reconnaissance — maps of river systems, assessments of agricultural and mineral resources, estimates of population and political organization, and information about the relative strength or weakness of African states — became directly usable by European governments and companies when, in the 1880s, they moved from informal influence to formal territorial claims. Students should understand this chapter's material not as separate from the story of colonial conquest but as its essential foundation: the exploration and mission era supplied the information, the personnel, the public enthusiasm, and much of the ideological justification that made the rapid partition of Africa in the following decade possible.

Section 46.5

Early Colonial Contact

Even before the formal partition of Africa in the 1880s, a small number of areas experienced more direct forms of European political and economic control that foreshadowed the wider conquest to come. In Algeria, French forces began a long and brutal conquest starting in 1830, eventually incorporating the territory as an integral part of France and encouraging substantial European settlement — a pattern of settler colonialism distinct from the trading-post and later "protectorate" models that characterized most of the rest of the continent. In southern Africa, British and Boer settler expansion, discussed in earlier chapters on Southern African societies, had already produced decades of conflict, land seizure, and political subordination of African communities, well before the Scramble for Africa formalized European claims over the wider continent. Along parts of the West African coast, particularly around British trading settlements such as Lagos, annexed in 1861, and French posts in Senegal, European powers had already established direct political control over limited coastal territories, using them as bases for expanding commercial and, eventually, military influence into the interior.

These early instances of direct colonial control remained the exception rather than the rule through most of the period covered by this chapter. Across the great majority of the continent, European presence in the 1850s, 1860s, and 1870s consisted of small numbers of traders, missionaries, and explorers operating with African permission, protection, and cooperation, and subject to African political authority. European "treaties" signed with African rulers during this period were frequently understood very differently by the two parties: African rulers often understood such agreements as establishing trade relationships, alliances, or limited concessions, while European negotiators — not always in good faith — later interpreted the same documents as transfers of sovereignty. This gap in interpretation, sometimes deliberate and sometimes the product of genuine linguistic and conceptual misunderstanding, would become a major mechanism of dispossession once the Scramble for Africa began in earnest.

Section 46.6

Ideologies of Empire

European justification for growing involvement in African affairs during this period drew on a set of interlocking ideas that would become fully developed into formal imperial ideology by the 1880s. Central among these was the concept of the "civilizing mission" — the claim that European powers had a duty to bring Christianity, commerce, and Western education and government to peoples portrayed as backward or barbaric. This idea drew on centuries-old European assumptions about African inferiority, but it took on new intensity and pseudo-scientific justification during the nineteenth century, as European racial theorists developed elaborate hierarchies of human races, typically placing Europeans at the top and African peoples at the bottom, based on now-discredited theories of biology, skull measurement, and cultural development.

Social Darwinism, an application — or misapplication — of Charles Darwin's evolutionary theory to human societies, provided further pseudo-scientific justification for European dominance, framing colonial expansion as a natural and inevitable consequence of European "fitness" and African "backwardness." These ideas circulated widely in European popular culture, scientific literature, missionary publications, and political rhetoric, and they profoundly shaped how European explorers, missionaries, and eventually colonial administrators described and treated African peoples, even when — as in some missionary contexts — genuine personal relationships and respect for individual Africans coexisted uneasily with sweeping and dehumanizing generalizations about African societies as a whole.

Antislavery sentiment, genuine and widespread in Britain especially following abolition, also became entangled with these justifying ideologies in ways that had lasting consequences. Livingstone and other antislavery campaigners argued that only expanded European commercial and religious influence could permanently eliminate the slave trade that continued, particularly in East and Central Africa, well after the Atlantic trade's formal abolition. This argument, sincerely held by many, nonetheless provided a humanitarian rationale that later imperial conquest would draw upon directly, framing military conquest and political domination as a form of liberation and protection rather than what it also was: the seizure of African land, labor, and sovereignty.

Section 46.7

African Responses to Foreign Intrusion

African societies responded to growing European presence with a wide range of strategies shaped by local political calculations, existing rivalries, religious traditions, and assessments of European strength and intentions. It is essential for students to recognize that African rulers and communities were not passive observers of these developments but active historical agents making calculated decisions based on the information and options available to them.

Some rulers pursued strategies of controlled engagement, permitting missionaries, traders, or explorers to operate within carefully defined limits while extracting maximum advantage — firearms, trade goods, diplomatic recognition, technical knowledge — from the relationship. King Mutesa I of Buganda, mentioned earlier in this chapter, exemplified this approach, balancing relationships with Muslim traders from Zanzibar, Christian missionaries of competing denominations, and occasional European travelers in ways designed to strengthen his own kingdom's position relative to regional rivals. Similar strategies were pursued by rulers across West Africa, where states such as Asante and the Sokoto Caliphate maintained substantial independence and negotiated trade relationships with European merchants on largely their own terms through most of this period.

Other African communities and rulers responded with suspicion or outright resistance, particularly where European activity appeared to threaten established political authority, religious practice, or economic interests. Some rulers restricted missionary movement, expelled missionaries who were seen as subversive, or limited European traders to specific coastal or border locations, refusing them permission to travel into the interior. African merchants who had built profitable positions as intermediaries in coastal trade — a pattern extending back centuries, as discussed in earlier chapters on trade networks — often resisted European efforts to bypass them and establish direct access to interior markets, since such efforts directly threatened their livelihoods and political influence.

African porters, guides, translators, and other laborers who made European exploration and missionary activity possible also deserve recognition as historical actors in their own right, rather than as anonymous background figures in European narratives. These individuals possessed essential knowledge of geography, language, and diplomacy that European expeditions depended upon entirely, and many negotiated the terms of their employment, exercised significant influence over expedition routes and decisions, and used these relationships for their own economic and social advancement. The near-total absence of their names and perspectives from most European accounts of exploration is itself an important historical problem, illustrating how colonial-era sources systematically obscured African agency even while depending upon it.

Section 46.8

The Road to Conquest

By the late 1870s, several developments converged to transform decades of exploration, missionary activity, and commercial penetration into the beginnings of formal territorial conquest. King Leopold II's creation of the International African Association in 1876, followed by Stanley's Congo expedition of 1874–1877 and Leopold's subsequent maneuvering to establish personal control over the Congo Basin, alarmed other European powers, particularly France and Portugal, who feared being excluded from African territorial gains. French expansion from Senegal into the West African interior accelerated during the same years. British commercial interests in the Niger Delta and Egypt intensified, especially after Britain's occupation of Egypt in 1882 in order to protect the Suez Canal, a strategically vital route to British India.

These overlapping and increasingly competitive European ambitions — Leopold's Congo claims, French expansion in West Africa, British interests in Egypt and the Niger, Portuguese claims based on centuries-old coastal presence, and growing German colonial ambition following unification — created rising diplomatic tension among European powers by the early 1880s. This tension, combined with the accumulated geographical, commercial, and political knowledge gathered over the preceding decades of exploration and mission work, set the stage for the Berlin Conference of 1884–1885 and the rapid formal partition of the continent that followed, examined in full in the next chapter.

Students should understand that this transition from informal influence to formal conquest was neither inevitable nor uniform. It resulted from specific decisions by specific European governments and individuals, responding to specific rivalries and opportunities, and it depended on the prior accumulation of knowledge and infrastructure — treaties, maps, missionary networks, trading posts, medical knowledge, and military technology — built up over the decades this chapter has described. Understanding the exploration and mission era, therefore, is essential to understanding not only what came before the Scramble for Africa but why the Scramble unfolded as rapidly and as thoroughly as it did.

Section 46.9

Africa in the Nineteenth-Century World

The developments examined in this chapter did not occur in isolation. They were part of a broader nineteenth-century process of European industrial and imperial expansion that reshaped much of the world, from South and Southeast Asia to the Pacific to the Americas. Africa's experience during this period shared important features with these other regions — the combination of missionary activity, commercial penetration, scientific "discovery," and eventual territorial conquest — while also reflecting circumstances specific to the continent, including its disease environment, its long history of coastal trade relationships with Europe, and the particular ideologies of race that Europeans had developed over centuries of engagement with Africa and the Atlantic slave trade.

Africa's internal diversity also shaped how this global process played out differently across the continent. North Africa's engagement with European power was shaped by its long Mediterranean and Ottoman connections and by direct French conquest in Algeria beginning in 1830. West Africa's experience was shaped by centuries of coastal Atlantic trade and by powerful interior states such as the Sokoto Caliphate and Asante that maintained substantial autonomy through most of this period. East Africa's experience was shaped by its Indian Ocean trading connections, the influence of Zanzibar and Omani Arab traders, and the strategic importance of routes to India following the opening of the Suez Canal. Southern Africa's experience, discussed extensively in earlier chapters, involved a much longer and more violent history of settler colonization dating back to the seventeenth century. Central Africa, the last major region to experience sustained European exploration, would become the site of the most notorious and violent colonial project of the era under King Leopold II, examined in the following chapter.

Historical Vocabulary
Legitimate Commerce

Legitimate commerce was the term nineteenth-century Europeans used to describe trade in goods such as palm oil, ivory, groundnuts, and rubber, framed as a morally acceptable replacement for the Atlantic slave trade. The term reflects the era's own moral framing rather than a neutral historical category — it implies that earlier trade in enslaved people was simply "illegitimate," while obscuring the coercive labor systems, including various forms of African slavery and forced labor, that often underpinned the production of these supposedly legitimate goods.

Historical Vocabulary
Paternalism

Paternalism describes an attitude, common among missionaries, explorers, and colonial officials, that treated African societies as childlike and in need of European guidance, protection, or discipline. Paternalistic ideology allowed Europeans to frame domination as benevolence, and it profoundly shaped missionary education, colonial administration, and popular European writing about Africa throughout this period and after.

Historical Vocabulary
Concession Company

A concession company was a private commercial firm granted extensive rights — often including administrative and even military authority — over a defined African territory by a European government or, as in Leopold II's case, by a monarch acting in a personal capacity. Concession companies became central instruments of early colonial economic exploitation, examined further in Chapters 47 and 49.

Primary Source Window

David Livingstone's Journals on the East African Slave Trade (1860s–1870s)

Creator: David Livingstone · Date: 1860s–1873 · Type: Missionary journal and travel narrative

Context: During his final Central African expeditions, Livingstone repeatedly encountered caravans of enslaved people being marched toward the East African coast for sale, primarily organized by Arab, Swahili, and some African traders operating through networks connected to Zanzibar. His detailed, often harrowing descriptions of these caravans were published in Britain and became powerful tools of antislavery advocacy.

Livingstone described witnessing captives, including women and children, driven in chains over long distances under brutal conditions, with many dying along the route; he wrote of these scenes with evident horror and used them to argue that only the expansion of "legitimate commerce" and Christian influence could ultimately end such practices.

Analysis: Livingstone's journals are an important primary source for understanding the scale and brutality of the East African slave trade in this period, complementing evidence discussed in Chapter 42. However, students should note that Livingstone's accounts were also shaped by his own missionary and antislavery agenda, and by his broader argument that European commercial and religious expansion was the necessary solution — an argument that, whatever its sincerity, would later be used to justify colonial conquest framed as humanitarian intervention.

Questions for students:

  1. What does this source reveal about the East African slave trade during this period?

  3. What can historians learn from this source that they could not learn from official trade records alone?
  4. How might the enslaved people themselves have described these same events, and why do we not have their own accounts?
Historical Debate

What Motivated European Expansion into Africa Before the Scramble?

The question: Historians have long debated which motive — economic, religious, scientific, or strategic — was most important in driving European exploration and mission activity in nineteenth-century Africa before formal colonial conquest began.

Interpretation A — Economic Motives Were Primary: Some historians argue that commercial interests, particularly the search for new markets and raw materials to replace the declining slave trade, were the fundamental driving force, with religious and scientific activity serving largely as supporting justifications that made commercial penetration more palatable to European publics.
Interpretation B — Religious and Ideological Motives Were Primary: Other historians emphasize the genuine intensity of the evangelical revival and antislavery sentiment, arguing that missionary and humanitarian motives were sincerely held by many participants and shaped the direction and character of European engagement independently of, and sometimes in tension with, purely commercial interests.

Evidence: Economic interpretations point to the rapid expansion of palm oil, ivory, and rubber trade and to merchants' persistent lobbying for government support. Religious and ideological interpretations point to the scale of missionary society funding, the personal writings of missionaries and explorers, and the genuine antislavery commitment reflected in figures like Livingstone.

Why it matters: How historians weigh these motives shapes how they understand the origins of colonial conquest itself — whether as primarily an economic project pursued through humanitarian language, or as a more genuinely mixed and contradictory process in which sincere religious conviction and commercial ambition reinforced one another.

Student task: Using evidence from this chapter, which interpretation do you find more persuasive? Is it possible that both motives operated simultaneously, even within the same individual?

Multiple Perspectives

The Mission Station in Nineteenth-Century Africa

From the perspective of a European missionary: The mission station represented a sacred duty — bringing salvation, literacy, and what missionaries viewed as civilization to peoples they believed were living in spiritual darkness, often at great personal risk from disease and isolation.
From the perspective of an African ruler: The mission station could be a useful tool — a source of literacy, medical care, and diplomatic connection to distant powers — to be permitted, regulated, or restricted according to careful political calculation about the kingdom's interests.
From the perspective of an African convert: The mission station might offer new spiritual meaning, education, and social opportunity, particularly for individuals marginalized within existing social structures, though often at the cost of pressure to abandon established family, cultural, and religious ties.
From the perspective of an established religious or political authority: The mission station could appear as a direct threat — a foreign institution undermining traditional religious practice, social hierarchy, and political loyalty, and a potential vanguard for further foreign intrusion.
Cause and Effect

From Exploration to the Threshold of Partition

Long-Term Causes

Centuries of coastal European-African trade; the decline of the Atlantic slave trade after abolition; the rise of European industrial economies seeking raw materials and markets; the evangelical revival driving missionary expansion; European racial ideology developed over centuries of contact with Africa.

Short-Term Causes

Quinine and steamship technology enabling deeper interior travel; celebrated expeditions publicizing African geography and resources; Leopold II's Congo ambitions after Stanley's 1874–1877 expedition; rising inter-European rivalry in the late 1870s.

The Event

By the early 1880s, competing European commercial and political claims across the African interior create diplomatic tension among European powers.

Effects

The convening of the Berlin Conference (1884–1885); the rapid formal partition of Africa; the transition from informal influence to direct colonial conquest, examined in Chapter 47.

Historical complexity: No single cause explains the shift from exploration to conquest. It resulted from the convergence of medical, technological, economic, religious, and political developments across several decades.

Continuity and Change Over Time

African Coastal Trade, 1800–1880

What Changed

  • The commodity composition of trade shifted from enslaved people toward palm oil, ivory, groundnuts, and rubber
  • European traders increasingly sought direct interior access rather than relying solely on coastal middlemen
  • Missionary and exploration networks extended European presence far beyond the coast for the first time
  • Steamships and quinine made sustained interior travel newly feasible

What Stayed the Same

  • Most of the continent remained under African political control through the 1870s
  • African merchants and rulers continued to set many of the terms of trade well into this period
  • European knowledge of the interior remained dependent on African guides, translators, and porters
  • Existing African trade routes and networks continued to structure the paths explorers and traders followed

Why the pattern matters: Recognizing this mixture of change and continuity helps students avoid treating the exploration era as a simple, one-directional slide toward inevitable European conquest.

Everyday Life and Social History
Mission Station Communities

Mission stations were not only religious institutions but small, evolving communities that reshaped daily life for the Africans who lived in or near them. Converts and their families often adopted new patterns of dress, housing, and schedule modeled on European Christian practice, attended mission schools that taught literacy alongside Christian doctrine, and sought treatment at small mission dispensaries and hospitals that introduced Western medical practices alongside existing healing traditions. Women frequently played central roles in these communities, both as converts seeking new forms of autonomy or protection and as targets of missionary efforts to reshape African marriage, family, and gender norms according to Victorian Christian models. Labor at mission stations — building construction, farming, and domestic service — was often provided by African residents, sometimes voluntarily and sometimes under considerable social or economic pressure. These small, often fragile communities became, over subsequent decades, important seedbeds for African Christian leadership, education, and eventually — in some cases — political organization and resistance to colonial rule itself.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has examined the decades preceding the formal colonial partition of Africa, when European exploration, missionary activity, commercial expansion, and scientific ambition combined to transform the nature of European engagement with the continent. For centuries, that engagement had been overwhelmingly coastal, conducted largely on terms set by African states and merchants. Beginning in the early to mid-nineteenth century, a combination of medical advances, technological change, religious revival, commercial reorientation following the decline of the Atlantic slave trade, and scientific ambition drove European explorers, missionaries, and traders deep into the African interior for the first time in sustained and significant numbers.

Explorers such as Heinrich Barth, David Livingstone, Richard Burton, John Hanning Speke, and Henry Morton Stanley became celebrated figures in Europe, their journeys publicized as triumphs of science and civilization. Yet this chapter has emphasized that their "discoveries" depended entirely on African knowledge, labor, and cooperation, and that the landscapes, rivers, and peoples they encountered were already thoroughly known to the African and Arab societies who had long inhabited and traveled through them. The framing of these journeys as heroic discovery obscured the reality of African historical agency and knowledge.

Christian missionary societies expanded rapidly across the continent during this period, driven by the evangelical revival in Europe and North America. Missionaries produced much of the detailed European written knowledge of African societies available during this era, but that knowledge was consistently filtered through assumptions of African inferiority and the conviction that African religious and social practices required transformation. African responses to missionary activity ranged from strategic engagement to cautious restriction to outright rejection, reflecting the diverse political calculations of African rulers and communities.

The shift from the Atlantic slave trade toward "legitimate commerce" in palm oil, ivory, groundnuts, and rubber reoriented European commercial interest toward direct access to African interior markets and resources, creating economic incentives that reinforced exploration and missionary activity. Ideologies of European racial superiority, the "civilizing mission," and social Darwinism provided justification for growing European intervention, often intertwining, in complicated and sometimes contradictory ways, with genuine antislavery sentiment and humanitarian conviction.

By the late 1870s, the convergence of accumulated geographical knowledge, expanding commercial and missionary networks, new medical and technological capabilities, and intensifying rivalry among European powers — symbolized above all by King Leopold II's ambitions in the Congo Basin following Stanley's 1874–1877 expedition — set the stage for the rapid formal partition of the African continent examined in the next chapter.

Throughout this chapter, students have been asked to hold multiple perspectives simultaneously: the sincere religious conviction of many missionaries alongside the paternalism and cultural destruction their work often produced; the genuine scientific curiosity of many explorers alongside the racial ideology that shaped their accounts; the real antislavery commitment of figures like Livingstone alongside the way that commitment would later be used to justify conquest; and, throughout, the active historical agency of African rulers, merchants, guides, translators, and converts who shaped these encounters as much as any European explorer or missionary did.

This period should not be understood as an inevitable, linear march toward colonial conquest. Most of the African continent remained under African political control through the 1870s, and African societies engaged with European visitors from positions of considerable strength, exercising choice, strategy, and control over the terms of encounter. Understanding this reality is essential both to accurate historical understanding and to recognizing that the conquest which followed in the 1880s and 1890s was the product of specific decisions, rivalries, and technological changes — not an inevitable outcome written into the nature of the encounter itself.

The chapter that follows, on the Scramble for Africa, will show how the knowledge, networks, and ideologies examined here were rapidly mobilized into formal territorial conquest, beginning with the Berlin Conference of 1884–1885. Understanding the exploration and mission era is therefore essential preparation for understanding how and why that conquest unfolded as it did.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter matters because it reveals that colonial conquest did not begin with the Berlin Conference of 1884–1885. It began decades earlier, in the accumulation of geographical knowledge, missionary networks, commercial relationships, and ideological justification that this chapter has traced. Understanding this slower, more gradual process helps students see colonialism not as a sudden event but as the culmination of long-building forces — some intentional, some contingent, some driven by sincere conviction and others by naked ambition.

This chapter also shaped later African history in ways that extended far beyond the immediate colonial conquest. The mission stations established in this period would become important sites of African education, Christian community, and eventually political organization, producing figures who would later challenge the very colonial order that missionary activity helped make possible. The commercial networks built around "legitimate commerce" would evolve into the extractive colonial economies examined in Chapter 49. The maps, treaties, and ethnographic knowledge gathered by explorers and missionaries would become tools that European governments used, at the Berlin Conference and after, to draw borders and stake claims with little regard for African political realities.

The larger theme this chapter reveals is the complexity and contingency of historical change. Nothing about European conquest of Africa was written in advance. It resulted from specific technological breakthroughs, specific individual ambitions, specific African political choices, and specific moments of rivalry and opportunity. Recognizing this complexity does not excuse the conquest that followed, but it does insist that students understand it as history — the product of human decisions, open to different outcomes — rather than as an inevitable clash of civilizations.

Finally, this chapter asks students to hold African agency and European power in view at the same time. African rulers, merchants, guides, translators, and converts were not passive subjects of European exploration and mission activity; they were active participants who shaped, resisted, redirected, and sometimes exploited these encounters for their own purposes. That so few of their names survive in the historical record is itself a consequence of the very power imbalance this chapter traces — a reminder that whose voices are preserved in history is never a neutral matter, and that recovering those voices, wherever possible, remains an essential task for historians of Africa today.

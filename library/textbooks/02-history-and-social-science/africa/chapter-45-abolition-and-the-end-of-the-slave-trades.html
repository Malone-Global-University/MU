Part VII — Slavery, Commerce, and the African Diaspora
45

Abolition and the End of the Slave Trades

Africa: A History of a Continent

Chapter Opening

"The abolition of the slave trade was not a single event but a long, uneven, and often violent transition, contested at every stage by those who profited from human bondage and advanced, unevenly, by those who resisted it."

In March 1807, the British Parliament passed the Slave Trade Act, prohibiting British subjects from participating in the transatlantic slave trade. It was a landmark moment celebrated for generations afterward as the triumph of moral conscience over commercial greed. Yet in the very same years that Britain congratulated itself on ending its participation in the trade, ships continued to leave the coasts of West and Central Africa loaded with captives bound for Brazil, Cuba, and the southern United States. Portuguese, Spanish, French, and American traders, along with African merchants and rulers who depended on the trade for wealth and political power, found ways to continue the business for decades. The passage of a law in London did not, by itself, end the enslavement and forced transport of Africans. It began a long, uneven, and often violent process that would take most of the nineteenth century to complete, and whose consequences would extend into the twentieth century and beyond.

This chapter examines that long process: the rise of abolitionist movements in Europe and the Americas, the diplomatic and naval campaigns to suppress the trade at sea, the responses of African states and societies whose economies and politics had become entangled with slave trading, the economic transformations that reshaped African commerce as the trade declined, the persistence of illegal trafficking long after formal abolition, and the complicated, incomplete nature of freedom for those who were liberated. It also examines a paradox that historians have long debated: the same decades that saw the Atlantic slave trade suppressed also saw the expansion of European colonial ambitions in Africa, often justified in the language of ending slavery and spreading "legitimate commerce."

Abolition did not happen in a single place or through a single cause. It emerged from religious revival movements that reconsidered slavery as a moral evil, from economic arguments that questioned whether slave labor was actually more profitable than free labor, from the resistance and rebellion of enslaved people themselves, from geopolitical rivalries between European powers, and from African political and economic actors who, for their own reasons, began to shift away from the slave trade toward other forms of commerce. Understanding abolition requires holding all of these forces together, rather than reducing the story to a simple narrative of European moral awakening.

It is also essential to recognize that abolition was experienced very differently depending on where a person stood. For enslaved people awaiting transport in coastal barracoons, the passage of anti-slave-trade laws in distant European capitals meant little unless it was enforced by patrol ships capable of intercepting slave vessels. For African rulers and merchants whose treasuries depended on the trade, abolition threatened political and economic collapse. For newly freed Africans resettled in places like Sierra Leone and Liberia, freedom came bundled with new forms of dependency, cultural dislocation, and colonial oversight. For enslaved Africans within the continent, the end of the Atlantic and Indian Ocean export trades did not necessarily mean the end of slavery itself, since internal African slavery in many regions expanded even as the external trades contracted.

This chapter also traces how the campaign against the slave trade became entangled with the origins of European colonialism in Africa. British anti-slavery policy, backed by the Royal Navy's West Africa Squadron, combined genuine humanitarian conviction with strategic and commercial interests. Naval suppression required treaties, coastal bases, and expanding influence over African rulers. Missionaries who followed abolitionist convictions into the African interior often became agents of cultural transformation and, eventually, colonial penetration. The rhetoric of ending slavery would later be used to justify the conquest and colonization of African territories in the late nineteenth century, even as forced labor systems under colonial rule frequently replicated many of the abuses that abolitionists had condemned.

The story told in this chapter, then, is not one of simple moral progress. It is a story of contested change, in which genuine humanitarian achievement coexisted with continued exploitation, in which formal legal victories took decades to translate into practical reality, and in which the end of one system of coerced labor often gave way to new forms of economic dependency and colonial control. Students should approach this chapter prepared to hold multiple truths simultaneously: that the abolition of the slave trade was a real and important historical achievement, that it was incomplete and unevenly enforced, that African societies played active and varied roles in the process, and that its legacies shaped the colonial era that followed.

By the end of this chapter, students should understand both the scale of the moral and political struggle required to formally end the Atlantic and Indian Ocean slave trades and the long shadow that slavery's afterlife cast over African economies, societies, and politics well into the modern era.

Historical Context

By the late eighteenth century, the Atlantic slave trade had operated for more than three hundred years, transporting an estimated twelve million or more captive Africans across the ocean to the Americas, with additional millions dying during capture, transport within Africa, and the Middle Passage itself. The trade had become deeply embedded in the economies of Western Europe, the Americas, and significant regions of West and Central Africa. European merchants built fortunes on the sale of textiles, firearms, alcohol, and manufactured goods exchanged for captives. American planters depended on enslaved labor to produce sugar, tobacco, cotton, and rice for world markets. African rulers and merchants who controlled the supply chains of captives, from inland raiding and warfare through coastal ports, had built political and economic systems around the trade's profits.

Yet by the second half of the eighteenth century, currents of change were building. The Enlightenment produced new philosophical arguments about natural rights and human equality that, when applied consistently, condemned slavery as a violation of fundamental human dignity. Religious revival movements, particularly among Quakers, Methodists, and evangelical Protestants in Britain and North America, increasingly viewed slavery as sin rather than simply an unfortunate but permissible economic institution. Enslaved people themselves resisted continuously through flight, rebellion, sabotage, and everyday resistance, and their actions, especially the Haitian Revolution beginning in 1791, demonstrated both the human cost of slavery and the vulnerability of slave societies to organized resistance.

Economic arguments also began to challenge the assumption that slave labor was the only viable foundation for profitable colonial agriculture. Some economists and merchants argued that free wage labor, combined with expanding "legitimate commerce" in African commodities such as palm oil, groundnuts, gum arabic, ivory, and gold, could replace the profits of the slave trade without its moral costs. These arguments would prove influential, though often self-serving, as European commercial interests sought new sources of profit in Africa even as they condemned the old trade.

It is important for students to understand that abolition was never simply a matter of European conscience acting upon a passive Africa. African societies were active participants throughout this history, both as suppliers of the trade and as agents of its eventual decline. Some African states, such as Dahomey and the Sokoto Caliphate, depended heavily on slave trading and slave labor and resisted abolition fiercely. Others, particularly rulers seeking new alliances with Britain or new sources of revenue from legitimate commerce, adapted more readily. Understanding this complexity is essential to avoiding a simplified narrative in which Europe alone acts and Africa alone is acted upon.

Section 45.1

The Rise of Abolitionism

The abolitionist movement that emerged in Britain during the 1780s did not appear from nowhere. It drew on decades of moral, religious, and philosophical debate about the ethics of slavery, combined with an increasingly organized network of activists prepared to campaign publicly for legal change. Quaker communities in Britain and North America were among the earliest to formally condemn slaveholding among their own members, arguing that the institution violated core religious principles of human equality before God. Evangelical Christians, energized by revival movements, increasingly viewed the slave trade as a national sin requiring atonement.

The Society for Effecting the Abolition of the Slave Trade, founded in London in 1787, brought together Quakers, evangelical Anglicans, and secular reformers in a coordinated campaign. It pioneered techniques of political mobilization that would become standard tools of later reform movements: mass petition drives, consumer boycotts of slave-produced sugar, public lectures, pamphlets illustrated with diagrams of slave ships packed with human cargo, and testimony from formerly enslaved people such as Olaudah Equiano, whose published narrative gave British readers a rare first-person account of enslavement, the Middle Passage, and eventual freedom.

Parliamentary leadership fell to William Wilberforce, who introduced abolition bills repeatedly through the 1790s and early 1800s, facing determined opposition from members of Parliament connected to the West Indian sugar interest and the shipping industry. It took twenty years of sustained campaigning, changing political circumstances, and growing public pressure before the Slave Trade Act finally passed in 1807, prohibiting British ships from carrying enslaved people. Critically, the 1807 act ended the trade but did not abolish slavery itself within British colonies; that would not occur until the Slavery Abolition Act of 1833, and even then only with a system of paid "apprenticeship" that kept many formerly enslaved people in coerced labor for several additional years.

Abolitionism developed somewhat differently in other Atlantic societies. In the United States, the 1808 ban on the importation of enslaved people coexisted with the continued expansion of the domestic slave trade and slavery itself for another half-century, ending only with the Civil War and the Thirteenth Amendment in 1865. In France, the revolutionary government abolished slavery in 1794 only to see Napoleon restore it in 1802; French abolition was not made final until 1848. Spanish and Portuguese colonies, along with Brazil, retained both the slave trade and slavery far longer, with Brazil not abolishing slavery until 1888. These varied timelines demonstrate that abolition was neither instantaneous nor uniform across the Atlantic world, but a protracted struggle shaped by each society's particular economic interests and political circumstances.

Section 45.2

Anti-Slavery Movements in the Atlantic World

Abolitionism was not confined to parliamentary campaigns in London. It was a transatlantic movement that connected activists, formerly enslaved people, religious congregations, and political organizations across Britain, the United States, the Caribbean, and, to varying degrees, continental Europe. American abolitionism developed its own distinctive character, shaped by the presence of a large enslaved population within the United States itself and by the activism of free Black communities in the North.

Figures such as Frederick Douglass, Harriet Tubman, and Sojourner Truth, along with organizations such as the American Anti-Slavery Society, combined moral argument with direct action, including participation in the Underground Railroad that helped enslaved people escape to freedom. American abolitionism was frequently more radical than its British counterpart in demanding immediate rather than gradual emancipation, reflecting the direct stakes that Black abolitionists, many of them formerly enslaved themselves, brought to the movement.

The enslaved people of the Atlantic world were themselves central actors in the anti-slavery movement, not merely its beneficiaries. The Haitian Revolution, in which enslaved people on the French colony of Saint-Domingue rose up beginning in 1791 and eventually established the independent nation of Haiti in 1804, demonstrated to enslavers and abolitionists alike that enslaved people were capable of organized, sustained, and successful resistance. The revolution terrified slaveholding societies throughout the Americas and inspired enslaved communities elsewhere, even as it was used by some abolitionists as evidence of the moral urgency of ending slavery before further violence occurred, and by proslavery advocates as evidence that emancipation would lead to chaos and racial violence.

Resistance within Africa itself, though less studied by earlier generations of historians, also shaped the trajectory of abolition. Enslaved people transported along African trade routes to coastal ports resisted through flight, rebellion aboard slave ships, and refusal to cooperate with captors. African communities that were targets of slave raiding sometimes organized defensive alliances or relocated to less accessible terrain to avoid capture. These forms of resistance, while rarely achieving the scale of the Haitian Revolution, constituted an ongoing pressure against the slave trade's operation that complemented the formal political campaigns unfolding in Europe and the Americas.

Section 45.3

Ending the Trade at Sea

Passing a law prohibiting the slave trade was one thing; enforcing it across thousands of miles of African coastline and open ocean was another matter entirely. Britain established the Royal Navy's West Africa Squadron in 1808, tasking it with patrolling the West African coast, intercepting suspected slave ships, and freeing their captives. Over the following decades, the squadron grew into a substantial naval commitment, eventually numbering dozens of ships and thousands of sailors, and it captured hundreds of slave vessels and liberated tens of thousands of captives.

Yet naval suppression faced enormous practical obstacles. The West African coastline stretched for thousands of miles, offering countless hidden inlets and river mouths where slave ships could load captives away from patrol routes. Slave traders adapted their vessels to be faster than naval patrol ships, and many outran pursuit. International law complicated enforcement further: Britain could only stop and search ships flying the flags of nations that had signed treaties granting mutual search rights, and several major slave-trading nations, including the United States for much of this period, refused to grant British ships the right to search vessels flying their flag. Slave traders routinely exploited this loophole by flying flags of convenience from nations that had not signed search treaties.

Britain pursued a parallel diplomatic strategy alongside naval enforcement, negotiating bilateral treaties with individual African coastal rulers that prohibited slave trading from their territories in exchange for subsidies, trade preferences, or military support. These treaties extended British anti-slavery policy directly into African political relationships, and their negotiation and enforcement became an important vector through which British influence over African coastal states expanded during the nineteenth century, laying groundwork that would later support formal colonial claims.

When naval patrols did intercept slave ships, the captives aboard, known as "recaptives" or "liberated Africans," were not simply returned to their homes, which were often impossible to identify or reach. Instead, most were taken to Sierra Leone, where the colony of Freetown had been established in 1787 partly as a settlement for freed and formerly enslaved people. Tens of thousands of recaptives were resettled there over the following decades, creating a remarkably diverse population drawn from many different African ethnic and linguistic backgrounds, thrown together under British colonial administration and often converted to Christianity through missionary efforts associated with the colony.

Section 45.4

African Responses to Abolition

African rulers, merchants, and communities responded to abolition in widely varying ways, shaped by how deeply their economies and political systems depended on the slave trade. For states whose wealth and military power rested heavily on slave raiding and slave exports, abolition posed an existential threat. The Kingdom of Dahomey, whose economy and royal treasury depended substantially on slave trading through the port of Ouidah, resisted British pressure for decades, continuing to export captives well after 1807 despite repeated British diplomatic efforts to negotiate an end to the trade. Dahomean rulers argued, not without justification, that abolition demanded by a foreign power threatened to collapse their kingdom's economic foundation without offering a viable alternative.

Other African states and coastal merchant communities adapted more readily, particularly where alternative sources of revenue were available. Along parts of the West African coast, merchants and rulers increasingly shifted toward the export of palm oil, groundnuts, and other agricultural commodities as European industrial demand for these products grew, providing a partial economic substitute for the declining slave trade. This shift, often described by contemporaries as the transition to "legitimate commerce," did not happen uniformly or without difficulty, and in some regions it took decades to develop sufficient scale to replace slave trade revenues.

Some African rulers actively cooperated with British anti-slavery policy, signing treaties, accepting subsidies, and in some cases using British alliance to strengthen their position against regional rivals who continued slave trading. Other communities, particularly those that had long been targets of slave raiding rather than beneficiaries of the trade, welcomed abolition as protection against continued predation, even as they remained skeptical of the broader political and economic motives behind British involvement in their affairs.

In the Sokoto Caliphate and other major states of the Central Sudan, slavery remained deeply embedded in agricultural production, military organization, and elite households well into the late nineteenth century, largely insulated from Atlantic abolitionist pressure because these states lay far from the coast and were not directly engaged in the export slave trade in the same way as coastal kingdoms. Internal African slavery in these regions would persist, in some cases, until early twentieth-century colonial administrations moved, often gradually and incompletely, to formally abolish it.

Historical Debate

Was British Abolition Driven Primarily by Humanitarian Conviction or Economic Self-Interest?

The question: Historians have long debated why Britain, the single largest slave-trading nation in the eighteenth century, became the leading force behind abolition in the nineteenth. Was this transformation driven primarily by genuine moral and religious conviction, or by shifting economic interests that made abolition profitable or strategically advantageous?

Interpretation A — Humanitarian Awakening: Some historians emphasize the sincerity of religious and philosophical conviction among abolitionists like Wilberforce, arguing that sustained moral campaigning, informed by genuine horror at the conditions of the Middle Passage and the testimony of formerly enslaved people, was the primary engine of change. This view credits the moral seriousness of the movement and its willingness to campaign for two decades against powerful economic interests.
Interpretation B — Economic Self-Interest (the "Decline Thesis"): Other historians, following the influential argument of Eric Williams, contend that British abolition coincided with the declining profitability of Caribbean sugar plantations relative to other investments, and that abolishing the slave trade served British commercial interests by undermining rival slave-trading powers such as France and by opening new markets for manufactured goods in Africa through "legitimate commerce." In this view, moral rhetoric provided useful cover for a shift in economic strategy.

Evidence: Supporters of Interpretation A point to the sustained, costly nature of the abolitionist campaign and Britain's willingness to maintain an expensive naval squadron for decades after abolition offered no direct economic return. Supporters of Interpretation B point to the timing of abolition relative to shifts in sugar markets and the ways British anti-slavery diplomacy expanded British commercial and political influence in Africa.

Why it matters: This debate shapes how historians understand the broader relationship between morality and economic interest in historical change, and it has direct relevance to how students evaluate the motives behind later European involvement in Africa, including colonization justified in anti-slavery language.

Student task: Consider the evidence for both interpretations. Is it possible that both moral conviction and economic self-interest operated simultaneously? What kind of evidence would help historians weigh the relative importance of each factor?

Section 45.5

New Economic Pressures

The decline of the external slave trade forced significant economic reorganization across many African regions, particularly along the West African coast where slave exports had long dominated commercial life. The shift toward "legitimate commerce," centered on the export of palm oil, groundnuts (peanuts), gum arabic, ivory, and later cocoa and rubber, reflected growing European industrial demand for raw materials, particularly vegetable oils used in soap and candle manufacturing during Europe's industrializing economies.

This economic transition was neither smooth nor equitable in its effects. Coastal merchant communities and rulers who successfully redirected their commercial networks toward legitimate commerce, such as several Niger Delta trading states, sometimes prospered under the new system, in some cases even expanding their wealth and political influence. Yet the transition also created new pressures. Palm oil production and export required different labor arrangements than slave trading; ironically, in some regions the demand for labor to produce palm oil and other agricultural exports increased the internal use of enslaved labor within Africa even as the external export of captives declined, since coerced agricultural labor could now be employed locally rather than exported.

The economic disruption of abolition also intensified competition and, in some cases, conflict between African coastal states and merchant groups as they competed for position within the reorganizing commercial system. European trading companies and, increasingly, colonial administrations began to exert greater direct control over African commodity production and trade routes, gradually eroding the commercial autonomy that African merchants and rulers had exercised during the era of the slave trade, when European traders had generally depended on African intermediaries to supply captives.

Historical Vocabulary
Legitimate Commerce

Legitimate commerce refers to the nineteenth-century shift in African-European trade away from the export of enslaved captives toward the export of agricultural and natural commodities such as palm oil, groundnuts, gum arabic, and ivory. The term was used by contemporaries to distinguish this trade from the slave trade, framing it as morally acceptable commerce that could replace the profits of slave trading without its human cost.

Historians note that "legitimate commerce" was not always as benign as the term implies. In some regions, the demand for export commodities increased the use of coerced or enslaved labor within Africa itself, since production of palm oil and other exports required substantial labor that was often supplied through existing systems of slavery. The term is therefore useful for understanding contemporary European perspectives but should not be taken as a complete or neutral description of the economic changes it describes.

Section 45.6

Illegal Trafficking and Persistence

Formal abolition laws did not immediately stop the slave trade, and in some periods illegal trafficking continued at substantial scale for decades after prohibition. The trade to Brazil and Cuba, in particular, persisted robustly through the 1840s, 1850s, and into the 1860s, driven by continued high demand for enslaved labor on sugar and coffee plantations and by the profitability of an increasingly risky but still lucrative trade.

Slave traders adapted to naval suppression through faster ships, false flags, bribery of local officials, and the use of remote embarkation points along the coast that were difficult for patrol ships to monitor. The trade to Cuba continued in significant volume until Spain finally moved to enforce suppression more seriously in the 1860s, while the trade to Brazil persisted until Brazilian domestic pressure and British diplomatic pressure combined to force more effective enforcement after 1850, following the passage of Brazil's Eusébio de Queirós Law.

The persistence of illegal trafficking illustrates an important historical lesson: formal legal prohibition, without effective enforcement mechanisms and without addressing the underlying economic demand that sustained the trade, was insufficient to end a deeply entrenched commercial system. It took the combination of naval suppression, diplomatic pressure, economic incentives toward alternative commerce, and eventually the abolition of slavery itself in major American receiving societies to finally bring the transatlantic slave trade to an end by the 1860s and 1870s.

The Indian Ocean and Red Sea slave trades, examined in greater detail elsewhere in this textbook, followed a somewhat different and, in some respects, even longer timeline, with trafficking continuing in parts of East Africa and the Arabian Peninsula into the early twentieth century, reflecting different colonial priorities, weaker naval presence, and continued demand in Middle Eastern and South Asian markets.

Historical Vocabulary
Recaptive (Liberated African)

A recaptive, also called a "liberated African," was a formerly enslaved person freed by naval patrols intercepting slave ships before they completed the Middle Passage. Recaptives were typically unable to return to their communities of origin, since their exact homelands were often unknown or unreachable, and were instead resettled in colonies such as Sierra Leone.

The experience of recaptives illustrates the incomplete and complicated nature of freedom achieved through naval suppression. While no longer enslaved, recaptives faced displacement, cultural dislocation, and integration into colonial societies under British administration, often accompanied by missionary efforts to convert them to Christianity and reshape their cultural practices.

Section 45.7

Social and Political Change

The suppression of the slave trade and the eventual abolition of slavery in the Americas produced significant social and political transformations, both within Africa and across the diaspora. Sierra Leone's Freetown settlement became a distinctive social laboratory, home to a diverse population of recaptives from many different African ethnic backgrounds who developed a new shared identity, language (Krio), and culture that blended African, British, and Christian influences. Freetown's educated elite, including graduates of Fourah Bay College founded in 1827, would later play significant roles as missionaries, traders, and administrators across West Africa, spreading education and Christianity while also, in some cases, serving as intermediaries for expanding British colonial influence.

Liberia followed a related but distinct trajectory, founded by the American Colonization Society beginning in 1822 as a settlement for freed African Americans and free-born Black Americans, some of whom emigrated voluntarily seeking greater freedom and opportunity, while others faced pressure or coercion from American colonization advocates who saw resettlement in Africa as an alternative to full racial integration within the United States. Liberia declared independence in 1847, becoming one of Africa's first independent republics, though its political structure privileged the settler ("Americo-Liberian") minority over indigenous Liberian populations for more than a century, creating deep and lasting social divisions.

Within African societies more broadly, the decline of the external slave trade altered political calculations for rulers and merchants who had built power on slave trading revenues, in some cases contributing to political instability, succession crises, or the decline of states whose economic foundations had rested heavily on the trade. Meanwhile, coastal communities engaged in legitimate commerce sometimes saw the rise of new merchant elites whose wealth derived from agricultural exports rather than slave trading, gradually reshaping social hierarchies in commercially active regions.

Multiple Perspectives

Experiencing Abolition: Different Vantage Points

From the perspective of a British abolitionist campaigner: Abolition represented the triumph of Christian conscience and human rights over greed, a hard-won victory after decades of political struggle against powerful commercial interests, and a source of national moral pride.
From the perspective of a Dahomean or Sokoto ruler dependent on slave trading or slave labor: Abolition, especially when imposed through foreign diplomatic and military pressure, threatened the economic and political foundations of the state and was often resisted as an unwelcome foreign intrusion into internal affairs, regardless of its moral claims.
From the perspective of a recaptive resettled in Sierra Leone: Freedom from the Middle Passage came bundled with displacement from one's homeland, loss of original language and community ties, and integration into a new colonial society under British authority, along with the opportunity, for some, to build new lives, education, and community in Freetown.
From the perspective of an enslaved person still held within an African society after the export trade declined: The end of the Atlantic slave trade often meant little immediate change, since internal slavery within many African states continued and, in some regions, even expanded as demand grew for coerced agricultural labor to supply the new legitimate commerce.
Everyday Life and Social History

Life in Freed Communities: Freetown and Freedom Villages

For recaptives resettled in Sierra Leone, daily life involved navigating a new colonial social order. Many were assigned to mission-run villages where they received basic education, religious instruction, and training in trades. Families often had to be rebuilt from scratch among strangers speaking different languages, since recaptives arrived from many different regions of West and Central Africa. Over time, a distinctive Krio culture and language emerged, blending elements of English, various African languages, and Christian practice.

Similar "freedom villages" emerged in other parts of Africa and the Atlantic world where formerly enslaved people settled after emancipation, often facing continued economic marginalization, limited land access, and social stigma even after achieving legal freedom. Everyday survival for freed communities frequently depended on small-scale farming, petty trade, wage labor, or entry into mission-supported education and craft occupations. The experience of freedom, for most formerly enslaved people, was thus a matter of ongoing struggle for economic security and social recognition rather than an immediate transformation of circumstances.

Section 45.8

Slavery's Long Afterlife

The formal abolition of the slave trade and, eventually, of slavery itself in the Americas did not erase the economic and social structures that slavery had created. In the Americas, formerly enslaved people and their descendants faced continued economic exploitation through systems such as sharecropping in the United States, debt peonage, and legal discrimination that persisted for generations after emancipation. In Africa, internal slavery in many regions persisted well into the colonial period, with formal abolition often delayed by colonial administrations reluctant to disrupt local labor systems on which colonial economies depended.

In the Sokoto Caliphate and surrounding regions, for example, British colonial authorities after their conquest in 1903 initially avoided direct confrontation with slavery, fearing political instability, and instituted only gradual measures toward abolition, allowing existing enslaved people to remain in bondage while prohibiting new enslavement. Full legal abolition and, more importantly, effective enforcement of freedom for those still enslaved, took additional decades to achieve in some regions.

The demographic and economic consequences of the slave trade also persisted long after its formal end. Regions of Africa that had experienced the heaviest slave raiding and export during the trade's peak centuries show, according to some economic historians, measurable long-term effects on population density, trust between communities, and economic development that persisted into the twentieth and twenty-first centuries, illustrating how deeply the slave trade's disruptions were woven into the long-term historical trajectory of affected regions.

Cause and Effect

The End of the Atlantic Slave Trade

Long-Term Causes

Enlightenment ideas about natural rights; evangelical religious revival condemning slavery as sin; continuous resistance by enslaved people; shifting economic calculations about slave versus free labor profitability.

Short-Term Causes

The Haitian Revolution (1791–1804); sustained British parliamentary abolitionist campaigning; the 1807 Slave Trade Act; diplomatic pressure and bilateral treaties with African and American states.

The Event/Transformation

Decades-long naval suppression campaign; gradual negotiation of anti-slave-trade treaties; decline and eventual collapse of the transatlantic trade by the 1860s–1870s.

Effects

Resettlement of recaptives in Sierra Leone and Liberia; economic reorganization of African coastal commerce toward "legitimate commerce"; persistence of internal African slavery; expansion of British influence over African coastal politics, laying groundwork for later colonization.

Historical complexity: The end of the slave trade cannot be attributed to a single cause or a single set of actors. It resulted from the interaction of moral campaigning, economic calculation, enslaved resistance, and shifting geopolitical interests, unfolding unevenly over nearly a century.

Section 45.9

Abolition and Historical Transition

The abolition of the slave trade marked a genuine historical turning point, but it should be understood as a transition rather than a clean break. The economic, social, and political structures that the slave trade had built over three centuries did not disappear with the passage of laws or the patrol of naval squadrons. Instead, African societies, European powers, and diaspora communities all navigated a prolonged period of adjustment, during which old patterns of exploitation frequently found new forms.

The 1890 Brussels Conference, which produced a General Act formally committing European powers to the suppression of the African slave trade, illustrates this transitional and often contradictory character. The conference's anti-slavery rhetoric provided moral justification for expanded European military and administrative presence in Africa at precisely the moment when the Scramble for Africa was intensifying colonial conquest across the continent. Anti-slavery, in other words, became simultaneously a genuine humanitarian cause and a convenient justification for imperial expansion, a duality that would shape the character of colonial rule in the decades that followed.

By the close of the nineteenth century, the Atlantic slave trade had effectively ended, and slavery itself had been formally abolished in nearly every American society. Yet the abolition of slavery within Africa itself remained incomplete, colonial forced labor systems would soon replicate many of slavery's abuses under different legal names, and the economic and social consequences of three centuries of slave trading continued to shape African societies well into the era of colonial rule and beyond.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Coerced Labor Before and After Formal Abolition

What changed: The legal prohibition of the Atlantic slave trade; the eventual abolition of slavery as a legal institution across the Americas; the resettlement and partial integration of recaptives into new communities; the reorientation of African coastal commerce toward agricultural exports.

What stayed the same: The persistence of internal African slavery in many regions for decades after external trade declined; the continued economic marginalization of formerly enslaved people and their descendants; the reliance of expanding colonial economies on various forms of coerced or compulsory labor, including forced labor systems that colonial administrations would later impose across much of Africa.

Why this pattern matters: The persistence of coerced labor in new forms after formal abolition demonstrates that legal change, while historically significant, does not automatically eliminate the underlying economic incentives and power structures that sustained the original system. Students should recognize this pattern as they study the forced labor systems of the colonial era in later chapters.

Primary Source Window

Anglo-African Anti-Slave-Trade Treaty (Typical Nineteenth-Century Example)

Type: Bilateral diplomatic treaty · Period: Mid-nineteenth century · Parties: British colonial or naval authorities and a West African coastal ruler

Context: Throughout the nineteenth century, British officials negotiated dozens of treaties with West African coastal rulers, promising subsidies, trade preferences, or protection in exchange for agreements to end slave exports from their territories. These treaties varied considerably in how they were negotiated, ranging from relatively balanced diplomatic exchanges to arrangements imposed under significant military or economic pressure.

Such treaties typically bound the African ruler to prohibit the sale or export of captives from ports under their authority, permitted British vessels to inspect suspected slave ships departing from those ports, and offered in exchange annual payments, gifts, or promises of British support against regional rivals.

Analysis: These treaties reveal the extent to which anti-slave-trade diplomacy became intertwined with the expansion of British political influence along the African coast. While framed as agreements between sovereign parties, the treaties were frequently negotiated from positions of unequal power, with African rulers facing the prospect of naval blockade or loss of British trade and support if they refused to sign. The treaties also demonstrate that African rulers were not passive subjects of British policy but active negotiators who used these agreements to pursue their own political interests, including protection against rival states.

  4. What questions would historians need to ask about any specific treaty to assess how voluntarily it was signed?

Chapter Summary

The abolition of the Atlantic slave trade unfolded over nearly a century, from the earliest organized British campaigning in the 1780s to the effective collapse of illegal trafficking to Brazil and Cuba by the 1870s. It was driven by a combination of religious conviction, Enlightenment philosophy, enslaved resistance, and shifting economic interests, and it was contested at every stage by those, both European and African, whose wealth and power depended on the continuation of slave trading.

British parliamentary abolitionists, led by figures such as William Wilberforce and informed by the testimony of formerly enslaved writers such as Olaudah Equiano, built a sustained political campaign that culminated in the 1807 Slave Trade Act and, later, the 1833 Slavery Abolition Act. Yet legal prohibition alone did not end the trade. The Royal Navy's West Africa Squadron spent decades attempting to enforce suppression at sea, facing enormous practical obstacles including the length of the African coastline, the speed and adaptability of slave ships, and the refusal of several nations to grant mutual search rights.

African societies responded to abolition in diverse ways shaped by their particular relationship to the slave trade. States like Dahomey and the Sokoto Caliphate, whose economies depended heavily on slave trading and slave labor, resisted abolition for decades. Coastal merchants and rulers who found opportunities in "legitimate commerce," particularly the export of palm oil and other agricultural commodities, adapted more readily, though this transition was neither smooth nor free of new forms of exploitation, including increased reliance on internal enslaved labor to produce export commodities.

The suppression of the trade produced significant new social formations, including the resettlement of tens of thousands of recaptives in Sierra Leone, where a new Krio culture and identity emerged, and the founding of Liberia by formerly enslaved and free-born African Americans through the American Colonization Society. Both settlements illustrate the complicated, incomplete nature of freedom achieved through abolition, combining genuine opportunity with displacement, cultural dislocation, and new forms of social hierarchy.

Illegal trafficking persisted for decades after formal prohibition, particularly to Brazil and Cuba, demonstrating that legal change without effective enforcement and without addressing underlying economic demand was insufficient to end a deeply entrenched commercial system. It took the combination of naval suppression, diplomatic pressure, and the eventual abolition of slavery itself in major American societies, culminating in Brazilian abolition in 1888, to finally bring the transatlantic trade to its end.

Even after formal abolition, slavery's effects persisted. Internal African slavery continued in many regions well into the colonial period, with formal abolition often delayed by colonial administrations reluctant to disrupt existing labor systems. In the Americas, formerly enslaved people and their descendants faced continued economic exploitation through systems that replicated many of slavery's abuses under new legal forms.

The 1890 Brussels Conference illustrates the complicated legacy of the anti-slavery movement, as its rhetoric of ending slavery in Africa became entangled with, and provided partial justification for, the European colonial conquest that was simultaneously accelerating across the continent. Anti-slavery, in other words, was both a genuine humanitarian achievement and, in the hands of colonial powers, a convenient justification for imperial expansion.

Students should take from this chapter an understanding of abolition as a long, contested, and incomplete historical process rather than a single moral triumph. The chapter's central lesson is that formal legal change, however historically significant, rarely translates immediately or completely into practical reality, and that the economic and social structures built by centuries of slave trading left legacies that persisted long after the trade itself had formally ended.

This chapter also sets the stage for the chapters that follow, which examine the intensifying European conquest of Africa in the late nineteenth century, the forced labor systems that colonial administrations would impose across much of the continent, and the long African struggle for genuine freedom, dignity, and self-determination that would continue well beyond the formal abolition of the slave trade.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Abolitionism
The organized movement to end the slave trade and, eventually, slavery itself, active primarily in Britain, the Americas, and parts of continental Europe from the late eighteenth through nineteenth centuries.
Legitimate commerce
The nineteenth-century shift in African-European trade toward agricultural and natural commodities such as palm oil, groundnuts, and ivory, framed as a moral alternative to the slave trade.
Recaptive (Liberated African)
An enslaved person freed by naval patrols before completing the Middle Passage, often resettled in colonies such as Sierra Leone.
Middle Passage
The transatlantic sea voyage that transported enslaved Africans to the Americas under brutal, often fatal conditions.
West Africa Squadron
The Royal Navy force tasked with patrolling the West African coast and intercepting slave ships after 1808.
Barracoon
A coastal holding pen or structure where captives were confined while awaiting transport aboard slave ships.
Bilateral treaty
A diplomatic agreement between two parties; in this context, agreements between Britain and African rulers to end slave exports.
Manumission
The formal act of freeing an individual enslaved person, often distinguished from broader legal emancipation or abolition.
Emancipation
The legal ending of slavery as an institution within a given jurisdiction or society.
Krio
The distinctive language and culture that developed among recaptives and settlers in Sierra Leone, blending African, English, and Christian influences.
Sokoto Caliphate
A major West African Islamic state whose economy relied heavily on slave labor and which resisted abolition pressures well into the colonial period.
Dahomey
A West African kingdom whose economy and political power were closely tied to slave trading through the port of Ouidah.
American Colonization Society
An American organization that promoted the resettlement of freed and free-born Black Americans in Africa, founding Liberia.
Freetown
The capital of Sierra Leone, established partly as a resettlement site for recaptives and freed people.
Eusébio de Queirós Law
An 1850 Brazilian law that marked a turning point toward more effective suppression of the slave trade to Brazil.
Lei Áurea
The 1888 Brazilian law that formally abolished slavery, the last major American society to do so.
Brussels Conference (1890)
An international meeting that produced a General Act formalizing anti-slavery commitments among European powers, entangled with colonial expansion.
Forced labor
Compulsory, uncompensated or poorly compensated labor systems, often imposed by colonial administrations, that in some cases replicated features of slavery under new legal forms.
Haitian Revolution
The 1791–1804 uprising of enslaved people in the French colony of Saint-Domingue that led to the founding of independent Haiti and influenced abolitionist and enslaved communities worldwide.
Diaspora
Communities of people descended from a common origin who live dispersed across different regions, in this context referring to Africans and their descendants dispersed through the slave trade.
Indentured labor
A system of contracted, semi-coerced labor that expanded in some regions after the decline of slavery, sometimes replicating conditions similar to enslavement.
Domestic slavery
Slavery practiced within African societies themselves, as distinct from the export slave trade, which persisted in many regions after the Atlantic trade declined.

Chapter Reflection

The abolition of the slave trade stands as one of the most significant moral and political transformations in the history of the Atlantic world, yet this chapter has shown that it must be understood as a long, contested, and incomplete process rather than a single triumphant event. The gap between the passage of laws in 1807 and the effective end of trafficking decades later, the persistence of internal African slavery long after external trade declined, and the entanglement of anti-slavery rhetoric with colonial conquest all demonstrate that historical progress rarely moves in a straight line.

This chapter matters for understanding the larger arc of African history because it sets the stage for the era of European conquest and colonial rule examined in the chapters that follow. The economic reorganization that abolition forced upon African coastal societies, the expansion of British influence through anti-slavery treaties and naval bases, and the moral language of "civilizing" and "liberating" Africa that abolitionists and, later, colonizers both employed, together shaped the political and ideological groundwork for the Scramble for Africa.

At the same time, this chapter reveals the depth and diversity of African agency throughout the abolition era. African rulers, merchants, and communities were not passive recipients of European policy but active participants who resisted, adapted, negotiated, and reshaped their societies in response to changing economic and political circumstances. Recaptives who rebuilt communities and identities in Sierra Leone, and freed African Americans who built a new republic in Liberia, demonstrate the resilience and creativity of people navigating the aftermath of slavery under extraordinarily difficult circumstances.

Finally, this chapter reminds students that formal legal change is only the beginning, not the end, of historical transformation. The persistence of coerced labor in new forms, the incomplete freedom experienced by many formerly enslaved people, and the long economic and social legacies of the slave trade all illustrate why the struggle for genuine freedom and dignity in Africa would continue well beyond the nineteenth century, into the colonial era and the long movements for independence and justice that this textbook will examine in the chapters ahead.

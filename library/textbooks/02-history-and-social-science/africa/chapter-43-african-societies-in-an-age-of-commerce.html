Part VII — Slavery, Commerce, and the African Diaspora
43

African Societies in an Age of Commerce

Africa: A History of a Continent

Chapter Opening

"Commerce did not arrive in Africa from outside. It deepened, expanded, and transformed systems of exchange that African societies had built over centuries — but at a cost that reshaped the continent in ways still felt today."

In the late seventeenth century, the town of Kumasi in what is now Ghana was still a modest settlement in the forest zone of West Africa. A generation later it was the capital of one of the most powerful states in the region — the Asante Empire — its palace courts receiving tribute from dozens of subordinate chiefs, its merchants trading gold and kola nuts northward into the Sahel and captives southward to European ships anchored off the coast. Kumasi's transformation was not accidental. It was produced by commerce: by the ability of Asante's founders to position their new state at the intersection of multiple trade routes, to extract wealth from the people and goods that moved through their territory, and to use that wealth to build armies, reward allies, and project political power across the forest and savanna zones of West Africa.

Asante's story is one example of a far wider pattern that transformed African societies between roughly the fifteenth and nineteenth centuries. Across the continent — in the forest kingdoms of the Guinea Coast, the Sahel empires of the Western Sudan, the city-states of the Swahili Coast, the river-port communities of Central Africa, the cattle-rich polities of Southern Africa — expanding commerce reshaped political authority, social hierarchies, religious institutions, cultural practices, and everyday life. Trade had always been part of African historical experience. But the expansion of long-distance commerce in this era — driven partly by internal African dynamics and partly by the growing integration of African economies into the Atlantic and Indian Ocean trading systems — accelerated, deepened, and in many cases fundamentally disrupted patterns that had developed over centuries.

This chapter examines how expanding trade transformed African societies. It explores how commercial wealth became a basis for political power, how new kinds of states emerged along critical trade routes, how African merchants and brokers built and controlled the networks through which goods and people moved, and how the demands of external trade — above all, the catastrophic Atlantic slave trade — altered patterns of warfare, social organization, and economic life. It also examines how African societies experienced commercial expansion from profoundly different positions: as rulers who taxed and controlled trade, as merchants who organized it, as farmers and artisans who produced the goods that moved through trading networks, as captives who were themselves converted into commodities, and as communities that bore the costs of a commerce in which they had little say.

The period examined in this chapter spans roughly four centuries, from the earliest sustained Portuguese contact with West and Central African coastal societies in the late fifteenth century through the abolition of the Atlantic slave trade in the mid-nineteenth century and the transition to what Europeans called "legitimate commerce." It is a period defined by paradox: enormous commercial dynamism alongside devastating human loss; the rise of powerful new African states alongside the destruction of older ones; the integration of African economies into global networks alongside the deepening of dependency and inequality. Understanding both dimensions — the transformation and the cost, the agency and the constraint — is essential to reading this period honestly.

A central argument of this chapter is that African rulers, merchants, and communities were not passive victims of forces imposed from outside. They were active participants in expanding commercial systems, making choices about what to trade, with whom, and on what terms. At the same time, the choices available to them were shaped by structural conditions — by the demand for enslaved labor in the Americas, by the military power and commercial capital that European traders brought to coastal ports, by the competition among African states that the slave trade intensified, and by the cumulative effects of centuries of demographic loss — that constrained African agency in ways that mattered enormously for long-term outcomes.

The evidence historians use to reconstruct this history is varied: European trading records and missionary accounts, the Arabic writings of North African and Saharan travelers, royal chronicles from Ethiopia and other literate African states, oral traditions preserved in many communities, the material remains of trading towns and forts, demographic data extracted from ship records and census-like documents, and a growing body of African-authored sources — letters, petitions, legal documents, and in some cases fully elaborated royal correspondence. Each type of source reflects a particular vantage point. Each must be read critically. Together, they allow historians to piece together a picture of African societies engaged with one of the most consequential transformations in human history: the making of a truly global commercial economy and Africa's complex, costly, and consequential role within it.

This chapter connects directly to earlier chapters on the Atlantic slave trade, the Indian Ocean slave trade, and the African diaspora. It also prepares students for later chapters on colonialism and resistance by showing how the commercial relationships, political structures, and social hierarchies of the Age of Commerce shaped the conditions that European colonizers would exploit in the late nineteenth century. Commerce did not merely enrich or impoverish. It reorganized African societies from within — and the consequences of that reorganization remain part of Africa's historical inheritance today.

Historical Context

Long before the first Portuguese ships rounded the West African cape at Bojador in 1434, African societies participated in extensive and sophisticated commercial systems. Trans-Saharan trade routes had connected West Africa to North Africa and the Mediterranean for more than a millennium, carrying gold, salt, kola nuts, copper, cloth, and enslaved people across the desert in camel caravans managed by Berber and Tuareg intermediaries. The empires of Ghana, Mali, and Songhai had each derived enormous wealth and political power from their ability to tax and regulate this trade. On the East African coast, Swahili city-states had been nodes in the Indian Ocean commercial world since at least the tenth century, exchanging African gold, ivory, iron, and enslaved people for Indian textiles, Chinese porcelain, and Persian metalwork. In Central and Southern Africa, regional exchange networks moved copper, iron, salt, and cattle across hundreds of kilometers, creating the material foundation for the political systems that archaeologists have reconstructed at sites like Great Zimbabwe and Mapungubwe.

What changed in the fifteenth century was not the existence of African commerce but the scale, intensity, and direction of external connections. Portuguese explorers, navigating southward along the Atlantic coast of Africa in search of a sea route to the Indian Ocean spice trade, found in West and Central Africa both trading partners and, increasingly, suppliers of enslaved labor for the sugar plantations being developed on the Atlantic islands and, after 1500, in Brazil. The resulting Atlantic slave trade grew slowly at first and then with devastating momentum over the following centuries, eventually removing more than twelve million people from the African continent between the early sixteenth and mid-nineteenth centuries. At the same time, the Indian Ocean world was transformed by the Portuguese intrusion into existing commercial networks, followed later by Dutch, English, and French traders who competed with each other and with Omani Arab merchants for control of East African commerce.

These external connections did not simply impose themselves on passive African societies. African rulers and merchants made choices about whether to engage with European traders, what goods to exchange, and on what terms. Many African states were initially in a position to dictate the conditions of trade; European merchants anchored offshore needed African cooperation to acquire goods and to protect themselves from the epidemiological and military dangers of the coastal and interior environments. Over time, however, the structural advantages available to African commercial partners were eroded by several forces: the growing military power of European traders, the distorting effects of the slave trade on African political competition, the cumulative demographic losses of the trade, and the eventual industrialization of European economies that shifted the terms of exchange decisively against African producers.

Within Africa, the Age of Commerce was also shaped by internal dynamics that had little to do with European demand. The spread of Islam across the Sahel and into the forest zones of West Africa created commercial networks that operated independently of European contact. The Dyula and Wangara merchants who carried kola nuts and gold across the West African forest and savanna, and the Hausa traders who connected the Lake Chad basin to North Africa and the Nile valley, were operating within systems of credit, trust, and market knowledge that had been refined over centuries. Similarly, the Swahili merchants of the East African coast and the Yao and Nyamwezi traders of the East African interior participated in the Indian Ocean commercial world on their own terms, managing relationships with Arab, Indian, and later European partners with considerable sophistication.

Understanding African societies in the Age of Commerce requires holding several truths simultaneously. African merchants, rulers, and communities were active historical agents. They built, managed, and profited from commercial networks that spanned continents. They made political calculations about war, alliance, and exchange that shaped the history of the period. At the same time, the growing integration of African economies into a global commercial system driven by European capital and American demand for enslaved labor created structural pressures that ultimately proved catastrophic for African demographic and political development. The Age of Commerce was not simply a story of African participation in global trade. It was a story of transformation, disruption, exploitation, and resilience — and its consequences reverberate across African history to the present day.

The most important Swahili city-state of the medieval and early modern era, controlling the gold trade from the interior and serving as the principal southern port in the Indian Ocean system until its decline in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries.

Section 43.1

Commerce and Political Change

Trade and political power have always been intertwined in African history. Rulers who controlled access to profitable trade routes could extract revenue, reward supporters, and build the military forces that sustained their authority. The Age of Commerce intensified this relationship dramatically, creating new forms of political organization, transforming existing states, and in some cases destroying communities that were unable to adapt to the demands and disruptions that expanding commerce brought.

The most fundamental political consequence of expanding trade was the growing connection between commercial wealth and coercive power. In earlier periods, many African political systems had been organized around agricultural surplus, cattle wealth, or the control of scarce natural resources like salt or iron. These remained important, but as long-distance trade expanded, rulers who could tax the movement of goods across their territories gained access to resources — imported metals, textiles, weapons, and luxury goods — that could be redistributed to subordinates and used to construct patronage networks of remarkable reach. Access to Atlantic imports, above all firearms, further shifted the balance of power toward states that were connected to external trade.

Not all states benefited equally from commercial expansion. Those best positioned were typically states that controlled critical junctures in trading networks: river crossings, coastal anchorages, mountain passes, or the borders between ecological zones where different communities had different goods to exchange. The Asante Empire in the forest zone of West Africa, for example, rose to dominance partly because Akan traders occupied a central position between the gold-producing forest communities to the south and the Sahelian merchants who brought manufactured goods from the north and the Atlantic traders who anchored off the coast. Control of this commercial hub allowed the Asante to accumulate the gold that paid for firearms and the firearms that fueled military expansion.

Commercial expansion could also destabilize existing political orders. States that had thrived under earlier trade regimes found their relative advantage eroded as new routes opened or as the commodities that had sustained their power lost value or were displaced. The Saharan-oriented empires of the Western Sudan — the successors of Mali and Songhai in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries — found their commercial position progressively weakened as Atlantic trade drew more and more of West Africa's commercial energy southward toward the coast. The kingdoms of the forest zone and the coastal hinterland, by contrast, gained new prominence as the Atlantic trade grew. This geographic reorientation was not merely economic; it was a redistribution of political power across the entire region.

In Central Africa, the consequences of Atlantic commercial integration for political organization were particularly dramatic. The Kingdom of Kongo, which had been one of the most sophisticated and well-organized African states of the late fifteenth century, was progressively destabilized by the pressures of Portuguese slave trading over the following two centuries. The Kongo monarchy, which had initially attempted to manage and limit the trade, found its authority undermined as provincial lords gained access to Atlantic goods by selling captives independently, bypassing royal control. By the mid-seventeenth century, the central authority of the Kongo kings had been fatally weakened, and the kingdom fragmented into competing territories none of which possessed the organizational capacity of the original state.

The relationship between commerce and political change was thus neither simple nor uniform across Africa. In some regions, expanding trade created conditions for political centralization and the emergence of powerful new states. In others, it intensified competition, undermined central authority, and produced prolonged instability. Understanding this variation requires attention to the specific circumstances of each region — the nature of the trade, the types of goods involved, the military technologies available, the structures of existing political systems, and the ways in which different communities were embedded in or marginalized by expanding commercial networks.

Historical Vocabulary
Age of Commerce

The term Age of Commerce was used by historian Anthony Reid to describe a period of rapid commercial expansion in Southeast Asia between approximately 1450 and 1680. African historians have adapted the concept to describe a broadly similar process in Africa — particularly along the Atlantic and Indian Ocean coasts and across the Saharan trade routes — during the roughly four centuries from 1400 to 1850. The term emphasizes that the expansion of long-distance trade during this period was not simply an external imposition but a process in which African societies were active participants, responding to commercial opportunities while simultaneously being transformed by the pressures and disruptions that expanding trade brought.

Using the concept of an "Age of Commerce" for Africa carries analytical risks as well as advantages. It risks obscuring the enormous differences between regions and periods — the West African Atlantic trade was very different from the East African Indian Ocean trade, and both were different from the trans-Saharan Sahel commerce. It also risks minimizing the devastation caused by the Atlantic slave trade by folding it into a broader narrative of commercial expansion. Historians who use the term are therefore careful to specify which aspects of commerce they are analyzing and for which regions and periods, and to keep the human costs of the trade in clear view alongside the economic and political transformations it produced.

Section 43.2

Wealth, Power, and State Formation

The relationship between commercial wealth and state formation was one of the defining features of African political history in the Age of Commerce. New states emerged and old ones transformed as rulers discovered that controlling the movement of valuable commodities — whether gold, kola, ivory, textiles, or human beings — could generate the surplus needed to build armies, construct courts, fund religious institutions, and project political authority across wide territories. The states that succeeded in this era were, above all, states that learned to harness commerce in the service of political ambition.

The rise of the Asante Empire offers perhaps the most clearly documented example of commercial wealth driving state formation in West Africa. The Akan peoples of the forest zone of present-day Ghana had long been organized in small independent chieftaincies, each controlling portions of the gold-bearing rivers and forests that made the region one of the most commercially important in all of Africa. In the late seventeenth century, Osei Tutu, the ruler of the small Asante chieftaincy of Kumasi, organized a military and political confederation that gradually subordinated neighboring Akan states into a unified empire. The ideological center of this confederation was the Golden Stool — a sacred object said to have descended from the sky and to embody the soul of the Asante nation — which Osei Tutu's court official Okomfo Anokye deployed with enormous political skill to legitimate the new state's authority over formerly independent chiefs.

But the material foundation of Asante's political ambition was commercial. Gold production in the Asante heartland generated revenue that could be taxed, and control of the trade routes connecting the forest to both the coast and the northern savanna gave the Asante state income from tolls and duties. As the Atlantic trade grew in the eighteenth century, the Asante increasingly participated in the export of enslaved people, exchanging captives taken in military campaigns for firearms that further enhanced their military capacity. This arms-trade relationship created a self-reinforcing cycle: military success generated captives who could be sold for guns; guns enabled more military success; and the prestige and material rewards of conquest attracted new allies and soldiers who expanded the empire's reach.

A different model of commerce-driven state formation emerged in the Kingdom of Dahomey on the Bight of Benin. Dahomey was founded in the interior in the early seventeenth century and expanded to the coast in the 1720s, capturing the port of Ouidah from the Hueda kingdom and establishing direct access to Atlantic traders. Unlike Asante, which was built primarily on gold wealth supplemented by slave trading, Dahomey organized slave raiding and trading as the central function of the state apparatus. The Dahomean military — including the famous female soldiers known as the Agojie or "Dahomey Amazons" — conducted annual military campaigns against neighboring peoples specifically to capture enslaved individuals for sale. The revenue from these sales funded the royal court, the military, and the elaborate ceremonial life that legitimated royal authority. Dahomey's political system was, in other words, directly organized around the production of the commodity — enslaved people — that Atlantic demand most urgently required.

Along the East African coast, the relationship between commerce and political authority operated through the institution of the Swahili city-state. Cities like Kilwa, Mombasa, Malindi, and Pate were organized around control of particular segments of the coastal trade, each with its own merchant elite, Islamic scholarly establishment, and ruling dynasty whose legitimacy rested as much on commercial success as on military power or religious authority. The Indian Ocean trading world rewarded cities that could offer reliable supplies of specific goods — above all gold from the Zimbabwe Plateau, ivory from the East African interior, and enslaved people from the mainland hinterland — and the political systems of the Swahili cities evolved to perform this commercial function efficiently. Rulers maintained warehouses, organized credit arrangements with Asian merchants, adjudicated commercial disputes through Islamic law, and managed the relationships with interior suppliers through a combination of diplomacy, tribute, and force.

In the interior of Central Africa, the expansion of the ivory and slave trades in the nineteenth century produced new forms of commercial-political organization. The Nyamwezi chiefs of west-central Tanzania built political authority around their control of caravan routes running from the interior to the East African coast. The Chokwe people of present-day Angola expanded dramatically in the nineteenth century as skilled hunters who developed the ivory trade in the Congo basin. And figures like Msiri in Katanga built personal trading empires that combined copper extraction, ivory commerce, and the slave trade into hybrid political economies that operated outside the frameworks of both traditional kinship-based political systems and older tributary empires.

What all these cases share is the insight that in the Age of Commerce, access to long-distance trade was not merely an economic advantage but a political one of the first order. States that could position themselves as indispensable nodes in trading networks — as producers, as transporters, as taxers, or as military enforcers of commercial order — gained the resources to build political structures that outlasted individual rulers and shaped the landscape of African politics for generations. The commercial logic of the era produced some of the most powerful African states of the past four centuries, and it left political legacies whose effects are still visible in the geography of modern African nations.

Section 43.3

Merchants and Trade Networks

The expansion of African commerce in the Age of Commerce was not primarily the work of rulers and states. It was built, day by day and season by season, by the merchants, brokers, caravan leaders, porters, and commercial agents who organized the actual movement of goods and people across thousands of kilometers of African territory. Understanding these commercial actors and the networks they constructed is essential to understanding how trade worked and who benefited from it.

The most important long-distance trading communities in West Africa were the Dyula and Wangara merchants, whose commercial networks by the eighteenth century spanned an enormous swath of the continent from Senegambia in the west to the Hausa lands in the east and from the Saharan edge in the north to the Atlantic-facing forest zone in the south. The Dyula were Muslim, and their commercial networks were inseparable from their religious identity. Mosques, Quranic schools, and networks of Islamic learning traveled with Dyula merchants to every community they settled in, giving those communities both a religious institution and an economic node. The commercial trust that underpinned Dyula trading relationships was rooted partly in Islamic law, which provided a shared framework for contracts, credit, and dispute resolution that transcended the political boundaries of the many states through which Dyula traders moved.

The Dyula carried kola nuts — the mildly stimulant seeds of a forest tree that were in enormous demand in Muslim communities across the Sahel and North Africa, where alcohol was forbidden — from the forest zone northward to Sahelian markets, and returned with salt from the Saharan mines, cloth from Hausa and North African weavers, and later with European manufactured goods obtained at coastal trading forts. This commerce in licit goods operated alongside and largely independently of the Atlantic slave trade, allowing Dyula merchants to maintain their commercial operations even as the slave trade convulsed the political landscape around them.

In East Africa, the Yao and Nyamwezi peoples performed functions analogous to the Dyula in the interior, organizing the long caravan routes that connected the Great Lakes region and the Congo basin to the Swahili coast. Nyamwezi caravans in the nineteenth century could number in the thousands — long columns of professional porters carrying ivory tusks, copper ingots, and other trade goods toward the coast, and returning inland with cloth, beads, firearms, and other imports. The leaders of these caravans were not simply commercial agents; they were political figures whose access to imported goods gave them leverage over the communities along the route, and whose ability to organize labor and maintain discipline over hundreds of kilometers made them indispensable to the entire East African commercial system.

The Swahili merchants of the East African coast occupied a pivotal position between the Indian Ocean world and the African interior. Many Swahili commercial families maintained long-term relationships with Indian merchant houses in Surat, Bombay, and Kutch that provided the credit financing on which caravan trade depended. Indian merchants advanced cloth and beads on credit to Swahili traders, who distributed them to interior trading partners and returned with ivory and other goods that could repay the debt and fund another cycle of commerce. This credit system, which stretched across the Indian Ocean and deep into the African interior, was one of the most elaborate commercial structures of the nineteenth-century world — and it depended entirely on personal relationships of trust built up over generations of commercial interaction.

Along the Atlantic coast of West Africa, a distinct class of commercial intermediaries emerged who were sometimes called "factors" or "brokers" by European traders. These individuals — often of mixed African and European descent, or African traders who had learned European languages and commercial customs — managed the interface between European trading ships and interior African suppliers. They negotiated prices, guaranteed the quality of goods, provided credit, arranged accommodation for European merchants who came ashore, and managed the complex political relationships with coastal and interior rulers that made orderly trade possible. Without these brokers, the Atlantic trade would have been far more difficult and expensive for all parties; with them, European merchants could operate in African commercial environments they did not fully understand, and African suppliers could access Atlantic markets without directly engaging with European traders whose intentions and methods were often opaque.

In Central Africa, the expansion of trade in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries was organized through networks of Vili, Bobangi, and other riverine peoples who used the Congo River and its tributaries as highways for commercial exchange. The Bobangi, a Bantu-speaking people who settled along the middle Congo River in the eighteenth century, became perhaps the most important commercial intermediaries of Central Africa, using their canoes and their knowledge of the river system to carry goods — slaves, ivory, copper, cloth — between the interior of the Congo basin and the coastal trading ports where Atlantic merchants anchored. Their monopoly over river transport gave them political leverage over both interior suppliers and coastal sellers, and their commercial success was reflected in the distinctive material culture and social organization of their communities.

Historical Vocabulary
Brokerage and Middlemen

In the history of commerce, a middleman or broker is a person or group that connects buyers and sellers who would otherwise have difficulty doing business with each other — because they speak different languages, operate under different legal systems, have different commercial customs, or lack the information needed to find each other and agree on prices. In the African Age of Commerce, middlemen and brokers played crucial roles in making long-distance trade possible.

Middlemen were not passive conduits. They added value by providing services — translation, credit, quality assurance, political protection, logistical organization — that were genuinely necessary for trade to function across the vast distances and cultural boundaries of continental African commerce. They were also in a position to extract rent for these services, keeping a share of the value of goods that passed through their hands and using their privileged position in commercial networks to accumulate wealth and political influence. The control of brokerage was therefore a source of political power as well as economic profit, and competition over who would serve as broker in key commercial junctions was a source of conflict throughout the period.

European colonial authorities, who arrived in Africa in the late nineteenth century seeking direct access to interior producers and consumers, often experienced African middlemen and brokers as obstacles to their commercial ambitions. One of the early goals of colonial economic policy was to eliminate African middlemen and establish direct commercial relationships between European firms and African producers — a policy that destroyed the livelihoods of entire commercial communities and disrupted the complex economic ecosystems that African brokers had built over centuries.

Section 43.4

Warfare and Economic Competition

One of the most devastating consequences of the Age of Commerce in Africa was the intensification of warfare. Trade in enslaved people — which was central to both the Atlantic and Indian Ocean commercial systems during this period — made captives a valuable commodity, and the acquisition of captives required, above all, military force. The result was a profound distortion of African political life: states that might otherwise have organized themselves around agricultural production, craft specialization, or pastoral wealth found themselves drawn into cycles of raiding, counter-raiding, and defensive military investment that consumed resources, displaced populations, and reshaped political landscapes across wide regions.

The most direct mechanism linking commerce and warfare was what historians call the arms-slave cycle. European traders arriving at West African coastal ports brought firearms — muskets, flintlocks, and later more advanced weapons — as goods to exchange for enslaved people and other commodities. African rulers who acquired these weapons gained military advantages over neighbors who lacked them, enabling them to conduct more successful slave raids and acquire more captives to exchange for more guns. This cycle, once initiated, created powerful incentives for participation: rulers who refused to engage with Atlantic slave traders risked being raided by neighbors who had already acquired firearms, so even those who might have preferred to abstain found themselves drawn into the trade for defensive reasons.

The scale of violence that this dynamic generated is difficult to overstate. In regions like the Gold Coast, the Bight of Benin, and the Bight of Biafra, annual slave-raiding campaigns became institutionalized features of political life. Dahomey's annual military campaigns — the "Annual Customs" — were partly ceremonial occasions celebrating royal power but were equally organized military operations designed to generate captives for Atlantic sale. In the Niger Delta, a network of trading states organized around the acquisition and export of enslaved people from the interior maintained what amounted to a permanent war footing. In the Congo basin, the expansion of slave raiding in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries produced what historians have described as a landscape of generalized insecurity in which no community could be confident of safety and in which the cumulative demographic impact of continuous raiding was catastrophic.

The relationship between warfare and the Indian Ocean slave trade produced equally devastating effects in East Africa, though with a somewhat different timeline. In the East African interior, the ivory trade of the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries initially coexisted with relatively limited violence; the primary commodity was ivory, and ivory could be obtained by hunting rather than warfare. But as elephant populations declined and demand for enslaved people grew in the nineteenth century — driven by the expansion of Omani Arab plantation agriculture in Zanzibar and the Omani-controlled coast, and by continued demand from Persian Gulf markets — the interior became the site of increasingly organized and destructive slave raiding. The Yao, the Chewa, the Bisa, and later the Swahili-Arab commercial empires of figures like Tippu Tip conducted operations that moved hundreds of thousands of people from the interior toward the coast, devastating communities across an enormous region.

Warfare in the Age of Commerce was not only driven by the demand for enslaved people. Competition over control of trade routes, commercial monopolies, and market access also produced armed conflict. The Asante Empire fought multiple wars against coastal Fante states and against European-backed forces partly over the question of who would control the commercial interface between the Asante interior and Atlantic shipping. The states of the Niger Delta — Bonny, Calabar, Brass, New Calabar — competed militarily as well as commercially for access to interior palm oil and slave suppliers. In East Africa, the rise of Buganda as a major commercial and military power in the nineteenth century was partly driven by competition with Bunyoro over control of the ivory trade routes running north from the Great Lakes toward the Nile valley.

It is important to note that warfare in the Age of Commerce was not simply a response to external demand. African rulers fought wars for political reasons that existed independently of commerce — to expand territory, to defend against aggression, to resolve succession disputes, to project power and prestige. What the Age of Commerce did was provide new incentives and new means for warfare: new commodities worth fighting over, new weapons with which to fight, and new markets in which the human spoils of war could be converted into durable commercial and military advantages. The result was a militarization of African political life in many regions that had profound and lasting consequences for demographic trends, state organization, and the distribution of power across the continent.

Section 43.5

Social Hierarchies and New Elites

The Age of Commerce did not merely change African political systems from the top down. It restructured social hierarchies at every level — creating new elites, new forms of status, new categories of dependency, and new relationships between gender, labor, and power that transformed the social fabric of communities across the continent. Commerce was not an abstract economic force acting on an inert social landscape; it was experienced by real people occupying particular positions within existing social orders, and it created new winners and new losers in ways that fundamentally altered who held power, who possessed wealth, and who bore the costs of commercial integration.

The most visible beneficiaries of commercial expansion were the merchant elites who controlled long-distance trade. In West Africa, wealthy traders in cities like Kano, Kumasi, Salaga, and Freetown accumulated fortunes that rivaled those of political rulers. In Kano, the center of the Hausa textile industry, successful cloth merchants financed the weaving workshops of hundreds of artisans and maintained commercial relationships extending across thousands of kilometers of the Sahel, savanna, and forest zones. Their wealth was expressed in the ownership of enslaved household laborers, in the construction of elaborate urban compounds, in generous patronage of Islamic scholars and institutions, and in the maintenance of networks of clients and commercial agents whose loyalty was as much social as economic.

In the coastal communities of West Africa, the emergence of commercial elites created social categories that did not fit neatly into existing hierarchical frameworks. The "merchant princes" of the Niger Delta — rulers of city-states like Bonny and Calabar who organized the export of enslaved people and later palm oil — occupied positions of enormous wealth and power, but their authority derived from commerce rather than from the hereditary lineage structures that had traditionally conferred political legitimacy. The "canoe houses" of the Delta — corporate groups organized around a leading merchant who maintained a fleet of trading canoes and a community of enslaved laborers, paddlers, and commercial agents — were novel social institutions that had no precise precedent in earlier Delta society, and they produced a form of social mobility, however constrained, that allowed talented traders of low birth to rise to positions of considerable influence.

The expansion of slavery within African societies was one of the most significant social consequences of the Age of Commerce. The same commercial systems that exported enslaved people across the Atlantic and Indian Ocean also expanded the scale of domestic slavery within Africa. Enslaved people were used in production — farming, weaving, mining, porterage — in domestic service, as soldiers, and as markers of social status. In the Sahel and savanna zones of West Africa, large-scale agricultural slavery on groundnut and cotton plantations emerged in the nineteenth century, partly in response to the labor demands of expanded export production. In East Africa, the Zanzibar-based clove plantations depended entirely on enslaved labor. In the Swahili hinterland, agricultural estates owned by coastal merchants used enslaved workers to produce food for the commercial port cities. The internal slave trade — the movement of enslaved people within Africa rather than across the ocean — was enormous: some historians estimate that more Africans were enslaved internally than were exported across the Atlantic and Indian Oceans combined.

Women occupied complex and varied positions within the commercial hierarchies of the Age of Commerce. In many African societies, women controlled local and regional markets, trading agricultural produce, cooked food, cloth, and other household goods through networks that were both economically important and socially constitutive of community identity. In the coastal communities of West Africa, some women achieved considerable commercial prominence: Efua Konadu, known to Europeans as "Doña Dorotéa," was a wealthy Fante trader in eighteenth-century Elmina whose commercial operations spanned the Gold Coast and who managed relationships with multiple European trading companies. Women also appeared in the commercial world as enslaved laborers — performing both productive and domestic work — and as wives in the strategic marriages through which commercial families cemented alliances and built the personal trust networks that made long-distance trade possible.

The social consequences of the Age of Commerce were thus deeply unequal. Rulers who controlled trade routes, merchants who organized long-distance networks, and brokers who managed the interface between different commercial systems accumulated wealth and power. The farmers, artisans, and ordinary community members whose surplus was taxed, whose children were captured in slave raids, and whose communities were disrupted by the political instability that commercial competition produced experienced the Age of Commerce very differently. And the hundreds of thousands of people who were themselves converted into commodities — enslaved, transported, and sold — bore costs that no amount of benefit to other members of their societies could justify or compensate.

Cause and Effect

How the Atlantic Slave Trade Transformed African Political Structures

Long-Term Causes

Existing African political competition among states; the presence of firearms and other military technology in European trading inventories; the profitability of enslaved labor in New World plantation agriculture; the vulnerability of smaller and less centralized African communities to more powerful neighbors.

Short-Term Causes

The rapid expansion of Atlantic demand for enslaved labor in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries; the arrival of European trading ships at West and Central African ports with firearms, cloth, and other desirable goods; competition among European trading companies that kept prices for African exports (including enslaved people) relatively high.

The Transformation

Some African states reorganized their political economies around slave raiding and trading; the arms-slave cycle intensified military competition; existing political hierarchies were disrupted by the redistribution of commercial wealth; populations in vulnerable areas were reduced by capture and flight; new commercial elites emerged alongside or in competition with traditional political authorities.

Long-Term Effects

Demographic losses weakened many African societies' capacity for agricultural production and economic development; patterns of political instability and distrust of governance, rooted in the slave-raiding era, had lasting effects on social organization; the commercial infrastructure built during the slave trade era was partially redirected toward "legitimate commerce" after abolition but within terms of trade that continued to disadvantage African producers; European colonizers in the late nineteenth century exploited political instability and commercial dependency that the slave trade era had helped create.

Historical complexity: The relationship between the Atlantic slave trade and African political transformation cannot be reduced to a simple narrative of African passivity or African complicity. It involved choices made by African rulers and merchants within structural conditions — commercial incentives, military pressures, political competition — that severely constrained those choices. Understanding both the agency and the constraint is essential to historical accuracy.

Section 43.6

Religion and Commercial Expansion

Religion and commerce were deeply intertwined across Africa in the Age of Commerce. Religious institutions, beliefs, and networks provided the moral frameworks, legal systems, social trust mechanisms, and community identities that made long-distance commercial relationships possible. At the same time, commercial expansion carried religious ideas and institutions to new communities, transforming spiritual landscapes across the continent in ways that were inseparable from the economic transformations of the era.

Islam's relationship to African commerce in this period was especially profound. Across the Sahel, savanna, and forest zones of West Africa, Muslim merchants — Dyula, Wangara, Hausa, Juula, and others — used Islamic commercial law to structure their trading relationships. Islamic law provided standardized frameworks for contracts, partnership arrangements, loans, and commercial disputes that allowed merchants who had never met to do business with each other across great distances on the basis of shared legal principles. The mudaraba partnership — in which one partner provided capital and another provided labor and commercial expertise, sharing profits at a predetermined ratio — was an instrument of Islamic commercial law that West African merchants used extensively, enabling the financing of long-distance caravans by wealthy urban investors who remained at home while their agents traveled the trade routes.

Sufi brotherhoods — organized Muslim religious associations built around devotion to a particular spiritual teacher or saint — played a particularly important role in the commercial geography of African Islam. The Qadiriyya brotherhood, whose networks extended from Morocco across the Sahara into the West African savanna, was closely associated with the commercial life of many trading communities. Membership in a Sufi brotherhood provided merchants with a network of trusted co-religionists across great distances: a Qadiriyya merchant traveling from Kano to Timbuktu could expect hospitality, commercial assistance, and social acceptance from fellow brotherhood members along the route. Brotherhood membership thus served some of the same trust-building functions as commercial diasporas everywhere, and the spread of Sufi organizations across the Sahel and savanna was partly driven by — and in turn reinforced — the expansion of Muslim commercial networks.

Christianity entered African commercial networks primarily through Portuguese and later broader European commercial contact. The Portuguese crown and the Catholic Church made evangelization an explicit component of the commercial enterprise on the African coast, and by the late fifteenth century, a Christian African kingdom had emerged in the Kingdom of Kongo, where King Afonso I and his successors maintained Christian courts, sent their sons to Portugal for education, and corresponded with the Pope in Rome. Christianity in Kongo was not simply an imposition from outside; it was adopted and adapted by the Kongolese elite as a framework for political legitimacy, diplomatic relationships, and cultural identity. But it was also inseparable from the commercial relationships with Portugal that accompanied it, and as those commercial relationships became increasingly dominated by the slave trade, the Christian framework that had given them ideological legitimacy became one of the most complex features of the Kongolese experience of the Atlantic era.

Along the East African coast, Islam had been established as the dominant religion of Swahili coastal society since at least the thirteenth century, and it provided the cultural and legal framework for the Indian Ocean commercial world. The mosque was not only a religious institution in Swahili towns; it was a social center where commercial relationships were maintained, marriages negotiated, and community affairs managed. Islamic scholarly networks connected Swahili traders with commercial partners and legal experts across the entire Indian Ocean world, providing a common religious culture within which relationships of trust and obligation could be constructed and maintained. The expansion of Omani Arab influence on the East African coast in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries was organized through Islamic commercial and political networks that gave it both cultural legitimacy and practical effectiveness.

Indigenous African religious traditions also shaped and were shaped by commercial expansion. Shrines and oracles in many African societies served commercial functions: they adjudicated disputes, guaranteed contracts, and provided spiritual insurance against the many risks of long-distance trade. The Aro oracle at Arochukwu in the Nigerian interior, for example, was an Igbo religious institution whose authority extended across a wide network of communities. The Aro people used the oracle's prestige to organize a long-distance trading network that moved enslaved people and other goods toward the Niger Delta coast, and the oracle's religious authority helped guarantee the commercial obligations of those who traded within the network. Religion, in this case as in many others, was not separate from commerce but embedded within it.

Primary Source Window

King Afonso I of Kongo to King João III of Portugal (1526)

Creator: Mvemba a Nzinga (King Afonso I of Kongo) · Date: 1526 · Type: Royal correspondence / diplomatic letter · Language: Portuguese (translated)

Context: King Afonso I of Kongo was baptized as a Christian, educated by Portuguese missionaries, and maintained an extensive diplomatic correspondence with the Portuguese crown over several decades. His letters are among the most important African-authored primary sources from the early Atlantic era. In this 1526 letter to the Portuguese king, Afonso protested the destructive effects of Portuguese slave trading on his kingdom. Portuguese traders had been encouraging Kongolese nobles and even ordinary people to sell fellow Kongolese into slavery in exchange for imported goods, undermining royal authority and depopulating the kingdom.

Afonso described to the Portuguese king a situation in which his country was being emptied of its free people — not by foreign conquest but by the eagerness of his own subjects to sell their neighbors, relatives, and countrymen in exchange for the cloth, wine, and other goods that Portuguese traders offered. He reported that Portuguese merchants and their African collaborators were seizing free people by night as well as by day, violating the royal authority that alone had the right to determine who could be enslaved. He asked the Portuguese king to end the slave trade in his kingdom entirely, or at minimum to restrict the Portuguese traders' access to the interior so that the commerce could be regulated and the export of free people stopped.

Analysis: Afonso's letter reveals several crucial features of the early Atlantic slave trade and its impact on African political systems. First, it shows that the trade was not conducted entirely through formal channels of military conquest and organized state raiding, but through the commercial incentives it created for anyone with access to captives. Second, it illustrates how the slave trade could undermine the political authority of rulers who tried to manage or limit it — because the commercial incentives it created were available to any actor with captives to sell, not only to states with organized militaries. Third, it shows an African ruler with a sophisticated understanding of the political and demographic consequences of the trade, making a reasoned diplomatic argument for its restriction.

The letter also has significant limitations as a historical source. It was written by a king defending his own political authority and using Christian and diplomatic language designed to appeal to a European audience. It does not give us access to the perspectives of the enslaved people whose fates Afonso discussed, nor to the provincial lords and traders who were bypassing royal authority to participate in the trade. It tells us about the political costs of the trade for the Kongolese monarchy; it does not fully represent the wider social experience of the trade within Kongo society.

Section 43.7

Coastal and Inland Exchange

One of the most important structural features of African commerce in the Age of Commerce was the relationship between coastal trading communities and the vast interior hinterlands that supplied the goods — gold, ivory, enslaved people, agricultural commodities — that flowed outward through coastal ports toward Atlantic and Indian Ocean markets. This relationship was never simply one of extraction. It was a negotiated, often contested, and constantly evolving commercial relationship in which the distribution of advantage between coastal and interior parties shifted across time and place.

The geography of African commerce created powerful incentives for specialization. Communities located at the interface between the coast and the interior — at river mouths, at mountain passes, at the boundary between ecological zones — were in a position to extract rent from the movement of goods through their territory, much as the Swahili cities had long done on the East African coast and the Sahelian states had done on the edges of the Sahara. Communities in the interior, by contrast, were typically in a stronger position as suppliers of the specific commodities — ivory, gold, enslaved people — that coastal and external markets demanded, but they were dependent on coastal and middleman communities for access to the imported goods that made long-distance trade worthwhile.

In West Africa, the relationship between coastal and interior communities was mediated by a succession of commercial zones. The Sahel-edge cities — Timbuktu, Djenné, Kano, Agadez — served as the northern interface between trans-Saharan routes and West African interior producers. A second zone of trading towns at the forest-savanna border — Salaga, Bondoukou, Kong, Bobo-Dioulasso — served as the primary exchange points between the forest zone and the savanna-Sahel commercial systems. And the coastal ports — Elmina, Cape Coast, Ouidah, Lagos, Bonny, Calabar — served as the interface between the Atlantic trading world and the interior. Goods moved through all these zones, changing hands multiple times, each transfer creating value for the intermediary and adding to the cost borne by the ultimate buyer or seller at the ends of the chain.

The control of critical junctions in this coastal-interior exchange system was both commercially profitable and politically consequential. States or communities that controlled a significant bottleneck — a river crossing, a mountain pass, a market town at the forest edge — could extract tolls and duties from all traffic passing through, generating revenues that could be used to build political authority and military strength. Conversely, the loss of control over such junctions — through military defeat, through the opening of competing routes, or through the establishment of direct European relationships with interior suppliers — could be commercially and politically devastating.

Rivers were particularly important as arteries of inland commerce. The Niger River and its tributaries connected the Sahel to the forest zone through a navigable system of over two thousand kilometers, carrying goods in both directions through a series of river-port markets that developed along its banks. The Congo River and its enormous tributary system performed a similar function in Central Africa, allowing the Bobangi and other riverine peoples to move goods over vast distances with relatively low transportation costs. The Zambezi and Limpopo rivers provided access routes from the East African interior to the Mozambican coast. In each case, rivers were not merely geographic features but commercial institutions — managed, taxed, and in some cases fought over by the communities that lived along their banks.

The relationship between coastal and inland communities was further complicated by the role of European and Asian trading partners who consistently sought to bypass African middlemen and establish direct commercial relationships with interior suppliers. From the Portuguese attempts to establish direct contact with the gold mines of the Zimbabwe Plateau in the sixteenth century through the British and French efforts to penetrate the West African interior in the nineteenth century, external commercial interests repeatedly attempted to short-circuit the middleman systems that African coastal and border communities had constructed. These efforts were largely unsuccessful until the colonial era — the environments, distances, and political complexities of the African interior proved too challenging for external merchants to navigate without African intermediaries — but they created persistent tensions between coastal brokers and external commercial partners that shaped the dynamics of African commerce throughout the period.

Everyday Life and Social History

A Trading Town in the Eighteenth Century: Life in Salaga

To understand what the Age of Commerce meant for ordinary people, it helps to look closely at one of the great trading towns that served as the commercial centers of the period. Salaga, located in the savanna zone of present-day northern Ghana at the forest-savanna border, was by the eighteenth century one of the most important market towns in West Africa, a place where the kola nut trade from the forest south, the slave trade from the interior north and east, and the cloth and salt trade from the Sahelian cities and the Sahara all converged in a vast weekly market that attracted traders from hundreds of kilometers in every direction.

Life in Salaga was organized around the market in ways that shaped almost every dimension of daily existence. The town's residential quarters were organized by commercial community: Hausa traders occupied one quarter, Dyula merchants another, Gonja and Dagomba traders their own sections. The mosque, where the weekly Friday prayer brought the Muslim commercial community together, was also a place where commercial relationships were maintained and disputes adjudicated. The zongo — the stranger's quarter found in trading towns across West Africa — provided temporary accommodation for visiting merchants, a physical institution whose very existence reflected the transient, cosmopolitan character of commercial towns that served as nodes in vast regional networks.

For the women of Salaga, the market was a primary economic arena. Women controlled the trade in cooked food — a vital service in a town full of traveling merchants who had no household to feed them — and in locally produced agricultural goods. Some women operated as commercial agents for absent male traders, managing credit relationships and collecting debts. Others sold imported cloth or other goods in the market on commission. The commercial opportunities available to women in trading towns like Salaga were considerably greater than those available in more agriculturally oriented communities, even as women remained excluded from the most lucrative long-distance wholesale trade.

For the enslaved people who passed through Salaga's markets, the town represented something very different: a place of terror, disorientation, and radical uncertainty. Captives brought from the north — from Mossi territory, from Hausa communities, from the violence of Fulani jihad states — arrived in Salaga after often brutal journeys and waited in temporary confinement while merchants negotiated their sale and transport southward toward the coast or northward toward Sahelian markets. The sheer scale of this traffic through Salaga — contemporary accounts suggest that many thousands of enslaved people passed through the market annually at its height — was an unavoidable feature of the town's commercial life, touching the lives of every resident even if they themselves were never enslaved.

Salaga's history illustrates the paradox at the heart of the Age of Commerce. It was a place of genuine commercial dynamism and cultural creativity — a cosmopolitan junction where peoples from dozens of different communities met, traded, argued, married, converted, and created a distinctive urban culture. It was also a place whose prosperity was inseparable from the traffic in human beings that ran through its markets and funded the commercial networks on which its merchants depended. Commerce and slavery were not separate features of Salaga's history; they were two dimensions of the same economic system.

Section 43.8

African Participation in Global Trade

Africa's integration into global trading systems in the Age of Commerce was neither a simple story of victimization nor one of unconstrained agency. African societies participated in Atlantic and Indian Ocean commerce in complex and varied ways — as producers, as transporters, as brokers, as political authorities who taxed and regulated trade, and sometimes as the commodities being traded. Understanding the nature of African participation requires attention to both the agency that African actors exercised and the structural constraints that limited the choices available to them.

The terms of trade — the relative prices of exports and imports — are a crucial measure of how advantageous commercial relationships were for African participants. In the early phases of Atlantic contact, African rulers and merchants generally occupied strong negotiating positions. European traders needed African cooperation to acquire goods, to navigate unfamiliar coastal and interior environments, and to protect themselves from the medical and political dangers that African environments presented. The tropical diseases that killed European traders in large numbers — malaria above all — gave African coastal communities a structural advantage in negotiating the terms of Atlantic commerce. European merchants who spent extended periods on the West African coast died at catastrophic rates; African commercial partners who could manage the interface between European ships and interior suppliers were therefore genuinely indispensable.

Over time, however, the terms of trade shifted in ways that disadvantaged African producers. The industrialization of European economies in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries made European manufactured goods — above all textiles, but also metalwork, firearms, and other items — progressively cheaper relative to the primary commodities that Africa exported. A given quantity of palm oil, ivory, or groundnuts purchased fewer and fewer yards of cloth or fewer and fewer metal tools as the nineteenth century progressed, as European manufacturing efficiency improved and African export volumes expanded without a corresponding increase in the productive capacity or bargaining power of African suppliers. This deterioration in Africa's terms of trade was gradual but cumulative, and it represented a structural disadvantage that political skill and commercial acumen could partly compensate for but not fully overcome.

African participation in the Indian Ocean trading system reflected both similar dynamics and important differences. East African Swahili merchants participated in a commercial world dominated by Asian commercial capital — primarily from India — that was already enormously sophisticated and well-organized by the time the Swahili coast was fully integrated into it. Indian textile merchants in Gujarat and other commercial centers provided the credit and the goods that financed East African commerce; in return, they claimed a substantial share of the value generated by that commerce. Swahili merchants were genuinely skilled commercial operators who built impressive personal and family fortunes, but they operated within a system of commercial relationships that was largely structured by Asian capital and demand.

What did Africa export to global markets in this period, and what did it import? The major African exports varied by region and period. Gold from the Gold Coast and the Zimbabwe Plateau was the most important African export commodity in the early centuries of the Atlantic and Indian Ocean trade. Ivory — elephant tusks — was in enormous demand in South and Southeast Asia, where it was used for jewelry, furniture inlays, and sacred objects, and in Europe, where it was used in a wide range of manufactured goods. Enslaved people, tragically, became the most volumetrically and monetarily significant African export commodity from the mid-seventeenth century onward. In the nineteenth century, "legitimate commerce" expanded the range of African agricultural and forest exports: palm oil (used as an industrial lubricant and food product in European markets), groundnuts (used for oil extraction), rubber, cotton, gum arabic, and beeswax all became significant commodities.

In exchange, Africa imported textiles above all others — Indian and later European cloth was the single most important import commodity across virtually all African trading regions. Metalwork — copper, brass, and iron goods — was another major import category, particularly in Central and Eastern Africa where local metal production was insufficient to meet demand. Cowrie shells, collected from the Maldive Islands and distributed through Indian Ocean trading networks, served as currency across wide swaths of West and Central Africa. Firearms and gunpowder were critically important imports in the Atlantic era, as their military significance made them both strategically valuable and commercially lucrative. Alcohol — European brandy and gin, and later rum from the Caribbean — was an important import in coastal West Africa that generated significant social controversy both within Africa and among European abolitionists.

The balance of African commercial participation in global trade was thus deeply ambivalent. African merchants and rulers were genuine actors in these systems — not passive recipients of externally imposed commercial relationships. At the same time, the structural conditions within which they operated — the demand for enslaved labor in the Americas, the superior military technology available to European trading companies, the deterioration of the terms of trade as European industrialization proceeded — constrained African agency in ways that accumulated over time into what Walter Rodney famously called the "underdevelopment" of Africa: the systematic channeling of African resources, labor, and commercial surplus toward the enrichment of other regions at the expense of African productive capacity and social well-being.

Historical Debate

Did African Rulers and Merchants Freely Choose to Participate in the Atlantic Slave Trade?

The question: One of the most contested debates in the historiography of the Atlantic slave trade concerns the nature of African participation. Were African rulers and merchants genuinely free agents who chose to engage with the slave trade because it served their interests? Or were they responding to structural pressures — military threats, commercial incentives, political competition — that made participation effectively compulsory regardless of their preferences? The question matters because it shapes how we understand African agency, African responsibility, and the distribution of blame for the catastrophe of the Atlantic slave trade.

Interpretation A — African Agency and Choice (John Thornton and others): Historian John Thornton and scholars who share his perspective argue that African rulers and merchants were genuine agents in the Atlantic slave trade, not victims of European imposition. They point out that African states were militarily powerful in the coastal zones where the trade operated, that European traders were entirely dependent on African cooperation to acquire goods, and that African rulers consistently determined who could trade, where, and on what terms. African elites profited substantially from the trade and used those profits to build powerful states and elaborate court cultures. The enslaved people exported through the Atlantic trade were in many cases individuals captured in wars that African states would have fought regardless of European demand. This interpretation emphasizes that the slave trade was built on existing African commercial and political systems, not imposed against African will.
Interpretation B — Structural Constraint and Coercion (Walter Rodney, Joseph Inikori, and others): Historians like Walter Rodney and Joseph Inikori argue that the emphasis on African agency in the slave trade obscures the structural conditions that made meaningful "choice" impossible for many African participants. They point to the arms-slave cycle as evidence that once firearms became available, states that refused to participate in the slave trade became militarily vulnerable to neighbors who did participate. They argue that the scale and intensity of African slave raiding was directly driven by Atlantic demand — that the wars producing captives were fought not because African rulers would have fought them anyway, but because the commercial incentives created by European demand made them far more attractive and extensive than they would otherwise have been. And they insist that the long-term consequences of the trade — demographic loss, political instability, economic underdevelopment — demonstrate that African societies as a whole were harmed rather than benefited by participation.

Evidence: Both interpretations draw on substantial historical evidence. Records of African commercial negotiations, royal correspondence, and the history of specific states show considerable African commercial sophistication and political control over the trade. Demographic data, records of warfare intensity before and after the trade's expansion, and analysis of the long-term economic consequences of the trade support the structural constraint interpretation. The debate is not resolvable by reference to a single type of evidence; it requires weighing different kinds of evidence and deciding which factors were most determinative.

Why it matters: How historians answer this question has implications for how we understand responsibility for the slave trade, how we contextualize contemporary discussions about reparations and historical accountability, and how we think about African agency in historical processes more broadly. Neither interpretation fully accounts for the enormous variation in African experiences of the trade — some rulers were genuinely powerful actors who profited substantially; others were caught in military and commercial dynamics that left them little real choice.

Section 43.9

Economic Transformation and Consequences

The long-term economic consequences of the Age of Commerce for African societies were complex, contested, and in many respects devastating, even as they also produced genuine commercial development, state building, and material accumulation for specific groups within African societies. Understanding these consequences requires separating several distinct types of effects: demographic, political, economic, and institutional — and recognizing that the same process of commercial expansion produced very different outcomes for different groups within African society.

The demographic consequences of the Atlantic slave trade were among the most profound. Estimates by historians Patrick Manning and others suggest that the total export of enslaved people across the Atlantic and Indian Oceans, combined with the demographic losses of internal slavery and the deaths that accompanied slave raiding and warfare, may have reduced the African population by tens of millions below what it would have been without the trade. This is not a simple subtraction problem: the people who were enslaved tended to be young adults of reproductive age, and their removal depressed not only the immediate population but the population's future growth potential. Some regions of West Africa — particularly those most intensively exploited for slave exports in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries — show demographic stagnation or decline during periods when comparable world regions were growing. The demographic consequences of this loss played out over generations, affecting productive capacity, agricultural development, and the social fabric of countless communities.

The transition to "legitimate commerce" in the nineteenth century — the shift away from slave exports toward agricultural and forest product exports following British and other European abolition efforts — represented a major commercial transformation, but one that preserved and in some ways deepened the structural inequalities of the earlier period. The palm oil trade that expanded rapidly in West Africa after 1807 created new commercial opportunities for producers and traders, but it also created new forms of labor exploitation: the palm oil boom in the Niger Delta and other regions was fueled partly by enslaved labor within Africa, as producers used enslaved agricultural workers to increase their output of palm fruit and palm oil for the export market. "Legitimate commerce" did not eliminate slavery from African societies; it redirected it from the export trade to domestic labor exploitation.

The terms of trade for African agricultural and forest product exporters in the "legitimate commerce" era were also generally unfavorable relative to those available to industrial commodity exporters elsewhere. African palm oil, groundnuts, and rubber were primary commodities whose prices were determined by supply and demand in European markets over which African producers had no influence. The manufactured goods that African traders received in exchange — textiles, metalwork, alcohol — were produced with improving industrial efficiency by European manufacturers whose cost advantages grew steadily through the nineteenth century. The result was a pattern of trade in which African commodity producers sent increasing quantities of exports to European markets in exchange for a declining real value of imports: a structural tendency toward what economists later called "unfavorable terms of trade" that would become even more pronounced in the colonial era.

The institutional consequences of the Age of Commerce were equally significant and long-lasting. The political systems that emerged in the Age of Commerce — states organized around the control of trade routes, the military capacity to conduct slave raiding, and the commercial networks needed to convert captives into imported goods — were shaped by the incentive structures of the slave trade era in ways that proved difficult to repurpose once those incentive structures changed. When British abolition pressure and, eventually, colonial conquest disrupted the slave trading economy, the states that had been built on that economy — Dahomey, the Niger Delta city-states, many Saharan and Sahelian trading polities — faced profound challenges of commercial and political reorganization. Some, like Dahomey, attempted to sustain their commercial economy through expanded palm oil production and attempted to maintain their political autonomy against growing European pressure. Others fragmented or were absorbed into European-backed commercial and political systems.

Perhaps the most consequential institutional legacy of the Age of Commerce was its effect on social trust and political legitimacy within African societies. The slave trade — which involved the capture and sale of neighbors, community members, and in some cases relatives — fundamentally disrupted the social bonds that made collective action, political cooperation, and economic development possible. Communities that had experienced intensive slave raiding were marked by a generalized insecurity, a suspicion of outsiders and authority figures, and a tendency toward social fragmentation that persisted long after the raiding itself had ended. Historians Nathan Nunn and Leonard Wantchekon have documented through quantitative analysis that regions most intensively exposed to the slave trade continue to show lower levels of social trust and weaker institutions of governance today — a statistical finding that gives concrete form to the intuition that the slave trade's consequences did not end with abolition but continued to shape African social and political life through subsequent generations.

Historical Vocabulary
Legitimate Commerce

The term "legitimate commerce" was used by nineteenth-century British abolitionists and colonial advocates to describe the trade in agricultural commodities, forest products, and other goods that they promoted as an alternative to the slave trade. The phrase reflected the view, associated most prominently with missionary and explorer David Livingstone, that if Africa could be integrated into the global economy through the export of palm oil, groundnuts, cotton, rubber, and other primary commodities, the economic incentives for slave trading would be eliminated and African societies would be "civilized" through participation in a morally acceptable commerce.

The concept of "legitimate commerce" has been criticized by historians on several grounds. First, it implicitly defined African-controlled commerce in enslaved people as "illegitimate" while treating European-controlled commercial relationships — including those built on the infrastructure and political systems created by the slave trade — as acceptable. Second, it glossed over the ways in which the transition to "legitimate" exports often perpetuated rather than ended enslaved labor within African societies, as producers used enslaved workers to grow palm oil or carry ivory. Third, it was closely associated with the broader project of European commercial and eventually political penetration of Africa, serving as ideological justification for the increasing European intervention in African affairs that culminated in the colonial scramble of the 1880s and 1890s.

Despite these limitations, the transition to "legitimate commerce" was a real historical phenomenon that produced genuine changes in African commercial systems, created new export industries, and in some cases provided economic opportunities for African producers and traders that the slave trade had not. Evaluating it requires holding in view both what it changed and what it preserved, and being alert to the ways in which the language of moral progress was used to advance European commercial and political interests.

Multiple Perspectives

Experiencing the Age of Commerce: Four Vantage Points

From the perspective of an African king controlling a major trade route: For rulers like the Asantehene or the king of Dahomey, the Age of Commerce offered unprecedented resources to consolidate political authority, reward loyal subordinates, expand military power, and project influence across wide territories. Access to Atlantic imports — firearms, textiles, metals — made it possible to build institutions of governance, religious patronage, and military organization that could outlast individual rulers. The slave trade, whatever its moral costs, provided a commodity that European buyers actively sought and paid for in the goods most useful for political consolidation. These rulers understood themselves as operating skillfully within a global commercial system, extracting advantages where they could while managing the political risks that commercial integration created.
From the perspective of a Dyula merchant in the West African interior: For professional trading communities like the Dyula, the Age of Commerce represented an expansion of opportunity within a system they had helped build. The growth of Atlantic trade at the coast added a new commercial destination for goods that had previously moved primarily northward across the Sahara. The expansion of Islam across West Africa created new networks of trust, legal frameworks for commercial transactions, and communities of co-religionists who could be relied upon across the many political boundaries that long-distance trade required crossing. Commerce, for the Dyula, was not merely an economic activity but a religious calling, a social institution, and a source of cultural identity that gave their communities coherence across enormous geographic space.
From the perspective of a farmer in the slave-raiding zone: For ordinary farming communities in the areas most intensively targeted by slave raiders — the Igbo-speaking areas of the Niger Delta hinterland, the communities north of Dahomey in what is now Benin, the societies of the upper Congo basin — the Age of Commerce was experienced primarily as a source of terror, insecurity, and loss. The periodic descent of raiding parties, the capture of family members and neighbors, the forced abandonment of villages and fields, the loss of young adults whose labor was essential to agricultural production: these were the dominant realities of the Age of Commerce for millions of ordinary Africans who participated in global trade not as buyers or sellers but as the commodity being bought and sold.
From the perspective of a European commercial company official: For European commercial interests — the Royal African Company, the Dutch West India Company, later independent British and French trading firms — the African coast in the Age of Commerce was above all a commercial opportunity to be managed with minimal cost and maximum return. African political relationships were instrumental: valued as long as they facilitated the acquisition of desired commodities, abandoned when they imposed inconvenient restrictions, and subject to coercion when African commercial partners demanded terms that European companies considered unprofitable. The ideological framework of "legitimate commerce" developed later in this period provided a more comfortable rationalization, linking commercial expansion to missionary and civilizationist goals that gave European commercial penetration a moral veneer that its actual practice rarely warranted.
Continuity and Change Over Time

African Economic Life Before and After the Age of Commerce (c. 1400–1900)

What Changed

  • The orientation of West African long-distance commerce shifted from primarily northward (trans-Saharan) to increasingly southward and westward (Atlantic), reorganizing the political geography of the region
  • The scale of enslaved person export from Africa grew from a relatively modest trans-Saharan flow to a catastrophic Atlantic and Indian Ocean drain removing millions across four centuries
  • Firearms became available to African states willing to engage in slave trading, dramatically altering the military balance of power and creating new incentives for warfare
  • New kinds of states emerged organized explicitly around commercial functions — taxing, brokering, and in some cases producing the commodity of enslaved people
  • New commercial elites — merchants, brokers, factors — achieved social prominence that had no precise equivalent in earlier political hierarchies
  • African economies were progressively integrated into global commodity markets in which African producers had limited influence over the prices they received for their exports
  • The transition to "legitimate commerce" in the nineteenth century created new export industries but within terms of trade that preserved structural disadvantages

What Stayed the Same

  • Agriculture remained the primary livelihood for the vast majority of Africans throughout the period; long-distance commerce involved a small though influential minority of the population
  • Local and regional markets — in agricultural produce, craft goods, and food — continued to operate largely independently of long-distance trading systems
  • The fundamental importance of personal relationships, kinship networks, and social trust in organizing economic activity persisted across the transition from older commercial systems to Atlantic-era ones
  • Islamic commercial law and the religious institutions of Muslim trading communities provided frameworks for commercial organization that proved durable across radical political and economic changes
  • Women's control of local market trade remained a structural feature of many African economies throughout the period
  • Slavery as an institution within African societies predated the Atlantic trade and continued after its abolition, though its scale and character were transformed by the period of intensive external slave trading

Why the pattern matters: The combination of transformation and continuity in African economic life during the Age of Commerce shows that external commercial integration did not simply replace older patterns. It layered new commercial structures over existing ones, creating hybrid economies in which long-distance trade and local market exchange, Atlantic-connected export production and subsistence agriculture, coexisted in complex and often tense relationships. Understanding this complexity is essential to avoiding both the error of treating Africa as a passive recipient of external change and the error of minimizing the profound disruptions that the Age of Commerce produced.

Section 43.10

Chapter Review and Age of Commerce Checkpoint

This chapter has examined how expanding trade transformed African societies between approximately 1400 and 1900. It has argued that the Age of Commerce was not a single unified process but a complex and varied set of transformations that produced profoundly different outcomes for different African communities, depending on their geographic location, their political organization, the nature of their integration into commercial networks, and the specific commodities that long-distance trade demanded from them.

Several major themes have emerged across the chapter's sections. First, commerce and political power were deeply intertwined: the states that thrived in the Age of Commerce — Asante, Dahomey, the Swahili city-states, the Niger Delta commercial polities — were states that found ways to position themselves as indispensable nodes in expanding trading networks and to convert commercial wealth into political authority. Second, African merchants and trading communities were genuine actors in these commercial systems, building networks of remarkable geographic extent and organizational sophistication that operated largely independently of European influence. Third, the demands of the Atlantic slave trade created devastating incentive structures for warfare, slave raiding, and political competition that disrupted social life, reduced populations, and generated cycles of violence across wide regions of the continent. Fourth, the transition to "legitimate commerce" in the nineteenth century preserved structural inequalities even as it ended the most direct forms of human commodification. And fifth, the long-term consequences of the Age of Commerce — demographic, institutional, and social — shaped the conditions that African societies brought to the colonial era and that continue to influence African development to the present day.

Students who have worked through this chapter should be equipped to analyze the complex relationship between commerce, power, and social life in African history; to evaluate competing historical interpretations of African agency in the slave trade; to understand the geographic and commercial structures that organized long-distance trade across the continent; and to connect the transformations of the Age of Commerce to broader patterns of African historical development. These skills will be essential in subsequent chapters on colonialism, resistance, and the struggles for independence and development that constitute the final parts of this textbook.

Understanding the Age of Commerce is therefore essential not only for understanding the period itself but for understanding everything that came after it: colonialism, resistance, decolonization, and the long unfinished project of African development that is the subject of this textbook's final chapters.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Age of Commerce
A concept used by historians to describe a period of rapid commercial expansion in Africa (c. 1400–1850) during which long-distance trade transformed political systems, social hierarchies, and economic life across much of the continent.
Arms-slave cycle
The self-reinforcing dynamic in which African rulers traded enslaved people for firearms that enabled more slave raiding, generating more captives to trade for more guns, drawing more states into a cycle of militarized commerce.
Brokerage
The commercial service of mediating between buyers and sellers who lack the language, trust, legal frameworks, or market knowledge to transact directly; in African commerce, brokers and middlemen were essential and powerful actors in long-distance trade.
Canoe house
A corporate trading group in the Niger Delta organized around a leading merchant who maintained a fleet of trading canoes and a community of enslaved workers, paddlers, and commercial agents; an institutional innovation of the Atlantic commercial era.
Caravan trade
Long-distance overland commerce organized through large groups of traders, porters, and animals moving together for security and logistical support; the primary form of trans-Saharan and East African interior commerce.
Commercial diaspora
A dispersed community of merchants from a common cultural, ethnic, or religious background who maintain commercial networks across large geographic areas, using shared identity and trust to facilitate long-distance trade.
Cowrie shell currency
Small shells from the Indian Ocean Maldive Islands that served as currency across wide areas of West and Central Africa, circulated through trading networks as a standard medium of exchange.
Dependency
A structural condition in which one economy becomes reliant on another for essential goods, markets, or credit in ways that limit its freedom to organize production, set prices, or make independent economic decisions.
Dyula (Wangara)
Muslim West African trading communities who organized long-distance commerce across the forest-savanna border zone, carrying kola nuts, gold, cloth, and other goods across a vast network from Senegambia to the Hausa lands.
Entrepôt
A trading hub where goods from multiple sources are gathered, exchanged, and redistributed; Zanzibar, Salaga, Kano, and Elmina all functioned as entrepôts connecting different commercial regions.
Export economy
An economic system organized primarily around the production and export of commodities for sale in distant markets, rather than for local consumption; the Age of Commerce progressively oriented many African economies toward export.
Factor
A commercial agent who manages trade on behalf of absent merchants or companies; on the West African coast, African commercial brokers who managed the interface between European ships and interior suppliers were called factors.
Hinterland
The inland area supplying goods to and consuming imports from a coastal port; controlling the relationship between coastal ports and their hinterlands was a central commercial and political challenge of the Age of Commerce.
Ivory trade
The commerce in elephant tusks, in enormous demand in South and Southeast Asia and later Europe; ivory was the most important African export commodity in the Indian Ocean system and a major commodity in both Atlantic and trans-Saharan trade.
Kola nut
The mildly stimulant seed of a forest tree native to West Africa; a major commodity in intra-African commerce because of its high demand in Muslim communities where alcohol was forbidden.
Legitimate commerce
The nineteenth-century British abolitionist term for trade in agricultural commodities and other goods promoted as an alternative to the slave trade; associated with commercial, missionary, and eventually colonial penetration of Africa.
Mercantilism
An economic doctrine holding that national wealth was based on accumulation of precious metals through trade surpluses; the commercial policies of European empires in the Atlantic era were broadly mercantilist, seeking to extract wealth from African trade.
Mudaraba
An Islamic commercial partnership in which one party provides capital and another provides commercial expertise, sharing profits at a predetermined ratio; widely used by West African Muslim merchants to finance long-distance caravans.
Nyamwezi
A trading people from west-central Tanzania who organized long-distance caravan trade to the East African coast; among the most important commercial intermediaries of nineteenth-century East Africa.
Palm oil trade
The commerce in oil extracted from the palm fruit, a major West African export commodity in the nineteenth century; used in European industrial processes and food products, it became central to the "legitimate commerce" era.
Political economy
The relationship between political power and economic systems; the concept emphasizes that economic decisions are always embedded in political relationships of power, authority, and coercion.
Redistribution
An economic system in which wealth flows to a central authority (a ruler or state) and is then distributed to subordinates as rewards, tribute, or public goods; a common mechanism for maintaining political authority in pre-commercial and commercial-era African states.
Slave raiding
Military or organized criminal operations designed specifically to capture enslaved people for sale; slave raiding became a major form of organized violence in regions where Atlantic and Indian Ocean demand for enslaved people created strong commercial incentives.
Sufi brotherhood
An organized Muslim religious community built around devotion to a particular spiritual teacher or saint; Sufi brotherhoods provided social networks, commercial trust mechanisms, and geographic reach that supported Muslim trading communities across Africa.
Terms of trade
The relative prices of exports compared to imports in a trading relationship; deteriorating terms of trade — receiving less for one's exports relative to the cost of imports — was a structural disadvantage faced by many African commercial producers in the Age of Commerce.
Zongo
A "stranger's quarter" found in trading towns across West Africa, providing temporary residential accommodation for visiting merchants from distant communities; a physical institution reflecting the cosmopolitan, mobile character of commercial town populations.

Major People and Places

Identification & Significance

King Afonso I of Kongo (c. 1456–1543)
Christian ruler of Kongo who corresponded with the Portuguese crown protesting the destructive effects of the slave trade on his kingdom. His letters are among the most important African-authored primary sources from the early Atlantic era, documenting both the aspirations of an African ruler who sought to manage European commercial contact and the limits of that ruler's ability to control forces the trade had set in motion.
Osei Tutu (c. 1660–1717)
Founder of the Asante Empire, who used military alliance, commercial wealth, and the ideological power of the Golden Stool to unify the Akan states of the Gold Coast forest zone into one of the most powerful states of eighteenth-century West Africa.
Tippu Tip (c. 1837–1905)
Born Hamad ibn Muhammad ibn Juma ibn Rajab el Murjebi, this Zanzibar-based Swahili-Arab merchant organized the most extensive ivory and slave trading empire in the Central African interior, with networks reaching deep into the Congo basin and the Great Lakes region. His career illustrates both the commercial reach of the nineteenth-century Indian Ocean trading system and the human costs of its expansion.
Asante Empire
Powerful forest-zone state in present-day Ghana, built from the late seventeenth century on gold wealth, slave trading, and military expansion; the paradigmatic example of an African state that used commercial wealth to build durable political authority during the Age of Commerce.
Kingdom of Dahomey
A centralized West African state that organized slave raiding and trading as the central function of the state apparatus, becoming one of the most active African participants in the Atlantic slave trade in the eighteenth century; also notable for the Agojie, the female military corps that was among the state's most important military forces.
Elmina
First permanent European trading fort in sub-Saharan Africa, established by the Portuguese in 1482 on the Gold Coast (present-day Ghana); the commercial and military headquarters from which European traders negotiated with African suppliers and from which enslaved people were exported across the Atlantic.
Zanzibar
Island hub of the nineteenth-century Indian Ocean commercial world, where the Omani sultan relocated his capital in 1840; the center of the clove plantation economy, the East African slave and ivory trade, and the commercial networks that ran deep into the African interior.
Kilwa Kisiwani
The most important Swahili city-state of the medieval and early Atlantic era, controlling the gold trade from the Zimbabwean interior and serving as the principal southern node in the Indian Ocean commercial system; its decline in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries illustrates the disruption that Portuguese intrusion caused in existing East African commercial networks.
Kano
The most important of the Hausa city-states in the trans-Saharan commercial system, famous for its leather goods and textiles and for the scale and organization of its merchant community; Kano's commercial networks reached from North Africa across the Sahel and savanna zones into the forest regions of West Africa.
Salaga
One of West Africa's most important market towns, located at the forest-savanna border in present-day northern Ghana; a major junction in the kola and slave trades connecting the Atlantic coastal system to the trans-Saharan Sahelian network.
Ouidah
The principal Atlantic port of the Kingdom of Dahomey, through which an enormous proportion of the enslaved people exported from the Bight of Benin region passed; one of the most active slave-exporting ports in the entire history of the Atlantic slave trade.
Nyamwezi peoples
Trading people from west-central Tanzania who organized the long caravan routes connecting the interior of East Africa to the coast; essential intermediaries without whom the expansion of Indian Ocean commerce into the East African interior would have been impossible.

Tracing a Commercial Network: Following a Commodity Through the Age of Commerce

This project asks you to follow a single commodity — kola nuts, gold, ivory, palm oil, enslaved people, or another significant trade good — through the Age of Commerce, tracing its production, transportation, exchange, and ultimate use across the full network of commercial relationships in which it moved. The goal is to understand the Age of Commerce not as a collection of abstract forces but as a set of specific human relationships organized around the production and exchange of material goods.

  1. Choose your commodity. Select one of the following or propose another with your instructor's approval: kola nuts in West African trade; gold from the Gold Coast or Zimbabwe Plateau; ivory in the East African Indian Ocean trade; palm oil in the nineteenth-century Atlantic trade; enslaved people as a commodity in the Atlantic trade; cotton or cloth in either the trans-Saharan or Indian Ocean system.
  2. Research the production of your commodity. Where was it produced, and by whom? What was the physical process of production, and what labor did it require? Were particular communities, genders, or social groups disproportionately involved in production? What were the environmental conditions that made production possible in those specific locations?
  3. Research the transportation of your commodity. How did the commodity move from the point of production to its ultimate markets? Who organized and performed the work of transportation — caravan leaders, river traders, coastal brokers? What were the major routes, and what political and geographic obstacles did transporters navigate? What was the cost of transportation, and who bore it?
  4. Research the exchange of your commodity. Where and how was your commodity bought and sold? What other goods were exchanged for it, and who set the terms of exchange? What commercial institutions — markets, partnerships, credit arrangements — organized these exchanges? How did the terms of trade for your commodity change over the period you are studying?
  5. Research the ultimate use of your commodity. What happened to your commodity at the end of the commercial chain? Who used it, and for what purposes? How did demand from ultimate consumers shape the commercial systems through which the commodity moved?
  6. Write your analysis. In a 4–6 page essay (or equivalent multimedia presentation), trace your commodity through the full commercial chain and analyze what this journey reveals about the Age of Commerce. How did commercial relationships shape the lives of the people involved in each stage of production, transportation, exchange, and use? What does following this commodity reveal about the distribution of commercial benefits and burdens in the Age of Commerce?

Chapter Reflection

Chapter 43 has examined one of the most consequential and morally complex periods in African history: the four centuries during which African societies were progressively integrated into a global commercial system that brought both opportunity and devastation, commercial dynamism and human catastrophe, political creativity and demographic loss. The Age of Commerce was not something that happened to Africa from outside; it was a process in which African rulers, merchants, soldiers, farmers, and enslaved people all participated — though in radically different ways and with radically different degrees of choice — and whose consequences were written into the social, political, and economic fabric of the continent.

This chapter matters to the larger story of African history for several reasons. First, the political systems that emerged from the Age of Commerce — the commercial empires, the slave-trading states, the merchant-dominated coastal city-states — were the political landscape that European colonizers encountered when they began their conquest of the continent in the 1880s and 1890s. Understanding the Age of Commerce is essential to understanding colonialism: not because African commercial history "explains" European conquest, but because the specific political and commercial structures that the Age of Commerce produced shaped both the conditions that European colonialists exploited and the forms of African resistance to their advance. Second, the patterns of commercial dependency, deteriorating terms of trade, and institutional damage that the Age of Commerce produced are part of the long history of African economic development — or underdevelopment — that continues to shape contemporary debates about poverty, inequality, and global economic justice.

The chapter also illuminates several larger themes that run through this entire textbook. It shows Africa's deep integration into global economic systems — not as a passive recipient of external forces but as an active participant whose commercial knowledge, political organization, and geographic resources were essential components of the Atlantic and Indian Ocean trading worlds. It shows the diversity of African historical experience: the enormous differences between the commercial cities of the Sahel and the raided communities of the forest hinterland, between the merchant princes of the Niger Delta and the captives they exported, between the Muslim scholarly networks of the Dyula and the Christian court culture of Kongo. And it shows the persistence of African agency even within systems of profound structural constraint — the creativity with which African commercial actors navigated unfavorable conditions, the sophistication with which African rulers managed relationships with external commercial partners, and the resilience with which African communities rebuilt social and economic life in the aftermath of the disruptions that the Age of Commerce produced.

Students who carry the lessons of this chapter into their study of subsequent periods will be better equipped to understand colonialism not as something that happened to an empty or helpless Africa but as a process that encountered and was shaped by African political systems, commercial structures, and social organizations built over centuries of complex historical development. They will also be better equipped to understand why the consequences of the slave trade and the Age of Commerce did not end with abolition but continued — through institutional damage, commercial dependency, demographic loss, and patterns of political instability — to shape African experiences across the colonial era and into the postcolonial present. History does not end cleanly at chapter boundaries, and the Age of Commerce is a powerful reminder of the ways in which the past inhabits and shapes the present in ways both visible and invisible, acknowledged and denied.

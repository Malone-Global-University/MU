Part VIII — Colonialism, Conquest, and Resistance
50

African Resistance and Adaptation

Africa: A History of a Continent

Chapter Opening

"They came with the Bible and we had the land. They said, 'Let us pray.' We closed our eyes. When we opened them, they had the land and we had the Bible." This saying, attributed in different forms to several African communities describing the colonial encounter, captures both loss and irony — but it does not capture the full story, because Africans did not simply close their eyes and lose. They fought, negotiated, adapted, endured, and rebuilt, in ways this chapter examines in detail.

In October 1905, in the forests and floodplains of southeastern German East Africa, thousands of farmers, fishers, and laborers rose in a coordinated uprising against colonial taxation, forced cotton cultivation, and the brutal labor demands of German administrators. The rebellion became known as the Maji Maji uprising, named for the water — maji in Swahili — that ritual specialists distributed to fighters, said to protect them from German bullets. The uprising drew together dozens of different ethnic and linguistic communities who had rarely cooperated before, united by shared grievance against a common colonial enemy. It was crushed with appalling violence: German forces adopted a scorched-earth strategy that destroyed crops and villages across the rebellion's territory, and the resulting famine killed far more people than the fighting itself, with death tolls estimated between 200,000 and 300,000. Yet the uprising also forced German colonial administrators to reconsider their most exploitative policies, and it left behind an example of interethnic cooperation against colonial rule that later nationalist movements in Tanzania would draw upon as a founding memory of unified resistance.

The Maji Maji uprising is one of hundreds of episodes of African resistance to European conquest and colonial rule that occurred across the continent between the late nineteenth century and the mid-twentieth century. This chapter examines that resistance in its full range: the military campaigns fought by African states and armies against European invasion; the religious movements that reinterpreted African and imported faiths to challenge colonial authority; the political strategies of negotiation, litigation, and diplomacy pursued by African rulers and elites; the quieter, often invisible forms of resistance practiced by ordinary farmers, workers, and families in their daily lives; and the adaptive strategies through which African societies preserved core elements of their cultures, institutions, and identities even under conditions of external domination.

It is essential for students to understand from the outset that African responses to colonial conquest were not uniform, and they were not simply a story of doomed opposition followed by inevitable defeat. Some African states fought and won important victories, at least temporarily — the Ethiopian defeat of an invading Italian army at the Battle of Adwa in 1896 remains one of the most celebrated moments in African military history, and it preserved Ethiopian independence for another four decades. Other states negotiated treaties that preserved significant autonomy for years or even decades before colonial control tightened. Still others fought protracted guerrilla campaigns that colonial forces struggled for years to suppress, as in the case of the Maji Maji uprising itself, or the resistance of the Asante Empire against British forces in West Africa, or the resistance led by Samori Ture across the western Sudan. And many African communities pursued strategies that combined elements of all of these approaches at different moments, shifting tactics as circumstances changed.

This chapter also insists that resistance did not end when military conquest succeeded and colonial administration was established. Resistance continued in new forms throughout the colonial period: in tax revolts and market boycotts, in independent churches that broke away from missionary control, in the deliberate slowing of labor on plantations and in mines, in the preservation of languages and religious practices that colonial authorities tried to suppress, and in countless small acts through which colonized people asserted their dignity and autonomy even while living under systems designed to deny both. Historians increasingly describe this broad range of behavior using the concept of African agency — the recognition that Africans were never simply passive victims of colonial rule but active historical actors who shaped outcomes, however constrained their circumstances, through their choices, strategies, and courage.

At the same time, this chapter does not romanticize resistance or understate the catastrophic costs that colonial conquest and its suppression of African resistance inflicted. Millions of Africans died in conquest wars, in the punitive campaigns that followed uprisings, and in the famines and epidemics that often followed in the wake of military violence. Colonial repression was frequently disproportionate, deliberately terroristic, and in some cases — as this chapter will examine in the case of German South-West Africa — amounted to what many historians and legal scholars now classify as genocide. Understanding African resistance requires holding two things together: the extraordinary courage, creativity, and persistence Africans demonstrated in the face of conquest, and the enormous human cost that resistance, and its suppression, exacted.

Finally, this chapter asks students to think carefully about how resistance is defined and measured. If resistance is understood only as pitched battles between armies, then a very large part of the African historical experience of colonialism disappears from view, because most people never picked up a rifle against colonial forces. But if resistance is understood more broadly — as any action, individual or collective, that challenged colonial power, preserved African autonomy, or asserted African dignity — then the historical record reveals resistance as a constant, pervasive feature of the colonial encounter, practiced by millions of people across the continent in ways both dramatic and quiet, from the founding of independent churches to the refusal to report for forced labor, from armed rebellion to the deliberate preservation of oral traditions that colonial schools tried to erase.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain the range of forms African resistance to colonial conquest and rule took, from armed conflict to everyday non-cooperation.
  2. Trace major military campaigns of African resistance, including the Battle of Adwa, the Asante wars, the campaigns of Samori Ture, and the Maji Maji uprising.
  3. Analyze the role of religious movements, including independent churches and prophetic movements, in organizing resistance to colonial authority.
  4. Compare strategies of political negotiation and diplomacy pursued by African rulers with strategies of armed resistance.
  5. Describe forms of cultural survival and adaptation that preserved African languages, religions, and social institutions under colonial rule.
  6. Identify everyday forms of resistance practiced by ordinary Africans, including labor slowdowns, tax evasion, and flight.
  7. Evaluate the specific roles women played in anti-colonial resistance, including the Women's War in southeastern Nigeria.
  8. Assess the methods and scale of colonial repression used against African resistance movements, including the Herero and Nama genocide.
  9. Interpret the concept of African agency and apply it to specific historical episodes discussed in this chapter.
  10. Contextualize resistance and adaptation within the broader colonial systems examined in earlier chapters of this textbook.
  11. Distinguish between resistance that succeeded in its immediate goals and resistance that nonetheless shaped later African history in important ways.
  12. Connect the resistance movements of this era to the nationalist and independence movements examined in later chapters.

Historical Context

By the final decades of the nineteenth century, the process examined in earlier chapters of this textbook — the Scramble for Africa, formalized at the Berlin Conference of 1884–1885 — had set in motion a continent-wide contest between European imperial ambition and African political sovereignty. European powers claimed vast territories on paper, drawing borders in European capitals with little regard for existing African political boundaries, ethnic distributions, or trade networks. But claiming territory on a map and actually controlling it were very different matters. Turning colonial claims into functioning administrations required conquering, or coercing into submission, hundreds of African kingdoms, empires, city-states, and decentralized societies, each with its own military traditions, political structures, and capacity for resistance.

African societies entering this period of conquest were not uniformly weak or unprepared. Some, like the Sokoto Caliphate in what is now northern Nigeria, the Asante Empire in the forest zone of West Africa, and Ethiopia in the highlands of the Horn of Africa, were substantial states with organized militaries, sophisticated administrations, and long histories of diplomatic engagement with outside powers. Others, like the societies encountered by German forces in East Africa or Portuguese forces in Mozambique, were more decentralized, without a single ruler who could negotiate or surrender on behalf of an entire population — a characteristic that in some cases made conquest more difficult, since there was no central authority whose defeat could end resistance, and in other cases made coordinated resistance harder to organize.

The military balance between African and European forces was shifting unfavorably for Africa during this period, though the shift was neither sudden nor uniform. European powers possessed increasingly sophisticated firearms, including repeating rifles and machine guns, which gave their forces a significant advantage over African armies that in many cases still relied on older firearms, or on traditional weapons such as spears and swords supplemented by whatever modern arms could be obtained through trade. European powers also benefited from steamships and, later, railways that allowed them to move troops and supplies more efficiently than African forces operating without comparable transportation infrastructure. And European powers could draw on colonial troops recruited from other parts of Africa or from Asia, exploiting existing rivalries between African states and peoples to build armies that included substantial numbers of African soldiers fighting for the colonizing power.

Yet the outcome of specific conflicts was rarely determined by military technology alone. Terrain, disease environment, logistics, alliance-building, and leadership all played significant roles. Disease often worked against European forces: malaria, sleeping sickness, and other tropical diseases killed European soldiers and animals at devastating rates, particularly in the early stages of conquest before quinine and other treatments became more widely available. African forces fighting on familiar terrain, with local knowledge of geography, water sources, and seasonal patterns, sometimes used this advantage to fight effective guerrilla campaigns that made conquest slow and costly even when European forces ultimately prevailed.

It is also important for students to understand that African resistance was not always directed against Europeans specifically, nor was cooperation with European powers automatically a betrayal of African interests as some later nationalist narratives have suggested. Many African rulers made calculated decisions to ally with European powers against rival African states, hoping to use the newcomers as leverage in existing regional conflicts — a strategy with deep roots in the long history of African diplomacy, in which alliance with outside powers had long been one tool among several for pursuing local political interests. Some of these alliances proved disastrous, as European partners turned on their former African allies once rival African states had been defeated. Others provided African rulers with genuine, if temporary, advantages. Understanding African resistance requires understanding this full complexity, not reducing it to a simple binary of resistance versus collaboration.

Section 50.1

Forms of Resistance

To understand African resistance to colonial conquest and rule, students must first understand the wide range of forms that resistance took. Historians commonly distinguish between what might be called primary resistance — the initial military and political opposition African states and societies mounted against European conquest between roughly the 1870s and the 1900s — and secondary resistance, the ongoing forms of opposition that continued once colonial administrations had been established, sometimes lasting for decades. Both categories included armed and unarmed forms of struggle.

Armed resistance took several distinct forms. Some African states fought conventional wars, mobilizing standing armies to meet European forces in open battle, as Ethiopia did at Adwa. Others waged prolonged guerrilla campaigns, avoiding direct confrontation with technologically superior European forces and instead using mobility, local knowledge, and hit-and-run tactics to make conquest costly and slow, as Samori Ture did across the western Sudan for nearly two decades. Still others rose in sudden, large-scale uprisings after enduring years of colonial rule, provoked by specific grievances such as taxation, forced labor, or land seizure, as in the Maji Maji uprising and the Herero and Nama War.

Political resistance operated alongside and sometimes instead of military resistance. African rulers negotiated treaties, sought alliances with rival European powers to balance against the most threatening colonizer, sent diplomatic missions to European capitals to appeal directly for recognition of their sovereignty, and used legal and administrative channels — where these existed — to contest specific colonial policies. Ethiopia's diplomatic strategy under Menelik II combined military preparation with active diplomacy, playing rival European powers against one another to secure arms and diplomatic recognition even while preparing for war.

Religious resistance drew on both indigenous African religious traditions and imported faiths, particularly Christianity and Islam, to organize opposition to colonial authority. Prophetic and millenarian movements promised divine intervention against colonial rule, as in the Maji Maji uprising's ritual use of water believed to protect fighters from bullets. Independent churches, breaking away from missionary control, provided African Christians with religious authority independent of colonial oversight, as in the movement founded by Simon Kimbangu in the Belgian Congo. Islamic resistance movements, particularly in West Africa and the Sudan, drew on traditions of jihad and religious renewal to mobilize opposition to European conquest.

Everyday resistance — sometimes called "weapons of the weak" by historians studying similar dynamics in other colonized regions — included the countless small acts through which ordinary Africans without access to arms or political power resisted colonial demands: working slowly or inefficiently on forced labor projects, hiding grain and livestock from tax collectors, migrating away from areas of harsh colonial administration, maintaining forbidden religious practices in secret, and preserving oral traditions, languages, and social structures that colonial education and administration sought to erase or transform.

Cultural adaptation and survival, finally, represented a form of resistance that did not necessarily involve direct confrontation with colonial authority but instead focused on preserving what could be preserved of African institutions, values, and ways of life under conditions of external domination. This included the selective adoption of European institutions — literacy, Christianity, Western education — in ways that African communities adapted to serve their own purposes rather than simply accepting them on colonial terms.

Section 50.2

Military Struggle Against Conquest

The military campaigns African states waged against European conquest varied enormously in scale, duration, and outcome, but several stand out as particularly significant for what they reveal about the possibilities and limits of armed resistance.

The most celebrated case is the Battle of Adwa, fought on March 1, 1896, in the mountains of northern Ethiopia. Italy, seeking to expand its colonial holdings in the Horn of Africa from its existing colony of Eritrea, invaded Ethiopian territory with an army of roughly 17,000 to 20,000 soldiers, including Italian troops and colonial askari recruited from Eritrea. Emperor Menelik II mobilized a much larger Ethiopian force, estimated at well over 100,000 soldiers, drawn from across the Ethiopian empire and armed in substantial part with modern rifles that Ethiopian diplomacy had secured from European powers eager to use Ethiopia as a counterweight to Italian ambitions. The resulting battle was a decisive Ethiopian victory: Italian forces suffered massive casualties, and the survivors retreated in disarray. The Treaty of Addis Ababa that followed forced Italy to recognize Ethiopian independence, and Ethiopia remained one of only two African states — along with Liberia — to avoid formal European colonization during the height of the Scramble for Africa.

Adwa's significance extended far beyond Ethiopia's borders. News of the victory spread across the African diaspora and became a powerful symbol for Pan-African and anti-colonial movements throughout the twentieth century, demonstrating that European conquest was not inevitable or unstoppable. Ethiopia's continued independence also made it a symbolically important refuge and inspiration: decades later, when Italy under Mussolini's fascist government invaded Ethiopia again in 1935, the Ethiopian cause drew international attention and support precisely because of the memory of Adwa.

In West Africa, Samori Ture's resistance offers a different model: a long guerrilla campaign of adaptation and retreat rather than a single decisive battle. Samori had built the Wassoulou Empire through decades of military and political consolidation before French forces began pressing against his territory in the 1880s. Rather than meeting French forces in conventional battle, where French artillery and repeating rifles gave a decisive advantage, Samori adopted a strategy of strategic withdrawal, rebuilding his state and army further east when French pressure became overwhelming in one region, and adapting his own military organization to incorporate modern rifles obtained through trade. This strategy allowed Samori to resist French conquest for nearly two decades, far longer than most other West African resistance movements, before his eventual capture in 1898 and exile, where he died in 1900.

In the forest kingdom of Asante, resistance took the form of a final, dramatic uprising after decades of prior conflict with British forces. When the British governor demanded that the Asante hand over their sacred Golden Stool — the symbol of Asante sovereignty and spiritual authority — Yaa Asantewaa, the queen mother of Ejisu, rallied Asante forces to war rather than surrender the symbol of their nationhood. The resulting War of the Golden Stool in 1900, though ultimately unsuccessful in preventing British annexation of Asante territory, became one of the most celebrated instances of African resistance led by a woman, and the Golden Stool itself was never surrendered, remaining hidden until it could later be safely revealed.

Not all military resistance succeeded even temporarily, and students should understand that the overall trajectory of the conquest era, between roughly 1880 and 1914, moved decisively toward European territorial control across nearly the entire continent. But the duration, cost, and difficulty of that conquest varied enormously depending on the strength of African resistance, and in some regions — particularly areas of Islamic West Africa, the Ethiopian highlands, and parts of Central and Southern Africa — resistance delayed and complicated colonial consolidation for years or decades after initial European claims had been made.

Section 50.3

Religious Resistance Movements

Religion provided one of the most powerful frameworks through which African societies organized resistance to colonial rule, drawing on indigenous cosmologies, transformed Christian traditions, and reformist currents within Islam.

The Maji Maji uprising of 1905–1907 in German East Africa illustrates the mobilizing power of religious ritual in organizing resistance across ethnic and linguistic lines that had rarely cooperated politically before. A spirit medium named Kinjikitile Ngwale, associated with a shrine at Ngarambe, distributed water — maji — that he claimed would protect fighters from German bullets. Though the promise of bulletproof protection proved tragically false, and though scholars debate exactly how literally participants understood the promise, the maji ritual served a genuine organizational function: it provided a shared symbol and belief system that helped unite dozens of distinct communities across a wide geographic area in coordinated resistance against German forced-cotton-cultivation policies and colonial taxation. The uprising's suppression, through a deliberate German scorched-earth campaign that destroyed food supplies across the rebellion's territory, produced a catastrophic famine that killed far more people than the military conflict itself.

Christian-inspired resistance took distinctive forms shaped by the specific ways mission Christianity had been introduced to African communities, often accompanied by land seizure, labor exploitation, and racial discrimination that contradicted the professed egalitarian teachings of the missionaries themselves. John Chilembwe, educated partly in the United States and deeply influenced by African American Christian traditions, returned to Nyasaland (present-day Malawi) to found his own independent church and school. Outraged by the exploitative conditions on European-owned plantations near his mission and by the conscription of Africans to fight and die for Britain in the First World War, Chilembwe led an uprising in January 1915. The rebellion was quickly and violently suppressed, and Chilembwe himself was killed, but his uprising became a foundational memory for later Malawian nationalism, and Chilembwe is honored today as a national hero.

Independent African churches — churches founded and led by Africans, separate from missionary control — proliferated across the continent during the colonial period, particularly in Southern and Central Africa, and represented a form of religious resistance that did not necessarily involve armed conflict but nonetheless directly challenged colonial religious authority. Simon Kimbangu, in the Belgian Congo, began preaching and reportedly performing healing miracles in 1921, quickly attracting a mass following that alarmed both Catholic missionary authorities and the Belgian colonial administration, despite Kimbangu's explicitly nonviolent message. Belgian authorities arrested Kimbangu within months of the movement's beginning and sentenced him to death, later commuted to life imprisonment; he remained imprisoned until his death in 1951. The movement he founded, known today as the Kimbanguist Church, survived underground and eventually became one of the largest independent Christian denominations in Africa.

Islamic resistance movements, particularly in West Africa and the Sudan, drew on traditions of religious renewal and jihad that predated European conquest but that colonial authorities frequently viewed with particular alarm, given the history of large-scale jihad-based state formation in the region, discussed in earlier chapters of this textbook. The Mahdist state in Sudan, though its most dramatic confrontation with British forces occurred slightly before the period this chapter emphasizes, established a model of religiously organized anti-colonial resistance that influenced later movements, and Islamic scholars and leaders across the Sahel continued to provide alternative sources of authority and social organization that complicated colonial administration throughout the colonial period.

Section 50.4

Political Negotiation and Adaptation

Not all African rulers chose armed confrontation as their primary strategy, and it would be a mistake to treat negotiation and diplomacy as somehow less legitimate or less significant than military resistance. Many African states pursued sophisticated diplomatic strategies, seeking to preserve as much sovereignty and autonomy as possible through treaty negotiation, alliance-building, and international appeals.

Ethiopia's success at Adwa was itself the product of skilled diplomacy as much as military strength. Menelik II had spent years before the Italian invasion carefully cultivating relationships with multiple European powers, playing French, Russian, and British interests against Italian ambitions to secure modern weapons and diplomatic support, while using earlier treaties with Italy — including a notoriously ambiguous agreement whose Amharic and Italian versions differed on the crucial question of whether Ethiopia had accepted Italian protectorate status — to buy time for military preparation. When Italy attempted to enforce its interpretation of the treaty by force, Ethiopia was ready to respond both militarily and diplomatically.

Elsewhere, African rulers who calculated that armed resistance offered little realistic prospect of success sought instead to negotiate the most favorable terms possible for submission, preserving local authority, land rights, or economic privileges even while accepting nominal European sovereignty. This strategy connected directly to the system of indirect rule examined in earlier chapters: rulers who cooperated with colonial administration, whether from calculation, coercion, or genuine belief that cooperation served their communities' interests, often retained significant local authority under indirect rule structures, at least in the short term, even as the underlying reality of colonial subordination remained.

This strategy carried real risks and real costs. Rulers who negotiated submission sometimes found colonial administrators reneging on promised terms once resistance elsewhere had been crushed and colonial power was more firmly established. And African communities frequently divided over whether negotiation or resistance offered the better path, with these divisions sometimes hardening into lasting political and social tensions. Historians today generally avoid the older, simplistic framework that celebrated resistance and condemned collaboration, recognizing instead that African rulers faced genuinely difficult strategic choices under conditions of vast power imbalance, and that both resistance and negotiation could reflect calculated efforts to protect African communities and interests under extraordinarily constrained circumstances.

Legal and administrative resistance also developed within colonial systems themselves, particularly as colonial rule matured and some African elites gained access to Western education, legal training, and colonial courts. African lawyers, journalists, and petitioners increasingly used colonial legal frameworks to contest specific policies — land alienation, discriminatory taxation, restrictions on African commercial activity — even while working within the broader colonial system rather than against it directly. This strategy, more fully developed in the nationalist movements examined in later chapters of this textbook, had roots in the political adaptation strategies African elites developed during the earlier conquest and consolidation period covered in this chapter.

Section 50.5

Cultural Survival and Change

Alongside military and political resistance, African societies engaged in extensive work of cultural survival and adaptation, preserving languages, religious practices, family structures, and social institutions even as colonial administration, missionary activity, and Western education pressed for transformation.

Language preservation represented one of the most fundamental and pervasive forms of cultural survival. Despite colonial education systems that frequently privileged European languages and, in some cases, actively punished the use of African languages in schools, the overwhelming majority of Africans continued to speak African languages in their homes, communities, and religious practices throughout the colonial period. This linguistic continuity ensured that oral traditions, proverbs, praise poetry, and historical narratives — the primary means by which African societies had transmitted history, law, and values for centuries, as discussed in earlier chapters of this textbook — continued to be passed down despite colonial disruption.

Religious syncretism — the blending of indigenous African religious practices with introduced Christian and Islamic traditions — represented another crucial form of cultural adaptation. Rather than simply accepting missionary Christianity or reformist Islam on the terms colonial religious authorities presented, African converts frequently integrated new religious frameworks with existing beliefs about ancestors, spiritual forces, healing, and community, producing distinctively African forms of both Christianity and Islam that missionaries and colonial religious authorities often viewed with suspicion or hostility but that proved remarkably durable and, over time, became the dominant forms these religions took across much of the continent.

Family and kinship structures, though placed under significant strain by colonial labor migration, taxation policies, and administrative interference, generally proved more resilient than colonial administrators anticipated or, in some cases, desired. Extended family networks continued to provide social security, labor organization, and mutual assistance even as male labor migration to mines, plantations, and colonial cities pulled working-age men away from rural communities for extended periods, placing enormous burdens on women who remained in rural areas managing agricultural production and household survival — a dynamic examined further in the everyday-resistance and women's-history sections of this chapter.

Artistic and cultural production also continued and adapted throughout the colonial period. Music, dance, sculpture, textile production, and oral performance continued to develop, often incorporating new materials, instruments, and themes introduced through colonial contact while retaining distinctively African aesthetic and social functions. Some cultural forms became directly political: songs and dances developed during this period frequently carried coded criticism of colonial authorities, chiefs perceived as collaborators, or specific colonial policies, allowing performers to voice resistance in forms that colonial authorities often could not fully monitor or suppress.

Section 50.6

Everyday Forms of Resistance

Beyond dramatic uprisings and formal negotiations, the great majority of African resistance to colonial rule took place in the ordinary rhythms of daily life, through countless individual and small-group decisions that collectively constrained, complicated, and contested colonial power even when no single act appeared politically significant on its own.

Tax evasion and avoidance were pervasive throughout the colonial period. Colonial administrations depended heavily on hut taxes, poll taxes, and other direct levies on African populations, both as a source of revenue and as a deliberate mechanism to compel Africans into wage labor, since cash needed to pay taxes often could only be earned through labor on colonial farms, mines, or public works. Africans across the continent responded with a wide range of evasive strategies: hiding livestock and grain from assessors, providing false information about household size or income, delaying payment as long as possible, and in some cases fleeing tax-collection zones entirely, migrating to regions of lighter colonial administration or across colonial borders where enforcement was weaker.

Labor resistance took similarly varied forms. Workers on forced-labor projects, plantations, and in mines frequently worked deliberately slowly, feigned illness, sabotaged equipment, or simply deserted when opportunities allowed, despite the severe punishments colonial labor codes typically prescribed for such behavior. These everyday acts of labor resistance, though individually small in scale, collectively reduced the efficiency and profitability of colonial economic extraction and forced colonial administrators and employers to continually adjust labor policies in response.

Flight and migration served as a widely used strategy of resistance and adaptation, particularly in regions where colonial administration was uneven or where colonial borders — often drawn with little regard for existing settlement patterns — created zones of lighter or heavier administrative control within relatively short distances of one another. Communities facing particularly harsh taxation, forced labor demands, or land seizure sometimes relocated entire villages to escape the reach of a demanding administrator, taking advantage of colonial Africa's often thin administrative presence outside major towns and government posts.

The preservation of forbidden or discouraged cultural and religious practices — including specific rituals, forms of dress, marriage customs, and healing practices that missionaries or colonial administrators sought to suppress — represented another pervasive form of everyday resistance, often conducted quietly, away from colonial or missionary observation, allowing communities to maintain practices central to their identity and social organization despite external pressure to abandon them.

Everyday Life and Resistance

The Rural Household Under Colonial Pressure

For the majority of Africans living in rural communities under colonial rule, resistance and adaptation were woven into the fabric of daily survival rather than separated out as distinct political acts. A rural household facing colonial taxation, labor recruitment, and cash-crop requirements had to make constant practical decisions: how much grain to report to tax assessors, whether to send a son to work in distant mines to earn tax money or to keep him at home for agricultural labor, whether to plant the cash crops colonial agricultural officers demanded or to prioritize food crops for family subsistence, and how to maintain religious and social obligations to extended family and community despite the pressures of colonial administration.

Women, who in many African societies bore primary responsibility for food-crop agriculture, frequently found themselves managing increased agricultural burdens as male labor migration removed working-age men from rural communities for extended periods, while also navigating colonial and mission efforts to reshape gender roles, education, and family structure according to European models. These daily negotiations, repeated across millions of households throughout the colonial period, constituted an enormous, largely undocumented history of adaptation and quiet resistance that historians have increasingly worked to recover through oral history and careful reading of colonial administrative records against the grain of their original purposes.

Questions for students:

  1. Why might everyday forms of resistance be harder for historians to document than armed uprisings or formal political negotiations?
  2. How did colonial taxation policy function as a deliberate mechanism to compel African labor, and how did Africans respond?
  3. What burdens did labor migration place specifically on women in rural communities?
Section 50.7

Women in Anti-Colonial Struggle

Women played essential and often distinctive roles in African resistance to colonial conquest and rule, roles that earlier generations of colonial and even nationalist historiography frequently minimized or ignored, but that recent historical scholarship has worked extensively to recover and analyze.

In some cases, women led military and political resistance directly. Yaa Asantewaa's leadership of the War of the Golden Stool in 1900, discussed earlier in this chapter, stands as the most celebrated example, but she was not unique: women across the continent held positions of religious, political, and military authority in various African societies before colonization, and some drew on these existing traditions of female authority to organize resistance to colonial conquest.

The Women's War of 1929, known in Igbo as Ogu Umunwaanyi, represents one of the most significant instances of mass female-led resistance in colonial African history. In southeastern Nigeria, British colonial authorities had imposed a system of indirect rule that installed "warrant chiefs" — men appointed by colonial authorities to exercise local administrative authority — in Igbo and Ibibio communities that had traditionally been governed through more decentralized, consensus-based political structures without hereditary chiefs. Many warrant chiefs proved corrupt or abusive, exploiting their colonially granted authority for personal gain, and Igbo women, who held significant economic and social authority within traditional Igbo political structures, deeply resented both the warrant chief system itself and rumors that colonial authorities intended to extend direct taxation to women, who had previously been exempt.

In November and December of 1929, tens of thousands of women across a wide area of southeastern Nigeria organized a coordinated protest movement, using traditional methods of collective female political action — including a practice sometimes described as "sitting on" a man, involving organized demonstrations, chanting, and symbolic actions outside the homes or offices of warrant chiefs and colonial officials to shame and pressure them — to demand the removal of specific warrant chiefs and the abandonment of plans to tax women. The protests escalated in some locations into attacks on colonial buildings and property. Colonial authorities responded with lethal force in several locations, and more than fifty women were killed by colonial troops and police during the suppression of the movement, most notably in the towns of Opobo and Abak.

Although the Women's War did not overturn the indirect rule system that had provoked it, it forced significant policy changes: colonial authorities abandoned plans to extend direct taxation to women in the region, conducted an official inquiry into the causes of the uprising, and made some reforms to the warrant chief system in response to documented grievances. The uprising also entered Nigerian historical memory as a foundational example of women's political mobilization, later invoked by Nigerian women's organizations in subsequent political struggles both during and after the colonial period.

More broadly, colonial rule frequently disrupted the economic and political authority women had exercised in many pre-colonial African societies, since colonial administrators, shaped by European gender assumptions, often negotiated exclusively with men, formalized male-only chieftaincy structures even in societies with traditions of female political authority, and structured colonial education and wage labor opportunities in ways that privileged men over women. This disruption of women's pre-colonial authority helps explain why gender-specific grievances, alongside broader anti-colonial grievances, motivated much of the women-led resistance activity examined in this chapter and in later chapters covering the nationalist period.

Section 50.8

Resistance and Colonial Repression

Colonial responses to African resistance were frequently severe, and in the most extreme cases amounted to campaigns of mass killing that historians and legal scholars today classify using the modern legal concept of genocide, even though the term did not exist at the time these events occurred.

The most thoroughly documented case is the German campaign against the Herero and Nama peoples of German South-West Africa, present-day Namibia. When Herero communities rose in January 1904 against German land seizure and abusive settler practices, followed shortly by Nama resistance, German colonial authorities under General Lothar von Trotha responded with a deliberate policy of extermination. Von Trotha's infamous "extermination order" of October 1904 explicitly declared that Herero people found within German-controlled territory, armed or unarmed, with or without cattle, would be shot, and that Herero seeking to flee would be denied access to water sources, driving survivors into the Omaheke Desert, where thousands died of thirst and starvation. Herero and Nama survivors who did not flee were confined in concentration camps, including the notorious camp on Shark Island, where forced labor, malnutrition, and disease killed large numbers of prisoners, and where German doctors conducted medical experiments on prisoners' remains that later contributed to the development of racial pseudoscience used to justify later atrocities in the twentieth century. Estimates suggest that between 24,000 and 65,000 Herero people — the majority of the pre-war Herero population — and approximately half of the Nama population died as a direct result of this campaign between 1904 and 1908. In 2021, the German government formally acknowledged these events as genocide, though disputes over reparations and the adequacy of Germany's response continue.

The suppression of the Maji Maji uprising in German East Africa, discussed earlier in this chapter, similarly relied on a deliberate scorched-earth strategy that destroyed food supplies across a wide territory specifically to induce famine among populations suspected of supporting the rebellion, killing far more people through starvation than had died in direct combat.

Other colonial powers employed comparably severe, if sometimes less systematically genocidal, methods of repression against resistance movements throughout this period and beyond: collective punishment of villages suspected of supporting resistance, destruction of crops and livestock, forced population relocation, public executions intended to deter further resistance, and the use of colonial courts to impose harsh sentences on resistance leaders and participants. These methods reflected both the practical military challenges colonial forces faced in suppressing resistance across difficult terrain with limited troop numbers, and the underlying racial ideologies of colonial rule, which frequently regarded African life as less valuable than European life and African resistance as a form of savagery to be crushed rather than a legitimate political response to conquest and exploitation to be negotiated.

The severity of colonial repression carries important historical significance beyond the immediate human toll, catastrophic as that toll was. It reveals the fundamentally coercive character underlying colonial rule even in its most "orderly" periods, since the administrative structures colonial powers established and the relative calm that sometimes prevailed in particular colonies during particular periods depended, ultimately, on the demonstrated willingness of colonial powers to use overwhelming and often disproportionate violence against any significant challenge to their authority.

Section 50.9

African Agency Under Empire

The concept of African agency has become central to how historians understand the colonial period, replacing older historiographical frameworks that treated Africans primarily as passive subjects of European decision-making. Agency refers to the capacity of historical actors to make meaningful choices and to shape historical outcomes through those choices, even under conditions of severe constraint, coercion, and unequal power.

Applying the concept of agency to African resistance and adaptation does not mean pretending that colonial power was not real, or that African choices were unconstrained, or that resistance always succeeded in achieving its immediate goals. The overwhelming majority of African military resistance to colonial conquest ultimately failed to prevent European territorial control, at least in the near term, and colonial administrations remained in place across most of the continent for many decades after the resistance movements examined in this chapter. Agency, properly understood, does not require success; it requires recognizing that African individuals and communities were active historical subjects who evaluated their circumstances, weighed available strategies, and made deliberate choices — to fight, to negotiate, to migrate, to convert, to adapt, to resist quietly — in pursuit of their own interests and values, rather than simply reacting passively to European initiative.

This reframing has significant implications for how the entire colonial period is understood. Colonial administration, however powerful, never operated as a simple top-down system imposing European will on a blank African slate. Colonial policies were constantly shaped, modified, and sometimes abandoned in response to African resistance, negotiation, and non-cooperation. The indirect rule systems examined in earlier chapters, for example, were themselves partly a colonial adaptation to the practical difficulties and resistance colonial administrations encountered when attempting more direct forms of control. Colonial economic policy was constantly adjusted in response to labor resistance, tax evasion, and market behavior that colonial administrators had not anticipated and often could not fully control.

The concept of agency also helps explain historical continuity between the resistance movements examined in this chapter and the nationalist movements examined in later chapters of this textbook. The organizational skills, religious and cultural frameworks, and historical memories generated by early resistance movements did not disappear with their military defeat. They persisted in African communities, were transmitted through oral tradition, religious institutions, and family memory, and were drawn upon by later generations of African political leaders who explicitly invoked earlier resistance figures — Samori Ture, Yaa Asantewaa, John Chilembwe, the Herero and Nama dead — as founding examples of the anti-colonial struggle that twentieth-century nationalist movements would eventually bring to fruition in the achievement of formal political independence, examined in Part IX of this textbook.

Historical Vocabulary
Agency

Agency, in historical analysis, refers to the capacity of individuals and communities to make meaningful choices and shape historical outcomes, even under conditions of significant constraint or unequal power. In the context of African colonial history, the concept of agency emphasizes that Africans were active historical participants — negotiating, resisting, adapting, and making strategic choices — rather than passive subjects who simply had history done to them by European powers.

Recognizing African agency does not mean denying the reality of colonial power, coercion, and violence. It means recognizing that within often extraordinarily constrained circumstances, African individuals and communities continued to exercise judgment, pursue strategies, and shape the course of events, and that understanding this active role is essential to an accurate historical account of the colonial period.

Historical Vocabulary
Indirect Rule and the Warrant Chief System

Indirect rule was a colonial administrative strategy, particularly associated with British colonial policy, in which colonial authorities governed through existing or newly appointed local rulers rather than through a fully staffed European administrative apparatus. In regions like southeastern Nigeria, where Igbo communities had traditionally been governed through decentralized, consensus-based structures without hereditary chiefs, British administrators appointed "warrant chiefs" — men granted colonial authority through official warrants — to fill roles that did not correspond to any pre-existing traditional office. This mismatch between colonial administrative assumptions and actual Igbo political structures contributed directly to the grievances that produced the Women's War of 1929.

Primary Source Window

Colonial-Era Testimony on the Maji Maji Uprising

Type: Oral testimony recorded by later researchers from participants and descendants · Context: German East Africa, 1905–1907, recorded in subsequent decades

Context: Because German colonial records of the Maji Maji uprising were produced almost entirely by colonial officials and military officers, historians seeking to understand the perspective of participants have relied heavily on oral testimony collected from survivors, descendants, and community elders in the decades following the uprising, supplemented by careful, critical reading of German administrative and missionary records.

Surviving oral accounts collected by later researchers describe how communities across ethnic and linguistic lines that had previously had little political cooperation came together around the shared belief in the protective power of the maji water, traveling significant distances to receive it from ritual specialists before joining the uprising, and describe the devastating famine that followed German scorched-earth tactics as, in the memory of many communities, an even greater catastrophe than the fighting itself.

Analysis: Oral testimony of this kind provides historians with perspectives largely absent from German colonial records, which focused overwhelmingly on military operations and rarely documented African motivations, beliefs, or experiences in detail. However, oral testimony collected decades after the events it describes carries its own limitations: memory can be reshaped by subsequent events, by the political purposes of later nationalist movements that drew on Maji Maji as a founding example of anti-colonial unity, and by the particular circumstances under which specific testimonies were collected. Historians triangulate such testimony against German administrative records, missionary accounts, and archaeological evidence to construct as accurate a picture as possible.

Historical Debate

Did African Resistance to Colonial Conquest "Fail"?

The question: Since the overwhelming majority of African military resistance movements did not prevent European colonial conquest, at least in the near term, some older historical accounts characterized African resistance as ultimately futile or ineffective. Contemporary historians largely reject this framing, but the underlying question of how to assess the historical significance of resistance that did not achieve its immediate objectives remains a genuine subject of historical interpretation.

Interpretation A — Resistance Should Be Judged by Immediate Military and Political Outcomes: Some historians argue that assessing the significance of resistance requires honestly acknowledging that most resistance movements did not prevent conquest, that the human cost of resistance and its suppression was often catastrophic, and that romanticizing resistance risks obscuring the genuine and overwhelming power imbalance that shaped the colonial encounter.
Interpretation B — Resistance Should Be Judged by Its Broader Historical Consequences: Other historians argue that judging resistance solely by whether it prevented conquest applies too narrow a standard. Resistance shaped colonial policy, forced colonial powers to moderate specific practices, preserved cultural and political traditions that later nationalist movements drew upon, and demonstrated African agency and capacity for organized opposition that directly informed twentieth-century independence movements. By this standard, resistance's significance extends well beyond its immediate military outcome.

Evidence: Evidence for Interpretation A includes the near-universal establishment of colonial administration across the continent by 1914 despite widespread resistance, and the catastrophic casualty figures associated with major uprisings. Evidence for Interpretation B includes documented colonial policy changes following specific resistance movements (such as the abandonment of plans to tax women after the 1929 Women's War), and the explicit invocation of earlier resistance figures and movements by twentieth-century nationalist leaders.

Why it matters: How historians answer this question shapes how the entire colonial period is narrated: as a story of overwhelming, largely unopposed European domination, or as a story of continuous African contestation that shaped colonial rule throughout its existence and laid essential groundwork for eventual independence.

Student task: Based on the evidence presented in this chapter, which interpretation do you find more persuasive? Is it possible for both interpretations to be correct simultaneously? What additional evidence would help you evaluate this question further?

Multiple Perspectives

The Battle of Adwa, 1896

From the perspective of Emperor Menelik II and the Ethiopian state: Adwa represented the vindication of years of careful diplomatic and military preparation, the preservation of Ethiopian sovereignty against an explicit European attempt at colonial conquest, and confirmation of Ethiopia's standing as an independent state worthy of international recognition and diplomatic respect.
From the perspective of Italian colonial officials and the Italian government: The defeat at Adwa was a national humiliation that provoked the collapse of the Italian government of Prime Minister Francesco Crispi and significantly damaged Italian colonial ambitions in the Horn of Africa for decades, though Italy would return to attempt conquest of Ethiopia again in 1935 under Mussolini's fascist government.
From the perspective of other African states and communities under colonial threat: News of the Ethiopian victory spread across the continent and demonstrated that European conquest, though overwhelming in most cases, was not automatically inevitable — a lesson that shaped the strategic calculations of other African rulers and later inspired Pan-African and anti-colonial movements throughout the twentieth century.
From the perspective of the African diaspora, particularly in the Americas: Adwa became a powerful symbol of Black achievement and capability at a time when scientific racism and colonial ideology in Europe and the Americas claimed African inferiority as established fact; the victory was widely celebrated in African American and Caribbean newspapers and became a touchstone reference for early Pan-Africanist thought, discussed further in later chapters of this textbook.
Cause and Effect

The Herero and Nama War and Genocide, 1904–1908

Long-Term Causes

German colonization of South-West Africa beginning in the 1880s; systematic seizure of Herero cattle and land by German settlers; increasing indebtedness and dispossession of Herero communities; racial ideology among German settlers and officials that regarded African life as expendable.

Short-Term Causes

Escalating settler abuses and land seizures in 1903–1904; Herero decision to rise in armed resistance in January 1904; German military reinforcement under General Lothar von Trotha; von Trotha's explicit extermination order of October 1904.

The Event

German forces drove Herero survivors into the Omaheke Desert and denied them access to water; survivors and Nama resisters who did not flee were confined in concentration camps, including Shark Island, where forced labor and disease caused mass death.

Effects

Deaths of an estimated 24,000–65,000 Herero people and roughly half the Nama population; long-term demographic and economic devastation of Herero and Nama communities; German medical experimentation on remains later linked to pseudo-scientific racial theory; formal German acknowledgment of genocide in 2021 and ongoing disputes over reparations.

Historical complexity: The Herero and Nama genocide cannot be explained as a spontaneous military overreaction; it was the product of years of settler-colonial land seizure and racial ideology that culminated in an explicit, documented policy decision to exterminate a resisting population, distinguishing it from many other instances of colonial military repression discussed in this chapter.

Continuity and Change Over Time

African Resistance from Conquest-Era Uprisings to Twentieth-Century Nationalism

What Changed

  • Resistance shifted from primarily military confrontation toward political organization, legal advocacy, and mass mobilization by the mid-twentieth century
  • Resistance increasingly organized around explicitly national and Pan-African political identities rather than solely local or ethnic loyalties
  • Access to Western education and print media gave later resistance leaders new organizational tools unavailable to earlier movements
  • International context shifted, with the World Wars and the emergence of international human rights discourse creating new opportunities for African political claims

What Stayed the Same

  • The fundamental grievances driving resistance — land dispossession, forced labor, taxation, and denial of political rights — remained consistent across the period
  • Religious institutions, both indigenous and independent Christian and Islamic movements, continued to provide organizational frameworks for resistance
  • Women continued to play essential, if often under-recognized, roles in resistance movements
  • Colonial authorities continued to respond to resistance with significant, sometimes lethal, repression

Why the pattern matters: Recognizing both the continuities and the changes between early conquest-era resistance and later nationalist movements helps students understand African independence not as a sudden mid-twentieth-century development but as the culmination of a much longer history of resistance and adaptation stretching back to the earliest years of colonial conquest.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has examined the extraordinary range of ways African societies responded to the conquest and imposition of colonial rule between the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. Resistance was never a single, uniform phenomenon. It included pitched military battles like Adwa, in which Ethiopian forces under Emperor Menelik II decisively defeated an invading Italian army and preserved Ethiopian sovereignty; prolonged guerrilla campaigns like that waged by Samori Ture across the western Sudan for nearly two decades; sudden mass uprisings like the Maji Maji rebellion, which united dozens of communities across German East Africa in shared resistance to forced cotton cultivation and taxation; and religiously organized movements, from the ritual water distributed by Maji Maji leaders to the independent churches founded by figures like Simon Kimbangu in the Belgian Congo.

The chapter has also emphasized that resistance extended far beyond dramatic confrontation. Political negotiation and diplomacy, pursued by rulers like Menelik II even alongside military preparation, represented a legitimate and often sophisticated strategy for preserving African sovereignty and autonomy under conditions of extreme power imbalance. Everyday forms of resistance — tax evasion, labor slowdowns, flight, and the quiet preservation of forbidden cultural and religious practices — constituted a pervasive, continuous form of struggle practiced by millions of ordinary Africans throughout the colonial period, even when no single act appeared politically dramatic.

Women's roles in resistance received particular attention in this chapter, from Yaa Asantewaa's direct military leadership of the War of the Golden Stool to the massive, coordinated mobilization of the 1929 Women's War in southeastern Nigeria, which forced British colonial authorities to abandon plans to extend taxation to women and to reform aspects of the deeply resented warrant chief system. These episodes demonstrate that women were not marginal to African resistance but central to it, drawing on pre-colonial traditions of female political and economic authority that colonial rule frequently disrupted and that resistance movements sometimes sought explicitly to defend.

The chapter has not minimized the catastrophic human cost that resistance, and its suppression, exacted. The German campaign against the Herero and Nama peoples of South-West Africa between 1904 and 1908 stands as one of the most extreme examples of colonial violence in African history, a campaign of deliberate extermination that killed the majority of the pre-war Herero population and that the German government formally acknowledged as genocide in 2021. The suppression of the Maji Maji uprising, through deliberate destruction of food supplies across a wide territory, produced a famine that killed far more people than the military conflict itself. These episodes reveal the fundamentally coercive character that underlay colonial rule even during periods that colonial administrators described, in their own records, as peaceful or well-ordered.

Throughout the chapter, the concept of African agency has served as an organizing framework, emphasizing that Africans were active historical participants who made deliberate, strategic choices under extraordinarily constrained circumstances, rather than passive subjects who simply had history done to them. This framework does not require pretending that resistance always succeeded in its immediate objectives — the overwhelming majority of resistance movements examined in this chapter did not prevent colonial conquest or administration, at least in the near term. But it insists on recognizing the courage, creativity, and persistence Africans demonstrated across every form of struggle examined in this chapter, and on tracing the connections between this earlier resistance and the twentieth-century nationalist movements that would eventually achieve formal political independence.

Understanding this chapter's material is essential preparation for the chapters that follow, which examine the social history of colonial Africa, African participation in the two World Wars, and the rise of nationalist and Pan-African movements that built directly on the organizational strategies, cultural resources, and historical memories generated by the resistance movements examined here. The line between the resistance of 1896 and the independence movements of the 1950s and 1960s is neither simple nor direct, but it is real, and tracing it is central to understanding African history as a continuous story of struggle, adaptation, and eventual transformation.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter matters because it corrects a historical distortion that shaped how African history was told for much of the twentieth century: the assumption that colonial conquest simply happened to Africa, with African societies as passive recipients of European decisions. The evidence examined in this chapter tells a different story — one of sustained, creative, courageous, and varied resistance, waged through military campaigns, religious movements, diplomatic strategy, cultural preservation, and countless acts of everyday non-cooperation, across every region of the continent and throughout the entire colonial period.

This chapter's material shaped later African history in direct and traceable ways. The organizational networks, religious institutions, and cultural memories generated by early resistance movements did not disappear with their military defeat; they persisted, were transmitted across generations, and were explicitly drawn upon by the nationalist and Pan-African leaders examined in later chapters of this textbook, who understood themselves as continuing a struggle for African sovereignty and dignity that stretched back to Samori Ture, Yaa Asantewaa, the Herero and Nama dead, and the women of southeastern Nigeria who marched against colonial taxation in 1929.

The larger theme this chapter reveals is one that runs throughout this textbook: Africa's history cannot be understood as a story of things simply happening to Africans. It is a story of African individuals and communities making choices, however constrained, and shaping outcomes through those choices, even under conditions of extreme power imbalance and, in cases like the Herero and Nama genocide, unspeakable violence. Understanding African history requires holding both truths simultaneously — the reality of colonial power and violence, and the reality of African agency, resilience, and resistance.

Students who understand this chapter's material are well prepared to understand Africa as this textbook consistently presents it: diverse, interconnected, creative, contested, sometimes violent, always adaptive, and never finished. The resistance examined in this chapter did not end colonial rule. But it constrained colonial power, shaped colonial policy, preserved essential elements of African identity and institutions, and built the foundations — organizational, cultural, and symbolic — upon which the independence movements of the twentieth century would eventually build.

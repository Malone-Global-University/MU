Part IV — West Africa and the Great Sahelian Empires
25

Hausa City-States and Kanem-Bornu

Africa: A History of a Continent

Chapter Opening

"A walled city is not only a fortress. It is a market, a mosque, a court of law, a school, and a memory, all gathered behind the same earthen rampart."

Travelers crossing the southern Sahara in the fifteenth century did not arrive in an empty land waiting to be discovered. They arrived at the gates of cities — tall earthen walls pierced by named entrances, each gate leading to a different quarter, each quarter home to dyers, blacksmiths, leatherworkers, grain merchants, Quranic teachers, and royal officials. Kano, Katsina, Zaria, Gobir, Daura, and Rano were not isolated settlements but nodes in a vast web of trade and learning that stretched from the Mediterranean coast to the forests of the Niger Delta and from the Nile Valley to the Atlantic shore. To the east, around the shores and floodplains of Lake Chad, an even older state tradition had been building empires for centuries: Kanem, and later its successor Bornu, ruled by a single royal dynasty for what may be the longest continuous line of kingship recorded anywhere in African history.

This chapter studies two related but distinct political worlds of the central Sudan and the Sahel: the cluster of independent Hausa city-states, of which Kano and Katsina became the most famous, and the Kanem-Bornu Empire, centered for a thousand years on the Lake Chad basin. Both regions sat astride the trans-Saharan trade routes that linked West Africa to North Africa, the Mediterranean, and the wider Islamic world. Both built powerful walled cities, sophisticated systems of taxation and law, and rich traditions of Islamic scholarship. Both also developed distinctive political structures: the Hausa world organized into competing, intermarrying, and frequently warring city-states bound by shared language and culture but never unified into a single empire, while Kanem-Bornu built one of Africa's most durable centralized monarchies, the Sayfawa dynasty, which by some counts ruled for nearly a thousand years.

Why does this region matter in African history? Because the Hausa city-states and Kanem-Bornu demonstrate that statecraft, urbanism, commerce, and Islamic scholarship in Africa did not depend on the rise and fall of a single famous empire like Mali or Songhai. Political order, economic specialization, and intellectual life could also flourish in a landscape of multiple, smaller, competing states linked by shared trade networks, common religious institutions, and overlapping diplomatic and marriage ties. This is a different model of power than the sprawling imperial systems examined in earlier chapters, and it is no less significant for African history or world history.

What changed during the period this chapter covers, roughly the eleventh through the nineteenth centuries? Hausa towns grew from fortified agricultural settlements into major commercial and manufacturing centers exporting leather goods, woven cloth, and dyed textiles across West Africa and North Africa. Islam, introduced gradually through trade contacts beginning in the eleventh century, became deeply woven into ruling institutions, law, and urban culture, while never fully displacing older religious practices among rural populations. Kanem's center of gravity shifted southwest to Bornu after centuries of pressure from rival groups, and the Sayfawa dynasty reorganized its capital, army, and administration to maintain power for additional centuries. By the early nineteenth century, the Fulani-led Sokoto Caliphate would absorb most of the Hausa states into a new political order, a development examined more fully in a later chapter.

What stayed the same? The fundamental importance of trans-Saharan trade as the economic backbone of the region persisted across this entire period. The pattern of walled cities as centers of political, religious, and commercial life persisted as well, even as individual cities rose and declined in relative importance. And the practice of combining indigenous Hausa and Kanuri institutions of kingship with imported Islamic law and scholarship, rather than simply replacing one with the other, remained a defining feature of governance in both regions for centuries.

Who was affected by these developments? Merchants who carried salt, copper, cloth, leather, kola nuts, and enslaved captives across the desert and the savanna; farmers whose grain surpluses fed growing urban populations; craftspeople whose dyed cloth and tanned leather, known across North Africa and the Mediterranean as "Moroccan leather" even though much of it originated in Kano, became valuable export commodities; scholars who built mosques, schools, and libraries; rulers and royal courts who taxed trade and administered law; and the many people captured in war and absorbed into systems of slavery that fed both regional labor demands and trans-Saharan and, later, trans-Atlantic markets.

What evidence helps historians reconstruct this history? Arabic-language chronicles produced within the region itself, most famously the Kano Chronicle and the Diwan of the Sultans of Bornu, preserve king lists, accounts of major events, and traditions of origin, though they were compiled and revised over centuries and must be read critically. Accounts by North African and Middle Eastern travelers and geographers, including descriptions from Arab scholars who never set foot in the region but compiled reports from merchants, supplement local sources. Archaeological excavation of city walls, palace sites, and trade goods adds material evidence. Oral traditions, royal genealogies, and surviving manuscripts from centers of Islamic learning round out the picture. As with much of African history before sustained written documentation, no single source type is sufficient alone, and historians must weigh chronicle traditions, oral memory, and material evidence against one another.

Finally, how does this chapter connect to the larger story of Africa and the world? The Hausa city-states and Kanem-Bornu sat at a crossroads connecting the Mediterranean world, the Sahara, the West African forest zone, and the Nile Valley. Their merchants carried goods and ideas across these zones for centuries before European ships ever appeared off the West African coast, and their political and religious institutions would shape West African history well into the colonial era and beyond, including the nineteenth-century Sokoto Caliphate and the modern states of Nigeria, Niger, and Chad.

  3. Describe the role of trans-Saharan trade in shaping urban growth, taxation, and political power in both regions.
  4. Analyze how Islam spread among ruling elites and urban populations and how it was integrated with existing institutions of kingship and law.

Historical Context

The Hausa city-states and Kanem-Bornu emerged within the broader Sahelian world examined in previous chapters. To the west, the empires of Ghana, Mali, and Songhai dominated the upper and middle Niger River and controlled the gold-salt trade that linked West Africa to North Africa across the western Sahara. The central Sudan — the region encompassing what is today northern Nigeria, southern Niger, and the Lake Chad basin spanning Nigeria, Niger, Chad, and Cameroon — developed its own parallel trading and political systems, connected to but distinct from the western Sahelian empires.

Geography shaped this development decisively. The Hausa heartland occupies a region of savanna grassland suitable for grain agriculture — sorghum, millet, and later maize — combined with cotton cultivation that fed a thriving textile industry. The region lacked a single great river comparable to the Niger, but its relatively flat, well-watered plains supported dense agricultural populations and frequent, closely spaced towns. Lake Chad, to the east, created its own distinctive ecology: a vast shallow freshwater lake whose flood cycles supported fishing, rice and sorghum cultivation, and cattle herding along its shifting shorelines, while the surrounding Sahel offered the open terrain ideal for cavalry warfare that became central to Kanem-Bornu's military power.

Trans-Saharan trade routes connected this region to North Africa from at least the eighth or ninth century CE. The Garamantian and later trans-Saharan routes running north from Lake Chad through the Fezzan to Tripoli and other Mediterranean ports made Kanem one of the earliest sub-Saharan African states to establish sustained contact with the Islamic Mediterranean world. Arab geographers writing in the ninth and tenth centuries already described a kingdom called "Kanem" ruled by a people they called the Zaghawa, providing some of the earliest written external evidence for state formation in the central Sudan.

Religiously, Islam entered the region gradually and unevenly, carried by merchants, traveling scholars, and pilgrims rather than by conquest. The Sayfawa rulers of Kanem are traditionally said to have converted to Islam in the late eleventh century under the ruler Umme Jilmi, making Kanem-Bornu's ruling dynasty Muslim centuries before most of its rural subjects converted. In the Hausa states, Islam arrived later and more gradually, beginning in the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries through contact with Wangara (Mande) traders from the Mali Empire and scholars traveling the trans-Saharan routes, and Islamic practice among Hausa rulers and urban elites developed alongside, rather than immediately replacing, older traditions of sacred kingship and spirit-based religious practice known as Bori.

Politically, the central Sudan developed without the kind of single dominant empire that periodically unified the western Sahel. Instead, the region saw a long-running pattern of multiple competing states: the seven traditional "Hausa Bakwai" city-states said to descend from a common legendary ancestor, alongside the "Banza Bakwai," states considered more peripheral to the core Hausa tradition, all competing for control of trade routes, agricultural land, and tribute from weaker neighbors, while Kanem and then Bornu built a more centralized monarchy to the east, sometimes extending tributary influence over Hausa towns and sometimes facing challenges from them. This pattern of overlapping, competing, and shifting political units — rather than a single linear story of imperial rise and fall — is essential to understanding the region's history.

Section 25.1

The Hausa Urban World

The Hausa city-states occupied the savanna grasslands of what is today northern Nigeria and southern Niger. Unlike the river-based empires of Ghana, Mali, and Songhai, which organized around control of the Niger River and its trade, the Hausa world organized around independent walled cities, each known as a birni, surrounded by farmland and smaller satellite villages and ruled by its own king, or sarki. There was no single Hausa empire; rather, there existed a shared Hausa language, a shared body of origin traditions, overlapping royal marriages and alliances, and intense, often violent, competition for trade routes, farmland, and tribute-paying dependencies.

According to the most widely repeated origin tradition, recorded in chronicles such as the Kano Chronicle and in oral tradition across the region, the Hausa states descend from the sons of a legendary hero named Bayajidda, who arrived from the east, married a local queen, and whose sons and grandsons founded the seven core Hausa states, the Hausa Bakwai: Kano, Katsina, Zazzau, Gobir, Daura, Rano, and Biram. A second set of states, the Banza Bakwai, were said to be founded by Bayajidda's sons born to a concubine rather than his principal wife, and were considered less central to the Hausa origin story even though some, such as Kebbi and Zamfara, became politically powerful in their own right. Historians treat this origin tradition cautiously: it almost certainly reflects later efforts to explain and legitimize a political landscape that had already taken shape through more gradual processes of settlement, conquest, and alliance, rather than a literal historical account of a single founding figure.

Archaeological evidence suggests that fortified settlements in the Hausa region developed gradually over centuries before the rise of the city-states described in later chronicles. Walled towns provided defense against raiding, concentrated population for agricultural surplus collection, and central locations for markets and religious institutions. Kano's walls, rebuilt and extended many times, eventually enclosed an area of many square miles, with separate gates controlling entry from different directions and trade routes. Within the walls, cities were divided into quarters often organized by craft specialization, ethnicity, or religious function — a pattern common to many Sahelian and North African Islamic cities.

Kano emerged as the largest and most economically significant of the Hausa states by the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, though Katsina rivaled and at times surpassed it as a center of trans-Saharan trade and Islamic scholarship. Zazzau (Zaria) controlled important southern trade routes toward the forest zone and was notable for periods of rule by powerful queens, including the famous Amina of Zazzau, remembered in oral tradition as a warrior-queen who expanded the kingdom's territory and built fortified walls around conquered towns. Gobir, on the northern frontier, faced repeated conflict with Tuareg and other Saharan groups and later became a center of resistance to, and eventually opposition during, the Sokoto Jihad. Daura held particular ceremonial and symbolic importance as the site of the Bayajidda legend's central events.

The relationship between the Hausa states was rarely peaceful for long. Cities fought for control of trade routes, raided each other for captives and tribute, and shifted alliances as circumstances changed. A weaker state might pay tribute to a stronger neighbor for a period, then break free when the stronger state's power waned, then face renewed pressure later. This pattern of shifting, competitive, multi-polar political relations — rather than a single linear story of imperial unification — is one of the defining features of Hausa history and distinguishes it from the more centralized empires studied in earlier chapters.

Section 25.2

Trade Routes and Commercial Life

The economic foundation of Hausa power was trade, both regional and trans-Saharan. Hausa cities sat at the intersection of north-south routes connecting the Sahara and North Africa to the forest zone and the Niger-Benue river system, and east-west routes connecting the western Sahel to the Lake Chad basin and, beyond it, the Nile Valley and the Red Sea world. This geographic position allowed Hausa merchants to act as middlemen in long-distance exchange while also developing major local manufacturing industries that became famous across an enormous trading area.

Kano in particular became renowned for two related industries: leatherworking and textile dyeing. Hausa craftsmen tanned and worked leather into sandals, bags, sheaths, and decorative goods that were exported north across the Sahara in such quantities that European traders in North Africa and the Mediterranean often labeled this leather "Moroccan leather," obscuring its true sub-Saharan origin. Indigo dyeing, centered on large dye pits still visible in old Kano today, produced deep blue cloth prized across West Africa, North Africa, and beyond. Cotton, grown extensively in the well-watered savanna around Hausa cities, supplied the raw material for a substantial weaving industry that produced cloth for both local use and long-distance export.

Caravan trade connected Hausa cities to North African termini such as Tripoli, Ghadames, and Murzuk, and to other West African trading centers including Timbuktu, Gao, and the Yoruba and Akan trading networks to the south. Caravans carried Saharan salt, North African manufactured goods, books, and horses southward, and carried Hausa leather goods, cloth, kola nuts obtained from the forest zone, ivory, and enslaved captives northward and eastward. The kola nut trade was especially significant: kola, a mild stimulant chewed widely across the Muslim Sahel where alcohol was religiously prohibited, was sourced from forest-zone producers far to the south and distributed northward through Hausa commercial networks, making Hausa merchants essential intermediaries between very different ecological and cultural zones.

Markets within Hausa cities were highly organized, often supervised by officials responsible for weights, measures, and tax collection, and segmented by commodity, with separate sections for grain, livestock, cloth, leather goods, and slaves. Long-distance merchants, including itinerant Wangara and later Hausa traders called fatake, organized caravans that might take months to travel between major trading centers, requiring complex systems of credit, hospitality networks, and trusted local agents in distant cities — a commercial infrastructure not unlike that described in earlier chapters covering trans-Saharan trade in the western Sahel.

Section 25.3

Political Organization in the City-States

Each Hausa city-state was ruled by a sarki, supported by a council of senior officials, court titleholders, and, after the spread of Islam, religious scholars and judges. Political authority combined indigenous Hausa concepts of sacred kingship, in which the sarki was understood to hold spiritual responsibility for the prosperity and protection of his people, with imported Islamic concepts of just rule under sharia law. Royal courts developed elaborate systems of titled offices, many surviving in modified form into the twentieth century, through which the sarki delegated authority over taxation, military mobilization, judicial matters, and relations with neighboring towns and villages.

Succession to the throne within Hausa states was often contested, with multiple eligible candidates from different branches of the royal lineage competing for support among officials, religious leaders, and sometimes external powers. This pattern of competitive succession, while sometimes destabilizing, also distributed political influence among multiple lineages and offices rather than concentrating power in a single unbroken line, a contrast to the dynastic continuity that characterized the Sayfawa rulers of Kanem-Bornu examined later in this chapter.

Taxation provided the fiscal foundation of Hausa state power. Rulers taxed agricultural production, craft production, and trade passing through or originating in their territory, while also extracting tribute from weaker dependent towns and villages. Fief-holding officials, granted rights to collect revenue from specific territories in exchange for military and administrative service, formed an important layer of provincial administration, somewhat comparable to systems found in other Sahelian states and in parts of the medieval Islamic world more broadly.

The reign of Sarkin Kano Muhammad Rumfa in the late fifteenth century is often identified by historians as a turning point in Hausa state organization. Rumfa is credited with introducing a more formalized council system, strengthening the role of Islamic legal scholars at court, reorganizing the royal palace and its ceremonial functions, and encouraging closer ties with North African and other Hausa centers of Islamic scholarship. These reforms, recorded in the Kano Chronicle, illustrate how individual rulers could reshape political institutions even within a system not organized around a single unifying empire.

Section 25.4

Islam and Urban Culture

Islam entered the Hausa states gradually, beginning in the fourteenth century, carried primarily by Wangara merchant-scholars from the Mali Empire rather than by military conquest. Initial conversion was concentrated among ruling elites and urban merchant communities who benefited from the literacy, legal frameworks, and trans-regional commercial and scholarly connections that Islam provided. Rural agricultural populations, by contrast, often continued practicing older religious traditions, including the spirit-possession tradition known as Bori, for centuries longer, and elements of Bori practice persisted even within nominally Muslim urban communities, illustrating a pattern of religious layering rather than sudden, total replacement.

Islamic law, or sharia, gradually became integrated into Hausa governance, particularly under reform-minded rulers such as Muhammad Rumfa, who appointed qadis (Islamic judges) and supported the construction of mosques and schools. Islamic scholarship flourished in Kano and especially in Katsina, which developed a reputation rivaling parts of the western Sahel as a center of learning, attracting scholars and students from across the region. Quranic schools taught literacy in Arabic script, religious law, and theology, producing a class of educated scribes, judges, and advisors essential to court administration and to the conduct of long-distance trade, which depended heavily on written contracts, correspondence, and credit arrangements.

Urban culture in Hausa cities blended Islamic and indigenous Hausa elements in architecture, dress, ceremony, and social organization. Mosques were typically built using the same vernacular mudbrick and adobe construction techniques used for palaces and ordinary housing, producing a distinctive regional architectural style rather than imported Middle Eastern or North African forms. Royal ceremony combined Islamic titles and Friday prayer observances with older Hausa rituals of kingship, including praise-singing, drumming, and symbols of sacred authority that predated Islamic influence.

Section 25.5

Kanem-Bornu Origins

East of the Hausa states, around the Lake Chad basin, a different and in some ways even older political tradition developed: the kingdom of Kanem, ruled from at least the ninth century by a dynasty later known as the Sayfawa. Arab geographers writing in the ninth and tenth centuries already referred to a kingdom in this region, associating it with a people called the Zaghawa, providing some of the earliest written external evidence for organized statehood anywhere in the central Sudan.

The Sayfawa dynasty traced its origins through a royal genealogy recorded in the Diwan of the Sultans of Bornu, claiming descent from a legendary figure named Sayf ibn Dhi Yazan, linked in tradition to ancient Yemen, though historians regard this genealogical claim as a later legitimizing tradition rather than literal history, similar to many royal origin traditions across medieval Africa and the wider Islamic world that linked African dynasties to prestigious Arabian or Middle Eastern ancestors. What is better documented is the dynasty's long, largely continuous rule, recorded across an unusually complete and lengthy king list, making the Sayfawa one of the longest-reigning documented dynasties anywhere in world history, with a span sometimes estimated at close to a thousand years.

Kanem's early capital, Njimi, lay northeast of Lake Chad, positioning the kingdom to control trans-Saharan trade routes running north through the Fezzan toward Tripoli. The traditional account credits the ruler Umme Jilmi with converting to Islam in the late eleventh century, after which Islam became the official religion of the Sayfawa court, even as many subjects in the kingdom's diverse population, including various pastoralist and agricultural groups, continued practicing other religious traditions for generations.

Kanem's power and territorial extent fluctuated over the following centuries. The kingdom faced sustained pressure from a rival group known as the Bulala beginning in the fourteenth century, eventually forcing the Sayfawa dynasty to relocate its capital southwest to the Bornu region on the western and southern shores of Lake Chad, a process completed by the early fifteenth century. This relocation, while reflecting a loss of the original Kanem heartland, did not end Sayfawa rule; instead, the dynasty reestablished itself in Bornu and over subsequent centuries rebuilt and eventually expanded its power from this new base, illustrating both significant geographic and political change and remarkable institutional continuity within the same ruling lineage.

Section 25.6

Kingship and State Power

The ruler of Kanem and later Bornu held the title mai, a position combining political authority, religious legitimacy as a Muslim sovereign, and elements of sacred kingship rooted in older Kanuri political tradition. The mai presided over an elaborate court hierarchy of titled officials, including military commanders, provincial governors, and religious advisors, many of whose titles and functions are recorded in the Diwan and in later European travelers' accounts.

Bornu's most celebrated ruler, Mai Idris Alooma, who reigned from approximately 1571 to 1603, is credited by chroniclers and later historians with a series of major reforms that strengthened centralized state power. He reorganized the kingdom's military forces, strengthened Islamic legal institutions by appointing qadis to apply sharia more consistently across the realm, built new fortified settlements, and expanded Bornu's territorial influence over neighboring peoples through both military campaigns and diplomatic alliance. Idris Alooma's reign is often treated by historians as the high point of Bornu's regional power, comparable to the way historians treat the reigns of Mansa Musa in Mali or Askia Muhammad in Songhai as high points for those empires.

Royal succession in the Sayfawa dynasty, unlike in the Hausa city-states, maintained a remarkable degree of continuity within a single lineage across many centuries, even as individual succession disputes, regencies, and periods of weak central rulers occurred. Historians attribute this durability partly to the dynasty's success in presenting itself as a stable, religiously legitimate institution whose continuity itself became a source of political authority, and partly to the kingdom's administrative flexibility in incorporating defeated rivals and tributary populations into a layered system of provincial governance rather than attempting total centralized control over a vast and ecologically diverse territory.

Section 25.7

Military Strength and Diplomacy

Bornu's military power rested on several pillars: a substantial cavalry force suited to the open Sahelian terrain around Lake Chad, fortified garrison towns that projected royal authority into outlying provinces, and, from the late sixteenth century onward, the adoption of firearms and Ottoman-trained musketeers, acquired through trans-Saharan diplomatic and commercial contacts with North Africa. Mai Idris Alooma is particularly credited with modernizing Bornu's army along these lines, including the use of a standing professional military core supplementing levies raised from tributary provinces.

Diplomatically, Bornu maintained relations with Ottoman Tripoli and other North African powers, exchanging embassies, gifts, and religious scholars, and participating in the wider diplomatic and commercial world of the Islamic Mediterranean and Sahara. These connections gave Bornu access to firearms, horses, and religious legitimacy through contact with the broader Muslim world, while also exposing the kingdom to the political fluctuations of North African power struggles, including periods when Ottoman, Moroccan, or local Saharan powers competed for influence over the desert trade routes that connected Bornu to the Mediterranean.

Bornu's relations with the Hausa states to the west alternated between periods of tributary domination, military conflict, and relative independence, depending on the relative strength of Bornu's central government and the cohesion or fragmentation of individual Hausa cities at any given moment. At various points, Hausa states such as Kano paid tribute to Bornu; at other points, Bornu's attention was absorbed by conflicts with the Bulala, the Tuareg, or other regional powers, allowing Hausa cities greater independence. This fluctuating relationship illustrates the broader pattern of shifting, multipolar power balances that characterized the central Sudan across these centuries, rather than a single stable hierarchy of dominance.

Section 25.8

Scholarship and Religious Life

Both the Hausa city-states and Kanem-Bornu developed significant traditions of Islamic scholarship, though on a somewhat smaller scale than the renowned centers of Timbuktu examined in an earlier chapter. Katsina, in particular, became known across the central Sudan and beyond as a center of Quranic and legal education, drawing scholars and students from a wide region and producing locally trained jurists, teachers, and manuscript copyists. Kano similarly developed networks of Islamic schools, supported by wealthy merchant patrons whose trans-Saharan trade profits funded mosque construction, scholarships for students, and the copying and preservation of Arabic-language manuscripts.

Bornu's scholarly tradition was closely tied to the royal court, with the mai's patronage supporting Islamic scholars who served simultaneously as religious authorities, legal advisors, and diplomatic envoys to North African powers. The Diwan of the Sultans of Bornu itself is a product of this scholarly tradition, maintained and updated by court scribes over many generations to preserve dynastic memory and legitimacy.

Religious life across the region remained layered rather than uniformly Islamic. Among rural populations, and even within some urban communities, older traditions persisted alongside Islamic practice. In the Hausa context, the Bori spirit-possession tradition, involving ceremonies addressing a pantheon of spirits associated with health, fertility, weather, and social order, continued to be practiced by many Hausa communities, including by individuals who also identified as Muslim, illustrating the complex and often non-exclusive ways religious identity operated in practice, in contrast to the more formal theological boundaries described in some chronicle accounts written by and for the Muslim scholarly elite.

Section 25.9

Sahelian Networks and Influence

The Hausa city-states and Kanem-Bornu did not develop in isolation. They were deeply connected to the broader Sahelian and trans-Saharan world examined throughout Part IV of this textbook. Hausa merchants traded with, and sometimes settled permanently within, communities as far west as Timbuktu and as far north as Ghadames and Tripoli. Bornu's diplomatic contacts extended to Ottoman Tripoli, to Mamluk and later Ottoman Egypt via pilgrimage routes to Mecca, and to other Sahelian states including, at various points, Songhai.

This regional network carried more than goods. It carried scholars, religious texts, architectural styles, political ideas about Islamic kingship, and enslaved people moving along well-established routes toward markets in North Africa and the Mediterranean. The same caravan routes that carried Kano's leather goods northward also carried captives taken in regional warfare, a grim but historically essential dimension of the trans-Saharan trading system examined in greater depth in the chapters on slavery in African history.

By the eighteenth century, the Hausa states had become so economically significant within this network that their commercial reach extended deep into the forest zone to the south and across the central Sudan to the east, making cities like Kano genuinely cosmopolitan commercial centers comparable in function, if not always in scale, to other major Sahelian trading cities studied earlier in this textbook.

Historical Vocabulary
Birni

A birni is the Hausa term for a major walled city, typically the political and religious capital of a Hausa state, surrounded by extensive earthen fortifications that could enclose farmland as well as densely built residential and market quarters. The birni functioned simultaneously as a military stronghold, a religious center containing the city's principal mosque and palace, and an economic hub housing specialized craft quarters and major markets. Kano's walls, among the most extensive of any Hausa birni, were rebuilt and expanded across several centuries and remained a defining feature of the city into the colonial period. Understanding the birni helps students see that Hausa political power was fundamentally urban in character, organized around defensible, economically self-sufficient cities rather than around control of an undefended agricultural countryside alone.

Historical Vocabulary
Zakat and Jizya

Zakat is the obligatory almsgiving required of Muslims under Islamic law, traditionally calculated as a portion of wealth or produce and distributed to the poor and other specified categories of recipients. Jizya is a tax historically levied on non-Muslim subjects living under Muslim rule in exchange for protection and exemption from military service. In Hausa and Bornu administration, the adoption of these Islamic fiscal categories, alongside older indigenous tribute and tax systems, illustrates how Islamic law was layered onto, rather than simply replacing, existing systems of taxation and political obligation. Rulers who wished to be seen as legitimate Muslim sovereigns needed to demonstrate proper administration of zakat and, where applicable, jizya, even as they continued collecting other forms of tribute and tax rooted in pre-Islamic political custom.

Primary Source Window

The Diwan of the Sultans of Bornu

Creator: Court scribes of the Sayfawa dynasty, compiled and updated across many generations · Date: Composed and revised over several centuries, with the surviving form likely reflecting later compilation · Type: Royal Arabic-language chronicle and king list

Context: The Diwan records the succession of Sayfawa rulers of Kanem and Bornu, along with brief notes on major events, conquests, and religious developments associated with particular reigns. It was maintained by court scribes as an official record of dynastic legitimacy and historical memory, intended primarily for use within the royal court and the kingdom's religious and administrative elite rather than for a general public readership.

The chronicle records, reign by reign, the names of rulers, the lengths of their reigns, and brief notices of notable events such as conversions, military campaigns, and the construction of mosques and fortifications, presenting the dynasty's history as a continuous, divinely sanctioned succession from one ruler to the next.

Analysis: The Diwan is an extraordinarily valuable source because it preserves an indigenous, centuries-long king list largely independent of external European or North African testimony, allowing historians to reconstruct Sayfawa chronology with more confidence than is possible for many other African dynasties of comparable age. However, like all chronicle traditions produced by and for a royal court, it emphasizes continuity, legitimacy, and the achievements of rulers while minimizing internal conflict, succession disputes, popular grievances, and the perspectives of non-elite subjects. Modern historians cross-reference the Diwan against Arab geographic accounts, archaeological evidence, and oral tradition to evaluate its reliability and fill in its silences.

Historical Debate

How Should Historians Interpret the Bayajidda Origin Tradition?

The question: The widely repeated tradition that the seven core Hausa states descend from sons of a single hero, Bayajidda, who arrived from the east and married a local queen, appears across multiple Hausa chronicle and oral traditions. How should historians interpret this story?

Interpretation A — The Tradition Reflects a Literal Founding Event: Some scholars and many Hausa oral traditions treat the Bayajidda story as preserving genuine historical memory of an actual founding figure or migration event, perhaps reflecting contact with or migration from groups further east, such as Borno or beyond, whose influence helped establish or reorganize political authority in the Hausa region during an early formative period.
Interpretation B — The Tradition Functions as Political and Religious Legitimation: Other historians argue that the Bayajidda story, like many royal origin traditions across Africa and the wider medieval world, was constructed or substantially elaborated later to explain and justify an already-existing political landscape, to link Hausa rulers to a prestigious shared ancestor, and to rank the various Hausa and Banza states in a hierarchy of legitimacy, rather than recording an actual historical migration.

Evidence: Supporters of Interpretation A point to the wide and largely consistent distribution of the story across independent Hausa oral traditions and its appearance in chronicle sources, arguing such consistency suggests an underlying historical kernel. Supporters of Interpretation B point to the story's clear legitimizing function, its similarity to origin legends found in many other African and Eurasian dynastic traditions, and the absence of independent corroborating evidence such as archaeological data tied specifically to the legend's events.

Why it matters: How historians interpret this tradition shapes how they understand the relationship between Hausa city-states: whether as states with a genuine common origin and inherent ties of kinship, or as independently developing polities that later constructed a shared mythology to manage their political relationships and rivalries.

Student task: Based on the evidence described above, which interpretation do you find more persuasive, and why? What kind of additional evidence, if discovered, might help resolve this debate?

Multiple Perspectives

Trans-Saharan Caravan Trade Through Hausa Territory

From the perspective of a Hausa merchant: Trans-Saharan trade was the foundation of personal and civic prosperity, offering opportunities for wealth, social status, and religious prestige through pilgrimage and patronage of Islamic scholarship funded by trading profits.
From the perspective of a North African trading partner: Hausa cities represented a reliable, well-organized source of valuable leather goods, cloth, and other commodities, accessed through established commercial relationships that depended on trust, credit, and stable political conditions in the central Sudan.
From the perspective of an enslaved captive transported along caravan routes: The same trade networks that enriched merchants and rulers represented violent capture, forced separation from family and community, and a perilous, often deadly journey across the Sahara toward distant and unfamiliar markets, with no control over one's own fate.
From the perspective of a Hausa ruler: Control over trade routes and the taxation of caravan traffic provided essential revenue for maintaining the court, the army, and religious institutions, making trade not simply an economic activity but a foundation of political power and legitimacy.
Cause and Effect

The Rise of Bornu Under Mai Idris Alooma

Long-Term Causes

Centuries of accumulated Sayfawa dynastic legitimacy and administrative experience; established trans-Saharan trade and diplomatic connections to North Africa; prior relocation and consolidation of the dynasty's base in Bornu after losing Kanem to the Bulala.

Short-Term Causes

Idris Alooma's personal military and administrative reforms; acquisition of firearms and Ottoman-trained musketeers through North African contacts; construction of new fortified settlements to project royal authority.

The Event

Bornu's territorial expansion and consolidation of regional dominance over much of the Lake Chad basin and parts of the Hausa world during Idris Alooma's reign, approximately 1571 to 1603.

Effects

Strengthened Islamic legal institutions across the realm; increased tribute extraction from neighboring states; a lasting reputation, recorded in the Diwan, as one of the dynasty's most successful rulers; temporary reassertion of Bornu's regional primacy over parts of the Hausa world.

Historical complexity: Bornu's rise under Idris Alooma cannot be reduced to one ruler's individual brilliance alone. It depended on centuries of prior institutional development, existing trade and diplomatic networks, and a particular set of military and technological opportunities available at that specific historical moment.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Hausa and Kanem-Bornu Statecraft, c. 1000–1800

What Changed

  • Islam moved from a minority elite religion to a central institution of law and court legitimacy in both regions
  • Kanem's territorial base shifted permanently from Njimi to Bornu
  • Hausa cities grew from fortified agricultural settlements into major manufacturing and trading centers
  • Military technology evolved from cavalry-centered warfare to include firearms in Bornu by the late sixteenth century
  • Relative power between individual Hausa states and Bornu fluctuated repeatedly across centuries

What Stayed the Same

  • The fundamental economic importance of trans-Saharan trade across the entire period
  • The walled city as the basic unit of Hausa political and commercial organization
  • Sayfawa dynastic continuity in Kanem-Bornu despite territorial loss and relocation
  • The layering of Islamic institutions onto, rather than complete replacement of, indigenous political and religious traditions
  • The absence of a single unifying empire across the Hausa region before the nineteenth century

Why the pattern matters: Recognizing both change and continuity prevents oversimplified narratives that treat this region either as static and unchanging or as a simple story of steady linear progress. Political fragmentation among the Hausa states persisted for centuries even as individual cities and their economies transformed dramatically, while Kanem-Bornu's dynastic continuity persisted even through major geographic and political upheaval.

Everyday Life and Social History

Life Inside the Walled City

Behind the political and commercial history of Kano, Katsina, and Bornu's capitals lay the daily lives of farmers, craftspeople, traders, students, enslaved laborers, and royal household members. Most residents of a Hausa birni were not merchants engaged in long-distance trade but farmers who worked fields inside and around the city walls, growing sorghum, millet, and cotton, and craftspeople, including dyers, weavers, leatherworkers, blacksmiths, and potters, whose specialized quarters gave Hausa cities much of their economic character.

Family life centered on extended household compounds, often organized around a senior male head of household with multiple wives and children, reflecting both older Hausa kinship patterns and, increasingly from the fourteenth century onward, Islamic norms regarding marriage and household structure. Women played essential roles in craft production, particularly in spinning cotton thread and in food processing and trading within local markets, even as formal political and religious authority remained predominantly male, with notable exceptions such as the warrior-queen Amina of Zazzau remembered in oral tradition.

Education for boys from merchant and elite families often began with Quranic schooling, where students known as almajirai studied under a local teacher, memorizing portions of the Quran and learning Arabic literacy, sometimes traveling to study under renowned scholars in Katsina or other centers of learning. Slavery formed an integral, if troubling, part of urban social structure: enslaved individuals, captured in regional warfare or raiding or transported via trans-Saharan networks, worked in households, on agricultural estates, and in craft production, while a smaller number of enslaved people, particularly those who had achieved positions of trust, could rise to influential roles within royal courts and military commands, illustrating the complex and often brutal range of conditions encompassed by slavery in this context.

Religious and ceremonial life punctuated the calendar through Islamic observances such as Friday communal prayer and the annual fasting month, alongside older agricultural and life-cycle ceremonies, and, for many Hausa communities, Bori spirit ceremonies addressing health, fertility, and social concerns that operated alongside, rather than in place of, Islamic religious practice.

Section 25.10

Chapter Review and Lake Chad Basin Checkpoint

This chapter has examined two related but distinct political traditions of the central Sudan: the competitive, multi-polar world of the Hausa city-states, and the long-lived centralized monarchy of Kanem-Bornu under the Sayfawa dynasty. Both regions were shaped decisively by trans-Saharan trade, by the gradual and layered spread of Islam, and by the central importance of fortified cities as units of political, economic, and religious organization.

Students should now understand why the Hausa region never unified into a single empire before the nineteenth-century Sokoto Jihad, how Kano and Katsina became major manufacturing and scholarly centers, how the Sayfawa dynasty maintained power across nearly a thousand years despite a major territorial relocation, and how chronicle sources like the Kano Chronicle and the Diwan of Bornu provide valuable but limited windows into this history.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has examined the Hausa city-states and the Kanem-Bornu Empire as two distinctive but interconnected political and economic worlds of the central Sudan and the Lake Chad basin. Unlike the large unified empires of Ghana, Mali, and Songhai studied in earlier chapters, the Hausa region developed as a cluster of independent, competing walled city-states bound together by shared language, culture, and overlapping origin traditions, but never unified under a single ruler before the nineteenth century.

Kano and Katsina emerged as the most economically and intellectually significant of these city-states, developing major leatherworking, indigo-dyeing, and textile industries whose products traveled across the Sahara to North Africa and the Mediterranean, while also becoming centers of Islamic scholarship that attracted students and teachers from across the region. The Bayajidda origin tradition, recorded in chronicles such as the Kano Chronicle, helped explain and legitimize the relationships among the various Hausa Bakwai and Banza Bakwai states, even though historians treat its literal historical accuracy with appropriate caution.

East of the Hausa world, the kingdom of Kanem, ruled by the Sayfawa dynasty from at least the ninth century, developed one of the longest continuous dynastic traditions documented anywhere in African or world history. Converting to Islam in the late eleventh century under Umme Jilmi, the Sayfawa rulers built a powerful state controlling trans-Saharan trade routes running north from Lake Chad toward the Mediterranean. Pressure from the rival Bulala eventually forced the dynasty to relocate its capital southwest into the Bornu region by the early fifteenth century, a major territorial setback that nonetheless did not end Sayfawa rule.

Bornu's power reached its height under Mai Idris Alooma in the late sixteenth century, who modernized the kingdom's military with firearms and a more professional standing army, strengthened Islamic legal institutions, and expanded Bornu's influence over neighboring peoples, including at times the Hausa states to the west. This reign illustrates how individual rulers could reshape state power even within long-established institutional traditions, while also depending on centuries of accumulated dynastic legitimacy and trade connections that made such reforms possible.

Both regions integrated Islamic law, scholarship, and political legitimacy into existing structures of kingship and administration, rather than simply replacing indigenous institutions wholesale. Sacred kingship traditions, tribute systems, and, in the Hausa case, the persistence of the Bori spirit-possession tradition alongside Islamic practice, all illustrate a pattern of religious and institutional layering rather than total transformation.

Trade was the economic lifeblood of both regions throughout this period. Hausa merchants exported leather goods, dyed cloth, and kola nuts obtained from the forest zone, while importing salt, horses, and manufactured goods from North Africa. Bornu's trans-Saharan connections gave its rulers access to firearms, diplomatic relationships with Ottoman Tripoli, and participation in the wider commercial and religious world of the Islamic Mediterranean.

This same trading system, however, also carried a darker dimension: enslaved captives taken in regional warfare and raiding moved along the same caravan routes that carried leather, cloth, and salt, feeding both regional labor demands and external markets in North Africa and the Mediterranean. Slavery shaped urban household structure, agricultural labor, and even, in some cases, pathways to influence within royal courts and armies, illustrating the complex and often troubling range of conditions slavery could encompass in this context.

Everyday life inside the walled cities of Kano, Katsina, and Bornu's capitals involved farming, craft production, Quranic education, household management, and religious observance that combined Islamic and older indigenous traditions. Women contributed essential labor in craft production and market trade even as formal political authority remained predominantly, though not exclusively, male, as illustrated by the remembered example of Queen Amina of Zazzau.

By the eighteenth century, internal tensions within the Hausa states, including recurring warfare and social inequality, created conditions that would culminate in the early nineteenth-century Sokoto Jihad led by Usman dan Fodio, which overturned most Hausa ruling dynasties and absorbed much of the region into a new caliphate, examined in a later chapter. Bornu survived this upheaval but lost its position of regional dominance, eventually passing from direct Sayfawa rule to a new dynasty.

The history of the Hausa city-states and Kanem-Bornu demonstrates that African statecraft took multiple successful forms: not only the large unified empire but also the durable competitive city-state system, and not only the rapid rise and fall of dynasties but also nearly a millennium of continuous royal succession. Both models produced sophisticated administration, flourishing trade, rich scholarly traditions, and enduring cultural legacies that continued to shape the region long after the political structures examined in this chapter gave way to new forms of rule.

  • Prompt 2 (Comparative): Compare the political structures of the Hausa city-states and the Kanem-Bornu Empire. Discuss succession, taxation, military organization, and the integration of Islamic law, using specific examples from the chapter.
  • Prompt 3 (Historical argument using evidence): Using evidence from the chapter, argue whether trans-Saharan trade or the spread of Islam was the more decisive force shaping the development of Hausa and Bornu political institutions between the eleventh and seventeenth centuries.
  • Prompt 4 (Connecting past and present): Many cities discussed in this chapter, including Kano, remain major urban centers in modern Nigeria. Research one connection between the historical role of Kano described in this chapter and its role in the region today, and analyze what has changed and what has persisted.
  • Prompt 5 (Causation): Choose either the Sayfawa relocation from Kanem to Bornu or the rise of Bornu under Mai Idris Alooma, and write an essay distinguishing short-term and long-term causes, explaining why reducing either development to a single cause would be insufficient.

Research or Library Extension

MGU Library Research Project

Building a Sayfawa Dynastic Timeline

This project asks you to research the long history of the Sayfawa dynasty of Kanem and Bornu and construct a detailed timeline tracing its development, challenges, and adaptations across nearly a thousand years.

  1. Research the dynasty's origins. Using library resources and reliable secondary sources, research what historians know about the early kingdom of Kanem and the earliest references to it in Arab geographic sources.
  2. Trace the conversion to Islam. Research the traditional account of Umme Jilmi's conversion and what historians can and cannot confirm about this event and its consequences.
  3. Investigate the relocation to Bornu. Research the pressures from the Bulala and the process by which the Sayfawa dynasty reestablished itself in Bornu.
  4. Examine the reign of Mai Idris Alooma. Research his military, administrative, and religious reforms and evaluate their long-term significance.
  5. Research the dynasty's end. Investigate how the Sokoto Jihad and subsequent developments affected Sayfawa rule in the nineteenth century.
  6. Construct your timeline. Build a detailed annotated timeline covering at least ten major events or developments, with brief explanations of significance for each, and a concluding paragraph reflecting on what the dynasty's history reveals about durability and adaptation in African statecraft.

Library resources to use: The MGU History Library's reference materials on West African and Sahelian states, scholarly works on Kanem-Bornu and Hausa history, translated excerpts from the Diwan of the Sultans of Bornu, and the Kano Chronicle.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter matters because it complicates any simple narrative in which African political history is told only through the rise and fall of a few famous empires. The Hausa city-states show that political fragmentation did not mean weakness, poverty, or historical insignificance; quite the opposite, the Hausa world produced some of West Africa's most economically dynamic and intellectually rich urban centers precisely while remaining politically divided among multiple competing states. Kanem-Bornu, meanwhile, shows that a single dynasty could maintain power across an extraordinary span of time through adaptation rather than uninterrupted dominance, surviving a major territorial loss and rebuilding itself in a new location while preserving its essential institutional identity.

This chapter shaped later African history in concrete ways. The commercial networks, urban traditions, and Islamic scholarly institutions built by the Hausa states and Bornu across these centuries did not disappear; they were absorbed, transformed, and in many respects intensified under the nineteenth-century Sokoto Caliphate, and they continue to shape the religious, cultural, and economic life of northern Nigeria, Niger, and the Lake Chad region today. Understanding this history helps explain the deep historical roots of cities like Kano that remain major centers of trade, manufacturing, and Islamic scholarship in West Africa in the present day.

The larger themes this chapter reveals connect directly to the wider purposes of this textbook. Africa's history cannot be told only through a handful of famous unified empires; it must also include durable systems of competing city-states, long-lived dynasties that survived major setbacks, and complex patterns of religious change in which new traditions layered onto rather than simply erased older ones. The presence of slavery within these societies, alongside genuine scholarly, commercial, and architectural achievement, reminds students that African history, like all history, contains both achievement and exploitation, often within the same institutions and the same period.

Finally, this chapter helps students understand Africa as a continent of diverse, interconnected, and adaptive political traditions rather than a single undifferentiated story. The Hausa city-states and Kanem-Bornu were neither simply victims of later colonial conquest nor simply glorious precursors to be celebrated without complexity; they were dynamic, contested, evolving societies that built lasting institutions, weathered serious setbacks, absorbed and reshaped new religious and commercial influences, and left a legacy that remains visible in the cities, scholarship, and trade networks of the central Sudan today.

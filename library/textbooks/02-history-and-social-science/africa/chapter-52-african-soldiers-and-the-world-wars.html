Part VIII — Colonialism, Conquest, and Resistance
52

African Soldiers and the World Wars

Africa: A History of a Continent

Chapter Opening

"We were told we were fighting for liberty, for the rights of small nations, for civilization itself. We fought. Many of us died. And when it was over, we returned to colonies that had not changed."

In the late summer of 1914, in villages across French West Africa, British East Africa, German Togoland, Belgian Congo, and Portuguese Mozambique, recruiting officers arrived with orders from distant capitals. Few of the men who received these orders had ever seen Europe. Fewer still understood why Britain, France, Germany, and their allies were suddenly at war with one another, or how a dispute among European monarchs and diplomats over an assassination in Sarajevo had anything to do with the rivers, farms, and families of Senegal, Nigeria, Kenya, or the Gold Coast. Yet within weeks, African men were being conscripted, recruited, coerced, and in some cases volunteered into the armies and labor corps of European empires, and African soil itself had become a battlefield. By the time the fighting ended in 1918, hundreds of thousands of African soldiers had served in Europe, the Middle East, and within Africa, and hundreds of thousands more African porters and laborers had carried the physical burden of a war fought largely for reasons that had nothing to do with African interests.

This chapter examines Africa's involvement in the First and Second World Wars — two conflicts that, though centered in Europe and later spreading across the globe, drew deeply on African manpower, African resources, and African territory. It asks how and why colonial powers mobilized African soldiers and laborers, what African participants experienced during the fighting, how the wars reshaped African economies and societies, and — crucially — how the experience of global war reshaped African political consciousness. The world wars were not simply European events that happened to touch Africa. They were global events in which Africa was a full participant, whose contributions were indispensable to the outcome and whose long-term consequences for African history were profound.

Understanding this chapter requires holding two realities together. The first is that African participation in the world wars was frequently coerced, exploitative, and deadly, carried out within colonial systems that denied Africans political rights, decent wages, and often basic dignity. Conscription could be violent. Labor recruitment for military transport, particularly in East Africa during the First World War, killed tens of thousands of African carriers through disease, exhaustion, and neglect — a toll historians increasingly recognize as one of the least remembered mass casualties of the war. The second reality is that African soldiers, despite the injustice of the systems that sent them to war, served with skill, courage, and endurance, and that their service — and the injustices that accompanied it — helped produce a generation of veterans, workers, and colonial subjects who returned home with new experiences, new expectations, and new networks that would feed directly into the nationalist and Pan-African movements examined in Part IX of this textbook.

The two world wars were separated by more than two decades, fought under different circumstances, and had different consequences for Africa. World War I was fought partly within Africa itself, as the colonial possessions of the belligerent powers became targets and battlegrounds — most dramatically in German East Africa, where a small German-led force under Paul von Lettow-Vorbeck waged a prolonged guerrilla campaign that devastated the countryside and consumed enormous numbers of African porters and soldiers on both sides. World War II drew African soldiers into a much wider geographic theater — North Africa, East Africa, the Horn, the Mediterranean, Burma, and Western Europe — and coincided with a global ideological struggle against fascism that placed the language of freedom, self-determination, and human dignity at the center of Allied propaganda, a language that African soldiers and civilians did not fail to notice was rarely extended to themselves.

This chapter also asks students to consider the wartime economy and society that developed on the African continent even for those who never held a rifle. Colonial administrations imposed new taxes, requisitioned crops and livestock, expanded forced labor, and redirected African production toward the war effort. Women took on new economic and household burdens as men were conscripted or migrated for wartime labor. Prices rose, goods became scarce, and the daily rhythms of rural and urban life were disrupted in ways that touched nearly every African community, whether or not that community sent soldiers to the front.

Finally, this chapter traces how the experience of global war became a turning point in African political history. Veterans who had fought for empires that promised freedom and democracy returned home to colonial systems that offered neither. Many veterans organized, protested, and became leaders of postwar political movements. The economic strain of war weakened colonial administrations and exposed the fragility of empires that had claimed permanence. International wartime rhetoric — the Atlantic Charter's promise of self-determination, the moral case against fascism and racial supremacy — gave African political leaders powerful language with which to challenge colonial rule itself. The world wars did not cause decolonization by themselves, but they accelerated, deepened, and reshaped the currents that would carry Africa toward independence in the decades that followed.

Evidence for this chapter comes from a wide range of sources: military records and casualty lists, colonial administrative correspondence, recruitment and taxation records, memoirs and oral histories of surviving veterans and their families, wartime propaganda and newspapers, letters written home by soldiers (where literacy and censorship permitted), photographs, and postwar veterans' association records. As with much colonial-era history, the voices of ordinary African soldiers and carriers are underrepresented in the written archive compared with the voices of European officers and administrators, and historians have worked to recover African perspectives through oral history projects, veterans' testimonies collected in later decades, and careful, critical reading of colonial records against the grain.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain why and how European colonial powers mobilized African soldiers and laborers during World War I and World War II.
  2. Describe the major African military campaigns and theaters of both world wars, including fighting within Africa itself.
  3. Analyze the human cost of wartime labor recruitment, particularly the carrier corps systems of World War I.
  4. Compare African experiences of World War I and World War II, noting continuities and differences.
  5. Evaluate how wartime service shaped the political consciousness of African veterans and colonial subjects.
  6. Assess the economic and social consequences of the world wars for African societies, including women, rural communities, and urban workers.
  7. Interpret primary sources — soldiers' testimony, colonial records, wartime propaganda — critically and in context.
  8. Explain how wartime weakness and contradiction within colonial empires contributed to postwar demands for change.
  9. Distinguish between coercion and agency in African participation in the world wars.
  10. Connect the experience of the world wars to the rise of African nationalism and Pan-Africanism examined in later chapters.
  11. Contextualize African participation in the world wars within the broader history of colonial rule and global conflict.
  12. Identify multiple perspectives on wartime service, including those of soldiers, laborers, women, colonial administrators, and nationalist leaders.

Guiding Historical Questions

Questions for Historical Inquiry

  1. Why were African soldiers and laborers mobilized for wars fought primarily among European powers?
  2. What was the human cost of African participation in the world wars, both on and off the battlefield?
  3. How did methods of recruitment — voluntary enlistment, conscription, and coercion — vary across colonies and change over time?
  4. In what ways did fighting occur within Africa itself, and what were the consequences for African civilians?
  5. How did wartime service change the outlook, expectations, and political consciousness of African soldiers?
  6. How did the wars affect African economies, agriculture, and household life, including the experiences of women?
  7. What contradictions existed between the ideals colonial powers claimed to be fighting for and their treatment of African colonial subjects?
  8. How did African veterans organize after the wars, and what role did they play in later political movements?
  9. How did global conflict expose or worsen the weaknesses of colonial rule?
  10. What continuities and changes can historians identify between the experiences of World War I and World War II in Africa?

Historical Context

By 1914, the Scramble for Africa examined in Chapter 47 had produced a continent almost entirely divided among European colonial powers. Britain, France, Germany, Belgium, Portugal, Italy, and Spain each controlled substantial African territories, administered through the systems of colonial rule described in Chapter 48 and sustained by the extractive economies described in Chapter 49. These colonial holdings were not merely economic assets; they were also, in the eyes of imperial military planners, reservoirs of manpower. Colonial armies had already been used extensively in the conquest of Africa itself — African soldiers serving in units such as France's Tirailleurs Sénégalais and Britain's King's African Rifles had been central to the very process of subjugating the continent in the decades before 1914. When war broke out in Europe, these existing colonial military structures, along with newly created recruitment and labor systems, were rapidly expanded and redirected toward the global conflict.

Africa's geographic position also made it strategically significant. German colonies — German East Africa (modern Tanzania, Rwanda, and Burundi), German Southwest Africa (modern Namibia), German Kamerun (modern Cameroon), and German Togoland — were surrounded by British, French, Belgian, and South African territory, making them immediate military targets once war began. Control of African ports, sea lanes, and resources mattered to the broader war effort, and African territory itself became contested ground.

The ideological context of both world wars also shaped African experience in important ways. World War I was fought, according to Allied propaganda, to defend small nations against aggression and to uphold international law and civilization — language that rang hollow to many educated Africans aware of the violent conquest that had produced the colonial map only a generation earlier. World War II, fought explicitly against Nazi Germany's ideology of racial hierarchy and conquest, placed anti-racist and pro-democratic rhetoric even more centrally in Allied propaganda, creating a sharper and more uncomfortable contrast with the racial hierarchies and denial of political rights that defined colonial rule throughout Africa. This contradiction — between the stated ideals of the wars and the lived reality of colonial subjects — became one of the most consequential political facts of the twentieth century in Africa.

It is also essential to understand that African involvement in the world wars did not begin or end at the battlefield. Colonial administrations used the wars to justify expanded taxation, forced labor, compulsory crop production, and requisitioning of livestock and food supplies — burdens that fell heavily on rural populations, especially in French and Belgian territories where forced labor practices were already deeply entrenched. The wars also created new opportunities: wage labor, urban migration, exposure to new technologies and ideas, and — for a significant minority — access to education, travel, and political networks that had previously been closed to them. The world wars were, in short, total events that reshaped nearly every dimension of African life for the duration of the conflicts and for decades afterward.

Major People, Groups, and Places

Reference — People, Groups & Places

Tirailleurs Sénégalais

British colonial military force recruited from East African territories including Kenya, Uganda, Nyasaland (Malawi), and Tanganyika; central to Britain's East African campaigns in both wars.

British colonial military force recruited from Nigeria, the Gold Coast (Ghana), Sierra Leone, and the Gambia; served in West Africa, East Africa, North Africa, and Burma.

Systems of African porters conscripted or recruited to transport military supplies, especially in East Africa during World War I, where mechanized transport was limited; suffered extraordinarily high mortality rates.
Paul von Lettow-Vorbeck
German commander who led a prolonged guerrilla campaign in German East Africa during World War I, relying heavily on African askari soldiers and imposing enormous burdens on the East African civilian population.

Extended World War I military campaign across present-day Tanzania, Mozambique, Zambia, and Malawi; caused widespread famine, disease, and displacement among African civilians.
North African Campaign (1940–1943)
Major World War II theater fought across Egypt, Libya, and Tunisia between Allied and Axis forces, involving significant African and colonial troop deployments and infrastructure.
East African Campaign (1940–1941)
World War II campaign in which British Commonwealth and colonial African forces defeated Italian forces occupying Ethiopia, Eritrea, and Italian Somaliland, restoring Emperor Haile Selassie to the Ethiopian throne.
Thiaroye Massacre (1944)
The killing of demobilized Senegalese Tirailleurs by French forces near Dakar after the soldiers protested unpaid wages and unequal treatment; became a powerful symbol of colonial betrayal.
Atlantic Charter (1941)
Joint declaration by the United States and Britain affirming the right of peoples to choose their own form of government; widely invoked by African and colonial nationalists despite Allied leaders' intent to apply it mainly to Europe.

Conference convened by Free French authorities in French Equatorial Africa proposing limited colonial reforms while explicitly rejecting self-government or independence for French African colonies.
League of Nations Mandate System
Post–World War I arrangement under which former German colonies in Africa were placed under the administration of Britain, France, Belgium, and South Africa, formally supervised by the League of Nations but functioning largely as continued colonial rule.
Emperor Haile Selassie
Ethiopian emperor forced into exile during the Italian occupation (1936–1941) and restored to power following the Allied East African campaign; became a global symbol of African resistance to fascist aggression.

Section 52.1

Africa and World War I

When the great powers of Europe went to war in August 1914, their colonial empires were drawn into the conflict immediately and automatically. Colonial subjects had no say in the decision. Governors in Dakar, Lagos, Nairobi, Salisbury, and Léopoldville received telegrams announcing that their metropoles were at war, and within days mobilization orders began moving through colonial administrative networks down to district officers, chiefs, and headmen, who were now responsible for producing soldiers, porters, food, and money for a war being fought thousands of miles away for reasons that had nothing to do with African political life.

Africa became a battlefield almost immediately because Germany possessed four African colonies — German East Africa, German Southwest Africa, Kamerun, and Togoland — each of which bordered territory controlled by Britain, France, Belgium, or South Africa. Togoland fell quickly to a joint British-French invasion in August 1914. Kamerun required a longer campaign lasting into 1916, involving British, French, and Belgian colonial forces against German defenders who relied heavily on African askari troops. German Southwest Africa was conquered by South African forces under Jan Smuts and Louis Botha by mid-1915, a campaign notable for the participation of South African troops who would, in the following decades, build the apartheid state examined in Chapter 57.

The most prolonged and consequential campaign was fought in German East Africa, where the German commander Paul von Lettow-Vorbeck led a small force — never more than a few thousand German officers supported by several thousand African askari soldiers — in a sustained guerrilla campaign against vastly larger British, Belgian, Portuguese, and South African forces. Lettow-Vorbeck's strategy was to tie down as many Allied troops and resources as possible rather than to win outright victory, and in this he succeeded remarkably: his forces remained undefeated in the field when the armistice was signed in November 1918, having fought across German East Africa, Portuguese Mozambique, and Northern Rhodesia. But the human cost of this campaign fell overwhelmingly on African civilians and laborers rather than on European soldiers. Villages were stripped of food to supply both armies, livestock was requisitioned or destroyed, and disease spread rapidly through displaced populations. Estimates of civilian deaths from war-induced famine and disease in East Africa during this campaign run into the hundreds of thousands — a toll rarely acknowledged in traditional accounts that focus on the campaign's tactical drama.

Beyond the fighting within Africa, colonial powers also drew heavily on African manpower for combat and labor in European and Middle Eastern theaters. France, facing catastrophic casualties on the Western Front, turned increasingly to its West African colonies for soldiers. Approximately 200,000 men served in the Tirailleurs Sénégalais during the war, recruited through a combination of voluntary enlistment, colonial obligation, and outright conscription that intensified as French casualties mounted and as figures such as the Senegalese political leader Blaise Diagne — the first African elected to the French National Assembly — became involved in recruitment efforts in exchange for promises of expanded rights for African veterans. Approximately 30,000 to 35,000 West African soldiers died in French service during the war, a mortality rate significantly higher than that of French metropolitan troops in comparable roles, reflecting both the dangerous assignments African units were often given and inadequate provisions for their welfare.

Britain drew on African forces primarily within African and Middle Eastern theaters rather than sending large numbers of African combat troops to the Western Front, relying instead on the King's African Rifles, the Royal West African Frontier Force, and South African units for campaigns in East and Southwest Africa and, later, in the Middle East. Belgium mobilized forces from the Congo — the Force Publique — for the Kamerun and East African campaigns. Portugal, whose colonies in Angola and Mozambique bordered German territory, was drawn into fighting in East Africa as well, with African soldiers bearing the brunt of a poorly resourced Portuguese war effort.

Section 52.2

African Soldiers and Laborers

Behind every African soldier who carried a rifle during World War I stood a much larger and less visible workforce: the porters, carriers, road builders, and laborers whose physical effort made colonial military campaigns possible in regions with little rail or motorized transport. In East Africa especially, where terrain, disease, and the absence of infrastructure made mechanized transport nearly impossible, armies depended almost entirely on human porterage to move supplies, ammunition, food, and equipment. The British Carrier Corps alone mobilized well over a million individual instances of labor service over the course of the East African campaign, drawing men — and in some cases women and children — from across British East Africa, Nyasaland, Northern Rhodesia, and Uganda.

The conditions endured by carriers were often catastrophic. Men were marched for weeks carrying loads of sixty pounds or more through disease-ridden terrain, with inadequate food, water, and medical care. Mortality rates among carriers in the East African campaign have been estimated by historians at between twenty and thirty percent of those mobilized — figures that, if accurate, mean that far more African carriers died in this single theater of World War I than African soldiers died in combat across the entire war. Because carriers were often unarmed noncombatants performing essential but undervalued labor, and because colonial administrations kept incomplete records of their recruitment, treatment, and deaths, this catastrophic loss of life has historically received far less attention than combat casualties — a pattern of historical erasure that modern historians have worked to correct.

Soldiers themselves, meanwhile, served under conditions shaped by the racial hierarchies of colonial rule. African troops in French and British colonial armies were typically paid less than European soldiers performing equivalent service, received inferior rations and equipment in many cases, and were frequently assigned to the most dangerous and physically demanding roles. French Tirailleurs Sénégalais units on the Western Front were sometimes used as shock troops in offensives with extremely high casualty rates, a pattern that some contemporaries and later historians have linked to racialized assumptions about African soldiers' supposed suitability for frontal assault — assumptions that had no basis in fact but that shaped tactical decisions with lethal consequences.

At the same time, African soldiers demonstrated skill, discipline, and courage that earned recognition even within the racially hierarchical military structures they served under. Askari troops under Lettow-Vorbeck's command in East Africa were widely regarded, even by their opponents, as highly effective soldiers whose endurance and combat skill sustained a campaign against overwhelming numerical odds for more than four years. Tirailleurs Sénégalais units earned numerous decorations for valor on the Western Front, even as the French military and political establishment continued to deny them full citizenship rights and equal treatment.

Section 52.3

Wartime Mobilization and Burdens

The demands of total war extended far beyond the battlefield and reshaped daily life across colonial Africa, whether or not a particular community sent soldiers to the front. Colonial administrations imposed new and expanded taxes to help finance the war effort, often collected in cash, which forced rural households further into wage labor or cash-crop production to meet their obligations — intensifying processes of economic transformation already underway under colonial rule. Compulsory crop production and requisitioning of food, livestock, and other resources for military use placed additional strain on rural economies, sometimes contributing directly to local famine conditions, as occurred in parts of East Africa during the Lettow-Vorbeck campaign.

Forced labor systems, already a defining and brutal feature of colonial economies in territories such as French Equatorial Africa, the Belgian Congo, and Portuguese Africa, were expanded and intensified during the war to meet military transport and construction needs. Road and rail construction, essential to supplying military campaigns, relied heavily on conscripted African labor working under harsh conditions with minimal compensation. In some regions, the wartime intensification of forced labor produced significant unrest and resistance; the war years saw a number of local uprisings and refusals across colonial Africa that, while often suppressed, reflected growing strain on colonial systems already resented for their exploitative character.

The recruitment of men for both military and labor service also disrupted household and community structures. With large numbers of able-bodied men absent — whether serving as soldiers, conscripted as carriers, or drawn into wartime wage labor — agricultural production in many regions depended even more heavily on the labor of women, children, and the elderly. Households lost income earners, communities lost social and political leadership figures conscripted into service, and in some cases entire regions experienced significant demographic disruption from combined military mortality, labor mortality, and wartime famine and disease.

These burdens were not distributed evenly. Regions and communities closer to active combat zones, such as those in German East Africa and the neighboring Belgian and Portuguese territories, bore disproportionate costs from requisitioning, displacement, and disease. Communities with existing ties to colonial military recruitment networks — often those already integrated into colonial administrative and economic systems — supplied larger numbers of soldiers and carriers than more remote or less-administered regions, reflecting patterns of colonial control that had been established well before 1914.

Section 52.4

Africa and World War II

When World War II began in September 1939, colonial powers once again turned to their African territories for soldiers, labor, and resources, but the scale, geography, and political context of this mobilization differed in important respects from World War I. African troops served across a much wider geographic range — North Africa, East Africa, the Middle East, Madagascar, Burma, Italy, and eventually Western Europe — and the ideological character of the conflict, fought explicitly against Nazi Germany's racial ideology and fascist expansion, created sharper contradictions with colonial racial hierarchies than had been the case in 1914.

The North African campaign, fought primarily across Egypt, Libya, and Tunisia between 1940 and 1943, became one of the war's major theaters, pitting British and Commonwealth forces — supported by extensive infrastructure, labor, and supply networks drawn from across British Africa — against Italian and later German forces under Erwin Rommel. While the combat forces in this theater were drawn mainly from Britain, its dominions, and Free French units, the campaign depended heavily on African labor for construction, transport, and logistics, and North African civilian populations experienced significant disruption from the fighting that crossed their territory.

The East African campaign of 1940–1941 was fought largely by African and Commonwealth colonial forces — including the King's African Rifles and West African units transported to the theater — against Italian forces that had occupied Ethiopia since 1936, following the invasion examined in later Ethiopian historiography as a preview of the fascist aggression that would engulf Europe. This campaign culminated in the defeat of Italian forces and the restoration of Emperor Haile Selassie to the Ethiopian throne in 1941, a moment celebrated across Africa and the African diaspora as a significant victory against fascist and racist aggression — one in which African soldiers played a central and often underrecognized role.

France's African colonies played a particularly complicated and significant role in World War II. Following France's defeat and occupation by Germany in 1940, French Equatorial Africa became one of the first territories to declare loyalty to Charles de Gaulle's Free French movement rather than to the collaborationist Vichy government, giving Free France a territorial and military base from which to continue the fight. Brazzaville, in French Equatorial Africa, became a symbolic capital of Free France for a period, and African soldiers — again drawn heavily from the Tirailleurs Sénégalais and other colonial units — played a major role in Free French military operations, including the liberation of France itself in 1944, though African units were often deliberately withdrawn from front-line roles during the final push into Paris in a policy of "whitening" the liberating forces that later became a notorious symbol of colonial ingratitude.

Belgium's Force Publique, recruited from the Congo, served in East Africa against Italian forces and later in other theaters, while other African forces contributed to campaigns in Madagascar (where Free French and British forces removed Vichy administration in 1942), Italian Somaliland, Eritrea, and eventually Italy itself, where units from French North and West Africa fought in the Italian campaign. By the war's end, more than a million Africans had served in various military and labor capacities across both world wars combined, a contribution to Allied victory that received limited recognition at the time and that historians have worked in subsequent decades to document and honor more fully.

Section 52.5

Military Service and Political Awakening

For many African soldiers, service in the world wars — and especially in World War II — provided experiences that fundamentally reshaped their understanding of colonial rule, of the wider world, and of their own political possibilities. Soldiers traveled to places they had never imagined visiting, encountered European societies and populations directly rather than through the mediated lens of colonial administration, fought alongside soldiers from other colonized territories, and, in many cases, discovered that the racial hierarchies they had been taught to accept as natural and permanent were neither natural nor universally accepted, even within the very empires that enforced them.

This exposure produced what historians often call a process of political awakening. African soldiers who fought against Nazi Germany's explicit ideology of racial supremacy could not easily reconcile that struggle with their own continued subordination under colonial rule that likewise denied them political rights on the basis of race. Soldiers who had commanded respect and demonstrated competence in combat, who had sometimes been decorated for valor, returned to colonies where they were denied the vote, restricted in movement and employment, and treated as permanent subordinates regardless of their service or sacrifice. The gap between wartime rhetoric — the Atlantic Charter's promise that all peoples had the right to choose their own government, propaganda that celebrated the defense of freedom and democracy — and the lived reality of colonial subjection became increasingly difficult to ignore or accept.

Veterans also brought home new organizational skills, new networks that crossed ethnic and regional lines within colonial territories, and, in some cases, direct exposure to nationalist and anti-colonial ideas circulating among colonial troops and workers from India, the Caribbean, and other parts of the British and French empires who served alongside them. Many of the political leaders who would drive African nationalist movements in the following decades — examined at length in Chapter 53 — either served in the world wars themselves or were profoundly shaped by conversations with veterans, by the political networks veterans helped build, and by the moral arguments veterans made about the debt colonial powers owed to those who had fought and died in their name.

Section 52.6

Wartime Economy and Social Change

World War II, like World War I before it, transformed African economies and societies far beyond the ranks of those who served in uniform. Colonial administrations again expanded taxation, requisitioned crops and resources, and in many territories intensified forced labor systems to meet wartime production demands. Strategic minerals — including uranium from the Belgian Congo's Shinkolobwe mine, used in the development of the atomic bombs dropped on Japan, and cobalt, copper, and other minerals essential to Allied war industries — made certain African territories objects of intense strategic interest, with production quotas enforced through coercive labor practices that placed enormous strain on mining communities.

Agricultural production was similarly reoriented toward wartime needs. Colonial administrations demanded increased output of crops such as rubber, palm oil, groundnuts, and cotton to supply Allied war industries, often through compulsory cultivation schemes that reduced land and labor available for food crops and contributed to localized food shortages and, in some cases, famine conditions — a pattern documented, for instance, in parts of French West Africa during the war years.

Urban migration accelerated during the war as demand for wage labor in ports, military installations, and expanding wartime industries drew workers from rural areas into cities and towns, contributing to the urbanization patterns examined more fully in Chapter 61. Women's roles shifted as well: with large numbers of men absent in military or labor service, women in many communities took on expanded responsibility for agricultural production, household economic management, and in some cases new forms of wage labor, market activity, and community leadership — experiences that, while often unrecognized in official records, contributed to longer-term changes in gender roles and expectations across the postwar decades.

The wartime economy also produced significant labor unrest. Wage controls, price inflation, scarcity of consumer goods, and the coercive character of much wartime labor recruitment generated strikes and protests in a number of colonial territories both during and immediately after the war, including major strikes among railway workers, dockworkers, and miners that foreshadowed the more organized labor and political movements of the postwar decades.

Section 52.7

Global Conflict and Colonial Weakness

Both world wars, in different ways, exposed and deepened structural weaknesses within European colonial empires that would prove significant in the postwar period. World War I severely strained the financial and administrative capacity of colonial powers, even as it demonstrated colonial territories' strategic and economic value; the interwar period that followed saw limited reform alongside continued extraction, setting the stage for renewed and even more extensive mobilization when war returned in 1939.

World War II inflicted far greater strain on the metropolitan powers themselves. France's defeat and occupation in 1940 shattered the image of French imperial invincibility and forced French colonial administration into an unprecedented and improvised relationship with Free French authorities operating partly from African soil. Britain, though undefeated, emerged from the war financially exhausted, deeply indebted, and increasingly dependent on colonial resources and labor even as domestic political pressure — and growing international scrutiny, particularly from the United States and the newly formed United Nations — made the long-term maintenance of empire increasingly difficult to justify or sustain. Belgium's occupation during the war likewise exposed the fragility of metropolitan control over the Congo, even as Congolese mineral production became strategically vital to the Allied war effort.

The war also transformed the international political environment in ways unfavorable to the continuation of empire. The United States, whose economic and military power was now indispensable to European recovery, was officially, if inconsistently, opposed to old-style colonialism, and the language of the Atlantic Charter — however narrowly its authors intended it to apply — provided rhetorical ammunition that African and Asian nationalists deployed effectively in the following years. The founding of the United Nations in 1945, with its charter affirming the principle of self-determination, created new international forums in which colonial rule could be challenged and scrutinized in ways that had not existed after World War I.

Section 52.8

Veterans and Postwar Politics

The return of African veterans to their home territories after both world wars, but especially after World War II, was a pivotal moment in African political history. Veterans often expected that their wartime service and sacrifice would be recognized through improved political rights, economic opportunity, and social respect. In most cases, these expectations collided directly with colonial administrations unwilling to grant meaningful change.

The starkest and most infamous example of this collision was the Thiaroye massacre of December 1944, in which French forces killed dozens of demobilized Tirailleurs Sénégalais soldiers who had gathered near Dakar to protest unpaid wages, unequal demobilization benefits compared to French soldiers, and broken promises regarding their treatment. The massacre, long minimized or misrepresented in official French accounts, became — as its full details emerged over subsequent decades — a powerful symbol of colonial betrayal and a rallying point for anti-colonial sentiment across French West Africa.

Veterans' organizations formed across colonial Africa in the years following both wars, providing structures through which former soldiers could advocate for pensions, benefits, and recognition, and which frequently evolved into broader political organizing networks. Veterans brought organizational discipline, cross-regional and cross-ethnic connections forged through shared military service, direct experience of the gap between imperial rhetoric and colonial reality, and, in many cases, personal courage and moral authority earned through wartime sacrifice — all resources that fed directly into the nationalist movements examined in the following chapters. Prominent postwar African political leaders in multiple territories had military service backgrounds or close family and political ties to veterans' networks, and the demand for veterans' rights frequently became entangled with broader demands for colonial reform and, eventually, independence.

Colonial powers responded to postwar pressure with a mixture of limited reform and continued repression. Britain and France both introduced modest political and economic reforms in the late 1940s — expanded local representation, limited social welfare programs, and increased investment in some colonial infrastructure — reflecting recognition that the prewar status quo could not simply continue unchanged. But these reforms fell far short of self-government, and the gap between reform and demand widened rapidly in the years that followed, setting the stage for the accelerating nationalist and independence movements examined in Chapters 53 and 54.

Section 52.9

The World Wars as Turning Points

Viewed together, the two world wars constitute one of the most significant turning points in modern African history, even though neither war was fought primarily for African reasons or primarily on African initiative. The wars mobilized African manpower, resources, and territory on an unprecedented scale, imposing enormous human and economic costs on African societies whose political leadership had no voice in the decisions that produced those costs. At the same time, the wars generated experiences, expectations, networks, and political arguments that African soldiers, laborers, and civilians drew upon in the postwar decades to challenge the colonial systems that had conscripted them.

It is important for students to resist two tempting oversimplifications. The first is to treat African participation in the world wars only as victimization — erasing the skill, courage, and agency African soldiers and civilians demonstrated, and the ways many actively used wartime experience and rhetoric to advance their own political goals. The second is to treat the wars only as a story of heroic service rewarded by gradual imperial enlightenment — erasing the coercion, exploitation, and violence, including massacres such as Thiaroye, that colonial powers inflicted on the very people who had served them. The historically accurate picture holds both realities together: African participation in the world wars was shaped by colonial coercion and inequality, and it also became a crucial source of the political consciousness, organization, and moral argument that helped drive the end of colonial rule itself.

Historical Vocabulary
Tirailleur

A French term meaning "rifleman" or "skirmisher," used specifically to designate colonial infantry units recruited from French African territories, most famously the Tirailleurs Sénégalais. Despite the regional name, these regiments drew recruits from across French West and Equatorial Africa, not solely from Senegal. Tirailleur units played major combat roles in both world wars while facing systemic pay inequality, unequal pension benefits, and, in the case of the Thiaroye massacre, deadly violence from the very French state they had served.

Historical Vocabulary
Carrier Corps

The system of African porters recruited or conscripted to transport military supplies on foot, especially prominent during the East African campaign of World War I, where mechanized transport was largely unavailable. Carrier mortality rates, driven by disease, exhaustion, and inadequate provisioning, were extraordinarily high — historians estimate that far more African carriers died during the East African campaign than African or European soldiers died in combat in that same theater, a fact long obscured in traditional military histories that centered on combat operations rather than logistics and labor.

Historical Vocabulary
Veteran Politics

The organized political activity of former soldiers seeking recognition, pensions, benefits, and broader political rights after military service. In colonial Africa, veteran politics became a crucial bridge between wartime service and postwar nationalist movements, as veterans applied organizational skills, cross-regional networks, and moral authority gained through military sacrifice to demands for colonial reform and, eventually, independence.

Creator: Unnamed West African veteran, recorded in postwar oral history collections · Date: Recollections of service, collected decades later · Type: Oral history testimony

Context: Because relatively few African soldiers left extensive written records during the world wars — a result of limited access to formal education under colonial rule and the constraints of military censorship — much of what historians know about African soldiers' direct experiences comes from oral histories recorded by researchers in the decades after the wars, when surviving veterans described their service, their treatment, and their reflections on what the war meant for them and for their communities.

Veterans recalled being told they were fighting for liberty and civilization, recalled the discipline and pride of military service, and recalled returning home to find that the promises made to them — of pay, of pensions, of recognition — were not kept, and that colonial officials treated them much as before, regardless of what they had done or suffered in Europe or North Africa.

Analysis: Oral history testimony of this kind is invaluable because it recovers perspectives that colonial military records, written primarily by European officers and administrators, rarely capture directly. However, students should recognize the limits of oral testimony recorded decades after the events described: memory can be shaped by intervening political developments, including the independence movements that many veterans went on to support, and by the process of interview itself. Cross-referencing oral testimony with military records, pension disputes, and contemporary colonial correspondence allows historians to build a fuller and more reliable picture.

Historical Debate

How Directly Did the World Wars Cause African Nationalism?

The question: Historians agree that African participation in the world wars mattered to the later rise of nationalist and independence movements, but they disagree about how direct and decisive that causal relationship was.

Interpretation A — The World Wars as a Direct Catalyst: Some historians emphasize a fairly direct causal chain: military service exposed African soldiers to new ideas, new networks, and the hypocrisy of colonial rhetoric; veterans returned with organizational skills and moral authority; postwar events such as the Thiaroye massacre and unmet promises radicalized veterans and their communities; and veterans became key organizers and leaders of the nationalist movements that followed within a decade or two of the wars' end.
Interpretation B — The World Wars as One Factor Among Many: Other historians caution against overstating the wars' causal role, pointing out that nationalist and Pan-African movements had deep roots predating both world wars — in earlier resistance movements, in the writings of early twentieth-century Pan-Africanist intellectuals, in labor organizing, and in the expansion of Western education and mission schooling. In this view, the wars accelerated and reshaped currents of political change that were already building, rather than creating those currents from nothing.

Evidence: Interpretation A draws on veteran testimony, the timing of major nationalist organizing in the years immediately following World War II, and documented cases of veterans becoming prominent political leaders. Interpretation B draws on the longer history of Pan-Africanism and anti-colonial thought examined in Chapter 53, which shows organized political opposition to colonial rule well before 1914.

Why it matters: How historians weigh this question shapes how they narrate the entire arc of twentieth-century African political history — whether as a story punctuated by the world wars as decisive turning points, or as a more gradual and continuous story of resistance and political development in which the wars were significant but not singular.

Student task: Using evidence from this chapter and from your own reasoning, evaluate which interpretation you find more persuasive, or explain how elements of both interpretations might be combined into a more complete account.

Multiple Perspectives

Wartime Mobilization in Colonial Africa

From the perspective of colonial administrators and military planners: African soldiers and laborers were viewed primarily as a strategic resource — manpower to be mobilized efficiently to support imperial war aims, with limited concern for the political consequences of that mobilization or for the welfare of those conscripted, particularly among carrier and labor populations.
From the perspective of African soldiers: Motivations for service varied widely — from limited economic opportunity and social pressure, to genuine desire for adventure and advancement, to outright coercion — but many soldiers who served came to see their sacrifice as creating a moral claim on colonial powers for improved treatment, rights, and recognition, a claim that was frequently denied or only partially honored.
From the perspective of African carriers and laborers: Porterage and forced labor mobilization was experienced overwhelmingly as coercive and dangerous, with little of the recognition, pay, or postwar advocacy available to uniformed soldiers, despite carriers frequently facing higher mortality risk than combat troops.
From the perspective of women and families at home: The absence of conscripted men shifted enormous economic and household burdens onto women, who managed farms, households, and, increasingly, wage labor and market activity, often without formal recognition or compensation, while facing anxiety over the fate of husbands, sons, and brothers serving or laboring in distant and dangerous theaters.
From the perspective of postwar nationalist leaders: The wars — and particularly the treatment of veterans like those killed at Thiaroye — became powerful evidence in the moral and political argument against colonial rule, demonstrating concretely that promises of imperial gratitude and reform could not be trusted and that only self-government would secure genuine rights and dignity.
Cause and Effect

Colonial Mobilization and Its Consequences

Long-Term Causes

Existing colonial military structures built during conquest; established taxation and labor extraction systems; racial hierarchies embedded in colonial administration and military organization; Africa's strategic and resource value to European empires.

Short-Term Causes

The outbreak of war in Europe in 1914 and 1939; military need for manpower amid catastrophic European casualties (especially World War I); strategic requirement to defend or seize African colonial territory; wartime demand for African raw materials and labor.

The Event

Mass conscription, recruitment, and coercion of African soldiers and laborers; military campaigns fought within Africa; expanded taxation, requisitioning, and forced labor across colonial territories.

Effects

Massive African casualties among soldiers and, especially, laborers; disrupted rural economies and households; expanded political awareness and veteran organizing; growing contradictions in colonial rule exposed by wartime rhetoric; direct contributions to postwar nationalist and independence movements.

Historical complexity: The consequences of wartime mobilization cannot be reduced to a single storyline of victimization or a single storyline of political awakening. Both dimensions operated simultaneously and were, in fact, deeply connected: the very exploitation and coercion of wartime mobilization generated the grievances and experiences that fed postwar political change.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Colonial Rule Before and After the World Wars

What Changed

  • Colonial powers' financial and administrative capacity was significantly weakened, especially after World War II
  • International rhetoric increasingly emphasized self-determination and human rights
  • Veterans returned with new political networks, organizational experience, and expectations
  • Limited political and economic reforms were introduced in the late 1940s in several colonies
  • Colonial powers faced growing international scrutiny through the United Nations

What Stayed the Same

  • Formal colonial rule and denial of self-government persisted in nearly all African territories through the late 1940s
  • Racial hierarchies in economic and political life remained largely intact
  • Extractive economic relationships between colonies and metropoles continued
  • Veterans' pensions and benefits remained unequal compared to European counterparts

Why the pattern matters: The persistence of colonial rule despite the wars' destabilizing effects demonstrates that structural change in political systems rarely follows immediately or automatically from even severe shocks; it required the sustained organizing and political pressure examined in the following chapters to convert wartime contradictions into actual decolonization.

Everyday Life and Social History

The African Home Front

For most Africans, the world wars were experienced not on distant battlefields but in the rhythms of daily life disrupted by taxation, requisitioning, labor conscription, and the absence of men serving in military or labor capacities. Rural households adjusted agricultural labor patterns, often relying more heavily on women's labor to maintain food production alongside expanded cash-crop requirements imposed by colonial administrations seeking to supply wartime industries. Market women in colonial towns and cities navigated wartime price controls, scarcity of imported goods, and currency fluctuations, while urban workers faced wage controls that failed to keep pace with wartime inflation, feeding labor unrest that intensified toward the end of both wars. Religious communities, including churches and mosques, often became sites where communities processed anxiety over absent family members, organized mutual aid, and, in some cases, articulated early moral and political criticism of colonial wartime demands. Children in many communities experienced disrupted schooling as teachers and resources were redirected, even as colonial and mission education systems used wartime curricula to reinforce loyalty to the imperial cause. These home-front experiences, though less dramatic than battlefield narratives, touched far more Africans directly and left lasting impressions on household economies, gender roles, and community expectations of colonial governance.

Section 52.10

Chapter Review and War and Empire Checkpoint

This chapter has examined Africa's extensive and consequential involvement in both world wars — as a source of soldiers and laborers, as a battlefield in its own right, as an economic resource base subject to intensified wartime extraction, and as a site of profound social disruption and political transformation. Before proceeding to the nationalist and independence movements examined in the following chapters, students should be able to demonstrate understanding of these foundational developments.

The core insights of this chapter are: colonial powers mobilized hundreds of thousands of African soldiers and laborers for wars fought primarily over European and global strategic interests; fighting occurred directly within Africa in both wars, with devastating consequences for African civilians, especially in East Africa; the human cost of wartime mobilization fell disproportionately on carriers and laborers whose suffering has often been underrecognized compared to combat casualties; wartime service exposed African soldiers to experiences and ideas that reshaped their political consciousness; the wars imposed severe economic and social burdens on African communities, including women who bore expanded household and economic responsibilities; the contradiction between wartime rhetoric of freedom and self-determination and the reality of continued colonial subjugation became an increasingly powerful political argument; and veterans, particularly after World War II, became crucial organizers and moral authorities within the nationalist movements that would ultimately dismantle colonial rule.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has examined one of the most consequential and, for many years, underrecognized chapters in modern African history: the mobilization of African soldiers, laborers, and resources for two world wars fought primarily among European powers over interests that had little direct connection to African political life. Between 1914 and 1945, colonial powers drew on their African territories for hundreds of thousands of soldiers and, in the case of World War I especially, an even larger number of porters and laborers whose contribution to the war effort has often been minimized in traditional military histories centered on combat operations.

The chapter has shown that fighting occurred directly within Africa in both wars — most dramatically in the prolonged German East Africa campaign of World War I and in the North and East African campaigns of World War II — with African civilians bearing significant costs from requisitioning, displacement, disease, and, in some regions, famine. The Carrier Corps system of World War I, in particular, imposed a catastrophic and long-underrecognized human toll, with mortality rates among African porters and laborers that likely exceeded combat deaths in the same theater.

African soldiers themselves served with skill and courage across multiple theaters, from the trenches of the Western Front to the deserts of North Africa to the jungles of Burma, even as they served within military and colonial systems that denied them equal pay, equal treatment, and equal political rights compared to European soldiers. This contradiction between service and recognition became one of the defining political facts to emerge from the world wars, most starkly symbolized by the 1944 Thiaroye massacre, in which French forces killed demobilized Senegalese veterans protesting unpaid wages and unequal treatment.

The wars also transformed African societies far beyond the ranks of those who served in uniform. Expanded taxation, requisitioning, forced labor, and compulsory crop production reshaped rural economies and placed new burdens — often disproportionately — on women, who took on expanded agricultural, household, and economic responsibilities during the absence of conscripted men. Urban migration accelerated, wartime labor unrest grew, and colonial economies were reoriented toward meeting metropolitan wartime demands, sometimes at the direct expense of local food security.

Perhaps most significantly for the longer arc of African history, this chapter has traced how wartime service and its aftermath contributed directly to the political awakening that fed the nationalist and Pan-African movements examined in the chapters that follow. Veterans who had fought against fascism, who had traveled and served alongside soldiers from other colonized territories, and who returned home to encounter continued denial of political rights and broken wartime promises became crucial organizers, leaders, and moral voices within the movements that would ultimately dismantle colonial rule across the African continent in the decades after 1945.

The chapter has also emphasized the importance of holding multiple realities together rather than reducing this history to a single narrative. African participation in the world wars involved profound coercion, exploitation, and loss of life, particularly among laborers and carriers whose suffering has often been historically obscured. It also involved genuine skill, courage, and, ultimately, political agency, as African soldiers, veterans, and civilians used the experience and contradictions of global war to build the case — and the organizational capacity — for ending colonial rule itself.

Students should leave this chapter understanding that the world wars were not simply European events with incidental African participation, but genuinely global conflicts in which Africa played an indispensable and costly role — a role whose full human toll and whose long-term political consequences are still being fully documented and understood by historians today.

The wars also illustrate, in a particularly vivid way, the central tension that runs throughout the history of colonial Africa examined in Part VIII of this textbook: the gap between the ideals that colonial and imperial powers claimed to represent — civilization, liberty, democracy, the defense of small nations, the fight against racial supremacy — and the systems of racial hierarchy, economic extraction, and political subjugation those same powers imposed and maintained across their African territories. That tension, sharpened rather than resolved by two world wars, would become one of the central engines of the nationalist and independence movements that reshaped the African continent in the second half of the twentieth century.

Key Terms

Askari
A term used in East Africa for African soldiers serving in colonial military forces, especially those serving under German command in World War I.
Atlantic Charter
A 1941 joint declaration by the United States and Britain affirming the right of peoples to choose their own government, widely invoked by anti-colonial nationalists.
Carrier Corps
Systems of African porters mobilized to transport military supplies, especially during the East African campaign of World War I, marked by extremely high mortality.
Conscription
Compulsory enrollment for military or labor service, a major method by which colonial powers mobilized African manpower during the world wars.
Colonial regiment
A military unit recruited from a colonial territory and organized as part of an imperial armed force, such as the Tirailleurs Sénégalais or King's African Rifles.
Demobilization
The process of releasing soldiers from military service after a conflict ends, often accompanied by disputes over pay, pensions, and benefits.
Force Publique
The colonial military and police force of the Belgian Congo, mobilized for campaigns in East Africa and elsewhere during both world wars.
Forced labor
Compulsory, uncompensated or poorly compensated labor extracted from colonial subjects, intensified during both world wars to meet military and economic needs.
King's African Rifles (KAR)
British colonial military force recruited from East African territories, central to Britain's East African campaigns in both world wars.
Mandate system
The League of Nations arrangement placing former German African colonies under the administration of Allied powers after World War I.
Mobilization
The process of organizing and deploying military forces and resources for war, encompassing recruitment, conscription, taxation, and requisitioning.
North African campaign
The World War II military campaign fought across Egypt, Libya, and Tunisia between Allied and Axis forces from 1940 to 1943.
Pan-African consciousness
A shared political and cultural awareness among Africans and people of African descent of common interests and struggles, strengthened by wartime experience and cross-colonial contact.
Political awakening
The process by which wartime experience reshaped African soldiers' and civilians' political consciousness and expectations regarding colonial rule.
Porterage
The system of transporting goods and military supplies by human carriers, especially significant in regions lacking rail or motorized transport.
Requisition
The compulsory acquisition of goods, food, or livestock by military or colonial authorities, often without fair compensation.
Royal West African Frontier Force (RWAFF)
British colonial military force recruited from Nigeria, the Gold Coast, Sierra Leone, and the Gambia.
Self-determination
The principle that peoples have the right to choose their own political status and government, invoked in the Atlantic Charter and later the United Nations Charter.
Thiaroye massacre
The 1944 killing of demobilized Senegalese soldiers by French forces near Dakar following protests over unpaid wages and unequal treatment.
Tirailleurs Sénégalais
French colonial infantry regiments recruited from across French West Africa, extensively deployed in both world wars.
Total war
A form of warfare that mobilizes an entire society's population and economy for the war effort, extending wartime demands far beyond the battlefield.
Veteran association
Organizations formed by former soldiers to advocate for pensions, benefits, and recognition, which frequently evolved into broader political organizing networks.
Wartime economy
The reorganization of production, labor, and trade to meet the demands of war, often through taxation, requisitioning, and compulsory production schemes.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter has examined a history that, for much of the twentieth century, occupied only a marginal place in accounts of both African history and the history of the world wars themselves — the massive, costly, and consequential participation of African soldiers, laborers, and societies in two global conflicts that were not of their making. Understanding this history matters not only because it recovers the experiences of hundreds of thousands of African soldiers and, especially, the carriers and laborers whose suffering has too often gone unrecorded, but because it illuminates one of the central mechanisms by which colonial rule itself was ultimately challenged and dismantled.

The world wars did not, by themselves, cause the end of European colonialism in Africa. As the following chapters will show, the nationalist and Pan-African movements that achieved independence across the continent drew on much deeper historical roots — on earlier traditions of resistance, on the intellectual and political work of Pan-Africanist thinkers, on labor organizing, and on the long history of African political agency that this textbook has traced from its opening chapters. But the world wars accelerated, deepened, and gave new moral and political force to those currents. They exposed contradictions that colonial powers could not indefinitely paper over. They created veterans with organizational experience, cross-regional networks, and, crucially, direct evidence — sometimes paid for with their own blood, as at Thiaroye — that imperial promises of gratitude and reform could not be trusted.

This chapter also asks students to sit with genuine moral complexity rather than reaching for easy conclusions. African soldiers who served in the world wars were not simply passive victims of colonial coercion, even though coercion was real and pervasive. They were not simply heroes rewarded for their service, even though many served with extraordinary skill and courage. They were historical actors navigating an unjust system, making choices within severe constraints, and — in the aftermath of their service — using the experiences, grievances, and networks that system produced to work toward its eventual undoing.

As you move forward into the chapters on African nationalism, Pan-Africanism, and decolonization, carry this chapter's central lesson with you: large historical transformations rarely have single causes, and the seeds of major political change are often planted, unintentionally, by the very systems of power that later movements will overturn. The colonial powers that mobilized African soldiers and laborers for their own wars could not have anticipated that this very mobilization would help produce the generation of veterans, workers, and political leaders who would demand, and ultimately win, the end of colonial rule itself.

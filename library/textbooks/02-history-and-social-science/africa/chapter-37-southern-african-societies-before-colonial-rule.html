Part VI — East, Central, and Southern Africa
37

Southern African Societies Before Colonial Rule

Africa: A History of a Continent

Chapter Opening

"Cattle were not simply property in Southern Africa before colonial rule. They were wealth, kinship, ritual, and political authority walking on four legs."

Long before the first permanent European settlement was established at the Cape of Good Hope in 1652, the southern third of the African continent was home to a remarkable diversity of societies whose histories stretched back many centuries. Pastoralist communities herded cattle and small stock across semi-arid grasslands. Farming communities cultivated sorghum, millet, and later maize in well-watered valleys. Hunter-gatherer peoples, whose ancestors had lived in the region for tens of thousands of years, moved across deserts and mountains with intimate knowledge of plants, animals, and water sources. Iron-working communities built stone-walled settlements on hilltops, organized chiefdoms and kingdoms, and participated in trade networks that stretched from the interior plateau to the Indian Ocean coast and, ultimately, to Asia.

This chapter asks students to set aside one of the most persistent and damaging myths in the history of Southern Africa: the idea that the region was an empty or nearly empty land before European arrival, available for the taking. The archaeological and historical record tells a different story. Southern Africa before colonial rule was densely peopled by societies with sophisticated political institutions, productive economies, distinctive cultural and religious traditions, and long histories of migration, settlement, conflict, and exchange.

The chapter covers a vast geographic area and a long span of time, from the deep history of the San and Khoekhoe peoples who descend from some of the oldest continuous lineages of human habitation in Africa, through the arrival and southward movement of Bantu-speaking farming and herding communities, to the rise of centralized chiefdoms and kingdoms in the centuries before sustained European colonization. It examines how geography and environment shaped the rhythms of pastoral and agricultural life, how cattle became both an economic resource and a political instrument, how political authority was built and exercised, how trade connected interior communities to coastal and even international markets, and how kinship, gender, and ritual organized daily life.

Students should understand that "Southern Africa" in this period was never a single society speaking one language or following one way of life. It included Khoesan-speaking hunter-gatherers and herders, Bantu-speaking farming and cattle-keeping peoples organized into many distinct chiefdoms and kingdoms — ancestors of communities today identified as Zulu, Xhosa, Sotho, Tswana, Swazi, Venda, Tsonga, Ndebele, and many others — as well as communities of mixed descent that emerged from centuries of contact and intermarriage along the frontier zones where these different peoples met.

The evidence historians use to reconstruct this history comes from multiple sources: archaeology, including excavated settlements, iron-smelting sites, and rock art; oral traditions preserved and transmitted across generations; early written accounts left by Portuguese, Dutch, and other European visitors and traders from the sixteenth century onward; linguistic evidence that traces the movement and relationships of language groups; and, for the centuries closest to colonial contact, the records of missionaries, travelers, and colonial administrators — sources that must be read critically, since their authors often misunderstood or deliberately distorted African societies to justify dispossession.

Understanding Southern Africa before colonial rule matters for reasons that extend well beyond antiquarian interest. The structures examined in this chapter — chiefdoms, cattle economies, kinship systems, patterns of land use, and forms of political authority — did not vanish with colonization. They were attacked, reshaped, and partially destroyed by colonial conquest and apartheid, but they also persisted, adapted, and continued to shape Southern African societies into the twentieth and twenty-first centuries. The history in this chapter is, in important ways, also the history of the present.

Historical Context

The human history of Southern Africa is among the longest and most continuous in the world. The ancestors of the San and Khoekhoe peoples — often grouped together under the linguistic label Khoesan — have occupied the region for tens of thousands of years, making them among the oldest continuous lineages of human habitation anywhere on Earth. For most of this long span, these communities lived by hunting, gathering, and, in the case of the Khoekhoe, herding sheep and cattle, organizing themselves in small, mobile, egalitarian bands adapted to the often arid environments of the western and southwestern regions of the subcontinent.

Beginning roughly two thousand years ago, and continuing in waves over the following centuries, Bantu-speaking peoples moved southward into the region as part of the broader Bantu expansion examined elsewhere in this textbook. These migrants brought with them iron-working technology, settled agriculture based on grain crops such as sorghum and millet, and cattle-keeping. They settled first in the better-watered eastern and northeastern regions of Southern Africa, where rainfall reliably supported crop cultivation, and gradually established farming and herding communities that interacted, traded, intermarried, and sometimes competed with the existing Khoesan populations.

By the first millennium CE, archaeological evidence shows increasingly settled, iron-using, cattle-keeping communities across much of present-day South Africa, Zimbabwe, Botswana, Mozambique, eSwatini, and Lesotho. Over the following centuries, these communities developed increasingly complex political structures. Some, such as the builders of Mapungubwe (examined in a separate chapter), developed substantial trading states linked to the Indian Ocean commercial system. Others remained organized as smaller, more decentralized chiefdoms.

By the time this chapter's main narrative begins — roughly the fifteenth through early nineteenth centuries — Southern Africa was a mosaic of societies at different scales of political organization: small, kin-based communities; larger chiefdoms with established ruling lineages; and, in some periods and places, more centralized kingdoms capable of mobilizing significant military and economic resources. This diversity, and the dynamic processes of migration, conflict, alliance, and trade that produced it, form the central subject of this chapter.

It is essential for students to understand that this history unfolded almost entirely independent of European influence until at least the seventeenth century, when Dutch settlement at the Cape began a slow process of frontier contact, conflict, and, eventually, dispossession that would intensify dramatically in the nineteenth century. The societies described in this chapter were not waiting for European contact to begin their histories; they had been making their own history, on their own terms, for many centuries.

Section 37.1

Southern African Landscapes

The physical geography of Southern Africa is varied and, in many respects, demanding. Much of the interior plateau lies at a substantial elevation, producing a temperate but highly seasonal climate with a single, often unreliable, summer rainy season. Rainfall decreases sharply from east to west: the eastern lowveld and highveld receive enough rain in most years to support grain agriculture, while the western regions — the Kalahari and Karoo — are arid or semi-arid, suited mainly to grazing and to the mobile, opportunistic foraging and herding strategies long practiced by Khoesan communities.

This east-west gradient in rainfall produced a corresponding gradient in economic strategy and population density. Bantu-speaking farming and herding communities concentrated in the better-watered eastern third of the subcontinent, where reliable summer rains supported sorghum, millet, and, eventually, maize cultivation alongside cattle-keeping. Further west and south, Khoekhoe pastoralists relied more heavily on mobile herding of sheep and cattle, moving their herds seasonally to follow available grazing and water. San hunter-gatherer communities occupied the most arid zones, including the Kalahari, where dense agricultural settlement was never possible and where intimate, highly specialized ecological knowledge was essential for survival.

The Drakensberg escarpment, running along the eastern edge of the highveld, created a significant barrier separating the interior plateau from the narrow, well-watered coastal strip along the Indian Ocean. This geography shaped patterns of settlement, defense, and political consolidation: communities in defensible highland or escarpment locations were often better positioned to resist raiding and to control surrounding lowland populations, a pattern visible in the rise of states such as the Sotho kingdom in the Lesotho highlands.

Rivers in Southern Africa, while important locally for water and fertile alluvial soils, were generally less navigable and less central to long-distance transport than the great river systems of West and Central Africa. Movement of people, cattle, and goods therefore depended heavily on overland routes, and control of strategic mountain passes, river crossings, and grazing corridors became an important source of political and economic power.

Climate variability — particularly the unpredictability of summer rainfall and the recurrence of severe multi-year droughts — was a constant feature of life across the region and a recurring driver of migration, conflict, and political change, a theme this chapter returns to repeatedly.

Section 37.2

Migration and Settlement

The settlement history of Southern Africa is a story of successive waves of migration and gradual, often contested, intermixture. The earliest inhabitants, ancestral to the San, occupied the region for tens of thousands of years as hunter-gatherers, developing extraordinarily detailed knowledge of plants, animals, water sources, and seasonal cycles across nearly every environment the subcontinent offered, from coastal forests to deep desert.

Sometime in the first centuries of the Common Era, communities ancestral to the Khoekhoe acquired livestock — likely sheep first, then cattle — through contact with farming peoples further north, and adopted a pastoral way of life centered on the western and southwestern regions of the subcontinent. This shift to pastoralism did not replace San hunting and gathering everywhere; rather, San and Khoekhoe communities coexisted, traded, intermarried, and sometimes competed across many centuries, producing complex and fluid social boundaries that early European observers, looking for simple racial or tribal categories, frequently misunderstood.

Beginning roughly two thousand years ago, Bantu-speaking farming and herding communities began moving into Southern Africa from the north, part of the broader migratory process examined in the chapter on the Bantu migrations. These communities settled first in the well-watered eastern regions, gradually establishing farming villages, cattle posts, and, over time, larger chiefdoms. Their movement southward was not a single event but a centuries-long process of settlement, subdivision, and further migration as population growth, succession disputes, and the search for new grazing and farming land repeatedly pushed groups to establish new communities.

By the time of sustained contact with Khoesan populations, Bantu-speaking communities had developed distinct linguistic and cultural groupings — ancestors of the Nguni-speaking peoples (including Zulu and Xhosa communities) along the eastern coastal regions, and Sotho-Tswana-speaking peoples across much of the interior plateau. These groupings were not rigid or unchanging; chiefdoms split, merged, and reorganized constantly, and individuals and even entire communities could and did move between political allegiances.

Section 37.3

Cattle Economies and Pastoral Life

Across much of Bantu-speaking Southern Africa, cattle occupied a position in economic and social life that is difficult to overstate. Cattle were a store of wealth, a medium through which marriages were sealed, a source of milk and occasionally meat, a measure of status, a focus of ritual and religious practice, and, crucially, an instrument of political power. A chief or king's authority depended significantly on his ability to accumulate cattle through tribute, raiding, or gift, and to redistribute that wealth strategically to build loyalty among followers.

The institution of lobola — the transfer of cattle from a groom's family to a bride's family to formalize a marriage — illustrates how thoroughly cattle were woven into the fabric of social relationships. Lobola was not simply a "bride price" in any crude commercial sense; it created enduring bonds of obligation and alliance between families, legitimated the status of children born of the marriage, and gave women's families an ongoing stake in a marriage's success. The size and composition of a lobola payment reflected and reinforced social status.

Cattle-keeping also structured patterns of land use and settlement. Homesteads were typically organized around a central cattle enclosure (often called a kraal), with cattle moved seasonally between near pastures and more distant grazing areas depending on rainfall and the condition of the land — a pattern of mobility historians describe using the broader pastoral concept of transhumance. Young men were frequently responsible for herding and protecting cattle at distant grazing posts, an experience that doubled as informal military training and a rite of passage toward full adult status.

Because cattle were so central to wealth and status, disputes over grazing land, cattle theft, and the right to cattle obtained through raiding were among the most common causes of conflict between communities, and successful cattle raiding could rapidly enhance a chief's power and reputation — a dynamic that helps explain the scale of conflict during the upheavals of the early nineteenth century discussed later in this chapter.

Section 37.4

Political Systems and Leadership

Political organization across pre-colonial Southern Africa ranged from small, loosely organized chiefdoms governing a few thousand people to substantial kingdoms capable of mobilizing large armies and controlling extensive territories. Despite this range in scale, several common institutional features recur across many Bantu-speaking societies in the region.

Most communities were organized around hereditary chieftainship, typically passed through a ruling lineage according to rules of succession that, while generally favoring sons of a chief's senior wife, were frequently contested in practice, producing succession disputes that could lead to the splitting of chiefdoms as defeated claimants moved away to establish new, independent communities. This pattern of "fission" — the repeated splitting off of new chiefdoms from existing ones — was a major mechanism by which Bantu-speaking societies spread across and settled new territory over the centuries.

Chiefs did not rule as absolute autocrats. Councils of senior men, often including representatives of important lineages within the chiefdom, advised and constrained chiefly authority, and a chief who ruled badly, failed to protect his people, or alienated key supporters risked losing followers to rival chiefs or being deposed altogether. Public assemblies, at which ordinary male community members could voice grievances and opinions, were an important — though not universal — feature of political life in many communities, a tradition sometimes compared, with appropriate caution against overstatement, to participatory deliberative practices in other parts of the world.

Political authority depended heavily on a chief's ability to attract and retain followers through patronage: redistributing cattle, granting access to land, offering protection, and arbitrating disputes fairly. Because followers in many of these societies retained some capacity to "vote with their feet" by relocating to a different chief's territory if they judged their treatment unjust, successful rulers needed to balance the accumulation of personal power against the practical necessity of legitimacy and consent.

Beginning in the early nineteenth century, this generally decentralized pattern of relatively small, competing chiefdoms gave way in some regions to far more centralized states — most dramatically the Zulu kingdom under Shaka, but also the Sotho kingdom under Moshoeshoe and several other significant polities. These more centralized kingdoms developed standing military organizations, more systematic tribute collection, and stronger mechanisms for incorporating conquered or allied communities, representing a significant transformation in the scale and intensity of political authority across the region.

Section 37.5

Trade and Regional Exchange

Although Southern Africa is sometimes imagined as isolated from broader currents of African and world trade, the historical record shows extensive networks of exchange connecting interior communities to one another and, ultimately, to the Indian Ocean commercial world examined in earlier chapters of this textbook.

Within the interior, communities exchanged cattle, iron tools and weapons, copper ornaments, salt, tobacco, dagga (cannabis), specularite (a mineral used as a cosmetic and ritual pigment), and foodstuffs across considerable distances, often through networks of reciprocal gift-giving and kinship obligation as much as through direct commercial barter. San hunter-gatherer communities frequently participated in these networks as well, trading specialized forest and desert products — ostrich eggshell beads, honey, animal skins, and medicinal plants — for cattle products, iron goods, or grain from farming neighbors.

Longer-distance trade connected the interior plateau to the Indian Ocean coast, particularly through intermediary trading communities along routes leading toward present-day Mozambique. Ivory was the most significant export good moving from the interior toward the coast, supplying demand from Swahili and ultimately South Asian and Middle Eastern markets, as documented in the chapter on the Swahili Coast and Africa's participation in the Indian Ocean world. In exchange, coastal and Indian Ocean goods — glass beads, cloth, and occasionally metal items — moved into the interior, where archaeologists have recovered them at sites far from the coast, demonstrating the reach of these exchange networks centuries before sustained European contact.

Control over trade routes and trade goods, much like control over cattle, became an important source of political power. Chiefdoms and kingdoms positioned along key routes — including the Sotho kingdom in the highlands and various Tsonga-speaking communities closer to the Mozambican coast — could tax or broker the movement of goods, accumulating wealth and prestige that reinforced their political authority.

Section 37.6

Community, Kinship, and Culture

Daily life across pre-colonial Southern African societies was organized around the homestead — typically a cluster of huts and cattle enclosures occupied by an extended family group under the authority of a senior male head, often with multiple wives in polygynous marriages, each maintaining her own household within the larger homestead. Polygyny was both an economic and a social institution: additional wives expanded a household's agricultural labor force and its network of kinship alliances, and the number of a man's wives and children was an important marker of social status.

Most Bantu-speaking Southern African societies organized descent and inheritance patrilineally, tracing lineage membership through the father's line, though the specific rules governing inheritance, succession, and the relative authority of a man's different wives and their children varied considerably between communities. Women, while generally excluded from formal political leadership in most though not all communities, exercised substantial practical authority within the household, controlled agricultural production on lands allocated to them, and in some societies held recognized ritual or advisory roles, including as rainmakers or as senior counselors to chiefs.

Initiation rituals marking the transition from childhood to adulthood were widespread and significant across many Southern African societies, often organized around age-sets — groups of young people initiated together who retained social bonds and mutual obligations throughout their lives. These initiation processes typically included periods of seclusion, instruction in community values and practical skills, and physical trials, and they served to bind individuals into broader networks of social obligation that extended well beyond their immediate families.

Religious and ritual life centered on belief in ancestral spirits, who were understood to remain actively involved in the lives of their living descendants, requiring regular ritual attention, including sacrifice of cattle, to maintain favor and avert misfortune. Diviners and healers played important roles in diagnosing the causes of illness, misfortune, and social conflict, often attributing such troubles to ancestral displeasure, witchcraft, or breaches of social and ritual obligation, and prescribing remedies that combined herbal treatment with ritual and social reconciliation.

Section 37.7

Technology and Subsistence

Southern African Bantu-speaking communities possessed well-developed iron-working technology, smelting iron ore in furnaces to produce tools, weapons, and implements essential to farming, herding, hunting, and warfare. Iron hoes made grain cultivation more efficient; iron spear points and, later, the short stabbing spear associated with reorganized Zulu military tactics, transformed warfare; and iron axes facilitated the clearing of land and the working of wood for construction.

Agricultural technology centered on hoe cultivation of sorghum and millet, supplemented over time by the adoption of maize, which had arrived in Africa following the Columbian Exchange and gradually spread inland, eventually becoming a dominant staple crop in many regions because of its high yields under favorable rainfall conditions, though its greater vulnerability to drought compared to traditional sorghum varieties introduced new agricultural risks.

Khoesan hunter-gatherer and pastoralist technologies were highly specialized for mobility and for efficient use of scarce resources: lightweight bows with poisoned arrows for hunting, detailed knowledge of edible and medicinal plants, sophisticated tracking skills, and, among Khoekhoe pastoralists, portable housing structures suited to a seasonally mobile herding economy.

Stone-walled settlements, particularly associated with Sotho-Tswana-speaking communities on the interior plateau, represented a distinctive architectural and engineering tradition, with extensive networks of stone-walled homesteads, cattle enclosures, and terraced agricultural features whose remains are still visible across parts of the highveld today and which provide archaeologists with valuable evidence of settlement scale and organization in the centuries before colonial contact.

Section 37.8

Environmental Adaptation

Southern African societies developed a range of strategies for managing the region's considerable environmental variability, particularly the recurring threat of drought. Pastoral communities practiced transhumance, moving herds seasonally between different grazing areas to balance the availability of water and forage across the year and to allow overgrazed pastures time to recover. Farming communities diversified their crops, planting drought-resistant sorghum and millet alongside, in later centuries, higher-yielding but more vulnerable maize, hedging against the failure of any single crop in a poor rainfall year.

Rainmaking rituals, often conducted by specialized ritual specialists working closely with chiefs, served both a practical function — attempting, through ritual means understood within the community's own belief system, to influence rainfall — and an important political and social function, reinforcing the legitimacy of chiefs who were seen as responsible for the wellbeing of their communities, including the success of the harvest and the health of the herds.

Severe, sustained drought could destabilize entire regions, triggering competition over remaining water and grazing resources, increased cattle raiding, and significant population movement as communities sought more reliable conditions elsewhere. Historians studying the major upheavals of the early nineteenth century have pointed to evidence of unusually severe drought conditions in the preceding years as one important contributing factor among several, a topic examined further in the Historical Debate feature below.

Communities also adapted architecturally and socially to environmental conditions, building homesteads in locations offering natural defense, reliable water access, and protection from extreme weather, and developing social norms — including widespread practices of reciprocal gift-giving, clientship, and the dispersal of cattle among trusted associates in different locations — that functioned, among other purposes, as informal insurance against localized environmental disaster.

Section 37.9

Diversity in Southern Africa

This chapter has deliberately resisted treating Southern Africa as a single, uniform society, because no such uniformity existed. The region encompassed Khoesan hunter-gatherers and herders with deep and distinct histories of their own; Nguni-speaking communities along the eastern coastal regions, including the ancestors of Zulu- and Xhosa-speaking peoples; Sotho-Tswana-speaking communities across much of the interior plateau; Tsonga-speaking communities closer to the Mozambican coast; Venda-speaking communities in the far northeast with their own distinctive political and religious traditions, including connections to the legacy of Mapungubwe and Great Zimbabwe; and numerous smaller, sometimes short-lived chiefdoms that formed, split, merged, and disappeared across the centuries.

Even within broad linguistic groupings, enormous political, social, and economic diversity existed. Some communities were organized as small, decentralized chiefdoms with limited hierarchy; others, particularly from the early nineteenth century onward, developed into substantial centralized kingdoms with standing armies and elaborate court structures. Some communities depended heavily on cattle-keeping; others balanced cattle-keeping with substantial grain agriculture or, in arid zones, depended primarily on hunting, gathering, and small-stock herding.

This diversity is historically important for several reasons. It corrects the colonial-era tendency, still influential in some popular understanding, to treat African societies as interchangeable or undifferentiated. It also helps explain why European colonization, when it intensified in the nineteenth century, encountered such varied African responses: some communities chose military resistance, others diplomatic accommodation, others strategic alliance with one colonial or settler power against another or against rival African states — choices that reflected each community's particular history, resources, and political circumstances rather than any single "African" response to colonialism.

Historical Vocabulary
Mfecane / Difaqane

The Mfecane (a Zulu-derived term, also widely known by the Sotho-Tswana term Difaqane) refers to a period of intense warfare, political consolidation, and large-scale migration that swept across much of Southern Africa roughly between the 1810s and 1840s. The period produced several powerful new centralized states, including the Zulu kingdom under Shaka and the Sotho kingdom under Moshoeshoe, while also causing significant population displacement, loss of life, and disruption to existing communities.

Historians no longer accept the once-common colonial-era narrative that portrayed the Mfecane as a spontaneous outbreak of uncontrolled "tribal" violence unrelated to outside pressures. Modern scholarship situates the Mfecane within a broader context that includes drought, competition over trade and resources, and — importantly — the destabilizing effects of expanding frontier trade and slave-raiding pressures linked to Delagoa Bay and, more controversially, early Cape colonial expansion. See the Historical Debate feature below for further discussion of this contested topic.

Historical Vocabulary
Lobola

Lobola refers to the transfer of cattle (and, in some contexts and later periods, other forms of wealth) from a groom's family to a bride's family to formalize a marriage in many Southern African Bantu-speaking societies. Far more than a simple economic transaction, lobola created lasting bonds of mutual obligation between two families, legitimated the marriage and the status of children born within it, and gave the bride's family an ongoing interest in her wellbeing and the marriage's success. The practice persisted, in adapted forms, well into the colonial and postcolonial periods and remains culturally significant in many Southern African communities today.

Primary Source Window

An Early Visitor's Account of an Interior Chiefdom (Early Nineteenth Century)

Creator: An unnamed European trader/traveler · Date: early nineteenth century · Type: Travel account / trade journal

Context: As Cape colonial frontiers expanded and trade with interior chiefdoms grew in the early nineteenth century, a number of European traders, missionaries, and travelers recorded their observations of Southern African societies. These accounts are valuable primary sources, but they were written by outsiders with limited language ability, commercial motives, and assumptions shaped by European ideas about "civilization" and hierarchy.

Such travelers commonly described being received formally by a chief and his counselors, having the purpose of their visit and the goods they carried examined carefully before any trade was permitted, and observing large herds of cattle, organized homesteads, and structured systems of tribute and exchange that surprised visitors who had expected to find no organized society at all.

Analysis: Accounts like this one are useful because they offer near-contemporary, eyewitness description of political protocol, economic organization, and material life in interior chiefdoms before extensive colonial disruption. However, they are limited by the observer's outsider status, commercial self-interest, frequent reliance on interpreters of uncertain accuracy, and tendency to judge African political and economic life against European assumptions. Historians must read such sources against archaeological evidence and African oral tradition wherever possible to construct a fuller and more balanced picture.

Historical Debate

What Caused the Mfecane?

The question: The early nineteenth-century period of warfare, migration, and state formation known as the Mfecane or Difaqane caused enormous disruption across Southern Africa. Historians have long debated its underlying causes.

Interpretation A — Internal Dynamics: Some historians emphasize internal factors: population growth straining available farmland and grazing, recurring drought cycles reducing resources, and the political innovations of ambitious leaders such as Shaka, whose military reorganization of Zulu forces around age-regiments and a short stabbing spear enabled unprecedented conquest and triggered a chain reaction of displacement as defeated and threatened communities fled and, in turn, displaced others.
Interpretation B — External Pressures: Other historians argue that internal explanations alone are insufficient and point to the destabilizing effects of expanding external trade, particularly a growing slave trade connected to Delagoa Bay and Portuguese and other traders, as well as pressures generated by early Cape colonial frontier expansion, including the displacement of communities by encroaching settler farms and the increased availability of European trade goods that altered the calculus of raiding and conquest.

Evidence: Evidence for Interpretation A includes oral traditions describing Shaka's military innovations and archaeological/climatic evidence of drought. Evidence for Interpretation B includes documentary records of growing Delagoa Bay trade volume and the timing of frontier expansion near the Cape relative to the onset of major upheaval further east.

Why it matters: The interpretation chosen affects whether the Mfecane is understood primarily as an internally generated African political transformation or as substantially shaped by the disruptive reach of expanding global and colonial trade networks — with significant implications for how historians narrate African agency versus external causation in this period.

Student task: Based on the evidence summarized above, which interpretation do you find more persuasive, or do you think both operated together? What additional evidence would help resolve the debate?

Multiple Perspectives

Cattle, Wealth, and Power in Southern African Society

From the perspective of a chief or ruling lineage: Cattle accumulated through tribute and successful raiding represented the foundation of political power, allowing redistribution to followers, payment for military service, and the cementing of alliances through strategic marriages.
From the perspective of a young herder: Years spent herding cattle at distant posts represented both hard, risky labor — exposed to raiding, predators, and weather — and a recognized pathway toward eventual independent adult status, marriage, and access to cattle of one's own.
From the perspective of a woman within a homestead: Lobola cattle paid for her marriage represented her family's recognition of her value and secured her status and her children's legitimacy, even as women themselves generally had no direct ownership claim over the cattle exchanged on their behalf.
From the perspective of a community displaced by cattle raiding: The loss of cattle herds could mean not only economic ruin but social collapse — the inability to pay lobola, sustain alliances, or maintain status — making cattle raiding one of the most consequential and feared forms of conflict in the region.
Cause and Effect

The Mfecane and Its Consequences

Long-Term Causes

Population growth and competition for farmland and grazing across the eastern regions; recurring drought cycles; long-standing competition among chiefdoms over cattle and trade access.

Short-Term Causes

Shaka's military reorganization of Zulu forces; intensified Delagoa Bay trade and slave-raiding pressure; early Cape frontier expansion displacing communities near colonial boundaries.

The Event

Roughly two decades (c. 1815–1835) of widespread warfare, conquest, and mass migration across much of the eastern and interior subcontinent.

Effects

Formation of powerful new centralized kingdoms (Zulu, Sotho, others); significant loss of life and large-scale displacement; depopulation of some regions later claimed by settlers as "empty land"; long-term reshaping of the region's political map.

Historical complexity: The Mfecane cannot be reduced to the actions of any single leader. It resulted from the interaction of environmental pressure, internal political ambition, and external trade and colonial dynamics operating simultaneously across a wide region.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Pastoral and Agricultural Life from the Early Iron Age to the Eve of Colonization

What Changed

  • Political organization shifted from small, scattered chiefdoms toward more centralized kingdoms in some regions by the early nineteenth century
  • Maize gradually supplemented and in places displaced sorghum and millet as a staple crop
  • Trade volume and the variety of imported goods (beads, cloth) increased over time through expanding Indian Ocean and, later, Cape frontier trade
  • Military organization in some kingdoms became more standing and professionalized

What Stayed the Same

  • Cattle remained the central measure of wealth, status, and political power
  • Lobola and patrilineal kinship structures continued to organize marriage and inheritance
  • Transhumant herding strategies remained essential to managing environmental variability
  • Ancestral veneration and ritual specialists continued to play central roles in religious and political life

Why the pattern matters: Recognizing both transformation and persistence helps students avoid two opposite errors: assuming pre-colonial Southern Africa was static and unchanging, or assuming that political centralization erased older cultural and economic foundations rather than building upon them.

Everyday Life and Social History

A Day in a Southern African Homestead

For most people in pre-colonial Southern Africa, daily life centered on the rhythms of the homestead rather than on the dramatic political events that dominate so much historical narrative. Women typically rose early to begin grinding grain, fetching water, and tending garden plots planted with sorghum, millet, pumpkins, and other crops, while also caring for young children and managing the household's daily food preparation, often including the brewing of sorghum beer used in both everyday consumption and important ritual occasions.

Men and older boys were frequently responsible for herding cattle, a task that could take them away from the central homestead to distant grazing posts for extended periods, particularly during dry seasons when better grazing lay farther afield. Younger boys often began herding smaller stock such as goats close to home before graduating to cattle-herding responsibilities as they grew older, an important stage in their progression toward full adult status.

Evenings often brought communities together around fires for storytelling, the recitation of praise poetry honoring chiefs and ancestors, and the transmission of history, values, and practical knowledge from elders to younger generations — an oral tradition that served, in the absence of written records, as the primary means by which Southern African societies preserved and passed on their own historical memory.

Illness, drought, conflict, and the ordinary uncertainties of agricultural and pastoral life were managed through a combination of practical skill, communal mutual aid, and religious and ritual practice, including consultation with diviners and the maintenance of proper relationships with ancestral spirits believed to watch over the wellbeing of the living.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has examined the societies of Southern Africa in the centuries before colonial rule intensified and ultimately overwhelmed African political independence in the region. It has shown that the subcontinent was home to a long and diverse human history, beginning with San hunter-gatherers and Khoekhoe pastoralists whose ancestors had occupied the region for tens of thousands of years, and continuing through the southward migration and settlement of Bantu-speaking farming and herding communities over the past two thousand years.

The chapter has emphasized that geography and climate — particularly the marked east-west gradient in rainfall — shaped the economic strategies available to different communities, producing a varied landscape of pastoral, agricultural, and mixed economies adapted to local environmental conditions, with cattle occupying an especially central place in the economic, social, political, and ritual life of most Bantu-speaking societies.

Political organization across the region ranged from small, decentralized chiefdoms governed through councils of elders and sustained by the loyalty of followers, to substantial centralized kingdoms — most dramatically the Zulu state under Shaka and the Sotho kingdom under Moshoeshoe — that emerged especially during the upheavals of the early nineteenth-century Mfecane or Difaqane. Historians continue to debate the relative weight of internal dynamics, such as drought and political ambition, against external pressures, such as expanding Delagoa Bay trade and early Cape frontier expansion, in explaining this period of intense transformation.

Trade connected Southern African communities not only to one another but, through ivory and other goods moving toward the Indian Ocean coast, to the much larger commercial world examined elsewhere in this textbook, demonstrating that the region's history cannot be understood in isolation from broader African and global processes.

Kinship, marriage, gender roles, and ritual life organized daily existence at the level of the homestead, with patrilineal descent, lobola marriage payments, polygynous households, age-set initiation, and ancestral veneration recurring as widespread, though not universal, features across many communities.

Above all, this chapter has insisted on the diversity of Southern African societies, rejecting any portrayal of the region as culturally or politically uniform, and has shown that the structures examined here — chiefdoms, cattle economies, kinship systems — did not disappear with colonization but persisted, in adapted form, well into the modern era, continuing to shape Southern African societies long after sustained European colonization began in the mid-to-late nineteenth century.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Pastoralism
An economic system centered on herding livestock, often involving seasonal movement to access grazing and water.
Transhumance
The seasonal movement of livestock and herders between different grazing areas in response to rainfall and resource availability.
Chiefdom
A political community led by a hereditary chief, typically smaller and less centralized than a kingdom.
Kingdom
A larger, more centralized political state, often incorporating multiple subordinate chiefdoms under a paramount ruler.
Lineage
A group of people who trace descent from a common ancestor, often the basis for inheritance and political succession.
Lobola
The transfer of cattle from a groom's family to a bride's family to formalize marriage in many Southern African societies.
Homestead
The basic residential and economic unit of many Southern African societies, typically comprising an extended family and its cattle.
Mfecane / Difaqane
A period of widespread warfare, migration, and state formation in early nineteenth-century Southern Africa.
Tribute
Goods, labor, or cattle owed by subordinate communities or individuals to a ruling authority.
Initiation
Ritual processes marking the transition from childhood to adulthood in many Southern African societies.
Age-set
A group of individuals initiated into adulthood together, sharing lifelong social bonds and obligations.
Patrilineal descent
A kinship system tracing lineage membership and inheritance through the father's line.
Khoesan
A linguistic and cultural grouping encompassing San hunter-gatherer and Khoekhoe pastoralist peoples.
Bantu expansion
The centuries-long migration and settlement of Bantu-speaking farming and herding peoples across sub-Saharan Africa.
Indian Ocean trade
The commercial network connecting eastern and southern African interior and coastal communities with Arabia, India, and Asia.
Iron Age
The period marked by the adoption of iron-working technology, beginning in Southern Africa roughly two thousand years ago.
Drought cycle
Recurring periods of reduced rainfall that significantly affected agricultural and pastoral production and could trigger conflict and migration.
Polygyny
The practice of a man having multiple wives, common in many pre-colonial Southern African societies.
Council of elders
A body of senior men who advised and constrained the authority of a chief.
Frontier zone
A region of contact, exchange, and conflict between different societies, such as the expanding Cape colonial frontier.
Stock theft
The raiding or theft of cattle, a common cause of conflict between communities.
Rainmaking
Ritual practices intended to influence rainfall, often linked to chiefly authority and legitimacy.
Clientship
A relationship in which a less powerful individual or group attaches themselves to a more powerful patron in exchange for protection and resources.
Diviner
A ritual specialist who diagnosed the causes of illness or misfortune, often attributing them to ancestral or social causes.
Praise poetry
An oral literary tradition honoring chiefs, ancestors, and significant events, serving as a form of historical memory.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter has shown that Southern Africa before colonial rule was neither empty nor static. It was a region of deep human history, diverse economic strategies, sophisticated political institutions, and dynamic processes of migration, conflict, and state formation that unfolded largely independent of European influence until at least the seventeenth century and that continued to evolve dramatically right up to the eve of intensified colonial conquest.

Understanding this history matters because the myth of "empty land," used for generations to justify settler dispossession and colonial conquest in Southern Africa, depended on erasing exactly the kind of evidence presented in this chapter: dense populations, productive economies, organized political authority, and long-distance trade connections. Recovering this history is not merely an academic correction; it is essential to understanding the moral and historical stakes of the colonial conquest and dispossession examined in later chapters of this textbook.

This chapter also illustrates broader themes that recur throughout *Africa: A History of a Continent*: the central importance of cattle and pastoral economies in shaping political power across much of the continent; the constant interplay between environmental conditions and human political and economic choices; the way internal African dynamics and external trade pressures often operated together rather than as separate, competing explanations; and the necessity of treating African societies with the same attention to internal diversity and historical complexity that students would expect when studying any other region of the world.

Finally, the structures examined here — chiefly authority, cattle economies, kinship and lobola, ritual and ancestral veneration — did not vanish with colonization. They were attacked, distorted, and partially dismantled by colonial rule and later by apartheid, but they also adapted and persisted, continuing to shape Southern African societies into the present. The history of pre-colonial Southern Africa is, in this sense, not a closed chapter but a living foundation for understanding the region today.

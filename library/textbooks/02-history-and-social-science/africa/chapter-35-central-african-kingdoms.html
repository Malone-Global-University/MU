Part VI — East, Central, and Southern Africa
35

Central African Kingdoms

Africa: A History of a Continent

Chapter Opening

"Among the Luba and the Lunda, political authority was not simply seized by force and held by fear. It was built, generation after generation, through institutions of kinship, sacred office, and tribute that bound distant communities to a center they had never seen."

In the woodland savannas south of the great equatorial forest, between the upper reaches of the Congo River system and the headwaters of the Zambezi, generations of farmers, fishers, hunters, and blacksmiths built some of the most durable and influential political systems in precolonial Central Africa. Today the region is divided among the modern states of the Democratic Republic of the Congo, Angola, and Zambia, but for several centuries before European colonization, much of this vast interior was organized — loosely in some places, tightly in others — around two related and overlapping political traditions: the kingdom of the Luba people, centered near Lake Kisale and the upper Lualaba River, and the kingdom of the Lunda, whose paramount ruler, the Mwaant Yaav, presided over a sprawling network of tributary chiefdoms stretching from the Kasai River basin deep into what is now northeastern Angola and northwestern Zambia.

These were not small chiefdoms governing a single village or valley. The Luba kingdom, at its height in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, integrated dozens of formerly independent communities under a paramount ruler whose authority rested on claims of sacred descent, control of trade in salt, copper, and iron, and a genius for political incorporation that allowed conquered or allied groups to retain local identity while accepting a place within a larger order. The Lunda kingdom, built in part by leaders trained in Luba political ideas, went further still, exporting a model of statecraft — the institution of "positional succession," in which titles and the political relationships attached to them passed to a successor rather than disappearing with an individual's death — across a territory larger than many contemporary European states. By the eighteenth century, Lunda-derived political models had spread westward to the Kingdom of Kasanje and other communities in the Kwango and Kwilu river valleys, and eastward to peoples along tributaries of the Zambezi, carried not primarily through conquest but through the deliberate movement of ambitious leaders who carried Lunda titles, symbols, and methods of rule into new territories and adapted them to local circumstances.

This chapter asks how peoples in this enormous and ecologically varied interior region built lasting states without many of the tools historians once assumed were necessary for large-scale political organization — without widespread literacy, without large standing armies of the kind found in some West African empires, and often without the dense urban centers that characterized contemporaneous societies on the Swahili coast or in the West African Sahel. The answer lies substantially in the flexibility and portability of certain political ideas: sacred kingship, structured tribute relationships, titled offices that could be filled and refilled across generations, and kinship systems that linked communities across great distances even where direct administrative control was thin or absent.

The chapter also asks who built these systems, who benefited from them, and who bore their costs. Central African kingship was rarely the simple, uncontested authority of court traditions and royal praise poetry suggests. Succession disputes were common and sometimes violent. Tributary relationships, while often providing real benefits in trade access and protection from raiding, could also be experienced as extraction by communities required to send goods, labor, or even people to a distant court. Women held significant ritual, political, and economic roles in many Central African systems — the Lunda royal capital itself depended on female titleholders whose offices were essential to legitimating succession — even as most paramount rulers were male. Understanding Central African kingdoms requires holding together both the genuine political sophistication these societies achieved and the real tensions, inequalities, and conflicts that ran through them.

Finally, the chapter situates Luba and Lunda history within larger continental and global processes. These kingdoms did not develop in isolation. They sat astride trade routes connecting the copper-rich Katanga region to markets on the Atlantic coast and, through intermediary peoples, to the Indian Ocean trading world. From the seventeenth century onward, growing Atlantic demand for slaves reached deep into the Central African interior, reshaping relationships between Lunda authorities and neighboring peoples and contributing to patterns of raiding and captive-taking whose consequences these societies and their descendants would feel for generations. The history of the Luba and Lunda kingdoms is therefore a history of indigenous political achievement and a history entangled, increasingly, with the wider Atlantic world.

Evidence for this history comes from several different kinds of sources, each with strengths and limitations that students should weigh carefully. Oral traditions preserved by royal genealogists and recorded by twentieth-century researchers preserve king-lists, origin narratives, and accounts of political institutions, though such traditions were often shaped to legitimate the authority of ruling lineages at the time they were recorded. Archaeological excavation at sites associated with early Luba political centers, particularly around the Upemba Depression, has revealed evidence of long-distance trade, craft specialization, and social differentiation reaching back many centuries before written accounts existed. Accounts by Portuguese traders and later European travelers, beginning in the seventeenth century, provide outside observation of Lunda court life, though these observers brought their own assumptions and often misunderstood institutions they described. Linguistic and comparative ethnographic evidence helps historians trace the spread of political titles and concepts across related Central African societies. Using these sources together, rather than relying on any single type, allows historians to reconstruct a much fuller picture than any one source could provide alone.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Describe the physical and ecological environment of Central Africa and explain how it shaped settlement, agriculture, and political organization.
  2. Trace the origins and development of the Luba kingdom, including the archaeological and oral-traditional evidence for early state formation in the Upemba Depression region.
  3. Explain how the Lunda kingdom developed from Luba political models and expanded across a vast territory through the spread of titled offices rather than primarily through military conquest.
  4. Analyze the structure and significance of regional and long-distance trade networks connecting Central Africa to the Atlantic coast, the Zambezi valley, and the broader Indian Ocean trading world.
  5. Compare systems of kingship, administration, and succession in the Luba and Lunda states, including the institution of positional succession.
  6. Evaluate the role of religion, sacred authority, and ritual office in legitimating political power in Central African kingdoms.
  7. Identify patterns of migration and political expansion associated with Lunda-derived states such as Kasanje, the Imbangala kingdoms, and eastern Lunda offshoots.
  8. Describe the social and cultural life of Central African communities, including the roles of women, blacksmiths, hunters, and titled officials.
  9. Assess the impact of the expanding Atlantic slave trade on Central African political and social structures from the seventeenth century onward.
  10. Distinguish between centralized and decentralized forms of political authority and explain why Central African states combined elements of both.
  11. Interpret primary and secondary sources, including oral tradition, archaeological evidence, and European travel accounts, while accounting for the perspective and limitations of each.
  12. Connect the history of Central African kingdoms to broader patterns of African state formation and to the long-term consequences of the slave trade and colonial conquest discussed in later chapters.

Historical Context

To understand the rise of the Luba and Lunda kingdoms, students need a clear sense of the region's geography and its deeper history of settlement. Central Africa, as discussed in this textbook, refers broadly to the vast interior basin drained by the Congo River and its many tributaries, bordered by the equatorial rainforest to the north and the higher, drier savanna woodlands — known by the Portuguese term miombo — that stretch southward toward the Zambezi and Kalahari. The Luba and Lunda heartlands lay within this savanna woodland zone, a region of broad plateaus, seasonal rivers, scattered lakes, and mixed grassland-woodland vegetation well suited to a combination of farming, fishing, hunting, and cattle-keeping in some areas, though tsetse fly infestation limited cattle-keeping across much of the region.

This savanna corridor had been settled by Bantu-speaking farming and ironworking communities for well over a thousand years before the rise of the kingdoms discussed in this chapter, part of the broader Bantu expansion examined in Chapter 14. By the early centuries of the common era, communities in the Upemba Depression — a marshy lake-and-river region along the upper Lualaba River in what is now southeastern Democratic Republic of the Congo — had developed fishing, farming, ironworking, and increasingly specialized craft production. Archaeological excavations at sites such as Sanga have revealed cemeteries containing finely worked iron tools, copper ornaments, and imported items suggesting participation in long-distance exchange networks reaching back many centuries before any written account of the region exists. These excavations also reveal increasing social differentiation over time — some burials contain far more wealth and ceremonial items than others — suggesting the gradual emergence of social hierarchy well before the historically documented Luba kingdom took shape.

By roughly the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, oral traditions and archaeological evidence converge to suggest the consolidation of a more centralized Luba political order under rulers who combined control of strategic resources — fish from Lake Kisale, salt from nearby pans, iron and copper worked by skilled smiths — with religious and ritual authority. From this Luba political core, ambitious leaders, sometimes the losers of succession disputes at the Luba court, carried Luba political concepts southward and westward into new territories, where they combined them with existing local political traditions to create new states, of which the Lunda kingdom became by far the most extensive and influential.

It is important for students to understand that Central African political development did not occur in isolation from the wider continent or the wider world. Trade routes connected the copper-rich Katanga plateau, the source of the famous copper crosses and ingots that circulated as currency and prestige goods across a wide region, to communities throughout Central and even Southern Africa. From the sixteenth century onward, Portuguese traders operating from coastal bases in Angola pushed trading networks steadily into the interior, seeking ivory and, increasingly, captives for the expanding Atlantic slave trade. By the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, the Lunda kingdom sat at the intersection of these older interior trade systems and the newer, increasingly destructive demands of Atlantic commerce — a position that brought the Lunda court considerable wealth and influence even as it entangled Central African societies in patterns of violence whose costs fell disproportionately on those without political power.

Section 35.1

The Central African Environment

The political history of the Luba and Lunda kingdoms cannot be separated from the environment in which they developed. The region at the heart of this chapter lies within the broad savanna woodland belt that stretches across much of south-central Africa, distinct from the dense equatorial rainforest that dominates the central Congo basin to the north. This savanna woodland environment, characterized by widely spaced trees, seasonal grasses, and a marked dry season alternating with a wet season, supported a different mix of economic activities than the rainforest zone: more extensive cereal and root-crop agriculture, more open hunting grounds, and, in areas free of tsetse fly infestation, some cattle-keeping, though cattle were far less central to the regional economy than in East or Southern Africa.

The Upemba Depression, where the earliest documented Luba political developments occurred, was an especially rich environment: a complex of lakes, marshes, and river channels along the upper Lualaba that supported abundant fish populations, fertile alluvial soils for farming, and access to nearby salt pans and iron ore deposits. This combination of resources — reliable protein from fishing, productive agricultural land, and access to materials for tool production and trade — gave communities in this region an economic foundation unusually well suited to supporting growing populations, craft specialization, and the social differentiation that often accompanies early state formation.

Further south and west, across the broader Lunda heartland in the Kasai-Lulua river system, the environment was somewhat different: more uniform savanna woodland, with fewer of the concentrated aquatic resources found in the Upemba Depression, but still capable of supporting substantial agricultural populations cultivating sorghum, millet, and, after their introduction from the Americas via the Atlantic trade networks beginning in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, maize and cassava — crops that would become increasingly important to Central African food systems in subsequent centuries. Cassava in particular, tolerant of poor soils and resistant to some pests, gradually transformed agricultural possibilities across the region, supporting denser populations even as it sometimes reduced the nutritional diversity of diets dependent on it.

Rivers played a critical role throughout the region, not primarily as routes for large-scale water transport in the way the Nile or Niger supported riverine trade and movement, but as sources of fish, boundaries between political units, and orientation points around which settlement clustered. The Lualaba, Kasai, Lulua, Kwango, and Zambezi river systems, along with countless smaller tributaries, structured the geography of settlement, trade, and political authority across the entire region discussed in this chapter.

Mineral resources were similarly significant. The Katanga copper belt, lying within and adjacent to areas under varying degrees of Luba and later Lunda influence, contained some of the richest copper deposits in Africa. Copper, smelted and cast into standardized ingot and cross shapes, circulated widely as a form of currency and a marker of wealth and status, much as cowrie shells or gold dust functioned elsewhere on the continent. Control over copper production and trade routes became an important — though not the only — economic foundation for political power in the region, comparable in some respects to the role gold played in shaping West African Sahelian empires discussed in earlier chapters, though operating on a different scale and through different institutional mechanisms.

Section 35.2

Luba Origins and State Formation

The historical reconstruction of early Luba state formation depends on combining several different kinds of evidence, each of which illuminates part of the picture while leaving other parts uncertain. Archaeological excavation, oral tradition recorded by royal genealogists and later transcribed by researchers, and comparative linguistic evidence together suggest a long process of gradual social and political consolidation rather than a single founding event, even though Luba oral tradition itself often compresses this process into a more dramatic narrative centered on legendary founding figures.

Archaeological work at sites in the Upemba Depression, particularly the cemetery site of Sanga, has revealed a sequence of occupation stretching back to at least the seventh or eighth century CE, with burials showing increasing wealth differentiation, evidence of iron and copper working, and items suggesting participation in exchange networks reaching well beyond the immediate locality. By around the tenth to twelfth centuries, some burials contained substantially more grave goods — iron tools, copper ornaments, ceramic vessels — than others, suggesting that some lineages or individuals had begun to accumulate wealth and status considerably beyond their neighbors. Historians interpret this archaeological pattern as evidence of gradually emerging social hierarchy, though the exact relationship between this early differentiation and the later, more clearly documented Luba kingdom remains a matter of ongoing scholarly interpretation and debate.

Luba oral tradition, as recorded from royal genealogists and ritual specialists in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, presents a narrative centered on a series of culture-hero figures credited with introducing key political and technological innovations — including, in some versions, the very concept of sacred, centralized kingship — to a region previously organized around smaller, more loosely connected communities. These traditions describe conflict between an earlier, less centralized political order and a new dynasty that established more durable institutions of sacred kingship, often framing the transition as a moral and political improvement brought by outsiders or culture heroes who introduced superior methods of rule.

Historians read such origin narratives carefully, recognizing that they often serve to legitimate the authority of the ruling dynasty that commissioned or preserved them, much as royal chronicles in many world historical traditions emphasize the superiority and legitimacy of the current ruling line over predecessors. This does not mean the traditions are simply false; they likely preserve genuine memories of significant political transformation, reorganized and dramatized through generations of retelling to serve the political needs of later rulers. The most defensible historical reading combines the archaeological evidence of gradual, long-term social differentiation with the oral traditions' testimony to a real, if perhaps less sudden, process of political consolidation under increasingly centralized and sacred forms of kingship by sometime in the fifteenth or sixteenth century.

Once consolidated, the Luba political order combined several distinctive features that would later influence Lunda statecraft. Royal authority was understood as sacred, embodied in a paramount ruler whose person was hedged with ritual restrictions and whose legitimacy rested on claimed descent from founding ancestral figures. Subordinate chiefs and titleholders, drawn from particular lineages with recognized claims to specific offices, administered local communities while maintaining tribute and ritual obligations to the royal center. Specialized knowledge-keepers, including those trained in the use of the lukasa memory board described later in this chapter, preserved genealogies, political histories, and sacred narratives that reinforced the legitimacy of the ruling order. This combination of sacred kingship, titled local administration, and structured tribute relationships proved remarkably adaptable, and it was this adaptable model — rather than direct Luba territorial conquest — that spread most significantly into the wider region in subsequent generations.

Historical Vocabulary
Positional Succession

Positional succession refers to a political and social institution, especially well developed among the Lunda and related peoples, in which a titled office — along with its associated social relationships, obligations, and even, in a sense, its social "identity" — passes from one holder to a successor, who is then treated by the community as continuing that office rather than founding a new one.

Under positional succession, when a titleholder died, a successor would be installed who took on not only the title but the web of kinship and political relationships associated with it. In some systems, the successor was even addressed using kinship terms appropriate to the relationships of the deceased predecessor, effectively allowing a political office to maintain continuous social relationships across multiple human lifetimes. This institution allowed Lunda-style states to maintain remarkably stable political structures even amid the inevitable deaths and successions of individual rulers, and it provided a flexible mechanism for incorporating new territories: a local leader could be brought into the Lunda system by being granted a Lunda title, which then structured that leader's relationship to the wider political order according to established, well-understood rules.

Historical Debate

How Centralized Was the Early Luba Kingdom?

The question: Historians disagree about how centralized and unified early Luba political authority actually was, and about how much weight to give oral tradition versus archaeological evidence in reconstructing this history.

Interpretation A — A Genuinely Centralized Sacred Kingdom: Some historians, drawing heavily on Luba oral tradition and on later, well-documented Luba political structures, argue that by the fifteenth or sixteenth century a substantially centralized kingdom had emerged, with a paramount ruler exercising real authority over subordinate chiefs across a fairly extensive territory, supported by specialized ritual and administrative institutions.
Interpretation B — A Looser, More Gradual Confederation: Other historians, emphasizing the archaeological evidence of gradual, uneven social differentiation and cautioning against taking later oral traditions as accurate descriptions of earlier centuries, argue that what is often called the "Luba kingdom" was, for much of its early history, a looser network of related communities and ritually linked chiefdoms, with centralization developing more fully and more recently than the oral traditions themselves suggest.

Evidence: Archaeological evidence shows gradual change over centuries rather than a single dramatic transformation. Oral traditions describe a clearer narrative of dynastic founding and centralization, but such traditions are recorded centuries after the events they describe and were preserved by specialists with an interest in legitimating the ruling dynasty.

Why it matters: How historians resolve this debate shapes how they understand the broader pattern of African state formation — whether centralized states typically emerged through dramatic founding events or through long, uneven processes of gradual consolidation that were later remembered and narrated as more sudden and dramatic than they actually were.

Student task: Considering both archaeological and oral-traditional evidence, which interpretation do you find more persuasive, and what additional evidence might help historians resolve the debate further?

Section 35.3

Lunda Political Development

The Lunda kingdom developed, according to most reconstructions, when a leader or leaders associated with Luba political traditions moved southwestward into the Kasai-Lulua region and combined Luba-derived concepts of sacred, titled kingship with existing local political structures among Lunda-speaking communities already settled there. Lunda oral tradition credits a foundational figure — often identified in tradition as a woman, Lueji, who married an outsider bearing Luba political knowledge — with establishing the dynastic line from which the paramount title of Mwaant Yaav descended. Whatever the precise historical details behind this tradition, the basic pattern it describes — the fusion of an externally introduced model of sacred kingship with existing local authority, symbolized through marriage and dynastic alliance — fits a broader pattern seen in the spread of state-building ideas across Central and other parts of Africa.

Once established, the Lunda kingdom under the Mwaant Yaav developed into a political structure of extraordinary geographic reach, though one whose internal cohesion varied considerably by distance from the royal capital. At the political and ritual center, the Mwaant Yaav presided over a court structured by an elaborate hierarchy of titled officials, many of them filling positions defined by positional succession, who managed administration, judicial functions, ritual observances, and relationships with subordinate chiefs. Moving outward from this center, authority became progressively less direct: nearby chiefdoms maintained closer and more regular tribute and political ties to the capital, while more distant communities, sometimes located hundreds of kilometers away, might recognize Lunda overlordship more nominally, sending periodic tribute and accepting Lunda-derived titles for their own leaders while retaining substantial local autonomy in day-to-day governance.

This variable, graduated structure of authority — tight at the center, loose at the periphery — allowed the Lunda system to expand across an enormous territory without requiring the kind of intensive, continuous military coercion that might otherwise have been necessary to control such a large area. Instead, expansion often proceeded through a process historians sometimes describe as "snowballing": ambitious leaders, including sons, brothers, or other relatives of reigning or deceased Mwaant Yaav who had lost out in succession competitions at the capital, would travel outward with a following of supporters, install themselves among or alongside existing local populations, and establish new chiefdoms or even new kingdoms that acknowledged a genealogical and political connection to the Lunda center while operating with substantial independence.

This pattern produced, over the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, a remarkable proliferation of Lunda-derived states across a vast swath of south-central Africa: the Imbangala kingdom of Kasanje in the Kwango valley to the west, incorporating Lunda political concepts into a state heavily oriented toward Atlantic trade with Portuguese Angola; various Lunda-affiliated chiefdoms spreading southward toward the upper Zambezi; and, perhaps most dramatically, the eastern Lunda kingdom of Kazembe, established along the Luapula River near Lake Mweru by a Lunda nobleman in the mid-eighteenth century, which itself grew into a substantial regional power controlling important trade routes between the copper-producing Katanga region and trading networks extending toward both the Atlantic and, eventually, the Indian Ocean coast.

Students should understand that this expansion was not simply a story of Lunda political and military dominance over passive populations. In many cases, local communities actively adopted Lunda political titles and concepts because doing so offered real advantages: access to wider trade networks, a recognized framework for resolving succession disputes and political conflicts, and association with the prestige of the increasingly influential Lunda political tradition. At the same time, the spread of Lunda authority also involved real conflict, displacement, and the subordination of some communities to others, and the benefits of incorporation into the Lunda system were distributed quite unevenly — falling disproportionately to titleholders and their immediate followers rather than to ordinary cultivators and laborers, whose tribute obligations sustained the entire system.

Section 35.4

Trade and Regional Exchange

Trade formed an essential pillar of Luba and Lunda political and economic life, connecting these interior kingdoms both to neighboring African societies and, increasingly from the seventeenth century onward, to the wider Atlantic and global trading systems. Several distinct but overlapping trade systems converged in the region: an older network of regional exchange in salt, iron, copper, and foodstuffs that had operated for centuries before the rise of centralized kingdoms; a network connecting the Katanga copper belt to consumers across a wide area of Central, East, and Southern Africa; and, from the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries onward, an expanding network connecting the Central African interior to Atlantic coastal markets controlled by Portuguese traders operating from Angola.

Copper occupied a particularly important place in this trading system. Mined and smelted in the Katanga region, copper was cast into standardized shapes — ingots and the distinctive cross-shaped pieces sometimes called handa or copper crosses — that circulated as a recognized form of currency and store of value across a wide trading zone. Control over access to copper, and over the routes by which it moved to distant markets, provided an important economic foundation for political authority, comparable in certain respects to control over gold and salt in West African Sahelian trade discussed in earlier chapters, though Central African copper trade operated through different institutional structures and connected to different regional and eventually global markets.

From the seventeenth century onward, the expanding Atlantic slave trade reshaped these older trading patterns in significant ways. Portuguese traders based in Luanda and other coastal settlements in Angola pushed trading networks steadily into the interior, exchanging imported textiles, firearms, and other goods for ivory and, increasingly, for captives destined for the Atlantic slave trade. The Imbangala kingdom of Kasanje, situated in the Kwango valley between the Lunda heartland and the Atlantic coast, developed as a crucial intermediary in this trade, channeling captives, ivory, and other goods from the interior toward Portuguese Angola in exchange for imported manufactured goods that flowed back into the interior.

The Lunda court itself became increasingly entangled in this trade over the course of the eighteenth century. While the precise scale of Lunda involvement in supplying captives to Atlantic markets remains a subject of historical research and some uncertainty, available evidence suggests that warfare, raiding, and the use of judicial penalties to generate captives for sale all increased as demand from coastal traders grew, with significant consequences for smaller, less politically organized communities living near Lunda-controlled territory, who were often more vulnerable to raiding than the better-organized core Lunda polities themselves. This dynamic illustrates a pattern visible across much of Atlantic-facing Africa during this period, discussed more fully in Chapter 41: growing Atlantic demand reshaped the incentives facing African political and economic actors, sometimes encouraging an intensification of indigenous forms of captive-taking and judicial enslavement that predated Atlantic contact but expanded considerably under its pressure.

At the same time, trade brought genuine benefits to many participants. Access to imported textiles, metal goods, and firearms strengthened the position of rulers and traders who controlled these flows. New crops introduced through Atlantic exchange networks, particularly maize and cassava, gradually transformed agricultural possibilities across the region, in some cases supporting larger populations even as the broader trading system in which these crops arrived also brought disruption and violence. Understanding Central African trade in this period therefore requires holding together both the genuine economic dynamism these networks generated and their increasingly destructive entanglement with the Atlantic slave trade.

Section 35.5

Kingship and Administration

Both the Luba and Lunda kingdoms organized political authority around a paramount ruler whose legitimacy rested on a combination of genealogical claims, ritual sanctification, and practical control over tribute, trade, and judicial authority. In the Lunda system, the paramount title of Mwaant Yaav was filled through a process that combined hereditary claims within the recognized royal lineage with political negotiation and, not infrequently, competition or conflict among rival claimants — a pattern of contested succession common to many African and indeed world monarchical systems, including, as discussed in earlier chapters, the succession disputes that periodically destabilized states such as Mali and Songhai.

Below the paramount ruler, an elaborate structure of titled officials managed the day-to-day business of governance. Some titles carried specific administrative responsibilities — overseeing tribute collection, managing judicial proceedings, organizing military levies when needed, or supervising particular categories of royal retainers. Other titles were primarily ritual or ceremonial, associated with particular roles in royal investiture ceremonies, religious observances, or the preservation of dynastic history and genealogy. Many of these titles operated under the institution of positional succession described earlier, meaning that the office itself, along with its associated social relationships and obligations, persisted across multiple generations of titleholders even as individual officeholders died and were replaced.

This system of titled offices served several important administrative functions. It provided a relatively stable framework for incorporating new territories and populations into the wider political order, since a local leader could be granted a recognized Lunda title that then defined that leader's rights, obligations, and relationship to the royal center according to well-understood rules, without requiring the center to develop entirely new administrative mechanisms for each newly incorporated community. It also provided continuity across the inevitable disruptions of royal succession, since most titled offices below the paramount rank continued largely undisturbed regardless of which individual held the paramount title at any given moment.

At the same time, this system contained significant tensions. Succession disputes for the paramount title could be intense and sometimes violent, since the office carried substantial control over tribute flows, trade relationships, and the ability to grant or withhold subordinate titles. The very mechanism that allowed peaceful expansion — ambitious relatives of the ruling family establishing new chiefdoms or kingdoms when they failed to secure the paramount title at the center — also meant that succession competition at the Lunda capital had consequences radiating outward across an enormous territory, as losing claimants and their followers sought new opportunities elsewhere rather than simply accepting subordination at home.

Tribute formed the essential economic relationship binding subordinate chiefdoms to the royal center in both the Luba and Lunda systems. Subordinate leaders were expected to send periodic tribute — in agricultural produce, craft goods, copper, salt, or, increasingly from the seventeenth century onward, captives and trade goods obtained through participation in long-distance commerce — to the paramount ruler, who in turn redistributed some portion of this tribute to maintain the loyalty of titled officials, support ritual and ceremonial functions, and provide a measure of protection and arbitration for subordinate communities in disputes with their neighbors. This tribute relationship, while it provided real benefits to many participants, also constituted a form of extraction that could be experienced as burdensome, particularly by communities with less political leverage to negotiate favorable terms.

Historical Vocabulary
Tribute System

A tribute system is a political and economic arrangement in which subordinate communities or rulers are obligated to provide periodic payments — in goods, produce, labor, or sometimes people — to a more powerful political center, typically in exchange for some combination of protection, recognition, access to trade networks, and the right to retain local autonomy in most matters of internal governance.

Tribute systems differ from direct administrative rule in that the dominant power generally does not seek to control the day-to-day governance of subordinate communities, focusing instead on extracting agreed-upon resources and maintaining recognition of its overarching authority. This made tribute systems an efficient way to project influence across large territories without the administrative costs of direct rule, but it also meant that the practical experience of being "ruled" by a distant tributary overlord varied enormously depending on local circumstances, the relative bargaining power of subordinate communities, and the willingness of the central authority to enforce its claims through raiding or military pressure when tribute was withheld.

Section 35.6

Religion and Sacred Leadership

Religious belief and ritual practice were deeply interwoven with political authority in both the Luba and Lunda kingdoms, in ways that parallel, while also differing significantly from, patterns of sacred kingship discussed elsewhere in this textbook, including ancient Egypt, Axum, and the kingdoms of the West African forest zone. Central African religious traditions generally understood the cosmos as populated by a hierarchy of spiritual forces, including a distant creator deity, ancestral spirits with continuing influence over the affairs of the living, and territorial or nature spirits associated with particular places, especially significant features such as lakes, rivers, and unusual rock formations.

Paramount rulers in both the Luba and Lunda systems were understood to occupy a position with special access to this spiritual hierarchy, mediating between the human community and the ancestral and spiritual forces believed to influence fertility, prosperity, health, and political order. This sacred status was reinforced through elaborate investiture ceremonies in which a new ruler underwent ritual transformation, sometimes including periods of seclusion, the performance of specific rites associated with royal ancestors, and the adoption of ritual objects, regalia, and praise names that marked the transition from ordinary individual to sacred office-holder.

This sacred status carried both authority and constraint. On one hand, the ruler's perceived connection to ancestral and spiritual forces reinforced obedience and reverence among subjects, who understood failure to respect royal authority as carrying not only political but cosmological risk. On the other hand, sacred kingship in many Central African traditions also imposed significant ritual restrictions on the ruler's own behavior — particular foods that could not be eaten, places that could not be visited, behaviors that had to be carefully regulated — reflecting a widespread African and indeed cross-cultural pattern in which sacred rulers are simultaneously elevated above ordinary social constraints and bound by an intensified set of ritual obligations precisely because their persons are understood to carry consequences for the entire community's well-being.

Religious specialists below the level of the paramount ruler also played important political roles. Diviners, healers, and ritual experts associated with particular shrines or ancestral cults provided guidance on matters ranging from agricultural timing to succession disputes, and their endorsement or opposition could carry real political weight. In the Lunda system, certain female titleholders held essential ritual roles in the investiture of the Mwaant Yaav, illustrating that despite the predominantly male character of the paramount office itself, women occupied indispensable positions within the broader structure of sacred and political authority — a pattern explored further in Chapter 18's broader discussion of women and gender in African history.

The lukasa memory board, mentioned earlier in this chapter, exemplifies the close connection between religious-historical knowledge and political legitimacy in the Luba system. These carved wooden boards, studded with patterns of beads, shells, and incised markings, served as mnemonic devices used by trained specialists to recall and recite complex genealogies, the locations of important historical events, and accounts of political and sacred history. Possession of the specialized knowledge required to interpret a lukasa board conferred significant status, and the board itself was treated with considerable reverence, illustrating how the preservation and transmission of historical and genealogical knowledge functioned as a form of religious as well as political practice.

Historical Vocabulary
Sacred Kingship

Sacred kingship refers to a form of political authority in which a ruler's legitimacy and power are understood to derive substantially from a special relationship to divine, ancestral, or spiritual forces, rather than — or in addition to — purely secular claims such as military strength or hereditary law alone. Sacred kingship typically involves elaborate investiture ceremonies, ritual restrictions on the ruler's behavior, and the belief that the ruler's person and conduct have consequences for the prosperity, fertility, and order of the entire community.

Sacred kingship appears across many African political traditions, including ancient Egypt, Axum, the Yoruba city-states, the Kingdom of Kongo, and the Luba and Lunda kingdoms discussed in this chapter, though the specific beliefs, ceremonies, and restrictions associated with sacred rule varied considerably from one society to another. Recognizing both the common pattern and the specific local variations helps students avoid treating "African sacred kingship" as a single, undifferentiated tradition.

Section 35.7

Migration and Expansion

The spread of Lunda political institutions across an enormous area of south-central Africa offers an instructive case study in how political ideas and titled offices, rather than direct territorial conquest alone, could drive large-scale political expansion. As discussed earlier in this chapter, much of this expansion proceeded through a pattern in which ambitious individuals — frequently relatives of the reigning or recently deceased Mwaant Yaav who had been unsuccessful in competitions for the paramount title — departed the Lunda capital with a following of supporters and established new political centers in distant territories, sometimes absorbing or allying with existing local populations and sometimes displacing or subordinating them through conflict.

The westward expansion that produced the Imbangala kingdom of Kasanje illustrates this pattern with particular clarity. Imbangala groups, militarized bands that had developed distinctive social and military organizations in the Kwango-Kwanza region partly in response to opportunities created by Portuguese presence in Angola, combined elements of Lunda political concepts with their own institutions to create a state well positioned to profit from intermediary trade between the Lunda interior and Portuguese coastal markets. Kasanje's rulers adopted titles and political concepts recognizably descended from Lunda tradition while developing a political and military culture substantially shaped by the particular pressures and opportunities of operating near the expanding Atlantic trading frontier.

The eastward expansion that produced the Kazembe kingdom along the Luapula River followed a somewhat different but related pattern. According to oral tradition, a Lunda nobleman named Kazembe led a following eastward in the mid-eighteenth century, eventually establishing a new kingdom that came to control important trade routes linking the copper-producing Katanga region with markets extending toward both the Atlantic coast, through connections with Lunda and Kasanje networks, and eventually toward the Indian Ocean coast, through connections with Bisa and other trading peoples operating across what is now Zambia and Malawi. The eastern Lunda kingdom of Kazembe grew into a substantial regional power in its own right by the early nineteenth century, illustrating how a single act of political migration could, over several generations, produce an entirely new center of political and economic gravity.

These patterns of migration and political expansion should not be understood as simple, one-directional processes of Lunda dominance spreading outward into passive territories. In each case, the success of expanding Lunda-derived political projects depended on complex negotiation, alliance, and sometimes conflict with existing local populations, whose own political traditions, economic interests, and capacity for resistance or accommodation shaped the specific form that each new Lunda-derived state ultimately took. The resulting political landscape of south-central Africa by the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries was therefore not a simple extension of a single Lunda political system but a diverse family of related states, each combining Lunda-derived institutions with substantial local variation.

Section 35.8

Culture and Social Life

Beyond the institutions of kingship, trade, and tribute that occupy much of this chapter, ordinary social and cultural life in Luba, Lunda, and related Central African communities deserves careful attention. Most people in these societies were farmers, fishers, hunters, or craft specialists living in villages organized around extended kinship groups, with daily life structured by the agricultural calendar, by seasonal patterns of fishing and hunting, and by the social and ritual obligations associated with kinship, age, and gender.

Kinship organization in much of the Luba-Lunda cultural zone followed matrilineal principles, meaning that descent, inheritance, and certain forms of social identity were reckoned through the mother's line rather than the father's. This matrilineal pattern, common across a wide swath of Central Africa, had significant implications for political and social organization: a man's heirs in matters of lineage succession were often his sister's children rather than his own children, and women, as the links through which lineage identity passed, frequently held significant social and ritual authority even within societies where the most prominent paramount political titles were generally held by men. The interplay between matrilineal kinship structures and the patrilineal or bilateral elements sometimes present within royal succession practices created complex and locally varied patterns of inheritance, authority, and obligation that historians and anthropologists continue to study closely.

Blacksmiths held a position of particular social and sometimes ritual significance in many Central African communities, reflecting a pattern visible across much of the continent, discussed in Chapter 16's broader treatment of African ironworking and technology. The transformation of ore into iron tools and weapons was often understood as carrying spiritual as well as practical significance, and smiths frequently occupied specialized social positions, sometimes organized into hereditary craft lineages, sometimes associated with particular ritual restrictions or privileges that set them apart from the broader agricultural population.

Hunting, too, carried both practical and cultural importance, particularly in forest-margin and woodland environments where game remained an important supplementary source of protein and where successful hunters could achieve significant social recognition. Hunting guilds and associations in some Central African societies developed specialized ritual practices, oral traditions, and forms of knowledge transmission related to tracking, hunting techniques, and the propitiation of spirits believed to govern the success of the hunt.

Art and material culture in the Luba-Lunda cultural zone achieved notable sophistication, particularly in wood carving, where skilled artisans produced finely worked stools, staffs, figures, and ceremonial objects — including the lukasa memory boards discussed earlier — that combined aesthetic accomplishment with religious and political significance. Many of these objects were closely associated with chiefly or royal authority, serving as visible markers of legitimate office and as repositories of historical and genealogical knowledge transmitted through specialized training rather than through written text.

Everyday Life and Social History

Villages of the Savanna

For the great majority of people living within the Luba and Lunda political orders, daily life centered not on the royal court but on the village, the extended kin group, and the agricultural and fishing calendar. A typical household cultivated sorghum, millet, and, increasingly from the seventeenth century onward, maize and cassava, supplementing farming with fishing where rivers and lakes permitted, hunting for additional protein and for skins and other materials, and the production of crafts such as pottery, basketry, and cloth made from raffia palm fiber.

Tribute obligations to local chiefs and, through them, to more distant political centers represented a recurring claim on household labor and produce, though the burden of these obligations varied considerably depending on a community's distance from major political centers, its relationship to local titleholders, and the broader political and economic conditions of a given period. Periods of expanding Atlantic trade and associated raiding could bring both new opportunities — access to imported goods, new crops, and trade income — and new dangers, particularly for communities lacking strong political protection.

Religious and ritual life pervaded the everyday: agricultural rites tied to planting and harvest, rites of passage marking transitions such as initiation into adulthood, marriage, and death, and ongoing relationships with ancestral spirits believed to remain involved in the affairs of living descendants. Village elders, lineage heads, and ritual specialists provided guidance and authority in these matters, operating within a social framework substantially shaped by, but also significantly independent of, the grander political dramas of royal succession and interstate relations that dominate the documentary and oral-traditional record this chapter has drawn upon.

Section 35.9

Central Africa in Historical Perspective

The history of the Luba and Lunda kingdoms offers students an important corrective to several common misconceptions about African political history. First, it demonstrates that sophisticated, large-scale political organization in Africa did not require the dense urban centers, extensive written administration, or large standing armies often assumed necessary for state formation in other world historical contexts. Instead, Luba and Lunda statecraft achieved remarkable territorial reach and institutional durability through flexible, portable political concepts — sacred kingship, titled office, positional succession, and structured tribute relationships — that could be carried by migrating individuals into new territories and adapted to widely varying local circumstances.

Second, this history illustrates the diversity of African state-building traditions even within a single broad cultural and linguistic zone. The Bantu-speaking peoples of Central Africa, despite sharing deep linguistic and cultural roots traceable to the Bantu expansion discussed in Chapter 14, developed multiple distinct political traditions — the Luba kingdom, the Lunda kingdom and its many offshoots, the Kingdom of Kongo discussed in Chapter 36, and numerous smaller chiefdoms and confederations — each combining shared underlying cultural elements with locally specific innovations in governance, religion, and economic organization.

Third, the Luba and Lunda case demonstrates the importance of situating even deeply interior African political histories within wider regional and global contexts. Despite their location far from any coastline, both kingdoms were shaped by participation in long-distance trade networks connecting them to the Katanga copper belt, to East African and Indian Ocean trading systems through intermediary peoples, and, increasingly from the seventeenth century onward, to the Atlantic trading world centered on Portuguese Angola. The growth of Atlantic demand for captives and ivory reshaped political incentives and intensified patterns of raiding and judicial enslavement within and around these kingdoms, illustrating how the consequences of the Atlantic slave trade, discussed at length in Chapter 41, extended far beyond the African coastal regions most directly engaged in transatlantic commerce.

Finally, this history should prompt students to consider carefully how the benefits and costs of political centralization were distributed within Luba and Lunda society. Titled officials, traders, and those positioned closely to centers of political power generally gained considerably from participation in these systems — access to tribute income, prestige goods, and political influence — while ordinary cultivators and especially communities without strong political organization of their own bore a disproportionate share of the burdens, including tribute obligations and vulnerability to raiding intensified by growing Atlantic demand. A historically accurate account of Central African kingdoms must hold together both genuine political achievement and these significant internal inequalities and external entanglements.

Section 35.10

Chapter Review and Central African Kingdoms Checkpoint

This chapter has examined how the Luba and Lunda kingdoms built durable, far-reaching political systems in the savanna woodlands of south-central Africa through institutions of sacred kingship, positional succession, titled office, and structured tribute relationships, rather than primarily through dense urbanization or large-scale military coercion. Before proceeding to the next chapter's examination of the Kingdom of Kongo, students should be able to demonstrate understanding of these foundational developments.

The core insights of this chapter are: Central African state formation drew on a deep archaeological and oral-traditional history of gradual social differentiation in resource-rich zones such as the Upemba Depression. The Luba kingdom developed a flexible political model combining sacred kingship with titled administration. The Lunda kingdom adapted and dramatically expanded this model across an enormous territory using positional succession and migration-driven political expansion rather than primarily through conquest. Trade in copper, salt, ivory, and, increasingly, captives connected these interior kingdoms to wider regional and Atlantic trading systems, with the growth of Atlantic demand reshaping political incentives in significant and often destructive ways. Religious belief and sacred kingship reinforced political legitimacy while imposing ritual constraints on rulers. Women held essential, if often underappreciated, roles within these political and ritual systems. And the benefits and burdens of these political structures were distributed unevenly, with titleholders and traders generally gaining more than ordinary cultivators and vulnerable neighboring communities.

Primary Source Window

A Nineteenth-Century Visitor's Account of a Lunda-Affiliated Royal Court

Creator: A European traveler associated with Portuguese trading and exploration networks · Date: Nineteenth century · Type: Travel narrative / outside observer's account

Context: Beginning in the eighteenth century and continuing through the nineteenth, a number of Portuguese-affiliated traders and later European explorers traveled into the Lunda-influenced interior and recorded descriptions of royal courts they visited, including the elaborate hierarchy of titled officials, the careful regulation of access to the ruler, and the prominence of tribute exchange in court life. These accounts provide outside observation of institutions that are otherwise documented primarily through oral tradition.

One such account described a royal capital organized around a central residence enclosure, approached only through a sequence of attendants and titled officials, with visitors required to present gifts before being granted an audience, and with the ruler's authority publicly reinforced through elaborate displays of regalia, music, and the formal recitation of praise names and genealogical claims by trained specialists.

Analysis: Accounts of this kind are valuable because they offer description from an outside observer present at a particular moment, supplementing oral tradition recorded later and archaeological evidence that cannot capture court ceremony directly. However, such travelers often brought assumptions shaped by their own commercial interests and by European political concepts that did not map neatly onto Central African institutions, and they generally had limited access to the deeper meanings of ceremonies and titles that local specialists would have understood far more fully. Historians must therefore use such accounts carefully, checking them against oral tradition and comparative evidence rather than treating them as objective, complete descriptions.

Cause and Effect

The Expansion of Lunda Political Influence

Long-Term Causes

Centuries of gradual social differentiation and craft specialization in the Upemba Depression; the development of flexible, portable Luba political concepts; the existence of rich trade resources, especially Katanga copper, providing an economic foundation for political authority.

Short-Term Causes

Succession disputes at the Luba and later Lunda capitals that propelled losing claimants and their followers outward; growing Portuguese commercial activity in Angola creating new trading opportunities and incentives for interior states to engage with Atlantic markets.

The Event/Transformation

The consolidation of the Lunda kingdom under the Mwaant Yaav and its subsequent expansion through migration-driven state formation, producing offshoot states such as Kasanje and Kazembe across a vast area of south-central Africa.

Effects

Creation of an extensive network of related political systems sharing common institutions; intensification of trade in copper, ivory, and increasingly captives; growing entanglement with the Atlantic slave trade and associated raiding; eventual vulnerability to European colonial conquest in the late nineteenth century.

Historical complexity: Lunda expansion cannot be reduced to a single cause. It resulted from the interaction of internal political dynamics, portable institutional innovations, regional trade opportunities, and the growing pull of Atlantic commerce, with consequences that were experienced very differently by titleholders, tributary communities, and vulnerable neighboring populations.

What Changed

  • The growing entanglement of political and economic life with long-distance Atlantic trade from the seventeenth century onward

Why the pattern matters: The coexistence of dramatic political transformation at the level of kingdoms and courts with substantial continuity in everyday kinship and community life illustrates that large-scale state formation did not necessarily require — or produce — equally large-scale transformation of local social structures, a pattern with important implications for how historians assess the depth and reach of precolonial African states.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has examined the rise, expansion, and institutional structure of the Luba and Lunda kingdoms, two of the most significant political traditions to develop in the savanna woodlands of south-central Africa before the era of European colonial conquest. Beginning with centuries of gradual social and economic development in resource-rich zones such as the Upemba Depression, communities in this region built increasingly centralized and sacred forms of kingship, culminating in the consolidation of the Luba kingdom by roughly the fifteenth or sixteenth century.

From this Luba political foundation, the Lunda kingdom emerged and, through the institution of positional succession and a pattern of migration-driven political expansion, grew into a network of related states spanning an extraordinary geographic area, stretching from the Kasai-Lulua heartland westward to the Kwango valley and eastward to the Luapula River and beyond. This expansion was achieved less through continuous military conquest than through the portability of political concepts — sacred kingship, titled office, and structured tribute relationships — that could be carried by ambitious leaders into new territories and adapted to local circumstances.

Trade formed an essential pillar of this political world, connecting interior kingdoms to the copper-rich Katanga plateau and, increasingly from the seventeenth century onward, to the expanding Atlantic trading system centered on Portuguese Angola. This growing Atlantic connection brought genuine economic benefits — access to imported goods, new crops, and trade income — while also intensifying patterns of raiding and captive-taking whose human costs fell disproportionately on smaller, less politically organized communities.

Religious belief and sacred kingship provided essential legitimation for political authority throughout this world, reinforcing obedience to paramount rulers while also imposing significant ritual constraints upon them. Specialized knowledge-keepers, including those trained in the use of lukasa memory boards, preserved genealogical and historical knowledge central to political legitimacy, while female titleholders held essential, if sometimes underappreciated, roles within the broader structure of sacred and political authority.

Beneath the political drama of kingship, succession, and interstate relations, the everyday life of most Luba, Lunda, and related communities centered on farming, fishing, kinship obligation, and ritual practice substantially shaped by matrilineal descent structures common across much of Central Africa. This everyday social world demonstrated both real continuity across centuries of political transformation and significant local variation shaped by the particular tribute relationships, trade opportunities, and political circumstances each community experienced.

The history of the Luba and Lunda kingdoms challenges simplified narratives of African political history in several important ways. It demonstrates that large-scale, durable political organization did not require dense urbanization, extensive written administration, or large standing armies. It illustrates the diversity of political traditions that developed even within a single broad Bantu-speaking cultural zone. And it shows how even deeply interior African societies were shaped by participation in wider regional and global trading systems, including the increasingly destructive Atlantic slave trade.

At the same time, this history requires honest attention to internal inequality and conflict. The benefits of Lunda and Luba political organization were distributed unevenly, generally favoring titleholders, traders, and those closely connected to centers of power over ordinary cultivators and vulnerable peripheral communities. Succession disputes could be violent, and growing entanglement with Atlantic trade intensified patterns of raiding and judicial enslavement whose consequences extended across generations.

Understanding the Luba and Lunda kingdoms also prepares students to engage with the chapters that follow, particularly the history of the Kingdom of Kongo in Chapter 36 and the broader history of African societies navigating an age of expanding commerce in Chapter 43. The patterns examined in this chapter — flexible political institutions, entanglement with long-distance and eventually Atlantic trade, and uneven distribution of benefits and burdens — recur, with significant local variation, across much of the African continent during this period.

Ultimately, this chapter asks students to hold together both the genuine political and institutional achievement represented by the Luba and Lunda kingdoms and the real human costs embedded within these systems, recognizing that historical accuracy requires neither uncritical celebration nor one-dimensional condemnation, but careful attention to evidence, perspective, and complexity.

The marshy lake-and-river region along the upper Lualaba River central to early Luba economic and political development.
Sanga archaeological site
A key excavated cemetery site providing evidence of growing social differentiation in the Upemba region centuries before the documented Luba kingdom.
Katanga copper belt
A major source of regional wealth and a key resource underpinning Luba and Lunda political and economic power.
Kasai and Lulua river system
The geographic core of the Lunda heartland and the political center from which Lunda expansion proceeded.
Kwango River valley
Location of the Imbangala kingdom of Kasanje, a key intermediary in trade between the Lunda interior and Portuguese Angola.
Luapula River and Lake Mweru
Location of the eastern Lunda offshoot kingdom of Kazembe, controlling trade routes linking Katanga copper to wider regional networks.
Portuguese Angola (Luanda)
The Portuguese colonial coastal base from which traders pushed networks into the Central African interior, connecting Luba and Lunda territories to the Atlantic trading world.
Imbangala groups
Militarized communities of the Kwango-Kwanza region who combined Lunda-derived political concepts with distinctive military organization to found Kasanje.

Mapping the Spread of Lunda Political Institutions

This project asks you to research and map the spread of Lunda-derived political institutions across south-central Africa, comparing how different offshoot states adapted core Lunda concepts to local circumstances.

  1. Select three Lunda-derived or Lunda-affiliated states. Choose from the Lunda kingdom itself, Kasanje, Kazembe, or another related polity identified through library research.
  2. Research each state's origins. Using library resources, identify when, how, and by whom each state was founded, and trace its political and genealogical connection to the Lunda center.
  3. Compare political institutions. For each state, identify what Lunda-derived institutions — titled offices, positional succession, tribute relationships — it retained, and what local adaptations or innovations it introduced.
  4. Map the network. Create a map showing the approximate locations of the Lunda center and your three chosen offshoot states, along with major trade routes connecting them to the Atlantic coast and to regional trading systems.
  5. Write your findings. In a 3–5 page essay (or equivalent multimedia presentation), explain what your comparison reveals about the relationship between political influence, migration, and adaptation in Central African state formation.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter matters because it demonstrates, with particular clarity, that durable, large-scale political organization in Africa took many different institutional forms, not all of which resembled the centralized, urbanized, militarily dominant states more familiar to students from earlier chapters on West African Sahelian empires or the Swahili coastal city-states. The Luba and Lunda kingdoms achieved remarkable territorial reach and institutional durability through flexible, portable political concepts — sacred kingship, titled office, and positional succession — that could travel with migrating leaders into new territories and take root in locally adapted forms.

This history shaped later African history in significant ways. The trading networks and political relationships established by Luba and Lunda states structured economic and political life across a vast region for centuries, and the consequences of growing entanglement with the Atlantic slave trade from the seventeenth century onward reverberated through subsequent generations, contributing to patterns of vulnerability and conflict that colonial powers would later exploit and intensify during the conquest and partition of the region examined in Part VIII of this textbook.

The larger themes this chapter reveals — the portability of political institutions, the variable and graduated nature of authority across distance, the uneven distribution of benefits and burdens within seemingly unified political systems, and the deep entanglement of even remote interior societies with global economic forces — recur throughout African history and indeed throughout world history more broadly. Students who grasp these themes here will be better equipped to recognize similar patterns when they encounter other African states and societies elsewhere in this textbook.

Finally, this chapter asks students to hold the Luba and Lunda kingdoms in view as Africa actually was: diverse, interconnected, creative, and contested. These were not static, unchanging societies awaiting European contact, nor were they simply victims of external forces. They were dynamic political communities that built sophisticated institutions, adapted to changing economic opportunities and dangers, and shaped the lives of millions of people across a vast and varied landscape — a history that remains essential to understanding the Democratic Republic of the Congo, Angola, and Zambia today, and to understanding the larger African past this textbook seeks to illuminate.

Climate, Environment, and Disease in African History

Africa: A History of a Continent

Chapter Opening

"Long before any king built a city or any merchant crossed a desert, rainfall, rivers, and disease had already drawn the map within which African history would unfold."

Ten thousand years ago, where the Sahara Desert now stretches in nearly unbroken aridity from the Atlantic to the Red Sea, there were lakes deep enough to support hippopotamuses, rivers lined with reeds, and grasslands grazed by herds of cattle, antelope, and giraffe. Rock paintings carved and painted onto sandstone cliffs in places now hundreds of kilometers from any permanent water show swimmers, fishermen, and herders moving through a landscape that no longer exists. This "Green Sahara" was not a myth or an exaggeration. It was a real climate regime, sustained by shifts in the Earth's orbit and the strength of monsoon rains, that supported human communities across what is today the world's largest hot desert. Its slow disappearance, beginning roughly six thousand years ago and accelerating over the following two millennia, was one of the most consequential environmental transformations in human history — and it happened entirely within the span of African history that this textbook studies.

This chapter is about climate, environment, and disease not as a backdrop to African history but as active forces within it. Rainfall patterns determined where farming was possible and where it was not. River cycles like the Nile's annual flood made some societies extraordinarily prosperous while leaving others dependent on unpredictable rains. Disease environments — shaped by insects, parasites, and the microorganisms that thrive in particular climates — opened some regions to certain kinds of economic activity, such as cattle-keeping, while closing them to others. Drought could topple kingdoms or force migrations of hundreds of kilometers. Desertification could turn farmland into rangeland, or rangeland into desert. None of this happened outside of history. It happened within it, shaping the choices available to African societies just as surely as politics, religion, or trade.

It is tempting to treat environmental history as a separate, scientific subject distinct from the human history of kings, trade, and religion covered elsewhere in this book. That separation is a mistake. Environmental conditions did not simply happen to African societies from the outside; African societies actively responded to, adapted to, and sometimes reshaped their environments. Farmers bred drought-resistant sorghum and millet over many centuries of selection. Pastoralists developed elaborate systems of seasonal movement that allowed cattle to survive in places where they could not have survived standing still. Communities living in malarial regions developed genetic adaptations — including the sickle-cell trait — that provided some protection against severe disease, a biological as well as cultural response to environmental pressure that took place over many human generations.

This chapter also introduces students to a set of evidentiary tools that differ somewhat from the documents, oral traditions, and archaeological remains discussed in earlier chapters. Environmental historians rely heavily on the natural archive: lake-bed sediment cores that preserve pollen and dust accumulated over thousands of years, ice cores from distant glaciers that record global climate shifts, tree rings that capture year-by-year rainfall variation, and the genetic and skeletal evidence of disease left in ancient human remains. These sources allow historians and scientists working together to reconstruct climate and disease history even where no written or oral record survives — and they often confirm, refine, or sometimes complicate the stories that oral traditions and written chronicles tell about famine, migration, and societal change.

Understanding environmental history also requires confronting a temptation that earlier generations of writers about Africa often indulged: the temptation to use climate and disease as an excuse for treating African history as simply a story of victimhood to nature, or worse, to use environmental determinism to explain away the achievements of African societies as accidents of favorable conditions, or to explain underdevelopment as an inevitable product of harsh environments rather than of historical choices, inequality, and exploitation. This chapter resists that temptation. Environment mattered enormously, but African societies were never simply passive victims of climate. They were active agents who studied their environments closely, developed sophisticated knowledge systems to manage environmental risk, and built institutions — granaries, irrigation systems, transhumance routes, trade networks that moved food across regions — specifically designed to manage the very real risks that climate and disease posed.

Finally, this chapter matters because the environmental forces it describes did not end in the ancient or medieval past. Drought, desertification, and disease have continued to shape African history into the colonial period, the era of independence, and the present day. The droughts that struck the Sahel in the 1970s and 1980s, the HIV/AIDS epidemic that devastated parts of the continent beginning in the 1980s, and the contemporary challenges of climate change discussed in this textbook's final chapter are not new phenomena unrelated to the deeper past. They are the latest chapters in a very long history of African societies confronting, adapting to, and sometimes being overwhelmed by environmental and biological forces — a history this chapter begins to tell.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain how climate change over thousands of years, including the desiccation of the Sahara, shaped the long-term development of African societies.
  2. Describe the relationship between rainfall variability, river cycles, and patterns of human settlement in Africa.
  3. Analyze the process and consequences of desertification in regions such as the Sahel.
  4. Identify major disease environments in Africa and explain how diseases such as malaria, trypanosomiasis, and others shaped where and how people lived.
  5. Evaluate the relationship between climate, weather, agriculture, and food security across different African regions.
  6. Explain how insects and animals, particularly the tsetse fly, influenced the geography of farming, herding, and state formation.
  7. Compare different adaptation and survival strategies developed by African societies in response to environmental risk.
  8. Trace connections between environmental pressure and patterns of migration in African history.
  9. Assess the kinds of evidence — sediment cores, oral tradition, written chronicles, genetic evidence — historians use to reconstruct environmental history.
  10. Distinguish between environmental determinism and a balanced historical account that treats environment as one among several shaping forces.
  11. Contextualize contemporary African environmental and health challenges within a much longer historical pattern.

Historical Context

To understand environmental history in Africa, students must first understand the staggering diversity of the continent's physical geography, introduced in Chapter 3. Africa spans the equator and stretches across nearly seventy-five degrees of latitude, from the Mediterranean coastlines of Tunisia and Morocco to the temperate Cape region of South Africa. This range produces an extraordinary diversity of climate zones: hyper-arid deserts, semi-arid steppe and Sahel, tropical savanna, dense equatorial rainforest, highland plateau climates, and Mediterranean coastal zones. No other inhabited continent contains such a complete range of climate types within a single landmass.

This diversity means that there is no single "African climate" and no single environmental story that applies to the whole continent. A history of climate in the Sahel, where rainfall arrives in a short, unreliable summer season and where the margin between adequate harvest and famine can be a matter of a few centimeters of rain, is a fundamentally different story from a history of climate in the Congo Basin, where rainfall is abundant nearly year-round but where dense forest, high humidity, and particular disease environments created different challenges. Similarly, the highland regions of Ethiopia, with their relatively cool temperatures and reliable monsoon rains, supported a very different agricultural and demographic history than the arid Kalahari or Namib deserts of southern Africa.

Climate in Africa, as everywhere, has never been static. Over the long span of the Quaternary period — roughly the last two and a half million years — Africa's climate has oscillated between wetter and drier phases linked to changes in the Earth's orbital geometry, ocean circulation patterns, and ice age cycles in the far north. These oscillations occurred long before agriculture, long before states, and long before written records, but they shaped the environments in which early hominins evolved, as discussed in Chapter 2, and they continued to shape human history after the emergence of Homo sapiens.

Disease, like climate, is not simply a biological fact separate from history; it is itself a product of environmental and ecological conditions that have changed over time, sometimes as a direct consequence of human activity. The clearing of forest for agriculture, the keeping of livestock in close proximity to human settlements, the growth of dense urban populations, and the development of long-distance trade and travel all created new opportunities for pathogens to spread, mutate, and establish themselves in human populations. Africa's disease environment has historically been shaped by a combination of climate — many disease vectors thrive in warm, humid conditions — and by the deep evolutionary history of humans and their pathogens on the continent where humanity itself evolved, meaning some of the longest-established host-pathogen relationships on Earth exist in Africa.

It is essential for students to understand that disease and climate were not simply obstacles imposed on Africa from outside. They were conditions African societies lived within, studied, and adapted to over very long periods of time, often with considerable sophistication. Long before European scientists understood the role of mosquitoes in transmitting malaria, many African communities had developed practical knowledge — about seasonal settlement patterns, about avoiding certain swampy areas at certain times of year, about using particular plants for treatment or repellence — that reflected centuries of accumulated environmental observation, even without a modern germ-theory explanation for why these practices worked.

Section 6.1

Climate as a Historical Force

Historians have not always taken climate seriously as a subject of historical inquiry. For much of the twentieth century, climate was treated as a fixed backdrop — the stage on which human history played out, but not itself a historical actor. This view has changed dramatically in recent decades, as scientists developed increasingly precise tools for reconstructing past climate conditions and as historians recognized that those tools revealed climate shifts dramatic enough to have reshaped entire regions of human settlement, agriculture, and political organization.

In Africa, the case for treating climate as a historical force is especially strong, because the continent has experienced some of the most dramatic climate transformations documented anywhere on Earth within the timeframe of human history. The transformation of the Sahara from a green, well-watered landscape into the world's largest hot desert occurred well within the period during which humans had already developed complex societies, language, art, and technology. This was not a transformation in deep geological time, disconnected from human experience. It was a transformation that humans lived through, adapted to, and were profoundly shaped by.

Climate operates as a historical force in several distinct ways. First, it sets the basic parameters of what kinds of human activity are possible in a given place: where farming can succeed, what crops can grow, where livestock can be kept, how large a population an area can sustainably support. Second, climate variability — the difference between an average year and an unusually wet, dry, hot, or cold year — creates risk that human societies must manage, through storage systems, trade networks, migration strategies, or social institutions of mutual support. Third, climate change over longer timescales can transform the basic parameters themselves, turning farmland into desert or, in the case of the African Humid Period, transforming desert into habitable grassland.

Crucially, climate does not determine history in any simple, mechanical way. The same climate conditions can produce very different historical outcomes depending on the technologies, institutions, and choices available to the people living within them. A drought that devastates one society might be survived with far less harm by a neighboring society that has developed more effective storage systems, more diversified economic strategies, or stronger networks of trade and mutual assistance. This is why historians insist on studying climate alongside — never instead of — the social, political, economic, and cultural history of African societies.

Historical Vocabulary
Paleoclimate

Paleoclimate refers to climate conditions in the past, often long before written or even oral records exist, reconstructed using scientific evidence such as lake and ocean sediment cores, ice cores, tree rings, and fossil pollen. Paleoclimate research allows historians to extend their understanding of environmental history far beyond the limits of human memory and writing.

In Africa, paleoclimate evidence has revealed the existence of the African Humid Period, a span of several thousand years when the Sahara was green, as well as numerous shorter periods of drought and abundance that affected specific regions at specific times. Because paleoclimate data come from natural sources rather than human testimony, they provide an independent way to check, confirm, or sometimes challenge claims found in oral tradition or written chronicles about droughts, floods, and famines.

Section 6.2

Drought, Rainfall, and River Cycles

Rainfall in much of Africa is highly seasonal, often concentrated into a single wet season lasting only a few months, with the rest of the year largely or entirely dry. In many regions, especially the Sahel and parts of eastern and southern Africa, the amount of rainfall received in any given year can vary enormously, sometimes by fifty percent or more compared to the long-term average. This variability, rather than simple aridity, has historically posed one of the greatest challenges to agricultural societies across much of the continent: a farmer might plant based on the expectation of an average rainy season, only to face a year of rainfall failure that destroys the harvest entirely.

River systems have provided one of the most important ways African societies managed this rainfall variability, because rivers often draw water from distant catchment areas with different and sometimes more reliable rainfall patterns than the areas through which they flow. The Nile is the most dramatic example: the annual Nile flood, which historically inundated the floodplain of Egypt each summer, depended not on rainfall in Egypt itself — a region that receives almost no rain — but on monsoon rains falling in the Ethiopian highlands, more than a thousand kilometers to the south. This decoupling of agricultural fertility from local rainfall allowed Egypt to develop one of the most stable and productive agricultural systems in the ancient world, discussed in detail in Chapter 7.

Other African river systems played similar, though less extreme, stabilizing roles. The Niger River's seasonal flooding created the fertile Inland Niger Delta, supporting fishing, farming, and herding communities and contributing to the agricultural surplus that helped sustain the great Sahelian trading empires discussed in Part IV of this textbook. The Congo River, fed by rainfall across a vast and largely year-round wet equatorial basin, maintained much steadier flow throughout the year, supporting different patterns of fishing, river transport, and settlement along its banks and tributaries.

Where reliable river systems were not available, societies depended directly on rainfall, and the historical record across the Sahel, the Horn of Africa, and parts of southern Africa contains repeated references — in oral tradition, in written chronicles where they exist, and increasingly in scientific paleoclimate reconstructions — to severe droughts that produced famine, displacement, and political crisis. Understanding these drought cycles, and the strategies societies developed to survive them, is essential to understanding much of African political and economic history.

Section 6.3

Desertification and Ecological Change

Desertification refers to the process by which previously productive land becomes increasingly arid and unable to support its former vegetation, agriculture, or grazing capacity. The most dramatic example of desertification in African history is the long transformation of the Sahara itself, a process driven primarily by changes in Earth's orbital geometry that weakened the African monsoon system over thousands of years, beginning roughly six thousand years ago and largely completing the Sahara's transition to its current arid state by around three thousand years ago.

This was not a sudden event but a gradual, uneven process that unfolded differently across different parts of the Sahara, with some areas drying centuries before others, and with temporary reversals along the way. Archaeological and rock art evidence shows that pastoralist communities adapted to the slowly worsening conditions for a very long time, moving their herds toward remaining water sources, adjusting their economic strategies, and only gradually abandoning the deepest desert areas as conditions became unsustainable. The communities that eventually concentrated along the Nile Valley, the Sahel, and the desert's northern and southern margins carried with them cultural, linguistic, and technological traditions developed during the long Green Sahara period — a reminder that the peoples who later built Sahelian and Nile Valley civilizations did not simply appear from nowhere but descended in part from populations displaced by one of history's great climate transformations.

Desertification has not been limited to the deep past. The Sahel region has experienced repeated episodes of land degradation linked to both climate variability and human land-use practices, including overgrazing, deforestation, and the expansion of agriculture into marginal land — sometimes intensified by colonial-era and postcolonial economic pressures discussed in later chapters. The severe Sahel drought of the 1970s and 1980s, which caused mass starvation and displacement across countries including Mali, Niger, Chad, and Mauritania, drew global attention to the ongoing vulnerability of the region and to the complex interaction between natural climate variability and human-driven environmental pressure.

Students should understand that desertification is rarely caused by climate alone. Human land-use decisions — driven by population growth, economic pressure, political instability, or external economic demands — frequently interact with natural climate variability to accelerate or intensify environmental degradation. Disentangling natural and human causes of desertification is one of the central challenges facing both historians and environmental scientists studying the Sahel and similar regions.

Section 6.4

Disease Environments and Human Health

Africa's disease environment has been shaped by its climate, its deep evolutionary history as the continent of human origin, and the particular ecological relationships between humans, animals, insects, and microorganisms that developed there over enormous spans of time. Several diseases have played especially significant roles in African history: malaria, transmitted by anopheles mosquitoes and historically one of the leading causes of illness and death across much of tropical Africa; trypanosomiasis, transmitted by the tsetse fly and devastating to cattle and, in its human form (sleeping sickness), to people as well; and a range of other parasitic, bacterial, and viral diseases whose prevalence has varied by region and era.

Because many of Africa's most significant diseases are vector-borne — meaning they are transmitted by insects such as mosquitoes and tsetse flies rather than by direct contact between people — their geographic distribution has historically tracked closely with climate and ecology. Malaria has been most severe in warm, humid lowland regions where anopheles mosquitoes thrive, while it has historically been less prevalent in cooler highland regions, a pattern that influenced settlement decisions across much of tropical Africa, including the preference for highland settlement in parts of Ethiopia, the East African highlands, and elsewhere.

The long coexistence of African human populations with malaria has also left a mark on human biology itself. The sickle-cell trait, in which a person carries one copy of a gene variant that in its double form causes sickle-cell disease, provides significant protection against severe malaria when carried in single form. This trait reaches its highest frequencies in populations with long histories in malaria-endemic regions of Africa, a striking example of how disease environment has shaped not only human behavior and settlement patterns but human genetics itself, through the historical process of natural selection operating over many generations.

Disease has shaped African history not only through individual illness and mortality but through its effects on settlement patterns, economic activity, and even political geography, as the following sections on the tsetse fly demonstrate in detail. Understanding disease environments is therefore essential not as a footnote to African history but as one of its central organizing forces.

Section 6.5

Agriculture, Weather, and Food Security

The relationship between weather and agriculture in Africa has always been a relationship managed through accumulated knowledge, technology, and social institutions rather than simply endured passively. Farmers across the continent developed crop varieties suited to local conditions over many centuries: drought-resistant sorghum and pearl millet across the Sahel and savanna regions, where rainfall is concentrated and unreliable; yams and oil palm in the more humid forest zones of West Africa; teff and enset in the Ethiopian highlands; and a wide range of indigenous and, later, introduced crops adapted to specific ecological niches across the continent.

Food storage systems, particularly granaries, played an essential role in converting a single good harvest into a buffer against future bad years. Across much of West Africa and the Sahel, elevated granaries built of mud, wood, or woven materials allowed communities to store grain for one or more years beyond a single harvest, smoothing out some of the worst effects of a poor rainy season. The size, organization, and control of granary systems also had political significance: rulers and elites who controlled large granaries could use stored food as a form of political power, distributing it during times of need to build loyalty or withholding it to exert control.

Trade also served as a crucial food security mechanism, allowing surplus from one region to offset shortfall in another. The long-distance trade networks discussed in Chapter 17 moved not only luxury goods like gold and salt but also grain and other foodstuffs between regions experiencing different rainfall conditions in any given year. A drought that devastated one part of the Sahel might coincide with an adequate or good harvest in another region linked by trade routes, allowing food to move toward the area of greatest need, provided trade infrastructure and political stability allowed such movement to occur.

Despite these adaptive strategies, severe and prolonged droughts could overwhelm even well-developed systems of storage and trade, producing famines documented in oral tradition, chronicles, and increasingly in paleoclimate evidence. Such famines could trigger significant historical consequences: large-scale migration, the weakening or collapse of political authority, increased vulnerability to external pressure or conquest, and sometimes long-term changes in settlement patterns and agricultural practices as communities adjusted their strategies in response to crisis.

Section 6.6

Animals, Insects, and Disease Spread

Few insects have shaped African history as profoundly as the tsetse fly. Found across a broad band of central, eastern, and parts of southern and western Africa, particularly in woodland and savanna habitats, the tsetse fly transmits trypanosomes — parasites that cause trypanosomiasis, a disease lethal to cattle, horses, and other livestock, and that causes sleeping sickness in humans. Regions infested with tsetse flies have historically been largely unsuitable for cattle-keeping and for the use of horses and other draft and pack animals, with profound consequences for the kinds of economies and political systems that could develop there.

In tsetse-free regions — including much of the Sahel, the highlands of eastern Africa, and parts of southern Africa — cattle-keeping became a central economic activity, supporting pastoralist societies whose wealth, social organization, and political structures were often built around cattle herds. In tsetse-infested regions, by contrast, societies generally could not rely on cattle to the same degree and instead developed agricultural systems based on crops, sometimes supplemented by smaller livestock such as goats and chickens that were less vulnerable to trypanosomiasis, or by hunting and fishing where conditions allowed.

The tsetse fly's distribution also affected military and political history. The absence of horses across much of tsetse-infested central Africa meant that cavalry warfare, which played a major role in the military history of the Sahel and the savanna regions to the north, was largely impossible in much of the forest and woodland zones further south. This contributed to different patterns of military organization, state formation, and the scale of political units that could be effectively governed and defended across different regions of the continent.

Other animals and insects also shaped disease history in significant ways: the anopheles mosquito and malaria, already discussed; various species of tick transmitting diseases affecting both livestock and humans; and the complex ecological relationships between wild animal reservoirs of disease, domestic animals, and human populations that varied by region and changed over time as land use, settlement density, and contact between wild and domestic animal populations shifted.

Historical Debate

How Much Did the Tsetse Fly Shape African State Formation?

The question: Some historians and geographers have argued that the distribution of the tsetse fly was one of the most powerful single factors shaping the political geography of precolonial Africa, while others caution against overstating its importance relative to social, political, and economic factors.

Interpretation A — The Tsetse Fly as a Major Structuring Force: Proponents of this view point to the striking correlation between tsetse-free zones and the rise of large, cavalry-supported states and empires across the Sahel and parts of eastern Africa, contrasted with the generally smaller-scale political units that developed across much of the tsetse-infested forest and woodland zones of central Africa, where the absence of cattle and horses limited certain forms of military and economic organization.
Interpretation B — A More Cautious View: Other historians argue that powerful states did develop in tsetse-infested regions — including Kongo, the Luba and Lunda kingdoms, and forest kingdoms such as Benin and Asante — demonstrating that disease environment alone cannot explain political outcomes. These historians emphasize trade, ideology, leadership, and social organization as equally or more important factors, and warn against environmental determinism that risks reducing complex political histories to a single biological cause.

Evidence: Supporters of Interpretation A point to maps overlaying tsetse distribution with patterns of cavalry warfare and large-scale Sahelian state formation. Supporters of Interpretation B point to detailed histories of powerful forest and Central African kingdoms, discussed in Chapters 24, 35, and 36, that achieved considerable scale and sophistication without cattle or horse-based military power.

Why it matters: This debate illustrates a larger question in African historiography: how much weight should be given to environmental versus social and political explanations of historical development, and how can historians avoid both ignoring environmental factors and reducing African history to environmental determinism.

Student task: Using the evidence about both tsetse-free Sahelian states and tsetse-infested forest kingdoms, evaluate which interpretation better accounts for the diversity of African political development. Can both interpretations be partially correct?

Section 6.7

Adaptation and Survival Strategies

African societies developed a remarkably broad range of strategies to manage environmental and disease risk over many centuries. Among pastoralist societies, transhumance — the seasonal movement of herds and often entire communities between wet-season and dry-season grazing areas — allowed cattle, sheep, and goats to access water and pasture that varied predictably by season, while avoiding areas where disease risk to livestock was highest at particular times of year.

Among farming societies, strategies included the diversification of crops to spread risk across plants with different rainfall and soil requirements, the staggering of planting times to reduce the chance that a single weather event would destroy an entire harvest, the development of granary storage systems already discussed, and the maintenance of social and kinship networks that obligated better-off relatives or community members to assist those facing hardship — an institutionalized form of risk-sharing that operated independently of, but often alongside, formal political and economic structures.

Communities living in disease-prone environments developed practical knowledge — sometimes embedded in seasonal settlement patterns, ritual practice, or herbal medicine — that reduced exposure to disease risk even without a modern scientific understanding of disease transmission. Settlement away from swampy, mosquito-prone lowlands during certain seasons, the use of smoke and certain plants believed to repel insects, and the avoidance of specific water sources at specific times all reflected centuries of accumulated environmental observation.

Trade and political alliance also functioned as adaptation strategies at a larger scale, allowing regions experiencing local environmental crisis to draw on resources, food, or political support from regions not currently affected by the same conditions. The interconnectedness of African trade networks, discussed throughout Part III and Part IV of this textbook, meant that environmental crisis in one region could sometimes be buffered by connections to less-affected regions — though such buffering was never perfect or guaranteed, and severe, widespread, or prolonged crises could overwhelm even well-developed adaptive systems.

Historical Vocabulary
Resilience

Resilience, in environmental and historical analysis, refers to the capacity of a society to absorb environmental shocks — such as drought, famine, or disease outbreak — and continue functioning, recovering, or adapting rather than collapsing entirely. Resilience is built through diverse strategies including food storage, crop diversification, trade networks, social support systems, and accumulated environmental knowledge.

Historians studying resilience in African history emphasize that no society was ever completely immune to environmental shock, but that the specific combination of strategies a society developed could make the difference between a difficult year and a catastrophic famine, or between temporary disruption and long-term societal collapse.

Section 6.8

Environmental Pressure and Migration

Environmental pressure has been one of several important factors driving migration throughout African history, though historians caution against treating environment as the sole or even primary cause of most major migrations, which typically resulted from a combination of environmental, economic, political, and social factors working together. The desiccation of the Sahara, discussed earlier in this chapter, almost certainly contributed to the gradual concentration of populations along the Nile Valley, the Sahel, and other better-watered margins of the desert, as communities that had once ranged across the Green Sahara found their traditional territories increasingly uninhabitable.

The Bantu migrations, examined in detail in Chapter 14, were driven primarily by the spread of agricultural and ironworking technology and population growth rather than by environmental crisis, but the routes and pace of this expansion were nonetheless shaped by environmental factors, including the distribution of land suitable for the crops Bantu-speaking farmers cultivated and the disease environments, including tsetse-infested zones, that influenced where cattle-keeping aspects of Bantu economic life could be practiced.

More localized and shorter-term migrations frequently followed severe drought or famine episodes, as communities moved toward better-watered areas, toward regions with surviving food stores, or toward areas controlled by rulers or communities able to offer relief or protection. Such drought-driven migrations are documented in oral traditions across many regions of the continent, sometimes describing the founding of new settlements or the absorption of refugee populations into existing communities, and increasingly corroborated by archaeological and paleoclimate evidence that confirms the timing of major drought episodes referenced in these traditions.

Students should understand migration in African history as a complex, multi-causal phenomenon. Environmental pressure rarely operated alone; it interacted with political conditions, including warfare and the availability of land under rival political authorities, with economic opportunity, including access to trade routes or fertile land, and with social factors, including kinship networks that might draw migrants toward areas where relatives or allies could offer support.

Section 6.9

Nature and Human History in Africa

This chapter closes by returning to a theme introduced at its opening: the relationship between nature and human history in Africa has always been reciprocal rather than one-directional. Environmental conditions shaped human possibilities, but human societies also actively shaped their environments, sometimes intentionally and sometimes as an unintended consequence of economic activity. The clearing of forest for agriculture, the management of grazing land by pastoralist societies, the construction of irrigation systems along river valleys, and the selective breeding of crops and livestock all represent ways that African societies actively modified their environments over long periods of time, rather than simply adapting passively to conditions imposed from outside.

At the same time, students should resist two opposite temptations when thinking about environment and African history. The first temptation is environmental determinism — the assumption that environmental conditions alone explain historical outcomes, which risks erasing the agency, creativity, and choices of African societies and can shade into harmful stereotypes that attribute African historical experience entirely to geography or climate rather than to the same complex mix of political, economic, social, and cultural factors that shape history everywhere. The second temptation is to ignore environment altogether, treating climate and disease as irrelevant background details rather than as forces that demonstrably shaped settlement patterns, economic systems, military history, and political geography across the continent.

The balanced approach this chapter has modeled treats environment as one among several important forces in African history, interacting with — never replacing — the political, economic, social, religious, and cultural forces examined throughout the rest of this textbook. This approach allows students to take environmental history seriously without reducing African history to a story about nature alone, and it prepares students to understand later chapters in which climate, disease, and environmental change continue to appear: in discussions of trade routes shaped by desert and savanna geography, in the disease environments that affected the Atlantic and Indian Ocean slave trades, in the environmental dimensions of colonial economic policy, and in the urgent climate challenges facing contemporary Africa discussed in this textbook's concluding chapter.

Primary Source Window

Nineteenth-Century Travelers' Accounts of Disease and Environment in West Africa

Source type: European travel and missionary accounts, nineteenth century · Context: Written observations by outsiders, later cross-checked against scientific and local knowledge

Context: Beginning in the nineteenth century, European explorers, missionaries, and colonial officials traveling in West and Central Africa wrote detailed accounts of the landscapes, climates, and diseases they encountered. These accounts, written before the germ theory of disease was fully developed and accepted, often described high rates of illness among European travelers and noted, sometimes with curiosity, that local populations seemed to manage certain environmental risks through practices the writers did not always understand.

Several such accounts described local communities avoiding settlement near certain swampy or forested areas during particular seasons, and noted detailed local knowledge of which areas were associated with high rates of fever, without the writers themselves understanding the mosquito-borne basis of the diseases they were describing.

Analysis: These sources are valuable because they document, often unintentionally, sophisticated local environmental and medical knowledge that predated formal scientific understanding of disease transmission. However, they must be read critically: they were written by outsiders with limited local language ability, often filtered through assumptions of European cultural superiority common in this period, and they tell historians more about European observation and interpretation than about the full depth of African knowledge systems, which are better understood through oral tradition, ethnobotanical study, and collaboration with descendant communities.

Questions for students:

  1. What does this type of source reveal about African environmental and medical knowledge?
  2. What are the limitations of relying primarily on European travelers' accounts to understand African environmental knowledge?
  3. What other types of sources might historians use to better understand local disease knowledge from an African perspective?
  4. Why is it important for historians to read this type of source critically rather than at face value?
Multiple Perspectives

Experiencing Drought in the Sahel

From the perspective of subsistence farming households: A severe drought could mean the destruction of an entire year's harvest, forcing difficult decisions about migration, the sale of livestock or other assets, or reliance on stored grain, kinship networks, or community support systems to survive until the next planting season.
From the perspective of pastoralist communities: Drought threatened the survival of herds on which wealth, food, and social status depended, often requiring long-distance movement in search of remaining water and pasture, sometimes bringing pastoralist groups into competition or conflict with farming communities over increasingly scarce resources.
From the perspective of rulers and political elites: Drought and famine could threaten political stability, requiring rulers to manage food distribution from royal or communal granaries, respond to population displacement, and maintain legitimacy in the eyes of subjects facing severe hardship — or, in some cases, allowing rulers who successfully managed crisis to strengthen their authority and prestige.
From the perspective of merchants and traders: Drought in one region could create opportunities as well as risks, as merchants moved grain and other goods from less-affected regions toward areas of shortage, sometimes profiting from price differences while also playing an essential role in regional food security.
Cause and Effect

The End of the Green Sahara

Long-Term Causes

Gradual shifts in Earth's orbital geometry weakened the African summer monsoon over thousands of years, reducing rainfall delivery to the Sahara region.

Short-Term/Accelerating Factors

Feedback loops, including reduced vegetation cover increasing surface reflectivity and further reducing rainfall, accelerated drying in some regions once initial aridification began.

The Transformation

Over roughly three thousand years, lakes dried, grasslands retreated, and pastoralist populations concentrated increasingly along the Nile Valley, the Sahel, and the desert's margins.

Effects

Concentration of population along the Nile contributed to the dense settlement that supported the rise of ancient Egyptian civilization; Sahelian populations developed new agricultural and trade strategies adapted to semi-arid conditions.

Historical complexity: The desiccation of the Sahara was not a single event with a single cause but a multi-thousand-year process involving astronomical, atmospheric, and ecological feedback mechanisms, unfolding unevenly across different parts of the desert and producing gradual, regionally varied human responses rather than a single dramatic migration event.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Disease and African Society from Antiquity to the Present

What Changed

  • Scientific understanding of disease transmission, developed mainly from the late nineteenth century onward, gradually supplemented older forms of practical and traditional knowledge
  • Modern medical interventions, including vaccines, antimalarial treatments, and antiretroviral therapy for HIV, dramatically changed mortality patterns in many regions by the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries
  • Urbanization and increased mobility changed patterns of disease spread compared to earlier, more localized rural disease environments

What Stayed the Same

  • Climate and ecology continued to shape the geographic distribution of vector-borne diseases such as malaria
  • Disease continued to interact with poverty, political instability, and unequal access to resources in shaping health outcomes
  • Communities continued to rely on a combination of formal medical systems and traditional and local knowledge in managing health risks

Why the pattern matters: Recognizing both the real changes brought by modern medicine and the persistent influence of underlying environmental and social conditions helps students understand contemporary African health challenges, including the HIV/AIDS epidemic and continued malaria burden, as part of a long historical pattern rather than as entirely new or disconnected phenomena.

Everyday Life and Social History

Living with Climate Risk in a Sahelian Village

For an ordinary farming family in a Sahelian village across many centuries, the rhythm of daily and seasonal life was organized substantially around the management of environmental risk. The arrival, strength, and duration of the rainy season determined when fields were planted, weeded, and harvested, and households closely observed weather signs accumulated through generations of practical knowledge to make planting decisions. Women in many Sahelian societies played central roles in food processing and storage, managing grain reserves that represented a household's primary buffer against a poor harvest, and often held specialized knowledge of wild food sources that could supplement diets during lean periods.

Children in farming households typically began contributing labor to weeding, bird-scaring, and harvest tasks from a young age, reflecting the labor-intensive nature of agriculture in environments without mechanized tools. Community-level institutions, including mutual labor-sharing arrangements and kinship-based obligations to assist relatives facing hardship, provided a social safety net that operated independently of, but often alongside, the storage and trade strategies discussed earlier in this chapter. Religious and ritual practices, discussed further in Chapter 26, frequently incorporated prayers, ceremonies, or rituals associated with rainfall and agricultural fertility, reflecting the deep cultural significance of climate risk in shaping the spiritual as well as material life of farming communities.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has examined climate, environment, and disease as active forces in African history rather than as a passive backdrop to political and economic developments. It began with one of the most dramatic environmental transformations documented anywhere in human history: the multi-thousand-year desiccation of the Green Sahara into the modern desert, a process that reshaped the geography of human settlement across the entire northern half of the continent and contributed to the population concentrations that later supported civilizations along the Nile and across the Sahel.

The chapter then examined how rainfall variability and river cycles created both risks and opportunities for African societies. The Nile's flood cycle, decoupled from local rainfall through its connection to distant Ethiopian highland monsoons, allowed an unusually stable and productive agricultural system to develop in Egypt and Nubia. Other river systems, including the Niger and Congo, played comparable though distinct roles in supporting agriculture, fishing, and trade across different regions.

Desertification, examined both as an ancient process affecting the Sahara and as an ongoing challenge in the modern Sahel, illustrated how natural climate variability and human land-use practices interact to produce environmental degradation, and how the resulting pressures have shaped migration, economic adaptation, and political crisis across many centuries.

The chapter devoted significant attention to disease environments, particularly malaria and trypanosomiasis, examining how these vector-borne diseases shaped settlement patterns, economic activity, and even human genetics, through the example of the sickle-cell trait. The tsetse fly's distribution across central Africa was examined in particular detail as a case study in the complex relationship between disease environment and political geography, including a Historical Debate feature exploring how much weight historians should give to environmental factors relative to social and political ones in explaining African state formation.

Agricultural adaptation to weather risk was examined through crop diversification, granary storage systems, and trade networks that allowed food to move between regions experiencing different conditions in any given year. Broader adaptation and survival strategies, including pastoralist transhumance, social risk-sharing institutions, and accumulated practical environmental knowledge, were presented as evidence of African societies' active, often sophisticated responses to environmental risk, rather than passive vulnerability to forces beyond their control.

The chapter examined environmental pressure as one of several factors contributing to historical patterns of migration, cautioning against treating environment as a sole or primary cause of major population movements such as the Bantu migrations, which are more fully explained by a combination of technological, demographic, political, and environmental factors working together.

Throughout, the chapter has emphasized a balanced analytical approach: taking environmental and disease history seriously as genuine, demonstrable forces shaping African historical development, while resisting environmental determinism that would reduce African history to a story about climate and disease alone, erasing the agency, creativity, and institutional sophistication that African societies brought to managing environmental risk across many centuries.

Finally, the chapter connected this ancient and medieval environmental history to the contemporary challenges of Sahelian drought, the HIV/AIDS epidemic, and anthropogenic climate change, demonstrating that the environmental forces examined in this chapter did not end in the distant past but continue to shape African history into the present day, a theme this textbook returns to in its concluding chapter on Africa's future.

Understanding climate, environment, and disease as historical forces equips students to read the rest of this textbook with a richer appreciation for why African societies developed where and how they did, why certain trade routes, agricultural systems, and political structures took the forms they did, and why environmental challenges facing Africa today are best understood not as new problems but as the latest chapter in an extraordinarily long history of environmental adaptation, resilience, and, at times, crisis.

Review Materials

Key Terms

African Humid Period
A climate phase from roughly 14,500 to 5,000 years ago during which the Sahara received far more rainfall and supported lakes, grassland, and human settlement.
Desertification
The process by which previously productive land becomes increasingly arid, losing vegetation, agricultural, and grazing capacity.
Sahel
A semi-arid belt south of the Sahara characterized by highly variable rainfall and historical vulnerability to drought and famine.
Monsoon
A seasonal wind and rainfall system; African monsoon strength historically determined Saharan rainfall and the flooding of the Nile via Ethiopian highland rains.
Drought
An extended period of below-average rainfall, historically a major cause of famine, migration, and political crisis in African history.
Famine
Severe and widespread food shortage, often resulting from drought, crop failure, or disruption of food distribution systems.
Disease environment
The combination of climate, ecology, and host-pathogen relationships that determines which diseases are prevalent in a given region.
Vector-borne disease
A disease transmitted by an intermediary organism, such as a mosquito or fly, rather than through direct human-to-human contact.
Trypanosomiasis
A disease transmitted by the tsetse fly, lethal to cattle and other livestock and causing sleeping sickness in humans.
Malaria
A parasitic disease transmitted by anopheles mosquitoes, historically a major cause of illness and death across tropical Africa.
Sickle-cell trait
A genetic variant providing partial protection against severe malaria, common in populations with long histories in malaria-endemic regions.
Transhumance
The seasonal movement of livestock herds, and often herding communities, between different grazing areas to access water and pasture.
Resilience
A society's capacity to absorb environmental shocks and continue functioning, recovering, or adapting rather than collapsing.
Food security
Reliable access to sufficient food, historically managed through storage, trade, diversification, and social support systems.
Granary economy
An economic and political system organized around the storage and controlled distribution of grain surplus, often controlled by rulers or elites.
Sediment core
A cylindrical sample extracted from lake or ocean beds, used by scientists to reconstruct past climate and environmental conditions.
Pollen analysis
The study of fossilized pollen grains preserved in sediment to reconstruct past vegetation and climate conditions.
Environmental determinism
The flawed assumption that environmental conditions alone determine historical outcomes, often criticized for erasing human agency and choice.
Ecological adaptation
The process by which societies (or species) adjust behavior, technology, or biology in response to environmental conditions.
Climate variability
Year-to-year or season-to-season fluctuation in climate conditions, distinct from long-term climate change.
River regime
The characteristic pattern of a river's flow and flooding over the course of a year, shaped by the climate of its catchment area.
Pastoralism
An economic system centered on the herding of livestock, often involving seasonal movement to access grazing land and water.
Famine relief
Organized efforts, historical or modern, to distribute food or resources to populations affected by famine.
Tsetse fly belt
The geographic zone across central, eastern, and parts of southern and western Africa where tsetse flies historically limited cattle-keeping.
Paleoclimate
Past climate conditions reconstructed using scientific evidence such as sediment cores, ice cores, and tree rings.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter has shown that African history cannot be fully understood without attention to the climates, environments, and diseases within which African societies lived, worked, and built their civilizations. From the dramatic transformation of the Green Sahara into the modern desert to the ongoing vulnerability of the Sahel to drought, environmental forces have shaped where people settled, what they farmed, how they organized their economies and political systems, and how they moved across the continent over many thousands of years.

This chapter matters for the rest of this textbook because environmental and disease history will recur throughout the chapters that follow: in the rise of Nile Valley civilizations made possible by a uniquely stable river regime, in the trans-Saharan trade routes that connected regions once joined by a now-vanished green landscape, in the disease environments that shaped both the Atlantic and Indian Ocean slave trades, in colonial economic policies that often misunderstood or disregarded African environmental knowledge, and in the urgent climate challenges facing the continent today.

The larger theme this chapter reveals is that African societies were never simply passive subjects of their environments. They studied, adapted to, and at times actively reshaped the climates and ecosystems within which they lived, developing agricultural techniques, storage systems, migration strategies, and bodies of practical knowledge refined over many centuries. At the same time, environmental forces could and did overwhelm even sophisticated adaptive systems, producing famine, migration, and political crisis that shaped the course of African history in ways too significant to ignore.

Understanding this chapter helps students see Africa as a continent of extraordinary environmental diversity and historical dynamism — a continent whose peoples have continuously negotiated, for tens of thousands of years, the relationship between human ambition and natural constraint. That negotiation continues today, as African societies confront climate change, persistent disease burdens, and questions of environmental resilience that are, in important ways, the latest chapter in a very long history.

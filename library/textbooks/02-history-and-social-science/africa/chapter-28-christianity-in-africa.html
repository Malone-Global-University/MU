Part V — Religion, Learning, and African Civilizations
28

Christianity in Africa

Africa: A History of a Continent

Chapter Opening

"Christianity is not a European import grafted onto Africa. It is one of the oldest African religious traditions, rooted on the continent within a generation of Christianity's founding and continuously practiced ever since."

In the highlands of northern Ethiopia stands the town of Lalibela, where, beginning in the twelfth century, builders carved eleven churches directly out of solid volcanic rock, working downward from the surface rather than building upward from a foundation. Pilgrims still climb narrow stone passages between these subterranean churches today, walking the same paths their ancestors walked eight centuries ago, observing fasts and feast days according to a liturgical calendar that has remained recognizably continuous since the Ethiopian church's earliest centuries. Lalibela was built roughly four hundred years before a Portuguese ship ever reached the Ethiopian coast, and more than three hundred years before any European missionary attempted to bring Christianity to sub-Saharan Africa. The church that built it was already old by the time European Christianity began arriving on African shores in the colonial era.

This fact unsettles a common assumption: that Christianity arrived in Africa through European colonialism and missionary activity in the nineteenth century. That assumption is true for parts of the continent and false for others, and even where it is true, it captures only part of a much longer and more complicated story. Christianity reached Egypt within decades of the crucifixion of Jesus, according to a tradition that places the evangelist Mark in Alexandria by the middle of the first century CE. By the second and third centuries, Christian communities existed across Roman North Africa, from Egypt to present-day Tunisia and Algeria, producing some of the most important theologians in the entire history of Christian thought. Christianity reached the kingdom of Aksum in the Horn of Africa by the fourth century, making Aksum one of the first states anywhere in the world — earlier than the Roman Empire itself — to adopt Christianity as an official religion. From Aksum, Christianity put down roots in the Ethiopian highlands that have never been dislodged, surviving Islamic expansion, regional wars, and centuries of relative isolation from other Christian centers.

This chapter studies the long, varied, and often contested history of Christianity in Africa — a history that spans nearly two thousand years and includes ancient North African churches that produced foundational Christian theology, an Ethiopian church with an unbroken history stretching back to antiquity, medieval Christian kingdoms in Nubia, a Kongo kingdom that adopted Catholicism as a matter of royal policy in the late fifteenth century, nineteenth-century missionary movements tied closely to European colonial expansion, and a modern explosion of African-led Christian movements, including independent churches, Pentecostal and charismatic movements, and African theological traditions that have reshaped global Christianity. Today, Africa is home to several hundred million Christians, and demographic projections suggest that within a few decades a larger share of the world's Christians may live in Africa than on any other continent. Understanding how this came to be requires moving well past the simple story of European missionaries bringing Christianity to a religiously empty continent.

It also requires confronting genuine complexity and real harm. Christianity arrived in different parts of Africa under dramatically different circumstances and with dramatically different consequences. In Egypt and Ethiopia, Christianity took root through trade contact, intellectual exchange, and voluntary royal conversion, becoming thoroughly African religious traditions within a few centuries. In much of sub-Saharan Africa during the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, by contrast, Christian missionary activity arrived alongside European conquest, often serving — intentionally or not — as a tool of cultural disruption and colonial control, even as individual missionaries sometimes opposed colonial abuses and African converts often used the same Christian institutions to resist colonial authority. Both of these histories are part of the same religious tradition, and a serious historical account must hold them together rather than choosing one story and ignoring the other.

This chapter also asks students to think carefully about a central historical pattern: the African appropriation, adaptation, and re-creation of Christianity. Wherever Christianity has taken root in Africa, African Christians have not simply received a foreign religion passively. They have interpreted it, translated it, debated it, modified its institutions, blended it with existing spiritual frameworks, and in many cases broken away from foreign-controlled churches to found their own. The result is not one African Christianity but many: Coptic and Ethiopian Orthodox traditions with roots in antiquity, Catholic and Protestant churches established through nineteenth- and twentieth-century missions, and a vast and constantly growing array of African-founded independent churches, Pentecostal movements, and prophetic traditions that have made Christianity in twenty-first-century Africa one of the most dynamic religious landscapes anywhere in the world.

The evidence historians use to study this subject is itself diverse: theological writings by ancient North African church fathers; Ethiopian royal chronicles, manuscripts, and church architecture; Portuguese and Kongo court correspondence; missionary society records and African converts' letters and diaries; colonial administrative documents; and, for the contemporary period, oral testimony, congregational records, and the sheer demographic and institutional evidence of churches, denominations, and movements that exist today. Reading these sources requires attention to whose voice is recorded and whose is missing, what purposes a document served, and how power shaped who could write and who could not.

Finally, this chapter connects to themes developed elsewhere in this textbook. It connects to the history of Indigenous African religions (Chapter 26), since Christian conversion in Africa nearly always involved encounter, negotiation, and blending with existing spiritual worldviews rather than their simple erasure. It connects to the history of Islam in Africa (Chapter 27), since Christianity and Islam have coexisted, competed, and sometimes clashed across the continent for over a thousand years. And it connects directly to the chapters on colonialism, resistance, and decolonization later in this textbook, since the history of Christian missions in Africa cannot be separated from the history of European conquest, even as African Christians turned the same religious tradition into a powerful resource for asserting dignity, education, and ultimately independence.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain how and when Christianity first reached North Africa, the Nile Valley, and the Horn of Africa, and why this history predates European colonialism by more than a thousand years.
  2. Trace the development of the ancient North African church and identify its major theological contributions to world Christianity.
  3. Describe the origins, survival, and institutions of Ethiopian Christianity from the Aksumite period to the present.
  4. Analyze the role of churches, monasteries, and scriptoria as centers of learning, manuscript production, and cultural memory.
  5. Evaluate the relationship between Christianity and political authority in African kingdoms and colonial states.
  6. Compare different missionary movements in Africa and assess their varied relationships to European colonialism.
  7. Identify the ways African Christians adapted, translated, and Africanized Christian belief and practice.
  8. Assess the consequences of religious change for African societies, families, education, and gender roles.
  9. Distinguish among major traditions of African Christianity, including Orthodox, Catholic, Protestant, independent, and Pentecostal/charismatic churches.
  10. Interpret primary sources, including theological writings, royal correspondence, and missionary records, with attention to perspective and bias.
  11. Connect the history of Christianity in Africa to broader themes of religion, colonialism, resistance, and cultural change across the continent.
  12. Contextualize contemporary African Christianity within both its ancient African roots and its rapid twentieth- and twenty-first-century growth.

Guiding Historical Questions

Questions for Historical Inquiry

  1. Why is it historically inaccurate to describe Christianity simply as a European religion brought to Africa by colonial missionaries?
  2. How did Christianity become rooted in Egypt and Ethiopia so early, and what allowed Ethiopian Christianity in particular to survive for nearly two thousand years?
  3. What role did churches and monasteries play as centers of learning, record-keeping, and cultural continuity?
  4. How did African rulers use Christianity to build, legitimate, or contest political power?
  5. What relationship existed between Christian missionary activity and European colonial conquest, and how did that relationship vary across regions and time periods?
  6. How did African converts reinterpret, adapt, and Africanize Christian belief and practice rather than simply adopting it unchanged?
  7. Why did so many African Christians break away from foreign-led mission churches to found independent African churches?
  8. How did Christianity affect family structure, gender roles, education, and everyday life in different African societies?
  9. What explains the explosive growth of Pentecostal and charismatic Christianity in contemporary Africa?
  10. What does the history of Christianity in Africa reveal about the relationship between religion, culture, and political power more broadly?

Historical Context

Before tracing the specific history of Christianity in Africa, students need to understand several background conditions. First, Africa in the first centuries of the common era was already deeply connected to the wider Mediterranean and Red Sea worlds. Egypt was a Roman province and one of the empire's most important centers of trade, agriculture, and learning, home to the great library and scholarly community of Alexandria. The kingdoms of Nubia, immediately south of Egypt, had a long history of contact, exchange, and conflict with Egyptian civilization (see Chapter 8). The kingdom of Aksum, in the highlands of present-day Ethiopia and Eritrea, was a major commercial power linked by Red Sea trade routes to Arabia, the Mediterranean, and South Asia (see Chapter 10). None of these regions was isolated from the religious and intellectual currents of the ancient world; all of them were positioned to receive, adapt, and transform new religious traditions arriving through trade and travel.

Second, religious life across ancient and medieval Africa was never uniform. Most African societies maintained indigenous religious traditions rooted in beliefs about ancestors, spiritual forces, sacred kingship, and ritual community life, as described in Chapter 26. When Christianity (and later Islam, as described in Chapter 27) arrived in particular regions, it did not arrive into a religious vacuum. It entered into societies with existing, often sophisticated, frameworks for understanding the sacred, morality, healing, and the relationship between the visible and invisible worlds. Christian missionaries and converts therefore always faced a process of translation and negotiation, not simple transmission.

Third, the timing and mechanism of Christian arrival varied enormously by region, and this variation is the single most important fact for understanding the diversity of African Christian history. In Egypt, Christianity spread through urban trade networks, intellectual centers, and eventually through state recognition under the later Roman and Byzantine empires. In Aksum, Christianity arrived through royal conversion at the highest level of the state, associated with court connections to the eastern Mediterranean. In Nubia, Christianity arrived through missionary activity sponsored by Byzantine emperors in the sixth century, leading to centuries of Christian Nubian kingdoms. In the Kongo kingdom of Central Africa, Christianity arrived through contact with Portuguese traders and missionaries beginning in 1483, and the Kongo monarchy adopted it as a matter of explicit royal policy. In most of the rest of sub-Saharan Africa, sustained Christian missionary activity did not arrive until the nineteenth century, and arrived overwhelmingly through European missionary societies operating in a context of expanding European commercial and, eventually, colonial power.

Fourth, students should understand from the outset that "Christianity in Africa" is not one tradition but several distinct families of tradition: the ancient Oriental Orthodox traditions of Egypt (the Coptic Orthodox Church) and Ethiopia and Eritrea (the Ethiopian and Eritrean Orthodox Tewahedo churches); Roman Catholicism, introduced in different periods through Portuguese, French, Belgian, and other European contact and missionary activity; Protestant Christianity, introduced through British, German, American, and other Protestant missionary societies beginning especially in the nineteenth century; and a vast and continuously expanding world of African-founded independent churches, including African Initiated Churches, Zionist and prophetic movements, and Pentecostal and charismatic churches, many of which emerged in the twentieth century as African Christians broke away from foreign mission control or founded entirely new movements combining Christian belief with African spiritual frameworks. Each of these traditions has its own history, theology, institutions, and relationship to political power, and each deserves to be understood on its own terms rather than treated as a variation on a single European model.

Finally, the history of Christianity in Africa cannot be separated from the history of power. In every period covered by this chapter, religious change intersected with questions of political authority, economic interest, and — especially from the nineteenth century onward — colonial domination. Understanding this intersection without reducing the entire history to a story of domination, and without ignoring the genuine religious conviction, agency, and creativity of African Christians across two thousand years, is the central interpretive challenge of this chapter.

Section 28.1

Early Christianity in Africa

Christianity's arrival in Africa is not a footnote to the history of European or Middle Eastern Christianity; it is part of the foundation of Christianity itself. Egypt, and specifically the city of Alexandria, was one of the most important centers of the early Christian world, on par with Jerusalem, Antioch, and Rome. According to church tradition, the evangelist Mark established the church in Alexandria in the middle of the first century CE, although the earliest verifiable documentary evidence for an organized Egyptian Christian community dates to the second century. By that time, Alexandria was already a city of extraordinary intellectual energy, home to a famous library, a large and diverse Jewish community, Greek philosophical schools, and a cosmopolitan population that included merchants, scholars, and travelers from across the Mediterranean and Red Sea worlds. Christianity, like many other religious and philosophical movements of the era, found fertile ground in this environment of intense intellectual exchange.

By the late second and early third centuries, Alexandria had become home to one of the most important centers of Christian theological education anywhere in the ancient world: the Catechetical School of Alexandria. Teachers associated with this school, including Clement of Alexandria and later Origen, developed sophisticated methods of biblical interpretation that drew on Greek philosophy, and their work shaped Christian theology for centuries afterward, well beyond Africa's borders. Origen in particular was an extraordinarily prolific scholar whose textual and theological work influenced both Eastern and Western Christianity, even though some of his specific theological positions were later judged unorthodox by church councils.

Christianity also spread rapidly across Roman North Africa west of Egypt, in the regions corresponding to modern Libya, Tunisia, and Algeria. By the late second century, Christian communities existed in Carthage and other North African cities (see Chapter 9 for the earlier history of Carthage), and North African Christianity quickly developed a reputation for intensity, theological creativity, and resistance to Roman persecution. Tertullian, a Carthaginian writer of the late second and early third centuries, became one of the first major Latin-language Christian theologians, credited with shaping much of the Latin Christian theological vocabulary still used today. Cyprian, bishop of Carthage in the mid-third century, was martyred during Roman persecution and became one of the most influential figures in debates over church authority and unity. Most significantly, Augustine of Hippo, born in 354 CE in present-day Algeria, became arguably the single most influential theologian in the history of Western Christianity; his works, including the Confessions and City of God, shaped Catholic and, much later, Protestant theology for the next sixteen centuries.

It is essential for students to recognize that these towering figures of early Christian thought were Africans, operating within African intellectual and religious institutions, even as they wrote in Greek and Latin and engaged with a broader Mediterranean Christian world. The history of Christian theology cannot be told accurately without recognizing North Africa's foundational role. This matters historically because later narratives — particularly those constructed during the era of European colonialism — sometimes presented Christianity as an exclusively European tradition exported to Africa for the first time in the modern era. The actual history shows the opposite: African Christian communities were shaping core Christian doctrine while much of northern and western Europe remained outside the Christian world entirely.

North African Christianity also experienced significant internal conflict, particularly the Donatist controversy of the fourth and fifth centuries, a dispute over how to treat Christians who had compromised with Roman authorities during persecution, and over the legitimacy of church leaders consecrated by those who had compromised. This controversy, which Augustine spent much of his career addressing, illustrates an important historical point: early African Christianity was not a single, unified tradition but a contested and evolving set of communities engaged in serious theological and institutional debate.

Historical Vocabulary
Autocephaly

Autocephaly refers to the status of a church that governs itself independently, with its own head bishop (such as a patriarch or pope) rather than being subordinate to the authority of another church's leadership. The Coptic Orthodox Church of Egypt has historically been governed by its own patriarch, based traditionally in Alexandria, and the Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church gained full autocephaly — meaning the right to consecrate its own head bishop without requiring approval from the Coptic patriarch in Egypt — only in 1959, after centuries during which the Ethiopian church's senior bishop (the abuna) was traditionally appointed from Egypt.

This history matters because it shows that even within deeply rooted African Christian traditions, questions of institutional independence and self-governance have been historically significant and sometimes contested for centuries — a theme that resonates with later African struggles for political independence from colonial powers.

Section 28.2

North African Christian Communities

The fate of North African Christianity after the height of the Roman and Byzantine periods illustrates how religious history is shaped by political conquest, demographic change, and the long-term resilience or disappearance of religious communities under changed political conditions. Two starkly different outcomes unfolded in Egypt and in the rest of North Africa, and understanding why requires careful attention to context.

In Egypt, Christianity remained the majority religion for centuries after the Arab-Islamic conquest of 639–642 CE, and the Coptic Christian community has persisted continuously to the present day, even as it gradually became a religious minority within a predominantly Muslim Egyptian society. The term "Coptic" derives from the Greek word for Egyptian and came to refer specifically to Egyptian Christians and their distinctive church. Coptic Christianity maintained its own liturgical language (Coptic, descended from ancient Egyptian), its own monastic tradition — Egypt is widely regarded as the birthplace of Christian monasticism, with figures such as Anthony of the Desert establishing patterns of ascetic withdrawal that spread throughout the Christian world — and its own theological identity, particularly its rejection of the Christological formula adopted at the Council of Chalcedon in 451 CE. This rejection placed the Coptic Church, along with the Ethiopian and several other ancient churches, within what is now called the Oriental Orthodox communion, theologically distinct from both the Roman Catholic Church and the Eastern Orthodox churches that accepted Chalcedon.

Under successive Islamic states — the early Caliphates, the Fatimids, the Mamluks, and the Ottomans — Coptic Christians experienced varying degrees of legal restriction, taxation (notably the jizya tax imposed on non-Muslim subjects), periods of relative tolerance, and periods of more severe persecution, alongside long stretches of ordinary coexistence in which Copts served as administrators, scribes, physicians, and craftsmen within Muslim-ruled states. The proportion of Egypt's population that remained Christian declined gradually over many centuries, through conversion, social pressure, and demographic change, until Copts became a substantial but clear minority — generally estimated today at somewhere between five and fifteen percent of Egypt's population, with debates over the precise figure reflecting both methodological difficulty and political sensitivity. Importantly, the Coptic community never disappeared; it remains one of the largest and oldest continuously existing Christian communities in the world, with its own pope (the Coptic Patriarch of Alexandria), monasteries, and growing global diaspora.

In the rest of Roman North Africa — the regions of modern Libya, Tunisia, Algeria, and Morocco — the outcome was markedly different. Christianity, which had been a major and intellectually vibrant presence in the third and fourth centuries, largely disappeared as an organized religious community within a few centuries of the Arab-Islamic conquests beginning in the seventh century. Historians debate the precise causes: the disruptive impact of earlier Vandal and Byzantine conquests in the fifth and sixth centuries, internal church divisions such as Donatism that weakened institutional cohesion, the absence of a strong indigenous monastic and linguistic tradition comparable to Coptic Egypt's, and the gradual integration of North African populations into an Arabic-speaking, Muslim-majority society over succeeding centuries. By the twelfth century, organized Christian communities in the Maghreb (the western part of North Africa) had effectively ceased to exist as a continuous indigenous tradition, even though the region's deep Christian past — the cities and writings of Tertullian, Cyprian, and Augustine — remained part of the shared heritage of world Christianity.

This contrast between Egypt's continuous Coptic Christian survival and the Maghreb's near-total disappearance of organized Christianity is historically significant. It demonstrates that the survival of a religious minority community under changed political conditions depends on a complex combination of institutional strength, monastic and linguistic continuity, demographic concentration, and political circumstance — not simply on the passage of time or the strength of religious conviction alone.

Section 28.3

Ethiopian Christianity

Ethiopian Christianity represents one of the most remarkable cases of religious continuity anywhere in world history. As described in Chapter 10, the kingdom of Aksum became one of the earliest states in the world to adopt Christianity as an official religion, traditionally dated to the reign of King Ezana in the fourth century CE. According to church tradition, a young man named Frumentius, shipwrecked on the Red Sea coast and later brought to the Aksumite court, gained influence with the royal family and eventually traveled to Alexandria, where the Coptic patriarch Athanasius consecrated him as the first bishop of Aksum. This connection established a centuries-long relationship in which the head bishop of the Ethiopian church was traditionally appointed by, and answerable to, the Coptic patriarch in Alexandria — a relationship of religious dependency that persisted, with periodic tension, until Ethiopian church autocephaly was finally achieved in 1959.

Christianity in Ethiopia did not remain confined to the royal court. Over subsequent centuries, it became deeply woven into Ethiopian highland culture, language, and political life. The Ethiopian church developed its own liturgical language, Geʽez, which, though it eventually ceased to be spoken as an everyday language, remained the sacred language of scripture, liturgy, and religious literature, somewhat analogous to the role of Latin in medieval Western Europe. Ethiopian Christian literature produced a vast body of religious texts, hagiographies (lives of saints), royal chronicles that blended history and theology, and devotional literature, much of it preserved in monasteries that functioned as centers of learning, manuscript production, and historical memory for over a thousand years.

The Zagwe dynasty (approximately the eleventh through thirteenth centuries) produced one of the most extraordinary architectural achievements of medieval African Christianity: the rock-hewn churches of Lalibela, eleven churches carved directly out of volcanic rock under King Gebre Mesqel Lalibela. According to tradition, the king intended to create a new Jerusalem in the Ethiopian highlands after the original Jerusalem became difficult for Ethiopian pilgrims to reach following the Crusader-era conflicts in the Holy Land. The churches at Lalibela remain active pilgrimage sites and centers of worship today, and UNESCO recognizes them as a World Heritage Site of outstanding cultural significance.

The Solomonic dynasty, which succeeded the Zagwe beginning in the late thirteenth century, claimed direct descent from the biblical King Solomon and the Queen of Sheba, a genealogical claim that fused Ethiopian political legitimacy directly to Christian and biblical history (see Chapter 12 for fuller treatment of the Solomonic dynasty and medieval Ethiopian political history). Ethiopian monarchs were crowned with elaborate religious ceremony, ruled in close partnership with the church hierarchy, and used Christian identity as a central element of Ethiopian state ideology, particularly in conflicts with neighboring Muslim sultanates and, beginning in the sixteenth century, in response to Ottoman-backed military pressure from the eastern lowlands.

Ethiopian Christianity survived not only Islamic military pressure but also brief and ultimately rejected attempts at union with the Roman Catholic Church in the early seventeenth century, when Jesuit missionaries gained temporary influence at the Ethiopian court before a popular and clerical backlash led to their expulsion and a long period of renewed isolation from other Christian traditions. This episode illustrates an important pattern: Ethiopian Christian identity was, and remains, tightly bound to a specific theological tradition (Oriental Orthodoxy) and a specific sense of historical and cultural distinctiveness, making the Ethiopian church highly resistant to externally imposed religious change, even from other Christians.

By the time European missionaries began extensive activity in sub-Saharan Africa in the nineteenth century, Ethiopia already possessed an ancient, sophisticated, self-sustaining Christian civilization with over fifteen centuries of continuous institutional history — a fact that later made Ethiopia an important symbol for African and African diaspora Christians seeking to demonstrate that Christianity was not inherently a tool of European domination but could be, and historically had been, a vehicle of independent African religious and political life.

Section 28.4

Churches, Monasteries, and Learning

Across the long history of Christianity in Africa, churches and monasteries served not only as places of worship but as some of the continent's most important centers of education, manuscript production, art, architecture, and historical memory. Understanding this institutional dimension is essential to seeing African Christian history as a history of intellectual and cultural achievement, not merely belief and ritual.

Egyptian Christian monasticism, emerging in the third and fourth centuries through figures such as Anthony of the Desert and Pachomius, established patterns of communal and solitary ascetic life that spread from Egypt to the entire Christian world, shaping the development of monasticism in Byzantium, Western Europe, and beyond. Egyptian monasteries, particularly those in the Nile Valley and the Wadi Natrun, became major centers of manuscript copying, theological writing, and the preservation of texts — some of the same monasteries founded in late antiquity remain active today, making them among the oldest continuously functioning religious institutions in the world.

In Ethiopia, monasteries similarly functioned as schools, scriptoria, and centers of artistic production. Ethiopian monastic scribes copied and illuminated biblical texts, royal chronicles, hagiographies, and theological treatises in Geʽez over many centuries, developing a distinctive tradition of manuscript illumination and a body of religious music and chant (zema) associated with the church's liturgy. Monastic schools trained generations of clergy, scholars, and administrators, and monasteries often held significant landed wealth, granted by royal patrons, that supported their educational and charitable functions. The relationship between Ethiopian monarchs and major monastic centers, such as Debre Libanos, was a central feature of Ethiopian political and religious life for centuries, with monasteries sometimes serving as a check on royal authority and sometimes as instruments of royal legitimation.

In medieval Christian Nubia, churches similarly functioned as centers of learning and administration. Archaeological evidence from sites such as Faras and Old Dongola reveals extensive church complexes, episcopal residences, and wall paintings depicting Nubian bishops, kings, and saints, demonstrating a sophisticated Christian visual and institutional culture that flourished for roughly eight centuries before gradually giving way to Islamic influence beginning in the later medieval period.

During the era of nineteenth- and twentieth-century missionary activity in sub-Saharan Africa, mission stations frequently combined churches with schools, and in many regions mission schools became the primary — sometimes the only — institutions providing Western-style literacy and formal education to African populations under colonial rule. This had profound long-term consequences, discussed further in Section 28.6 and in Chapter 51, since access to mission education shaped opportunities for employment, social mobility, and eventually political leadership across much of colonial and postcolonial Africa. Many of the first generation of African nationalist leaders, discussed in Chapter 53, received their early education in mission schools, even as many of those same leaders later criticized the broader colonial and missionary system within which those schools operated.

Section 28.5

Christianity and State Power

Throughout African history, Christianity has been closely intertwined with political authority, sometimes legitimating rulers and states, sometimes serving as a tool of foreign domination, and sometimes providing a basis for resistance to political power. This section examines several distinct patterns of this relationship.

In Aksum and medieval Ethiopia, Christianity became deeply fused with royal ideology. Ethiopian kings were anointed in elaborate Christian ceremonies, claimed descent from biblical figures, and used the church's moral and institutional authority to reinforce their legitimacy, while in turn granting land, privileges, and protection to the church and its monasteries. This mutual reinforcement of crown and church persisted for over a millennium and helped Ethiopia maintain political independence and cultural cohesion even as neighboring regions experienced Islamic expansion or, much later, European colonial conquest.

In medieval Nubia, Christian kingship similarly combined religious and political authority, with Nubian kings depicted in church art alongside images of Christ and the saints, and bishops playing important roles in royal administration. The eventual decline of Christian Nubia, beginning around the thirteenth and accelerating through the fifteenth century, resulted from a combination of internal political fragmentation, demographic and economic pressures, and growing Islamic influence and migration into the region — a gradual transformation rather than a single conquest.

In the kingdom of Kongo, beginning in 1483 with Portuguese contact and culminating in the 1491 baptism of King Nzinga a Nkuwu, Christianity became a matter of explicit royal policy adopted from the top down, in some ways resembling the Aksumite pattern centuries earlier. Kongo rulers, particularly Nzinga a Nkuwu's son and successor Afonso I, embraced Catholic Christianity, built churches, sent young Kongo nobles to Portugal and Rome for religious education, and corresponded directly with the Portuguese crown and the Pope as Christian sovereigns negotiating among equals — at least in their own self-understanding (see Chapter 36 for the fuller political history of Kongo). Kongo Christianity, however, also became entangled with the expanding Portuguese Atlantic slave trade, creating profound contradictions: the same Portuguese relationship that brought Catholic missionaries, literacy, and diplomatic recognition to the Kongo court also brought escalating demand for enslaved captives that destabilized the kingdom and ultimately contributed to its long-term decline (see Chapters 36 and 41).

From the nineteenth century onward, the relationship between Christianity and political power in Africa became dominated by the connection between missionary activity and European colonial expansion, examined in detail in Section 28.6. European colonial administrations in many regions actively supported, funded, or relied upon Christian missions to provide education, healthcare, and social services that colonial states themselves often declined to fund adequately, while missionaries in turn sometimes depended on colonial military and administrative protection to operate, and sometimes provided colonial administrations with information, translation, and legitimating moral language. At the same time, as discussed in Section 28.7, African converts frequently used Christian institutions, networks, and ideas — including biblical concepts of equality, liberation, and divine judgment — to criticize colonial abuses and to build political organizations that eventually contributed to independence movements.

Section 28.6

Missionary Movements

The nineteenth and early twentieth centuries saw an unprecedented expansion of Christian missionary activity across sub-Saharan Africa, conducted primarily by European and, increasingly, American missionary societies representing a wide range of Catholic and Protestant traditions. This expansion coincided closely — though not identically — with the period of European exploration, commercial penetration, and eventual colonial conquest described in Chapters 46 and 47, and the relationship between missions and colonialism is one of the most carefully studied and debated topics in African religious history.

Missionary motivations in this period were varied and often combined religious conviction with broader European cultural assumptions. Many missionaries were driven by genuine religious conviction and a sense of evangelistic obligation, sometimes combined with humanitarian concern, particularly regarding the Atlantic and Indian Ocean slave trades that European and American abolitionist Christians had campaigned against earlier in the century (see Chapter 45). David Livingstone, the Scottish missionary and explorer active in southern and central Africa from the 1840s through the 1870s, explicitly framed his mission as combining "Christianity, commerce, and civilization," believing that legitimate trade and Christian conversion together would help end the slave trade and improve African societies — a framework that, however well-intentioned, also reflected European assumptions about African societies needing to be "civilized" according to European standards.

Missionary societies established mission stations across the continent, often combining churches, schools, clinics, and sometimes agricultural training centers. These stations became important nodes of cultural contact, education, and — for many African converts — opportunity, providing access to literacy, certain skilled trades, and sometimes positions within colonial administrations or commercial enterprises. At the same time, missions frequently demanded the abandonment of indigenous religious practices, family structures (particularly polygamous marriage), and cultural customs that missionaries viewed as incompatible with Christian morality, generating significant social disruption and, in many communities, deep and lasting tension between Christian converts and those who maintained indigenous religious traditions.

The relationship between missions and colonial states varied considerably by colony and by missionary society. In some colonies, particularly under Portuguese and Belgian rule, missions operated in close partnership with colonial administrations, sometimes receiving direct government subsidies in exchange for providing education and social services that lightened the financial burden on colonial budgets. In other contexts, missionaries criticized colonial abuses, sometimes vocally: certain Protestant missionaries documented and publicized colonial violence and forced labor abuses in the Congo Free State in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, contributing to international pressure that eventually forced reforms (see Chapter 49 on colonial economies and labor). Missionary attitudes toward African culture, political rights, and colonial governance therefore cannot be reduced to a single pattern; they ranged from active complicity in colonial domination to genuine — if often paternalistic — advocacy on behalf of African communities.

Comity agreements among missionary societies, dividing African territory into geographic spheres assigned to particular denominations to avoid competition, also shaped the religious map of colonial Africa, helping to explain why particular regions and ethnic groups in many African countries today are strongly associated with specific Christian denominations inherited from this missionary partition.

Section 28.7

African Christian Adaptation

One of the most important and historically significant patterns in African Christian history is African Christians' active reinterpretation, adaptation, and Africanization of Christianity, rather than passive acceptance of missionary teaching unchanged. This pattern appears across every period covered in this chapter, from ancient Ethiopia to contemporary Pentecostalism, and it represents one of the central themes of African religious history more broadly.

From the earliest centuries, African Christian communities developed distinctive theological emphases, liturgical practices, and institutional forms that diverged from, and sometimes directly challenged, Mediterranean and later European Christian norms. Ethiopian Christianity, for example, developed practices — including the observance of certain Hebrew Bible dietary laws, distinctive fasting practices, and a strong veneration of the Ark of the Covenant, which Ethiopian tradition holds is housed in the city of Aksum — that distinguish it from other Christian traditions and that reflect a particular synthesis of biblical, Jewish, and indigenous Ethiopian cultural elements developed over many centuries.

In the era of nineteenth- and twentieth-century missionary Christianity, African converts frequently combined Christian teaching with existing beliefs about ancestors, spiritual healing, prophecy, and the relationship between physical and spiritual well-being, producing distinctively African forms of Christian practice even within mission-founded denominations. This process is sometimes described by historians using terms such as inculturation (the deliberate adaptation of Christian teaching and practice to local cultural forms) or syncretism (the blending of different religious traditions), though scholars increasingly caution that the term syncretism can carry an implication that African Christianity is somehow less authentic or more "mixed" than other forms of Christianity — when in fact all Christian traditions, including European ones, have historically absorbed and adapted local cultural elements.

The most dramatic institutional expression of African Christian adaptation was the rise of African Independent Churches (also called African Initiated Churches), beginning in the late nineteenth century and accelerating dramatically through the twentieth. These churches were founded and led by Africans, often after breaking away from foreign mission control over disputes regarding leadership, polygamy, indigenous custom, healing practice, or simple frustration with the slow pace at which mission churches promoted African clergy to positions of real authority. The broader "Ethiopianist" movement, named for the symbolic importance of Ethiopia as an independent African Christian state, encouraged African Christians across the continent and the diaspora to seek African leadership of African churches, drawing explicitly on the biblical phrase "Ethiopia shall stretch forth her hands unto God" (Psalm 68:31) as a scriptural basis for African Christian self-determination.

Prophetic and Zionist movements, particularly prominent in Central and Southern Africa, combined Christian belief with practices of spiritual healing, prophecy, and direct revelation, often led by charismatic individual prophets who claimed divine calling independent of any mission hierarchy. Simon Kimbangu's ministry in the Belgian Congo beginning in 1921, though suppressed by colonial authorities who imprisoned him until his death in 1951, gave rise to the Kimbanguist Church, which survived underground and later became one of the largest African-founded Christian denominations, eventually gaining recognition from the World Council of Churches. Similar patterns produced the Zion Christian Church and numerous other Zionist churches across Southern Africa, and the Aladura ("praying people") churches of Yoruba-speaking West Africa.

This pattern of African-led religious adaptation and independent institution-building did not end with decolonization; it accelerated through the later twentieth and into the twenty-first century with the explosive growth of African Pentecostal and charismatic Christianity, discussed further in Section 28.9.

Section 28.8

Religion and Cultural Change

The spread of Christianity across Africa produced significant changes in family structure, gender roles, education, and social organization, with consequences that varied considerably by region, denomination, and historical period, and that affected men, women, elders, and youth in different ways.

Mission Christianity in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries frequently challenged existing family structures, particularly polygamous marriage, which many missionaries viewed as incompatible with Christian monogamy and sought actively to discourage or prohibit among converts. This created significant tension in many societies where polygamous marriage carried important economic, social, and political functions, and it placed particular burdens on women in polygamous households who faced pressure to leave marriages, sometimes losing economic security and social status as a result. At the same time, mission Christianity in some contexts provided women with new opportunities for literacy, education, and limited forms of leadership within church institutions — opportunities that, while still constrained by both missionary and indigenous patriarchal norms, were nonetheless historically significant, particularly through girls' mission schools and women's church associations and prayer groups, which became important spaces of female community organization and, later, of social and political mobilization (see Chapter 18 for broader treatment of women and gender in African history).

Mission education more broadly reshaped opportunities for social mobility across colonial Africa. Access to literacy and formal schooling, concentrated heavily in mission institutions during much of the colonial period, became closely tied to access to clerical employment, certain skilled trades, and eventually to positions within colonial bureaucracies and, after independence, within postcolonial governments and professions. This created new social hierarchies based partly on mission education and religious affiliation, sometimes reinforcing or creating new ethnic and regional inequalities depending on which areas and groups had earlier or more extensive access to mission schools.

Christian conversion also reshaped funeral practices, naming customs, attitudes toward ancestor veneration, healing practices, and community ritual life, sometimes producing significant generational tension between Christian converts and relatives who maintained indigenous religious practice, and sometimes producing creative blending in which Christian and indigenous elements coexisted within the same family or even the same individual's religious life — a pattern that remains common across much of contemporary Africa, where many Christians (and Muslims) continue to draw on indigenous beliefs about ancestors, spiritual causation of illness, and ritual protection alongside formal religious practice.

Everyday Life and Social History

Faith, School, and Family in a Mission Community

For many African families living near a mission station in the late nineteenth or early twentieth century, conversion to Christianity was rarely a single dramatic event but rather a gradual process embedded in everyday life. A family might first encounter the mission through its school, sending a son (and, somewhat later and less commonly, a daughter) to learn to read and write, skills that increasingly mattered for employment as a clerk, catechist, or trader. Attendance at mission worship services often followed school enrollment rather than preceding it. Baptism might come years later, sometimes prompted by marriage, illness, or a desire for the social respectability and trading connections associated with the mission community.

Daily life at a mission station combined religious instruction with practical training: girls might learn sewing, hygiene, and domestic skills alongside catechism; boys might learn carpentry, masonry, or agricultural techniques alongside literacy. Sunday worship structured the week, but missionaries often also intervened in matters of marriage, requiring monogamous Christian marriage for full church membership, a requirement that could divide households and provoke difficult choices, particularly for men with existing polygamous marriages and for women whose social and economic security depended on the household structures missionaries sought to dismantle.

Many families navigated these pressures by adopting Christian practice selectively: attending church, sending children to mission schools, and adopting Christian names, while maintaining indigenous practices regarding ancestor respect, healing, and certain rites of passage, sometimes openly and sometimes privately. This pattern of selective, negotiated adoption — rather than wholesale replacement of one religious system by another — was far more common, and far more historically significant, than the simpler narrative of straightforward "conversion" often presented in missionary records themselves.

Section 28.9

Christian Diversity Across the Continent

By the twenty-first century, Christianity in Africa had become extraordinarily diverse, encompassing ancient Oriental Orthodox traditions, mainline Catholic and Protestant denominations descended from colonial-era missions, a vast array of African Independent Churches, and a particularly dynamic and rapidly growing world of Pentecostal and charismatic Christianity.

The ancient Oriental Orthodox traditions — the Coptic Orthodox Church of Egypt and the Ethiopian and Eritrean Orthodox Tewahedo churches — remain major institutions today, with tens of millions of adherents, an active monastic tradition, and a significant global diaspora presence, particularly in Europe and North America, reflecting twentieth- and twenty-first-century migration.

Catholic and mainline Protestant churches established through nineteenth- and twentieth-century missionary activity remain numerically significant across much of sub-Saharan Africa, particularly in countries such as the Democratic Republic of the Congo, Nigeria, Kenya, Uganda, and much of southern Africa. These churches have undergone substantial Africanization since the mid-twentieth century, with African clergy, bishops, and even cardinals assuming leadership roles that were largely closed to them under colonial-era mission structures, and with growing theological reflection on how to express Catholic and Protestant Christian teaching in distinctly African cultural and liturgical forms.

African Independent Churches, discussed in Section 28.7, continue to represent a major and growing segment of African Christianity, ranging from large, well-organized denominations such as the Kimbanguist Church and various Zionist and Aladura churches to countless smaller, locally rooted congregations led by individual prophets, healers, or founding families.

The most dramatic recent development in African Christianity has been the explosive growth of Pentecostal and charismatic Christianity from the later twentieth century onward, a movement emphasizing direct personal experience of the Holy Spirit, spiritual gifts including healing and prophecy, dramatic conversion experiences, and often a "prosperity gospel" theology linking faith, financial giving, and material blessing. Pentecostal and charismatic churches — ranging from small storefront congregations to enormous megachurches with memberships in the tens of thousands and substantial media operations — have grown especially rapidly in Nigeria, Ghana, Kenya, Zimbabwe, and much of urban Africa more broadly, reshaping religious life, popular culture, and in some cases political influence. African Pentecostal movements have also become significant missionary senders in their own right, establishing congregations among African diaspora communities in Europe, North America, and elsewhere, reversing the historical direction of missionary flow that characterized the nineteenth and twentieth centuries.

This diversity means that any single description of "African Christianity" risks oversimplification. A Coptic monk in an Egyptian desert monastery, an Ethiopian Orthodox pilgrim at Lalibela, a Catholic parishioner in rural Tanzania, a member of the Kimbanguist Church in the Democratic Republic of the Congo, and a congregant at a Lagos megachurch all participate in distinct, historically rooted Christian traditions that share certain core beliefs while differing dramatically in liturgy, theology, organization, and historical origin. Understanding this diversity, rather than collapsing it into a single narrative, is essential to an accurate historical account.

Historical Vocabulary
Inculturation and Religious Adaptation

Inculturation refers to the deliberate process by which a religious tradition is expressed through the cultural forms, language, music, art, and ritual practices of a particular society, rather than requiring converts to adopt the cultural forms of the society from which the religion originally spread. Catholic theology in particular developed an explicit doctrine of inculturation in the twentieth century, encouraging African Catholic communities to develop distinctively African liturgical music, art, and ritual elements within an orthodox theological framework.

The related but more contested term syncretism describes the blending of elements from different religious traditions into a new combined practice or belief system. Historians use this term carefully, since it has sometimes been applied selectively to non-European Christianity in ways that imply impurity or inauthenticity, while overlooking the ways European and other Christian traditions have also absorbed local pre-Christian customs (such as the timing and symbolism of certain European Christmas and Easter traditions) without those traditions being labeled "syncretic." A careful historian recognizes that religious adaptation and blending have occurred in every Christian tradition throughout history, including in Africa.

Historical Vocabulary
Mission Station

A mission station was a settlement established by a Christian missionary society, typically combining a church, a school, and often a clinic, agricultural demonstration plot, or craft workshop. Mission stations became important sites of cultural contact during the colonial era, providing access to literacy and certain skills while also serving as centers from which missionaries sought to transform indigenous religious practice, family structure, and customary law according to Christian and often specifically European cultural norms. Mission stations varied enormously in their relationship to colonial administrations, ranging from close partnership and government subsidy to genuine tension and occasional missionary criticism of colonial abuses.

Primary Source Window

Correspondence Between the Kongo Court and the Portuguese Crown (Early Sixteenth Century)

Creator: King Afonso I of Kongo and royal scribes · Date: Early sixteenth century · Type: Diplomatic correspondence between Christian sovereigns

Context: Following his father's baptism in 1491, King Afonso I of Kongo ruled as a committed Catholic monarch who corresponded directly with the kings of Portugal and with the Pope, sent Kongo nobles to be educated in Portugal, and worked to establish churches and Christian education throughout his kingdom. His letters survive in Portuguese archives and constitute some of the earliest sub-Saharan African royal documents addressing Christianity, diplomacy, and the growing problem of the Portuguese slave trade.

In several letters, Afonso described his efforts to build churches and promote Christian education in his kingdom, while also raising concerns about Portuguese traders who were obtaining captives through methods Afonso considered illegitimate, including raiding and kidnapping rather than the more limited forms of exchange he had sanctioned, and asked the Portuguese crown to help regulate and restrain such practices.

Analysis: This correspondence reveals a Kongo monarch who understood himself as a Christian sovereign engaging diplomatically with Portugal as something close to an equal Christian power, rather than as a subordinate. It also reveals the deep contradiction at the heart of early Kongo Christianity: the same Portuguese relationship that brought priests, churches, and literacy to the Kongo court also brought an expanding demand for enslaved captives that Afonso himself recognized as destabilizing and morally troubling, even as the trade continued to grow regardless of his objections. Historians must read this source carefully, recognizing both Afonso's genuine religious conviction and political agency, and the limits of his ability to control a trade increasingly driven by Portuguese commercial interests and by African intermediaries and rival polities also seeking access to Portuguese goods and weapons.

Historical Debate

Were Nineteenth-Century Christian Missions Primarily Agents of Colonialism, or Were They Independent — and Sometimes Critical — Actors?

The question: Historians have long debated the relationship between Christian missionary activity and European colonial conquest in nineteenth- and twentieth-century Africa. Were missionaries best understood as instruments of colonial domination, or as a more independent — and sometimes critical — force whose relationship to colonialism varied by time, place, and individual conviction?

Interpretation A — Missions as Agents of Colonial Domination: Some historians emphasize that missionary activity expanded in close chronological and geographic association with European commercial and military penetration, that missionaries often depended on colonial military protection, that mission education served colonial labor and administrative needs, and that the suppression of indigenous religion, language, and custom by missionaries served to undermine African cultural resistance to colonial rule, regardless of individual missionaries' personal intentions.
Interpretation B — Missions as Independent and Sometimes Critical Actors: Other historians emphasize that many missionaries operated independently of, and sometimes in direct conflict with, colonial commercial and administrative interests; that some missionaries publicly documented and opposed colonial abuses, most famously regarding forced labor in the Congo Free State; that mission education, whatever its limitations, provided literacy and skills that African communities and later nationalist leaders used to challenge colonial rule; and that African converts actively used Christian institutions, networks, and theological ideas for their own purposes, including eventual political resistance, rather than simply absorbing colonial ideology passively.

Evidence: Evidence for Interpretation A includes mission society reliance on colonial protection, government subsidies to missions, and missionary suppression of indigenous custom. Evidence for Interpretation B includes specific missionary publications criticizing colonial abuses, the role of mission-educated Africans in founding nationalist and independence movements, and the agency African Christians exercised in founding independent churches that broke explicitly with both foreign mission control and, eventually, colonial political authority.

Why it matters: This debate shapes how historians understand the broader relationship between religion and power in colonial Africa, and whether Christianity should be understood primarily as an imported tool of domination or as a religious tradition that African communities actively appropriated, debated, and ultimately used for their own purposes — including resistance to the very colonial order with which missions were often associated.

Student task: Based on the evidence described above, which interpretation do you find more persuasive, or do you think a more accurate account would combine elements of both? What additional evidence would help you decide?

Multiple Perspectives

The Mission Encounter in Colonial Africa

From the perspective of a European missionary: Many missionaries understood their work as a religious calling combining the saving of souls with what they considered the moral and material uplift of African societies, often genuinely believing they were opposing the slave trade, improving health and literacy, and offering African converts access to salvation and a wider world — while frequently failing to recognize the sophistication of the indigenous religious, legal, and social systems they sought to replace.
From the perspective of an African convert seeking education and opportunity: Conversion and mission education could represent genuine religious conviction combined with practical opportunity — access to literacy, skilled employment, and social mobility within a colonial economy that otherwise offered Africans few such avenues, even as conversion sometimes required difficult breaks with family expectations, marriage customs, and indigenous religious practice.
From the perspective of an elder or traditional religious leader within the same community: Mission Christianity could appear as a direct threat to community cohesion, ancestral religious obligation, and customary authority, particularly where missionaries actively discouraged polygamous marriage, ancestor veneration, and indigenous ritual practice — generating real generational and social conflict within families and communities.
From the perspective of a colonial administrator: Missions were often viewed instrumentally, as useful and cost-effective providers of education, healthcare, and social order among colonized populations, valuable for producing literate clerks and a stable, governable population, while missionary criticism of colonial abuses could be seen as an unwelcome complication to administrative authority.
Cause and Effect

Why Ethiopian Christianity Survived Nearly Two Thousand Years

Long-Term Causes

Early and deep royal-level adoption of Christianity in the fourth century CE; integration of Christian identity with Ethiopian highland political and cultural life over many centuries; geographic position in highland terrain that limited the reach of some external military pressures; a strong indigenous monastic, literary, and liturgical tradition (Geʽez literature, distinctive liturgy and calendar).

Short-Term/Episodic Pressures

Military pressure from neighboring Muslim sultanates in the medieval and early modern periods; the sixteenth-century Ottoman-backed invasion under Ahmad ibn Ibrahim; the brief and ultimately rejected seventeenth-century attempt at union with Roman Catholicism under Jesuit influence; later European colonial pressure, which Ethiopia largely avoided through military victory at the Battle of Adwa in 1896 (see Chapter 47).

Outcome

Ethiopian Christianity survived each of these pressures, sometimes through military resistance, sometimes through internal religious and political consolidation, sometimes through rejection of externally imposed religious change, maintaining continuous institutional existence from the Aksumite period to the present.

Long-Term Effects

Ethiopia became, alongside Liberia, one of only two African states never subjected to sustained European colonial rule; Ethiopian Christianity became a powerful symbol for African and African diaspora Christians seeking models of independent African religious and political life, fueling the broader Ethiopianist movement discussed in Section 28.7.

Historical complexity: Ethiopian Christianity's survival cannot be explained by any single factor. It resulted from a combination of early and deep institutional roots, geographic and military circumstance, and the choices of specific historical actors who might, at several points, have allowed different outcomes.

Continuity and Change Over Time

From Mission Control to African Independent Churches (c. 1880s–Present)

What Changed

  • African Christians moved from predominantly foreign mission leadership toward African-led denominations and independent churches
  • New theological emphases emerged, including prophecy, healing, and direct revelation, often led by African founders without formal mission training
  • Catholic and Protestant mission churches gradually promoted African clergy and bishops to leadership positions, particularly accelerating around the era of political independence
  • Pentecostal and charismatic Christianity emerged as a major new force, largely independent of older mission denominational structures

What Stayed the Same

  • Christianity in Africa continued to combine formal doctrine with beliefs about ancestors, spiritual healing, and protection from misfortune across nearly all traditions
  • Religious institutions continued to provide education, social welfare, and community organization functions, regardless of denominational origin
  • Tension between formal religious authority and lay religious experience, and between institutional church structures and prophetic or charismatic individual leadership, persisted across the entire period

Why the pattern matters: The shift from foreign mission control toward African-led Christianity paralleled, and in some cases directly fed into, the broader political shift from colonial rule to independence discussed in Chapters 53 and 54, illustrating how religious and political history in Africa are deeply intertwined rather than separate stories.

Section 28.10

Chapter Review and Christianity in Africa Checkpoint

This chapter has traced Christianity's African history across nearly two thousand years, from its earliest documented arrival in first-century Egypt through the explosive growth of contemporary African Pentecostalism. Before moving to subsequent chapters, students should be able to demonstrate understanding of the major patterns, periods, and debates this chapter has introduced.

The core insights of this chapter are: Christianity is one of Africa's oldest religious traditions, not a recent European import; North African and Egyptian Christians shaped foundational Christian theology; Ethiopian Christianity achieved nearly two thousand years of continuous institutional history; medieval Christian Nubia and the Kongo kingdom demonstrate additional independent African Christian state traditions; nineteenth- and twentieth-century missionary Christianity arrived in close, complicated relationship with European colonialism; African Christians consistently adapted, reinterpreted, and Africanized Christian belief and practice rather than passively receiving it; and contemporary African Christianity is extraordinarily diverse, ranging from ancient Orthodox traditions to rapidly growing Pentecostal and charismatic movements.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has examined the long and varied history of Christianity in Africa, beginning with its earliest documented presence in first-century Egypt and extending to the dynamic and rapidly growing Christian movements of contemporary Africa. The central argument of this chapter is that Christianity is, and has long been, an African religious tradition in its own right, not simply a foreign import introduced through European colonialism, even though European missionary activity and colonial conquest profoundly shaped Christianity's spread across large parts of the continent from the nineteenth century onward.

The chapter began with ancient North African and Egyptian Christianity, showing how Alexandria became one of the most important centers of early Christian theological education anywhere in the ancient world, and how North African theologians including Tertullian, Cyprian, and Augustine of Hippo shaped the foundations of Christian thought for the entire subsequent history of the religion. It then traced the differing fates of Egyptian Coptic Christianity, which survived as a continuous minority tradition after the Arab-Islamic conquest, and Christianity in the rest of Roman North Africa, which largely disappeared as an organized tradition within a few centuries of Islamic expansion — a contrast that illustrates how religious survival depends on institutional strength, linguistic continuity, and demographic concentration rather than the simple passage of time.

The chapter then turned to Ethiopian Christianity, tracing its origins in the fourth-century royal conversion of Aksum, its development of a distinctive liturgical, literary, and artistic tradition over many centuries, its extraordinary architectural achievements such as the rock-hewn churches of Lalibela, and the combination of military resistance, internal consolidation, and geographic circumstance that allowed it to survive Islamic military pressure, a brief Jesuit attempt at religious union, and the era of European colonial expansion, emerging as one of the longest continuously functioning Christian traditions anywhere in the world.

The chapter examined churches and monasteries as centers not only of worship but of learning, manuscript production, and historical memory, from Egyptian desert monasticism — the historical origin point of Christian monastic tradition worldwide — to Ethiopian monastic scriptoria and medieval Nubian church complexes, and later to the educational role of mission stations in colonial-era sub-Saharan Africa.

The chapter analyzed the close historical relationship between Christianity and political power across multiple African contexts: the fusion of crown and church in Aksumite and medieval Ethiopia; Christian kingship in medieval Nubia; the explicit royal adoption of Catholicism in the kingdom of Kongo beginning in 1491, and the tragic entanglement of that conversion with the expanding Portuguese slave trade; and the complex, varied relationship between nineteenth- and twentieth-century Christian missions and European colonial conquest, in which missions sometimes facilitated colonial domination and sometimes criticized its worst abuses.

The chapter gave particular attention to African Christian adaptation: the ways African Christians, across every period from antiquity to the present, reinterpreted, translated, and Africanized Christian belief and practice rather than receiving it passively. This pattern produced distinctive Ethiopian religious practices in antiquity, widespread blending of Christian and indigenous belief during the missionary era, and, most dramatically, the rise of African Independent Churches and Ethiopianist movements from the late nineteenth century onward, as African Christians broke away from foreign mission control to found their own churches under African leadership.

The chapter also examined the social consequences of Christian conversion for family structure, gender roles, and education, showing how mission Christianity both disrupted existing family and marriage structures, particularly through opposition to polygamy, and created new — if constrained — opportunities for women's education and community organization, while mission schools became central institutions of social mobility and, eventually, nationalist political leadership across much of colonial Africa.

Finally, the chapter surveyed the extraordinary diversity of contemporary African Christianity, encompassing ancient Oriental Orthodox traditions, Africanized Catholic and Protestant churches descended from colonial-era missions, a vast array of African Independent Churches, and the rapidly growing world of Pentecostal and charismatic Christianity that has made Africa one of the most dynamic centers of global Christianity in the twenty-first century.

Throughout, the chapter has asked students to hold multiple truths together: that Christianity in Africa includes both ancient, independent African religious traditions and histories of religious change closely tied to European colonial domination; that missionary activity produced both cultural disruption and genuine opportunity; and that African Christians, across every period, exercised real religious agency, even within structures of unequal power. Understanding this complexity, rather than reducing it to a single simple story, is the central goal of this chapter.

Key Terms

Coptic Christianity
The Christian tradition of Egypt, with roots in antiquity, its own patriarch, liturgical language, and distinctive theological identity within Oriental Orthodoxy.
Oriental Orthodoxy
The family of ancient Christian churches, including the Coptic, Ethiopian, and Eritrean churches, that rejected the Christological formula adopted at the Council of Chalcedon in 451 CE.

The term used by the Ethiopian and Eritrean Orthodox churches to describe their distinctive theological understanding of the unity of Christ's divine and human nature.

The status of a church governing itself independently under its own head bishop, achieved formally by the Ethiopian church in 1959 after centuries of dependence on the Coptic patriarchate.
Monasticism
A tradition of religious life, originating in Egypt in the third and fourth centuries, involving withdrawal from ordinary society for prayer, asceticism, and often communal religious discipline.
Scriptorium
A monastic workshop for copying and illuminating manuscripts, central to the preservation and transmission of Christian literature across the medieval period.
Geʽez
The ancient liturgical and literary language of the Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church, used for scripture, liturgy, and religious literature.
Mission station
A settlement established by a missionary society, typically combining a church, school, and sometimes a clinic or workshop, important in the colonial-era spread of Christianity.
Comity agreement
An arrangement among missionary societies dividing African territory into geographic spheres of denominational activity to avoid direct competition.
Ethiopianism
A late nineteenth- and early twentieth-century movement emphasizing African leadership of African churches, inspired by Ethiopia's ancient independent Christian tradition.
African Independent (Initiated) Church
A church founded and led by Africans, often breaking from foreign mission control, frequently blending Christian belief with indigenous spiritual practice, prophecy, or healing tradition.
Zionist churches
A family of African Independent Churches, especially prominent in Southern Africa, emphasizing healing, prophecy, and direct spiritual experience.
Aladura churches
A family of African Independent Churches among Yoruba-speaking peoples of West Africa, whose name means "praying people," emphasizing prayer, healing, and prophecy.
Kimbanguism
A Congolese Christian movement founded by the prophet Simon Kimbangu in 1921, suppressed by Belgian colonial authorities but surviving to become a major African-founded denomination.
Inculturation
The deliberate adaptation of religious teaching and practice to local cultural forms, language, music, and ritual.
Syncretism
The blending of elements from different religious traditions into a combined practice or belief system; a term historians use carefully to avoid implying that non-European religious blending is uniquely inauthentic.
Pentecostalism
A Christian movement emphasizing direct personal experience of the Holy Spirit, spiritual gifts such as healing and prophecy, and dramatic conversion, which has grown explosively across contemporary Africa.
Charismatic Christianity
A broader movement within and across denominations emphasizing spiritual gifts and direct religious experience, closely related to but distinct from formal Pentecostal denominations.
Prosperity gospel
A theological emphasis, common in much contemporary African Pentecostalism, linking religious faith, financial giving, and material blessing or success.
Donatism
A fourth- and fifth-century North African Christian controversy over how to treat clergy who had compromised with Roman authorities during persecution, addressed extensively by Augustine of Hippo.
Jizya
A tax historically imposed on non-Muslim subjects, including Coptic Christians, under various Islamic states governing Egypt.
Council of Chalcedon
A 451 CE Christian church council whose Christological formula was rejected by the Egyptian, Ethiopian, and several other ancient churches, producing the lasting Oriental Orthodox tradition.
Patristics
The scholarly study of the early Christian "church fathers," many of whom, including Origen, Tertullian, Cyprian, and Augustine, were African.
Liberation theology
A theological movement, with African as well as Latin American expressions, emphasizing Christian commitment to social justice and the liberation of the oppressed.
Ecumenism
Efforts to promote cooperation and unity among different Christian denominations and traditions, relevant to relationships among Africa's many Christian churches.

Major People and Places

Identification & Significance

Origen of Alexandria
Influential third-century theologian and biblical scholar associated with the Alexandrian catechetical school, whose interpretive methods shaped centuries of Christian theology.
Tertullian of Carthage
Late second- and early third-century North African writer, often considered the first major Latin Christian theologian.
Cyprian of Carthage
Mid-third-century bishop and martyr whose writings shaped debates over church unity and authority.
Augustine of Hippo
North African bishop and theologian (354–430 CE) whose writings, including the Confessions and City of God, remain among the most influential texts in the history of Christian thought.
King Ezana of Aksum
Fourth-century Aksumite ruler whose conversion made Aksum one of the earliest Christian states in world history.
Lalibela
Ethiopian town famous for eleven rock-hewn churches built under King Gebre Mesqel Lalibela in the twelfth and thirteenth centuries, a major center of Ethiopian Orthodox pilgrimage.
Christian Nubia
Medieval Christian kingdoms (Nobatia, Makuria, Alodia) along the Nile south of Egypt, converted through Byzantine missions in the sixth century and Christian for roughly eight centuries.
King Nzinga a Nkuwu and King Afonso I of Kongo
Father and son Kongo rulers whose adoption of Catholic Christianity beginning in 1491 made Kongo a major early sub-Saharan center of African Christianity, entangled from the outset with the growing Portuguese slave trade.
David Livingstone
Scottish missionary and explorer active in southern and central Africa from the 1840s through the 1870s, associated with the "Christianity, commerce, and civilization" missionary framework.
Simon Kimbangu
Congolese prophet whose 1921 ministry, suppressed by Belgian colonial authorities, gave rise to the Kimbanguist Church, one of the largest African-founded Christian denominations.
Dona Beatriz Kimpa Vita
Early eighteenth-century Kongo prophetess who founded the Antonian movement blending Catholic and Kongo religious tradition; executed in 1706.
Ethiopianism
A movement among African and diaspora Christians promoting African leadership of African churches, drawing on Ethiopia's ancient independent Christian tradition.
African Independent Churches
A broad category of churches founded and led by Africans, often blending Christian belief with indigenous religious practice, prophecy, and healing tradition.
The Battle of Adwa (1896)
Ethiopian military victory over Italian invasion forces, helping Ethiopia remain one of the only African states to avoid sustained European colonial rule, reinforcing its symbolic importance for Ethiopianist Christianity.
Alexandria
Egyptian city central to the early history of Christian theology and the traditional seat of the Coptic patriarchate.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter has told a story that spans nearly two thousand years and nearly the entire African continent, from the desert monasteries of ancient Egypt to the megachurches of twenty-first-century Lagos and Nairobi. That span itself is the chapter's central lesson: Christianity in Africa is not a single event or a single era but a continuous, evolving, deeply African historical process that long predates, ran alongside, and ultimately outlasted European colonial rule.

Understanding this history matters for several reasons developed throughout the chapter. It corrects a historically inaccurate but persistent assumption that Christianity is fundamentally a European religion exported to Africa in the modern era — an assumption that obscures over a thousand years of African Christian theology, institution-building, and cultural achievement before the first European missionary ever set foot south of the Sahara. It also requires honest engagement with real harm: the entanglement of Christian mission with colonial conquest, the disruption of African family and religious life, and the genuine power imbalances that shaped the missionary encounter in many regions during the nineteenth and twentieth centuries.

At the same time, this chapter has emphasized African religious agency at every turn: African theologians shaping the foundations of Christian thought in antiquity; African monarchs adopting and adapting Christianity for their own political and spiritual purposes; African converts blending Christian and indigenous belief into new and lasting religious forms; and, especially from the late nineteenth century onward, African Christians breaking decisively from foreign mission control to found their own churches, their own theological traditions, and ultimately their own path toward a Christianity that is unmistakably, confidently African.

This chapter also connects directly to the larger themes of this textbook. It connects to the history of Indigenous African religions and of Islam in Africa, since religious change in Africa has always involved encounter, negotiation, and coexistence among multiple traditions rather than the simple replacement of one belief system by another. It connects to the history of African kingdoms and empires, since Christianity shaped political legitimacy from Aksum to Kongo. And it connects directly to the chapters on colonialism, resistance, and independence that follow, since the same mission schools, networks, and theological ideas that sometimes served colonial interests also equipped a generation of African Christians with the tools, organizations, and language to imagine and ultimately build independent African nations.

Africa's Christian history, like so much of the history covered in this textbook, resists simple narratives of pure achievement or pure victimization. It is a story of profound continuity across two millennia, of creative adaptation in the face of changing political and cultural circumstance, and of African peoples — kings and commoners, ancient theologians and modern prophets — making a global religious tradition unmistakably their own.

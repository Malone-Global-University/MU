Part II — Ancient African Civilizations
9

Carthage and North Africa

Africa: A History of a Continent

Chapter Opening

"Carthage must be destroyed" — a phrase attributed to the Roman senator Cato the Elder, repeated until Rome finally leveled the city in 146 BCE. That a Roman statesman felt the need to repeat the demand for decades is itself a measure of how powerful, how wealthy, and how deeply feared the African city of Carthage had become.

On the northern coast of Africa, on a peninsula jutting into the Mediterranean Sea near the modern city of Tunis, there once stood one of the wealthiest and most powerful cities in the ancient world. Founded, according to its own traditions, by settlers from the Phoenician city of Tyre, Carthage grew over several centuries from a small coastal trading post into the center of a vast commercial and political network that stretched across the western Mediterranean, into Spain, Sardinia, Sicily, and the African interior. At its height, Carthage commanded fleets that dominated Mediterranean trade, armies that included soldiers and elephants from across Africa and Europe, and a treasury so large that other ancient peoples spoke of Carthaginian wealth as a byword for riches. For nearly seven hundred years, this African city stood as one of the great powers of the ancient Mediterranean world — until a long and devastating series of wars with the rising power of Rome ended in the city's complete destruction.

This chapter studies Carthage and the broader history of ancient North Africa west of Egypt, a region historians often call the Maghrib, stretching from modern Libya through Tunisia, Algeria, and Morocco. It examines how Phoenician merchants and settlers first established trading posts along the North African coast beginning in the early first millennium BCE, how one of those settlements grew into an independent and enormously wealthy city-state, and how that city-state built an empire of trade, tribute, and military alliance that rivaled — and eventually clashed catastrophically with — the rising Roman Republic. It also examines the societies that Carthage interacted with and depended upon: the Berber (Amazigh) peoples of North Africa, whose kingdoms and confederations were essential partners, rivals, and sometimes subjects of Carthaginian power, and whose own history continued long after Carthage fell.

Why does this history matter for the study of Africa as a continent? Carthage is often remembered, when it is remembered at all in general world history courses, primarily through Roman eyes — as Rome's great enemy, the home of the general Hannibal, the city that nearly destroyed the Roman Republic before being erased from the map. This chapter asks students to see Carthage differently: as an African city, built on African soil, drawing on African resources, African trade routes, African soldiers, and African political alliances, even as it also drew on Phoenician religious traditions and Mediterranean commercial networks brought from the eastern Mediterranean. Carthage was not simply a transplanted piece of the Near East sitting incongruously on the African coast. It was a genuinely African Mediterranean civilization, one that fused Phoenician origins with deep, centuries-long engagement with the peoples, environments, and trade systems of the African continent.

What changed across the period this chapter studies? At the beginning, the North African coast was a patchwork of small Phoenician trading posts, established to support trade routes running from the eastern Mediterranean to Spain and the Atlantic. By the height of Carthaginian power in the fourth and third centuries BCE, those trading posts had given way to a sophisticated imperial system: a wealthy capital city ruling a network of colonies, allied cities, tributary territories, and military alliances with Berber kingdoms, supported by a navy that controlled much of the western Mediterranean and an economy built on agriculture, manufacturing, and long-distance trade reaching as far as West Africa and possibly down the Atlantic coast. By the end of the period, that entire system had been destroyed by Rome, the city razed, its population killed, enslaved, or dispersed, and its territory absorbed into the Roman province of Africa — a name that would eventually, through a long and complicated linguistic history, give the entire continent its modern name.

What continued, despite this destruction? The Berber peoples of North Africa, whose societies predated Carthage and outlasted it, continued to inhabit the region, continued to farm, herd, trade, and govern themselves through kingdoms such as Numidia and Mauretania, and continued to shape North African history long after Carthage's walls had been pulled down. Carthaginian commercial networks, agricultural techniques, and even aspects of Punic language and religion persisted for centuries under Roman rule, particularly in the countryside, among populations far from the gaze of Roman administrators. The Mediterranean orientation of North Africa — its deep commercial, cultural, and political connections to Italy, Spain, Sicily, and the eastern Mediterranean — also continued, shaping the region's history through the Roman, Vandal, Byzantine, and eventually Islamic periods that followed.

Historians reconstruct this history from a difficult and uneven body of evidence. Almost no Carthaginian historical writing survives in its original form; what we know of Carthaginian history comes overwhelmingly from Greek and Roman authors — writers such as Polybius, Livy, and Appian — who wrote about Carthage from the perspective of its enemies and rivals, often centuries after the events they described. This creates a fundamental evidentiary problem that runs through this entire chapter: students of Carthaginian history must constantly ask whose voice they are hearing, and what has been lost because Carthage's own historical traditions were destroyed along with its city. Archaeology has become increasingly important in correcting and supplementing this one-sided literary record, recovering inscriptions, religious sanctuaries, harbor installations, and the physical remains of Carthaginian daily life that no Roman historian thought worth describing.

This chapter connects to the larger story of African history in several important ways. It shows that North Africa's deep historical connections to the wider Mediterranean world long predate the conventional emphasis on European colonization in the nineteenth century; Mediterranean Africa had been integrated into international trade, diplomacy, and warfare for well over a thousand years before any modern European empire arrived. It also shows the diversity of historical experience within Africa itself: the history of Carthage and the Maghrib looks very different from the history of the Nile Valley, the Horn of Africa, or West Africa studied in neighboring chapters, even though all are part of the same continental story. Finally, it introduces themes — long-distance trade, urbanization, the relationship between coastal cities and inland peoples, and the devastating consequences of imperial warfare — that will recur throughout this textbook's exploration of African history.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain how and why Phoenician settlers established trading posts along the North African coast, and why Carthage grew to dominate them.
  2. Trace the rise of Carthage from a coastal trading post to the center of a Mediterranean commercial and political empire.
  3. Describe the structures of Carthaginian trade, including its maritime networks, manufactured goods, agricultural exports, and connections to inland Africa.
  4. Analyze the political institutions of Carthage, including its government, social classes, and relationship with subject and allied territories.
  5. Compare the perspectives of Carthaginians, Romans, and Berber peoples on the conflicts known as the Punic Wars.
  6. Evaluate the causes, course, and consequences of the three Punic Wars, distinguishing short-term triggers from long-term structural rivalry.
  7. Describe Carthaginian society and culture, including religion, social structure, family life, and the role of Berber populations within and around Carthaginian territory.
  8. Assess the historical debate over Carthaginian religious practice, including the contested evidence regarding child sacrifice.
  9. Explain the destruction of Carthage in 146 BCE and its consequences for North Africa and the wider Mediterranean world.
  10. Identify continuities in North African society, economy, and culture that survived the fall of Carthage.
  11. Connect the history of Carthage to the broader history of African engagement with Mediterranean and world trade networks.
  12. Interpret the limitations of the historical evidence for Carthaginian history and explain why most surviving sources come from Carthage's enemies.

Guiding Historical Questions

Questions for Historical Inquiry

  1. Why did Phoenician merchants establish settlements along the North African coast, and what made Carthage's location especially advantageous?
  2. How did Carthage transform from a single trading colony into the capital of a Mediterranean empire?
  3. What kinds of goods, routes, and partnerships made Carthaginian trade so profitable, and how did that trade connect North Africa to West Africa, Spain, and the wider Mediterranean?
  4. How was Carthaginian government organized, and how did it compare to the political systems of its Mediterranean rivals?
  5. Why did Carthage and Rome go to war three times, and could that conflict have been avoided?
  6. How did Berber kingdoms and peoples shape the history of Carthage, and how did Carthage shape Berber history in turn?
  7. What can historians know about Carthaginian religion and culture given that almost all surviving written sources were produced by Carthage's enemies?
  8. What were the human consequences of the destruction of Carthage in 146 BCE?
  9. What aspects of Carthaginian and North African society persisted after Carthage's fall, and what was permanently lost?
  10. Why does Carthage matter for understanding Africa's long history of international trade and exchange?

Historical Context

To understand Carthage, students must first understand the world of Phoenician trade from which it emerged. The Phoenicians were a Semitic-speaking, Canaanite people whose homeland lay along the eastern Mediterranean coast in the region of modern Lebanon, organized into a cluster of independent city-states including Tyre, Sidon, and Byblos. From the early second millennium BCE onward, these cities built their wealth and influence not on territorial conquest but on maritime trade, shipbuilding, and craft production, especially the manufacture of a prized purple dye extracted from a Mediterranean sea snail — a dye so valuable and so closely associated with Phoenician commerce that the Greeks called these people "Phoinikes," likely a reference to this purple-red color, and the term "Phoenician" entered Western historical vocabulary from that root.

Beginning in the eleventh and tenth centuries BCE, and accelerating through the ninth and eighth centuries BCE, Phoenician city-states — most prominently Tyre — began establishing trading posts and colonies across the Mediterranean, driven by a combination of commercial ambition, competition for resources such as metals, and pressure from expanding empires in the Near East, including Assyria, which placed increasing political and economic demands on the Phoenician homeland cities. These colonies served as way stations along trade routes, sources of raw materials such as silver, tin, and other metals, and outlets for Phoenician manufactured goods. Phoenician settlements appeared on Cyprus, in Sicily, Sardinia, Spain, Malta, and along the coast of North Africa.

The North African coast held particular strategic and commercial value. It lay roughly midway along the sea route connecting the Phoenician homeland to the silver-rich regions of southern Spain, making North African settlements valuable as supply stations and as secure harbors along an otherwise long and exposed voyage. The North African coastal plain also offered fertile agricultural land, access to the products of the African interior through long-established Saharan and sub-Saharan trade contacts, and proximity to important fishing grounds and salt-production sites. According to Carthaginian tradition as later recorded by Roman writers, the city of Carthage itself was founded around 814 BCE by settlers from Tyre, traditionally associated with the legendary queen Elissa, known to Roman writers as Dido — though, as with much of early Carthaginian history, this founding narrative comes to us filtered through later Greek and Roman retellings rather than contemporary Carthaginian sources.

It is equally important to understand that North Africa was not an empty landscape awaiting Phoenician settlement. The region was home to indigenous Berber, or Amazigh, peoples, who had inhabited North Africa for millennia before Phoenician ships ever appeared on the horizon. Berber-speaking communities practiced agriculture, pastoralism, and trade across the coastal plains, the Atlas Mountains, and the fringes of the Sahara, and they had developed their own forms of political organization, often centered on kinship groups, confederations, and, by the period this chapter studies, increasingly centralized kingdoms. The relationship between Phoenician and later Carthaginian settlers and these Berber populations — sometimes cooperative, sometimes exploitative, sometimes openly hostile — is one of the central themes of this chapter and a corrective to historical narratives that treat Carthage as simply an extension of the Near East rather than a society fundamentally shaped by its African context.

By the time Carthage rose to prominence in the sixth and fifth centuries BCE, the eastern Phoenician homeland cities had themselves come under increasing pressure from larger empires — first Assyria, then Babylon, then Persia — and had lost much of their earlier independence and initiative. Carthage increasingly operated independently of its founding city, eventually eclipsing Tyre and the other Phoenician homeland cities as the dominant center of what historians call the western Phoenician, or "Punic," world — a term derived from the Latin "Poenus," itself derived from the Greek word for Phoenician, and used by modern historians to distinguish the western Mediterranean Phoenician-descended civilization centered on Carthage from the eastern Phoenician homeland.

Section 9.1

Phoenician Settlement in North Africa

The history of Carthage begins not with Carthage itself but with the broader pattern of Phoenician maritime expansion that brought settlers to the North African coast in the first place. By the early first millennium BCE, the Phoenician homeland cities of Tyre, Sidon, and Byblos had developed a distinctive economic model based on seaborne trade rather than territorial agriculture or conquest. Phoenician merchants built fast, durable ships, developed sophisticated navigational skills that allowed them to sail far from coastlines when necessary, and cultivated trading relationships across the Mediterranean and into the Atlantic, exchanging manufactured goods such as glass, textiles, and metalwork for raw materials, especially metals like silver, tin, copper, and gold, which were essential to the bronze and, later, growing iron economies of the ancient Mediterranean world.

North Africa attracted Phoenician attention for several specific reasons. First, geography made it strategically valuable: the North African coast lay along the most direct sailing route between the eastern Mediterranean and the metal-rich regions of Spain, particularly the silver mines of the Iberian Peninsula. Ships making this long voyage needed secure harbors, freshwater sources, and supply depots along the way, and the North African coast — particularly the area around the Gulf of Tunis, with its natural harbors and defensible peninsulas — provided exactly this kind of way station. Second, North Africa itself offered valuable resources and trade connections: fertile agricultural land capable of producing grain and other foodstuffs, access to fishing grounds and the production of salt and a fermented fish product called garum that was widely traded across the ancient Mediterranean, and crucially, access through inland trade networks to products of the wider African interior, including gold, ivory, and other goods that traveled along trans-Saharan and sub-Saharan routes that predated Phoenician arrival by centuries.

Phoenician settlement in North Africa did not happen all at once, nor did it happen in an empty landscape. Archaeological and historical evidence suggests that Phoenician traders first established small seasonal or semi-permanent trading posts, often called emporia, at various points along the coast beginning perhaps as early as the eleventh or tenth century BCE, though the most substantial and best-documented settlements date from the ninth and eighth centuries BCE onward. These included settlements at Utica, traditionally considered older than Carthage itself, as well as at Hadrumetum, Hippo, and other sites along the Tunisian and Libyan coast, and eventually further west along the coasts of modern Algeria and Morocco.

These settlements depended on, and had to negotiate relationships with, the Berber peoples who already inhabited the region. Berber communities along the coastal plains practiced settled agriculture, raising grain, olives, and livestock, while other Berber groups, particularly in more arid zones bordering the Sahara, practiced various forms of pastoralism, herding cattle, sheep, goats, and eventually horses and camels across seasonal grazing territories. Berber political organization in this early period appears to have been built primarily around kinship-based confederations rather than large centralized states, though this would change over subsequent centuries, partly in response to the growing power of Carthage itself, which both depended on and increasingly sought to dominate its Berber neighbors.

The relationship between Phoenician settlers and Berber populations in this early period was shaped by mutual interest as much as by conflict. Phoenician traders needed access to land, agricultural surplus, and inland trade goods; Berber communities gained access to manufactured goods, new agricultural and craft technologies, and new market opportunities for their own products. Intermarriage between Phoenician settlers and Berber populations appears to have been common, and over generations, the population of Carthage and other Phoenician settlements in North Africa became increasingly mixed, drawing on both Phoenician and Berber ancestry, language, and cultural practice — a pattern of fusion that should caution students against imagining Carthaginian civilization as simply transplanted Near Eastern culture rather than something new, produced by genuine African and Mediterranean interaction.

At the same time, this relationship was never purely cooperative or equal. As Phoenician and, later, Carthaginian settlements grew wealthier and more powerful, they increasingly sought to extract tribute, labor, and military service from surrounding Berber communities, and conflicts over land, resources, and political authority became a recurring feature of North African history throughout the Carthaginian period. Understanding this dual character of cooperation and domination is essential to understanding the entire arc of Carthaginian history covered in this chapter.

Section 9.2

The Rise of Carthage

Among the many Phoenician settlements along the North African coast, Carthage — whose name derives from the Phoenician "Qart-hadasht," meaning "new city" — rose to a position of unrivaled dominance. Several factors explain this rise. Carthage occupied an exceptionally strong defensive and commercial position: built on a peninsula with access to two natural harbors, surrounded on the landward side by a fertile agricultural plain, and positioned at the narrowest point of the central Mediterranean, within sight of Sicily on a clear day, Carthage commanded one of the most strategically valuable locations in the entire Mediterranean basin.

Carthage's rise also coincided with, and partly resulted from, the decline of its founding city. Tyre and the other Phoenician homeland cities came under increasing pressure during the eighth, seventh, and sixth centuries BCE from the expanding empires of Assyria, Babylon, and eventually Persia, which repeatedly besieged, conquered, or imposed tribute upon the Phoenician homeland. As Tyre's ability to support and direct its western colonies weakened, Carthage increasingly acted independently, eventually establishing its own colonies and trading posts across the western Mediterranean — in Sardinia, western Sicily, the Balearic Islands, and southern Spain — effectively inheriting and expanding the western Phoenician trading network that Tyre could no longer sustain.

By the sixth century BCE, Carthage had transformed from one Phoenician colony among several into the unquestioned leader and protector of what historians call the Punic world: the network of western Mediterranean cities and trading posts of Phoenician origin, now increasingly oriented around Carthaginian leadership, Carthaginian military protection, and Carthaginian commercial interests rather than around the distant and weakened cities of the Phoenician homeland. This transition reflects a broader pattern common in the ancient Mediterranean: a colony or trading network outgrowing its founding city and becoming an independent power in its own right.

Carthaginian expansion in this period took several forms. Carthage established new colonies and reinforced existing ones, extending direct or indirect control over much of the North African coast from modern Libya to the Atlantic coast of Morocco. It extended its influence into Sardinia and western Sicily, bringing it into direct and prolonged conflict with the Greek city-states that had established their own colonies in eastern Sicily, a rivalry that produced major conflicts such as the Battle of Himera in 480 BCE. It deepened its commercial and political relationships with the kingdoms of southern Spain, gaining access to the silver and other metal resources that had originally drawn Phoenician traders to the western Mediterranean centuries earlier. And it developed an increasingly sophisticated relationship — alternately cooperative, exploitative, and conflictual — with the Berber kingdoms and confederations of its African hinterland, on whom it depended for agricultural production, trade goods, and military manpower.

By the fourth and early third centuries BCE, Carthage stood as one of the great powers of the Mediterranean world, ruling — directly or through alliance, tribute, and influence — territory across North Africa, Sardinia, western Sicily, and southern Spain, commanding a navy widely regarded as the most powerful in the Mediterranean, and possessing a level of commercial wealth that ancient writers consistently described in superlative terms. It was at this height of power that Carthage entered into the conflicts with the rising Roman Republic that would eventually destroy it.

Section 9.3

Trade and Maritime Power

Carthage's power rested fundamentally on trade, and understanding Carthaginian trade requires understanding both its maritime networks across the Mediterranean and its overland connections into the African interior. Carthaginian merchant ships ranged across the entire Mediterranean basin and beyond, carrying manufactured goods — textiles, pottery, glassware, metalwork, and the famous purple dye inherited from Phoenician tradition — to markets from Spain to the eastern Mediterranean, and returning with metals, agricultural products, slaves, and luxury goods.

Carthaginian agriculture was itself a major source of wealth. The fertile plains surrounding Carthage and other North African coastal cities produced grain, olives, and grapes on a large scale, much of it organized on substantial agricultural estates that, according to later Roman accounts, employed enslaved and tenant labor under increasingly sophisticated agricultural management — Carthaginian agricultural writing, particularly the work of a Carthaginian author named Mago whose treatise on farming was later translated and used by Roman writers, suggests a tradition of agricultural science and estate management that was admired even by Carthage's eventual conquerors.

Equally important were Carthage's connections to the wider African interior. Trans-Saharan and sub-Saharan trade routes, some of which long predated Carthaginian power, brought gold, ivory, exotic animals, and other goods northward to Mediterranean markets, often passing through or near Carthaginian-controlled territory and Berber intermediary networks. Some ancient accounts, including a famous narrative attributed to the Carthaginian navigator Hanno, describe Carthaginian voyages along the Atlantic coast of Africa, possibly reaching as far south as the coast of modern Senegal or beyond, though the exact extent of these voyages remains debated among historians because the surviving account is a later Greek translation of uncertain reliability.

Carthaginian commercial success depended on naval power. Carthage built and maintained large fleets of warships, including vessels known as quinqueremes, and used naval strength both to protect its trade routes and to enforce what amounted to a trade monopoly across much of the western Mediterranean, restricting or taxing the commercial activity of rival cities and excluding competitors from certain ports and markets. This combination of commercial wealth and naval power made Carthage simultaneously one of the richest and one of the most militarily formidable states in the ancient Mediterranean — and made it, in the eyes of an expanding Roman Republic, an obstacle that would eventually have to be confronted.

Historical Vocabulary
Emporium

An emporium (plural emporia) was a trading post or market settlement, often established by a maritime trading people such as the Phoenicians or Greeks at a strategic coastal location, designed primarily to facilitate exchange between local populations and seaborne merchants rather than to function as a fully independent city in its own right. Many of the earliest Phoenician settlements in North Africa began as emporia before some, like Carthage, grew into major independent cities. Understanding the emporium as a historical institution helps students see how trade networks, rather than military conquest alone, often drove the earliest phases of overseas settlement in the ancient Mediterranean.

Section 9.4

Politics and Imperial Expansion

Carthaginian government has long fascinated historians, partly because the Greek philosopher Aristotle himself praised the Carthaginian constitution as a well-balanced mixture of monarchic, aristocratic, and democratic elements, comparing it favorably to the constitutions of Sparta and Crete. Carthage was governed not by a hereditary king but by a republic dominated by its wealthy merchant aristocracy. Executive authority rested with two annually elected officials called suffetes, roughly comparable to the Roman consuls, who presided over civic affairs and the judicial system, though without the extensive military command authority that Roman consuls held. A Senate composed of members of the leading aristocratic families debated and set policy, and a smaller body, sometimes called the Hundred and Four, exercised oversight, including judicial authority over military commanders, reflecting Carthaginian aristocratic wariness of any individual general accumulating too much independent power.

Military command in Carthage was typically delegated to professional generals, often drawn from leading families such as the Barca family that produced Hannibal, who led armies that depended heavily on mercenary and allied troops rather than a citizen militia of the kind that dominated Roman or Greek city-state warfare. Carthaginian armies famously included Numidian cavalry recruited from Berber kingdoms, Iberian and Celtiberian infantry from Spain, Gallic mercenaries, Balearic slingers, and the war elephants for which Carthaginian armies became particularly famous in ancient and modern memory. This reliance on diverse mercenary and allied forces reflected both Carthage's wealth — which allowed it to pay for large professional armies without depriving its own commercial economy of labor — and a structural weakness, since the loyalty of mercenary forces depended on continued payment and could become unreliable during financial crises, as the Mercenary War following the First Punic War would dramatically demonstrate.

Carthaginian imperial expansion took the form less of direct territorial annexation in the Roman style and more of a layered system of direct rule, tributary relationships, and military alliance. Carthage directly governed its home territory in Tunisia and a network of colonial cities along the North African coast and in Sardinia and western Sicily. It extracted tribute and military service from many surrounding Berber communities and kingdoms, while maintaining more equal alliance relationships with others, particularly powerful Numidian kingdoms whose cavalry forces Carthage prized and depended upon. In Spain, Carthaginian power expanded dramatically in the decades following the First Punic War, particularly under the leadership of Hamilcar Barca and later Hannibal, who built a substantial territorial and tributary empire among the Iberian peoples of the peninsula, partly to compensate for losses suffered elsewhere and partly to access Spain's valuable silver mines.

This layered imperial structure gave Carthage access to enormous resources of wealth, manpower, and strategic territory, but it also created vulnerabilities. Subject and allied peoples who had been conquered, taxed, or coerced into supplying troops did not always feel loyalty to Carthage, and Carthaginian authority could prove fragile during moments of military crisis or financial strain — a vulnerability that became starkly apparent during and after the Punic Wars.

Section 9.5

Conflict with Rome

The defining conflict of Carthaginian history was the long rivalry with the Roman Republic, fought across three major wars over more than a century and conventionally known as the Punic Wars, from the Latin term for Phoenician. These wars transformed the Mediterranean world and ultimately destroyed Carthage entirely.

The First Punic War (264–241 BCE) began over control of Sicily, an island that lay at the boundary between Carthaginian and emerging Roman spheres of influence and that hosted both Carthaginian and Greek settlements as well as independent local communities. A dispute involving the city of Messana drew Rome and Carthage into direct conflict, and the war that followed was fought primarily at sea, forcing Rome — previously a land power with little naval tradition — to build a large war fleet essentially from nothing, a remarkable feat of mobilization that ultimately allowed Rome to defeat Carthaginian naval forces and force Carthage to abandon its claims to Sicily, pay a substantial war indemnity, and cede the island, which became Rome's first overseas province.

The aftermath of the First Punic War nearly destroyed Carthage from within, as the financially exhausted state struggled to pay its mercenary armies, triggering the brutal Mercenary War of 241–238 BCE, in which unpaid mercenary and allied troops, including many Berber soldiers, rebelled against Carthaginian authority. Rome took advantage of Carthage's weakness during this crisis to seize Sardinia as well, an action widely regarded by Carthaginians, then and since, as an opportunistic violation of the previous peace settlement and a significant grievance feeding into future conflict.

The Second Punic War (218–201 BCE) is the most famous of the three conflicts, largely due to the campaign of the Carthaginian general Hannibal Barca, who led an army that included war elephants across the Alps into Italy, winning a series of stunning victories against Roman armies, most devastatingly at the Battle of Cannae in 216 BCE, where Roman casualties may have exceeded fifty thousand soldiers in a single day. Despite these victories, Hannibal was never able to capture Rome itself or permanently break the alliance system that bound Rome's Italian allies to it, and the war eventually turned against Carthage as Rome carried the fighting to Spain and then to North Africa itself, where the Roman general Scipio, allying with the Numidian king Masinissa against Carthage, decisively defeated Hannibal at the Battle of Zama in 202 BCE. The peace settlement stripped Carthage of its overseas territories, imposed a crushing war indemnity, restricted Carthaginian military capacity, and forbade Carthage from waging war without Roman permission.

The Third Punic War (149–146 BCE) emerged from this lopsided settlement, as a weakened but still commercially prosperous Carthage faced relentless pressure and provocation from Rome, encouraged in part by the ambitious Numidian king Masinissa, whose territorial encroachments Carthage was forbidden to resist militarily without Roman approval. When Carthage finally took limited military action against Numidian incursions in violation of its treaty obligations, Rome used this as justification for total war, besieging the city for three years until it fell in 146 BCE. Roman forces destroyed the city, killed or enslaved much of its surviving population, and — according to later, possibly embellished tradition — sowed its fields with salt to render the land permanently unusable, though many historians now consider this specific detail a later legendary addition rather than verified historical fact.

Multiple Perspectives

The Punic Wars from Different Vantage Points

From the perspective of Roman political and military leaders: The wars were a necessary struggle for survival and supremacy against a wealthy and dangerous rival, particularly after Hannibal's invasion brought devastating war to the Italian peninsula itself. Many Roman leaders, especially in the war's later stages, came to see Carthage's mere existence as an intolerable long-term threat.
From the perspective of Carthaginian merchants and political leaders: The wars represented an existential struggle to preserve Carthaginian independence, trade networks, and territorial holdings against an increasingly aggressive and expansionist Roman power that repeatedly violated or reinterpreted previous peace settlements to its own advantage, as in the seizure of Sardinia after the First Punic War.
From the perspective of Berber kingdoms such as Numidia: The wars offered an opportunity to escape Carthaginian dominance and expand independent Berber political power by allying strategically with Rome, even though this ultimately also exposed Berber kingdoms to long-term Roman influence and eventual incorporation into the Roman world.
From the perspective of ordinary inhabitants of Sicily, Spain, and North Africa: These wars brought repeated invasion, requisition of crops and livestock, forced military service, and devastating violence to communities that often had little say in the conflict between the two great powers contesting their homelands.
Section 9.6

North African Society and Culture

Carthaginian society was structured around a wealthy merchant and landowning aristocracy that dominated political life, a substantial population of free citizens engaged in trade, craft production, and agriculture, and a large enslaved population that labored on agricultural estates, in workshops, and in households. Carthaginian religious life centered on a pantheon of deities inherited from Phoenician tradition, most prominently the god Baal Hammon and the goddess Tanit, worshipped at temples and open-air sanctuaries across Carthaginian territory.

Family life in Carthage, as in much of the ancient Mediterranean world, was organized around the household, with men generally holding formal legal and political authority, though Carthaginian women, particularly from wealthy families, could own property and exercised significant influence within households and, occasionally, religious and civic life. Carthaginian education for the elite included literacy in the Punic language and script, derived from Phoenician writing, and training in commerce, administration, and, for young men of leading families, military leadership.

The population of Carthage and its North African territories was never simply "Phoenician" in any narrow sense. Centuries of intermarriage, cultural exchange, and shared economic life had produced a genuinely mixed Punic-Berber society, particularly in the countryside and smaller towns away from the cosmopolitan port of Carthage itself. Berber populations within and around Carthaginian territory retained their own languages, social structures, and increasingly, under the pressure and example of Carthaginian state organization, their own more centralized kingdoms, most notably Numidia and Mauretania, which developed sophisticated governments, minted their own coinage, and engaged in diplomacy with both Carthage and Rome as independent actors in their own right.

Historical Vocabulary
Punic

Punic is the term modern historians use to describe the western Mediterranean civilization centered on Carthage, its language (a dialect of Phoenician), and its culture, distinguishing it from the eastern Phoenician homeland cities of Tyre, Sidon, and Byblos. The term derives from the Latin "Poenus," itself derived from the Greek word for Phoenician, and it is the root of the term "Punic Wars," the Roman name for their conflicts with Carthage. Using this term carefully helps students understand that Carthaginian civilization, while rooted in Phoenician origins, developed its own distinct character through centuries of African and western Mediterranean development.

Historical Vocabulary
Suffete

A suffete was one of two chief executive magistrates elected annually to lead the Carthaginian government, roughly comparable to the Roman office of consul, though without the same degree of independent military command authority. The existence of an elected, term-limited executive office, alongside a Senate dominated by leading families, led the Greek philosopher Aristotle to describe Carthage's government admiringly as a balanced mixed constitution, comparable to those of Sparta and Crete. Understanding Carthaginian political institutions helps correct the common assumption that all ancient Mediterranean states beyond Greece and Rome were simple monarchies.

Historical Debate

Did Carthage Practice Child Sacrifice?

The question: Greek and Roman writers frequently accused Carthage of practicing ritual child sacrifice to the gods Baal Hammon and Tanit, particularly during times of crisis. Archaeologists have excavated burial sites, called tophets, in Carthage and other Punic sites containing the cremated remains of infants and young children alongside animal remains and inscriptions referring to vows made to these deities. Historians disagree sharply about how to interpret this evidence.

Interpretation A — Child sacrifice was a real, if probably limited, religious practice: Some historians and archaeologists argue that the tophet burials, combined with consistent ancient testimony from multiple independent Greek and Roman sources, support the conclusion that Carthaginians did practice ritual child sacrifice, particularly of infants, as an extreme religious offering during periods of crisis, though probably never on the scale suggested by the most hostile ancient accounts.
Interpretation B — The evidence reflects infant mortality and Roman/Greek propaganda rather than systematic sacrifice: Other historians and archaeologists argue that the tophet burial sites may simply reflect cemeteries for infants who died of natural causes — given how common infant mortality was in the ancient world — and that ancient Greek and Roman accounts of Carthaginian child sacrifice were shaped by a long literary tradition of portraying foreign and rival peoples as practicing barbaric religious customs, a pattern visible in how Greek and Roman writers described many non-Greek and non-Roman peoples.

Evidence: Supporters of Interpretation A point to the consistency of multiple independent ancient sources and to inscriptional evidence using vocabulary historians associate with sacrificial vows. Supporters of Interpretation B point to the absence of any surviving Carthaginian source confirming the practice, the well-documented Greek and Roman tendency to attribute human sacrifice to feared foreign rivals (including, at times, to each other), and statistical analysis of the burial remains suggesting ages consistent with natural infant mortality patterns in some studies, though other studies of the same remains have reached different conclusions.

Why it matters: This debate illustrates a central challenge of Carthaginian history: nearly everything we know about Carthaginian religion and culture comes from sources produced by Carthage's enemies and rivals, making it extremely difficult to separate historical fact from hostile propaganda, and reminding students that archaeological evidence, while invaluable, often remains open to multiple reasonable interpretations.

Student task: Considering both the literary and archaeological evidence summarized above, which interpretation do you find more persuasive, and what specific kind of additional evidence would help historians resolve this debate with greater confidence?

Section 9.7

War, Empire, and Decline

The destruction of Carthage in 146 BCE was one of the most complete acts of state annihilation in the ancient world. After a brutal three-year siege during the Third Punic War, Roman forces under the command of Scipio Aemilianus broke into the city, fighting street by street against a desperate population that included many noncombatants pressed into the city's defense after years of siege and famine. Ancient accounts describe days of intense urban warfare before Roman forces secured the city, which was then systematically burned, its walls demolished, and its surviving population — perhaps fifty thousand people, a small fraction of its prewar population, since many had already died of starvation, disease, and violence during the siege — sold into slavery.

Rome formally annexed Carthaginian territory as the province of Africa, governed directly by Roman officials and increasingly settled by Roman and Italian colonists in subsequent generations, particularly after Julius Caesar and later Augustus established new Roman colonies on or near the site of Carthage itself, rebuilding the city as a Roman provincial capital that would, in later centuries, become one of the wealthiest and most important cities of the Roman Empire, a striking irony given the city's earlier complete destruction.

The destruction of Carthage did not, however, mean the end of independent African political power in the region. Berber kingdoms, particularly Numidia under Masinissa and his successors, and Mauretania further west, continued to govern substantial territories as independent or semi-independent states allied with Rome for more than a century after Carthage's fall, illustrating that the end of Carthaginian power was not simply the end of African political agency in North Africa but rather a transition to a different configuration of power, in which Berber kingdoms, no longer overshadowed by Carthaginian dominance, briefly flourished as independent actors before eventually being absorbed, kingdom by kingdom, into the expanding Roman Empire over the following century and a half.

Section 9.8

North Africa in the Mediterranean World

Carthage's history demonstrates that North Africa was never peripheral to the ancient Mediterranean world but rather one of its central arenas of trade, diplomacy, and conflict. Carthaginian commercial networks connected North Africa to Spain, Sardinia, Sicily, the Italian peninsula, and the eastern Mediterranean, while Carthaginian relationships with Berber kingdoms and trans-Saharan trade routes connected this Mediterranean world, in turn, to the wider African continent.

This integration worked in multiple directions. Mediterranean demand for African goods — gold, ivory, agricultural products, and enslaved people — drew Mediterranean trading networks deeper into the African interior, while African demand for Mediterranean manufactured goods, and African political and military engagement with Mediterranean states such as Carthage and later Rome, drew North African polities increasingly into Mediterranean diplomatic, commercial, and military systems. This pattern — coastal African societies serving as intermediaries and active participants between Mediterranean and deeper African trade networks — recurs throughout African history and will appear again in later chapters covering Egypt, Axum, and the Swahili Coast in their respective Mediterranean, Red Sea, and Indian Ocean contexts.

Section 9.9

Carthage's Historical Legacy

Carthage's most direct legacy lies, paradoxically, in the writings of its enemies. Because Rome destroyed Carthage so completely, almost no Carthaginian literature, historical writing, or administrative records survive in their original form; what we know of Carthaginian history comes overwhelmingly from Greek and Roman authors writing from perspectives shaped by rivalry, warfare, and, in many cases, a desire to justify Rome's eventual destruction of the city. This evidentiary imbalance has shaped — and distorted — the historical memory of Carthage for more than two thousand years, and modern historians and archaeologists continue working to recover a fuller and fairer picture of Carthaginian civilization from material and inscriptional evidence less filtered by hostile testimony.

Carthage's legacy also persisted, more quietly, in the continuing life of North Africa under Roman, and later Vandal, Byzantine, and Islamic rule. Punic language and religious practice survived for centuries in the North African countryside even after the political destruction of Carthage, gradually fading but leaving traces in place names, religious practices, and, according to some linguists, possibly in elements of regional dialects long after Latin had become the dominant written language of the region. The agricultural techniques, urban infrastructure, and commercial networks that Carthage had developed over centuries also continued to shape North African economic life under Roman rule, which built directly upon Carthaginian foundations even as it eliminated Carthaginian political independence.

Perhaps the most striking element of Carthage's legacy is linguistic: the Roman province established on former Carthaginian territory was named Africa, after a Berber people called the Afri who lived near Carthage, and this regional name — originally referring to a relatively small area of North Africa — gradually expanded in geographic usage over the following centuries until, by the medieval and early modern periods, "Africa" had come to refer to the entire continent. The very name by which this textbook and this entire field of study refers to the continent thus traces back, through a long and complicated linguistic history, to a small Berber community living near ancient Carthage.

Section 9.10

Chapter Review and Mediterranean Africa Checkpoint

This chapter has traced the rise and fall of Carthage from its origins as a Phoenician trading post to its position as one of the great powers of the ancient Mediterranean world, and finally to its complete destruction at the hands of Rome. Students should now be able to explain how Phoenician maritime trade brought settlers to North Africa, how Carthage grew to dominate the western Phoenician world, how Carthaginian wealth and naval power rested on extensive Mediterranean and trans-Saharan trade networks, how Carthaginian government and society combined aristocratic, commercial, and military institutions, why Carthage and Rome fought three devastating wars, and what happened to North Africa and its Berber kingdoms after Carthage's fall.

Cause and Effect

The Destruction of Carthage in the Third Punic War

Long-Term Causes

Decades of Roman fear and resentment of Carthaginian commercial and military power; the punishing terms imposed after the Second Punic War, including restrictions on Carthaginian military independence; the influence of Roman political figures, including Cato the Elder, who argued Carthage's continued existence posed a permanent threat.

Short-Term Causes

Continued territorial encroachment by the Numidian king Masinissa, which Carthage was forbidden by treaty to resist militarily without Roman permission; Carthage's eventual unauthorized military response to Numidian provocation, which Rome treated as a treaty violation justifying war.

The Event

Rome besieged Carthage from 149 to 146 BCE, eventually breaking into the city after prolonged urban warfare, systematically destroying it and enslaving its surviving population.

Effects

End of Carthaginian independence; creation of the Roman province of Africa; temporary expansion of independent Berber kingdoms before their eventual absorption into Rome; permanent loss of most Carthaginian historical and literary records; the eventual extension of the name "Africa" to the entire continent.

Historical complexity: The destruction of Carthage cannot be reduced to a single cause. It resulted from decades of structural rivalry between two expanding Mediterranean powers, the specific grievances and provocations of the postwar settlement, and the political choices of individual leaders on both sides — including Berber leaders whose own ambitions shaped the conflict's final outcome.

Continuity and Change Over Time

North Africa Before and After the Fall of Carthage

What Changed

  • Carthaginian political independence ended permanently; the city was destroyed and later rebuilt as a Roman provincial capital
  • North Africa became directly incorporated into the Roman administrative and tax system as the province of Africa
  • Berber kingdoms briefly expanded their independent territory before being gradually absorbed into the Roman Empire over the following century and a half
  • Latin increasingly became the dominant administrative and literary language of the region, displacing Punic in formal contexts

What Stayed the Same

  • Berber peoples continued to inhabit, farm, and herd across North Africa as they had for millennia before Carthage existed
  • North Africa's agricultural economy, built on grain, olives, and grapes, continued to flourish under Roman rule, now feeding Rome itself
  • North Africa's deep commercial and political orientation toward the wider Mediterranean world persisted and even intensified
  • Punic language and religious practice survived for generations in rural areas even after the loss of Carthaginian political power

Why the pattern matters: The fall of a powerful state does not erase the peoples, economies, and cultural patterns beneath it. North Africa's Berber population and Mediterranean economic orientation persisted through Carthaginian, Roman, Vandal, Byzantine, and eventually Islamic rule, illustrating how political conquest changes who governs a region far more readily than it changes the underlying society living there.

Primary Source Window

Polybius, The Histories (2nd century BCE)

Creator: Polybius, a Greek historian who lived in Rome as a hostage and later associate of leading Roman families · Date: written mid-to-late 2nd century BCE · Type: historical narrative

Context: Polybius was a Greek statesman and historian brought to Rome as a hostage following Rome's conquest of Greece, where he became closely associated with the Scipio family, including Scipio Aemilianus, who commanded Roman forces at the final destruction of Carthage. Polybius wrote one of the most detailed surviving accounts of the Punic Wars, drawing on both Roman and, where available, Carthaginian sources, and he claimed to value careful, evidence-based historical method, though his close personal connections to Roman leadership inevitably shaped his perspective.

Polybius described Scipio Aemilianus weeping at the sight of burning Carthage, reportedly reflecting aloud on the inevitable fall of all great cities and empires, including, he suggested, Rome itself one day — a moment Polybius presents as revealing Roman awareness of the tragedy and historical weight of the city's destruction, even amid Roman triumph.

Analysis: This account is invaluable because it is one of the few surviving narratives describing the actual destruction of Carthage in detail, and it reveals something of how thoughtful Romans themselves understood the moral and historical significance of annihilating a rival civilization. However, students should note that Polybius's personal closeness to Scipio's family may have shaped a portrayal of the Roman commander as a reflective, even sympathetic figure, and that no equivalent Carthaginian voice survives to describe the same events from inside the besieged city.

  3. What can historians learn from this account about Roman attitudes toward empire and historical change?
  4. What is missing from this account that a Carthaginian source, had one survived, might have included?

Everyday Life and Social History
A Carthaginian Port at Work

Beyond the generals and senators who dominate surviving accounts, most residents of Carthage and its territories led lives centered on trade, craft, and agriculture. In Carthage's harbor district, dockworkers loaded and unloaded grain, olive oil, wine, dried fish, and manufactured goods bound for markets across the Mediterranean, while craftsmen in nearby workshops produced pottery, textiles, and metal goods for both local use and export. Many of these workers were enslaved people, captured in war or purchased through Mediterranean and African slave-trading networks, while others were free Carthaginian, Berber, or immigrant laborers.

In the agricultural countryside surrounding Carthage, large estates worked by enslaved and tenant laborers produced the grain, olives, and wine that fed the city and supplied its export trade, following agricultural methods recorded in the now-lost treatise of the Carthaginian writer Mago. Berber farming and herding communities continued their own agricultural and pastoral traditions alongside this Carthaginian estate economy, sometimes drawn into Carthaginian markets and tax systems, sometimes remaining largely independent depending on their distance from Carthaginian centers of power and the particular political relationships their communities maintained with the city.

Carthaginian women managed households, and in wealthier families, sometimes oversaw substantial property and family business interests, while religious festivals dedicated to deities such as Tanit and Baal Hammon structured the rhythms of community and family life across the Carthaginian year, offering ordinary residents — not only the aristocratic families who dominated formal politics — a shared cultural and religious identity that helped bind together this diverse, multilingual Punic-Berber Mediterranean society.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has examined the rise and fall of Carthage, one of the great civilizations of ancient Africa and the ancient Mediterranean world. Carthage began as a Phoenician trading settlement on the North African coast, established by settlers from the eastern Mediterranean city of Tyre seeking secure harbors and trade access along routes connecting the eastern Mediterranean to the metal-rich regions of Spain. Over several centuries, as the Phoenician homeland weakened under pressure from larger Near Eastern empires, Carthage transformed from one colony among several into the dominant center of an independent western Phoenician, or Punic, civilization.

Carthaginian power rested on a foundation of maritime trade, agricultural wealth, naval strength, and a layered system of direct rule, tribute, and alliance extending across North Africa, Sardinia, western Sicily, and eventually substantial territory in Spain. Carthaginian government combined elected executive officials, called suffetes, with an aristocratic Senate, producing a mixed constitution that ancient observers such as Aristotle admired. Carthaginian society fused Phoenician origins with deep, centuries-long engagement with the Berber peoples of North Africa, producing a genuinely mixed Punic-Berber civilization rather than a simple transplant of Near Eastern culture onto African soil.

The chapter has paid particular attention to Carthage's relationship with its Berber neighbors — a relationship of both interdependence and domination that shaped Carthaginian military power, economic life, and eventual political fate. Berber kingdoms such as Numidia provided essential cavalry forces to Carthaginian armies, but Berber rulers such as Masinissa also recognized opportunities to expand their own independent power by allying with Rome against Carthage during the climactic conflicts of the Punic Wars.

Those wars — fought across three major conflicts between 264 and 146 BCE — ultimately determined Carthage's fate. The First Punic War cost Carthage Sicily and triggered a near-catastrophic internal crisis in the Mercenary War. The Second Punic War, despite Hannibal's extraordinary military campaign across the Alps and into Italy, ended in decisive defeat and crippling peace terms. The Third Punic War ended in the complete physical destruction of Carthage itself, the death or enslavement of its surviving population, and the absorption of its territory into the Roman province of Africa.

The chapter has emphasized that this destruction, devastating as it was, did not erase North Africa's history or its peoples. Berber kingdoms briefly flourished as independent powers in Carthage's absence before their own eventual incorporation into the Roman Empire. Punic language, religious practice, agricultural techniques, and commercial patterns persisted for generations under Roman rule, particularly outside the major cities where Roman administrative and cultural influence was less immediate and complete.

Students have also been asked to grapple with a fundamental evidentiary challenge that runs throughout Carthaginian history: because Rome destroyed Carthage so thoroughly, virtually no Carthaginian written sources survive, and almost everything historians know about Carthaginian civilization comes from Greek and Roman writers who were, at best, outside observers and, at worst, active participants in Carthage's destruction with strong motives to justify it. This evidentiary imbalance — explored particularly through the historical debate over Carthaginian child sacrifice — should make students cautious about accepting any single ancient account of Carthaginian society and culture without careful critical evaluation.

Finally, the chapter has connected Carthaginian history to the larger continental story this textbook tells. North Africa's Mediterranean orientation, its long history of international trade reaching from West Africa to Spain to the eastern Mediterranean, and its complex relationship between coastal commercial cities and inland indigenous peoples are patterns that recur throughout African history, from ancient Egypt and Nubia to medieval Ghana and Mali to the early modern Swahili Coast. Carthage demonstrates, centuries before any modern European colonial encounter, that African societies were active, sophisticated participants in international trade, diplomacy, and warfare — not isolated from world history, but central to it.

The legacy of Carthage persists today most visibly in the very name of the continent this textbook studies: the Roman province of Africa, named for a Berber people near Carthage, gradually expanded in usage over many centuries until it came to designate the entire continent. A small North African city, destroyed more than two thousand years ago, thus left behind a linguistic legacy far larger and more enduring than its own walls, harbors, or temples — a fitting final reminder of how deeply North African history is woven into the larger story of Africa and the world.

  4. A historian argues that Carthage was "essentially a Near Eastern colony with little genuine African character." Using evidence from this chapter, explain why this claim is historically inaccurate.
  5. Explain the continuities in North African society and economy that survived the destruction of Carthage in 146 BCE.

Discussion Questions

  1. This chapter argues that Carthage should be understood as a genuinely African Mediterranean civilization rather than simply a transplanted Phoenician colony. Do you find this argument persuasive? What evidence supports or challenges it?
  2. What does the destruction of Carthage suggest about the consequences of prolonged imperial rivalry between great powers?
  3. How should historians weigh ancient sources, such as Polybius, that are detailed and valuable but also shaped by close personal ties to one side of a conflict?
  4. What does the eventual expansion of the name "Africa" from a small region near Carthage to an entire continent suggest about how historical names and categories change over time?
  5. Why might it matter, for understanding African history more broadly, that North Africa was deeply integrated into Mediterranean trade and diplomacy more than two thousand years ago?
  6. Berber kingdoms allied with Rome against Carthage to expand their own independence, yet were eventually absorbed by Rome themselves. What does this suggest about the risks and limits of alliance strategies for smaller or weaker powers?

Research or Library Extension

MGU Library Research Project

Carthage Through the Eyes of Its Enemies

This project asks you to investigate the problem of historical bias by examining how ancient Greek and Roman writers described Carthage, and by comparing their accounts with what archaeology has revealed about Carthaginian civilization.

  1. Select a topic. Choose one aspect of Carthaginian history or culture covered in this chapter — for example, Carthaginian religion, government, trade, or the Punic Wars — to investigate further.
  2. Research ancient literary sources. Using library resources and reliable databases, identify what Greek and Roman authors, such as Polybius, Livy, or Appian, wrote about your chosen topic. Take notes on their claims and their possible biases or motivations.
  3. Research archaeological evidence. Investigate what archaeological discoveries — inscriptions, ruins, burial sites, coinage, or other physical remains — reveal about your chosen topic, and how this evidence compares with or challenges the literary sources.
  4. Analyze the gap. Identify specific points where the literary sources and the archaeological evidence agree, disagree, or leave open questions. Consider what might explain any differences.
  5. Write your findings. In a 3–5 page essay (or equivalent multimedia presentation), explain what ancient writers claimed, what archaeology suggests, and what a careful historian should conclude about your topic given the limitations of the available evidence.

Library resources to use: Translated editions of Polybius, Livy, and Appian; archaeological reports and museum catalogs on Carthaginian and Punic sites; the MGU History Library's reference materials on ancient Mediterranean civilizations and historical source evaluation.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter has told the story of a great African Mediterranean civilization, built by settlers from the eastern Mediterranean but transformed, over centuries of African settlement, trade, and intermarriage, into something genuinely new — a fusion of Phoenician and Berber peoples whose city became one of the wealthiest and most powerful in the ancient world before its complete destruction by Rome. Understanding Carthage matters because it reminds students that North Africa's history of international engagement, commercial sophistication, and political power did not begin with European colonization in the modern era but stretches back well over two thousand years.

Carthage's history shaped the later course of North African and Mediterranean history in lasting ways. The Roman province built on its ashes became one of the wealthiest regions of the Roman Empire, a status that owed much to agricultural and commercial foundations Carthage had built. Berber kingdoms, having gained and then lost independence in the aftermath of Carthage's fall, continued to shape North African political life through subsequent centuries of Roman, Vandal, Byzantine, and eventually Islamic rule, illustrating a deep continuity of African political agency beneath the surface of successive foreign powers.

This chapter also reveals a larger theme that will recur throughout this textbook: the challenge of writing African history from evidence often produced by outsiders, rivals, or conquerors. Just as later chapters on the Atlantic slave trade or European colonialism must grapple with sources shaped by the perspectives of slave traders and colonial administrators, this chapter on Carthage has shown that even in the ancient world, African history was frequently recorded by those who fought, feared, or sought to justify the destruction of African societies. Learning to read such sources critically — neither accepting them uncritically nor dismissing them entirely, but weighing them carefully against archaeological and comparative evidence — is a skill students will need throughout their study of African history.

Finally, Carthage's legacy in the very name of the African continent offers a fitting closing reflection. A civilization destroyed more than two thousand years ago, whose own voice has been almost entirely silenced by the loss of its written records, nonetheless left behind a name that now encompasses an entire continent of enormous diversity, far beyond anything the Carthaginians themselves could have imagined. This is itself a lesson in historical complexity: the past leaves traces in unexpected and sometimes ironic ways, and understanding those traces — their origins, their limitations, and their long afterlives — is central to the work of historical study that this textbook undertakes.

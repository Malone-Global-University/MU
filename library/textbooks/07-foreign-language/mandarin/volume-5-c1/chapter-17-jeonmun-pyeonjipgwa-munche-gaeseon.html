Chapter 17 전문 편집과 문체 개선
Professional Editing and Style Improvement

Chapter Overview

In Chapter 16 you argued and debated under pressure. In this chapter you step back from producing new arguments and instead learn to edit — to take a Korean text that is already grammatically correct and make it clearer, more cohesive, more precise, and more rhetorically effective for its intended audience. Professional editing is a distinct skill from composition: you will practice diagnosing weak cohesion, trimming redundancy, tightening register, and improving the rhetorical flow of documents you or others have written, using only grammar and vocabulary already active in your Korean.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, learners will be able to:

  • identify weaknesses in clarity, cohesion, precision, and rhetorical effect in a Korean text;
  • explain, in Korean, why a specific revision improves a passage;
  • revise a document for register consistency, redundancy, and paragraph-level cohesion;
  • compare an original draft and an edited version and justify each change;
  • produce a short professional style-guide note giving editing feedback to a colleague;
  • present an edited passage aloud, explaining the reasoning behind revisions to a small audience.

Opening Communication Scenario

당신은 한 시민단체의 소식지 편집팀에서 일하고 있습니다. 동료가 작성한 보도자료 초안을 검토하고, 더 명확하고 간결하며 설득력 있는 문체로 다듬어서 피드백을 전달해야 합니다.

Hangeul, Orthography, and Digital Focus

This chapter's orthographic focus is spacing and punctuation in editorial markup: how to write clean, unambiguous track-changes-style comments in Korean (e.g., 삭제, 추가, 수정 필요), and how spacing conventions shift between a rough draft and a polished formal document. Digital tasks include typing brief inline editorial comments and reformatting a document's paragraph breaks for readability, using standard Hangeul input.

Grammar, Style, and Sentence Patterns Principal Focus

Principal scheduled systems for Chapter 17: clarity, cohesion, precision, and rhetorical effect. No new grammar, particle, or speech-level system is introduced. Instead, previously activated systems are reorganized around editorial judgment.

1. Diagnosing weak cohesion

Use previously active connectors (그러나, 따라서, 반면에, -는데, -(으)므로) to evaluate whether a paragraph's logical relationships are marked clearly enough, or whether a connector is missing, redundant, or mismatched with the intended logic.

editorial comment pattern: "이 문장과 앞 문장의 관계가 불분명합니다. 인과 관계라면 '따라서'를, 대조라면 '반면에'를 넣는 것이 좋겠습니다."
(The relationship between this sentence and the previous one is unclear. If it's causal, add "therefore"; if it's contrastive, "on the other hand" would be better.)

2. Trimming redundancy and unnecessary modifiers

Practice replacing repeated content words with pronouns, omission, or synonyms already known, and removing modifiers that add no new information.

3. Register consistency

Identify sentences that break the document's chosen speech level (e.g., a casual expression inside a formal -습니다 press release) and revise for consistency, without changing meaning.

4. Active versus passive, and rhetorical emphasis

Review when an overused passive (수동태 남용, often a symptom of translation from English) weakens directness, and when converting to active voice improves clarity — while recognizing that Korean institutional writing sometimes legitimately favors impersonal passive constructions for objectivity. The choice must be justified by audience and genre, not applied mechanically.

  • Solo alternative: Record yourself reading a passage, then record a second version after revision, and compare the two aloud, narrating your editorial reasoning.

Writing Workshop

Task: You have received a colleague's draft (provided below or one you write yourself in an earlier register). Produce:

  1. An edited version of the passage (150–250 words) with improved clarity, cohesion, precision, and register consistency;
  2. A short written feedback note (3–5 sentences) to the colleague, explaining the main categories of revision using this chapter's vocabulary;
  3. A one-sentence justification of one change you chose not to make, and why (e.g., a passive construction you kept for institutional objectivity).

Success criteria: maintains the original meaning; consistent formal register throughout; no unjustified redundancy; feedback note uses accurate editorial vocabulary; justification shows genre awareness rather than mechanical rule-following.

Culture and Communication

In many South Korean workplaces and publishing houses, editorial feedback (첨삭, 피드백) is often delivered indirectly and cushioned with softening language, even among close colleagues, particularly when a senior editor is reviewing a junior writer's work. Direct criticism such as "이 문장은 틀렸어요" is less common in professional settings than softened alternatives such as "이 부분은 조금 더 다듬으면 좋을 것 같아요." This is a professional-register convention, not a universal personality trait, and it varies by workplace culture, publication, and individual relationship.

Common Mistakes

  • Over-correcting to sound "more Korean": learners sometimes delete every passive construction, even when Korean institutional writing appropriately uses passive/impersonal structures for objectivity. Not every passive is a translation-style error.
  • Confusing 교정 (proofreading — spelling, punctuation) with 윤문/퇴고 (style revision): these are related but distinct editorial tasks; feedback should specify which is meant.
  • Register drift within a single edit: learners fix one sentence's register but leave a neighboring sentence in a different style, creating a new inconsistency.

Chapter Summary

This chapter reframed learners as editors rather than only writers, applying already-active Korean systems — connectors, register control, vocabulary precision — to the specific task of diagnosing and revising someone else's (or one's own) prose. Learners practiced identifying redundancy, register inconsistency, and weak cohesion, and delivering professional, appropriately softened editorial feedback in Korean.

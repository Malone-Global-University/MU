Chapter 19 전문 프로젝트
Specialized Project

Chapter Overview

Chapter 18 asked you to reshape one set of facts for several audiences at once. This chapter asks you to go further: you will select a field of personal or professional interest — for example education, healthcare, technology, the arts, business, environmental policy, or another domain of your choosing — and build a single sustained project inside it, drawing on outside sources, integrating field-specific vocabulary, and designing a coherent final presentation. This chapter introduces no new grammar, particle, or speech-level system. Instead, it consolidates nearly every system activated across Volume V — register control, argument structure, mediation, professional and academic genres, source synthesis, and presentation skills — into a single extended, self-directed task that prepares you directly for the Chapter 20 capstone.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, learners will be able to:

  • select and narrow a specialized field of interest suitable for a sustained Korean-language project;
  • identify and responsibly integrate information from two or more source texts in the target field;
  • build and correctly use a field-specific productive vocabulary set of appropriate density and register;
  • organize a multi-part project with a clear structure, argument, and evidentiary basis;
  • design a presentation that combines spoken explanation with supporting visual or written material;
  • respond to questions about the project's content, sources, and choices in real time;
  • reflect, in Korean, on which language systems and register choices best served the project.

Opening Communication Scenario

당신은 관심 있는 한 분야(교육, 의료, 기술, 예술, 경영, 환경 정책 등)를 선택하여, 그 분야에 대한 전문 프로젝트를 준비하고 있습니다. 최소 두 개의 자료(기사, 보고서, 인터뷰, 통계 자료 등)를 참고하여 내용을 정리하고, 발표 자료를 설계한 뒤, 동료들 앞에서 발표하고 질문에 답해야 합니다.

(You have chosen a field of interest — education, healthcare, technology, the arts, business, environmental policy, or similar — and are preparing a specialized project on that field. You must draw on at least two sources (articles, reports, interviews, statistical data, etc.), organize the content, design presentation materials, and deliver the project to peers while responding to questions.)

Hangeul, Orthography, and Digital Focus

This chapter's orthographic focus is source-integration formatting: consistent in-text citation phrasing (e.g., "OO에 따르면," "OO의 조사에서 밝혀진 바와 같이"), a clearly formatted reference list, and consistent spacing and punctuation across slides, handouts, and a written project summary. Digital tasks include typing a short reference list in Hangeul with correct spacing for titles and publishers, formatting slide headers and bullet points for readability, and searching for field-specific vocabulary using Korean search terms.

Grammar, Style, and Sentence Patterns Principal Focus

Principal scheduled systems for Chapter 19: field-specific Korean, source integration, presentation design. No new grammar, particle, or speech-level system is introduced. This chapter reorganizes and applies previously activated systems — nominalization, indirect quotation, formal connectors, noun-modifying clauses, and register control — toward a single sustained, source-based, presentation-oriented task.

1. Attributing and integrating sources

Review and apply patterns for crediting a source before or after presenting its claim, choosing between direct paraphrase and indirect quotation according to genre and formality.

PatternExample
OO에 따르면, ...한 연구 기관에 따르면, 원격 근무가 생산성에 긍정적인 영향을 미친다고 한다.
OO은/는 ...(이)라고 밝혔다해당 보고서는 도입 초기에 일부 부서의 저항이 있었다고 밝혔다.
OO에서 제시한 자료를 보면, ...해당 기관에서 제시한 자료를 보면, 참여율이 매년 증가하고 있다.

2. Structuring an extended project

Practice organizing content into 도입부 (introduction), 본론 (body, often two or three sub-sections), and 결론 (conclusion), with explicit transitions connecting each part and a closing statement that returns to the opening question or scenario.

3. Field-specific density and audience calibration

Apply the audience-adaptation skills from Chapter 18 within a single sustained project: decide, section by section, how much field-specific terminology to use unexplained, how much to gloss, and how much to replace with plain paraphrase, based on who will attend the presentation.

4. Anticipating and handling questions

Prepare short, complete answers to likely questions using hedging and qualification appropriate to formal spoken registers (e.g., "제가 알기로는," "확실하지는 않지만," "자료에 따르면"), rather than either overclaiming certainty or refusing to answer.

Model Dialogue

민재 (학습자): 교수님, 이번 전문 프로젝트 주제로 원격 근무와 생산성의 관계를 다뤄 보려고 하는데, 괜찮을까요?

한 교수: 좋은 주제예요. 다만 범위가 넓으니까 조금 좁혀 보는 게 어때요? 예를 들어 특정 업종이나 특정 연령대로 한정할 수도 있고요.

Identify: (1) how the presenter introduces the source, (2) how the presenter structures the transition from body to conclusion, and (3) how the presenter hedges the answer to the question.

Speaking Practice

  • Presentation rehearsal: Deliver a five-minute segment of your specialized project to a partner, including at least one clearly attributed source, then receive one question and answer it using a hedging expression.
  • Peer feedback exchange: After listening to a partner's rehearsal, give two pieces of spoken feedback in Korean: one about content organization, one about register or vocabulary density.
  • Solo alternative: Record your full presentation, then listen back and note, in Korean, one transition you would revise and one place where your hedging felt too weak or too strong.

Writing Workshop

Task: Select a field of interest and produce a complete written specialized-project draft (350–500 words) that includes:

  1. a clearly marked 도입부 stating the topic, its narrowed scope, and why it matters;
  2. a 본론 that integrates at least two distinct sources, each properly attributed, with at least one piece of statistical or case-study evidence;
  3. a 결론 that states the project's implications and at least one clearly labeled limitation;
  4. a short reference list (2–4 entries) in consistent Korean formatting;
  5. a brief self-evaluation note (2–3 sentences) identifying one register or vocabulary choice you made deliberately for your intended audience.

Success criteria: the project has a clear three-part structure; every factual claim is either common knowledge or attributed to a source; register and terminology density remain consistent with the stated intended audience; the limitation stated is genuine and specific rather than generic; the reference list is complete and consistently formatted.

Culture and Communication

Korean universities, research institutes, and many workplaces place strong institutional weight on properly crediting sources (출처를 밝히다) and on structuring formal presentations with a visible 도입-본론-결론 architecture; audiences in academic and professional settings commonly expect a stated 한계점 near the conclusion rather than an unqualified claim of complete success. These expectations vary by institution, discipline, and era, and should be treated as common professional and academic norms rather than as fixed rules governing all Korean-language communication.

Common Mistakes

  • Unattributed claims: learners sometimes present a source's specific finding as their own general statement, without any attribution phrase, which can misrepresent the claim's origin and strength.
  • Overclaiming in the conclusion: some learners state findings as universally true, omitting a genuine limitation, which weakens credibility with an academic or professional audience.
  • Terminology mismatch with stated audience: using dense, unexplained field-specific terms after having stated the presentation is for a general or beginner audience, or over-explaining basic terms to an expert audience.

Chapter Summary

This chapter asked learners to build a single sustained, source-based specialized project: narrowing a topic, integrating at least two outside sources with proper attribution, organizing content into a clear introduction-body-conclusion structure, and designing a presentation calibrated to a specific audience, including handling live questions with appropriate hedging. No new grammar system was introduced; instead, learners consolidated source-integration, register-calibration, and presentation-design skills built across Volume V, in direct preparation for the Chapter 20 capstone project.

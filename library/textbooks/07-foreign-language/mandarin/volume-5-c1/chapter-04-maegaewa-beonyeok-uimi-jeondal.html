매개와 번역, 의미 전달Mediation and Translation

Continuing Volume V · Speak and Write Korean: Mastery, Precision, Rhetoric, and Specialized Expression

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Produce a functional Korean-to-English or English-to-Korean rendering of a short passage that preserves meaning and intent rather than word order.
  2. Paraphrase a Korean sentence or short passage in different Korean wording while preserving its core meaning.
  3. Transfer the same content across registers, moving a passage from formal written Korean into everyday spoken Korean and back.
  4. Summarize a short Korean source text accurately in both Korean and English, distinguishing summary from translation.
  5. Identify where literal, word-for-word rendering distorts meaning and choose a functional alternative instead.
  6. Mediate between two parties by relaying the substance of what one party said in language appropriate for the other, without adding or omitting meaning.

This chapter continues Unit I: 정확성, 문체, 그리고 매개 (Precision, Style, and Mediation). It builds on Chapter 3's control of compression and expansion and introduces no new endings, particles, or predicate systems. Its new material is mediation skill: carrying meaning accurately across languages, wordings, and registers using grammar that is already active.

Opening Scenario

수아의 회사에 해외 지사에서 온 동료가 방문한다. 이 동료는 한국어를 전혀 못 하고, 오늘 회의에서는 팀장이 지난 챕터에서 다듬은 보고서 내용을 한국어로 설명한다. 수아는 팀장의 설명을 영어로 옮겨서 동료에게 전달해야 하는데, 팀장은 이렇게 당부한다: "제가 하는 말을 단어 하나하나 그대로 옮기려고 하지 마세요. 제 말의 요점과 의도가 정확하게 전달되면 됩니다. 그리고 회의가 끝나고 나면, 오늘 논의한 내용을 이메일로 간단히 요약해서 양쪽에 보내 주세요." 수아는 통역, 의역, 요약이라는 세 가지 다른 과제를 동시에 수행해야 한다는 것을 깨닫는다.

  1. You will study a model exchange in which a mediator relays a Korean explanation into English without translating word for word.
  2. You will read a Korean source text alongside two different mediated versions of it — a paraphrase and a summary — and compare what each preserves and what each leaves out.
  3. You will listen to a short passage and produce an oral paraphrase and a written summary of the same content.
  4. You will practice moving the same content between formal and everyday registers without changing its underlying meaning.
  5. You will mediate a short exchange between two parties, relaying content accurately without adding personal commentary.

발음과 억양 — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation and Delivery Focus: Signaling a Mediated Voice

When a speaker relays someone else's words rather than speaking in their own voice, experienced speakers often mark the shift prosodically as well as lexically. This chapter introduces no new phonology; it reviews how a brief pause and a slight shift in pitch or pacing before a mediated phrase such as 다시 말하면 or 요약하자면 helps a listener track when the speaker is paraphrasing, summarizing, or quoting rather than speaking independently.

Pronunciation Note — Marking a shift into mediated speech

A short pause immediately before a framing expression such as 다시 말하면 gives the listener a moment to register that a restatement is coming, rather than new independent content. When relaying another person's words directly, speakers often slightly flatten their own intonation on the relayed portion, reserving fuller pitch movement for their own commentary, so that the listener can distinguish "this is what they said" from "this is what I think about it."

Practice: read the following pair aloud, pausing briefly before 다시 말하면 and keeping your pitch relatively even through the paraphrase, then compare it to reading the two sentences with no pause or pitch adjustment at all.

  • 이 정책은 시행 초기 단계에서 예산 배정의 우선순위를 재조정할 필요가 있다.This policy needs to have its budget-allocation priorities readjusted during the initial implementation stage.
  • 다시 말하면, 지금 이 정책에 돈을 어떻게 쓸지 다시 정해야 한다는 뜻이다.In other words, this means we need to decide again how to spend money on this policy right now.
매개와 번역 — Mediation and Register-Transfer Architecture

Mediation and Translation: Functional Translation, Paraphrase, Register Transfer, and Summary

This chapter's principal system is mediation skill rather than new sentence-level grammar. Every structure used below is already active from earlier volumes and from Chapter 3; what is new is deploying that grammar deliberately to carry the same meaning across languages, wordings, and registers without distortion.

1. Functional translation versus literal translation

A functional translation preserves the meaning and intent of the source for the target audience, even when it departs from the source's exact word order or word choice. A literal, word-for-word rendering can distort meaning when the two languages package information differently, particularly around nominalized Korean noun phrases of the kind studied in Chapter 3.

  • 통근 시간 감소로 인한 피로도 완화가 생산성 향상으로 이어진다. → literal: "The fatigue-degree relief caused by commuting-time decrease leads to productivity improvement." → functional: "Shorter commutes reduce fatigue, which in turn boosts productivity."The literal rendering mirrors the Korean noun phrases and reads awkwardly in English; the functional rendering restates the same causal relationship in natural English clauses.

2. Paraphrase within Korean

Paraphrase restates Korean content in different Korean wording while preserving meaning, using already-active synonyms, connectors, and clause structures. Paraphrase is useful for checking comprehension, avoiding repetition, and adjusting emphasis without changing the underlying claim.

  • 이 정책은 시행 초기 단계에서 예산 배정의 우선순위를 재조정할 필요가 있다. → 다시 말하면, 이 정책을 처음 시행할 때는 어디에 돈을 먼저 쓸지 다시 정해야 한다.This policy needs to have its budget-allocation priorities readjusted during the initial implementation stage. → In other words, when this policy is first implemented, it is necessary to decide again where to spend money first.

3. Register transfer

Register transfer moves the same content between formal written style and everyday spoken style, using the speech-level and connector choices already active from earlier volumes rather than changing the underlying meaning. This differs from Chapter 3's density control: density concerns how packed a sentence is, while register transfer concerns how formal or familiar its wording and endings are.

  • 본 정책의 시행 초기 단계에서는 예산 배정의 우선순위 재조정이 요구된다. → 이 정책을 처음 시작할 때는 돈을 어디에 먼저 쓸지 다시 정해야 해요.In the initial implementation stage of this policy, readjustment of budget-allocation priorities is required. → When we first start this policy, we need to decide again where to spend money first.

4. Summary

Summary condenses a longer source to its essential points, in the mediator's own words, using already-active nominalization and connector patterns. A summary differs from a paraphrase in scope: paraphrase restates a passage at roughly the same length, while summary reduces a longer passage to fewer, more general sentences.

  • [3문단 분량의 정책 설명] → 요약하자면, 이 정책은 예산 우선순위를 재조정하여 생산성을 높이는 것을 목표로 한다.[a three-paragraph policy explanation] → To summarize, this policy aims to increase productivity by readjusting budget priorities.

5. Neutral mediation between two parties

When relaying one party's words to another, a mediator keeps the relayed content faithful to the source's meaning, marks clearly what is being relayed versus the mediator's own addition, and avoids inserting personal opinion into the relayed portion, using already-active quotation and hedging patterns.

  • 팀장님 말씀은, 이 정책의 예산 우선순위를 다시 정해야 한다는 것입니다. 제 개인적인 의견을 덧붙이자면, 저는 그 방향에 동의합니다.What the team leader is saying is that the budget priorities for this policy need to be reset. If I may add my own personal opinion, I agree with that direction.

Grammar Note — Judgment, not new structures

Every connector, ending, and nominalization pattern used in this chapter's examples is already active from earlier volumes, including Chapter 3's compression and expansion patterns. The new skill is deciding, source by source, whether the task calls for translation, paraphrase, register transfer, or summary, and executing that task without adding meaning that was not in the source or omitting meaning that changes its sense.

  4. Write a one-sentence functional English translation of the summary, without translating word for word.

Writing Workshop: Mediating a Short Text

Take the compressed report-style paragraph you produced in Chapter 3's performance task, or a similar short formal Korean paragraph, and mediate it into three additional forms.

    • The English translation is functional rather than literal, and reads naturally in English.
    • The Korean paraphrase uses different wording from the source while preserving the same meaning.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — The mediator's role in Korean professional settings

    In South Korean workplaces with international colleagues, a bilingual staff member is often expected to mediate not only language but also tone, softening or reframing direct statements so that meaning is preserved while the interaction remains appropriately polite for both parties. A mediator who translates literally, including blunt phrasing that would sound harsh once carried across languages, can unintentionally create friction that the original speaker did not intend.

    Regional and Generational Note — Attitudes toward literal versus free rendering

    Some fields, such as legal and technical translation, place a high value on staying close to the source wording, since small differences in phrasing can carry legal or technical weight. Other fields, such as marketing, journalism, and general business communication, more often favor functional, natural-sounding renderings over literal ones. Learners should ask what a specific field or workplace expects rather than assuming one approach is always correct.

    Common Mistakes to Avoid

    Common Mistake — Translating word for word

    Error: Rendering a Korean nominalized noun phrase or a Korean sentence structure directly into English word order, producing an unnatural or confusing English sentence.

    Correction: Identify the underlying meaning and relationship first, then express it in natural English clauses, even if the sentence structure looks quite different from the Korean original.

    Common Mistake — Adding the mediator's own opinion into a relayed message

    Error: Blending a speaker's actual words with the mediator's own commentary or opinion without marking the difference, so the listener cannot tell what was said versus what the mediator thinks.

    Correction: Keep the relayed content clearly separated from personal commentary, using framing such as 팀장님 말씀은 ~ 것입니다 for the relayed content and 제 개인적인 의견을 덧붙이자면 for anything added.

    Common Mistake — Confusing summary with paraphrase

    Error: Producing a "summary" that is nearly as long as the source, restating every detail in different wording rather than reducing the content to its essential points.

    Correction: Before summarizing, identify the one or two claims that must survive; a summary should be noticeably shorter than the source, while a paraphrase may remain close to the source's original length.

    Section D — Sample four-version mediation

    본 정책은 시행 초기 단계에서 예산 배정의 우선순위를 재조정할 필요가 있다.

    Translation: "This policy needs its budget priorities reordered in its initial rollout stage."

    Paraphrase: 이 정책을 처음 시행할 때는 예산을 어디에 먼저 쓸지 다시 정해야 한다.

    Summary: 요약하자면, 이 정책은 예산 순서를 다시 정해야 한다.

    Register-shifted: 이 정책, 처음 시작할 때는 돈 쓰는 순서를 다시 정해야 해요.

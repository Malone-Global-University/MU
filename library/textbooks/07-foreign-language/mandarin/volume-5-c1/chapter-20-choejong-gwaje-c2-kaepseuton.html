Chapter 20 최종 과제: C2 캡스톤
Final C2 Capstone

Chapter Overview

Chapter 19 asked you to build one complete specialized project. This final chapter asks you to bring that project to its fullest form and to demonstrate, in a single sustained performance, the full range of Korean systems developed across Volume V — precise register control, advanced argument structure, concision and elaboration, mediation, specialized public and professional discourse, research and critique, intellectual and literary reading, academic writing, debate and persuasion, professional editing, and multi-audience communication. This chapter introduces no new grammar, particle, morphology, speech-level, honorific, or discourse system. It is the capstone synthesis chapter for Volume V: learners revise, expand, and finalize the Chapter 19 project into a polished C2-oriented portfolio piece and deliver it as a complete spoken and written performance, with a closing reflection on their growth across the five-volume series.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, learners will be able to:

  • revise and finalize a sustained specialized project into a polished, publication-ready written document;
  • integrate multiple sources with consistent, accurate attribution and a complete, correctly formatted reference list;
  • sustain precise register control across an entire multi-part written and spoken performance;
  • deliver an extended, well-organized oral presentation calibrated to a specified audience;
  • respond fluently and appropriately to unscripted questions, including questions that challenge the project's claims;
  • evaluate their own and a peer's performance using explicit, level-appropriate criteria; and
  • reflect, in Korean, on their overall development from the beginning of the series to C2-oriented mastery.

Hangeul, Orthography, and Digital Focus

This chapter's orthographic focus is final-document polish: consistent formatting across the finished written project and its reference list, correct spacing and punctuation across a longer multi-section document, and clean, readable slide or handout formatting for the oral presentation. Digital tasks include a final proofreading pass on the complete document, correcting any residual spacing or punctuation inconsistencies introduced during revision, and preparing a clean, correctly formatted digital or printed version for submission.

3. Closing a spoken performance with a reflective statement

Practice ending an oral defense with a brief, genuine statement of what the project could not fully resolve, followed by a forward-looking statement, rather than an artificially confident close.

Model Dialogue

지은 (학습자): 교수님, 최종본을 제출하기 전에 결론 부분의 논조가 도입부와 다르게 느껴지는데, 확인해 주실 수 있을까요?

한 교수: 한번 볼게요. ...음, 도입부는 신중한 어조인데 결론에서는 확신에 찬 어조로 바뀌었네요. 결론에서도 한계점을 언급한 만큼 어조를 좀 낮추는 게 좋겠어요.

지은: 알겠습니다. 그리고 발표 중에 "이 결과가 다른 나라에도 적용될 수 있나요?"라는 질문을 받으면 어떻게 답해야 할지 고민이에요.

한 교수: 좋은 예상 질문이에요. 국내 자료만 다루었다는 점을 인정하면서도, 유사한 조건이라면 비슷한 경향이 나타날 가능성을 언급하는 정도로 답하면 됩니다.

지은: 네, 침착하게 대응해 보겠습니다. 발표를 마무리할 때는 어떻게 끝내는 게 좋을까요?

Writing Workshop

Task: Produce the finalized version (500–700 words) of your specialized project, incorporating feedback from Chapter 19, and add the following capstone-specific elements:

  1. a revised 도입부, 본론, and 결론 with fully consistent register throughout;
  2. at least two properly attributed sources and a complete, consistently formatted reference list;
  3. a genuine, specific stated limitation, expanded from your Chapter 19 draft based on feedback received;
  4. a short written response (3–4 sentences) to one challenging question you anticipate receiving, using at least one hedging expression;
  5. a closing self-reflection (4–6 sentences) in Korean describing your growth from Volume I through Volume V, naming at least two specific language systems you now control with confidence and one you plan to keep developing.

Success criteria: the document reads as a single coherent, professionally polished piece rather than a patchwork of revisions; register remains consistent from the first sentence to the last; the stated limitation is specific and credible; the response to the anticipated question is composed rather than defensive; the closing reflection is genuine, specific, and appropriately self-aware rather than generic praise or generic criticism of oneself.

Culture and Communication

In many Korean academic and professional settings, a capstone or final defense (구술 발표, 졸업 작품 발표) is expected to include a composed response to direct challenge, a candid acknowledgment of limitations, and a brief, forward-looking closing statement rather than an unqualified claim of success; presenters who acknowledge 미흡한 점 openly are often regarded as more credible, not less. These expectations vary across institutions, disciplines, and eras, and should be treated as common professional and academic norms rather than as fixed rules governing every Korean-language defense or presentation.

Common Mistakes

  • Register drift across a long document: starting with careful, hedged academic register and drifting into overconfident or casual language by the conclusion or during the question round.
  • Defensive responses to challenge: treating a pointed question as an attack rather than a legitimate request for clarification, which can come across as evasive or overly defensive rather than composed.
  • Generic self-reflection: closing with vague praise ("제가 많이 늘었어요") instead of naming specific systems, skills, or moments of genuine growth.
  • Omitting the forward-looking statement: ending only on the limitation, which can leave a presentation feeling unfinished rather than deliberately closed.

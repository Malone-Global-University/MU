公共信息和通知

Volume II, Chapter 13: Public Information and Notices · Speak and Write Mandarin Chinese: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Overview and Learning Objectives

This chapter moves from workplace communication toward the kind of Mandarin you encounter in public spaces: signs, posted notices, building announcements, and short official messages. You will learn to read and interpret these texts, use formal vocabulary connected to rules and permissions, and recognize — but not yet produce — the passive marker 被 as it commonly appears in notices and short news-style headlines. The chapter also strengthens your ability to talk about what a sign or notice tells people to do.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Read and understand common public signs and posted notices.
  2. Explain in your own words what a notice instructs people to do or not do.
  3. Use formal vocabulary related to rules, permissions, and prohibitions.
  4. Recognize 被 in short notices and headlines and explain its basic meaning.
  5. Write a short notice or announcement for a building, class, or community space.

Communication Scenario

Several notices have appeared in your apartment building and at your school. You need to understand what they say, discuss them with a neighbor or classmate, and post a short notice of your own about a schedule change or a building rule.

  1. You will listen to two people discussing a notice posted in their building.
  2. You will read several short public notices and signs.
  3. You will write your own short notice announcing information or a rule.
  4. You will speak with a partner about a notice and what it requires people to do.

发音和读写 — Pronunciation and Literacy Focus

Pronunciation and Literacy Focus

  • Tone pair practice: 禁止 (jìnzhǐ) combines two third-tone syllables; remember that the first syllable rises before the second, so it is pronounced closer to jín zhǐ in connected speech.
  • Formal reading rhythm: notices are read more slowly and evenly than casual conversation. Practice reading each notice in the Reading Practice section aloud with clear phrase breaks after each comma.
  • Typing: practice typing formal vocabulary such as 通知, 规定, and 禁止 through Pinyin input, paying attention to selecting the correct character among similar-sounding IME candidates.

No new handwriting characters are introduced beyond the productive inventory already scheduled through Chapter 12. This chapter's core vocabulary is a recognition and typing target; handwriting remains limited to the previously scheduled inventory.

语法 — Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

1. Reading Public Instructions: 禁止, 请勿, and 允许

Public notices often use compact, formal patterns rather than full conversational sentences.

禁止 + verb — a strong prohibition, common on signs.
禁止停车。
Jìnzhǐ tíngchē.
No parking.

请勿 + verb — a more formal, polite way of saying "please do not."
请勿吸烟。
Qǐng wù xīyān.
Please do not smoke.

不允许 / 不可以 + verb — the conversational equivalent of a prohibition, used when explaining a rule to someone rather than posting it.
这里不允许带宠物。
Zhèlǐ bù yǔnxǔ dài chǒngwù.
Pets are not allowed here.

Notice that 禁止 and 请勿 are formal, written patterns typical of signs, while 不允许 and 不可以 are how you would explain the same rule out loud to a friend.

2. Describing Temporary Situations: 暂停 and 恢复

电梯暂停使用，下星期一恢复正常。
Diàntī zàntíng shǐyòng, xià xīngqīyī huīfù zhèngcháng.
The elevator is temporarily out of service; it will return to normal next Monday.

3. Recognizing 被 in Notices and Headlines (Receptive Only)

You will see 被 in some formal notices and short headline-style sentences. 被 introduces the person or thing that performed an action, when the sentence's main focus is the person or thing affected by the action.

违反规定的人会被罚款。
Wéifǎn guīdìng de rén huì bèi fákuǎn.
People who violate the rules will be fined.

For now, focus on recognizing the pattern subject + 被 + (agent) + verb and understanding its meaning when you read it. You are not yet expected to form new sentences with 被 on your own; this control develops later in the series.

4. Basic Notice Structure

A short public notice typically states who it is for, what has changed or is required, and who to contact with questions.

通知全体居民：本星期六上午电梯暂停使用，用于维修。请大家注意安全，走楼梯。有问题请联系物业。
Tōngzhī quántǐ jūmín: běn xīngqīliù shàngwǔ diàntī zàntíng shǐyòng, yòngyú wéixiū. Qǐng dàjiā zhùyì ānquán, zǒu lóutī. Yǒu wèntí qǐng liánxì wùyè.
Notice to all residents: the elevator will be out of service this Saturday morning for maintenance. Please pay attention to safety and use the stairs. Contact property management with any questions.

对话 — Model Dialogue

Model Dialogue

Context: two neighbors reading a notice posted in the lobby, semi-formal register

王阿姨：你看到楼下的通知了吗？
小刘：看到了。电梯这星期六要维修，暂停使用。
王阿姨：那我们星期六得走楼梯了。
小刘：对，通知上说，请大家注意安全。
王阿姨：星期天能恢复吗？
小刘：应该可以。通知说星期一早上恢复正常。
王阿姨：好，那我星期六尽量少出门。
小刘：有问题的话，通知上说可以联系物业。

Aunt Wang: Did you see the notice downstairs? Xiao Liu: Yes. The elevator is going to be repaired this Saturday and will be out of service. Aunt Wang: Then we'll have to use the stairs on Saturday. Xiao Liu: Right, the notice says to pay attention to safety. Aunt Wang: Will it be back by Sunday? Xiao Liu: It should be. The notice says it will return to normal Monday morning. Aunt Wang: Okay, then I'll try to go out as little as possible on Saturday. Xiao Liu: If there's a problem, the notice says you can contact property management.

写作练习 — Writing Workshop

Writing Workshop

Write a short public notice (five to eight sentences) for your building, school, or class, announcing information or a rule.

写作任务 — Writing task: Type your notice with Pinyin input. Your notice must include:

  1. A clear audience statement, such as 通知全体....
  2. A description of what has changed or is required, using 暂停, 恢复, 规定, or 禁止/请勿.
  3. A safety or courtesy reminder, using 注意 or a related phrase.
  4. Contact information or next steps for readers with questions.
  5. Consistent, formal notice register throughout, avoiding casual conversational phrases.

修改清单 — Editing checklist:

  • 禁止/请勿 and 不允许 are not mixed within the same notice inconsistently.
  • 暂停 and 恢复 are used correctly to show a temporary change and its end.
  • The notice states its audience and purpose within the first two sentences.
  • Chinese punctuation (，。？！：) is used correctly throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture Note — Public notices and shared spaces

In many Mandarin-speaking residential and institutional settings, printed notices posted in lobbies, elevators, and stairwells are a common way of communicating with residents, students, or staff about schedule changes, maintenance, and rules. These notices tend to use a formal, impersonal register — third-person statements, formal vocabulary, and compact instructions — rather than the conversational tone used when explaining the same information to a friend. This pattern is common but not universal, and the exact wording and level of formality can vary by building, institution, and region.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using 禁止 in casual spoken explanation: 禁止 and 请勿 belong on signs and formal notices; when explaining a rule out loud, 不允许 or 不可以 sounds more natural.
  • Confusing 暂停 with 停止: 暂停 specifically implies a temporary pause with an expected return to normal, unlike a general stop.
  • Trying to produce new 被 sentences at this stage: focus on recognizing and understanding 被 in notices; productive control of 被 sentences is developed later in the series.
  • Omitting the audience or contact information in a written notice: a notice without a clear audience statement or a way to ask questions can confuse readers.

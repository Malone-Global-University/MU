工作、责任和邮件

Volume II, Chapter 12: Work, Responsibilities, and Email · Speak and Write Mandarin Chinese: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Overview and Learning Objectives

This chapter continues Unit III by moving from learning methods toward work, responsibilities, and workplace-adjacent written communication. You will talk about tasks you are responsible for, describe a typical day at work or in a part-time job, and write your first short formal email in Chinese. The chapter introduces 应该, 得, and 必须 — three related but distinct ways of expressing obligation — and teaches the basic structure of a polite, task-oriented email.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Distinguish 应该 (should), 得 (have to, in speech), and 必须 (must) when describing obligations.
  2. Describe your responsibilities at work, school, or home.
  3. Talk about a typical workday, including tasks, schedule, and coworkers.
  4. Write a short formal email requesting information or reporting a problem.
  5. Use appropriate opening and closing phrases in a formal written message.

Communication Scenario

You have a part-time job or internship and need to communicate with a supervisor about your schedule and responsibilities. You will discuss what you are responsible for, compare it with a coworker's experience, and write a short formal email asking about next week's schedule.

  1. You will listen to a short conversation between two coworkers discussing their responsibilities.
  2. You will read a short formal email between an employee and a supervisor.
  3. You will write your own short formal email asking about a schedule or reporting a small problem.
  4. You will speak with a partner about your responsibilities at work, school, or home.

发音和读写 — Pronunciation and Literacy Focus

Pronunciation and Literacy Focus

  • Tone pair practice: 责任 (zérèn) moves from second tone to falling tone — keep the second syllable from flattening out.
  • Neutral tone review: the second syllable of 麻烦 is light and short; contrast it with the fuller tone of 安排, where both syllables carry full tones.
  • Typing: practice typing longer strings through Pinyin input for full sentences, including selecting the correct characters when the IME offers several candidates for common syllables like an and ren.

No new handwriting characters are introduced beyond the productive inventory already scheduled through Chapter 11.

语法 — Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

1. Expressing Obligation: 应该, 得, and 必须

All three words can be translated with English "should" or "must," but they differ in strength and in how they are used.

应该 — what is generally advisable or expected; a recommendation or moral/social expectation.
你应该跟经理谈谈这个问题。
Nǐ yīnggāi gēn jīnglǐ tántan zhège wèntí.
You should talk to your manager about this problem.

得 (děi) — a personal necessity, common in speech; something you have to do because of the situation.
我今天得加班。
Wǒ jīntiān děi jiābān.
I have to work overtime today.

必须 — a strong, often official or rule-based obligation; stronger and more formal than 得.
员工必须准时上班。
Yuángōng bìxū zhǔnshí shàngbān.
Employees must arrive at work on time.

A useful test: if you are giving advice or describing what is generally expected, use 应该. If you are describing a personal necessity in casual speech, use 得. If you are stating a firm rule or official requirement, use 必须. In everyday conversation, 应该 and 得 overlap somewhat, but 必须 always signals something stronger and less optional.

2. Describing Responsibilities

Use 负责 (to be responsible for) or 的责任是 (the responsibility is) to describe what someone is in charge of.

我负责回复客户的邮件。
Wǒ fùzé huífù kèhù de yóujiàn.
I'm responsible for replying to customer emails.

3. Talking About a Typical Workday

Combine time expressions (from earlier chapters) with work vocabulary to describe a sequence of daily tasks.

我每天九点上班，先看邮件，然后开会，下午完成经理安排的任务。
Wǒ měitiān jiǔ diǎn shàngbān, xiān kàn yóujiàn, ránhòu kāihuì, xiàwǔ wánchéng jīnglǐ ānpái de rènwu.
I start work at nine every day. First I check email, then I have a meeting, and in the afternoon I complete the tasks my manager has assigned.

4. Basic Formal Email Structure

A short formal email in Chinese typically follows this pattern: a formal greeting, a brief statement of purpose, the specific request or information, a polite closing request, and a formal sign-off.

您好，
我想请问一下下星期的工作安排。麻烦您尽快回复我，谢谢！
Nín hǎo, wǒ xiǎng qǐngwèn yíxià xià xīngqī de gōngzuò ānpái. Máfan nín jǐnkuài huífù wǒ, xièxie!
Hello, I'd like to ask about next week's work schedule. Sorry to trouble you, but could you reply as soon as possible? Thank you!

对话 — Model Dialogue

Model Dialogue

Context: two coworkers discussing responsibilities before the end of the workday, semi-formal register

陈明：你今天的任务都完成了吗？
丽丽：差不多了。我负责回复客户的邮件，今天回复了十几封。你呢？
陈明：我得先开会，然后才能开始做经理安排的报告。
丽丽：你应该早一点告诉经理，这个报告得花很多时间。
陈明：对，我打算写一封邮件问问她，能不能晚一天交。
丽丽：好主意。别忘了邮件要写得客气一点。
陈明：我知道，公司的员工必须注意邮件的礼貌。
丽丽：没错，这一点很重要。

Chen Ming: Have you finished all your tasks today? Lili: Almost. I'm responsible for replying to customer emails — I replied to a dozen or so today. What about you? Chen Ming: I have to go to a meeting first, and only then can I start the report my manager assigned. Lili: You should tell your manager earlier — this report is going to take a lot of time. Chen Ming: Right, I'm planning to write her an email asking whether I can turn it in a day late. Lili: Good idea. Don't forget the email needs to be written politely. Chen Ming: I know — employees at this company must pay attention to politeness in email. Lili: That's right, that point is important.

Speaking Practice

写作练习 — Writing Workshop

Writing Workshop

Write a short formal email (five to eight sentences) to a supervisor, teacher, or landlord, either asking for information or reporting a small problem.

写作任务 — Writing task: Type your email with Pinyin input. Your email must include:

  1. A formal greeting, such as 您好.
  2. A clear statement of purpose, using 请问 or 我想.
  3. A description of a responsibility or task, using 负责 or 任务.
  4. At least one sentence with obligation, using 应该, 得, or 必须.
  5. A polite closing request, using 麻烦您 and 谢谢.

修改清单 — Editing checklist:

  • 应该, 得, and 必须 are each used correctly and not interchanged.
  • The greeting and closing match a formal, respectful register.
  • The email states its purpose clearly within the first two sentences.
  • Chinese punctuation (，。？！) is used correctly throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture Note — Politeness in workplace email

In many Mandarin-speaking workplace and academic contexts, a short formal email tends to open with a greeting and a brief statement of purpose before making a request, rather than beginning directly with the request itself. Phrases like 麻烦您 ("sorry to trouble you") and 谢谢 at the end are common softening devices. This pattern is common but not universal — expectations vary by workplace, generation, and individual relationship, so it is worth observing how colleagues and supervisors write before assuming a fixed rule.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Treating 必须 as a casual "should": 必须 signals a strong, often rule-based obligation, not a mild suggestion — use 应该 for suggestions.
  • Using 得 in very formal writing: 得 is common in speech; formal written requests more often use 应该 or 需要.
  • Starting a formal email directly with a request: a brief greeting and purpose statement usually comes first.
  • Omitting a polite closing: a bare request without 谢谢 or a closing phrase can sound abrupt in a formal email.

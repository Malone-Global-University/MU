我病了

Volume I, Chapter 14: Health and Changes of State · Speak and Write Mandarin Chinese: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Overview and Learning Objectives

Last chapter, you described what you and other people are doing right now. This chapter turns to a related but different idea: describing a change — from well to sick, from not tired to tired, from not having something to having it. You will learn 了 in its second major function, marking a change of state, and you will practice two ways to give advice or instructions: 应该/要 for what someone should do, and 别/不要 for what someone should not do. Together, these systems let you describe how you feel, explain what changed, and give or receive simple health-related advice.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use change-of-state 了 to say that a new situation has begun.
  2. Describe common symptoms and health problems using controlled vocabulary.
  3. Use 应该 and 要 to say what someone should do.
  4. Use 别 and 不要 to say what someone should not do.
  5. Write and respond to a short message about feeling sick.
  6. Combine health language with the ongoing-action, description, accompaniment, and reason-result systems from Chapter 13 and earlier chapters.

Communication Scenario

安娜 wakes up not feeling well the morning after her tea date with 大卫. She sends him a message explaining what happened, and he writes back with some simple advice.

  1. You will listen to and read short exchanges about getting sick and giving health advice.
  2. You will learn to describe a change in your condition and to tell someone what they should or should not do.
  3. Everything in this chapter builds directly on the ongoing-action, expanded 的, accompaniment, and reason-result language from Chapter 13.

发音 — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation and Listening Focus

This chapter continues tone-pair practice and adds a few sounds that are easy for English-speaking learners to flatten.

  • 疼 (téng) vs 头 (tóu): both begin with similar-sounding consonants but have different finals. Practice keeping the -eng and -ou finals clearly distinct.
  • 应该 (yīnggāi): first tone followed by first tone. Keep both syllables level and steady rather than letting the second syllable drift downward.
  • 咳嗽 (késou): the second syllable is neutral tone. Keep it short and light, the way you practiced with 朋友 and 东西 in earlier chapters.
  • 别 (bié) vs 病 (bìng): these two words are easy to confuse for beginners because both involve the sound "b" plus a high front element. Practice the full rising glide of bié against the falling tone and nasal ending of bìng.

Practice the model dialogue below aloud at least three times, paying special attention to keeping change-of-state 了 light and unstressed at the end of the sentence.

语法 — Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Change-of-state 了: a new situation has begun

At the end of a sentence, 了 can signal that a situation has changed — something that was not true before is true now. This is a different function from the completed-action 了 you will study directly after a verb in a later chapter. Here, 了 appears at the very end of the sentence and marks the whole situation as new.

Pattern: Subject + (Description/Verb Phrase) + 了

我病了。 Wǒ bìng le. I've gotten sick. (I was fine; now I'm sick.)

我感冒了。 Wǒ gǎnmào le. I've caught a cold.

我发烧了。 Wǒ fāshāo le. I've developed a fever.

Compare this with a plain statement of fact without 了: 我很忙 simply describes a current state ("I am busy"), while 我很忙了 would suggest a change — "I've become busy" (when I wasn't before). In this chapter, focus on using 了 with health words to describe a new symptom or condition, not on every possible use of change-of-state 了.

应该 and 要: saying what someone should do

应该 expresses what someone should do — a recommendation or expectation. 要 can express a similar idea, often with a slightly stronger sense of need. Both come directly before the main verb, in the same position as the modal verbs you learned in Chapter 11.

Pattern: Subject + 应该/要 + Verb Phrase

你应该多休息。 Nǐ yīnggāi duō xiūxi. You should rest more.

你要去看医生。 Nǐ yào qù kàn yīshēng. You need to go see a doctor.

医生说我应该多喝水。 Yīshēng shuō wǒ yīnggāi duō hē shuǐ. The doctor said I should drink more water.

In this chapter, use 应该 and 要 mainly for health-related advice. Their fuller range of meanings, including 要 as future intention, is developed further in later chapters.

别 and 不要: saying what someone should not do

别 and 不要 both tell someone not to do something. 别 is common in everyday spoken advice; 不要 works the same way and is slightly more explicit or emphatic in many contexts.

Pattern: 别/不要 + Verb Phrase

别去上课了。 Bié qù shàngkè le. Don't go to class.

你不要吃冷的东西。 Nǐ búyào chī lěng de dōngxi. Don't eat cold things.

别担心，你休息一下就好了。 Bié dānxīn, nǐ xiūxi yíxià jiù hǎo le. Don't worry, you'll be fine after you rest a bit.

Use 别 and 不要 as simple advice-giving expressions for now. Do not combine them yet with the more advanced connectors from later chapters, such as 虽然...但是....

对话 — Model Dialogue

Model Dialogue

我病了 — I've Gotten Sick:
大卫：安娜，你今天怎么样？
安娜：我不太舒服，我病了，头很疼，也有点儿发烧。
大卫：是感冒吗？你应该去看医生。
安娜：对，我今天要请假，也要去医院。
大卫：好，你去医院以后应该多休息，也要多喝水。
安娜：好，我知道了。谢谢你！
大卫：别担心，你休息一下就好了。今天别工作了！
安娜：好，我今天不工作了，我在家休息。

Gloss: David asks Anna how she's doing today. She says she doesn't feel well — she's gotten sick, has a bad headache, and has a bit of a fever. David asks if it's a cold and says she should go see a doctor. Anna agrees, saying she needs to take the day off and go to the hospital. David tells her that after the hospital she should rest a lot and drink a lot of water. Anna thanks him. David tells her not to worry — she'll be fine after resting, and tells her not to work today. Anna agrees that she won't work today and will rest at home.

Reading Practice

Read the short message below and answer the comprehension questions.

安娜，你好吗？你今天没有来上课，老师说你病了。你应该多休息，多喝水，别太累了。如果你还是不舒服，你要去医院看医生，别一个人在家等。我明天可以跟你一起去医院，你不要担心。
Hi Anna, how are you? You didn't come to class today — the teacher said you're sick. You should rest more, drink more water, and not tire yourself out too much. If you're still not feeling well, you need to go to the hospital to see a doctor — don't just wait at home alone. I can go to the hospital together with you tomorrow, so don't worry.

理解问题 — Comprehension questions:

  1. 安娜今天为什么没有来上课？ (Why didn't Anna come to class today?)
  2. 朋友说她应该做什么？不应该做什么？ (What does the friend say she should and shouldn't do?)
  3. 如果安娜还是不舒服，她应该做什么？ (If Anna is still not feeling well, what should she do?)

Writing Workshop

Write a short message of five to seven sentences responding to a friend who tells you they are sick, using only vocabulary and grammar from Chapters 1-14.

写作任务 — Writing task: Type your sentences with Pinyin input if possible, or copy/handwrite the characters you have already been assigned for handwriting. Your message must include:

  1. A change-of-state statement. Use 了 to describe a new condition, such as 我病了 or 我感冒了.
  2. At least one piece of advice with 应该 or 要. Tell the person what they should do.
  3. At least one instruction with 别 or 不要. Tell the person what they should not do.
  4. An expanded 的 phrase or an accompaniment statement, recycled from Chapter 13.
  5. At least one word from the health vocabulary, such as 医生, 休息, 发烧, or 舒服.

修改清单 — Editing checklist:

  • Change-of-state 了 appears at the end of the sentence, not directly after the verb.
  • 应该/要 and 别/不要 come directly before the verb phrase.
  • The message reads as natural, caring advice rather than a list of disconnected commands.
  • A Chinese period (。) or question mark (？) ends each sentence.

Culture and Communication

Culture Note — Talking about illness

In many Mandarin-speaking contexts, friends, classmates, and coworkers commonly offer direct, practical health advice — such as telling someone to rest, drink more water, or avoid cold food and drinks — as an ordinary expression of care rather than as overstepping. Direct advice-giving of this kind is common but not universal, and comfort with receiving unsolicited advice varies by relationship, generation, and individual preference. As with earlier culture notes, treat these patterns as common tendencies rather than fixed rules.

Common Mistakes

  • Placing change-of-state 了 in the wrong position: avoid *我了病. Change-of-state 了 goes at the end of the sentence: 我病了。
  • Treating 应该 and 要 as always interchangeable: 应该 usually expresses a recommendation, while 要 often carries a stronger sense of necessity. Both are acceptable for simple health advice in this chapter, but do not assume they always mean exactly the same thing.
  • Putting 别/不要 after the verb: avoid *你吃别冷的东西. The negative instruction comes first: 你别吃冷的东西。
  • Confusing 疼 (hurt) with 病 (sick): 疼 describes pain in a specific part of the body (头疼, 肚子疼), while 病 describes being generally ill. Do not use them interchangeably.

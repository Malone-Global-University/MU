我是学生

Volume I, Chapter 2: People and Classroom Identity · Speak and Write Mandarin Chinese: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Point out and identify people and things using 这 and 那.
  2. Make a statement negative using 不是, and answer a 吗 question in the negative.
  3. Ask "what" with 什么 and "which" with 哪.
  4. Identify classroom people and objects with a small set of new nouns.
  5. Continue producing the four tones and neutral tone accurately on new and familiar syllables.
  6. Recognize your first character components, including the person component 亻 and the woman component 女, and begin basic stroke-direction principles.
  7. Complete a short classroom identification exchange, in speech and in writing.

Communication Scenario

It is still the first night of Sarah's Mandarin class, but now the room has filled up. 王老师 walks around, pointing at people and things — a classmate here, a book there — and asking simple questions. Sarah needs to be able to point things out herself, say what something is or is not, and ask a classmate a quick "what is that?" or "which one?"

  1. This chapter gives you the language for pointing things out, saying what something is not, and asking basic identification questions.
  2. It also gives you your first character components and stroke-direction principles, so the characters you met in Chapter 1 start to make more visual sense.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 2 builds directly on the greetings, pronouns, and 是 sentences from Chapter 1. It adds no new tense or agreement system — Mandarin still does not require either — but it does add your first demonstrative pronouns, your first negation pattern, your first two question words, and your first look at how characters are built from smaller components.

Coming Into This Chapter

Greetings, 我是.../我叫..., the pronouns 你/我/他/她, 也, and 吗 questions from Chapter 1.

New in This Chapter

这/那 as demonstratives; 不是 for negation; 什么 for "what"; 哪 for "which"; a first set of classroom nouns; and a first look at semantic components and stroke direction.

Looking Forward

Tones, Characters, and Stroke Direction

2.1 Tone review and a new tone pair

This chapter's key new tone pattern is 不是 (bú shì). 不 is normally fourth tone (bù), but it changes to second tone (bú) whenever it comes directly before another fourth-tone syllable. Since 是 is fourth tone, 不 shifts here: bú shì, not bù shì.

Pronunciation Focus — 不 Sandhi

不 is fourth tone (bù) on its own or before a first-, second-, or third-tone syllable. Before a fourth-tone syllable, it changes to second tone (bú). Compare: 不来 (bù lái, not coming — 来 is second tone, so 不 stays bù) versus 不是 (bú shì, not to be — 是 is fourth tone, so 不 becomes bú). This is a real, predictable sound pattern, not a spelling inconsistency, and you will see it again with other fourth-tone words later in the volume.

2.3 First character components

Many Mandarin characters are built from smaller, reusable pieces called components. Some components give a clue about meaning (semantic components); others give a clue about sound (phonetic components). Neither kind of clue works every time, so treat components as a memory aid, not a guarantee.

Language Note — 亻 and 女 as semantic components

You already met this pair in Chapter 1: 他 (he) is built from the person component 亻 on the left, while 她 (she) is built from the woman component 女 on the left. Both characters share the same right-hand part and the same pronunciation (tā); the left-hand component is what distinguishes them in writing. This is a useful pattern to notice, but not every character with 亻 refers to a specific person, and not every character with 女 refers only to women — treat these as clues, not rules.

2.4 Stroke direction basics

Simplified characters are written with a consistent internal order, not drawn freely. Two basic principles apply to nearly every character you will meet:

  • Top before bottom: strokes at the top of a character are written before strokes below them.
  • Left before right: in characters built side by side, like 你, 他, and 她, the left-hand part is written before the right-hand part.

You are not expected to handwrite every character in this chapter. Focus your handwriting practice on the characters marked for handwriting in the Writing Workshop below, and use these two principles as your starting checklist.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

2.5 这 and 那 for pointing things out

Purpose: identify a person or thing as near ("this") or away ("that").

这 and 那 function like English "this" and "that" at the front of a sentence, followed by 是 and then the noun that names the person or thing.

Language Focus — 这是.../那是...

The basic pattern is demonstrative + 是 + noun: 这是[名词]。 or 那是[名词]。 As with all Mandarin verbs, 是 does not change form depending on what comes before it.

  • 这是我的同学。 Zhè shì wǒ de tóngxué. This is my classmate.
  • 那是老师的书。 Nà shì lǎoshī de shū. That is the teacher's book.

The particle 的 appears above only inside these two fixed phrases so that the examples sound natural. Full instruction on 的 begins in Chapter 3 — treat 我的 and 老师的 here as formulaic units, not as a system to analyze yet.

2.6 不是 for negation

Purpose: say that something is not the case.

To negate 是, place 不 directly in front of it. Remember the tone shift from section 2.1: 不是 is pronounced bú shì.

  • 这不是我的书。 Zhè bú shì wǒ de shū. This is not my book.
  • 她不是老师，她是学生。 Tā bú shì lǎoshī, tā shì xuésheng. She is not a teacher; she is a student.
Language Building Block — Answering 吗 questions in the negative
不，我不是学生。
To answer a 吗 question negatively, start with 不 and then restate the sentence with 不是. Compare with the affirmative answer pattern from Chapter 1, which starts with 是.
你是老师吗？不，我不是老师，我是学生。
Are you a teacher? No, I am not a teacher, I am a student.

2.7 什么 for "what"

Purpose: ask what something is.

什么 takes the exact place in the sentence where its answer would go — it does not move to the front of the sentence the way an English question word does.

  • 这是什么？ Zhè shì shénme? What is this?
  • 这是书。 Zhè shì shū. This is a book.
Grammar Note — Question words stay in place

In 这是什么？, 什么 sits exactly where 书 sits in the answer. This "question word in situ" pattern is a core feature of Mandarin question formation, and you will see it again with other question words in later chapters.

2.8 哪 for "which"

Purpose: ask which one, out of a set, someone means.

At this stage, use 哪 only in the fixed phrase 哪个 (which one), placed where the answer would go, just like 什么.

  • 哪个是你的书？ Nǎge shì nǐ de shū? Which one is your book?
  • 这个是我的书。 Zhège shì wǒ de shū. This one is my book.

个 is a measure word. You are only asked to use it here inside the fixed phrases 哪个 and 这个/那个; the full measure-word system develops starting in Chapter 3.

Model Dialogue

Setting: 王老师 walks around the classroom, and Sarah talks with a new classmate, 大卫.

王老师 这是什么？ Zhè shì shénme? What is this?
莎拉 这是我的书。 Zhè shì wǒ de shū. This is my book.
王老师 那也是你的书吗？ Nà yě shì nǐ de shū ma? Is that also your book?
莎拉 不，那不是我的书，那是大卫的本子。 Bù, nà bú shì wǒ de shū, nà shì Dàwèi de běnzi. No, that is not my book, that is David's notebook.
大卫 对，那是我的本子。这是我的同学，她叫莎拉。 Duì, nà shì wǒ de běnzi. Zhè shì wǒ de tóngxué, tā jiào Shālā. That's right, that is my notebook. This is my classmate; she is called Sarah.
王老师 哪个是你的笔？ Nǎge shì nǐ de bǐ? Which one is your pen?
大卫 这个是我的笔。 Zhège shì wǒ de bǐ. This one is my pen.

理解问题 — Comprehension questions:

  1. 那是谁的本子？ (Whose notebook is that?)
  2. 莎拉是大卫的什么？ (What is Sarah to David?)
  3. How does Sarah say "no" and correct the teacher's guess?

Writing Workshop

Create a short set of classroom labels, following the models above. Your labels should use 这/那, at least one 不是 sentence, and one 什么 or 哪个 question with its answer.

写作任务 — Writing task: Write three or four short sentences in simplified characters, typing them with Pinyin input if possible, or copying the characters carefully by hand. Handwrite 这, 那, and 不 at least three times each, following the top-before-bottom and left-before-right principles from section 2.4.

  1. Point something out. Start with 这是...。 or 那是...。
  2. Add a negative sentence. Use 这不是...。 or 那不是...。
  3. Ask a question. Use 这是什么？ or 哪个是...？
  4. Check your characters. Compare each simplified character you wrote against the character chart above, and check stroke direction on 这, 那, and 不.

修改清单 — Editing checklist:

  • The labels use 这 and 那 accurately for near and far.
  • At least one sentence uses 不是 correctly.
  • At least one question uses 什么 or 哪个 in its correct in-place position.
  • Handwritten characters follow top-before-bottom and left-before-right order.
  • A Chinese period (。) or question mark (？) ends each sentence.

Speaking Practice

控制性练习 — Controlled practice: reading the model dialogue aloud

Read the model dialogue above aloud twice: once slowly, sounding out each tone, and once at a more natural pace. Pay close attention to the 不 sandhi in 不是, and to keeping 什么 and 哪个 in their normal statement position rather than moving them to the front.

交际任务 — Communicative task: classroom object identification

With a partner or small group, point to real or pictured classroom objects and take turns asking 这是什么？ or 哪个是你的...？, answering with 这是..., 那是..., or a 不是 correction when the first guess is wrong.

独立练习 — Solo alternative

Record yourself naming five objects near you in Mandarin, first with 这是... and then correcting yourself with a 不是 sentence for one of them. Listen back and check the tone shift in 不是.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — 同学 as a classroom address term

同学 is commonly used both to mean "classmate" and, directly, to address a fellow student whose name you may not yet know, similar to saying "excuse me, classmate" in English. Practices for addressing classmates vary by setting, age group, and region, so treat this as one common pattern rather than a fixed rule for every classroom.

Culture in Context — Correcting politely with 不对/不是

Short correction responses such as 不对 or 不是 are common and direct in classroom and everyday settings, but tone of voice and context still matter for politeness. As you progress through the volume, you will learn additional softening strategies for disagreeing or correcting someone gently.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Forgetting the 不 tone shift

Error: Pronouncing 不是 as bù shì, using the citation-form fourth tone for 不.

Correction: Before a fourth-tone syllable like 是, 不 changes to second tone: bú shì. Review the Pronunciation Focus box in section 2.1.

Common Mistake — Moving 什么 to the front of the sentence

Error: Saying *什么这是？, moving the question word to the front by analogy with English "What is this?"

Correction: Keep 什么 exactly where the answer word would go: 这是什么？, matching the position of 书 in 这是书。

Common Mistake — Confusing 他 and 她 in writing

Error: Writing 他 for "she" or 她 for "he," since the two characters sound identical.

Correction: Check the left-hand component: 亻 (person) signals 他 (he); 女 (woman) signals 她 (she). The components are your visual guide, since pronunciation cannot distinguish them.

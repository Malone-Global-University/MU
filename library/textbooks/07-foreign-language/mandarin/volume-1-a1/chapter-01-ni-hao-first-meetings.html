你好！第一次见面

Volume I, Chapter 1: First Meetings · Speak and Write Mandarin Chinese: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Greet someone and respond to a greeting in Mandarin.
  2. Introduce yourself using 是 and identify others using 你, 我, 他, and 她.
  3. Ask a yes/no question with 吗 and answer it.
  4. Use 也 to add information about yourself or someone else.
  5. Produce the four lexical tones and the neutral tone on familiar syllables with reasonable accuracy.
  6. Recognize, read, and begin to type or copy a first set of high-frequency simplified characters.
  7. Complete a short, supported greeting and self-introduction exchange, in speech and in writing.

Communication Scenario

Sarah has just arrived for the first meeting of her evening Mandarin class. The instructor, 王老师 (Teacher Wang), greets every new student at the door, asks a simple question, and helps each person say a short sentence about themselves. Everyone in the room is meeting everyone else for the first time, so the whole evening runs on short, repeated exchanges: a greeting, a name, a simple "yes," and a seat.

  1. This chapter gives you the exact language Sarah and her classmates use on that first night: greetings, basic self-introduction, and a first yes/no question.
  2. It also gives you your very first look at Mandarin sounds, tones, Pinyin, and simplified characters — the systems you will build on throughout the whole series.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 1 opens Volume I and Unit I. It introduces no full tense system, no case marking, and no verb conjugation — Mandarin does not use these — but it does introduce your very first tones, your first Pinyin spellings, and your first simplified characters. This chapter gives you a small number of complete, usable sentence frames, so that Chapters 2 through 4 can build classroom identity, family, and time expressions on a stable first step.

Coming Into This Chapter

No prior Mandarin knowledge is assumed. This is the very first chapter of the very first volume.

New in This Chapter

你好 and other greeting formulas; 我是... for giving your name; 你/我/他/她 as basic pronouns; 吗 for yes/no questions; 也 for "also"; the four lexical tones and neutral tone; and a first set of about twenty high-frequency simplified characters.

Looking Forward

Pinyin, Tones, and First Characters

Mandarin is a tonal language: the pitch contour of a syllable is part of its meaning, not decoration added on top of it. Hanyu Pinyin is the romanization system used throughout this course to show pronunciation. Pinyin is a support tool for sound, typing, and dictionary lookup — it is not Chinese writing, and it will be used less and less as your character literacy grows.

1.1 The four tones and the neutral tone

Every Mandarin syllable (except neutral-tone syllables) carries one of four tones. Practice these on the syllable ma, said aloud slowly and clearly.

māfirst tone: high, level
másecond tone: rising
mǎthird tone: low/dipping
màfourth tone: falling
maneutral: short, light

The third tone is often taught as a "dipping" contour in isolation, but in connected speech it frequently behaves as a low tone without the full dip. You will meet this pattern again as tone sandhi develops across the volume.

1.2 Tone pairs in this chapter's vocabulary

Notice how tones combine in words you already know: 你好 (nǐ hǎo) is third tone plus third tone, which is commonly pronounced with the first syllable shifting toward a rising contour — written the same in Pinyin, but sounding slightly different in connected speech. 谢谢 (xièxie) is fourth tone followed by a neutral-tone repetition of the same syllable.

Pronunciation Focus — Third-Tone Sandhi in 你好

When two third-tone syllables occur next to each other, as in 你好 (nǐ hǎo), the first syllable is commonly pronounced closer to a second tone (rising), while the second syllable keeps its low or dipping quality: it often sounds closer to ní hǎo. The Pinyin spelling itself does not change to reflect this — you will continue to see nǐ hǎo in writing. This is a real, systematic pattern in Mandarin, not a casual shortcut, and it will be developed further in later chapters.

1.3 First simplified characters

Characters are the primary script of this course. Each character below is a word or part of a word you already met in the vocabulary list. For now, focus on recognizing the shape and connecting it to its sound and meaning; you are not expected to handwrite every character shown here.

你nǐ — you

他 and 她 are pronounced identically (tā) but written differently: 他 uses the person component 亻 on the left, while 她 uses the woman component 女 on the left. This is your first small look at how semantic components can distinguish characters that sound the same — a pattern you will meet again, not a rule that always predicts meaning.

1.4 Listening for greetings and tones

Listen for the overall shape of a greeting exchange before you try to isolate every tone. Mandarin questions formed with 吗 generally rise gently at the very end of the sentence, similar to many yes/no questions in English, even though the individual syllables keep their own lexical tones.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

1.5 Greeting formulas

Purpose: greet someone and take leave.

你好 is a safe, all-purpose greeting usable with almost anyone you are meeting for the first time, at any time of day. 再见 is used when parting.

  • 你好！ Nǐ hǎo! Hello!
  • 你好，再见。 Nǐ hǎo, zàijiàn. Hello, goodbye.

1.6 我是... for giving your name or identity

Purpose: say who you are, using basic SVO word order.

是 functions as an identifying or equational verb: it links a subject to a noun that names, defines, or identifies it. Mandarin verbs do not change form for person, number, or tense, so 是 stays exactly the same whether the subject is 我, 你, 他, or 她.

Language Focus — 我是...

The basic pattern is subject + 是 + noun: 我是[名字]。 This is Mandarin's ordinary subject-verb-object order, and it will recur throughout the course. 是 does not change form for different subjects — there is no separate word for "am," "is," or "are."

  • 我是学生。 Wǒ shì xuésheng. I am a student.
  • 她是王老师。 Tā shì Wáng lǎoshī. She is Teacher Wang.

1.7 我叫... as an alternative way to give your name

Purpose: give your name using the verb 叫.

Language Building Block — 我叫...
我叫[名字]。
Treat this as a second complete frame for giving your name, alongside 我是.... 叫 literally means "to be called" or "to call," so 我叫莎拉 literally means "I am called Sarah." Both frames are natural; you will meet 叫 again when asking for someone else's name.
请问，你叫什么名字？
May I ask, what is your name? (formulaic at this stage — treat the whole question as one fixed unit; 什么 is analyzed further in Chapter 2.)

1.8 Pronouns: 你/我/他/她

Purpose: refer to yourself and others without gender or number marking on the verb.

Grammar Note — No verb agreement with pronouns

Unlike English, Mandarin verbs never change form to agree with the subject. 我是, 你是, and 他是/她是 all use the identical verb form 是. This is true for every verb in Mandarin, not just 是 — there is no conjugation to memorize.

他 and 她 sound identical in speech; only the written character shows the distinction. In this chapter, focus on using both pronouns accurately in writing and understanding both from context in speech.

1.9 也 for "also"

Purpose: add matching information about another person.

也 is an adverb and is placed directly before the verb, following the general Mandarin pattern of adverbs preceding the verb they modify.

  • 我是学生，他也是学生。 Wǒ shì xuésheng, tā yě shì xuésheng. I am a student; he is also a student.

1.10 吗 questions

Purpose: ask a simple yes/no question by adding one word to a statement.

To turn any statement into a yes/no question, add 吗 to the end. The word order of the statement itself never changes — this is simpler than English, which often requires reordering words or adding "do/does."

  • 你是学生吗？ Nǐ shì xuésheng ma? Are you a student?
  • 是，我是学生。 Shì, wǒ shì xuésheng. Yes, I am a student.

A full system of question types — including 呢, question words, and A-not-A questions — is introduced gradually starting in Chapter 2. For now, 吗 is your only productive question tool.

Model Dialogue

Setting: 王老师 greets Sarah at the door on the first night of the community Mandarin class.

王老师 你好！ Nǐ hǎo! Hello!
莎拉 你好！ Nǐ hǎo! Hello!
王老师 请问，你叫什么名字？ Qǐngwèn, nǐ jiào shénme míngzi? May I ask, what is your name?
莎拉 我叫莎拉。你是王老师吗？ Wǒ jiào Shālā. Nǐ shì Wáng lǎoshī ma? I'm called Sarah. Are you Teacher Wang?
王老师 是，我是王老师。你是学生吗？ Shì, wǒ shì Wáng lǎoshī. Nǐ shì xuésheng ma? Yes, I am Teacher Wang. Are you a student?
莎拉 是，我是学生。他也是学生吗？ Shì, wǒ shì xuésheng. Tā yě shì xuésheng ma? Yes, I am a student. Is he also a student?
王老师 是，他也是学生。谢谢，再见！ Shì, tā yě shì xuésheng. Xièxie, zàijiàn! Yes, he is also a student. Thank you, goodbye!

Notice that 老师 is used as a title after a family name, as in 王老师, similar to how "Teacher" or "Professor" might be used before a name in English, but placed after the name in Mandarin.

理解问题 — Comprehension questions:

  1. 莎拉叫什么名字？ (What is Sarah's name?)
  2. 王老师是学生吗？ (Is Teacher Wang a student?)
  3. How does Sarah ask whether "he" is also a student?

Speaking Practice

控制性练习 — Controlled practice: reading the model dialogue aloud

Read the model dialogue above aloud twice: once slowly, sounding out each tone, and once at a more natural pace. Pay attention to the rising pitch at the end of each 吗 question, and to the third-tone sandhi in 你好.

交际任务 — Communicative task: greet and introduce yourself

With a partner or small group, greet one another as if you were meeting for the first time on the opening night of a class. Use 你好, give your name with either taught frame, ask 你是学生吗？, and close with 谢谢，再见。

独立练习 — Solo alternative

Record yourself completing both sides of a short greeting and introduction exchange, playing both 王老师's and 莎拉's parts. Listen back and check your four tones, especially on 你, 我, and 好, all of which are third tone.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — 你好 across settings

你好 is widely understood across Mandarin-speaking communities, but everyday greeting habits vary. In many casual contexts, people may simply say a person's name, ask "have you eaten?" (吃了吗？), or use no fixed greeting formula at all. Greeting practices also vary by region, generation, and setting. Do not assume that one greeting routine represents every Mandarin-speaking person or every Chinese-speaking community.

Culture in Context — 老师 and titles

Titles such as 老师 (teacher) are commonly placed after a family name, as in 王老师, and are used respectfully with instructors in many educational settings. As you meet more titles in later chapters, you will develop a fuller sense of which titles fit which relationships and settings.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Treating tones as optional

Error: Saying 我 (wǒ) or 你 (nǐ) without a clear tone contour, as if tone were just an accent detail.

Correction: Practice the tone contours from the Pronunciation Focus box above. Tones change meaning in Mandarin; a flat or incorrect tone can produce a different word or an unintelligible syllable.

Common Mistake — Adding "am/is/are" to 是

Error: Trying to change 是 to match "am," "is," or "are," because English changes its verb forms for different subjects.

Correction: 是 never changes form. 我是, 你是, and 他是 all use the exact same word.

Common Mistake — Reordering words to form a 吗 question

Error: Trying to move words around to form a question, by analogy with English "Are you...?"

Correction: Keep the statement word order exactly the same and simply add 吗 at the end: 你是学生。 → 你是学生吗？

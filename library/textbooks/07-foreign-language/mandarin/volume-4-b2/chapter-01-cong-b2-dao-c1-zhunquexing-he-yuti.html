从B2到C1：准确性和语体

Volume IV, Chapter 1: From B2 to C1 — Precision and Register · Speak and Write Mandarin Chinese: Professional, Academic, Media, and Institutional Mandarin

Chapter Overview and Learning Objectives

This chapter opens Volume IV by turning explicit attention to something learners have been doing informally throughout Volume III: choosing words and structures that fit a situation. At B2, learners can communicate accurately in most everyday, academic-preparatory, and professional-adjacent contexts. At C1, learners must additionally control how they say something — recognizing when a sentence is too casual, too abrupt, or too imprecise for a formal audience, and revising it accordingly. This chapter introduces no new vocabulary domain or character set. Instead, it introduces disciplined attention to register, formal stance markers, and paragraph-level organization, using a graduate-student's email exchange with a professor as the anchor scenario.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify whether a Mandarin sentence or message is casual, neutral, or formal in register.
  2. Revise a casual sentence into a formal one without changing its basic meaning.
  3. Use a small set of formal stance markers to soften or strengthen a claim appropriately.
  4. Organize a short formal message into clearly separated, logically ordered paragraphs.
  5. Read and interpret a formal email exchange between a student and a professor.
  6. Explain, in Mandarin, why a given expression is or is not appropriate for a formal context.

Communication Scenario

A learner beginning a graduate exchange program in a Mandarin-speaking academic setting has drafted an email to a professor requesting an extension on a paper deadline. A friend who works in academic advising reviews the draft together with the learner, pointing out places where the wording is too casual for the audience and helping revise it into an appropriately formal message.

  1. You will listen to the advisor and the student discussing the draft email.
  2. You will read the professor's formal reply.
  3. You will practice converting casual sentences into formal ones.
  4. You will write your own short formal email requesting something from an instructor or supervisor.

发音 — Pronunciation and Reading-Aloud Focus

Pronunciation and Reading-Aloud Focus

No new sound system is introduced in this chapter. Formal register in Mandarin is carried by wording and structure rather than by pitch or stress, but a formal reading-aloud style still differs from casual speech: it is typically slower, more evenly paced, and avoids the casual contractions and rising intonation patterns common in everyday chat. Practice reading the phrases below with steady, even pacing appropriate to a formal message read aloud.

PhrasePractice Focus
我恳请您考虑延长交作业的时间。even, unhurried pacing; clear tone on 恳请 (kěnqǐng)
这次生病耽误了我的写作进度，希望您能谅解。third-tone sandhi in 我的; steady phrasing across the clause boundary
这封邮件写得很有条理。clear separation of 得 and 很; no reduction on 条理 (tiáolǐ)
语法 — Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Grammar and Sentence Patterns: Register, Stance, and Paragraph Order

This chapter's focus is not a new grammatical structure but disciplined control of previously consolidated systems: word order, particles, aspect, and clause linkage, now applied with attention to formal register, stance marking, and paragraph organization. Register in Mandarin is signaled through vocabulary choice, sentence length, particle use, and the presence or absence of casual sentence-final particles, not through a separate grammar system.

1. Recognizing Casual vs. Formal Wording

Casual speech often uses short clauses, sentence-final particles such as 啊, 吧, and 呀, and everyday verbs. Formal writing favors fuller sentences, more precise verbs, and restrained use of casual particles.

CasualFormalNotes
老师，我这次作业写不完了，能不能晚几天交啊？老师，这次作业恐怕无法按时完成，恳请您允许我晚几天提交。casual particle 啊 dropped; 写不完 replaced with more precise 无法按时完成
我生病了，所以耽误了。由于近期生病，写作进度受到了影响。由于...影响 replaces 所以 in formal written register

2. Formal Stance Markers

Use 恐怕 to soften a negative statement, 希望您能... to make a polite request, and 由于... to introduce a reason in written register, rather than the more spoken 因为 at the start of a sentence.

由于身体原因，我恐怕无法按时完成这次作业，希望您能理解并允许延期。

3. Paragraph-Level Organization

A formal message should separate its purpose, its explanation, and its request into distinct, logically ordered sentences or short paragraphs rather than one long run-on statement.

我写这封邮件是想说明一下我的情况。上周我因为生病住院，没能按计划完成论文的初稿。现在身体已经好转，但是还需要几天时间才能完成写作。因此，我恳请您将交稿日期延长到本周五，谢谢您的理解。

Putting It Together

A well-formed formal message should: (1) state its purpose early, (2) give a clear, appropriately worded reason, (3) make the request using a softened stance marker, and (4) close politely. None of these steps requires new grammar — they require applying familiar systems with attention to register.

范文 — Model Text

Model Text: 给教授的一封邮件 — An Email to a Professor

王老师：

您好！我写这封邮件是想跟您说明一下我这次论文的情况。上周我因为生病住院，没能按计划完成初稿的写作。现在身体已经好转，正在抓紧时间修改和补充内容，但是恐怕还需要几天时间才能完成一份让自己满意的稿子。因此，我恳请您将交稿日期从这周三延长到这周五。如果这样安排给您带来不便，也请您告诉我，我会尽量调整。谢谢您的理解，祝好！

学生 李明

练习 — Guided Practice

Guided Practice

  1. State your purpose: 我写这封邮件是想跟您说明一下______的情况。
  2. Give a formal reason: 由于______，我恐怕无法______。
  3. Make a softened request: 因此，我恳请您______。
  4. Close politely: 谢谢您的理解，______。

口语练习 — Speaking Component

Speaking Component

交际任务 — Communicative task: 修改语体

Working with a partner playing the role of an academic advisor, take turns reading aloud three casual sentences you have written about a real or invented academic situation. Your partner should identify which words or particles are too casual and suggest a more formal alternative, using 这句话太口语化了，可以改成... as a model frame.

独立练习 — Solo alternative

Record yourself reading both a casual version and a formal version of the same three sentences, explaining aloud in Mandarin what changed and why the formal version is more appropriate for a professor or supervisor.

写作练习 — Writing Component

Writing Component: A Formal Request Email

Complete the following writing task, applying the register and organization patterns introduced in this chapter.

  1. Choose a situation in which you need to make a formal request of an instructor, supervisor, or institution (for example, requesting an extension, a meeting, or a document).
  2. Draft a casual version of your message first, as you might say it to a friend.
  3. Revise it into a formal email of four to six sentences, using at least one stance marker (恐怕, 希望您能..., or 由于...) and organizing it into purpose, reason, request, and closing.
  4. Check your draft against the checklist below before considering the task complete.

语体检查清单 — Register checklist:

  • No casual sentence-final particles such as 啊, 呀, or 呗 appear in the formal version.
  • The reason for the request is stated clearly and precisely, not vaguely.
  • At least one formal stance marker softens or frames the request appropriately.
  • The message is organized into a clear purpose, reason, request, and closing, rather than one run-on statement.
  • Characters, punctuation, and measure words are checked for accuracy.

Culture and Communication Check

Culture Note — Register and hierarchy in academic and institutional communication

In many Mandarin-medium academic and institutional settings, written communication with a professor, supervisor, or official tends to favor a more formal register than spoken conversation with peers, particularly around requests, apologies, and disagreements. This is not unique to Mandarin-speaking contexts, but the specific markers that signal formality — careful word choice, softened requests, and complete, well-organized sentences — differ from language to language. Using an appropriately formal register in this kind of message is generally understood as a sign of respect for the relationship and the institution, not as excessive stiffness, though norms do vary by individual, institution, and region.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Carrying over casual particles into formal writing: particles such as 啊, 呀, and 呗 read as overly casual in a formal email; remove them and rely on clear wording instead.
  • Starting a formal sentence with 因为 in written register: while common in speech, 由于 is generally preferred to open a formal written reason clause.
  • Making a request too bluntly: a bare request without a softening stance marker can sound abrupt in a formal context; use 恳请 or 希望您能... rather than a plain imperative.
  • Writing one long run-on sentence: a formal message should separate purpose, reason, and request into distinct sentences rather than joining everything with 然后 or 而且.

Chapter Summary

This chapter introduced no new grammar, vocabulary domain, or character set. Instead, it asked you to apply previously consolidated Mandarin systems with new attention to register, formal stance marking, and paragraph-level organization. You practiced recognizing casual versus formal wording, softening requests with stance markers such as 恐怕 and 希望您能..., introducing reasons with 由于, and organizing a short message into a clear purpose, reason, request, and closing. These skills form the foundation for the more extensive institutional, academic, and public-discourse writing developed across the rest of Volume IV.

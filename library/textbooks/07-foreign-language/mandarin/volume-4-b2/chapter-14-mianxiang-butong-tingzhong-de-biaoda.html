面向不同听众的表达

Volume IV, Chapter 14: Mediation for Different Audiences · Speak and Write Mandarin Chinese: Professional, Academic, Media, and Institutional Mandarin

Chapter Overview and Learning Objectives

Chapter 13 taught you how to turn a written academic paragraph into a spoken presentation for a single audience, and how to restate and hedge in response to questions. This chapter asks a different question: what changes when the audience itself changes? The same library-usage findings you have been working with since Chapter 12 might need to be explained to a library director, summarized for a general campus newsletter, or condensed into a two-sentence update for a busy colleague. This chapter introduces no new sentence-level grammar. Instead, it develops the vocabulary and discourse skills of mediation -- identifying what an audience already knows, deciding what to keep, cut, or simplify, and adjusting register and vocabulary accordingly, while keeping the underlying claim accurate.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify and use core vocabulary for audience analysis, summarizing, and register adjustment.
  2. Explain what a given audience already knows and what they need explained.
  3. Produce a short mediated summary of the same content for two different audiences.
  4. Adjust vocabulary, sentence length, and register when moving between an expert audience and a general audience.
  5. Preserve the accuracy of a claim while simplifying or condensing its expression.
  6. Evaluate whether a mediated version has lost important information or added unsupported certainty.

Communication Scenario

The presentation you delivered in Chapter 13 went well, and the library director has asked for two follow-up items: a short paragraph for the library's internal staff newsletter (written for people who already work with the data every day) and a two-to-three-sentence update for the general campus newsletter (written for students and staff who have never seen the report). Your task is to take the same underlying finding -- library usage has risen, likely because of digitized services -- and mediate it for these two different audiences without changing what is actually true.

  1. You will listen to two short spoken updates on the same topic and identify which audience each one addresses.
  2. You will read a short internal memo and a short public announcement on the same finding and compare their vocabulary and sentence length.
  3. You will practice identifying what an audience already knows before you decide what to explain.
  4. You will write two short mediated texts -- one internal, one public -- based on the same finding.

语法和篇章 — Grammar and Discourse Focus

Grammar and Discourse Focus: Audience Adaptation, Summary, and Register Transfer

This chapter introduces no new sentence-level grammar. Instead, it develops a discourse skill: producing the same underlying content in two different forms depending on who receives it, using vocabulary and sentence patterns you already control.

Naming the audience before writing or speaking

A mediation task begins with a simple question, stated explicitly rather than assumed: 这篇文章/这次报告的对象是谁？他们已经有什么背景知识？他们需要什么样的信息？ Answering this question first determines every later choice -- vocabulary, sentence length, level of detail, and whether 专业术语 can be used directly or must be explained.

  • 内部读者：图书馆工作人员，已经熟悉报告里的专业说法，不需要基础解释。
  • 大众读者：普通学生和教职员工，没有看过原始报告，需要通俗、简短的说法。

Deciding what to keep, simplify, or omit

Mediating for a new audience is not simply making sentences shorter. It requires three separate decisions for each piece of information: 保留 it as is, 简化 it into more accessible language, or 省略 it because it is not essential for this audience. A number, a caveat, or a methodological detail that matters to a specialist reader may be omitted for a general reader without changing what is actually claimed -- but a claim's overall accuracy must never be lost through simplification. This is the difference between simplifying language and letting the content 失真.

Register transfer in practice

Compare the same finding mediated for two audiences, using vocabulary and structures already active from earlier chapters:

  • 面向内部读者：官方年度报告显示，到馆人数比前一年增加了百分之十五，这一变化可能与图书馆推行的数字化服务政策有关，但目前的分析仍属初步阶段。 — retains the percentage and the hedge about the analysis stage
  • 面向大众读者：图书馆最近的使用人数明显上升，这可能跟图书馆新推出的一些线上服务有关。 — 简化 the percentage into "明显上升," 省略 the caveat about the analysis stage, but 保留 the core claim and its likely cause

Notice that the general-audience version is not vaguer in a careless way -- it deliberately keeps the claim's direction (usage rose, likely because of digital services) while dropping detail that would not help this reader and could even distract from the main point.

Summarizing with 概括 and relaying with 转述

Use 概括 to compress a longer text into one clear sentence, and 转述 to convey someone else's spoken or written point in your own words for a new audience, rather than quoting it directly. Both skills depend on the discourse markers already active from Chapters 11-13 (首先, 其次, 简单来说, 换句话说) but apply them to the task of moving content from one audience to another rather than only organizing a single presentation.

范文 — Model Text

Model Text: Two Mediated Versions of the Same Finding

内部工作人员简报：
各位同事，简单来说，官方年度报告显示，到馆人数比前一年增加了百分之十五。据我了解，这个变化很可能与我们去年推行的数字化服务政策有关，不过这仍然是初步的分析，还需要更多数据确认。建议大家在接下来的季度报告里继续关注这个趋势。

校园公众通讯：
好消息！最近图书馆的使用人数明显上升，这很可能跟图书馆新推出的线上服务有关，比如在线预约座位和电子资源。欢迎大家继续多多使用图书馆的各项服务！

  1. 这两段话面向的对象分别是谁？你是怎么判断出来的？
  2. 公众通讯版本省略了哪些内部简报里提到的内容？
  3. 公众通讯版本有没有让原来的意思失真？为什么？

口语练习 — Speaking Practice

Speaking Practice

交际任务 — Communicative task: 面向两种听众的口头更新

Using the library-usage finding you have worked with since Chapter 12, prepare two short spoken updates: a 30-45 second version for a colleague who already knows the report (you may use 专业术语 and keep specific numbers), and a 15-20 second version for a general campus audience (通俗, no specific percentages required). Deliver both to a partner and ask them to identify which audience each version was written for and why.

独立练习 — Solo alternative

Record both spoken updates yourself. Afterward, listen back and check: did the general-audience version lose any information that actually mattered, or only detail that this audience did not need?

阅读练习 — Reading Practice

Reading Practice: Comparing an Internal Memo and a Public Notice

Read the two short texts below and answer the questions that follow.

内部备忘录：
各部门请注意：根据最新的年度报告，到馆人数同比增长百分之十五，电子资源访问量同比增长百分之二十二。初步分析显示，这一增长与去年第三季度推行的数字化服务政策存在正相关关系，但因果关系尚待进一步确认。请各部门在下季度工作总结中继续跟踪相关数据。

写作练习 — Writing Workshop

Writing Workshop: Two Mediated Texts

Write two short texts based on the same library-usage finding you have developed since Chapter 12.

  1. Audience analysis: Before writing, write one sentence for each audience answering: 这篇文章的对象是谁？他们已经有什么背景知识？
  2. Internal staff paragraph: Write a 3-4 sentence paragraph for library staff who already know the report. You may use 专业术语 and specific numbers, and you may include a hedge about the analysis being preliminary.
  3. Public newsletter update: Write a 2-3 sentence update for the general campus newsletter. Use 通俗 vocabulary, decide what to 保留, 简化, or 省略, and avoid specific percentages unless truly necessary.
  4. Self-check: Compare your two texts and confirm that the public version has not let the core claim 失真.

写作检查清单 — Writing checklist:

  • Each text clearly matches the background knowledge of its stated audience.
  • The internal paragraph retains at least one specific figure and one hedge.
  • The public update uses accessible, 通俗 language and omits unnecessary technical detail.
  • The core claim (usage rose, likely linked to digital services) appears accurately in both versions.
  • Neither version adds certainty that the original report did not support.

Culture and Communication Check

Culture Note — Audience expectations vary by setting and platform

Expectations for how much detail, hedging, and technical language belongs in a public-facing update vary across institutions, publication platforms, and professional fields, and this variation is not unique to any one Mandarin-speaking community. A university notice board post, a WeChat public account article, and a printed internal newsletter may each call for a different balance of 通俗 language and specific data, even within the same organization. A careful writer or speaker checks the norms of the specific platform and audience rather than assuming that one mediation style fits every context.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Skipping audience analysis: writing a mediated version without first asking who the audience is and what they already know often produces a text that is either too technical or too vague.
  • Confusing simplifying with distorting: cutting detail is appropriate; quietly changing the direction or certainty of a claim is not. 简化 should never become 失真.
  • Keeping every number for every audience: a general audience rarely needs an exact percentage; a rounded, 通俗 description is often more effective and just as accurate.
  • Removing all hedging for a general audience: a caveat about preliminary analysis can often be simplified rather than deleted outright, especially if omitting it would overstate certainty.
  • Using 专业术语 without checking the audience: terminology appropriate for an internal memo can confuse or alienate a general reader if used without adjustment.

部分 B — 口语 (Speaking)

1. I can deliver two spoken updates on the same finding, adjusted for two different audiences.

2. I can explain my choices about what to keep, simplify, or omit for a given audience.

部分 C — 写作 (Writing)

1. I can write an internal-audience paragraph and a general-audience update based on the same finding without letting the claim lose accuracy.

Chapter Overview and Learning Objectives

Chapter 11 gave you tools for citing, paraphrasing, and evaluating individual sources. This chapter widens the lens from the sentence to the paragraph and the essay: how a piece of academic Mandarin writing is organized, how nominal groups compress information into formal noun phrases, and how a short paper builds an argument out of a claim, supporting points, and a conclusion. No new sentence-level grammar is introduced here. Instead, you will learn to combine grammar and vocabulary you already control -- including the attribution and paraphrase tools from Chapter 11 -- into a more formal, more tightly organized written register.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify and use core vocabulary for academic writing structure and stance.
  2. Recognize and build formal nominal groups that compress modifiers, quantities, and clauses before a head noun.
  3. Distinguish everyday spoken sentence patterns from their more formal written equivalents.
  4. Organize a short academic paragraph using an opening claim, supporting points, and a closing statement.
  5. Use 首先, 其次, 此外, and 总的来说 to organize an argument section.
  6. Write a short academic paragraph that states a position, supports it with paraphrased source material, and closes with a summary statement.

Communication Scenario

The research note you helped draft in Chapter 11 has been well received, and your classmate has now been asked to expand it into a short academic paragraph for a class assignment: a single, well-organized paragraph that states a position on why library usage has increased, supports the position with evidence from the sources you already evaluated, and closes with a clear summary statement. Your task is to help turn the informal research note into a paragraph that reads like academic writing rather than spoken commentary.

  1. You will listen to a short recorded academic paragraph and identify its opening claim, supporting points, and closing statement.
  2. You will read a short academic-style paragraph and compare its sentence patterns with a more conversational version of the same content.
  3. You will practice converting simple noun phrases into formal nominal groups.
  4. You will write a short academic paragraph that states, supports, and closes an argument.

语法和篇章 — Grammar and Discourse Focus

Grammar and Discourse Focus: Nominal Groups and Formal Written Style

This chapter introduces no new sentence-level grammar. Instead, it develops two things you already have the pieces for: compressing information into formal nominal groups, and organizing a paragraph the way academic writing is organized.

Building formal nominal groups

A nominal group stacks modifiers -- quantity, source, description, and modifying clauses -- in front of a head noun using 的, rather than spreading the same information across several short spoken-style sentences. Compare:

  • 口语：图书馆做了一个报告。这个报告是去年发布的。它谈到了使用人数的变化。 — spoken, three short sentences
  • 书面语：图书馆去年发布的、谈到使用人数变化的报告 — one compressed nominal group

Build the nominal group from the head noun outward: identify the head noun first (报告), then attach its modifying clauses with 的, ordering them from broader background information to the most specific description closest to the noun. Do not stack more than two or three modifying layers in a single nominal group, since longer chains become difficult to parse even in formal writing.

Paragraph organization

An academic paragraph in this chapter follows a simple, consistent shape: an opening sentence that states a claim or 观点, two or three supporting points introduced with 首先, 其次, and 此外, and a closing sentence introduced with 总的来说 that restates the claim in light of the evidence. This organization should feel familiar from the attribution and paraphrase tools in Chapter 11: supporting points are often exactly the kind of paraphrased, attributed claims you practiced there.

Written versus spoken register

Formal written Mandarin favors nominal groups, 论点/论据 vocabulary, and discourse markers such as 首先 and 总的来说 over the looser, more repetitive connectors common in speech (然后, 还有, 反正). It does not require different core grammar -- word order, particles, and aspect marking remain the same -- but it does require tighter organization and denser noun phrases.

范文 — Model Text

Model Text: A Short Academic Paragraph

今年校园图书馆使用人数明显增加，这一变化很可能与图书馆近年推行的数字化服务政策有关。首先，据校报报道，图书馆去年发布的年度报告显示，到馆人数比前一年增加了百分之十五。其次，图书馆主任在采访中指出，新的在线预约座位系统降低了学生使用图书馆的门槛。此外，电子资源使用量的上升也支持了这一观点，说明学生获取资料的方式正在发生变化。需要指出的是，网上流传的"使用人数翻了一倍"这一说法出处不明，本文未将其作为论据采用。总的来说，现有的可靠数据表明，数字化服务政策是使用人数上升的一个重要原因。

  1. 这段话的开头提出了什么观点？
  2. 作者用了哪些discourse markers来组织论据？
  3. 找出一个名词性短语，说明它是由哪几个修饰成分组成的。

写作练习 — Writing Workshop

Writing Workshop: A Short Academic Paragraph

Expand your Chapter 11 research note into a single academic paragraph.

  1. Opening claim: State your 观点 in one sentence: why did library usage increase?
  2. Supporting points: Introduce two or three 论据 using 首先, 其次, and 此外, drawing on the attributed and paraphrased source material from Chapter 11.
  3. Nominal groups: Compress at least one set of modifying details into a formal nominal group rather than spreading them across several short sentences.
  4. Closing statement: Close with 总的来说 and restate your claim in light of the evidence.

写作检查清单 — Writing checklist:

  • The paragraph opens with a clearly stated claim (观点).
  • At least two supporting points are introduced with 首先/其次/此外.
  • At least one formal nominal group replaces several short spoken-style sentences.
  • The paragraph closes with 总的来说 and restates the claim.
  • Any unreliable or unclear claim from Chapter 11 is excluded or clearly flagged rather than used as evidence.

Culture and Communication Check

Culture Note — Academic paragraph conventions vary by field and institution

The claim-evidence-conclusion shape used in this chapter is a widely useful starting structure, but specific conventions for academic paragraphs and papers vary by discipline, institution, and publication, and continue to evolve. Some fields favor a more explicit statement of the argument at the very start; others build toward it gradually. A careful writer checks the expectations of their specific academic or professional context rather than assuming one paragraph shape applies everywhere in the Mandarin-speaking academic world.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Overstacking nominal groups: piling on more than two or three modifying layers before the head noun makes the sentence hard to parse; break an overloaded nominal group back into two sentences if needed.
  • Dropping discourse markers: without 首先/其次/此外/总的来说, a paragraph can read as a loose list of facts rather than an organized argument.
  • Mixing spoken fillers into formal writing: words such as 反正, 然后, and 挺...的 belong to spoken register and should not appear in an academic paragraph.
  • Treating every sentence as a nominal group: not every idea needs to be compressed; use nominal groups where they genuinely tighten the writing, not as decoration.
  • Losing the reliability distinctions from Chapter 11: an academic paragraph should still separate reliable 论据 from unclear or unattributed claims.

协商和问题解决

Volume IV, Chapter 19: Negotiation and Problem Solving · Speak and Write Mandarin Chinese: Professional, Academic, Media, and Institutional Mandarin

Chapter Overview and Learning Objectives

Chapter 18 taught you to narrate the past carefully, separating documented fact from attributed claim and commemorative framing. This chapter turns from recounting what already happened to managing what should happen next: proposing solutions, offering and requesting concessions, stating conditions, and reaching a workable compromise in a professional setting. No new sentence grammar is introduced. You already control conditional clauses, modal verbs, comparison, and contrast connectors; this chapter asks you to redeploy that grammar for the specific discourse work of negotiation — proposing, conceding, conditioning, and closing.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Present a proposal clearly and invite a response using already-consolidated sentence patterns.
  2. State a condition under which an offer or agreement becomes acceptable (前提是..., 只要...就...).
  3. Offer and recognize a concession or compromise without abandoning a core position.
  4. Use modal verbs and softened stance markers to negotiate diplomatically rather than confrontationally.
  5. Participate in a short, realistic professional negotiation and summarize its outcome in writing.

Communication Scenario

The anniversary feature on the old reading room from Chapter 18 has led to new interest: the alumni association wants to fund a renovation of the room, but the university's facilities office has a limited budget and a different timeline in mind. You have been asked to sit in on a planning meeting between an alumni-association representative and a facilities-office administrator, who must negotiate scope, budget, and schedule for the project.

  1. You will listen to part of the planning meeting and note each side's opening proposal.
  2. You will read a short follow-up email in which one side offers a compromise.
  3. You will discuss what conditions would make a proposal acceptable to each side.
  4. You will write a short summary of an agreement reached in a negotiation of your choosing.

协商重点 — Negotiation and Professional-Stance Focus

Negotiation and Professional-Stance Focus

No new sentence grammar is introduced in this chapter. Instead, three habits of professional negotiation help you use grammar you already control more purposefully:

  • Proposing without overcommitting: use 我建议... or 是不是可以考虑... rather than a flat statement of what must happen. Compare a blunt statement with a softened proposal: 我们必须马上开始。 versus 我建议我们尽快开始，大家觉得怎么样？
  • Stating conditions clearly: 只要...就... and 前提是... let you state exactly what would make a proposal acceptable, without sounding like a demand. 只要预算够，我们就可以按原计划做。
  • Conceding without losing your position: 虽然...但是..., together with 不过 and 可以考虑, let you acknowledge the other side's point while keeping your own priorities visible. 虽然时间比较紧，但是如果对方愿意让步，我们也可以考虑调整计划。

Modal verbs you already control (可以, 应该, 能, 得) take on a more diplomatic function here: 应该 can soften a recommendation, and 可以考虑 in particular signals openness without commitment. As in Chapter 18, the new material is functional and lexical; the underlying sentence patterns were consolidated earlier in the volume.

Example: Proposal, Condition, and Concession in One Exchange

句子：校友会代表：我们建议在明年春天完成整个阅览室的翻新。
行政人员：这个时间比较紧，不过如果校友会愿意提供一部分资金，我们可以考虑提前安排。前提是，工程不能影响下学期的正常使用。

Every sentence here uses grammar you already control — proposal framing, contrast connectors, modal softening, and a condition clause. What is new is the discourse pattern: propose, acknowledge difficulty, offer a conditional concession, and state a boundary condition, all without either side abandoning its position.

范文 — Model Text

Model Text: Planning Meeting for the Reading Room Renovation

校友会代表：我们很高兴大家都同意翻新老阅览室。校友会建议尽快开始，最好在明年校庆之前完成。
行政人员：我理解校友会的心情，不过学校这边的预算要到下学期才能批下来，时间上恐怕来不及。
校友会代表：如果校友会先垫付一部分费用，学校这边是不是就可以提前动工？
行政人员：这是个可以考虑的办法。不过前提是，工程必须分阶段进行，不能一次性关闭整个阅览室，因为学生还要用。
校友会代表：没问题，我们可以接受分阶段施工，只要能在校庆前完成主要部分就行。
行政人员：好，那我们就按这个方向准备一份具体的方案，下周再谈细节。

  1. 校友会一开始提出的建议是什么？行政人员为什么没有马上同意？
  2. 双方最后达成的妥协包括哪两个条件？
  3. 这段对话里，哪些词让语气显得比较委婉、不那么强硬？

写作任务 — Writing Task

Writing Task: Summarizing an Agreement

Write a short paragraph (120–180 characters) summarizing the outcome of a negotiation, real or invented, clearly stating the proposal, at least one condition, and the compromise reached, as the model text and follow-up email do.

  1. Choose your negotiation: Decide what was originally proposed and by whom.
  2. State the condition: Use 前提是 or 只要...就... to mark what made the agreement possible.
  3. Name the concession: Use 让步 or 妥协 to show what each side gave up or adjusted.
  4. Keep the grammar familiar: Use only sentence patterns, conditionals, and connectors you have already studied in Volume IV.

写作检查清单 — Writing checklist:

  • The paragraph clearly states an original proposal.
  • At least one condition is marked with 前提是 or 只要...就....
  • At least one concession or compromise is named using 让步 or 妥协.
  • No new grammar has been introduced.
  • The paragraph is understandable to someone unfamiliar with the negotiation.

Culture and Communication Check

Culture Note — Diplomatic negotiation register

In many professional and institutional settings across Mandarin-speaking communities, negotiation tends to favor indirect, face-preserving language over blunt demands, particularly in early stages of discussion. Softened proposals (是不是可以考虑...), conditional framing (前提是...), and explicit acknowledgment of the other side's difficulty (虽然...但是...) are common ways of moving toward agreement without either side appearing to simply give in. Practices vary by organization, relationship, and how much time has already been invested in the relationship; this chapter's model stays with a low-stakes, cooperative example (a renovation project) so you can practice the *language* of negotiation without needing specialized legal or diplomatic vocabulary.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Stating proposals as demands: 我们必须... can sound confrontational; 我们建议... or 是不是可以考虑... is usually more effective in a professional negotiation.
  • Leaving conditions vague: if an agreement depends on a specific condition, state it explicitly with 前提是 or 只要...就..., rather than implying it and hoping the other side infers it.
  • Conceding without naming the concession: if you are adjusting your position, say so clearly with 让步 or 妥协, rather than simply changing the topic.
  • Treating a first proposal as final: the model text and email both show a negotiation moving through more than one round; a single exchange rarely settles everything.
  • Adding new grammar to sound more "official": the professional effect at this stage should come from vocabulary and framing, not from unstudied structures.

Answer Key

Guided Practice (sample answers)

  • 我们建议尽快开始翻新工程 — proposal; marked with 建议, presenting a course of action for the other side to consider.
  • 前提是，工程不能影响下学期的正常使用 — stated condition; marked with 前提是, specifying what must hold for the proposal to be acceptable.
  • 虽然时间比较紧，但是我们可以考虑分阶段施工 — concession; the contrast connector acknowledges the difficulty, and 可以考虑 signals a willingness to adjust the original position.

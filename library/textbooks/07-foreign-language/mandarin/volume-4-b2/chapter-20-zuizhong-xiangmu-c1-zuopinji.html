Volume IV, Chapter 20: Final C1 Portfolio · Speak and Write Mandarin Chinese: Professional, Academic, Media, and Institutional Mandarin

Chapter Overview and Learning Objectives

This chapter closes Volume IV. It introduces no new grammar, no new high-burden character set, and no new pronunciation or register system. Instead, it asks you to draw together the discourse, register, literacy, and communicative systems built across all four units — formal register and argument (Unit I), media and public issues (Unit II), research and extended writing (Unit III), and style, cultural interpretation, and negotiation (Unit IV) — into a single connected portfolio built around the reading-room renovation story that has run through the volume since Chapter 18. The portfolio is a capstone demonstration of what you can already do, not an introduction to anything new.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Select and organize a coherent set of listening, speaking, reading, and writing evidence drawn from consolidated Volume IV systems.
  2. Produce an extended, multi-paragraph written portfolio piece that integrates argument, source attribution, register control, and negotiation outcomes.
  3. Deliver a short prepared oral presentation with follow-up questions and answers on the same project.
  4. Explain, in Mandarin, how your own language choices reflect audience, register, and purpose.
  5. Assess your own C1-oriented command of Mandarin against clear, previously introduced criteria.

Communication Scenario

The reading-room renovation project has now moved from a historical feature (Chapter 18) through negotiation (Chapter 19) toward a signed agreement. The university newsletter has asked you, as the student who has followed the story most closely, to prepare a short portfolio package: a written summary report, a brief spoken presentation for a student-affairs meeting, and answers to likely questions. You will also reflect briefly, in Mandarin, on the language choices you made.

  1. You will listen to a short closing update on the renovation's progress.
  2. You will read a brief public notice announcing the agreement to the university community.
  3. You will write an integrated portfolio report combining narrative, argument, sourcing, and negotiated outcome.
  4. You will present your report orally and respond to follow-up questions.

整合重点 — Portfolio Planning and Synthesis Focus

Portfolio Planning and Synthesis Focus

No new sentence grammar, no new character set, and no new register system are introduced in this chapter. The synthesis work is organizational: deciding which previously consolidated systems belong in which part of the portfolio, and moving smoothly between them.

  • Narrative to argument: the portfolio report should move from a brief narrative frame (recycled from Ch. 18's historical narrative work) into a stated position (recycled from Ch. 4) about why the renovation matters, without re-teaching either system.
  • Argument to evidence: claims in the report should be supported with attributed information, using the citation and paraphrase language from Ch. 11 (根据..., 有人认为..., 数据显示...) rather than unsupported assertion.
  • Evidence to outcome: the report should close by describing the negotiated outcome from Ch. 19, using 前提是, 让步, and 达成协议 to state what was agreed and under what conditions.
  • Written to spoken: the same content, when presented orally, should be adapted for a listening audience — recycling the audience-adaptation and mediation work from Ch. 14, including shorter sentences, clearer signposting, and space for questions.

Reflective language about one's own choices (为什么用这个说法, 这样写是为了...) draws on register and pragmatics awareness built throughout the volume; it is being applied here, not introduced.

Example: Moving from Narrative to Argument to Outcome

句子：老阅览室建成已经有八十多年的历史，是很多校友共同的记忆。我认为，保护这样一个空间不只是维修一栋建筑，更是保护学校的公共记忆。根据校方提供的数据，目前的结构基本安全，但设备已经老化。经过几轮协商，校友会和学校最终达成协议：工程分两个阶段进行，前提是不能影响学生正常使用阅览室。

Each sentence recycles a system already consolidated earlier in the volume — historical framing, stated position, cited evidence, and negotiated outcome — joined into one short passage. The chapter's work is the joining, not the introduction of new material.

范文 — Model Text

Model Text: Portfolio Report Excerpt

老阅览室建成已经有八十多年的历史，是很多校友共同的记忆，也是这次校庆报道的重点之一。我认为，保护这样一个空间不只是维修一栋建筑，更是保护学校的公共记忆。根据校方提供的数据，目前的结构基本安全，但采光和座位设备已经明显老化，也不再符合学生现在的学习需求。

为了解决这个问题，校友会和学校行政部门进行了几轮协商。校友会最初建议尽快完成整个翻新工程，但学校方面提出，预算和时间都比较紧张。经过讨论，双方最终达成协议：工程将分两个阶段进行，校友会先提供第一阶段所需资金的三分之一，前提是工程不能影响学生正常使用阅览室。这个结果既回应了校友们的期待，也考虑了学校的实际情况，可以说是一个比较理想的妥协。

  1. 这段文字先讲历史背景，再提出立场，最后说明结果。请找出每一部分分别从哪句话开始。
  2. 文中用了哪个短语来说明信息的出处？
  3. 最后一句话是怎样评价这个协商结果的？这体现了怎样的立场？

写作任务 — Writing Task

Writing Task: The C1 Portfolio Report

Write an integrated portfolio report (250–350 characters) on the reading-room renovation, or a comparable project of your own choosing, that combines all four components practiced in this chapter. Conclude with a short reflective note (in Mandarin, 40–60 characters) explaining one language choice you made and why.

  1. Open with narrative background: briefly establish the historical or contextual frame, recycling narrative language from Chapter 18.
  2. State your position: take a clear stance on why the issue matters, recycling argument framing from Chapter 4.
  3. Cite evidence: support your position with attributed information, using citation language from Chapter 11.
  4. Describe the negotiated outcome: state what was agreed and under what condition, using 前提是, 让步, or 达成协议 from Chapter 19.
  5. Reflect briefly: in one or two sentences, explain a register, word-choice, or structural decision you made and why it suited your audience.

写作检查清单 — Writing checklist:

  • The report includes narrative background, a stated position, cited evidence, and a negotiated outcome.
  • Sourcing is marked clearly (根据..., 有人认为..., 数据显示..., etc.).
  • At least one condition and one concession are named using 前提是, 让步, or 妥协.
  • A brief reflective note explains one language choice.
  • No grammar or vocabulary beyond what has been studied in Volume IV has been introduced.

Culture and Communication Check

Culture Note — Audience across genres

Across the reading-room story, the same underlying facts have appeared in several different genres: a historical feature, a planning-meeting dialogue, a follow-up email, an oral update, and a public notice. In many professional and institutional settings across Mandarin-speaking communities, the same information is routinely repackaged for different audiences — colleagues, funders, students, and the public — with real differences in length, formality, directness, and level of technical detail. Comparing the model texts across Chapters 18 through 20 is a useful way to notice how the same underlying content shifts register and structure depending on who is reading or listening, and how much has already been agreed. Practices vary by institution and context; this chapter's texts remain illustrative rather than a fixed formula.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Listing components instead of connecting them: narrative, position, evidence, and outcome should read as one connected report, not four disconnected paragraphs.
  • Dropping source attribution under time pressure: a capstone report should still mark sourcing clearly, even when writing quickly.
  • Repeating the written text verbatim when speaking: the oral presentation should be adapted for a listening audience, not simply read aloud from the written report.
  • Skipping the reflective note: explaining a language choice is part of the task, not an optional add-on.
  • Introducing new grammar or vocabulary to sound more advanced: the portfolio should demonstrate control of what has already been studied, not new material.

This chapter concluded Volume IV with a cumulative C1 portfolio built around the reading-room renovation story that ran from Chapter 18 through Chapter 20. No new grammar, characters, or register systems were introduced; instead, you combined narrative framing, stated position, cited evidence, and negotiated outcome into one integrated written report, adapted that report for oral presentation, and reflected briefly on your own language choices. You also compared how the same underlying facts were presented differently across a historical feature, a negotiation dialogue, an email, an oral update, and a public notice. This completes the professional, academic, media, and institutional work of Volume IV and prepares you for the specialized mastery, rhetorical control, and expert-level communication developed in Volume V.

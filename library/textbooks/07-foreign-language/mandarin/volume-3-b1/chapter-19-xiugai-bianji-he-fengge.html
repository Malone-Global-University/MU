修改、编辑和风格

Volume III, Chapter 19: Revision, Editing, and Style · Speak and Write Mandarin Chinese: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Overview and Learning Objectives

The magazine's editorial team has sent back the short structured report from Chapter 18 with a few marked-up notes, and the study group is asked to revise it before it can be published. This chapter introduces no major new grammar system. Instead, it takes writing that learners have already produced — including the report from Chapter 18 — and develops the skills needed to make it clearer, more connected, more accurate, and more appropriate in style. You will practice looking at a piece of writing with an editor's eye: tightening loose sentences, connecting ideas more smoothly, checking characters and punctuation, and adjusting tone so that a report reads as a finished, formal piece of writing rather than a rough draft.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify sentences that repeat information unnecessarily and revise them for concision.
  2. Improve cohesion between sentences and paragraphs using appropriate connectors and reference.
  3. Check and correct commonly confused characters and homophone-based typing errors.
  4. Distinguish spoken-style wording from written, formal-style wording, and choose the appropriate one for a report.
  5. Use a structured editing checklist to review a piece of writing systematically.
  6. Explain, in Mandarin, what change was made to a sentence and why.
  7. Revise a full short report so that it reads as a polished, formal piece of writing.

Communication Scenario

The editor has returned the Chapter 18 report with a few handwritten comments: some sentences repeat the same idea twice, one paragraph feels disconnected from the one before it, and a few characters were clearly mistyped through the input method. The study group must revise the report before resubmitting it, and must also be ready to explain their changes if the editor asks about them at the next meeting.

  1. You will listen to a short conversation in which a writer explains revisions to an editor.
  2. You will read a marked-up draft alongside its revised version and compare the two.
  3. You will discuss and justify specific revisions to a piece of writing.
  4. You will revise your own Chapter 18 report — or a new short piece — using an editing checklist.

发音 — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus: Reading a Revised Draft Aloud

No new sound system is introduced in this chapter. The focus is on reading a piece of writing aloud as a way of checking whether it is 通顺 (smooth and coherent). When a sentence is awkward, over-long, or repetitive, a fluent reader will often hesitate, run out of breath, or lose track of the sentence's structure partway through — this is a useful, practical signal that the sentence needs revision, independent of any grammar rule. Practice reading both the original and the revised versions of the Model Text aloud, paying attention to where your pacing becomes uneven.

语法 — Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Grammar and Sentence Patterns: Talking About Revision, Not New Grammar

This chapter introduces no new productive grammar system. It develops a small, functional cluster of expressions for describing changes to a piece of writing and for recognizing the difference between spoken-style and written-style wording, drawing on structures already consolidated in earlier chapters.

1. Describing a Problem in a Draft

这句话 + problem names a specific sentence and states what is wrong with it, using vocabulary such as 重复, 口语化, or 不通顺.

这句话有点重复，前面已经说过一次了。

2. Suggesting a Specific Change

可以 + action (删掉/改成/加上) proposes a concrete edit rather than a vague comment.

这句话可以删掉；那句话可以改成更简洁的说法。

3. Explaining the Reason for a Change

This chapter recycles 因为...所以... (Chapter 3) and 这样...更/会... patterns to justify an edit rather than simply making it.

我们把这两句话连起来了，因为它们说的是同一个意思；这样读起来更通顺。

4. Written Style versus Spoken Style

The same idea can often be expressed in a more spoken way or a more written way. In a formal report, learners should recognize which register a sentence belongs to and adjust it when necessary. This is a recognition-to-revision skill, not a new grammar point.

口语 (Spoken)书面语 (Written, formal)Note
我们觉得挺有意思的。我们认为这个结果值得关注。vague 挺...的 replaced with a specific formal claim
反正大家都这么说。根据调查，多数学生也持相同看法。casual 反正 replaced with a sourced statement
这个事儿挺麻烦的。这是这次调查的一个限制。colloquial 事儿 replaced with the chapter's technical term 限制

Putting It Together

A responsible revision in this chapter should: (1) identify a specific problem in a sentence, (2) propose a concrete change, (3) explain why the change improves the writing, and (4) check that the resulting register is appropriately formal for a report.

我们把第二段的两句话连了起来，因为它们的意思重复了，这样读起来更简洁、更通顺。另外，我们把"挺有意思的"改成了"值得关注"，因为报告应该用书面语，不应该用口语化的说法。

范文 — Model Text

Model Text: 草稿和修改后的版本 — Draft and Revised Version

修改前 — Before revision:

我们做了一个调查。这个调查是关于学生压力的。调查发现百分之六十的学生说压力主要来自考试。这个跟考试有关系，压力主要是考试造成的。我们觉得这个结果挺有意思的，反正很多人都这么想。我们的调查样本比较小，这个事儿是个问题。

修改后 — After revision:

我们做了一次关于学生压力的调查，发现百分之六十的学生说压力主要来自考试。我们认为这个结果值得关注，根据调查，多数学生也持相同看法。不过，这次调查的样本比较小，这是我们报告的一个限制。

Reading Practice: Comparing a Marked-Up Draft and Its Revision

Read the marked-up draft and its revised version below. Identify what kind of problem each marked sentence has, and explain how the revision addresses it.

编辑批注（草稿）： 这次调查一共问了三十个学生。这个调查是问学生的。结果是百分之六十的学生说压力主要来自考试，这个结果跟考试有关系。（这两句话意思重复，建议删掉一句） 我们建议学校可以帮帮学生。（"帮帮"太口语化）

写作练习 — Writing Workshop

Writing Workshop: Revising Your Own Report

Take the short structured report you wrote in Chapter 18 (or a new short piece of similar length) and revise it. Your revision must include:

  1. At least one deletion or combination of two sentences that repeated the same information.
  2. At least one wording change from a colloquial expression to a written, formal-style expression.
  3. A character check for at least two words that are commonly mistyped through Pinyin input, confirming the correct character was selected.
  4. A short written explanation, in Mandarin, of the two most significant changes you made and why they improve the report.

修改清单 — Editing checklist:

  • No two sentences repeat the same information without adding something new.
  • Colloquial or vague wording has been replaced with written, formal-style wording appropriate to a report.
  • Characters have been checked against the intended meaning, not just accepted from the first IME suggestion.
  • Sentences and paragraphs are connected clearly, without abrupt or confusing jumps.
  • Chinese punctuation (，。？！) is used correctly throughout.

Culture and Communication Check

Culture Note — Revision as a normal, expected part of formal writing

In many academic, professional, and media contexts where Mandarin is used for writing, a first draft is rarely treated as a finished product — editors, colleagues, teachers, and reviewers routinely mark up drafts, and revising a piece of writing several times before it is shared or published is considered ordinary practice rather than a sign that the original writer did something wrong. Receiving detailed editing comments, including comments about wording, repetition, or register, is generally understood as part of producing careful, publishable writing rather than as personal criticism. This expectation is not unique to any one institution or region, but it is worth naming explicitly for learners who may be more accustomed to treating a first draft as final.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Deleting a repeated sentence without checking what information it carried: confirm no unique detail is lost before removing a sentence entirely.
  • Leaving colloquial wording in a formal report: expressions natural in conversation, such as 挺...的 or 事儿, usually need to be replaced in written, formal-style text.
  • Accepting the first IME suggestion without checking meaning: Pinyin input can easily select a correct-sounding but incorrect character; always confirm the character matches the intended word.
  • Making changes without being able to explain them: a good editor, and a good writer revising their own work, should be able to state why each change improves the text.

Chapter Summary

This chapter introduced no new grammar system. Instead, it developed vocabulary and functional language for identifying problems in a draft, proposing and explaining specific revisions, distinguishing spoken-style from written, formal-style wording, and checking for commonly mistyped characters, all applied to the report-writing skills built in Chapter 18. You practiced naming a problem with 这句话有点..., proposing a change with 可以..., and explaining a reason with 因为...所以...更... You applied these patterns through a before-and-after Model Text, a marked-up draft comparison, a paired editor-writer speaking task, and a full revision of your own written report.

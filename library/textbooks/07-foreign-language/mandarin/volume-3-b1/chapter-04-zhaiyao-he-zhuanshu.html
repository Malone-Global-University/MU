摘要和转述

Volume III, Chapter 4: Summary and Paraphrase · Speak and Write Mandarin Chinese: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Overview and Learning Objectives

Chapter 3 taught you to explain a decision using cause, purpose, and result. This chapter asks you to take someone else's words — something a friend said, something you heard on the radio, something you read — and pass it on to a third person in your own, shorter words. You will report what someone said using 说, 觉得, and 认为, mark the source of information with words like 听说 and 据..., and practice condensing a longer passage into a short, accurate summary. This is not translation and it is not quotation: it is mediation, moving information from one speaker or one text to another listener, in Mandarin, using systems you have already built.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Report what someone said or thinks using 他说..., 她觉得..., and 他们认为... in both spoken and written form.
  2. Mark secondhand or reported information with 听说 and the more formal 据...说.
  3. Condense a longer spoken or written passage into a short, accurate spoken or written summary.
  4. Distinguish direct quotation from reported (paraphrased) information in Mandarin.
  5. Complete an integrated listening, reading, speaking, and writing task that summarizes and reports information from another source.

Communication Scenario

A friend missed an important conversation, announcement, or short article and asks you to fill them in. You will listen to a short interview, read a short passage, and then pass on the main content to someone else — briefly, accurately, and in your own words, rather than repeating every sentence.

  1. You will listen to a short interview and identify what the speaker said and thought.
  2. You will read a short passage and identify its main points.
  3. You will practice reporting what you heard or read to a partner, in summary form.
  4. You will write a short summary paragraph based on a passage, using reported-speech language.

发音 — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus: Rhythm When Reporting Speech

This chapter continues the syllable-rhythm work introduced in Chapter 3, now applied to sentences that report someone else's words. Reported-speech sentences often stack a reporting verb (说, 觉得, 认为) in front of a full clause, which can tempt learners to rush the reporting verb. Keep it clear and separate.

PhraseNote
他说 tā shuōgive 说 its own full fourth-tone fall; do not clip it before the reported clause
听说 tīngshuō听 stays first tone and level; 说 is neutral in this fixed expression — do not add stress to 说 here
据他说 jù tā shuō三 syllables, each clear; treat this as one small breath group before the reported information begins

听说 is a common word where 说 is unstressed and toneless in natural speech, unlike the fuller 他说 above. Notice this contrast when you listen to the model dialogue.

语法 — Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Grammar and Sentence Patterns: Reporting, Summarizing, and Paraphrasing

This chapter does not introduce a new sentence-building system. Instead, it develops your control of reporting verbs you already know (说, 觉得, 认为) into a full system for passing on information, and adds a small set of formulaic markers for secondhand information and concise restatement.

1. Reporting What Someone Said or Thought

PatternUseExample
Person + 说/觉得/认为 + full clausereport what someone said, felt, or believed, without quotation marks他说他不想换工作了。
Person1 + 跟/对 + Person2 + 说 + clausespecify who the information was reported to她跟我说她下个月要搬家。

Unlike English, Mandarin reported speech does not require a shift in tense markers — since Mandarin has no tense morphology, the reported clause keeps its own aspect particles (了, 过, 着) exactly as the original speaker used them. Do not add extra grammar to signal that the clause is reported; word order and the reporting verb already do that work.

2. Marking Secondhand Information

PatternUseExample
听说 + clauseintroduce information you heard from someone else, conversational register听说那家餐厅要关门了。
据 + source + 说，clauseintroduce information from a named or more formal source据报道说，那条路明天要修。

听说 and 据...说 are introduced here as formulaic markers for recognition and controlled production; they are not a license to build longer formal citation structures, which belong to later volumes.

3. Direct Quotation Versus Reported Information

TypeFormExample
Direct quotation说：“...” with quotation marks, repeats the exact original words他说：“我不想换工作。”
Reported information说 + clause, no quotation marks, restates the content in connected discourse他说他不想换工作。

A summary or paraphrase task in Mandarin, as in English, should normally use reported information rather than direct quotation. Save direct quotation for the rare case where the exact original wording matters.

4. Signaling a Summary or Restatement

PatternUseExample
简单来说，...open a brief summary of a longer point简单来说，他觉得那份工作压力太大。
换句话说，...restate a point in different, usually simpler, words换句话说，他打算换一个新工作。

The new skill in this chapter is combining reporting verbs, secondhand markers, and summary phrases with the narration and explanation systems from Chapters 1 through 3 — not new individual grammar points, but a fuller use of connectors and reporting language you have already met.

对话 — Model Dialogue

Model Report: 我把你没听到的话告诉你

Context: two classmates talk after a meeting one of them missed; casual, familiar register.

小美：昨天的会议我没去，你能告诉我重点吗？
大伟：可以。简单来说，老师说下个月要开始一个新项目，大家要选一个题目。
小美：那老师对时间有什么要求？
大伟：他说时间不会太紧张，不过听说很多同学已经开始准备了。
小美：换句话说，我也应该早点儿开始了？
大伟：对，我觉得是这样。据一个同学说，老师这次对内容的要求比较高。

Xiaomei: I didn't go to yesterday's meeting — can you tell me the main points? Dawei: Sure. Simply put, the teacher said that next month we're starting a new project, and everyone needs to choose a topic. Xiaomei: So what did the teacher say about the timeline? Dawei: He said the timing wouldn't be too tight, but I heard that a lot of classmates have already started preparing. Xiaomei: In other words, I should also start early? Dawei: Right, I think so. According to one classmate, the teacher's requirements for content are pretty high this time.

写作 — Writing Workshop

Writing Workshop: Writing a Summary

Write a connected summary paragraph (six to eight sentences) based on the Reading Practice passage above, reporting its content in your own words rather than copying full sentences.

写作任务 — Writing task: Type your paragraph using Pinyin input. Your paragraph must include:

  1. At least two reporting-verb sentences using 说, 觉得, or 认为 to report what李红 said or felt.
  2. One sentence using 据...说 or 听说 to report secondhand information from the passage.
  3. One sentence using 简单来说 or 换句话说 to introduce a brief restatement.
  4. At least two words from this chapter's new vocabulary (摘要, 转述, 内容, 重点, 听说, 据...说, 简单来说, 换句话说).
  5. No direct quotation — restate the passage's content rather than copying full sentences from it.

修改清单 — Editing checklist:

  • The paragraph reports the passage's content in the writer's own words, not copied sentences.
  • 说/觉得/认为, 据...说/听说, and 简单来说/换句话说 are each used correctly and appropriately.
  • Reported clauses keep their own aspect particles (了, 过, 着) rather than adding unnecessary extra grammar.
  • At least two new vocabulary words from this chapter appear naturally.
  • Chinese punctuation (，。？！) is used correctly throughout, with no unnecessary quotation marks.
  • No grammar beyond what was consolidated in earlier chapters and reviewed in this chapter is required to complete the task.

Culture and Communication

Culture Note — Passing on information carefully

When relaying secondhand information in Mandarin conversation, many speakers mark it clearly with words like 听说 or 据...说 rather than presenting it as their own firsthand knowledge, especially in workplace or public settings where accuracy and source awareness matter. This habit helps listeners judge how reliable the information is. Practices vary by speaker, setting, and how well the people involved know each other, and casual conversation among close friends is often more relaxed about marking sources explicitly.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Adding quotation marks to reported information: reported clauses after 说/觉得/认为 do not need quotation marks unless the exact original wording is being quoted directly.
  • Changing aspect particles in reported speech: unlike English tense-shifting, Mandarin reported clauses keep the same aspect particles (了, 过, 着) the original speaker used.
  • Treating 听说 and 据...说 as interchangeable: 听说 is conversational and personal, while 据...说 sounds more formal and often introduces a named or public source.
  • Summarizing by copying full sentences: a summary should restate the main content in fewer, simpler words, not repeat the original passage sentence by sentence.
  • Introducing untaught grammar or vocabulary: this chapter should showcase fluent reporting and summarizing using systems you already control, combined in a new way.

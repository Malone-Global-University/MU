正式邮件和申请

Volume III, Chapter 9: Formal Email and Applications · Speak and Write Mandarin Chinese: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Overview and Learning Objectives

Chapter 8 took you to a library service counter to register a study group in person. This chapter moves the same institutional world onto the page: you will learn to write and understand a formal email, using the polite requests (麻烦您, 请) and confirmation questions (是不是, 对不对) you developed in Chapter 8, but now shaped into the opening, body, and closing of a written formal message. You will continue to recycle 根据, 通过, and 虽然...但是, and you will meet 未 and 无 for the first time as a recognition-only preview of formal written negation in short glossed notices, alongside continued recognition of 被 and 把 from Chapter 8. This chapter closes Unit II and prepares you for the Unit II assessment in Chapter 10.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use vocabulary connected to email, formal correspondence, and written applications.
  2. Open and close a formal email using appropriate polite address and sign-off language.
  3. State a written request clearly using 麻烦您 or 请 in email form.
  4. Ask a confirmation question in writing to check a detail before submitting an application.
  5. Recognize 未 and 无 in short formal notices without being required to produce them.
  6. Read a model formal email and a short notice about an application deadline.
  7. Listen to a description of what a well-formed formal email should include.
  8. Write a complete, correctly formatted formal email requesting something from an office.

Communication Scenario

Xiao Chen and Xiao Lin's study group has now been approved to use the library study room, but they want to request a regular weekly time slot rather than booking room-by-room each time. Because this is a special request beyond the normal procedure, the staff member suggests they put it in writing as a formal email to the student services office.

  1. You will listen to a short explanation of what a formal email to an office should include.
  2. You will read a model formal email and a short posted notice about an application deadline.
  3. You will orally explain what you would include in a formal email of your own.
  4. You will write a complete formal email requesting something from an office, following the correct format.

语法 — Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Grammar and Sentence Patterns: Formal Email Structure and Written Stance

This chapter does not introduce a new core grammar structure. Instead, it takes the formal requests and confirmation questions consolidated in Chapter 8 and shows how they are organized into the fixed structure of a formal email, while continuing to recycle 根据, 通过, and 虽然...但是. 未 and 无 are previewed here for recognition only, alongside the continuing recognition-only preview of 被 and 把 from Chapter 8.

此致 and 敬礼 always appear together as a fixed pair at the end of a formal letter or email; they are learned as a set formula, not analyzed word by word at this stage.

2. Formal Requests and Confirmation Questions in Written Form

PatternUseExample
麻烦您 + verb phrasea polite written request, as effective in email as at a counter麻烦您帮我们看一下这个申请。
请 + verb phrasea clear, standard polite instruction or request in written correspondence请告诉我们这个申请需要多长时间。

The same patterns you practiced in speech in Chapter 8 now appear inside a written document; the grammar has not changed, only the medium and the surrounding formal structure.

3. Explaining a Situation with 根据, 通过, and 虽然...但是 (Recycled)

This chapter continues to recycle 根据 (to explain what a request is based on), 通过 (to explain how something was done or communicated), and 虽然...但是 (to contrast a rule with a special circumstance) inside the body of a formal email. No new complement, aspect, comparison, or modification system is introduced.

4. First Look at 未 and 无 (Recognition Only)

未收到回复的申请，请在三天后重新发送。
无附件的申请恕不受理。
Applications that have not received a reply should be resent after three days. (未 = formal written "not yet," glossed) Applications without an attachment cannot be processed. (无 = formal written "without/no," glossed)

You will see 未 and 无 in official notices and posted instructions like these throughout this chapter's reading material. For now, simply recognize that 未 functions like a more formal, written version of "not yet," and 无 functions like a more formal, written version of "没有" (without/there isn't). You are not expected to produce sentences with 未 or 无 in this volume, just as you are not yet expected to produce 被 or 把 sentences; full productive control of formal written negation is scheduled for later study.

对话 — Model Dialogue

Model Exchange: 写邮件之前的讨论

Context: before writing their email, Xiao Chen and Xiao Lin talk through what it should say; spoken planning that leads into the written email in the Reading Practice section.

小林：我们要写一封正式邮件，申请固定的学习室时间，对不对？
小陈：对，先写清楚我们是谁，然后说明我们已经用了学习室三个星期。
小林：根据你上次问到的规定，好像可以申请固定时间，但是需要说明原因。
小陈：那我们可以说，因为小组每个星期都在同一个时间见面，所以想申请固定的学习室。
小林：好，最后麻烦你再问一下，如果这次申请没有被批准，我们是不是可以再申请一次？
小陈：好主意，我把这个问题也写进邮件里。
小林：邮件最后别忘了写"此致，敬礼"，再写上我们两个人的名字。
小陈：没问题，我们发了邮件以后，通过邮箱等他们的回复就行了。

Xiao Lin: We need to write a formal email applying for a fixed study-room time, right? Xiao Chen: Right — first write clearly who we are, then explain that we've already used the study room for three weeks. Xiao Lin: Based on what you asked about last time, it seems we can apply for a fixed time, but we need to explain the reason. Xiao Chen: Then we can say that because the group meets at the same time every week, we'd like to apply for a fixed study room. Xiao Lin: OK, and finally, please also ask whether, if this application isn't approved, we can apply again. Xiao Chen: Good idea, I'll put that question in the email too. Xiao Lin: Don't forget to write "Sincerely, With respect" at the end of the email, then put both our names. Xiao Chen: No problem — once we've sent the email, we just wait for their reply through email.

写作 — Writing Workshop

Writing Workshop: A Complete Formal Email

Write a complete, correctly formatted formal email requesting something from an office of your choice (a school, a workplace, a service provider, or a similar institution).

写作任务 — Writing task: Type your email using Pinyin input. Your writing must include:

  1. A subject line that states the purpose of your email clearly.
  2. A formal opening address using 尊敬的 plus a title or name, followed by a colon.
  3. A body paragraph that explains who you are and what you are requesting, using 麻烦您 or 请 at least once and 根据 or 通过 at least once.
  4. At least one confirmation question, using 是不是 or ，对不对？, checking a detail about your request.
  5. A formal closing, using 此致 and 敬礼 on separate lines, followed by your name.

修改清单 — Editing checklist:

  • The subject line clearly states the purpose of the email.
  • 尊敬的 + title/name + colon opens the email correctly.
  • 麻烦您 or 请 is used correctly to make the request polite.
  • The confirmation question is grammatically correct and genuinely checks a detail.
  • 此致 and 敬礼 appear on separate lines before the sender's name.
  • Chinese punctuation (，。？！：) is used correctly throughout.
  • No grammar or vocabulary beyond what has been consolidated through Chapter 9 is required to complete the task; 被, 把, 未, and 无 are not required in your own writing.

Culture and Communication

Culture Note — Formal correspondence and written distance

Formal written Mandarin, like formal written English, tends to be more structured and more distant than everyday speech, even when the underlying request is simple. Fixed formulas such as 尊敬的 and 此致/敬礼 signal that a message belongs to a formal genre before the reader even reaches the content, much as "Dear" and "Sincerely" do in English correspondence. The exact conventions for formal email — including which title to use, how much background to give, and how quickly a reply is expected — vary by school, workplace, and region, so it is worth treating any single office's expectations as one example of common practice rather than as a fixed universal rule.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Opening a formal email like a casual chat message: starting with 你好 alone can feel too informal for an office email; 尊敬的 + title + colon is the expected formal opening.
  • Forgetting the closing pair: 此致 and 敬礼 belong together on two separate lines; using only one, or placing them elsewhere in the email, breaks the expected formal pattern.
  • Leaving out the confirmation question: a formal email that never checks understanding can leave important details unclear; a well-placed 是不是 or 对不对 question shows careful attention to the process.
  • Trying to produce your own 未 or 无 sentences: this chapter only asks you to recognize these patterns in reading, just as with 被 and 把 from Chapter 8; producing your own sentences with them is not expected until a later stage of study.
  • Assuming every office expects identical formatting: greetings, titles, and expected response times vary by institution and region; treat this chapter's model as one common pattern rather than a universal standard.

小型研究和报告

Volume III, Chapter 18: Short Research and Report · Speak and Write Mandarin Chinese: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Overview and Learning Objectives

The magazine's editorial team from Chapters 16 and 17 has one more request: a short follow-up report that goes beyond a single interview and draws on a few different sources — a survey, an article, and a short interview excerpt — to give readers a fuller picture of the topic. This chapter treats that task as a structured whole: choosing a narrow research question, pulling relevant information from more than one source, organizing that information into a short spoken or written report, and handling questions from an audience afterward. You will practice naming where information comes from, presenting findings in a logical order, and responding to follow-up questions on the spot.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. State a narrow research question and explain why it is worth investigating.
  2. Identify and name the source of a piece of information (a survey, an article, an interview, a chart) when reporting it.
  3. Organize a short report using an introduction, findings, and a conclusion.
  4. Report simple statistics and percentages accurately in context.
  5. Present short findings orally in a logical, signposted sequence.
  6. Answer follow-up questions about a presentation, asking for clarification when a question is unclear.
  7. Write a short structured report that integrates information from more than one source.

Communication Scenario

The magazine's editor wants a short, evidence-based follow-up piece on the same topic as Chapters 16 and 17 — this time based on more than one source. The study group is asked to design a narrow question, gather a few small pieces of evidence (a short survey result, a short article excerpt, and a remark from the earlier interview), and put together a short spoken report for an editorial meeting, followed by questions from the editor and other staff.

  1. You will listen to a short presentation of research findings and the question-and-answer session that follows it.
  2. You will read a short survey summary and a short article excerpt and identify what each source contributes.
  3. You will present short findings orally, in order, naming your sources, and answer follow-up questions.
  4. You will write a short structured report that draws on more than one source.

发音 — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus: Signposting a Spoken Report

No new sound system is introduced in this chapter. The focus is on the light stress and pacing that signal the structure of a spoken report — where the introduction ends, where a new finding begins, and where the conclusion starts. A brief pause after signposting phrases such as 首先 (first), 另外 (in addition), and 总的来说 (overall) helps a listener track the report's organization without needing visual slides.

PhrasePractice Focus
首先，……slight stress on 首先; brief pause after, to mark the first finding
另外，根据……even pacing; slight lengthening on 根据 before naming the source
总的来说，我们的结论是……slower, clearer pacing on 结论是 to mark the report's main claim
语法 — Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Grammar and Sentence Patterns: Citing Sources, Reporting Data, and Structuring a Report

This chapter introduces no major new grammar system. It develops a small, functional cluster of expressions for citing sources, reporting simple statistics, and organizing a short report, building on the summarizing and mediating language from Chapters 4 and 17.

1. Naming a Source with 根据

根据 + source + comment states where a piece of information comes from before presenting it.

根据这次调查，很多学生觉得压力主要来自考试。

2. Reporting Percentages

百分之 + number expresses a percentage. It typically appears as the subject of a sentence describing what a group thinks or does.

百分之六十的学生说，他们每天的学习时间超过八个小时。

3. Signposting the Structure of a Report

首先 (first), 另外 (in addition), and 总的来说 (overall) mark the introduction, additional findings, and conclusion of a short report.

首先，我们看看调查的数据；另外，我们也参考了一篇文章；总的来说，这两个来源的看法是一致的。

4. Handling Questions: Clarifying and Answering

您是说……吗？我想澄清一下 checks understanding of a question before answering it; 这个问题我可以回答一下 signals a direct response.

您是说这个数据可能不太准确吗？我想澄清一下——这个调查的样本确实比较小，这是一个限制。

Putting It Together

A responsible short report in this chapter should: (1) state a narrow question, (2) name each source before reporting what it says, (3) signpost the introduction, findings, and conclusion clearly, and (4) note at least one limitation of the evidence.

我们这次报告要回答的问题是：年轻人的压力主要来自哪里？首先，根据我们的调查，百分之六十的学生说压力主要来自考试和成绩。另外，一篇文章也提到，这种压力在不同城市的表现不完全一样。总的来说，我们的结论是，压力的来源不是单一的，需要从不同的角度理解。不过，这个调查的样本比较小，这是一个限制，结论不能代表所有学生。

范文 — Model Text

Model Text: 报告和提问 — Report and Question-and-Answer Session

小林：我们这次报告要回答的问题是，年轻人的压力主要来自哪里。首先，根据我们自己做的调查，百分之六十的学生说，压力主要来自考试和成绩。
小陈：另外，我们也参考了一篇网络文章，文章提到，这种压力在不同城市、不同学校的表现不完全一样。
小马：总的来说，我们的结论是，压力的来源不是单一的，不能只用一个原因解释。
编辑：谢谢，我可以提一个问题吗？你们的调查是在哪里做的？样本大不大？
小林：您是说调查的代表性吗？我想澄清一下——我们只调查了一个学校的三十个学生，样本比较小，这是我们报告的一个限制。
编辑：明白了，那你们建议接下来怎么做？
小陈：我们建议以后可以在更多学校做同样的调查，这样结论会更可靠。

Guided Practice

阅读练习 — Reading Practice

Reading Practice: Survey Summary and Article Excerpt

Read the short survey summary and article excerpt below. Identify what each source contributes to the research question, and note any limitation each source has. 样本 and 代表性 are glossed for recognition only.

调查摘要： 这次调查一共问了三十个学生，问题是"你的学习压力主要来自哪里？"结果显示，百分之六十的学生选择了"考试和成绩"，百分之二十五选择了"父母的期望"，剩下的学生选择了其他原因。因为这次调查的样本（yàngběn）比较小，只在一个学校做的，所以结论的代表性（dàibiǎoxìng）有限。
文章节选： 一篇讨论年轻人压力的文章提到，不同城市、不同学校的学生，压力的来源和表现不完全一样：有的地方考试压力更明显，有的地方家庭期望的影响更大。文章建议，理解这个问题的时候，不应该只看一个学校或者一个城市的情况。
Vocabulary note: 样本 (yàngběn, sample) and 代表性 (dàibiǎoxìng, representativeness) describe how research methods affect how much a conclusion can be trusted. Both are receptive only in this chapter.
  1. 调查发现，学生的压力主要来自哪两个原因？
  2. 调查摘要提到了什么限制？
  3. 文章节选补充了调查摘要没有提到的哪一点？
  4. 如果只用调查摘要，不看文章节选，报告会有什么问题？
口语练习 — Speaking Practice

Speaking Practice

交际任务 — Communicative task: 小型研究报告和提问

Working from the survey summary and article excerpt above (or a short research question of your own), present a short spoken report of two to three minutes. Use 首先, 另外, and 总的来说 to signpost your structure, name each source with 根据, report at least one percentage, and state one limitation. After your report, a partner (or you, working alone) should ask at least two follow-up questions; practice using 您是说……吗？我想澄清一下 before answering.

独立练习 — Solo alternative

Record yourself presenting the report above as the Model Text does, then record two follow-up questions you imagine an editor might ask, followed by your own answers using 澄清 and 建议 at least once each.

写作练习 — Writing Workshop

Writing Workshop: A Short Structured Report

Write a short report (one to two paragraphs) that integrates information from at least two sources — for example, the survey summary and article excerpt above, or two sources of your own choosing. Your writing must include:

  1. An opening sentence stating the research question your report addresses.
  2. At least two findings, each introduced with 首先 or 另外 and each naming its source with 根据.
  3. At least one percentage, reported using 百分之.
  4. A concluding sentence introduced with 总的来说, plus one explicitly stated 限制 of your evidence.

修改清单 — Editing checklist:

  • The research question is clearly stated in the opening sentence.
  • At least two findings appear, each with a signposting word and a named source.
  • At least one percentage is reported using 百分之 correctly.
  • A conclusion appears with 总的来说, and one limitation is explicitly named.
  • Chinese punctuation (，。？！) is used correctly throughout.

Culture and Communication Check

Culture Note — Naming limitations is a sign of credibility, not weakness

In many academic and professional settings where Mandarin is used for reporting research or findings, openly stating a study's limitations — a small sample, a single location, a narrow question — is expected and is seen as a sign of a careful, trustworthy presenter rather than a weak one. A presenter who hides these limitations, or who presents a small, local survey as if it applied to everyone, is more likely to lose an audience's trust than one who states plainly what the evidence can and cannot show. This expectation is not unique to any one region or institution, but it is worth naming explicitly for learners moving from everyday conversation into more formal or academic reporting contexts.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Reporting a finding without naming its source: always attach 根据 + source before or with the finding, not just the number itself.
  • Overgeneralizing from a small sample: a survey of thirty students at one school cannot support a claim about "all students"; name the 限制 explicitly.
  • Answering before understanding the question: if a question is unclear, use 您是说……吗？我想澄清一下 rather than guessing.
  • Presenting findings with no structure: a report without 首先/另外/总的来说 is harder for a listener to follow, even if the content is accurate.

Media Analysis and Reported Information · Speak and Write Swahili, Volume IV: Professional, Academic, Civic, and Cross-Regional Swahili

Chapter Overview

Chapter 6 follows Zainabu as her civic-education volunteers move from reading office notices to following the news that shapes the questions residents bring to Taasisi ya Elimu ya Uraia. After a radio report about a proposed change to the registration procedure sparks confusion at the ward office, Zainabu must teach her volunteers to distinguish what a report actually says a source claimed from what the report itself asserts as fact. This chapter builds the grammar of mediated information: reported speech, which lets a speaker relay someone else's words without quoting them directly; source attribution, the phrases journalists and speakers use to identify who said what; evidential framing, which signals a speaker's degree of certainty about a claim; and complex connectors that link claims, sources, and consequences across longer stretches of discourse. You will practice these tools by analyzing a news report, listening to an interview excerpt, and writing a short media summary of your own.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Form and use reported speech to relay statements, questions, and claims made by another speaker or source.
  2. Use source-attribution phrases to identify who made a claim in a report, interview, or article.
  3. Recognize and use evidential frames that signal certainty, alleged fact, or attributed opinion.
  4. Use complex connectors to link claims, sources, evidence, and consequences across sentences.
  5. Read and interpret a short news report about a civic or public issue.
  6. Understand a spoken interview excerpt and extract the main claims and their sources.
  7. Explain the difference between a reporter's claim and a source's claim to another person.
  8. Write a short, accurate media summary that attributes claims correctly and does not blur reported speech with fact.

Opening Communication Scenario

A local radio station broadcasts a short report claiming that "afisa mmoja alisema kuwa utaratibu wa usajili utabadilika mwezi ujao" ("one official said that the registration procedure will change next month"). By the next morning, several residents arrive at the Ward Office insisting that the procedure has already changed, even though the report only relayed what one unnamed official said might happen — it did not report a confirmed decision. Zainabu realizes her volunteers need to be able to separate a reported claim from an established fact, and to explain that difference clearly to worried residents. She decides to walk the volunteer team through the actual report, sentence by sentence.

  1. This chapter teaches reported speech as it appears in real news reports and interviews.
  2. Learners develop source-attribution language for identifying who said what.
  3. Learners practice evidential frames that distinguish confirmed fact from attributed claim.
  4. Zainabu's walkthrough models how to read a report critically and explain it accurately to someone else.

Pronunciation and Orthography Focus

Focus — Listening to Media Tempo and Style

Broadcast news is typically read faster and with less pausing between clauses than an office announcement, since a reporter is often compressing several claims and their sources into a short segment. Listen for the small connector words — kwamba, kuwa, kwa mujibu wa — that signal a new attributed claim is beginning; these words often carry only light stress but mark an important structural boundary in the sentence.

In writing, attributed claims are conventionally introduced with a clear reporting verb (alisema, alidai, aliripoti) followed by kuwa or kwamba, and the claim itself is written in its own complete clause. Avoid letting the reported clause and the reporter's own framing run together without a clear boundary, since this is exactly the kind of ambiguity that confused the residents in the Opening Scenario.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This section develops four tools for interpreting mediated information: reported speech, source attribution, evidential framing, and complex connectors that link claims, sources, and consequences.

Reported Speech with Kuwa and Kwamba

Pattern — Relaying What Someone Else Said

Reported speech in Swahili typically uses a reporting verb followed by kuwa or kwamba ("that") to introduce the relayed statement. The verb tense inside the reported clause generally stays as it was in the original statement rather than shifting backward the way English sometimes requires; a Swahili report of "the procedure will change" remains future-tense inside the reported clause, regardless of the tense of the reporting verb itself.

  • Afisa mmoja alisema kuwa utaratibu utabadilika mwezi ujao. One official said that the procedure will change next month. alisema (past) + kuwa + utabadilika (future, unchanged)
  • Waziri alidai kwamba tatizo hilo limetatuliwa. The minister claimed that the problem has been solved. alidai (past, claim-marking) + kwamba + limetatuliwa (perfect passive)
  • Mwandishi aliuliza kama utaratibu mpya utaanza lini. The reporter asked when the new procedure would begin. aliuliza + kama (reported question)

Choosing alisema versus alidai is not a minor stylistic choice: alisema reports a statement neutrally, while alidai marks the statement as a claim whose truth is not confirmed. Learners must track this distinction, not just the grammar of the reported clause.

Source Attribution

Pattern — Identifying Who Said What

Swahili media and public commentary use consistent phrases to identify a source: kwa mujibu wa ("according to"), chanzo kimoja kilisema ("one source said"), and naming an official title or institution rather than a private individual when the source must remain unnamed. Precise attribution lets a listener or reader judge how reliable a claim is likely to be.

  • Kwa mujibu wa Ofisi ya Mtaa, huduma hazitasitishwa. According to the Ward Office, services will not be suspended. kwa mujibu wa + institutional source
  • Chanzo kimoja cha ndani cha wizara kilisema mabadiliko bado hayajaamuliwa. One internal source at the ministry said the changes have not yet been decided. chanzo + kilisema (class 7 subject agreement)
  • Wananchi kadhaa waliliambia gazeti hilo kwamba hawajapata taarifa rasmi. Several residents told that newspaper that they have not received official information. waliliambia (object-marked: told it, the newspaper)
Building Block — Reading a News Sentence
[Source phrase] + [reporting verb + kuwa/kwamba] + [reported claim, tense preserved] + [optional evidential marker]

Mfano: Kwa mujibu wa afisa mmoja aliyeomba jina lake lisitajwe, utaratibu wa usajili "unaweza" kubadilika mwezi ujao, ingawa hakuna uthibitisho rasmi bado. Example: According to one official who asked not to be named, the registration procedure "may" change next month, although there is no official confirmation yet.

Notice that the sentence carefully marks the claim as possible ("unaweza") and unconfirmed ("hakuna uthibitisho rasmi bado"); a careful reader should not treat this sentence as announcing a confirmed decision.

Evidential Framing

Pattern — Signaling Certainty, Claim, or Doubt

Swahili speakers and writers mark how confident they are in a claim using words and phrases such as inaelekea ("it appears that"), inasemekana ("it is said that," passive of kusema), bila shaka ("without doubt"), and bado haijathibitishwa ("it has not yet been confirmed"). These markers must be read carefully; they are the primary signal of how much weight a claim deserves.

  • Inasemekana kuwa dirisha jipya litafunguliwa. It is said that a new window will be opened. inasemekana (passive-derived evidential, unconfirmed rumor)
  • Inaelekea mabadiliko hayo yataanza mwezi wa nane. It appears that those changes will begin in August. inaelekea (moderate confidence)
  • Bado haijathibitishwa ikiwa ada itapanda. It has not yet been confirmed whether the fee will rise. bado + haijathibitishwa (explicit non-confirmation)

Complex Connectors for Claims and Consequences

Pattern — Linking Sources, Claims, and Outcomes

Media analysis often requires linking a claim to its source, a claim to a contrasting claim, or a claim to its likely consequence. Useful connectors include ingawa ("although"), hata hivyo ("however"), kwa sababu ("because"), and hivyo basi ("therefore, and so"). These connectors let a summary move beyond a list of isolated sentences into a coherent account of what was claimed, by whom, and with what degree of certainty.

  • Waziri alisema huduma zitaendelea kama kawaida, ingawa chanzo kingine kilidai vinginevyo. The minister said services would continue as usual, although another source claimed otherwise. ingawa (contrast between sources)
  • Kwa sababu hakuna uthibitisho rasmi, wananchi wanashauriwa kusubiri taarifa zaidi. Because there is no official confirmation, residents are advised to wait for further information. kwa sababu (reason clause linking evidential status to advice)

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: Zainabu anawasaidia wajitolea wawili, Fatuma na Hamisi, kuchambua taarifa ya redio kuhusu mabadiliko yanayodaiwa katika utaratibu wa usajili.

(Setting: Zainabu helps two volunteers, Fatuma and Hamisi, analyze a radio report about a claimed change to the registration procedure.)

HAMISIZainabu, redioni walisema utaratibu tayari umebadilika. Kwa nini wananchi wanasema mambo tofauti ofisini?Zainabu, on the radio they said the procedure has already changed. Why are residents saying different things at the office?
ZAINABUTusikilize taarifa tena kwa makini. Taarifa ilisema "afisa mmoja alidai kuwa utaratibu unaweza kubadilika," si kwamba tayari umebadilika.Let's listen to the report again carefully. The report said "one official claimed that the procedure may change," not that it has already changed.
FATUMAKwa hiyo hilo ni dai tu, si uthibitisho?So that's just a claim, not confirmation?
ZAINABUSahihi kabisa. Taarifa yenyewe ilisema pia kwamba "bado haijathibitishwa ikiwa mabadiliko hayo yataanza mwezi ujao."Exactly right. The report itself also said that "it has not yet been confirmed whether those changes will begin next month."
HAMISIJe, chanzo cha taarifa hiyo ni nani hasa?So who exactly is the source of that report?
ZAINABUKwa mujibu wa mtangazaji, chanzo ni "afisa mmoja aliyeomba jina lake lisitajwe." Hatujui ni afisa gani, wala wadhifa wake kamili.According to the announcer, the source is "one official who asked not to be named." We don't know which official, nor their exact position.
FATUMAKwa hiyo tunapaswa kuwaambiaje wananchi wanaokuja ofisini wakiwa na wasiwasi?So how should we tell residents who come to the office worried?
ZAINABUTuwaambie kwamba taarifa hiyo ni dai la afisa mmoja asiyejulikana, na kwamba Ofisi ya Mtaa haijathibitisha mabadiliko yoyote bado.Let's tell them that this report is the claim of one unidentified official, and that the Ward Office has not confirmed any changes yet.
HAMISINadhani ni vizuri tuandike muhtasari mfupi wa jinsi ya kusoma taarifa kama hizi.I think it would be good if we wrote a short summary of how to read reports like these.
ZAINABUWazo zuri. Tutaanza na tofauti kati ya "alisema," "alidai," na "inasemekana."Good idea. We'll start with the difference between "said," "claimed," and "it is said that."

Notice how Zainabu repeatedly separates the reported claim from confirmed fact, and how she tracks the source's identity carefully even when it is unnamed.

  3. Chanzo cha taarifa hiyo ni nani, na tunajua nini kumhusu?

Guided Practice

Zoezi 1 — Kutumia Usemi Uliohabarishwa (Using Reported Speech)

Practice: relaying statements using kuwa/kwamba with tense preserved.

  1. Rewrite three direct statements as reported speech: "Ofisa alisema: 'Ada itapanda mwezi ujao,'" "Waziri alisema: 'Tatizo limetatuliwa,'" "Mwandishi aliuliza: 'Utaratibu utaanza lini?'"
  2. For each, note whether the reporting verb you chose (alisema, alidai) signals neutral relay or an unconfirmed claim.

Zoezi 2 — Kutambua Chanzo (Source Attribution)

Practice: identifying and using source-attribution phrases.

  1. Write three sentences reporting a claim, each using a different source-attribution phrase: "kwa mujibu wa," "chanzo kimoja kilisema," and naming an institution directly.
  2. Explain in one sentence how the reliability of the claim changes depending on how specific the source is.

Zoezi 3 — Kupima Uhakika (Evidential Framing)

Practice: choosing the correct evidential marker for the intended certainty level.

  1. Write one sentence using "inasemekana" for an unconfirmed rumor, one using "inaelekea" for a moderately likely claim, and one using "bado haijathibitishwa" for an explicitly unconfirmed matter.
  2. Link two of your sentences with an appropriate connector ("ingawa," "hata hivyo," or "kwa sababu").

Writing Workshop

  1. Chagua taarifa ya kuchambua. In English, choose a short news item, interview, or piece of public commentary (real, adapted, or invented) that you will summarize.
  2. Tambua madai makuu. Identify the two or three main claims made in the source, and note who made each one.
  3. Andika kwa kutumia usemi uliohabarishwa. Write two or three sentences relaying the main claims using reported speech and correct source attribution.
  4. Ongeza viashiria vya uhakika. Add at least one evidential marker that accurately reflects how confirmed or unconfirmed each claim is.
  5. Unganisha kwa viunganishi changamano. Link at least two sentences using a complex connector such as "ingawa," "hata hivyo," or "kwa sababu."
  6. Kagua kazi yako. Reread your summary and check it against the Editing Checklist below.

Prompt: Write a short media summary (100–130 words) of a news item, interview, or piece of commentary, following the model in the Reading Practice passage.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • At least two claims are relayed using correctly formed reported speech, with tense preserved appropriately.
  • Each claim is attributed to a clearly identified source, even if the source itself is unnamed in the original.
  • At least one evidential marker accurately reflects the claim's confirmed or unconfirmed status.
  • At least one complex connector links two claims, a claim and a consequence, or two contrasting sources.
  • Noun-class concord, subject/object markers, and TAM forms are accurate throughout.
  • The summary does not blur an attributed claim with a confirmed fact.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Reading News Critically

Across the Swahili-speaking world, radio remains an important source of public information alongside newspapers, television, and social media, and reports frequently rely on unnamed or loosely identified sources for sensitive or preliminary information. Learning to notice attribution and evidential framing helps learners read and listen critically, distinguishing what a report confirms from what it merely relays, which is a valuable skill in any media environment.

Regional Variation Note

Specific media outlets, broadcasting conventions, and the formality of reporting style vary across Tanzania, Kenya, and other Swahili-speaking regions. This chapter models a broadly applicable pattern of attribution and evidential framing rather than the house style of any single news organization.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Shifting Tense Inside Reported Speech

Error: shifting the tense of the reported clause backward the way English sometimes does, producing an incorrect form such as treating "utabadilika" (will change) as if it needed to become a past form inside the reported clause

Correction: keep the tense of the reported clause as it was in the original statement, as in "alisema kuwa utaratibu utabadilika," as practiced in Zoezi 1.

Common Mistake — Treating a Claim as a Confirmed Fact

Error: summarizing "afisa mmoja alidai kuwa..." as if it were an established fact, dropping the attribution and evidential framing entirely

Correction: preserve the attribution and evidential marker when summarizing, as in "kwa mujibu wa afisa mmoja, inasemekana kuwa...," as practiced in Zoezi 2 and Zoezi 3.

Common Mistake — Confusing Alisema and Alidai

Error: using "alisema" (said, neutral) and "alidai" (claimed, unconfirmed) interchangeably, obscuring how reliable a claim is meant to be

Correction: reserve "alidai" for claims whose truth is not confirmed and "alisema" for neutral statements, as discussed in the Model Dialogue and practiced in Zoezi 1.

Business, Finance, and Workplace Documentation · Speak and Write Swahili, Volume IV: Professional, Academic, Civic, and Cross-Regional Swahili

Chapter Overview

Chapter 13 continues Unit 3 as Zainabu, Fatuma, and Hamisi move from public advisories to a new kind of task: their volunteer group has grown enough that the Ward Office has asked them to submit a short workplace-style proposal requesting modest funding for printing and transport, along with a simple report accounting for supplies used during the health advisory campaign. This chapter consolidates formal requests, conditionals, passive constructions, and precise handling of numbers, quantities, and deadlines, so that learners can read and produce the kind of documentation ordinary workplaces and small organizations rely on: requests, budgets, simple invoices, and short reports. You will learn to make a polite formal request, to describe what would happen under certain conditions, to state figures and dates accurately, and to draft a short workplace report or funding proposal appropriate for a non-specialist reader.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Make and respond to formal requests for funding, materials, or approval.
  2. Use conditional structures to describe what would happen if a request is approved or delayed.
  3. Use passive constructions to describe how funds, supplies, or tasks are handled without always naming who is responsible.
  4. State quantities, prices, and totals accurately in Swahili.
  5. State and discuss deadlines and time frames precisely.
  6. Read and summarize a simple workplace report, budget, or invoice.
  7. Write a short workplace report or funding proposal appropriate for a general, non-specialist reader.
  8. Recognize when a financial or business matter requires an accountant, procurement officer, or other qualified professional rather than a volunteer's own judgment.

Opening Communication Scenario

After the advisory campaign, the Ward Office coordinator tells Zainabu's team that if they submit a short written request, the office may be able to approve a small budget for printing more advisory sheets and covering transport for two more visits to the riverside neighborhood. Hamisi wants to ask for a large, round figure "to be safe," but Fatuma insists they list the actual items and costs so the office can evaluate the request properly. Zainabu proposes they also attach a short report showing exactly how many advisory sheets were printed and distributed during the first campaign, so the request is grounded in real numbers rather than guesswork.

  1. Learners see a formal request structured around real quantities, deadlines, and reasons.
  2. Learners see conditional sentences used to describe what would follow if funding is approved.
  3. Learners see passive constructions used to describe how funds and supplies are typically handled.
  4. Fatuma models the boundary between a reasonable volunteer request and matters that require an accountant or procurement officer.

Pronunciation Focus

Focus — Numbers and Formal Terms in Speech

Workplace documents depend on numbers being heard and read correctly the first time. Practice saying compound numbers clearly and slowly, keeping each syllable distinct: shilingi elfu thelathini na tano (thirty-five thousand shillings) should be said with a small pause after elfu and again after thelathini na, rather than run together.

Loanword business terms such as bajeti, ankara, and risiti keep the five stable Swahili vowels; avoid pulling them toward their English source pronunciation. Keep penultimate stress accurate even in longer compound figures and dates.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter consolidates formal requests, conditionals, and passive constructions already active in the volume, and develops precise, controlled use of numbers, quantities, and deadlines for workplace documentation.

1. Formal Requests

Grammar Note — Making a Polite, Precise Request

A formal written request typically opens with a respectful framing, states exactly what is being requested, gives a reason, and specifies quantities or a deadline. Common frames include Tunaomba idhini ya... (We request approval for...), Tungependa kuomba... (We would like to request...), and Kwa heshima, tunaomba... (Respectfully, we request...).

  • Tunaomba idhini ya bajeti ya shilingi elfu thelathini na tano kwa ajili ya uchapishaji na usafirishaji. We request approval of a budget of thirty-five thousand shillings for printing and transport.
  • Tungependa kuomba vifaa vitano zaidi vya uchapishaji kabla ya tarehe kumi na tano. We would like to request five more printing supplies before the fifteenth.

Notice that the reason and the exact figure or date usually follow directly after the request itself, rather than appearing only at the end of the message.

2. Conditionals for Approval and Planning

Building Block — Describing What Would Happen
kama + present/future clause → result clause (real condition); -ki- or -nge- → less certain or hypothetical condition

Kama ofisi itaidhinisha ombi hili, tutachapisha tahadhari nyingine mbili. If the office approves this request, we will print two more advisories.

Tungepata bajeti hii, tungeweza kutembelea mtaa huo mara mbili zaidi. If we got this budget, we would be able to visit that neighborhood twice more.

Use kama with a present or future clause for a realistic condition tied to an actual pending decision, and -nge- for a more tentative or hypothetical plan that has not yet been decided.

3. Passive Constructions and Precise Figures

Grammar Note — Describing Handling of Funds and Reporting Numbers

Workplace reports often use the passive to describe how funds or supplies are handled without naming every individual responsible, and they depend on numbers, quantities, and dates being stated with complete accuracy.

  • Fedha zilitumika kununua karatasi na wino pekee. The funds were used only to buy paper and ink. -tumia + passive -wa → -tumika
  • Jumla ya tahadhari mia moja na hamsini zilichapishwa na kugawiwa kati ya tarehe tano na kumi na tano ya mwezi. A total of one hundred fifty advisories were printed and distributed between the fifth and the fifteenth of the month.

Combine these three systems — the formal request frame, the conditional, and the passive with precise figures — to build a short, well-organized proposal or report sentence by sentence.

Specialist Note — General Reporting Language, Not Financial or Legal Advice

This chapter teaches you to write and read simple, honest workplace requests and reports responsibly. It does not train you to perform an audit, prepare tax filings, or give binding financial or legal advice. Any real budget request or financial report should be reviewed by an accountant, treasurer, or qualified organizational officer before submission, exactly as Fatuma insists in the dialogue below.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: Zainabu, Fatuma, na Hamisi wanaandaa ombi la bajeti na ripoti fupi kwa Ofisi ya Mtaa.

(Setting: Zainabu, Fatuma, and Hamisi prepare a budget request and a short report for the Ward Office.)

HAMISITuombe tu shilingi elfu hamsini, ili tuwe na uhakika wa kutosha.Let's just request fifty thousand shillings, so we're sure we'll have enough.
FATUMAHapana, Hamisi. Lazima tuorodheshe gharama halisi: karatasi, wino, na usafiri. Ofisi haitaidhinisha kiasi kisicho na maelezo.No, Hamisi. We have to list the real costs: paper, ink, and transport. The office won't approve an amount without an explanation.
ZAINABUSawa. Karatasi na wino ni shilingi elfu ishirini, na usafiri ni shilingi elfu kumi na tano. Jumla ni shilingi elfu thelathini na tano.Right. Paper and ink are twenty thousand shillings, and transport is fifteen thousand shillings. The total is thirty-five thousand shillings.
HAMISIKama ofisi itaidhinisha ombi hili, tutafanya nini?If the office approves this request, what will we do?
ZAINABUTutachapisha tahadhari nyingine mia moja na kutembelea mtaa mara mbili zaidi kabla ya tarehe thelathini.We'll print another hundred advisories and visit the neighborhood two more times before the thirtieth.
HAMISINa kama hawataidhinisha bajeti yote?And if they don't approve the whole budget?
FATUMATungeweza kuchapisha idadi ndogo zaidi na kutumia fedha zetu wenyewe kwa usafiri. Ndiyo maana tunahitaji mpango wa pili.We could print a smaller number and use our own money for transport. That's why we need a second plan.
ZAINABUSasa, kuhusu ripoti: jumla ya tahadhari mia moja na hamsini zilichapishwa na kugawiwa kati ya tarehe tano na kumi na tano.Now, about the report: a total of one hundred fifty advisories were printed and distributed between the fifth and the fifteenth.
HAMISINionesheje risiti za gharama hizo?How should I show the receipts for those costs?
FATUMAZiambatishe kwenye ripoti, lakini usijaribu kufanya ukaguzi wa hesabu wewe mwenyewe. Mweka hazina atakagua namba hizo baadaye.Attach them to the report, but don't try to do an audit yourself. The treasurer will review those numbers later.
ZAINABUBasi tuandike rasimu fupi: ombi, gharama, jumla, na ripoti ya matumizi.So let's write a short draft: request, costs, total, and a report of what was used.

Notice how Fatuma redirects Hamisi twice — away from a vague round figure and toward itemized costs, and away from an informal audit and toward the treasurer's review — a pattern this chapter asks you to follow in your own request and report.

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension questions:

  1. Hamisi anataka kuomba kiasi gani mwanzoni, na kwa nini Fatuma hakubaliani naye?
  2. Jumla ya gharama ya karatasi, wino, na usafiri ni kiasi gani?
  3. Timu itafanya nini kama ofisi haitaidhinisha bajeti yote?
  4. Nani atakayekagua risiti na namba za gharama baadaye?

Writing Workshop

  1. Chagua hali. In English, choose a realistic (or invented) small project or task needing modest funding or a short report (supplies, transport, a small event).
  2. Andika ombi rasmi. Write one polite, formal request sentence stating exactly what is being requested and why.
  3. Ongeza masharti. Add one conditional sentence describing what would happen if the request is approved, and optionally one describing what would happen if it is not.
  4. Ongeza namba na jumla. Add sentences stating specific quantities, costs, and a total, using accurate figures.
  5. Ongeza kauli tendwa na rufaa. Add a passive sentence describing how funds or supplies were or will be handled, and end with a responsible note referring financial review to a qualified person such as a treasurer or accountant.

Prompt: Write a short workplace report or funding proposal (100–140 words) for an imagined small project or organization, using at least one formal request sentence, one conditional sentence, one passive construction, and accurate figures for costs, quantities, or a deadline, ending with a responsible note about financial review.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • At least one formal request sentence is clear and polite.
  • At least one conditional sentence correctly links approval (or denial) to a result.
  • At least one passive construction accurately describes handling of funds or supplies.
  • All figures, quantities, and dates are stated precisely and consistently.
  • The report or proposal ends with a responsible note about financial review rather than a final financial judgment.
  • Register remains formal and consistent throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Small Organizations and Local Offices

Community groups, cooperatives, small businesses, and volunteer organizations across the Swahili-speaking world regularly submit short written requests and reports to local offices, employers, or partner organizations, especially when seeking modest funding or accounting for how funds were used. Doing this well means keeping requests specific and itemized rather than vague, keeping reports honest and precise about figures, and clearly separating a volunteer's reasonable summary from matters that require a treasurer, accountant, or other qualified reviewer. This distinction builds trust between community groups and the offices or organizations they work with.

Regional Variation Note

The specific offices, forms, and conventions used for small budget requests and expenditure reports can differ between Tanzanian, Kenyan, and other Swahili-speaking institutional contexts, including how currency amounts are formally written and which office grants approval. This chapter's model remains within the stable Standard Swahili productive baseline established across the volume; broader comparative variation across regions is developed further in Chapter 14.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Requesting a Vague Round Figure Instead of Itemized Costs

Error: requesting a large, unexplained amount "to be safe," as Hamisi first suggests

Correction: list the actual items and their individual costs, then state the total, as Fatuma insists in the dialogue.

Common Mistake — Confusing Real and Hypothetical Conditionals

Error: using -nge- for a condition that is a genuine pending decision ("Tungeidhinisha ombi hili" for a request that is actually under review)

Correction: use kama with present/future for a realistic pending condition ("Kama ofisi itaidhinisha ombi hili..."), and reserve -nge- for a more tentative or currently undecided plan.

Common Mistake — Performing an Informal Audit Instead of Deferring to a Reviewer

Error: declaring the figures fully verified or certified by the volunteer team itself, as Hamisi is tempted to do

Correction: present the figures honestly and clearly, attach supporting receipts, and explicitly ask a treasurer or qualified reviewer to check them before final approval.

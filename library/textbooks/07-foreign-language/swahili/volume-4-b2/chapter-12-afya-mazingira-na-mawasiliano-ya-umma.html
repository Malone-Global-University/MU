Chapter Overview

Chapter 12 continues Unit 3 as Zainabu, Fatuma, and Hamisi turn from the volunteer registration policy to a new task: the Ward Office has asked their group to help prepare a short public advisory sheet about a seasonal health and environmental concern affecting their neighborhood. This chapter consolidates cause/result structures, passive constructions, applicative forms, and abstract nouns so that learners can explain why something happens, what should be done about it, and who is affected, in language suitable for a public information sheet. You will learn to describe causes and results precisely, to use the applicative extension to show who a message or action is intended to help, and to draft a short advisory that is clear, accurate, and appropriately cautious about matters that require expert medical or environmental judgment.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe cause-and-result relationships between environmental conditions and public health effects.
  2. Use passive constructions to describe advisories, warnings, and precautions without always naming who issues them.
  3. Use the applicative verb extension to show who an action, warning, or service is done for, to, or on behalf of.
  4. Use abstract nouns built through nominalization to discuss concepts such as prevention, cleanliness, and safety.
  5. Read and summarize a public health or environmental advisory in plain language.
  6. Write a short, clearly organized advisory or information sheet for a community audience.
  7. Distinguish general public-health guidance from specific medical advice that requires a qualified professional.
  8. Recognize when a topic requires referring the audience to a health worker, clinic, or official environmental authority rather than offering a textbook opinion.

Opening Communication Scenario

Following the success of their policy summary, the Ward Office invites Zainabu's team to help prepare a short public advisory about heavy rains expected the following week, which local health workers say tend to increase the risk of waterborne illness in low-lying parts of the neighborhood. Hamisi wants to write detailed medical guidance about symptoms and treatment, but Fatuma reminds him that their role is to explain the general precaution clearly and to direct anyone with specific symptoms to the neighborhood clinic. Zainabu suggests they focus on causes (why the rain increases risk), results (what residents should do), and a clear applicative structure showing who the advisory is written for — namely, families living near the river and anyone using shared water sources.

  1. Learners see cause/result structures used to connect weather, environment, and health outcomes.
  2. Learners see passive constructions used to state precautions and advisories generally.
  3. Learners see the applicative extension used to show who a warning or service benefits.
  4. Fatuma models the boundary between general public-health communication and specific medical advice.

Pronunciation Focus

Focus — Public Announcement Prosody

Public advisories are usually read aloud slightly more slowly and deliberately than ordinary conversation, with clear pauses between the cause, the result, and the recommended action. Practice reading a sentence like Mvua kubwa inaweza kusababisha maji machafu; kwa hiyo, chemsha maji kabla ya kunywa in three clear phrases, with a brief pause after machafu and after kwa hiyo.

Keep penultimate stress accurate even across longer compound sentences, and do not let the voice trail off at the end of the sentence — the final recommendation is the most important part of an advisory and should remain clear and audible.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter consolidates cause/result structures, passive constructions, and abstract nouns already active in the volume, and develops the applicative extension specifically for public-communication purposes.

1. Cause and Result Structures

Grammar Note — Explaining Why Something Happens

Public health and environmental communication depends on clearly linking a cause to its result. Swahili expresses this with connectors such as kwa sababu (because), kwa hiyo (therefore), hivyo (thus), and the causative extension -sha/-za added to a verb stem, meaning "to cause X to happen."

  • Mvua kubwa inasababisha mafuriko katika maeneo ya chini. Heavy rain causes flooding in low-lying areas. -sababisha (causative-derived verb)
  • Maji machafu yanaweza kueneza magonjwa; kwa hiyo, tunapaswa kuchemsha maji kabla ya kunywa. Contaminated water can spread diseases; therefore, we must boil water before drinking it.
  • Kwa sababu ya mafuriko, zahanati imeongeza huduma za usafi. Because of the flooding, the clinic has increased sanitation services.

Notice that the cause typically comes first, followed by kwa hiyo or hivyo to introduce the recommended result or action.

2. The Applicative Extension in Public Messages

Building Block — Showing Who a Message Is For
verb stem + -i-/-e- (applicative) → action done for, to, or on behalf of someone

-andika (to write) → -andikia (to write for/to someone): Tunawaandikia wakazi tahadhari hii. We are writing this advisory for the residents.

-eleza (to explain) → -elezea (to explain for/about something): Zahanati imewaelezea wakazi hatari za maji machafu. The clinic has explained to the residents the dangers of contaminated water.

The applicative extension lets a writer show exactly who benefits from or receives an action, which is essential in advisories aimed at a specific audience, such as "families living near the river."

3. Passive Constructions and Abstract Nouns in Advisories

Grammar Note — Stating Precautions Generally

As in Chapter 11, advisories often use the passive to state a precaution without naming who is responsible for enforcing it, and they rely on class 14 abstract nouns such as kinga (prevention), usafi (cleanliness), and usalama (safety) to discuss general concepts.

  • Wakazi wanashauriwa kuchemsha maji kabla ya kunywa. Residents are advised to boil water before drinking it. -shauri + passive -wa → -shauriwa
  • Kinga dhidi ya magonjwa ya maji huanza na usafi wa nyumbani. Prevention against waterborne diseases begins with cleanliness at home.

Combine these three systems — cause/result, applicative, and passive with abstract nouns — to build a concise, well-organized advisory sentence by sentence.

Specialist Note — General Advisory Language, Not Medical Guidance

This chapter teaches you to write and read general public-health and environmental advisories responsibly. It does not train you to diagnose illness, prescribe treatment, or evaluate specific environmental hazards. Any advisory you write for real use should direct readers with specific symptoms or concerns to a clinic, health worker, or the relevant environmental authority, exactly as Fatuma insists in the dialogue below.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: Zainabu, Fatuma, na Hamisi wanaandaa tahadhari fupi kuhusu mvua kubwa inayotarajiwa na hatari za maji machafu.

(Setting: Zainabu, Fatuma, and Hamisi prepare a short advisory about expected heavy rain and the risks of contaminated water.)

HAMISINadhani tunapaswa kueleza dalili zote za magonjwa ya maji, ili watu wajue wanachopaswa kuangalia.I think we should explain all the symptoms of waterborne diseases, so people know what to look for.
FATUMAHapana, Hamisi. Sisi si wataalamu wa afya. Kazi yetu ni kueleza sababu na tahadhari kwa ujumla, si kuorodhesha dalili za kitabibu.No, Hamisi. We're not health professionals. Our job is to explain the cause and the general precaution, not to list medical symptoms.
ZAINABUFatuma ana haki. Tunaweza kusema mvua kubwa inasababisha maji machafu, kwa hiyo watu wanashauriwa kuchemsha maji.Fatuma is right. We can say heavy rain causes contaminated water, therefore people are advised to boil water.
HAMISINa kama mtu ana dalili, tuseme nini?And if someone has symptoms, what should we say?
FATUMATuwaelezee wasomaji wetu wawasiliane na zahanati mara moja, badala ya kutegemea tahadhari yetu peke yake.Let's explain to our readers that they should contact the clinic right away, instead of relying only on our advisory.
ZAINABUSawa. Tunaandikia hasa familia zinazoishi karibu na mto, kwa hiyo tutaje hilo mwanzoni.Good. We're writing this especially for families living near the river, so let's mention that at the beginning.
HAMISINaona sasa: kinga huanza na usafi, sivyo? Tunaweza kutumia neno hilo badala ya kueleza kila hatua.I see now: prevention begins with cleanliness, right? We can use that word instead of explaining every step.
FATUMAHasa. Nomino kama kinga na usafi zinatusaidia kueleza wazo kubwa bila kuandika sentensi ndefu sana.Exactly. Nouns like prevention and cleanliness help us explain a big idea without writing overly long sentences.
ZAINABUBasi tuandike rasimu fupi: sababu, tahadhari, na rufaa kwa zahanati.So let's write a short draft: cause, precaution, and referral to the clinic.

Notice how Fatuma redirects Hamisi twice — away from medical detail and toward general precaution plus a clear referral — a pattern this chapter asks you to follow in your own advisory.

  1. Hamisi anataka kuongeza nini kwenye tahadhari mwanzoni?
  2. Fatuma anasema kazi yao ni nini badala ya kuorodhesha dalili za kitabibu?
  3. Timu inapendekeza watu wafanye nini kama wana dalili?
  4. Tahadhari hii imeandikiwa hasa familia gani?

Writing Workshop

  1. Chagua hali. In English, choose a realistic (or invented) local environmental condition (heavy rain, drought, dust, heat) and its general public-health implication.
  2. Andika sababu na matokeo. Write one sentence linking the environmental cause to its general health result, using kwa sababu, kwa hiyo, or a causative verb.
  3. Ongeza kauli ya kutendea. Add a sentence using an applicative verb to show who the advisory is written for.
  4. Ongeza tahadhari na nomino dhahania. Add a passive sentence stating a precaution, using at least one abstract noun such as kinga, usafi, or usalama.
  5. Andika rufaa. End your advisory with a sentence responsibly referring anyone with specific symptoms or concerns to a clinic, health worker, or official authority.

Prompt: Write a short plain-language advisory or information sheet (100–140 words) for an imagined community audience, using at least one cause/result sentence, one applicative verb, one passive construction with an abstract noun, and ending with a responsible referral to a professional resource.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • At least one cause/result sentence is used accurately.
  • At least one applicative verb correctly shows who the advisory benefits.
  • At least one passive construction with an abstract noun states a precaution.
  • The advisory clearly identifies its intended audience.
  • The advisory ends with a responsible referral rather than specific medical guidance.
  • Register remains formal and consistent throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Community Health Messaging

Community groups, religious organizations, schools, and neighborhood associations across the Swahili-speaking world frequently help distribute public health and environmental information alongside official health authorities, especially during seasonal risks such as heavy rains or drought. Doing this responsibly means keeping messages general, clear, and action-oriented, and consistently directing specific concerns to a clinic or qualified authority rather than offering personal medical opinions. This distinction protects both the audience and the volunteers preparing the message.

Regional Variation Note

The specific health authorities, terminology, and channels used for public advisories can differ between Tanzanian, Kenyan, and other Swahili-speaking institutional contexts, including which office issues a seasonal advisory and how it is distributed. This chapter's model remains within the stable Standard Swahili productive baseline established across the volume; broader comparative variation across regions is developed further in Chapter 14.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Listing Medical Symptoms Instead of General Precaution

Error: writing a detailed list of medical symptoms or treatment steps as though the textbook writer were a health professional

Correction: describe the general cause and precaution, and refer anyone with specific symptoms to a clinic or health worker, as Fatuma insists in the dialogue.

Common Mistake — Confusing the Applicative with the Passive

Error: using a passive form ("-andikwa") where an applicative form ("-andikia") is needed to show who a message is written for

Correction: use the applicative when the sentence names or implies a beneficiary ("Tunawaandikia wakazi..."), and reserve the passive for stating an action without naming who performs or receives it.

Common Mistake — Weak or Missing Cause/Result Connection

Error: stating a precaution without explaining the cause that makes it necessary, leaving the advisory unclear or unpersuasive

Correction: connect cause and result explicitly with kwa sababu, kwa hiyo, or a causative verb, as in "Maji machafu yanaweza kueneza magonjwa; kwa hiyo, chemsha maji."

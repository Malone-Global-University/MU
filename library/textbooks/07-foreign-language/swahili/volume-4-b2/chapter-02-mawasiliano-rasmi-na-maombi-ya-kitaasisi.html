Chapter Overview

Chapter 2 turns Chapter 1's self-audit into action. Having named formal register as her main reinforcement target, Zainabu now begins the concrete work of learning to write in it. This chapter teaches the building blocks of formal Swahili correspondence: standard opening and closing formulas, subjunctive forms used to make polite requests, and passive and applicative verb constructions that let institutional writing describe actions without naming a first-person actor. Zainabu's first task at Taasisi ya Elimu ya Uraia is to draft a request for meeting-room access and a short welcome email to incoming volunteers. You will follow the same process, learning to move from a conversational request to a request an institution would recognize as properly formal.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use standard formal openings and closings in Swahili emails and letters.
  2. Form and use the subjunctive final -e to make polite requests and express purpose.
  3. Construct passive verb forms to describe institutional actions without naming an agent.
  4. Construct applicative verb forms to specify who a formal action is performed for or on behalf of.
  5. Write a short, properly formatted formal email requesting institutional action.
  6. Draft a short formal welcome message appropriate for new volunteers or colleagues.
  7. Identify formal correspondence conventions in a model institutional email.
  8. Distinguish an appropriately formal request from an overly blunt or overly casual one.

Opening Communication Scenario

Zainabu's first assignment at Taasisi ya Elimu ya Uraia is smaller than she expected but no less demanding: she must write an email to the facilities office requesting use of the main meeting room for the upcoming volunteer orientation, and a short welcome email that will be sent to all new volunteers before the session. Her supervisor, Bw. Mwakalinga, reminds her that both messages will be read by people she has never met, so the tone must be respectful and impersonal rather than familiar. Zainabu's first draft of the room request reads almost like a text message to a friend — direct, first-person, and missing a proper opening or closing. Bw. Mwakalinga sits with her to revise it line by line, showing her how a request becomes formal not by adding more words, but by changing whose action the sentence foregrounds.

Pronunciation and Orthography Focus

Focus — Reading Formal Prose Aloud

Formal Swahili correspondence tends to use longer sentences than everyday speech, often built from a subject, a passive or applicative verb, and one or more prepositional phrases. When reading such sentences aloud, keep penultimate stress steady on each word rather than rushing the sentence as a single blur, and let a brief pause fall at each comma to mark the sentence's logical parts.

In writing, formal correspondence in Swahili follows the same standard spelling and punctuation rules as any other register: full stops end sentences, commas separate clauses and lists, and ng' keeps its apostrophe wherever it occurs, even in institutional titles and department names.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This section develops three tools for formal correspondence: standard opening and closing formulas, the subjunctive final -e for polite requests, and passive and applicative verb constructions for institutional description.

Formal Openings and Closings

Pattern — Framing a Formal Message

A formal Swahili email or letter typically opens with a respectful address to the recipient and states the purpose of the message early. It closes with a formula of thanks or respect and a name or title, rather than trailing off or ending abruptly.

  • Opening: Ndugu Mkuu wa Idara ya Huduma, Dear Head of the Services Department,
  • Purpose statement: Naandika barua hii kwa niaba ya Taasisi ya Elimu ya Uraia ili kuomba kibali cha kutumia ukumbi wa mikutano. I am writing this letter on behalf of the Civic Education Institute in order to request permission to use the meeting hall. kwa niaba ya + purpose clause "ili kuomba"
  • Closing: Nashukuru kwa ushirikiano wenu. Wako, Zainabu, Taasisi ya Elimu ya Uraia. Thank you for your cooperation. Yours, Zainabu, Civic Education Institute.

The Subjunctive Final -e for Polite Requests and Purpose

Pattern — Requesting Politely with -e

Where the ordinary indicative final vowel is -a, the subjunctive replaces it with -e. In formal correspondence, the subjunctive appears after verbs of requesting, wishing, or recommending, and inside purpose clauses introduced by ili.

  • Tunaomba mtupatie kibali cha kutumia ukumbi. We request that you grant us permission to use the hall. tunaomba + subjunctive "mtupatie" (mtu- + -pa- + -i- + -e, "you give us")
  • Naomba nijulishwe iwapo tarehe hiyo inafaa. I request to be informed whether that date is suitable. naomba + subjunctive passive "nijulishwe"
  • Alipanga ratiba mapema ili wajitolea wasichelewe. She arranged the schedule early so that the volunteers would not be late. ili + negative subjunctive "wasichelewe"

Notice that the subjunctive can combine with object markers and with the negative infix -si-, as in wasichelewe ("so that they not be late"). The final vowel changes to -e, but the rest of the verb template stays in its usual slot order.

Passive and Applicative Constructions in Institutional Language

Pattern — Removing the Actor, Naming the Beneficiary

Institutional Swahili frequently avoids naming who performs an action, preferring the passive extension -w- (or its allomorphs) so the focus falls on the action and the person or thing affected. The applicative extension -i-/-e- does the opposite work: it adds a beneficiary, recipient, or reason to a verb that would otherwise not include one.

  • Ukumbi utatumiwa na wajitolea zaidi ya thelathini. The hall will be used by more than thirty volunteers. -tumia + passive -w- → -tumiwa
  • Barua hii imeandikiwa idara ya huduma kwa niaba ya wajitolea wote. This letter has been written to the services department on behalf of all the volunteers. -andika + applicative -i- → -andikia, then passive-adjacent perfect "imeandikiwa"
  • Wajitolea watapatiwa nyenzo kabla ya mafunzo. The volunteers will be provided with materials before the training. -pa (give) + applicative -patia + passive -patiwa

Combining the applicative and the passive, as in watapatiwa, is common in institutional Swahili: the applicative first adds the recipient ("give to"), and the passive then removes the agent, leaving the recipient as the sentence's grammatical subject.

Building Block — Converting a Casual Request into a Formal One
[Casual: first-person verb + direct object] → [Add opening address] → [Replace first-person verb with tunaomba/naomba + subjunctive] → [Shift agent-focused verbs to passive where appropriate] → [Add formal closing]

Casual: Nataka kutumia ukumbi Jumamosi. I want to use the hall on Saturday.

Formal: Tunaomba tupatiwe kibali cha kutumia ukumbi tarehe hiyo ya Jumamosi. We request to be granted permission to use the hall on that Saturday date.

Notice that the formal version does not simply add more words; it replaces the wanting verb with a requesting verb, adds the subjunctive passive tupatiwe, and reframes the date more precisely.

Zoezi la Kuchambua — Analysis Practice:

  1. Underline the subjunctive verb form in "Tunaomba mtupatie kibali cha kutumia ukumbi," and state which final vowel it replaces.
  2. Identify the passive verb in "Ukumbi utatumiwa na wajitolea zaidi ya thelathini," and explain what has happened to the agent of the action.
  3. Rewrite the casual sentence "Nataka barua ijibiwe haraka" ("I want the letter answered quickly") as a fully formal request, including an opening address.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: Bw. Mwakalinga anamsaidia Zainabu kurekebisha rasimu yake ya kwanza ya barua ya kuomba ukumbi.

(Setting: Mr. Mwakalinga helps Zainabu revise her first draft of the hall-request letter.)

(Zainabu anaandika upya sentensi yake.)
(Zainabu rewrites her sentence.)
ZAINABUSawa: "Tunaomba tupatiwe kibali cha kutumia ukumbi tarehe ya Jumamosi." Je, hivyo ni bora?Alright: "We request to be granted permission to use the hall on the Saturday date." Is that better?
MWAKALINGAVizuri zaidi. Sasa ongeza sentensi inayoeleza idadi ya watu watakaotumia ukumbi, kwa kutumia hali ya kutendwa.Much better. Now add a sentence explaining the number of people who will use the hall, using the passive.

Writing Workshop

  1. Chagua madhumuni ya barua yako. In English, state the purpose of the formal email you will write (a request, an application, or a notice).
  2. Andika salamu na sentensi ya madhumuni. Write an opening address and a sentence stating your purpose, using "ili kuomba" or a similar formula.
  3. Tumia hali ya kutaka kwa adabu. Write at least two subjunctive request forms using "tunaomba" or "naomba."
  4. Tumia hali ya kutendwa. Write at least one sentence using a passive construction to describe an institutional action without naming its agent.
  5. Funga barua yako. Write a formal closing that thanks the recipient and identifies your role.
  6. Kagua kazi yako. Reread your email and check it against the Editing Checklist below.

Prompt: Write a 90–120-word formal email requesting institutional action (for example, permission to use a space, resources for an event, or a scheduling change), following the model in the Reading Practice passage.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • The email opens with a respectful address naming or describing the recipient.
  • The purpose of the message is stated clearly and early, using a formal request formula.
  • At least two subjunctive request forms appear, correctly formed with final -e.
  • At least one passive or applicative construction appears where appropriate.
  • The email closes with a formal thanks-and-identification formula.
  • Noun-class concord, subject/object markers, and TAM forms are accurate throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Formal Correspondence in East African Institutions

Formal Swahili correspondence within government offices, schools, civic organizations, and businesses across East Africa commonly follows the pattern modeled in this chapter: a respectful opening, a clearly stated purpose, polite subjunctive requests, agentless institutional description, and a formal closing. Requests are often expected to be specific about dates, quantities, and next steps, since institutional readers may act on many such messages each day.

Regional Variation Note

Exact conventions for salutations, closings, and titles vary somewhat between Tanzanian, Kenyan, and other Swahili-speaking institutional contexts, and between government, religious, educational, and private-sector organizations. This chapter models a broadly applicable formal pattern rather than the house style of any single institution or country.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using "Nataka" Instead of a Request Verb

Error: beginning a formal request with "nataka" (I want), which sounds abrupt and personal in institutional writing

Correction: use "tunaomba" or "naomba" followed by a subjunctive verb, as modeled in the Grammar section and Model Dialogue.

Common Mistake — Forgetting the Subjunctive Final Vowel

Error: writing "tunaomba mtupatia" instead of "tunaomba mtupatie," keeping the indicative -a after a requesting verb

Correction: check every verb that follows "tunaomba" or "naomba" for the subjunctive final -e, as practiced in Zoezi 2.

Common Mistake — Overusing the Passive Until the Sentence Becomes Unclear

Error: stacking passive forms until the reader cannot tell who is responsible for any action at all

Correction: use the passive to remove an unimportant or obvious agent, as in Zoezi 3, but keep at least one clear actor (often the institution itself) named somewhere in the message.

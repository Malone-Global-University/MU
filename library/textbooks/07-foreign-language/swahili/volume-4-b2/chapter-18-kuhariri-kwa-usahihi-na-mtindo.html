Kuhariri kwa Usahihi na Mtindo

Editing for Accuracy and Style · Speak and Write Swahili, Volume IV: Professional, Academic, Civic, and Cross-Regional Swahili

Chapter Overview

Chapter 18 continues Unit 4 as the team turns from building its argument in Chapter 17 to revising its written materials before the capstone project. Fatuma notices that their draft argument still contains a few agreement slips and an inconsistent register, while Hamisi worries that small errors could undercut an otherwise strong case in front of the community council. This chapter does not introduce a major new grammar system. Instead, it consolidates the volume's grammar, morphology, and register control by developing a systematic editing process: checking noun-class concord, verb morphology, register consistency, and paragraph coherence, and learning to diagnose an error by category rather than guessing at a fix. Learners will apply this editing checklist to a flawed sample text and to their own writing.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Apply a systematic editing checklist to a piece of advanced Swahili writing.
  2. Identify and correct noun-class and concord errors across a text.
  3. Identify and correct verb-morphology errors, including subject marker, TAM, object marker, extension, and final-vowel mistakes.
  4. Identify and correct register inconsistencies within a formal document.
  5. Diagnose an error by category (agreement, verb morphology, register, or coherence) rather than by guesswork.
  6. Improve paragraph and discourse coherence through connector choice and reference tracking.
  7. Explain, in Swahili and in English, why a specific revision improves a text.
  8. Revise a piece of your own extended writing and justify your changes.

Opening Communication Scenario

With the community council meeting approaching, the team sits down to review the written argument they drafted in Chapter 17. Fatuma reads the draft aloud and stops twice: once where an adjective does not agree with its noun, and once where a formal sentence suddenly shifts into a casual, conversational phrase that would sound out of place in front of the council. Zainabu suggests they stop editing line by line and instead work through the draft category by category — first checking noun-class agreement throughout, then checking verb forms, then checking register, and finally checking whether the paragraph holds together as a connected argument. Hamisi is skeptical that this will be faster, but Fatuma points out that fixing errors by category, rather than jumping around, catches patterns that a single read-through misses.

  1. Learners see a team notice specific error types in their own draft rather than a vague sense that "something sounds off."
  2. Learners see the team propose a category-by-category editing process instead of an unsystematic read-through.
  3. Learners see a formal-register slip identified and corrected.
  4. Fatuma models explaining why a correction improves the text, not just what the correction is.

Pronunciation Focus

Focus — Reading for Proofreading

Reading a text aloud slowly, word by word, is one of the most reliable ways to catch an agreement or verb-morphology error that the eye skips over silently. When proofreading aloud, use a slightly slower pace than normal speech, give each word its full penultimate stress, and pause briefly at the end of every clause rather than running clauses together. A missing or mismatched class prefix, a wrong TAM marker, or an incorrect final vowel is often easier to hear than to see.

When reading a formal text aloud for register consistency, listen for a sudden shift in tone: a colloquial connector or a shortened form appearing in the middle of otherwise formal, complete sentences is often audible as a change in rhythm before it is consciously identified as a register problem.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no major new grammar system. Instead, it consolidates the volume's grammar and morphology by organizing revision into four checked categories: noun-class and concord accuracy, verb-morphology accuracy, register consistency, and paragraph coherence. Each category below reviews what to check and how to name and correct a problem.

1. Editing for Noun-Class and Concord Accuracy

Building Block — Naming a Concord Error
Sentensi hii ina kosa la upatanisho. Nimeibadilisha kwa sababu + [kivumishi/kiwakilishi/kionyeshi] hakiendani na tabaka la nomino.

Rasimu: Mradi mzuri huu unahitaji msaada. Toleo la mwisho: Mradi mzuri huu unahitaji msaada. Kosa: kivumishi "mzuri" na kionyeshi "huu" vinapaswa kuendana na tabaka la nomino "mradi" (tabaka la 3). Nimeibadilisha kwa sababu upatanisho lazima uwe sahihi katika sentensi rasmi. Draft: This good project needs support. Error: the adjective and demonstrative must agree with the noun's class. I changed it because agreement must be accurate in a formal sentence.

When checking a text for concord, isolate every noun phrase and confirm that its adjective, possessive, demonstrative, and any relative marker all match the noun's class before moving to the next sentence.

2. Editing for Verb-Morphology Accuracy

Grammar Note — Checking the Verb Slot by Slot

When editing a verb, check each slot in order: subject marker, TAM marker, object marker if present, verb root, extension if present, and final vowel. An error in one slot does not mean the whole verb is wrong; identifying the specific slot makes the correction precise and the explanation clear.

  • Rasimu: Baraza watapaswa kuidhinisha upanuzi. Kosa: kiambishi cha nafsi hakiendani na "baraza" (tabaka la 5/6, umoja). Toleo la mwisho: Baraza litapaswa kuidhinisha upanuzi. Draft error: the subject marker does not agree with "baraza" (class 5/6, singular). Corrected: the council will need to approve the expansion. [subject-marker error]
  • Rasimu: Tuliamua kuwa mradi utaanza. Kosa: kiambishi cha wakati "-ta-" hakiendani na muktadha wa wakati uliopita ulioelezwa hapo awali. Toleo la mwisho: Tuliamua kuwa mradi ungeanza mwezi uliofuata. Draft error: the future marker does not fit a past-oriented context already established. Corrected: we decided that the project would start the following month. [TAM error]

3. Editing for Register Consistency

Building Block — Naming a Register Error
Sentensi hii ina kosa la mtindo. Nimeibadilisha kwa sababu maneno haya ni ya mazungumzo, si rasmi.

Rasimu: Msimamo wangu ni kwamba mradi unapaswa kupanuliwa, kwa sababu tu ni poa sana. Toleo la mwisho: Msimamo wangu ni kwamba mradi unapaswa kupanuliwa kwa sababu una manufaa dhahiri kwa jamii. Kosa: "poa" ni neno la mazungumzo lisiloendana na hoja rasmi. Nimeibadilisha kwa sababu hoja hii inaelekezwa kwa baraza rasmi. Draft: My position is that the project should be expanded, just because it's really cool. Corrected: ...because it has clear benefits for the community. Error: "cool" is a colloquial word that does not fit a formal argument.

A single colloquial word or shortened phrase inside an otherwise formal document is enough to break register consistency; check every sentence, not only the ones that seem obviously informal.

4. Editing for Paragraph Coherence

Grammar Note — Connectors and Reference Chains

Coherence editing checks whether connectors accurately signal the relationship between sentences (reason, contrast, result, sequence) and whether pronoun and noun-class reference stays clear across the paragraph. A vague "hii" or "hilo" with no clear noun-class antecedent, or a "lakini" used where "kwa sababu" is meant, both weaken coherence even when individual sentences are grammatical.

  • Rasimu: Mradi utapanuliwa. Lakini gharama zitaongezeka. Toleo la mwisho: Mradi utapanuliwa, na kwa sababu hiyo, gharama zitaongezeka kidogo. Draft: The project will be expanded. But costs will increase. Corrected: The project will be expanded, and because of that, costs will increase slightly. [connector correction: result, not contrast]
Culture Note — Revision as a Shared Professional Norm

In many Swahili-speaking professional, academic, and civic settings, a formal document is expected to pass through at least one round of careful revision before it is submitted or presented, often with a colleague or supervisor checking it. Treating a first draft as a finished product, rather than as material to be revised, is generally seen as a sign of insufficient care rather than confidence.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: Timu inahariri rasimu ya hoja yao kabla ya kikao na baraza la jamii.

(Setting: The team edits the draft of their argument before the meeting with the community council.)

FATUMANikisoma rasimu kwa sauti, naskia tatizo hapa: "Mradi mzuri huu unahitaji msaada." Kuna kosa la upatanisho.When I read the draft aloud, I hear a problem here: "This good project needs support." There is an agreement error.
HAMISIKosa liko wapi hasa? Sioni tatizo mara moja.Where exactly is the error? I don't see the problem right away.
ZAINABU"Mradi" ni tabaka la tatu. Kivumishi na kionyeshi vinapaswa kuendana nalo. Hebu tuangalie kila sentensi kwa upatanisho kwanza, kabla ya kuangalia mtindo."Mradi" is class three. The adjective and demonstrative must agree with it. Let's check every sentence for agreement first, before checking style.
FATUMASawa. Baada ya hapo, nimeona sentensi hii ina kosa la mtindo: "kwa sababu tu ni poa sana." Hilo ni neno la mazungumzo.Okay. After that, I noticed this sentence has a style error: "just because it's really cool." That's a colloquial word.
HAMISITulibadilishaje?How did we change it?
ZAINABUNimeibadilisha kwa sababu hoja hii inaelekezwa kwa baraza rasmi. Sasa inasema: "kwa sababu una manufaa dhahiri kwa jamii."I changed it because this argument is directed at a formal council. Now it says: "because it has clear benefits for the community."
FATUMANa hapa, kiungo "lakini" hakifai; uhusiano ni wa matokeo, si wa mkinzano. Ni bora tuseme "na kwa sababu hiyo."And here, the connector "but" doesn't fit; the relationship is one of result, not contrast. It's better to say "and because of that."
HAMISIKweli, mpangilio wa hoja unaonekana wazi zaidi sasa. Tuendelee kuhariri kifungu kizima kwa utaratibu huu.True, the organization of the argument seems clearer now. Let's continue editing the whole passage using this method.

Notice how the team edits by category — agreement, then style, then coherence — rather than fixing whatever they notice first.

Reading Practice

Rasimu Yenye Makosa — A Draft with Errors

Msimamo wangu ni kwamba mradi mzuri huu unapaswa kupanuliwa, kwa sababu tu ni poa sana. Wao atakubali mpango huo kesho, na baraza watapaswa kuidhinisha upanuzi wiki ijayo. Ni kweli kwamba gharama zitaongezeka. Lakini faida za muda mrefu ni kubwa zaidi. Hii hii inaonyesha kwamba mradi utafanikiwa.

Maswali ya ufahamu na uchambuzi — Comprehension and analysis questions:

  1. Tafuta kosa moja la upatanisho katika aya hii. Liko wapi, na ni nini kosa lake?
  2. Tafuta makosa mawili ya kitenzi. Ni viambishi vipi vilivyo na tatizo?
  3. Tafuta neno moja la mtindo lisilofaa kwa hoja rasmi.
  4. Andika upya aya hii, ukisahihisha makosa yote uliyoyapata, na eleza kwa sentensi mbili kwa nini marekebisho yako yanaboresha aya hiyo.

Note: this passage is adapted textbook material written for language practice, not an authentic published document. It has been deliberately written with errors for this editing task.

Writing Workshop

  1. Chagua rasimu. Choose your completed Writing Workshop argument from Chapter 17, or another piece of extended writing from earlier in this volume.
  2. Angalia upatanisho. Read through the entire text checking only noun-class and concord accuracy. Mark and correct every error you find.
  3. Angalia kitenzi. Read through the text a second time checking only verb morphology, slot by slot. Mark and correct every error you find.
  4. Angalia mtindo. Read through the text a third time checking only register consistency. Mark and correct any word or phrase that does not fit a formal audience.
  5. Angalia mshikamano. Read through the text a final time checking connector accuracy and reference clarity across the whole passage.
  6. Eleza marekebisho. Write three to five sentences explaining your most important revisions and why each one improves the text, using "Nimeibadilisha kwa sababu...".

Prompt: Submit your revised text alongside your original draft and your explanation of revisions.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • Every noun phrase shows correct class agreement across adjectives, possessives, and demonstratives.
  • Every verb shows correct subject-marker, TAM, object-marker, extension, and final-vowel forms.
  • No colloquial or overly casual word or phrase remains in a formal passage.
  • Every connector accurately signals the relationship between the sentences it joins.
  • Pronoun and demonstrative reference is clear throughout the passage.
  • The explanation of revisions names the error category for each major change.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Peer Review in Professional and Academic Settings

Across many Swahili-speaking professional, civic, and academic communities, having a colleague, classmate, or supervisor review a formal document before submission is a normal and expected step rather than a sign of weakness. Asking someone to check a document for agreement, verb form, register, and coherence is generally seen as thorough professional practice.

Regional Variation Note — Editorial Conventions Across Settings

Specific editorial conventions, such as how formally a report or letter should read, can vary somewhat by country, institution, and publication. The category-based editing process introduced in this chapter — checking concord, verb morphology, register, and coherence — is broadly applicable across Standard Swahili contexts, even where the precise formality expectations differ.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Editing Line by Line Instead of by Category

Error: reading through a text once and fixing whatever is noticed first, which often misses a pattern of errors in one category

Correction: read through the text multiple times, checking one category at a time — concord, then verb morphology, then register, then coherence — as modeled in the Model Dialogue.

Common Mistake — Correcting Without Naming the Error Category

Error: silently changing a sentence without identifying what kind of error was present, which prevents the same mistake from being recognized next time

Correction: name the error category explicitly using "Sentensi hii ina kosa la...", as practiced throughout the Grammar and Writing Workshop sections.

Common Mistake — Treating a First Draft as a Final Version

Error: submitting or presenting a first draft (rasimu) without a dedicated revision pass, assuming it is already the final version (toleo la mwisho)

Correction: apply the full editing checklist before treating any advanced document as finished, in keeping with the professional norm described in the Culture Note.

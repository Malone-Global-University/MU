Kutoa Maelekezo na Taratibu

Giving Instructions and Procedures · Speak and Write Swahili, Volume II: Expanding Everyday and Community Communication

Chapter Overview

Chapter 17 builds on the relative-identification skills from Chapter 16 and turns to a new communicative task: explaining how to do something, step by step, clearly enough for someone else to follow. You will develop the imperative for direct commands and instructions, the subjunctive final -e for polite requests and suggestions, sequence connectors such as kwanza, kisha, and mwisho, and infinitive ku- complements for explaining purpose and next steps. By the end of the chapter, you will be able to give and follow instructions for a recipe, a set of directions, or a simple technology procedure, in both speech and writing.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Form and use imperative verbs to give direct instructions and commands.
  2. Use the subjunctive final -e to make polite requests and suggestions.
  3. Sequence steps in a process using kwanza, kisha, halafu, baadaye, and mwisho.
  4. Use infinitive ku- complements to explain the purpose of a step or an action.
  5. Give and follow spoken instructions for a recipe, a set of directions, or a technology procedure.
  6. Read and write a clear, ordered set of step-by-step instructions.
  7. Identify and repair common errors involving imperative form, subjunctive form, and sequence connectors.

Opening Communication Scenario

With James's umbrella found and the lost-and-found notice posted, Zainabu wants to thank the woman who helped by inviting her to a small lunch and shows James how to prepare a simple dish. James also needs to reset a shared printer near the notice board, and Zainabu offers to walk him through the steps one at a time.

  1. This chapter introduces the imperative for direct instructions, the subjunctive final -e for polite requests and suggestions, and the sequence connectors needed to order steps clearly.
  2. It connects giving instructions to the infinitive ku- complements recycled from Chapter 8 and developed further here, and to the identification tools from Chapter 16.
  3. Recipes, directions, and simple technology steps provide the practical context for this chapter's new tools.
  4. It keeps the language within controlled, everyday procedures, not specialized technical or professional instructions, which belong to later volumes.

Pronunciation Focus

Command and Suggestion Intonation

A direct imperative such as Bonyeza kitufe hiki! ("Press this button!") is usually spoken with a slightly firmer, more level pitch and a clear final drop, while a polite subjunctive suggestion such as Tuchanganye viungo kwanza ("Let's mix the ingredients first") is spoken with a gentler, more even pitch that invites agreement rather than demanding action. Practice saying the same instruction first as a firm imperative and then as a softer subjunctive suggestion, and notice how your pitch and pacing change even though the core meaning is similar.

Keep the five vowels stable through common imperative and subjunctive endings, and remember that the final vowel itself carries important grammatical information: -a often signals an ordinary indicative form, while -e signals the subjunctive. Do not blur or reduce this final vowel, since doing so can change the meaning of the instruction.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This section develops the imperative and subjunctive for giving instructions and requests, and consolidates sequence clauses and infinitive complements for explaining ordered steps.

The Imperative — Direct Commands

Building Block — verb stem + final vowel -a (singular) / -eni (plural)
[stem] + -a (you, singular)  ·  [stem] + -eni (you, plural / polite group)

Kata vitunguu kwanza. Cut the onions first.

Bonyezeni kitufe cha kijani. (All of you) press the green button.

Geuka kulia kwenye barabara ya pili. Turn right at the second street.

Most Bantu-origin verb stems form the singular imperative simply by using the bare stem ending in -a: kata from kukata, geuka from kugeuka. Add -eni for more than one listener, or as a more formal or respectful plural form: kateni, geukeni. The imperative carries no separate subject marker, since the person addressed is already clear from context.

The Subjunctive Final -e — Polite Requests and Suggestions

Building Block — subject marker + verb stem + final vowel -e
[subject marker] + [stem] + -e = let ... / should ... / polite instruction

Tuchanganye viungo vyote kwanza. Let's mix all the ingredients first.

Ni vizuri ubonyeze kitufe hiki mara moja tu. It is good that you press this button only once.

Naomba ugeuke kushoto baada ya duka lile. Please turn left after that shop.

The subjunctive replaces the ordinary indicative final vowel -a with -e, and is used after expressions of request, suggestion, purpose, and polite necessity, such as naomba, ni vizuri, and tafadhali. Unlike the plain imperative, the subjunctive keeps a subject marker, which makes it useful for suggestions that include the speaker (tu-, "let's") as well as polite requests directed at someone else.

Sequencing Steps — Kwanza, Kisha, Halafu, Baadaye, Mwisho

Building Block — sequence connector + clause
Kwanza, ... . Kisha / Halafu, ... . Baadaye, ... . Mwisho, ... .

Kwanza, kata vitunguu. Kisha, vichanganye na nyanya. Mwisho, vipike kwa dakika kumi.

First, cut the onions. Then, mix them with the tomatoes. Finally, cook them for ten minutes.

These connectors organize a set of instructions into a clear order without requiring a new grammatical structure for each step. Kwanza opens the sequence, kisha and halafu are largely interchangeable for "then" or "next" and can be used more than once, baadaye suggests a slightly longer gap or a later point, and mwisho signals the final step.

Infinitive Ku- Complements — Explaining Purpose

Consolidation — Infinitive Complements from Chapter 8

The infinitive ku- forms introduced for study and work tasks in Chapter 8 now support explaining why a step matters: Bonyeza kitufe hiki ili kuzima printa. ("Press this button in order to turn off the printer.") Kata vitunguu vizuri ili kupika haraka. ("Cut the onions well in order to cook quickly.") The pattern ili + ku- + verb links an instruction to its purpose, and remains fully compatible with the imperative and subjunctive forms above.

  • Kwanza, geuka kulia; kisha, nenda moja kwa moja hadi kwenye duka la pili. First, turn right; then, go straight ahead to the second shop. imperative + sequence connectors
  • Naomba tuzime kompyuta kabla ya kuondoka ofisini. Please let's turn off the computer before leaving the office. subjunctive with tu- + infinitive complement

Zoezi la Sarufi — Grammar Practice:

  1. Change to the singular imperative: kukata (cut) vitunguu.
  2. Change to the plural imperative: kubonyeza (press) kitufe hiki.
  3. Complete with the subjunctive: Naomba (wewe) ___ (kugeuka) kulia hapa.
  4. Order these steps with connectors: kata vitunguu / changanya viungo / pika kwa dakika kumi.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: Zainabu anamwonyesha James jinsi ya kuandaa mchuzi rahisi wa nyanya kwa ajili ya chakula cha mchana watakachomwalika mwanamke aliyemsaidia kupata mwavuli wake.

(Setting: Zainabu shows James how to prepare a simple tomato sauce for the lunch they will offer the woman who helped find his umbrella.)

ZAINABUKwanza, kata vitunguu vidogo vidogo.First, cut the onions into small pieces.
JAMESSawa. Kisha nifanye nini?Okay. Then what should I do?
ZAINABUKisha, changanya vitunguu na nyanya kwenye sufuria.Then, mix the onions with the tomatoes in the pot.
JAMESJe, naongeze chumvi sasa?Should I add salt now?
ZAINABUNdiyo, ongeza chumvi kidogo ili ladha iwe nzuri. Baadaye, pika kwa dakika kumi.Yes, add a little salt so that the flavor is good. Afterward, cook it for ten minutes.
JAMESSawa. Na mwisho?Okay. And finally?
ZAINABUMwisho, zima jiko na uache mchuzi upoe kidogo.Finally, turn off the stove and let the sauce cool a little.
JAMESAsante kwa kunifundisha. Sasa naomba unisaidie na printa ile karibu na ubao wa matangazo.Thank you for teaching me. Now please help me with that printer near the notice board.
ZAINABUSawa. Kwanza, bonyeza kitufe cha nyuma kwa sekunde tano ili kuizima. Kisha, isubiri dakika moja, halafu uiwashe tena.Okay. First, press the back button for five seconds to turn it off. Then wait one minute, and then turn it on again.
JAMESNaomba turudie hatua ya kwanza; sikuelewa vizuri.Please let's repeat the first step; I didn't understand it well.
ZAINABUHakuna shida. Bonyeza kitufe cha nyuma, kishikilie kwa sekunde tano, kisha uiache.No problem. Press the back button, hold it for five seconds, then release it.

This dialogue combines singular and plural imperatives, the subjunctive with naomba, sequence connectors, and infinitive complements, and recycles vocabulary from earlier chapters.

  3. James anaomba Zainabu afanye nini kuhusu printa?
  4. Find one example of the subjunctive final -e in the dialogue and say what request or suggestion it expresses.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Softening Direct Instructions

In many Swahili-speaking workplaces and households, a bare imperative can sound abrupt between people who are not close or who differ in age or status, so speakers often soften instructions with tafadhali ("please"), a subjunctive suggestion, or a question form. Notice that Zainabu uses the plain imperative freely with James, a close colleague, in the cooking dialogue, but James shifts to naomba when asking for help with the printer and when asking to repeat a step, which is a common and useful pattern for polite requests in everyday Swahili.

Regional Variation Note

The imperative and subjunctive forms in this chapter, along with kwanza, kisha, halafu, baadaye, and mwisho, are used consistently across Tanzanian and Kenyan Standard Swahili. In some regions and informal registers, speakers may prefer halafu over kisha or vice versa as their default connector; both remain acceptable core production choices in this course.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing Imperative and Subjunctive Final Vowels

Error: Naomba ugeuka kulia (using the indicative -a ending after naomba, which requires the subjunctive)

Correction: Naomba ugeuke kulia — expressions such as naomba require the subjunctive final -e, not the ordinary indicative final -a.

Common Mistake — Adding a Subject Marker to the Imperative

Error: Unakata vitunguu used as a command (this actually states "you are cutting the onions," not a command)

Correction: Kata vitunguu — the plain imperative uses only the stem and final vowel -a, with no subject marker, since the person addressed is understood from context.

Common Mistake — Overusing Kisha for Every Step

Error: Kisha kata vitunguu. Kisha changanya. Kisha pika. Kisha zima jiko. (repeating kisha for the first, middle, and final steps alike)

Correction: Kwanza, kata vitunguu. Kisha, changanya. Baadaye, pika. Mwisho, zima jiko. — use kwanza to open the sequence and mwisho to close it, reserving kisha/halafu and baadaye for the steps in between.

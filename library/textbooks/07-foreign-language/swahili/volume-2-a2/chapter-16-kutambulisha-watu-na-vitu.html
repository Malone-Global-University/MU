Unit IV — Consolidation and Capstone Preparation (Chapters 16–20)
16

Kutambulisha Watu na Vitu

Relative Identification · Speak and Write Swahili, Volume II: Expanding Everyday and Community Communication

Chapter Overview

Chapter 16 builds on the public-information vocabulary, class 14 abstract nouns, and nominalized ku- forms from Chapter 15 and turns to a new communicative task: identifying and describing specific people and things clearly enough for someone else to recognize them. You will learn to use ambaye, ambao, ambacho, and ambavyo productively in controlled sentences to say things like "the woman who called," "the notice that was posted," or "the office that is closed," while continuing to treat fuller class-sensitive relative clauses as a receptive system for now. By the end of the chapter, you will be able to describe a person, a lost item, or a specific thing precisely enough for someone to identify it.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use ambaye to identify a person in a controlled sentence.
  2. Use ambao, ambacho, and ambavyo to identify things according to their noun class.
  3. Recognize other class-sensitive relative forms in reading and listening without being required to produce them independently.
  4. Describe a lost item clearly enough for someone else to identify it.
  5. Give a simple spoken or written definition of a person, place, or thing using a relative structure.
  6. Read and understand short identification texts, such as lost-and-found notices and simple definitions.
  7. Identify and repair common errors involving relative-pronoun class agreement.

Opening Communication Scenario

Back at their desks, James finds that his umbrella is missing from the stand near the notice board, and Zainabu remembers a woman from the meeting who was carrying a similar one. To sort this out, they need a way to point out exactly which umbrella, which woman, and which meeting they mean — not just "an umbrella" or "someone," but "the umbrella that is black" and "the woman who came late."

  1. This chapter introduces ambaye, ambao, ambacho, and ambavyo as productive tools for identifying a specific person or thing already mentioned or clearly understood from context.
  2. It connects relative identification to the class 14 nouns and nominalized ku- forms from Chapter 15, and to the object markers, adjective concord, and comparatives developed in earlier chapters.
  3. It keeps other class-sensitive relative markers receptive, so that you can recognize them in notices and short texts while your own productive practice stays with the four controlled forms in this chapter.
  4. Lost-and-found language, simple definitions, and descriptions of people and things provide the practical context for this new tool.

Pronunciation Focus

Relative-Clause Phrasing

Sentences built with ambaye, ambao, ambacho, and ambavyo tend to have a short pause right before the relative word, and a slightly falling pitch across the identifying clause that follows it, similar to the way an English speaker's voice dips slightly when adding "who came late" after a name. Practice reading a full identification sentence such as mwanamke ambaye alikuja kuchelewa ("the woman who came late") as one connected phrase rather than as two separate sentences glued together.

Keep the five vowels stable and even through ambaye, ambao, ambacho, and ambavyo, and place stress on the second-to-last syllable of each word, as usual: am-BA-ye, am-BA-o, am-BA-cho, am-BA-vyo.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This section introduces ambaye, ambao, ambacho, and ambavyo as productive tools for identifying a specific person or thing, and keeps other class-sensitive relative markers receptive.

Ambaye — Identifying a Person

Building Block — noun (class 1/2) + ambaye + clause
[person] + ambaye + [what they did, are, or have] = the person who ...

Huyu ni mwanamke ambaye alikuja kuchelewa. This is the woman who came late.

Nampenda mwalimu ambaye anafundisha vizuri. I like the teacher who teaches well.

Je, unamjua mtu ambaye alipoteza funguo hizi? Do you know the person who lost these keys?

Ambaye is used for class 1/2 human nouns, singular or referring back to a single person already established in context. It attaches directly after the noun it identifies, and the clause that follows behaves like an ordinary sentence with its own subject marker and tense.

Ambao, Ambacho, Ambavyo — Identifying Things

Building Block — noun (class 3/4, 7/8, 9/10) + relative form + clause
ambao = class 3/4 (m-/mi-)  ·  ambacho = class 7/8 singular (ki-)  ·  ambavyo = class 7/8 plural (vi-)

Mwavuli ambao ni mweusi ni wangu. The umbrella that is black is mine.

Kitambulisho ambacho kimepotea ni cha bluu. The ID card that is lost is blue.

Vitu ambavyo vimeachwa mezani ni vya nani? Whose are the things that were left on the table?

Each relative form must agree with the noun class of the thing being identified, in the same way that ambaye agrees with class 1/2. In this chapter, only ambaye, ambao, ambacho, and ambavyo are productive; forms for other classes, such as ambalo (class 5/6) or amba­zo (class 9/10 plural), appear in reading passages as recognition-only vocabulary and are not yet required for independent production.

Class-Sensitive Relative Clauses — Receptive Beyond This Chapter

Recognition Only — Full Relative Paradigm

Swahili has a full set of relative forms that agree with every noun class, and you may see forms such as ambalo, ambazo, or ambako in notices, readings, or dialogue in this and later chapters. You should be able to recognize that these forms serve the same identifying function as ambaye, ambao, ambacho, and ambavyo, even though full productive control of the entire relative paradigm is not required until later in the volume.

  • Begi ambalo lilipatikana ofisini ni la kahawia. The bag that was found in the office is brown. ambalo, class 5/6, receptive
  • Mwanamke ambaye alikuja kuchelewa ana begi ambalo ni kahawia. The woman who came late has a bag that is brown. ambaye productive + ambalo receptive, combined

Zoezi la Sarufi — Grammar Practice:

  1. Choose the correct relative form: Mwanamume ___ alipiga simu ni rafiki yangu. (ambaye / ambao)
  2. Choose the correct relative form: Mwavuli ___ ni mweusi ulipotea jana. (ambao / ambavyo)
  3. Choose the correct relative form: Kitambulisho ___ kimepotea kina picha yangu. (ambacho / ambaye)
  4. Combine two sentences with a relative form: Huyu ni mwalimu. Mwalimu anafundisha Kiswahili.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: James anatafuta mwavuli wake uliopotea karibu na ubao wa matangazo, na Zainabu anajaribu kumsaidia kwa kumkumbuka mwanamke aliyekuwa na mwavuli unaofanana.

(Setting: James is looking for his lost umbrella near the notice board, and Zainabu tries to help by recalling a woman who had a similar umbrella.)

JAMESSioni mwavuli wangu popote. Nafikiri niliuacha karibu na ubao wa matangazo.I don't see my umbrella anywhere. I think I left it near the notice board.
ZAINABUMwavuli ambao ni mweusi na mrefu kidogo?The umbrella that is black and a little long?
JAMESNdiyo, huo huo! Je, umeuona?Yes, that one! Have you seen it?
ZAINABUNakumbuka mwanamke ambaye alikuwa amesimama pale asubuhi hii alikuwa na mwavuli unaofanana na huo.I remember a woman who was standing there this morning had an umbrella similar to that.
JAMESUnamjua mwanamke huyo ambaye ulimwona?Do you know that woman whom you saw?
ZAINABUSijui jina lake, lakini ana alama moja: kitambulisho ambacho anavaa shingoni, chenye picha kubwa.I don't know her name, but she has one distinguishing mark: an ID card that she wears around her neck, with a big photo.
JAMESSawa. Tuandike maelezo mafupi na tuyabandike karibu na ubao wa matangazo.Okay. Let's write a short description and post it near the notice board.
ZAINABUWazo zuri. Andika: "Mwavuli ambao ni mweusi, wa mtu ambaye anavaa kitambulisho chenye picha kubwa."Good idea. Write: "An umbrella that is black, belonging to a person who wears an ID card with a big photo."
JAMESNzuri. Tutabandika karibu na tangazo lile ambalo tulisoma jana kuhusu matengenezo.Good. We'll post it near that notice that we read yesterday about the repairs.

This dialogue combines the productive relative forms ambaye, ambao, and ambacho with the receptive relative ambalo, and recycles vocabulary and passive recognition from Chapter 15.

  1. James alipoteza kitu gani, na wapi alifikiri aliviacha?

  3. Alama gani inamsaidia Zainabu kumtambua mwanamke huyo?
  4. Find one relative form in the dialogue that is receptive rather than productive at this stage, and say which noun class it agrees with.

Writing Workshop

  1. Chagua kitu au mtu. Choose a lost item or a person to describe for identification purposes.
  2. Bainisha alama muhimu. Identify two or three distinguishing features (color, size, markings, location).
  3. Andika rasimu. Write a short 60–90-word lost-and-found notice or identification description using at least two productive relative forms.
  4. Kagua kwa orodha. Check your draft against the checklist below.
  5. Andika nakala safi. Produce a clean final version for assessment.

Prompt: Write a short lost-and-found notice or identification description, describing the person or item precisely enough for someone else to recognize it.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • At least two productive relative forms (ambaye, ambao, ambacho, ambavyo) are used correctly.
  • Each relative form agrees with the correct noun class.
  • The description gives enough detail for identification without unnecessary information.
  • Any receptive relative forms used from a model text are understood, not merely copied.
  • Class 14 nouns and nominalized ku- forms from Chapter 15 remain accurate where used.
  • Capitalization, spacing, punctuation, and ng' are correct throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Describing People Respectfully

When identifying a person by description rather than by name, many Swahili-speaking workplaces and communities favor neutral, respectful features such as clothing, role, or an object a person is carrying, rather than physical characteristics that could feel intrusive. Notice that the model dialogue identifies the woman by her ID card and where she was standing, not by personal physical description, which is a useful pattern to follow when describing someone you do not know well.

Regional Variation Note

The productive forms ambaye, ambao, ambacho, and ambavyo are used consistently across Tanzanian and Kenyan Standard Swahili. In some informal spoken varieties, speakers shorten or drop the relative marker in quick conversation; such shortened forms are recognition-only at this stage and are not part of this chapter's productive standard.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using Ambaye for a Non-Human Noun

Error: Mwavuli ambaye ni mweusi (using the class 1/2 relative form for an inanimate object)

Correction: Mwavuli ambao ni mweusi — ambao agrees with class 3/4 nouns such as mwavuli; ambaye is reserved for class 1/2 human nouns.

Common Mistake — Confusing Ambacho and Ambavyo

Error: Vitu ambacho vimeachwa mezani (using the singular form with a plural noun)

Correction: Vitu ambavyo vimeachwa mezani — ambacho agrees with singular class 7 nouns (kitu), while ambavyo agrees with plural class 8 nouns (vitu).

Common Mistake — Attempting to Produce Every Relative Form Independently

Error: guessing at forms such as ambalo, ambazo, or ambako in independent speech or writing before they have been taught productively

Correction: at this stage, recognize these forms in reading and listening, but restrict your own productive identification sentences to ambaye, ambao, ambacho, and ambavyo, or rephrase using vocabulary and structures you already control.

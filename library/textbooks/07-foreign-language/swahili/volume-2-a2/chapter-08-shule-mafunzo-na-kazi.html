8

Shule, Mafunzo, na Kazi

School, Training, and Work Tasks · Speak and Write Swahili, Volume II: Expanding Everyday and Community Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe study and work duties, tasks, and daily schedules.
  2. Use infinitive ku- complements after verbs of wanting, needing, liking, knowing how, and starting/finishing.
  3. Express ability and inability using kuweza and its negative.
  4. Express need and obligation using kuhitaji, lazima, and lazima combined with infinitive complements.
  5. Form and use the habitual hu- tense to describe routines and general truths.
  6. Compare your own schedule or responsibilities with someone else's.
  7. Interpret a dialogue, a workplace notice, and a short reading about school and work routines.
  8. Write a paragraph describing responsibilities and give an oral comparison of two schedules.

Communication Scenario

James has recovered from his illness and returns to his language course and part-time job. His classmate Halima is preparing for a new training program at her workplace, and their friend Juma, a college student, is comparing his class schedule with his weekend job. As the three friends catch up, they discuss what they must do, what they know how to do, what they need to learn, and what they usually do on a typical day.

  1. This chapter develops infinitive ku- complements, which allow one verb to combine directly with another verb of wanting, needing, knowing how, or starting/finishing.
  2. Ability, need, and obligation language expands beyond the advice formulas of Chapter 7 into fuller productive patterns with kuweza, kuhitaji, and lazima.
  3. The habitual hu- form becomes productive for the first time, allowing learners to describe routines and general truths without reference to a specific day.
  4. Vocabulary expands to cover education, training programs, workplace tasks, and skills.

Pronunciation and Spelling

Kugawanya maneno marefu ya vitenzi — Segmenting long verb forms

This chapter introduces verb sequences in which one conjugated verb is followed immediately by an infinitive, such as ninataka kujifunza ("I want to learn"). These longer stretches of speech can feel dense at first. Practice segmenting each word by its slots: subject marker, TAM marker, stem, and final vowel for the first verb, then the separate infinitive ku- plus stem for the second verb.

Full phraseSegmentedListening/speaking focus
ninataka kujifunzani-na-tak-a / ku-jifunz-aSlight pause between the two verb words, not inside either one.
anaweza kufundishaa-na-wez-a / ku-fundish-aKeep penultimate stress on wéza and fundísha separately.
tunahitaji kumalizatu-na-hitaj-i / ku-maliz-aNote the negative-looking -i ending on hitaji is part of the stem, not a negative marker.
Sound Focus — The habitual hu- prefix

The habitual prefix hu- replaces the ordinary subject marker entirely and does not change by person: hufanya can mean "he/she usually does," "they usually do," or, in context, "one usually does." Because hu- looks similar to the negative second-person singular marker hu- from Chapter 7 (as in hutaki, "you don't want"), listen carefully for surrounding context, such as time expressions like kila siku ("every day") or kawaida ("usually"), to tell the two apart.

Grammar Instruction

This chapter develops infinitive complements, expands ability, need, and obligation language, and introduces the habitual hu- tense in study and work contexts.

8.1 Infinitive ku- complements

Purpose: combine a conjugated verb with a second verb to express wanting, needing, knowing how, liking, or starting/finishing an action.

Language Building Block
conjugated verb (subject marker + TAM + stem + -a/-i) + ku- + infinitive stem
Ninataka kujifunza Kiswahili zaidi.
I want to learn more Swahili.
Halima anajua kuandika ripoti.
Halima knows how to write a report.
Juma ameanza kufanya mradi mpya.
Juma has started doing a new project.

Many high-frequency verbs are regularly followed by an infinitive complement rather than by a noun object. The first verb carries the subject marker and tense; the second verb stays in its infinitive ku- form and does not change for tense or person.

VerbMeaningExample with infinitive complement
kutakato wantAnataka kusoma zaidi.
kujuato know (how)Tunajua kutumia kompyuta.
kupendato likeNapenda kufundisha.
kuanzato beginWaliaanza kufanya kazi saa mbili.
kumalizato finishNimemaliza kuandika ripoti.

8.2 Ability and inability: kuweza

Purpose: say what someone can or cannot do, especially regarding skills and tasks.

Language Building Block
subject marker + -na-/-li-/-ta- + weza + ku- + infinitive stem
Ninaweza kuzungumza Kiswahili kidogo.
I can speak a little Swahili.
Hawezi kuhudhuria mkutano leo.
He/she cannot attend the meeting today.

The negative of kuweza follows the regular negative present pattern from Chapter 7: negative subject marker + -wez- + -i, as in sifwezi → siwezi ("I cannot"). The infinitive complement after kuweza does not change.

8.3 Need and obligation: kuhitaji and lazima with infinitives

Purpose: express what is needed or required, expanding on the advice formulas from Chapter 7.

Language Building Block
subject marker + -na-/-ta- + hitaji + ku- + infinitive stem
Halima anahitaji kujifunza ujuzi mpya.
Halima needs to learn a new skill.
lazima + subject marker + -e (subjunctive) — reviewed from Ch. 7
Lazima tukamilishe mradi wiki hii.
We must complete the project this week.

Kuhitaji behaves like a regular verb and is commonly followed by an infinitive complement. Lazima, introduced in Chapter 7 for health advice, is now developed further for school and work obligations, still followed by a subject marker plus the subjunctive final vowel -e.

8.4 The habitual hu- form

Purpose: describe routines, habits, and general truths that are not tied to one specific day.

Language Building Block
hu- + stem + -a (no person-marked subject prefix)
Juma husoma chuoni kila siku.
Juma studies at college every day. (habitual/routine)
Wanafunzi hufanya mtihani kila muhula.
Students take an exam every term. (general truth)

Unlike other TAM markers, hu- does not combine with a separate person or class subject marker; the subject is understood from context or stated separately as a noun or pronoun before the verb. The habitual form contrasts with -na-, which describes an action happening now or currently in progress, rather than as a general routine.

FormStructureExampleMeaning
Present (-na-)subject marker + -na- + stem + -aJuma anasoma sasa.Juma is studying right now.
Habitual (hu-)hu- + stem + -aJuma husoma kila siku.Juma studies every day (as a routine).

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: James, Halima, na Juma wanazungumza kuhusu ratiba zao za shule na kazi baada ya darasa.

(Setting: James, Halima, and Juma talk about their school and work schedules after class.)

JAMESHabari za kazi, Halima? Nasikia una mafunzo mapya.How's work, Halima? I hear you have new training.
HALIMANdiyo. Ninahitaji kujifunza ujuzi mpya wa kompyuta kwa kazi yangu. Mafunzo huanza Jumatatu.Yes. I need to learn new computer skills for my job. The training starts on Monday.
JUMAMimi pia nina mengi ya kufanya. Husoma chuoni asubuhi na hufanya kazi dukani jioni.I also have a lot to do. I study at college in the morning and work at the shop in the evening.
JAMESUnaweza kufanya kazi mbili kwa wakati mmoja?Can you do two jobs at the same time?
JUMAWakati mwingine ninaweza, lakini leo sina nafasi. Lazima nikamilishe mradi wangu wa shule kwanza.Sometimes I can, but today I don't have time. I must complete my school project first.
HALIMARatiba yako ni tofauti na yangu. Mimi hufanya kazi asubuhi na jioni ninahudhuria mafunzo.Your schedule is different from mine. I work in the morning and attend training in the evening.
JAMESRatiba zenu zote mbili ni ngumu! Mimi ninataka tu kumaliza kozi yangu ya Kiswahili wiki hii.Both your schedules are difficult! I just want to finish my Swahili course this week.
JUMAUnaweza kufanya hivyo. Unajua kusoma na kuandika vizuri sana sasa.You can do that. You already know how to read and write very well now.
JAMESAsante. Napenda kujifunza, hata kama ni vigumu wakati mwingine.Thank you. I like learning, even though it's difficult sometimes.

Vocabulary note: ninahitaji kujifunza, anajua kusoma, and napenda kujifunza show infinitive complements from 8.1; husoma and hufanya show the habitual form from 8.4; unaweza and sina nafasi/lazima nikamilishe show ability and obligation from 8.2–8.3.

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension questions:

  1. Halima anahitaji kujifunza nini?
  2. Juma hufanya nini kila siku, asubuhi na jioni?
  3. Kwa nini Juma hawezi kufanya kazi mbili leo?
  4. Find two examples of the habitual hu- form in the dialogue. What does each one describe?
  5. Find two examples of infinitive complements after kutaka, kujua, or kupenda. Who is speaking, and what do they express?

Writing Workshop

  1. Soma mfano. Reread Maandishi 1 (Ratiba Mbili). Underline every habitual hu- form and every infinitive complement you find.
  2. Panga mawazo yako. Note your own typical daily or weekly routine, including school, training, or work responsibilities, and one thing you need or must do soon.
  3. Andika rasimu yako ya kwanza. Write a connected 90–120-word paragraph in Swahili describing your responsibilities and comparing your schedule to a friend's or family member's. Use at least two habitual forms and at least two infinitive complements.
  4. Kagua kwa orodha hapa chini. Check your draft against the editing checklist before writing your final version.
  5. Andika nakala safi. Produce a clean final version to share with a partner or keep for your records.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • At least two habitual hu- forms are used correctly (no extra subject marker attached).
  • At least two infinitive complements are used correctly after verbs such as kutaka, kujua, kuhitaji, or kuanza/kumaliza.
  • Negative forms and advice formulas from Chapter 7 remain accurate wherever they appear.
  • All verbs are written in normal unhyphenated Swahili spelling.
  • Capitalization, spacing, punctuation, and ng' are correct throughout.

Kazi ya ziada — Additional task: Exchange your draft with a partner. Write one sentence comparing your schedule with theirs, using a comparative expression you already know or a simple contrast connector.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Education, training, and lifelong learning

Continuing education and workplace training programs are common across many Swahili-speaking communities, and adults frequently combine study, work, and family responsibilities. Attitudes toward balancing these commitments, and the availability of formal training programs, vary by country, sector, and individual circumstance.

Culture in Context — Describing busy schedules modestly

When describing a demanding schedule, speakers often avoid sounding as though they are complaining or boasting, favoring factual descriptions of tasks and responsibilities over dramatic language. Comparing schedules with a friend is usually framed as mutual understanding rather than competition.

Regional Variation — Training and workplace terms

Terms for workplace training, courses, and certifications can vary by country, institution, and industry; this chapter's fictional training center, Kituo cha Mafunzo ya Ujuzi, represents a common naming pattern rather than a specific real institution. Learners should confirm current program names, requirements, and terminology locally.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Adding a subject marker to the habitual hu- form

Error: Ahusoma kila siku. (subject marker a- added before hu-)

Correction: Husoma kila siku. — The habitual hu- prefix replaces the subject marker entirely; the subject is understood from context or stated as a separate noun before the verb, as in Juma husoma kila siku.

Common Mistake — Conjugating the infinitive complement

Error: Ninataka ninajifunza Kiswahili. (second verb conjugated instead of left as infinitive)

Correction: Ninataka kujifunza Kiswahili. — Only the first verb carries the subject marker and tense; the second verb stays in its infinitive ku- form.

Common Mistake — Confusing habitual hu- with negative hu-

Error: Misreading hufanya kazi as "you don't do work," confusing it with the negative hu- from Chapter 7.

Correction: The negative hu- attaches only to second-person singular verbs and requires the final vowel -i (hufanyi, "you don't do"); the habitual hu- attaches to any subject and keeps the final vowel -a (hufanya, "usually does"). Context and the final vowel distinguish the two.

Common Mistake — Using lazima without the subjunctive -e

Error: Lazima nikamilisha mradi. (final vowel left as -a)

Correction: Lazima nikamilishe mradi. — As reviewed from Chapter 7, lazima is typically followed by a subject marker plus the subjunctive final vowel -e, not the ordinary indicative -a.

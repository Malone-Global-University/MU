Kilele cha Kiwango cha Pili

Volume II Capstone: Independent Everyday Communication · Speak and Write Swahili, Volume II: Expanding Everyday and Community Communication

Chapter Overview

Chapter 20 is the Volume II capstone. It introduces no new grammar. Instead, it asks you to demonstrate independent control of everything Volume II has developed — noun-class concord, object marking, the full present/past/future/perfect system, negation, locatives, relative identification, kama conditions, cause-result connectors, imperatives, and subjunctive requests — through a single integrated portfolio. Building on the interview project Zainabu and James completed in Chapter 19, you will now produce your own portfolio: a personal narrative, a service role-play, a listening task, a reading task, a short message, and an oral presentation. This chapter closes Volume II and prepares you for the independent-communication and connected-discourse work of Volume III.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Narrate a connected past event using accurate tense, aspect, and sequence.
  2. Handle a service encounter or workplace problem using appropriate register and polite request forms.
  3. Demonstrate listening comprehension of an extended, natural-paced conversation.
  4. Demonstrate reading comprehension of a longer informational or narrative text.
  5. Write a short practical message that correctly applies noun-class concord, tense, and connectors.
  6. Present a short oral summary of your own everyday life or community involvement, and respond to questions about it.
  7. Self-assess your performance across listening, speaking, reading, and writing using the Volume II rubric.

Opening Communication Scenario

A few weeks after finishing their community project, Zainabu and James are asked to put together a short portfolio of their own Swahili: a story about something that happened to them, a role-play about a problem they solved, a written message to a friend, and a short spoken summary of their life in the neighborhood. Their teacher reminds them that nothing new is being taught in this chapter — the task is simply to show, independently, everything they can already do.

  1. This chapter introduces no major new grammar. It assesses the full range of Volume II productive systems established in Chapters 1–19.
  2. Vocabulary in this chapter supports the four portfolio genres: personal narrative, service role-play, practical message, and oral self-presentation.
  3. Pronunciation and orthography work in this chapter focuses on final self-monitoring across an independent, unscripted performance.
  4. Receptive-only relative forms, passive constructions, and regional or colloquial variants introduced for recognition earlier in the volume are not required for independent production in this capstone unless a specific task states otherwise.

Pronunciation Focus

Final Self-Monitoring Across an Independent Performance

This chapter introduces no new sound pattern. Its pronunciation goal is entirely evaluative: across your narrative, role-play, and oral presentation, check that your five vowels remain stable and clear, that stress consistently falls on the second-to-last syllable of longer words such as uwasilishaji and kujitathmini, that ng' and other digraphs are pronounced and spelled correctly, and that your phrase rhythm sounds like connected speech rather than a list of memorized words.

Before recording or performing your final portfolio, read each piece aloud once for meaning and once specifically for pronunciation, marking any word you hesitate over so you can practice it separately.

Reviewing the Whole Volume

This section introduces no new grammar. It reviews, in condensed form, the full set of Volume II systems your capstone portfolio must demonstrate, and shows how they combine across narrative, service, and self-presentation tasks.

Narrating a Past Event — Sequence, Cause, and Result

Building Block — Sequence connector + past/perfect + kwa hiyo/hivyo + result
[Kwanza/Halafu/Baadaye], [subject marker-li/me-stem]. Kwa hiyo/Hivyo, [result].

Kwanza, basi langu lilichelewa. Halafu, nilikosa mkutano muhimu kazini. First, my bus was late. Then, I missed an important meeting at work.

Nilipomwambia meneja wangu, kwa hiyo alinielewa na hakunikasirikia. When I told my manager, so he understood me and was not angry with me. sequence connector + past + cause-result

Your narrative should move through at least three connected events using past and perfect forms, sequence connectors (kwanza, halafu, baadaye), and at least one cause-result connector, exactly as practiced from Chapter 11 onward.

The Service Role-Play — Problem, Request, and Condition

Review — Negation, Polite Requests, and Kama Conditions

Your service role-play should combine a clear statement of a problem (often in the negative: haifanyi kazi, hazitoshi, sijapata), a polite subjunctive or imperative request (tafadhali..., naomba...), and, where natural, a kama condition connecting the request to its result (kama mtaweza kusaidia, tatizo litatatuliwa). This is the same combination Zainabu and James used in their letter to the halmashauri in Chapter 18 and their project report in Chapter 19.

Identifying People and Things — Relative Recognition

Building Block — Noun + ambaye/ambao/ambacho/ambavyo + clause
[Noun] + [ambaye/ambao/ambacho/ambavyo] + [clause]

Huyu ndiye jirani ambaye alinisaidia. This is the neighbor who helped me.

Hilo ndilo tatizo ambalo tuliliripoti. That is the problem that we reported. class-sensitive relative, productive since Chapter 16

Use the controlled relative forms introduced in Chapter 16 when identifying a specific person, place, or thing in your narrative or presentation. Other class-sensitive relative forms remain receptive; you are not expected to produce them independently in this capstone.

  • Nimejifunza mengi mwaka huu kuhusu kuzungumza na majirani na kutatua matatizo. I have learned a lot this year about talking with neighbors and solving problems. perfect -me- + infinitive complements
  • Ningependa kuendelea kujifunza Kiswahili zaidi mwaka ujao. I would like to continue learning more Swahili next year. receptive conditional -nge-, glossed, not required for production

Zoezi la Sarufi — Grammar Practice:

  1. Write two connected sentences narrating a past event, using kwanza and halafu.
  2. Turn this problem statement into a polite request: (Mtandao haufanyi kazi) → ___.
  3. Combine with kama: (Mtaweza kusaidia) / (tatizo litatatuliwa).
  4. Write one sentence identifying a person using ambaye.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: James anamweleza mwalimu wake tukio lililotokea wiki iliyopita, kama mfano wa hadithi anayoitayarisha kwa ajili ya kumbukumbu yake.

(Setting: James tells his teacher about an event that happened last week, as an example of the narrative he is preparing for his portfolio.)

MWALIMUJames, unaweza kunieleza tukio moja lililotokea wiki iliyopita?James, can you tell me about one event that happened last week?
JAMESNdiyo. Kwanza, jokofu letu la nyumbani liliharibika Jumatatu. Halafu, chakula kingi kilianza kuharibika.Yes. First, our home refrigerator broke on Monday. Then, a lot of food started to spoil.
MWALIMUUlifanya nini?What did you do?
JAMESNilimpigia simu fundi ambaye alikuwa amerekebisha friji ya jirani yetu mwaka jana.I called a technician who had repaired our neighbor's fridge last year.
MWALIMUAlikuja lini?When did he come?
JAMESAlikuja siku iliyofuata. Nilimwomba tafadhali aje mapema, kwa sababu chakula kilikuwa kikiharibika haraka.He came the next day. I politely asked him to come early, because the food was spoiling quickly.
MWALIMUKama asingekuja, ungefanya nini?If he hadn't come, what would you have done?
JAMESNadhani ningenunua jokofu jipya, lakini kwa bahati nzuri, alilirekebisha vizuri, na tatizo likaisha.I think I would have bought a new refrigerator, but luckily, he repaired it well, and the problem ended.
MWALIMUHadithi nzuri, James. Umetumia wakati uliopita, kiunganishi cha sababu, na kitenzi cha uhusiano vizuri sana.Good story, James. You used the past tense, a reason connector, and a relative verb form very well.

This dialogue recycles sequence connectors, past and perfect tense, relative identification with ambaye, cause-result and reason connectors, and a polite request, all inside a single connected narrative. The teacher's hypothetical question with kama asingekuja is glossed for comprehension only and is not a productive target.

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension questions:

  1. Ni nini kilichoharibika nyumbani kwa James?
  2. James alimpigia simu nani, na kwa nini alimchagua mtu huyo?
  3. Fundi alikuja lini, na James alimwomba nini?
  4. Ni matokeo gani ya mwisho ya tukio hili?

Guided Practice

Zoezi 1 — Kupanga Hadithi Yako (Organizing Your Narrative)

Practice: outlining a personal narrative using sequence and connectors.

  1. Choose a real or realistic event from your own life (a problem, a trip, a surprise, a success).
  2. List three connected moments in order using kwanza, halafu, and baadaye.
  3. Add one cause-result sentence and one sentence using ambaye/ambacho to identify a person or thing.

Zoezi 2 — Kuandaa Ombi la Huduma (Preparing a Service Request)

Practice: describing a problem and making a polite request with a condition.

  1. Describe a service problem using a negative sentence.
  2. Write a polite subjunctive or imperative request addressing that problem.
  3. Add a kama sentence connecting the request to its likely result.

Zoezi 3 — Kujitambulisha kwa Ufupi (Brief Self-Presentation)

Practice: preparing short spoken notes for your oral presentation.

  1. Write three sentences describing your everyday life or community involvement.
  2. Write one sentence about something you have learned this year (nimejifunza...).
  3. Write one sentence about what you would like to do next (ningependa...), understanding this uses a receptive hypothetical form.

Portfolio Piece 1 — Listening: An Extended Conversation

Ask your instructor, a partner, or a text-to-speech tool to read the following extended conversation aloud twice, at a natural pace. This passage is longer and more connected than earlier chapters' listening tasks, as appropriate for a capstone assessment.

Mazungumzo Marefu — An Extended Conversation

Zainabu: Habari za asubuhi, James. Kuna nini? Unaonekana umechoka. James: Habari, Zainabu. Ndiyo, jana usiku kulikuwa na tatizo la maji nyumbani kwetu. Kwanza, maji yalikatika ghafla, halafu bomba likavuja chumbani. Nilimpigia simu fundi wa mabomba ambaye kawaida hufanya kazi mtaani mwetu, lakini hakuweza kuja mpaka asubuhi hii. Zainabu: Pole sana. Ulifanya nini usiku kucha? James: Nilijaribu kuzuia maji kwa taulo, kisha nikamsaidia mama yangu kuhamisha vitu vidogo. Kwa hiyo, hatukupata usingizi mzuri. Zainabu: Kama tatizo kama hilo likitokea tena, ungefanya nini tofauti? James: Nadhani ningehifadhi namba ya fundi mwingine pia, ili kama mmoja hawezi kuja, niwe na chaguo jingine. Zainabu: Wazo zuri. Naomba unijulishe kama tatizo litatatuliwa leo. James: Bila shaka, nitakujulisha mara tu fundi atakapomaliza kazi.

Kazi A — Kubaini mambo makuu (Main-idea task): Note what happened at James's house, what he did during the night, and what he says he would do differently next time.

Kazi B — Maswali ya ufahamu:

  1. Ni tatizo gani lililotokea nyumbani kwa James?
  2. James alifanya nini usiku kucha kwa sababu ya tatizo hilo?
  3. Kama tatizo kama hilo likitokea tena, James angefanya nini tofauti?
  4. Zainabu anaomba James amjulishe nini?

Portfolio Piece 2 — Speaking: Service Role-Play and Oral Presentation

Sehemu ya kwanza — Mchezo wa Huduma (Service Role-Play)

With a partner playing the role of a service provider, neighbor, or official, act out a short problem-and-solution exchange: describe a problem, make a polite request, and use at least one kama condition connecting the request to a likely result. Use the notes you prepared in Zoezi 2.

Sehemu ya pili — Uwasilishaji Binafsi (Oral Self-Presentation)

Present a short, two-to-three-minute spoken summary of your everyday life, community involvement, or the narrative you have prepared for this chapter. Use your notes from Zoezi 1 and Zoezi 3 as a guide, but speak without reading word for word.

Mbadala wa kujifunza peke yako — Solo alternative

Record both the role-play (playing both parts, or improvising realistic responses) and the oral presentation. Listen back for pronunciation, grammar accuracy, and natural rhythm, and note two things you would like to improve before your final performance.

Portfolio Piece 3 — Reading: A Connected Narrative Report

Mwaka Wangu wa Kwanza wa Kiswahili — My First Year of Swahili

Nilipoanza kujifunza Kiswahili mwaka mmoja uliopita, sikuweza kusema chochote isipokuwa maneno machache ya salamu. Kwanza, nilijifunza kutambulisha jina langu na kuuliza maswali rahisi. Halafu, nilianza kuzungumzia familia yangu, kazi yangu, na maisha yangu ya kila siku. Baadaye, nilijifunza kutunga sentensi ndefu zaidi, kutumia wakati uliopita na ujao, na kuelezea matatizo pamoja na masuluhisho yake. Wiki iliyopita, niliweza kufanya mahojiano na jirani yangu kuhusu maisha ya jamii, na hata niliweza kuandika ripoti fupi kuhusu matokeo ya mahojiano hayo. Bado kuna mengi ninayohitaji kujifunza, kama vile sentensi ngumu zaidi na msamiati wa kitaaluma zaidi, lakini nina uhakika kwamba nimepiga hatua kubwa. Kama nikiendelea kujifunza kwa bidii, nadhani nitaweza kuzungumza Kiswahili kwa ujasiri zaidi mwaka ujao.

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension and interpretation questions:

  1. Mwandishi aliweza kufanya nini alipoanza kujifunza Kiswahili?
  2. Ni hatua gani tatu za maendeleo anazozitaja mwandishi, kwa mpangilio?
  3. Mwandishi anasema bado anahitaji kujifunza nini?
  4. Find the sequence connectors, one cause-result connector if present, and the kama sentence at the end, and explain what each links.

Portfolio Piece 4 — Writing: Narrative and Message

  1. Chagua tukio lako. Choose the personal event you outlined in Zoezi 1.
  2. Andika hadithi. Write a short 100–150-word narrative describing the event in sequence, using past and perfect tense, at least one sequence connector, one cause-result connector, and one relative identification with ambaye/ambacho.
  3. Andika ujumbe. Write a short practical message (40–60 words) to a friend or neighbor, either asking for help related to your narrative's problem or reporting how it was resolved.
  4. Kagua kwa orodha. Check both pieces against the checklist below.
  5. Andika nakala safi. Produce clean final versions of both the narrative and the message for your portfolio.

Prompt: Write a short personal narrative about a real or realistic event, and a short practical message related to that event.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • The narrative presents events in a clear, connected sequence.
  • At least one cause-result connector (kwa hiyo or hivyo) is used correctly.
  • At least one relative identification (ambaye/ambao/ambacho/ambavyo) is used correctly.
  • The message is appropriately polite and uses correct register for its audience.
  • Noun-class concord is correct across both texts' noun phrases.
  • Verb forms (present, past, perfect, or future as needed) are chosen accurately for meaning.
  • Capitalization, spacing, punctuation, and ng' are correct throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Sharing Personal Experience Respectfully

When narrating a personal problem or difficulty, such as a household breakdown or an unresolved service issue, many Swahili-speaking communities favor a measured, matter-of-fact tone over dramatic complaint, often closing the account with a note of resolution, gratitude, or perspective, as James does at the end of the model dialogue. This tendency is worth keeping in mind when preparing your own narrative and presentation.

Regional Variation Note

The vocabulary and structures reviewed in this capstone — narrative connectors, service-request language, and self-presentation vocabulary — are stable across Tanzanian and Kenyan Standard Swahili in everyday, educational, and community contexts. As in earlier chapters, more specialized regional or institutional variants remain outside this volume's productive scope and are addressed, where relevant, in Volume III.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Narrating Without Sequence or Connection

Error: Jokofu liliharibika. Nilimpigia fundi simu. Alikuja. Alirekebisha. (isolated sentences with no connectors)

Correction: Kwanza, jokofu liliharibika. Halafu, nilimpigia fundi simu, ambaye alikuja siku iliyofuata na kulirekebisha. — a capstone narrative should connect events with sequence, cause-result, and relative tools, not present them as a disconnected list.

Common Mistake — Overusing the Conditional in Production

Error: Attempting to freely produce complex -nge- and -ngali- hypotheticals throughout the narrative because they appeared receptively in earlier chapters.

Correction: Use kama with the present, past, or future as your main productive condition tool, as scheduled across Volume II; a single well-formed -nge- sentence, closely modeled on chapter examples, is sufficient if you choose to include one.

Common Mistake — Mismatched Register in the Written Message

Error: Using the same very informal tone for a message to a close friend and a message to a landlord or official.

Correction: Match your greeting, request form, and closing to your audience — casual and warm for a friend, more formal and measured (as in Chapter 18's letter to the halmashauri) for an official or service provider.

9

Matukio ya Jamii na Mialiko

Community Events and Invitations · Speak and Write Swahili, Volume II: Expanding Everyday and Community Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Invite someone to an event and respond to an invitation, including accepting, declining, and asking for details.
  2. Give and follow commands and instructions using consolidated affirmative and negative imperative forms.
  3. Make polite requests and suggestions using the subjunctive final vowel -e after tafadhali and naomba.
  4. Use person object markers (-ni-, -ku-, -m-, -tu-, -wa-) to talk about inviting, helping, calling, and thanking specific people.
  5. Describe event logistics: time, place, guests, and arrangements.
  6. Interpret a dialogue, an invitation message, and a short reading about community events.
  7. Write an invitation and a response, and role-play making event arrangements.

Communication Scenario

Halima is organizing a small community gathering to celebrate finishing her computer training, and she wants to invite James and Juma. Juma is also helping his neighborhood plan a welcome party for new families on their street. As the three friends talk about invitations, arrangements, and who will bring what, they practice commands, polite requests, and ways of talking about specific people — inviting them, helping them, and thanking them.

  1. This chapter consolidates the imperative forms learners have encountered informally in earlier chapters, giving them full command of affirmative and negative commands.
  2. Subjunctive requests become productive for the first time, allowing learners to soften commands into polite requests and suggestions with tafadhali and naomba.
  3. Person object markers, previewed in Chapters 6–8 for people-related help and travel language, now develop into fuller productive control across a wider set of verbs.
  4. Vocabulary expands to cover events, hospitality, social planning, and public spaces.

Pronunciation and Spelling

Sauti ya mwisho katika amri na mapendekezo — Final vowel control in commands and suggestions

This chapter contrasts three verb endings that look similar but carry different meanings: the ordinary indicative -a, the imperative plural/polite -eni, and the subjunctive -e. Because these endings often distinguish a plain statement from a command or a polite request, clear articulation of the final vowel is essential to being understood correctly.

EndingFunctionExampleListening/speaking focus
-aordinary statementunakujaLevel pitch, ordinary sentence rhythm.
-e (singular imperative/subjunctive)command or polite requestnjoo / ujeSlightly crisper, shorter final vowel; often falling pitch.
-eniplural or polite/group imperativekaribuniClear final syllable -ni; do not swallow it.
Sound Focus — Tafadhali and softened requests

When tafadhali ("please") introduces a subjunctive request, speakers commonly place slight stress and a brief pause after tafadhali, then continue smoothly into the subject marker and subjunctive verb, as in tafadhali, uje mapema. This small pause signals politeness and gives the listener a moment to prepare for the request that follows.

Grammar Instruction

This chapter consolidates imperative forms, introduces subjunctive requests, and develops person object markers in event and hospitality contexts.

9.1 Imperatives, consolidated

Purpose: give direct commands, instructions, and invitations to one person or to a group.

Language Building Block
Singular affirmative: verb stem + -a (bare stem for most verbs)
Njoo! Karibu!
Come! Welcome! (to one person)
Plural or polite affirmative: verb stem + -eni
Njooni! Karibuni!
Come! Welcome! (to more than one person, or as a polite group form)
Negative singular: usi- + stem + -e
Usichelewe!
Don't be late!
Negative plural: msi- + stem + -e
Msisahau vinywaji!
Don't (you all) forget the drinks!

Learners have already encountered isolated imperative forms such as karibu and tafadhali as fixed expressions since Volume I. This chapter organizes the full system: affirmative singular, affirmative plural, negative singular, and negative plural, so that commands can be produced accurately for any addressee.

9.2 Subjunctive requests with tafadhali and naomba

Purpose: soften a command into a polite request or suggestion.

Language Building Block
Tafadhali + subject marker + stem + -e
Tafadhali uje mapema.
Please come early.
Naomba + subject marker + stem + -e
Naomba uniletee jibu kesho.
I request that you bring me an answer tomorrow.

The subjunctive final vowel -e, reviewed from lazima constructions in Chapters 7–8, now becomes productive on its own after tafadhali and naomba. Unlike the bare imperative, a subjunctive request names a subject marker, which allows requests to be made about any person, not only "you."

9.3 Person object markers, developed

Purpose: refer to a specific person as the object of inviting, helping, calling, thanking, or bringing something to them.

Language Building Block
subject marker + TAM + object marker + stem + final vowel
Nitakualika kwenye sherehe.
I will invite you to the party.
Halima anamwalika James.
Halima is inviting James.
Tunawashukuru wageni wote.
We thank all the guests.
PersonObject markerExampleMeaning
me-ni-Ananialika → AnanialikaHe/she is inviting me.
you (sg.)-ku-NitakualikaI will invite you.
him/her-m(w)-AnamwalikaHe/she is inviting him/her.
us-tu-WatatualikaThey will invite us.
them (people)-wa-TunawaalikaWe are inviting them.

The human object marker occupies the slot immediately before the verb stem, after the TAM marker, as introduced in Chapter 6 and developed through travel and service contexts in Chapters 6–8. Note that -m- becomes -mw- before a vowel-initial stem, as in -alika → anamwalika. This chapter extends productive use of all five person object markers to event, hospitality, and gratitude verbs.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: Halima anampigia simu James kumwalika kwenye sherehe yake, na baadaye anazungumza na Juma kuhusu maandalizi.

(Setting: Halima calls James to invite him to her party, and later talks with Juma about the arrangements.)

HALIMAHabari, James! Ninaandaa sherehe ndogo Jumamosi kwa sababu nimemaliza mafunzo yangu.Hi, James! I'm organizing a small party on Saturday because I've finished my training.
JAMESHongera sana! Utanialika?Congratulations! Will you invite me?
HALIMANdiyo, ninakualika sasa hivi! Njoo saa mbili mchana, tafadhali usichelewe.Yes, I'm inviting you right now! Come at eight in the morning, please don't be late.
JAMESNakubali kwa furaha! Naomba unipe anwani ya nyumbani kwako.I gladly accept! Please give me your home address.
HALIMABila shaka. Sherehe itakuwa mtaani kwangu, karibu na uwanja mdogo.Of course. The party will be in my neighborhood, near the small field.
(Baadaye, Halima anazungumza na Juma.)
HALIMAJuma, naomba unisaidie kuandaa viti na hema.Juma, I'd like you to help me set up chairs and a tent.
JUMANitakusaidia kwa furaha. Nitakuletea pia vinywaji.I'll gladly help you. I'll also bring you drinks.
HALIMAAsante sana! Tafadhali waalike majirani wetu pia. Wakaribishe wote.Thank you so much! Please invite our neighbors too. Welcome them all.
JUMANitawaalika leo jioni. Usiwe na wasiwasi, kila kitu kitakuwa tayari.I'll invite them this evening. Don't worry, everything will be ready.

Vocabulary note: njoo, usichelewe, waalike, wakaribishe, usiwe show imperative forms from 9.1; naomba unipe, naomba unisaidie show subjunctive requests from 9.2; utanialika, ninakualika, nitakusaidia, nitakuletea, nitawaalika show person object markers from 9.3.

Writing Workshop

  1. Soma mfano. Reread Maandishi 1 (Ujumbe wa Mwaliko). Underline every imperative or subjunctive-request form and every person object marker you find.
  2. Panga mawazo yako. Choose an event you would like to invite a friend or family member to, and note the day, time, place, and one thing you would like them to do or bring.
  3. Andika rasimu yako ya kwanza. Write a connected 90–120-word invitation message in Swahili, followed by a short response accepting or declining. Use at least two imperative or subjunctive-request forms and at least two person object markers.
  4. Kagua kwa orodha hapa chini. Check your draft against the editing checklist before writing your final version.
  5. Andika nakala safi. Produce a clean final version to share with a partner or keep for your records.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • At least two imperative or subjunctive-request forms are used correctly, matching the addressee (singular or plural).
  • At least two person object markers are used correctly and placed immediately before the verb stem.
  • Negative imperatives, if used, correctly combine usi-/msi- with the subjunctive -e ending.
  • Infinitive complements and habitual forms from Chapter 8 remain accurate wherever they appear.
  • Capitalization, spacing, punctuation, and ng' are correct throughout.

Kazi ya ziada — Additional task: Exchange your invitation with a partner. Write a one-sentence reply accepting or politely declining, using a subjunctive request or a person object marker.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Hospitality and community gatherings

Community gatherings, whether for a celebration, a welcome for new residents, or a shared meal, are a common way that neighbors and friends maintain relationships across many Swahili-speaking communities. Hospitality is often expressed through generous welcome and shared food, though the scale and formality of an event vary by occasion, family, and region.

Culture in Context — Softening requests

Direct commands are common in close relationships and clear instructions, but many speakers prefer softened requests with tafadhali or naomba when asking for a favor, addressing someone unfamiliar, or making a request that involves someone's time or effort. Choosing between a direct imperative and a softened request often depends on relationship, urgency, and setting.

Regional Variation — Event vocabulary

Words for specific celebrations, such as weddings, religious festivals, or community fundraising events, vary by country, community, and occasion, and are often accompanied by locally specific customs. This chapter's vocabulary covers general event and hospitality language; learners should confirm event-specific terms and customs locally.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using the singular imperative for a group

Error: Njoo, wageni wote! (singular imperative used for multiple guests)

Correction: Njooni, wageni wote! — When addressing more than one person, or when using a polite group form, the imperative takes the ending -eni, not the bare singular stem.

Common Mistake — Leaving the indicative -a ending after tafadhali

Error: Tafadhali unakuja mapema. (indicative -a ending kept after tafadhali)

Correction: Tafadhali uje mapema. — A request introduced by tafadhali or naomba requires the subjunctive final vowel -e, not the ordinary indicative -a.

Common Mistake — Omitting the object marker for a named person

Error: Ninaalika James kwenye sherehe. (object marker omitted before a known, specific person)

Correction: Ninamwalika James kwenye sherehe. — When the object is a specific, known person, Swahili commonly includes the object marker on the verb even when the person's name is also stated.

Common Mistake — Forgetting the negative imperative pattern

Error: Usichelewa! (final vowel left as -a in a negative command)

Correction: Usichelewe! — Negative commands combine usi- (singular) or msi- (plural) with the subjunctive final vowel -e, not the indicative -a.

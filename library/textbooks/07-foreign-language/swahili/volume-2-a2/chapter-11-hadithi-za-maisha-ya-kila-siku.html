Unit III — Independent Communication and Connected Discourse (Chapters 11–15)
11

Hadithi za Maisha ya Kila Siku

Stories from Daily Life · Speak and Write Swahili, Volume II: Expanding Everyday and Community Communication

Chapter Overview

Chapter 11 opens Unit 3 by moving learners from isolated sentences into connected narration. You will develop stronger control of the past tense (-li-) and the perfect/resultative (-me-), learn sequence and cause connectors that hold a story together, and meet the relative pronouns ambaye and ambalo for the first time — as receptive, glossed forms rather than as a productive target. By the end of the chapter, you will be able to tell and write a short personal narrative about something that happened to you, in an order that makes sense to a listener or reader.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Narrate a sequence of past events using -li- and connectors such as kwanza, halafu, baadaye, and mwishowe.
  2. Use the perfect/resultative -me- to describe a present state that resulted from a past action.
  3. Explain the reason for an event using kwa sababu.
  4. Recognize ambaye (who, for people) and ambalo (which, for class 5/6 nouns) in reading and listening, without being required to produce them independently.
  5. Understand a spoken narrative and a written narrative that combine several Chapter 1–10 systems.
  6. Tell and write a short, connected personal narrative about an event, accident, visit, or story from daily life.
  7. Identify and repair common narrative errors involving tense choice and sequence.

Opening Communication Scenario

A week after the neighborhood clean-up planned in Chapter 10, Halima, James, and Juma meet again. This time, Juma has a story to tell: something unexpected happened to him on his way to the clean-up. As he tells it, he uses the past tense, the perfect form, and connectors to keep the story clear and in order — exactly the tools this chapter introduces.

  1. This chapter introduces new grammar for narration: past-tense sequencing, the perfect/resultative, and cause connectors.
  2. It also previews ambaye and ambalo receptively. You only need to recognize their meaning in context; you are not asked to produce them yet.
  3. Everything from Chapters 1–10 marked C or A in the Volume II matrices remains available and is recycled throughout this chapter.

Pronunciation Focus

Listening for Sequence Markers

Connectors like kwanza, halafu, baadaye, and mwishowe often carry a small pause or slight rise in pitch just before them, marking a new step in the story. Practice reading the sample narrative below aloud, pausing briefly before each connector, and listen for this same pattern when a partner or recording narrates a story to you.

Continue applying five-vowel stability and penultimate stress to longer past-tense and perfect forms, such as a-li-po-te-za and a-me-fi-ka, where the stress falls on the second-to-last syllable even as the word grows longer.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This section develops past-tense narration, the perfect/resultative, sequence and cause connectors, and introduces the relative pronouns ambaye and ambalo as receptive-only forms.

Past Tense in Connected Narration

Building Block — Subject Marker + -li- + Stem + -a
SM + li + stem + a

Alifika saa mbili. He/she arrived at eight o'clock.

Tulikutana mjini. We met in town.

Sentences in the past narrate what happened. In a connected story, several past-tense clauses are linked by sequence connectors rather than repeated as isolated statements.

The Perfect/Resultative -me-

Building Block — Subject Marker + -me- + Stem + -a
SM + me + stem + a

Amepoteza pochi yake. He/she has lost his/her wallet (and it is still lost now).

Nimechoka. I am tired (I have become tired, and I am tired now).

Use -me- when a past action produced a state that is still true at the moment of speaking. Use -li- when you are simply narrating that an event happened, without focusing on its present result.

  • Juma alianguka njiani. Juma fell on the way. SM-li-stem-a (simple past narration)
  • Juma amevunjika mkono. Juma has broken his arm (and it is still broken). SM-me-stem-a (resultative)

Cause with kwa sababu

Building Block — Clause + kwa sababu + Clause
Result clause + kwa sababu + reason clause

Nilichelewa kwa sababu basi lilikuwa limejaa. I was late because the bus was full.

Receptive Preview — ambaye and ambalo

Language Note — Recognize, Do Not Produce Yet

Ambaye ("who") refers back to a class 1/2 person, and ambalo ("which") refers back to a class 5/6 noun. You will see both in this chapter's reading and listening passages, always glossed.

  • Rafiki ambaye alinisaidia ni Juma. The friend who helped me is Juma.
  • Tukio ambalo lilinishangaza ni hili. The event which surprised me is this one.

Full class-sensitive relative clauses are scheduled for productive use later in Volume II. For now, simply understand what ambaye and ambalo point back to when you meet them in context.

Zoezi la Sarufi — Grammar Practice:

  1. Rewrite as a resultative (perfect) statement: Alichoka jana. → Sasa ___.
  2. Complete with the correct connector: ___, niliamka. ___, nilioga. ___, nilikwenda kazini.
  3. Join the two clauses with kwa sababu: Sikwenda sokoni. Mvua ilinyesha.
  4. Underline ambaye or ambalo in the sentence and say what it refers back to: Daktari ambaye alinitibu ni mzuri sana.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: Juma anawaeleza Halima na James kuhusu jambo lililompata njiani kuja kwenye usafi wa mtaa.

(Setting: Juma tells Halima and James about something that happened to him on the way to the clean-up.)

JAMESJuma, kwa nini ulichelewa asubuhi hii?Juma, why were you late this morning?
JUMAHadithi ndefu! Kwanza, niliondoka nyumbani mapema kabisa.Long story! First, I left home very early.
HALIMAHalafu nini kilitokea?Then what happened?
JUMAHalafu, ghafla, niliteleza na kuanguka karibu na uwanja.Then, suddenly, I slipped and fell near the field.
JAMESPole sana! Umeumia?I'm so sorry! Are you hurt?
JUMASikuumia sana, lakini nimepoteza simu yangu wakati wa kuanguka.I wasn't hurt much, but I lost my phone while I was falling.
HALIMABaadaye, uliipata?Afterward, did you find it?
JUMANdiyo. Mtoto ambaye alikuwa akicheza karibu aliiona na kunipa.Yes. A child who was playing nearby saw it and gave it to me.
JAMESMwishowe, kila kitu kilikuwa sawa basi.In the end, everything was fine then.
JUMAKwa sababu ya mtoto huyo mwema, ndiyo. Nimefika hapa salama.Because of that kind child, yes. I've arrived here safely.

This dialogue combines past-tense narration (niliondoka, niliteleza, nilianguka), the perfect/resultative (nimepoteza, nimefika), sequence connectors (kwanza, halafu, baadaye, mwishowe), cause with kwa sababu, and one receptive relative clause (mtoto ambaye alikuwa akicheza).

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension questions:

  1. Kwa nini Juma alichelewa?
  2. Nini kilimpata Juma karibu na uwanja?
  3. Ni nani aliyempatia simu yake tena?
  4. Find one perfect/resultative form and explain what present state it describes.

Practice: choosing between simple past and perfect/resultative.

  1. Halima ___ (kupika) chakula jana. Bado kipo mezani sasa. (which form fits the ongoing result?)
  2. James ___ (kwenda) sokoni asubuhi hii, lakini amerudi sasa. (narrate that it happened)
  3. Juma ___ (kuvunjika) mkono na bado ana maumivu. (result still true now)

Speaking Practice

Sehemu ya kwanza — Kusimulia Hadithi (Telling a Story)

With a partner, or alone using the solo alternative, tell a short true or invented story about an event, accident, or visit. Use at least two sequence connectors, one past-tense verb, and one perfect/resultative verb.

Sehemu ya pili — Maswali ya Ufuatiliaji (Follow-up Questions)

After hearing your partner's story, ask at least two follow-up questions using kwa nini (why) or halafu nini kilitokea (then what happened), and listen for their answers using cause with kwa sababu.

Mbadala wa kujifunza peke yako — Solo alternative

Record a two-to-three-minute narrative about something that happened to you this month. Structure it with kwanza, halafu, baadaye, and mwishowe, include at least one perfect/resultative form, and explain one cause with kwa sababu. Listen back and check your tense choices.

Reading Practice

Barua Fupi kwa Rafiki — A Short Letter to a Friend

Rafiki mpendwa, nakuandikia kukueleza kuhusu ziara yangu ya wikendi iliyopita. Kwanza, nilisafiri kwa basi kwenda mjini kumtembelea shangazi yangu. Halafu, tulitembea sokoni na kununua matunda mengi. Baadaye, tulikutana na mwalimu wangu wa zamani, ambaye alifurahi sana kuniona. Mwishowe, tulirudi nyumbani jioni, tukiwa tumechoka lakini tumefurahi. Nimefurahia sana ziara hiyo kwa sababu nimeona watu wengi wanaonipenda.

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension and interpretation questions:

  1. Mwandishi alisafiri wapi na kwa nini?
  2. Ni nani waliokutana naye sokoni?
  3. Kwa nini mwandishi amefurahia ziara hiyo?
  4. Find the one relative clause with ambaye and say what noun it refers back to.
  5. Identify all four sequence connectors and one perfect/resultative verb in the letter.

Writing Workshop

  1. Chagua tukio. Choose a real or invented event, accident, or visit to narrate.
  2. Panga mpangilio. List the events in order and decide which connector fits each step.
  3. Andika rasimu. Write a connected 100–130-word narrative in Swahili using past tense, at least one perfect/resultative form, sequence connectors, and one cause clause with kwa sababu.
  4. Kagua kwa orodha. Check your draft against the checklist below.
  5. Andika nakala safi. Produce a clean final version for assessment.

Prompt: Write a short personal narrative about something that happened to you recently — a visit, a minor accident, or a memorable event — using the tools from this chapter.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • Events are presented in a clear order using at least two sequence connectors.
  • Past tense (-li-) is used to narrate what happened.
  • At least one perfect/resultative (-me-) form is used to describe a present result.
  • At least one cause is expressed with kwa sababu.
  • Noun-class concord, negation, and locatives from earlier chapters remain accurate.
  • Capitalization, spacing, punctuation, and ng' are correct throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Storytelling as Social Connection

In many Swahili-speaking communities, telling a personal story — about a journey, a minor mishap, or a visit — is a common way of maintaining relationships and showing care after time apart. Listeners often respond with short expressions of sympathy or interest, such as pole ("sorry for what happened to you") or kweli? ("really?"), rather than staying silent. As you practice narrating, notice how a good storyteller invites these responses by pausing at key moments.

Regional Variation Note

Some communities favor slightly different connectors for "then" or "afterward" in casual speech, such as regional alternatives to halafu. This chapter's productive standard uses kwanza, halafu, baadaye, mwishowe as the core narrative sequence; regional alternatives encountered elsewhere are recognition-only at this stage.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing -li- and -me-

Error: Nilipoteza simu yangu na bado sina simu. (using simple past when the present result is the point)

Correction: Nimepoteza simu yangu. — Use -me- when the past action has a result that is still true now.

Common Mistake — Stringing events without connectors

Error: Niliamka. Nilioga. Nilikula. Nilikwenda kazini. (four disconnected sentences)

Correction: Kwanza niliamka, halafu nilioga, baadaye nilikula, na mwishowe nilikwenda kazini. — Connectors turn a list of events into a story.

Common Mistake — Trying to produce ambaye/ambalo forms for other classes

Error: Attempting to invent class-sensitive relative forms for classes 7/8 or 9/10 based on ambaye and ambalo.

Correction: Only ambaye (class 1/2) and ambalo (class 5/6) are previewed in this chapter, and only receptively. Full class-sensitive relative production is scheduled for later in Volume II.

  3. Sikwenda sokoni kwa sababu mvua ilinyesha.

  2. James alikwenda sokoni asubuhi hii. (simple narration of the event)

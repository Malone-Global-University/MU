Kuelezea Watu na Uhusiano

Describing People and Relationships · Speak and Write Swahili, Volume II: Expanding Everyday and Community Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe people in greater detail using developed class 1/2 adjective, possessive, and demonstrative concord.
  2. Refer to the same person across several connected sentences without losing subject-marker agreement.
  3. Use person object markers (-ni-, -ku-, -m-, -tu-, -wa-) to refer back to people already mentioned in a conversation.
  4. Talk about family relationships, friendships, and community roles using appropriate vocabulary.
  5. Use possessive forms (wangu, wako, wake, wetu, wenu, wao) accurately with class 1/2 nouns.
  6. Interpret a dialogue and two short readings describing people and relationships.
  7. Write a connected 130–160-word paragraph describing at least three people in your family or community.

Communication Scenario

It is the second meeting of the term at Klabu ya Kiswahili in Nairobi. Tonight's activity is "Watu Muhimu Maishani Mwangu" — Important People in My Life. Members bring a photo or simply describe, from memory, three people who matter to them: a family member, a friend, and someone from their community. As members listen to each other, they practice tracking who is being talked about across several sentences, without repeating the person's name every time.

  1. This chapter develops class 1/2 concord — the noun class you reviewed in Chapter 1 — into a full descriptive and reference system for talking about people.
  2. The new productive system this chapter is the person object marker, which lets speakers refer back to someone without repeating their name in every sentence.

Pronunciation and Spelling

Mdundo wa vishazi virefu — Rhythm in longer noun phrases

As noun phrases grow longer — noun plus adjective plus possessive, for example — each word keeps its own penultimate stress. Do not shift stress to the end of the whole phrase as English speakers sometimes do.

PhraseStress pattern
mtotomTO-to
mtoto wangumTO-to WA-ngu
mtoto wangu mdogomTO-to WA-ngu DO-go
mtoto wangu mdogo mchangamfumTO-to WA-ngu DO-go cha-NGAm-fu
Sound Focus — Stacking modifiers without flattening rhythm

Practice building the phrase above one word at a time, saying it aloud after each addition. Keep every vowel clear and every word's own stress intact, even as the phrase gets longer. Pausing slightly between words is more natural in Swahili than rushing through a long noun phrase the way English speakers often do.

Grammar Instruction

This chapter develops class 1/2 concord into a full system for describing and referring to people, and introduces person object markers as a new productive system for tracking referents across sentences.

2.1 Adjective concord with class 1/2 nouns

Purpose: describe people accurately by matching adjectives to class 1/2 subject and object nouns.

Language Building Block
class 1/2 noun + m-/mw- (singular) or wa- (plural) + adjective root
Mwalimu wangu ni mrefu.
My teacher is tall. (m- + -refu)
Walimu wetu ni wachangamfu.
Our teachers are lively. (wa- + -changamfu)
NounSingular adjectivePlural adjective
mtoto / watotomdogo (small)wadogo
rafiki / marafikimkarimu (generous)wakarimu
kiongozi / viongozi*mkali (strict)wakali

*Note: kiongozi takes a KI-VI plural form (viongozi) but, because it refers to a person, still triggers class 1/2 human agreement (kiongozi mkali, not kiongozi mkali wa KI-VI pattern). This human-reference override is common with a small set of person nouns and will be revisited as it arises.

2.2 Possessive concord with class 1/2 nouns

Purpose: express ownership and relationship ("my," "your," "his/her," and so on) accurately with people.

PersonPossessive form (singular noun)Possessive form (plural noun)

your (sg.)wakowako
his/herwakewake
ourwetuwetu
your (pl.)wenuwenu

  • Baba yangu ni mfanyabiashara. My father is a businessperson.
  • Rafiki zake ni wachangamfu. His/her friends are lively. rafiki (pl. marafiki, but zake here follows the class 1/2 plural pattern used with rafiki) → wachangamfu

2.3 Demonstrative concord: huyu and hawa

Purpose: point out or single out a specific person among others.

MeaningSingularPlural
this / thesehuyuhawa
  • Huyu ni dada yangu, Amina. This is my sister, Amina.
  • Hawa ni marafiki zangu wa klabuni. These are my friends from the club.

2.4 Person object markers: tracking who you are talking about

Purpose: refer back to a person already mentioned, without repeating their name in every sentence — a skill essential for connected description.

The object marker slots into the verb template between the tense marker and the verb root:

Language Building Block
subject marker + tense/aspect marker + object marker + verb root + final vowel
Ninampenda.
I love him/her. (ni- + -na- + -m- + pend- + -a)
Tunawaheshimu.
We respect them. (tu- + -na- + -wa- + heshim- + -u)
PersonObject markerExample
me-ni-Ananisaidia. (He/she helps me.)
you (sg.)-ku-Ninakusikia. (I hear you.)
him/her-m-Ninampenda. (I love him/her.)
us-tu-Anatusaidia. (He/she helps us.)
you (pl.)-wa-Ninawaona. (I see you all.)
them-wa-Tunawaheshimu. (We respect them.)
Language Note — Why object markers matter for description

Compare Ninampenda dada yangu. Dada yangu ananisaidia kila wakati. with a version that repeats the noun in every clause: Ninampenda dada yangu. Dada yangu ananisaidia dada yangu kila wakati — the second version is unnatural, because Swahili, like English, avoids repeating a known noun as an object once it has been established. The object marker -m- does this work inside the verb.

Object markers for non-human, non-class-1/2 nouns (things, places, abstract ideas) remain receptive only at this stage; they become productive later in this volume once the relevant noun classes are more fully developed.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: Ni jioni ya "Watu Muhimu Maishani Mwangu" katika Klabu ya Kiswahili. Halima anamwelezea James familia yake.

(Setting: It is "Important People in My Life" evening at the Swahili Club. Halima is describing her family to James.)

JAMESHalima, niambie kuhusu familia yako. Una ndugu wangapi?Halima, tell me about your family. How many siblings do you have?
HALIMANina dada mmoja na kaka wawili. Dada yangu anaitwa Amina. Yeye ni mrefu na mchangamfu sana.I have one sister and two brothers. My sister is named Amina. She is tall and very lively.
JAMESUnamfahamu vizuri, sivyo? Mnaonana mara ngapi?You know her well, right? How often do you see each other?
HALIMANdiyo, ninamfahamu vizuri sana. Tunaonana kila wiki. Ananisaidia sana ninapokuwa na matatizo.Yes, I know her very well. We see each other every week. She helps me a lot when I have problems.
JAMESVizuri. Na huyu bwana katika picha — ni nani?Good. And this man in the photo — who is he?
HALIMAHuyu ni jirani wetu, Bwana Otieno. Yeye ni mkulima mwenye bidii. Tunamheshimu sana kwa sababu anasaidia kila mtu mtaani.This is our neighbor, Mr. Otieno. He is a hardworking farmer. We respect him a lot because he helps everyone in the neighborhood.
JAMESNzuri. Mimi pia nina jirani mkarimu. Anaitwa Bi. Wanjiru. Ninampenda kwa sababu ni mnyenyekevu.Nice. I also have a generous neighbor. Her name is Mrs. Wanjiru. I like her because she is humble.
HALIMAWewe na jirani zako mnaonana mara nyingi?Do you and your neighbors see each other often?
JAMESNdiyo, tunaonana karibu kila siku. Wanatusaidia, na sisi tunawasaidia pia.Yes, we see each other almost every day. They help us, and we help them too.

Vocabulary note: Bwana and Bi. = respectful titles for a man and a woman, similar to "Mr." and "Mrs./Ms."; mtaani = in the neighborhood (locative form — full locative agreement is developed later in this volume).

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension questions:

  1. Halima ana ndugu wangapi? Dada yake anaitwaje, na ana tabia gani?
  2. Halima na Amina wanaonana mara ngapi?
  3. Bwana Otieno ni nani, na kwa nini Halima na familia yake wanamheshimu?
  4. Find two sentences that use a person object marker instead of repeating a noun. Identify the object marker in each.
  5. Je, James na jirani zake wanaonana mara ngapi? Wanasaidiana namna gani?

Writing Workshop

  1. Soma mfano. Reread Maandishi 1 (Watu Muhimu Maishani Mwangu). Underline every possessive, every demonstrative, and every object marker you find.
  2. Panga mawazo yako. Choose three people important to you: one family member, one friend, and one person from your community. Note one relationship word, one appearance adjective, and one character adjective for each.
  3. Andika rasimu yako ya kwanza. Write a connected 130–160-word paragraph in Swahili describing all three people. Use at least one possessive, one demonstrative, and two different person object markers, with consistent class 1/2 agreement throughout.
  4. Kagua kwa orodha hapa chini. Check your draft against the editing checklist before writing your final version.
  5. Andika nakala safi. Produce a clean final version to share with a partner or keep for your records.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • Every class 1/2 noun has matching adjective agreement (m-/mw- singular, wa- plural).
  • Possessive forms (wangu, wako, wake, wetu, wenu, wao) match the person and number intended.
  • Demonstratives (huyu/hawa) are used correctly for singular and plural people.
  • At least two person object markers are used correctly and refer clearly to a previously mentioned person.
  • Tense markers from Chapter 1 remain consistent with the intended time reference.
  • All verbs are written in normal unhyphenated Swahili spelling.
  • Capitalization, spacing, punctuation, and ng' are correct throughout.

Kazi ya ziada — Additional task: Exchange your draft with a partner. Find one object marker in your partner's writing and confirm together which person it refers back to.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Titles of respect

Bwana and Bi. (short for Bibi) are commonly used with a surname to address or refer to adults respectfully, similar in function to "Mr." and "Mrs./Ms." in English, in many formal and semi-formal Swahili-speaking contexts. Titles of respect for elders, teachers, and community leaders are generally valued across Swahili-speaking communities, though the specific titles and expectations vary by setting, generation, and relationship.

Culture in Context — Extended family and community roles

In many Swahili-speaking communities, jamaa (extended family) and close neighbors often play a significant role in daily life, sometimes similar to how close family is treated elsewhere. Community leaders, like Bwana Mwangi in Maandishi 2, are frequently described in terms of the practical help they provide, not only their formal position. This reflects a broader pattern, common in many communities worldwide, in which respect is closely tied to demonstrated care for others.

Regional Variation — Kinship terms

Some Swahili-speaking communities use additional or slightly different kinship vocabulary depending on region, family structure, and language contact. The terms in this chapter (baba, mama, kaka, dada, babu, bibi) represent widely understood Standard Swahili forms. Learners who spend time with a specific community may encounter additional or more specific kinship terms; these remain a recognition-only extension of this chapter's core system.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Repeating the noun instead of using an object marker

Error: Ninampenda dada yangu. Dada yangu ananisaidia kila wakati dada yangu. (repeating "dada yangu" unnecessarily)

Correction: Ninampenda dada yangu. Dada yangu ananisaidia kila wakati. — Once a person has been named, use the object marker inside the verb (already present in ananisaidia) rather than repeating the noun as an object.

Common Mistake — Placing the object marker in the wrong slot

Error: Ninapenda-m-a. (attempting to attach the object marker after the verb root)

Correction: Ninampenda. — The object marker sits between the tense marker and the verb root: ni- + -na- + -m- + pend- + -a.

Common Mistake — Using a class 1/2 possessive form with a non-person noun

Error: Kitabu wangu (using the class 1/2 possessive wangu with the KI-VI noun kitabu)

Correction: Kitabu changu. — Possessive concord must match the noun class of the possessed item, not just the person of the possessor. Class 1/2 possessives (wangu, wako, wake…) are reserved for class 1/2 nouns.

Common Mistake — Confusing huyu (this person) with hii (this thing)

Error: Hii ni mwalimu wangu. (using the non-person demonstrative hii to introduce a person)

Correction: Huyu ni mwalimu wangu. — Huyu/hawa are reserved for people (class 1/2); other demonstratives are used for other noun classes.

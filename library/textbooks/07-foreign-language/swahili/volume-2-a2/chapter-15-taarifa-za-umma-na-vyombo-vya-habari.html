Taarifa za Umma na Vyombo vya Habari

Public Information and Media Basics · Speak and Write Swahili, Volume II: Expanding Everyday and Community Communication

Chapter Overview

Chapter 15 builds on the comparatives, adjective concord, and reason clauses you developed in Chapter 14 and turns your attention to reading and discussing the kind of language you encounter every day in public life: signs, office notices, weather reports, and short news items. You will meet class 14 abstract nouns such as usalama ("safety") and umuhimu ("importance"), learn to use nominalized ku- forms such as kusoma ("reading") as nouns in a sentence, and begin to recognize the passive voice in the kind of announcements that appear on notice boards and in the news. By the end of the chapter, you will be able to read a short public notice or weather report, understand its main point, and summarize it in your own words.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize and use a small set of class 14 abstract nouns in context.
  2. Use nominalized ku- forms (verbal nouns) as the subject or object of a sentence.
  3. Recognize the passive voice in public notices, announcements, and short news items without being required to produce it independently.
  4. Name vocabulary for public notices, media, weather, and civic services.
  5. Read aloud public-register words and short announcements with accurate pronunciation.
  6. Understand the main idea and key details of a short notice, weather report, or news item.
  7. Summarize a short notice or announcement in your own words, in speech and in writing.
  8. Identify and repair common errors involving abstract nouns and nominalized ku- forms.

Opening Communication Scenario

James and Zainabu have decided on a new office computer, but on the way back to their desks they stop at the notice board in the hallway, where a maintenance announcement, a weather notice, and a short news clipping about local transport are posted. Reading this kind of everyday public text — office notices, weather updates, short announcements — is a skill separate from personal conversation, and it uses some grammar you have not yet met directly.

  1. This chapter introduces class 14 abstract nouns, which are common in notices, policies, and reports because they name qualities and conditions such as safety, importance, and progress rather than physical things.
  2. It introduces nominalized ku- forms, letting you talk about an activity itself — such as reading or traveling — as the subject or object of a sentence.
  3. It introduces the passive voice receptively, so that you can understand notices such as "the office is closed" or "the meeting has been announced" without yet being asked to produce passive sentences on your own.
  4. The comparatives, adjective concord, and reason clauses from Chapter 14 continue to be recycled, especially in discussing which notice matters most and why.

Pronunciation Focus

Reading Aloud Public-Register Words

Public notices and news items often contain longer words built from class 14 abstract nouns and nominalized ku- forms, such as usalama, umuhimu, and kutangazwa. These words keep the same five stable vowels and the same penultimate stress pattern as shorter words; the stress simply moves to the second-to-last syllable of the longer word, so kutangazwa is stressed on -gaz-, not on the first syllable.

When reading a notice aloud, read each word with even, open syllables, pause briefly at commas and full stops, and let your pitch fall gently at the end of each announcement, the way a calm public announcement is read rather than a personal remark.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This section introduces class 14 abstract nouns, nominalized ku- forms, and the passive voice as a receptive system for reading public notices.

Class 14 Abstract Nouns

Building Block — u- + [quality/condition]
u- + stem = abstract noun naming a quality, condition, or state

Usalama ni muhimu barabarani. Safety is important on the roads.

Tangazo hili linaeleza umuhimu wa kufika mapema. This notice explains the importance of arriving early.

Nchi hii ina maendeleo makubwa katika miaka ya hivi karibuni. This country has had great development in recent years.

Class 14 nouns typically begin with u- and name an abstract quality or condition rather than a physical object or person. Most class 14 nouns in this chapter do not take a regular plural; a few, such as maendeleo, appear only in a plural-looking class 6 form even though the meaning is singular and abstract. Agreement with class 14 nouns in this volume remains receptive: you should recognize class 14 subject agreement in notices, but you are not required to produce full class 14 concord independently yet.

Nominalized Ku- Forms

Building Block — ku- + [verb stem] used as a noun
kusoma, kuandika, kusafiri = "reading," "writing," "traveling" (the activity itself, as a noun)

Kusoma habari kila siku ni muhimu. Reading the news every day is important.

Kusafiri wakati wa mvua ni hatari kidogo. Traveling during rain is a little dangerous.

Ninapendelea kusoma gazeti kuliko kusikiliza redio. I prefer reading the newspaper to listening to the radio.

You already know the infinitive ku- form as part of verb complements (for example, "I want to read"). This chapter adds a new use: the same ku- form can act as the subject or object of a sentence, naming the activity itself, the way English uses "-ing" forms such as "reading" or "traveling" as nouns. Notice that the third example combines this pattern with the kuliko comparative from Chapter 14.

Passive Voice in Public Notices — Receptive

Recognition Only — The Passive Extension

Public notices and news items often describe an action without naming who performed it, using a passive verb form such as -fungwa ("be closed"), -tangazwa ("be announced"), or -andikwa ("be written"). You should be able to recognize sentences like Ofisi imefungwa leo ("The office is closed today") or Tangazo limetangazwa asubuhi hii ("The announcement was announced this morning") and understand who or what is affected, even though you are not yet asked to build passive verbs on your own. Productive control of the passive extension is developed later in the volume.

  • Barabara imefungwa kwa sababu ya matengenezo. The road is closed because of repairs. passive, receptive + kwa sababu
  • Kusoma tangazo hili ni muhimu kuliko kusoma lile la zamani. Reading this notice is more important than reading that old one. nominalized ku- + kuliko + umuhimu-related idea

Zoezi la Sarufi — Grammar Practice:

  1. Identify the class 14 noun: "Usalama wa wafanyakazi ni muhimu sana ofisini."
  2. Turn the verb into a nominalized noun phrase: "kusoma" + "ni muhimu" (Reading is important.)
  3. Underline the passive form and say what it means: "Mkutano umetangazwa kwa saa tatu."
  4. Combine using kuliko: Kusoma gazeti ni muhimu. Kusikiliza redio ni muhimu kidogo.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: James na Zainabu wanasimama mbele ya ubao wa matangazo ofisini, wakisoma tangazo, taarifa ya hali ya hewa, na habari fupi kuhusu usafiri.

(Setting: James and Zainabu stand in front of the office notice board, reading an announcement, a weather report, and short transport news.)

ZAINABUAngalia hili tangazo. Linasema ofisi ndogo ya chini imefungwa kwa sababu ya matengenezo.Look at this notice. It says the small downstairs office is closed because of repairs.
JAMESKweli? Umuhimu wa tangazo hili ni nini basi, kwa kazi zetu?Really? What's the importance of this notice, then, for our work?
ZAINABUTunapaswa kutumia ofisi ya juu badala yake. Pia, angalia taarifa ya hali ya hewa; mvua kubwa imetangazwa kwa mchana huu.We should use the upstairs office instead. Also, look at the weather report; heavy rain has been announced for this afternoon.
JAMESUsalama ni muhimu zaidi kuliko kufika kazini haraka, kwa hivyo tutaondoka mapema kidogo leo.Safety is more important than getting to work quickly, so we'll leave a little early today.
ZAINABUKwa maoni yangu, kusoma habari za usafiri kila asubuhi ni muhimu kuliko watu wengi wanavyodhani.In my opinion, reading the transport news every morning is more important than many people think.
JAMESNakubaliana nawe. Habari hii ndogo inasema barabara moja imefungwa kwa sababu ya maendeleo ya ujenzi.I agree with you. This short news item says one road is closed because of construction development.
ZAINABUBasi, kusafiri leo kutakuwa polepole kuliko kawaida. Tuulize dereva kabla ya kuondoka.Well then, traveling today will be slower than usual. Let's ask the driver before we leave.
JAMESWazo zuri. Kwa maoni yangu, kusoma matangazo haya kwa makini kila siku ni bora kuliko kuyapuuza.Good idea. In my opinion, reading these notices carefully every day is better than ignoring them.

This dialogue combines class 14 abstract nouns (usalama, umuhimu, maendeleo), nominalized ku- forms (kusoma habari, kusafiri leo), receptive passive forms (imefungwa, imetangazwa), and the kuliko comparatives and kwa sababu reason clauses recycled from Chapter 14.

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension questions:

  1. Tangazo la kwanza linasema nini kuhusu ofisi ndogo?
  2. Taarifa ya hali ya hewa inasema nini?
  3. Kwa nini James na Zainabu wataondoka mapema kidogo leo?
  4. Find one passive form in the dialogue and say what it communicates without naming who performed the action.

Writing Workshop

  1. Chagua tangazo. Choose a short notice, weather report, or announcement (real or invented) to summarize.
  2. Bainisha mambo makuu. Identify the main point and two or three key details.
  3. Andika rasimu. Write a connected 80–110-word summary using at least one class 14 noun and one nominalized ku- form.
  4. Kagua kwa orodha. Check your draft against the checklist below.
  5. Andika nakala safi. Produce a clean final version for assessment.

Prompt: Write a short summary of a notice or announcement, stating its main point and why it matters.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • The summary states the main point of the notice clearly and in your own words.
  • At least one class 14 abstract noun is used correctly.
  • At least one nominalized ku- form is used as a subject or object.
  • Any passive forms copied from the source notice are understood, not merely repeated without comprehension.
  • Comparatives with kuliko and reason clauses with kwa sababu from Chapter 14 remain accurate where used.
  • Capitalization, spacing, punctuation, and ng' are correct throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Public Notices and Shared Responsibility

Office and community notices in many Swahili-speaking workplaces are often phrased in a way that emphasizes shared responsibility and collective safety rather than singling out an individual, which is part of why the passive voice and abstract nouns such as usalama appear so often in this register: the notice describes what is important or what has happened, without necessarily naming who is responsible.

Regional Variation Note

Weather and transport reports on Tanzanian and Kenyan radio and television use largely the same Standard Swahili vocabulary taught in this chapter, though some stations use additional specialized or borrowed terms for specific weather phenomena or transport authorities. Such station-specific terms are recognition-only at this stage and are not required for this chapter's productive standard.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Treating a Class 14 Noun as Class 5/6

Error: usalama mzuri (using class 1/2-style agreement with an abstract noun)

Correction: usalama mzuri is not the expected pattern for this noun in this context; instead, describe the abstract noun with a simple predicate such as Usalama ni muhimu ("Safety is important") until full class 14 agreement is scheduled for productive use.

Common Mistake — Using a Bare Verb Instead of a Nominalized Ku- Form

Error: Soma habari ni muhimu (leaving off the ku- prefix that turns the verb into a noun)

Correction: Kusoma habari ni muhimu — the nominalized ku- form is required when the activity itself is the subject of the sentence.

Common Mistake — Trying to Produce a Passive Form Independently

Error: attempting to build new passive verbs freely in speech or writing before the extension has been taught productively

Correction: at this stage, recognize passive forms such as imefungwa and imetangazwa in notices and news, but express your own statements using the active forms you already control, such as Wamefunga ofisi ("They have closed the office") if the agent is known or unimportant to specify actively.

Chapter Summary

You learned to use nominalized ku- forms, such as kusoma and kusafiri, as the subject or object of a sentence, allowing you to talk about an activity itself. You also began to recognize the passive voice in public notices, understanding sentences such as Ofisi imefungwa without yet producing passive forms independently. The comparatives, adjective concord, and reason clauses from Chapter 14 continued to support clear, connected explanation of what a notice says and why it matters.

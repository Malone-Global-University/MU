Mradi wa Mawasiliano wa B1

B1 Communication Project · Speak and Write Swahili, Volume II: Expanding Everyday and Community Communication

Chapter Overview

Chapter 19 does not introduce major new grammar. Instead, it asks you to bring together everything Volume II has developed — noun-class concord, object marking, negation, locatives, relative identification, kama conditions, cause-result connectors, imperatives, and subjunctive requests — into a single, connected community-life project. Building directly on the letter to the council that Zainabu and James began in Chapter 18, you will interview someone about a community topic, complete a small service-related task, and write a practical report that presents what you learned and what you recommend. This chapter is your bridge between individual chapters and the Volume II capstone in Chapter 20.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Plan and conduct a short interview about a community topic using questions built from Volume II grammar.
  2. Take notes on spoken responses and organize them for later use in writing.
  3. Complete a small practical service task (such as reporting a problem or making a request) using appropriate register.
  4. Write a short, organized practical report that presents a problem, describes what you learned, and proposes a solution.
  5. Present your project findings orally to a partner, group, or recording.
  6. Ask and answer peer questions about another learner's project.
  7. Self-monitor and correct your own pronunciation, grammar, and register across an extended, multi-skill task.

After sending their letter to the halmashauri about the broken streetlight and the rubbish near the notice board, Zainabu and James decide to go further: they will interview a neighbor about community life, note what she says, and turn their findings into a short written report — the same kind of project you will complete in this chapter, using a topic of your own choosing.

Pronunciation Focus

Self-Correction Across a Longer Task

This chapter does not introduce a new sound pattern. Instead, it asks you to monitor your own speech across a longer, multi-part task: an interview, a short service exchange, and a spoken presentation of your findings. Record yourself during at least one stage of the project. Afterward, listen back and check three things: are your five vowels staying stable and clear, is your stress falling on the second-to-last syllable of longer words such as mahojiano and mapendekezo, and are you pausing naturally after condition clauses with kama rather than running them into the result clause.

Self-correction is a B1 skill in its own right: noticing your own errors, without a partner or instructor pointing them out first, is part of becoming an independent user of Swahili.

Reviewing the Toolkit

This section does not introduce new grammar. It reviews the Volume II systems you will draw on throughout this chapter's project, and shows how they combine naturally in interview questions, service requests, and a practical report.

Interview Questions — Combining Question Words with Familiar Tenses

Building Block — Question word + subject marker + TAM + stem
[Question word] + [subject marker-TAM-stem-final vowel]?

Unaishi hapa kwa muda gani? How long have you lived here?

Ni tatizo gani unaloliona karibu na hapa? What problem do you see near here? object-marker + class-sensitive relative recognition

Kama huduma za jamii zingeboreshwa, ungependa nini kwanza? If community services were improved, what would you like first?

Interview questions in this chapter should use present, past, future, perfect, and habitual forms already active from earlier chapters, together with the object markers, relative identification (ambaye/ambao/ambacho/ambavyo), and kama conditions developed in Chapters 11–18. No new question pattern is introduced here; the goal is fluent, natural combination of familiar tools.

The Service Task — Polite Requests and Reports

Review — Imperative, Subjunctive, and Register

Your project's service task should reuse the polite request and report forms developed earlier in this volume: subjunctive requests such as Tafadhali mturidhishe..., obligation language such as lazima and ni vizuri, and the respectful, cooperative tone modeled in Chapter 18's letter to the halmashauri. Choose a register appropriate to who you are addressing — a neighbor, a shopkeeper, a local office, or a service provider.

Reporting What You Learned — Kwa Hiyo, Hivyo, and Kama in the Report

Building Block — [finding]. Kwa hiyo/Hivyo, [conclusion]. Kama [condition], [recommendation].
[Cause/finding]. Kwa hiyo, [result/conclusion].  ·  Kama [condition], [result/recommendation].

Wakazi wengi wanasema huduma za usafi hazitoshi. Kwa hiyo, tunapendekeza ukusanyaji wa taka uongezwe. Many residents say sanitation services are not enough. So, we recommend that rubbish collection be increased.

Kama halmashauri itashirikiana na wakazi, mtaa utakuwa bora zaidi. If the council cooperates with residents, the neighborhood will be much better.

These connectors, already active since Chapter 18, are exactly the tools that turn a set of interview notes into an organized report: findings lead to conclusions, and conditions lead to recommendations.

  • Je, umewahi kuripoti tatizo kwa halmashauri? Have you ever reported a problem to the council? perfect -me- + object review
  • Ungependekeza nini kama ungekuwa kiongozi wa mtaa? What would you recommend if you were the neighborhood leader? receptive hypothetical, glossed, not required for production

Zoezi la Sarufi — Grammar Practice:

  1. Turn this statement into an interview question: (Anaishi hapa) → ___?
  2. Combine with kwa hiyo: (Watu wengi hawana taarifa) / (tunapendekeza mkutano ufanyike).
  3. Write one polite subjunctive request you could use during your service task.
  4. Combine with kama: (Huduma zinaboreshwa) / (wakazi watafurahi).

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: Zainabu anamhoji Bi. Fatuma, jirani yao mzee zaidi, kuhusu maisha ya jamii katika mtaa wao, kama sehemu ya mradi wao.

(Setting: Zainabu interviews Bi. Fatuma, an older neighbor, about community life in their neighborhood, as part of their project.)

ZAINABUShikamoo, Bi. Fatuma. Tunafanya mradi kuhusu maisha ya jamii yetu. Je, tunaweza kukuhoji kidogo?Greetings, Bi. Fatuma. We are doing a project about our community life. May we interview you a little?
BI. FATUMAMarahaba, Zainabu. Karibu, uliza tu.Response to shikamoo, Zainabu. Welcome, just ask.
ZAINABUUmeishi hapa kwa muda gani?How long have you lived here?
BI. FATUMANimeishi hapa kwa miaka ishirini.I have lived here for twenty years.
ZAINABUNi tatizo gani unaloliona karibu na hapa siku hizi?What problem do you see near here these days?
BI. FATUMAKwa kweli, taa ya barabarani na taka ni matatizo makubwa. Kama halmashauri haitachukua hatua, hali itakuwa mbaya zaidi.Honestly, the streetlight and rubbish are big problems. If the council does not take action, the situation will get worse.
ZAINABUUngependekeza nini kwanza?What would you recommend first?
BI. FATUMANadhani wakazi wote wanapaswa kushirikiana na kuandika barua pamoja.I think all residents should cooperate and write a letter together.
ZAINABUAsante sana kwa muda wako, Bi. Fatuma. Maoni yako yatatusaidia sana katika ripoti yetu.Thank you very much for your time, Bi. Fatuma. Your views will help us a lot in our report.
BI. FATUMAKaribu sana. Nawatakia mradi mzuri.You're very welcome. I wish you a good project.

This dialogue recycles respectful greeting routines, perfect and present tense, object-marker relative identification, kama conditions, and cause-result connectors, all combined naturally within an interview format.

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension questions:

  1. Bi. Fatuma ameishi katika mtaa huo kwa muda gani?
  2. Ni matatizo gani mawili anayoyataja Bi. Fatuma?
  3. Bi. Fatuma anapendekeza nini kifanyike kwanza?
  4. Find the greeting exchange at the start of the dialogue and explain why Zainabu uses shikamoo.

Mahojiano Mafupi — A Short Interview

Mwandishi wa mradi anamhoji mfanyabiashara wa sokoni kuhusu maisha ya jamii. Mfanyabiashara anasema ameuza bidhaa sokoni hilo kwa miaka kumi. Anasema tatizo kubwa ni usafiri: mabasi hayaji mara kwa mara, na hivyo wateja wengine hawawezi kufika sokoni kwa urahisi. Stage 2 — Speaking: Conducting the Interview

Sehemu ya kwanza — Kuandaa Maswali (Preparing Questions)

Choose a community topic (housing, transport, health, safety, environment, education, or another topic from earlier chapters) and prepare four to six interview questions, using at least one question with kama, one with an object marker or relative form, and one asking for a recommendation.

Sehemu ya pili — Kufanya Mahojiano (Conducting the Interview)

With a partner playing the role of a community member, or alone using the solo alternative, conduct your interview. Take brief notes in Swahili or English on the responses.

Mbadala wa kujifunza peke yako — Solo alternative

Record yourself asking your prepared interview questions, pausing to imagine or improvise realistic answers, or interview a Swahili-speaking contact if one is available. Listen back and check your questions for accuracy and natural rhythm.

Stage 3 — Reading: A Model Report

Ripoti ya Mradi — Project Report

Ripoti hii inawasilisha matokeo ya mahojiano tuliyofanya na wakazi watano wa mtaa wetu kuhusu maisha ya jamii. Wakazi wengi walisema kwamba huduma za usafi hazitoshi, na kwamba taa za barabarani zimeharibika mahali kadhaa. Kwa hiyo, watu wengi wanaogopa kutembea usiku. Pia, wakazi walitaja kwamba mabasi hayaji mara kwa mara, na hivyo baadhi ya wafanyabiashara wanapoteza wateja. Kwa upande mwingine, wakazi wengi walisifu ushirikiano mzuri kati ya majirani wakati wa matatizo. Tunapendekeza kwamba halmashauri iboreshe huduma za usafi na taa za barabarani haraka iwezekanavyo. Kama mapendekezo haya yatafanyiwa kazi, tunaamini mtaa wetu utakuwa salama na bora zaidi kwa wakazi wote.

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension and interpretation questions:

  1. Ripoti hii inawasilisha matokeo ya mahojiano na wakazi wangapi?
  2. Ni matatizo gani mawili yanayotajwa katika ripoti?
  3. Ni jambo gani zuri linalotajwa kuhusu mtaa huu?
  4. Ripoti inapendekeza nini kifanyike?
  5. Find one kama sentence and one cause-result connector in the report, and explain what each one links.

Stage 4 — Writing: The Practical Report

  1. Chagua mada. Choose the community topic your interview addressed.
  2. Panga maelezo yako. Organize your interview notes into a problem, a cause or finding, and a recommendation.
  3. Andika rasimu. Write a short 100–140-word report presenting what you learned and what you recommend, using at least one kama sentence, one cause-result connector, and one polite request or recommendation form.
  4. Kagua kwa orodha. Check your draft against the checklist below.
  5. Andika nakala safi. Produce a clean final version for the performance task.

Prompt: Write a short practical report presenting your interview findings about a community topic and your recommendation.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • The report clearly presents a topic, a finding or problem, and a recommendation.
  • At least one kama condition-result sentence is used correctly.
  • At least one cause-result connector (kwa hiyo or hivyo) links a finding to a conclusion.
  • Noun-class concord is correct across the report's noun phrases.
  • Verb forms (present, past, perfect, or future as needed) are chosen accurately for meaning.
  • Capitalization, spacing, punctuation, and ng' are correct throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Interviewing Elders and Community Members

When interviewing an older community member, opening with a respectful greeting such as shikamoo and responding to their marahaba sets an appropriate tone before any questions are asked. Thanking the person clearly for their time and views at the end of the interview, as Zainabu does in the model dialogue, remains an important closing routine across many Swahili-speaking communities, regardless of how informal the rest of the conversation becomes.

Regional Variation Note

Interview and report vocabulary such as mahojiano, ripoti, and mapendekezo is used consistently across Tanzanian and Kenyan Standard Swahili in community, school, and workplace contexts. Some institutions may use additional specialized reporting formats or terminology, which remain outside this chapter's scope.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Reporting Notes as a List Instead of Connected Sentences

Error: Tatizo. Taa. Taka. Watu wanaogopa. (stringing notes together as fragments)

Correction: Taa haifanyi kazi, na kwa hiyo watu wanaogopa kutembea usiku. — a report should connect findings with the cause-result and condition tools already active in this volume, not present them as a raw list of notes.

Common Mistake — Skipping the Recommendation

Error: Ending a report after describing the problem, with no stated recommendation.

Correction: Tunapendekeza kwamba halmashauri iboreshe huduma za usafi haraka iwezekanavyo. — a practical report at this level should always close with a clear, actionable recommendation, not only a description of the problem.

Common Mistake — Overusing English Interview Question Order

Error: *Kwa muda gani wewe umeishi hapa? (word-for-word English question order)

Correction: Umeishi hapa kwa muda gani? — Swahili question words such as gani typically follow the phrase they question rather than opening the sentence in the English pattern.

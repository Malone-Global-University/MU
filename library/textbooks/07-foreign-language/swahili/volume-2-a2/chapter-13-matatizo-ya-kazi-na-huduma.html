13

Matatizo ya Kazi na Huduma

Workplace and Service Problems · Speak and Write Swahili, Volume II: Expanding Everyday and Community Communication

Chapter Overview

Chapter 13 builds on the possessive and associative control you developed in Chapter 12 and applies it to a new communicative field: reporting problems at work and requesting solutions from service providers. You will learn the negative future (hataenda-type forms) and the negative perfect (hajafanya-type forms), which let you say that something will not happen or has not happened yet. You will also meet the stative/neuter verb extension receptively, recognizing forms like imeharibika ("it is broken") without yet being asked to produce every possible extension form. By the end of the chapter, you will be able to describe what is not working, explain what has not been done, and ask for a repair or solution.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Form and use the negative future (ha- + subject marker + -ta- + stem) to say what will not happen.
  2. Form and use the negative perfect (ha- + subject marker + -ja- + stem) to say what has not happened yet.
  3. Recognize the stative/neuter verb extension (-ika/-eka) in common lexicalized forms describing broken, spoiled, or malfunctioning things.
  4. Name common workplace, repair, and customer-service vocabulary.
  5. Report a problem, request a solution, and explain what has not been fixed.
  6. Understand a spoken or written complaint or service exchange.
  7. Write a short complaint or service request using accurate negative forms.
  8. Identify and repair common errors involving the negative future and negative perfect.

Opening Communication Scenario

James's laptop has stopped working properly before an important deadline, and the office printer is also broken. He needs to report both problems to the office technician, explain what is not working, and find out when they will be fixed. This kind of workplace troubleshooting depends heavily on being able to say what will not happen and what has not happened yet — exactly the systems this chapter introduces.

  1. This chapter introduces the negative future systematically, building on the affirmative future you already control.
  2. It introduces the negative perfect systematically, building on the affirmative perfect/resultative from earlier chapters.
  3. It introduces the stative/neuter extension receptively, in a small set of common lexicalized forms such as imeharibika ("it is broken/has broken down").
  4. Possessive and associative constructions from Chapter 12 continue to be recycled throughout this chapter's dialogue and readings.

Pronunciation Focus

Negative Marker Listening

The negative prefix ha- is short and often unstressed, but it is the single most important signal that a sentence is negative rather than affirmative. Practice listening for the difference between atakuja ("he/she will come") and hatakuja ("he/she will not come"), and between amefika ("he/she has arrived") and hajafika ("he/she has not arrived yet"). Note that the negative perfect also changes the tense marker itself, from -me- to -ja-, so two things change at once: the prefix and the tense marker.

Keep penultimate stress steady across the whole verb word even when ha- is added at the front. Adding a negative prefix does not shift the stress pattern of the rest of the word.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This section introduces the negative future and negative perfect systematically, and introduces the stative/neuter extension receptively in a small set of common forms.

The Negative Future

Building Block — ha- + Subject Marker + -ta- + Stem + -a
ha- + [subject marker] + -ta- + stem + -a

Sitakuja kesho. I will not come tomorrow.

Fundi hatafika leo. The technician will not arrive today.

Hatutamaliza kazi wiki hii. We will not finish the work this week.

Notice that the first-person singular subject marker ni- combines with ha- to become si- (sitakuja, not hanitakuja). All other subject markers keep their usual form after ha-.

The Negative Perfect

Building Block — ha- + Subject Marker + -ja- + Stem + -a
ha- + [subject marker] + -ja- + stem + -a

Sijamaliza ripoti. I have not finished the report yet.

Fundi hajaitengeneza printa. The technician has not fixed the printer yet.

Hawajanijibu bado. They have not answered me yet.

The negative perfect uses -ja- in the tense slot where the affirmative perfect uses -me-. It regularly pairs with bado ("not yet, still") to emphasize that the action remains outstanding.

Receptive Note — The Stative/Neuter Extension

Recognition Only — -ika/-eka

Some Swahili verbs add -ika or -eka to a root to describe a state or a spontaneous change of state, often without naming who caused it. Kuharibika ("to break down, to get spoiled") is built this way, and its perfect form imeharibika ("it is broken / it has broken down") is common in workplace and repair talk.

At this stage, you only need to recognize and use -ika forms as whole lexical items, such as imeharibika. You are not yet asked to build new -ika forms from other verbs on your own; the full stative/neuter pattern is developed productively in a later volume.

  • Kompyuta yangu imeharibika. My computer is broken. recognized lexical -ika form
  • Printa haijatengenezwa bado. The printer has not been repaired yet. negative perfect + bado

Zoezi la Sarufi — Grammar Practice:

  1. Change to the negative future: Fundi atakuja kesho.
  2. Change to the negative perfect: Tumemaliza kazi.
  3. Change to the negative future: Nitamjibu leo.
  4. Change to the negative perfect: Wameitengeneza printa.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: James anamweleza fundi wa ofisi kuhusu matatizo ya kompyuta na printa.

(Setting: James explains his computer and printer problems to the office technician.)

JAMESSamahani, nina tatizo. Kompyuta yangu imeharibika tangu asubuhi.Excuse me, I have a problem. My computer has been broken since morning.
FUNDIPole sana. Je, umejaribu kuiwasha upya?Sorry about that. Have you tried restarting it?
JAMESNdiyo, lakini bado haijafanya kazi vizuri. Na printa yetu ya ofisi pia haitachapisha leo.Yes, but it still isn't working properly. And our office printer also won't print today.
FUNDINitaangalia hitilafu hiyo mara moja. Sitaweza kuitengeneza kompyuta yako sasa hivi, lakini nitaifanyia kazi baada ya saa moja.I will look at that fault right away. I won't be able to fix your computer right now, but I will work on it after an hour.
JAMESAsante. Ninahitaji ripoti hii ifikapo saa nane. Je, itakuwa tayari?Thank you. I need this report by two o'clock. Will it be ready?
FUNDISitakuhakikishia kompyuta, lakini nitakuazima kompyuta nyingine hivi punde ili usikwame.I won't guarantee the computer, but I will lend you another computer shortly so you're not stuck.
JAMESVizuri sana, asante kwa msaada. Nitalalamika kwa meneja kama hitilafu hii itarudia tena.Very good, thank you for the help. I will complain to the manager if this fault happens again.
FUNDINaelewa. Hatujapokea vipuri vipya bado, kwa hiyo matatizo kama haya yamekuwa yakitokea.I understand. We haven't received new parts yet, so problems like this have been happening.

This dialogue combines the negative future (haitachapisha, sitaweza, sitakuhakikishia), the negative perfect (haijafanya, hatujapokea), the recognized stative form imeharibika, and possessive/associative constructions recycled from Chapter 12 (kompyuta yangu, printa yetu ya ofisi).

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension questions:

  1. Tatizo la James ni lipi?
  2. Fundi atafanya nini baada ya saa moja?
  3. James atafanya nini kama hitilafu itarudia tena?
  4. Find one negative perfect form in the dialogue and say what has not yet happened.

Speaking Practice

Sehemu ya kwanza — Kuripoti Tatizo (Reporting a Problem)

With a partner, or alone using the solo alternative, role-play reporting a workplace or household problem to a technician or service provider. Use at least one negative future form and one negative perfect form.

Sehemu ya pili — Kutatua Tatizo (Responding as the Technician)

Take the role of the technician or service provider and respond, explaining what you will not be able to do immediately and what has not yet been finished, such as Sitaweza kuitengeneza sasa hivi or Sijapokea vipuri bado.

Mbadala wa kujifunza peke yako — Solo alternative

Record a two-minute complaint or service request about something that is not working. Use at least two negative future forms and two negative perfect forms. Listen back and check your negative markers and tense slots.

  1. Chagua tatizo. Choose a real or invented workplace or service problem to describe.
  2. Orodhesha maelezo. List what is broken or unfinished and what you need done.
  3. Andika rasimu. Write a connected 100–130-word complaint or service request using at least two negative future forms and two negative perfect forms.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • Negative future forms agree correctly with the subject and use ha- + -ta-.
  • Negative perfect forms agree correctly with the subject and use ha- + -ja-.
  • Any recognized stative/neuter form (such as imeharibika) is used as a whole lexical item, not invented freely.
  • Workplace and service vocabulary is used accurately.
  • Possessive and associative constructions from Chapter 12 remain accurate where used.
  • Capitalization, spacing, punctuation, and ng' are correct throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Reporting Problems Politely

In many Swahili-speaking workplaces and service settings, complaints are typically framed politely and indirectly before becoming more direct, and an initial apology or expression of regret (samahani, pole) from the person receiving the complaint is common even when the fault is not theirs personally. Escalating a complaint, as the writer does in the reading passage, is generally treated as a last resort after reasonable patience has been shown.

Regional Variation Note

The negative future and negative perfect forms taught in this chapter are stable across Standard Swahili in Tanzania and Kenya. Some spoken varieties shorten or contract certain negative forms in casual speech; such contractions are recognition-only at this stage and are not part of this chapter's productive standard.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Forgetting the ni- to si- Change

Error: hanitafika kesho (keeping ni- after ha-)

Correction: sitafika kesho — the first-person singular subject marker contracts with ha- to form si-, not hani-.

Common Mistake — Using -me- Instead of -ja- in the Negative Perfect

Error: hajamemaliza or hamemaliza (keeping the affirmative tense marker)

Correction: hajamaliza — the negative perfect replaces -me- with -ja-; the two markers never combine.

Common Mistake — Inventing New Stative/Neuter Forms Freely

Error: producing an unfamiliar -ika form on the spot for a verb not yet studied this way

Correction: at this stage, use only recognized lexical stative forms such as imeharibika; full productive control of the stative/neuter extension is developed in a later volume.

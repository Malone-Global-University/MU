Chakula, Kiasi, na Manunuzi

Food, Quantities, and Shopping Choices · Speak and Write Swahili, Volume II: Expanding Everyday and Community Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name and describe common foods and market goods using class 5/6 (ji-/ma- and zero/ma-) concord.
  2. Apply adjective agreement correctly to class 5/6 nouns, alongside the class 7/8 and 9/10 nouns developed in Chapter 3.
  3. Use the quantifier -ingi ("many, much") in controlled agreement patterns across active noun classes.
  4. Use the quantifier -ote ("all") in controlled agreement patterns across active noun classes.
  5. Ask about prices, quantities, and preferences, and state simple alternatives when something is unavailable.
  6. Consolidate polite request forms (naomba, tafadhali nipe, ningependa) for independent productive use in shopping contexts.
  7. Interpret a dialogue and two short readings set in a market and a shop.
  8. Perform a detailed shopping transaction that includes quantities, prices, preferences, and a polite request.

Communication Scenario

The Klabu ya Kiswahili in Nairobi has planned its next meeting as a shared meal, and members are responsible for buying ingredients at the local market before the group cooks together. Halima and James, who described their important objects in Chapter 3, now need to navigate a market stall — asking about prices, choosing between similar items, requesting specific quantities, and deciding what to buy when a favorite item has sold out.

  1. This chapter develops class 5/6 — a noun class used for many fruits, foods, and everyday nouns — into a system for describing market goods.
  2. The quantifiers -ingi and -ote are introduced in a limited, controlled set of patterns; you are not expected to produce every possible agreement form yet.
  3. Polite request language from earlier chapters becomes a consolidated productive system this chapter, used confidently and flexibly rather than as a memorized formula.

Pronunciation and Spelling

Namba katika mazungumzo — Numbers in connected speech

Numbers used for quantities and prices tend to run together with the noun they describe. Keep each syllable of the number clear and maintain penultimate stress on the word as a whole, rather than pausing awkwardly between the number and the noun.

PhraseMeaningListening focus
kilo mbilitwo kilogramsKeep the phrase as one smooth rhythmic unit.
machungwa matanofive orangesDo not let the plural ma- prefix collapse before the number.
shilingi mia tanofive hundred shillingsPractice the full price phrase at natural speed.
Sound Focus — Request intonation

Polite requests such as naomba... and tafadhali nipe... typically use a gentle falling intonation at the end of the phrase, rather than the rising pattern common in English polite requests. A flat or gently falling tone, combined with the word tafadhali ("please") and a warm facial expression, signals politeness more than pitch alone.

Grammar Instruction

This chapter develops class 5/6 into a system for describing market goods, introduces the quantifiers -ingi and -ote in a limited controlled set of agreement patterns, and consolidates polite request forms for confident productive use.

4.1 Adjective concord with class 5/6 nouns

Purpose: describe fruits, foods, and market goods accurately by matching adjectives to class 5/6 nouns.

Language Building Block
class 5 noun (zero prefix) + adjective root (zero prefix, singular) — class 6 noun (ma-) + adjective root + ma- (plural)
Tunda hili ni kubwa.
This fruit is big. (zero + -kubwa, singular)
Matunda haya ni makubwa.
These fruits are big. (ma- + -kubwa, plural)
NounSingular adjectivePlural adjective
tunda / matundazuri (nice)mazuri
chungwa / machungwabivu (ripe)mabivu
duka / madukadogo (small)madogo

Note that class 5 singular adjective agreement often has no visible prefix at all (tunda zuri, not tunda lizuri), which distinguishes it from the class 6 plural, which regularly takes ma-. This zero-prefix singular pattern is worth noticing now and will be reinforced through practice.

4.5 Consolidating polite requests

Purpose: make and respond to shopping requests confidently and flexibly, without relying on a single memorized phrase.

You have met polite request language in earlier chapters as formulaic expressions. This chapter consolidates that system: you should now be able to choose among request forms depending on formality and combine them with quantities, prices, and preferences.

  • Naomba kilo moja ya machungwa. I would like one kilogram of oranges.
  • Tafadhali nipe mkate na chupa moja ya maziwa. Please give me bread and one bottle of milk.
  • Ningependa kujua bei ya samaki huyu. I would like to know the price of this fish.
  • Je, mna sukari? Kama hamna, naomba chumvi badala yake. Do you have sugar? If you don't, I'd like salt instead.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: Halima yuko sokoni akinunua vitu kwa ajili ya mkutano wa Klabu ya Kiswahili. Anazungumza na Bwana Musa, muuzaji wa matunda.

(Setting: Halima is at the market buying items for the Swahili Club meeting. She is speaking with Mr. Musa, a fruit seller.)

HALIMAHabari za asubuhi, Bwana Musa. Je, mna machungwa leo?Good morning, Mr. Musa. Do you have oranges today?
MUSAHabari za asubuhi! Ndiyo, tuna machungwa mengi na matamu. Unataka ngapi?Good morning! Yes, we have many sweet oranges. How many do you want?
HALIMANaomba machungwa kumi. Bei yake ni ngapi?I'd like ten oranges. What is the price?
MUSAMachungwa haya ni shilingi mia moja kwa tano. Kwa kumi, ni shilingi mia mbili.These oranges are one hundred shillings for five. For ten, it's two hundred shillings.
HALIMASawa, bei ni nafuu. Je, mna maembe pia?Okay, the price is affordable. Do you have mangoes too?
MUSASamahani, leo hatuna maembe. Yote yameuzika. Lakini tuna matunda mengine mazuri.Sorry, today we don't have mangoes. They're all sold out. But we have other good fruit.
HALIMAHakuna shida. Ningependa ndizi badala yake. Nipe kilo moja, tafadhali.No problem. I'd like bananas instead. Give me one kilogram, please.
MUSAKaribu! Hizi ndizi ni mbivu na tamu. Chochote kingine?Here you go! These bananas are ripe and sweet. Anything else?
HALIMAHapana, hilo ni tosha. Asante sana, Bwana Musa.No, that's enough. Thank you very much, Mr. Musa.

Vocabulary note: ndizi (banana) is a class 9/10 noun, like simu from Chapter 3; its adjectives (mbivu, tamu) do not change between singular and plural.

Writing Workshop

  1. Soma mfano. Reread Maandishi 1 (Siku ya Ununuzi). Underline every class 5/6 noun with its agreement, every quantifier, and every polite request form you find.
  2. Panga mawazo yako. Imagine a shopping trip. Note three or four items you would buy, their noun classes, quantities, and approximate prices.
  3. Andika rasimu yako ya kwanza. Write a connected 110–140-word paragraph in Swahili describing your shopping trip. Use at least one class 5/6 noun, one quantifier (-ingi or -ote), and at least one polite request form, with consistent agreement throughout.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • Every class 5/6 noun has matching adjective agreement (zero prefix singular, ma- plural).
  • Demonstratives (hili/haya) are used correctly for the intended noun class.
  • -ingi and -ote are used only in the controlled agreement forms taught this chapter.
  • At least one polite request form (naomba, tafadhali nipe, ningependa) is used naturally, not mechanically repeated.
  • Class 7/8 and 9/10 agreement from Chapter 3 remains accurate wherever those nouns appear.
  • All verbs are written in normal unhyphenated Swahili spelling.
  • Capitalization, spacing, punctuation, and ng' are correct throughout.

Kazi ya ziada — Additional task: Exchange your draft with a partner. Find one class 5/6 noun in your partner's writing and confirm together that the adjective agreement is correct.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Markets and daily shopping

In many Swahili-speaking communities, open-air markets (masoko) remain an important part of daily life alongside supermarkets and small shops, especially for fresh produce. Shopping habits vary widely by country, city, and household, but a personal relationship with a regular seller — who may offer a familiar customer a better price or set items aside — is common in many market settings.

Culture in Context — Bargaining and price flexibility

In some market settings, prices for certain goods are treated as a starting point for polite negotiation rather than fixed, while in shops, supermarkets, and among some sellers, prices are typically fixed. Expectations vary by country, setting, and item, so it is often useful to observe local norms or ask before assuming that bargaining is appropriate.

Regional Variation — Currency and price expressions

This chapter uses shilingi (shillings) in price examples, reflecting currency used in Kenya and Tanzania, but currency names and amounts vary across Swahili-speaking regions and countries. The request and quantity language taught here — naomba, kilo, -ingi, -ote — remains widely understood Standard Swahili regardless of the specific currency in use.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Adding ma- to a class 5 singular adjective

Error: Tunda hili ni makubwa. (using the plural adjective form with a singular class 5 noun)

Correction: Tunda hili ni kubwa. — The class 5 singular typically takes no visible adjective prefix; ma- belongs to the class 6 plural form, as in matunda haya ni makubwa.

Common Mistake — Using the wrong demonstrative for class 5/6 nouns

Error: Hii ni tunda zuri. (using the class 9/10 demonstrative hii with a class 5/6 noun)

Correction: Hili ni tunda zuri. — Class 5/6 nouns take hili/haya, not the class 9/10 forms hii/hizi learned in Chapter 3.

Common Mistake — Overgeneralizing -ingi or -ote to unscheduled classes

Error: Guessing at an -ingi or -ote form for a noun class not yet covered in this chapter's controlled set.

Correction: This chapter limits productive use of -ingi and -ote to classes 2, 6, 8, and 10. For other classes, it is acceptable to use a simpler alternative, such as repeating the noun or using a number, until later chapters expand the pattern.

Common Mistake — Making a request sound like a command

Error: Nipe machungwa! as the default request in a shop, without softening language.

Correction: Naomba machungwa or Tafadhali nipe machungwa is more appropriate for ordinary shop interactions. Reserve the bare imperative for very informal or urgent situations, or soften it with tafadhali.

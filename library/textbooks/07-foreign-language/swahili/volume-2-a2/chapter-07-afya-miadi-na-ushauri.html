7

Afya, Miadi, na Ushauri

Health, Appointments, and Advice · Speak and Write Swahili, Volume II: Expanding Everyday and Community Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe common symptoms and health problems.
  2. Form negative present-tense sentences systematically, including with high-frequency verbs.
  3. Form negative past-tense sentences systematically, and distinguish them from the negative present.
  4. Schedule, confirm, and change a medical appointment.
  5. Give simple advice and express obligation using lazima, afadhali, and ni vizuri.
  6. Ask for clarification politely and signal urgency appropriately.
  7. Interpret a dialogue, a clinic announcement, and a short reading about health and appointments.
  8. Write a short clinic message and role-play giving advice.

Communication Scenario

After the group's trip to Mombasa, James wakes up with a headache and a fever. Halima, who has some first-aid experience, encourages him to call the clinic that Amina, a nurse the group met on the trip, recommended. James must describe his symptoms to the receptionist, understand the receptionist's questions and instructions, and later receive advice from Amina about what he should and should not do while he recovers.

  1. This chapter systematically develops negative present and negative past forms, building directly on the affirmative tense system consolidated in Chapter 6.
  2. Obligation and advice formulas (lazima, afadhali, ni vizuri) become productive for the first time, always tied to health and self-care contexts.
  3. Vocabulary expands to cover symptoms, appointments, the pharmacy, and simple medical advice.

Pronunciation and Spelling

Kuomba ufafanuzi kwa heshima — Polite clarification and urgency

When describing symptoms or asking for help, speakers commonly soften urgent requests with polite openers, even when the situation feels pressing. Pay attention to how stress and intonation stay level and controlled, rather than rising sharply, even in an urgent request such as asking to see a doctor quickly.

PhraseFunctionListening/speaking focus
Samahani, unaweza kurudia?Ask someone to repeatLevel, unhurried intonation even when anxious.
Naomba nikuulize tena.Ask permission to ask againClear penultimate stress on ulize.
Ni jambo la dharura.Signal urgency without raising volumeEven stress across the phrase, not a shout.
Sound Focus — The negative marker ha-

The negative prefix ha- and its person-marked forms (si-, hu-, ha-, hatu-, ham-, hawa-) are short and can be easy to miss before a fast-spoken verb. Listen for the negative marker at the very start of the verb word, before the subject-marker position you already know from affirmative sentences.

Grammar Instruction

This chapter systematically introduces negative present and negative past forms and develops obligation and advice formulas in health-related contexts.

7.1 Negative present tense

Purpose: say what someone does not do, is not doing, or is not the case right now.

Language Building Block
negative subject marker + stem + -i (final vowel change from -a to -i)
Sisomi habari leo.
I am not reading the news today.
Hasikii vizuri.
He/she does not hear well.
Hatupendi dawa hii.
We do not like this medicine.

The negative present uses its own set of subject markers, different from the affirmative ones you already know, and the final vowel of most verbs changes from -a to -i.

PersonAffirmative markerNegative markerExample
Ini-si-Sipendi kikohozi hiki.
you (sg.)u-hu-Hutaki dawa?
he/shea-ha-Hajisikii vizuri.
wetu-hatu-Hatuendi hospitali leo.
you (pl.)m-ham-Hamsikii?
theywa-hawa-Hawapendi sindano.

Kumbuka — Note: A few very common verbs, such as kujua (to know), keep an irregular negative present form (sijui, "I don't know"). You will meet these as formulaic items; they do not change the regular pattern above.

7.2 Negative past tense

Purpose: say what someone did not do or what did not happen at a specific past time.

Language Building Block
negative subject marker + -ku- + stem + -a
Sikuenda kazini jana.
I did not go to work yesterday.
Hakupiga simu asubuhi.
He/she did not call in the morning.
Hatukumwona daktari wiki iliyopita.
We did not see the doctor last week.

The negative past uses the same negative subject markers from 7.1, adds -ku- immediately after the marker, and keeps the final vowel -a, unlike the negative present.

TenseStructureExampleMeaning
Negative presentneg. marker + stem + -iSipendi sindano.I don't like injections.
Negative pastneg. marker + -ku- + stem + -aSikupenda sindano ile.I didn't like that injection.

7.3 Obligation and advice: lazima, afadhali, ni vizuri

Purpose: tell someone what they must do, what would be better, or what is a good idea, especially about health.

Language Building Block
lazima / afadhali / ni vizuri + subject marker + -e (subjunctive) or infinitive ku- + stem
Lazima uende hospitali.
You must go to the hospital.
Afadhali upumzike leo.
It would be better if you rested today.
Ni vizuri kunywa maji mengi.
It is good to drink plenty of water.

Lazima and afadhali are typically followed by a subject marker plus the subjunctive final vowel -e, matching the person being advised. Ni vizuri is commonly followed by the infinitive form (ku- + stem) for general advice not aimed at one specific person. All three formulas are treated as fixed patterns in this chapter; the subjunctive itself is developed further in later chapters.

FormulaStrengthTypical follow-up
lazimaStrong — necessity, obligationsubject marker + -e
afadhaliMedium — recommendation, a better choicesubject marker + -e
ni vizuriMild — general good practiceku- + stem (infinitive)

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: James anapiga simu kliniki kupata miadi, kisha anazungumza na Amina, muuguzi ambaye klabu ilikutana naye Mombasa.

(Setting: James calls the clinic to get an appointment, then speaks with Amina, the nurse the club met in Mombasa.)

MPOKEZIKliniki ya Afya Njema, habari?Afya Njema Clinic, how can I help?
JAMESHabari. Naomba miadi. Sijisikii vizuri; nina homa na maumivu ya kichwa.Hello. I'd like an appointment. I'm not feeling well; I have a fever and a headache.
MPOKEZIPole sana. Je, una kikohozi pia?I'm very sorry. Do you also have a cough?
JAMESHapana, sina kikohozi, lakini nina kizunguzungu kidogo.No, I don't have a cough, but I have a little dizziness.
MPOKEZISamahani, unaweza kurudia? Sikusikia vizuri.Sorry, can you repeat that? I didn't hear well.
JAMESNina homa, maumivu ya kichwa, na kizunguzungu kidogo.I have a fever, a headache, and a little dizziness.
MPOKEZISawa. Hatuna nafasi asubuhi, lakini kuna nafasi saa nane mchana. Lazima ufike dakika kumi mapema.Okay. We don't have space in the morning, but there's space at two o'clock in the afternoon. You must arrive ten minutes early.
JAMESAsante. Nitakuja saa nane.Thank you. I'll come at two o'clock.
(Kliniki — At the clinic)
AMINAJames! Sikutarajia kukuona hapa. Unajisikiaje?James! I didn't expect to see you here. How are you feeling?
JAMESSijisikii vizuri sana, lakini si mbaya sana pia.I'm not feeling very well, but it's not too bad either.
AMINAAfadhali upumzike siku mbili na unywe maji mengi. Ni vizuri pia kutumia dawa hii mara mbili kwa siku. Usiende kazini kesho.It would be better if you rested for two days and drank plenty of water. It's also good to take this medicine twice a day. Don't go to work tomorrow.
JAMESSawa, nitafuata ushauri wako. Asante, Amina.Okay, I'll follow your advice. Thank you, Amina.

Vocabulary note: sijisikii, sina, hatuna, and sikusikia show negative present and negative past forms from 7.1–7.2; lazima, afadhali, and ni vizuri show the advice formulas from 7.3.

Writing Workshop

  2. Panga mawazo yako. Note your (real or imagined) symptoms, what you have not been able to do because of them, and what advice you received or would give.
  3. Andika rasimu yako ya kwanza. Write a connected 90–120-word clinic message in Swahili. Use at least two negative forms (one present, one past) and at least one obligation or advice formula.
  4. Kagua kwa orodha hapa chini. Check your draft against the editing checklist before writing your final version.
  5. Andika nakala safi. Produce a clean final version to share with a partner or keep for your records.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • At least one negative present and one negative past form are used correctly.
  • At least one obligation or advice formula (lazima, afadhali, or ni vizuri) is used correctly.
  • Object markers and tense forms from Chapter 6 remain accurate wherever they appear.
  • All verbs are written in normal unhyphenated Swahili spelling.
  • Capitalization, spacing, punctuation, and ng' are correct throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Clinics, pharmacies, and appointments

Clinics and pharmacies play a central role in everyday healthcare across many Swahili-speaking communities, alongside hospitals for more serious concerns. Appointment systems, walk-in availability, and the role of pharmacists in offering initial advice can vary by country, city, and type of facility.

Culture in Context — Softening advice and concern

Advice about health is often introduced gently, with expressions like afadhali or ni vizuri rather than a blunt command, even when the advice is important. Directly telling someone what they must do, using lazima, is generally reserved for situations that genuinely call for urgency or authority, such as a nurse or doctor speaking to a patient.

Regional Variation — Clinic and pharmacy naming

Clinic and pharmacy names, opening hours, and prescription requirements vary by country and region; this chapter's fictional clinic, Kliniki ya Afya Njema, and fictional pharmacy, Duka la Dawa la Uzima, represent common naming patterns rather than specific real establishments. Learners should confirm current facilities, hours, and requirements locally.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Keeping the final vowel -a in the negative present

Error: Sipenda dawa hii. (final vowel left as -a)

Correction: Sipendi dawa hii. — The negative present regularly changes the final vowel from -a to -i; only the negative past keeps -a.

Common Mistake — Confusing negative present and negative past forms

Error: Sikwenda kazini leo. when the speaker means they are not going today (present/future intention), not that they failed to go in the past.

Correction: Siendi kazini leo. — Use the negative present for a current or planned state; reserve -ku- negative past forms for a specific completed past event, as in Sikuenda kazini jana ("I didn't go to work yesterday").

Common Mistake — Using lazima where afadhali or ni vizuri fits better

Error: Lazima unywe maji mengi. spoken casually between friends about a mild recommendation, sounding stronger than intended.

Correction: Ni vizuri kunywa maji mengi. — Reserve lazima for genuine necessity or authority; use afadhali or ni vizuri for softer, more general advice.

Common Mistake — Dropping the negative subject marker

Error: Nasikii vizuri. intended to mean "I don't hear well."

Correction: Sisikii vizuri. — The negative subject marker (si-, hu-, ha-, hatu-, ham-, hawa-) replaces the affirmative one; it does not simply attach a negative word to the affirmative form.

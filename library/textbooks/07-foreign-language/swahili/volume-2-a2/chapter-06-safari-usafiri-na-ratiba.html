Safari, Usafiri, na Ratiba

Travel, Transport, and Itineraries · Speak and Write Swahili, Volume II: Expanding Everyday and Community Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Talk about future travel plans using the future tense productively.
  2. Contrast future, past, and perfect/resultative forms when narrating a trip.
  3. Ask about and understand transport options, schedules, and ticket prices.
  4. Use person object markers (-ni-, -ku-, -m-, -tu-, -wa-) in travel and help contexts.
  5. Describe a travel problem and ask someone for help solving it.
  6. Follow and produce a simple travel itinerary.
  7. Interpret a dialogue, an announcement, and a short reading about travel.
  8. Write a short account of a trip and a travel-related message.

Communication Scenario

The Klabu ya Kiswahili has decided to take a weekend trip together to Mombasa. Halima is in charge of buying the bus tickets and checking the schedule, while James has already traveled that route before and can help the group plan around a problem: the earlier bus they wanted is full. Together the club must decide on a new departure time, talk about lodging, and prepare a short itinerary for the group.

  1. This chapter develops future-tense travel planning alongside past and perfect narration of trips already taken.
  2. Person object markers become productive in the context of asking for and offering help while traveling.
  3. Vocabulary expands to cover tickets, routes, lodging, and common travel problems.

Pronunciation and Spelling

Kusikia alama za wakati — Listening for tense markers

The tense markers -ta-, -li-, and -me- are short and easy to miss in fast, connected speech, especially in travel announcements. Train your ear to catch the vowel and consonant of each marker immediately after the subject marker, rather than relying only on context.

MarkerMeaningListening focus
-ta-futureClear /a/ vowel; often the fastest of the three markers.
-li-pastShort, crisp /i/ vowel immediately after the subject marker.
-me-perfect/resultativeNasal onset; do not confuse with the subject marker m- on class 1 nouns.
Sound Focus — Announcement rhythm

Public announcements, such as those heard at a bus station, tend to be spoken slightly more slowly and with more even stress than casual conversation. Use this to your advantage when practicing listening: announcements are often easier to parse than fast dialogue between friends.

Grammar Instruction

This chapter contrasts future, past, and perfect forms in travel narration and consolidates person object markers in travel and help contexts.

6.1 Future, past, and perfect in travel narration

Purpose: plan a future trip, narrate a past trip, and describe its present relevance.

Language Building Block
subject marker + -ta- + stem (future) · subject marker + -li- + stem (past) · subject marker + -me- + stem (perfect/resultative)
Tutasafiri kwenda Mombasa kesho.
We will travel to Mombasa tomorrow.
Tulisafiri kwenda Mombasa mwaka jana.
We traveled to Mombasa last year.
Tumefika Mombasa.
We have arrived in Mombasa. (relevant now)

These three markers already exist in your productive system from earlier chapters. This chapter's task is to choose among them correctly when narrating a connected trip: a plan is -ta-, a completed past event is -li-, and a result still relevant to the present moment is -me-.

FormUse in travel narrationExample
-ta-Plans, itineraries, predictionsTutaondoka saa mbili asubuhi.
-li-A completed trip or event with a clear past timeTulifika Mombasa Jumamosi.
-me-A result still true now, or very recent newsBasi limejaa.

6.2 Person object markers in travel and help contexts

Purpose: refer to specific people as the object of a verb, especially when asking for or offering help.

Language Building Block
subject marker + TAM marker + object marker + stem + final vowel
Karani alinisaidia kununua tiketi.
The clerk helped me buy a ticket. (-ni- = me)
Nitakusaidia kubeba mzigo.
I will help you carry the luggage. (-ku- = you)
Tumemsaidia dereva kutafuta njia.
We have helped the driver find the route. (-m- = him/her)
Object markerRefers toStatus this chapter
-ni-meProductive
-ku-you (singular)Productive
-m-/-mw-him/herProductive
-tu-usProductive
-wa-them (people)Productive

Note that -m-/-mw- is placed immediately before the verb stem, and its exact shape depends on the vowel that follows. You met human object markers receptively in earlier chapters; this chapter consolidates all five person forms for independent production, always in the context of specific, identifiable people.

6.3 Asking about schedules and prices

Purpose: ask practical questions needed to plan and buy transport.

  • Basi la Mombasa litaondoka saa ngapi? What time will the Mombasa bus leave?
  • Tiketi moja ni bei gani? How much is one ticket?
  • Kuna basi lingine baadaye? Is there another bus later?

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: Halima na James wako stesheni ya basi wakinunua tiketi za safari ya klabu kwenda Mombasa.

(Setting: Halima and James are at the bus station buying tickets for the club's trip to Mombasa.)

HALIMASamahani, basi la Mombasa litaondoka saa ngapi?Excuse me, what time will the Mombasa bus leave?
KARANIBasi la saa tatu limejaa, samahani. Kuna basi lingine saa tano.The nine o'clock bus is full, sorry. There is another bus at eleven.
JAMESTafadhali, unaweza kutusaidia kupata viti sita kwenye basi la saa tano?Please, can you help us get six seats on the eleven o'clock bus?
KARANINdiyo, nitawaandikia majina yenu sasa hivi. Tiketi moja ni shilingi elfu mbili.Yes, I'll write down your names right now. One ticket is two thousand shillings.
HALIMASawa. Mwaka jana, nilisafiri kwa basi hili hili kwenda Mombasa, na safari ilikuwa nzuri sana.Okay. Last year, I traveled on this very bus to Mombasa, and the trip was very nice.
KARANIVizuri. Nimewaandikia tiketi sita. Tafadhali fikeni stesheni dakika thelathini kabla ya safari.Good. I've written you six tickets. Please arrive at the station thirty minutes before departure.
JAMESAsante sana. Umetusaidia sana leo.Thank you very much. You've helped us a lot today.
(Baadaye, njiani — Later, on the road)
HALIMAJames, tutafika Mombasa saa ngapi, kwa maoni yako?James, what time do you think we'll arrive in Mombasa?
JAMESTutafika jioni, karibu saa kumi na mbili. Hoteli yetu imepanga kutupokea stesheni.We'll arrive in the evening, around six o'clock. Our hotel has arranged to receive us at the station.

Vocabulary note: litaondoka, limejaa, and nilisafiri show the three tense markers from 6.1 in context; kutusaidia, tumewaandikia, and umetusaidia show object markers from 6.2.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Bus travel in East Africa

Long-distance bus travel is a common and often social way to move between cities in many parts of East Africa, alongside trains, shared taxis, and air travel over longer distances. Bus companies frequently operate multiple daily departures on popular routes, and ticket prices, schedules, and comfort levels can vary considerably between companies.

Culture in Context — Asking for help while traveling

Station staff, drivers, and fellow travelers are commonly approached for help with schedules, luggage, or unexpected problems, as in this chapter's dialogue. A polite request, often opening with samahani or tafadhali, is the expected way to begin such an exchange in most settings.

Regional Variation — Naming transport companies and routes

Bus company names, route numbering, and departure-time conventions vary by country and city; this chapter's fictional company, Safari Njema, represents a common naming pattern rather than a specific real company. Learners traveling in a specific city should confirm current companies, routes, and prices locally.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using -li- for a result still relevant now

Error: Basi lilijaa. when the bus is still full right now and the speaker means to explain why boarding is currently impossible.

Correction: Basi limejaa. — Use -me- when the past action produced a result that is still true at the moment of speaking; use -li- for a completed event with a clear past time reference.

Common Mistake — Misplacing the object marker

Error: Nitasaidia ku Halima. (object marker left as a separate word)

Correction: Nitamsaidia Halima. — The object marker attaches directly inside the verb, immediately before the stem; it is not a separate word, and it may also make the following noun optional once the reference is clear.

Common Mistake — Confusing -tu- (us) with -wa- (them)

Error: Using -wa- to mean "us" or -tu- to mean "them."

Correction: -tu- always refers to "us"; -wa- always refers to "them" (people). Check the intended subject carefully before choosing the object marker.

Common Mistake — Overusing the future for near-certain scheduled events

Error: Treating every travel statement as automatically future, even when narrating something already completed.

Correction: Choose the tense marker based on the actual timing of the event relative to the moment of speaking, not simply because the sentence is about travel.

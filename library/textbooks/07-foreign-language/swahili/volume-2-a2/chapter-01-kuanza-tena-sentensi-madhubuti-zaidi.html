Kuanza Tena: Sentensi Madhubuti Zaidi

Re-entry Review and Stronger Sentences · Speak and Write Swahili, Volume II: Expanding Everyday and Community Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Reintroduce yourself and re-establish a conversation after a break, with more accurate and more detailed sentences than in Volume I.
  2. Distinguish and correctly select among present -na-, past -li-, future -ta-, and perfect -me- in review contexts.
  3. Refresh and apply subject-marker agreement for noun classes 1/2 (M-WA), 5/6 (JI-MA), 7/8 (KI-VI), and 9/10 (N).
  4. Form negative present-tense and negative past-tense statements systematically, using the correct negative subject markers and final-vowel changes.
  5. Identify and repair common Volume I errors in pronunciation, agreement, and tense selection.
  6. Interpret a short review dialogue and a short review reading passage.
  7. Write a connected 120–150-word "return" profile describing who you are and what you have been doing.

Communication Scenario

Klabu ya Kiswahili — a community Swahili conversation club — is starting a new term after a break between courses. Some members are returning from Volume I; others are joining the club for the first time this term. On the first evening, members mingle, reintroduce themselves, ask what others have been doing, and correct small mistakes together as they warm back up to speaking Swahili. This chapter helps you re-enter Swahili communication with stronger, more accurate sentences before Volume II introduces new material.

  1. This chapter introduces one new productive system — systematic negative present and negative past forms — and otherwise reviews and strengthens material from Volume I.
  2. Self-correction and peer correction are treated as normal parts of returning to a language after a break, not as signs of failure.

Grammar Instruction

This chapter reviews and strengthens the verb template, tense-marker contrast, and noun-class agreement from Volume I, and introduces one new system: systematic negative present and negative past forms.

1.1 Reviewing the verb template

Since Volume I, you have built Swahili verbs from ordered positions. Review the basic productive template before adding negation to it:

Language Building Block
subject marker + tense/aspect marker + verb root + final vowel
Ninasoma Kiswahili.
I am studying Swahili. (ni- + -na- + som- + -a)
Tulisafiri Zanzibar mwaka jana.
We traveled to Zanzibar last year. (tu- + -li- + safir- + -i)
Nimemaliza mapumziko yangu.
I have finished my break. (ni- + -me- + maliz- + -a)
MarkerMeaningExample
-na-presentninasoma — I study/am studying
-li-pastnilisoma — I studied
-ta-futurenitasoma — I will study
-me-perfect / current resulting statenimesoma — I have studied
Language Note — Choosing the right marker under pressure

When speaking quickly after a break, learners often default to -na- for everything. Before you speak, ask: Is this happening now (-na-)? Did it happen and end (-li-)? Will it happen later (-ta-)? Is a past action still relevant right now (-me-)? Slowing down for one extra second to answer this question will fix most tense errors.

1.3 Systematic negation: present and past

Purpose: move from the isolated negative expressions used in Volume I to a systematic, productive negative pattern for present and past statements.

Swahili negative sentences use a different set of subject markers than affirmative sentences, and the negative present has its own final vowel. Study the negative subject markers first:

PersonAffirmative markerNegative marker
Ini-si-
you (sg.)u-hu-
he/shea-ha-
wetu-hatu-
you (pl.)m-ham-
theywa-hawa-
Language Building Block
negative subject marker + verb root + -i (present)  |  negative subject marker + ku + verb root + -a (past)
Sisomi Kifaransa.
I don't study French. (si- + som- + -i — note the vowel changes to -i)
Hukusoma jana.
You didn't study yesterday. (hu- + ku + som- + -a — the tense marker -li- is replaced by ku)
Hatukwenda sokoni wiki iliyopita.
We didn't go to the market last week.
Language Note — Two different negative patterns

Negative present replaces the final vowel -a with -i and drops the tense marker -na- entirely: ninasoma → sisomi. Negative past replaces -li- with ku and keeps the normal final vowel -a: nilisoma → sikusoma. These two patterns are different from each other and must each be learned on their own terms.

Negative future (sitasoma) and negative perfect (sijasoma) follow their own patterns and are previewed here for recognition only; they become fully productive in later chapters of this volume.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: Ni jioni ya kwanza ya muhula mpya katika Klabu ya Kiswahili huko Nairobi. Wanachama wawili, Halima na James, wanaonana tena baada ya mapumziko.

(Setting: It is the first evening of a new term at the Swahili Club in Nairobi. Two members, Halima and James, see each other again after a break.)

JAMESHalima! Habari za mapumziko? Umerudi!Halima! How was the break? You're back!
HALIMANzuri, asante. Ndiyo, nimerudi, lakini nimekosa mazoezi. Sikusoma Kiswahili kwa miezi mitatu.Good, thank you. Yes, I'm back, but I'm out of practice. I didn't study Swahili for three months.
JAMESUsijali. Mimi pia. Lakini bado ninakumbuka mengi. Niambie, unafanya kazi gani sasa?Don't worry. Me too. But I still remember a lot. Tell me, what work are you doing now?
HALIMABado ninafanya kazi hospitalini, lakini sasa sifanyi kazi ya usiku tena.I still work at the hospital, but now I don't work night shifts anymore.
JAMESVizuri sana. Na wikendi iliyopita, ulifanya nini?Very good. And last weekend, what did you do?
HALIMANilitembelea rafiki yangu Mombasa. Hatukwenda baharini kwa sababu ya mvua, lakini tulikula chakula kitamu sana.I visited my friend in Mombasa. We didn't go to the sea because of the rain, but we ate very tasty food.
JAMESNadhani ulisema "hatukwenda," sivyo? Hiyo ni sahihi — hasi ya wakati uliopita.I think you said "hatukwenda," right? That's correct — the negative past.
HALIMANdiyo, sahihi! Ngoja, nirudi nyuma kidogo — pia nilitaka kusema kwamba rafiki yangu hapendi mvua kabisa.Yes, correct! Wait, let me go back a little — I also wanted to say that my friend doesn't like rain at all.
JAMESHaha, mimi pia sipendi mvua. Sasa, tuendelee na darasa. Mwalimu wetu anakuja sasa hivi.Haha, I don't like rain either. Now, let's continue with class. Our teacher is coming right now.
HALIMASawa. Nina furaha kurudi klabuni.Okay. I'm happy to be back at the club.

Vocabulary note: hospitalini, klabuni = locative forms of hospitali and klabu (recognition — full locative agreement is developed later in this volume); kwa sababu ya = because of.

Writing Workshop

  1. Soma mfano. Reread Maandishi 2 (Tafakari ya Halima). Underline every tense marker and every negative verb you find.
  2. Panga mawazo yako. Plan a personal "return" profile covering: who you are, what you have been doing since you last used Swahili, one thing you did not do or could not do during that time (negative past), one thing you do not do anymore or do not like (negative present), and one plan for this term (future).
  3. Andika rasimu yako ya kwanza. Write a connected 120–150-word profile in Swahili. Use at least three different noun classes with correct agreement, all four core tense/aspect markers reviewed in this chapter, and at least one negative present and one negative past verb.
  4. Kagua kwa orodha hapa chini. Check your draft against the editing checklist before writing your final version.
  5. Andika nakala safi. Produce a clean final version to share with a partner or keep for your records.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • Every tense/aspect marker matches the intended time reference.
  • Negative present verbs use the negative subject marker and end in -i.
  • Negative past verbs use the negative subject marker, ku, and end in -a.
  • Subject markers agree with the noun class of the subject throughout.
  • Possessive and adjective agreement match the noun class of the noun described.
  • All verbs are written in normal unhyphenated Swahili spelling.
  • Capitalization, spacing, punctuation, and ng' are correct throughout.

Kazi ya ziada — Additional task: Exchange your draft with a partner. Find one negative verb in your partner's writing and confirm together whether it follows the correct pattern for its tense.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Re-entry and community learning

In many Swahili-speaking community classes, clubs, and study groups, returning members are commonly welcomed warmly, and a period of shared review is often expected at the start of a new term, as in many language-learning communities worldwide. Acknowledging that some members are "rusty" and need review is generally seen as considerate group management, not as a sign that the group is behind. Specific practices vary by setting, instructor, and community.

Culture in Context — Correcting each other kindly

Peer correction, when offered gently and accepted openly, is a normal and valued part of language-learning communities in Swahili-speaking contexts, as in most learning communities. Phrases such as nadhani nimekosea and hiyo si sahihi, sivyo? allow learners to raise a possible error without appearing confrontational. Treat correction — of yourself and of others — as an act of care for shared progress.

Regional Variation — Returning to your Volume I model

Volume I established Standard Swahili productive forms as your default model, with labeled Tanzanian and Kenyan alternatives where relevant. As you resume study in Volume II, continue using that same core productive model. A conversation partner who studied with a different regional or informal starting point may use forms that differ slightly; this remains a normal feature of a living language rather than a sign that your Volume I training was incorrect.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using -na- for a completed past action

Error: Wiki iliyopita, ninaenda Mombasa. (using present -na- with a clear past time expression)

Correction: Wiki iliyopita, nilienda Mombasa. — A time expression that establishes a completed past event requires past -li-, not present -na-. This is the single most common tense error after a break from Swahili study.

Common Mistake — Forming the negative present like the negative past

Error: Sikusoma Kiswahili sasa hivi. (using the negative past pattern to express a present negative meaning)

Correction: Sisomi Kiswahili sasa hivi. — Negative present uses the negative subject marker plus final vowel -i and no ku. Negative past uses the negative subject marker plus ku and final vowel -a. Confusing the two patterns changes the time reference of the sentence.

Common Mistake — Losing noun-class agreement partway through a sentence

Error: Nina kitabu kizuri. Anakisoma kila siku. (using the class 1/2 subject marker a- for a KI-VI object being read)

Correction: Nina kitabu kizuri. Ninakisoma kila siku. — Reserve a- for class 1/2 human subjects. When referring back to a KI-VI noun as the object of a verb, use the KI-VI object marker -ki- attached to the correct subject.

Common Mistake — Treating "rustiness" as a reason to avoid speaking

Error: Staying silent or switching to English because Volume I material feels unfamiliar again

Correction: Use review-specific repair language instead — Nadhani nimesahau hii, ngoja... ("I think I've forgotten this, wait...") or Bado ninakumbuka kidogo ("I still remember a little"). Speaking through initial rustiness, in Swahili, is the fastest way to rebuild fluency.

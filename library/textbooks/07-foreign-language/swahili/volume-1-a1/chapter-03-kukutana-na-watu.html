Kukutana na Watu

Meeting People · Speak and Write Swahili: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Introduce another person by name and background.
  2. Ask and answer where someone comes from, using kutoka wapi.
  3. State nationality, home country, and the language or languages someone speaks.
  4. Name your occupation and ask about someone else's.
  5. Use first- and second-person subject markers (ni-, u-, tu-, m-) and the M-WA third-person markers (a-, wa-) in simple present-tense statements.
  6. Recognize that language names beginning with Ki- form a specific pattern in Swahili, distinct from ordinary nouns that happen to begin with those letters.
  7. Ask information questions using nani, nini, wapi, and kutoka wapi.
  8. Count from 21 to 100.

Communication Scenario

You are at an East African community event in your city. People are arriving from Tanzania, Kenya, Uganda, and other parts of the region — and from further afield. You want to strike up real conversations: find out where people are from, what languages they speak, and what kind of work they do. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to do all of that in Swahili — and introduce your new acquaintances to others.

  1. Think about the last time you met someone new. What three or four questions did you ask them first? Which of those can you already express in Swahili from Chapters 1 and 2?
  2. In English, how do you introduce a friend to someone who doesn't know them? Write down the words you would use. You will learn the Swahili equivalent in this chapter.

Pronunciation and Spelling

The sounds r and l

Swahili has both r and l, and they represent distinct sounds that affect meaning. In many East African English accents these two sounds can merge, so Swahili learners need to distinguish them carefully in both spelling and speech.

The Swahili r is typically a flap or tap, similar to the r in some British or American speech when it falls between vowels — think of the t in the American English word butter. It is not the strongly rolled Spanish r, nor the French back-of-throat r. It is a quick, light contact of the tongue tip against the ridge behind the upper front teeth.

The Swahili l is a clear lateral sound, like the English l at the start of a word such as light.

Sound Focus — r and l in contrast

Say these pairs aloud and listen for the difference. Spelling them correctly matters because they represent different words:

  • reli (railway)  vs.  leli (today — colloquial form of leo)
  • kura (vote)  vs.  kula (to eat)
  • roho (spirit / soul)  vs.  loho (not a Standard Swahili word — notice the difference when you speak it)

When spelling country names or language names aloud, be careful: Kireno (Portuguese) has an r; Kiingereza has both ng and r. Always say the word from the spelling, not from an English approximation.

j, y, and ny

Three sounds that can trip up English speakers:

j in Swahili is pronounced like the English j in jump. Examples from this chapter: Juma (a name), jibu (answer).

y in Swahili is always a consonant sound, like the English y in yes, never a vowel. It is very common at the start of words and between morphemes. Examples: yeye (he / she), yangu (my — introduced in Chapter 4).

ny is a single sound, the palatalized nasal heard in the English word onion or the Spanish ñ. In Swahili it always appears as the two letters ny written together. Examples: nchi (country), nyumbani (at home — introduced in Chapter 6). The word nchi has a syllabic n- before the ch; this is discussed further in Chapter 6.

Stress in country and language names

Swahili places stress on the penultimate (second-to-last) syllable as the productive default for most words. Apply this consistently to country and language names:

  • Ki-swa-hi-liStress falls on hi, the second-to-last syllable.
  • Ki-in-ge-re-zaStress falls on re.
  • Ta-n-za-ni-aStress falls on ni.
  • U-fa-ran-saStress falls on ran.

Proper names, especially borrowed names from other languages, can sometimes deviate from penultimate stress. When in doubt, listen to a qualified native speaker rather than relying on analogy alone.

Practice — Spelling aloud: With a partner, take turns spelling any five country or language names from this chapter's vocabulary, letter by letter, in Swahili. Your partner writes what they hear and then says the word aloud with correct stress. Then check the original spelling.

Grammar Instruction

3.1 The Swahili verb template — first look

Purpose: understand why a Swahili verb can express what takes several English words.

In Chapters 1 and 2 you met the copular ni and si for simple statements such as Mimi ni mwalimu. Now you will work with full finite verbs — verbs that carry a subject marker and a tense marker inside a single word.

A Swahili finite verb is built from ordered slots. At this stage, the three slots you need are:

Subject marker ni- / u- / a- who does the action
Tense marker -na- present time
Verb root + vowel -toka / -sema the action

Combine the slots and you get a complete verb:

  • Ninatoka Tanzania. ni-na-toka → I-PRESENT-come.from I come from Tanzania.
  • Unasema Kiswahili. u-na-sema → you(sg)-PRESENT-speak You speak Swahili.
  • Anafanya kazi kama daktari. a-na-fanya → he/she-PRESENT-do He / She works as a doctor.
Grammar Note — Analysis versus normal spelling

The hyphenated form ni-na-toka is a pedagogical analysis that helps you see the morphemes. In normal Swahili writing, the word is always spelled without hyphens: ninatoka. Never write hyphens in ordinary sentences, dialogue, or your own Swahili production. Hyphens appear only when an explanation specifically asks you to identify the parts.

Purpose: match the correct subject marker to who is speaking, being spoken to, or being spoken about.

The third-person markers a- (singular) and wa- (plural) belong to the M-WA noun class — the class of people introduced in Chapter 1. When a subject noun refers to one person, use a-; when it refers to more than one person, use wa-.

  • Amina anatoka Kenya. Amina comes from Kenya. (one person → a-)
  • Amina na Juma wanatoka Kenya. Amina and Juma come from Kenya. (two people → wa-)
Grammar Note — Pronouns and subject markers

In Chapter 1 you learned the independent pronouns: mimi, wewe, yeye, sisi, ninyi, wao. In Swahili, the subject marker on the verb already identifies who is doing the action. Independent pronouns are used for emphasis, contrast, or clarity — not as a required grammatical step before every verb. Ninatoka Kenya is complete and natural. Mimi ninatoka Kenya adds emphasis to "me specifically." Do not treat Swahili independent pronouns the way English treats subject pronouns — in English, I is required; in Swahili, mimi is optional emphasis.

3.3 ni and si for identity — review and extension

Purpose: use ni and si to state and deny identity, nationality, and occupation.

The copular ni and negative si from Chapter 1 are heavily used in this chapter. They do not use the subject-plus-tense-plus-root template; they are unchanging words that link a subject to an identity:

  • Yeye ni mwalimu.He / She is a teacher.
  • Sisi ni wanafunzi.We are students.
  • Mimi si daktari.I am not a doctor.
  • Wao si Watanzania.They are not Tanzanians.

Notice that ni and si are the same regardless of who the subject is. They do not change for person or number. However, the noun after them must be correct — singular or plural to match meaning.

3.4 The present tense with -na-

Purpose: describe ongoing and habitual present actions — speaking languages, doing work, coming from a place.

The tense marker -na- sits between the subject marker and the verb root. It signals present time — broadly equivalent to both English simple present (I speak) and present progressive (I am speaking) in many contexts. At this stage, treat it as the marker of present-tense action and let context clarify whether the meaning is habitual or momentary.

Verb (infinitive)Root1sg (I)3sg (he/she)3pl (they)

The infinitive ku- prefix is the citation form — the form you find in a dictionary. When you build a finite verb, the ku- drops and the root carries the meaning: ku-toka → ni-na-toka.

3.5 Information questions — nani, nini, wapi, kutoka wapi

Purpose: ask who someone is, what something is, where someone is, and where someone comes from.

Neno la swali (Question word)MaanaMfano (Example)
naniwhoHuyu ni nani? — Who is this?
niniwhatUnafanya kazi gani? — What work do you do?
wapiwhereUnakaa wapi? — Where do you live?
kutoka wapifrom where / where … fromUnatoka wapi? — Where are you from?
ganiwhich / what kindUnasema lugha gani? — What language do you speak?

In Swahili, question words do not require inversion of the verb and subject in the way English does. The basic statement word order is maintained, and the question word typically appears at the end or in the position of the information being sought:

  • Unatoka wapi?Where do you come from? (literally: you-come-from where?)
  • Huyu ni nani?Who is this? (literally: this is who?)
  • Unasema lugha gani?What language do you speak? (literally: you-speak language which?)
Language Focus

One Swahili verb word does the work of several English words. When you say ninatoka, you have expressed "I," the present tense, and the meaning "come from" — all inside one word. This is because Swahili is an agglutinative language: it builds meaning by adding meaningful slots onto a root. English, by contrast, keeps these elements separate: pronoun + auxiliary (sometimes) + verb. As you learn Swahili, resist the habit of looking for separate English-style subject pronouns and helpers. The verb already contains that information. Focus on learning the slot order: subject marker + tense marker + verb root + final vowel.

A predictable trap: English speakers sometimes say *Mimi ninatoka in every sentence, treating mimi as obligatory the way English I is. Use mimi only for emphasis or contrast. Let the verb do its job.

Model Dialogue

Mazingira: Sherehe ya jamii. Baraka anaongea na mgeni mpya, Fatuma. Kisha Amina anajiunga nao. Lugha: Kiswahili cha mazungumzo ya kawaida, rejista ya heshima wastani.
Setting: A community gathering. Baraka is talking with a new acquaintance, Fatuma. Then Amina joins them. Register: everyday conversational Swahili, semi-formal.

BARAKA Habari za asubuhi? Good morning! (lit. What is the news of the morning?)
FATUMA Nzuri, asante. Habari zako? Good / Fine, thank you. And your news?
BARAKA Salama. Mimi ni Baraka. Unaitwa nani? Well / Fine. I am Baraka. What are you called? (What is your name?)
FATUMA Ninaitwa Fatuma. Ninafurahi kukujua, Baraka. I am called Fatuma. I am pleased to meet you, Baraka.
BARAKA Mimi pia. Fatuma, unatoka wapi? Me too. Fatuma, where do you come from?
FATUMA Ninatoka Tanzania, kutoka Dodoma. Na wewe? I come from Tanzania, from Dodoma. And you?
BARAKA Ninatoka Kenya, kutoka Mombasa. Ninakaa hapa sasa — nimekuja kwa sherehe hii. I come from Kenya, from Mombasa. I am living here now — I came for this gathering.
FATUMA Kwa kweli? Unasema lugha gani? Really? What languages do you speak?
BARAKA Ninasema Kiswahili na Kiingereza. Na wewe? I speak Swahili and English. And you?
FATUMA Mimi ninasema Kiswahili tu, kidogo kidogo Kiingereza. I speak only Swahili, a little English.
BARAKA Vizuri sana. Unafanya kazi gani? Very good. What work do you do?
FATUMA Mimi ni mwalimu. Ninafundisha shuleni Dodoma. Na wewe? I am a teacher. I teach at a school in Dodoma. And you?
BARAKA Mimi ni mhandisi. Ninafanya kazi Nairobi. I am an engineer. I work in Nairobi.

(Amina anakuja — Amina arrives.)

BARAKA Amina! Nakutambulisha Fatuma. Huyu ni Fatuma — anatoka Tanzania, ni mwalimu. Amina! Let me introduce you to Fatuma. This is Fatuma — she comes from Tanzania, she is a teacher.
AMINA Habari, Fatuma! Ninafurahi kukujua. Mimi pia ninatoka Tanzania — kutoka Arusha. Hello, Fatuma! I am pleased to meet you. I too come from Tanzania — from Arusha.
FATUMA Kwa kweli! Karibu sana. Really! Welcome / Very welcome.

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension questions:

  1. Baraka anatoka wapi?
  2. Fatuma ni nani — mwalimu, daktari, au mhandisi?
  3. Fatuma anasema lugha gani?
  4. Amina anatoka wapi Tanzania?
  5. Baraka anamtambulisha nani kwa Amina? Andika sentensi ya utambulisho kwa maneno yako mwenyewe. (Who does Baraka introduce to Amina? Write the introduction sentence in your own words.)
Grammar Note — One word in the dialogue to notice

In the dialogue, Baraka says nimekuja kwa sherehe hii — "I came for this gathering." This sentence uses nimekuja, a form with the perfect tense marker -me-. This tense is not taught productively until Chapter 19, so do not worry about building it yet. Recognize it here as a real communication need and treat the phrase as a formulaic expression. The word hii (this) will be formally explained in Chapter 6 with the demonstrative system.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Swahili as a regional language

Swahili is not the mother tongue of most of its speakers. It is a major East African lingua franca — a shared language used across communities whose members speak different home languages. This means that when you meet a Swahili speaker, you are meeting someone who is likely also competent in at least one other language. The characters in this chapter's reading — a Rwandan speaker of Kinyarwanda, a Sudanese speaker of Arabic, and an Indian speaker of Hindi — reflect a real pattern: Swahili connects people whose first languages differ entirely.

As a learner, you are joining a multilingual tradition, not acquiring a minority language. Swahili is used in government, media, and education across Tanzania, Kenya, Uganda, Rwanda, the Democratic Republic of Congo, and beyond, and is an official language of the African Union.

Culture in Context — Greetings and the art of meeting

In many Swahili-speaking communities, the greeting exchange is not a quick formality — it is the beginning of a relationship. Asking Habari? and receiving Nzuri or Salama in return is the entry point to any further conversation. Rushing past this stage to get immediately to your question or request can signal rudeness or social disconnection.

Greetings vary by time of day, relationship, age, region, and personal or religious identity. Habari za asubuhi (morning news/greetings), Habari za mchana (midday), and Habari za jioni (evening) are time-specific forms. The response Salama (peaceful / well) and Nzuri (good) are both common. Among Muslim speakers, Salaam aleikum and the response Wa aleikum salaam are widely used. The respectful greeting Shikamoo — addressed to elders or those of higher social standing — receives the response Marahaba. Do not use Shikamoo casually or with peers; it signals a specific social relationship.

No single greeting pattern applies to all Swahili speakers in all settings. Pay attention to context and follow the lead of the people around you.

Regional Variation — Nationality vocabulary

The nationality terms in this chapter follow the Standard Swahili pattern used in Tanzania and widely understood across East Africa. Some terms vary by country or speaker community. For example, speakers may use different expressions for a person from Uganda depending on regional context, ethnic background, and setting. Terms for people from the United States include Mmarekani (the form used in this course) and, less formally, Mwamerika. Where you encounter variation, it is worth noting which community uses which form — and recognizing that difference does not signal error on anyone's part.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Overusing independent pronouns

Error: Mimi ninasema Kiswahili. Mimi ninatoka Kenya. Mimi ni mwalimu. (using mimi in every sentence)

Correction: Use mimi for emphasis or contrast — not as a required word before every verb. Ninasema Kiswahili. Ninatoka Kenya. Mimi ni mwalimu (the last one could use mimi for a light emphasis, but even then it is optional). The verb marker ni- already tells the listener "I."

Common Mistake — Confusing ni (copula) with the subject marker ni-

Error: *Ninatoka ni Tanzania. (inserting the copula into a finite verb sentence)

Correction: The copula ni and the subject marker ni- look similar but are different words with different functions. In the sentence Ninatoka Tanzania, the ni- is the first-person subject marker built into the verb. In the sentence Mimi ni mwalimu, ni is the separate copular word linking subject to identity. Never use both in the same clause: *Mimi ni ninatoka Kenya is not grammatical.

Common Mistake — Treating Ki- as a universal language-name rule

Error: Assuming any Swahili word beginning with ki- is a language name, or inventing language names with Ki- without verification. For example, *Kiamerika for American English is not a standard Swahili form.

Correction: The standard Swahili term for English is Kiingereza. Not every country or nationality has a standard Ki- language name, and not every noun beginning with ki- is a language. Only use language names you have learned or verified. When in doubt, describe the language by saying lugha ya… (the language of…) until you can confirm the standard term.

Common Mistake — Wrong plural for M-WA nationality nouns

Error: *Mtanzanias, *Watanzanians — mixing Swahili and English plural markers.

Correction: M-WA nouns use the prefix Wa- for the plural: one Mtanzania, several Watanzania. Do not add -s to Swahili nouns. The plural information is already encoded in the prefix. The same principle applies to all M-WA nouns: mwalimu → walimu, daktari → madaktari.

Common Mistake — Writing hyphens in ordinary sentences

Error: Writing ni-na-toka Kenya in a dialogue or paragraph.

Correction: Hyphens are only for grammatical analysis in a labeled exercise. In all ordinary Swahili writing — sentences, dialogues, profiles, messages — write the word as one unit without hyphens: ninatoka Kenya.

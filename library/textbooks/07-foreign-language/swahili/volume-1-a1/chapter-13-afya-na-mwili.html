Afya na Mwili

Health and the Body · Speak and Write Swahili: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common body parts and use them in simple possessive phrases and symptom descriptions.
  2. Describe basic symptoms using kuumwa and common symptom frames.
  3. Use -weza to express ability and inability in affirmative and negative forms.
  4. Use lazima and inabidi to express necessity and obligation in controlled patterns.
  5. Make polite requests for help using naomba and other courtesy frames.
  6. Apply possessive and subject agreement to body-part nouns across the noun classes you have learned.
  7. Read a clinic sign, appointment note, or short health advisory.
  8. Write a short message describing a health problem and requesting assistance.
  9. Pronounce negative and modal verb forms clearly, with correct stress placement.

Communication Scenario

You wake up feeling unwell on the morning of an important appointment. You need to explain your symptoms to someone, ask for help finding a clinic or pharmacy, and understand the advice you receive. Whether you are in Nairobi, Dar es Salaam, or any other Swahili-speaking city, this situation requires you to speak clearly, listen carefully, and make polite requests. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to describe what hurts, ask what you should do, and follow basic advice — all in Swahili.

  1. Think about how you describe pain or illness in English. You might say "I have a headache" or "my stomach hurts." Swahili expresses these ideas differently — not always through a "have" construction. Notice the specific patterns in this chapter rather than translating word for word from English.
  2. The verb -weza (to be able to) is your key grammar tool in this chapter. It works together with another verb in the infinitive. Think about how phrases like "I cannot walk well" or "I am able to come tomorrow" depend on combining two verbs. This is a pattern you will use again and again in Swahili.

Pronunciation and Spelling

Msisitizo katika vitenzi vya ukanushi na vyenye kishirikishi — Stress in negative and modal constructions

The stress rule you learned at the beginning of the course — stress falls on the second-to-last syllable — continues to apply across all verb forms, including negatives and modal constructions. However, learners often make errors here because negative forms can be longer than their affirmative counterparts, which shifts the stressed syllable.

Compare these affirmative and negative pairs and mark the stressed syllable in each:

AffirmativeStressNegativeStress
ninawezani-na-WE-zasiwezisi-WE-zi
anawezaa-na-WE-zahaweziha-WE-zi
tunawezatu-na-WE-zahatuweziha-tu-WE-zi
ninajisikiani-na-ji-si-KI-asijisikiisi-ji-si-KI-i
Sound Focus — Final vowel changes in negative forms

Notice that ninaweza ends in -a, while its negative siwezi ends in -i. This final-vowel change is a feature of present-tense negation in Swahili for many verbs. The stress stays on the same position relative to the end — second from last — so pronouncing the final syllable clearly is important for the listener to hear the correct vowel. Do not reduce the final -i to a schwa or an English-style unstressed sound. Keep it a clear, short i.

Matamshi ya maombi — Intonation in requests for help

When making a polite request or asking for help, the intonation in Swahili typically rises slightly toward the end of a yes-no question and falls at the end of a statement or command. Practise the following contrasts aloud:

Ninaweza kupata dawa? (rising — yes-no question)
Naomba dawa, tafadhali. (falling — polite request)
Kliniki iko wapi? (rising on wapi — information question)
Siwezi kutembea vizuri. (falling — statement of inability)

Kusoma lebo za dawa — Reading medicine labels and clinic notices

Medicine labels and clinic signs often contain a mixture of Swahili and internationally recognizable abbreviations or loanwords. When reading these aloud, apply standard Swahili pronunciation to all Swahili words and use context clues for any unfamiliar borrowed terms. The following common instructions appear on labels and notices:

  1. Tembe mbili mara tatu kwa siku. — Two tablets three times a day.
  2. Baada ya chakula. — After meals.
  3. Kabla ya kulala. — Before sleeping.
  4. Usichanganye na pombe. — Do not mix with alcohol. (recognition item)
  5. Hifadhi mahali pa baridi. — Store in a cool place. (recognition item)

Practise reading items 1–3 aloud three times each, paying attention to penultimate stress in multi-syllable words.

Grammar Instruction

13.1 Expressing pain and symptoms with kuumwa and symptom frames

Purpose: describe what hurts, using body parts as subjects and the kuumwa construction accurately.

Swahili has two main ways to describe pain. Understanding both — and knowing that they are not direct translations of English "I have a headache" — will prevent common errors.

Pattern 1 — Body part as subject + -uma: The body part itself is the subject of the sentence. The verb kuuma (to hurt; to ache) takes the subject marker of the body-part noun's class.

  • Kichwa kinauma. — My head hurts. / I have a headache. ki- (KI-VI subject marker) + -na- + -uma kichwa = KI-VI class; ki- is the subject marker for KI-VI singular
  • Tumbo linauma. — My stomach hurts. / I have a stomachache. li- (JI-MA subject marker) + -na- + -uma tumbo = JI-MA class; li- is the subject marker for JI-MA singular
  • Mguu unauma. — My leg hurts. u- (M-MI subject marker) + -na- + -uma mguu = M-MI class; u- is the subject marker for M-MI singular
  • Mikono inauma. — My arms hurt. / My hands hurt. i- (M-MI plural subject marker) + -na- + -uma mikono = M-MI plural; i- is the subject marker for M-MI plural
  • Macho yanauma. — My eyes hurt. ya- (JI-MA plural subject marker) + -na- + -uma macho = JI-MA plural; ya- is the subject marker for JI-MA plural
Language Note — The body part is the subject, not the person

In English you say "I have a headache" — the person (I) is the grammatical subject. In Swahili, the common symptom pattern uses the body part as the grammatical subject: Kichwa kinauma — literally "The head hurts" or "A head aches." The person's identity is usually clear from context; you can add a possessive phrase if needed: Kichwa changu kinauma — My head hurts. Do not translate "I have a headache" word for word as Nina maumivu ya kichwa — this is possible but less natural for simple everyday symptoms than the pattern above.

Pattern 2 — Ninaumwa na (I am suffering from …): This pattern places the person as subject and introduces the cause with na. It is particularly useful for illnesses that do not map neatly onto a single body part.

  • Ninaumwa na homa. — I have a fever. (I am suffering from fever.) ni- + -na- + -umw- + -a + na + homa
  • Ninaumwa na kikohozi. — I have a cough. (I am suffering from a cough.) ni- + -na- + -umw- + -a + na + kikohozi
  • Ninaumwa na maumivu ya mgongo. — I have back pain. Literally: I am suffering from back pains.

13.2 Expressing ability and inability with -weza

Purpose: say what you can and cannot do, using -weza with an infinitive complement.

The verb -weza (to be able to; can; to manage to) works together with a second verb in its infinitive form. The infinitive always begins with ku-. The complete pattern is:

subject marker + tense marker + -weza + infinitive (ku- + verb root)

  • Ninaweza kutembea. — I can walk. / I am able to walk. ni- + -na- + -weza + ku-tembea
  • Anaweza kuja leo. — He/she can come today. a- + -na- + -weza + ku-ja
  • Tunaweza kupumzika. — We can rest. tu- + -na- + -weza + ku-pumzika

For the negative, the negative subject marker replaces the affirmative subject marker, and the final vowel of -weza changes from -a to -i:

  • Siwezi kutembea vizuri. — I cannot walk well. si- (negative I) + -wezi + ku-tembea + vizuri
  • Hawezi kula. — He/she cannot eat. ha- (negative he/she) + -wezi + ku-la
  • Hatuwezi kwenda hospitali sasa. — We cannot go to the hospital now. ha-tu- (negative we) + -wezi + kw-enda + hospitali + sasa

13.3 Expressing necessity with lazima and inabidi

Purpose: say what someone must or needs to do, using lazima and inabidi in controlled productive patterns.

Two common expressions of necessity in everyday Swahili are lazima and inabidi. Both mean "it is necessary" or "must," and both are followed by a verb. In the patterns you learn here, the verb following them is either an infinitive or a present-tense form.

lazima + infinitive (or present-tense verb form):

  • Lazima upumzike. — You must rest. lazima + subjunctive-origin form; learn as a fixed pattern for now
  • Lazima aende hospitali. — He/she must go to the hospital. Learn as a fixed pattern; the form after lazima is introduced for recognition here
  • Lazima tunywe maji mengi. — We must drink plenty of water. Fixed health advice pattern — productive for this chapter

inabidi + infinitive: Inabidi is a widely used everyday alternative to lazima. It is followed directly by the infinitive and is slightly more conversational in register.

  • Inabidi kupumzika. — It is necessary to rest. / You need to rest. inabidi + ku-pumzika; the subject of the action is understood from context
  • Inabidi kwenda kliniki. — You need to go to the clinic. inabidi + kw-enda + kliniki
  • Inabidi kunywa dawa. — You need to take the medicine. (Literally: to drink medicine) inabidi + ku-nywa + dawa
Language Note — lazima and the form of the following verb

Lazima triggers a verb form derived from the subjunctive in Standard Swahili — for example, upumzike rather than unapumzika. At this stage, learn the most common patterns as fixed productive expressions: Lazima upumzike, Lazima aende, Lazima tunywe. The full subjunctive paradigm is beyond the scope of this foundations course. When in doubt about whether to use lazima or inabidi, inabidi + infinitive is a safe and natural everyday choice that does not require a subjunctive form.

13.4 Making polite requests with naomba and other frames

Purpose: ask for help, medicine, or information in a way that is courteous and natural in a health context.

You have already used naomba (I request; I ask for) in food and market contexts. In health situations, polite and clear requests are especially important. The following frames are productive at this level:

  • Naomba msaada, tafadhali. — I am asking for help, please. Most common and broadly polite request opener
  • Naomba dawa ya maumivu. — I would like some pain medicine. naomba + noun phrase; direct pharmacy request
  • Naweza kuona daktari? — Can I see a doctor? ninaweza (can I?) + infinitive; slightly less formal than naomba
  • Tafadhali niambie dawa hii inachukuliwaje. — Please tell me how to take this medicine. Recognition item; models a real pharmacy interaction
  • Ninahitaji … — I need … Direct expression of need; slightly more urgent in tone than naomba

Mazoezi D — Maombi (Requests):

  1. You are at a pharmacy. Ask for medicine for a headache. (Use naomba.)
  2. You are at a clinic. Ask if you can see a doctor. (Use naweza.)
  3. You are in pain and need help urgently. (Use ninahitaji.)
  4. Ask where the nearest clinic is. (Use kliniki iko wapi.)

13.5 Possessive agreement with body-part nouns

Purpose: use "my," "your," and "his/her" correctly with body-part nouns across the noun classes you have learned.

Body-part possessives follow the same rule as all Swahili possessives: the possessive concord is controlled by the noun class of the body part, not by the person who possesses it. Review the concords for the classes active in this chapter:

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: Farida hajisikii vizuri asubuhi. Anakwenda kliniki na kuzungumza na muuguzi, Bwana Omondi, kabla ya kuona daktari. Kliniki iko Nairobi.

(Setting: Farida is not feeling well in the morning. She goes to a clinic and speaks with the nurse, Mr. Omondi, before seeing a doctor. The clinic is in Nairobi.)

OMONDIHabari za asubuhi. Karibu kliniki. Ninaweza kukusaidia?Good morning. Welcome to the clinic. Can I help you?
FARIDAHabari nzuri. Asante. Sijisikii vizuri leo. Naomba kuona daktari.Good, thank you. I am not feeling well today. I would like to see a doctor.
OMONDISamahani kusikia hivyo. Inakuumaje? Nini kinauma?Sorry to hear that. What is hurting you? What hurts?
FARIDAKichwa changu kinauma sana. Na ninaumwa na homa — ninajisikia moto mwilini.My head hurts a lot. And I have a fever — I feel hot in my body.
OMONDIUna kikohozi au mafua?Do you have a cough or a cold?
FARIDANdiyo, nina kikohozi kidogo. Lakini siwezi kulala vizuri kwa sababu kichwa kinauma sana.Yes, I have a slight cough. But I cannot sleep well because my head hurts a lot.
OMONDIUmekuwa na homa tangu lini?Since when have you had a fever? (recognition item)
FARIDATangu jana usiku. Nilikuwa napumzika lakini sikupona. Mguu pia unauma kidogo.Since last night. I was resting but I did not recover. My leg also hurts a little.

(Omondi anapima joto la Farida. / Omondi takes Farida's temperature.)

OMONDIJoto lako ni juu kidogo. Inabidi kukaa na kusubiri daktari. Daktari atakuona hivi karibuni. Wakati huo, naomba unywe maji mengi.Your temperature is a little high. You need to wait and see the doctor. The doctor will see you soon. In the meantime, please drink plenty of water.
FARIDAAsante sana. Daktari anaweza kuandika dawa leo?Thank you very much. Can the doctor prescribe medicine today?
OMONDINdiyo, daktari anaweza kukusaidia. Lazima kwanza akuangalie vizuri.Yes, the doctor can help you. First he/she must examine you properly. (recognition item)
FARIDASawa. Ninasubiri. Duka la dawa liko karibu na kliniki hii?Alright. I will wait. Is there a pharmacy near this clinic?
OMONDINdiyo, liko karibu — mita mia moja tu upande wa kulia.Yes, it is nearby — just about one hundred metres to the right. (recognition item)
FARIDAAsante sana kwa msaada wako.Thank you very much for your help.
OMONDIKaribu. Pole sana. Upone haraka.You are welcome. Sorry to hear it. May you recover quickly. (recognition item)

Vocabulary note: moto mwilini = hot in the body; tangu = since; kupona = to recover; pima joto = take temperature; kuandika dawa = to prescribe medicine; kuangalia = to examine; to look at; pole sana = so sorry; expression of sympathy; Upone haraka = May you recover quickly (recognition item — a common cultural expression of goodwill). Items labeled recognition items in the dialogue are not assessed productively.

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension questions:

  1. Farida ana dalili ngapi? Zitaje kwa Kiswahili. (How many symptoms does Farida have? List them in Swahili.)
  2. Kwa nini Farida hawezi kulala vizuri? (Why can Farida not sleep well?)
  3. Omondi anamwambia Farida afanye nini wakati anasubiri daktari? (What does Omondi tell Farida to do while she waits for the doctor?)
  4. Find two sentences in the dialogue that use -weza. Write them out and identify whether each is affirmative or negative.
  5. What expression does Omondi use to show sympathy? Why might a learner want to know this even before they can produce it independently?

KLINIKI YA AFYA YA JAMII — NAIROBI

Masaa ya kazi: Jumatatu – Ijumaa, saa tatu asubuhi hadi saa kumi na mbili usiku.

Huduma tunazoitoa:

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Healthcare access and forms of care

Access to healthcare across East Africa varies considerably depending on location, economic circumstances, and the type of care needed. In urban centres such as Nairobi and Dar es Salaam, a range of public hospitals, private clinics, pharmacies, and community health posts are available. In rural and peri-urban areas, community health workers, local dispensaries, and pharmacies may be the primary point of contact for common illnesses.

The vocabulary in this chapter equips you to communicate in a range of healthcare settings. When interacting with medical professionals in Swahili, use respectful address — Daktari for a doctor and Muuguzi or a title for a nurse — and be as clear and specific as possible about your symptoms. Healthcare workers appreciate patients who can describe what is wrong rather than simply saying Sijisikii vizuri.

This chapter is designed for language learning and does not constitute medical advice. Use the language you learn here to communicate effectively in real situations; seek qualified medical attention for any genuine health concern.

Culture in Context — Expressing sympathy and well-wishing

When someone is unwell, Swahili speakers typically express sympathy with pole or pole sana — an expression of condolence, sympathy, or commiseration that applies to illness, pain, difficulty, or loss. It is one of the most widely used social expressions in everyday East African Swahili. A common response is asante or nashukuru.

The expression Upone haraka (May you recover quickly) or simply Pona haraka is a natural and appreciated farewell when someone is ill. Learners who use these expressions — even before they can produce them independently — signal cultural awareness and earn goodwill in real interactions.

Pole is broadly used across Tanzania, Kenya, and other East African contexts, though the precise social conventions around its use can vary slightly by region, relationship, and formality. It is safe to use in virtually any setting where someone has experienced discomfort, difficulty, or misfortune.

Regional Variation — Healthcare vocabulary

Some healthcare vocabulary varies between Tanzania and Kenya. The word dawa for medicine is universal. However, the term for a pharmacy may be duka la dawa (common in Tanzania) or simply chemist (widely understood in Kenya, borrowed from British English). Hospitali is standard in both countries. In some Kenyan contexts, kliniki may refer specifically to a smaller clinic, while larger facilities are hospitali. The terms for "nurse" — muuguzi in Standard Swahili — and common illness names like homa and mafua are consistent across both settings. Always identify the setting when regional variation matters for communication.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Translating "I have a headache" word for word

Error: Nina maumivu ya kichwa as the only or default form for "I have a headache"

Correction: The most natural everyday pattern for a simple symptom is the body-part-as-subject pattern: Kichwa kinauma. This literally means "The head hurts" but is the ordinary Swahili way to say you have a headache. Nina maumivu ya kichwa (I have a headache, literally "I have pains of the head") is grammatically possible but less common for straightforward symptom descriptions. Train yourself to use the body-part pattern first.

Common Mistake — Using the wrong subject marker with a body-part noun

Error: Tumbo inaumia. (using i- instead of li- for a JI-MA singular noun)

Correction: Tumbo linauma. — The subject marker on the verb must match the noun class of the body-part noun acting as subject, not the person who feels the pain. tumbo is JI-MA class and takes li- as its singular subject marker. Similarly, kichwa kinauma (KI-VI: ki-), mguu unauma (M-MI singular: u-), and mikono inauma (M-MI plural: i-). Always identify the noun class before writing the verb.

Common Mistake — Keeping -a in the negative form of -weza

Error: Siweza kutembea. (using the affirmative final vowel -a in the negative)

Correction: Siwezi kutembea. — The negative present form of -weza ends in -i, not -a. This final-vowel change is a regular feature of present-tense negation in Standard Swahili. The full negative paradigm is: siwezi, huwezi, hawezi, hatuwezi, hamwezi, hawawezi. Memorize the negative form as a distinct set rather than constructing it mechanically from the affirmative by replacing the subject marker alone.

Common Mistake — Using inabidi without an infinitive

Error: Inabidi pumzika. (omitting the infinitive prefix ku-)

Correction: Inabidi kupumzika. — Inabidi must be followed by a complete infinitive, which in Swahili begins with ku-. The bare verb root — pumzika without ku- — is not an infinitive; it is the form used in finite verb constructions. Always attach ku- to the root when a verb follows inabidi or any expression requiring an infinitive complement.

Common Mistake — Using animate agreement with body-part nouns

Error: Kichwa changu ana maumivu. (using the animate M-WA subject marker a- because the body part belongs to a person)

Correction: Kichwa changu kina maumivu. — Body parts are not people, so they do not take animate agreement. Even though a head belongs to a person, kichwa is KI-VI class and takes ki- (singular) or vi- (plural) agreement. Animate agreement is reserved for nouns that refer to people or animals. The class of the noun itself — not its relationship to a person — controls agreement.

Common Mistake — Using lazima with a straightforward infinitive without knowing the required form

Error: Lazima kupumzika. (using a bare infinitive directly after lazima in contexts where a subject-specific form is expected)

Correction: For subject-specific necessity (you must rest, he must go), lazima requires a subjunctive-derived form: Lazima upumzike. However, if you are not yet certain which form to use, Inabidi kupumzika is a safe and natural alternative that works with the infinitive directly. At this stage, use the fixed lazima patterns taught in this chapter — Lazima upumzike, Lazima aende, Lazima tunywe — and use inabidi + infinitive when you need to express necessity with other verbs or persons.

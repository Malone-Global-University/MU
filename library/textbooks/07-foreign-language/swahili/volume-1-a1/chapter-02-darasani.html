In the Classroom · Speak and Write Swahili: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common classroom objects using KI-VI nouns in the singular (kitabu, kiti, kalamu) and plural (vitabu, viti, kalamu).
  2. Ask what something is using Hiki ni nini? (singular) and Hivi ni nini? (plural).
  3. Identify objects using the near demonstratives hiki and hivi.
  4. Recognize that modifiers in Swahili agree with the noun class they describe.
  5. Give and understand simple classroom commands in the singular and plural.
  6. Count from 1 to 20 in Swahili.
  7. Ask for clarification and help using polite classroom expressions.
  8. Pronounce Swahili consonant combinations including ch, sh, dh, gh, and ng' accurately.

Communication Scenario

It is the first day of a Swahili language class in Dar es Salaam. The instructor holds up objects one by one — a book, a chair, a pen — and you realize you need to learn their names quickly if you are going to follow the lesson. You also need to know how to ask the teacher to repeat something, how to say you do not understand, and how to count the objects on your desk. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to do all of this.

  1. Think of three objects on a typical desk or table in a learning environment. How would you find out their names in a new language? What would you say to a teacher if you missed something?
  2. In English, "this" and "these" work differently depending on whether a noun is singular or plural. Swahili takes this idea much further. As you work through the chapter, notice how Swahili links the word for "this" to the noun it describes.

Pronunciation and Spelling

Consonant combinations

Swahili spelling is closely linked to pronunciation. Most letters represent one consistent sound. In this chapter, you will encounter several important consonant combinations. Each represents a single sound — do not try to pronounce them as two separate consonants.

CombinationApproximate soundExampleMeaning
chlike English ch in chairchakichalk
shlike English sh in shipshuleschool
dhlike English th in this (voiced)dhahabugold
gha voiced sound at the back of the throat; similar to a soft French rghaliexpensive
nglike ng in English sing — within a wordngapihow many
ng'the same ng sound, but at the start of a syllable or word — marked by the apostropheng'ombecow
Sound Focus — ng versus ng'

The apostrophe in ng' is not a decoration. It signals a different sound from ng. Both sound like the ng at the end of English sing, but ng' can begin a syllable, while ng almost never does in Standard Swahili. Contrast: ng'ombe (cow) — the ng' starts the word; ngapi (how many) — the ng is followed immediately by a. Omitting the apostrophe changes the word's spelling and can affect its meaning.

Vowel sequences

When two or more vowel letters appear next to each other in Swahili, each vowel is pronounced separately. Do not combine them into an English-style diphthong.

  • kiooPronounced ki-o-o, three syllables — not kyoo
  • mwalimuPronounced mwa-li-mu — the mw blends but the vowels remain separate

Grammar Instruction

2.1 The KI-VI noun class

Purpose: identify and name common classroom objects; understand that Swahili nouns belong to classes that shape surrounding words.

Swahili organizes nouns into groups called noun classes. Each class has its own prefix pattern and its own set of agreement markers — words that "echo" the class of the controlling noun. In this chapter, you are working with one of the most common and regular classes: KI-VI.

Number Noun prefix Example Meaning
Singular ki- kitabu book
Plural vi- vitabu books
Singular ki- kiti chair
Plural vi- viti chairs
Singular ki- kikapu basket
Plural vi- vikapu baskets
Language Note — Noun class is not gender

Swahili noun classes are sometimes compared to gender in languages like Spanish or French, but this comparison is misleading. Spanish nouns are either masculine or feminine, and this often relates to natural sex. Swahili noun classes are categories based on grammatical agreement, not on biological sex. KI-VI contains inanimate objects, but it is not a category called "things" — other classes also contain inanimate nouns, and the assignment of a noun to a class follows historical and grammatical logic, not simple rules. Learn each noun with its class, just as you learn its meaning.

2.2 Near demonstratives: hiki and hivi

Purpose: point to a nearby object and ask or state what it is.

A demonstrative is a word that points to something — like English "this" or "these." In Swahili, the word for "this" changes depending on the noun class of the noun it accompanies. For KI-VI nouns, the near demonstratives are:

  • hiki — this (with a KI-class singular noun) Hiki ni kitabu. — This is a book.
  • hivi — these (with a VI-class plural noun) Hivi ni vitabu. — These are books.

The demonstrative agrees with the noun — hiki echoes the ki- prefix of the singular, and hivi echoes the vi- prefix of the plural. This is the beginning of a principle that runs through the entire Swahili language: agreement.

Language Building Block
noun-class demonstrative + ni + noun
Hiki ni kitabu.
This is a book. (singular)
Hivi ni vitabu.
These are books. (plural)

2.3 Asking what something is: Hiki ni nini? / Hivi ni nini?

Purpose: ask the name of an unfamiliar object.

To ask "What is this?" about a KI-class singular object, use Hiki ni nini? For a VI-class plural, use Hivi ni nini? The question word nini (what) does not change.

  • Hiki ni nini?What is this? — (pointing to one object)
  • Hiki ni kitabu.This is a book. — (answer)
  • Hivi ni nini?What are these? — (pointing to several objects)
  • Hivi ni vitabu.These are books. — (answer)

Notice that the same ni from Chapter 1 — which you used to state identity (Mimi ni Amina) — is also used here to link a demonstrative to the noun it names.

2.4 Singular and plural commands

Purpose: understand and give simple classroom instructions.

A Swahili command addressed to one person uses the verb stem alone, often ending in -a. A command addressed to a group adds the suffix -eni to the verb stem:

Singular (one person) Plural (group) Meaning
Soma.Someni.Read.
Andika.Andikeni.Write.
Simama.Simameni.Stand up.
Kaa.Kaeni.Sit down.
Sikiliza.Sikilizeni.Listen.
Angalia.Angalieni.Look.
Fungua.Fungueni.Open.
Funga.Fungeni.Close.

You can soften a command by adding tafadhali (please): Tafadhali soma. (Please read.) In a classroom setting, tafadhali is appropriate when addressing peers or individuals in a non-authoritative context.

2.5 Agreement as a recurring feature

Purpose: understand why modifiers change their form in Swahili.

You have now seen two patterns of agreement: the demonstratives hiki/hivi that reflect the KI-VI class of the noun they point to, and the -eni suffix that reflects whether a command targets one or several people. Both are examples of a central Swahili principle: words that relate to a noun or its participants must match them in grammatical features.

This means that as you learn more noun classes in later chapters, you will also learn the corresponding demonstratives, possessives, and other modifiers for each class. The pattern you are learning now — ki- → hiki, vi- → hivi — is your first example of this system at work.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: Mwalimu anaanza somo. Wanafunzi wawili — Baraka na Zawadi — wanakaa pamoja. Lugha: Kiswahili cha kawaida cha darasani.

(Setting: The teacher begins a lesson. Two students — Baraka and Zawadi — sit together. Language: normal classroom Swahili.)

MWALIMUHabari za asubuhi, wanafunzi.Good morning, students.
WANAFUNZINzuri, mwalimu. Asante.Fine, teacher. Thank you.
MWALIMUSikilizeni. Hiki ni nini?Listen (everyone). What is this?

(Mwalimu anashikilia kitabu. / The teacher holds up a book.)

BARAKAHiki ni kitabu.This is a book.
MWALIMUVizuri sana. Na hivi ni nini?Very good. And what are these?

(Mwalimu anashikilia vitabu viwili. / The teacher holds up two books.)

ZAWADIHivi ni vitabu.These are books.
MWALIMUSahihi. Hiki ni kitabu kimoja. Hivi ni vitabu viwili. Sasa — Baraka, andika "kitabu" ubaoni.Correct. This is one book. These are two books. Now — Baraka, write "kitabu" on the board.
BARAKASamahani, mwalimu. Sijaelewa vizuri. Tafadhali rudia.Sorry, teacher. I did not understand well. Please repeat.
MWALIMUSawa. Andika neno "kitabu" ubaoni, tafadhali.All right. Write the word "kitabu" on the board, please.

Mazoezi ya msingi — Controlled practice

Pick up or point to five objects in the room. For each one, ask your partner Hiki ni nini? or Hivi ni nini? depending on whether you are pointing to one or several. Your partner gives a full-sentence answer using hiki ni or hivi ni. Then switch roles.

Mchezo wa "Ninafikiri" — Guessing game

One person thinks of a classroom object from this chapter's vocabulary list. The other asks yes-no questions in English to narrow it down, but the answers must be given in Swahili: Ndiyo (yes) or Hapana (no). When you think you know the object, ask Hiki ni kitabu? (or whichever noun you think it is). The person confirms with a full sentence: Ndiyo, hiki ni kitabu. or Hapana, hiki si kitabu.

Mchezo wa amri — Commands game

One person gives a classroom command from this chapter's vocabulary list, choosing the singular or plural form depending on how many people they are addressing. The other person or group performs the action. If the action is performed incorrectly (for example, the group form was given but only one person reacted), the speaker must give a new command.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Classroom interaction

In many Swahili-speaking learning environments, greeting the teacher before the lesson begins is a normal and expected part of the classroom routine. In some school settings in Tanzania and Kenya, students may greet a teacher formally when the teacher enters, using greetings such as Habari za asubuhi, mwalimu (Good morning, teacher). The response to a learner's greeting acknowledges both the courtesy and the start of learning. These conventions vary by school level, institution, and region — they are not universal across all East African classrooms. When in doubt, follow the lead of others in the setting.

The word mwalimu is widely used as both a title and a form of respectful address — comparable to "Teacher" used as a title in some English-speaking contexts. Using mwalimu when addressing a teacher is typically appropriate and respectful across a range of settings.

Regional Variation — Classroom vocabulary

The word ubao commonly refers to the board at the front of a classroom in Standard Swahili. In some educational settings in Kenya and Tanzania, you may also hear the English borrowing bodi (from "board") used informally. This course uses ubao as the standard productive form. Similarly, kalamu is the standard word for pen or pencil, though some speakers use penseli (from English "pencil") specifically for a pencil. Both forms are widely understood; kalamu is the default here.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using the wrong demonstrative

Error: Hivi ni kitabu. (using the plural demonstrative with a singular noun)

Correction: Hiki ni kitabu. — The demonstrative must match the noun class and number. Singular KI-class nouns take hiki; plural VI-class nouns take hivi. Check the noun prefix first: ki- → hiki; vi- → hivi.

Common Mistake — Forgetting to change ki- to vi- in the plural

Error: Hivi ni kitabu vitatu. (keeping the singular noun prefix in a plural context)

Correction: Hivi ni vitabu vitatu. — When you pluralize a KI-class noun, replace the ki- prefix with vi- throughout: the noun itself and, eventually, any agreeing words. Learn each noun's plural form alongside its singular from the start.

Common Mistake — Omitting the apostrophe in ng'

Error: Writing ngombe instead of ng'ombe

Correction: ng'ombe — The apostrophe is part of the spelling and signals a specific sound pattern. Standard Swahili orthography requires it. Omitting it is a spelling error, not just a stylistic choice. Always check your vocabulary list for the presence of the apostrophe.

Common Mistake — Using the singular command with a group

Error: Saying Soma when addressing the whole class

Correction: Someni — When giving an instruction to more than one person, add -eni to the verb stem. The singular form is appropriate only when speaking to one individual. Using the singular with a group can sound unnatural or unclear in classroom Swahili.

Common Mistake — Assuming every noun starting with ki- is KI-VI class

Error: Treating Kiswahili or Kiingereza as KI-VI class nouns

Correction: Language names like Kiswahili and Kiingereza begin with Ki- for a different historical reason — they are derived forms, not ordinary KI-VI nouns. Do not assume that every Swahili word beginning with ki- follows the KI-VI agreement pattern. Always check the noun class in the vocabulary list.

Looking Ahead

Unachojua sasa — What you know now

You can now name classroom objects in Swahili, ask what something is, give singular and plural commands, count to twenty, and ask for help when you need it. You have also encountered Swahili's agreement system for the first time — seeing how the demonstratives hiki and hivi echo the ki-/vi- prefix of the KI-VI class. This principle of agreement will grow with you through every chapter that follows.

Sura ya 3: Kukutana na Watu — Meeting People

In Chapter 3, you move beyond the classroom and into conversations about people. You will learn how to introduce someone else, ask and answer questions about where a person comes from, what language they speak, and what their profession is. You will also encounter your first complete Swahili verb forms — the subject markers and the present tense marker -na- — and begin to see how a single Swahili verb can carry the information that English expresses with several separate words.

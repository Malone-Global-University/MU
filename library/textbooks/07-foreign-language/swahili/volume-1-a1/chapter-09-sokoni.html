Sokoni

At the Market · Speak and Write Swahili: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify common market goods and clothing items using accurate Swahili vocabulary.
  2. Ask and state prices using Bei gani? and Shilingi ngapi?
  3. Recognize U-class nouns through high-frequency market and clothing vocabulary.
  4. Use agreeing numerals and the question word -ngapi to ask and state quantities.
  5. Use -ote to express totality across noun classes already introduced.
  6. Apply adjective agreement, including size and quality descriptions, across M-WA, N-class, and U-class nouns.
  7. Use object markers from Chapter 8 in purchase and negotiation contexts.
  8. Pronounce large numbers, prices, and quantities clearly and accurately.
  9. Complete a market transaction involving at least three items, with quantities and prices, and produce a written record of the purchase.

Communication Scenario

You are spending a Saturday morning at a market in Arusha with a Tanzanian friend who is showing you around. Vendors call out prices, weigh fruit, and fold cloth for customers moving between stalls. Your friend wants you to practice buying a few things yourself — some vegetables for dinner, a kitenge cloth for a gift, and a pair of sandals you noticed at a shoe stall. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to ask what something costs, ask for a specific quantity, comment on quality and size, and complete a polite purchase from start to finish.

  1. In English, numbers used for counting do not usually change form depending on what is being counted — "three apples" and "three friends" both use the same word "three." In Swahili, some numerals change their form to agree with the noun class of what is being counted, while other numbers stay the same no matter what they count. As you work through this chapter, notice which numbers change and which do not.
  2. Think about how you ask for "two" of something or "how many" of something in your own language. Swahili builds the question word for "how many" out of a recognizable concord pattern — the same kind of agreement marker you have already used with possessives and adjectives. Watch for this connection as you study the U-class and numeral agreement in this chapter.

Numbers above one hundred are built from familiar parts you already know: mia (hundred), elfu (thousand), and the numbers 1-99 from Chapters 2 and 3, joined with na ("and"). Pause briefly between major parts of a number so listeners can follow.

Sound Focus — Chunking prices and quantities

When reading a price aloud, divide the number into manageable chunks the way you would divide a long phone number in English. Elfu kumi na tano, mia mbili ("fifteen thousand, two hundred") is easier to produce and understand when spoken with a brief pause after elfu kumi na tano than when run together as one unbroken phrase. Practice reading the price examples above aloud slowly first, then at a more natural conversational pace.

Grammar Instruction

9.1 The U-class — recognition through market vocabulary

Purpose: recognize U-class nouns and understand why their plurals must be learned individually rather than predicted from a rule.

You have now studied M-WA, KI-VI, N, JI-MA, and M-MI nouns. The U-class is your fifth noun class. U-class singular nouns typically begin with the prefix u-, as in ugali, unga, and uji. This is a useful recognition cue, but it is only a starting point — not every word beginning with u- is necessarily a U-class noun, and U-class nouns do not share one single plural pattern.

The U-class has two features that distinguish it from the classes you already know:

Feature 1 — Many U-class nouns describe mass or abstract concepts. Words like unga (flour), uji (porridge), and ugali (the maize-meal staple) refer to substances rather than individually countable items. These nouns typically have no ordinary plural form, much as English does not usually pluralize "flour" or "rice."

Feature 2 — When a U-class noun does have a plural, the plural is irregular and must be learned individually. Some U-class nouns borrow a plural pattern from another class; others have no productive plural at all. Do not guess a U-class plural by analogy with M-WA, KI-VI, or any other class.

Language Note — U-class scope at this stage

The U-class also contains a smaller group of countable nouns with their own irregular plurals, often borrowed from the N-class or JI-MA class. These are not required for productive use in this chapter. At this stage, focus on recognizing U-class mass nouns through the vocabulary above and on retrieving the agreement patterns taught in Section 9.2. You will encounter additional U-class agreement in Chapters 12-14 as the matrix expands this class.

9.2 Numeral agreement and -ngapi

Purpose: ask and answer "how many?" and use agreeing numerals correctly with familiar noun classes.

You learned the numbers 1-100 in Chapters 2 and 3 as invariant forms — that is, you used them without changing their shape. In fact, Swahili numerals fall into two groups: invariant numerals, which never change, and agreeing numerals, which take a concord matching the noun class of what is being counted.

Invariant numerals — these never change form regardless of noun class: tano (five), sita (six), saba (seven), nane (eight), tisa (nine), and all numbers built from kumi (ten), mia (hundred), and elfu (thousand).

Agreeing numerals — these take a concord matching the noun class of what is counted: moja (one), mbili (two), tatu (three), and nne (four). You have already used the M-WA forms of these numbers, such as watoto wawili (two children) in Chapter 4. This chapter expands agreeing numerals to KI-VI, N-class, and JI-MA nouns.

N-class nouns use the unmarked base forms moja, mbili, tatu, nne because the N-class concord has no separate visible prefix in this position. This is consistent with what you learned about N-class agreement in Chapter 4: the class is recognized by its behavior, not always by a visible marker.

The question word -ngapi ("how many?") also agrees with the noun class of what is being asked about, using the same concord pattern as the agreeing numerals:

  • Vitabu vingapi? — How many books? KI-VI plural concord vi- + -ngapi
  • Watu wangapi? — How many people? M-WA plural concord wa- + -ngapi
  • Matunda mangapi? — How many fruits? JI-MA plural concord ma- + -ngapi
  • Nguo ngapi? — How many garments? N-class concord (unmarked) + -ngapi

9.3 -ote for totality

Purpose: express "all" or "the whole" across the noun classes introduced so far.

The stem -ote means "all" or "the whole" and takes a concord matching the noun class it describes, following the same agreement pattern you have already studied for numerals and adjectives.

*M-WA singular wote is rarely used since "the whole" of one person is uncommon in ordinary speech; you are most likely to use M-WA wote with plural nouns. Focus your productive practice on plural and N-class uses, which are most useful for market and quantity contexts.

  • Nataka mboga zote. — I want all the vegetables. Note: mboga as a mass/collective plural sense pairs with N-class zote in some constructions; yote is also heard. Learn yote as your productive default for this chapter.
  • Matunda yote ni mazuri. — All the fruits are good. JI-MA plural concord ya- (yote) + matunda
  • Watu wote wananunua. — All the people are buying. (recognition item — full verb agreement studied later)
Common Mistake — Treating -ote as one invariant word

Error: vitabu ote (using a bare stem without the correct concord)

Correction: vitabu vyote — Like adjectives and numerals, -ote requires a class concord before it. KI-VI plural takes vy-, producing vyote. Always check the noun class before attaching -ote.

9.4 Adjective agreement expansion — color, size, and quality

Purpose: describe market goods and clothing using color, size, and quality adjectives with correct agreement.

You studied adjective agreement with animate M-WA and N-class nouns in Chapter 4. This chapter expands adjective agreement to KI-VI and N-class inanimate nouns, which is essential for describing goods at the market.

For KI-VI singular nouns, adjective stems take the prefix ki- (or ch- before a vowel):

  • kiatu kizuri — a good/nice shoe KI-VI singular noun + ki- + adjective stem -zuri
  • kitenge kikubwa — a large kitenge cloth KI-VI singular noun + ki- + adjective stem -kubwa

For KI-VI plural nouns, adjective stems take the prefix vi-:

  • viatu vizuri — good/nice shoes KI-VI plural noun + vi- + adjective stem -zuri
  • viatu vyeusi — black shoes KI-VI plural noun + vy- + adjective stem -eusi (vowel-initial stem)

For N-class nouns (singular and plural look the same), adjective stems take the prefix n- or no visible prefix, depending on the stem's first sound. With vowel-initial stems such as -eupe and -ekundu, the prefix appears as ny-:

  • nguo nyeupe — white clothing N-class noun + ny- + adjective stem -eupe
  • nguo nyekundu — red clothing N-class noun + ny- + adjective stem -ekundu
  • mboga nzuri — good vegetables N-class noun + n- + adjective stem -zuri
  • kanzu kubwa — a large robe N-class noun + (no visible prefix before k-) + adjective stem -kubwa
Language Note — N-class adjective agreement varies by sound

N-class adjective agreement is more variable in shape than KI-VI or M-WA agreement because the concord interacts with the first sound of the adjective stem. Before vowel-initial stems like -eupe and -ekundu, the concord surfaces as ny-. Before most consonant-initial stems, the concord is reduced or barely visible, as in kanzu kubwa. Learn each N-class adjective combination as a unit rather than deriving it from a single rule, and rely on the examples provided here as your productive model.

9.5 Asking and stating prices, with object-marker review

Purpose: ask the price of an item and use object markers from Chapter 8 to refer back to it without repeating the noun.

Two question patterns are used to ask about price:

  • Bei gani? — What is the price? (literally: "price what kind?") bei (price) + gani (what kind of)
  • Shilingi ngapi? — How many shillings? shilingi (shillings, N-class) + ngapi (how many)

A vendor's answer typically states the price directly, often with ni:

  • Bei yake ni shilingi elfu mbili. — Its price is two thousand shillings. bei yake (its price, N-class possessive) + ni + amount
  • Kiatu hiki ni shilingi elfu kumi. — This shoe is ten thousand shillings.

Once an item has been mentioned, you can refer back to it using an object marker rather than repeating the noun, just as you practiced in Chapter 8:

  • Nataka kitenge hiki. Ninaweza kukiona vizuri zaidi? — I want this kitenge. May I see it more closely? ni-na-weza ku-ki-on-a = I am able to see-it (ki- refers back to kitenge, KI-VI)
  • Viatu hivi vinafaa. Nitavinunua. — These shoes fit well. I will buy them. ni-ta-vi-nunu-a = I-will-buy-them (vi- refers back to viatu, KI-VI plural)
  • Mboga hizi ni nzuri. Naomba unipakie. — These vegetables are good. Please pack them for me. (recognition item — applicative form)
Common Mistake — Asking price with the wrong question pattern

Error: Bei ngapi? (mixing the two question patterns)

Correction: Use either Bei gani? or Shilingi ngapi? as complete, separate patterns. Bei pairs with gani ("what kind"), while shilingi pairs with ngapi ("how many"). Do not combine bei with ngapi.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: Amara yuko sokoni Arusha pamoja na rafiki yake, Halima, ambaye anamwonyesha soko hilo. Amara anataka kununua mboga, kitenge, na viatu.

(Setting: Amara is at the market in Arusha with her friend Halima, who is showing her around. Amara wants to buy vegetables, a kitenge, and shoes.)

HALIMAKaribu sokoni, Amara! Unataka kununua nini leo?Welcome to the market, Amara! What do you want to buy today?
AMARANataka mboga kwa ajili ya chakula cha jioni, na pia kitenge kimoja.I want vegetables for dinner, and also one kitenge cloth.

(Wanafika kwenye duka la mboga. / They arrive at the vegetable stall.)

MCHUUZIHabari za asubuhi! Unataka mboga gani?Good morning! What vegetables do you want?
AMARANzuri, asante. Naomba nyanya na vitunguu. Bei gani kwa kilo moja?Well, thank you. I'd like tomatoes and onions. What's the price per kilo?
MCHUUZIKilo ya nyanya ni shilingi elfu moja, na kilo ya vitunguu ni shilingi mia nane.A kilo of tomatoes is one thousand shillings, and a kilo of onions is eight hundred shillings.
AMARANaomba kilo mbili za nyanya na nusu kilo ya vitunguu.I'd like two kilos of tomatoes and half a kilo of onions.
MCHUUZISawa. Jumla ni shilingi elfu mbili na mia nne.Okay. The total is two thousand four hundred shillings.
AMARAHapa kuna pesa. Asante sana.Here is the money. Thank you very much.

(Wanafika kwenye duka la nguo. / They arrive at the cloth stall.)

HALIMATazama vitenge hivi. Vina rangi nzuri.Look at these kitenge cloths. They have nice colors.
AMARANdiyo! Naomba kitenge hiki chenye rangi nyekundu. Bei gani?Yes! I'd like this red one. What's the price?
MCHUUZI WA NGUOKitenge hicho ni shilingi elfu kumi na tano.That kitenge is fifteen thousand shillings.
AMARAHiyo ni ghali kidogo. Unaweza kupunguza bei?That's a bit expensive. Can you lower the price?
MCHUUZI WA NGUONaweza kukupa kwa shilingi elfu kumi na mbili.I can give it to you for twelve thousand shillings.
AMARASawa, nitakinunua.Okay, I will buy it.

(Wanafika kwenye duka la viatu. / They arrive at the shoe stall.)

AMARANaomba kuona viatu vyeusi vile.May I see those black shoes.
MCHUUZI WA VIATUKaribu. Unataka namba ngapi?Welcome. What size do you want?
AMARANamba arobaini. Viatu hivi ni shilingi ngapi?Size forty. How many shillings are these shoes?
MCHUUZI WA VIATUViatu vyote vya hapa ni shilingi elfu ishirini.All the shoes here are twenty thousand shillings.
AMARAVizuri sana. Nitavinunua. Asante kwa msaada wako, Halima.Very good. I will buy them. Thank you for your help, Halima.
HALIMAKaribu sana! Twende tukapate chai sasa.You're very welcome! Let's go get tea now.

Vocabulary note: mchuuzi = vendor, seller; nyanya = tomato(es); vitunguu = onions; jumla = total; chenye = which has (recognition item); namba = size, number; twende tukapate chai = let's go get tea (recognition item using sequential -ka-, previewed receptively; full instruction in Chapter 19).

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • Each agreeing numeral matches the noun class of what is being counted.
  • Invariant numerals (five and above, except multiples of ten, hundred, and thousand markers) are not given an unnecessary agreement prefix.
  • -ngapi and -ote carry the correct class concord.
  • Adjectives describing market goods agree with their noun's class — KI-VI, N-class, or another class already taught.
  • Prices are written out in full using mia, elfu, and na correctly.
  • Bei gani? and Shilingi ngapi? are not combined into one ungrammatical question.
  • Object markers used to refer back to an item match the correct noun class.
  • Each sentence ends with a full stop and begins with a capital letter.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Market environments vary widely

"Sokoni" can describe many different settings: a large open-air market like Kariakoo in Dar es Salaam or City Market in Nairobi, a smaller neighborhood market, a roadside stall, or increasingly, a fixed-price shop or supermarket. Shopping practices, price negotiation, and the pace of a transaction differ across these settings and across individual vendors. Do not assume that every Swahili-speaking shopping environment works the same way as the market scenario in this chapter.

Digital payment methods, including mobile money, are now common in many East African markets alongside cash transactions. This chapter focuses on cash vocabulary and patterns as a foundation, but learners should be aware that real transactions may also involve mobile money terminology that goes beyond this chapter's scope.

Culture in Context — Bargaining is context-dependent

Whether and how a customer negotiates price depends heavily on the setting. In many open-air markets, especially for items like clothing and crafts, gentle negotiation is a normal and expected part of a transaction, as modeled in this chapter's dialogue. In contrast, fixed-price shops, supermarkets, and many food stalls do not expect bargaining, and attempting to negotiate there may seem out of place. Observe local norms, watch how other customers behave, and use a polite, low-pressure approach such as Unaweza kupunguza bei? rather than an aggressive bargaining style.

Regional Variation — Currency and price expectations

Tanzania uses the Tanzanian shilling (TZS) and Kenya uses the Kenyan shilling (KES); these are separate currencies with different values, even though both are called shilingi in Swahili. Typical prices for the same item can differ substantially between the two countries and between urban and rural areas. The price examples in this chapter are illustrative for instructional purposes and should not be treated as current, accurate pricing in either country. When traveling or transacting in a specific location, confirm the relevant currency and current prices directly.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Adding agreement to invariant numerals

Error: vitabu vitano treated as if -tano changes form like -tatu does (it does not, but learners sometimes also try to add an unnecessary prefix to numbers like sita or saba, producing forms such as visita)

Correction: Numbers five and above (other than multiples built from agreeing numerals) are invariant: tano, sita, saba, nane, tisa never change shape. Only moja, mbili, tatu, and nne take class concords. Check whether the number you are using is one of the four agreeing numerals before adding a prefix.

Common Mistake — Combining bei and ngapi

Error: Bei ngapi?

Correction: Bei gani? or Shilingi ngapi? — Bei requires the question word gani ("what kind"), while -ngapi ("how many") attaches to a countable noun like shilingi. These are two separate, complete question patterns and should not be mixed.

Common Mistake — Guessing a U-class plural by analogy

Error: Inventing a plural such as maunga for unga by applying a JI-MA-style prefix

Correction: Most U-class mass nouns like unga (flour), uji (porridge), and ugali have no ordinary plural at all, just as English does not normally pluralize "flour." Do not apply a plural prefix from another class to a U-class noun without verifying that the plural actually exists in standard usage.

Common Mistake — Forgetting -ote agreement

Error: matunda ote (bare stem without the JI-MA plural concord)

Correction: matunda yote — -ote requires a class concord just as adjectives and numerals do. JI-MA plural nouns take the concord ya-, producing yote. Always check the noun class before attaching -ote, the same way you check before attaching an adjective prefix.

Common Mistake — Mispronouncing similar-sounding numbers

Error: Confusing sitini (sixty) with sabini (seventy), or saba (seven) with tisa (nine), leading to miscommunication about price or quantity

Correction: Practice these number pairs slowly and deliberately, paying attention to the distinct vowels and consonants in each: sitini vs. sabini; saba vs. tisa. When a price or quantity is important, it is appropriate and normal to ask the speaker to repeat it or to confirm by writing the number down.

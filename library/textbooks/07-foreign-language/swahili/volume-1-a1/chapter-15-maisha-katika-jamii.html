Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Combine directions, transportation vocabulary, and locative expressions to help someone navigate a community.
  2. Discuss how weather affects a plan and adjust that plan using familiar conditional patterns with -ki-.
  3. Explain a basic health need, request assistance, and understand simple advice.
  4. Discuss work or school responsibilities and state a future task using -ta-.
  5. Give and understand basic instructions and polite requests in public-service settings.
  6. Express ability, inability, and necessity using -weza, lazima, and inabidi.
  7. Link a familiar action to its purpose using ili.
  8. Use confirmation, clarification, and repair expressions to solve a practical problem.
  9. Sustain a five- to seven-minute guided interaction that moves through more than one everyday scenario.
  10. Write a connected 120–150-word practical account or plan that draws on directions, weather, health, and work or school language.

Communication Scenario

It is a Tuesday morning. You wake up with a mild fever and need to see a doctor before you go to work. You do not know exactly where the nearest clinic is, and the sky looks like rain, which may affect how you get there. Once you arrive, you will need to explain your symptom, listen to simple advice, and then contact your workplace or school to explain why you will be late and to talk about when you can make up the missed time. This chapter brings together everything you studied in Unit III — directions, weather, health, and work or school responsibilities — so that you can handle a real, connected problem from beginning to end, entirely in Swahili.

  1. Think of a time you had to reschedule something because of an unexpected problem — illness, weather, or a delay. How many separate conversations did that involve? Notice, as you work through this chapter, how a single Swahili exchange can move smoothly from directions, to a health explanation, to a work update, using grammar you already control.
  2. This chapter introduces no major new grammar. Instead, it asks you to combine locatives, imperatives, ability and necessity, purpose clauses, and the conditional -ki- in longer, connected speech and writing — the way a fluent beginner actually uses the language.

Pronunciation and Spelling

Toni katika maombi, maelekezo, na ufafanuzi — Intonation in requests, instructions, and clarification

This chapter's tasks require you to move quickly between different sentence functions in a single conversation: giving directions, making a request, explaining a symptom, and confirming a plan. Each function carries a distinct intonation pattern in careful Standard Swahili.

FunctionPatternExample
Direction / instructionFalling pitch on the final stressed syllable; even pacing between clausesNenda moja kwa moja, kisha kata kulia.
Polite requestSlightly rising pitch through tafadhali or naomba, falling at the endTafadhali, unaweza kunisaidia?
Clarification questionRising pitch across the whole questionSielewi vizuri — unaweza kusema tena?
ConfirmationLevel pitch, slower pacing, brief pause before the confirmed detailSawa — Jumatatu, saa tatu asubuhi.

Muundo wa sentensi zenye vishazi vingi — Multi-clause phrasing

As sentences grow longer in this chapter, pause briefly at each clause boundary rather than rushing through the whole sentence. This is especially important with conditional and purpose clauses, which contain two connected ideas.

Mvua ikinyesha, (pause) tutachukua teksi.

Ninaenda hospitalini (pause) ili nione daktari.

Sound Focus — Stress across present and future forms

In connected speech, English speakers sometimes flatten the stress difference between present and future verb forms because both begin with a subject marker. Keep the penultimate stress rule active in every verb, regardless of tense: ni-na-KWEN-da (I am going) versus ni-ta-KWEN-da (I will go). The tense marker changes the meaning, but stress placement is calculated the same way in both — count back two syllables from the end of the complete word.

Grammar Instruction

This chapter introduces no major new grammatical system. Instead, it reviews and combines locatives and motion, imperatives and requests, ability and necessity, present and future affirmative and negative forms, purpose with ili, and conditional -ki- — all previously taught — so that you can use them together in one connected exchange.

15.1 Locatives, motion, and imperatives — cumulative review

Review the PA/KU/MU locative concepts and motion verbs from Chapter 11, together with singular and plural imperatives.

  • Hospitali iko wapi? — Where is the hospital? General location question with -ko
  • Nenda moja kwa moja, kisha kata kushoto. — Go straight, then turn left. Singular imperative + sequencing word from Ch. 7
  • Ingieni ofisini, tafadhali. — Please come into the office. (addressed to more than one person) Plural imperative + polite marker
  • Hospitali iko karibu na benki. — The hospital is near the bank. Locative phrase with karibu na, Ch. 6 and Ch. 11

15.2 Ability and necessity — cumulative review

Review -weza for ability and lazima / inabidi for necessity, taught in Chapter 13.

  • Siwezi kwenda kazini leo. — I cannot go to work today. Negative ability + infinitive complement
  • Lazima nimwone daktari. — I must see a doctor. lazima + subjunctive-based verb, fixed pattern
  • Inabidi nibadilishe ratiba yangu. — I need to change my schedule. inabidi + verb, controlled pattern

15.3 Present and future — affirmative and negative review

This chapter requires fluent, accurate switching between present -na- and future -ta-, in both affirmative and negative forms, according to the time context of each part of a longer exchange.

TimeAffirmativeNegative
PresentNinaumwa.Sijisikii vizuri. / Siwezi kufika.
FutureNitafika saa nne.Sitafika kazini asubuhi.

15.4 Purpose clauses with ili — review and connected use

Review purpose clauses from Chapter 14. In this chapter, ili connects a motion or request to its reason within a longer, multi-clause sentence.

  • Ninaenda hospitalini ili nione daktari. — I am going to the hospital in order to see a doctor. Motion verb + ili + purpose clause
  • Nitampigia simu mwalimu wangu ili nimweleze tatizo. — I will call my teacher in order to explain the problem to him/her. Future verb + ili + purpose clause

15.5 Conditional -ki- — review in familiar patterns

Review conditional -ki- from Chapter 12. In this chapter, it links a weather or circumstantial condition to a practical decision.

  • Mvua ikinyesha, tutachukua teksi. — If it rains, we will take a taxi. -ki- condition + future main clause
  • Nikichelewa, nitampigia simu mkurugenzi. — If I am late, I will call the director. -ki- condition + future main clause
Language Note — Combining systems does not create new grammar

Placing a locative, a purpose clause, and a conditional in the same conversation does not require any new verb form. Each clause still follows the verb template you already know: subject marker + tense or aspect marker + object marker when needed + verb root + final vowel. What changes in this chapter is your ability to move between clauses smoothly and to select the correct connector — ili, -ki-, kwa sababu, kwa hiyo — for the relationship between two ideas.

Language Building Block
locative or motion clause + purpose clause (ili) + conditional clause (-ki-)
Ninaenda hospitalini ili nione daktari. Mvua ikinyesha, nitachukua teksi.
I am going to the hospital in order to see a doctor. If it rains, I will take a taxi.
Lazima nimpigie simu mwalimu wangu ili nimweleze kwamba nitachelewa.
I must call my teacher in order to explain to him/her that I will be late.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: Neema anatembea mtaani asubuhi. Anaumwa kidogo na anahitaji kufika hospitalini. Anamwuliza Elias, jirani yake, maelekezo. Baadaye, anampigia simu mkurugenzi wa ofisi yake kueleza kwamba atachelewa.

(Setting: Neema is walking in the neighborhood in the morning. She feels somewhat unwell and needs to reach the hospital. She asks Elias, her neighbor, for directions. Afterward, she calls her office director to explain that she will be late.)

NEEMAHabari za asubuhi, Elias. Naomba msaada.Good morning, Elias. I would like some help, please.
ELIASNzuri, Neema. Kuna nini?Fine, Neema. What's going on?
NEEMANinaumwa kidogo. Nina homa. Hospitali iko wapi?I'm a bit unwell. I have a fever. Where is the hospital?
ELIASPole sana. Nenda moja kwa moja hadi kwenye kona, kisha kata kulia. Hospitali iko karibu na duka la dawa.I'm sorry to hear that. Go straight to the corner, then turn right. The hospital is near the pharmacy.
NEEMASielewi vizuri — unaweza kusema tena?I don't understand well — can you say that again?
ELIASBila shaka. Nenda moja kwa moja, kisha kata kulia pale kwenye kona. Hospitali iko mbele yako.Of course. Go straight, then turn right at the corner. The hospital is right in front of you.
NEEMAAsante kwa msaada. Mvua ikianza kunyesha, nitachukua teksi badala ya kutembea.Thank you for the help. If it starts to rain, I will take a taxi instead of walking.
ELIASWazo zuri. Karibu, na pole tena.Good idea. You're welcome, and I'm sorry again.

(Baadaye, hospitalini. / Later, at the hospital.)

NEEMALazima nimpigie simu mkurugenzi wangu ili nimweleze kwamba nitachelewa kazini.I must call my director to explain to him that I will be late for work.
MKURUGENZIHabari, Neema. Kuna nini?Hello, Neema. What's the matter?
NEEMASamahani, ninachelewa. Ninaumwa, na niko hospitalini sasa. Siwezi kufika kazini asubuhi.Sorry, I'm running late. I'm unwell, and I am at the hospital now. I cannot make it to work this morning.
MKURUGENZIPole sana. Usijali kuhusu mkutano wa asubuhi. Tutabadilisha ratiba.I'm very sorry. Don't worry about the morning meeting. We will change the schedule.
NEEMAAsante sana. Nitakuja kazini mchana, ikiwezekana.Thank you very much. I will come to work in the afternoon, if possible.
MKURUGENZISawa. Karibu, na uwe mzima haraka.Alright. You're welcome, and get well soon.

Vocabulary note: badala ya = instead of; ikiwezekana = if possible (recognition item, related to -weza); usijali = don't worry (negative command, recognition item); mzima = well, healthy. These recognition items support natural dialogue without requiring productive control at this stage.

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension questions:

  1. Kwa nini Neema anahitaji kwenda hospitalini? (Why does Neema need to go to the hospital?)
  2. Elias anampa Neema maelekezo gani? (What directions does Elias give Neema?)
  3. Neema atafanya nini mvua ikinyesha? (What will Neema do if it rains?)
  4. Neema anampigia simu nani, na kwa nini? (Who does Neema call, and why?)
  5. Mkurugenzi anasema atafanya nini kuhusu mkutano wa asubuhi? (What does the director say he will do about the morning meeting?)

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Respectful interaction in public services

Interactions in clinics, offices, schools, and other public-service settings generally open with a greeting before any request or explanation of a problem, even when the situation is urgent. Beginning directly with a request or complaint, without any greeting, can come across as abrupt in many East African settings. A short greeting such as Habari or Hujambo, followed by a polite request such as Naomba msaada, is a natural and respectful way to open such an exchange.

Titles and respectful forms of address — for a teacher, a director, a nurse, or an official — are commonly used in these settings. This chapter's dialogue models Mwalimu and Mkurugenzi as respectful address forms; learners should use comparable titles when addressing someone in a professional or institutional role.

Culture in Context — Directness and context-appropriate communication

The right level of directness in explaining a problem — such as illness, a delay, or a missed appointment — depends on the relationship, the setting, and the seriousness of the situation. Among close friends or family, a direct and detailed explanation is often welcome. In more formal or institutional settings, a brief, respectful explanation, followed by a clear practical request (for example, changing a schedule), is often more appropriate than a long personal account. These are general tendencies, not fixed rules, and learners should observe context carefully.

Regional Variation — Transport, health, school, and workplace institutions

Transportation systems, clinic and hospital procedures, school schedules, and workplace expectations vary considerably across Tanzania, Kenya, and other Swahili-speaking communities, and even within a single country between urban and rural areas. A word such as daladala (a common minibus term in Tanzania) or matatu (a common minibus term in Kenya) may be used regionally alongside the more general basi. This textbook uses general Standard Swahili terms as the productive default; learners traveling to a specific community should expect to encounter and learn local institutional vocabulary and procedures.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing lazima and -weza

Error: Ninaweza kumwona daktari. when the intended meaning is obligation, not ability

Correction: Lazima nimwone daktari. — -weza expresses ability or possibility ("I am able to"), while lazima expresses obligation or necessity ("I must"). Check whether your sentence describes what someone is capable of doing or what they are required to do before choosing between these forms.

Common Mistake — Mixing present and future forms in one connected account

Error: Ninaenda hospitalini kesho. (using present -na- for a clearly future event marked by kesho)

Correction: Nitaenda hospitalini kesho. — Even though Swahili verb morphology can carry meaning that a time expression later confirms, the productive beginner pattern taught in this course uses future -ta- for events explicitly placed in future time. Check the time expression in your sentence and match the tense marker to it.

Common Mistake — Placing ili or -ki- incorrectly in a sentence

Error: Ili ninaenda hospitalini nione daktari. (placing ili before the main clause)

Correction: Ninaenda hospitalini ili nione daktari. — Ili introduces the purpose clause and normally follows the main action it explains. Similarly, the -ki- conditional clause may come first or second in the sentence, but the condition and the result must remain in separate clauses, each with its own complete verb: Mvua ikinyesha, tutachukua teksi.

Common Mistake — Overusing English-style directness in a request

Error: Opening a request for help with only Nataka msaada (I want help) and no greeting

Correction: Habari — naomba msaada, tafadhali. — A brief greeting before a request is expected in most East African public-service interactions, even when time is short. Pair naomba with a greeting rather than opening abruptly with a bare statement of want.

Common Mistake — Losing clause boundaries in longer sentences

Error: Running a locative, a purpose clause, and a conditional together as one unbroken sentence without clear breaks: Ninaenda hospitalini ili nione daktari mvua ikinyesha nitachukua teksi

Correction: Ninaenda hospitalini ili nione daktari. Mvua ikinyesha, nitachukua teksi. — Keep each connected idea as its own sentence or clearly punctuated clause. Longer connected speech and writing should still respect sentence boundaries, even as you combine more information.

Answer Key / Ufunguo wa Majibu

Dictation Source Sentences

  2. Nenda moja kwa moja, kisha kata kulia.
  3. Mvua ikinyesha, tutachukua teksi.
  4. Lazima nimwone daktari kesho asubuhi.

Mazoezi A — Maelekezo na amri (Directions and imperatives)

  1. Nenda moja kwa moja hadi kwenye kona.
  2. Kata kulia pale dukani.
  3. Ingieni ofisini, tafadhali.
  4. Duka la dawa liko karibu na shuleni.

Mazoezi B — Uwezo na ulazima (Ability and necessity)

  1. Siwezi kufika kazini saa mbili.
  2. Lazima nimwone daktari.
  3. Inabidi abadilishe ratiba yake.

Mazoezi C — Sasa na wakati ujao (Present and future — model answers)

  1. Ninakwenda kazini. / Sikwendi kazini. / Nitakwenda kazini. / Sitakwenda kazini.
  2. Anaumwa. / Haumwi. / Ataumwa. / Hataumwa.
  3. Tunafika saa tatu. / Hatufiki saa tatu. / Tutafika saa tatu. / Hatutafika saa tatu.

Mazoezi D — Kusudi na sharti (Purpose and condition)

  1. Ninaenda dukani ili nunue dawa.
  2. Mvua ikinyesha, tutakaa nyumbani.
  3. Ninampigia simu mwalimu ili nimweleze tatizo.
  4. Nikichelewa, nitampigia simu mkurugenzi.

Other accurate, well-formed sentences are equally acceptable. Check clause order, connector choice, and tense agreement carefully.

Dialogue Comprehension

  1. Neema anahitaji kwenda hospitalini kwa sababu anaumwa na ana homa. (Neema needs to go to the hospital because she is unwell and has a fever.)
  2. Elias anamwambia aende moja kwa moja hadi kwenye kona, kisha akate kulia. Hospitali iko karibu na duka la dawa. (Elias tells her to go straight to the corner, then turn right. The hospital is near the pharmacy.)
  3. Mvua ikianza kunyesha, Neema atachukua teksi badala ya kutembea. (If it starts to rain, Neema will take a taxi instead of walking.)
  4. Neema anampigia simu mkurugenzi wake ili amweleze kwamba atachelewa kazini. (Neema calls her director to explain that she will be late for work.)
  5. Mkurugenzi anasema watabadilisha ratiba ya mkutano wa asubuhi. (The director says they will change the schedule of the morning meeting.)

Listening Responses

Kazi A (Mazungumzo A): Nenda moja kwa moja hadi kwenye taa za barabarani → kata kushoto → kituo cha polisi kiko karibu na benki. Destination: kituo cha polisi.

Kazi B (Mazungumzo B): Problem: maumivu ya tumbo tangu asubuhi. Necessity: lazima amwone daktari leo; inabidi abadilishe miadi ya kazi. Plan: atampigia simu mkurugenzi wake ili amweleze hali yake.

Kazi C (Mazungumzo C): Condition 1: Mvua ikinyesha mchana → hawataweza kutembea, watachukua basi. Condition 2: Jua likitoka → watatembea kama kawaida.

Reading Comprehension

  1. Kliniki inafunguliwa saa mbili asubuhi hadi saa kumi na mbili jioni, Jumatatu hadi Jumamosi. (The clinic is open from 8:00 a.m. to 6:00 p.m., Monday through Saturday.)
  2. Mgonjwa anayehisi vibaya sana anapaswa kuja moja kwa moja bila miadi. (A patient who feels very unwell should come directly without an appointment.)
  3. Doreen hawezi kuja shuleni kwa sababu ana homa na lazima amwone daktari. (Doreen cannot come to school because she has a fever and must see a doctor.)
  4. Mvua ikinyesha sana, Doreen atampigia simu rafiki yake ili amweleze kazi aliyoikosa. (If it rains heavily, Doreen will call her friend to explain the work she missed.)
  5. Purpose clause: nitampigia simu rafiki yangu ili anieleze kazi niliyoikosa — connects the phone call to its reason. Conditional clause: mvua isiponyesha / mvua ikinyesha sana — connects the weather condition to Doreen's plan.

You have practiced moving smoothly between present and future forms according to the time context of each part of a longer conversation, and you have used public-service and scheduling language to solve a realistic community problem from beginning to end.

Hali ya Hewa na Majira

Weather and Seasons · Speak and Write Swahili: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe current weather conditions using common impersonal expressions with kuna and ni.
  2. Ask about weather conditions in another place and respond with appropriate detail.
  3. Discuss seasonal and climate patterns relevant to East Africa, including the long and short rains.
  4. Relate weather conditions to clothing choices, travel plans, and outdoor activities.
  5. Recognize noun-class subject agreement with weather-related and natural-phenomena nouns already introduced.
  6. Form and interpret simple real conditions using -ki- in transparent, high-frequency patterns.
  7. Express cause and result using kwa sababu and kwa hiyo.
  8. Pronounce weather vocabulary containing mv, ny, and j accurately, and use appropriate clause-level intonation.
  9. Write a short weather forecast and a conditional plan based on it.

Communication Scenario

You are planning a weekend visit to a friend who lives in a different town. Before you travel, you check the weather for both your city and hers, because the conditions are often different — her town is near the coast, while yours is inland and at a higher elevation. As you message back and forth, you each describe the current weather, talk about what the next two days will likely bring, and decide what to pack and what activities make sense if it rains. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to describe weather conditions, discuss simple seasonal patterns, and make a plan that depends on what the weather does.

  1. In English, weather is often described with "it" — "it is raining," "it is hot." Swahili does not use a dummy subject the way English does. As you work through this chapter, pay attention to how Swahili expresses weather without a word that corresponds directly to English "it."
  2. Many regions near the equator organize the year around rainfall rather than four temperate seasons. As you study seasonal vocabulary, notice how the Swahili year may be described in terms of dry and rainy periods rather than spring, summer, autumn, and winter.

Pronunciation and Spelling

Sauti za mv, ny, na j — The combinations mv, ny, and j

Weather vocabulary in this chapter introduces several useful sound combinations. Practice them carefully, since they appear in some of your most frequently used new words.

CombinationApproximate soundExampleMeaning

Sound Focus — mv as a single onset

As with the prenasalized consonants you learned in Chapter 4 (mb, nd, ng, nj), the combination mv in mvua is pronounced as one smooth unit, not as two separate sounds. Avoid inserting a vowel between the m and the v. Say mvu-a in two syllables, not e-mvu-a in three.

Kiimbo cha sentensi yenye sharti — Intonation in conditional sentences

Sentences using -ki- to express a condition have two parts: the condition and the result. In natural speech, there is a brief pause between the two parts, and the voice often rises slightly at the end of the condition clause before falling across the result clause.

Mvua ikinyesha, (pause, slight rise) tutakaa nyumbani. (fall)

Practice reading the conditional sentences in this chapter aloud, pausing clearly after the comma.

Grammar Instruction

12.1 Talking about weather without "it"

Purpose: describe weather conditions using the Swahili structures that replace the English dummy subject "it."

English describes weather with the placeholder subject "it" — "it is raining," "it is cold," "it is sunny." Swahili has no equivalent placeholder. Instead, Swahili weather expressions use one of two familiar structures you have already studied: kuna (there is/are) for conditions expressed as a noun that exists, or ni (is) for conditions expressed as a description.

Pattern 1 — kuna + weather noun, used when the condition is described as something present:

  • Kuna jua. It is sunny. (Literally: There is sun.)
  • Kuna upepo. It is windy. (Literally: There is wind.)
  • Kuna mawingu. It is cloudy. (Literally: There are clouds.)

Pattern 2 — ni + descriptive noun, used for temperature and general conditions:

  • Ni joto leo. It is hot today.
  • Ni baridi asubuhi. It is cold in the morning.

Pattern 3 — noun subject + verb, used when rain or a similar phenomenon is the grammatical subject of its own sentence:

  • Mvua inanyesha. It is raining. (Literally: Rain is falling.) mvua (N-class, i- subject marker) + -na- + nyesha
Language Note — kuna versus ni in weather expressions

You learned in Chapter 6 that kuna expresses general existence and ni expresses identity or description. Weather language uses both, but for different kinds of conditions. Conditions you can think of as "something present" — sun, wind, clouds, fog — use kuna. Conditions you describe as a quality — hot, cold — use ni. There is no single rule that predicts every case; learn each fixed pattern as presented in the vocabulary section.

Weather and nature nouns belong to the same noun classes you have already studied, and they follow the same subject-marker rules. This section is a review and application of agreement, not a new grammatical system.

12.3 Introductory conditional with -ki-

Purpose: express a simple real condition — "if X happens, Y will happen" — in high-frequency, transparent weather and planning contexts.

Swahili expresses a real condition — one that is genuinely possible — using the marker -ki- in the verb of the condition clause. The structure is similar to the verb template you already know, with -ki- taking the position of the tense marker:

subject marker + -ki- + verb root + final vowel

The main clause — the result — uses a tense marker you already know, most often the future -ta-.

  • Mvua ikinyesha, tutakaa nyumbani. If it rains, we will stay home. mvua + i-ki-nyesh-a, tu-ta-ka-a nyumbani
  • Jua likiwaka, tutaenda baharini. If the sun comes out, we will go to the sea. (recognition)
  • Ukipenda, twende kesho. If you like, let's go tomorrow.
Language Note — -ki- is introduced narrowly

The Swahili conditional system is broader than what is presented here. At this stage, you are learning -ki- only in a small set of high-frequency, transparent weather and planning patterns, paired with a future-tense result clause. Do not attempt to apply -ki- to every kind of condition yet; broader use of conditional structures is outside the scope of this chapter. Treat Mvua ikinyesha, tutakaa nyumbani as your core productive model and build other sentences carefully on that pattern.

Language Building Block
-ki- condition + main clause
Mvua ikinyesha, tutakaa nyumbani.
If it rains, we will stay home. — i-ki-nyesh-a (condition) + tu-ta-ka-a (result)
Ukienda sokoni, nunua mwavuli.
If you go to the market, buy an umbrella. — u-ki-end-a (condition) + nunua (command, result)

12.4 Cause and result: kwa sababu and kwa hiyo

Purpose: connect two related ideas by explaining why something happens or what results from it.

kwa sababu ("because") introduces a reason. kwa hiyo ("therefore," "so") introduces a result. Both connectors join two complete clauses.

  • Tunakaa nyumbani kwa sababu mvua inanyesha. We are staying home because it is raining.
  • Mvua inanyesha. Kwa hiyo, tunakaa nyumbani. It is raining. Therefore, we are staying home.
  • Ninavaa koti kwa sababu ni baridi. I am wearing a coat because it is cold.

Notice that kwa sababu typically joins two clauses within one sentence, while kwa hiyo often begins a new sentence that follows the reason. Both orders are natural in connected speech and writing.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: Amara yuko Nairobi. Anamwandikia ujumbe rafiki yake, Zawadi, ambaye yuko Mombasa. Wanapanga ziara ya wikendi.

(Setting: Amara is in Nairobi. She is messaging her friend Zawadi, who is in Mombasa. They are planning a weekend visit.)

AMARAHabari za leo, Zawadi! Hali ya hewa ikoje huko Mombasa?How are things today, Zawadi! What is the weather like there in Mombasa?
ZAWADINzuri sana, asante! Hapa kuna jua na ni joto. Pwani daima ni joto, unajua.Very good, thank you! Here it is sunny and hot. The coast is always hot, you know.
AMARAHapa Nairobi ni baridi kidogo asubuhi, lakini mchana kuna jua pia. Kesho nataka kuja Mombasa.Here in Nairobi it is a little cold in the morning, but in the afternoon there is also sun. Tomorrow I want to come to Mombasa.
ZAWADIVizuri sana! Lakini kesho mvua itanyesha jioni, wanasema.Very good! But tomorrow rain will fall in the evening, they say.
AMARAMvua ikinyesha, tutafanya nini?If it rains, what will we do?
ZAWADIMvua ikinyesha jioni, tutakaa nyumbani na kuangalia filamu. Lakini asubuhi kutakuwa na jua, kwa hiyo tunaweza kuogelea kwanza.If it rains in the evening, we will stay home and watch a film. But in the morning there will be sun, so we can swim first.
AMARASawa! Nivae nini? Ni joto sana huko, kwa hiyo sitaleta koti.Okay! What should I wear? It is very hot there, so I will not bring a coat.
ZAWADISahihi. Lakini leta mwavuli kwa sababu ya mvua jioni. Na kofia kwa sababu ya jua mchana!Correct. But bring an umbrella because of the rain in the evening. And a hat because of the sun in the afternoon!
AMARAAsante kwa habari! Tuonane kesho.Thank you for the information! See you tomorrow.
ZAWADIKaribu! Safari njema.You're welcome! Safe travels.

Vocabulary note: Hali ya hewa ikoje? = What is the weather like? (fixed receptive question pattern); daima = always; wanasema = they say; kutakuwa na = there will be (future existence, recognition item); kwa sababu ya = because of (followed by a noun rather than a clause); Tuonane kesho = See you tomorrow (literally: let's see each other tomorrow); Safari njema = Safe travels. These are recognition items at this stage except where they reuse patterns already taught.

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension questions:

  1. Hali ya hewa ikoje Mombasa leo? (What is the weather like in Mombasa today?)
  2. Hali ya hewa ikoje Nairobi leo? (What is the weather like in Nairobi today?)
  3. Mvua itanyesha lini kesho? (When will it rain tomorrow?)
  4. Watafanya nini ikiwa mvua itanyesha jioni? (What will they do if it rains in the evening?)
  5. Kwa nini Zawadi anamwambia Amara alete mwavuli na kofia? (Why does Zawadi tell Amara to bring an umbrella and a hat?)

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Rainfall-organized seasons

Much of East Africa lies near the equator, where the year is commonly organized around rainfall patterns rather than the four temperate seasons familiar to many English speakers. Many regions experience two main rainy periods — often called the long rains and the short rains — separated by drier periods. The exact timing and intensity of these rains vary by year, elevation, and specific location, and are increasingly affected by broader climate patterns.

Coastal areas such as Dar es Salaam, Mombasa, and Zanzibar tend to be warm and humid throughout the year, while highland areas such as those near Nairobi, Arusha, or Mount Kilimanjaro can be considerably cooler, especially in the early morning and at night. Avoid assuming that a single seasonal pattern applies uniformly across every Swahili-speaking region.

Culture in Context — Weather and daily planning

Weather conditions, especially rainfall, can meaningfully affect daily plans, travel, agriculture, and outdoor commerce in many East African communities. Checking and discussing the weather before finalizing a plan — as Amara and Zawadi do in this chapter's dialogue — is a practical and common part of everyday conversation, much as it is in many other parts of the world. This is a matter of practical planning rather than a fixed cultural ritual, and its importance varies by occupation, location, and individual circumstance.

Regional Variation — Describing temperature

The core forms ni joto (it is hot) and ni baridi (it is cold) are widely understood across Standard Swahili-speaking communities. In casual conversation, some speakers add intensifiers such as sana (very) or kidogo (a little) more freely than in formal writing, and some regions favor slightly different idiomatic ways of describing extreme heat or cold. The forms taught in this chapter represent the stable, broadly intelligible productive standard; treat additional idiomatic variations as receptive material to be learned as you encounter them.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Inventing a Swahili word for "it"

Error: Attempting to translate English "it" directly, such as producing an unnecessary subject marker before kuna or ni

Correction: Kuna jua. / Ni joto. — Swahili weather expressions do not require a placeholder subject corresponding to English "it." Kuna and ni already function as complete predicates in these patterns. Do not add an extra subject marker or pronoun before them.

Common Mistake — Confusing kuna and ni in weather expressions

Error: Kuna baridi. when the intended meaning is "It is cold" as a general description

Correction: Ni baridi. — While Kuna baridi is not always wrong (it can describe coldness as present in a place), the standard productive pattern for describing general temperature in this course is ni + descriptive noun: Ni joto, Ni baridi. Reserve kuna for conditions you are describing as something present, like sun, wind, or clouds, following the fixed patterns taught in this chapter.

Common Mistake — Leaving out the result clause in a -ki- sentence

Error: Mvua ikinyesha. presented as a complete sentence with the intended meaning "If it rains."

Correction: Mvua ikinyesha, tutakaa nyumbani. — A -ki- condition clause is not a complete sentence on its own; it must be paired with a result clause, most often in the future tense. Mvua ikinyesha alone leaves the meaning incomplete, much like saying "If it rains" in English without finishing the sentence.

Common Mistake — Reversing kwa sababu and kwa hiyo

Error: Mvua inanyesha kwa hiyo tunakaa nyumbani kwa sababu. (using both connectors incorrectly in one sentence, or using kwa hiyo to introduce a reason)

Correction: Tunakaa nyumbani kwa sababu mvua inanyesha. (reason) or Mvua inanyesha. Kwa hiyo, tunakaa nyumbani. (result) — kwa sababu always introduces the reason or cause; kwa hiyo always introduces the result or consequence. Use only one connector per logical link, and make sure it points in the correct direction.

Common Mistake — Adding a vowel inside mv

Error: Pronouncing mvua as e-mvua or with a pause after the m

Correction: mvua — Like the prenasalized consonants you learned in Chapter 4, mv is a single blended sound. Move directly from the nasal into the v and the following vowel: mvu-a, in two syllables.

Common Mistake — Assuming one season pattern fits everywhere

Error: Describing "the Swahili rainy season" as though it occurs on identical dates everywhere Swahili is spoken

Correction: Rainfall timing varies by region, elevation, and year across East Africa. Use general expressions like msimu wa mvua (rainy season) and msimu wa kiangazi (dry season) without asserting a single fixed calendar for the entire Swahili-speaking world. When specific dates matter, check current, location-specific information rather than relying on a single generalized rule.

Chapter Review — Sehemu B

Question 1 (kuna/ni):

  • Kuna jua leo.
  • Ni baridi sana asubuhi.
  • Kuna mawingu mchana.
  • Ni joto pwani daima.

Question 2 (conditionals): model answers — Mvua ikinyesha kesho, tutakaa nyumbani. Jua likiwaka, tutaenda baharini.

Question 3 (error correction):

  • Ni kuna baridi leo — Error: ni and kuna cannot both introduce the same predicate. Correction: Ni baridi leo.
  • Mvua ikinyesha. as a standalone answer — Error: the condition clause has no result clause. Correction: Mvua ikinyesha, tutakaa nyumbani. (or another logical result)
  • Tunakaa nyumbani kwa hiyo mvua inanyesha — Error: kwa hiyo is used where a reason is intended. Correction: Tunakaa nyumbani kwa sababu mvua inanyesha.

Question 4: False. Swahili weather expressions such as kuna jua and ni joto do not include a word corresponding to English "it"; the existential or descriptive structure stands on its own.

Question 5: Mvua ikinyesha kesho, tutakaa nyumbani na kuangalia filamu.

Looking Ahead

Unachojua sasa — What you know now

You can now describe weather conditions, discuss seasonal patterns relevant to East Africa, and connect weather to practical decisions about clothing and plans. You have learned that Swahili expresses weather without a placeholder subject, reviewed noun-class agreement by applying it to nature vocabulary, and taken your first controlled step into the conditional system with -ki- in transparent, high-frequency patterns. You have also learned to connect cause and result with kwa sababu and kwa hiyo. These tools — weather description, conditional sentences, and cause-and-result connectors — will expand further as the course continues, particularly when you study necessity and ability in Chapter 13 and future planning in Chapter 14.

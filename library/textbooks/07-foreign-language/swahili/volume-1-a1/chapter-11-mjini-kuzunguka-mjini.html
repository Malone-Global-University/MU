Getting Around Town · Speak and Write Swahili: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify common buildings, public services, and forms of transportation.
  2. Ask where a place is located using Wapi? and related question patterns.
  3. Use the general locative marker -ko, the specific locative marker -po, and the interior locative marker -mo in controlled, high-frequency expressions.
  4. Give and follow simple directions using motion verbs including kwenda, kuja, kuingia, and kutoka.
  5. Form affirmative and negative commands addressed to one person and to more than one person.
  6. State origin, destination, and general location using prepositions and locative phrases already introduced.
  7. Understand common public signs and short travel instructions.
  8. Pronounce direction and command intonation clearly, and read route information aloud accurately.
  9. Write a short set of step-by-step directions to a familiar place.

Communication Scenario

You are visiting a friend in a town you do not know well. You need to find the bank, the market, and the bus stop, and you want to be able to explain to a taxi driver or a stranger on the street exactly where you are trying to go. Your friend, in turn, asks you for directions to a nearby pharmacy. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to ask where a place is, understand and give basic directions, and talk about getting from one point in town to another.

  1. Think about how you ask for directions in your own language. Do you usually say where you are, where you want to go, or both? Swahili distinguishes these ideas carefully using different locative markers and verbs. Notice this distinction as you work through the chapter.
  2. In English, "go," "come," "enter," and "leave" are four separate verbs with no shared form. Swahili motion verbs work in a similar way — each carries its own meaning — but they combine predictably with the present-tense pattern you already know from Chapter 7. Watch for how familiar verb structure supports brand-new vocabulary.

Mkazo wa sauti katika maswali na maagizo — Intonation in questions and commands

Direction-giving combines two intonation patterns you must distinguish clearly: the rising, inquisitive contour of a location question, and the level, purposeful contour of a command. Practice the contrast below.

Mdundo wa kifungu — Phrase-level rhythm in route sequences

When giving a full set of directions, Swahili speakers typically pause briefly between each instruction, similar to listing steps. Read the following sequence aloud, pausing at each comma and full stop, and keeping word-level stress on the penultimate syllable throughout.

Nenda moja kwa moja, geuka kulia kwenye kona, pita mbele ya shule, na utafika benki.

Grammar Instruction

11.1 The PA/KU/MU locative system — three ways to talk about "there"

Purpose: understand that Swahili distinguishes general, specific, and interior location, and recognize the three markers that express this distinction.

In Chapter 6, you learned kuna for general existence and locative phrases such as katika, juu ya, and chini ya. Swahili also has a system of three locative reference markers attached to forms of kuwa and to demonstratives. These markers are traditionally grouped under three labels: PA, KU, and MU. They do not work like an ordinary singular-plural noun class; instead, each one expresses a different type of location.

MarkerType of locationTypical use
-poSpecific, definite locationA particular point or place that is identifiable
-koGeneral locationA general area or location, often used by default
-moInterior locationInside an enclosed or bounded space
Language Note — PA/KU/MU are not ordinary noun classes

Unlike M-WA, KI-VI, or N-class, the PA/KU/MU system does not organize nouns into singular-plural pairs. Instead, it organizes types of location. At this stage, you only need to recognize the basic distinction among specific (-po), general (-ko), and interior (-mo) location, and to use a small set of high-frequency forms productively. A complete locative concord system is outside the scope of this volume.

11.2 Person-location forms with -ko, -po, and -mo

Purpose: say where a person or thing is located, using controlled high-frequency forms.

The most common productive use of this system at the beginner level is the person-location form, which combines a subject marker with -ko. You saw this briefly in Chapter 4.

  • Niko nyumbani. — I am at home. ni- (I) + -ko (general location)
  • Uko wapi? — Where are you? u- (you) + -ko (general location) + wapi (where)
  • Yuko sokoni. — He/she is at the market. yu- (he/she) + -ko (general location)
  • Tuko mjini. — We are in town. tu- (we) + -ko (general location)

Nouns referring to places also take a locative form built on the same three markers. The most frequent beginner pattern uses -ko for general location:

  • Benki iko wapi? — Where is the bank? benki (bank, N-class) + i- (it, N-class subject marker) + -ko (general location) + wapi (where)
  • Soko liko karibu na shule. — The market is near the school. soko (market, JI-MA sg.) + li- (it) + -ko + karibu na shule

Specific location with -po is used when a place has already been identified and the speaker is confirming or pointing to it directly:

  • Hapa ndipo benki ipo. — Right here is exactly where the bank is. hapa (here) + ndipo (it is right there) + benki + i- + -po (specific location)

Interior location with -mo is used when something is inside an enclosed space:

  • Watu wamo ndani ya basi. — The people are inside the bus. watu (people) + wa- (they) + -mo (interior location) + ndani ya basi (inside the bus)
Language Note — -ko as the productive default

For most beginner communication, -ko is the productive default for stating location, whether the location is general or simply unspecified in detail. Reserve -po for situations where you are confirming a precise, already-identified point, and -mo for situations where something is clearly inside an enclosed space. You are not expected to choose among all three automatically in every sentence; focus on recognizing the distinction and producing -ko forms confidently.

11.3 Motion verbs and destinations

Purpose: talk about going, coming, entering, and leaving, and connect a motion verb to a destination or point of origin.

Motion verbs combine with the present-tense template you already know from Chapter 7: subject marker + -na- + verb root + final vowel. A destination or point of origin can follow directly, often introduced by a locative phrase or preposition you already know.

  • Ninaenda sokoni. — I am going to the market. ni- (I) + -na- + -end- (go) + -a; sokoni = market + locative -ni
  • Anakuja shuleni. — He/she is coming to school. a- (he/she) + -na- + -j- (come) + -a
  • Tunaingia dukani. — We are entering the shop. tu- (we) + -na- + -ingi- (enter) + -a
  • Wanatoka nyumbani. — They are leaving home. / They are coming from home. wa- (they) + -na- + -tok- (leave/come from) + -a
Language Building Block
motion verb + destination or locative phrase
Nenda moja kwa moja hadi benki.
Go straight ahead until the bank. — imperative "nenda" + route description + "hadi" (until) + destination
Ninatoka shuleni, ninaenda sokoni.
I am coming from school; I am going to the market. — kutoka + origin, kwenda + destination

The locative suffix -ni, used productively here in words like sokoni, shuleni, and nyumbani, attaches to many place nouns to mean "at," "to," or "in" that place. You encountered this pattern in earlier chapters with nyumbani and jikoni. It now extends to the town vocabulary in this chapter.

  • benki → benki (no change; already ends appropriately) — "at/to the bank" is usually expressed as benki in context, or with katika benki Not every noun takes -ni; some places use a preposition instead. Learn each form individually.
  • soko → sokoni — "at/to the market"
  • shule → shuleni — "at/to school"
  • nyumba → nyumbani — "at/to home"

11.4 Affirmative and negative imperatives

Purpose: give instructions and directions to one person or to more than one person, and tell someone not to do something.

You learned singular and plural affirmative commands briefly in Chapter 2. This chapter expands that pattern to direction-giving and introduces negative commands.

Affirmative commands (singular — one person): For most verbs ending in -a, the singular command is simply the verb stem with its final -a, with no subject marker.

  • Geuka kulia. — Turn right. (to one person) geuk- (turn) + -a
  • Nenda moja kwa moja. — Go straight ahead. (to one person) nenda is the irregular imperative of kwenda
  • Pita hapa. — Pass through here. (to one person) pit- (pass) + -a

Affirmative commands (plural — more than one person): Add -eni to the verb stem.

  • Geukeni kulia. — Turn right. (to more than one person) geuk- (turn) + -eni
  • Pendeni hapa. — Wait here. (to more than one person; recognition variant) illustrative form; "ngojeni hapa" is the more common phrase for "wait here"
  • Ingieni. — Enter. (to more than one person) ingi- (enter) + -eni

Negative commands (singular): Use usi- + verb root + -e. Note that the final vowel changes from -a to -e in the negative command, unlike most other negative forms you have studied.

  • Usigeuke kulia. — Do not turn right. (to one person) usi- + geuk- (turn) + -e
  • Usiingie hapa. — Do not enter here. (to one person) usi- + ingi- (enter) + -e

Negative commands (plural): Use msi- + verb root + -e.

  • Msigeuke kulia. — Do not turn right. (to more than one person) msi- + geuk- (turn) + -e
  • Msisimame hapa. — Do not stop/stand here. (to more than one person) msi- + simam- (stand/stop) + -e
Language Note — Imperatives and politeness

A direct imperative is appropriate in many practical situations, such as public signs, urgent instructions, or close relationships, but it can sound abrupt between strangers or in formal settings. When giving directions to someone you do not know well, soften the instruction with tafadhali (please) placed before the command, or use a polite frame such as Unaweza kugeuka kulia ("You can turn right"). The bare imperative remains correct and necessary — it is not impolite by default — but context and relationship affect how directly you should phrase a request.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: Tariq ni mgeni mjini Arusha. Anamwomba Halima, mkazi wa mtaa huo, msaada wa kupata benki.

(Setting: Tariq is a visitor in the town of Arusha. He asks Halima, a resident of the neighborhood, for help finding a bank.)

TARIQSamahani, naomba msaada. Benki iko wapi?Excuse me, I would like some help. Where is the bank?
HALIMAKaribu sana. Unaona barabara hii?Very close. Do you see this road?
TARIQNdiyo, naiona.Yes, I see it.
HALIMASawa. Nenda moja kwa moja hadi kwenye kona, kisha geuka kulia. Benki iko karibu na shule, mbele ya duka kubwa.Okay. Go straight ahead until the corner, then turn right. The bank is near the school, in front of the big shop.
TARIQNenda moja kwa moja, kisha kulia kwenye kona. Iko mbele ya duka?Go straight ahead, then right at the corner. It's in front of the shop?
HALIMASahihi! Usigeuke kushoto — njia hiyo inaenda sokoni, sio benki.Correct! Do not turn left — that way goes to the market, not the bank.
TARIQAsante sana. Na kituo cha basi kiko wapi? Ninahitaji kwenda Dar es Salaam kesho.Thank you very much. And where is the bus stop? I need to go to Dar es Salaam tomorrow.
HALIMAKituo cha basi kiko mbali kidogo, karibu na stesheni. Unaweza kupanda teksi au boda boda kutoka hapa.The bus stop is a little far, near the station. You can take a taxi or a motorcycle taxi from here.
TARIQVizuri. Asante kwa msaada wako.Good. Thank you for your help.
HALIMAKaribu! Safari njema kesho.You're welcome! Have a good trip tomorrow.

Vocabulary note: mgeni = visitor, guest; mkazi = resident; unaona = you see; kwenye = at/on (general locative preposition); kupanda = to board, to climb; safari njema = a fixed travel-farewell expression meaning "safe travels." These are recognition items at this stage.

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension questions:

  1. Tariq anataka kufika wapi mwanzoni? (Where does Tariq want to get to at the start?)
  2. Halima anamwelekeza vipi Tariq kufika benki? Andika hatua zote. (How does Halima direct Tariq to the bank? Write all the steps.)
  3. Kwa nini Halima anasema "Usigeuke kushoto"? (Why does Halima say "Do not turn left"?)
  4. Tariq anataka kwenda wapi kesho, na anawezaje kufika kituo cha basi? (Where does Tariq want to go tomorrow, and how can he get to the bus stop?)
  5. Find one affirmative singular command and one negative singular command in the dialogue. Write both.

Writing Workshop

  1. Soma mfano. Reread Maandishi 1 (Ramani ya mtaa wangu) above. Underline every locative form (words with -ko, -po, -mo, or the -ni suffix). Circle every motion verb.
  2. Panga mawazo yako. Choose a real or imagined route from one place you know well (such as your home) to another place (such as a school, market, or bank). List the buildings or landmarks you would pass along the way.
  3. Andika rasimu yako ya kwanza. Write six to eight connected Swahili sentences giving step-by-step directions along your chosen route. Use at least two motion verbs, one location question or statement with -ko, and at least one affirmative command.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • Each motion verb agrees with its subject and uses the present-tense template correctly.
  • Location statements use -ko (general), and -po or -mo only where their specific meaning is clearly intended.
  • Affirmative singular commands end in -a; affirmative plural commands end in -eni.
  • Negative commands use usi- (singular) or msi- (plural) and end in -e, not -a.
  • Direction and position words (kushoto, kulia, mbele ya, karibu na) are spelled correctly and placed naturally.
  • The locative suffix -ni is used only with nouns that take it productively; other place nouns use a preposition instead.
  • Each sentence ends with a full stop and begins with a capital letter.

Speaking Practice

Did your negative commands end in -e?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Asking for directions

Asking a stranger for directions is a common, everyday interaction across East African towns and cities, and it is generally expected that the request will begin with a greeting or a polite opener such as samahani (excuse me) before the actual question. Moving directly to the question without any greeting can come across as abrupt in many settings, even though the urgency of the situation (for example, being lost or in a hurry) is usually understood and accommodated.

People giving directions often combine landmarks (a particular shop, school, or church) with general distance and direction words rather than precise measurements. This reflects how directions are commonly communicated in many communities, but it should not be treated as the only valid style — some travelers may also receive directions using street names, especially in larger or more recently planned urban areas.

Culture in Context — Public transportation

Transportation options and conventions vary considerably across East African towns and cities. Minibuses (commonly called daladala in Tanzania or matatu in Kenya), boda boda motorcycle taxis, regular taxis, and walking are all common ways to get around, and the available options depend heavily on the specific city, town, or rural area. This textbook uses general terms such as basi and boda boda as a stable productive core; learners traveling to a specific city should expect to encounter additional local terms and practices.

Regional Variation — Minibus terminology

The word for a shared minibus differs by country: daladala is the common term in Tanzania, while matatu is the common term in Kenya. Both are widely understood as referring to similar forms of shared local transportation, but they are not interchangeable as the "correct" word in every country. This chapter uses the more general term basi for the productive core, while daladala and matatu are introduced here as receptive, regionally labeled vocabulary.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing -ko, -po, and -mo

Error: Using -po in every location sentence, modeled on the first form learned

Correction: Use -ko as your default for general location, such as Niko nyumbani (I am at home) or Benki iko wapi? (Where is the bank?). Reserve -po for confirming a specific, already-identified point, and -mo only for something clearly inside an enclosed space, such as Watu wamo ndani ya basi (The people are inside the bus). When in doubt at this stage, -ko is almost always the safer choice.

Common Mistake — Wrong final vowel in negative commands

Error: Usigeuka kulia. (keeping the final -a in a negative command)

Correction: Usigeuke kulia. — Negative commands change the verb's final vowel from -a to -e: usi- + verb root + -e. This is different from most other negative forms you have studied, which do not change the final vowel of an -a verb. Always check the final vowel when forming a negative command.

Common Mistake — Using the singular command form for a group

Error: Geuka kulia when addressing several people

Correction: Geukeni kulia. — When addressing more than one person, add -eni to the verb stem. The singular command form (geuka) is appropriate only when speaking to one person. This distinction matters in Swahili even when English "turn right" does not change between one listener and several.

Common Mistake — Adding -ni to every place noun

Error: benkini (adding -ni to benki by analogy with sokoni)

Correction: benki — Not every place noun takes the locative suffix -ni productively. Soko becomes sokoni and nyumba becomes nyumbani, but benki is typically used as benki or with a preposition such as katika benki. Learn the locative behavior of each noun individually rather than applying -ni automatically.

Common Mistake — Treating kwenda as a fully regular verb

Error: Ninakwenda sokoni. (applying the regular pattern mechanically)

Correction: Ninaenda sokoni. — In ordinary present-tense speech, kwenda (to go) is commonly used as naenda, unaenda, anaenda, and so on, dropping the kw- of the infinitive. Learn this irregular pattern directly rather than assuming every infinitive behaves the same way in the present tense.

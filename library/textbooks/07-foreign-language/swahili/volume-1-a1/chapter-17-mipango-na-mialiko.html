Mipango na Mialiko

Plans and Invitations · Speak and Write Swahili: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Discuss future events, personal goals, and intentions using the future marker -ta- across all persons, affirmative and negative.
  2. Use kutaka to express wants and intentions in planning contexts.
  3. Use tunaweza and related suggestion frames to propose an activity.
  4. Invite someone to an event or activity and respond to an invitation — accepting, declining, or proposing an alternative.
  5. Confirm an arrangement by stating activity, time, and place together.
  6. Revise a plan politely when circumstances change.
  7. Use object markers inside future-tense verbs in planning exchanges.
  8. Contrast present, past, and future time reference within a single connected exchange.
  9. Write an invitation and a response that confirms the details of a plan.

Communication Scenario

A classmate from your Swahili course is organizing a small gathering before the term ends. She sends a message inviting you and a few others to meet for a meal and to talk about summer plans. You need to read her invitation, decide whether you can attend, and reply — either accepting and confirming the time and place, or declining politely and suggesting another day. Over the course of the week, plans change: someone cannot come at the original time, and the group must agree on a revised arrangement. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to invite, respond, and negotiate a plan entirely in Swahili.

  1. Think about how you make plans in your own language. Do you state a time first and then an activity, or the other way around? Notice how Swahili speakers in this chapter's dialogue and readings organize a plan: activity, then time, then place, confirmed together.
  2. In Chapter 14 you learned to state a single future action with -ta-. In this chapter, you will use future forms across a full conversation — proposing, accepting, declining, and revising — while also drawing on present and past forms you already know. Watch for how the tense marker, not the time word alone, tells the listener when something happens.

Pronunciation and Spelling

Mkazo wa sauti katika mialiko na majibu — Intonation in invitations and responses

An invitation offered as a genuine question rises gently at the end, inviting a response: Unaweza kuja? A confident acceptance falls smoothly: Ndiyo, nitakuja. A polite decline often begins with a softened, slightly lower opening — Samahani… — before the reason follows. Practice the contrast between an inviting rise and a settled, falling confirmation.

Mkazo katika vitenzi vya wakati ujao — Stress in future and suggestion forms

Stress remains on the second-to-last syllable even as verbs grow longer with the future marker and object markers attached. Divide and practice these forms aloud.

ni-ta-ku-ta-NA (nitakutana)  ·  tu-na-WE-za (tunaweza)  ·  ni-ta-ku-PI-gi-a (nitakupigia)  ·  tu-ta-o-NA-na (tutaonana)  ·  ha-ta-ku-JA (hatakuja)

Tarehe na saa — Dates and times

Review the pronunciation of days of the week (Chapter 7) and clock-time expressions when reading arrangement details aloud. Pause briefly between the day, the time, and the place when confirming a plan, so that each piece of information is clear to the listener: Jumamosi — saa tatu — mkahawani.

Uakifishaji katika ujumbe ulioandikwa — Punctuation in written messages

Written invitations and replies use standard sentence punctuation and, in informal messages, an exclamation point to convey warmth: Karibu sana! Use a question mark for genuine invitations phrased as questions, and a comma to separate the day, time, and place when they appear together in one sentence: Tuonane Jumamosi, saa tatu, mkahawani.

Grammar Instruction

17.1 Future -ta- across all persons: affirmative and negative

Purpose: discuss and negate future plans and predictions with full person agreement.

In Chapter 14 you learned to build single future statements with the marker -ta-. This chapter consolidates that pattern across all persons and adds the negative future, which you will need constantly when making, accepting, and declining plans.

PersonAffirmative futureNegative futureMeaning
mimi (I)nitakujasitakujaI will / will not come
wewe (you sg.)utakujahutakujayou will / will not come
yeye (he/she)atakujahatakujahe/she will / will not come
sisi (we)tutakujahatutakujawe will / will not come
ninyi (you pl.)mtakujahamtakujayou (pl.) will / will not come
wao (they)watakujahawatakujathey will / will not come
Language Note — Future negation keeps -ta-

Present negation (Chapter 7) drops -na- entirely and often changes the final vowel: sisomi (I do not study). Future negation works differently: the tense marker -ta- stays in the verb, and only the subject marker changes to its negative form — si-, hu-, ha-, hatu-, ham-, hawa-. Compare: Ninasoma → Sisomi (present) but Nitasoma → Sitasoma (future). Do not apply the present-negative pattern to future verbs.

17.2 kutaka for wants and intentions

Purpose: state what you want to do when discussing a plan.

kutaka (to want) was introduced in Chapter 8 for food preferences. In planning contexts, it combines with an infinitive complement, a pattern you practiced in Chapter 14.

  • Nataka kwenda kwenye tamasha. — I want to go to the festival. na-taka (I want) + kwenda (to go) + kwenye tamasha (to the festival)
  • Anataka kupanga safari. — He/she wants to plan a trip. a-na-taka (he/she wants) + kupanga (to plan) + safari (trip)
  • Hatutaki kuahirisha mkutano. — We do not want to postpone the meeting. hatu-taki (we do not want, present negative) + kuahirisha (to postpone) + mkutano (meeting)

17.3 tunaweza and suggestion frames

Purpose: propose an activity, time, or place using a shared, non-committal frame.

Tunaweza ("we can") uses the ability verb -weza from Chapter 13 in the present tense to make a suggestion sound collaborative rather than demanding. The same pattern works with other subjects to ask whether someone is available.

  • Tunaweza kukutana kesho. — We can meet tomorrow. tu-na-weza (we can) + kukutana (to meet) + kesho (tomorrow)
  • Je, unaweza kuja Jumamosi? — Can you come on Saturday? je (question particle) + u-na-weza (you can) + kuja (to come) + Jumamosi (Saturday)
  • Tunaweza kwenda mahali pengine. — We can go somewhere else. tu-na-weza (we can) + kwenda (to go) + mahali pengine (somewhere else)

17.4 Polite invitation and response patterns

Purpose: invite someone, accept, decline, and propose an alternative with appropriate courtesy.

Swahili invitations range from a direct statement of intention to a more deferential question. Learn the following controlled frames as complete communicative units.

  • Nakualika kwenye karamu yangu. — I invite you to my party. ni-na-ku-alik-a (I invite you) — object marker -ku- refers to "you"
  • Je, unaweza kuja? — Can you come? polite invitation phrased as a question
  • Ndiyo, nitakuja. Asante kwa mwaliko. — Yes, I will come. Thank you for the invitation. acceptance + fixed courtesy expression
  • Samahani, siwezi kuja. Nina shughuli nyingine. — Sorry, I can't come. I have another commitment. samahani (sorry) + si-wezi (I cannot, present negative) + reason
  • Kwa bahati mbaya, sitaweza kuja Jumamosi. Tunaweza kukutana siku nyingine? — Unfortunately, I won't be able to come Saturday. Can we meet another day? decline + softening phrase + alternative suggestion
Language Note — A fixed proposal pattern: "Tukutane…"

When two people have already agreed to meet and are settling the details, Swahili speakers commonly use a short proposal form such as Tukutane saa kumi ("Let's meet at four o'clock"). This form belongs to the subjunctive mood, which is not formally taught until a later volume. Learn tukutane and similar fixed proposal expressions as ready-made communicative units for confirming a time, without analyzing their internal grammar yet.

17.5 Confirming an arrangement: activity, time, and place

Purpose: state a complete arrangement so that both people share the same understanding.

A plan is only settled once the activity, the time, and the place have all been confirmed. Swahili speakers typically state these three pieces of information together, often in that order.

Language Building Block
proposal + response + arrangement
Tunaweza kukutana kesho.
We can meet tomorrow. — proposal using tunaweza
Ndiyo, tukutane saa kumi.

Tuonane mkahawani, saa kumi, Jumamosi.

17.6 Object markers in future-tense planning verbs

Purpose: use person and class object markers inside future verbs to refer to people already mentioned in a plan.

Object marking, introduced in Chapter 8 and expanded in Chapters 9, 13, and 14, is especially frequent in planning language, since plans usually involve doing something to, for, or with another person.

  • Nitakupigia simu kesho. — I will call you (on the phone) tomorrow. ni-ta-ku-pigi-a (I will call you) — object marker -ku- = "you"
  • Tutawaalika kwenye sherehe. — We will invite them to the celebration. tu-ta-wa-alik-a (we will invite them) — object marker -wa- = "them," referring to M-WA/animate plural
  • Atanikumbuka. — He/she will remember me. a-ta-ni-kumbuk-a (he/she will remember me) — object marker -ni- = "me"

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: Nadia anampigia simu Kofi kumwalika kwenye karamu ndogo kabla ya likizo. Wanapanga siku, saa, na mahali.

(Setting: Nadia calls Kofi to invite him to a small party before the break. They arrange a day, time, and place.)

NADIAHabari, Kofi! Nakualika kwenye karamu yangu wiki ijayo.Hello, Kofi! I'm inviting you to my party next week.
KOFIAsante kwa mwaliko! Ni siku gani?Thank you for the invitation! What day is it?
NADIATunaweza kukutana Jumamosi. Utaweza kuja?We can meet on Saturday. Will you be able to come?
KOFIKwa bahati mbaya, sitaweza kuja Jumamosi asubuhi. Nina miadi na daktari.Unfortunately, I won't be able to come Saturday morning. I have an appointment with a doctor.
NADIAHakuna tatizo. Karamu itaanza jioni, saa mbili.No problem. The party will start in the evening, at eight o'clock.
KOFIVizuri sana! Nitakuja jioni basi. Mahali gani?Very good! I'll come in the evening then. What place?
NADIANyumbani kwangu, karibu na chuo. Nitakutumia ramani.At my house, near the college. I'll send you a map.
KOFIAsante. Nani mwingine atakuja?Thank you. Who else will come?
NADIANitawaalika Amara na Tariq pia. Tutakuwa watu wanane hivi.I'll invite Amara and Tariq too. We'll be about eight people.

(Siku chache baadaye, Kofi anampigia simu Nadia tena. / A few days later, Kofi calls Nadia again.)

KOFINadia, samahani kukusumbua. Naweza kubadilisha mpango kidogo?Nadia, sorry to bother you. Can I change the plan a little?
NADIABila shaka. Kuna tatizo gani?Of course. What's the problem?
KOFIMiadi yangu na daktari imebadilika. Sasa nitaweza kufika mapema — saa moja badala ya saa mbili.My appointment with the doctor has changed. Now I'll be able to arrive earlier — seven o'clock instead of eight.
NADIAHiyo ni sawa kabisa! Tutaonana saa moja basi, nyumbani kwangu.That's perfectly fine! We'll see each other at seven then, at my house.
KOFISawa. Tuonane Jumamosi jioni!Okay. See you Saturday evening!

Vocabulary note: hakuna tatizo = no problem; basi = then, so (discourse connector); samahani kukusumbua = sorry to bother you; bila shaka = of course, without doubt; imebadilika = has changed (perfect -me-, recognition item previewing Chapter 19); badala ya = instead of. These are recognition items at this stage.

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension questions:

  1. Nadia anamwalika Kofi kwenye nini? (What does Nadia invite Kofi to?)
  2. Kwa nini Kofi hawezi kuja Jumamosi asubuhi? (Why can't Kofi come Saturday morning?)
  3. Karamu itaanza saa ngapi, na wapi? (What time will the party start, and where?)
  4. Ni watu wangapi watakaokuwepo karamuni, hivi karibuni? (About how many people will be at the party?)
  5. Nini kilibadilika kwenye mazungumzo ya pili? (What changed in the second conversation?)

  1. Soma mfano. Reread Maandishi 1 and Maandishi 2 above. Underline every future-tense verb. Circle the sentences that state activity, time, and place together.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • Every future verb uses the correct subject marker and keeps -ta-, including in the negative.
  • The invitation clearly states activity, day, time, and place.
  • A decline includes a polite softening expression, such as kwa bahati mbaya or samahani.
  • Object markers are used correctly when referring to a person already mentioned.
  • Courtesy expressions — asante, tafadhali — appear where appropriate.
  • Each sentence ends with correct punctuation and begins with a capital letter.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Punctuality and flexibility

Attitudes toward punctuality and timing vary across settings, professions, and individuals in Swahili-speaking communities, just as they do elsewhere. A formal business appointment or a scheduled examination typically calls for arriving on time, while a social gathering among friends may allow more flexibility about the exact start time. Neither approach should be presented as the single "Swahili" or "East African" way of managing time — context, relationship, and setting all matter.

Culture in Context — Extending and receiving hospitality

Invitations to a home, meal, or celebration are often accompanied by warmth and generosity, and guests are commonly encouraged to eat, stay, or participate more than they initially planned. At the same time, individuals and households vary widely in how they extend and receive hospitality, and learners should not assume that every gathering follows an identical pattern. Expressing genuine thanks — asante sana — for an invitation, whether accepted or declined, is broadly appropriate.

Culture in Context — Declining an invitation tactfully

A direct, unexplained "no" can sound abrupt in many social contexts. Softening expressions such as kwa bahati mbaya (unfortunately) or samahani (sorry), paired with a brief reason and, where appropriate, a proposed alternative, are common ways to decline while preserving the relationship. This is a general tendency rather than an absolute rule, and directness is also valued in many settings, particularly among close friends or in fast-moving practical arrangements.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Applying present-negative patterns to future verbs

Error: Sikuji (intended as "I will not come," modeled on present-negative -i endings)

Correction: Sitakuja — Future negation keeps the tense marker -ta- and only changes the subject marker to its negative form. Present-tense negative patterns, which drop -na- and often change the final vowel, do not apply to the future. Always check which tense you are negating before choosing a pattern.

Common Mistake — Confirming a time without an activity or place

Error: Saa mbili. (stated alone as if it settles the entire plan)

Correction: Tutakutana saa mbili, mkahawani. — A time alone does not confirm a plan. State the activity or verb, the time, and the place together so both people share the same understanding, following the pattern practiced in this chapter's Language Building Block.

Common Mistake — Declining without any softening language

Error: Sitaweza kuja. (used alone, with no acknowledgment of the invitation)

Correction: Asante kwa mwaliko, lakini kwa bahati mbaya sitaweza kuja. — A bare decline can sound abrupt. Acknowledge the invitation, use a softening expression, and, where appropriate, offer a brief reason or an alternative. This is a matter of pragmatics, not grammatical correctness — the shorter sentence is not "wrong," but it is less courteous in most social contexts.

Common Mistake — Misplacing the object marker in a future verb

Error: Nitapigia kuku simu (object marker omitted or placed incorrectly, producing an unclear sentence)

Correction: Nitakupigia simu. — The object marker occupies a fixed position: subject marker + tense marker + object marker + verb root + final vowel. In ni-ta-ku-pigi-a, the object marker -ku- ("you") sits directly before the verb root, not after the complete verb.

Common Mistake — Treating every time expression as requiring the future marker

Error: Assuming that any sentence containing kesho (tomorrow) must automatically be correct without checking the verb

Correction: A time expression like kesho signals future meaning, but the verb itself must still carry the tense marker -ta-: Kesho nitakuja, not Kesho ninakuja if genuine future time is intended. Tense marking on the verb, not the time word alone, carries the primary grammatical information in Swahili.

Kupanga Mkutano wa Kikundi — Negotiating a Group Plan

Work with two or three partners (or take multiple roles independently) to negotiate a group plan for an event of your choosing — a meal, a trip, or a celebration. Then complete the following tasks:

  • Speaking: Propose the event using tunaweza, invite the group, and negotiate a day, time, and place until everyone agrees. Include at least one instance of declining an original suggestion and proposing an alternative.
  • Interaction: Each participant must confirm the arrangement using a complete statement of activity, time, and place.
  • Writing: Submit a written version of the final arrangement — six to eight sentences — as a message you might send to confirm the plan with everyone involved. Apply the editing checklist from the Writing Workshop section before submitting.

If you are studying independently: role-play both the invitation and at least one response, including one revision to the original plan, recording yourself in each role. In your written version, include the original proposal, the change, and the final confirmed arrangement.

Dialogue Comprehension

  1. Nadia anamwalika Kofi kwenye karamu yake wiki ijayo. (Nadia invites Kofi to her party next week.)
  2. Kofi hawezi kuja Jumamosi asubuhi kwa sababu ana miadi na daktari. (Kofi can't come Saturday morning because he has a doctor's appointment.)
  3. Karamu itaanza saa mbili jioni, nyumbani kwa Nadia. (The party will start at eight in the evening, at Nadia's house.)
  4. Watakuwa watu wanane hivi. (There will be about eight people.)
  5. Miadi ya Kofi ilibadilika, kwa hiyo ataweza kufika mapema zaidi — saa moja badala ya saa mbili. (Kofi's appointment changed, so he'll be able to arrive earlier — seven instead of eight.)

Listening Responses

Kazi A (Mazungumzo A): Proposed activity — kukutana kuzungumzia mradi (meeting to discuss the project); two options offered — saa nne asubuhi ofisini, au mkahawani karibu na chuo; Peter is asked to look at — mipango (plans).

Kazi B (Mazungumzo B): Declined — Ijumaa (Friday); accepted — Jumamosi (Saturday); confirmed time/place — saa tatu, langoni (at the gate).

Kazi C (Mazungumzo C): Original time — saa mbili; new time — saa nne; the speaker promises — kupiga simu akimaliza (to call once finished).

Reading Comprehension

  1. Amara anawaalika marafiki zake kwenye sherehe ndogo nyumbani kwake, mwishoni mwa wiki ijayo, Jumamosi saa mbili jioni. (Amara invites her friends to a small party at her house, next weekend, Saturday at eight in the evening.)
  2. Kofi atakuja saa mbili jioni. (Kofi will come at eight in the evening.)
  3. Tariq hataweza kuja kwa sababu ana safari ya kazi wiki hiyo. (Tariq can't come because he has a work trip that week.)
  4. Accept any two correctly identified future verbs, e.g.: tutakutana (tu- subject marker); kutakuwa (impersonal ku- subject marker, recognition); niweze (subjunctive, recognition item — not required).
  5. atakupigia — object marker -ku- refers to Amara ("you," the letter's recipient).

Chapter Review — Sehemu B

Question 1 (future forms):

  • Nitafika / Sitafika
  • Atakualika / Hatakualika
  • Mtasafiri / Hamtasafiri

Question 2 (object markers): Nitawaona kesho. Tutakualika. Atatupigia simu.

Question 3 (error correction):

  • Sikuji Jumamosi — Error: present-negative pattern used with future meaning. Correction: Sitakuja Jumamosi.
  • Tutaonana. Saa ngapi? Mahali gani? — Missing: a stated time and place to make the plan complete, e.g., Tutaonana saa mbili, mkahawani.

Question 4: False. Future negation keeps the tense marker -ta- and only changes the subject marker to its negative form; present negation drops -na- and often changes the final vowel. The two patterns are different.

Question 5: Tunaweza kukutana kesho mkahawani.

Looking Ahead

Unachojua sasa — What you know now

You can now discuss future plans and personal goals with full control of person agreement in both affirmative and negative future forms. You have learned to invite someone, accept, decline politely, and negotiate an alternative, and you can confirm a plan by stating activity, time, and place together. You have also practiced placing object markers inside future verbs, and you have contrasted future negation with the present-negative patterns learned earlier. These planning tools will support the connected narration and comparison you begin in the next two chapters.

Sura ya 18: Utamaduni na Mila — Culture and Traditions

Chapter 18 shifts from planning ahead to discussing holidays, celebrations, arts, and traditions. You will learn to compare customs and experiences using kuliko, zaidi, and sawa na, and to describe how often activities occur using frequency expressions such as mara nyingi and kila. Adjective and possessive agreement will expand further as you build longer, comparative descriptions, and you will practice integrating present, past, and future forms within a single cultural description.

Nyumbani

Around the Home · Speak and Write Swahili: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name rooms, furniture, and common household items using M-MI class nouns alongside the M-WA, KI-VI, and N-class nouns you already know.
  2. Use kuna to state the general existence of something in a place.
  3. Use kuwa na to say what a home, room, or person has.
  4. Distinguish kuna, kuwa na, and ni and choose the appropriate construction for each communicative purpose.
  5. Express location using common phrases with katika, juu ya, chini ya, and karibu na.
  6. Use near, referential, and far demonstratives for M-MI nouns and review those already taught for M-WA and KI-VI.
  7. Apply possessive agreement across M-WA, KI-VI, N-class, and M-MI nouns.
  8. Recognize that nouns beginning with m- may belong to either M-WA (animate) or M-MI (inanimate) class, and that agreement — not prefix shape — determines which class a noun belongs to.
  9. Pronounce the syllabic nasal m correctly in words such as mti and mbu.
  10. Write a descriptive paragraph of 80–100 words about a room or home.

Communication Scenario

You have just arrived in Moshi, Tanzania, and are staying with a host family for three weeks. Your host, Mama Rehema, wants to show you around the house so you will feel at home. She points to each room, names the objects in it, and tells you where things are kept. Later, your new host-sibling Bupe asks you what your home is like back in your own country. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to describe any home — room by room, object by object — in Swahili.

  1. Think about the rooms in your own home or the home you grew up in. How many rooms does it have? What is in each room? As you work through this chapter, build a mental picture of that space so you have real content for your speaking and writing tasks.
  2. You already know that Swahili nouns belong to classes that control agreement. In this chapter you meet the M-MI class, which includes trees, plants, and many household objects. Notice that some M-MI singular nouns start with m-, just like M-WA nouns — but their agreement is completely different. The class label is the guide, not the prefix alone.

Pronunciation and Spelling

M kama silabi — The syllabic nasal m

In Swahili, the letter m can stand alone as a complete syllable before a consonant, without any vowel beside it. This happens in certain noun class prefixes and in common words. English speakers often try to add a vowel — saying something like em-ti for mti — but the m alone carries the syllable.

WordSyllable divisionMeaning
mtim · titree
mtum · tuperson
mkekam · ke · kamat
mlangom · lan · godoor
mtom · topillow (also: river)
mbum · bumosquito
mwambamwam · barock; cliff
Sound Focus — Syllabic m before a consonant

To practice the syllabic m, hold the m sound briefly — lips closed — and then release it directly into the next consonant without opening your mouth for a separate vowel. The word mti has exactly two syllables: m (closed lips) and ti. mtu also has two syllables: m and tu. Compare these with mama (three syllables: ma · ma), where the m is followed by a vowel and functions as a normal consonant. Practicing minimal pairs — mti versus miti — will help you hear and produce this distinction clearly.

Grammar Instruction

6.1 The M-MI class — nouns of the natural and made world

Purpose: recognize and use M-MI class nouns and their agreement patterns alongside the M-WA, KI-VI, and N-class nouns you already know.

The M-MI class is a major Swahili noun class whose singular nouns typically carry the prefix m- or mw- and whose plurals carry the prefix mi-. Trees, plants, and many physical objects — furniture, tools, implements — belong to this class. The M-MI class does not refer to people or animals except in a few special cases; its nouns are generally inanimate.

The critical difference between M-MI and M-WA nouns is visible in the plural: M-WA plurals use wa-, while M-MI plurals use mi-.

Singular (m-/mw-)Plural (mi-)Meaning
mtimititree / trees
mlangomilangodoor / doors
mkekamikekamat / mats
mtomitopillow; cushion / pillows; cushions
mshumaamishumaacandle / candles
mfukomifukobag / bags
mmeamimeaplant / plants
mwambamiambarock / rocks
Language Note — M-MI agreement differs from M-WA even when the singular prefix looks the same

Because some M-MI and M-WA singular nouns both begin with m-, learners sometimes apply the wrong agreement. The safest check is the plural form: if the plural uses mi-, all agreement words use the M-MI pattern. If the plural uses wa-, it is M-WA. You cannot determine a noun's class from its singular prefix alone — you must learn the plural alongside the singular.

6.2 Demonstrative agreement with M-MI nouns

Purpose: point to specific trees, plants, furniture, and household items using demonstratives for the M-MI class.

You already know near demonstratives for M-WA (huyu/hawa) and KI-VI (hiki/hivi) nouns. Here are the demonstratives for M-MI nouns. Swahili distinguishes three reference points: near the speaker, not immediately near (referential), and far from the speaker.

ReferenceM-MI SingularM-MI PluralExample (singular)
Near (this / these) huu hii Huu mti ni mkubwa. — This tree is large.
Referential (this / these — already mentioned) huo hiyo Huo mlango ni mpya. — That door (we were discussing) is new.
Far (that / those) ule ile Ule mti ni mrefu sana. — That tree (over there) is very tall.

For comparison, here is a summary of demonstrative forms across the noun classes active in this chapter:

ClassNear Sg.Near Pl.Referential Sg.Far Sg.
M-WA (animate) huyuhawahuyoyule
KI-VI hikihivihichokile

The far demonstrative ule is shared by M-MI singular and M-WA singular in many contexts. The noun and context make the intended class clear. Focus on learning the near forms first — huu/hii — as these are the most immediately useful for describing your surroundings.

6.3 Possessive agreement with M-MI nouns

Purpose: say "my door," "your mat," "her candle" — extending the possessive system to the M-MI class.

In Chapter 4, you learned possessive agreement for M-WA nouns (concord w-) and N-class nouns (concord y-). The possessive stems — -angu, -ako, -ake, -etu, -enu, -ao — remain exactly the same. Only the concord prefix before the stem changes.

For M-MI singular nouns, the possessive concord is w-:

Possessive formMeaningExample

wakoyour (M-MI sg.)mkeka wako — your mat

wetuour (M-MI sg.)mti wetu — our tree
wenuyour pl. (M-MI sg.)mlango wenu — your (pl.) door
waotheir (M-MI sg.)mfuko wao — their bag

For M-MI plural nouns, the possessive concord is y-:

Possessive formMeaningExample
yangumy (M-MI pl.)miti yangu — my trees
yakoyour (M-MI pl.)mikeka yako — your mats
yakehis/her (M-MI pl.)milango yake — his/her doors

yenuyour pl. (M-MI pl.)miti yenu — your (pl.) trees

Language Note — Possessive concord follows the possessed noun's class and number

You now have four possessive concord sets to manage. The concord is always determined by the noun that is possessed — not by who owns it. Here is a quick reference: M-WA singular and M-MI singular both use w- concord; M-WA plural and N-class singular both use w- concord; N-class singular uses y- concord; M-MI plural uses y- concord; KI-VI singular uses ch- concord. When you are unsure, check the class and number of the noun being possessed. The stem — -angu, -ako, etc. — never changes.

6.4 Adjective agreement with M-MI nouns

Purpose: describe household objects using adjectives with correct M-MI agreement.

Adjective agreement follows a pattern consistent with what you have already learned. For M-MI nouns:

M-MI singular: adjective stems take the prefix m- or mw- before a vowel — the same prefix shape as M-WA singular. Context and the noun itself clarify whether an animate or inanimate noun is being described.

  • mlango mkubwa — a large door M-MI singular noun + m- + adjective stem -kubwa
  • mti mrefu — a tall tree M-MI singular noun + m- + adjective stem -refu
  • mkeka mzuri — a beautiful mat M-MI singular noun + m- + adjective stem -zuri

M-MI plural: adjective stems take the prefix mi-:

  • milango mikubwa — large doors M-MI plural noun + mi- + adjective stem -kubwa
  • miti mirefu — tall trees M-MI plural noun + mi- + adjective stem -refu
  • mishumaa mizuri — beautiful candles M-MI plural noun + mi- + adjective stem -zuri

6.5 Kuna — expressing existence in a place

Purpose: say there is a table in the room, there are trees in the garden — using kuna for general existence.

Kuna is an invariant existential expression. It does not change to agree with any noun class, number, person, or tense in its basic form. It means "there is" or "there are." It introduces a noun into the conversation, often alongside a location phrase.

  • Kuna meza jikoni. — There is a table in the kitchen. kuna + meza (N-class noun) + jikoni (in the kitchen)
  • Kuna viti vinne sebuleni. — There are four chairs in the living room. kuna + viti (KI-VI pl.) + vinne (four) + sebuleni
  • Kuna miti mingi bustanini. — There are many trees in the garden. kuna + miti (M-MI pl.) + mingi (many, M-MI agreement) + bustanini
  • Hakuna jokofu hapa. — There is no refrigerator here. hakuna (negative of kuna) + jokofu + hapa (here)
Language Note — kuna introduces new or indefinite information

Kuna is best used when you are introducing something for the first time or asserting that something exists in a general place. It is the equivalent of "there is / there are" in a neutral, informational sense. Compare: Kuna meza jikoni — There is a table in the kitchen (introducing the table). Once the table is known to both speakers, you might use a different structure to say where it is specifically. At this stage, kuna for general existence and hakuna for absence are your productive forms.

Language Building Block
kuna + noun + location phrase
Kuna meza jikoni.
There is a table in the kitchen.
Kuna miti mitatu bustanini.
There are three trees in the garden.
Hakuna kitanda chumba hiki.
There is no bed in this room.

6.6 Kuwa na — expressing what a home or person has

Purpose: say what a house has, what a room contains, or what a person owns — using kuwa na with person subject markers.

You met kuwa na in Chapter 4 to express having family members. The same construction applies to houses, rooms, and possessions. Here the controlled subject markers are the same ones you learned in Chapter 3.

  • Nyumba yetu ina vyumba vitano. — Our house has five rooms. nyumba yetu (our house, N-class) + i-na (it has) + vyumba (KI-VI pl.) + vitano (five) Subject marker i- agrees with N-class noun nyumba. This is a preview of N-class subject marking; learn it as a formulaic expression now.
  • Nina meza jikoni mwangu. — I have a table in my kitchen. ni-na (I have) + meza + jikoni mwangu
  • Nyumba hii haina bustani. — This house does not have a garden. nyumba hii + hai-na (it does not have) + bustani
  • Una chumba cha kulala kikubwa? — Do you have a large bedroom? u-na (you have) + chumba cha kulala (bedroom) + kikubwa (large, KI-VI sg.)

The subject markers i- (for nouns) and hai- (negative) in ina / haina are part of the noun-class subject-marker system taught fully in Chapter 7. At this stage, learn nyumba ina and nyumba haina as formulaic patterns for describing what a house has or lacks.

6.7 Locative expressions — saying where something is

Purpose: place objects in rooms and describe what is near, above, below, or inside a space.

The location expressions in your vocabulary list — juu ya, chini ya, karibu na, katika, and others — combine with a noun phrase to state where something is. They do not change to agree with noun classes. Place them after kuna or after the thing being located.

  • Kitabu kipo juu ya meza. — The book is on the table. kitabu (KI-VI) + kipo (it is located, KI-VI class) + juu ya meza kipo is a recognition item — learn the full sentence as a formula now.
  • Kuna kiti karibu na mlango. — There is a chair near the door. kuna + kiti + karibu na + mlango
  • Mfuko wako uko chini ya kitanda. — Your bag is under the bed. mfuko wako + uko (it is there) + chini ya kitanda uko is a recognition item — learn as a formula.
  • Kuna maua mbele ya nyumba. — There are flowers in front of the house. kuna + maua + mbele ya + nyumba
Language Note — Locative expressions are not noun-class agreement words

The location phrases juu ya, chini ya, karibu na, katika, and the others in your vocabulary list are relational phrases that work like English prepositions. They do not change to match the class or number of the noun they follow. You can combine them freely with any noun: juu ya meza, juu ya kitanda, juu ya mlango. The noun after ya remains in its ordinary form. This makes location phrases among the most portable tools in Swahili — learn them once and use them everywhere.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: Amara amefika nyumba ya familia ya Moshi. Mama Rehema anaonyesha nyumba yao. Wanazungumza sebuleni.

(Setting: Amara has arrived at a host family's home in Moshi. Mama Rehema is showing her around the house. They are talking in the living room.)

MAMA REHEMAKaribu nyumbani, Amara! Hii ni nyumba yetu. Inafurahi kukupokea.Welcome to our home, Amara! This is our house. It is happy to receive you.
AMARAAsante sana, Mama Rehema. Nyumba yenu ni nzuri sana!Thank you very much, Mama Rehema. Your house is very beautiful!
MAMA REHEMAAsante. Hii ni sebule yetu. Kuna sofa mbili na meza moja hapa. Kiti hiki ni kizuri — ni kipya.Thank you. This is our living room. There are two sofas and one table here. This chair is nice — it is new.
AMARANi kizuri sana. Na hii nyumba ina vyumba vingapi?It is very nice. And how many rooms does this house have?
MAMA REHEMAIna vyumba vitano. Kuna vyumba vitatu vya kulala, jikoni moja, na choo kimoja.It has five rooms. There are three bedrooms, one kitchen, and one bathroom.

(Wanaenda jikoni. / They go to the kitchen.)

MAMA REHEMAHii ni jikoni yetu. Kuna sufuria nyingi na vyombo vingi hapa. Jokofu liko pembeni mwa ukuta.This is our kitchen. There are many pots and many utensils here. The refrigerator is next to the wall.
AMARAJikoni yenu ni safi sana. Na mti mkubwa ule nje ya nyumba — ni mti gani?Your kitchen is very clean. And that large tree outside the house — what kind of tree is it?
MAMA REHEMAHuo ni mti wa mwembe — mti wa maembe. Miti mirefu ile nyuma ya nyumba ni miti ya ndizi.That is a mango tree. Those tall trees behind the house are banana trees.
AMARABustani yenu ina miti mingi! Nyumba yenu ni ya kupendeza.Your garden has many trees! Your house is lovely.
MAMA REHEMAAsante. Sasa, chumba chako cha kulala kiko hapa. Kuna kitanda kimoja, mkeka mzuri, na mto laini juu ya kitanda.Thank you. Now, your bedroom is here. There is one bed, a nice mat, and a soft pillow on the bed.
AMARAAsante sana, Mama Rehema. Chumba hiki ni kizuri. Nitafurahi hapa.Thank you very much, Mama Rehema. This room is nice. I will be happy here.

Vocabulary note: inafurahi kukupokea = it is happy to receive you (a warm welcome formula — recognition item); mwembe = mango tree; maembe = mangoes; ndizi = banana; ya kupendeza = lovely, charming; laini = soft; ukuta = wall; nitafurahi = I will be happy (future tense — recognition item at this stage).

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension questions:

  1. Nyumba ya familia ina vyumba vingapi? Vitaje. (How many rooms does the house have? Name them.)
  2. Kuna nini jikoni? Toa mifano miwili. (What is in the kitchen? Give two examples.)
  3. Mti mkubwa nje ya nyumba ni mti gani? (What kind of tree is the large tree outside the house?)
  4. Chumba cha Amara kina nini? (What does Amara's room have?)
  5. Find the phrase "your garden" in the dialogue. Write it out. Which possessive form is used and why?

Maandishi 1 — Nyumba yangu — A description of a home

Jina langu ni Zawadi. Ninaishi Dar es Salaam na familia yangu. Nyumba yetu ni ya kati — si kubwa sana, si ndogo sana. Ina vyumba vinne: vyumba viwili vya kulala, jikoni, na sebule.

Sebule yetu ina sofa moja kubwa na viti vitatu. Kuna meza ndogo karibu na sofa. Taa yetu ya sebuleni ni nzuri — iko juu ya meza. Milango ya nyumba yetu ni mipya na mizuri. Kuna maua mengi pembeni mwa mlango mkubwa wa mbele.

Bustani yetu ndogo ina miti miwili: mti mmoja wa mnazi na mti mmoja wa mwembe. Kuna mimea mingi pia. Ninapenda bustani yangu sana — ni mahali pazuri pa kukaa asubuhi.

Vocabulary note: ninaishi = I live; ya kati = medium-sized; ya mbele = at the front; mnazi = coconut palm; mahali pazuri = a beautiful place; pa kukaa = to sit/stay; asubuhi = in the morning. These are recognition items.

Maandishi 2 — Ujumbe wa WhatsApp (A WhatsApp message)

Habari yako, Kofi!

Nina habari nzuri! Niko nyumba mpya sasa. Nyumba yangu ina chumba kimoja cha kulala, jikoni ndogo, na sebule ndogo. Sebule yangu haina sofa bado — nina kiti kimoja tu na mkeka wangu wa zamani. Lakini chumba changu cha kulala kina kitanda kipya! Kuna mto mzuri laini juu ya kitanda na mikeka miwili mizuri chini.

Bustani yangu ina mti mmoja mkubwa na mimea midogo. Ninafurahi sana hapa. Kuja kutembelea hivi karibuni!

Kwa heri,
Amara

Vocabulary note: wa zamani = old; lakini = but; kuja kutembelea = come to visit; hivi karibuni = soon. These are recognition items.

Writing Workshop

  1. Soma mfano. Reread Maandishi 1 (Nyumba yangu). Underline every M-MI noun. Circle every location expression. Put a box around every possessive form.
  2. Chora mpango wa nyumba yako. Draw a simple floor plan of a real or imagined home. Label at least four rooms in Swahili. Then, for each room, list three to four objects that are in it, noting their noun class.
  3. Andika rasimu yako ya kwanza. Write eight to ten connected Swahili sentences describing your home. Include: at least two rooms, at least three objects using correct noun classes, at least two location expressions, one use of kuna, and one use of kuwa na. Use an adjective for at least two objects.
  4. Kagua kwa orodha hii. Check your draft against the editing checklist before writing a final version.
  5. Andika nakala safi. Write a clean final version of 80–100 words to include in your portfolio.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • Every M-MI singular noun is paired with its m-/mw- prefix form; every M-MI plural uses mi-.
  • Adjective prefixes match the noun class and number: M-MI singular uses m-, M-MI plural uses mi-, KI-VI singular uses ki-, KI-VI plural uses vi-.
  • Demonstratives match the noun class and number.
  • Possessive concords are correct: check whether the noun is singular or plural, then choose the right concord.
  • Kuna is used for existence (there is/are); kuwa na is used for possession (has/have).
  • Location phrases follow the noun they describe or follow kuna.
  • All prenasalized consonants and syllabic m forms are spelled correctly.
  • Each sentence ends with a full stop; each begins with a capital letter.

Using your floor plan from the Writing Workshop (or a simple sketch), point to each room and name it in Swahili. For each room, say one sentence using kuna to describe what is in it. Your partner listens and draws or notes down what you describe, then reads it back to verify.

Take turns asking and answering these questions. Give full-sentence answers in Swahili.

  1. Nyumba yako ina vyumba vingapi?
  2. Kuna nini sebuleni kwako?
  3. Chumba chako cha kulala kina nini?
  4. Kuna mti bustanini kwako?
  5. Kuna kitu gani karibu na mlango wako mkubwa?

Ziara ya nyumba — Home tour

Imagine you are giving a visitor a tour of your home or a home you know well. Without notes, describe four rooms in connected speech. For each room, mention at least two objects, use one location expression, and include one adjective. Aim for four to five sentences per room.

Mbadala wa kujifunza peke yako — Solo alternative

Walk through your own home or sit in a room with several objects around you. Record yourself naming and describing what you see, using kuna, possessives, adjectives, and location expressions. Listen back and check: Did M-MI nouns have m-/mi- prefixes in the right places? Were demonstratives correct? Were syllabic m words pronounced clearly?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Housing forms across East Africa

Housing in East Africa is diverse, shaped by geography, climate, household size, income, and local building traditions. Urban homes in Dar es Salaam or Nairobi may be apartment flats, small bungalows, or large family compounds. In rural areas, homes may be made from local materials including mud, stone, timber, or coral, and their layout may differ significantly from the room-by-room floor plan familiar to many English speakers. Coastal Swahili architecture, including the intricately carved wooden doors of Zanzibar's Stone Town, reflects centuries of Indian Ocean trade and contact.

The vocabulary in this chapter — kitchen, living room, bedroom, bathroom — represents a functional set for practical communication. Learners should be aware that actual home layouts, the objects in them, and the names used for specific rooms and features vary considerably across communities and generations. Present the vocabulary as a communication tool, not as a single picture of how East African homes are organized.

Culture in Context — Arriving at someone's home

In many Swahili-speaking communities, arriving at someone's home involves a greeting sequence before anything else happens. Hodi! — an announcement of arrival, similar to knocking — is called out at the gate or door. The host responds with Karibu! (come in; welcome). The exchange of greetings, including inquiries about health and the journey, typically precedes any practical conversation. A guest who moves directly to business without greeting can appear rude. The dialogue in this chapter models a welcoming exchange; pay attention to the way Mama Rehema's greeting and the word karibu establish a hospitable tone before the home tour begins.

Regional Variation — Terms for rooms and household items

Some terms for rooms and household items show variation across and within Tanzania and Kenya. sebule (living room) is widely used, but you may also encounter ukumbi in some settings to refer to a hall or reception area. In some households, a covered outdoor veranda — an important living and social space — may be called baraza, particularly in coastal communities, where the baraza has historically been a place for community gathering and conversation. Learners working or traveling in specific settings should listen for local terms and use them when appropriate, while keeping the standard terms taught here as their productive base.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Treating all m- nouns as M-WA (animate)

Error: mlango wazuri (applying M-WA plural adjective prefix to an M-MI noun)

Correction: milango mizuri — mlango is an M-MI class noun. Its plural is milango (not walango), and its adjective prefix in the plural is mi- (not wa-). The prefix m- on the singular does not mean a noun is M-WA. Always check the plural: if it uses mi-, the noun is M-MI. Use M-WA agreement only for nouns whose plural uses wa-.

Common Mistake — Using ni where kuna is needed for existence

Error: Meza ni jikoni. (using ni for location or existence)

Correction: Kuna meza jikoni. — Ni is used to state identity between two noun phrases: Hii ni meza (This is a table). To state that something exists in a place, use kuna: Kuna meza jikoni (There is a table in the kitchen). The two constructions serve different communicative purposes and are not interchangeable.

Common Mistake — Confusing kuna and kuwa na

Error: Using kuna where kuwa na is needed: Kuna vyumba vitano nyumba yetu.

Correction: Nyumba yetu ina vyumba vitano. — Kuna introduces the general existence of something in a place: "there is/are ___." Kuwa na with a subject says what that subject possesses or contains: "our house has ___." When a specific owner or container is the subject, use kuwa na. When you are simply asserting that something is present in a general location, use kuna.

Common Mistake — Adding a vowel to the syllabic m

Error: Pronouncing mti as emti or imti, or mlango as imlango

Correction: mti (two syllables: m + ti) and mlango (three syllables: m + lan + go) — The m before a consonant is a complete syllable in Swahili. It does not need a vowel before it. Producing one adds a syllable that does not exist in the word and may make it unrecognizable. Practice moving directly from the closed-lips nasal into the following consonant.

Common Mistake — Using the wrong possessive concord for M-MI plural nouns

Error: miti wangu (using M-MI singular concord w- for a plural noun)

Correction: miti yangu — M-MI plural nouns use the concord y- for possessives. M-MI singular nouns use w-. So: mti wangu (my tree, singular) but miti yangu (my trees, plural). This singular-plural shift in the possessive concord is a distinct feature of M-MI nouns that requires explicit attention.

Common Mistake — Applying noun-class agreement to location expressions

Error: Trying to make juu ya agree with a noun: juu wangu ya meza or similar constructions

Correction: juu ya meza — Location phrases such as juu ya, chini ya, karibu na, and katika are invariant relational expressions. They do not change to agree with any noun class. The noun that follows them simply stays in its ordinary citation form: juu ya meza, juu ya kitanda, juu ya mlango — the phrase itself does not alter.

Looking Ahead

Unachojua sasa — What you know now

You can now describe a home in Swahili — room by room, object by object. You have added the M-MI class to your noun-class knowledge, along with its demonstratives, possessives, and adjective agreement. You can use kuna and hakuna to assert or deny the presence of something in a place, and you can use kuwa na to say what a home or person has. You have a set of portable location expressions — juu ya, chini ya, karibu na, katika, and others — that work across all noun classes. And you can read, write, and say the syllabic nasal m that appears in so many Swahili words.

Sura ya 7: Ratiba ya Kila Siku — Daily Routines

Chapter 7 takes the home you have just described and fills it with action. You will learn to describe what you do every day — from waking up to going to sleep — using the full present tense with -na-. You will tell and ask the time, name the days of the week, sequence a routine with words like kwanza, halafu, and baadaye, and say what you do not do using present-tense negation. The noun classes, possessives, and location expressions from this chapter will return as the setting for those everyday activities.

Unit II — Daily Life
7

Ratiba ya Kila Siku

Daily Routines · Speak and Write Swahili: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe your daily activities using the full affirmative present tense with -na-.
  2. Say what you do not do using the present negative with negative subject markers.
  3. Tell and ask the time in both international and Swahili clock conventions.
  4. Name the days of the week and common parts of the day.
  5. Use the infinitive ku- after common verbs and as the base form for new verbs.
  6. Sequence activities using kwanza, halafu, and baadaye.
  7. Understand that a single Swahili verb carries subject, tense, and action meaning simultaneously.
  8. Pronounce multi-morpheme verbs with correct syllable rhythm and penultimate stress.
  9. Write an organized paragraph of 80–100 words describing a daily routine.

Communication Scenario

A new colleague at work in Dar es Salaam asks you what a typical day looks like for you. She wants to know when you wake up, what you do in the morning, how you get to work, and what you do in the evenings. You want to ask her the same questions in return. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to describe a complete daily routine in Swahili — what you do, when you do it, and in what order — and say clearly what you do not do.

  1. In English, you say "I study," "I am studying," and sometimes "I do study" as three separate forms. Swahili often uses one present-tense form where English uses several. As you study this chapter, pay attention to how one Swahili verb can cover what English expresses with different constructions.
  2. Swahili has its own way of expressing clock time that counts from a different starting point than the international system. You do not need to master every detail in one chapter, but watch for how time expressions work and how context helps you interpret them.

Pronunciation and Spelling

Mdundo wa silabi katika vitenzi — Syllable rhythm in verbs

A productive Swahili verb in the present tense combines several meaningful parts into one word. Each part forms its own syllable, and the penultimate stress rule still applies to the whole word — stress falls on the second-to-last syllable of the entire form, not on the individual parts.

Look at this example and identify the stressed syllable before reading it aloud:

Written formParts (analysis only)Syllable breakdownStressed syllable
ninasomani-na-som-ani · na · so · maSO-ma → ni-na-SO-ma
anafanyaa-na-fany-aa · na · fa · nyaFA-nya → a-na-FA-nya
tunasafishatu-na-saffish-atu · na · sa · fi · shaFI-sha → tu-na-sa-FI-sha
wanaandikawa-na-andik-awa · na · an · di · kaDI-ka → wa-na-an-DI-ka
hawaendihawa-end-iha · wa · en · diEN-di → ha-wa-EN-di
Sound Focus — Stress in multi-morpheme verbs

Many English speakers, when they first encounter a long Swahili verb, try to split it back into its parts and stress each one separately. This breaks the natural rhythm of the word. Practice treating the whole verb — subject marker, tense marker, verb root, and final vowel — as a single word and applying the penultimate stress rule to the complete form. Say ni-na-SO-ma, not NI-na-SO-ma.

Mpaka wa mofimu — Vowels across morpheme boundaries

When the subject marker ends in a vowel and the tense marker -na- begins with one, the two vowels belong to separate syllables. Do not merge them into a single long vowel or drop one.

ni-na-amka → four syllables: ni · na · am · ka
a-na-oga → four syllables: a · na · o · ga
u-na-andika → five syllables: u · na · an · di · ka

Grammar Instruction

7.1 The full present tense with -na-

Purpose: say what you and others do or are doing in the present.

In Chapter 3 you encountered the verb template in model verbs, and in Chapter 6 you practised subject markers with kuna and kuwa na. This chapter makes the full present tense with -na- productive. You will now build and use it across a wide range of verbs.

The affirmative present tense follows this template:

Language Building Block
subject marker + -na- + verb root + final vowel
ninasoma
I study / I am studying — ni- + -na- + som- + -a
anafanya kazi
He/She works / He/She is working — a- + -na- + fany- + -a + kazi
tunakula
We eat / We are eating — tu- + -na- + l- + -a

The subject markers you need for the six persons are:

Person Subject marker Complete present form (with -soma) Meaning
Ini-ninasomaI study / I am studying
you (singular)u-unasomayou study / you are studying
he / shea-anasomahe/she studies / is studying
wetu-tunasomawe study / we are studying
you (plural)m-mnasomayou (pl.) study / are studying
theywa-wanasomathey study / they are studying
Language Note — One Swahili form, many English translations

The present form with -na- covers the ground of English "I study," "I am studying," and "I do study." The same Swahili form serves all three without change. Context — a time expression, a question, a surrounding sentence — tells the listener whether the action is a general habit or something happening right now. You do not need three separate verb forms to convey these distinctions in Swahili.

7.2 The infinitive ku-

Purpose: recognize the infinitive as the dictionary form of a verb and use it after certain other verbs.

The infinitive is the form of the verb that appears in vocabulary lists and dictionaries. It begins with ku- and is followed by the verb root and the final vowel. The infinitive is not a finite verb — it does not carry a subject marker or a tense marker — but it is used in several important ways.

In this chapter, practise using the infinitive in two main ways:

1. As the name of an activity:

  • Kupika ni kazi muhimu. Cooking is important work. (The infinitive acts like a noun meaning "cooking.")
  • Ninakupenda kusoma. I like to read / I like reading. — infinitive as the second verb after the main verb.

2. After verbs of liking, wanting, and starting:

  • Ninapenda kusoma. I like to study / I like studying. — ni- + -na- + pend- + -a + infinitive kusoma
  • Unapenda kufanya nini? What do you like to do?
  • Anaanza kupika saa moja. He/She starts cooking at seven. — recognition item: kuanza = to begin (introduced receptively here)
Language Note — Infinitive ku- versus negative past -ku-

The prefix ku- at the start of an infinitive — kusoma, kufanya, kulala — is not the same as the -ku- that appears in past negative verbs, which you will study in Chapter 16. Do not confuse them. The infinitive ku- stands alone at the beginning of a word with no preceding subject marker. The negative past -ku- occurs inside a verb after a negative subject marker. They look similar in isolation but occupy very different positions in the sentence. Whenever you see ku- at the start of a standalone word, it is almost certainly an infinitive.

7.3 The present negative

Purpose: say what someone does not do.

The present negative requires two changes from the affirmative form:

  1. The subject marker changes to its negative form.
  2. The tense marker -na- is dropped. The negative verb goes directly from the negative subject marker to the verb root.

The negative subject markers are:

Person Affirmative subject marker Negative subject marker Negative present form (with -soma) Meaning
Ini-si-sisomiI do not study
you (singular)u-hu-husomiyou do not study
he / shea-ha-hasomihe/she does not study
wetu-hatu-hatusomiwe do not study
you (plural)m-ham-hamsomiyou (pl.) do not study
theywa-hawa-hawasomithey do not study

Notice also that the final vowel changes from -a to -i in most verbs in the present negative. This is a standard feature of Standard Swahili and must be applied consistently.

  • Ninasoma. → Sisomi. I study. → I do not study. ni-na-som-a → si-som-i
  • Anafanya kazi. → Hafanyi kazi. She works. → She does not work. a-na-fany-a → ha-fany-i
  • Wanakula nyumbani. → Hawakuli nyumbani. They eat at home. → They do not eat at home. wa-na-kul-a → hawa-kul-i
  • Tunalala mapema. → Hatulali mapema. We go to sleep early. → We do not go to sleep early. tu-na-lal-a → hatu-lal-i
Language Note — Verbs not ending in -a

Most Swahili verbs end in -a in the affirmative and change to -i in the present negative. A small number of common verbs end in other vowels — for example, kurudi (to return) ends in -i already, and kuambia (to tell) ends in -ia. These verbs do not change their final vowel further in the present negative — the negative subject marker alone signals negation. Learn the final vowel behavior together with each new verb rather than assuming every verb changes from -a to -i. At this stage, all productive verbs in this chapter end in -a and follow the standard pattern.

7.4 Swahili clock time

Purpose: tell and ask the time in Swahili.

Swahili has its own system for expressing clock time. The key difference from the international system is the starting point: the Swahili day begins at sunrise — approximately 6 a.m. — which is counted as saa moja, "hour one." This means that Swahili hours are offset from international hours by approximately six.

International timeSwahili expressionLiteral meaning
6:00 a.m.saa moja asubuhihour one (of the) morning
7:00 a.m.saa mbili asubuhihour two of the morning
9:00 a.m.saa tatu asubuhihour three of the morning
12:00 noonsaa sita mchanahour six of midday
3:00 p.m.saa tisa alasirihour nine of the afternoon
6:00 p.m.saa kumi na mbili jionihour twelve of the evening
7:00 p.m.saa moja usikuhour one of the night
12:00 midnightsaa sita usikuhour six of the night

For minutes, add the part-of-day word after the hour number, then use na dakika (plus minutes) for minutes past, or kasoro (minus) for minutes before the next hour:

  • Ni saa mbili na dakika kumi. It is 7:10 a.m. — hour two plus ten minutes
  • Ni saa tano kasoro robo. It is 10:45 a.m. — hour five minus a quarter
  • Ni saa sita na nusu mchana. It is 12:30 p.m. — hour six and a half of midday

To ask the time, use:

  • Ni saa ngapi? What time is it? (It is how many hours?)
  • Unaenda kazi saa ngapi? What time do you go to work?
Language Note — When to use which system

Both the Swahili time system and the international (twelve-hour and twenty-four-hour) system are widely used across East Africa. Written schedules, transport timetables, workplace rosters, digital devices, and formal announcements commonly use international time. Everyday conversation often uses the Swahili system, particularly when specifying parts of the day. Learners should be able to interpret both systems and produce the Swahili system accurately in the controlled contexts of this chapter. When you read a schedule in this chapter, the time expression given will always specify the part of the day so that no ambiguity arises.

7.5 Sequencing with kwanza, halafu, and baadaye

Purpose: arrange activities in a clear order.

To connect a series of actions in sequence, place one of these adverbs at the beginning of the clause it introduces, or between clauses:

  • Kwanza ninaoga, halafu ninavaa nguo, baadaye ninakula chakula cha asubuhi. First I shower, then I get dressed, later I eat breakfast.
  • Kwanza anafanya kazi, halafu anapumzika. First she works, then she rests.

These words can begin a sentence or appear as a bridge between two clauses separated by a comma. You do not need any other connecting word — kwanza, halafu, and baadaye carry the sequence meaning on their own.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: Farida na Juma ni wafanyakazi wenzake ofisini Dar es Salaam. Wanazungumza wakati wa mapumziko ya mchana.

(Setting: Farida and Juma are colleagues in an office in Dar es Salaam. They are talking during a lunch break.)

FARIDAHabari za mchana, Juma.Good afternoon, Juma.
JUMANzuri sana, asante. Na wewe, habari?Very well, thank you. And you, how are things?
FARIDANzuri. Leo ninachoka kidogo — ninaanza kazi mapema sana.Fine. Today I am a little tired — I start work very early.
JUMAKweli? Unaanza kazi saa ngapi?Really? What time do you start work?
FARIDANinaanza saa mbili asubuhi — saa nane kwa wakati wa kimataifa. Kwanza ninaoga, halafu ninakula chakula kidogo, baadaye naenda ofisini.I start at eight a.m. — that is eight o'clock international time. First I shower, then I eat a little food, later I come to the office.
JUMAMimi siamki mapema sana. Ninaamka saa tatu asubuhi — saa tisa kwa wakati wa kimataifa. Lakini ninafanya kazi kwa bidii.I don't wake up very early. I wake up at nine a.m. international time. But I work hard.
FARIDAUnakula chakula cha asubuhi nyumbani?Do you eat breakfast at home?
JUMAWakati mwingine. Mara nyingi sina wakati — ninakula mkate tu njiani.Sometimes. Often I don't have time — I just eat bread on the way.
FARIDANa jioni? Unarudi nyumbani saa ngapi?And in the evening? What time do you go home?
JUMAKila siku ninamaliza kazi saa kumi na mbili jioni — saa sita usiku kwa wakati wa kimataifa. Halafu ninapika, ninakula, ninasoma kidogo, na baadaye ninalala saa tatu usiku.Every day I finish work at midnight international time — no, sorry, at six p.m. Then I cook, eat, study a little, and later I sleep at nine p.m.

(Farida anacheka kidogo. / Farida laughs a little.)

FARIDAUnasoma kila usiku? Unasoma nini?You study every night? What do you study?
JUMANasoma Kiingereza na hesabu. Ninapenda kusoma — ni starehe kwangu.I study English and maths. I like to study — it is my enjoyment.
FARIDAVizuri sana! Mimi sijisomi usiku. Ninachoka sana. Ninapumzika na kutazama televisheni kidogo, halafu ninalala.That's great! I don't study at night. I get very tired. I rest and watch a little television, then I go to sleep.
JUMASawa. Sisi wote tuna ratiba tofauti, sivyo?Right. We all have different schedules, don't we?
FARIDANdiyo, kweli. Haya, ni saa saba mchana — ninapumzika kidogo kabla ya kuendelea kufanya kazi.Yes, truly. Well, it is one p.m. — I am resting a little before continuing to work.

Vocabulary note: ninachoka = I am tired; ninaanza = I begin/start; kwa bidii = diligently; hard; mkate = bread (N-class); njiani = on the way; ninamaliza = I finish; hesabu = mathematics; starehe = enjoyment; comfort (recognition items); sijisomi = I don't study (by myself); kabla ya = before (recognition item); tofauti = different; sivyo? = isn't that right? These are recognition items at this stage.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • Every present-tense affirmative verb uses the pattern: subject marker + -na- + verb root + -a.
  • Every present-tense negative verb uses the negative subject marker and drops -na-; the final vowel changes from -a to -i.
  • At least two different subject markers appear (e.g. ni- and a-, or tu- and wa-).

  • No pedagogical hyphens appear in the final written verbs.
  • Each sentence ends with a full stop and begins with a capital letter.
  • Spelling of verb roots is accurate — no vowels are merged across morpheme boundaries.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Daily schedules across East Africa

Daily routines in Swahili-speaking communities vary considerably depending on many factors: urban or rural setting, occupation, religion, family structure, season, and individual preference. A nurse in Nairobi, a teacher in Zanzibar, a farmer near Mwanza, and a university student in Kampala will each have a very different rhythm to their day.

In some communities, the rhythms of daily prayer — particularly for Muslim speakers along the coast and in many inland towns — shape the timing of meals, rest, and work in ways that may differ from what learners are familiar with. In agricultural settings, seasonal demands on time can make a "typical day" highly variable across the year. These differences are worth noticing, because asking about someone's daily routine is a natural part of getting to know them — and the answer may surprise you.

Avoid assuming that the schedule described in any one textbook dialogue represents the routine of all, most, or even many Swahili speakers.

Culture in Context — The split-shift day

In many East African cities and towns, a working day divided into two parts — with a substantial midday break — has historically been common, particularly in warmer coastal areas. During this break, some workers return home for a meal and rest before returning to work in the late afternoon. This practice is not universal and is becoming less common in some urban environments where longer commutes make it impractical. You saw a version of this pattern in Tiwa's message from Kampala. Learners should recognize this as one real pattern among many, not as the definition of how East African people organize their day.

Regional Variation — Time expressions and clock systems

While the Swahili time system described in this chapter is widely understood across Tanzania and Kenya, its use in casual conversation varies. In Zanzibar and the Tanzanian coast, it is common and unmarked; in Nairobi and other heavily urbanized Kenyan settings, international time is frequently used even in informal speech. In Uganda, the Swahili time system may be familiar to Swahili speakers but is less consistently used in everyday conversation than in Tanzania. Some speakers use both systems interchangeably or clarify by adding kwa wakati wa kimataifa (in international time) when there might be ambiguity — just as Juma does in the model dialogue. Learners should learn both systems and be alert to which is being used.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Keeping -na- in the negative

Error: Sinaenda kazini. (keeping -na- in the negative)

Correction: Siendi kazini. — The present negative drops the tense marker -na- entirely. The negative subject marker alone links directly to the verb root. If you keep -na- in the negative, you are mixing affirmative and negative structures. The negative form is: negative subject marker + verb root + final vowel -i (for most verbs). Check: si- + end- + -i = siendi.

Common Mistake — Forgetting to change the final vowel in the negative

Error: Hasoma (dropping -na- but keeping the final -a)

Correction: Hasomi. — The present negative requires two changes: (1) the subject marker changes to its negative form, and (2) the final vowel of the verb changes from -a to -i. Both changes together signal present negation. Missing either one produces a non-standard or confusing form. Practise the negative as a complete unit: ha- + som- + -i = hasomi.

Common Mistake — Writing hyphens in ordinary verbs

Error: Writing ni-na-soma or a-na-fanya in a sentence or paragraph

Correction: ninasoma, anafanya — Hyphens are a tool for grammatical analysis in your grammar notes. They do not appear in standard written Swahili. When you write a sentence, a dialogue, a message, or a paragraph, write the verb as a single unbroken word. Use the hyphenated analysis only when a grammar exercise or a teacher's instruction explicitly asks you to identify morpheme boundaries.

Common Mistake — Converting international time incorrectly

Error: Saying saa tisa for 9 a.m. (subtracting 9 from 12 instead of 6)

Correction: saa tatu asubuhi — The Swahili day starts at 6 a.m., which is saa moja. To convert an international a.m. time to Swahili time, subtract 6: 9 a.m. − 6 = 3, so 9 a.m. is saa tatu asubuhi. For p.m. times from 6 p.m. onward, subtract 6 again: 7 p.m. − 6 = 1, so 7 p.m. is saa moja usiku. When uncertain, add the part of the day (asubuhi, usiku, etc.) to remove ambiguity.

Common Mistake — Stressing each morpheme separately

Error: Pronouncing ni-NA-so-MA (stressing each part individually)

Correction: ni-na-SO-ma — The entire verb form is one phonological word and follows the penultimate stress rule. Count the syllables of the complete verb, find the second-to-last, and stress that one. Do not stress individual morphemes. ninasoma has four syllables — the stress falls on so, the third syllable: ni-na-SO-ma.

Common Mistake — Using the infinitive as a finite verb

Error: Mimi kusoma kila usiku. (using the infinitive without a subject marker and tense marker)

Correction: Ninasoma kila usiku. — The infinitive kusoma is not a complete finite verb. It cannot stand alone as the main verb of a sentence. To say that you do something, you need the full verb form: subject marker + tense marker + verb root + final vowel. The infinitive appears in vocabulary lists and after certain verbs such as ninapenda kusoma — but in that case, ninapenda is the finite verb and kusoma is its complement.

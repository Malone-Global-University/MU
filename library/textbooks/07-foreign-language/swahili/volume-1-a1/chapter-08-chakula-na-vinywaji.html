Chakula na Vinywaji

Food and Drink · Speak and Write Swahili: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common East African foods, drinks, meals, and containers using vocabulary drawn from multiple noun classes.
  2. Recognize JI-MA class nouns through high-frequency food vocabulary and understand how the JI-MA class differs from noun classes already studied.
  3. Use the verbs kutaka, kupenda, kula, and kunywa in affirmative and negative present-tense sentences to express wants, preferences, and actions.
  4. Use high-frequency object markers — -ni-, -ku-, -m-/-mw-, -wa-, -ki-, and -vi- — to refer back to a noun already in context.
  5. Understand that the object marker occupies a fixed position inside the verb template, before the verb root.
  6. Order food and make polite requests in a restaurant or café using naomba and other approved patterns.
  7. Ask and answer questions about meals, preferences, and food items using question words already studied and the new question pattern Unapenda nini?
  8. Pronounce vowel sequences in common food words without merging distinct vowels into one sound.
  9. Write a short order, shopping note, or meal description of several connected sentences.

Communication Scenario

You are visiting a small restaurant near the University of Dar es Salaam with a classmate. The menu is written in Swahili. Your classmate asks what you feel like eating. You want to find out what dishes are available, say what you like, ask for a recommendation, and order politely when the server arrives. By the end of this chapter, you will have the language to navigate exactly this kind of everyday food situation — in a restaurant, at home, or in a market — entirely in Swahili.

  1. Think about how you express "I want it" or "I like it" in English. The word "it" refers back to something already mentioned. Swahili does the same thing, but it builds the reference directly into the verb rather than adding a separate word. As you study this chapter, notice how the verb template grows by one slot to include this reference.
  2. You have already encountered KI-VI nouns (classroom objects, Chapter 2) and JI-MA nouns will be introduced here through food vocabulary. Before you begin, predict: when you name a food in Swahili, what information might you need in order to use it correctly in a sentence? The answer — its class, plural, and agreement — will become clear as the chapter unfolds.

Pronunciation and Spelling

Mfululizo wa irabu — Vowel sequences

Several food words in this chapter contain two or more vowels written next to each other. A key feature of Standard Swahili is that each written vowel represents a separate, distinct vowel sound. Unlike English — where vowel combinations often form diphthongs (gliding sounds like the oi in "coin" or the ou in "out") — Swahili vowels in a sequence are pronounced as two (or more) separate, equally weighted syllables.

WordSyllable breakdownMeaningCommon error

Sound Focus — Two vowels side by side means two syllables

When you see two vowels written next to each other in Swahili — ai, ao, ae, ua, ia, oa — pronounce each one fully and separately. Do not slide or glide between them. The word naomba has three distinct syllables: na-om-ba. The word chai has two: cha-i. Practice by tapping one finger for each syllable as you say each word. If your finger only taps once for a two-vowel sequence, slow down and separate the vowels.

Tahajia — Spelling focus

Two common spelling challenges arise in this chapter's vocabulary.

1. JI-MA plural prefix ma-: The plural of JI-MA nouns adds ma- to the singular base. Some words already begin with a vowel in the singular, so the plural starts with ma- followed directly by that vowel: embe → maembe, papai → mapapai. Write both vowels — the a of the prefix and the opening vowel of the noun — separately. Do not drop either one.

2. The verb naomba: This is the first-person present form of the verb kuomba (to request). Write it as one word: naomba, not na omba. The subject marker ni- and the tense marker -na- combine with the root -omb- and the final vowel -a. In normal writing the word is not hyphenated.

Grammar Instruction

8.1 The JI-MA class — a new noun class through food vocabulary

Purpose: recognize JI-MA class nouns in this chapter's vocabulary and understand how they differ from noun classes already studied.

You have now encountered four noun classes. As a reminder:

  • M-WA: mostly people and animate beings; singular m-/mw-, plural wa-/w-
  • M-MI: mostly plants, long objects; singular m-/mw-, plural mi-
  • KI-VI: tools, objects, languages; singular ki-/ch-, plural vi-/vy-
  • N-class: a wide range of nouns; singular and plural often look the same

The JI-MA class adds a fifth category. Its defining characteristic is the plural prefix ma-. The singular form is less predictable — it may have a visible ji- or j- prefix, or it may look like it has no class prefix at all. The safest strategy is to learn each JI-MA noun with both its singular and plural forms.

SingularPlural (ma-)MeaningNote on singular form
tundamatundafruit / fruitsNo visible ji- prefix on singular
embemaembemango / mangoesNo visible prefix; plural adds ma- before vowel
papaimapapaipapaya / papayasNo visible prefix; two vowels in singular
chungwamachungwaorange / orangesNo visible prefix
majimajiwaterCommonly used in plural form; mass noun
maziwamaziwamilkCommonly used in plural form; mass noun
maharagemaharagebeansCommonly used in plural form
Language Focus — The JI-MA class and its plural

The most reliable signal of a JI-MA noun is its plural: if the plural begins with ma-, the noun is JI-MA class. The singular form alone may not tell you much, since some JI-MA singulars carry a visible ji- prefix while others do not. Always record the plural when you learn a JI-MA noun. The ma- prefix in the plural is also how you identify JI-MA agreement on adjectives and other modifiers.

Notice that maji, maziwa, and maharage appear most often in the ma- form because they refer to substances used in quantity. Do not try to create a singular *jiwa or *jiharagi — use these nouns in the form given.

8.2 kutaka, kupenda, kula, and kunywa in the present tense

Purpose: say what you want, like, eat, and drink using the verb template you have been building since Chapter 7.

You know the full affirmative present template from Chapter 7:

subject marker + -na- + verb root + final vowel -a

The four verbs central to this chapter follow this template without exception. Their infinitives, roots, and meaning are:

InfinitiveRootMeaning
kutaka-tak-to want
kupenda-pend-to like; to love
kula-l-to eat (monosyllabic root — note special behavior below)
kunywa-nyw-to drink (monosyllabic root — note special behavior below)
Language Note — Monosyllabic verb roots

The roots of kula (-l-) and kunywa (-nyw-) consist of a single consonant or consonant cluster. Monosyllabic roots behave slightly differently in the affirmative present: they keep the infinitive prefix ku- attached to the root. The present form is therefore ninakula (not *ninala) and ninakunywa (not *ninanywa). Learn these forms as fixed productive patterns at this stage. The underlying rule for monosyllabic roots will be revisited when it becomes relevant to other verb forms.

Notice that present-tense negatives change the final vowel from -a to -i for verbs like kupenda. This pattern was introduced in Chapter 7.   • Anataka chai asubuhi. He/She wants tea in the morning.

  • Sikunywi kahawa — ninakunywa chai. I do not drink coffee — I drink tea.
  • Hawapendi pilipili kali. They do not like hot/spicy chilli.

8.3 Introduction to object markers

Purpose: refer back to a person or thing already in context without repeating the full noun — using a slot built directly into the verb.

In English, when you have already mentioned something, you use a pronoun to refer back to it: "I want the mango — I want it." Swahili expresses the same idea by inserting an object marker into the verb, before the verb root. This is the most important new slot in the verb template this chapter.

The expanded template is:

Language Building Block
subject marker + tense marker + object marker + verb root + final vowel
Ni-na-ki-taka.
I want it. (referring to a KI-class item, e.g. kikombe)
Ni-na-m-penda.
I like/love him/her. (referring to a person)
A-na-vi-taka.
He/she wants them. (referring to VI-class items, e.g. vikombe)

Note: In ordinary writing these forms are not hyphenated. The analysis above uses hyphens only to show the structure. Write: ninakilaka, ninapenda, anavilaka — as one word each.

The object marker you use depends on the noun class (or person) of the thing you are referring back to. The table below gives the high-frequency object markers introduced in this chapter.

Category Object marker Example Gloss
1st person singular (me) -ni- Ananiletea chai. He/She is bringing me tea. (recognition item)
2nd person singular (you) -ku- Ninakupenda. I love you.
3rd person singular animate (him/her) -m- / -mw- Ninamtaka. I want him/her.
1st person plural (us) -tu- Anatuletea chakula. He/She is bringing us food. (recognition item)
2nd person plural (you pl.) -wa- Ninawaona. I see you all. (recognition item)
3rd person plural animate (them) -wa- Ninawapigia simu. I am calling them. (recognition item)
KI-VI singular (it — KI noun) -ki- Ninakitaka. I want it. (referring to e.g. kikombe)
KI-VI plural (them — VI nouns) -vi- Unavitaka? Do you want them? (referring to e.g. vijiko)
N-class singular/plural (it/them) -i- Unaipenda? Do you like it? (referring to e.g. chai)
JI-MA singular (it — JI noun) -li- Unalitaka? Do you want it? (referring to e.g. tunda)
JI-MA plural (them — MA nouns) -ya- Unayapenda? Do you like them? (referring to e.g. matunda)
Language Note — When is an object marker used?

In Swahili, an object marker is most naturally used when the object has already been mentioned in the conversation and you want to refer back to it without repeating the full noun — similar to using "it" or "them" in English. It is also commonly used when the object is a specific, definite, or discourse-salient noun. It is not mechanically required every time you mention an object. A sentence like Ninapenda chai (I like tea) is complete and correct without an object marker. The object marker -i- would be added if chai had already been mentioned and you wanted to say "I like it": Ninaipenda. Do not insert an object marker simply because English uses "it" — check that the noun has already been established in context.

  • Unataka embe? — Ndiyo, ninaliTaka. Do you want a mango? — Yes, I want it. JI-MA singular object marker -li-: ni-na-li-tak-a
  • Unajua kama kuna matunda? — Ndiyo, ninayapenda sana. Do you know if there is fruit? — Yes, I like them very much. JI-MA plural object marker -ya-: ni-na-ya-pend-a
  • Unataka kikombe? — Ndiyo, ninakitaka. Do you want a cup? — Yes, I want it. KI-VI singular object marker -ki-: ni-na-ki-tak-a
  • Ninapenda chai hii — ninaikunywa kila asubuhi. I like this tea — I drink it every morning. N-class object marker -i-: ni-na-i-kunyw-a

8.4 Polite requests with naomba

Purpose: order food and make requests courteously in a restaurant, café, or home setting.

The verb kuomba means to request or to ask for something. Its first-person present form is naomba — literally "I request" — and it is the most natural and polite way to place an order or make a request in many East African settings. It is considerably warmer than the more direct nataka (I want) when speaking to a server or host.

  • Naomba wali na mchuzi wa kuku. I would like rice and chicken stew.
  • Naomba chai moja tafadhali. I would like one tea, please.
  • Naomba maji baridi. I would like cold water.
  • Naomba bila sukari. I would like it without sugar. / Without sugar, please.
  • Kuna ugali leo? Is there ugali today?
  • Chakula gani kuna leo? What food is available today?
Language Note — naomba versus nataka

Both naomba and nataka can precede a noun to express wanting or requesting something. The difference is one of register and relationship. Naomba — from the verb meaning "to request" — implies deference to the other person and is considered more courteous in service settings. Nataka is a neutral statement of wanting and is appropriate among friends or in informal contexts. In a restaurant, a market stall, or when speaking to someone you do not know well, naomba is generally the preferred choice. Model the distinction the way you would explain "I'd like" versus "I want" in English — both are usable, but context determines which is more appropriate.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: Zawadi na rafiki yake Kofi wanaingia mkahawa mdogo karibu na chuo kikuu. Wanaangalia orodha ya chakula. Mhudumu anakuja.

(Setting: Zawadi and her friend Kofi enter a small café near the university. They look at the menu. A server approaches.)

ZAWADIHabari za mchana, Kofi. Njoo, tumia hapa.Good afternoon, Kofi. Come, sit here.
KOFINzuri, asante. Una njaa?Well, thank you. Are you hungry?
ZAWADINdiyo, nina njaa sana. Na kiu pia. Unataka kula nini leo?Yes, I am very hungry. And thirsty too. What do you want to eat today?
KOFINinapenda wali na mchuzi wa samaki. Na wewe?I like rice and fish stew. And you?
ZAWADIMimi ninapendelea ugali. Ninaupenda sana. Tutaona kama kuna.I prefer ugali. I like it very much. We'll see if there is some.

(Mhudumu anakuja. / The server comes over.)

MHUDUMUKaribu sana. Hamjambo?Welcome. How are you (plural)?
ZAWADIHatujambo, asante. Chakula gani kuna leo?We are well, thank you. What food is available today?
MHUDUMULeo kuna wali, ugali, mchuzi wa kuku, mchuzi wa maharage, na samaki wa kukaanga. Matunda pia — maembe na machungwa.Today there is rice, ugali, chicken stew, bean stew, and fried fish. Fruit too — mangoes and oranges.
KOFIVizuri sana. Naomba wali na mchuzi wa samaki tafadhali.Very good. I would like rice and fish stew please.
MHUDUMUSawa. Na kunywa?Alright. And to drink?
KOFINaomba maji baridi tafadhali.I would like cold water please.
ZAWADINaomba ugali na mchuzi wa maharage. Na chai moja — bila sukari tafadhali.I would like ugali and bean stew. And one tea — without sugar please.
MHUDUMUHaya. Na matunda? Maembe ni mazuri sana leo — yameiva vizuri.Alright. And fruit? The mangoes are very good today — they are nicely ripe.
ZAWADIMm, ninapenda maembe sana. Ninayataka! Kofi, unataka?Mm, I like mangoes very much. I want them! Kofi, do you want some?
KOFINdiyo, ninayataka pia. Tutachukua mawili tafadhali.Yes, I want them too. We will take two please.
MHUDUMUSawa kabisa. Chakula kitakuja hivi karibuni.Absolutely. The food will come shortly.
ZAWADIAsante sana.Thank you very much.

Vocabulary notes: tumia = use; sit (informal invitation); njaa = hunger; kiu = thirst; ninapendelea = I prefer (recognition item; the extension -le- is not yet taught productively); ninaupenda — note U-class object marker -u- for ugali (recognition item); wa kukaanga = fried; yameiva = they are ripe (recognition item); tutachukua = we will take (future tense, Chapter 14); mawili = two of them (JI-MA class numeral agreement — recognition item).

  • Every JI-MA noun is written with the correct plural (ma- prefix). Singular and plural forms are not confused.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Ugali, wali, and East African staple foods

Ugali is a stiff, cooked porridge made most commonly from maize flour (unga wa mahindi) and is a central staple food in much of Tanzania and Kenya. It is typically eaten with a stew — mchuzi — made from meat, fish, beans, or vegetables. Wali, cooked rice, is also widely eaten, particularly in coastal regions and in areas with strong historical Indian Ocean trading connections. Which staple a household prefers may reflect region, family tradition, economic factors, and individual taste — not a single national pattern.

Learners should approach food vocabulary as a gateway to community life and hospitality, not as a fixed list of "traditional" dishes. Meals in Swahili-speaking communities vary by region, religion, season, and household. Coastal Swahili cuisine, for instance, makes extensive use of coconut milk (nazi), spices, and seafood in ways that differ significantly from highland or inland cooking.

Culture in Context — Hospitality and shared meals

Sharing food is an important part of social life in many Swahili-speaking communities. Offering food to a guest before asking whether they are hungry, insisting that a visitor eat, and thanking a host after a meal are widely practiced courtesies — though the exact form these take varies by community, household, and relationship. Common expressions of appreciation include Chakula kizuri! (Delicious food!) and Asante kwa chakula (Thank you for the food).

In restaurant settings, it is common to greet the server and establish a cordial exchange before ordering, rather than ordering immediately upon sitting down. The greeting is part of the transaction, not separate from it.

Regional Variation — Food names and chai

The word chai in much of Tanzania and Kenya refers to tea prepared with milk and sugar together — not black tea with milk on the side, and not an herbal infusion. If you want black tea or tea without milk, you would need to specify: chai ya rangi (literally "tea of colour" — black tea without milk) is a common term in Tanzania. Chai ya maziwa (tea with milk) is a common way to specify the standard preparation in some contexts.

Some food terms also differ between Tanzania and Kenya. Ugali is the standard Tanzanian term; in Kenya the same dish is widely known by the same name, though regional terms exist. Vocabulary for snack foods, street food, and restaurant items may differ considerably between cities and regions. When in doubt, listen to how local speakers refer to familiar items and adjust accordingly.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Dropping ku- from kula and kunywa in the present tense

Error: *Ninala chakula. or *Ninanywa maji.

Correction: Ninakula chakula. and Ninakunywa maji. — The roots of kula and kunywa are monosyllabic. In the affirmative present tense, monosyllabic roots keep the infinitive prefix ku- attached. The result is ninakula and ninakunywa, not forms without ku-. Apply the same rule to other monosyllabic verbs you encounter. Learners familiar with verbs like kupenda (ninapenda — no extra ku-) should be aware that monosyllabic roots are an exception.

Common Mistake — Placing the object marker after the verb root

Error: *Ninataka-ki. or *Ninatakaki. (object marker placed at the end)

Correction: Ninakitaka. — The object marker occupies a fixed position in the verb: after the tense marker and before the verb root. The full template is: subject marker + tense marker + object marker + verb root + final vowel. The object marker never moves to the end of the verb. Check the template every time you insert an object marker until the position feels natural.

Common Mistake — Using the wrong object marker for a JI-MA noun

Error: *Ninaipenda when referring to tunda (a JI-MA singular noun)

Correction: Ninalipenda — The object marker for a JI-MA singular noun is -li-: ninalitaka, ninalipenda. The marker -i- is for N-class nouns (like chai or nyama). Always identify the noun class of the item you are referring to before choosing the object marker. For JI-MA plurals, the object marker is -ya-: ninayataka (I want them — matunda). Keeping a list of noun classes with their object markers as you learn them will help avoid this mix-up.

Common Mistake — Adding an object marker to every sentence with an object

Error: *Ninaikula chai kila siku. (inserting -i- even though chai has not been established in context)

Correction: Ninakunywa chai kila siku. — Object markers are used when the object is already known from context, not mechanically for every sentence that has an object noun. When you introduce a noun for the first time or when the noun is present in the sentence, a full noun phrase is used without an object marker. The object marker replaces a noun that has already been mentioned: only after you have said ninapenda chai would it be natural to say ninaipenda kila siku. Think of the object marker as the equivalent of "it" or "them" in English — you use "it" only when the thing in question is already known.

Common Mistake — Forgetting the final vowel change in present-tense negatives

Error: *Sipenda pilipili. (using the final vowel -a in a negative present)

Correction: Sipendi pilipili. — Standard Swahili present-tense negatives change the final vowel of the verb from -a to -i for most verbs. This applies to kupenda → sipendi, kutaka → sitaki, kupika → sipiki, and so on. Review your negative forms carefully whenever you learn a new verb. Monosyllabic verbs such as kula and kunywa have their own negative forms (sikuli, sikunywi) that require individual attention.

Common Mistake — Merging vowels in food words into single English-style sounds

Error: Pronouncing maembe as if the ae were one English diphthong (like the sound in "eye")

Correction: ma-em-be — In Standard Swahili, every written vowel represents a separate sound and a separate syllable. The sequence ae in maembe is two distinct vowels: a as in the ma- prefix, and e as the start of -embe. Say them one after the other without a glide. Practice the syllable tap technique: tap once per syllable as you say the word. Maembe has three taps: ma — em — be.

Jana na Wiki Iliyopita

Yesterday and Last Week · Speak and Write Swahili: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Form and use the past tense marker -li- with common verbs to narrate completed events.
  2. Negate past events accurately using negative subject markers and -ku-, and distinguish this -ku- from the infinitive prefix ku-.
  3. Ask and answer questions about what happened yesterday or last week.
  4. Use object markers inside past-tense verbs when the referent is clear from context.
  5. Sequence several past events using kwanza, kisha, halafu, baadaye, and mwishowe.
  6. Say what did not happen, using past negative forms.
  7. Distinguish present -na-, future -ta-, and past -li- in connected speech and writing.
  8. Write a connected account of 130-160 words describing completed events in chronological order.

Communication Scenario

You have joined a Swahili conversation group that meets weekly. Every session begins the same way: each person shares what they did the day before or over the past week. This week, it is your turn. You want to describe your recent activities clearly and in order — what you did first, what happened next, and how your week ended — using accurate past-tense Swahili that your conversation partners can follow and respond to.

  1. Think about yesterday or the past week. What did you do, in what order? English narrates the past mostly with one past-tense form and a scatter of time words ("then," "after that," "later"). As you work through this chapter, notice how Swahili narrates a sequence of completed events using one consistent tense marker plus connecting words.
  2. English forms a negative past by adding "did not" before the verb ("I did not go"). Swahili forms a negative past differently — through a change to the subject marker and a distinct tense marker inside the verb itself. Watch for this contrast as you study negation in this chapter.

Pronunciation and Spelling

Kutofautisha alama za wakati — Distinguishing tense markers

You now know three tense markers that occupy the same position in the verb: present -na-, future -ta-, and past -li-. These three vowels and consonants are short and can be easy to mishear or misspell in fast speech. Practice hearing and pronouncing the contrast clearly.

PresentFuturePastMeaning
ninasomanitasomanilisomaI study / I will study / I studied
anafanya kaziatafanya kazialifanya kazihe/she works / will work / worked
tunanunuatutanunuatulinunuawe buy / will buy / bought
Sound Focus — Stress in negative past forms

Negative past verbs such as sikwenda (I did not go) and hakusoma (he/she did not study) follow the regular penultimate stress rule: stress falls on the second-to-last syllable of the complete word — si-KWEN-da, ha-ku-SO-ma. English speakers sometimes place extra stress on the negative subject marker at the start of the word. Keep the stress near the end of the word, as with any other Swahili verb.

Mdundo wa masimulizi — Narrative pausing and sequence markers

When narrating several events in order, Swahili speakers typically pause briefly after each sequencing word — kwanza, kisha, halafu, baadaye, mwishowe — before continuing with the next clause. This short pause signals to the listener that a new step in the sequence is beginning. Practice reading a short sequence aloud with a clear pause after each connector.

Mgawanyo sahihi wa maandishi — Accurate written segmentation

Past-tense verbs, like present and future verbs, are written as one unhyphenated word. Practice writing the following forms without hyphens, then mark the tense-marker syllable.

  1. ni + li + soma → _____
  2. u + li + nunua → _____
  3. a + li + kula → _____
  4. tu + li + fanya kazi → _____ (two words: verb + object)

Grammar Instruction

16.1 The past tense marker -li-

Purpose: narrate completed actions and events that took place before now.

The past tense marker -li- occupies the same position in the verb template that you have already used for present -na- and future -ta-. The template remains stable — only the tense-marker slot changes:

subject marker + tense/aspect marker + object marker if needed + verb root + final vowel

SubjectSubject markerPast formMeaning
mimini-nilisomaI studied
weweu-ulisomayou studied
yeyea-alisomahe/she studied
sisitu-tulisomawe studied
ninyim-mlisomayou (pl.) studied
waowa-walisomathey studied
  • Nilikwenda dukani jana. — I went to the store yesterday. ni-li-kwend-a
  • Alipika chakula kizuri. — She/he cooked good food. a-li-pik-a
  • Tulisoma Kiswahili wiki iliyopita. — We studied Swahili last week. tu-li-som-a
Language Note — The verb template stays the same

You already know how to build present and future verbs. The past follows exactly the same construction principle — only the tense-marker slot changes, from -na- or -ta- to -li-. If you can build ninasoma and nitasoma, you can build nilisoma by the same process. This is why the verb template introduced in Chapter 3 and used throughout the course is worth knowing well: each new tense adds to a system you already understand rather than requiring you to learn an unrelated new pattern.

16.2 Past negation with -ku-

Purpose: say what did not happen, using the correct negative subject markers and the negative past marker.

Negative past verbs use the negative subject markers you learned for present negation in Chapter 7, combined with the negative past tense marker -ku- in place of -li-. Unlike present negation, the final vowel does not change to -i — the past negative keeps the ordinary final vowel -a.

SubjectNegative subject markerNegative past formMeaning
mimisi-sikwendaI did not go
wewehu-hukwendayou did not go
yeyeha-hakwendahe/she did not go
sisihatu-hatukwendawe did not go
ninyiham-hamkwendayou (pl.) did not go
waohawa-hawakwendathey did not go

Note the spelling: negative subject marker + ku + root. Before a consonant-initial root such as -kwend-, the ku and the root's initial consonant combine directly in writing: sikwenda, not sikuenda.

  • Sikula asubuhi. — I did not eat in the morning. si-ku-l-a
  • Hakusoma jana. — He/she did not study yesterday. ha-ku-som-a
  • Hatukununua chochote. — We did not buy anything. hatu-ku-nunu-a
Language Note — Two different ku- forms

Swahili has two separate elements that are both spelled ku-, and beginners frequently confuse them:

  • Infinitive ku- is the prefix that turns a verb root into a dictionary form: ku-soma = "to study."
  • Negative past -ku- is a tense marker that appears inside a finite negative verb, after the negative subject marker: si-ku-soma = "I did not study."

These two morphemes look identical but serve entirely different grammatical functions. The infinitive ku- never combines with a subject marker; the negative past -ku- always does. Use the position in the word — and whether a subject marker precedes it — to tell them apart.

16.3 Past questions

Purpose: ask what someone did, where they went, or what happened.

Past questions use the same question words you already know — nini, wapi, nani, lini — combined with a past-tense verb rather than a present or future one.

  • Ulifanya nini jana? — What did you do yesterday?
  • Mlienda wapi wikendi iliyopita? — Where did you (pl.) go last weekend?
  • Alikutana na nani? — Who did he/she meet?

16.4 Object markers inside past-tense verbs

Purpose: refer back to something or someone already mentioned, without repeating the full noun.

Just as with present and future verbs, an object marker may occupy the slot immediately before the verb root when the referent is clear from context or discourse. The object marker's position and form do not change with tense.

  • Nilikinunua jana. — I bought it yesterday. (referring to a KI-class item already mentioned) ni-li-ki-nunu-a
  • Tuliwaona wikendi iliyopita. — We saw them last weekend. (referring to people already mentioned) tu-li-wa-on-a
  • Sikumwona jana. — I did not see him/her yesterday. si-ku-mw-on-a

As in earlier chapters, use an object marker only when the discourse has already established the referent. Do not insert an object marker mechanically for every English object pronoun.

Language Building Block
subject marker + -li- + object marker if needed + verb root + final vowel
Nilikinunua jana.
I bought it yesterday. — ni- (I) + -li- (past) + -ki- (it, KI-class) + -nunu- (buy) + -a
Hatukununua chochote.
We did not buy anything. — hatu- (we, negative) + -ku- (negative past) + -nunu- (buy) + -a

16.5 Sequencing past events

Purpose: connect several past events into a clear chronological account.

To narrate more than one event, repeat the past-tense pattern for each verb and connect the clauses with sequencing words. This produces a clear, learner-appropriate narrative without requiring more advanced narrative tenses.

  • Kwanza niliamka saa moja asubuhi. Kisha nilikunywa chai. Halafu nilikwenda kazini. Mwishowe nilirudi nyumbani jioni. First I woke up at seven o'clock. Then I drank tea. Then I went to work. Finally I returned home in the evening.
Common Mistake — Overusing sequential -ka- at this stage

Note: Authentic Swahili narratives often use a form called sequential -ka- after an initial -li- verb to continue a story (for example, nikaenda, "and then I went"). This is a genuine and common feature of natural narration.

At this stage: Sequential -ka- is not yet part of your productive grammar — it is introduced in Chapter 19. For now, build your narratives by repeating -li- in each clause and connecting them with kwanza, kisha, halafu, baadaye, and mwishowe. This produces clear, correct, and natural-sounding Swahili at your current level.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: Amara na Kofi wanakutana darasani Jumatatu asubuhi. Wanazungumza kuhusu wikendi iliyopita.

(Setting: Amara and Kofi meet in class on Monday morning. They talk about last weekend.)

KOFIHabari za asubuhi, Amara. Wikendi ilikuwaje?Good morning, Amara. How was the weekend?
AMARANzuri sana, asante. Nilifanya mambo mengi. Na wewe, ulifanya nini?Very good, thank you. I did a lot of things. And you, what did you do?
KOFIJumamosi nilikaa nyumbani tu. Lakini wewe, niambie — ulienda wapi?On Saturday I just stayed home. But you, tell me — where did you go?
AMARAJumamosi asubuhi nilienda sokoni na dada yangu. Kwanza tulinunua matunda na mboga. Kisha tulikutana na rafiki yetu, Zawadi.Saturday morning I went to the market with my sister. First we bought fruit and vegetables. Then we met our friend Zawadi.
KOFIMlifanya nini baadaye?What did you do afterward?
AMARAHalafu tulipika chakula pamoja nyumbani kwangu. Tulikula saa mbili mchana. Chakula kilikuwa kizuri sana.Then we cooked food together at my place. We ate at eight o'clock in the morning — midday. The food was very good.
KOFINa Jumapili? Ulifanya nini?And Sunday? What did you do?
AMARAJumapili sikwenda popote. Nilipumzika nyumbani. Niliandika barua pepe kwa familia yangu Dar es Salaam. Sikufanya kazi kabisa.Sunday I did not go anywhere. I rested at home. I wrote an email to my family in Dar es Salaam. I did not work at all.
KOFIMwishowe, ulilala saa ngapi jana usiku?Finally, what time did you go to sleep last night?
AMARANililala mapema — saa nne usiku. Nilikuwa nimechoka!I went to sleep early — ten o'clock at night. I was tired!

(Kofi anacheka. / Kofi laughs.)

KOFIWikendi yako ilikuwa na shughuli nyingi kuliko yangu!Your weekend had many more activities than mine!

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • Every past-tense verb includes a subject marker, -li-, the correct root, and the final vowel.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Sharing daily experiences

Asking someone how their day or weekend went, and describing your own in return, is a common way of maintaining relationships in many Swahili-speaking communities, much as it is in many English-speaking ones. The exchange often begins with a general question such as Ilikuwaje? ("How was it?") before moving into specific details. This chapter's personal narratives should reflect ordinary, varied experiences — work, study, family time, errands, rest — rather than only travel or leisure activities, since daily life for most people includes a mixture of routine and occasional events.

What counts as noteworthy to share, and how much detail is appropriate, varies by relationship, setting, and individual communication style. A close friend may expect more detail than a casual acquaintance or a workplace colleague.

Culture in Context — Explaining absence or delay

The negative past tense is frequently used in everyday Swahili to explain why something did not happen as expected — for example, why someone did not call, did not attend an event, or did not complete a task on time. Offering a brief, honest explanation (as Neema does in Maandishi 2) is a common courteous practice in both personal and professional communication. The level of detail expected in such an explanation varies by relationship and context.

Regional Variation — Narrating shared time

The vocabulary and time expressions used to describe weekends and daily schedules can differ slightly by country and setting — for example, the specific days considered part of "the weekend" for work or school purposes are not identical everywhere, and some communities organize the work week differently. This chapter uses widely understood Standard Swahili time expressions. When a specific institution or community uses a different schedule, that detail should be learned in context rather than assumed from this course alone.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing infinitive ku- with negative past -ku-

Error: Treating kusoma (to study) and the -ku- in sikusoma (I did not study) as the same element with the same function

Correction: The infinitive ku- attaches directly to a bare root to form the dictionary form of a verb: ku-soma. The negative past -ku- is a tense marker that appears only after a negative subject marker inside a complete finite verb: si-ku-soma. They are spelled the same but occupy different positions and serve different grammatical functions. Check whether a subject marker comes before the ku-: if it does, you are looking at the negative past marker, not the infinitive.

Common Mistake — Using -li- in a negative sentence

Error: Sililisoma. or Silisoma. (combining a negative subject marker with -li-)

Correction: Sikusoma. — Swahili negative past verbs replace -li- with -ku- entirely; the two tense markers are not combined. Use the negative subject marker (si-, hu-, ha-, hatu-, ham-, hawa-) together with -ku-, never with -li-.

Common Mistake — Adding "did not" as a separate word

Error: Mimi hapana kwenda jana. (translating "I did not go" word for word with a separate negative word)

Correction: Sikwenda jana. — Swahili negation is built into the verb itself through the negative subject marker and the negative tense marker. There is no separate word equivalent to English "did not." Build the negative form as one complete word: negative subject marker + -ku- + root + final vowel.

Common Mistake — Stacking too many sequencing words in one sentence

Error: Kwanza kisha halafu nilikwenda sokoni. (using three sequencing words for one event)

Correction: Kwanza nilikwenda sokoni. — Use one sequencing word per event or transition, placed at the start of the clause it introduces. Reserve kwanza for the first event, and choose one connector — kisha or halafu — for each subsequent step, ending with mwishowe for the last event.

Common Mistake — Forgetting the subject marker in a past verb

Error: Lisoma jana. (dropping the subject marker)

Correction: Alisoma jana. — Every finite Swahili verb, in every tense, requires a subject marker. The tense marker -li- cannot stand alone at the beginning of a verb. Always begin the verb with the subject marker that matches the doer of the action.

Chapter Review — Sehemu B

Question 1 (past-tense forms):

  • aliandika
  • tulinunua
  • uliona
  • walisafiri

Question 2 (negation):

  • Sikula chakula cha jioni.
  • Hakwenda kazini.
  • Hatukupiga simu.

Question 3 (error correction):

  • Sililisoma jana. — Error: -li- and negative marking cannot combine. Correction: Sikusoma jana.
  • Mimi hapana kwenda sokoni. — Error: negation is not a separate word. Correction: Sikwenda sokoni.
  • Lifanya kazi jana. — Error: missing subject marker. Correction: Alifanya kazi jana.

Question 4: Infinitive ku- attaches to a bare root to form a verb's dictionary form (kusoma, "to study") and never follows a subject marker. Negative past -ku- is a tense marker that always follows a negative subject marker inside a complete finite verb (sikusoma, "I did not study").

Question 5: Kwanza tulisoma. Kisha tulikula. Mwishowe tulipumzika.

Looking Ahead

Unachojua sasa — What you know now

You can now narrate completed events from yesterday or last week, in the correct order, and say what did not happen. You have added the past tense marker -li- to the present and future markers you already knew, learned to build accurate negative past forms with -ku-, and practiced telling infinitive ku- apart from this new negative marker. You have also used object markers inside past verbs and connected several events using kwanza, kisha, halafu, baadaye, and mwishowe. These tools give you full control of present, future, and past time reference — the core of the Swahili verb system at this level.

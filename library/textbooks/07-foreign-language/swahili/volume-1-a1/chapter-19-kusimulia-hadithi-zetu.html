Telling Our Stories · Speak and Write Swahili: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Narrate a personal or imagined experience made up of several connected events.
  2. Use the perfect marker -me- to describe a current state resulting from a completed action.
  3. Distinguish the perfect -me- from the past -li- and explain what each communicates in a story.
  4. Recognize the sequential marker -ka- in narrative reading and use it in tightly controlled sentence chains.
  5. Connect events using lakini, kwa sababu, kwa hiyo, kisha, and mwishowe.
  6. Maintain clear participant reference across a paragraph using pronouns, noun repetition, and object markers.
  7. Describe the people, places, and circumstances relevant to a story.
  8. Explain a result or reaction connected to a narrated event.
  9. Ask and answer follow-up questions about a story someone else has told.
  10. Write a coherent narrative of 180-220 words with a clear beginning, sequence, and outcome.

Communication Scenario

During a weekend gathering in Dar es Salaam, a group of friends take turns telling short stories about memorable days — a trip that went wrong, a surprise visit from family, a first day at a new job. Everyone listens closely and asks questions afterward. You want to join in: to tell a connected story about something that happened to you, or something you imagine happening to a character, with a clear beginning, a sequence of events, and an outcome that explains why the story matters. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to tell and write such a story in Swahili.

  1. Think about a memorable day from your own life, or a short story you could invent. What happened first? What happened next? How did it end, and how did you feel about it? Sketch the shape of the story in your mind before you begin the chapter.
  2. In English, storytellers often shift between past tense, present tense for dramatic effect, and the present perfect ("I have never seen anything like it"). Swahili narrators also choose among several verb forms — but the choices are not identical to English ones. As you work through this chapter, notice how Swahili storytellers use -li-, -me-, and, in a tightly controlled way, -ka- to shape a narrative.

Pronunciation and Spelling

Mdundo wa masimulizi — Narrative intonation and pausing

Connected storytelling in Swahili, as in English, uses intonation to hold a listener's attention. A falling pitch typically signals the end of a completed event; a slight rise or sustained pitch at a connector such as kisha or halafu signals that more is coming. Practice reading a short sequence of sentences aloud, pausing briefly after each connector rather than rushing through it.

Sound Focus — Pausing at connectors

English speakers sometimes read connected Swahili sentences at a single unbroken speed, which can make a story difficult for a listener to follow. Insert a brief pause after connectors like kisha, halafu, lakini, and mwishowe — long enough to mark a new step in the story, but not so long that it breaks the flow. Practice: Nilifika nyumbani... kisha... nilipika chakula... halafu... nililala mapema.

Kutofautisha alama za wakati — Discriminating tense markers by ear

This chapter requires you to distinguish three tense/aspect markers in connected speech: past -li-, perfect -me-, and sequential -ka-. All three occupy the same position in the verb template, directly after the subject marker, so careful listening is essential.

ni-li-soma (I studied)  ·  ni-me-soma (I have studied)  ·  ni-ka-soma (and then I studied — sequential, narrative only)

Tahajia ya sentensi ndefu — Punctuating longer connected sentences

As sentences grow longer with connectors, correct comma and full-stop placement becomes more important for clarity. Use a comma before lakini when it joins two complete clauses, and begin a new sentence at a clear narrative break, such as after mwishowe.

  1. Nilifika shuleni mapema _____ nilikuwa nimechoka. (Punctuate and add the connector.)
  2. Mvua ilianza kunyesha _____ tulikimbia nyumbani. (Punctuate and add the connector.)
  3. Write one sentence using kwa sababu and correctly punctuate it.

Grammar Instruction

19.1 The perfect marker -me-

Purpose: describe a current state that results from a completed action.

You have used the past marker -li- since Chapter 16 to narrate completed events. This chapter introduces the perfect marker -me-, which occupies the same position in the verb template but communicates something different: not simply that an action happened, but that its result is relevant right now.

SwahiliStructureMeaning
Nimemaliza kazi.ni-me-maliz-aI have finished the work. (It is now done.)
Amefika.a-me-fik-aHe/she has arrived. (He/she is here now.)
Tumechoka.tu-me-chok-aWe have become tired. (We are tired now.)
Hujambo? Sijambo, lakini nimechelewa.si-ja-mbo... ni-me-chelew-aHow are you? I'm fine, but I am late. (recognition of -ja- negative pattern)
Language Note — -me- is not simply the English present perfect

English speakers often want to translate every English present-perfect sentence ("I have eaten," "she has traveled") directly with -me-. This works in many cases, but the Swahili perfect specifically marks that a resulting state is true right now. Amefika means "he has arrived" in the sense of "he is here now" — if he arrived yesterday and left again, Swahili speakers would generally use the past -li- instead: Alifika jana. Focus on current relevance, not simply on whether an English sentence uses "has" or "have."

19.2 Past -li- and perfect -me- in the same story

Purpose: choose the correct marker depending on whether an event is simply reported or its result is currently relevant.

Most narrative sentences use -li- to report what happened, in the order it happened. A storyteller may switch to -me- at a moment when the current, ongoing result of an event matters to the story — often near the end, or when describing the situation the speaker is in as a consequence of what happened.

  • Niliamka mapema, nikaoga, na nikala chakula. I woke up early, washed, and ate. (a sequence of completed events)
  • Sasa nimechoka kwa sababu nililala kidogo tu. Now I am tired because I only slept a little. (the current state of tiredness, resulting from a past event)

Do not switch between -li- and -me- at random. Use -li- for the events themselves, in order, and reserve -me- for describing a present condition that those events produced.

19.3 Sequential -ka- in tightly controlled narrative use

Purpose: recognize the sequential marker in narrative reading and use it in short, controlled chains of connected events sharing one subject.

Swahili narrators often link a sequence of events that share the same subject using the sequential marker -ka- after the first verb has established the tense with -li-. Once the time frame is set, later verbs in the chain replace -li- with -ka-, producing a smooth "and then... and then..." effect without repeating -li- on every verb.

Language Building Block
orientation (li-) + event sequence (ka- chain) + result or reflection (me- or li-)
Niliamka mapema, nikaoga, nikala chakula, na baadaye nikaenda shuleni.
I woke up early, then washed, then ate, and later went to school. — ni-li-amk-a sets the past time frame; ni-ka-og-a, ni-ka-l-a, and ni-ka-end-a continue the chain without repeating -li-.
Mwishowe, nimefika salama.
In the end, I have arrived safely. — the story closes with -me- to mark the current, relevant result.
Language Note — -ka- is a narrative tool, not a replacement for -li-

Do not use -ka- on every verb in a past story, and do not use it as the first verb of a narrative. Sequential -ka- depends on a preceding -li- verb (or another established time frame) to anchor the timeline; it continues a chain that another tense has already begun. At this stage, use -ka- only in short, controlled chains of two or three linked actions performed by the same subject, immediately following a -li- verb. Recognize -ka- more broadly in reading, where it appears extensively in longer authentic narratives.

19.4 Connectors for cause, contrast, and result

Purpose: link events and explain why something happened, what stood in contrast, or what followed as a result.

  • Nilitaka kwenda pwani, lakini mvua ilinyesha sana. I wanted to go to the coast, but it rained heavily. (contrast)
  • Nilichelewa kwa sababu basi halikufika kwa wakati. I was late because the bus did not arrive on time. (cause)
  • Basi halikufika; kwa hiyo, nilitembea hadi shuleni. The bus did not arrive; therefore, I walked to school. (result)

These connectors were introduced earlier in the course (Chapter 12 for kwa sababu and kwa hiyo; Chapter 7 for basic sequencing). This chapter expands their use to organize a full narrative, alongside lakini.

19.5 Maintaining participant reference across a paragraph

Purpose: keep a story's people and things clearly identified from sentence to sentence.

Once a person or thing has been introduced by name or full noun phrase, later sentences can refer back using an independent pronoun, a subject marker alone, an object marker, or a repeated noun for clarity. Choosing among these options — rather than repeating the full name in every sentence — is part of writing a coherent paragraph.

  • Rafiki yangu Amina alinipigia simu. Alisema kwamba amefika Dar es Salaam. My friend Amina called me. She said that she has arrived in Dar es Salaam. (subject marker a- continues reference to Amina without repeating her name)
  • Nilimwona Baraka sokoni. Nilimsalimia, na tukazungumza kidogo. I saw Baraka at the market. I greeted him, and we talked a little. (object marker -m- refers back to Baraka)

Do not repeat a full name in every sentence of a paragraph once the referent is clear from context. Do not switch pronouns or subject markers in a way that leaves the listener uncertain who is doing what.

Muktadha: Amara na Kofi, marafiki tuliowafahamu katika Sura ya 4, wanakutana tena baada ya wiki mbili. Kofi anamuuliza Amara kuhusu safari yake ya hivi karibuni.

(Setting: Amara and Kofi, the friends from Chapter 4, meet again after two weeks. Kofi asks Amara about her recent trip.)

KOFIAmara! Umerudi lini kutoka safari yako?Amara! When did you get back from your trip?
AMARANimerudi jana usiku. Nilikwenda Zanzibar wiki iliyopita.I got back last night. I went to Zanzibar last week.
KOFIVizuri sana! Niambie, ilikuwaje?Very good! Tell me, how was it?
AMARASiku ya kwanza, nilifika mapema asubuhi. Nikaingia hotelini, nikaweka mizigo yangu, na nikaenda pwani mara moja.On the first day, I arrived early in the morning. I went into the hotel, put down my luggage, and went to the beach right away.
KOFINzuri! Kisha ulifanya nini?Nice! Then what did you do?
AMARANilikutana na wenyeji wazuri sana. Tulizungumza, tukala samaki, na tukatazama machweo pamoja. Nilifurahi sana siku hiyo.I met some very nice locals. We talked, ate fish, and watched the sunset together. I was very happy that day.
KOFILakini nasikia kulikuwa na tatizo siku ya pili?But I hear there was a problem on the second day?
AMARANdiyo! Nilipanga kwenda Kisiwa cha Prison Island, lakini mvua ilianza kunyesha sana asubuhi. Kwa hiyo, nilingoja hotelini hadi mchana.Yes! I planned to go to Prison Island, but it started raining heavily in the morning. So, I waited at the hotel until midday.
KOFIUlishangaa? Ulisikitika?Were you surprised? Were you sad?
AMARANilisikitika kidogo, lakini sikuwa na wasiwasi. Mwishowe, mvua ilisimama, nikapanda mashua, na nikafika kisiwani jioni.I was a little sad, but I wasn't worried. In the end, the rain stopped, I boarded a boat, and I arrived on the island in the evening.
KOFIKwa hiyo, safari yako imekuwaje kwa ujumla?So, how has your trip been overall?
AMARAImekuwa safari nzuri sana. Nimechoka kidogo, lakini nimefurahi sana. Nataka kurudi tena mwaka ujao.It has been a very good trip. I am a little tired, but I am very happy. I want to go back again next year.

Vocabulary note: wenyeji = locals, hosts; machweo = sunset; kisiwa (pl. visiwa) = island; wasiwasi = worry, anxiety; imekuwaje = how has it been (perfect of kuwa + question word). These are recognition items at this stage.

  1. Soma mfano. Reread Maandishi 1 (Safari ya Zawadi). Underline every -li- verb in one color, every -ka- verb in a second color, and every -me- verb in a third color. Notice where each is used.

Write a connected narrative of 180-220 words with: a clear opening using a time expression, a sequence of events using -li- and at least one controlled -ka- chain, at least two connectors (lakini, kwa sababu, or kwa hiyo), a description of at least one person or place, at least one reaction or feeling, and a closing that uses -me- to state a current result.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • The story has a clear opening that establishes the time frame.
  • Each -ka- verb follows an established -li- verb and shares the same subject.
  • The perfect -me- is used only for a current, presently relevant state — not for every completed action.
  • At least two connectors from this chapter (lakini, kwa sababu, kwa hiyo) are used correctly.
  • Participant reference is clear: names are not repeated unnecessarily, and pronouns or subject/object markers are used naturally.
  • Noun-class agreement is correct throughout, including possessives, adjectives, and demonstratives from earlier chapters.
  • Verbs are written normally, without pedagogical hyphens.
  • Spelling, capitalization, and punctuation are correct, including paragraph breaks at natural narrative divisions.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Oral storytelling traditions

Storytelling holds an important place in many Swahili-speaking communities, taking diverse forms across regions, generations, and settings. Traditional oral narratives, including hadithi told by elders to younger family members, exist alongside everyday conversational storytelling, radio and television narratives, written literature, and contemporary social-media storytelling.

Do not treat any single narrative style, structure, or opening formula as representative of all Swahili oral or literary tradition. Storytelling purposes vary — entertainment, moral instruction, historical record, humor, and everyday social bonding are all common purposes across different communities and contexts.

Culture in Context — Listening and responding to a story

Active listening matters in Swahili conversational storytelling. Listeners often provide short verbal responses — such as brief acknowledgments or follow-up questions — while a story is being told, rather than remaining silent until the end. Showing engagement through appropriate follow-up questions, as Kofi does in the Model Dialogue, is a natural and expected part of participating in a conversation, not an interruption.

Regional Variation — Narrative style

The density of sequential -ka- chains and other narrative devices in spoken storytelling can vary by speaker, region, and register. Some speakers use extended -ka- chains freely in casual spoken narration; formal written Swahili and news narration may use -ka- more sparingly or rely more heavily on -li- throughout. The tightly controlled pattern taught in this chapter reflects a widely usable beginner strategy, not the only acceptable narrative style.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using -ka- as the first verb of a narrative

Error: Nikaamka mapema. Nikaoga. (starting a story with -ka- and no preceding -li- verb)

Correction: Niliamka mapema, nikaoga. — Sequential -ka- must follow an established time frame, usually set by an initial -li- verb. A story cannot open with -ka- because there is no anchoring tense for the sequence to continue.

Common Mistake — Using -me- for every completed action

Error: Nimeamka mapema jana, nimeoga, na nimeenda shuleni. (using -me- throughout a narrated sequence of past events)

Correction: Niliamka mapema jana, nikaoga, na nikaenda shuleni. — When narrating a sequence of completed past events in order, use -li- to open the sequence and -ka- for the events that follow, in controlled use. Reserve -me- for a currently relevant result, typically at the end of the story or when describing a present state.

Common Mistake — Breaking the -ka- chain with a new subject

Error: Nilifika sokoni, nikanunua mboga, akaenda nyumbani. (switching from ni- to a- mid-chain without clarifying who "he/she" is)

Correction: Nilifika sokoni, nikanunua mboga, nikaenda nyumbani. — A sequential -ka- chain should generally continue with the same subject established by the initial -li- verb. If the subject genuinely changes, restate the new subject clearly rather than relying on the chain alone.

Common Mistake — Overusing full names instead of pronouns or markers

Error: Juma alienda dukani. Juma alinunua mkate. Juma alirudi nyumbani. (repeating the full name in every sentence)

Correction: Juma alienda dukani. Alinunua mkate. Kisha alirudi nyumbani. — Once a participant is clearly established, later sentences can rely on the subject marker alone, or on yeye for emphasis, rather than repeating the full name. Repeated full names throughout a paragraph read as unnatural in Swahili narrative writing.

Common Mistake — Confusing kwa sababu and kwa hiyo

Error: Basi halikufika kwa hiyo lilikuwa limejaa. (using kwa hiyo where a cause is being given)

Correction: Basi halikufika kwa sababu lilikuwa limejaa. — Kwa sababu introduces a reason or cause ("because"); kwa hiyo introduces a result ("therefore," "so"). Check whether the clause you are adding explains why something happened or describes what happened as a consequence.

You can now tell a connected story with a clear beginning, sequence, and outcome.

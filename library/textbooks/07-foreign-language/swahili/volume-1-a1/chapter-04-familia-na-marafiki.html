Familia na Marafiki

Family and Friends · Speak and Write Swahili: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name immediate and extended family members and express relationships using common Swahili kinship vocabulary.
  2. Use possessive stems -angu, -ako, -ake, -etu, -enu, and -ao with M-WA and N-class nouns to express ownership and belonging.
  3. Understand that possessive agreement is controlled by the noun that is possessed, not by the person who possesses it.
  4. Recognize N-class nouns as a major Swahili noun class encountered through high-frequency family and relationship vocabulary.
  5. Describe people using common adjectives with introductory animate agreement.
  6. Use kuwa na to state what someone has or how many people are in a family.
  7. Ask and state ages using Ana umri gani? and related patterns.
  8. Pronounce prenasalized consonants — mb, nd, ng, nj — and consonant clusters mw accurately.
  9. Write a connected paragraph of several sentences describing family members or a social network.

Communication Scenario

A friend you met at a Swahili language class in Nairobi shows you a photograph of her family. She introduces each person by name, relationship, and age, and describes what they are like. You want to do the same — to point to the people who matter to you and say, in Swahili, who they are, how they are related to you, how old they are, and what kind of people they are. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to describe your own family or chosen social network in connected Swahili sentences.

  1. Think about how you refer to people close to you. Do you use the same word for "aunt" regardless of whether she is your mother's sister or your father's sister? Many languages make this distinction. As you work through the chapter, notice how Swahili kinship terms work and how social relationships sometimes extend the meaning of family words.
  2. In English, "my" does not change depending on what you possess — "my book," "my friend," "my parents" all use the same word. Swahili works differently: the word for "my" varies depending on the noun class of the item or person you are talking about. Watch for this pattern as you study possession in this chapter.

Pronunciation and Spelling

Konsonanti zinazoanza na sauti ya pua — Prenasalized consonants

In this chapter's vocabulary you will encounter several consonants that are preceded by a nasal sound — either m or n. These are called prenasalized consonants. They count as a single sound in Swahili, not as two separate consonants. Do not pause between the nasal and the following consonant; pronounce them as one smooth, rapid sound.

CombinationApproximate soundExampleMeaning
mbLike m + b blended quickly — begins with lip closurembilitwo
ndLike n + d blended quickly — tongue tip at ridgendugusibling; relative
ngLike ng in English singer, blended into the next vowelngapihow many
njLike n + j blended quicklynjiapath; way (recognition item)
mwm immediately followed by w before a vowel — one quick syllablemwanason; child of
Sound Focus — Prenasalized consonants are one sound

English speakers sometimes try to pronounce nd in ndugu or mb in mbili as two distinct syllables: en-dugu or em-bili. This adds an extra syllable that does not belong. In Standard Swahili, mb, nd, ng, and nj are each a single consonant unit and form one syllable with the vowel that follows. Practice by saying the nasal and the following consonant without any separation: mbi-li, ndu-gu, mwa-na.

Grammar Instruction

4.1 The N-class — your second major noun class

Purpose: recognize N-class nouns through high-frequency family and relationship vocabulary, and understand how this class differs from M-WA and KI-VI.

You have already worked with two noun classes. In Chapter 1 you encountered M-WA nouns — nouns referring primarily to people, with singular prefix m-/mw- and plural prefix wa-/w-. In Chapter 2 you studied KI-VI nouns — mostly inanimate objects, with singular ki- and plural vi-. Now you meet the N-class, which is one of the largest noun classes in Swahili and contains a great many words for people, animals, languages, and everyday objects.

The N-class has two important features that make it distinctive:

Feature 1 — Prefix patterns vary. Some N-class nouns carry an n- or m- prefix visible on the word; others have no visible prefix at all. You cannot always identify an N-class noun from its shape alone. Always learn each noun with a class label.

Feature 2 — Singular and plural look the same. Most N-class nouns do not change between singular and plural. Context — and the agreement words around the noun — tells you whether you are talking about one or more than one.

Singular formPlural formMeaningNote
mamamamamotherNo visible n- prefix; animate agreement
babababafatherNo visible n- prefix; animate agreement
ndugundugusibling; relativend- is prenasalized; animate agreement
rafikimarafikifriend / friendsPlural borrows JI-MA ma- prefix; still takes animate agreement
jiranimajiranineighbor / neighborsPlural borrows JI-MA ma- prefix; still takes animate agreement
Language Note — N-class and animate agreement

When an N-class noun refers to a person or animal, it takes animate agreement — the same agreement markers used for M-WA nouns. This means that even though mama, rafiki, and ndugu are grammatically N-class, the words that agree with them — subject markers, possessives, adjectives — follow the M-WA pattern because they refer to people. You will see this clearly in the possessive and adjective sections below. Think of it as: class determines noun shape; meaning determines agreement when it refers to a person.

4.2 Possessive stems and M-WA agreement

Purpose: say whose something is — "my mother," "your friend," "our family."

In Swahili, possession is expressed by combining a possessive concord — a short prefix that echoes the noun class of the possessed noun — with a possessive stem that carries the meaning of "my," "your," "his/her," and so on. Together they form one word that follows the noun.

The possessive stems are:

Possessive stem Meaning
-angumy (belonging to me)
-akoyour (belonging to you, singular)
-akehis / her / its (belonging to him, her, or it)
-etuour (belonging to us)
-enuyour (belonging to you, plural)
-aotheir (belonging to them)

The possessive concord — the prefix that attaches before the stem — depends on the noun class of the thing possessed, not on the identity of the possessor. In this chapter, you are working with two controlled sets of agreement:

4.3 Animate agreement with N-class nouns referring to people

Purpose: understand why possessives with N-class people-nouns sometimes use w- concord for plurals.

You saw above that rafiki (friend) has the plural marafiki, and jirani (neighbor) has the plural majirani. These plurals use a ma- prefix borrowed from the JI-MA class — but they still refer to people. As a result, their agreement follows animate patterns.

  • rafiki yangu — my friend (singular N-class, concord y-) One friend → N-class concord applies
  • marafiki wangu — my friends (plural animate concord w-) More than one friend → animate plural concord applies because they are people
  • jirani yangu — my neighbor (singular N-class) One neighbor → N-class concord applies
  • majirani wangu — my neighbors (plural animate) More than one neighbor → animate plural concord applies

The principle to remember: when a noun refers to a person, animate agreement applies in the plural. Learn whether a noun's plural is a standard N-class form (no change) or a JI-MA-style form with ma-, and apply animate agreement to the plural when it refers to people.

4.4 Introductory adjective agreement with animate nouns

Purpose: describe people using common adjectives with correct agreement for M-WA nouns and N-class people-nouns.

Like possessives, Swahili adjectives agree with the noun they describe. For now, you are learning agreement with animate nouns — nouns that refer to people. The pattern is consistent with what you have already seen in the possessive section.

For M-WA singular animate nouns, adjective stems take the prefix m- (or mw- before a vowel):

  • mtu mzuri — a good/beautiful person M-WA singular noun + m- + adjective stem -zuri
  • mwalimu mrefu — a tall teacher M-WA singular noun + m- + adjective stem -refu
  • mtoto mdogo — a young/small child M-WA singular noun + m- + adjective stem -dogo

For M-WA plural animate nouns (and animate N-class plurals), adjective stems take the prefix wa- (or w- before a vowel):

  • watu wazuri — good/beautiful people M-WA plural noun + wa- + adjective stem -zuri
  • watoto wadogo — young/small children M-WA plural noun + wa- + adjective stem -dogo
  • marafiki wazuri — good friends Animate plural noun + wa- + adjective stem -zuri

N-class singular nouns referring to people use the same m-/mw- adjective prefix as M-WA singulars because of animate agreement:

  • mama mzuri — a good mother N-class singular noun + animate m- + adjective stem -zuri
  • rafiki mpole — a gentle friend N-class singular noun + animate m- + adjective stem -pole

4.5 Expressing possession and family with kuwa na

Purpose: say what someone has — family members, friends, relationships.

Kuwa na is the expression used to say that someone "has" something or someone. You will study the full verb kuwa in later chapters. For now, learn three controlled present-tense patterns using the subject markers you encountered in Chapter 3:

  • Nina dada mmoja. — I have one older sister. ni- (I) + -na + dada (older sister) + mmoja (one)
  • Ana watoto watatu. — He/she has three children. a- (he/she) + -na + watoto (children) + watatu (three)
  • Tuna familia kubwa. — We have a big family. tu- (we) + -na + familia (family) + kubwa (big)
  • Je, una ndugu? — Do you have siblings? je (question particle) + u- (you) + -na + ndugu (siblings)
  • Sina kaka. — I do not have an older brother. si- (negative I) + -na + kaka (older brother)
Language Note — kuwa na versus ni

Ni connects two nouns or noun phrases with a meaning of identity: Yeye ni mama yangu — She is my mother. Kuwa na expresses possession or having: Ana mtoto mmoja — She has one child. Do not use ni where Swahili requires kuwa na, and do not replace kuwa na with ni when expressing what someone has. These two constructions are not interchangeable, even though both can translate into "is" or "has" depending on the English context.

4.6 Asking and stating age

Purpose: ask how old someone is and give an age in response.

The standard way to ask age uses the question pattern Ana umri gani? — literally "He/she has what age?" — and the answer uses Ana miaka … — "He/she has … years."

  • Ana umri gani? — How old is he/she? a- (he/she) + -na + umri (age) + gani (what kind of?)
  • Ana miaka thelathini. — He/she is thirty years old. a- (he/she) + -na + miaka (years) + thelathini (thirty)
  • Una miaka mingapi? — How old are you? (How many years do you have?) u- (you) + -na + miaka (years) + mingapi (how many — M-MI class)
  • Nina miaka ishirini na mbili. — I am twenty-two years old. ni- (I) + -na + miaka (years) + ishirini na mbili (twenty-two)

The word miaka (years) is the plural of mwaka (year), an M-MI class noun. The question mingapi (how many?) shows M-MI class agreement — you will study M-MI fully in Chapter 6. Learn Una miaka mingapi? and Nina miaka … as productive patterns now.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: Amara na Kofi ni wanafunzi wenzake darasani. Wanazungumza wakati wa mapumziko. Amara anaonyesha picha ya familia yake kwenye simu yake.

(Setting: Amara and Kofi are classmates. They talk during a break. Amara shows a photo of her family on her phone.)

KOFIHabari za mchana, Amara.Good afternoon, Amara.
AMARANzuri sana, Kofi. Na wewe?Very well, Kofi. And you?
KOFINzuri, asante. Picha nzuri! Hao ni nani?Well, thank you. A nice photo! Who are those people?
AMARAHawa ni familia yangu. Huyu ni mama yangu. Jina lake ni Aisha. Yeye ni mzuri sana na mpole.These are my family. This is my mother. Her name is Aisha. She is very kind and gentle.
KOFINa huyu mtu mrefu ni nani?And who is this tall person?
AMARAHuyu ni baba yangu. Jina lake ni Hassan. Ana miaka hamsini na mbili.This is my father. His name is Hassan. He is fifty-two years old.
KOFIUna ndugu?Do you have siblings?
AMARANdiyo. Nina kaka mmoja na dada wawili. Huyu ni kaka yangu mkubwa, Tariq. Yeye ni mhodari sana — ni daktari.Yes. I have one older brother and two older sisters. This is my older brother Tariq. He is very capable — he is a doctor.
KOFIDada zako wana miaka mingapi?How old are your older sisters?

(Amara anafikiri kidogo. / Amara thinks for a moment.)

AMARADada yangu mkubwa, Nadia, ana miaka thelathini. Dada yangu mdogo, Layla, ana miaka ishirini na tano.My older sister Nadia is thirty years old. My younger sister Layla is twenty-five years old.
KOFIFamilia yako ni kubwa! Una marafiki wa karibu hapa Nairobi?Your family is big! Do you have close friends here in Nairobi?
AMARANdiyo, nina marafiki wazuri hapa. Wao ni watu wapole na wachangangamfu. Tunasoma Kiswahili pamoja!Yes, I have good friends here. They are gentle and lively people. We study Swahili together!
KOFIVizuri sana. Na familia yako iko Tanzania au Kenya?Very good. And is your family in Tanzania or Kenya?
AMARAFamilia yangu iko Dar es Salaam, Tanzania. Mimi niko Nairobi kwa masomo yangu.My family is in Dar es Salaam, Tanzania. I am in Nairobi for my studies.

Vocabulary note: huyu = this person (near demonstrative, animate); hawa = these people (animate plural); hao = those people (animate, away from speaker); wa karibu = close, nearby (in the context of "close friends"); masomo = studies. These are recognition items at this stage.

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension questions:

  2. Baba ya Amara ana miaka mingapi? (How old is Amara's father?)
  3. Tariq ni nani, na yeye ni nini kazi yake? (Who is Tariq, and what is his job?)
  4. Amara ana marafiki Nairobi? Wao ni watu wa aina gani? (Does Amara have friends in Nairobi? What kind of people are they?)
  5. Write the phrase "my family" as it appears in the dialogue. What possessive form is used, and why?

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • Each possessive form matches the noun class of the noun being possessed — not the possessor.
  • N-class singular nouns take y- concord (yangu, yako, yake…); M-WA nouns take w- concord (wangu, wako, wake…).

  • All prenasalized consonants are spelled correctly — mb, nd, ng, nj — with no extra vowel added.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Family vocabulary and household structures

Swahili kinship vocabulary reflects a wide range of family and household structures. The word ndugu illustrates this breadth well: it can mean biological sibling, close relative, compatriot, or fellow community member, depending on the context. In some communities, ndugu is used as a general term of solidarity among people who share a national or social identity — comparable to "brother" or "sister" in certain English-speaking contexts.

Words like mama and baba can also extend beyond biological parenthood. An aunt who raised you, an older neighbor who looked after you, or a mentor may be referred to by kinship terms in many East African communities. Learners should understand that Swahili kinship terms often encompass a broader social circle than their direct English translations suggest.

Household and family structures vary considerably across and within Tanzania, Kenya, and other Swahili-speaking communities, and across generations, regions, religions, and socioeconomic contexts. Present these terms as tools for communication, not as a single model of how families must be organized.

Culture in Context — Asking about age

Asking someone's age is handled differently in different social settings. Among friends or in an informal context, Una miaka mingapi? or Ana umri gani? is a natural question. In more formal settings, or when addressing an elder, an indirect approach — observing or commenting on life stage rather than asking directly — may feel more natural. The expression mtu mzima (adult) and the respectful term mzee (elder) are useful social tools that do not require knowing someone's exact age.

When in doubt about the appropriateness of an age question, follow the lead of those around you in the social setting. These are guidelines for context-sensitivity, not absolute rules.

Regional Variation — Kinship terms

Kinship vocabulary shows meaningful variation across Swahili-speaking communities. The term for "aunt" differs depending on the family side: shangazi commonly refers to a father's sister in Standard Swahili, while a mother's sister may be called mama mdogo (younger mother) or mama mkubwa (older mother) depending on birth order relative to one's mother. mjomba typically refers to a mother's brother. These distinctions are not uniformly observed across all communities, and some speakers use shangazi more broadly for all aunts. Learn the standard forms taught here as your productive base, and be aware that the specific term used may vary by speaker and community.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using the wrong possessive concord

Error: mama wangu (using M-WA concord w- with an N-class noun)

Correction: mama yangu — The possessive concord is controlled by the noun class of the thing possessed, not by the possessor. mama is an N-class noun, so its possessive uses y- concord: yangu, yako, yake. The M-WA concord w- is for M-WA class nouns: mtoto wangu, mwalimu wako. Always check the class of the noun you are talking about before choosing a possessive form.

Common Mistake — Treating "my" as a word that never changes

Error: Using yangu for all nouns because "my" is always the same in English

Correction: In Swahili, the form of "my" depends on the noun class of the possessed noun. yangu is correct for N-class nouns (mama yangu, rafiki yangu), but M-WA nouns require wangu (mtoto wangu, mwalimu wangu). In later chapters, you will encounter additional concord forms for other noun classes. The possessive stem -angu is always the same; only the concord prefix before it changes.

Common Mistake — Forgetting adjective agreement

Error: mama zuri (using a bare adjective stem without the agreement prefix)

Correction: mama mzuri — Swahili adjectives must carry an agreement prefix that matches the noun class. For animate nouns referring to one person (singular), use m- (or mw- before a vowel): mzuri, mrefu, mpole. For animate nouns referring to more than one person (plural), use wa-: wazuri, warefu, wapole. The adjective stem alone is not a complete word in this context.

Common Mistake — Confusing ni and kuwa na when expressing age

Error: Ana ni miaka thelathini. (inserting ni before the object of kuwa na)

Correction: Ana miaka thelathini. — The pattern for expressing age uses kuwa na: subject marker + -na + years. Do not insert ni between the verb and the object. Ni links two noun phrases for identity; kuwa na expresses having or possession. These two constructions work differently.

Common Mistake — Adding an extra vowel to prenasalized consonants

Error: Pronouncing ndugu as en-dugu or ndugu with a pause after the n

Correction: ndugu — Prenasalized consonants such as nd, mb, ng, and nj are single sound units in Swahili. They do not have a separate syllable before them. The nd in ndugu forms one onset, not two syllables. Practice moving directly from the nasal into the following consonant and then into the vowel: ndu-gu.

Common Mistake — Assuming N-class nouns change form in the plural

Error: Writing mamas or babas (applying English pluralization) or inventing a Swahili plural prefix

Correction: Most N-class nouns do not change their form between singular and plural: mama (one mother), mama (mothers in context). A few N-class people nouns — like rafiki / marafiki and jirani / majirani — do have a distinct plural. Learn each noun's plural explicitly rather than guessing from English or from another Swahili class.

Maisha ya Kila Siku

Everyday Life in Action · Speak and Write Swahili: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Combine home, routine, food, and shopping language in a single connected interaction.
  2. Ask and answer follow-up questions across several everyday topics without changing subject abruptly.
  3. Resolve a simple everyday misunderstanding using clarification and confirmation language.
  4. Describe personal preferences and practical needs using vocabulary from Chapters 6 through 9.
  5. Sustain a short transaction entirely in Swahili from greeting to closing.
  6. Apply present affirmative and negative verb forms accurately across new combinations of subject and verb.
  7. Select correct demonstrative, possessive, adjective, numeral, and quantity agreement across M-WA, KI-VI, N, M-MI, JI-MA, and U-class nouns.
  8. Write a connected 100–120-word description of everyday life that integrates several communicative domains.
About This Chapter — A Consolidation Chapter

Chapter 10 introduces no major new grammar. Its purpose is to bring together everything you learned in Chapters 6 through 9 — home and location, daily routines and present tense, food and object markers, and market transactions and quantity agreement — so that you can use these systems together in realistic, connected communication. You will see familiar vocabulary and grammar combined in new ways, not new rules.

Communication Scenario

It is Saturday morning. Your housemate, Furaha, is planning the day with you: deciding what to eat, organizing the apartment, and making a trip to the market before friends come over in the evening. To take part in the day's plans, you need to talk about your home, describe your usual Saturday routine, discuss what you want to eat, and help complete the shopping — all in the same conversation, moving naturally from one topic to the next. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to sustain exactly this kind of everyday, multi-topic interaction in Swahili.

  1. Think about a typical Saturday at your own home. In a single conversation with a housemate or family member, how many different topics might come up — chores, food, plans, errands — without anyone announcing a "topic change"? Real conversations move fluidly between domains. Notice how the dialogue and exercises in this chapter move between home, routine, food, and shopping without restarting.
  2. Up to this point, each chapter has focused on one main communicative domain at a time. This chapter asks you to draw on all of them together. As you work through the material, notice which earlier vocabulary and grammar you reach for automatically, and which you need to review.

Pronunciation and Spelling

Mdundo wa vitenzi vyenye mofimu nyingi — Fluency with multi-morpheme verbs

By this stage you have built verbs with subject markers, the present marker -na-, and object markers. This chapter's pronunciation focus is fluency: producing these forms smoothly in connected speech rather than pausing between each part.

ni-na-PEN-da  ·  tu-na-FA-nya  ·  a-na-VI-taka  ·  ha-tu-NU-nui  ·  wa-na-KI-soma

Sound Focus — Number and price discrimination

In market and price contexts, numbers that sound similar can cause real misunderstandings — for example kumi (ten) and kumi na moja (eleven), or mia moja (one hundred) and mia tatu (three hundred). Practice listening for the full number phrase rather than just the first word, and practice saying prices at a natural but clear pace, pausing slightly between the number and the currency word: shilingi … elfu tano.

Grammar Consolidation

Purpose: combine the present-tense verb template, negation, object marking, and noun-class agreement from Chapters 6–9 in connected, multi-topic communication. No new grammatical system is introduced in this chapter.

10.1 The present-tense template across domains

Review the productive template you have been building since Chapter 7:

Language Building Block — Review
subject marker + -na- + (object marker) + verb root + final vowel
Tunasafisha nyumba.
We are cleaning the house. (no object marker — full noun object stated)
Tunaisafisha.
We are cleaning it. (object marker -i- referring to nyumba, N-class)
Ninavitaka.
I want them. (object marker -vi- referring to KI-VI plural items, e.g. vyakula)

Notice that the same template now serves four different communicative domains — home, routine, food, and shopping — simply by changing the subject, verb root, and object referent. This is the consolidation skill of this chapter: applying one stable structure flexibly across topics.

10.2 Negation review across present-tense contexts

Review the present negative pattern from Chapter 7, now applied across all four domains:

  • Sitaki kusafisha leo. — I don't want to clean today. si- (negative I) + -taki (want, negative stem)
  • Hatununui nyama wiki hii. — We are not buying meat this week. ha-tu- (negative we) + -na- + -nunui (buy, negative stem)
  • Hapendi chai bila sukari. — He/she doesn't like tea without sugar. ha-a- → ha- (negative he/she) + -penda (negative stem -pendi)

Recall that the present negative changes the final vowel of the verb from -a to -i in the standard pattern (-taka → -taki, -penda → -pendi, -nunua → -nunui), in addition to using a negative subject marker. Review this pattern from Chapter 7 if it feels unfamiliar.

Language Note — Why consolidation matters

Real conversations rarely stay inside one noun class or one grammatical topic. A single exchange about Saturday plans might require N-class agreement for nyumba, KI-VI agreement for vyakula, and M-MI agreement for miti in the garden — all within a few sentences. The skill this chapter builds is not a new rule, but the flexibility to retrieve the right agreement quickly as the topic shifts. This flexibility is exactly what later chapters and real communication will require.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: Furaha na mwenzake wa nyumba, Imani, wanapanga Jumamosi yao. Wanazungumza nyumbani asubuhi.

(Setting: Furaha and her housemate Imani are planning their Saturday. They talk at home in the morning.)

FURAHAHabari za asubuhi, Imani! Tunafanya nini leo?Good morning, Imani! What are we doing today?
IMANINzuri, asante. Kwanza, ninataka kusafisha sebule. Halafu tunahitaji kwenda sokoni.Fine, thanks. First, I want to clean the living room. Then we need to go to the market.
FURAHASawa. Tunapika nini kwa chakula cha jioni? Marafiki wanakuja, sivyo?Okay. What are we cooking for dinner? Our friends are coming, right?
IMANINdiyo, wanakuja saa mbili usiku. Ninapenda kupika wali na mboga. Wewe unapenda nini?Yes, they're coming at eight in the evening. I like cooking rice and vegetables. What do you like?
FURAHANinapenda hilo. Lakini sipendi pilipili nyingi — unajua, sikula chakula chenye pilipili nyingi sana.I like that. But I don't like a lot of pepper — you know, I don't eat food with too much pepper.
IMANINaona. Hatutaongeza pilipili nyingi basi. Tunahitaji kununua nini sokoni?I see. We won't add a lot of pepper then. What do we need to buy at the market?
FURAHATunahitaji mchele, mboga, na matunda. Pia tunahitaji maji mengi kwa wageni.We need rice, vegetables, and fruit. We also need a lot of water for the guests.

(Wanafika sokoni. / They arrive at the market.)

MUUZAJIKaribu! Mnataka nini leo?Welcome! What do you want today?
IMANITunataka mchele kilo mbili na mboga kidogo. Bei gani?We want two kilos of rice and some vegetables. What's the price?
MUUZAJIMchele ni shilingi elfu tatu kwa kilo. Mboga zote ni shilingi elfu moja.Rice is three thousand shillings per kilo. All the vegetables are one thousand shillings.
FURAHASamahani, sema tena bei ya mchele?Sorry, say the price of the rice again?
MUUZAJIShilingi elfu tatu kwa kilo moja.Three thousand shillings per one kilo.
IMANISawa, tunanunua kilo mbili. Naomba pia matunda manne.Okay, we'll buy two kilos. I'd also like four pieces of fruit, please.
MUUZAJIKaribu sana. Jumla ni shilingi elfu kumi.You're very welcome. The total is ten thousand shillings.
FURAHAAsante sana. Sasa twende nyumbani tupike!Thank you very much. Now let's go home and cook!

Vocabulary note: muuzaji = seller; sivyo? = right? isn't that so? (recognition item); kwenye/chenye = with; having (recognition item); kuongeza = to add (recognition item); wageni = guests; jumla = total. These are recognition items at this stage and should not be required for productive use.

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension questions:

  1. Furaha na Imani wanafanya nini kwanza asubuhi? (What do Furaha and Imani do first in the morning?)
  2. Furaha hapendi nini katika chakula? (What does Furaha not like in food?)
  3. Wanahitaji kununua nini sokoni? Orodhesha vitu vinne. (What do they need to buy at the market? List four items.)
  4. Bei ya mchele ni shilingi ngapi kwa kilo? Jumla ni shilingi ngapi? (What is the price of rice per kilo? What is the total?)
  5. Marafiki wanakuja saa ngapi? (At what time are the friends coming?)

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • Every finite verb includes a correct subject marker and the appropriate tense marker.
  • Negative verbs use the correct negative subject marker and final-vowel change where required.
  • Object markers, when used, match the noun class of their referent.
  • Demonstratives, possessives, and adjectives agree with the noun class of the noun they modify.

  • The description moves between at least two domains (home, routine, food, shopping) without an abrupt or ungrammatical transition.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Appropriate requests and transaction closings

Everyday transactions in Swahili-speaking communities, like elsewhere, generally proceed more smoothly with a greeting before the request and a clear, polite closing. Expressions such as karibu (welcome) from a seller, and asante sana (thank you very much) from a customer, are common closing exchanges in many markets and shops across Tanzania and Kenya. The exact wording and warmth of these exchanges vary by setting, relationship, and individual style, and should not be treated as a fixed script that every transaction must follow.

Culture in Context — Respectful clarification

Asking someone to repeat information — a price, a time, an instruction — is a normal and expected part of communication, not a sign of failure. Phrases like Samahani, sema tena? (Sorry, say that again?) are used freely by speakers at every proficiency level, including native speakers in noisy or unfamiliar settings. Using clarification language confidently is itself a marker of growing communicative competence, not a weakness to hide.

Culture in Context — Avoiding assumptions about daily life

Household routines, food choices, and purchasing habits vary widely across and within Tanzania, Kenya, and other Swahili-speaking communities — by household size, income, location, religion, and personal preference. The Saturday described in this chapter's dialogue and readings represents one plausible household, not a universal pattern. When describing your own life or imagining someone else's, avoid assuming that any single routine is typical of "Swahili-speaking life" as a whole.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Forgetting the final-vowel change in negation

Error: Sipenda nyama. (keeping the affirmative final vowel -a in a negative verb)

Correction: Sipendi nyama. — In the standard present negative pattern for verbs ending in -a, the final vowel changes to -i: kupenda → sipendi, kutaka → sitaki, kununua → sinunui. Review this pattern from Chapter 7 whenever you build a negative sentence.

Common Mistake — Mismatched object marker and referent class

Error: Ninaitaka referring to vyakula (KI-VI plural), using the N-class object marker -i-

Correction: Ninavitaka. — The object marker must match the noun class of the noun it replaces, not the English pronoun "it" or "them." Vyakula is KI-VI plural, so the correct object marker is -vi-. Before choosing an object marker, identify the class of the noun being replaced.

Common Mistake — Switching agreement mid-sentence when the topic changes

Error: Ninataka mboga hii na matunda hiki. (using KI-VI demonstrative hiki for JI-MA plural matunda)

Correction: Ninataka mboga hii na matunda haya. — When a sentence moves between nouns of different classes — as often happens in multi-topic conversation — each noun keeps its own agreement. Slow down at class boundaries within a sentence and check each noun individually rather than carrying the previous noun's agreement forward.

Common Mistake — Treating large numbers as invariant when they affect totals

Error: Misreading or mis-stating a total because individual item prices were not added carefully (e.g., stating shilingi elfu tano when items total shilingi elfu kumi)

Correction: Always state each item's price clearly and confirm the total separately, as in the model dialogue: Jumla ni shilingi elfu kumi. Practice number discrimination actively in transactional speaking tasks, since price confusion is a common and consequential real-world error, not merely a grammar mistake.

  1. Halima anasafisha sebule, jikoni, na chumba chake. (Halima cleans the living room, kitchen, and her room.)
  2. Anaandika orodha kwa sababu hapendi kununua vitu vingi kwa wakati mmoja. (She writes a list because she doesn't like buying many things at once.)
  3. Mchele ni shilingi elfu mbili na nusu kwa kilo. (Rice is two thousand five hundred shillings per kilo.)
  4. Accept any correct example, e.g.: Sina muda mwingi — negative subject marker si- (I) with kuwa na, indicating lack of time.
  5. Maandishi 1 is a personal message (greeting, closing, first-person narration, signed by name). Maandishi 2 is a public notice/advertisement (business name, prices, hours, no personal greeting).

Looking Ahead

Unachojua sasa — What you know now

You can now move smoothly between talking about your home, your daily routine, food and preferences, and market transactions — all within a single connected conversation or piece of writing. You have consolidated the present-tense verb template, present negation, introductory object marking, and agreement across six active noun classes: M-WA, KI-VI, N, M-MI, JI-MA, and U. This flexibility, rather than any single new rule, is the achievement of Unit II. These same tools will expand in Unit III to include directions, locatives, weather, health, and work and school.

Sura ya 11: Mjini — Getting Around Town

Chapter 11 opens Unit III, Community and Travel. You will learn to identify buildings, services, and transportation; ask where a place is; ask for and give simple directions; and understand common signs and travel instructions. New grammar includes the PA/KU/MU locative system, motion verbs, and affirmative and negative imperatives — your first systematic look at locative reference beyond the introductory expressions from Chapter 6.

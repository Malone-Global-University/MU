Utetezi wa Mdomo, Mahojiano, na Uwasilishaji

Oral Defense, Interview, and Presentation · Speak and Write Swahili, Volume V: Advanced Precision, Interpretation, and Specialization

Chapter Overview

Chapter 18 asks learners to stand behind the long-form project they planned, drafted, and revised in Chapter 17 — presenting it aloud, explaining the choices behind it, and responding to questions about it. Having finished their outline, draft, and four-pass edit, Zainabu, Hamisi, and Fatuma now prepare to present their report, essay, and proposal to Bi Mwajuma and to each other, defending their organizational and register choices and fielding follow-up questions. This chapter introduces no new grammar. Its purpose is to build advanced speaking strategies — clarification, concession, repair, and audience adaptation — that let a speaker present prepared material fluently while also managing the unpredictable give-and-take of an interview or question-and-answer session, in preparation for the portfolio integration that completes this volume.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify and use core vocabulary for presenting, defending, and discussing a piece of extended writing.
  2. Deliver a structured oral presentation of a long-form project, summarizing its purpose, organization, and key content.
  3. Use clarification strategies to check or restate meaning when a question or comment is unclear.
  4. Use concession strategies to acknowledge a valid criticism or alternative view before responding.
  5. Use repair strategies to correct, rephrase, or recover smoothly from a misstatement during live speech.
  6. Adapt register, pacing, and explanation depth to different audiences within the same presentation.
  7. Respond to follow-up questions by explaining and defending specific choices made in a written project.
  8. Conduct a full oral defense of a self-authored long-form text, including presentation and a question-and-answer segment.

Opening Communication Scenario

With their final drafts complete, Zainabu, Hamisi, and Fatuma meet with Bi Mwajuma for a round of practice presentations before the volume's capstone. Bi Mwajuma explains that a strong presentation is not simply reading the draft aloud: "Kuwasilisha si kusoma tu; ni kueleza kwa nini uliandika hivyo, na kujibu maswali bila kuchanganyikiwa" — "Presenting is not just reading; it's explaining why you wrote it that way, and answering questions without getting flustered." She reminds them that when a question is unclear, they should ask for clarification rather than guess; when a critique has merit, they should concede the point before explaining their reasoning; and when they misspeak, they should repair the error calmly rather than freeze. Zainabu practices presenting her water-project report to Bi Mwajuma as if she were an official audience, Hamisi rehearses his personal essay for a more informal, supportive audience, and Fatuma prepares to defend her funding proposal to a skeptical shopkeeper who might push back on her request. Each of them discovers that defending a text well depends as much on how they handle unexpected questions as on how well the text itself was written.

  1. An oral presentation of written work must explain the reasoning behind choices, not merely repeat the text.
  2. Clarification requests prevent a speaker from answering the wrong question.
  3. Conceding a valid point before responding strengthens, rather than weakens, an oral defense.
  4. Repair strategies allow a speaker to recover smoothly from a misstatement without losing the audience's confidence.
  5. The same content may need to be presented differently depending on whether the audience is official, informal, or skeptical.

Prosody and Q&A Management

Focus — Pacing, Pausing, and Recovering Aloud

Advanced spoken defense depends on prosody that signals confidence even under pressure. A brief, controlled pause before answering a difficult question — marked by a falling pitch and a short silence rather than a filled hesitation — reads as thoughtful rather than unprepared. By contrast, rushing to answer before fully understanding the question often produces an answer that misses the point and then requires a longer, more awkward repair.

When a speaker needs to correct something already said, a brief self-repair phrase such as samahani, narudia ("sorry, let me repeat that") delivered calmly and at normal speed sounds far more confident than a rushed, quiet mumble. Fatuma finds that pausing briefly before responding to a skeptical question, rather than rushing to fill the silence, gives her presentation a steadier, more persuasive rhythm.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new grammar. Instead, it organizes register control, concession connectors, and discourse strategies already consolidated in this volume into four functional speaking strategies: clarification, concession, repair, and audience adaptation.

1. Asking for Clarification

Building Block — Requesting Clarification Before Answering
Samahani, unaweza kurudia swali? / Je, unamaanisha ... au ...?

Samahani, unaweza kufafanua zaidi unachomaanisha kwa "hatua zijazo"?

Sorry, could you clarify further what you mean by "next steps"?

A speaker who is not certain what a question is asking should request clarification rather than guess and risk answering the wrong question. This strategy uses familiar question forms and polite requests already active in this volume; what is new is the discourse function — using a question to manage an interaction, not merely to gather information.

  • Je, unauliza kuhusu mpangilio wa ripoti, au kuhusu maudhui yake? Are you asking about the report's organization, or about its content? [clarification: offering two interpretations]
  • Naomba unirudishie swali lako kwa maneno mengine. Please rephrase your question for me in other words.

2. Conceding a Point Before Responding

Building Block — Concession Connectors in Oral Defense
Ni kweli kwamba ... hata hivyo, ... / Nakubali kwamba ..., lakini ...

Ni kweli kwamba fungu la pili ni fupi kuliko mengine; hata hivyo, nililenga kufanya hivyo ili kusisitiza umuhimu wa hatua zijazo.

It's true that the second section is shorter than the others; however, I aimed to do that in order to emphasize the importance of next steps.

Concession uses contrast connectors already developed in this volume's clause-linking system, applied to a specific interactional function: acknowledging that a critique has some validity before explaining a reason or a differing view. Conceding first, rather than immediately disagreeing, keeps the exchange respectful and makes the speaker's explanation more persuasive.

  • Nakubali kwamba pendekezo langu linaweza kuonekana la gharama; hata hivyo, faida za muda mrefu ni kubwa zaidi. I agree that my proposal may seem costly; however, the long-term benefits are greater. [concession + counter-explanation]

3. Repairing a Misstatement

Building Block — Self-Repair During Live Speech
Samahani, narudia: ... / Nataka kusahihisha; nilimaanisha ...

Ripoti yangu ina mafungu manne — samahani, narudia: ina mafungu matatu.

My report has four sections — sorry, let me repeat that: it has three sections.

Repair strategies let a speaker correct a factual or grammatical error mid-sentence without losing composure. A brief repair phrase, delivered calmly, signals accuracy and control; silence or a long stumble signals the opposite. This strategy builds on register and discourse control already consolidated in this volume, applied specifically to the pressure of live, unscripted speech.

4. Adapting to the Audience

Building Block — Shifting Register and Depth by Audience
Kwa hadhira rasmi: mtindo rasmi na maelezo kamili. Kwa hadhira ya kawaida: mtindo wa kawaida na muhtasari mfupi.

Kwa mmiliki wa duka, Fatuma anatumia mtindo rasmi na anaeleza kwa kina faida za pendekezo lake; kwa Bi Mwajuma na wenzake, anaweza kufupisha maelezo hayo.

With the shop owner, Fatuma uses a formal register and explains the benefits of her proposal in detail; with Bi Mwajuma and her classmates, she can shorten that explanation.

Audience adaptation draws on the register-consistency policy already applied in Chapters 16 and 17, extended here to a live, interactive setting where the speaker must judge, in real time, how much explanation an audience needs and which register best suits them.

Review and Extension — Combining All Four Strategies

A single answer during an oral defense often combines more than one strategy: a speaker may ask for clarification, concede a valid point once the question is understood, repair a small error while explaining, and adjust register depending on who asked the question. These four strategies work together rather than in isolation.

  • Samahani, unauliza kuhusu bajeti au kuhusu muda? — Kuhusu muda? Nakubali kwamba muda niliopendekeza ni mfupi — samahani, narudia — ni mfupi kidogo; nitaeleza kwa nini. Sorry, are you asking about the budget or the timeline? — About the timeline? I agree that the timeline I proposed is short — sorry, let me repeat — is a little short; I'll explain why. [clarification + concession + repair]
Specialist Note — Oral Defense Conventions Vary by Setting

The presentation-and-questions format practiced in this chapter offers learners a manageable model for oral defense; it is not identical to the specific conventions of academic thesis defenses, job interviews, or formal institutional hearings, which vary by field and institution. Learners preparing for a specific professional or academic oral defense should consult the conventions of that particular setting.

Common Mistake — Answering Before Understanding the Question

Error: responding immediately to a question that was not fully understood, producing an answer that does not address what was actually asked

Correction: request clarification first, as in "samahani, unaweza kurudia swali?" (sorry, could you repeat the question?), before formulating a response.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: Fatuma anawasilisha pendekezo lake kwa Bi Mwajuma, ambaye anacheza nafasi ya mmiliki wa duka mwenye maswali magumu.

(Setting: Fatuma presents her proposal to Bi Mwajuma, who plays the role of the shop owner with difficult questions.)

FATUMAHabari za asubuhi. Ninawasilisha pendekezo la kuomba msaada wa matangazo kwa jarida letu la jamii.Good morning. I'm presenting a proposal requesting advertising support for our community newsletter.
BI MWAJUMA(kama mmiliki wa duka) Kwa nini niwekeze katika jarida dogo kama hili? Gharama ni kubwa kwangu.(as the shop owner) Why should I invest in a small newsletter like this? The cost is significant for me.
FATUMANakubali kwamba gharama ni jambo la kufikiria kwa uzito. Hata hivyo, jarida letu linasomwa na kaya zaidi ya mia tatu kila mwezi, kwa hivyo tangazo lako litafikia wateja wengi wa eneo hili.I agree that cost is something to consider seriously. However, our newsletter is read by more than three hundred households every month, so your advertisement will reach many customers in this area.
BI MWAJUMASamahani, unamaanisha kaya mia tatu za eneo hili peke yake, au eneo pana zaidi?Sorry, do you mean three hundred households in this area alone, or a wider area?
FATUMASwali zuri — ni kaya za eneo hili peke yake, ambazo ndizo wateja wako wa moja kwa moja.Good question — it's households in this area alone, which are precisely your direct customers.
BI MWAJUMAVizuri. Lakini pendekezo lako linasema tangazo litachapishwa kila wiki mbili — samahani, ni kila wiki tatu?Good. But your proposal says the advertisement will be published every two weeks — sorry, is it every three weeks?
FATUMASamahani, narudia: ni kila wiki mbili, si kila wiki tatu. Nashukuru kwa kuniuliza; nataka hilo liwe wazi kabisa.Sorry, let me repeat: it's every two weeks, not every three weeks. Thank you for asking; I want that to be completely clear.
BI MWAJUMASawa. Umejibu vizuri, na umekiri hoja yangu kabla ya kunieleza sababu zako. Hilo ni jambo zuri katika utetezi wa kazi yako.Good. You answered well, and you conceded my point before explaining your reasons. That's a good thing in defending your work.

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension questions:

  1. Bi Mwajuma, akicheza nafasi ya mmiliki wa duka, anauliza hoja gani kuhusu gharama?
  2. Fatuma anajibuje kabla ya kueleza sababu zake?
  3. Fatuma anatumia mkakati gani anapogundua kwamba amesema "wiki tatu" badala ya "wiki mbili"?

Guided Practice

Zoezi 1 — Kuomba Ufafanuzi (Requesting Clarification)

Practice: with a partner, take turns asking a deliberately ambiguous question about a written project, and respond by requesting clarification before answering.

  1. Ask an ambiguous question about your partner's project (real or invented).
  2. Respond with a clarification request, then answer only once the question is clear.

Zoezi 2 — Kukiri Hoja na Kutetea Uamuzi (Conceding and Defending)

Practice: respond to a critique of a written choice by conceding the valid part of the critique before explaining your reasoning.

  1. Receive a critique of a choice in your Chapter 17 project (e.g., section length, word choice, organization).
  2. Respond using a concession connector followed by your explanation.

Zoezi 3 — Kujirekebisha kwa Utulivu (Repairing Calmly)

Practice: deliberately introduce a small factual error while speaking about your project, then repair it calmly using a repair phrase.

  1. State one fact about your project incorrectly on purpose.
  2. Notice the error, pause briefly, and repair it using a self-repair phrase.

Writing Workshop

  1. Andika muhtasari wa uwasilishaji wako. Write a short presentation outline: an opening statement, a summary of your Chapter 17 project's purpose and organization, and a closing statement.
  2. Tayarisha majibu ya maswali yanayoweza kutokea. Prepare written notes anticipating three likely questions about your project and how you would answer each one.
  3. Andika sentensi za ufafanuzi, kukiri hoja, na kujirekebisha. Write one example sentence for each of the four speaking strategies (clarification, concession, repair, audience adaptation), tailored to your own project.
  4. Andika toleo fupi kwa hadhira rasmi na toleo fupi kwa hadhira ya kawaida. Write a short formal-audience version and a short informal-audience version of your opening statement.
  5. Kagua maandishi yako kwa msamiati na tahajia. Check your notes for vocabulary accuracy and correct spelling before rehearsing aloud.

Prompt: Prepare written presentation notes and anticipated question-and-answer material for the oral defense of your Chapter 17 long-form project, including at least one example of each of the four speaking strategies covered in this chapter.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • A clear opening and closing statement for the presentation are prepared.
  • At least three anticipated questions and draft answers are written out.
  • Each of the four speaking strategies appears at least once in the prepared notes.
  • A formal-audience and an informal-audience version of the opening statement are both included.
  • Vocabulary and spelling in the notes have been checked for accuracy.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Respectful Questioning and Deference to Experience

In many Swahili-speaking community settings, an oral defense such as Fatuma's proposal to a shop owner or Zainabu's report to a project supervisor involves a visible balance between confidently defending one's own work and showing appropriate deference to the age, status, or experience of the person asking questions — a pattern that shapes how directly a speaker disagrees, how a concession is phrased, and how much explanation is offered unprompted.

Regional Variation Note — Formality Expectations in Oral Defense Settings

The level of formality expected in an oral defense or interview can vary by institution, workplace, and region; some settings favor more elaborate greeting and politeness sequences before substantive questions begin, while others move to the topic more directly. This chapter's model dialogue illustrates one workable pattern rather than a single fixed standard. Learners preparing for a specific real-world oral defense should observe the conventions of that particular setting.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Treating Every Question as a Challenge to Refute

Error: responding to every critical question with immediate disagreement, without acknowledging any valid part of the questioner's point

Correction: concede the valid part of the critique first, as in "nakubali kwamba ..., hata hivyo ..." (I agree that ..., however ...), before offering a counter-explanation.

Common Mistake — Freezing After a Misstatement Instead of Repairing It

Error: stopping speech entirely, or trailing off in silence, after noticing a factual or grammatical error mid-sentence

Correction: use a calm repair phrase such as "samahani, narudia" (sorry, let me repeat that) and continue speaking at a normal pace, as Fatuma does when she corrects the publication schedule in the model dialogue.

Common Mistake — Using the Same Register for Every Audience

Error: presenting the same project in exactly the same words and register regardless of whether the audience is formal, informal, or skeptical

Correction: prepare a formal and an informal version of the opening statement, as practiced in the Writing Workshop, and adjust explanation depth and register to the audience actually present.

Sehemu C, Question 1: accept explanations noting that conceding a valid point builds credibility and makes the following explanation more persuasive, rather than signaling weakness. Question 2: accept any well-reasoned explanation connecting the chosen audience type to specific adaptations in register, pacing, or explanation depth. Other accurate, well-formed answers are acceptable where the task is open-ended.

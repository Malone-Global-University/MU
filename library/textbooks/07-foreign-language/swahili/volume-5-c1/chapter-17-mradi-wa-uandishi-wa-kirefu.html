Chapter Overview

Chapter 17 asks learners to plan, draft, and revise an extended piece of writing on a topic of their own choosing — an essay, a report, or a professional document — bringing together the integrated advanced grammar, discourse control, and editorial workflow this volume has built. Encouraged by their newsletter proofreading work with Bi Mwajuma, Zainabu, Hamisi, and Fatuma each choose a long-form project of their own: Zainabu drafts a report on the clean-water project's first month, Hamisi writes a personal essay about learning to edit his own writing, and Fatuma prepares a short professional proposal for expanding the newsletter's readership. This chapter introduces no new grammar. Its purpose is to guide learners through a staged planning-drafting-revising process for a text longer and more demanding than anything produced earlier in the course, applying Chapter 16's four-pass editorial workflow to their own writing before the volume's final oral defense and portfolio chapters.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify and use core vocabulary for planning, drafting, and revising an extended piece of writing.
  2. Choose a long-form genre — essay, report, or professional document — appropriate to a communicative purpose and audience.
  3. Build a working outline that organizes an extended text into a coherent sequence of sections or paragraphs.
  4. Draft an extended text that sustains a consistent register from beginning to end.
  5. Apply the four-pass editorial workflow from Chapter 16 to a self-authored long-form draft.
  6. Revise a draft for coherence, cohesion, paragraph organization, and audience appropriateness, not only for isolated sentence-level accuracy.
  7. Read a completed draft aloud to check rhythm, register consistency, and overall flow.
  8. Produce a publication-ready long-form text supported by a brief reflective account of the drafting process.

Opening Communication Scenario

A week after helping Bi Mwajuma proofread the newsletter article, Zainabu, Hamisi, and Fatuma each decide to write something longer of their own. Bi Mwajuma warns them that a long text cannot be drafted the way a short paragraph can: "Huwezi kuandika ukurasa mzima kwa pumzi moja na kutarajia iwe safi" — "You can't write a whole page in one breath and expect it to be clean." She suggests they first make an outline, then draft section by section, then step back and revise the whole for organization before applying the concord-verb-register-spelling workflow they already know. Zainabu outlines a short report on the clean-water project's progress, with sections for background, current status, and next steps. Hamisi plans a personal essay about how learning to edit his own writing changed the way he thinks in Swahili. Fatuma drafts a professional proposal addressed to a local business, asking for advertising support for the newsletter. Each of them discovers that organizing the whole text before writing sentence by sentence makes the later editing pass far more manageable.

  1. A long text needs a stage of planning and outlining before continuous drafting begins.
  2. Drafting section by section, rather than all at once, keeps a long text organized and manageable.
  3. Whole-text revision — checking coherence and organization — must happen before the sentence-level four-pass editing workflow.
  4. Different long-form genres (report, personal essay, professional proposal) share a planning-drafting-revising process even though their content and audience differ.

Reading Your Draft Aloud

Focus — Using Read-Aloud Rhythm to Judge a Whole Text

This section applies the read-aloud technique consolidated in Chapter 16 to a new purpose: judging the flow and register consistency of an entire long text, not only catching individual spelling errors. Reading a whole section aloud at natural speed, rather than sentence by sentence, reveals places where the rhythm breaks down — a paragraph that suddenly grows choppy, a sentence that runs on too long to breathe through, or a section that shifts from formal to conversational phrasing without warning.

For example, reading Fatuma's proposal aloud in one continuous pass, rather than checking each sentence in isolation, is what lets her notice that the opening paragraph is formal and the closing paragraph slips into a casual tone more suitable for a message to a friend than a request to a business owner. Isolated sentence-level proofreading, by contrast, might judge each sentence grammatically correct without ever revealing the whole-text inconsistency.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new grammar. Instead, it organizes the integrated advanced grammar and discourse control already consolidated in this volume — concord, verb-template accuracy, register, cohesion, and clause-linking — into a staged planning-drafting-revising process appropriate to an extended text.

1. From Outline to Draft: A Three-Stage Process

Building Block — Planning, Drafting, and Whole-Text Revision
Hatua ya kwanza: muhtasari / Hatua ya pili: rasimu ya kwanza kwa hatua / Hatua ya tatu: kurekebisha muundo mzima

Kabla sijaandika sentensi moja, ninaandaa muhtasari wenye mafungu matatu: usuli, hali ya sasa, na hatua zijazo.

Before I write a single sentence, I prepare an outline with three sections: background, current status, and next steps.

The process separates long-form writing into three stages: first, an outline (muhtasari) that names each section and its main point; second, a first draft (rasimu ya kwanza) written section by section rather than all at once; and third, whole-text revision (kurekebisha muundo mzima), which checks organization, cohesion, and register consistency across the entire text before any sentence-level editing begins. Attempting to combine outlining, drafting, and sentence-level correction in a single pass, as Bi Mwajuma warns, produces a text that is difficult to organize and difficult to fix.

  • Katika fungu la kwanza, nitaeleza historia fupi ya mradi; katika fungu la pili, nitaeleza hali yake ya sasa. In the first section, I will explain the project's brief history; in the second section, I will explain its current status. [outline-to-draft mapping]
  • Kabla ya kuhariri sentensi moja moja, nasoma rasimu nzima ili kuona kama mawazo yanafuatana vizuri kutoka fungu moja hadi lingine. Before editing sentence by sentence, I read the whole draft to see whether the ideas flow well from one section to the next.

2. Cohesion Across Sections

Review and Extension — Linking Sections into One Text

Building on this volume's clause-linking and discourse-connector systems, an extended text needs transitions that connect one section to the next, not only connectors within a single sentence. A reader should be able to tell why a new section follows the one before it.

  • Baada ya kueleza historia ya mradi, sasa tuangalie hali yake ya sasa. (Having explained the project's history, let us now look at its current status.) A section-level transition connecting background to current status. [section transition]
  • Kutokana na maendeleo haya, hatua zifuatazo zinapendekezwa. (Given this progress, the following steps are recommended.) A transition connecting current status to next steps, using a causal connector at the section level.

Section-level transitions are a productive extension of clause-linking and discourse-organization systems already active in this volume. They do not introduce new grammar; they apply familiar connectors at a larger structural scale.

3. Sustaining One Register from Start to Finish

Building Block — Checking Register Across an Entire Draft
Je, mtindo huu unabaki mmoja kutoka fungu la kwanza hadi la mwisho?

Fatuma anagundua kwamba aya ya kwanza ya pendekezo lake ni rasmi, lakini aya ya mwisho inatumia mtindo wa kawaida usiofaa kwa hadhira ya mfanyabiashara.

Fatuma discovers that the first paragraph of her proposal is formal, but the last paragraph uses a casual style unsuited to a business audience.

Following this volume's register-consistency policy — already applied to a single paragraph in Chapter 16 — an extended text requires the same register from its first sentence to its last, unless a shift in register is a deliberate, signposted choice appropriate to the genre. Checking register at the whole-text level, rather than paragraph by paragraph in isolation, is what allows Fatuma to catch her proposal's inconsistency.

Specialist Note — This Process Is a Teaching Model, Not a Fixed Professional Method

The three-stage planning-drafting-revising process in this chapter offers learners a manageable, repeatable method for producing extended text; it is not the only method professional writers use, nor a substitute for genre-specific conventions of a particular institution, publisher, or workplace. Learners preparing a document for actual professional or academic use should consult conventions specific to that context.

Common Mistake — Skipping the Outline and Drafting Straight Through

Error: beginning to write continuous sentences without first deciding what each section will cover, leading to a draft that repeats ideas or loses its organization partway through

Correction: prepare a short outline naming each section and its main point before drafting a single sentence, as in "muhtasari: fungu la kwanza — usuli; fungu la pili — hali ya sasa; fungu la tatu — hatua zijazo" (outline: first section — background; second section — current status; third section — next steps).

Guided Practice

Zoezi 1 — Kuandaa Muhtasari (Building an Outline)

Practice: choose a long-form genre (report, essay, or proposal) and a topic, then build a three-to-five-section outline naming each section's main point.

  1. Name your genre, topic, and intended audience in one sentence.
  2. List each section of your outline with a one-sentence description of its content.

Zoezi 2 — Kuandika kwa Hatua (Drafting Section by Section)

Practice: draft one section of your outline at a time, checking that each section stays focused on its stated purpose before moving to the next.

  1. Draft the first section fully before beginning the second.
  2. After each section, briefly check that it matches the outline's description before continuing.

Zoezi 3 — Kurekebisha Muundo Mzima (Whole-Text Revision)

Practice: read your complete first draft aloud in one pass, checking only organization, section transitions, and register consistency — not individual sentence errors.

  1. Note any section that repeats an earlier idea or breaks the outline's order.
  2. Note any point where the register shifts unintentionally from formal to casual or the reverse.

Writing Workshop

  1. Chagua aina ya maandishi na mada. Choose a long-form genre (report, essay, or professional document) and a topic that interests you.
  2. Andaa muhtasari wenye mafungu matatu hadi matano. Prepare an outline of three to five sections, each with a one-sentence description.
  3. Andika rasimu ya kwanza fungu kwa fungu. Draft the first version of your text, one section at a time, checking each section against its outline description before continuing.
  4. Soma rasimu nzima kwa sauti kwa ajili ya muundo. Read the whole draft aloud, checking only organization, section transitions, and register consistency.
  5. Fanya pasi nne za Sura ya 16. Apply the four-pass editorial workflow from Chapter 16 — concord, verb template, register, and orthography — one category at a time.
  6. Andika rasimu ya mwisho, tayari kwa kuchapishwa. Produce a final, publication-ready version incorporating all whole-text and sentence-level corrections.

Prompt: Plan, draft, and revise an extended text of your choosing (report, essay, or professional document) of at least four paragraphs, applying both whole-text revision and the Chapter 16 four-pass workflow, and submit your outline alongside your final draft.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • An outline was prepared and followed before continuous drafting began.
  • Each section stays focused on its stated purpose and connects clearly to the section before it.
  • The whole draft was read aloud for organization and register consistency before sentence-level editing.
  • All four Chapter 16 passes (concord, verb template, register, orthography) were completed separately.
  • Register remains consistent from the first section to the last, appropriate to the intended audience.
  • Any legitimate regional or stylistic variant is preserved and noted rather than corrected.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Community Writing and Local Audiences

Long-form writing in community settings, such as Zainabu's project report, Hamisi's personal essay, and Fatuma's proposal to a local shop owner, is often addressed to a specific, known audience rather than an anonymous public — a pattern common to much practical writing in community and small-business contexts across Swahili-speaking areas, where personal relationships and local reputation frequently shape how a written request or report is framed.

Regional Variation Note — Scope of This Chapter's Genre Examples

The report, essay, and proposal genres featured in this chapter illustrate common long-form writing tasks; they are not a complete catalog of every genre a writer might need, nor a full account of formatting conventions that vary by institution or region. Learners preparing a document for a specific organization, publication, or academic setting should consult that context's own conventions and, where appropriate, a qualified reviewer.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Editing Sentences Before Checking Whole-Text Organization

Error: beginning the four-pass sentence-level workflow immediately after drafting, before checking whether the sections themselves are well organized and consistently connected

Correction: always complete a whole-text revision pass first, as in "kwanza, kurekebisha muundo mzima; kisha, pasi nne za sentensi" (first, revise the whole structure; then, the four sentence-level passes), since a well-edited sentence in a poorly organized section is still a problem.

Common Mistake — Letting Register Drift Across a Long Text

Error: beginning a document in a formal register and gradually shifting to a casual one as the draft grows longer, without noticing the drift

Correction: read the whole draft aloud in one pass specifically checking register, as Fatuma does with her proposal, and revise any section that drifts from the audience-appropriate register established at the start.

Common Mistake — Drafting Without an Outline

Error: beginning to write continuous sentences immediately, without first deciding what each section will cover, resulting in repeated ideas or a draft that loses direction

Correction: prepare a short outline before drafting, as in "muhtasari: fungu la kwanza — usuli," and check each drafted section against its outline description before continuing to the next.

  4. Mfano: "Ni rahisi kwa mwandishi kuangalia aya moja peke yake na kuiona sahihi, lakini asione kwamba aya hiyo haifuatani vizuri na aya iliyotangulia" — sentensi hii inaeleza kwamba usahihi wa aya moja hauhakikishi kwamba aya hiyo inafuatana vizuri na muktadha mzima wa maandishi.

Independent Study Tools

If you are studying without a partner or instructor, use these strategies to complete this chapter's work:

  • Before drafting anything for this chapter, write your outline on a separate page and keep it visible while you draft, checking each section against it as you go.

Chapter Overview

Chapter 12 continues Unit 3 by moving from the academic register of Chapter 11 into the business, finance, and administrative domain. Zainabu, Hamisi, and Fatuma have taken on a shared task outside the seminar: helping a small family shop formalize its record-keeping and communicate more effectively with a supplier and a bank. This chapter follows them as they draft a formal request letter, read and interpret an invoice, and discuss the conditions attached to a small business loan. You will learn how Swahili expresses formal requests, how numbers and monetary amounts are handled in business writing, how passive constructions distance a statement from its agent in procedural language, and how conditional structures state the terms under which something will happen. As in Chapter 11, these are not new grammar systems so much as new applications of concord, verb architecture, and register control — now directed at the communicative demands of business and administration.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify and use core vocabulary for business, finance, administration, and logistics.
  2. Write a formal request using appropriate register and structure.
  3. Read and interpret an invoice, receipt, or simple financial statement.
  4. Use passive constructions to describe procedures without naming the agent.
  5. State the conditions attached to an agreement, loan, or contract using conditional structures.
  6. Produce accurate numbers, amounts, and dates in formal written and spoken contexts.
  7. Draft a short administrative document, such as a formal letter or memo.
  8. Read a short business-related passage and identify its formal requests, passives, and conditions.
  9. Deliver a short oral summary of an administrative matter using appropriate professional register.

Opening Communication Scenario

After their seminar, Zainabu, Hamisi, and Fatuma agree to help Hamisi's aunt, who runs a small shop selling household goods, prepare three pieces of written business communication. First, the shop needs a formal letter requesting a delayed payment date from a supplier after a shipment arrived with damaged goods. Second, an invoice from a different supplier has arrived, and the aunt wants help reading its totals, taxes, and due date correctly. Third, the shop is considering a small loan from a local bank and wants help understanding the conditions listed in the bank's offer letter, particularly a clause describing what will happen if a payment is late. As Zainabu drafts the request letter, Hamisi notices she has used a very direct, almost blunt request structure that would sound demanding in a formal letter. Fatuma, reading the invoice aloud, mispronounces one of the larger numbers, causing brief confusion about the total amount owed. Hamisi, reading the loan conditions, at first misunderstands a passive construction as stating who is responsible for a decision, when it in fact leaves the responsible party unspecified. Their work together becomes the model for this chapter's focus on formal requests, numerical accuracy, and conditional and passive language in business writing.

  1. A formal business request must soften directness while remaining clear about what is being asked.
  2. Numbers and monetary amounts must be read and written with complete accuracy, since a small error can misrepresent a financial obligation.
  3. Passive constructions are common in procedural and administrative Swahili because they describe an action without naming who performs it.
  4. Conditional structures state precisely what will happen under specified circumstances, which is essential for understanding contracts and agreements.

Numbers and Formal Terms

Focus — Accurate Numbers, Amounts, and Formal Vocabulary

This section does not introduce a new sound feature; it asks you to apply five-vowel stability and penultimate stress with particular care to numbers, which carry real consequences in business contexts if misread. Compound numbers in Swahili are built by combining smaller units with na ("and"), and each unit must be pronounced clearly, with a brief pause between major place values in long numbers to aid the listener.

For example, the amount shilingi laki tano, elfu ishirini na tano (five hundred twenty-five thousand shillings) should be spoken with a light pause after laki tano and again before na tano, so that a listener can track each component of the total. Formal loanword-derived terms such as ankara and mkataba should retain stable five-vowel pronunciation rather than being reduced toward their English or Arabic source pronunciation.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops three interconnected business-writing tools: formal requests, passive constructions for procedural language, and conditional structures for stating terms. Each depends on grammar already consolidated in earlier chapters — noun-class concord, verb-template control, and TAM/modality — and applies it to administrative and financial communicative purposes.

1. Writing a Formal Request

Building Block — The Softened Request Frame
Tunaomba kwa heshima... / Tungeshukuru iwapo... / Ni matumaini yetu kwamba...

Tunaomba kwa heshima mruhusu tulipe deni hili baada ya wiki mbili, kwa kuwa mzigo uliofika ulikuwa na uharibifu.

We respectfully request that you allow us to pay this debt after two weeks, since the shipment that arrived had damage.

A formal request softens directness by using a request verb such as -omba together with a politeness marker such as kwa heshima (respectfully), or by placing the request inside a conditional frame such as tungeshukuru iwapo... (we would be grateful if...). The reason for the request should follow, introduced by kwa kuwa or kwa sababu.

  • Tunaomba kwa heshima mpokee maelezo haya kabla ya tarehe ya mwisho. We respectfully request that you receive this explanation before the deadline. [softened request + reason clause structure]
  • Tungeshukuru iwapo mngeweza kutupatia muda zaidi wa kulipa. We would be grateful if you could give us more time to pay.

2. Passive Constructions in Procedural Language

Review and Extension — The Passive Extension in Administrative Sentences

The passive extension (typically -w- or -liw-/-lew- inserted before the final vowel) removes the agent from focus and places the action or the affected entity at the center of the sentence. This is common in invoices, contracts, and procedural notices, where the person responsible for an action is often left unstated or unimportant to the message.

  • -toza (to charge) → kutozwa (to be charged) "Wateja wanatozwa ushuru wa asilimia kumi." — Customers are charged a ten percent tax. [agent, e.g. "by the government," is left unstated]
  • -kubali (to approve) → kukubaliwa (to be approved) "Mkopo huu utakubaliwa baada ya wiki moja." — This loan will be approved after one week.
  • -lipa (to pay) → kulipwa (to be paid) "Deni hilo litalipwa kabla ya mwisho wa mwezi." — That debt will be paid before the end of the month.

Do not assume a passive sentence hides blame. It simply shifts communicative focus away from the agent and toward the action or its result, which is often exactly what formal or procedural writing requires.

3. Conditional Structures for Terms and Agreements

Building Block — Stating Conditions with kama and -ki-
Kama/Iwapo + [condition clause], + [result clause]

Iwapo malipo hayatafika ndani ya siku saba baada ya tarehe ya mwisho, riba ya ziada itatozwa.

If payment does not arrive within seven days after the due date, additional interest will be charged.

Business and administrative Swahili frequently uses kama or the more formal iwapo to introduce a condition, followed by a result clause that often contains a passive verb, since the agent enforcing the condition (a bank, an institution) is frequently left unstated. Learners should distinguish this real, likely condition (using future or -ki- forms) from the more hypothetical -nge- and -ngali- forms introduced in Chapter 5, which are used for less certain or contrary-to-fact situations.

Common Mistake — Reading a Passive as Naming a Responsible Party

Error: interpreting "riba itatozwa" (interest will be charged) as specifying who charges the interest, when no agent is stated

Correction: recognize that a passive sentence without an na-phrase (such as na benki, "by the bank") leaves the agent unspecified; do not add an agent that the text does not name.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: Zainabu, Hamisi, na Fatuma wanamsaidia shangazi yake Hamisi kuandaa barua, kusoma ankara, na kuelewa masharti ya mkopo kwa duka lake.

(Setting: Zainabu, Hamisi, and Fatuma help Hamisi's aunt prepare a letter, read an invoice, and understand loan conditions for her shop.)

HAMISIZainabu, barua yako inasema "tunataka mtupe muda zaidi" — hilo linasikika kama amri zaidi kuliko ombi.Zainabu, your letter says "we want you to give us more time" — that sounds more like a command than a request.
ZAINABUAh, kweli, sikutumia muundo wa heshima. Wacha niandike "tunaomba kwa heshima" badala yake.Ah, true, I did not use a polite structure. Let me write "we respectfully request" instead.
FATUMAWakati nasoma ankara hii kwa sauti, nimekwama kwenye jumla — je, ni laki tano na elfu ishirini na tano, au laki tano ishirini na tano tu?While I was reading this invoice aloud, I got stuck on the total — is it five hundred and twenty-five thousand, or just five hundred twenty-five?
HAMISINi laki tano, elfu ishirini na tano — tusimame kidogo baada ya "laki tano" ili tusichanganyikiwe.It is five hundred [thousand], twenty-five thousand — let's pause a little after "five hundred [thousand]" so we don't get confused.
HAMISIKwenye barua ya benki, inasema "mkopo huu utakubaliwa baada ya ukaguzi" — je, hii inamaanisha shangazi ndiye atakayeamua?In the bank's letter, it says "this loan will be approved after review" — does this mean my aunt is the one who will decide?
FATUMAHapana, hiyo ni sentensi tendwa — haisemi ni nani atakayeidhinisha, huenda ni kamati ya benki, si shangazi yako.No, that is a passive sentence — it does not say who will approve it, it is probably the bank's committee, not your aunt.
ZAINABUNa sharti lile la riba — "iwapo malipo hayatafika ndani ya siku saba, riba itatozwa" — hilo ni sharti la hakika, si la kudhania tu.And that condition about interest — "if payment does not arrive within seven days, interest will be charged" — that is a real, likely condition, not just a hypothetical one.

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension questions:

  1. Hamisi anasema tatizo gani kuhusu barua ya Zainabu?
  2. Fatuma anachanganyikiwa na nini akisoma ankara?
  3. Fatuma anamweleza Hamisi nini kuhusu sentensi "mkopo huu utakubaliwa baada ya ukaguzi"?

Writing Workshop

  1. Chagua hali ya biashara. Choose a short business situation requiring a formal request (a delay, a discount, a complaint about damaged goods).
  2. Andika ombi lako. Write one softened, formal request sentence using a frame from this chapter, followed by a reason clause.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • The request uses a softened, formal frame rather than a blunt command structure.
  • The reason clause is clearly connected to the request using kwa kuwa or kwa sababu.
  • At least one passive construction is used accurately, with a deliberate choice about naming the agent.
  • The conditional sentence correctly uses a real-condition structure appropriate to the situation.
  • Any numbers or amounts included are written and would be read aloud accurately.
  • Register is consistently formal and professional throughout the document.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Politeness in Business Correspondence

In many Swahili-speaking business communities, a formal letter that opens with respectful language and clearly states a reason before making a request, as in the model letter above, is considered both professionally appropriate and more likely to receive a favorable response than a letter that states a demand without softening or explanation.

Regional Variation Note — Business and Administrative Conventions

Specific conventions for invoices, contracts, and formal correspondence — including required formatting, tax terminology, and currency presentation — vary across countries and institutions in the Swahili-speaking world. This chapter presents a general instructional model; learners handling real business or legal documents should confirm the specific conventions required by the relevant institution or jurisdiction.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Writing a Request That Sounds Like a Command

Error: "Tunataka mtulipe haraka" (we want you to pay us quickly) — a direct demand with no softening or reason

Correction: soften the request and add a reason, as in "Tunaomba kwa heshima mzingatie malipo haya kwa wakati, kwa kuwa yanahitajika kukamilisha utaratibu wetu wa kifedha" (we respectfully request that you attend to this payment on time, since it is needed to complete our financial process).

Common Mistake — Misreading Compound Numbers

Error: reading "laki tano, elfu ishirini na tano" without a pause, causing the listener to hear it as a single unclear figure

Correction: pause briefly after each major place value, and, in writing, consider grouping digits clearly so the amount can be read back accurately.

Common Mistake — Confusing Real and Hypothetical Conditions

Error: using a hypothetical -nge- form ("Kama malipo yasingefika...") to describe an ordinary, likely late-payment situation in a contract

Correction: use a real-condition structure with kama/iwapo plus future or -ki- forms for likely, expected situations, reserving -nge-/-ngali- for genuinely hypothetical or contrary-to-fact cases, as distinguished in Chapter 5.

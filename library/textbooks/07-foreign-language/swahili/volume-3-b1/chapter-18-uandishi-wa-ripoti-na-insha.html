Longer Writing: Report and Personal Essay · Speak and Write Swahili, Volume III: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Overview

Chapter 18 turns the reading skills you consolidated in Chapter 17 toward your own extended writing. You will plan, draft, revise, and edit a multi-paragraph report or personal essay in Standard Swahili, using an editing checklist to audit three areas that most often break down in longer learner writing: noun-class agreement across a full paragraph, clause-linking accuracy, and verb-form consistency. As in Chapter 17, no major new grammar is introduced here; instead, you apply and tighten control of systems already active in this volume — concord, TAM and negation, relative clauses, verb extensions, and connectors — inside a real piece of extended writing. The chapter treats revision as a distinct skill from drafting, and gives you a concrete process for finding and fixing your own errors before producing a final version.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Plan a multi-paragraph report or personal essay in Swahili using an outline that separates main idea, supporting points, and conclusion.
  2. Draft connected paragraphs that use consolidated grammar rather than a series of disconnected simple sentences.
  3. Audit a completed draft for noun-class agreement across an entire paragraph, not only within single sentences.
  4. Audit a completed draft for accurate clause-linking, checking that connectors, relative clauses, and subordinate clauses are used correctly and not overused.
  5. Audit a completed draft for verb-form accuracy, including TAM choice, negation, object marking, and verb extensions.
  6. Apply a structured editing checklist to move from a rough draft to a polished final version.
  7. Distinguish revision (content and organization) from editing (grammar and mechanics) as two separate stages of the writing process.

Pronunciation and Orthography Focus

Focus — Proofreading Aloud

Reading your own draft aloud, slowly and with attention to penultimate stress and five-vowel stability, is one of the most reliable ways to catch errors before a reader does. A sentence that sounds wrong when spoken carefully often is wrong; a sentence that sounds smooth in fluent speech usually reflects grammar you already control well.

In spelling, take particular care with the words in this chapter's vocabulary that contain a double vowel or a consonant cluster prone to error, such as marekebisho and tafakari; check these against the vocabulary table rather than guessing from pronunciation alone.

Grammar for Writing: Auditing Agreement, Clause-Linking, and Verb-Form Accuracy

This section consolidates systems you already control for stronger extended writing. No new productive grammar is introduced; the goal is applying a structured audit process to your own drafts.

Agreement Audit: Tracking Class Across a Paragraph

Consolidation — Noun-Class Concord (from Chapters 1–16)

A single sentence rarely reveals an agreement error, because the noun and its modifiers usually sit close together. Longer writing is different: a noun introduced in one sentence may be referred back to several sentences later by a subject marker, an object marker, a demonstrative, or a relative clause, and by then it is easy to lose track of its class.

  • Mradi ule ulikuwa mgumu. Baadaye, ulifanikiwa. That project was difficult. Later, it succeeded. class 3 "mradi" tracked correctly across two sentences with u- subject markers
  • Incorrect: Mradi ule ulikuwa mgumu. Baadaye, ilifanikiwa. class 9/10 "i-" marker wrongly used for a class 3 noun

To audit agreement across a paragraph: circle every noun that reappears as a pronoun, subject marker, object marker, or demonstrative later in the paragraph, then trace a line back to its first mention and confirm the class matches throughout.

Clause-Linking Audit: Checking Connectors and Subordination

Consolidation — Connectors and Relative Clauses (from Chapters 2, 3, 4, 5, and 15)

Extended writing depends on clause-linking to connect ideas smoothly, but two opposite problems are common: stringing together short, disconnected sentences with no connectors at all, or overusing connectors and subordinate clauses until the reader loses the main line of thought.

  • Disconnected: Niliwasili Nairobi. Nilipata nyumba. Nilianza kazi. I arrived in Nairobi. I found a house. I started work. grammatical but lacks connectors showing relationship between events
  • Connected: Baada ya kuwasili Nairobi, nilipata nyumba haraka; hivyo basi, niliweza kuanza kazi bila usumbufu. After arriving in Nairobi, I found a house quickly; therefore, I was able to start work without trouble.

To audit clause-linking: read each paragraph and ask whether the relationship between adjacent sentences (sequence, reason, contrast, result) is clear. Where it is not clear, add a connector. Where a sentence contains more than one relative clause or more than two subordinate clauses stacked together, consider splitting it so the main idea stays visible.

Verb-Form Audit: TAM, Negation, Object Marking, and Extensions

Consolidation — Verb Template Control (from Chapters 1–9)

Longer texts often move between time frames — background, main events, current results, future plans — and a verb-form audit checks that each shift is marked correctly and consistently.

  • Nilipofika Nairobi mwaka jana, sikuwa na marafiki wengi; sasa nimejijengea mtandao mzuri wa marafiki. When I arrived in Nairobi last year, I didn't have many friends; now I have built myself a good network of friends. past negative → perfect/resultative with reflexive+applicative, contrast marked by time reference

To audit verb form: underline every verb in the draft, label its TAM marker, and check that the marker matches the intended time reference; confirm that any negative verb uses the correct negative pattern for that TAM category; and confirm that any object marker or extension (applicative, causative, passive, reciprocal) matches the intended meaning and argument structure.

Building Block — A Three-Pass Editing Process
[Pass 1: Agreement — trace every noun's class through the paragraph] → [Pass 2: Clause-linking — check connectors and subordination clarity] → [Pass 3: Verb form — label TAM, negation, object marking, and extensions on every verb]

Draft a paragraph fully before beginning any pass. Complete one pass at a time across the whole draft rather than trying to catch every error type in a single reading.

This three-pass process is slower than a single read-through, but it catches error types that a single general proofreading pass tends to miss.

Zoezi la Kuchambua — Analysis Practice:

  1. In the sentence "Mradi ule ulikuwa mgumu, lakini baadaye ulifanikiwa kwa sababu wakazi walishirikiana," identify each verb's TAM marker and confirm the subject-marker class is consistent throughout.
  2. Rewrite "Nilifika. Nilikuwa na njaa. Nilitafuta chakula." as a single connected sentence using at least one connector.
  3. Identify the connector and the verb extension in "Kwa sababu wakazi walishirikiana, mradi uliwezeshwa kukamilika haraka" and explain what each contributes to the sentence's meaning.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: Wanjiru anarudisha rasimu ya kwanza ya insha ya Zainabu, akiwa ameweka alama tatu tofauti: moja kwa makubaliano ya ngeli, moja kwa uunganishaji wa sentensi, na moja kwa umbo la vitenzi.

(Setting: Wanjiru returns Zainabu's first draft essay, having marked three different kinds of issues: one for noun-class agreement, one for sentence-linking, and one for verb form.)

WANJIRURasimu yako ni nzuri, lakini nimeweka alama tatu tofauti. Alama ya kwanza ni kuhusu makubaliano ya ngeli.Your draft is good, but I've marked three different things. The first mark is about noun-class agreement.
ZAINABUNaona. Hapa nimeandika "mradi... ilifanikiwa," lakini "mradi" ni ngeli ya tatu, si ya tisa. Nitarekebisha kuwa "ulifanikiwa."I see. Here I wrote "project... it succeeded," but "mradi" is class 3, not class 9. I'll fix it to "ulifanikiwa."
WANJIRUSahihi kabisa. Alama ya pili ni kuhusu uunganishaji; una sentensi fupi fupi tatu mfululizo bila kiunganishi chochote.Exactly right. The second mark is about linking; you have three short sentences in a row with no connector at all.
ZAINABUNaelewa. Nitaunganisha kwa "baada ya" na "hivyo basi" ili aya isomeke vizuri zaidi.I understand. I'll connect them with "after" and "therefore" so the paragraph reads better.
WANJIRUVizuri. Alama ya tatu ni kitenzi kimoja; umetumia wakati ujao pale ambapo tukio tayari limekwisha tokea.Good. The third mark is one verb; you used the future tense where the event has already already happened.
ZAINABUAh, kweli! Ilipaswa kuwa "-me-" kwa sababu ni matokeo yanayoonekana sasa. Asante kwa kunisaidia kuyaona.Ah, true! It should have been "-me-" because it's a result visible now. Thank you for helping me see them.

Zainabu's revisions follow the Building Block's three-pass process: agreement first, then clause-linking, then verb form — the same order Wanjiru used when marking the draft.

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension questions:

  1. Wanjiru aliweka alama ngapi tofauti kwenye rasimu ya Zainabu?
  2. Kosa la kwanza la Zainabu lilikuwa la aina gani?
  3. Zainabu alitumia viunganishi gani kurekebisha sentensi fupi fupi?
  4. Kwa nini Zainabu alipaswa kutumia "-me-" badala ya wakati ujao?

Guided Practice

Zoezi 1 — Ukaguzi wa Makubaliano ya Ngeli (Agreement Audit)

Practice: tracing noun class across a short paragraph.

  1. In a provided three-sentence paragraph, circle every noun that is referred back to later, and trace whether every subject marker, object marker, and demonstrative matches its class.
  2. Correct any agreement errors you find and explain the correction.

Zoezi 2 — Ukaguzi wa Uunganishaji (Clause-Linking Audit)

Practice: improving connection between sentences.

  1. Rewrite a set of disconnected short sentences as a connected paragraph using at least three different connectors.
  2. In a separate overloaded sentence with three stacked relative and subordinate clauses, split it into two clearer sentences without losing meaning.

Zoezi 3 — Ukaguzi wa Umbo la Vitenzi (Verb-Form Audit)

Practice: labeling and correcting verb forms.

  1. Underline every verb in a provided paragraph and label its TAM marker and polarity.
  2. Identify one verb whose TAM choice does not match the intended time reference and correct it.

Reading Practice

Insha ya Kielelezo: Mwezi Wangu wa Kwanza Nairobi — Model Essay: My First Month in Nairobi

Mwezi wangu wa kwanza hapa Nairobi ulikuwa na changamoto nyingi, lakini pia ulinifundisha mengi kuhusu maisha ya mjini. Nilipowasili, sikuwa na nyumba wala marafiki, na barabara nyingi za mtaa zilinichanganya. Hata hivyo, baada ya siku chache, jirani mmoja mkarimu, Wanjiru, alinisaidia kupata nyumba na kunieleza jinsi ya kutumia usafiri wa mtaa. Aidha, alinialika kwenye mkutano wa jumuiya ya mtaa, ambao ulinipa nafasi ya kukutana na wakazi wengine. Kwa upande mwingine, changamoto kubwa zaidi ilikuwa lugha; nilipohitaji kuzungumza haraka, mara nyingi nilisahau ngeli sahihi ya nomino, na hilo lilinifanya nichanganyikiwe kidogo katika mazungumzo. Hitimisho langu ni kwamba, ingawa mwezi wa kwanza haukuwa rahisi, msaada wa jirani na jumuiya ulinisaidia kuzoea haraka zaidi kuliko ningetegemea peke yangu.

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension and interpretation questions:

  1. Ni changamoto gani mbili zilizotajwa katika aya ya kwanza?
  2. Wanjiru alimsaidiaje msemaji?
  3. Ni tatizo gani linalotajwa baada ya "kwa upande mwingine"?
  4. Kulingana na hitimisho, ni nini kilichomsaidia msemaji kuzoea haraka?
  5. Tafuta kitenzi kimoja chenye kiambishi cha nafsi (object marker) katika aya hii na eleze maana yake.

Note: this essay is an adapted textbook model written for language practice, not an authentic personal document.

Writing Workshop

  1. Panga wazo lako. In English, jot a brief outline: main idea, two or three supporting points, one limitation or challenge, and one conclusion or recommendation.
  2. Andika rasimu ya kwanza. Write a 100–150-word Standard Swahili report or personal essay from your outline, without pausing to edit as you go.
  3. Kagua kwa mapito matatu. Apply the three-pass editing process from the Building Block: first agreement, then clause-linking, then verb form, marking each issue you find.
  4. Andika rasimu ya mwisho. Produce a clean, corrected final version incorporating all three passes.

Prompt: Write a short Standard Swahili report or personal essay on a real or invented experience of your own — such as a first month somewhere new, a community project, or a workplace change — following the structure of the model essay above: an opening situation, two or three supporting points, one limitation or challenge, and a conclusion or recommendation.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • Every noun that reappears later in the essay keeps its class consistent across subject markers, object markers, and demonstratives.
  • Sentences are linked with appropriate connectors; no sentence stacks more than two subordinate or relative clauses.
  • Every verb's TAM marker and polarity accurately match the intended time reference and meaning.
  • The essay states a clear main idea, at least two supporting points, one limitation, and a conclusion or recommendation.
  • Capitalization, spacing, punctuation, and ng' are correct throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Peer Feedback and Community Writing

Sharing personal reflections and short reports with neighbors, community groups, or colleagues for feedback is a normal part of civic and social life in many East African communities, whether through a neighborhood newsletter, a community meeting, or an informal read-through with a friend. Learning to give and receive constructive feedback on written Swahili — as Wanjiru does in the Model Dialogue — is a practical skill that extends well beyond the classroom.

Regional Variation Note

The editing priorities in this chapter — agreement, clause-linking, and verb-form accuracy — apply equally to Tanzanian and Kenyan Standard Swahili writing, since both rest on the same core grammatical architecture. Personal preferences for certain connectors or slightly more formal versus conversational phrasing in personal essays may vary by writer and context; this chapter focuses on the shared editing process rather than on regionally specific stylistic preference.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Losing Noun Class Across Sentences

Error: introducing a noun correctly, then using an inconsistent subject marker, object marker, or demonstrative for that same noun several sentences later

Correction: during the agreement pass of editing, trace every noun that reappears later in the paragraph and confirm every later reference matches its original class.

Common Mistake — Overloading a Sentence with Clauses

Error: stacking multiple relative clauses and subordinate clauses into a single sentence until the main idea becomes difficult to follow

Correction: during the clause-linking pass, split any sentence containing more than two subordinate or relative clauses into two clearer sentences, connected if needed by a simple connector.

Common Mistake — Editing Only Once, for Everything at the Same Time

Error: reading a draft only once and trying to catch agreement, clause-linking, and verb-form errors all in the same pass, which usually means several errors are missed

Correction: complete the three-pass editing process separately, focusing on only one error type per pass, as modeled in the Building Block.

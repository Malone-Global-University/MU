Kauli ya Kutendwa

Passive and Public Register · Speak and Write Swahili, Volume III: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Overview

Chapter 7 continues Unit II by turning from the applicative extension of Chapter 6 to a second productive verb extension: the passive. In public notices, rules, and announcements, the person performing an action is often unstated or unimportant; what matters is the action itself and the thing or person affected by it. This chapter introduces the passive extension for the first time, in a controlled set of high-frequency verbs, so that you can read, understand, and produce agentless statements of the kind found on signs, in institutional announcements, and in short media items.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize the form of the passive extension in high-frequency verbs.
  2. Form and use passive verbs to describe an action without naming who performs it.
  3. Distinguish an active sentence from its passive counterpart in meaning and structure.
  4. Read and understand common public notices, rules, and announcements that use passive forms.
  5. Identify and correct common errors in passive formation.
  6. Write a short public notice or announcement using passive verbs.
  7. Summarize the key information in a short piece of public or institutional text.

Opening Communication Scenario

With the helping tasks from Chapter 6 underway, James notices that the neighborhood safety plan needs to be posted somewhere everyone can see it — on the community noticeboard, at the small shop, and near the water point. Writing the plan for public posting is different from writing it as a personal message: notices, rules, and official announcements in Swahili usually describe what is done, what is required, or what is not allowed, without naming exactly who is doing it. Bi Amina explains that Swahili has a regular way of building this "unnamed doer" meaning directly into the verb: the passive extension.

  1. This chapter introduces the passive extension for the first time as a productive system.
  2. Passive forms are built from verbs you already know, so the emphasis is on a small, reliable set of high-frequency examples rather than every possible derived form.
  3. Passive verbs are especially common in rules, notices, announcements, and other public or institutional statements.
  4. Vocabulary in this chapter supports reading and writing short public information texts.

Pronunciation Focus

The Passive Suffix in Connected Speech

The passive suffix -w- is inserted just before the final vowel of the verb. When you read a word like kufungwa (to be closed) or kuruhusiwa (to be allowed), keep the -w- clearly audible rather than dropping it: fu-ngwa, ru-hu-si-wa.

Penultimate stress still falls on the second-to-last syllable of the whole derived word. Practice saying active and passive pairs side by side, such as funga / fungwa and ruhusu / ruhusiwa, so you can hear how the extra element changes the shape and stress of the word without changing the basic rhythm you already know.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This section introduces the passive extension for the first time, in a controlled set of high-frequency verbs, with an emphasis on agentless public statements.

What the Passive Extension Does

New System — Verb Stem + Passive Suffix + Final Vowel

The passive extension is added to a verb stem, before the final vowel, to shift attention from the person doing the action to the person or thing affected by it. In most common verbs, the passive suffix is -w-, added directly before the final vowel: funga (to close) becomes fungwa (to be closed); andika (to write) becomes andikwa (to be written).

When a verb stem already ends in a vowel other than a consonant plus a, or when the base form ends in -ua such as ruhusu (to allow), the passive suffix combines with the stem as -iwa: ruhusu becomes ruhusiwa (to be allowed).

A passive sentence may simply omit the doer of the action altogether, which is exactly what makes it useful for rules and public notices: the rule applies to everyone, and naming a specific doer is unnecessary.

Forming and Using Passive Verbs

Building Block — Subject Marker + Verb Stem + Passive + Final Vowel
Subject marker + (TAM) + stem + -w-/-iw- + final vowel

Mlango utafungwa saa mbili usiku. The door will be closed at eight in the evening. u- + ta- + fung + wa

Magari hayaruhusiwi mtaani hapa baada ya saa tatu usiku. Cars are not allowed in this neighborhood after nine at night. ha- + ya- + ruhusi + wi (negative)

Tangazo hili limeandikwa kwa Kiswahili na Kiingereza. This announcement has been written in Swahili and English. li- + me- + andik + wa

Notice that the subject of a passive sentence is the thing affected by the action (the door, the cars, the announcement), and its subject marker agrees with that noun's class exactly as you already control from earlier chapters. No object marker for a doer appears inside these verbs, because the doer is not being named.

Active vs. Passive

Contrast — Active Sentence vs. Passive Sentence

Compare an active sentence with its passive counterpart:

  • Walimu wanafunga mlango saa mbili. — The teachers close the door at eight. (names who closes it)
  • Mlango unafungwa saa mbili. — The door is closed at eight. (no doer named; the rule matters, not who enforces it)

Use the active sentence when the doer is important or already known. Use the passive sentence when the action or the affected thing matters more than who performs it, as in rules, schedules, and public announcements.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: James na Bi Amina wanaandaa tangazo la mtaa kuhusu mpango wa usalama, ili liwekwe kwenye ubao wa matangazo.

(Setting: James and Bi Amina are preparing a neighborhood announcement about the safety plan, to be posted on the noticeboard.)

JAMESBi Amina, tangazo hili litaandikwaje? Je, tuseme nani atafunga lango la mtaa usiku?Bi Amina, how should this announcement be written? Should we say who will close the neighborhood gate at night?
BI AMINAHapana, si lazima. Tuseme tu, "Lango la mtaa litafungwa saa tatu usiku." Kila mtu ataelewa bila kutaja jina.No, that isn't necessary. Let's just say, "The neighborhood gate will be closed at nine at night." Everyone will understand without naming anyone.
JAMESSawa. Na kuhusu magari? Nadhani hayaruhusiwi kuegeshwa karibu na mlango wa shule.Okay. And about cars? I think they are not allowed to be parked near the school gate.
BI AMINANdiyo, sheria hiyo tayari ipo. Tuandike: "Magari hayaruhusiwi kuegeshwa karibu na mlango wa shule wakati wowote."Yes, that rule already exists. Let's write: "Cars are not allowed to be parked near the school gate at any time."
JAMESVizuri. Na taarifa kuhusu mkutano wa mtaa, itaandikwa kwa lugha zote mbili, sivyo?Good. And the notice about the neighborhood meeting will be written in both languages, right?
BI AMINASahihi. Taarifa hiyo itaandikwa kwa Kiswahili na Kiingereza, ili kila mtu asome kwa lugha anayoielewa vizuri zaidi.Correct. That notice will be written in Swahili and English, so that everyone can read it in whichever language they understand best.
JAMESNaona bango hili litawekwa dukani na karibu na kisima cha maji pia?I see this poster will also be put up at the shop and near the water point?
BI AMINANdiyo. Bango litawekwa maeneo matatu: ubaoni, dukani, na karibu na kisima. Kila mkazi ataona angalau mahali pamoja.Yes. The poster will be put up in three places: on the noticeboard, at the shop, and near the water point. Every resident will see it at least in one place.
JAMESNafikiri tangazo hili litasomwa na watu wengi haraka. Asante kwa msaada wako, Bi Amina.I think this announcement will be read by many people quickly. Thank you for your help, Bi Amina.

This dialogue develops the passive extension productively across several high-frequency verbs (kufungwa, kuruhusiwa, kuandikwa, kuwekwa, kusomwa) in the agentless, rule-stating style typical of public notices.

Writing Workshop

  1. Chagua mahali pa umma. Choose a real or invented public place (a park, a clinic, a small shop, a community hall) that needs a short set of rules or a notice.
  2. Panga taarifa muhimu. List, in plain English notes first, three or four pieces of information or rules that need to be communicated.
  3. Andika tangazo fupi. Write a short 90–130-word notice or announcement, using at least three passive verbs that describe rules or actions without naming a specific doer.
  4. Kagua kwa orodha. Check your notice against the checklist below.
  5. Andika nakala safi. Produce a clean final version, formatted the way a real public notice might look.

Prompt: Write a short public notice or set of rules for a place of your choice, using passive verbs to describe what is done, allowed, or forbidden.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • Each passive verb is formed correctly from its base verb (-w- or -iw-, as appropriate).
  • Each passive verb's subject marker agrees with the noun class of the affected thing.
  • At least one sentence contrasts an active statement with its passive counterpart, showing the difference in emphasis.
  • Applicative verbs from Chapter 6 are reused correctly where natural.
  • Capitalization, spacing, punctuation, and ng' are correct throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Public Notices and Shared Space

Public notices, community noticeboards, and posted rules are a common feature of neighborhood, institutional, and civic life in many Swahili-speaking communities. Because these texts are meant to apply to everyone equally, they typically describe rules and actions without naming a specific enforcer, which is exactly the function the passive extension serves. Learning to read and write this kind of agentless, rule-stating language is an important step toward participating in public and institutional communication.

Regional Variation Note

The passive verbs practiced in this chapter are standard across Tanzanian and Kenyan Standard Swahili and are widely used in public and institutional writing throughout the wider Swahili-speaking world. Some formal, legal, or bureaucratic registers use additional passive constructions with more specialized meanings; these will be addressed as receptive, labeled material in later volumes rather than as part of this chapter's productive system.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Missing Passive Suffix

Error: Mlango utafunga saa mbili. (uses the active form when a passive is intended)

Correction: Mlango utafungwa saa mbili. — when the door is the one being closed, rather than doing the closing, the passive suffix -w- must be added before the final vowel.

Common Mistake — Wrong Passive Suffix Shape

Error: kuruhusu → kuruhusua (attaches -w- directly to a stem ending in -u without the linking vowel)

Correction: kuruhusu → kuruhusiwa — verbs whose stem does not end in a simple consonant plus a typically take the fuller -iwa passive form.

Common Mistake — Naming an Unnecessary Doer

Error: Learners translate every passive sentence by adding "by someone" even when Swahili public style leaves the doer unstated, producing unnatural sentences such as Mlango unafungwa na mtu. ("The door is closed by a person.")

Correction: In rules and notices, Swahili passive sentences typically omit the doer entirely, since naming "a person" or "someone" adds no useful information. Leave the doer unstated unless a specific, relevant agent needs to be identified.

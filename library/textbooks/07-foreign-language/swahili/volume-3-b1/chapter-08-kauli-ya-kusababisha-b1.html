Kauli ya Kusababisha

Causative and Change · Speak and Write Swahili, Volume III: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Overview

Chapter 8 continues Unit II by turning from the passive extension of Chapter 7 to a third productive verb extension: the causative. Where the passive removed the doer from a sentence, the causative adds a new doer — someone or something that causes, makes, enables, or brings about a change in someone or something else. This chapter introduces the causative extension for the first time, in a limited set of common, high-frequency verbs, so that you can describe causing, making, enabling, and changing states in everyday contexts such as education, health, work, and the environment.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize the form of the causative extension in a limited set of common verbs.
  2. Form and use causative verbs to describe causing, making, enabling, or changing a state.
  3. Distinguish a simple transitive verb from its causative counterpart in meaning and structure.
  4. Use causative verbs to talk about education, health, work, and environmental change.
  5. Identify and correct common errors in causative formation.
  6. Write a short explanation of what caused a particular result.
  7. Discuss cause-and-effect relationships in familiar topics.

Opening Communication Scenario

After posting the neighborhood notices in Chapter 7, James joins a discussion with Bi Amina and a small group of neighbors about a community project: planting trees along the road to reduce dust and heat near the school. As the group plans, they keep needing to talk about what will cause a change — what will make the road cooler, what will make the children safer, what will make the project succeed. Bi Amina explains that Swahili has a regular way of building this "causing" meaning directly into the verb: the causative extension.

  1. This chapter introduces the causative extension for the first time as a productive system.
  2. Causative forms are built from a limited set of common verbs you already know, rather than every possible derived form.
  3. Causative verbs are especially useful for talking about education, health, work, and environmental change.
  4. Vocabulary in this chapter supports discussing causes, results, and community projects.

Pronunciation Focus

Derived Verb Rhythm in the Causative

The causative suffix changes the shape of the final syllable of the verb, so the derived word is often one syllable longer than the base verb. Compare pona (to recover) and ponya (to cure, make someone recover), or jua (to know) and juza (to inform, make someone know).

Keep penultimate stress on the second-to-last syllable of the whole derived word, not on the base verb's original stressed syllable: po-nya, ju-za, e-le-we-sha. Practice saying base and causative pairs side by side so you can hear how the extra element shifts the stressed syllable one position to the right.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This section introduces the causative extension for the first time, in a limited set of common, high-frequency verbs, with an emphasis on describing causing, making, enabling, and changing states.

What the Causative Extension Does

New System — Verb Stem + Causative Suffix + Final Vowel

The causative extension is added to a verb stem, before the final vowel, to add a new doer who causes, makes, enables, or brings about the action or state described by the base verb. In this limited set of common verbs, the causative suffix appears in a few related shapes: -sha, -za, or -ya, depending on the base verb.

Anguka (to fall) becomes angusha (to cause to fall, to knock down). Jua (to know) becomes juza (to inform, to make someone know). Pona (to recover) becomes ponya (to cure, to make someone recover). Elewa (to understand) becomes elewesha (to explain, to make someone understand).

Because each causative form in this chapter is drawn from a specific, lexically confirmed pair, treat the four verbs above as a controlled set to learn individually rather than as a rule to apply freely to every verb you know.

Forming and Using Causative Verbs

Building Block — Subject Marker + Causative Verb + Object Marker
Subject marker + (TAM) + causative stem + final vowel (+ object marker for the person or thing affected)

Miti hii itatusaidia kupunguza joto na kuboresha mazingira. These trees will help us reduce heat and improve the environment. i- + ta- + tu- + saidia (helping verb frame)

Daktari alimponya mgonjwa kwa dawa mpya. The doctor cured the patient with new medicine. a- + li- + m- + ponya

Mwalimu alinieleweleza somo hilo vizuri. The teacher explained that lesson to me well. a- + li- + ni- + elewesha

Notice that the subject of a causative sentence is the person or thing causing the change (the doctor, the teacher), and an object marker often refers to the person affected (the patient, "me"). This is the opposite pattern from the passive in Chapter 7: instead of removing a doer, the causative adds one.

Simple Transitive vs. Causative

Contrast — Simple Verb vs. Causative Verb

Compare a simple transitive sentence with its causative counterpart:

  • Mtoto ameanguka. — The child has fallen. (the child is the one who falls)
  • Mvulana amemwangusha mtoto. — The boy has knocked the child down. (the boy causes the child to fall)

Use the causative when you need to name who or what causes a change in someone or something else. Use the simple verb when the subject simply experiences the state or action without an outside cause being expressed.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: James, Bi Amina, na jirani wanajadili mradi wa kupanda miti karibu na shule ili kupunguza joto na vumbi.

(Setting: James, Bi Amina, and a neighbor are discussing a project to plant trees near the school to reduce heat and dust.)

JAMESBi Amina, nadhani miti hii itatusaidia kupunguza joto karibu na shule. Lakini je, itawaelewesha wanafunzi kwa nini mradi huu ni muhimu?Bi Amina, I think these trees will help us reduce the heat near the school. But will they help the students understand why this project is important?
BI AMINATutawaelewesha kwa kuwaonyesha jinsi miti inavyoboresha mazingira na kupunguza vumbi. Mwalimu mkuu ataweza kuwajuza wanafunzi wote kabla ya kupanda.We will help them understand by showing them how trees improve the environment and reduce dust. The headteacher will be able to inform all the students before the planting.
JIRANIMwaka jana upepo mkali uliangusha miti michache karibu na kisima. Je, miti hii mipya itaanguka pia?Last year strong wind knocked down a few trees near the well. Will these new trees fall too?
BI AMINAHapana, tutapanda miti yenye mizizi imara, na hiyo itatusaidia kuzuia mti kuangushwa na upepo.No, we will plant trees with strong roots, and that will help us prevent a tree from being knocked down by wind.
JAMESSawa. Na kuhusu afya? Nasikia hewa safi zaidi inaweza kuponya matatizo ya kupumua kwa watoto wengine.Okay. And about health? I hear that cleaner air can cure breathing problems for some children.
BI AMINANi kweli. Hewa safi haiwezi kuponya kila tatizo, lakini inaweza kuwasaidia watoto wenye matatizo ya kupumua kujisikia vizuri zaidi.That's true. Clean air can't cure every problem, but it can help children with breathing problems feel better.
JIRANIBasi hebu tuwajuze wazazi wote kuhusu mradi huu, ili wote washiriki siku ya kupanda.Then let's inform all the parents about this project, so everyone participates on planting day.
JAMESNakubaliana. Mradi huu utawaelewesha wote umuhimu wa mazingira, na utaboresha maisha ya mtaa wetu kwa muda mrefu.I agree. This project will help everyone understand the importance of the environment, and it will improve our neighborhood's life in the long run.

This dialogue develops the causative extension productively across several high-frequency verbs (kuelewesha, kujuza, kuangusha, kuponya) in the community-project, cause-and-effect style typical of education, health, and environmental discussion.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • Each causative verb is formed correctly from its base verb, using the verb's confirmed causative form.
  • Each causative verb's object marker (where used) correctly refers to the person or thing affected.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Community Projects and Shared Responsibility

Community projects such as tree planting, water access, and health education are common features of neighborhood and civic life in many Swahili-speaking communities, often organized through cooperation among neighbors, local committees, schools, and community leaders. Talking about these projects usually involves explaining causes and results: what a project will improve, prevent, or enable. Learning to use causative verbs supports exactly this kind of practical, forward-looking community discussion.

Regional Variation Note

The causative verbs practiced in this chapter are standard across Tanzanian and Kenyan Standard Swahili and are widely used in everyday, educational, and community contexts throughout the wider Swahili-speaking world. Some verbs have additional or specialized causative forms with more particular meanings; these will be addressed as receptive, labeled material in later volumes rather than as part of this chapter's productive system.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Missing Causative Suffix

Error: Mwalimu alinielewa somo hilo. (uses the base verb when a causative is intended)

Correction: Mwalimu alinieleweleza somo hilo. — when the teacher causes the learner to understand, the causative suffix must be added; the base verb elewa describes only the learner's own understanding, not the teacher's action of explaining.

Common Mistake — Overgeneralizing the Causative Pattern

Error: Learners invent a causative form for a verb not confirmed in this chapter, such as manufacturing *kusomesha incorrectly from an unrelated pattern, or applying -sha mechanically to every verb.

Correction: Use only the causative forms confirmed in this chapter's vocabulary and verb table: kuangusha, kujuza, kuponya, kuelewesha, kutoa, kuingiza. Do not manufacture new causative forms from other verbs until a later chapter or reliable source confirms them.

Common Mistake — Confusing Causative with Passive

Error: Learners confuse the causative meaning ("cause someone to do something") with the passive meaning ("have something done to oneself") because both extensions change the base verb's meaning, producing sentences with the wrong emphasis, such as using a causative verb where an agentless passive statement was intended.

Correction: Remember the contrast: the passive (Chapter 7) removes the doer and highlights the thing affected; the causative (this chapter) adds a new doer who brings about a change. Ask yourself whether your sentence needs to leave out a doer (passive) or introduce one (causative).

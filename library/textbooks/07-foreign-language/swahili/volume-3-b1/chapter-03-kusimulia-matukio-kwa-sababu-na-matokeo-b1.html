Kusimulia Matukio kwa Sababu na Matokeo

Narrating Events with Cause and Result · Speak and Write Swahili, Volume III: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Overview

Chapter 3 moves from defining people and things to narrating what happened to them. Building on the relative clauses, demonstratives, and associative phrases you consolidated in Chapter 2, you will now consolidate the contrast between the past tense (-li-) and the perfect/resultative tense (-me-), and learn to link events with connectors of cause and result such as kwa sababu, kwa hiyo, and hivyo. You will also begin developing object markers for people and things, so that once someone or something has been introduced, you can refer back to it inside the verb itself rather than repeating the full noun. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to tell a connected, causally organized account of a personal experience or community event.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Distinguish clearly between -li- (simple past, completed event) and -me- (perfect/resultative, present relevance) in narration.
  2. Link events using cause connectors (kwa sababu, kwa vile) and result connectors (kwa hiyo, hivyo, ndiyo maana).
  3. Use person object markers (-ni-, -ku-, -m-, -tu-, -wa-) to refer back to people already introduced in a narrative.
  4. Use class 7/8 and 9/10 object markers for things already introduced in a narrative, in controlled contexts.
  5. Narrate a connected sequence of at least four to five events with clear cause-and-result organization.
  6. Identify and correct errors in tense contrast, object-marker placement, and connector use.
  7. Tell and write a narrative about a personal experience or community incident, explaining why it happened and what followed.

Opening Communication Scenario

James wants to tell Bi Amina about something that happened in his neighborhood last week — a small fire in a shop that was quickly put out. Bi Amina notices that James keeps repeating the shop's name and the shopkeeper's name in every sentence, which makes his story sound choppy rather than connected. She asks him to try referring back to people and things using object markers once he has already named them, and to explain clearly why each event led to the next.

  1. This chapter consolidates the -li- / -me- tense contrast established in Chapter 1 and used throughout Volume II.
  2. Cause-and-result connectors are introduced for the first time as productive discourse tools.
  3. Person object markers are developed further, and object markers for things (classes 7/8, 9/10) are introduced in controlled contexts, building on the relative clauses from Chapter 2.
  4. Vocabulary in this chapter supports narrating news events, personal experiences, and community incidents.

Pronunciation Focus

Listening for Discourse Connectors

Connectors such as kwa sababu, kwa hiyo, and hivyo often carry a small pitch drop and a brief pause just before them, marking the boundary between a cause and its result. Practice hearing this boundary in the model dialogue and reading passage below, and reproduce it yourself: let your voice fall slightly and pause briefly before kwa hiyo or hivyo, then continue smoothly into the result clause.

Also continue monitoring the -li- versus -me- contrast in connected speech. Because both markers occur in the same slot of the verb, clear articulation of the vowel and following consonant is important: alikuja (he/she came) versus amekuja (he/she has come) must remain distinct.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This section consolidates the past/perfect tense contrast, introduces cause-and-result connectors for the first time, and develops person object markers alongside controlled object markers for things.

Consolidating -li- and -me-

Building Block — Past Event vs. Present Relevance
[Subject marker] + li + [stem] + a vs. [Subject marker] + me + [stem] + a

Moto ulitokea jana usiku. The fire happened last night. -li- : completed event, located in past time

Wafanyakazi wa zimamoto wameufikia mahali hapo. The firefighters have reached that place. -me- : result still relevant now

Use -li- when you are simply reporting that something happened at a specific past time. Use -me- when the event's result is still relevant to the present moment — for example, when describing the current state of things after a narrative event.

Cause-and-Result Connectors

New System — kwa sababu, kwa vile, kwa hiyo, hivyo, ndiyo maana

Kwa sababu and kwa vile ("because") introduce a reason and normally come before the clause they explain. Kwa hiyo and hivyo ("therefore, so") introduce a result and normally come at the start of the result clause. Ndiyo maana ("that is why") also introduces a result, with a slightly stronger sense of explanation.

Moto ulitokea kwa sababu jiko lilisahauliwa likiwaka. The fire happened because the stove was left burning.

Jirani aliona moshi haraka. Kwa hiyo, aliwapigia simu wazimamoto mara moja. A neighbor saw the smoke quickly. So, he called the firefighters right away.

Wazimamoto walifika haraka sana. Ndiyo maana hakuna mtu aliyeumia. The firefighters arrived very quickly. That is why no one was hurt.

Object Markers: People and Things

Building Block — Object Marker Position
[Subject marker] + [TAM marker] + [object marker] + [stem] + [final vowel]

Jirani alimwona mtoto akikimbia. The neighbor saw him/her, the child, running. -m- : class 1 human object marker

Wazimamoto waliwaokoa watu wote. The firefighters rescued them, all the people. -wa- : class 2 human object marker

Duka lilikuwa limefungwa, lakini moto uliliharibu haraka. The shop was closed, but the fire damaged it quickly. -li- : class 5 object marker for "duka"

Once a person or thing has already been named in a narrative, refer back to it with an object marker inside the verb rather than repeating the noun. This chapter develops person object markers (-ni-, -ku-, -m-, -tu-, -wa-) fully, and introduces class 7/8 and 9/10 object markers for things in controlled, clearly established contexts. Class 5/6 object markers for things remain receptive at this stage.

Zoezi la Sarufi — Grammar Practice:

  1. Choose -li- or -me- for each sentence based on context: Ajali ___tokea jana. / Wazimamoto ___fika sasa hivi, ndiyo maana moshi ___punguza. / Duka ___fungwa tangu asubuhi.
  2. Combine each pair of sentences using a cause or result connector: (Jiko lilisahauliwa likiwaka. Moto ulitokea.) / (Jirani aliona moshi. Aliwapigia simu wazimamoto.)
  3. Rewrite each sentence, replacing the repeated noun with the correct object marker: Niliwaona watoto wakikimbia. Niliwasaidia watoto. / Nilinunua kitabu. Nilikisoma jioni ile ile.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: James anamweleza Bi Amina kuhusu moto mdogo uliotokea katika duka la ujirani wake wiki iliyopita.

(Setting: James tells Bi Amina about a small fire that happened in a shop in his neighborhood last week.)

BI AMINAJames, nimesikia habari za moto katika ujirani wako. Ulitokeaje?James, I've heard news of a fire in your neighborhood. How did it happen?
JAMESNdiyo, ulitokea wiki iliyopita katika duka dogo la mboga. Ulitokea kwa sababu jiko lilisahauliwa likiwaka usiku.Yes, it happened last week at a small vegetable shop. It happened because a stove was left burning at night.
BI AMINAKwani ni nani aliyeuona moto kwanza?So who saw the fire first?
JAMESJirani wa duka aliuona moshi ghafla. Kwa hiyo, aliwapigia simu wazimamoto mara moja.The shop's neighbor suddenly saw the smoke. So, he called the firefighters right away.
BI AMINANa mtoto wa mwenye duka, alikuwa salama?And the shopkeeper's child, was he/she safe?
JAMESNdiyo, jirani alimwona mtoto akikimbia mapema, na alimsaidia kutoka nje ya duka.Yes, the neighbor saw the child running out early, and helped him/her get outside the shop.
BI AMINAWazimamoto walifika lini?When did the firefighters arrive?
JAMESWalifika haraka sana. Ndiyo maana hakuna mtu aliyeumia. Wameuzima moto kabisa sasa, na duka limefungwa kwa ukarabati.They arrived very quickly. That is why no one was hurt. They've put out the fire completely now, and the shop is closed for repairs.
BI AMINAUmesimulia kwa uwazi sana, James. Nimeelewa sababu na matokeo yote.You've narrated very clearly, James. I've understood all the causes and results.

This dialogue contrasts -li- narrated events (ulitokea, aliuona, walifika) with -me- present-relevant results (wameuzima, limefungwa, nimesikia), and uses -m- and other object markers to refer back to the child and the fire once each has been introduced.

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension questions:

  1. Moto ulitokea wapi, na ulisababishwa na nini?
  2. Jirani alifanya nini alipouona moshi?
  3. Mtoto wa mwenye duka alisaidiwa na nani?
  4. Kwa nini hakuna mtu aliyeumia?

Guided Practice

Zoezi 1 — Sababu na Matokeo (Cause and Result Chains)

Practice: linking simple event sentences with cause and result connectors.

  1. Take five short event sentences (provided by your instructor or written by you) and link them in pairs using kwa sababu, kwa hiyo, or ndiyo maana.
  2. Check that each connector matches its intended function: reason before the connector for kwa sababu, result after the connector for kwa hiyo.

Zoezi 2 — Kubadilisha na Vitanzi vya Kirejeshi (Object-Marker Substitution)

Practice: replacing repeated nouns with the correct object marker.

  1. Read a short set of two-sentence pairs in which a noun is repeated, and rewrite the second sentence using the correct object marker instead.
  2. Check that each object marker matches the noun class or person of the noun it replaces.

Zoezi 3 — Ukaguzi wa Wakati (Tense Contrast Audit)

Practice: identifying whether -li- or -me- fits each sentence and correcting mismatches.

  1. Read a short narrative with some tense markers deliberately mismatched to their context and identify each error.
  2. Correct each sentence and explain whether the meaning requires a completed past event or a present-relevant result.

Speaking Practice

Sehemu ya kwanza — Kusimulia Tukio (Narrating an Event)

With a partner, take turns narrating a real or invented personal experience or community incident in four to five connected sentences, using at least one cause connector and one result connector. Your partner should ask one follow-up question about the cause or the result.

Sehemu ya pili — Nani Alimsaidia Nani? (Who Helped Whom?)

Describe a short scenario involving two or three people, using object markers to refer back to each person once you have named them, without repeating their names. Have your partner retell the scenario using the names to check they followed the reference correctly.

Mbadala wa kujifunza peke yako — Solo alternative

Record yourself narrating an event from your own life in five connected sentences, using at least two object markers and one cause-and-result connector. Listen back and check your tense contrast and object-marker accuracy.

Writing Workshop

  1. Chagua tukio. Choose a personal experience or community incident you want to narrate — real or invented.
  2. Panga mfululizo wa matukio. List the sequence of events in order, noting which caused which.
  3. Andika aya. Write a short 90–130-word paragraph narrating the event, using at least one cause connector, one result connector, the -li-/-me- contrast where appropriate, and at least two object markers referring back to people or things already introduced.
  4. Kagua kwa orodha. Check your paragraph against the checklist below.
  5. Andika nakala safi. Produce a clean final version.

Prompt: Write a short narrative about a personal experience or a community event, explaining what happened, why it happened, and what the result was.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • Tense choice (-li- vs. -me-) matches whether the event is simply past or still relevant now.
  • At least one cause connector and one result connector are used accurately.
  • At least two object markers correctly replace a previously introduced person or thing.
  • Relative clauses or demonstratives from Chapter 2 are reused correctly where natural.
  • Capitalization, spacing, punctuation, and ng' are correct throughout.
  • No unscheduled verb extensions or conditionals were attempted productively.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Sharing Community News

In many Swahili-speaking communities, sharing news of an event — an accident, a small emergency, or an act of neighborly help — often carries an expectation of acknowledging who assisted and how the community responded together, not only what went wrong. Narratives commonly move quickly from the problem to the response, reflecting a communicative value placed on resolution and mutual support alongside factual reporting.

Regional Variation Note

The connectors kwa sababu, kwa hiyo, and ndiyo maana practiced in this chapter are used consistently in Tanzanian and Kenyan Standard Swahili for everyday and news narration. Some spoken registers substitute kwa alone or shortened forms in rapid casual speech; that variation is addressed receptively in a later volume and is not a production target here.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing -li- and -me- in Narration

Error: Wazimamoto walifika sasa hivi, na moto umezima kabisa. (mixes a present-relevant time expression with the simple past marker inconsistently)

Correction: Wazimamoto wamefika sasa hivi, na moto umezima kabisa. — when the event's result is still relevant right now, -me- is required throughout, not only in one clause of the sentence.

Common Mistake — Placing the Result Connector Before the Reason

Error: Kwa hiyo, jiko lilisahauliwa likiwaka, moto ulitokea. (result connector placed before the cause it should follow)

Correction: Jiko lilisahauliwa likiwaka. Kwa hiyo, moto ulitokea. — kwa hiyo introduces the result and must come after the cause has been stated, not before it.

Common Mistake — Repeating the Noun Instead of Using an Object Marker

Error: Jirani alimwona mtoto. Jirani alimsaidia mtoto. (the noun mtoto is repeated where an object marker alone is expected)

Correction: Jirani alimwona mtoto. Alimsaidia. — once mtoto has been introduced, the object marker -m- alone is enough to refer back to him or her; repeating the full noun sounds unnaturally heavy in connected narration.

B2-Bridge Integrated Project · Speak and Write Swahili, Volume III: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Overview

Chapter 19 asks you to bring together everything you consolidated in Chapters 17 and 18 into a single extended project of your own design. You will choose a real-world community, media, or professional topic, research and organize it, and produce both a written report or essay and a spoken presentation on that topic, followed by a short question-and-answer exchange and a related listening task. No new grammar is introduced in this chapter. Instead, you are asked to select, from your own control of Volume III systems — noun-class agreement, TAM and negation, relative clauses, verb extensions, connectors, and register — the tools that best serve your chosen topic, and to apply the three-pass editing process from Chapter 18 to your own writing before you present it aloud. This chapter functions as a bridge: it previews the kind of independent, self-directed, multi-skill work expected at the B2 level and in Volume IV.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Select a real-world community, media, or professional topic suitable for an independent Swahili project.
  2. Plan a project that combines a written component (report or essay) and a spoken component (presentation) on the same topic.
  3. Apply the three-pass editing process from Chapter 18 to your own extended writing before presenting it.
  4. Select and combine grammar and discourse tools already consolidated in this volume — agreement, TAM, relative clauses, verb extensions, and connectors — appropriate to your topic and register.
  5. Deliver a spoken presentation of your project without reading it word for word, using self-monitoring for pronunciation and fluency.
  6. Respond to follow-up questions about your project in real time.
  7. Complete a listening task connected to a classmate's or a model project topic and summarize what you understood.

Opening Communication Scenario

Zainabu's neighborhood newsletter editor liked her essay about her first month in Nairobi so much that he invites her to present a short project at the newsletter's monthly community meeting. He tells her she may choose any topic connected to community life, media, or work — as long as she can explain it clearly, answer questions about it, and hand in a short written version for the newsletter afterward. Zainabu decides to write and present about the neighborhood's community water project, the same topic she read about in Chapter 17, but now from her own perspective as a resident who attended a follow-up meeting.

  1. This chapter develops an independent, self-selected project combining writing and speaking on one topic.
  2. No new grammar is introduced. Learners choose from the full range of systems consolidated across Chapters 1–18.
  3. The project must include a written component, an oral presentation, a question-and-answer exchange, and a connected listening response.
  4. Zainabu's project models the process: choose a topic, plan, draft, edit using the three-pass process, and present.

Pronunciation and Orthography Focus

Focus — Self-Monitoring During Live Presentation

Presenting a project aloud is different from reading a rehearsed dialogue: you must monitor your own pronunciation, stress, and rhythm in real time while also tracking content. Before presenting, mark your written notes with light stress marks over any long or unfamiliar words, especially derived vocabulary such as uwasilishaji and tathmini, so your eye catches the correct stress pattern at a glance rather than defaulting to English-style stress.

If you notice yourself rushing or flattening the five vowels under pressure, pause briefly rather than pushing through; a short, controlled pause preserves intelligibility far better than a rushed, vowel-reduced phrase.

Project Toolkit: Integrating Volume III Systems

This section does not introduce new grammar. It reviews how to select, from your existing control, the tools best suited to a real-world project, and how to plan a presentation that stays within a natural speaking register rather than simply reading your report aloud.

Choosing Tools to Fit Your Topic

Review — Matching Grammar to Communicative Purpose

A community topic, a media topic, and a professional topic each tend to call on somewhat different combinations of the systems you have consolidated in this volume. A community project may lean on relative clauses to define local roles and institutions, and on the applicative extension to describe who did something for whom. A media summary may lean on passive constructions and reported-speech formulae from Chapter 12. A professional or workplace topic may lean more heavily on formal register and obligation structures from Chapter 11.

  • Mradi wa maji unaosimamiwa na kamati ya wakazi umewezesha familia nyingi kupata maji safi. The water project managed by the residents' committee has enabled many families to get clean water. relative clause "unaosimamiwa" + causative/applicative "umewezesha"

Before drafting, list two or three grammar tools from earlier chapters that best fit your chosen topic, and plan to use them deliberately rather than defaulting only to simple sentences.

From Written Report to Spoken Presentation

Review — Adapting Register for Speaking

A written report and a spoken presentation on the same topic are not identical texts. Spoken presentation typically uses shorter sentences, more direct address to the audience, and signposting language that helps listeners follow the structure in real time, even when the underlying content and grammar tools are the same as in the written version.

  • Written: Ripoti hii inachunguza changamoto tatu kuu zinazoikabili jumuiya. This report examines three main challenges facing the community.
  • Spoken: Leo nitawaeleza changamoto tatu kuu tunazokabiliana nazo. Today I will explain to you the three main challenges we are dealing with. direct address "nitawaeleza" replaces the more formal, agentless written framing

When you convert your written draft into presentation notes, do not simply read the report aloud; rephrase it for direct spoken address to your audience.

Building Block — A Four-Stage Project Process
[Stage 1: Choose topic and gather content] → [Stage 2: Draft the written report/essay] → [Stage 3: Apply the three-pass edit from Chapter 18] → [Stage 4: Adapt the edited draft into spoken presentation notes]

Complete each stage fully before moving to the next. Do not begin adapting spoken notes until the written draft has passed all three editing passes.

This four-stage process extends the three-pass editing process from Chapter 18 by adding a final stage: converting a polished written text into a natural spoken register.

  2. Rewrite the sentence "Ripoti hii inachunguza changamoto tatu kuu zinazoikabili jumuiya" as a direct spoken sentence addressed to an audience.
  3. Identify one place in your own planned topic where a relative clause, a verb extension, or a connector from earlier chapters would strengthen your explanation, and draft that sentence.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: Zainabu anawasilisha mradi wake kuhusu mradi wa maji mbele ya kikundi kidogo cha jumuiya, na baadaye anajibu maswali kutoka kwa hadhira.

(Setting: Zainabu presents her project about the water project to a small community group, and afterward answers questions from the audience.)

ZAINABULeo nitawaeleza kuhusu mradi wa maji unaosimamiwa na kamati ya wakazi wa mtaa wetu.Today I will explain to you about the water project managed by our neighborhood's residents' committee.
ZAINABUKabla ya mradi huu, familia nyingi zilitembea umbali mrefu kuchota maji; sasa, shukrani kwa mradi huu, wamewezeshwa kupata maji karibu na nyumba zao.Before this project, many families walked a long distance to fetch water; now, thanks to this project, they have been enabled to get water near their homes.
(Hadhira inasikiliza kwa makini; mkono mmoja unainuka.)
(The audience listens attentively; one hand goes up.)
MSIKILIZAJIAsante kwa uwasilishaji mzuri. Je, kamati ya wakazi inashirikianaje na serikali ya mtaa?Thank you for the good presentation. How does the residents' committee cooperate with the local government?
ZAINABUSwali zuri. Kamati inashirikiana na serikali ya mtaa kwa kuwasilisha ripoti kila mwezi, na kwa kuombana rasilimali pale zinapohitajika.Good question. The committee cooperates with the local government by submitting a report every month, and by requesting resources from each other when they are needed.
MSIKILIZAJIJe, kuna changamoto zozote zilizojitokeza tangu mradi uanze?Are there any challenges that have arisen since the project began?
ZAINABUNdiyo, changamoto kubwa zaidi imekuwa matengenezo ya bomba wakati wa msimu wa mvua, lakini kamati inatafakari suluhisho pamoja na wahandisi wa manispaa.Yes, the biggest challenge has been pipe maintenance during the rainy season, but the committee is considering a solution together with municipal engineers.

Notice how Zainabu's spoken presentation uses direct address ("nitawaeleza," "asante kwa uwasilishaji") while still relying on the same relative clauses, passive/causative extensions, and connectors she used in her written draft.

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension questions:

  1. Zainabu anawasilisha mradi gani?
  2. Hali ya familia ilikuwaje kabla ya mradi kuanza?
  3. Kamati ya wakazi inashirikianaje na serikali ya mtaa?
  4. Ni changamoto gani kubwa zaidi iliyotajwa?

Guided Practice

Zoezi 1 — Kuchagua Mada (Choosing a Topic)

Practice: narrowing a broad domain into a workable project topic.

  1. From the domains community, media, or professional life, choose one specific, narrow topic you can realistically research and discuss (for example, not "health" broadly, but a specific community health initiative you know about or can plausibly research).
  2. Write one sentence in Swahili stating your topic and why it matters to you or your community.

Zoezi 2 — Kuandaa Vifaa vya Sarufi (Preparing a Grammar Toolkit)

Practice: matching grammar tools to your chosen topic.

  1. List three grammar tools from Volume III (a relative-clause type, a verb extension, a connector, or a register feature) that fit your topic well.
  2. Draft one sentence using each tool, connected to your topic.

Zoezi 3 — Kubadilisha Maandishi kuwa Mazungumzo (Converting Writing to Speech)

Practice: adapting written register for spoken presentation.

  1. Take one written sentence from your own draft and rewrite it as direct spoken address to an audience, following the Building Block model.
  2. Practice saying both versions aloud and note which one feels more natural to deliver without reading word for word.

Speaking Practice

Sehemu ya kwanza — Muhtasari wa Dakika Moja (One-Minute Pitch)

Before drafting your full project, explain your chosen topic aloud in Standard Swahili for 60–90 seconds, covering what the topic is, why it matters, and what you plan to present.

Sehemu ya pili — Uwasilishaji Kamili na Maswali (Full Presentation and Q&A)

Ripoti hii inachunguza mradi wa maji unaosimamiwa na kamati ya wakazi wa mtaa wetu, ambao umeanzishwa miaka miwili iliyopita. Writing Workshop

  1. Chagua mada yako. In English, state your chosen community, media, or professional topic and why it matters.
  2. Panga vifaa vya sarufi. List at least three grammar tools from this volume that fit your topic, as practiced in Zoezi 2.
  3. Andika ripoti au insha yako. Write a 150–200-word Standard Swahili report or essay on your topic, covering background, main points, one challenge, and a recommendation or conclusion.
  4. Kagua kwa mapito matatu. Apply the three-pass editing process from Chapter 18: agreement, then clause-linking, then verb form.
  5. Badilisha kuwa maandishi ya uwasilishaji. Adapt your edited draft into spoken presentation notes using direct address, as modeled in the Project Toolkit section.

Prompt: Write a short Standard Swahili report or essay on a real or invented community, media, or professional project of your own choosing, following the structure of the model report above: background, main points, one challenge, and a recommendation or conclusion.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • Every noun that reappears later in the report keeps its class consistent across subject markers, object markers, and demonstratives.
  • Sentences are linked with appropriate connectors; no sentence stacks more than two subordinate or relative clauses.
  • Every verb's TAM marker and polarity accurately match the intended time reference and meaning.
  • The report states clear background, at least two main points, one challenge, and a recommendation or conclusion.
  • The presentation notes use direct address rather than simply repeating the written register word for word.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Community Meetings and Public Presentation

Community meetings, neighborhood newsletters, and public reporting on shared projects — such as water access, sanitation, or youth initiatives — are a common part of civic life in many East African communities. Presenting a project to a community group, as Zainabu does in the Model Dialogue, typically combines a respectful greeting, clear background information, and an openness to questions, since community accountability often depends on this kind of two-way exchange rather than a one-directional announcement.

Regional Variation Note

The structure of a community or project presentation — background, main points, challenge, recommendation, and question-and-answer — is broadly shared across Tanzanian and Kenyan community and civic contexts, since both rest on similar public-communication expectations. Specific formulas for opening a public presentation, closing it, or thanking an audience may vary somewhat by community and setting; this chapter uses a widely applicable pattern rather than a single fixed script.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Reading the Written Report Aloud Word for Word

Error: presenting a project by reading the formal written report directly, without adapting it to direct spoken address for an audience

Correction: during Stage 4 of the project process, rewrite key sentences using direct address ("nitawaeleza," "kama mnavyoona") rather than the more formal, agentless written framing.

Common Mistake — Choosing a Topic Too Broad to Cover Well

Error: selecting an overly broad domain, such as "education" or "health," without narrowing it to a specific, manageable project

Correction: during Zoezi 1, narrow any broad domain to one specific initiative, event, or issue that can realistically be researched and discussed within the length limits of this project.

Common Mistake — Skipping the Editing Passes Before Presenting

Error: moving directly from a first draft to a spoken presentation without applying the three-pass editing process from Chapter 18

Correction: complete all three editing passes on the written draft first, since a clean written draft makes it much easier to adapt an accurate, natural spoken version.

Independent Study Tools

If you are studying without a partner or instructor, use these strategies to complete this chapter's work:

  • Search for a short, real (adapted-level or simplified) Swahili news item or community announcement on a topic similar to yours, and note any useful vocabulary or structures, being careful to write your own original report rather than copying the source.
  • Record your one-minute pitch, your full presentation, and your Q&A responses separately, and listen back to each for pronunciation and fluency self-monitoring.
  • Write out two or three questions you expect to be asked and prepare brief answers in advance, then practice responding to unexpected variations of those questions.
  • Ask a Swahili-speaking contact, tutor, or qualified reviewer to check your audited draft, final version, and recorded presentation against the rubric in the Practical Performance Task.

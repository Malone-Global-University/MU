Elimu, Kazi, na Mawasiliano ya Kikazi

Education, Work, and Professional Interaction · Speak and Write Swahili, Volume III: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Overview

Chapter 11 opens Unit 3 by moving the connected discourse you consolidated in Chapters 1–10 into workplace and education settings. This chapter introduces no new noun classes and no new verb extensions; instead, it develops two new communicative tools — formal requests and obligation — while reviewing the passive and applicative extensions inside professional and academic vocabulary. You will discuss training, meetings, workplace responsibilities, and school or university plans, and you will write the kind of message a colleague, classmate, or supervisor might actually receive: a request for leave, a note about a missed deadline, or a short plan for an upcoming project. As with every chapter in this unit, precision in noun-class agreement matters even more here, because professional vocabulary draws on several different classes at once.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Make a formal request using polite indirect forms appropriate to workplace and academic settings.
  2. Express obligation and necessity using lazima, ni lazima, and inabidi constructions.
  3. Discuss meetings, training, deadlines, and responsibilities using consolidated tense-aspect-mood forms.
  4. Recognize and use passive and applicative forms accurately within professional and academic vocabulary.
  5. Apply noun-class agreement precisely across a wider set of workplace and education nouns.
  6. Write a formal message requesting something or explaining a work- or study-related situation.
  7. Present a short work or study plan clearly and in an appropriately formal register.

Opening Communication Scenario

Monday morning has arrived at an office in Dar es Salaam. Fatuma, who works in the training department, needs to reschedule a meeting, request three days of leave to attend her sister's wedding, and prepare a short training plan for two new employees. Meanwhile, her colleague Juma is finishing a certificate course in the evenings and needs to explain to his supervisor why he must leave work early twice a week. Both conversations require the same underlying skill: making requests and explaining obligations clearly, politely, and in language appropriate to a professional setting.

  1. This chapter introduces formal requests and obligation as new productive grammar.
  2. Passive and applicative forms are reviewed, not retaught, inside workplace and education vocabulary.
  3. Tasks combine listening, speaking, reading, and writing around one connected office-and-classroom context.
  4. No task in this chapter requires a noun class, relative clause type, or verb extension that Chapters 1–10 have not already activated.

Pronunciation and Orthography Focus

Focus — Formal Phrasing and Clear Sentence Rhythm

Formal requests in Swahili often use longer sentences with a polite frame at the start, such as Ningependa kuomba... ("I would like to request...") or Ningeshukuru kama... ("I would be grateful if..."). These longer openings carry their own rhythm: keep the five vowels stable through the string of unstressed syllables in ningependa and ningeshukuru, and let penultimate stress fall naturally on each word rather than flattening the whole phrase into one rushed unit.

In writing, formal messages should use complete sentences, correct capitalization for names and titles such as Bwana, Bibi, and Mheshimiwa, and a clear closing such as Nawasilisha kwa heshima ("I submit this respectfully").

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This section introduces formal requests and obligation as new productive grammar, and reviews the passive and applicative extensions from Chapters 6 and 7 inside professional and academic vocabulary.

Formal Requests

New — Making Polite, Indirect Requests

Everyday requests often use the imperative or a simple naomba ("I ask for"). Workplace and academic requests typically soften the request further using the conditional-like frame ningependa ("I would like") or ningeshukuru kama ("I would be grateful if"), followed by an infinitive or a kama clause.

  • Ningependa kuomba likizo ya siku tatu. I would like to request three days of leave.
  • Ningeshukuru kama tungeweza kuahirisha mkutano hadi Alhamisi. I would be grateful if we could postpone the meeting until Thursday.
  • Je, ningeweza kuzungumza na msimamizi kuhusu jukumu langu jipya? Might I speak with the supervisor about my new responsibility?

Obligation: Lazima and Inabidi

New — Expressing Necessity and Duty

Lazima and ni lazima express strong necessity and are typically followed by a subjunctive verb (final -e). Inabidi functions similarly and is common in spoken and written workplace language.

  • Lazima nifike ofisini kabla ya saa mbili. I must arrive at the office before eight o'clock.
  • Ni lazima ripoti hii ikamilike kabla ya muda wa mwisho. This report must be completed before the deadline.
  • Inabidi niondoke mapema mara mbili kwa wiki kwa sababu ya masomo yangu. I have to leave early twice a week because of my studies.

Reviewing Passive and Applicative in Professional Vocabulary

Review — Passive and Applicative at Work and School

Chapters 6 and 7 introduced the applicative (-i-/-e-) and passive (-w-) extensions. In professional and academic contexts, these forms appear frequently: reports are submitted (passive), tasks are done for a supervisor or client (applicative), and training is given to new employees (applicative).

  • Ripoti iliwasilishwa jana. The report was submitted yesterday. wasilisha → wasilishwa (passive)
  • Msimamizi aliwafundishia wafanyakazi wapya kutumia mfumo mpya. The supervisor taught the new employees how to use the new system (for their benefit). fundisha → fundishia (applicative)
Building Block — Formal Request Plus Obligation
Ningependa/Ningeshukuru + kuomba/kama + [request], kwa sababu lazima/inabidi + [subjunctive verb] + [reason]

Ningependa kuomba ruhusa ya kuondoka mapema Jumanne na Alhamisi, kwa sababu lazima nifike chuoni kwa wakati kwa ajili ya mafunzo yangu. I would like to request permission to leave early on Tuesdays and Thursdays, because I must arrive at college on time for my training.

Notice how the polite request frame and the obligation frame combine in one connected sentence — both are new this chapter, but they build directly on the reason clauses and subjunctive forms already consolidated in earlier chapters.

Zoezi la Sarufi — Grammar Practice:

  1. Rewrite five direct requests as formal requests using ningependa or ningeshukuru kama.
  2. Write three sentences expressing obligation using lazima or inabidi, each with a different subjunctive verb.
  3. Identify the passive or applicative form in five sentences drawn from workplace or academic contexts.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: Fatuma anazungumza na msimamizi wake, Bwana Kileo, ofisini kuhusu likizo na mkutano ujao.

(Setting: Fatuma is speaking with her supervisor, Bwana Kileo, at the office about leave and an upcoming meeting.)

FATUMABwana Kileo, ningependa kuomba likizo ya siku tatu wiki ijayo. Dada yangu anaolewa Jumamosi.Bwana Kileo, I would like to request three days of leave next week. My sister is getting married on Saturday.
BWANA KILEOHongera kwa dada yako! Sawa, lakini ripoti ya mafunzo lazima iwasilishwe kabla hujaondoka.Congratulations to your sister! Alright, but the training report must be submitted before you leave.
FATUMANimeshaikamilisha. Niliwafundishia wafanyakazi wapya jana, na ripoti iliandikwa mara moja.I have already finished it. I taught the new employees yesterday, and the report was written right away.
BWANA KILEOVizuri sana. Kwa upande mwingine, ningeshukuru kama tungeahirisha mkutano wa Jumanne hadi Alhamisi.Very good. On another note, I would be grateful if we could postpone Tuesday's meeting until Thursday.
FATUMAHakuna tatizo. Nitawajulisha wafanyakazi wengine kwa barua pepe leo.No problem. I will inform the other employees by email today.
(Baadaye, Juma anaingia ofisini kwa Bwana Kileo.)
JUMABwana Kileo, naomba dakika chache. Inabidi niondoke mapema Jumanne na Alhamisi kwa sababu ya masomo yangu chuoni.Bwana Kileo, may I have a few minutes. I have to leave early on Tuesdays and Thursdays because of my studies at college.
BWANA KILEONakuelewa. Lazima ujaze fomu ya ombi, kisha itasainiwa na mimi.I understand you. You must fill out a request form, and then it will be signed by me.
JUMAAsante sana. Ningependa pia kujua kama vyeti vya mafunzo vitatolewa mwishoni mwa mwaka.Thank you very much. I would also like to know if the training certificates will be given out at the end of the year.

This dialogue combines formal requests (ningependa kuomba, ningeshukuru kama, naomba dakika chache), obligation (lazima iwasilishwe, lazima ujaze, inabidi niondoke), and reviewed passives and applicatives (iliandikwa, itasainiwa, vitatolewa, niliwafundishia) inside workplace and education vocabulary.

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension questions:

  1. Kwa nini Fatuma anaomba likizo?
  2. Ripoti ya mafunzo lazima ifanyike lini, kulingana na Bwana Kileo?
  3. Bwana Kileo anaomba nini kuhusu mkutano wa Jumanne?
  4. Kwa nini Juma anahitaji kuondoka mapema siku mbili kwa wiki?

Guided Practice

Zoezi 1 — Kuomba kwa Heshima (Requesting Politely)

Practice: transforming direct requests into formal requests.

  1. Rewrite each direct request using ningependa or ningeshukuru kama: "Nipe siku moja ya likizo." / "Ahirisha mkutano." / "Nisaidie na ripoti hii."
  2. Read your rewritten requests aloud, maintaining stable vowels through the longer polite frame.

Zoezi 2 — Majukumu na Wajibu (Duties and Obligations)

Practice: expressing obligation with a partner or independently.

  1. List three of your own real or invented work or study obligations, using lazima or inabidi for each.
  2. Exchange lists with a partner and ask a follow-up question about one obligation using a conditional (for example: "Nini kitatokea kama hutakamilisha ripoti kwa wakati?").

Zoezi 3 — Kutambua Vitenzi Vilivyoathiriwa (Identifying Extended Verbs)

Practice: finding passive and applicative forms in workplace vocabulary.

  1. Underline every passive and applicative verb form in the model dialogue and state the base verb for each.
  2. Write two original sentences about your own workplace or school using a passive form and an applicative form.

Writing Workshop

  1. Panga muundo. Plan a formal message requesting something from a supervisor, instructor, or colleague (leave, a deadline extension, help with a task, or a schedule change), including a reason and at least one obligation you will meet.
  2. Andika rasimu. Write a 100–150-word formal message following the model letter's structure: greeting, purpose, reason, a commitment or obligation, a polite closing.
  3. Kagua kwa orodha. Check your message against the checklist below.
  4. Andika nakala safi. Produce a clean final version.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • The message opens and closes with an appropriately formal frame.
  • At least one formal request is expressed using ningependa or ningeshukuru kama.
  • At least one obligation is expressed using lazima or inabidi with a correct subjunctive verb.
  • At least one passive or applicative form is used accurately from reviewed workplace/education vocabulary.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Formality in Workplace Requests

In many East African workplaces and schools, written requests to supervisors, employers, or instructors are expected to be formal in structure, even when the underlying relationship is friendly. A written request typically states the purpose clearly near the beginning, gives a brief reason, and closes with a respectful phrase such as Nawasilisha kwa heshima ("I submit this respectfully"). Spoken requests in the same settings often soften directness with phrases like ningependa or ningeshukuru kama, even between colleagues who know each other well.

Regional Variation Note

The formal request and obligation structures in this chapter are stable across Tanzanian and Kenyan workplace and academic Swahili. Some institutions use additional set phrases or letterhead conventions in official correspondence; these institution-specific conventions are treated as receptive, specialist material and are not required for productive use at this stage.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using the Indicative After Lazima

Error: Lazima ninafika ofisini mapema. (uses the present indicative -na- after lazima)

Correction: Lazima nifike ofisini mapema. — lazima requires the subjunctive final vowel -e, not the indicative -na- form; the verb changes from ninafika to nifike.

Common Mistake — Overusing the Imperative in Formal Requests

Error: Nipe likizo ya siku tatu. (blunt imperative addressed to a supervisor)

Correction: Ningependa kuomba likizo ya siku tatu. — the imperative is appropriate among peers or in very informal contexts, but a formal workplace request should use the softened ningependa or ningeshukuru kama frame.

Common Mistake — Confusing Passive and Applicative in Reports

Error: Ripoti iliandikia msimamizi. (uses the applicative where a passive is intended)

Correction: Ripoti iliandikwa na msimamizi. — if the meaning is simply "the report was written by the supervisor," the passive -w- is required; -i-/applicative would instead mean the report was written for or to someone.

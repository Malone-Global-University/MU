Tathmini ya Kati

Mid-Volume B2-Bridge Assessment · Speak and Write Swahili, Volume III: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Overview

Chapter 10 is a cumulative assessment chapter. It introduces no new grammar. Instead, it gathers together the connected discourse, relative clauses, conditionals, and the applicative, passive, causative, and reciprocal extensions developed across Chapters 1–9 and asks you to show what you can do with them, together, in realistic tasks. This chapter functions as a bridge: it confirms that the foundations of Unit I and the three verb extensions of Unit II are solid enough to support the heavier discourse, professional, and civic work of Units 3 and 4. Every task in this chapter draws only on systems already marked developed or consolidated in the Volume III matrices; nothing here should feel unfamiliar in kind, even if it asks you to combine familiar pieces in longer, more connected ways.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Narrate a connected sequence of events using consolidated past, perfect, and habitual forms with accurate cause-and-result connectors.
  2. Use relative clauses with amba- to define and describe people, places, and things across active noun classes.
  3. Express conditions and their consequences using kama and -ki- in practical, everyday scenarios.
  4. Recognize and use the applicative, passive, causative, and reciprocal verb extensions accurately in context.
  5. Distinguish a simple verb form from its extended counterpart in meaning and in the resulting sentence structure.
  6. Integrate listening, speaking, reading, and writing evidence into one connected community-project narrative.
  7. Identify and correct agreement, verb-form, and extension errors in your own and others' work.

Opening Communication Scenario

Saturday has arrived. The tree-planting project that has run through Chapters 6–9 — with James, Bi Amina, Juma, Fatuma, and the rest of the group — reaches its planting day. As the group works together, waters trees, resolves a last scheduling question, and reflects on the weeks that led up to this morning, you will narrate, describe, question, and reflect on everything the group did, for each other, to each other, and because of each other. This chapter does not introduce a new stage of the story; it asks you to tell the story so far, accurately and completely, using the tools you have already built.

  1. This chapter introduces no new grammar. It reviews and assesses Chapters 1–9.
  2. Tasks combine relative clauses, conditionals, and the three verb extensions from Unit II within one connected community-project context.
  3. Evidence is collected across listening, speaking, reading, and writing, as in a normal chapter, but every task here counts as cumulative assessment.
  4. No task in this chapter requires a form, class, or structure that has not already been developed in Chapters 1–9.

Pronunciation and Fluency Checkpoint

Checkpoint — Rhythm and Stress Across Derived Verbs

Chapters 6–9 added applicative, passive, causative, and reciprocal endings to familiar verb stems, each one adding syllables and shifting the location of penultimate stress. This checkpoint reviews, rather than introduces, that pattern: as you read the assessment dialogue and reading passage aloud, notice how a base verb such as saidia lengthens into saidiwa (passive), saidiana (reciprocal), or saidisha-type causative shapes, and how the stressed syllable moves with each added ending.

Continue to maintain five stable vowel qualities and avoid English-style vowel reduction, especially in these longer derived forms, where the temptation to swallow an unstressed syllable is strongest.

Grammar Review: Unit II Systems

This section introduces no new grammar. It consolidates the systems developed across Chapters 1–9 so that you can use them together, accurately, in connected discourse.

Connected Narration: Cause, Result, and Sequence

Review — Narrating with Reason and Result

Chapter 3 developed connectors such as kwa sababu ("because"), hivyo and kwa hiyo ("so, therefore"), and past/perfect contrast for sequencing events. This chapter asks you to use these connectors across a longer stretch of narration, not just within a single sentence.

Relative Clauses with amba-

Review — Defining People, Places, and Things

Chapter 2 developed amba- relative clauses across active noun classes: mtu ambaye (the person who), mradi ambao (the project that), kazi ambayo (the work that). This chapter asks you to use these relatives to define and identify people and things inside a connected narrative, not only in isolated definition sentences.

Conditionals: kama and -ki-

Review — Conditions and Consequences

Chapter 5 developed kama clauses and the -ki- conditional marker for practical, likely conditions: Kama mvua ikinyesha, hatutapanda miti. (If it rains, we will not plant trees.) This chapter asks you to use conditionals to discuss contingencies within the tree-planting project.

Building Block — Combining Systems in Connected Discourse
Connector + subject marker + TAM + (extension) + stem + final vowel (+ relative or conditional clause)

Kwa sababu wanakikundi hawakuelewana wiki iliyopita, waliamua kwamba kama mzozo mwingine ukitokea, watazungumza mara moja badala ya kungoja. Because the group members did not understand each other last week, they decided that if another dispute arises, they will talk right away instead of waiting.

Notice how this single connected sentence draws on cause (kwa sababu), the reciprocal (hawakuelewana), reported decision, and a conditional (kama... ukitokea) — all systems already active from earlier chapters, combined rather than expanded.

  2. Combine a relative clause and a conditional in one sentence about the tree-planting project.
  3. Rewrite a short one-directional narrative as a connected passage using at least one cause/result connector and one reciprocal verb.

Assessment Dialogue

Muktadha: Ni Jumamosi asubuhi. Kikundi kimekusanyika mahali pa mradi kupanda miti, na wanazungumza kuhusu kazi iliyofanyika na iliyobaki.

(Setting: It is Saturday morning. The group has gathered at the project site to plant trees, and they are talking about the work that has been done and what remains.)

BI AMINAMiti ambayo tuliipanda wiki iliyopita inaonekana vizuri. Kama tutaendelea hivi, mradi utakamilika mapema kuliko tulivyopanga.The trees that we planted last week look good. If we continue like this, the project will finish earlier than we planned.
JAMESKwa sababu tulisaidiana vizuri wiki iliyopita, kazi ya leo itakuwa rahisi zaidi. Miti hii ilipandwa na kikundi kizima, si mtu mmoja tu.Because we helped each other well last week, today's work will be easier. These trees were planted by the whole group, not just one person.
JUMAKabla ya kuanza, ningependa kuwaeleweshe wanakikundi wapya jinsi ya kutumia zana hizi kwa usalama.Before we start, I would like to help the new group members understand how to use these tools safely.
FATUMAWazo zuri. Mtu ambaye hajawahi kutumia jembe anapaswa kufundishwa kwanza.Good idea. A person who has never used a hoe before should be taught first.
BI AMINASawa. Kama mtu akijisikia mchovu, atapumzika chini ya mti ule mkubwa. Tunahitaji kusaidiana, si kushindana.Okay. If someone feels tired, they will rest under that big tree. We need to help each other, not compete against each other.
JAMESKwa hiyo, tuseme tumeelewana: tutapanda miti hadi saa saba, kisha tutaonana tena wiki ijayo kuangalia matokeo.So, let's say we understand each other: we will plant trees until one o'clock, then we will see each other again next week to look at the results.
FATUMANakubali. Mradi huu umetufundisha kwamba kazi inayofanywa pamoja huleta matokeo bora zaidi kuliko kazi inayofanywa peke yake.I agree. This project has taught us that work that is done together brings better results than work that is done alone.

This dialogue integrates a relative clause (miti ambayo tuliipanda, mtu ambaye hajawahi), conditionals (kama tutaendelea, kama mtu akijisikia), a passive (ilipandwa, inayofanywa), a causative (kuwaeleweshe), and reciprocals (tulisaidiana, tumeelewana, tutaonana, kusaidiana) — all previously active systems used together.

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension questions:

  1. Kwa nini kazi ya leo itakuwa rahisi zaidi, kulingana na James?
  2. Juma anataka kufanya nini kabla ya kuanza kupanda miti?
  3. Kikundi kitafanya nini ikiwa mtu atajisikia mchovu?
  4. Mradi umewafundisha nini wanakikundi kuhusu kazi ya pamoja?

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Community Projects as Shared Achievement

Community-driven projects like the tree-planting effort in this unit are common across Swahili-speaking communities and are often described in terms of collective rather than individual accomplishment: the trees were planted "by the group," the results belong to the neighborhood, and setbacks are worked through collectively rather than assigned to a single person. Reviewing passive, causative, and reciprocal forms in this chapter supports exactly this kind of collective description.

Regional Variation Note

The narration, description, and conditional structures reviewed in this chapter are stable across Tanzanian and Kenyan Standard Swahili. As in earlier chapters, more specialized or regionally marked ways of expressing conditions and consequence exist and will be addressed as labeled, receptive material in later volumes.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing Passive and Reciprocal Endings

Error: Miti ilipandana na kikundi. (uses the reciprocal ending where passive meaning is intended)

Correction: Miti ilipandwa na kikundi. — when trees are planted by a group (a one-directional action with an optional agent), the passive -w- is required. The reciprocal -ana would incorrectly suggest that the trees planted each other.

Common Mistake — Relative Clause Not Agreeing with Head Noun Class

Error: Mradi ambaye ulianzishwa na Bi Amina... (uses a class 1/2 relative marker with a class 3/4 noun)

Correction: Mradi ambao ulianzishwa na Bi Amina... — mradi belongs to class 3, so the relative marker must be -o- (ambao), not the class 1 marker -ye- used for people.

Common Mistake — Mixing kama and -ki- Redundantly

Error: Kama mvua ikinyesha kama, hatutapanda miti. (repeats kama unnecessarily)

Correction: Kama mvua ikinyesha, hatutapanda miti. — the conditional marker -ki- inside the verb already signals the condition together with the initial kama; a second kama at the end of the clause is redundant and non-standard.

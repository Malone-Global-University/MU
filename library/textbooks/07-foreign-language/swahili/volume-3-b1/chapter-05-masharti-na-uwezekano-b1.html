Masharti na Uwezekano

Conditionals and Practical Hypotheticals · Speak and Write Swahili, Volume III: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Overview

Chapter 5 closes Unit I by moving from requests and advice to conditions and consequences. Building on the subjunctive you developed in Chapter 4, you will now develop kama ("if") clauses for stating conditions, introduce the -ki- conditional for the first time as a productive way of expressing "if" or "when" a likely condition is met, and preview the -nge- conditional receptively for talking about less likely or contrary-to-fact situations. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to discuss plans, safety measures, choices, and everyday problem-solving in terms of conditions and their outcomes.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Form and use kama clauses to state a condition and its result.
  2. Form and use the -ki- conditional to express a likely or general "if/when" condition.
  3. Recognize the -nge- conditional receptively for less likely or contrary-to-fact situations, without being required to produce it independently.
  4. Distinguish kama clauses from the -ki- conditional and choose the appropriate pattern for a given situation.
  5. Discuss plans, safety measures, and choices using conditional structures.
  6. Identify and correct common errors in conditional formation.
  7. Explain what to do in different practical scenarios using conditional reasoning, in speaking and writing.

Opening Communication Scenario

The fire-safety meeting that James organized in Chapters 3 and 4 has now taken place, and the neighborhood is drafting a simple safety plan. James needs to explain what residents should do under different conditions — if there is smoke, if the power goes out, if someone smells gas — and Bi Amina points out that this kind of planning language depends heavily on conditional sentences. She shows James two related but different patterns: kama clauses, which state a condition plainly, and the -ki- conditional, which is often used for general or likely "if/when" situations, especially in instructions and plans.

  1. This chapter develops kama clauses productively, using the subjunctive and TAM forms already available from earlier chapters.
  2. The -ki- conditional is introduced for the first time as a productive tool for general and likely conditions.
  3. The -nge- conditional is previewed receptively only; learners are not expected to produce it independently in this chapter.
  4. Vocabulary in this chapter supports plans, safety, choices, and problem-solving.

Pronunciation Focus

Conditional Phrasing

Conditional sentences in Swahili are typically spoken as two connected phrases, with a small rising pitch and a brief pause at the end of the condition, followed by a falling pitch across the result. Compare Kama mvua ikinyesha, (rising pitch, slight pause) tutabaki nyumbani. (falling pitch to the end of the sentence).

Continue to keep the -ki- marker's vowel clear and full, and avoid collapsing kama and the following subject marker together in rapid speech, since doing so can obscure which noun class or person is the subject of the condition.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This section develops kama clauses, introduces the -ki- conditional for the first time, and previews the -nge- conditional receptively.

Kama Clauses

Building Block — Stating a Condition with Kama
Kama + [condition clause], + [result clause]

Kama moshi ukitokea, tutaondoka haraka. If smoke occurs, we will leave quickly. kama + u- (class 3 subject) + ki- + tokea

Kama umeme ukikatika, tutumie tochi. If the power goes out, let's use a flashlight. kama + u- (class 3 subject) + ki- + katika

Kama huna uhakika, muulize mheshimiwa. If you are not sure, ask the official. kama + hu- (negative subject) + na + uhakika

Kama introduces a condition and may be followed by a present, past, future, or negative clause, depending on the situation described. The result clause may use present, future, imperative, or subjunctive forms, depending on what is being recommended or predicted.

The -ki- Conditional

New System — Subject Marker + -ki- + Stem

The -ki- conditional is formed by inserting -ki- between the subject marker and the verb stem. It is commonly used for general, likely, or habitual "if/when" conditions, often in plans, instructions, and safety guidance, and can appear with or without kama.

Ukiona moshi, toka nje mara moja. If/when you see smoke, go outside at once.

Wakazi wakifika mapema, tutaanza mkutano kwa wakati. If/when the residents arrive early, we will start the meeting on time.

Kama mtoto akipotea, mwambie mlinzi haraka. If a child gets lost, tell the guard quickly.

Notice that kama and the -ki- conditional may combine in the same sentence, with kama marking the overall conditional relationship and -ki- carrying the conditional meaning inside the verb itself.

Previewing the -nge- Conditional (Receptive Only)

Receptive Preview — Not a Production Target

Swahili also has an -nge- conditional, used for less likely or contrary-to-fact situations ("if X were to happen..." or "if X had happened..."). You may encounter it in this chapter's readings or listening passages, glossed in context, but you are not expected to produce it independently. It will become a productive system in a later chapter.

Kama ningejua, ningekuja mapema. If I had known, I would have come early. (recognition only)

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: James na Bi Amina wanaandaa mpango rahisi wa usalama wa moto kwa mtaa wao, baada ya mkutano wa Sura ya 4.

(Setting: James and Bi Amina are drafting a simple fire-safety plan for their neighborhood, after the meeting in Chapter 4.)

BI AMINAJames, tuanze na hali ya hatari zaidi. Kama moshi ukitokea ndani ya nyumba, watu wafanye nini?James, let's start with the most dangerous situation. If smoke occurs inside a house, what should people do?
JAMESKama moshi ukitokea, wakazi watoke nje mara moja na wasirudi ndani.If smoke occurs, residents should go outside at once and not go back inside.
BI AMINASawa. Na kama umeme ukikatika usiku, watu wafanye nini?Okay. And if the power goes out at night, what should people do?
JAMESKama umeme ukikatika, watumie tochi badala ya mishumaa, ili kuzuia hatari nyingine ya moto.If the power goes out, they should use a flashlight instead of candles, in order to prevent another fire risk.
BI AMINAWazo zuri. Je, kama mtoto akipotea wakati wa dharura?Good idea. What about if a child gets lost during an emergency?
JAMESKama mtoto akipotea, mzazi amwambie mlinzi wa mtaa haraka, na wakazi wote wasaidie kutafuta.If a child gets lost, the parent should tell the neighborhood guard quickly, and all residents should help search.
BI AMINANzuri. Kwa kweli, kama tungekuwa na mpango kama huu miaka mitano iliyopita, tungeepuka matatizo mengi.Good. Honestly, if we had had a plan like this five years ago, we would have avoided many problems.
JAMESNi kweli, lakini sasa tuna nafasi ya kuandaa mpango mzuri. Nafikiri kama tukishirikiana, mpango huu utafanikiwa.That's true, but now we have the chance to prepare a good plan. I think if we cooperate, this plan will succeed.

This dialogue develops the -ki- conditional productively (moshi ukitokea, umeme ukikatika, mtoto akipotea) and includes one glossed -nge- example (tungekuwa, tungeepuka) for recognition only, not for independent production at this stage.

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension questions:

  1. Wakazi wafanye nini kama moshi ukitokea ndani ya nyumba?
  2. Kwa nini James anashauri watu watumie tochi badala ya mishumaa?
  3. Mzazi afanye nini kama mtoto akipotea wakati wa dharura?
  4. Bi Amina anasema nini kuhusu miaka mitano iliyopita? (Recognition only — do not attempt to reproduce this pattern productively.)

Guided Practice

Zoezi 1 — Kama... Basi... (If... Then...)

Practice: building complete kama clauses with a condition and a result.

  1. Match five conditions (provided by your instructor or written by you) to an appropriate result clause, using kama to connect them.
  2. Check that the verb tense or form in each clause matches the situation described (present, future, negative, or subjunctive).

Zoezi 2 — Mabadiliko ya -ki- (Transforming to -ki-)

Practice: transforming plain statements into -ki- conditionals.

  1. Take five pairs of simple statements (a situation and a result) and combine each pair into one sentence using the -ki- conditional.
  2. Check that the subject marker in the -ki- verb agrees with its subject in person or noun class.

Zoezi 3 — Mpango wa Dharura (Emergency Plan)

Practice: applying kama and -ki- conditionals to real safety scenarios.

  1. Read a short list of household or neighborhood emergency scenarios and write one kama or -ki- conditional sentence recommending what to do in each case.
  2. Compare your sentences with a partner and discuss whether each recommendation is clear and appropriately phrased.

Speaking Practice

Sehemu ya kwanza — Kuelezea Masharti (Explaining Conditions)

With a partner, take turns describing three household or neighborhood scenarios (a power outage, a lost child, an unclear safety rule) and stating what should happen in each case, using kama or the -ki- conditional.

Sehemu ya pili — Kutatua Tatizo (Solving a Problem)

Describe a short everyday problem (a friend is worried about a choice, a neighbor is unsure how to prepare for a trip) and give conditional advice, such as kama ukifanya hivi, basi.... Have your partner respond with agreement, a follow-up question, or an alternative condition.

Mbadala wa kujifunza peke yako — Solo alternative

Record yourself describing three conditional scenarios using kama and the -ki- conditional, one for a household situation, one for a community situation, and one for a personal choice. Listen back and check your conditional formation and subject-marker agreement.

Writing Workshop

  1. Chagua hali. Choose a real or invented household, neighborhood, or personal situation that involves a choice or a safety concern.
  2. Panga masharti yako. List two or three conditions and their recommended results, in plain English notes first.
  3. Andika mpango mfupi. Write a short 90–130-word plan or set of recommendations, using at least two kama clauses and one -ki- conditional, with clear and appropriate register.
  4. Kagua kwa orodha. Check your plan against the checklist below.
  5. Andika nakala safi. Produce a clean final version.

Prompt: Write a short set of conditional recommendations for a household, neighborhood, or personal situation involving safety or an important choice.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • Each kama clause is followed by an appropriately formed result clause.
  • At least one -ki- conditional is used accurately, with correct subject-marker agreement.
  • No independent -nge- conditionals were attempted productively.
  • Subjunctive requests and purpose clauses from Chapter 4 are reused correctly where natural.
  • Capitalization, spacing, punctuation, and ng' are correct throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Planning and Community Responsibility

In many Swahili-speaking communities, safety and household planning are often discussed as a shared, community-level responsibility rather than a purely private matter. Conditional language of the kind practiced in this chapter is commonly used in neighborhood meetings, public announcements, school guidance, and household discussions to prepare people for a range of possible situations, from minor inconveniences to genuine emergencies.

Regional Variation Note

The kama and -ki- conditional patterns practiced in this chapter are standard across Tanzanian and Kenyan Standard Swahili for everyday and public planning language. The -nge- conditional previewed here receptively will become a full productive system in a later chapter, where its regional and register range will also be addressed.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Missing -ki- Marker in a General Condition

Error: Kama moshi unatokea, toka nje. (uses the ordinary present tense -na- instead of the -ki- conditional)

Correction: Kama moshi ukitokea, toka nje. — a general or likely "if/when" condition of this kind is typically expressed with the -ki- conditional rather than the plain present tense.

Common Mistake — Wrong Subject Marker in the -ki- Conditional

Error: Kama umeme ikikatika, tutumie tochi. (uses i- instead of the correct class 3 subject marker u- for umeme)

Correction: Kama umeme ukikatika, tutumie tochi. — the subject marker inside the -ki- verb must agree with the noun class of its subject, here class 3 u- for umeme.

Common Mistake — Attempting to Produce -nge- Independently

Error: Learners attempt to freely construct new -nge- sentences for ordinary likely conditions in this chapter's exercises.

Correction: Use kama or the -ki- conditional for likely conditions in this chapter's productive work. The -nge- conditional is for recognition only at this stage and will be developed productively in a later chapter.

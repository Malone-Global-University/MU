Usomaji wa Makala na Ripoti

Longer Reading: Articles and Reports · Speak and Write Swahili, Volume III: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Overview

Chapter 17 moves your reading into longer, more formally structured texts: adapted news articles and public reports of the kind you now recognize from Chapter 16's register work. Rather than introducing new grammar, this chapter consolidates systems you already control — nominalization, the passive voice (Chapter 7), relative clauses (Chapters 2 and onward), and discourse connectors (Chapters 3 and 15) — so that you can read confidently even when several of these systems appear together in a single formal paragraph. You will practice pulling out main ideas, tracking who or what a passive sentence is really about, and following a writer's line of reasoning through connectors like hata hivyo, hivyo basi, and kwa upande mwingine. Your own speaking and writing continue to rest on the productive systems already active in this volume.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Read an adapted Swahili newspaper article or public report and identify its main idea and supporting details.
  2. Recognize nominalized forms (verb-derived nouns such as maendeleo, mabadiliko, and uongozi) and connect them back to the verbs they come from.
  3. Identify passive constructions in formal prose and correctly determine who or what is performing the underlying action, even when the agent is not stated.
  4. Recognize relative clauses with amba- and locative/object relative forms inside longer sentences without losing the main clause.
  5. Follow a writer's reasoning by recognizing connectors of contrast, consequence, addition, and conclusion.
  6. Summarize the content of an adapted article or report in your own Standard Swahili, using consolidated grammar rather than copying sentence structure directly from the source.
  7. Distinguish a generated adapted text for language practice from an authentic published source.

Opening Communication Scenario

Zainabu has settled into her first few days in Nairobi. Wanjiru's neighborhood association has been discussing a new water-project report published in a community newsletter, and Wanjiru asks Zainabu, half as a joke, whether her "very correct" Swahili can handle a real report instead of just conversation. Zainabu accepts the challenge. She reads the report carefully, works out which parts describe completed actions, which describe ongoing plans, and who is actually responsible for each action even where the writer uses the passive voice, and then gives Wanjiru a short spoken summary in her own words.

Pronunciation and Orthography Focus

Focus — Reading Formal Prose Aloud

Formal written Swahili often contains longer sentences than everyday speech, with several clauses joined by connectors. When reading such sentences aloud, keep your five-vowel stability and penultimate stress steady even as sentences lengthen, and use a brief pause at each comma or connector to mark the clause boundary for a listener. This helps both your own fluency and a listener's comprehension of dense written material read aloud.

In spelling, nominalized forms such as maendeleo and mabadiliko follow standard class 6 ma- patterns; keep the double vowel sequence in maendeleo and the medial di in mabadiliko accurate, since these are common sources of learner spelling errors.

Grammar for Reading: Consolidating Nominalization, Passives, Relatives, and Connectors

This section consolidates systems you already control for stronger reading comprehension. No new productive grammar is introduced; the goal is recognizing how familiar systems combine in formal prose.

Nominalization: Reading Verb-Derived Nouns

Consolidation — Connecting Nouns Back to Verbs

Formal Swahili frequently uses abstract nouns derived from familiar verbs, especially class 6 ma- nouns and class 14 u- nouns. Recognizing the underlying verb helps you understand the noun quickly, even the first time you meet it.

  • maendeleo ← kuendelea "development" ← "to continue/progress"
  • mabadiliko ← kubadilika "changes" ← "to change"
  • uongozi ← kuongoza "leadership" ← "to lead"

When you meet an unfamiliar abstract noun in a report, try removing the nominal prefix and asking whether a familiar verb stem remains. This strategy will not work for every noun, but it works often enough to be a useful reading habit.

Passives in Reports: Finding the Real Agent

Consolidation — Passive Voice (from Chapter 7)

Reports and public notices favor the passive voice because it foregrounds the action or result rather than the actor. Your task as a reader is to ask: who or what most plausibly performs this action, even when the sentence does not name them?

  • Mradi wa maji ulianzishwa mwaka jana. The water project was established last year. passive; agent unstated but implied (the association/council)
  • Ripoti hiyo iliandikwa na kamati ya mradi. That report was written by the project committee. passive; agent stated with na

Where an agent is introduced, it follows na, exactly as you learned in Chapter 7. Where no agent appears, formal Swahili is often deliberately general; do not invent an agent that the text does not support.

Relative Clauses Inside Longer Sentences

Consolidation — Relative Clauses (from Chapter 2 onward)

Formal texts often embed a relative clause inside a longer sentence to add detail without starting a new sentence. Locate the noun being described, then find the class-appropriate relative marker that refers back to it.

  • Mradi ambao ulianzishwa mwaka jana sasa unawanufaisha wakazi wengi. The project that was established last year now benefits many residents. amba- + class 3 -o, referring back to "mradi"

Do not let a long relative clause distract you from the main verb of the sentence; here, the main-clause verb is unawanufaisha ("benefits"), and the relative clause simply adds background information about the project.

Following the Argument: Connectors in Formal Prose

Consolidation — Discourse Connectors (from Chapters 3 and 15)

Reports and articles frequently signal their structure with connectors. Recognizing these lets you predict what kind of information is coming next.

  • hata hivyo "however" — signals contrast or a limitation to what was just said
  • hivyo basi / kwa hiyo "therefore" — signals a conclusion or consequence
  • kwa upande mwingine "on the other hand" — signals a contrasting perspective
  • aidha "furthermore, in addition" — signals an added point, common in formal writing
Building Block — A Reading Strategy for Dense Sentences
[Find the main verb] → [Identify any passive and its implied agent] → [Bracket any relative clause as extra detail] → [Note the connector linking this sentence to the last one]

Mradi ambao ulianzishwa mwaka jana [relative clause: extra detail] sasa unawanufaisha wakazi wengi [main clause]; hata hivyo [contrast connector], baadhi ya wakazi wanasema maji bado hayatoshi. The project that was established last year now benefits many residents; however, some residents say the water is still not enough.

Apply this four-step strategy whenever a sentence in this chapter's readings feels dense. It slows you down at first but speeds up your overall comprehension.

Zoezi la Kuchambua — Analysis Practice:

  1. In the sentence "Ripoti hiyo iliandikwa na kamati ya mradi, na baadaye ilijadiliwa na wakazi," identify the passive verbs and their agents (stated or implied).
  2. Find the verb hiding inside "mabadiliko" and give its basic meaning.
  3. Identify the connector in "Kwa upande mwingine, baadhi ya wakazi wanataka mradi mpya" and explain what kind of information it introduces.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: Wanjiru anampa Zainabu jarida la mtaa lenye ripoti kuhusu mradi wa maji, kisha wanazungumza kuhusu ripoti hiyo baada ya Zainabu kuisoma.

(Setting: Wanjiru gives Zainabu a neighborhood newsletter containing a report about a water project, then they discuss it after Zainabu has read it.)

WANJIRUHaya, msomi wa Kiswahili sanifu, umeisoma ripoti? Umeelewa nini?Okay, Standard Swahili scholar, have you read the report? What did you understand?
ZAINABUNimeelewa vizuri. Ripoti inasema mradi wa maji ulianzishwa mwaka jana na unawanufaisha wakazi wengi sasa.I understood well. The report says the water project was established last year and now benefits many residents.
WANJIRUSawa. Je, ripoti inasema nani aliuanzisha mradi huo?Okay. Does the report say who established that project?
ZAINABUSentensi hiyo iko katika hali ya kutendwa, kwa hiyo haitaji jina moja kwa moja; lakini ripoti inaeleza baadaye kwamba kamati ya mradi ndiyo iliyoandika ripoti nzima.That sentence is in the passive voice, so it doesn't name anyone directly; but the report explains later that the project committee is the one who wrote the whole report.
WANJIRUUmebobea kweli! Je, kuna sehemu inayoonyesha matatizo, au ni habari njema tu?You've really mastered it! Is there a part showing problems, or is it only good news?
ZAINABUKuna sehemu moja inayoanza kwa "hata hivyo." Baada ya hapo, ripoti inasema baadhi ya wakazi bado wanasema maji hayatoshi.There's one section that starts with "however." After that, the report says some residents still say the water isn't enough.

Zainabu's analysis moves through the reading strategy from the Building Block: locating the passive verb, noting the missing agent, and using the connector hata hivyo to predict a contrasting point.

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension questions:

  1. Ripoti inasema mradi wa maji ulianzishwa lini?
  2. Kwa nini sentensi ya kwanza haitaji nani aliyeanzisha mradi?
  3. Ni nani hasa aliyeandika ripoti nzima, kulingana na Zainabu?
  4. Ni tatizo gani linalotajwa baada ya "hata hivyo"?

Reading Practice

Makala: Maendeleo ya Elimu Mashinani — Article: Educational Development in Rural Areas

Katika miaka mitano iliyopita, mabadiliko makubwa yameonekana katika shule za mashambani nchini Kenya na Tanzania. Shule nyingi mpya zimejengwa, na walimu wapya wameajiriwa ili kupunguza idadi ya wanafunzi kwa kila mwalimu. Aidha, vitabu vingi vimesambazwa kupitia mradi wa serikali unaolenga kuboresha usomaji miongoni mwa watoto. Mradi huo uliratibiwa na wizara ya elimu, kwa kushirikiana na mashirika kadhaa yasiyo ya kiserikali. Hata hivyo, wataalamu wanasema kwamba maeneo ya mbali zaidi bado hayajanufaika sawa na maeneo ya karibu na miji. Kwa upande mwingine, viongozi wa elimu wanasisitiza kwamba uongozi wa shule za mashambani umeimarika, na hilo peke yake ni hatua muhimu. Hitimisho la makala hiyo ni kwamba, ingawa maendeleo ni ya kweli, kazi zaidi inahitajika ili kila mtoto anufaike sawa.

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension and interpretation questions:

  1. Ni mabadiliko gani mawili yametajwa katika aya ya kwanza?
  2. Mradi wa vitabu uliratibiwa na nani?
  3. Ni tatizo gani linalotajwa baada ya "hata hivyo"?
  4. Kulingana na hitimisho, ni nini bado kinahitajika?
  5. Tafuta neno moja lenye asili ya kitenzi katika aya hii, na andika kitenzi hicho.

Note: this article is an adapted textbook passage written for language practice. It is not an authentic published news article.

Writing Workshop

  1. Soma tena makala. Reread the Reading Practice article and note its main idea and two supporting details.
  2. Panga mawazo yako. In English, jot a brief outline: main idea, two details, one limitation, one conclusion.
  3. Andika muhtasari kwa Kiswahili sanifu. Write a 70–100-word Standard Swahili summary of the article in your own words, using at least one nominalized noun, one passive construction, one relative clause, and one connector from this chapter.
  4. Kagua kwa orodha. Check your summary against the checklist below, then produce a clean final version.

Prompt: Write a short Standard Swahili summary of the article "Maendeleo ya Elimu Mashinani," as if explaining its content to someone who has not read it, using your own sentence structures.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • The summary uses your own wording rather than copying full sentences from the article.
  • At least one nominalized noun, one passive construction, one relative clause, and one connector are used accurately.
  • The summary states the main idea, at least one supporting detail, and the article's conclusion.
  • Noun-class agreement, capitalization, spacing, punctuation, and ng' are correct throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Reports and Public Accountability

Community newsletters, neighborhood association bulletins, and local government reports are common features of civic life across East African communities, providing residents with updates on projects, budgets, and services. Being able to read this kind of material independently is a genuinely useful skill for a Swahili learner, whether for participating in community life, understanding local news, or following public projects during travel or residence.

Regional Variation Note

Formal reporting style is broadly similar across Tanzanian and Kenyan Standard Swahili, since both rely on the same core grammatical tools of nominalization, passives, and formal connectors. Some vocabulary choices for specific institutions, such as the names of local government bodies, differ between the two countries; this chapter focuses on the shared grammatical strategies for reading rather than on institution-specific vocabulary.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Inventing an Agent for an Agentless Passive

Error: assuming a specific named person performed an action in a passive sentence when the text never states or clearly implies an agent

Correction: when a passive sentence has no stated agent and no clear contextual implication, describe the action or result without naming a performer, and note that the writer may be deliberately general.

Common Mistake — Losing the Main Verb Inside a Long Relative Clause

Error: treating the verb inside a relative clause as the sentence's main verb, leading to a misreading of the sentence's core meaning

Correction: bracket the relative clause mentally as additional detail about a noun, then locate the verb that follows the full noun phrase for the sentence's main action.

Common Mistake — Copying Article Sentences Verbatim in a Summary

Error: a writing summary that reproduces the article's sentences almost word for word instead of restating the content independently

Correction: after reading, close the source text and write the summary from your outline and memory, checking back only to confirm facts, not to copy phrasing.

Answer Key / Ufunguo wa Majibu

Grammar for Reading — Zoezi la Kuchambua

  1. "iliandikwa" (agent: kamati ya mradi, stated with na) and "ilijadiliwa" (agent unstated; plausibly the residents or committee, implied by context).
  2. mabadiliko ← kubadilika ("to change").
  3. "Kwa upande mwingine" — introduces a contrasting perspective or a different group's view from what was just stated.

Guided Practice — Zoezi 1, 2, and 3

Accept any accurate identification of agents (stated or plausibly implied), correctly labeled connectors (contrast: hata hivyo, kwa upande mwingine; consequence/conclusion: hivyo basi, kwa hiyo; addition: aidha), and correctly matched noun-verb pairs (e.g., maendeleo ← kuendelea; uongozi ← kuongoza).

Model Dialogue Comprehension

  3. Kamati ya mradi ndiyo iliyoandika ripoti nzima.
  4. Baadhi ya wakazi bado wanasema maji hayatoshi.

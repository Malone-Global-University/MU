Kutenda kwa Ajili ya Mwingine

Applicative Meaning: For, To, With · Speak and Write Swahili, Volume III: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Overview

Chapter 6 opens Unit II by moving from conditions and consequences to the language of service and mutual help. Building on the connected discourse you developed across Unit I, you will now be introduced for the first time to the applicative verb extension, a productive way of describing actions done for, to, with, or because of someone or something. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to describe practical acts of assistance, coordinate the applicative with object markers you already control, and talk about service and helping tasks in your home, neighborhood, and workplace.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize the form of the applicative extension in high-frequency verbs.
  2. Form and use applicative verbs to describe an action done for, to, or with someone.
  3. Coordinate applicative verbs with person and noun-class object markers already under your control.
  4. Distinguish a simple transitive verb from its applicative counterpart in meaning and structure.
  5. Identify and correct common errors in applicative formation and object-marker placement.
  6. Describe everyday service, helping, and work tasks in speaking and writing.
  7. Discuss cooperation and mutual assistance in a community or workplace context.

Opening Communication Scenario

With the neighborhood safety plan from Chapter 5 now written down, James and Bi Amina turn to actually carrying it out. Several tasks need doing: someone must fetch supplies for the elderly residents, someone must fix the broken gate for a neighbor, and someone must translate the plan for a family that reads better in English than in Swahili. Bi Amina points out that James keeps needing to say things like "I will buy it for her" or "he fixed it for us," and that Swahili has a single, regular way of building this meaning directly into the verb: the applicative extension.

  1. This chapter introduces the applicative extension for the first time as a productive system.
  2. Applicative forms are built from verbs you already know, so the emphasis is on a small, reliable set of high-frequency examples rather than every possible derived form.
  3. Object markers you developed in earlier chapters combine naturally with applicative verbs to say exactly who benefits from an action.
  4. Vocabulary in this chapter supports service, helping, and coordinated work tasks.

Pronunciation Focus

Segmenting Longer Derived Verbs

Applicative verbs are longer than their base forms, since a suffix is added before the final vowel. When you read or say a word like tengenezea (fix-for) or nunulia (buy-for), keep each syllable open and clear rather than compressing the extra syllable: te-nge-ne-ze-a, nu-nu-li-a.

Penultimate stress still falls on the second-to-last syllable of the whole derived word, so the stress moves slightly compared to the base verb. Practice saying base and applicative pairs side by side, such as tengeneza / tengenezea and nunua / nunulia, so you can hear where the extra syllable falls.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This section introduces the applicative extension for the first time, in a controlled set of high-frequency verbs, and coordinates it with object markers already under your control.

What the Applicative Extension Does

New System — Verb Stem + Applicative Suffix + Final Vowel

The applicative extension is added to a verb stem, before the final vowel, to show that the action is done for, to, with, or because of someone or something. In most common verbs, the applicative suffix is -ia or -ea, depending on the vowel already in the stem: verbs with a, i, or u in the last stem syllable typically take -ia, while verbs with e or o typically take -ea.

Adding the applicative extension usually adds a new participant to the sentence: the person or thing that benefits from, receives, or is otherwise affected by the action.

Forming and Using Applicative Verbs

Building Block — Subject Marker + Verb Stem + Applicative + Final Vowel
Subject marker + (TAM) + (object marker) + stem + -ia/-ea + final vowel

Nitamnunulia mzee dawa. I will buy medicine for the elder. ni- + ta- + m- + nunul + ia

James alimtengenezea jirani lango lake. James fixed the gate for his neighbor. a- + li- + m- + tengenez + ea

Tunawaandikia wageni barua kwa Kiingereza. We are writing a letter in English for the guests. tu- + na- + wa- + andik + ia

Notice that the object marker inside the verb (-m-, -wa-, and so on) refers to the person who benefits from the action, not necessarily the thing being bought, fixed, or written. The applicative extension itself, and the object marker that usually accompanies it, work together to make clear who the action is being done for.

Simple Transitive vs. Applicative

Contrast — Base Verb vs. Applicative Verb

Compare a simple transitive sentence with its applicative counterpart:

  • Nilinunua dawa. — I bought medicine. (no beneficiary expressed)
  • Nilimnunulia mzee dawa. — I bought medicine for the elder. (applicative adds the beneficiary)

Use the base verb when only the thing acted upon matters. Use the applicative verb when you need to name who the action was done for, to, or with.

Zoezi la Sarufi — Grammar Practice:

  1. Change each base verb into its applicative form: kupika → ___ / kuandika → ___ / kutengeneza → ___.
  2. Complete each applicative sentence with an appropriate object marker: Ni___tengenezea jirani lango. (for him) / Tu___pikia wageni chakula. (for them)
  3. Rewrite each base-verb sentence as an applicative sentence naming a beneficiary: Nilinunua chakula. → ___ (for your grandmother)

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: James na Bi Amina wanagawana majukumu ya kutekeleza mpango wa usalama uliopangwa katika Sura ya 5.

(Setting: James and Bi Amina are dividing up the tasks needed to carry out the safety plan drafted in Chapter 5.)

BI AMINAJames, tuna kazi kadhaa. Nani atamnunulia Bibi Fatuma tochi na betri?James, we have several tasks. Who will buy Bibi Fatuma a flashlight and batteries?
JAMESNitamnunulia mimi. Nitampigia simu kwanza ili nijue anahitaji nini hasa.I will buy them for her. I will call her first so I know exactly what she needs.
BI AMINASawa. Na lango la Bwana Hassan bado limevunjika. Je, unaweza kumtengenezea?Okay. And Mr. Hassan's gate is still broken. Can you fix it for him?
JAMESNdiyo, lakini nitahitaji msaada. Je, mtoto wako anaweza kunisaidia kubeba zana?Yes, but I will need help. Can your child help me carry the tools?
BI AMINAAtakusaidia kwa furaha. Na familia ya Bi Grace, wanasoma vizuri zaidi kwa Kiingereza. Je, tumwandikie mpango kwa lugha zote mbili?He will help you happily. And Bi Grace's family reads better in English. Should we write the plan for them in both languages?
JAMESWazo zuri. Nitawaandikia toleo la Kiingereza jioni hii, na wewe uwaandikie toleo la Kiswahili.Good idea. I will write them the English version this evening, and you write them the Swahili version.
BI AMINAVizuri. Ukishamaliza, unipigie simu, nami nitakupikia chakula kama shukrani.Good. Once you finish, call me, and I will cook you food as thanks.
JAMESAsante sana, Bi Amina. Tukisaidiana hivi, mtaa wetu utakuwa salama zaidi haraka.Thank you very much, Bi Amina. If we help each other like this, our neighborhood will become safer more quickly.

This dialogue develops the applicative extension productively across several high-frequency verbs (kununulia, kutengenezea, kuandikia, kupikia) and coordinates each one with a person object marker naming the beneficiary.

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension questions:

  1. James atamnunulia Bibi Fatuma nini?
  2. Kwa nini James anahitaji msaada wa mtoto wa Bi Amina?
  3. James na Bi Amina wataandikia familia ya Bi Grace nini, na kwa lugha ngapi?
  4. Bi Amina atamfanyia nini James kama shukrani?

Writing Workshop

  1. Chagua watu na majukumu. Choose a real or invented group of family members, neighbors, or coworkers and two or three tasks that need doing.
  2. Panga nani anafanyia nani nini. List, in plain English notes first, who will do each task and for whose benefit.
  3. Andika mpango mfupi. Write a short 90–130-word plan describing who will help whom, using at least three applicative verbs with correct object markers.
  4. Kagua kwa orodha. Check your plan against the checklist below.
  5. Andika nakala safi. Produce a clean final version.

Prompt: Write a short plan describing who will help whom with which tasks, using applicative verbs to name each beneficiary.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • Each applicative verb is formed correctly from its base verb (-ia or -ea, as appropriate).
  • Each applicative verb is paired with an object marker that agrees with its beneficiary.
  • At least one sentence contrasts a base verb with its applicative counterpart, showing the difference in meaning.
  • Kama and -ki- conditionals from Chapter 5 are reused correctly where natural.
  • Capitalization, spacing, punctuation, and ng' are correct throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Reciprocal Help in Community Life

In many Swahili-speaking communities, small acts of mutual help — buying something for an elder, fixing something for a neighbor, cooking for a family during a difficult time — are understood as an ordinary part of maintaining community relationships, not as unusual favors. The applicative extension gives Swahili a compact, natural way to name exactly who benefits from an action, which makes it especially useful for describing this kind of everyday reciprocity.

Regional Variation Note

The applicative verbs practiced in this chapter are standard across Tanzanian and Kenyan Standard Swahili. Some regions and communities have additional lexicalized applicative forms with specialized meanings beyond a simple beneficiary reading; these will be addressed as receptive, labeled vocabulary in later volumes rather than as part of this chapter's productive system.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Wrong Applicative Vowel

Error: kutengeneza → kutengenezia (uses -ia where the stem vowel calls for -ea)

Correction: kutengeneza → kutengenezea — verbs whose last stem syllable contains e or o typically take the -ea applicative suffix rather than -ia.

Common Mistake — Missing Object Marker for the Beneficiary

Error: Nitanunulia dawa mzee. (uses the applicative form but leaves out the object marker that identifies the elder as beneficiary)

Correction: Nitamnunulia mzee dawa. — when the beneficiary is a specific, known person, Swahili typically marks that person with an object marker inside the verb in addition to naming them in the sentence.

Common Mistake — Treating Every Suffix as Applicative

Error: Learners assume any verb ending in -ia or -ea must be an applicative form of some shorter base verb.

Correction: Some verbs, such as kusaidia (to help), already contain a benefactive meaning as part of their basic form and are not built by adding the applicative extension to a separate, shorter verb in ordinary use. Learn the six verbs and forms in this chapter's vocabulary and verb table as your controlled starting set, rather than generating new applicative forms freely.

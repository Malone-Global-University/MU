Kuanza Tena kwa Kiwango cha Tatu

B1 Re-entry and Discourse Accuracy · Speak and Write Swahili, Volume III: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Overview

Chapter 1 opens Volume III by reassessing and stabilizing what you already built in Volume II, so that it can support the longer, more connected discourse this volume requires. You will audit your noun-class concord across full sentences, review how subject agreement, object marking, and negation combine inside the verb, and practice narrating and describing at greater length than before. No major new grammar system is introduced here. Instead, this chapter repairs and consolidates your foundation — pronunciation, agreement, tense-aspect-mood, and negation — so that Chapters 2 onward can build relative clauses, verb extensions, and conditionals on solid ground.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify and correct noun-class agreement errors across a full sentence, not just within a single phrase.
  2. Narrate personal history and daily routines using present, past, future, and perfect/resultative forms accurately.
  3. Produce accurate affirmative and negative sentences across the core tense-aspect system.
  4. Use human and basic noun-class object markers correctly inside the verb template.
  5. Describe your own life, routines, and community connections in extended, connected speech and writing.
  6. Diagnose your own pronunciation for stress, vowel stability, and digraph accuracy across longer utterances.
  7. Produce a diagnostic narrative, interview, and written sample demonstrating stabilized B1 control.

Opening Communication Scenario

Zainabu and James have finished Volume II and are beginning a new term of study. Their teacher, Bi Amina, wants to know exactly what they can already do before introducing anything new. She asks them each to tell her about their lives so far, describe a typical week, and talk about people and things in their community — not as isolated drills, but as connected, natural speech. As they talk, she listens for where their noun-class agreement, verb forms, and negation are already strong, and where a quick repair is needed before Volume III moves forward.

  1. This chapter introduces no major new grammar. It reviews and stabilizes Volume II systems for use in longer discourse.
  2. Vocabulary in this chapter supports personal history, daily routines, and community-context narration.
  3. Pronunciation work in this chapter is diagnostic: identifying and repairing stress and vowel issues across longer utterances.
  4. Relative clauses, verb extensions, and full conditional forms appear only in glossed, receptive form in this chapter; they are not production targets until later chapters activate them.

Pronunciation Focus

Diagnostic Self-Monitoring — Stress and Vowels in Longer Speech

This chapter introduces no new sound pattern. Its pronunciation goal is diagnostic: as your sentences grow longer and more connected, check that your five vowels remain distinct and stable, that penultimate stress still falls correctly even on longer derived words such as utaratibu and mazingira, and that digraphs such as ng', ny, and dh stay crisp rather than blurring together as your speech speeds up.

A common Volume III risk is that learners who pronounced individual words correctly in Volume I and II begin to blur stress and vowel quality once they string several clauses together. Practice reading a three- or four-sentence passage aloud slowly, then again at a natural pace, checking that accuracy does not disappear as speed increases.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This section introduces no new grammar system. It audits and repairs the Volume II systems — noun-class concord, agreement propagation, the core tense-aspect-mood system, negation, and basic object marking — so they function reliably across longer, connected discourse.

Agreement Propagation Audit

Building Block — Noun class carries through the whole sentence
[Noun-class prefix] + [adjective agreement] + [subject marker] + [verb] ... [demonstrative/possessive agreement]

Vitabu vile vikubwa vilikuwa vyangu tangu shule ya msingi. Those big books were mine since primary school.

Miji mikubwa ya nchi yetu inabadilika kila mwaka. The big cities of our country change every year. class 4 agreement: mi-kubwa, i-na-badilika, class 9 yetu

A single class-7/8 or class-9/10 noun in Volume II drills might have controlled only one adjective. In Volume III, the same noun class must control agreement across adjectives, subject markers, possessives, and demonstratives within one sentence, and often across two or three connected sentences.

By Volume III, learners should be able to select the correct affirmative or negative form based on meaning without pausing to recall the rule. A diagnostic goal of this chapter is fluent, accurate switching between these forms inside one connected narrative.

Object Markers — Reviewing People and Things

Review — Human and Basic Noun-Class Object Markers

Volume II introduced human object markers such as -ni-, -ku-, -m-, -tu-, and -wa-, and some noun-class object markers for familiar classes. In Volume III, these must work at discourse level — tracking who or what was already mentioned across two or more sentences, rather than only inside a single drilled sentence.

Nilimwona jirani yangu jana. Nilimsalimia, na akanijibu kwa furaha. I saw my neighbor yesterday. I greeted him, and he answered me happily.

A Glimpse Ahead — Relative Clauses (Receptive Only)

  • Jirani ambaye ninamzungumzia ni rafiki wa familia yetu. The neighbor whom I am talking about is a friend of our family. receptive relative, glossed, not a production target this chapter
  • Kama nitapata muda, nitatembelea familia yangu wiki ijayo. If I get time, I will visit my family next week. receptive kama-conditional, glossed

You will encounter forms like these in reading and listening this chapter. Understand them for meaning; do not worry about producing them independently yet — Chapter 2 begins their controlled development.

Zoezi la Sarufi — Grammar Practice:

  1. Write three connected sentences about your own personal history, ensuring adjective and possessive agreement match their nouns throughout.
  2. Turn each of these affirmative sentences into the negative: (Ninafanya kazi kila siku) / (Nimemaliza masomo yangu) / (Nitasafiri mwaka ujao).
  3. Write two sentences about a person in your community, using a human object marker in at least one.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: Bi Amina anazungumza na Zainabu kuhusu maisha yake na ratiba yake ya kila wiki, mwanzoni mwa muhula mpya.

(Setting: Bi Amina talks with Zainabu about her life and weekly routine, at the start of a new term.)

BI AMINAZainabu, tafadhali nieleze kuhusu maisha yako tangu mwaka jana.Zainabu, please tell me about your life since last year.
ZAINABUBila shaka. Mwaka jana nilimaliza kiwango cha pili, na sasa ninafanya kazi mpya jijini.Of course. Last year I finished the second level, and now I work a new job in the city.
BI AMINAVizuri sana. Ratiba yako ya kila wiki ikoje sasa?Very good. What is your weekly schedule like now?
ZAINABUKila siku, ninaamka mapema na kwenda kazini. Jioni, ninashiriki katika mazoezi ya jamii na jirani zangu.Every day, I wake up early and go to work. In the evening, I participate in community activities with my neighbors.
BI AMINAUmezoea maisha ya jijini?Have you gotten used to city life?
ZAINABUBado sijazoea kabisa, lakini ninajifunza kila wiki. Sikuwa na marafiki wengi mwanzoni, lakini sasa ninawajua majirani wangu vizuri.I haven't fully gotten used to it yet, but I'm learning every week. I didn't have many friends at first, but now I know my neighbors well.
BI AMINAKuna jirani ambaye amekusaidia sana?Is there a neighbor who has helped you a lot?
ZAINABUNdiyo, kuna mwanamke mmoja karibu na nyumbani kwangu ambaye amenisaidia mengi. Nadhani tutaendelea kuwa marafiki wazuri.Yes, there's a woman near my house who has helped me a lot. I think we will continue to be good friends.
BI AMINAUmeeleza vizuri sana, Zainabu. Umetumia wakati uliopita, uliopo, na ujao vizuri sana katika hadithi hiyo.You explained that very well, Zainabu. You used the past, present, and future well in that account.

This dialogue reviews present, past, future, and perfect tense across connected personal narration, along with negation (bado sijazoea) and noun-class agreement. The relative clause with ambaye is glossed for comprehension only and is not a productive target in this chapter.

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension questions:

  1. Zainabu alimaliza nini mwaka jana?
  2. Ratiba ya Zainabu ya kila siku ikoje?
  3. Zainabu amezoea kabisa maisha ya jijini?
  4. Ni nani aliyemsaidia Zainabu sana?

Guided Practice

Zoezi 1 — Ukaguzi wa Kihusiano (Agreement Audit)

Practice: finding and correcting agreement errors across full sentences.

  1. Read three short sentences (provided by your instructor or written by you) and check every adjective, possessive, and demonstrative for correct class agreement.
  2. Rewrite any sentence with an agreement error, and explain which noun class governs the correction.

Zoezi 2 — Kutofautisha Nyakati (Tense Contrast)

Practice: choosing the correct tense-aspect form by meaning, affirmative and negative.

  1. Write four sentences about your week: one present, one past, one future, and one perfect.
  2. Rewrite each sentence in the negative.

Zoezi 3 — Kuunganisha Watu na Vitu (Object-Marker Review)

Practice: using human and basic object markers to track people and things across sentences.

  1. Write two connected sentences about a person you know, using a human object marker in the second sentence to refer back to them.
  2. Write one sentence about an object (a book, a house, a phone) using a class-appropriate object marker.

Speaking Practice

Sehemu ya kwanza — Mahojiano ya Uchunguzi (Diagnostic Interview)

With a partner playing the role of Bi Amina, answer questions about your own personal history, your current routine, and a person in your community who has helped you. Aim for at least six connected sentences using present, past, future, and perfect tense.

Sehemu ya pili — Maelezo ya Ratiba (Routine Description)

Mbadala wa kujifunza peke yako — Solo alternative

Record yourself answering both prompts above. Listen back and mark any place where stress, vowel quality, agreement, or tense choice broke down, then repeat the recording once corrected.

Reading Practice

Kumbukumbu za Mwaka Uliopita — Memories of Last Year

Mwaka uliopita ulikuwa mwaka wa mabadiliko mengi kwangu. Nilimaliza masomo yangu ya kiwango cha pili, na baadaye nilianza kazi mpya. Mwanzoni, sikujua watu wengi katika ujirani wangu, na sikuzoea desturi za mahali hapo. Hata hivyo, kila wiki nilijaribu kushiriki katika shughuli za jamii, na polepole nilianza kuwafahamu majirani zangu. Kuna mwanamke mmoja ambaye alinisaidia sana mwanzoni; alinieleza kuhusu mazingira ya eneo hilo na kunitambulisha kwa watu wengine. Sasa, ninajisikia sehemu ya jamii hiyo, na ninashukuru kwa hatua nilizopiga mwaka huu.

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension and interpretation questions:

  1. Ni mabadiliko gani makubwa yaliyotokea mwaka uliopita kwa mwandishi?
  2. Mwandishi alishirikije katika jamii yake mpya?
  3. Ni nani aliyemsaidia mwandishi, na alimsaidiaje?
  4. Find the sentence containing a relative clause with ambaye, and explain what it identifies, without producing a similar sentence yourself yet.

Writing Workshop

  1. Chagua vipengele vya maisha yako. Choose three or four moments or facts about your own personal history to include.
  2. Panga ratiba yako. Note your typical weekly routine in a few key points.
  3. Andika aya. Write a short 80–120-word paragraph combining your personal history and current routine, using present, past, future, and perfect tense, at least one negative sentence, and at least one sentence about a person in your community.
  4. Kagua kwa orodha. Check your paragraph against the checklist below.
  5. Andika nakala safi. Produce a clean final version.

Prompt: Write a short paragraph about your personal history, your current routine, and a person in your community who matters to you.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • Noun-class concord is correct across all adjectives, possessives, and demonstratives used.
  • Present, past, future, and perfect tense are each used at least once, accurately.
  • At least one negative sentence is included and correctly formed.
  • At least one human or noun-class object marker is used correctly.
  • Capitalization, spacing, punctuation, and ng' are correct throughout.
  • No unscheduled relative clauses, verb extensions, or conditionals were attempted productively.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Talking About Personal Change

When describing a period of adjustment, such as moving to a new job or a new neighborhood, many Swahili-speaking communities favor acknowledging both the difficulty and the gradual progress made, often crediting the support of neighbors, family, or colleagues, as Zainabu and the reading passage's narrator both do. This balance of honesty and gratitude is a useful model for your own extended narration.

Regional Variation Note

The vocabulary and structures reviewed in this chapter — personal-history narration, routine description, and community vocabulary — are stable across Tanzanian and Kenyan Standard Swahili in everyday and educational contexts. More specialized regional, institutional, or media-specific vocabulary is addressed later in this volume, with production status labeled at that point.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Agreement Drift in Longer Sentences

Error: Miji mikubwa ya nchi yetu inabadilika, na watu wanapenda mji hizo. (agreement breaks down partway through the sentence)

Correction: Miji mikubwa ya nchi yetu inabadilika, na watu wanapenda miji hiyo. — as sentences grow longer, class-4 agreement (miji ... hiyo) must be maintained to the end, not only in the first clause.

Common Mistake — Overusing -na- for All Time Reference

Error: Mwaka jana ninamaliza shule. (using present -na- to describe a completed past event)

Correction: Mwaka jana nilimaliza shule. — a time expression like mwaka jana requires past -li-, not present -na-; choose TAM markers by meaning, not by habit.

Common Mistake — Attempting Productive Relative Clauses Too Early

Error: Trying to freely construct new amba- relative clauses in this chapter's writing task because they appeared in the reading and dialogue.

Correction: Understand relative clauses receptively for now; wait for Chapter 2, which begins their controlled, productive development across noun classes.

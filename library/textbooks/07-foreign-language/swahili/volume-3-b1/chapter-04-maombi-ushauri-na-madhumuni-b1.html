Maombi, Ushauri, na Madhumuni

Requests, Advice, and Purpose · Speak and Write Swahili, Volume III: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Overview

Chapter 4 moves from narrating what happened to shaping what should happen next. Building on the cause-and-result connectors and object markers you developed in Chapter 3, you will now develop the subjunctive mood for making refined requests and giving advice, introduce purpose clauses with ili ("so that"), and learn to soften the imperative according to who you are speaking to and how formal the situation is. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to ask for something politely, advise someone on what to do, and explain the purpose behind a request or a plan.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Form and use the subjunctive final -e to make polite requests, suggestions, and recommendations.
  2. Use the subjunctive after verbs of wanting, asking, and advising (nataka..., ninaomba..., nashauri...).
  3. Form purpose clauses with ili to explain why an action is done or requested.
  4. Distinguish direct imperative forms from softened, register-appropriate requests.
  5. Choose an appropriately formal or informal request depending on the listener and setting.
  6. Identify and correct errors in subjunctive formation, purpose-clause structure, and register choice.
  7. Give spoken advice and write a polite request message appropriate to a real situation.

Opening Communication Scenario

James's neighborhood is organizing a small community meeting to discuss fire safety after the incident in Chapter 3. James wants to write a polite message inviting a local official to attend, and he wants to ask his neighbors for small favors — bringing chairs, helping with tea, arriving on time — without sounding like he is giving orders. Bi Amina reminds him that Swahili has a specific verb form for polite requests and suggestions, and that the plain imperative, while useful, sounds too blunt for many of these situations.

  1. This chapter develops the subjunctive final -e, building on the imperative forms learners already know from earlier volumes.
  2. Purpose clauses with ili are introduced for the first time as a productive tool for explaining why something should be done.
  3. The plain imperative is reviewed and contrasted with softened, subjunctive-based requests appropriate to different registers.
  4. Vocabulary in this chapter supports giving advice, making plans, offering assistance, and describing procedures.

Pronunciation Focus

Intonation for Indirect Requests

Polite subjunctive requests in Swahili often carry a gentle rising-then-falling intonation across the whole clause, softer and less clipped than the falling pitch of a direct imperative. Compare Fungua mlango! (a plain command, sharper falling pitch) with Tafadhali, ufungue mlango. (a softened request, smoother pitch across the phrase, with a slight lift on tafadhali).

Also continue monitoring the vowel quality of the subjunctive final -e, which must remain a clear, full front vowel and must not be reduced toward an English schwa sound, even in rapid, polite speech.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This section develops the subjunctive for polite requests and advice, introduces purpose clauses with ili for the first time, and reviews the plain imperative alongside register-sensitive softened requests.

The Subjunctive Final -e

Building Block — Polite Requests and Suggestions
[Subject marker] + [stem] + e

Tafadhali, ufungue mlango. Please, open the door. u- (2nd sg. subject) + fungu + e

Ninaomba mheshimiwa afiki. I request that the official attend. a- (class 1 subject) + fik + e

Nashauri tuandae viti mapema. I advise that we prepare the chairs early. tu- (1st pl. subject) + anda + e

The subjunctive replaces the ordinary final vowel -a with -e. It is used after verbs such as kutaka (to want), kuomba (to request), and kushauri (to advise), and on its own as a softened way of making a request or suggestion, especially when preceded by tafadhali.

Purpose Clauses with ili

New System — ili + subjunctive

Ili ("so that, in order to") introduces a purpose clause and is always followed by a subjunctive verb, not by the ordinary indicative form. The purpose clause explains why the main action is done or requested.

Tunaandaa mkutano ili tuzungumze kuhusu usalama wa moto. We are organizing a meeting so that we can talk about fire safety.

Ninamwomba mheshimiwa afike mapema ili awasalimie wakazi kabla ya mkutano. I am asking the official to arrive early so that he can greet the residents before the meeting.

Tafadhali leta viti vya ziada ili wageni wote wakae vizuri. Please bring extra chairs so that all the guests can sit comfortably.

Imperative vs. Softened Request

Building Block — Choosing the Right Level of Directness
[Stem] + a/eni (plain imperative) vs. Tafadhali, [subject marker] + [stem] + e (softened request)

Njoo hapa! Come here! plain imperative: direct, appropriate among close friends or in urgent situations

Tafadhali, uje hapa kidogo. Please, could you come here for a moment. softened subjunctive request: appropriate with elders, strangers, officials, or in formal settings

The plain imperative (singular -a, plural -eni) remains useful for urgent situations, close relationships, and simple instructions. For requests to elders, officials, customers, or in any formal or public setting, the subjunctive request with tafadhali is the appropriate default.

Zoezi la Sarufi — Grammar Practice:

  1. Change each plain imperative into a softened subjunctive request with tafadhali: Leta kiti! / Fungua dirisha! / Njooni mapema!
  2. Complete each purpose clause with ili and a subjunctive verb: Tunaandaa mkutano ili wakazi ___ (kujua) kuhusu usalama. / Ninamwandikia barua mheshimiwa ili ___ (kuhudhuria) sherehe yetu.
  3. Choose the appropriately formal or informal request for each situation: asking a close friend to pass the salt; asking a government official to attend a meeting; asking a younger sibling to close the door.

Model Dialogue

Muktadha: James anamwandikia na kisha anamweleza Bi Amina jinsi anavyopanga mkutano wa usalama wa moto katika mtaa wao.

(Setting: James is writing to, and then telling Bi Amina how he is planning a fire-safety meeting in their neighborhood.)

BI AMINAJames, nimesikia unapanga mkutano mtaani. Utawaomba nini wakazi?James, I've heard you're planning a meeting in the neighborhood. What will you ask the residents for?
JAMESNinawaomba wahudhurie mkutano ili tuzungumze kuhusu usalama wa moto. Nitawaandikia ujumbe leo.I'm asking them to attend the meeting so that we can talk about fire safety. I'll write them a message today.
BI AMINANashauri uandike kwa heshima, hasa kwa mheshimiwa wa serikali.I advise you to write respectfully, especially to the government official.
JAMESNdiyo, nitamwandikia hivi: "Ninaomba mheshimiwa afike mapema ili awasalimie wakazi kabla ya mkutano."Yes, I'll write to him like this: "I request that the official arrive early so that he can greet the residents before the meeting."
BI AMINAVizuri sana. Na wakazi wenyewe, utawaomba nini?Very good. And the residents themselves, what will you ask them for?
JAMESTafadhali, waambie waje na viti vyao wenyewe ili tusipungukiwe. Pia ninawaomba wachangie chai kidogo.Please, tell them to bring their own chairs so that we don't run short. I'm also asking them to contribute a little tea.
BI AMINASawa. Lakini kwa rafiki zako wa karibu, sema tu "Njooni mapema!" Hakuna haja ya kutumia maneno rasmi sana.Okay. But with your close friends, just say "Come early!" There's no need to use overly formal language.
JAMESAsante kwa ushauri wako, Bi Amina. Nafikiri sasa nimeelewa vizuri ni lini nitumie maombi ya heshima na ni lini nitumie amri rahisi.Thank you for your advice, Bi Amina. I think I now understand well when to use polite requests and when to use simple commands.

This dialogue contrasts polite subjunctive requests (ninaomba...afike, nashauri uandike) and purpose clauses with ili against the plain imperative reserved for close friends (Njooni mapema!), showing how register shapes the choice.

Maswali ya ufahamu — Comprehension questions:

  1. James anawaomba nini wakazi wa mtaa wake?
  2. Kwa nini Bi Amina anamshauri James aandike kwa heshima kwa mheshimiwa?
  3. James atawaambia nini wakazi kuhusu viti?
  4. Bi Amina anasema nini kuhusu jinsi ya kuzungumza na rafiki wa karibu?

  1. Read a short set of main-action sentences and add a purpose clause with ili and a correctly formed subjunctive verb explaining why the action is done.
  2. Check that the verb after ili uses the subjunctive final -e, not the ordinary indicative -a.

Zoezi 3 — Ukaguzi wa Usajili (Register Audit)

Practice: choosing the appropriately formal or informal request for a given relationship and setting.

  1. Read a set of short scenarios describing a speaker, a listener, and a relationship, and choose or write the appropriately formal or informal request for each.
  2. Explain why a plain imperative or a softened subjunctive request is more appropriate in each case.

Speaking Practice

Sehemu ya kwanza — Kuomba kwa Heshima (Making a Polite Request)

With a partner, take turns making a polite request in a formal situation (asking a teacher, official, or elder for something), using the subjunctive and at least one purpose clause with ili. Your partner should respond appropriately, either agreeing or asking a follow-up question.

Sehemu ya pili — Kutoa Ushauri (Giving Advice)

Describe a short problem (a friend is often late, a neighbor's shop needs repairs, a family member wants to save money) and give advice using nashauri... and the subjunctive. Have your partner respond by agreeing, disagreeing politely, or adding another suggestion.

Mbadala wa kujifunza peke yako — Solo alternative

Record yourself making three requests at three different levels of formality (to a close friend, to a stranger, and to an official), and one piece of advice with a purpose clause. Listen back and check your subjunctive formation and register choice.

Writing Workshop

  1. Chagua hali. Choose a real or invented situation in which you need to make a polite request — to an official, employer, teacher, or landlord.
  2. Panga maombi yako. List what you are requesting and why, in plain English notes first.
  3. Andika barua fupi. Write a short 90–130-word polite message or email, using at least two subjunctive requests, one purpose clause with ili, and appropriate formal register throughout.
  4. Kagua kwa orodha. Check your message against the checklist below.
  5. Andika nakala safi. Produce a clean final version.

Prompt: Write a short, polite request message to someone in a position of authority, explaining what you are asking for and why.

Orodha ya ukaguzi — Editing checklist:

  • The subjunctive final -e is correctly formed in every request.
  • At least one purpose clause with ili is used accurately, with a subjunctive verb following it.
  • Register is consistently formal throughout the message, appropriate to the recipient.
  • Object markers and cause-and-result connectors from Chapter 3 are reused correctly where natural.
  • Capitalization, spacing, punctuation, and ng' are correct throughout.
  • No unscheduled conditionals or verb extensions were attempted productively.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Requesting Respectfully

In many Swahili-speaking communities, the way a request is phrased often matters as much as what is being requested. A direct command aimed at an elder, an official, or a stranger can be perceived as disrespectful, even when the underlying request is entirely reasonable. Softened requests, often introduced by tafadhali and built with the subjunctive, are commonly expected in these situations, while directness is more acceptable among close friends, family members, or in urgent circumstances.

Regional Variation Note

The subjunctive request patterns and ili purpose clauses practiced in this chapter are standard across Tanzanian and Kenyan Standard Swahili for formal and everyday politeness. Some coastal and religious contexts use additional layered politeness formulas before a request; that variation is addressed receptively in a later volume and is not a production target here.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using the Indicative -a Instead of the Subjunctive -e

Error: Ninaomba mheshimiwa anafika mapema. (uses the ordinary indicative -a form instead of the subjunctive after ninaomba)

Correction: Ninaomba mheshimiwa afike mapema. — after verbs such as kuomba, kutaka, and kushauri, the following verb must take the subjunctive final -e, not the ordinary present-tense form.

Common Mistake — Forgetting the Subjunctive After ili

Error: Tunaandaa mkutano ili tunazungumza kuhusu usalama. (uses the indicative -a form after ili instead of the subjunctive)

Correction: Tunaandaa mkutano ili tuzungumze kuhusu usalama. — a purpose clause introduced by ili always requires the subjunctive final -e.

Common Mistake — Using the Plain Imperative in a Formal Setting

Error: Njoo mapema, mheshimiwa! (uses the blunt plain imperative to address a government official)

Correction: Tafadhali, mheshimiwa afike mapema. — in formal settings or when addressing officials, elders, or strangers, a softened subjunctive request is expected rather than a direct command.

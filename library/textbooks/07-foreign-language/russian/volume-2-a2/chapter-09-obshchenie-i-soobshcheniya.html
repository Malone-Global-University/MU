Обще́ние и сообще́ния

Communication and messages, dative recipients, and introductory reported speech · Speak and Write Russian: Expanding Independent Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name a recipient in the dative case after communication verbs such as звони́ть/позвони́ть, писа́ть/написа́ть, отвеча́ть/отве́тить, and говори́ть/сказа́ть.
  2. Use these four communication verb pairs accurately in past, present, and future contexts, choosing aspect according to the patterns developed in Chapter 6.
  3. Report, at an introductory level, what someone said, asked, or wrote, using simple reported statements and reported yes/no and information questions.
  4. Distinguish a direct quotation from a simple reported statement, and choose the pronoun, verb form, and word order that reported speech requires.
  5. Describe a call, message, or exchange, including who contacted whom, what was said, and how the recipient responded.
  6. Recognize how digital and spoken communication conventions can vary by generation and community, without treating any one convention as universal.

This chapter builds directly on the prefixed motion verbs from Chapter 8 and the aspect patterns from Chapter 6. It introduces the dative recipient pattern and only the introductory forms of reported speech needed to summarize a call or message. It does not attempt a full survey of Russian indirect speech or sequence-of-tense phenomena, which remain for a later volume.

Communication Scenario

Серге́й has finally arrived at his sister Ири́на's apartment after the delayed trip from Chapter 8. Over the next two days, several people try to reach him: his coworker Тама́ра calls about a work matter, his friend Дми́трий sends a message about weekend plans, and Ири́на wants to know what everyone said. Серге́й has to relay each message accurately, first to himself in his notes, and then aloud to his sister.

  1. You will read and listen to a series of calls and messages exchanged among Серге́й, Ири́на, Тама́ра, and Дми́трий.
  2. You will answer questions about who contacted whom, what was said, and how each message was answered.
  3. You will write and speak about a call or message exchange of your own, reporting what someone said or asked using the introductory patterns from this chapter.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Stress in сказа́ть and its forms

The perfective verb сказа́ть keeps stress on the ending in the infinitive and in most conjugated forms, but the past-tense forms shift stress onto the stem in some persons. Compare сказа́л, with stress on the ending, and сказа́ла, which keeps the same pattern, with the fixed expression ска́жем, meaning let us say, which some speakers use with stem stress in casual speech.

  • Practice скажу́, ска́жешь, ска́жет, ска́жем, ска́жете, ска́жут aloud, noticing that stress moves to the stem after the first-person singular.
  • Practice позвони́л, позвони́ла, позвони́ли aloud, keeping the stress on the ending throughout the past tense.
  • Do not assume that every verb in a pair shares identical stress behavior across all forms. Confirm the stress of each new form individually, rather than guessing from a related verb.

Grammar: Dative Recipients and Reported Speech

Communication verbs in Russian typically name the recipient of the communication in the dative case, without a preposition. This chapter also introduces the simplest patterns for reporting what someone said, asked, or wrote, so that learners can summarize a call or message rather than repeat it word for word.

The dative recipient

Building Block
Кто + verb + кому́ (dative) = who communicates to whom
The person who receives the call, message, or words appears in the dative case immediately after the verb, with no preposition.
Тама́ра позвони́ла Серге́ю.
Tamara called Sergei. (dative recipient, no preposition)
  • Я обы́чно пишу́ роди́телям ка́ждую неде́лю.I usually write to my parents every week.imperfective, habitual, dative plural recipient
  • Он отве́тит тебе́ за́втра.He will answer you tomorrow.perfective future, dative pronoun recipient
Language Focus — The recipient is not the direct object

English speakers sometimes expect an accusative direct object after a communication verb, since English says call someone or tell someone, with someone functioning like an object. Russian treats the recipient of звони́ть, писа́ть, отвеча́ть, and говори́ть as a dative recipient, not an accusative object. Do not substitute the accusative here.

Reporting a statement

Building Block
Он сказа́л, что... = He said that...
Use сказа́ть, что plus a full clause to report a statement. The reported clause keeps its own subject, verb, and tense, adjusted only for the change of speaker, exactly as in English indirect speech.
Дми́трий написа́л, что он свобо́ден в суббо́ту.
Dmitri wrote that he is free on Saturday. (introductory reported statement)
  • Тама́ра сказа́ла, что за́втра бу́дет ва́жная встре́ча.Tamara said that there will be an important meeting tomorrow.reported statement, future tense retained
  • Ири́на отве́тила, что она́ уже́ купи́ла биле́ты.Irina answered that she had already bought the tickets.reported statement, past tense retained

Reporting a question

Building Block
Он спроси́л, ... = He asked...
For a reported yes/no question, use спроси́л, спроси́ла, or спроси́ли plus ли after the verb being questioned. For a reported information question, keep the original question word, such as когда́, где, or почему́, immediately after the reporting verb.
Она́ спроси́ла, прие́дешь ли ты за́втра.
She asked whether you will arrive tomorrow. (reported yes/no question with ли)
  • Тама́ра спроси́ла, когда́ Серге́й вернётся на рабо́ту.Tamara asked when Sergei would be back at work.reported information question, question word retained
  • Дми́трий спроси́л, свобо́дна ли Ири́на в суббо́ту.Dmitri asked whether Irina is free on Saturday.reported yes/no question with ли
Language Focus — This chapter's reported speech is introductory only

This chapter introduces only the simplest reported statements and reported questions, with the reported clause keeping its original tense rather than shifting backward the way English sometimes requires. It does not cover reported commands, reported speech with complex tense shifts, or the full range of Russian indirect discourse. Those systems remain for later volumes.

Model Dialogue: Кто звони́л?

Context: Серге́й has just returned from an errand. Ири́на tells him about a call and a message that came in while he was out.

Ири́на: Серге́й, тебе́ звони́ла Тама́ра, пока́ ты был в магази́не. Sergei, Tamara called you while you were at the store.
Серге́й: Она́ сказа́ла, почему́ звони́т? Did she say why she was calling?
Ири́на: Да, она́ сказа́ла, что за́втра бу́дет ва́жная встре́ча, и спроси́ла, отве́тишь ли ты ей сего́дня. Yes, she said that there will be an important meeting tomorrow, and asked whether you will answer her today.
Серге́й: Хорошо́, я ей перезвоню́ че́рез час. А Дми́трий писа́л? All right, I'll call her back in an hour. Did Dmitri write?
Ири́на: Да, он написа́л сообще́ние. Он спроси́л, свобо́дна ли я в суббо́ту, и переда́л тебе́ приве́т. Yes, he wrote a message. He asked whether I am free on Saturday, and sent you his regards.
Серге́й: Что ты ему́ отве́тила? What did you answer him?
Ири́на: Я ещё не отве́тила. Ду́маю, ска́жем ему́, что мы свобо́дны, и переда́м, что ты то́же хо́чешь прийти́. I haven't answered yet. I think we'll tell him we're free, and I'll pass along that you also want to come.
Серге́й: Хорошо́, напиши́ ему́, пожа́луйста, а я пока́ позвоню́ Тама́ре. All right, please write to him, and I'll call Tamara in the meantime.

Notice how the dialogue moves between direct statements about who contacted whom, звони́ла, написа́л, and introductory reported speech, она́ сказа́ла, что..., он спроси́л, свобо́дна ли я..., exactly as the Grammar section above describes.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Calls, messages, and generational habits

Preferences for calling, texting, or writing a longer message vary among Russian-speaking communities, generations, and individuals, much as they do elsewhere. Some people prefer a quick phone call for anything time sensitive, while others prefer a written message that the recipient can answer when convenient. There is no single correct convention, and learners should ask or observe rather than assume.

Language Note — The dative recipient is not optional

Leaving out the dative recipient, or replacing it with an accusative form by analogy with English, changes or breaks the sentence. Saying я звоню́ Ири́ну instead of я звоню́ Ири́не is a common learner error. Always check that the recipient of звони́ть, писа́ть, отвеча́ть, and говори́ть is in the dative case.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Using the accusative instead of the dative recipient

Error: Writing я позвони́л Тама́ру to mean I called Tamara.

Correction: Use the dative case for the recipient: я позвони́л Тама́ре. The communication verbs in this chapter govern the dative, not the accusative.

Common Mistake — Dropping что or ли in reported speech

Error: Writing она́ сказа́ла он придёт to report a statement.

Correction: Keep the connector: она́ сказа́ла, что он придёт. For a reported yes/no question, keep ли after the questioned verb: она́ спроси́ла, придёт ли он.

Common Mistake — Shifting tense inside the reported clause

Error: Writing он сказа́л, что он был свобо́ден за́втра, shifting the tense backward as English sometimes does.

Correction: Keep the original tense of the reported statement: он сказа́л, что он свобо́ден за́втра. Russian reported speech in this chapter does not shift tense the way English indirect speech often does.

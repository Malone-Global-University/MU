Лю́ди, ве́щи и вы́бор

People, things, and choices · Speak and Write Russian: Expanding Independent Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use the accusative case correctly for plural animate nouns, distinguishing them clearly from inanimate plural nouns.
  2. Point to and identify people and things using э́тот, э́та, э́то, and э́ти, matched correctly to gender and number.
  3. Use personal, possessive, and demonstrative pronouns in the case forms required by the verbs and prepositions around them.
  4. Describe people and belongings using adjectives and pronouns that agree correctly in gender, number, and case.
  5. Ask and answer practical questions about people, possessions, and preferences.
  6. Read and write a short connected account describing several people, their things, and the choices they make.

This chapter continues to use only cases already active in your Russian, the nominative, genitive, prepositional, and accusative. It does not introduce a new case. Instead, it develops the accusative more fully, brings pronoun case forms fully into active use, and adds demonstratives so that you can refer precisely to people and things around you.

Communication Scenario

Тама́ра has settled into her new neighborhood and is now getting to know the other students at the language center. Серге́й introduces her to several friends and neighbors, and the conversation naturally turns to people, their belongings, and the small choices everyone is making, which book to buy, which friend to invite, which bag to take on a trip. As they talk, Тама́ра practices referring to specific people and things precisely, without repeating full names and nouns unnecessarily.

  1. You will identify and describe several people, using demonstratives and pronoun case forms.
  2. You will talk about belongings and choices, using the accusative case for both things and people.
  3. You will read and listen to short accounts in which someone chooses among people, friends, or objects.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Unstressed genitive-looking endings

The animate accusative plural ending often looks identical to the genitive plural ending, and both are frequently unstressed. Reading these endings clearly, without swallowing the final syllable, helps a listener track whether you mean people or things.

  • Practice saying friend́е́й, студе́нтов, and сосе́дей slowly, holding the final unstressed vowel long enough to be heard.
  • Contrast an animate plural phrase such as я зна́ю студе́нтов with an inanimate plural phrase such as я зна́ю кни́ги, noticing how the ending itself carries the animacy information.
  • Read the Model Dialogue below aloud once, paying attention to every plural accusative ending you encounter.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This section develops the accusative case you have already used for singular nouns, extending it to plural people, and brings pronoun and demonstrative case forms fully into your active Russian.

Animate accusative plural

Pattern
animate plural noun in accusative = genitive plural form
For all genders, the accusative plural of an animate noun matches its genitive plural form. Inanimate plural nouns keep the nominative plural form instead.
Тама́ра приглаша́ет друзе́й и сосе́док на у́жин.
Tamara is inviting friends and female neighbors to dinner.
  • Серге́й хорошо́ зна́ет всех студе́нтов в гру́ппе.Sergei knows all the students in the group well.animate accusative plural = genitive plural
  • А телефо́ны и су́мки лежа́т на столе́.And the phones and bags are lying on the table.inanimate plural, unaffected by animacy rule

Compare зна́ю студе́нтов, people, with зна́ю кни́ги, things. The verb and the sentence structure stay the same, but the ending on the plural noun changes because animacy changes.

Demonstratives: э́тот, э́та, э́то, э́ти

Pattern
э́тот (m.) / э́та (f.) / э́то (n.) / э́ти (pl.) + noun
Demonstratives agree with the noun in gender and number, and decline into whichever case the sentence requires, including the accusative.
Тама́ра выбира́ет э́тот пода́рок, а не тот.
Tamara is choosing this gift, not that one.
  • Ты уже́ ви́дел э́ту фотогра́фию?Have you already seen this photograph?э́ту, feminine accusative
  • Мы пригласи́ли э́тих студе́нтов на пра́здник.We invited these students to the celebration.э́тих, animate accusative plural

Pronoun case forms

Pattern
я / ты / он / она́ / мы / вы / они́ → меня́ / тебя́ / его́ / её / нас / вас / их
Personal pronouns decline into the accusative, genitive, dative, and other active cases, just as nouns do, and appear after the same verbs and prepositions that govern noun case.
Серге́й хорошо́ зна́ет её, но пло́хо зна́ет их.
Sergei knows her well, but knows them poorly.
  • Ты меня́ ви́дишь? Да, я тебя́ ви́жу хорошо́.Do you see me? Yes, I see you well.меня́, тебя́, accusative pronouns
  • Э́то пода́рок для него́, а э́то для неё.This is a gift for him, and this is for her.него́, неё, genitive after preposition для
Language Focus — Third-person pronouns after prepositions

The third-person pronouns он, она́, оно́, and они́ add an initial н when they follow a preposition, becoming него́, неё, and них. This pattern is required, not optional, and it applies across every case and every preposition already active in your Russian.

Model Dialogue: Лю́ди, ве́щи и вы́бор

Context: Тама́ра and Серге́й are at the market, looking at gifts and talking about friends they might invite to a small gathering.

Тама́ра: Смотри́, кака́я краси́вая су́мка. Ду́маю, я вы́беру э́ту. Look, what a beautiful bag. I think I will choose this one.
Серге́й: Хоро́ший вы́бор. А кого́ ты пригласи́шь на пра́здник? Всех на́ших студе́нтов? Good choice. And whom will you invite to the celebration? All our students?
Тама́ра: Не всех, но я пригласи́ла бы мои́х сосе́док и, коне́чно, тебя́. А ты уже́ зна́ешь мои́х друзе́й? Not all of them, but I would invite my female neighbors and, of course, you. And do you already know my friends?
Серге́й: Я зна́ю то́лько не́которых, но с удово́льствием познако́млюсь с ни́ми. А э́ти фотогра́фии, кого́ они́ пока́зывают? I know only some of them, but I would gladly get acquainted with them. And these photographs, whom do they show?
Тама́ра: Э́то мои́ друзья́ из university, а э́то моя́ подру́га, кото́рую я зна́ю уже́ мно́го лет. These are my friends from university, and this is my friend, whom I have known for many years already.
Серге́й: Она́ то́же живёт здесь, и́ли она́ то́лько прие́хала в го́сти? Does she also live here, or has she only come to visit?
Тама́ра: Она́ живёт в друго́м го́роде, но я о́чень люблю́ её, и мы ча́сто пи́шем друг дру́гу. She lives in another city, but I love her very much, and we often write to each other.

Notice how the dialogue moves between full nouns, demonstratives, and pronoun case forms, always referring to the same people and things without unnecessary repetition.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Gifts, gatherings, and hospitality

Among many Russian-speaking families and friend groups, small gifts are commonly brought to gatherings, even informal ones, and the choice of gift often reflects the closeness of the relationship rather than its monetary value. Practices vary considerably by family, region, and generation, and no single gift-giving custom applies to every Russian-speaking community.

Language Note — Друг дру́га as a reciprocal expression

The fixed expression друг дру́га, meaning "each other," declines only in its second part, which takes whichever case the sentence requires, as in пи́шем друг дру́гу, "we write to each other," using the dative. Learn this expression as a formulaic unit for now, and treat its internal structure as receptive information rather than a pattern to generate freely.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Using the nominative plural for animate direct objects

Error: Writing Я зна́ю студе́нты when describing people you know, using the nominative plural form instead of the required accusative form.

Correction: Use the animate accusative plural, which matches the genitive plural: Я зна́ю студе́нтов. Reserve the nominative plural, студе́нты, for the subject of a sentence.

Common Mistake — Forgetting the initial н after a preposition

Error: Writing для его́ or с ей, omitting the required н on a third-person pronoun after a preposition.

Correction: Add the initial н whenever a third-person pronoun follows a preposition: для него́, с ней. This rule applies consistently across every preposition already active in your Russian.

Common Mistake — Treating all plural nouns the same way in the accusative

Error: Applying the animate accusative plural ending to an inanimate noun, writing Я купи́л кни́г instead of the correct nominative-like plural form.

Correction: Inanimate plural nouns keep the nominative plural form in the accusative: Я купи́л кни́ги. Only animate plural nouns shift to the genitive-like form: Я пригласи́л друзе́й.

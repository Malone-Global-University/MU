Исто́рия собы́тия

Telling a story with cause, result, and reaction · Speak and Write Russian: Expanding Independent Communication

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chapter 17 continues Unit IV and moves from personal memory toward the fuller shape of an event narrative, a story with a clear cause, a sequence of actions, a result, and a reaction. Ири́на's friend Макси́м tells her about the day he missed an important train, and Ири́на later tells the story again to her grandmother. This chapter asks learners to track a story across several linked sentences, choosing aspect not sentence by sentence but as part of a coherent narrative arc, using motion verbs to move the story's characters from place to place, and keeping pronouns, names, and references clear as the cast of a story grows. No new case or motion pair is introduced. The chapter develops the aspect and motion systems already active from earlier chapters into longer, more connected narration.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Narrate a short event story that includes a cause, a sequence of actions, a result, and a reaction.
  2. Select aspect across a connected narrative, not only within a single sentence, so that background, sequential events, and result states remain distinct.
  3. Use already active motion verbs, prefixed and unprefixed, to move characters through a story's locations.
  4. Maintain clear reference to people and things across several sentences, using pronouns and repeated nouns so that listeners and readers do not lose track of who did what.
  5. Listen to and read a short event narrative and identify its cause, its main actions, its result, and the reaction of the people involved.
  6. Tell and write a short story about an event, real or invented, that a listener or reader can follow from beginning to end.

This chapter develops aspect in narration, motion in narration, and object and reference cohesion. It recycles the case, motion, and verb systems already active from earlier chapters, and it introduces no new case, motion, orthographic, or pronunciation system, in keeping with the Version 5 matrix architecture governing this volume.

Communication Scenario

Макси́м, a friend of Ири́на's, nearly missed an important train last week, and the story of that morning has already become one that he tells often. Ири́на hears it from him over coffee, and later that evening she retells it to her grandmother, Ве́ра Никола́евна, who enjoys a good story with a proper beginning, middle, and end. Along the way, Ири́на has to keep straight who left the apartment, who called whom, and who finally reached the platform in time.

  1. You will listen to and read Макси́м's story about the morning he nearly missed his train.
  2. You will identify the story's cause, its main events, its result, and how the people involved reacted.
  3. You will practice using motion verbs to track movement between locations within a story.
  4. You will tell and write a short event story of your own, keeping references to people and things clear throughout.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Stress in побежа́ть and rhythm in cause-result chains

Confirm the stress of побежа́ть on the final syllable, distinct from the unprefixed бежа́ть, which also carries final stress but shifts in some present-tense forms already reviewed in earlier chapters. Keep the two forms distinct in speech, since the prefix marks the beginning of the running rather than a change in meaning of the root.

  • Practice reading a short cause-result chain aloud with a falling intonation on the causal clause and a slightly rising, then falling, intonation on the result clause, so that a listener hears the connection between из-за and поэ́тому even without seeing them written.
  • Give a light stress to к сча́стью and к сожале́нию when they open a sentence, since they frame the listener's expectation for what follows.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops three connected systems that together let learners tell a fuller event story: aspect choice across a connected narrative, motion verbs used to move characters through a story, and reference cohesion that keeps people and things clear across several sentences. All three systems rely on the case, aspect, and motion systems already active from earlier chapters; no new case or motion pair is introduced.

Aspect across a connected narrative
imperfective sets background and process · perfective moves the story forward, event by event
In a longer narrative, a chain of perfective verbs usually marks the main sequence of events, one action leading to the next, while imperfective verbs step outside that chain to describe background, an ongoing state, or a repeated habit. Choosing aspect for a whole narrative means asking whether each verb belongs to the main chain of events or steps outside it.
У́тром Макси́м гото́вился к пое́здке. Он собра́л ве́щи, вы́шел из до́ма и пошёл на ста́нцию.
In the morning Maksim was getting ready for the trip. (background, imperfective гото́вился) He packed his things, left the house, and set off for the station. (event chain, all perfective)
Motion verbs in narration
unprefixed motion for the general journey · prefixed motion for arrival, departure, or a bounded stage
A narrative often needs several motion verbs, one for the overall journey and others for specific stages of it, such as leaving a building, arriving somewhere, or running to catch something. Already active prefixed motion verbs mark these bounded stages, while unprefixed motion verbs describe the ongoing trip itself.
Он е́хал в метро́ два́дцать мину́т, а пото́м вы́шел на ста́нции и побежа́л к платфо́рме.
He rode the metro for twenty minutes, and then got off at the station and ran toward the platform. (е́хал for the ongoing ride, вы́шел and побежа́л for the bounded stages)
Object and reference cohesion
full noun on first mention · pronoun or omission on later mentions, unless clarity requires the noun again
Russian narration usually names a person or thing fully once, then refers back with a pronoun or, where the verb form already signals the subject, with no pronoun at all. Reintroducing the full noun repeatedly, in the English style, makes a Russian narrative sound unnatural and mechanical. The noun should return only when the referent has genuinely changed or when the pronoun would be unclear.
Макси́м вы́шел из до́ма. Он забы́л биле́т на столе́ и верну́лся за ним.
Maksim left the house. He forgot the ticket on the table and went back for it. (он refers to Макси́м, ним refers to биле́т, both without repeating the full noun)

Model Dialogue: Опозда́ние на по́езд

Context: Ири́на meets her friend Макси́м for coffee, and he tells her about a morning last week when he nearly missed an important train.

Ири́на: Макси́м, ты обеща́л рассказа́ть мне, что случи́лось на про́шлой неде́ле. Что произошло́? Maksim, you promised to tell me what happened last week. What happened?
Макси́м: О, э́то была́ настоя́щая исто́рия! Из-за буди́льника я почти́ опозда́л на по́езд. Oh, that was quite a story! Because of the alarm clock, I almost missed the train.
Ири́на: Расскажи́ подро́бнее. Что произошло́ снача́ла? Tell me more. What happened first?
Макси́м: Снача́ла буди́льник про́сто не зазвони́л. Я просну́лся сам, но уже́ по́здно. Я бы́стро оде́лся, забы́л биле́т на столе́ и верну́лся за ним. First the alarm simply did not go off. I woke up on my own, but already late. I got dressed quickly, forgot the ticket on the table, and went back for it.
Ири́на: И пото́м? Как ты дое́хал до вокза́ла? And then? How did you get to the station?
Макси́м: Я вы́бежал из кварти́ры и побежа́л к метро́. Пока́ я е́хал в метро́, я звони́л дру́гу, что́бы он предупреди́л мои́х колле́г. Наконе́ц я вы́шел на ста́нции и побежа́л пря́мо к платфо́рме. I ran out of the apartment and ran to the metro. While I was riding the metro, I was calling a friend so that he would warn my colleagues. Finally I got off at the station and ran straight to the platform.
Ири́на: И ты успе́л? And did you make it?
Макси́м: К сча́стью, да, я успе́л, то́чно в после́дний моме́нт. Снача́ла я испуга́лся, что по́езд уже́ ушёл, а пото́м, когда́ я уви́дел его́ на платфо́рме, я о́чень обра́довался. Че́рез пять мину́т я уже́ успоко́ился. Fortunately, yes, I made it, right at the last moment. At first I got scared that the train had already left, and then, when I saw it on the platform, I was very glad. Within five minutes I had already calmed down.

This exchange moves through cause (буди́льник не зазвони́л), a chain of perfective events (оде́лся, верну́лся, вы́бежал, побежа́л, вы́шел), motion in narration at several bounded stages, and a clear reaction sequence (испуга́лся, обра́довался, успоко́ился), without introducing any new case or motion system.

Listening script

  4. Find one motion verb marking a bounded stage of the journey and one imperfective verb describing background or an ongoing process.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Telling a story worth retelling

A story about a near miss, a small mishap, or an unexpected turn is a common and welcome subject of everyday conversation in many Russian-speaking settings, often told with humor once the tension of the moment has passed, though how much detail is shared, and how dramatically, varies from speaker to speaker and setting to setting.

Language Note — A story may be invented

As in the previous chapter, an invented event story, built honestly from the grammar and vocabulary developed here, serves the same communicative purpose as a true one. Learners should feel free to invent a plausible chain of events rather than describe a real situation they would prefer not to share.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Using the imperfective for a chained event

Error: Writing он выходи́л из до́ма, потом он бежа́л к ста́нции, using the imperfective for actions that belong to the main event chain.

Correction: A chain of specific, sequential events normally takes the perfective: он вы́шел из до́ма, а пото́м побежа́л к ста́нции. Reserve the imperfective for background, an ongoing process, or a habitual action outside the chain.

Common Mistake — Repeating the full noun in every sentence

Error: Writing Макси́м вы́шел из до́ма. Макси́м забы́л биле́т. Макси́м верну́лся за биле́том, repeating Макси́м and биле́т in every sentence.

Correction: Once a referent is established, a pronoun usually carries later mentions: Макси́м вы́шел из до́ма. Он забы́л биле́т и верну́лся за ним. Bring back the full noun only if the referent has changed or a pronoun would be unclear.

Common Mistake — Overusing one motion verb for every stage of a journey

Error: Writing он шёл из до́ма, он шёл в метро́, он шёл на платфо́рму, using the same unprefixed motion verb for every stage.

Correction: Use the prefixed motion verbs already active from earlier chapters to mark distinct bounded stages: он вы́шел из до́ма, спусти́лся в метро́ и вы́шел на платфо́рму. Save the unprefixed verb for the ongoing middle of a single stage, not for the whole journey.

Chapter Review

Aspect across a narrative

A chain of perfective verbs typically carries a story's main sequence of events, while imperfective verbs step outside it for background or process.

Motion in narration

Already active prefixed motion verbs mark bounded stages of a journey, while unprefixed motion verbs describe the ongoing trip itself.

Reference cohesion

Pronouns, not repeated full nouns, normally carry later mentions of an already established person or thing.

Cause, result, and reaction

Из-за and поэ́тому connect a story's cause and result, while verbs such as испуга́ться and обра́доваться report how people responded.

Cause and resultThe learner states a clear cause and connects it to a stated result.

Reference cohesionThe learner keeps references to people and things clear, relying on pronouns rather than unnecessary repetition.

This chapter is not a cumulative unit assessment. It develops aspect in narration, motion in narration, and object and reference cohesion as new productive systems for Unit IV, while recycling all case, motion, and other verb systems already active from earlier chapters.

Answer Key

Grammar and Sentence Patterns — Identify the Pattern

  1. Background, imperfective просну́лся с причи́ной: буди́льник не рабо́тал explains the cause; просну́лся itself is a single event.
  2. Main event chain, all perfective: оде́лся, вы́бежал, побежа́л; побежа́л marks the beginning stage of running toward the stop.
  3. Background process inside a пока́-clause: бежа́л, ongoing while ду́мал also describes an ongoing mental state.
  4. Main event chain, perfective вбежа́л, marking arrival on the platform at a precise moment.

Guided Practice — Exercise 1

  1. Из-за того́, что буди́льник не рабо́тал, Макси́м просну́лся по́здно.
  2. Он забы́л биле́т, поэ́тому он верну́лся домо́й.
  3. Он бежа́л о́чень бы́стро, поэ́тому он успе́л на по́езд.

Guided Practice — Exercise 2

Sample order: Она вы́шла из до́ма. Она пошла́ на остано́вку. Она узна́ла, что авто́бус опозда́л. Она реши́ла пойти́ пешко́м. Other logical orders that keep the motion sequence coherent are also correct.

Guided Practice — Exercise 3

  1. Макси́м уви́дел по́езд и побежа́л к нему́.
  2. Ири́на позвони́ла ба́бушке, и она́ сра́зу отве́тила ей.
  3. Друзья́ прие́хали на ста́нцию и жда́ли по́езд на платфо́рме.

Listening — Comprehension

  1. Heavy rain made the bus late, causing the neighbor's problem that morning.
  2. She waited at the stop, then decided to walk, then went back home and called a taxi.
  3. She made it to the meeting on time, and the client did not notice her rushing at all; the meeting went well.
  4. Sample answer: верну́лась and вы́звала mark bounded stages; гото́вилась describes an ongoing background process.

Model Dialogue — Comprehension

  1. Maksim's alarm clock did not go off, so he woke up late.
  2. He was frightened when he thought the train had already left, and then delighted when he saw it on the platform.

Reading

  1. A broken elevator kept Maksim from leaving right away, and he began to worry because of an important meeting.
  2. He waited by the door, then decided to walk down the stairs, ran outside, and went to the bus stop; the bus came on time and he made it without problems.
  3. His colleague got stuck in traffic and was twenty minutes late, introduced by к сожале́нию.
  4. Sample answer: сбежа́л marks the bounded stage of running down the stairs.

Writing

Sample account: Из-за поло́мки маши́ны мой брат не смог сра́зу пое́хать на рабо́ту. Снача́ла он ждал такси́ у до́ма, но такси́ не приезжа́ло до́лго. Пото́м он реши́л пойти́ пешко́м и бы́стро вы́шел на у́лицу. Он шёл два́дцать мину́т, а зате́м уви́дел авто́бус и побежа́л к остано́вке. Он успе́л на авто́бус в после́дний моме́нт и о́чень обра́довался. Поэ́тому он прие́хал на рабо́ту то́лько на пять мину́т по́зже обы́чного, и его́ нача́льник да́же не заме́тил э́того. Other accounts, real or invented, that accurately combine the systems from this chapter are also correct.

Looking Ahead

Что вы уже уме́ете — What you can do now

You can narrate a connected event story with a cause, a chain of sequential actions, a result, and a reaction, choosing aspect and motion verbs across the whole narrative rather than sentence by sentence, and keeping reference to people and things clear from beginning to end.

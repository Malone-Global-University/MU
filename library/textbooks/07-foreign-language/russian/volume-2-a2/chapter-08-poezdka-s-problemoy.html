Пое́здка с пробле́мой

A trip with a problem, prefixed motion verbs, and asking for help along the way · Speak and Write Russian: Expanding Independent Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use the prefixed motion verbs прийти́/прие́хать, уйти́/уе́хать, войти́/въе́хать, вы́йти/вы́ехать, and перейти́/перее́хать to describe arrival, departure, entry, exit, and crossing.
  2. Explain a travel disruption, such as a delay, a missed connection, or a change of platform, gate, or route.
  3. Ask for help from a stranger, an employee, or a fellow traveler in a transit setting.
  4. Distinguish a prefixed motion verb from the unprefixed forms reviewed in Chapter 7, and choose the correct prefix for arrival, departure, entry, exit, or crossing.
  5. Combine prefixed motion verbs with the case and preposition patterns already active from earlier chapters.
  6. Narrate a short sequence of travel events in the past tense, using prefixed motion verbs alongside the aspect patterns from Chapter 6.

This chapter builds directly on the unprefixed motion verbs from Chapter 7. It introduces only the five prefixes listed above, in their most common and useful meanings. It does not introduce a new case, and it does not attempt the full inventory of Russian motion prefixes.

Communication Scenario

Серге́й is traveling to visit his sister in another city, and his trip does not go according to plan. His train is delayed, his platform changes, and he needs to ask station staff and other passengers for help along the way. His sister, Ири́на, is waiting at home, texting him for updates, and Тама́ра, his coworker from Chapter 7, calls him partway through the trip to see how the visit is going.

  1. You will read and listen to a series of exchanges describing a travel disruption.
  2. You will answer questions about what went wrong, what changed, and how Серге́й solved each problem.
  3. You will write and speak about a trip of your own that did not go as planned, using prefixed motion verbs to describe arrival, departure, and the change of plan.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Stress shift with prefixes

Adding a prefix to a motion verb often moves the stress. Compare идти́, stressed on the ending, with прийти́, still stressed on the ending, but compare вы́йти, where the prefix вы- pulls the stress forward onto itself in the perfective. The imperfective partner выходи́ть keeps the stress on the ending instead.

  • Practice прие́ду, прие́дешь, прие́дет aloud, keeping stem stress throughout, as in the unprefixed е́хать.
  • Practice вы́йду, вы́йдешь, вы́йдет aloud, keeping the stress fixed on the prefix вы- in every form.
  • Do not assume every prefixed form keeps the stress pattern of its unprefixed source. Confirm each new form's stress individually rather than guessing by analogy.

Grammar: Prefixed Motion Verbs

Russian prefixes attach directional meaning onto the unprefixed motion stems reviewed in Chapter 7. Each prefix in this chapter marks a specific kind of movement relative to a place: arrival, departure, entry, exit, or crossing. The imperfective and perfective members of each pair behave like the aspect pairs developed in Chapter 6, so aspect choice still depends on whether the sentence describes a process, a habit, or a single completed event.

Arrival: при-

Building Block
прийти́ / прие́хать = to arrive at a destination
Use the perfective form for a single completed arrival, and the imperfective form for a habitual arrival or for describing the arrival as a process.
По́езд прие́хал по́здно.
The train arrived late. (single completed event, perfective)
  • Я обы́чно прихожу́ на рабо́ту в де́вять часо́в.I usually arrive at work at nine o'clock.imperfective, habitual arrival
  • Он прие́дет за́втра ве́чером.He will arrive tomorrow evening.perfective future, single planned arrival

Departure: у-

Building Block
уйти́ / уе́хать = to leave, to depart from a place
Use the perfective form for a single completed departure, and the imperfective form for a habitual pattern of leaving or for the departure as a process.
Мой по́езд уже́ уе́хал!
My train has already left! (single completed event, perfective)
  • Она́ уезжа́ет из го́рода ка́ждое ле́то.She leaves the city every summer.imperfective, habitual departure
  • Мы уйдём отсю́да че́рез де́сять мину́т.We will leave from here in ten minutes.perfective future, single planned departure

Entry and exit: в- and вы-

Building Block
войти́/въе́хать = to enter  ·  вы́йти/вы́ехать = to exit
Use в- for entering a place and вы- for exiting or getting off. Both pairs follow the same aspect logic as при- and у-.
Он вошёл в ко́мнату, а пото́м вы́шел.
He entered the room, and then he left it. (two completed events in sequence)
  • Вы выхо́дите на сле́дующей остано́вке?Are you getting off at the next stop?imperfective, in-progress or immediate question, common transit formula
  • Мы въе́хали в го́род о́коло полу́дня.We drove into the city around noon.perfective past, single completed entry by vehicle

Crossing: пере-

Building Block
перейти́/перее́хать = to cross, to go across
Use пере- for crossing a street, a bridge, a border, or a similar boundary. This chapter does not extend перее́хать to its separate meaning of moving house.
Переходи́те у́лицу здесь, э́то безопа́снее.
Cross the street here, it is safer.
  • Мы перешли́ че́рез мост и пошли́ к вокза́лу.We crossed the bridge and went toward the station.perfective past, completed crossing followed by another motion event
Language Focus — Prefix plus preposition plus case

Each prefix pairs naturally with certain prepositions and cases, recycled directly from earlier chapters. Arrival and entry use в/на plus the accusative for the destination. Departure uses из/с plus the genitive for the source. Do not treat the prefix alone as sufficient. The preposition and case still carry essential meaning.

  • Мы прие́хали в Москву́.We arrived in Moscow.при- plus в plus accusative, destination
  • Он уе́хал из Москвы́.He left Moscow.у- plus из plus genitive, source
  • Она́ вы́шла из авто́буса на остано́вке.She got off the bus at the stop.вы- plus из plus genitive, exit from an enclosed vehicle
Language Focus — Aspect still governs the choice within each pair

The prefix tells you the direction of the movement. Aspect, developed in Chapter 6, still tells you whether the sentence describes a process, a habit, or a single completed event. Choosing the right prefix without checking aspect, or choosing the right aspect without checking the prefix, will each produce an inaccurate sentence.

Model Dialogue: Опозда́ние на по́езд

Context: Серге́й has just learned that his train is delayed. He asks a station employee for help, then calls his sister Ири́на with an update.

Серге́й: Извини́те, вы не зна́ете, почему́ по́езд не прие́хал? Excuse me, do you happen to know why the train hasn't arrived?
Слу́жащая: Да, есть небольша́я заде́ржка. По́езд прие́дет че́рез два́дцать мину́т, на платфо́рму но́мер три. Yes, there is a small delay. The train will arrive in twenty minutes, on platform number three.
Серге́й: Пла́тформа но́мер три? Ра́ньше говори́ли, но́мер оди́н. Как мне туда́ перейти́? Platform number three? They said number one before. How do I get over there?
Слу́жащая: Вы́йдите отсю́да, перейди́те че́рез мост, и вы уви́дите платфо́рму но́мер три сле́ва. Go out from here, cross over the bridge, and you'll see platform number three on your left.
Серге́й: Спаси́бо большо́е! (звони́т сестре́) Ири́на, приве́т! Я ещё на вокза́ле, по́езд заде́рживается. Thank you very much! (calls his sister) Irina, hi! I'm still at the station, the train is delayed.
Ири́на: Опя́ть? Когда́ ты прие́дешь тепе́рь? Again? When will you arrive now?
Серге́й: Я вы́еду из го́рода че́рез два́дцать мину́т, зна́чит, прие́ду к тебе́ о́коло восьми́. I'll be leaving the city in twenty minutes, so I'll arrive at your place around eight.
Ири́на: Хорошо́, я тебя́ бу́ду ждать. Напиши́ мне, когда́ вы́йдешь со ста́нции. All right, I'll wait for you. Write to me when you get off at the station.

Notice how the dialogue moves between the imperfective process of a delay, заде́рживается, and completed prefixed events, прие́хал, вы́еду, прие́ду, вы́йдешь, exactly as the Language Focus notes above describe.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Asking strangers for help while traveling

Asking a stranger, an employee, or a fellow passenger for help during a travel disruption is an ordinary part of getting around in Russian-speaking cities. The polite opener извини́те, вы не зна́ете... softens the request and signals politeness toward someone you do not know, and it is used across many everyday situations, not only at train stations.

Language Note — Prefixes are not decoration

Adding a prefix to a motion verb is not a stylistic upgrade. Each prefix carries a specific directional meaning, and choosing the wrong one changes what actually happened. Saying он вы́шел when you mean он прие́хал tells a listener something different from what you intended.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Using an unprefixed verb where a prefix is needed

Error: Writing по́езд е́хал в семь часо́в to mean the train arrived at seven.

Correction: Use the prefixed form for a specific completed arrival: по́езд прие́хал в семь часо́в. The unprefixed forms from Chapter 7 describe motion in progress or habitual movement, not arrival at a specific destination.

Common Mistake — Confusing entry and exit prefixes

Error: Saying я войду́ из по́езда to mean getting off the train.

Correction: Use вы- for exiting: я вы́йду из по́езда. Reserve в- for entering: я войду́ в по́езд.

Common Mistake — Choosing the prefix correctly but ignoring aspect

Error: Writing ка́ждый день он прие́дет на рабо́ту в де́вять to describe a daily habit.

Correction: Use the imperfective form for a habitual pattern: ка́ждый день он приезжа́ет на рабо́ту в де́вять. Reserve the perfective прие́дет for one specific future arrival.

Model Dialogue — Comprehension

Reading

  2. Irina asks whether Sergei waited long at the station, and whether he found the right platform without problems.
  3. Irina says she will drive over to pick Sergei up as soon as he gets off the train.

Writing

Sample message: Приве́т! Хочу́ рассказа́ть о свое́й пое́здке. Я вы́ехал из до́ма в семь часо́в утра́, но авто́бус заде́рживался, и мне пришло́сь ждать до́лго. Извини́те, вы не зна́ете, спроси́л я у одного́ пассажи́ра, когда́ прие́дет сле́дующий авто́бус? Он отве́тил, что че́рез два́дцать мину́т. Наконе́ц авто́бус прие́хал, я вошёл, и мы перее́хали че́рез мост. Я вы́шел на свое́й остано́вке и прие́хал на встре́чу то́лько на де́сять мину́т по́зже. Other messages drawing accurately on this chapter's prefixed motion verbs and aspect patterns are also correct.

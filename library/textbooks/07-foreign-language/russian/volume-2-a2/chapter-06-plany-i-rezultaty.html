Пла́ны и результа́ты

Plans and results, aspect pair expansion and the perfective future · Speak and Write Russian: Expanding Independent Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize and use several verified imperfective and perfective aspect pairs in context.
  2. Form and use the perfective future to talk about a single planned or expected event.
  3. Form and use the imperfective compound future with бу́ду plus an imperfective infinitive to talk about ongoing plans, processes, and repeated future activity.
  4. Distinguish process from result in the past tense, choosing the imperfective for an activity itself and the perfective for its completed outcome.
  5. Talk about plans, projects, and their results using vocabulary and sentence patterns already active from Chapters 1 through 5.
  6. Combine aspect choice with the location, reference, and quantity systems recycled in Chapter 5.

This chapter introduces no new case, no new alphabet or pronunciation system, and no new motion verb. Aspect pairs are treated as verified lexical relationships, not as an automatic prefixing rule.

Communication Scenario

Тама́ра, from Chapter 5's correspondence, is now planning a small project with her neighbor Па́вел, a shared garden on their street. They need to talk about what they are doing this week, what they will finish by a certain date, and what has already been done. Their conversation moves constantly between ongoing plans and finished results, which is exactly the contrast this chapter teaches.

  1. You will read and listen to a conversation about a shared plan, distinguishing what is still in process from what is already finished.
  2. You will answer questions about intentions, single planned events, and completed results.
  3. You will write and speak about your own plans, choosing the correct aspect for process, single events, and results.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Stress shift between aspect partners

Stress sometimes moves when an imperfective and perfective partner are otherwise built the same way. Compare пишу́, I write, with напишу́, I will write, where the stress stays on the ending in both, and говорю́, I say, with скажу́, I will say, where the whole word changes because the pair is suppletive.

  • Read each pair in the vocabulary table aloud, checking the marked stress on both members.
  • Do not assume stress stays in the same place just because the perfective adds a prefix.
  • Where a pair is suppletive, such as говори́ть/сказа́ть, treat the two verbs as separate words that happen to share one meaning relationship, not as one word with an ending change.

Grammar: Aspect, Future, and Result

Russian aspect is a meaning system, not a tense label. This section teaches three related patterns: the perfective future for a single planned or expected event, the imperfective compound future for ongoing or repeated plans, and the choice between process and result in the past.

Perfective future: one complete event

Building Block
perfective non-past ending = future meaning
A perfective verb has no true present tense. Its non-past form refers to a single bounded event in the future.
Я напишу́ письмо́ за́втра.
I will write the letter tomorrow. (one complete act, a single letter, finished)
  • Мы сде́лаем э́то к суббо́те.We will get this done by Saturday.perfective future, сде́лаем, single completed result
  • Я купи́ю... (incorrect form, shown for contrast only)

Note: the incorrect example above is intentionally marked as incorrect and is not a model to copy. The correct form is я куплю́, shown in the table below.

Imperfective compound future: process, habit, and ongoing plans

Building Block
бу́ду / бу́дешь / бу́дет / бу́дем / бу́дете / бу́дут + imperfective infinitive
This form describes an activity as ongoing, repeated, or without a stated single outcome, not a finished event.
Я бу́ду писа́ть письмо́ ве́чером.
I will be writing the letter in the evening. (the activity itself, not a promise it will be finished)
  • Мы бу́дем рабо́тать в саду́ ка́ждый день на э́той неде́ле.We will be working in the garden every day this week.imperfective future, repeated activity
  • Я бу́ду чита́ть э́ту кни́гу до́лго.I will be reading this book for a long time.imperfective future, ongoing process, no completion implied
Language Focus — Choosing between the two futures

Ask what the sentence actually communicates. If the point is a single event with a clear outcome, such as finishing, arriving, buying, or writing one specific thing, use the perfective future. If the point is an ongoing activity, a repeated action, or a plan described without a guaranteed finish, use the imperfective compound future. Do not choose an aspect by translating the English word will alone, since both Russian futures translate that way.

Process versus result in the past

The same contrast applies in the past tense. The imperfective past describes an activity, an attempt, or a repeated action without focusing on its outcome. The perfective past describes a single completed event and its result.

  • Я де́лал прое́кт весь ве́чер.I was working on the project all evening.imperfective past, activity, outcome not stated
  • Я сде́лал прое́кт.I finished the project. (it is done)perfective past, result-state meaning
  • Он чита́л кни́гу, но не ко́нчил её.He was reading the book, but did not finish it.imperfective past for the attempt, perfective past for the negated result
Language Focus — Talking about a finished result

To say that something is already finished right now, Russian speakers most often use the perfective past together with уже́, already. Я уже́ сде́лал э́то communicates a present result, it is done now, even though the verb form itself is grammatically past. This is the chapter's main tool for result-state language. Do not attempt to build result-state meaning with participles, which remain outside this chapter's scope.

Model Dialogue: Пла́ны на ле́то

Context: Тама́ра and her neighbor Па́вел are planning a small shared garden on their street and checking on progress partway through the week.

Тама́ра: Приве́т, Па́вел! Что ты де́лаешь сего́дня? Hi, Pavel! What are you doing today?
Па́вел: Я рабо́таю в саду́. Я уже́ купи́л всё, что ну́жно, а сего́дня бу́ду рабо́тать до ве́чера. I am working in the garden. I already bought everything necessary, and today I will be working until evening.
Тама́ра: А когда́ ты ко́нчишь? Мы хоти́м пригласи́ть сосе́дей в суббо́ту. And when will you finish? We want to invite the neighbors on Saturday.
Па́вел: Я ду́маю, я сде́лаю всё к пя́тнице. Тогда́ у нас бу́дет вре́мя пригото́виться. I think I will get everything done by Friday. Then we will have time to get ready.
Тама́ра: Отли́чно. Я напишу́ сосе́дям сего́дня ве́чером и скажу́, что мы всё почти́ ко́нчили. Excellent. I will write to the neighbors this evening and say that we have almost finished everything.
Три дня спустя́. Three days later.
Тама́ра: Ну как, ты ко́нчил? So, did you finish?
Па́вел: Да, я уже́ ко́нчил! Сад гото́в, и сосе́ди уже́ ви́дели его́ фотогра́фии. Yes, I already finished! The garden is ready, and the neighbors have already seen photos of it.

Notice how the dialogue moves between imperfective forms for ongoing work, рабо́таю, бу́ду рабо́тать, and perfective forms for single completed results, купи́л, сде́лаю, ко́нчил.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Talking about shared projects with neighbors

Small shared undertakings between neighbors, such as a garden, a stairwell cleanup, or a group gift, are common in many Russian-speaking communities, though the exact form and frequency vary widely by building, neighborhood, generation, and country. Reporting progress on a shared task, and being asked directly whether something is finished, is a normal, friendly part of this kind of cooperation rather than a sign of impatience.

Language Note — Aspect carries information, not just grammar

Choosing between я де́лал and я сде́лал is not a stylistic choice. It tells the listener whether you are describing an activity or reporting a finished result. Native speakers rely on this contrast constantly, and a mismatched aspect can genuinely confuse whether something is done.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Using the imperfective for a single completed result

Error: Writing я де́лал прое́кт to mean the project is finished, when the sentence actually describes only the activity.

Correction: Use the perfective past to report a finished result: я сде́лал прое́кт. Reserve я де́лал for describing the activity itself, without confirming its outcome.

Common Mistake — Treating perfective verbs as if they have a present tense

Error: Using я сде́лаю to describe something happening right now.

Correction: Perfective non-past forms always point to the future, never the present: я сде́лаю э́то за́втра. For a present ongoing activity, use the imperfective: я де́лаю э́то сейча́с.

Common Mistake — Inventing an aspect pair by adding any prefix

Error: Assuming that any prefix added to говори́ть creates its perfective partner.

Correction: The verified perfective partner of говори́ть is the separate verb сказа́ть. Use only the aspect pairs presented and verified in this chapter's vocabulary table.

Здоро́вье и самочу́вствие

Health and well-being · Speak and Write Russian: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common body parts, symptoms, and expressions of well-being.
  2. Use the dative experiencer pattern to say how someone feels, physically and emotionally.
  3. Use мне нужно, мне можно, and мне нельзя to express need, permission, and prohibition.
  4. Use the dative case to say who receives an action, a call, a piece of advice, or a gift.
  5. Offer softened advice using patterns such as вам лучше and вам стоит.
  6. Describe a simple visit to a pharmacy or a doctor's appointment.
  7. Connect a plan from Chapter 12 to a change caused by how someone feels.

This chapter continues Unit III and introduces new grammar. It also continues to recycle leisure, weather, and planning vocabulary from Chapter 12, along with earlier material on daily routine and preferences.

Communication Scenario

Anna and Sergéy had a plan to meet in the park, but Sergéy wakes up on Saturday feeling unwell. He needs to explain how he feels, decide whether he can still meet Anna, and figure out what he needs to do about it, perhaps a trip to the pharmacy, perhaps a call to a doctor.

  1. You will learn the language needed to talk about how someone feels, physically and emotionally.
  2. You will practice saying what someone needs, may, or may not do.
  3. You will practice giving and softening advice, and connecting a change in plans to how someone feels.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Stress in dative pronouns and softened advice Review, no new system

No new pronunciation system is introduced in this chapter. Two patterns deserve special attention here, because this chapter uses the dative case so often.

  • The dative pronouns мне, тебе́, and вам are short and unstressed in normal speech when they open a sentence, as in мне пло́хо, but the stressed content word that follows still carries the main pitch movement.
  • Continue reducing unstressed о and а, as in голова́ and больно́й.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Dative Experiencer New

Use the dative case of the person, with no verb needed, plus a short adverb or a body part construction, to describe how someone feels, physically or emotionally.

Building Block
Dative pronoun/noun + adverb or verb of feeling
The person who experiences the feeling appears in the dative case, not the nominative. This is the same dative experiencer pattern already familiar from мне хо́лодно and мне жа́рко.
Мне пло́хо.
I feel bad. (literally, to me, badly)
У меня́ боли́т голова́.
My head hurts. (literally, at me, the head is hurting)
  • Серге́ю пло́хо сего́дня.Sergéy feels bad today.dative experiencer, third-person dative noun
  • У меня́ боли́т го́рло.My throat hurts.у + genitive experiencer, боле́ть with body part as subject
  • Как ты себя́ чу́вствуешь?How do you feel?чу́вствовать себя́, reflexive verb of feeling, recycled from Chapter 7

Боле́ть, to hurt, agrees with the body part that hurts, not with the person: голова́ боли́т, the head hurts; но́ги боля́т, the legs hurt. The person who feels the pain appears with у plus the genitive, as already familiar from Chapter 4.

мне ну́жно, мо́жно, нельзя́ New

Use мне ну́жно plus an infinitive to say what someone needs to do. Use мо́жно and нельзя́ to say what is allowed or not allowed, with or without a dative person.

Building Block
Dative pronoun/noun (optional) + ну́жно/мо́жно/нельзя́ + infinitive
Ну́жно, мо́жно, and нельзя́ do not change form for person or number. Only the dative pronoun or noun before them changes.
Мне ну́жно отдыха́ть.
I need to rest.
Тебе́ нельзя́ игра́ть в футбо́л сего́дня.
You must not play soccer today.
  • Мне ну́жно пойти́ в апте́ку.I need to go to the pharmacy.мне ну́жно + infinitive, need
  • Мо́жно откры́ть окно́?May I open the window?мо́жно + infinitive, permission, no dative person stated
  • Вам нельзя́ рабо́тать с высо́кой температу́рой.You should not work with a high fever.вам нельзя́ + infinitive, prohibition, formal/plural вам

The Dative Recipient New

Use the dative case to say who receives an action, such as a phone call, a piece of advice, a gift, or an explanation.

  • Я звоню́ врачу́.I am calling the doctor.dative recipient, звони́ть + dative
  • Врач даёт мне реце́пт.The doctor gives me a prescription.dative recipient, дава́ть + dative
  • Она́ говори́т мне, что ну́жно де́лать.She tells me what I need to do.dative recipient, говори́ть + dative
Language Focus — Two functions of the dative case

You now know the dative case in two connected functions, the experiencer who feels something and the recipient who receives something. In both functions, the dative answers the question кому́, to whom or for whom, and the case ending on the noun works the same way in both.

Softened Advice New

Use вам лу́чше or вам сто́ит plus an infinitive to offer advice more gently than a direct command or a flat statement of necessity.

  • Вам лу́чше пойти́ к врачу́.You had better go to the doctor.dative + лу́чше + infinitive, softened advice
  • Тебе́ сто́ит отдохну́ть сего́дня.It is worth it for you to rest today.dative + сто́ит + infinitive, softened advice

Compare тебе́ ну́жно отдыха́ть, a statement of need, with тебе́ сто́ит отдохну́ть, softer advice that leaves more room for the listener's own decision. This chapter activates ну́жно, мо́жно, нельзя́, лу́чше, and сто́ит as productive dative-based predicates; a fuller conditional and comparative system is not active until later in the volume.

Model Dialogue: Что у тебя́ боли́т?

Context: Anna calls Sergéy on Saturday morning after he does not arrive at the park.

А́нна: Приве́т, Серге́й! Ты где? Уже́ два часа́. Hi, Sergéy! Where are you? It's already two o'clock.
Серге́й: Прости́, А́нна. Мне сего́дня о́чень пло́хо. У меня́ боли́т голова́ и боли́т го́рло. Sorry, Anna. I feel very bad today. My head hurts and my throat hurts.
А́нна: Ой, как жаль! У тебя́ есть температу́ра? Oh, that's a shame! Do you have a fever?
Серге́й: Да, ка́жется, есть. Я, наве́рное, бу́ду лежа́ть до́ма весь день. Yes, it seems so. I'll probably just lie down at home all day.
А́нна: Тебе́ ну́жно пойти́ в апте́ку и купи́ть лека́рство. И тебе́ лу́чше позвони́ть врачу́, е́сли температу́ра высо́кая. You need to go to the pharmacy and buy medicine. And you had better call the doctor, if the fever is high.
Серге́й: Хорошо́, спаси́бо. А тебе́ мо́жно прийти́ ко мне за́втра, е́сли мне бу́дет лу́чше? Okay, thank you. And may you come to me tomorrow, if I feel better?
А́нна: Коне́чно! Но сего́дня тебе́ нельзя́ ду́мать о пла́нах, тебе́ ну́жно то́лько отдыха́ть. Of course! But today you must not think about plans, you only need to rest.

Notice how the dialogue moves between the dative experiencer, мне пло́хо and у меня́ боли́т, the dative-based predicates, ну́жно, мо́жно, and нельзя́, and softened advice, тебе́ лу́чше, all within a natural conversation about feeling unwell.

Speaking Practice

Управля́емая пра́ктика — Controlled practice: symptoms and dative experiencer

With a partner, take turns naming a body part and producing a sentence with боле́ть, such as у меня́ боли́т рука́, and a sentence with the dative experiencer, such as мне пло́хо or мне уста́ло.

Коммуникати́вное зада́ние — Communicative task: giving advice

With a partner, one person describes how they feel today, and the other person responds with at least one мне ну́жно, мо́жно, or нельзя́ sentence, plus one softened advice sentence using лу́чше or сто́ит.

Самостоя́тельная пра́ктика — Solo alternative

Record yourself describing how you feel today, including at least one dative experiencer sentence, one мне ну́жно/мо́жно/нельзя́ sentence, and one piece of softened advice you would give a friend who feels the same way.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Pharmacies as a first stop

In many Russian-speaking communities, a trip to the pharmacy is often the first response to minor symptoms, with pharmacists able to give some guidance before a formal doctor's visit, but pharmacy practices, prescription requirements, and appointment systems vary by country, region, and healthcare system. Treat the вра́ч and апте́ка expressions in this chapter as a useful starting point, not a description of every healthcare system.

Regional Variation — Softened advice in different settings

How directly people offer health advice can vary by relationship, age, and setting. A close friend may use тебе́ ну́жно quite directly, while advice to a stranger, a colleague, or an older person often leans on softer patterns such as вам, наве́рное, сто́ит. Adjust your directness to the relationship, not only to the grammar.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Making боле́ть agree with the person

Error: Saying Я боли́т голова́ instead of У меня́ боли́т голова́.

Correction: Боле́ть agrees with the body part, голова́ боли́т, the head hurts, not with the person who feels it. The person appears with у plus the genitive: у меня́ боли́т голова́, not я боли́т голова́.

Common Mistake — Using the nominative instead of the dative person

Error: Saying Я ну́жно отдыха́ть instead of Мне ну́жно отдыха́ть.

Correction: Ну́жно, мо́жно, and нельзя́ take a dative person, not a nominative subject: мне ну́жно, not я ну́жно, just as with мне хо́лодно and мне жа́рко.

Common Mistake — Treating нельзя́ as a simple “no”

Error: Using нельзя́ for every kind of refusal or disagreement, including personal opinions.

Correction: Нельзя́ specifically expresses that something is not allowed or not advisable, usually for a practical or health-related reason: тебе́ нельзя́ рабо́тать с высо́кой температу́рой. For a personal opinion or a simple disagreement, use other expressions instead.

3

Отку́да вы?

Origin and Languages · Speak and Write Russian: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Say where you are from using the formulaic pattern из + [country or city].
  2. Ask and answer Отку́да ты? and Отку́да вы?.
  3. Say where you live using the supported pattern жить в/на + [place].
  4. Use the present tense of жить and говори́ть for я, ты, он/она́, мы, вы, они́.
  5. Name several countries, cities, and languages, and state which languages you speak.
  6. Distinguish nationality, language, and country of residence in simple statements.
  7. Exchange basic contact information, such as a phone number or an email address, in Russian.

Communication Scenario

Anna's class has a new video call scheduled with a partner class studying English in Kazan. Before the two groups can talk about anything else, each learner needs to be able to say where they are from, where they live now, and which languages they speak. Sonya, the student who arrived at the end of Chapter 2, turns out to be from Kazan herself, and the class uses her as a first example before the call begins.

  1. You will learn the fixed pattern Я из... for saying where you are from, without yet studying the full genitive case.
  2. You will begin using real present-tense verb endings for the first time, moving beyond the zero copula patterns from Chapters 1 and 2.

Pronunciation: Vowel Reduction in Unstressed Position

You have already learned to mark the stressed syllable in a Russian word. This chapter introduces the first rule that follows directly from that stress: unstressed о and unstressed а do not keep their full "clear" sound. This is a receptive awareness point in this chapter; you are not yet asked to produce every reduction rule independently.

Pronunciation Focus — Unstressed о and а

An unstressed о is pronounced closer to a short "a" sound, and an unstressed о that is two or more syllables before the stress, or any unstressed а, becomes even weaker, closer to a short neutral vowel. Compare the two syllables in Москва́, where the first, unstressed о is short and weak, and the second, stressed а́ is full and clear.

  • Say Москва́ slowly, then at a natural pace, listening for the difference between the two vowels.
  • Say Кана́да the same way. Note that the first and third а are both unstressed and weak, while the second, stressed а́ stands out clearly.
  • This rule will be developed further, with fuller production practice, later in the volume.

Language Focus: Where Are You From?

Purpose: state origin, residence, and languages spoken, using present-tense verbs for the first time and the formulaic pattern из + [place].

3.1 Я из... — a formulaic pattern for origin

Language Focus

To say where you are from, use Я из followed by the country or city name. At this stage, learn Я из Росси́и, Я из Кана́ды, and similar phrases as fixed expressions, matched to the specific place names in this chapter's vocabulary. The change you see at the end of the place name, such as Росси́я becoming Росси́и, belongs to the genitive case, which is not taught as a full system until Chapter 4. For now, treat each phrase as a unit to memorize correctly, not as a rule to apply freely to new words.

  • Отку́да ты? — Я из Кана́ды. Where are you from? — I am from Canada.
  • Отку́да вы? — Я из Каза́ни, а он из Москвы́. Where are you from? — I am from Kazan, and he is from Moscow.

3.2 Present tense: жить and говори́ть

Building Block — Your first full present-tense verb endings
я живу́ · ты живёшь · он/она́ живёт · мы живём · вы живёте · они́ живу́т
Chapters 1 and 2 used фиксед expressions such as Меня́ зову́т... without explaining verb endings. This chapter introduces real present-tense conjugation for the first time, using two common verbs. Notice that the ending changes to match the subject, and that the stress moves onto the ending in every form of жить.
я говорю́ · ты говори́шь · он/она́ говори́т · мы говори́м · вы говори́те · они́ говоря́т
I speak · you speak · he/she speaks · we speak · you (plural/formal) speak · they speak
  • Я живу́ в Москве́. I live in Moscow. жить, я-form: живу́
  • Ты говори́шь по-англи́йски? Do you speak English? говори́ть, ты-form: говори́шь
  • Мы понима́ем ру́сский язы́к, но говори́м ме́дленно. We understand Russian, but we speak slowly.

Only the present-tense forms of жить, говори́ть, понима́ть, and изуча́ть shown or directly modeled on this pattern are productive in this chapter. Other verbs remain outside the chapter's scope.

3.3 Supported в/на + place

Language Focus

To say where someone lives, use жить в or жить на followed by the place. As with the genitive pattern above, the ending on the place name changes; this belongs to the prepositional case, which is introduced as a full system in Chapter 6. For now, learn the phrases below as fixed combinations tied to this chapter's vocabulary.

3.4 Languages: по-ру́сски and related forms

Language Focus

To say that someone speaks a language, use говори́ть по- plus the language, as in говори́ть по-ру́сски. This по-...-и pattern is a fixed adverbial form and is learned as a formulaic unit for each language in this chapter's vocabulary. To name the language itself as a noun, rather than describe how someone speaks it, use the full phrase, such as ру́сский язы́к.

Model Dialogue: Отку́да ты?

Context: Before the video call, Anna and Sonya introduce themselves properly and talk about where they are from.

А́нна: Со́ня, отку́да ты? Sonya, where are you from?
Со́ня: Я из Каза́ни. А ты отку́да? I am from Kazan. And where are you from?
А́нна: Я из Кана́ды, но сейча́с я живу́ в Москве́. I am from Canada, but now I live in Moscow.
Со́ня: Как интере́сно! Ты говори́шь по-ру́сски о́чень хорошо́. How interesting! You speak Russian very well.
А́нна: Спаси́бо. Я изуча́ю ру́сский язы́к, а та́кже понима́ю немно́го по-испа́нски. Thank you. I am studying Russian, and I also understand a little Spanish.

The teacher joins the call for a moment to check that everyone is ready.

Учи́тель: Со́ня, дай мне, пожа́луйста, твой но́мер телефо́на. Sonya, give me your phone number, please.
Со́ня: Коне́чно. А вот мой электро́нный а́дрес: sonya, соба́ка, mail, то́чка, ru. Of course. And here is my email address: sonya, at, mail, dot, ru.

Notice that сейча́с я живу́ в Москве́ uses the same word order pattern you have already seen: time expression, subject, verb, place. Word order stays consistent even as new verb endings appear.

Speaking Practice

Управля́емая пра́ктика — Controlled practice: origin and residence

Using the formulaic phrases from Language Focus 3.1 and 3.3, say where three different people are from and where each one lives now, choosing only from this chapter's countries and cities.

Коммуникати́вное зада́ние — Communicative task: Отку́да ты?

With a partner, ask and answer Отку́да ты?, Где ты живёшь?, and Ты говори́шь по-...? for two languages from this chapter's vocabulary. If your real origin, residence, or languages fall outside the chapter's vocabulary, choose a place and language from the chapter and imagine the answer.

Самостоя́тельная пра́ктика — Solo alternative

Record yourself answering Отку́да ты?, Где ты живёшь?, and Каки́е языки́ ты понима́ешь? in three or four full sentences. Listen back and check your stress, your verb endings, and your formulaic phrases.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Origin, language, and identity are not the same

Saying where someone is from, where they live now, and which languages they speak describes three separate facts, not one identity label. A person can be from one country, live in another, and speak several languages at different levels, exactly as Maria and Pyotr do in this chapter's reading passage. Keep these three pieces of information distinct as you talk about yourself and others.

Regional Variation — Russian speakers live in many countries

People who speak Russian live in many countries and communities, not only in Russia. This chapter's examples include speakers living in Kazan, Moscow, Toronto, and London, and later volumes will introduce Russian-speaking communities in other regions as well. Speaking Russian does not by itself indicate a person's citizenship, ethnicity, or political views.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Inventing new из and в endings

Error: Guessing a new ending for a place name that was not modeled in this chapter, such as changing an untaught country name by analogy with Росси́я becoming Росси́и.

Correction: Use only the exact из-phrases and в-phrases listed in Language Focus 3.1 and 3.3. Wait for Chapters 4 and 6 to apply these endings freely to new nouns.

Common Mistake — Confusing a language noun with the по- adverbial form

Error: Saying Я говорю́ ру́сский язы́к instead of Я говорю́ по-ру́сски.

Correction: Use the noun phrase ру́сский язы́к when naming the language itself, such as after изуча́ть, and use the по-... form after говори́ть, as in говори́ть по-ру́сски.

Common Mistake — Leaving out the verb ending change

Error: Using the same verb form for every subject, such as saying ты живу́ instead of ты живёшь.

Correction: Match the verb ending to its subject pronoun, using the full conjugation table in Language Focus 3.2 as a reference.

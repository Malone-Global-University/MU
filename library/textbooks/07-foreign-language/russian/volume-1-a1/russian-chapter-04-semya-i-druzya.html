Семья́ и друзья́

Family and Friends · Speak and Write Russian: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name immediate family members and describe basic friendships.
  2. Say what someone has or does not have, using у меня́ есть and у меня́ нет.
  3. Ask and answer У тебя́ есть...? and У вас есть...? about family, friends, and pets.
  4. Use possessive words мой, твой, его́, её, наш, ваш, их to identify whose family member or object something is.
  5. Ask and state age using the dative formula Мне... год/го́да/лет.
  6. Make adjectives agree with the nouns they describe, in the nominative case, for common descriptive words.
  7. Talk about pets using the same possession and description patterns.

Communication Scenario

The video call with the partner class in Kazan went well, and now Anna's classmates are preparing short family photographs to share with their new pen pals. Sonya has brought in a photograph of her own family, and the class uses it, along with Dima's and Maria's stories from earlier chapters, to practice talking about who is in a family, how old people are, and what belongs to whom.

  1. You will learn the fixed pattern у меня́ есть / у меня́ нет for possession and absence, without yet studying the full genitive case as a system.
  2. You will use the dative case for the first time, but only in the fixed age formula Мне... год/го́да/лет, as formulaic language.
  3. You will begin making adjectives agree with nouns in gender and number, for the descriptive words in this chapter.

Pronunciation: Stress in Family Words

Several family words in this chapter carry stress on an unexpected syllable for an English speaker, and one word, ребёнок, contains the letter ё, which is always stressed. This section continues the vowel reduction awareness from Chapter 3 and applies it to new vocabulary.

Pronunciation Focus — Stress patterns in семья́ and сестра́

Notice that the stress in семья́ and сестра́ falls on the final syllable, so the earlier vowels are unstressed and weakened, following the same reduction pattern you practiced with Москва́ in Chapter 3. Compare this with ба́бушка and де́душка, where the stress falls on the first syllable and the later syllables are weak.

  • Say семья́ and сестра́ slowly, then at a natural pace, keeping the final syllable clear and full.
  • Say ба́бушка and де́душка the same way, keeping the first syllable clear and letting the later syllables weaken.
  • Remember that ё, as in ребёнок, is always stressed and is never marked with an additional accent mark.

Language Focus: My Family, My Friends

Purpose: describe family, friends, ages, and possessions, using genitive possession with у, есть/нет, possessive words, dative age formulas, and adjective agreement.

4.1 У меня́ есть... — a formulaic pattern for possession

Language Focus

To say that you have something or someone, use у меня́ есть followed by the person or thing. To say that you do not have it, use у меня́ нет followed by a changed form of the word, such as бра́та instead of брат. This changed form belongs to the genitive case, which is introduced here only as a fixed pattern tied to this chapter's vocabulary, not as a system you apply freely to new words. The full genitive case is developed later in the volume.

Formulaic possession phrases
Personу меня́ есть...у меня́ нет...
брату меня́ есть брату меня́ нет бра́та
сестра́у меня́ есть сестра́у меня́ нет сестры́
соба́кау меня́ есть соба́кау меня́ нет соба́ки
ко́шкау меня́ есть ко́шкау меня́ нет ко́шки
  • У тебя́ есть брат и́ли сестра́? — У меня́ есть сестра́, а бра́та у меня́ нет. Do you have a brother or a sister? — I have a sister, but I do not have a brother.
  • У вас есть соба́ка? — Нет, у нас есть ко́шка. Do you have a dog? — No, we have a cat.

Use у тебя́ есть...? with someone you address as ты, and у вас есть...? with someone you address as Вы or with more than one person, exactly as you learned for ты and вы in Chapters 1 and 2.

4.2 Possessive words: мой, твой, его́, её, наш, ваш, их

Building Block — Possessive agreement with masculine, feminine, and neuter nouns
мой брат · моя́ сестра́ · моё и́мя
Possessive words agree with the noun they describe, not with the owner. Мой, твой, наш, and ваш change form to match a masculine, feminine, or neuter noun, while его́, её, and их stay the same regardless of the noun.
твой брат · твоя́ сестра́ · на́ша ба́бушка · ваш де́душка · его́ жена́ · её муж · их де́ти
your brother · your sister · our grandmother · your grandfather · his wife · her husband · their children
  • Моя́ сестра́ живёт в Москве́. My sister lives in Moscow. моя́ + сестра́ (feminine)
  • Его́ де́ти говоря́т по-англи́йски. His children speak English. его́ does not change for de children
  • Как зову́т ва́шу ба́бушку? — Мою́ ба́бушку зову́т Ната́лья. What is your grandmother's name? — My grandmother's name is Natalya.

Only мой, моя́, моё, твой, твоя́, твоё, наш, на́ша, на́ше, ваш, ва́ша, ва́ше, его́, её, and их are productive in this chapter. Other case forms of these possessives, such as мою́ in the last example above, are recognition-only until case is developed later in the volume.

4.3 Age: Мне... год/го́да/лет

Language Focus

To ask or state age in Russian, use the dative pronoun мне, тебе́, ему́, ей, нам, вам, or им followed by the number and the correct word for "year(s)." This dative pattern is introduced here only as a fixed formula. The full dative case is developed later in the volume.

Choosing год, го́да, or лет
NumberWord for "year(s)"Example
1, 21, 31...годМне два́дцать оди́н год.
2–4, 22–24, 32–34...го́даЕй два́дцать два го́да.
5–20, 25–30, 35...летЕму́ два́дцать пять лет.
  • Ско́лько тебе́ лет? — Мне два́дцать лет. How old are you? — I am twenty years old.
  • Ско́лько лет ва́шей ба́бушке? — Ей се́мьдесят лет. How old is your grandmother? — She is seventy years old.

Only the dative pronouns мне, тебе́, ему́, ей, нам, вам, and их-equivalent им are productive, and only within this fixed age formula. Do not extend the dative case to new functions until it is developed in Chapter 13.

4.4 Adjective agreement in the nominative case

Building Block — Adjective endings for masculine, feminine, and plural nouns
молодо́й брат · молода́я сестра́ · молоды́е роди́тели
A Russian adjective takes an ending that matches the gender and number of the noun it describes. Masculine nouns pair with adjectives ending in -ый or -о́й, feminine nouns pair with adjectives ending in -ая, and plural nouns of any gender pair with adjectives ending in -ые.
до́брый де́душка · до́брая ба́бушка · весёлые де́ти
a kind grandfather · a kind grandmother · cheerful children
  • Мой брат высо́кий и весёлый. My brother is tall and cheerful. высо́кий, весёлый: masculine
  • Моя́ сестра́ высо́кая и краси́вая. My sister is tall and pretty. высо́кая, краси́вая: feminine
  • На́ша ко́шка небольша́я, но о́чень до́брая. Our cat is small, but very kind.

Only nominative singular and plural adjective endings for the adjectives in this chapter's vocabulary are productive. Oblique adjective case forms are recognition-only until Chapter 9 develops them further.

Model Dialogue: У тебя́ есть бра́т и́ли сестра́?

Context: Sonya shows Anna a photograph of her family before class, and they talk about who is in it.

А́нна: Со́ня, кто э́то на фотогра́фии? Sonya, who is this in the photograph?
Со́ня: Э́то моя́ семья́. Вот мой па́па, моя́ ма́ма и мой брат. This is my family. Here is my dad, my mom, and my brother.
А́нна: А у тебя́ есть сестра́? And do you have a sister?
Со́ня: Нет, сестры́ у меня́ нет, то́лько брат. Его́ зову́т Ми́ша, ему́ два́дцать два го́да. No, I do not have a sister, only a brother. His name is Misha, he is twenty-two years old.
А́нна: А кто э́та же́нщина? Она́ о́чень краси́вая. And who is this woman? She is very pretty.
Со́ня: Э́то моя́ ба́бушка. Она́ ста́рая, но о́чень весёлая. This is my grandmother. She is old, but very cheerful.

Anna takes out her phone to show her own photograph.

А́нна: А э́то моя́ соба́ка. Её зову́т Ла́ки. And this is my dog. Her name is Lucky.
Со́ня: Кака́я краси́вая соба́ка! Ско́лько ей лет? What a beautiful dog! How old is she?
А́нна: Ей три го́да. She is three years old.

Notice that сестры́ у меня́ нет places the genitive-form noun before у меня́ нет for emphasis, while у меня́ нет бра́та in Language Focus 4.1 places it after. Both orders are natural; word order for focus is developed further in later chapters.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Talking about family and age

Sharing a family photograph and naming who is in it, as Sonya and Anna do in this chapter, is a common way to get to know someone in many Russian-speaking settings, just as it is in many other communities. Asking someone's age directly, using Ско́лько тебе́ лет?, is generally comfortable among peers and with children, though it becomes a more careful question with people you do not know well or with older adults, where Вы and a more indirect approach are often preferred.

Regional Variation — Families look different across Russian-speaking communities

Family structures, living arrangements, and the roles of grandparents vary widely among Russian-speaking families in Russia, Kazakhstan, the Baltic states, North America, and elsewhere. This chapter's vocabulary describes immediate family members in general terms, and it does not represent any single family structure as the only correct or typical one.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Leaving the adjective unchanged for gender

Error: Saying моя́ сестра́ молодо́й instead of моя́ сестра́ молода́я.

Correction: Match the adjective ending to the gender of the noun it describes, using -ая for feminine nouns like сестра́ and -ый or -о́й for masculine nouns like брат.

Common Mistake — Using есть with нет

Error: Saying у меня́ есть нет бра́та instead of у меня́ нет бра́та.

Correction: Use есть only for possession and нет alone for absence. The two words never appear together in the same phrase.

Common Mistake — Forgetting to change год for the number

Error: Saying мне два́дцать пять год instead of мне два́дцать пять лет.

Correction: Check the age formula table in Language Focus 4.3 and choose год, го́да, or лет according to the last digit of the number, remembering that eleven through fourteen always take лет.

7

Мой обы́чный день

Daily Routine · Speak and Write Russian: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Ask and tell the time using the formulaic pattern Кото́рый час? and в + [hour] часо́в.
  2. Name common daily actions, distinguishing reflexive verbs in -ся/-сь from ordinary verbs.
  3. Name common direct objects of daily actions, using the accusative case for inanimate nouns.
  4. Sequence a description of your day using снача́ла, пото́м, and ве́чером.
  5. Say how often you do something, using frequency adverbs such as обы́чно and иногда́.
  6. Negate routine actions correctly with не.

Communication Scenario

Anna and Nikolai continue their conversation from Chapter 6. A few days after their first meeting, Nikolai asks Anna about her daily schedule, since he wants to know when it might be a good time to visit or invite her for coffee. Anna walks him through a typical weekday, from waking up to going to bed, mentioning what she usually does, what she sometimes skips, and what she never does on a work day.

  1. Every new pattern in this chapter answers one question: when do you do it, what do you do, and how often.
  2. The accusative case appears only for the inanimate objects of the daily-routine verbs introduced in this chapter.
  3. Reflexive verbs appear only in the specific forms listed in the vocabulary table, not as a general rule for translating every English reflexive idea.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — The reflexive ending -ся/-сь

After a consonant, the reflexive ending is spelled -ся and pronounced with a hard, unpalatalized с, close to -sa: занима́ется. After a vowel, the ending is spelled -сь: занима́юсь, ложу́сь. In careful speech, keep the stress on the syllable marked in the vocabulary table rather than shifting it onto -ся itself.

  • Practice the contrast between одева́ю and одева́юсь, noticing that the reflexive ending adds a syllable but does not move the stress.
  • In ложи́ться, remember that the stress falls on the ending itself in every present-tense form: ложу́сь, ложи́шься, ложи́тся.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Accusative Case for Inanimate Objects

Use the accusative case to mark the direct object of an action.

Building Block
[subject] + [verb] + [object in the accusative case]
Masculine and neuter inanimate nouns keep the nominative form in the accusative. Feminine nouns in -а/-я change to -у/-ю.
Я чита́ю кни́гу.
I am reading a book.
  • Она́ чита́ет газе́ту у́тром.She reads the newspaper in the morning.feminine: газе́та → газе́ту
  • Он смо́трит телеви́зор ве́чером.He watches television in the evening.masculine inanimate: no change
  • Мы пи́шем письмо́.We are writing a letter.neuter: no change
Language Focus — Only inanimate objects for now

This chapter activates the accusative case only for inanimate nouns, where masculine and neuter forms match the nominative and feminine nouns in -а/-я shift to -у/-ю. Animate direct objects, such as people or animals, follow a different accusative pattern that is scheduled for a later chapter. Do not extend the accusative to animate nouns yet.

Reflexive Verbs in -ся/-сь

Use reflexive verbs for a specific, memorized set of daily-routine actions.

Building Block
[subject] + [verb stem] + -юсь/-ешься/-ется, etc.
-ся/-сь attaches to the end of the conjugated verb form and does not take a separate object.
Я просыпа́юсь в семь часо́в.
I wake up at seven o'clock.
  • Ты одева́ешься бы́стро.You get dressed quickly.одева́ться, ты-form
  • Он занима́ется ка́ждый день.He studies every day.занима́ться, он-form
  • Мы ложи́мся спать в оди́ннадцать часо́в.We go to bed at eleven o'clock.ложи́ться, мы-form
Grammar Note — Reflexive verbs are not English reflexives

A Russian reflexive verb in -ся/-сь is a single word with its own dictionary entry, not an ordinary verb combined with a separate word for "myself," "yourself," or "himself." Я умыва́юсь does not mean "I wash myself" in the sense of translating each word separately, it simply means "I wash up," and the verb is learned as a whole unit. Do not assume that every English sentence with a reflexive pronoun needs -ся/-сь in Russian, and do not assume that every -ся/-сь verb corresponds to an English reflexive sentence. Learn each reflexive verb in this chapter as its own vocabulary item.

Negating Routine Actions with не

Use не directly before the verb to say that an action does not happen.

Building Block
[subject] + не + [verb]
не comes immediately before the conjugated verb, including reflexive verbs, with no separate helper word.
Я не за́втракаю у́тром.
I do not eat breakfast in the morning.
  • Он не рабо́тает в суббо́ту.He does not work on Saturday.не + рабо́тает
  • Мы никогда́ не смо́трим телеви́зор у́тром.We never watch television in the morning.никогда́ + не + verb, both required together

Frequency and Sequencing

Use frequency adverbs and sequencing words to describe a routine clearly.

  • Я обы́чно встаю́ в семь часо́в.I usually get up at seven o'clock.frequency adverb before the verb
  • Снача́ла я умыва́юсь, а пото́м за́втракаю.First I wash up, and then I eat breakfast.снача́ла...пото́м sequencing
  • Она́ иногда́ чита́ет кни́гу ве́чером, а её брат чита́ет ре́дко.She sometimes reads a book in the evening, but her brother reads rarely.contrastive frequency
Common Mistake — Dropping не with никогда́

Error: Saying Я никогда́ за́втракаю to mean "I never eat breakfast."

Correction: Russian requires both никогда́ and не together: Я никогда́ не за́втракаю. This double marking is required, not optional, and is not a mistake in Russian even though English uses only one negative word.

Model Dialogue: Мой обы́чный день

Context: A few days after Chapter 6, Nikolai asks Anna about her weekday schedule.

Никола́й: А́нна, во ско́лько ты обы́чно встаёшь? Anna, what time do you usually get up?
А́нна: Я просыпа́юсь в семь часо́в, а встаю́ в полови́не восьмо́го. Снача́ла я умыва́юсь и одева́юсь, а пото́м за́втракаю. I wake up at seven o'clock, and I get up at half past seven. First I wash up and get dressed, and then I eat breakfast.
Никола́й: Ты чита́ешь газе́ту у́тром? Do you read the newspaper in the morning?
А́нна: Иногда́ чита́ю, но обы́чно я не чита́ю у́тром, я рабо́таю. А ве́чером я отдыха́ю и смотрю́ телеви́зор. Sometimes I read it, but I usually don't read in the morning, I work. And in the evening I rest and watch television.
Никола́й: А во ско́лько ты ложи́шься спать? And what time do you go to bed?
А́нна: Обы́чно я ложу́сь спать в оди́ннадцать часо́в, но в суббо́ту я никогда́ не ложу́сь ра́но. I usually go to bed at eleven o'clock, but on Saturday I never go to bed early.

Notice that Anna's routine moves through the day with снача́ла and пото́м, and that she uses frequency adverbs such as обы́чно, иногда́, and никогда́ to add detail to each action.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Weekday and weekend rhythms

Many people in Russian-speaking communities distinguish clearly between бу́дни, weekdays with a fixed work or school schedule, and the weekend, when routines such as bedtime and screen time often relax considerably. Asking a friend or new acquaintance about their daily schedule, as Nikolai does in the Model Dialogue, is a natural way to find a convenient time to meet.

Regional Variation — Work and school schedules vary

Typical work hours, school schedules, and meal timing differ across Russian-speaking regions and among Russian-speaking communities outside the Russian Federation. Do not assume that every workday follows the same hours, or that обе́д always falls at the same time; the examples in this chapter describe one common pattern, not a universal one.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Adding an extra pronoun with reflexive verbs

Error: Saying Я умыва́ю себя́ to mean "I wash up."

Correction: Use the reflexive verb умыва́ться on its own: Я умыва́юсь. The -ся ending already carries the reflexive meaning, so no separate word for "myself" is needed.

Common Mistake — Leaving a feminine object in the nominative

Error: Saying я чита́ю кни́га instead of я чита́ю кни́гу.

Correction: A feminine noun in -а/-я that is the direct object of a verb must change to -у/-ю. Review the accusative table above before writing or speaking.

Common Mistake — Placing не after the verb

Error: Saying я рабо́таю не instead of я не рабо́таю.

Correction: не always comes directly before the word it negates, in this chapter always the conjugated verb: я не рабо́таю.

Answer Key

Listening — Comprehension

  1. Dima first washes up, then gets dressed, then eats breakfast.
  2. Dima always drinks coffee in the morning; he never reads in the morning.
  3. Dima gets up at a quarter past six and goes to bed at ten o'clock.

Model Dialogue — Comprehension

  1. Anna wakes up at seven o'clock and gets up at half past seven.
  2. Anna says she never goes to bed early on Saturday.

Writing

  1. 1. Я просыпа́юсь в семь часо́в. 2. Ты чита́ешь кни́гу ве́чером? 3. Он не рабо́тает в воскресе́нье. 4. Мы ложи́мся спать в де́сять часо́в. 5. Она́ смо́трит телеви́зор иногда́.
  2. Sample answer: Я обы́чно встаю́ в семь часо́в. Снача́ла я умыва́юсь и одева́юсь, а пото́м за́втракаю. Я иногда́ чита́ю кни́гу, но никогда́ не чита́ю газе́ту у́тром. Other combinations using this chapter's vocabulary and patterns are also correct.
  3. одева́ться → я одева́юсь, ты одева́ешься; ложи́ться → я ложу́сь, ты ложи́шься.

Reading

  1. Lena first washes up and gets dressed, then eats breakfast and drinks coffee, then reads a book for fifteen minutes.
  2. Lena always reads a book in the morning; she rarely reads the newspaper.
  3. On weekdays Lena does not usually watch television, but on Saturday she watches television all evening and never goes to bed early.

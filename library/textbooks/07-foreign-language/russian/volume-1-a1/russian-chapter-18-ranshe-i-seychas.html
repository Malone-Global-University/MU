Ра́ньше и сейча́с

Then and now · Speak and Write Russian: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use the imperfective past tense to describe habits, routines, and ongoing situations that existed over a period of time in the past.
  2. Distinguish an imperfective habitual description, such as what someone used to do, from a perfective completed event, such as what someone did on one occasion.
  3. Describe change over time using станови́ться and стать, choosing the correct aspect according to whether a change is in progress or has occurred as a completed shift.
  4. Compare an earlier period of time with the present, using comparative forms already active from earlier chapters together with time expressions such as ра́ньше and сейча́с.
  5. Ask and answer basic questions about how someone's life, a place, or a habit used to be different.
  6. Narrate a short connected account comparing then and now, combining imperfective habitual description with the past-tense and aspect skills from Chapters 16 and 17.

This chapter continues Unit IV. It introduces habitual past narration with imperfective aspect, change with станови́ться/стать, and comparison of past and present as productive systems for the first time. All vocabulary, cases, and other grammar from Chapters 1 through 17 remain active and continue to appear throughout this chapter.

Communication Scenario

Дми́трий visits his grandmother, Ба́бушка Ве́ра, and asks her what life was like when she was young, in the town where she grew up. She describes her childhood routines, and how the town has changed since then. Later, О́льга writes a short message to an old classmate, comparing what their school friend group used to do together with what has changed now that everyone has moved away.

  1. You will learn how to describe habitual actions and ongoing situations from the past using imperfective verbs.
  2. You will practice describing change over time with станови́ться and стать.
  3. You will practice comparing an earlier period with the present, using ра́ньше, сейча́с, and comparative forms.

Станови́ться and стать are new verbs of change, active for the first time in this chapter. Ме́ньше, бо́льше, лу́чше, and ху́же are irregular comparative forms of already familiar adjectives; their attributive comparative forms remain receptive for now and will be developed in later volumes.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Stress in стать and станови́ться

Стать is a short, one-syllable perfective infinitive, and its past-tense forms keep stress on the root throughout: стал, ста́ла, ста́ли, with a shift to the ending only in the feminine form, following the same mobile pattern you practiced with был/была́ in Chapter 16. Станови́ться is longer and keeps stress on the syllable -ви́- in the infinitive and in most present-tense forms: станови́тся, станови́мся.

  • стал, ста́ла, ста́ли — stress moves to the ending only in the feminine.
  • станови́тся, станови́мся — stress stays on -ви́- in the present tense.
  • ра́ньше, тепе́рь — both words carry fixed stress and should be learned together with their stress marks.

Continue reducing unstressed о and а in longer words such as станови́ться, and continue applying the hard-soft consonant contrasts you have practiced since Chapter 2.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Imperfective Past for Habits and Ongoing Situations New

In Chapters 16 and 17 you used the past tense to move a story forward through completed events. This chapter develops a different use of the same imperfective forms you already know, describing what someone used to do regularly, or what a situation used to be like over a period of time, rather than a single completed event.

Building Block — Habitual description versus completed event
Я ка́ждый день игра́л(а) во дворе́. — imperfective, habitual
describes a repeated action across an extended period, with no single completed result in focus
В де́тстве я ча́сто игра́л(а) во дворе́ с друзья́ми.
As a child I often played in the yard with friends. (habit, imperfective)
Одна́жды я упа́л(а) и слома́л(а) ру́ку.
One time I fell and broke my arm. (single completed event, perfective)
  • Ра́ньше мы жи́ли в ма́леньком го́роде.We used to live in a small town.жить, imperfective, ongoing situation over a past period
  • В ю́ности она́ ка́ждое ле́то е́здила к ба́бушке.In her youth she used to go to her grandmother's every summer.е́здила, imperfective, repeated habitual trips, not one journey

This chapter treats the imperfective past as a productive form for habitual and ongoing description. The imperfective/perfective past contrast itself was already introduced in Chapter 16; this chapter develops one specific communicative use of that contrast.

Change with Станови́ться and Стать New

Станови́ться/стать describes becoming something or someone, or a quality developing over time. Стать is perfective and reports a completed change; станови́ться is imperfective and describes a change in progress or a repeated pattern of change. Both verbs take the instrumental case for the noun or adjective that follows.

  • Он стал врачо́м два го́да наза́д.He became a doctor two years ago.стать, perfective, completed change; instrumental врачо́м
  • Го́род постепе́нно стано́вится бо́льше.The city is gradually becoming bigger.станови́ться, imperfective, change in progress; instrumental бо́льше
  • С года́ми она́ ста́ла бо́лее споко́йной.Over the years she became calmer.стать, perfective, completed change; instrumental споко́йной
Language Focus — Стать is not the same as быть

Стать describes a shift from one state to another, while был/была́/бы́ли, active since Chapter 16, simply reports that something was the case in the past without focusing on the change itself. Compare: Он был учи́телем, meaning he was a teacher at some point, with Он стал учи́телем, meaning he became a teacher, marking the moment or process of change.

Comparing Then and Now New

Comparison from Chapter 9 continues here, now applied to time rather than to objects in a shop. Use ра́ньше and сейча́с to frame two points in time, and use comparative forms, including irregular comparatives such as лу́чше, ху́же, бо́льше, and ме́ньше, to state the difference between them.

  • Ра́ньше в го́роде бы́ло ме́ньше маши́н, а сейча́с их намно́го бо́льше.Before, there were fewer cars in the city, and now there are far more.ме́ньше, бо́льше; irregular comparatives active in predicative use
  • По сравне́нию с про́шлым, жизнь ста́ла бы́стрее.In comparison with the past, life has become faster.по сравне́нию с + instrumental; стать + instrumental comparative

Talking About Memory New

Вспомина́ть and по́мнить allow learners to frame a habitual description as a memory, connecting this chapter's grammar to natural storytelling about the past.

  • Я хорошо́ по́мню, как мы игра́ли во дворе́ до тёмноты́.I remember well how we used to play in the yard until dark.по́мнить + habitual imperfective clause
  • Ба́бушка ча́сто вспомина́ет своё де́тство.Grandmother often recalls her childhood.вспомина́ть, imperfective, habitual recollection

Model Dialogue: Ра́ньше и сейча́с

Context: Дми́трий visits his grandmother, Ба́бушка Ве́ра, and asks her what life was like when she was young in her home town.

Дми́трий: Ба́бушка, расскажи́, как ты жила́, когда́ была́ ма́ленькой? Grandma, tell me, how did you live when you were little?
Ба́бушка Ве́ра: Ра́ньше мы жи́ли в ма́леньком го́роде, недалеко́ от реки́. Ка́ждое ле́то я е́здила к тёте в дере́вню и помога́ла ей в саду́. Before, we lived in a small town, not far from a river. Every summer I used to go to my aunt's in the village and help her in the garden.
Дми́трий: А чем вы обы́чно занима́лись ве́чером, без телефо́нов и интерне́та? And what did you usually do in the evening, without phones and the internet?
Ба́бушка Ве́ра: Мы игра́ли во дворе́ до тёмноты́, чита́ли кни́ги и́ли слу́шали ра́дио. Жизнь тогда́ была́ ме́дленнее, но, по-мо́ему, споко́йнее. We used to play in the yard until dark, read books, or listen to the radio. Life back then was slower, but in my opinion calmer.
Дми́трий: А го́род си́льно измени́лся с тех пор? And has the town changed a lot since then?
Ба́бушка Ве́ра: О́чень! Он стал намно́го бо́льше, появи́лись но́вые райо́ны, и маши́н тепе́рь гора́здо бо́льше, чем ра́ньше. Very much! It became much bigger, new districts appeared, and now there are far more cars than before.
Дми́трий: Тебе́ нра́вится, каки́м стал го́род, и́ли ты скуча́ешь по ста́рым времена́м? Do you like what the town has become, or do you miss the old times?
Ба́бушка Ве́ра: И то, и друго́е. Го́род ста́л удо́бнее, но иногда́ я вспомина́ю, как всё бы́ло ти́ше. Both. The town became more convenient, but sometimes I recall how everything used to be quieter.

Notice how habitual imperfective verbs such as жи́ли, е́здила, and игра́ли describe ongoing past routines, while стал and появи́лись mark completed changes.

Listening script
Когда́ я учи́лся в шко́ле, я ка́ждый день ходи́л туда́ пешко́м вме́сте с бра́том. У нас не́ было телефо́нов, и мы про́сто игра́ли во дворе́ и разгова́ривали с сосе́дями. Тепе́рь всё по-друго́му: де́ти обы́чно е́здят в шко́лу на маши́не и́ли на авто́бусе, а вечера́ми чаще сидя́т до́ма с телефо́ном. Я не могу́ сказа́ть, что ра́ньше бы́ло лу́чше, но жизнь то́чно ста́ла бы́стрее, и, по-мо́ему, немно́го ме́нее споко́йной.

Speaking Practice

With a partner, take turns describing three things you used to do as a child, using the imperfective past for each. Include at least one frequency word, such as ча́сто, обы́чно, or ка́ждый день.

Коммуникати́вное зада́ние — Communicative task: How has it changed?

With a partner, role-play a conversation like the Model Dialogue. Ask each other Как ра́ньше бы́ло? and Что стало по-друго́му?, and include at least one sentence with станови́ться or стать.

Самостоя́тельная пра́ктика — Solo alternative

Record yourself comparing a place, a habit, or a person then and now, using at least three imperfective habitual verbs and one sentence with станови́ться or стать.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Talking with older relatives about the past

Asking an older relative or family friend how life used to be is a common and welcome way to practice this chapter's grammar in many Russian-speaking families, and it can prompt genuinely interesting conversation regardless of background. One person's memory of childhood, however, reflects one time, one place, and one set of personal circumstances. Do not treat a single family memory, including any example in this chapter, as evidence about how an entire country, generation, or region lived.

Language Note — Aspect carries the meaning, not just the topic

It can be tempting to treat "used to" as an automatic signal to reach for a special past tense form in Russian. Russian marks habitual and ongoing meaning through the imperfective aspect, using the same past-tense forms that also report single ongoing actions, such as the process verbs from Chapter 16. The habitual meaning comes from context, frequency words, and the imperfective choice itself, not from a separate grammatical tense dedicated only to habits.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using a perfective verb for a repeated habit

Error: Saying Ка́ждое ле́то я пое́хал к ба́бушке to describe a childhood summer habit.

Correction: Use the imperfective for a repeated action across many summers: Ка́ждое ле́то я е́здил(а) к ба́бушке. Save the perfective пое́хал for a single, specific departure, as in В про́шлом ию́не я пое́хал(а) к ба́бушке.

Common Mistake — Nominative instead of instrumental after станови́ться/стать

Error: Saying Он стал врач instead of Он стал врачо́м.

Correction: Станови́ться and стать require the instrumental case for the following noun or adjective, the same case pattern you learned for roles in Chapter 14: Он стал врачо́м, Она́ ста́ла бо́лее споко́йной.

Common Mistake — Confusing быть and стать

Error: Saying Он был врачо́м два го́да наза́д when the intended meaning is that he became a doctor two years ago and still is one.

Correction: Use стать to mark the moment or process of becoming something: Он стал врачо́м два го́да наза́д. Use был only to report that something was the case at a past point, without focusing on the change itself.

Answer Key

Listening — Comprehension

  1. The speaker used to walk to school every day together with his brother.
  2. According to the speaker, children now usually travel to school by car or bus and more often spend evenings at home with a phone, rather than playing outside or talking with neighbors.
  3. The speaker does not say life used to be better. He says life has definitely become faster, and in his opinion a little less calm.

Model Dialogue — Comprehension

  1. As a child, Ба́бушка Ве́ра used to play in the yard until dark, read books, or listen to the radio.
  2. She says the town became much bigger, new districts appeared, and there are now far more cars than before.

Writing

  1. 1. игра́л. 2. стал. 3. ме́ньше. 4. стано́вится. 5. вспомина́ем.
  2. Sample answer: В де́тстве я ка́ждый день игра́л(а) на у́лице с сосе́дскими детьми́. У нас не́ было телефо́нов, и мы про́сто разгова́ривали и гуля́ли до ве́чера. Со вре́менем всё ста́ло по-друго́му: сейча́с у ка́ждого есть телефо́н, и мы гора́здо ре́же встреча́емся про́сто так. Ра́ньше вре́мени бы́ло бо́льше, а сейча́с его́ намно́го ме́ньше. Reading

  1. The writer and her classmates used to sit at a café on the corner every day after school and talk for a long time about everything.
  2. Everyone now lives in a different town and has work and a family, so they meet much less often; the form of the friendship changed, not its size.
  3. No, the writer does not think the friendship has become smaller. She uses the phrase не ста́ла ме́ньше, saying that only its form changed.

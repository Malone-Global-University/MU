Привет! Первые встречи

First Encounters · Speak and Write Russian: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize and begin to write a first set of Cyrillic letters, and understand that Russian stress is fixed to a word rather than predictable from spelling.
  2. Greet someone and say goodbye using level-appropriate expressions.
  3. Introduce yourself using the formula Меня зовут..., and ask someone else's name.
  4. Ask and answer a small set of controlled questions using only formulaic language.
  5. Distinguish an informal ты greeting from a polite Вы greeting in fixed, recognizable contexts.
  6. Count from 0 to 10 in Russian.

Communication Scenario

Anna is starting a beginner Russian class in the evening. On the first night, the teacher greets everyone at the door, and the students greet each other before the lesson begins. Nobody in the room has met before, so the entire first evening is built from the same small handful of expressions, said again and again, until they start to feel automatic. This chapter follows that first evening, greeting by greeting, name by name.

  1. You will meet the same expressions in several short exchanges, so that repetition, not memorization, builds your first working Russian.
  2. Every word introduced in this chapter is marked for stress, since stress in Russian must be learned with each word from the very beginning.

Pronunciation: The Cyrillic Alphabet and Word Stress

Russian is written with the Cyrillic alphabet, a complete writing system in its own right, not a code layered over the Latin alphabet. This chapter introduces a first working set of letters through the words above, rather than the full thirty three letter alphabet at once. The remaining letters follow in Chapter 2.

Pronunciation Focus — Russian word stress

Every Russian word of more than one syllable has one stressed syllable, and that syllable is a fact about the word itself, not something you can predict from its spelling. This textbook marks stress with an acute accent mark, as in приве́т, so that you can hear and say each new word correctly from the start.

  • Say each vocabulary word aloud, leaning slightly on the marked syllable.
  • Notice that an unstressed о often sounds closer to an a sound, as in хорошо́. This pattern will be explained fully once more vocabulary is available.
  • The stress mark itself never appears in ordinary Russian text outside of dictionaries and language textbooks, so do not write it in your own free writing later in the course.

Language Focus: Introducing Yourself

Purpose: greet someone, introduce yourself with a fixed formula, ask a name, and choose between an informal and a polite greeting in clearly marked situations.

1.1 The formula Меня зовут...

Language Focus

The expression меня́ зову́т... means "my name is..." but its word for word structure is different from English, closer to "me they call...". At this stage, treat the whole phrase as one fixed unit. You do not yet need to understand every grammatical piece inside it, since that analysis is scheduled for a later volume.

To ask someone's name, Russian also uses two different formulas, one polite and one informal, matching the ты/Вы distinction introduced below.

  • Меня́ зову́т А́нна. My name is Anna.
  • Как вас зову́т? — Меня́ зову́т Ми́ша. What is your name? — My name is Misha.

1.2 Choosing ты or Вы

Language Focus

Russian distinguishes an informal ты from a polite singular Вы, and a separate plural вы used for more than one person. In this chapter, you only need to recognize the difference in two fixed contexts, a classmate greeting another classmate informally, and a teacher or stranger being greeted politely. The full pronoun system is developed gradually across later chapters.

  • Приве́т! Как тебя́ зову́т? Hi! What is your name? (informal, classmate to classmate)
  • Здра́вствуйте! Как вас зову́т? Hello! What is your name? (polite, to a teacher or a stranger)

1.3 Zero copula: sentences without "is"

Building Block — No word for "is" in the present tense
Это [noun].
Russian does not use a present tense form of "to be" in simple identification sentences. The word э́то, meaning "this is" or "that is," stands directly before the noun, with no separate verb needed.
Э́то А́нна.
This is Anna.

You will meet this pattern again in Chapter 2, where it expands to classroom objects and people.

Model Dialogue: Знакомство

Context: The teacher greets a new student at the door on the first evening of class.

Учи́тель: Здра́вствуйте! Как вас зову́т? Hello! What is your name?
А́нна: Меня́ зову́т А́нна. My name is Anna.
Учи́тель: О́чень прия́тно, А́нна. Проходи́те, пожа́луйста. Nice to meet you, Anna. Please, come in.

Anna sits down next to another student, who turns to greet her informally.

Ми́ша: Приве́т! Как тебя́ зову́т? Hi! What is your name?
А́нна: Приве́т! Меня́ зову́т А́нна. А тебя́? Hi! My name is Anna. And you?
Ми́ша: Меня́ зову́т Ми́ша. О́чень прия́тно. My name is Misha. Nice to meet you.

Notice that Anna greets the teacher with Здра́вствуйте and greets Misha with Приве́т, choosing her greeting based on who she is speaking to, not on the situation alone.

Listening Practice

If no audio recording is available, ask a partner, instructor, or text to speech tool to read the lines below aloud, or read them aloud to yourself slowly, paying attention to the marked stress.

Listening script
Здра́вствуйте! Меня́ зову́т Оле́г. А как вас зову́т?
Hello! My name is Oleg. And what is your name?
  1. Listen to (or read aloud) the script above, then answer, in Russian, with your own name using the correct formula.
  2. Listen for the greeting word at the start of the script. Is it the informal or the polite greeting? How do you know?
  3. Listen for the stressed syllable in Оле́г. Which syllable carries the stress?

Speaking Practice

Управля́емая пра́ктика — Controlled practice: greet and respond

Practice saying each greeting and courtesy word from the vocabulary table aloud, three times each, paying attention to the marked stress.

Коммуникати́вное зада́ние — Communicative task: meet a classmate

With a partner, greet each other informally, ask and answer each other's names, and say goodbye. Then repeat the exchange as if meeting a teacher, using the polite forms.

Самостоя́тельная пра́ктика — Solo alternative

Record yourself greeting an imaginary teacher politely and an imaginary classmate informally, introducing yourself in both exchanges. Listen back and check your stress against the vocabulary table.

  3. Find the word that means "thank you" and the word that means "bye."

Writing Workshop

Complete the following linked writing tasks in order.

Зада́ние 1 — Copy and mark stress: Copy each word from the Core Vocabulary tables by hand, including its stress mark, three times each.

Зада́ние 2 — Complete the exchange: Fill in your own name in the blank, then write the full formulaic answer.

Fill in the blank
Как вас зову́т? — Меня́ зову́т ______.
What is your name? — My name is ______.

Зада́ние 3 — Short written introduction: Write three short sentences introducing yourself, using only the vocabulary and patterns from this chapter, such as your greeting, your name, and a polite closing.

  1. Copy the vocabulary with stress marks first. This builds accurate handwriting habits from the start.
  2. Fill in the blank exchange. Check that your answer uses the full formula, not just your name alone.
  3. Draft your three sentence introduction. Keep every word within this chapter's vocabulary.
  4. Read your introduction aloud once. Listen for the stressed syllable in each word.
  5. Check your Cyrillic letter shapes against the vocabulary tables. Correct any letter that does not match.

Спи́сок прове́рки — Editing checklist:

  • Every word is written in Cyrillic, with no Latin letters.
  • Stress marks are included where the vocabulary table shows them.
  • The formula Меня зовут... is used correctly and completely.
  • The greeting chosen matches the intended relationship, informal or polite.
  • No new vocabulary from outside this chapter has been used.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Choosing a greeting

Among many Russian speakers, the choice between an informal and a polite greeting is one of the first social judgments a speaker makes on meeting someone, based on age, familiarity, and setting rather than on a single fixed rule. Classmates of a similar age commonly greet one another informally from the start, while a teacher, an older stranger, or a service worker is more commonly greeted politely.

Regional Variation — Russian speakers beyond one country

Russian is spoken as a first or additional language by communities in many countries, including the Russian Federation, Belarus, Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, Ukraine, the Baltic states, Israel, and diaspora communities elsewhere. Speaking Russian does not by itself indicate a person's citizenship, ethnicity, or political views, and this course will return to that distinction directly in Chapter 3.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Inserting "is" into a Russian sentence

Error: Adding an extra word for "is" in a simple identification sentence, by analogy with English, such as treating Это as if it needed a following verb.

Correction: Remember that Russian uses no present tense verb "to be" in sentences like Это А́нна. The noun follows это directly.

Common Mistake — Skipping the stress mark

Error: Copying a new word without its stress mark, then guessing at pronunciation later from spelling alone.

Correction: Copy every new word together with its stress mark from the vocabulary table, since Russian stress cannot reliably be predicted from spelling.

Common Mistake — Using ты with everyone

Error: Greeting a teacher or an unfamiliar older person with Приве́т and как тебя́ зову́т, simply because these forms feel easier to remember.

Correction: Match your greeting to the relationship, using Здра́вствуйте and как вас зову́т with a teacher or a stranger.

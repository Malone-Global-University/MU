16

Что вы де́лали? Что вы сде́лали?

Completed events · Speak and Write Russian: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Form the Russian past tense for regular and common irregular verbs, and correctly match past-tense endings to the gender and number of the subject.
  2. Distinguish an imperfective past verb, used for a process, a repeated action, or an unfinished activity, from a perfective past verb, used for a single completed action with a clear result.
  3. Narrate a sequence of past events using time markers such as снача́ла, пото́м, зате́м, and в конце́.
  4. Ask and answer Что вы де́лали? for an ongoing or repeated past activity, and Что вы сде́лали? for a completed action with a result.
  5. Describe a weekend, an errand, or a daily routine from yesterday, using a connected sequence of past-tense sentences.
  6. Recognize how negation interacts with aspect in the past tense.

This chapter opens Unit IV. It introduces the Russian past tense as a productive system for the first time, together with the imperfective/perfective past contrast, sequencing, and time markers. All vocabulary, cases, and other grammar from Chapters 1 through 15 remain active and continue to appear throughout this chapter.

Communication Scenario

It is Monday morning. Ири́на, Серге́й, and Ната́ша meet for coffee before Ната́ша returns home, and they talk about how the weekend went. Ири́на describes a productive Saturday of errands and study, Серге́й describes a quieter day at home, and Ната́ша describes her last day visiting the city. Each of them moves between saying what they were doing and what they actually finished.

  1. You will learn how to form the past tense of verbs you already know, and how to mark gender and number correctly.
  2. You will practice choosing between an imperfective and a perfective verb in the past, depending on whether you are describing a process or a completed result.
  3. You will practice connecting several past events into one natural narrative, using sequencing words and time expressions.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Stress in past-tense forms

Most Russian verbs keep the same stress across all past-tense forms. A small group of common verbs, however, shift stress onto the ending in the feminine past form only. Compare был, бы́ло, and бы́ли with была́, where the stress moves to the final syllable in the feminine form alone. You already encountered this pattern with бы́ло пло́хо in Chapter 15.

  • жил, жи́ло, жи́ли, but жила́ — feminine stress shifts to the ending.
  • на́чал, на́чало, на́чали, but начала́ — feminine stress shifts to the ending.
  • Most verbs, such as рабо́тал, рабо́тала, рабо́тали, keep stable stress throughout.

Continue reducing unstressed о and а, as in сде́лала and купи́ла, and keep distinguishing hard and soft consonants in the past-tense endings -л, -ла, -ло, and -ли.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Forming the Past Tense New

Russian past-tense verbs agree with the gender and number of the subject, not with person. To form the past tense of most verbs, remove the infinitive ending -ть and add -л, -ла, -ло, or -ли.

Building Block — Past-tense endings
де́лать → де́лал / де́лала / де́лало / де́лали
masculine subject — -л · feminine subject — -ла · neuter subject — -ло · plural subject, any gender — -ли
Серге́й де́лал дома́шнее зада́ние.
Sergéy was doing his homework. (masculine subject)
Ири́на де́лала дома́шнее зада́ние.
Irína was doing her homework. (feminine subject)
Мы де́лали дома́шнее зада́ние.
We were doing our homework. (plural subject)
  • Я купи́л(а) проду́кты.I bought groceries.купи́ть, perfective, masculine/feminine subject choice
  • Они́ убра́ли кварти́ру.They cleaned the apartment.убра́ть, perfective, plural subject

A small number of common verbs form the past tense irregularly, including быть → был/была́/бы́ло/бы́ли and мочь → мог/могла́/могло́/могли́. Learn these forms individually rather than by rule.

Imperfective and Perfective Past New

The choice between an imperfective and a perfective verb in the past does not depend on how long ago something happened. It depends on whether you are describing a process, a repeated action, or an unfinished activity, imperfective, or a single completed action viewed as a whole, with a clear result, perfective.

  • Я чита́л(а) кни́гу весь ве́чер.I was reading a book all evening.imperfective, process, duration emphasized, no claim about finishing
  • Я прочита́л(а) кни́гу.I read the whole book. (I finished it.)perfective, completed action, result emphasized
  • Мы обе́дали в кафе́ два часа́.We had lunch at a café for two hours.imperfective, duration
  • Мы пообе́дали и пошли́ домо́й.We had lunch and went home.perfective, completed event, followed by the next event
Language Focus — Что вы де́лали? versus Что вы сде́лали?

Что вы де́лали? asks about an activity in progress or a general account of how time was spent. Что вы сде́лали? asks about a specific completed action with a result. Both questions are useful, and the aspect of your answer should match the aspect of the question, though a natural conversation may move between both questions.

Sequencing Past Events New

Connect a series of past actions using sequencing words. Perfective verbs are especially common for narrating a chain of completed events, since each action is presented as finished before the next one begins.

  • Снача́ла я вста́л(а), пото́м поза́втракал(а), и зате́м пошёл/пошла́ на рабо́ту.First I got up, then I had breakfast, and then I went to work.снача́ла, пото́м, зате́м; sequence of perfective actions
  • В конце́ дня мы отдохну́ли до́ма.At the end of the day we rested at home.в конце́ + genitive; final event in the sequence

Negation in the Past New

Negated past-tense verbs follow the same gender and number agreement as affirmative past-tense verbs. Choice of aspect under negation is a receptive concern in this volume; for now, negate whichever verb your sentence already uses.

  • Я не убра́л(а) кварти́ру вчера́.I did not clean the apartment yesterday.не + perfective past
  • Он не рабо́тал в суббо́ту.He did not work on Saturday.не + imperfective past

Model Dialogue: Как прошли́ выходны́е?

Context: Ири́на, Серге́й, and Ната́ша meet for coffee on Monday morning before Ната́ша's train home.

Ири́на: Приве́т! Как прошли́ выходны́е? Что вы де́лали в суббо́ту? Hi! How was the weekend? What were you doing on Saturday?
Ната́ша: У́тром я гуля́ла по го́роду, а пото́м встре́тилась с друзья́ми в кафе́. Мы сиде́ли там два часа́ и разгова́ривали. In the morning I was walking around the city, and then I met up with friends at a café. We sat there for two hours and talked.
Серге́й: А я в суббо́ту сде́лал дела́. Снача́ла я купи́л проду́кты, пото́м убра́л кварти́ру, и зате́м написа́л дли́нное письмо́ роди́телям. And on Saturday I ran errands. First I bought groceries, then I cleaned the apartment, and then I wrote a long letter to my parents.
Ири́на: Мо́лодец! А я весь день гото́вилась к экза́мену, но, че́стно говоря́, не прочита́ла всю кни́гу. Well done! And I spent the whole day preparing for the exam, but, honestly, I didn't finish reading the whole book.
Ната́ша: Ничего́, ты ещё успе́ешь. А что вы де́лали в воскресе́нье? It's fine, you'll still have time. And what did you do on Sunday?
Серге́й: В воскресе́нье мы отдохну́ли до́ма, посмотре́ли фильм, и лёг спать ра́но. Мне ну́жно бы́ло вы́спаться. On Sunday we rested at home, watched a movie, and I went to bed early. I needed a good night's sleep.
Ната́ша: А я, к сожале́нию, то́лько что уви́дела, что мой по́езд ухо́дит че́рез час! Спаси́бо вам за всё, что вы для меня́ сде́лали. And I, unfortunately, just now saw that my train leaves in an hour! Thank you for everything you did for me.

Notice how the dialogue moves between imperfective forms for ongoing activities, such as гуля́ла and сиде́ли, and perfective forms for completed actions, such as купи́л, убра́л, and уви́дела, connected with снача́ла, пото́м, and зате́м.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Describing a weekend

Asking how someone's weekend went, and giving a short account in return, is a common way to open a conversation among friends and colleagues across Russian-speaking communities, much as it is in many other communities. Some speakers give a brief general summary, while others enjoy narrating several specific events in sequence, as in the Model Dialogue above. Both approaches are natural, and the right level of detail depends on the relationship and the setting.

Language Note — Aspect is a meaning system, not a shortcut

It can be tempting to treat imperfective as "past continuous" and perfective as "simple past," borrowing categories from English. Russian aspect does not map onto English tense categories this way. An imperfective past verb can describe a single action that took time, a repeated action, or even a completed action when the process itself, not the result, is what matters to the speaker. Focus on what the speaker wants to emphasize, process or result, rather than translating an English tense directly.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Forgetting gender agreement in the past tense

Error: A woman saying Я купи́л проду́кты instead of Я купи́ла проду́кты.

Correction: The past-tense verb must agree with the gender of the subject, not simply follow the pronoun я automatically: a woman says Я купи́ла проду́кты, and a man says Я купи́л проду́кты.

Common Mistake — Using a perfective verb for an ongoing or repeated action

Error: Saying Я прочита́л кни́гу два часа́ instead of Я чита́л кни́гу два часа́.

Correction: A perfective verb presents an action as a single completed whole and does not naturally combine with an expression of ongoing duration such as два часа́. Use the imperfective verb for duration: Я чита́л кни́гу два часа́, not Я прочита́л кни́гу два часа́.

Common Mistake — Treating perfective as simply "the past tense of" the imperfective

Error: Assuming чита́л and прочита́л mean exactly the same thing, only with чита́л sounding more "basic."

Correction: Both чита́л and прочита́л are past-tense forms, but they express different meanings: чита́л describes reading as a process, without confirming the book was finished, while прочита́л confirms that the reading was completed. Choose based on meaning, not on formality.

Answer Key

Listening — Comprehension

Reading

  1. The writer finally completed all the errands on Wednesday evening: buying groceries, cleaning the apartment, and writing several letters.
  2. The writer had dinner with colleagues for almost three hours; у́жинали is imperfective, emphasizing the duration of the meal.
  3. The writer finally finished a book bought a month earlier; the verb прочита́л confirms completion, rather than an imperfective verb that would only describe reading in progress.

Сре́дства ма́ссовой информа́ции

Reading headlines and short articles with nominalizations, passive recognition, and reported speech · Speak and Write Russian: Complex Communication and Discourse

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chapter 2 opens the first new productive development of Volume III. After Chapter 1's review of connected B1 discourse, this chapter turns to a text type learners will meet constantly outside the classroom, media language, headlines, short articles, and spoken news summaries. Three systems work together here. Nominalizations let Russian turn an action into a noun, which is how many headlines compress a whole sentence into a short phrase. Passive recognition helps learners read sentences in which the agent of an action is left out or moved out of focus, without requiring learners to produce the passive independently yet. Reported speech lets learners describe, in their own words, what a report, an official, or an article said, using verbs of saying and thinking together with что and constructions already active from Volume II. None of these systems is taught as an isolated grammar point, each is introduced through the concrete task of understanding and summarizing real seeming Russian media texts.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize and interpret short, unpacked nominalized phrases typical of Russian headlines.
  2. Recognize passive and passive like constructions in short articles, without being required to produce them independently.
  3. Report what a short article or announcement said, using verbs of saying and thinking with что.
  4. Identify the main idea and key details of a short news item from a headline, a lead sentence, and a short body paragraph.
  5. Summarize a short media text aloud and in writing, using only active grammar and reported speech.
  6. Distinguish factual reporting language from opinion language in a short text.

This chapter activates its systems for controlled recognition and for limited, formula supported production. Independent, unsupported passive production and full nominalization production remain outside this chapter's scope, in keeping with the Version 5 matrix architecture governing this volume.

Communication Scenario

Ди́ма starts his morning, as he often does, by scrolling through a few headlines on his phone before work. Some stories interest him, some do not, but he has noticed that headlines rarely sound like ordinary spoken sentences, they compress information, they hide who did what, and they often summarize what someone else said rather than stating a fact directly. Over coffee, he tells his colleague Ната́ша what he read, translating the compressed headline language into something closer to normal connected speech, and reporting, in his own words, what the article claimed.

  1. You will read a set of short Russian headlines and identify their compressed, nominalized structure.
  2. You will read a short article and notice where the passive is used and why.
  3. You will listen to Ди́ма reporting a headline's content to a colleague, using verbs of saying with что.
  4. You will summarize a short media text yourself, in speech and in writing, reporting what it said.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Reading headlines aloud at natural speed

No new sound or stress pattern is introduced in this chapter. Headlines, however, compress a great deal of stressed and unstressed material into a short phrase, so this section asks learners to practice reading a compressed nominalized phrase aloud smoothly, without pausing between every word.

  • Read the headline Откры́тие но́вой доро́ги заплани́ровано на ию́нь aloud three times, keeping the primary stress on откры́тие and доро́ги clear while the smaller words stay light and unstressed.
  • Practice the reporting phrase по слова́м представи́теля компа́нии as one smooth breath group, since по слова́м functions as a single formulaic unit rather than as two separately stressed words.

Nominalizations, the Passive, and Reported Speech

This chapter introduces three connected systems, all aimed at helping learners read and summarize media Russian. Nominalizations and passive recognition are introduced for reading comprehension, learners are not expected to produce new nominalized forms independently. Reported speech is introduced for both recognition and controlled, formula supported production, since summarizing what a text said is the chapter's central communicative task.

Nominalizations in headlines
verb → deverbal noun, often ‑ение, ‑ание, ‑ие, ‑ка
A Russian headline often compresses a full sentence into a noun phrase by turning the main verb into a noun. Отре́ть doorway becomes откры́тие, an opening, and постро́ить, to build, becomes постро́йка, a building or construction, so that a sentence such as Компа́ния постро́ила заво́д can appear in a headline as Постро́йка заво́да компа́нией, with the agent optionally marked by an instrumental phrase. Learners are asked here only to recognize this pattern and to unpack a nominalized headline back into a full sentence, not to generate new nominalizations on demand.
Заголо́вок: Откры́тие но́вой доро́ги заплани́ровано на ию́нь. → По́лное предложе́ние: Но́вую доро́гу откро́ют в ию́не.
Headline: The opening of the new road is planned for June. → Full sentence: They will open the new road in June.
Passive recognition, third person only
‑ся reflexive passive (обсужда́ется, рассма́тривается) and short passive participle (приня́т, при́нято, при́няты)
Russian news writing often removes or de emphasizes the agent of an action, either with a reflexive verb ending in ‑ся used passively, or with a short form passive participle agreeing with the grammatical subject. Both patterns are common in headlines and lead sentences and both are treated here as receptive, third person forms to be recognized while reading, not produced independently.
Э́тот вопро́с обсужда́ется на встре́че мини́стров. Реше́ние уже́ при́нято.
This question is being discussed at the ministers' meeting. The decision has already been made.
Reported speech with verbs of saying
сообщи́ть / заяви́ть / подчеркну́ть + что + clause
To summarize what an article, an official, or a source said, use a verb of saying such as сообщи́ть, заяви́ть, or подчеркну́ть, followed by что and a full clause. This pattern draws on что clauses already active from Volume II and simply applies them to the new communicative task of reporting media content, rather than introducing a new clause structure.
По да́нным газе́ты, но́вую доро́гу откро́ют уже́ в ию́не. Представи́тель компа́нии заяви́л, что прое́кт идёт по гра́фику.
According to the newspaper's information, they will already open the new road in June. The company's representative stated that the project is on schedule.

Model Exchange: Что пи́шут в новостя́х

Context: Over morning coffee, Ди́ма tells his colleague Ната́ша what he read in the news that morning.

Ната́ша: Что но́вого пи́шут сего́дня? What's new in the news today?
Ди́ма: Заголо́вок звучи́т сло́жно, Откры́тие но́вой доро́ги заплани́ровано на ию́нь, но е́сли просто́ говори́ть, доро́гу отбро́ют уже́ в ию́не. The headline sounds complicated, "Opening of new road planned for June", but if you just say it plainly, they'll open the road already in June.
Ната́ша: А кто э́то реши́л? В заголо́вке ведь не ска́зано, кто и́менно. And who decided that? The headline doesn't actually say who exactly.
Ди́ма: Ты права́, в статье́ то́же испо́льзуется э́тот приём, вопро́с обсужда́ется на у́ровне го́рода, но кто и́менно реша́ет, не совсе́м я́сно. По слова́м представи́теля мэ́рии, реше́ние уже́ при́нято. You're right, the article also uses this device, the question is being discussed at the city level, but who exactly decides isn't entirely clear. According to the mayor's office representative, the decision has already been made.
Ната́ша: Пра́вильно ли я понима́ю, что а́втор статьи́ то́лько передаёт чужи́е слова́, а свое́го мне́ния не даёт? Do I understand correctly that the article's author is only relaying someone else's words, and doesn't give their own opinion?
Ди́ма: В це́лом да. А́втор сообща́ет, что прое́кт идёт по гра́фику, и подчёркивает, что дви́жение бу́дет закры́то то́лько на не́сколько дней. Своего́ отноше́ния он не выска́зывает. On the whole, yes. The author reports that the project is on schedule, and emphasizes that traffic will be closed for only a few days. He doesn't express his own attitude.
Ната́ша: Как ты счита́ешь, сто́ит ли доверя́ть тако́й статье́, и́ли лу́чше подожда́ть други́х исто́чников? What do you think, is it worth trusting an article like this, or is it better to wait for other sources?

This exchange combines nominalized headline language, passive and passive like recognition, and reported speech with verbs of saying, all applied to a short media text. Notice that Natasha's closing question moves naturally toward the evaluative, opinion focused discourse this volume develops further in Chapter 9.

  • The headline uses a nominalized phrase, and a full sentence version appears elsewhere in the text.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Reading media critically

Readers of Russian language media, like readers of media in any language, benefit from noticing whose voice a text represents, a named source, an unnamed source, or the author's own claim, and from comparing more than one outlet before forming an opinion on a reported event. This habit is a general media literacy skill rather than a claim about any particular outlet, region, or political context, and it applies equally across the many communities that read and produce Russian language media.

Language Note — Why headlines compress

Nominalized headlines are a stylistic convention of written Russian media, not a sign that the underlying grammar has changed. The same event can always be restated as a full sentence with a conjugated verb, which is exactly the skill this chapter practices, moving between the compressed written form and the fuller spoken form a learner already controls.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Treating a nominalized headline as an ordinary noun phrase

Error: Reading a headline such as Откры́тие доро́ги заплани́ровано and interpreting откры́тие only as a static object, an opening, without recognizing the underlying verbal event, someone will open something.

Correction: When reading a nominalized headline, first identify the underlying verb, then ask who might perform that action, even when the agent is not stated, exactly as practiced in the Guided Practice unpacking exercises.

Common Mistake — Attempting to produce new passive forms independently

Error: Trying to build new passive constructions freely, for example inventing new short passive participles from verbs not covered in this chapter's vocabulary, because the reading passages contain several passive examples.

Correction: Use the passive and passive like forms in this chapter only for recognition. When speaking or writing your own sentences, prefer the active constructions already active from Volume II. Independent passive production is scheduled for later in the volume.

Common Mistake — Blurring reported claims with the learner's own opinion

Error: Summarizing an article and mixing what the source claimed with the learner's own assessment, without marking the difference, so that a reader cannot tell what the article actually said.

Correction: Keep reported content clearly marked with a verb of saying and что, or with по да́нным or по слова́м, and introduce any personal reaction separately, using language such as по-мо́ему or я счита́ю, already active from Volume II.

Chapter Review

Nominalizations

Deverbal nouns such as откры́тие, строи́тельство, and повыше́ние compress a full sentence into a short headline phrase.

Passive Recognition

Reflexive passive like verbs and short passive participles let a text de-emphasize or omit the agent of an action.

Reported Speech

Сообщи́ть, заяви́ть, and подчеркну́ть with что, together with по да́нным and по слова́м, mark what a source said.

Media Reading Strategy

Headline, lead sentence, and body paragraph work together to signal the main idea, the source, and the supporting detail.

Listening

Follow a short spoken news report and identify its main claims, sources, and unstated agents.

Speaking

Unpack headlines aloud and report invented news items using verbs of saying.

Reading

Interpret a short news item, identifying its nominalized headline, sources, and passive constructions.

Writing

Compose a short news item combining a nominalized headline, reported statements, and passive recognition.

Answer Key

Grammar Section — Unpack the Headline

  1. Но́вую шко́лу начну́т стро́ить весно́й.
  2. Прое́кт бу́дут обсужда́ть и на сле́дующей неде́ле.
  3. Це́ны повы́сили, потому́ что появи́лись но́вые расхо́ды.
  4. Вопро́с отложи́ли до а́вгуста.

Guided Practice — Exercise 1

  1. Agent unclear, откры́тие заплани́ровано does not state who planned it.
  2. Agent unclear, обсужда́ется names no specific participants.
  3. Agent named, представи́тель мэ́рии is explicitly identified as the source.
  4. Agent named, а́втор is the identified subject of сообща́ет.

Guided Practice — Exercise 2

Sample answers, other accurate answers are also correct. 1. Прави́тельство одо́брило повыше́ние зарпла́т учителе́й. 2. В понеде́льник начну́т обсужда́ть но́вый зако́н. 3. Ста́нцию метро́ постро́ят в бу́дущем году́.

Guided Practice — Exercise 3

Sample answers, other accurate answers are also correct. 1. Представи́тель компа́нии сообщи́л, что прое́кт идёт по гра́фику. 2. Мэр го́рода заяви́л, что но́вую шко́лу отбро́ют в сентябре́. 3. Журнали́ст подчеркну́л, что э́тот вопро́с ещё не решён оконча́тельно.

Listening — Comprehension

  1. The report describes construction of a new bridge across the river, beginning in July.
  2. The representative emphasized that the old bridge would remain open during the entire construction period.
  3. According to the chief engineer, residents of the northern district will benefit through a significant reduction in travel time.
  4. Sample answer, вопро́с сейча́с обсужда́ется отде́льно leaves unstated who exactly is discussing the noise concern.

Model Exchange — Comprehension

  1. Dima reads the headline about the new road's opening, and unpacks it as доро́гу отбро́ют уже́ в ию́не.
  2. Natasha notices that the headline does not say who exactly made the decision, only that it was made.

Reading

  1. The article describes the opening of a new park, planned for Saturday.
  2. The chief architect stated that special attention was given to paths for pedestrians and cyclists, and the mayor's office representative stated that entry would be free for everyone.
  3. Sample answer, откры́тие но́вого па́рка unpacks as отбро́ют но́вый парк.
  4. Sample answer, вопро́с пока́ ещё рассма́тривается отде́льно leaves unstated who exactly is considering the parking concern.

Writing

Sample account: Заголо́вок, Откры́тие но́вой библиоте́ки заплани́ровано на о́сень. По да́нным департа́мента культу́ры, библиоте́ку стро́или бо́лее го́да. Гла́вный архите́ктор заяви́л, что зда́ние спроекти́ровано с осо́бым внима́нием к до́ступности для люде́й с ограни́ченными возмо́жностями. Он подчеркну́л, что вопро́с расположе́ния кни́жных фо́ндов обсужда́лся с ме́стными жи́телями на протяже́нии всего́ прое́кта. По слова́м предста́вителя мэ́рии, вход бу́дет свобо́дным для всех жи́телей го́рода. Е́сли говори́ть про́сто, но́вую библиоте́ку отбро́ют уже́ о́сенью. Other summaries, real or invented, that accurately combine the reviewed systems are also correct.

Интервью́ и биогра́фия

Asking interview questions and narrating a life story with reported speech, past narration, and names and patronymics · Speak and Write Russian: Complex Communication and Discourse

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chapter 3 takes the reported speech patterns introduced in Chapter 2 and applies them to a new, more personal task, interviewing someone and writing a profile of their life. Three systems work together here. Reported speech, already active from Chapter 2, now extends into the past, so learners can relay not only what a source states but what someone told them in an earlier conversation. Past narration draws together aspect choice, so learners can distinguish a single completed event from an ongoing state or a repeated pattern when telling someone's story. Names and patronymics become an active register system for the first time in this volume, since interviews and biographies constantly require learners to decide how to refer to a person appropriately, by first name, by first name and patronymic, or by surname and title. None of these systems is new from nothing, each builds directly on material already active from Volume II, redirected toward the communicative task of interviewing and narrating.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Ask a set of interview questions about someone's life, education, and work.
  2. Report what an interviewee said, using past tense verbs of saying with что.
  3. Narrate a sequence of past life events, choosing imperfective or perfective aspect according to whether an event is background, repeated, or a single completed step.
  4. Refer to a person appropriately using first name, first name with patronymic, or surname with title, depending on register.
  5. Read a short biographical text and identify its main events in chronological order.
  6. Write a short biographical profile that combines interview content, past narration, and consistent naming choices.

This chapter develops reported speech into the past tense and activates past narration and the names and patronymics system for controlled production. It does not introduce new case forms, new motion verb prefixes, or new passive structures beyond what Chapter 2 already made receptive, in keeping with the Version 5 load control principles governing this volume.

Communication Scenario

Ната́ша is writing a short profile of a colleague, Михаи́л Петро́вич Соколо́в, for their team's internal newsletter. She has never interviewed anyone before, so she prepares a short list of questions, meets him during a coffee break, and takes notes as he tells her about his childhood, his studies, and how he came to work at the company. Afterward, she needs to decide how to refer to him throughout the profile, and she needs to turn his spoken answers into a organized written narrative that a reader who never met him could follow.

  1. You will prepare and ask a short set of interview questions about someone's life and work.
  2. You will listen to an interview and identify which events are background and which are single completed steps.
  3. You will report what the interviewee said, using past tense verbs of saying with что.
  4. You will write a short biographical profile, choosing names, patronymics, or titles consistently throughout.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Names and patronymics at natural speed

No new sound or stress pattern is introduced in this chapter. Full names with a patronymic, however, are commonly reduced in fast, natural speech, so this section asks learners to notice and practice this reduction, without treating it as a spelling change.

  • Practice the full form Михаи́л Петро́вич slowly, then practice the commonly reduced spoken form, in which the patronymic's middle syllables soften and shorten, while the written spelling stays the same.
  • Read Ната́лья Влади́мировна aloud three times, keeping the stress on Ната́лья and on ‑ди́мировна clear even as the whole phrase speeds up.

Past Narration, Aspect, and Names

This chapter develops three connected systems, all aimed at helping learners interview someone and write their story. Reported speech extends into the past tense for controlled production. Aspect choice in narration is developed for controlled production, using only verb pairs already verified from earlier volumes. Names and patronymics become an active register system, since referring to a person consistently is essential to any biography.

Reported speech in the past
рассказа́ть / сказа́ть / отве́тить + что + past tense clause
Chapter 2 practiced reporting what a text says using present tense verbs of saying. An interview, by contrast, usually needs to be reported afterward, so the reporting verb itself moves into the past, while the content of the что clause reflects whatever tense the interviewee actually used. This is the same pattern already active from Volume II, simply extended to a new communicative task.
Михаи́л Петро́вич рассказа́л, что он роди́лся в небольшо́м го́роде и что его́ семья́ переехала в столи́цу, когда́ ему́ бы́ло де́сять лет.
Mikhail Petrovich told me that he was born in a small town and that his family moved to the capital when he was ten years old.
Aspect choice in biographical narration
imperfective for background, state, or repetition · perfective for a single completed step
When telling someone's life story, a learner must constantly choose between imperfective and perfective past forms. Use the imperfective for background information, an ongoing state, or something that happened repeatedly, such as жил, учи́лся, or ча́сто е́здил. Use the perfective for a single completed step that moves the story forward, such as роди́лся, поступи́л, or зако́нчил. This chapter only uses aspect pairs already verified from earlier volumes, it does not introduce new pairs or new prefixation patterns.
Он до́лго жил в ма́леньком го́роде, а пото́м поступи́л в университе́т в Москве́. По́сле университе́та он неско́лько лет рабо́тал в шко́ле, а зате́м устро́ился в э́ту компа́нию.
He lived in a small town for a long time, and then he enrolled at a university in Moscow. After university he worked at a school for several years, and then he got a job at this company.
Names, patronymics, and consistent reference
и́мя · и́мя + о́тчество · фами́лия + ти́тул
A Russian speaker may refer to the same person by first name alone among friends, by first name and patronymic in a respectful or professional interview, or by surname with a title in a very formal written profile. A biography or interview write up should choose one register and stay consistent, switching only when the register of the text itself genuinely changes, for example moving from a formal introduction to a warmer closing paragraph.
Михаи́л Петро́вич Соколо́в согласи́лся отве́тить на не́сколько вопро́сов. Как рассказа́л Михаи́л Петро́вич, его́ путь к нынешней рабо́те был совсе́м не прямы́м.
Mikhail Petrovich Sokolov agreed to answer a few questions. As Mikhail Petrovich told me, his path to his current job was not at all direct.

Пра́ктика — Choose the aspect: For each sentence, choose the correct past tense form of the verb in parentheses, imperfective for background or repetition, perfective for a single completed step.

  1. В де́тстве она́ ча́сто (е́здить / пое́хать) к ба́бушке в дере́вню.
  2. По́сле шко́лы она́ (поступа́ть / поступи́ть) в медици́нский институ́т.
  3. Он до́лго (рабо́тать / порабо́тать) в ма́ленькой компа́нии, пре́жде чем перешёл в кру́пную фи́рму.
  4. В про́шлом году́ она́ (зака́нчивать / зако́нчить) университе́т и (устра́иваться / устро́иться) на пе́рвую рабо́ту.

Model Exchange: Расскажи́те о себе́

Context: Ната́ша interviews her colleague Михаи́л Петро́вич Соколо́в for the team newsletter, then later reports the interview to another colleague.

Ната́ша: Михаи́л Петро́вич, расскажи́те, пожа́луйста, где вы роди́лись и вы́росли. Mikhail Petrovich, please tell me, where were you born and where did you grow up.
Михаи́л П.: Я роди́лся в небольшо́м го́роде на ю́ге, а вы́рос уже́ в Москве́, семья́ перее́хала, когда́ мне бы́ло де́сять лет. I was born in a small town in the south, but I grew up in Moscow, my family moved when I was ten years old.
Ната́ша: А где вы учи́лись, и когда́ вы поступи́ли в университе́т? And where did you study, and when did you enroll at university?
Михаи́л П.: По́сле шко́лы я поступи́л в техни́ческий университе́т, учи́лся там пять лет и зако́нчил его́ с отли́чием. After school I enrolled at a technical university, I studied there for five years, and I finished it with honors.
Ната́ша: А как вы попа́ли и́менно в на́шу компа́нию? And how exactly did you end up at our company?
Михаи́л П.: По́сле университе́та я неско́лько лет рабо́тал в друго́й компа́нии, а пото́м мне предложи́ли рабо́ту здесь, я согласи́лся и устро́ился в отде́л разрабо́тки. After university I worked at another company for several years, and then I was offered a job here, I agreed, and I got a job in the development department.
Later, Ната́ша tells her colleague О́льга about the interview.
О́льга: Ну, и что тебе́ рассказа́л Михаи́л Петро́вич? So, what did Mikhail Petrovich tell you?
Ната́ша: Он рассказа́л, что роди́лся на ю́ге, а вы́рос в Москве́, и что зако́нчил университе́т с отли́чием. Он та́кже сказа́л, что до́лго рабо́тал в друго́й компа́нии, пре́жде чем устро́ился к нам. He told me that he was born in the south but grew up in Moscow, and that he finished university with honors. He also said that he worked at another company for a long time before he got a job with us.

This exchange combines past tense reported speech, aspect choice between background information and single completed steps, and consistent reference to Михаи́л Петро́вич by first name and patronymic throughout the interview register.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Names, patronymics, and respect

Using a first name together with a patronymic is a common way to address or refer to someone respectfully in many professional, academic, and interview contexts across Russian speaking communities, without implying distance or coldness. This practice varies by generation, workplace, and personal preference, and it should not be treated as a fixed rule for every Russian speaking community or every situation, some workplaces and some individuals prefer first names alone even in fairly formal settings.

Language Note — Consent and privacy in interviews

When writing a biography or a profile, a careful writer confirms what the interviewee is comfortable sharing publicly, and separates what was said in the interview from any outside assumption about the person's life. This chapter's tasks are designed around invented or willingly shared material, and this same care should extend to any real interview a learner might conduct outside the classroom.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Using perfective for every past verb in a narration

Error: Writing every verb in a biography as perfective, for example рабо́тал becoming порабо́тал for a long stretch of ongoing employment, because perfective forms feel more like a completed story.

Correction: Reserve the perfective for single completed steps that move the story forward, and use the imperfective for background states and repeated actions, exactly as practiced in the Guided Practice aspect exercises.

Common Mistake — Switching naming style without reason

Error: Referring to the same person as Михаи́л in one sentence, Михаи́л Петро́вич in the next, and Соколо́в in a third, within the same paragraph and register, so a reader cannot easily tell whether these refer to one person or several.

Correction: Choose one naming style appropriate to the text's register, and keep it consistent, switching only when the register of the text itself genuinely shifts, for example from a formal introduction to an informal closing remark.

Common Mistake — Reporting the interviewee's tense literally instead of adjusting it

Error: Reporting an answer such as "Я рабо́таю здесь пять лет" word for word without adjusting the reporting frame, so the reported sentence sounds like a direct quotation rather than a summary.

Correction: Keep the что clause's tense accurate to what the interviewee described, but frame the whole sentence clearly as reported content, with рассказа́л or сказа́л, что, exactly as practiced in the Guided Practice reporting exercises.

  2. Conduct or invent a short interview. Ask at least three questions about childhood, education, and work.
  3. Write your short biography, at least eight sentences long. Include the birth, at least two further completed steps, one background detail, and one reported statement.
  4. Deliver your biography aloud. Report at least two details using verbs of saying, and refer to the person consistently.
  5. Answer one invented follow up question aloud. A partner, instructor, or your own recorded voice may ask, "А что бы́ло да́льше?" or a similar question.

Answer Key

Grammar Section — Choose the Aspect

  1. е́здила, repeated action across childhood summers, imperfective.
  2. поступи́ла, a single completed step, perfective.
  3. рабо́тал, an ongoing state before the later change, imperfective.
  4. зако́нчила, устро́илась, two single completed steps in sequence, both perfective.

Guided Practice — Exercise 1

  1. Single completed step, перее́хала marks the family's move as one event.
  2. Single completed step, поступи́л marks enrollment as one event.
  3. Background or duration, учи́лся describes an ongoing five year period.
  4. Background or duration, рабо́тал describes an ongoing period before the next step.

Listening — Comprehension

  1. Elena Vladimirovna was born in a small northern town, and as a child she often went to her grandmother's in the countryside for the summer.
  2. She attended a pedagogical institute, and she finished it with honors.
  3. Sample answer, she enrolled at the institute, and later she was offered a position at a university department.
  4. Sample answer, ча́сто е́здила describes a repeated childhood activity, so the imperfective is used.

Model Exchange — Comprehension

  1. Mikhail Petrovich was born in a small town in the south, and he grew up in Moscow.
  2. Natasha refers to him as Михаи́л Петро́вич, keeping the respectful, professional register of the interview consistent even when reporting it to a colleague.

Reading

  1. Anna Dmitrievna was born in a small town in the Urals, and she often said she wanted to become an engineer.
  2. Sample answer, she enrolled at a technical university, finished it with honors, worked at a factory, moved to Moscow, and eventually got a job at a large construction firm.
  3. Sample answer, как расска́зывает сама́ Анна Дми́триевна reports her own words, using the verb расска́зывать.
  4. The passage refers to her consistently as Анна Дми́триевна throughout, matching the respectful register of a written profile.

Writing

Sample account: Серге́й Ива́нович Петро́в роди́лся в небольшо́м го́роде на Во́лге. В де́тстве он мно́го занима́лся спо́ртом и ча́сто говори́л, что хо́чет стать врачо́м. По́сле шко́лы Серге́й Ива́нович поступи́л в медици́нский университе́т, учи́лся там шесть лет и зако́нчил его́ с отли́чием. По́сле университе́та он неско́лько лет рабо́тал в небольшо́й больни́це, а зате́м перее́хал в о́бластной це́нтр, потому́ что ему́ предложи́ли рабо́ту в кру́пной кли́нике. Как расска́зывает сам Серге́й Ива́нович, ка́ждый его́ шаг был свя́зан с жела́нием помога́ть лю́дям. Other biographies, real or invented, that accurately combine the reviewed systems are also correct.

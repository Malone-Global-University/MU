Kuɗi, Tattalin Arziki, da Iyalai

B1 Development · Unit II, Chapter 4 · Speak and Write Hausa: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse
Unit II Continues — Final content chapter before the Chapter 10 integrated assessment. Systems consolidated further: imperfective/habitual and formulaic future TAM frames, object pronouns, possessive constructions, noun gender/plural as lexical entries, market/service repair formulas extended to financial and household contexts.

Chapter Overview

Chapter 9 continues Unit II and moves the conversation from technology and communication into money, economy, and households. You will learn to talk about prices, budgets, saving, spending, household expenses, and financial choices, while consolidating the systems developed in Chapters 6 through 8. The imperfective/habitual frame and the formulaic future-time expression with za continue to be used with growing independence, now applied to describing spending habits and financial plans. Object pronouns and possessive constructions continue as independently productive systems, this time referring to money, bills, and household items. Noun gender and plural continue to be recorded as lexical facts for every new noun, and the repair and negotiation formulas from Chapters 6 through 8 are recycled and extended into financial contexts, such as clarifying a price or correcting a misunderstanding about an amount of money. All vocabulary and grammar from Chapters 1 through 8 remain active. This chapter is the final content chapter of Unit II; Chapter 10 will integrate everything developed across Chapters 6 through 9 in a formal assessment.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify and use at least twelve new active vocabulary items related to money, prices, budgeting, and household economy.
  2. Describe a household budget or spending routine using recycled location, possession, and quantity clauses.
  3. Use object pronouns with confidence to refer to money, bills, and household items already mentioned in a conversation.
  4. Use possessive constructions with na/ta to indicate ownership of money, an account, or a household item.
  5. Use the imperfective/habitual frame across a fuller range of subject markers to describe financial routines.
  6. Use the formulaic future-time pattern za a/za ka/za ki/za mu + verb to talk about paying, saving, or budgeting soon.
  7. Record the gender and plural form of new money- and household-related nouns as part of learning them.
  8. Use repair and clarification formulas in a financial context, such as asking someone to confirm a price or correct a miscounted amount.
  9. Read a short passage about a household budget and answer factual and inferential questions.
  10. Write a short paragraph describing a personal or household financial routine or choice, real or invented.

Communication Scenario

Maryam and her husband Salisu sit down at the end of the month to plan their household budget. Maryam explains how much she usually spends on food and how much she is trying to save each month. Salisu says he thinks the price of rice has gone up again and worries that their money will not stretch far enough this month. Maryam suggests that they reduce spending on a few things and offers to keep track of household expenses herself. Salisu agrees and asks her to remind him before he spends money on anything unplanned. Later, Maryam tells her sister how she and Salisu are trying to manage their household budget more carefully.

  1. New vocabulary domain: money, prices, budgeting, saving, spending, and household economy.
  2. Consolidated grammar: fuller imperfective/habitual frame, the formulaic future-time expression, object pronouns, and possessive constructions.
  3. Continued system: noun gender and plural recorded as part of each new vocabulary entry.
  4. Extended pragmatic system: repair and clarification formulas applied to financial contexts.
  5. All Chapter 1-8 vocabulary and grammar remain active and should be recycled throughout this chapter's tasks.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Vowel length in number and quantity words

Several words used when talking about money and prices, such as kuɗi and farashi, contain vowel-length contrasts that affect meaning and should continue to be pronounced carefully. This chapter does not introduce a new tone or vowel-length rule, but it does ask you to continue applying the vowel-length awareness developed in earlier chapters when producing numbers, prices, and quantities in natural speech, consistent with the pronunciation policy established in the Hausa master instructions. Practice saying prices aloud slowly, paying attention to vowel length in both the number words and the nouns they modify.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter continues to consolidate (C) the systems developed in Chapters 6 through 8: the imperfective/habitual TAM frame is expected to be used independently across a fuller range of subject markers, the formulaic future-time expression with za continues as a reliable high-frequency pattern, object pronouns and possessive constructions are expected to be produced independently, noun gender/plural continues as a lexical-entry system, and repair/negotiation formulas extend into financial contexts. No new predicate type, negative TAM paradigm, or focus structure is introduced in this chapter, consistent with the Volume III scope for Chapter 9, which prepares learners for the Unit II integrated assessment in Chapter 10.

9.1 Consolidating the imperfective/habitual frame

Language Building Block — Describing Financial Routines
ina/kana/tana/yana/muna/kuna/suna + [verbal noun]
Muna ajiye kuɗi kowane wata don gaggawa.
We save money every month for emergencies.
Suna kashe kuɗi da yawa a kan abinci.
They spend a lot of money on food.

Chapter 7 introduced the full subject-marker set for this frame (ina/kana/tana/yana/muna/kuna/suna). This chapter asks you to use the frame independently to describe your own and other people's financial habits, such as saving, spending, or budgeting.

9.2 The formulaic future-time expression, continued

Language Building Block — Talking About Financial Plans
za + [subject marker] + [verb]
Za mu shirya kasafin kuɗi gobe da safe.
We will prepare the budget tomorrow morning.
Za ta biya harajin gida a wannan makon.
She will pay the household tax this week.

This pattern remains formulaic and high-frequency, as in Chapters 7 and 8. It continues to be useful for near-term plans and promises, this time in the context of money, saving, and paying bills. Full productive control of the future TAM system, including its negative and dependent forms, remains scheduled for later volumes.

9.3 Object pronouns, consolidated

  • Ban biya shi ba tukuna.I have not paid it yet.shi = masculine object pronoun referring to lissafi (bill)
  • Salisu ya ajiye ta a banki.Salisu saved it in the bank.ta = feminine object pronoun referring to ajiya (savings)
  • Maryam ta gaya mini kuɗin gida.Maryam told me the household expenses.mini = first-person object/benefactive pronoun, recycled from earlier chapters

Continue choosing the object pronoun according to the gender and number of the noun it replaces. In this chapter, object pronouns are used to talk about money, bills, and household items that have already been mentioned, without repeating the full noun each time.

9.4 Possessive constructions with na/ta, continued

  • Asusun nan na Salisu ne.This account belongs to Salisu.na (masculine possessive linker, agrees with asusu)
  • Ajiyar nan ta Maryam ce.This savings belongs to Maryam.ta (feminine possessive linker, agrees with ajiya)

Continue linking a possessor to the thing possessed with na after a masculine noun and ta after a feminine noun. As in earlier chapters, the linker's gender depends on the noun being possessed, not on the possessor.

9.5 Noun gender and plural, continued

Continue recording gender and plural for every new noun, as shown in the Core Vocabulary table above. Some money-related nouns, such as kuɗi and kuɗin gida, do not have a common plural form and should be learned as lexical facts.

9.6 Repair and clarification formulas, extended to financial contexts

  • Ban gane ba, nawa ne farashinsa?I didn't understand, how much is it?
  • Ka sake fadi, nawa muka kashe wannan wata?Say it again, how much did we spend this month?
  • To, na gane yanzu, matsalar ita ce farashin ya hau.Okay, I understand now, the problem is the price went up.

The confirmation formula from earlier chapters (to, na fahimta/na gane yanzu) continues to be useful here. This chapter adds two formulas specific to financial situations: asking someone to confirm a price and asking someone to repeat an amount that was misheard or miscounted.

Atisayen ƙa'ida — Controlled practice: Complete each sentence with the correct word or phrase.

  1. Maryam ___ (tana/ina) ajiye kuɗi kowane wata.
  2. Asusun nan ___ (na/ta) Salisu ne.
  3. Salisu ya ajiye ___ (ta/shi) a banki.
  4. ___ (Za mu/Muna) shirya kasafin kuɗi gobe.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Maryam da Salisu suna shirya kasafin kuɗin gidansu a ƙarshen wata.

(Setting: Maryam and Salisu are preparing their household budget at the end of the month.)

SALISU Maryam, mun kashe kuɗi da yawa wannan wata. Farashin shinkafa ya hau kuma.Maryam, we spent a lot of money this month. The price of rice also went up.
MARYAM Ban gane ba, nawa ne farashinsa yanzu?I didn't understand, how much is it now?
SALISU Ya hau da naira dari biyu. Ina tsoron kuɗin gida ba zai isa ba wannan wata.It went up by two hundred naira. I'm afraid the household money won't be enough this month.
MARYAM To, na gane yanzu. Za mu rage kashe kuɗi a kan wasu abubuwa. Zan riƙa lissafin gida da kaina.Okay, I understand now. We'll reduce spending on some things. I'll keep track of the household accounts myself.
SALISU Madalla. Kuma za ki iya tuna mini kafin in kashe kuɗi a kan abin da ba mu shirya ba?Good. And can you remind me before I spend money on something we didn't plan?
MARYAM Haka zan yi. Kuma ina ajiye kuɗi kaɗan kowane wata a asusunmu, don gaggawa.That's what I'll do. And I save a little money every month in our account, for emergencies.
SALISU Ka sake fadi, nawa muke ajiyewa a kowane wata?Say it again, how much do we save each month?
MARYAM Kusan naira dubu biyar. Ajiyar nan tana taimaka mana idan matsala ta faru.About five thousand naira. This savings helps us when a problem happens.
SALISU Na gode. Za mu shirya kasafin kuɗi mai kyau gobe da safe.Thank you. We'll prepare a good budget tomorrow morning.

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. Why is Salisu worried about the household money this month?
  2. What does Maryam offer to do to help manage the budget?
  3. What does Salisu ask Maryam to remind him about?
  4. What repair formula does Salisu use when he mishears an amount, and why?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Household budgeting and shared financial decisions

In many Hausa-speaking households, financial planning is often discussed and managed jointly between spouses or among family members, though the specific roles, responsibilities, and decision-making patterns vary widely by household, region, income level, and personal preference. Maryam and Salisu's conversation represents one plausible household arrangement rather than a description of how every Hausa-speaking family manages money.

Regional Variation — Currency and price expressions

Currency names, common price ranges, and everyday expressions about money can differ between Nigeria, Niger, and other Hausa-speaking regions, and prices themselves change over time. This chapter uses the Nigerian naira as an illustrative example and general vocabulary for prices and budgeting; learners in other regions should adapt currency names and typical amounts to their own context.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Wrong gender on the possessive linker

Error: *Ajiyar nan na Maryam ce. (using the masculine linker na with the feminine noun ajiya)

Correction: Ajiyar nan ta Maryam ce. — Choose na or ta according to the gender of the noun being possessed, not the gender of the possessor.

Common Mistake — Dropping the subject marker before za

Error: *Za biya harajin gida. (missing subject marker)

Correction: Za ta biya harajin gida. — The future-time pattern requires a subject marker between za and the verb; za alone is not sufficient.

Sashe na 2 — Unit II: Hulɗar Zamani (Modern Life and Interaction)
7

Aiki, Sana'a, da Kasuwanci

B1 Development · Unit II, Chapter 2 · Speak and Write Hausa: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse
Unit II Continues — Systems developed further: imperfective/habitual and early future TAM frames, object pronouns, possessive constructions, noun gender/plural as lexical entries, market/service repair formulas.

Chapter Overview

Chapter 7 continues Unit II and moves the conversation from education into work, trade, and enterprise. You will learn to talk about jobs, small businesses, markets, contracts, and services, while developing the systems introduced in Chapter 6 further. The imperfective/habitual TAM frame expands to cover a wider range of routines, and you will be introduced to a first future-time expression built on the same subject-marker pattern. Object pronouns and possessive constructions become active systems in their own right, no longer limited to the fixed phrases seen earlier. Noun gender and plural continue to be recorded as lexical facts for every new noun, and the market/service repair formulas from Chapter 6 are recycled and extended into negotiation contexts. All vocabulary and grammar from Chapters 1 through 6 remain active.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify and use at least twelve new active vocabulary items related to work, trade, and enterprise.
  2. Describe a job, business, or service using recycled location, possession, and quantity clauses.
  3. Use object pronouns to refer to people and things already mentioned in a conversation.
  4. Use possessive constructions with na/ta to indicate ownership of a business, tool, or product.
  5. Expand the imperfective/habitual frame to describe work routines across a wider range of verbs.
  6. Recognize and begin producing a first future-time expression using za a/za ka/za ki/za mu + verb.
  7. Record the gender and plural form of new work- and trade-related nouns as part of learning them.
  8. Use repair formulas in a negotiation context, such as asking for a lower price or confirming a price.
  9. Read a short passage about a small business and answer factual and inferential questions.
  10. Write a short paragraph describing a job, business, or service, real or invented.

Communication Scenario

Sule, Halima's brother, runs a small phone-repair stall near the market in Kaduna. A customer, Amina, brings a broken phone to the stall and asks Sule what is wrong with it, how much the repair will cost, and how long it will take. Sule explains the problem, quotes a price, and tells her when the phone will be ready. Amina asks him to lower the price slightly, and Sule agrees to a small discount because she is a regular customer. Later, Sule tells a friend about his week at the stall and his plans for the business.

  1. New vocabulary domain: work, trade, entrepreneurship, markets, contracts, and services.
  2. Developed grammar: expanded imperfective/habitual frame, a first future-time expression, object pronouns, and possessive constructions.
  3. Continued system: noun gender and plural recorded as part of each new vocabulary entry.
  4. Extended pragmatic system: repair and negotiation formulas for price discussion and clarification.
  5. All Chapter 1-6 vocabulary and grammar remain active and should be recycled throughout this chapter's tasks.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — The apostrophe/glottal stop in sana'a, kwangila, and 'yan kasuwa

Several new words in this chapter contain the glottal stop written with an apostrophe, as in sana'a and 'yan kasuwa. This chapter does not introduce a new tone or vowel-length rule, but it does require careful attention to the apostrophe as a real consonantal sound rather than a spelling mark, consistent with the Boko orthography policy established in Chapters 1 through 6. Practice pausing briefly at the apostrophe in sana'a and at the start of 'yan kasuwa rather than gliding over it.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops (D) the systems introduced in Chapter 6: the imperfective/habitual TAM frame expands across more verbs and a first future-time expression is introduced, object pronouns and possessive constructions become independently productive, noun gender/plural continues as a lexical-entry system, and market/service repair formulas extend into negotiation. No new predicate type, negative TAM paradigm, or focus structure is introduced in this chapter, consistent with the Volume III scope for Chapter 7.

7.1 Expanding the imperfective/habitual frame

Language Building Block — Describing Work Routines
ina/kana/tana/yana/muna/kuna/suna + [verbal noun]
Yana gyara wayoyi kullum a shago.
He repairs phones every day at the stall.
Muna sayar da kayayyaki a kasuwa kowace Talata.
We sell goods at the market every Tuesday.

Chapter 6 introduced this frame with ina/kana/tana. This chapter adds yana (he), muna (we), kuna (you plural), and suna (they), so that the habitual/imperfective frame can describe the routines of any person, not only the speaker or a person directly addressed.

7.2 A first future-time expression: za + subject marker + verb

Language Building Block — Talking About Plans
za + [subject marker] + [verb]
Za a gyara wayarki gobe da safe.
Your phone will be repaired tomorrow morning.
Za ka biya kuɗi bayan gyara.
You will pay the money after the repair.

This chapter presents za + subject marker + verb as a formulaic, high-frequency future-time pattern used for near-term plans and promises. Learners are expected to recognize and use this pattern in fixed, high-frequency frames such as scheduling repairs or agreeing on a payment time. Full productive control of the future TAM system, including its negative and dependent forms, remains scheduled for later Unit II chapters and is not assessed here.

7.3 Object pronouns

  • Sule ya gyara ta.Sule repaired it (the phone).ta = feminine object pronoun
  • Amina ta tambaye shi farashin.Amina asked him the price.shi = masculine object pronoun
  • Za ka ba ni rangwame?Will you give me a discount?ni = first-person object pronoun

Object pronouns replace a noun already identified in the conversation, in the same way English uses "it," "him," or "me." Choose the object pronoun according to the gender and number of the noun it replaces, following the noun gender information you have been recording since Chapter 6.

7.4 Possessive constructions with na/ta

  • Shagon nan naSule ne.This shop is Sule's.na (masculine possessive linker)
  • Wayar ta Amina ce.The phone is Amina's.ta (feminine possessive linker)
  • Sana'ar Sule ita ce gyaran waya.Sule's trade is phone repair.

Use na after a masculine noun and ta after a feminine noun to link a possessor to the thing possessed. This system connects directly to the noun gender you have been recording since Chapter 6: choosing the wrong linker is a gender error, not merely a vocabulary error.

7.5 Noun gender and plural, continued

Continue recording gender and plural for every new noun, as shown in the Core Vocabulary table above. Some plurals in this domain, such as sana'o'i and 'yan kasuwa, are irregular and must be learned as lexical facts rather than derived from a general rule.

7.6 Repair and negotiation formulas

  • Don Allah, ka sake maimaita farashin?Please, could you repeat the price?
  • Za ka iya rage farashin kaɗan?Can you lower the price a little?
  • To, na fahimta yanzu.Okay, I understand now.

The first two repair formulas from Chapter 6 continue to be useful here. This chapter adds a negotiation-specific formula, za ka iya rage farashin kaɗan, and a confirmation formula used once understanding is reached.

  2. Wayar nan ___ (ta/na) Amina ce.
  3. Amina ta tambaye ___ (shi/ta) farashin gyara.
  4. ___ (Za a/Ina) gyara wayarki gobe.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Amina ta kawo wayarta da ta lalace zuwa shagon Sule.

(Setting: Amina brings her broken phone to Sule's stall.)

AMINA Sannu, Sule. Wayata ta lalace. Ba ta da wuta.Hello, Sule. My phone is broken. It has no power.
SULE Sannu, Amina. Bari na duba ta.Hello, Amina. Let me look at it.
SULE Battirinta ne matsalar. Za a gyara ta, amma za ki biya kuɗin battiri sabo.The battery is the problem. It will be repaired, but you'll pay for a new battery.
AMINA Nawa ne farashin gyara?How much is the repair price?
SULE Dubu biyu ne, kuma za a gama gyaranta cikin sa'a ɗaya.It's two thousand, and the repair will be finished within one hour.
AMINA Don Allah, ka sake maimaita farashin? Ban gane sosai ba.Please, could you repeat the price? I didn't understand well.
SULE Dubu biyu. Za ki biya bayan gyaran.Two thousand. You'll pay after the repair.
AMINA To, na fahimta. Za ka iya rage farashin kaɗan? Kwastoma ce ta kullum.Okay, I understand. Can you lower the price a little? I'm a regular customer.
SULE To, saboda kai kwastoma ne na kullum, za ki biya dubu ɗaya da rabi kawai.Okay, because you're a regular customer, you'll only pay one thousand five hundred.

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. What is wrong with Amina's phone?
  2. What is the original price, and how much does Amina end up paying?
  3. What repair formula does Amina use, and why?
  4. How long will the repair take?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Small enterprise and the market economy

Small stalls and shops offering repair, retail, and personal services are a significant part of daily economic life in many Hausa-speaking towns and cities, but the scale, formality, and organization of such enterprises vary widely by location, capital, and family circumstance. Sule's and Zainab's businesses represent individual, plausible situations rather than a description of how all traders in Hausa-speaking communities operate.

Regional Variation — Bargaining norms

The degree to which prices are negotiable, and the phrases used to request a discount, can differ between markets, shop types, and regions, and even between individual sellers within the same market. This chapter's negotiation formulas reflect a common, general pattern and should be treated as a starting point rather than a fixed rule for every setting.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Wrong gender on the possessive linker

Error: *Shagon nan ta Sule ne. (using the feminine linker ta with the masculine noun shago)

Correction: Shagon nan na Sule ne. — Choose na or ta according to the gender of the noun being possessed, not the gender of the possessor.

Common Mistake — Dropping the subject marker before za

Error: *Za gyara wayarki gobe. (missing subject marker)

Correction: Za a gyara wayarki gobe. — The future-time pattern requires a subject marker between za and the verb; za alone is not sufficient.

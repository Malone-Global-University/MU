Bita 3: Bayani da Muhawara

B1 Development · Unit III Assessment · Speak and Write Hausa: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse
Unit III Integrated Assessment — Chapters 11-14. This chapter does not introduce new vocabulary or grammar. It integrates media reports, opinions, community issues, and moderated discussion, and formally assesses negation, prepositions/relational nouns, and focus/contrast with ne/ce at the Consolidated level, together with the opinion, moderating-tone, cause, and response formulas.

Chapter Overview

Chapter 15 closes Unit III. It does not add new vocabulary, grammar, or formulaic language. Instead, it brings together the culture and religion vocabulary of Chapter 11, the media-report skills of Chapter 12, the opinion and moderating-tone formulas of Chapter 13, and the community-problem, cause, and response formulas of Chapter 14, and evaluates learners' ability to combine them in connected, moderated discussion. Negation (ba... ba, babu), prepositions and relational nouns (domin, saboda, among others consolidated since Chapter 11), and focus/contrast with ne/ce are formally assessed at the Consolidated (C) level, consistent with the Volume III matrices at C15.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Understand a short media-style report or public announcement and summarize its main points.
  2. State an opinion and support it with a reason, using formulas from Chapter 13.
  3. Name a problem, give a cause, and propose a response, using the formulas from Chapter 14.
  4. Use negation (ba... ba, babu) accurately to disagree with a claim, a cause, or a proposed response.
  5. Use prepositions and relational nouns accurately to explain reasons, sources, and relationships between people, places, and events.
  6. Use focus/contrast with ne/ce with correct gender agreement to identify who or what is most responsible, most affected, or most urgent.
  7. Take part in a moderated discussion of a public issue, acknowledging a counterpoint respectfully with wataƙila or duk da haka.
  8. Read and listen to connected texts about public issues and answer factual, inferential, and evaluative questions.
  9. Write an organized paragraph that reports on an issue, gives an opinion, and proposes a response.

Communication Scenario

Aisha, Zainab, and Bashir have followed a short radio report on their neighborhood's rubbish-collection problem, the same issue they discussed in Chapter 14. A caller and a local official each state an opinion about the cause and the best response. Aisha, Zainab, and Bashir listen, then discuss the report together, drawing on everything they have practiced across Unit III.

  1. Domain: integrated review of culture/religion, media reports, opinions, and community problems from Chapters 11-14.
  2. Assessed grammar: negation (ba... ba, babu), prepositions/relational nouns, and focus/contrast (ne/ce), all at the Consolidated (C) level.
  3. Assessed formulaic language: opinion formulas (a ganina, ina ganin cewa), moderating-tone formulas (wataƙila, duk da haka), the cause formula (sanadin haka), and the response formula (mafita ita ce).
  4. All Chapter 1-14 vocabulary and grammar remain available for this assessment.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns Review

This section reviews, rather than introduces, the three systems assessed in this chapter: negation, prepositions/relational nouns, and focus/contrast with ne/ce, together with the opinion, moderating-tone, cause, and response formulas. No new grammar or formula appears here.

15.1 Review: focus/contrast with ne/ce

Language Building Block — Consolidated (review)
[Focused element] + ne/ce ...
Hukumar birni ce ke da babban alhaki.
It's the city authority that has the main responsibility.

Remember that ne agrees with masculine or plural focused elements, and ce agrees with feminine focused elements, as consolidated since Chapter 6 and practiced across Chapters 11-14.

15.2 Review: negation with ba... ba

Language Building Block — Consolidated (review)
Ba ... ba ne / Ba na jin cewa ... ba
Ba sanadin rashin kuɗi kaɗai ba ne; akwai kuma rashin tsari.
It isn't only a matter of lack of money; there is also poor planning.

Remember that the closing ba remains required even when a new clause follows, as reviewed in Chapter 14's Common Mistakes section.

15.3 Review: prepositions and relational nouns

Language Building Block — Consolidated (review)
domin / saboda + clause or noun phrase
Shara ba ta tattaru akai-akai, domin motocin hukuma kaɗan ne.
Rubbish isn't collected regularly, because the authority's vehicles are few.

Remember that domin and saboda both introduce a reason and may be used with a following clause, as consolidated across Chapters 11-14.

15.4 Review: opinion, moderating-tone, cause, and response formulas

Language Building Block — Formulaic (review)
a ganina / ina ganin cewa · wataƙila / duk da haka · sanadin haka · mafita ita ce
A ganina, sanadin haka shi ne rashin isassun motoci; duk da haka, mafita ita ce hukuma da al'umma su haɗa kai.
In my opinion, the cause of that is the lack of sufficient vehicles; even so, the solution is for the authority and the community to work together.

Use these formulas as fixed communicative units, as introduced in Chapters 13 and 14. Their internal grammar remains outside this chapter's assessment scope.

Atisayen bita — Review practice: Complete each sentence with the correct word or phrase.

  2. Ba sanadin rashin kuɗi kaɗai ___ (ba ne/ne); akwai kuma rashin tsari.
  3. Shara ba ta tattaru akai-akai, ___ (domin/ita ce) motocin hukuma kaɗan ne.
  4. ___ (Sanadin haka/Mafita ita ce) hukuma da al'umma su haɗa kai.
  5. ___ (Wataƙila/Sanadin) haka ne a wani bangare, amma ba na jin cewa motoci kaɗai ne matsalar.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Bayan sauraren gajeren rahoto a rediyo kan matsalar shara, Aisha, Zainab, da Bashir sun tattauna abin da suka ji.

(Setting: After listening to a short radio report on the rubbish problem, Aisha, Zainab, and Bashir discuss what they heard.)

AISHA Rahoton ya ce hukuma ce kawai za ta magance matsalar shara; ba na jin haka ne ke daidai.The report said only the authority will solve the rubbish problem; I don't think that's exactly right.
BASHIR Me ya sa? A ganinki, wa ke da alhaki?Why is that? In your opinion, who bears the responsibility?
AISHA Hukuma ce ke da babban alhaki, domin ita ce ta tsara tsarin, amma ƴan al'umma su ma suna da nasu bangaren.It's the authority that has the main responsibility, because it's the one that designed the system, but community members also have their own part.
ZAINAB Wataƙila haka ne. Kuma sanadin haka shi ne, a ganina, rashin isassun motoci a hannun hukuma.Maybe that's so. And the cause of that, in my opinion, is the lack of sufficient vehicles in the authority's hands.
BASHIR Ba sanadin motoci kaɗai ba ne; wasu ƴan al'umma ma ba sa jiran motar hukuma.It isn't only the vehicles' cause; some community members also don't wait for the authority's truck.
ZAINAB Duk da haka, mafita ita ce hukuma ta ƙara motoci tare da al'umma su ƙara hankali.Even so, the solution is for the authority to add vehicles while the community pays more attention.
AISHA Ba matsala. Kowa ya ba da ra'ayinsa; sai duka bangarorin sun haɗa kai, matsalar ba za ta warware ba.No problem. Everyone gave their opinion; unless every side works together, the problem won't be solved.

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. What claim in the radio report does Aisha disagree with?
  2. What reason does Aisha give for her disagreement?
  3. What cause does Zainab give, and how does Bashir respond to it?
  4. What response do the three friends move toward by the end of the dialogue?

Sauraro — Rahoto Kan Matsalar Shara a Gundumar (Listening: A Report on the Rubbish Problem in the District)

"Rahoto na yau ya shafi matsalar rashin tattara shara akai-akai a gundumarmu. Wani mai kira ga shirin rediyo ya ce sanadin haka shi ne rashin isassun motocin hukuma, kuma a ganinsa, hukuma ce kawai ke da alhakin gyara matsalar. Amma wata jami'ar hukuma ta ce ba sanadin motoci kaɗai ba ne; ta ce ƴan al'umma su ma suna da nasu bangaren, domin wasu ba sa jiran motar hukuma kafin su zubar da shara. A ganinta, mafita ita ce hukuma da ƴan al'umma su haɗa kai, kuma hukuma za ta ƙara motoci nan da watanni uku. Reading Assessment

Read the short passage below. Underline every focus/contrast phrase with ne/ce, circle every negation, and box every opinion, moderating-tone, cause, or response formula.

Karatu — Wasiƙar Ra'ayi Kan Rahoton Rediyo (Reading: An Opinion Letter Responding to the Radio Report)

Bayan sauraren rahoton rediyo kan matsalar shara a gundumarmu, ina son bayyana ra'ayina. A ganina, mai kiran da ya ce hukuma ce kawai ke da alhaki bai yi la'akari da bangaren al'umma ba. Ba na jin cewa hukuma ce kaɗai take da wannan alhaki ba; ƴan al'umma su ma suna buƙatar canja hali. Sanadin haka a ganina shi ne rashin isassun motoci tare da rashin kulawa daga wasu ƴan al'umma. Jami'ar hukumar ta gabatar da hujja mai kyau: mafita ita ce hukuma da al'umma su haɗa kai. Wataƙila hakan zai buƙaci lokaci, amma duk da haka, ina fatan gundumarmu za ta zama wurin da kowa ke jin daɗin zama.

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. Whose opinion does the writer disagree with, and how do you know?
  2. What cause does the writer give for the problem?
  3. What response does the writer support, and whose view does it come from?
  4. How does the writer soften the closing of the letter?

Writing Assessment

  1. Karanta wasiƙar ra'ayi da hankali. Reread the opinion letter and notice how it responds to a report, disagrees respectfully, gives a cause, and proposes a response.
  2. Tsarawa. Plan a short paragraph responding to a public issue familiar to you, noting the issue, your opinion, one opposing view you will acknowledge respectfully, a cause, and a proposed response.
  3. Rubuta. Write 120-160 words in Hausa, using at least one negation, one focus/contrast statement with ne/ce, one preposition or relational noun expressing a reason, the cause formula, the response formula, and one moderating-tone formula.
  4. Duba jerin bincike. Check your draft against the checklist below.
  5. Rubuta siffa ta ƙarshe. Produce a clean final version.

Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

  • The paragraph responds to a clearly named public issue.
  • At least one negative construction (ba... ba or babu) appears and is used accurately, including the closing ba.
  • At least one focus/contrast statement with ne/ce appears with correct gender agreement.
  • At least one preposition or relational noun (such as domin or saboda) expresses a reason.
  • The cause formula (sanadin haka) and the response formula (mafita ita ce) both appear.
  • At least one moderating-tone formula (wataƙila or duk da haka) appears.
  • A different or opposing view is acknowledged respectfully.
  • Vocabulary and grammar remain within the cumulative Chapter 1-15 scope; no unscheduled forms appear.
  • Special characters (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ, ƴ) and apostrophe/glottal conventions are used accurately.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Discussing public issues respectfully

As reviewed across Chapters 11-14, discussions of public issues in many Hausa-speaking communities often move between acknowledging authority and recognizing shared community responsibility. Moderated disagreement, softened with formulas such as wataƙila and duk da haka, allows speakers to hold different opinions while keeping the conversation cooperative. Treat this pattern as one common and useful approach rather than the only acceptable one.

Regional Variation — Reporting and discussing public issues

Media reports, official statements, and everyday discussion of public issues can differ in register and vocabulary across regions, generations, and media contexts. The formulas and patterns reviewed in this chapter remain a reliable, widely usable default appropriate to this course's productive standard.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing sanadin haka with mafita ita ce

Error: *Sanadin haka shi ne hukuma da al'umma su haɗa kai. (using the cause formula to introduce a response)

Correction: Mafita ita ce hukuma da al'umma su haɗa kai. — Reserve sanadin haka for causes and mafita ita ce for responses, as reviewed in Chapter 14.

Common Mistake — Wrong gender agreement on ne/ce

Error: *Hukuma ne ke da alhaki. (using ne with the feminine noun hukuma)

Correction: Hukuma ce ke da alhaki. — Match ce to feminine focused elements and ne to masculine or plural ones, as consolidated since Chapter 6.

Common Mistake — Disagreeing without acknowledging the other view

Error: *Ba haka ba ne. Ku yi kuskure. (blunt disagreement with no softening or acknowledgment)

Correction: Wataƙila haka ne a wani bangare, amma ba na jin cewa haka ne kawai. — Soften disagreement with wataƙila or duk da haka and acknowledge part of the other view before disagreeing, as modeled throughout Unit III.

Tambayoyin fahimta na tattaunawa (Dialogue comprehension)

  1. Aisha disagrees with the claim that only the authority will solve the rubbish problem.
  2. She reasons that community members also have a part to play.
  3. Zainab gives the cause as a lack of sufficient vehicles; Bashir responds that vehicles are not the only cause, since some community members also do not wait for the truck.
  4. They move toward the response that the authority should add vehicles while the community pays more attention.

Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

(1) The report describes irregular rubbish collection in the district. (2) The caller says the cause is a lack of sufficient authority vehicles and believes the authority alone is responsible. (3) The official disagrees that vehicles are the only cause and says community members share responsibility, since some do not wait for the truck. (4) The official proposes that the authority and community work together, with the authority adding vehicles within three months.

Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

  1. The writer disagrees with the caller who said only the authority is responsible; the writer explicitly says this view did not consider the community's part.
  2. The writer gives the cause as a lack of sufficient vehicles together with some community members' lack of care.
  3. The writer supports the response of cooperation between the authority and the community, a view attributed to the official.
  4. The writer softens the closing with wataƙila and duk da haka.

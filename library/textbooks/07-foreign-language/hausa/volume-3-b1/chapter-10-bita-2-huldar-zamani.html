Bita 2: Hulɗar Zamani

B1 Development · Unit II Integrated Assessment · Speak and Write Hausa: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse
Unit II Integrated Assessment — No new vocabulary or grammar is introduced in this chapter. Chapter 10 formally assesses the systems developed in Chapters 6 through 9: education, work/trade, technology, and money/economy vocabulary; the imperfective/habitual and formulaic future TAM frames; object pronouns; possessive constructions; noun gender/plural as lexical entries; and repair/negotiation formulas.

Chapter Overview

Chapter 10 is the Unit II integrated assessment. It does not introduce new vocabulary, grammar, pronunciation targets, or writing conventions. Instead, it brings together everything developed across Chapters 6 through 9 — education and training, work and enterprise, technology and communication, and money and household economy — and asks you to demonstrate consolidated, independent control of these systems in a single connected set of tasks. You will describe routines and plans using the imperfective/habitual and formulaic future TAM frames across a full range of subject markers, refer to people and things already mentioned using object pronouns, indicate ownership using possessive constructions with na/ta, apply noun gender and plural correctly as lexical facts, and use repair and clarification formulas naturally across several everyday domains. All Chapter 1-9 vocabulary and grammar remain in scope for this assessment.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use active vocabulary from the education, work/trade, technology, and money/economy domains accurately and in combination.
  2. Describe routines, plans, and choices across these four domains using the imperfective/habitual frame with a full range of subject markers.
  3. Use the formulaic future-time expression za + subject marker + verb to talk about near-term plans in any of the four domains.
  4. Use object pronouns accurately to refer to people, items, or amounts already mentioned in a conversation.
  5. Use possessive constructions with na/ta, correctly agreeing with the gender of the noun possessed.
  6. Apply correct gender and plural forms for nouns from the four Unit II domains.
  7. Use at least one repair or clarification formula naturally in a service, workplace, technology, or financial context.
  8. Listen to and read connected passages that combine two or more Unit II domains and answer factual and inferential questions.
  9. Speak about a real or invented situation that combines at least two Unit II domains, using consolidated grammar accurately.
  10. Write an organized paragraph that combines at least two Unit II domains and demonstrates the consolidated grammar of Chapters 6 through 9.

Communication Scenario

Halima has just finished a short training course and is starting a small tailoring business from home. She uses her phone to advertise her work and to message customers about prices and delivery times. At the end of each week, she looks at how much money she has earned and spent, and she is trying to save toward buying a better sewing machine. Her brother Ibrahim, who works at a bank, calls her to ask how the business is going, and the two of them talk about her training, her customers, her phone problems, and her small budget, all in the same conversation.

  1. Domains combined: education/training (Ch. 6), work/trade (Ch. 7), technology/communication (Ch. 8), and money/economy (Ch. 9).
  2. Assessed grammar: the imperfective/habitual frame, the formulaic future-time expression, object pronouns, and possessive constructions with na/ta, all used independently and in combination.
  3. Assessed system: noun gender and plural as lexical facts across the four domains.
  4. Assessed pragmatic system: repair and clarification formulas used naturally across domains.
  5. All Chapter 1-9 vocabulary and grammar remain active and available for use throughout this chapter's tasks.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns Review

This chapter introduces no new grammar. It formally assesses (A) the systems consolidated in Chapters 6 through 9: the imperfective/habitual TAM frame across a full range of subject markers, the formulaic future-time expression with za, object pronouns, possessive constructions with na/ta, and noun gender/plural as a lexical-entry system, together with repair and negotiation formulas extended across the four Unit II domains. This matches the Volume III scope for Chapter 10, the Unit II integrated assessment.

10.1 Review: the imperfective/habitual frame

Language Building Block — Review
ina/kana/tana/yana/muna/kuna/suna + [verbal noun]
Halima tana koyon ɗinki a horaswa.
Halima is learning tailoring at a training course.
Ibrahim yana aiki a banki kowace rana.
Ibrahim works at the bank every day.

Use this frame independently and accurately across all seven subject markers to describe routines in any of the four Unit II domains.

10.2 Review: the formulaic future-time expression

Language Building Block — Review
za + [subject marker] + [verb]
Za ta sayi sabuwar na'urar ɗinki idan ta samu ajiya sosai.
She will buy a new sewing machine when she has saved enough.

This pattern remains formulaic and high-frequency, as in Chapters 7 through 9. Full productive control of the future TAM system, including negative and dependent forms, remains scheduled for later volumes.

10.3 Review: object pronouns and possessive constructions

  • Ibrahim ya tambaye ta labarin kasuwancinta.Ibrahim asked her about her business.ta = feminine object pronoun
  • Na'urar nan ta Halima ce.This machine belongs to Halima.ta (feminine possessive linker, agrees with na'ura)

Continue choosing object pronouns and possessive linkers according to the gender and number of the noun they refer to, not the gender of the speaker or the possessor.

10.4 Review: noun gender/plural and repair formulas

Continue recording gender and plural for every active noun, and continue using repair and clarification formulas such as ban gane ba, ka/ki sake fadi, and to, na gane yanzu naturally when a price, a schedule, a message, or an instruction is unclear.

Atisayen ƙa'ida — Controlled review practice: Complete each sentence with the correct word or phrase.

  1. Halima ___ (tana/ina) sayar da tufafi da ta ɗinka.
  2. Na'urar nan ___ (na/ta) Halima ce.
  3. Ibrahim ya tambaye ___ (ta/shi) game da kuɗin shiga.
  4. ___ (Za ta/Tana) sayi sabuwar na'ura idan ta samu kuɗi.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Ibrahim ya kira Halima a waya don tambayarta yadda sabon kasuwancinta yake.

(Setting: Ibrahim calls Halima on the phone to ask how her new business is going.)

IBRAHIM Halima, yaya kasuwancin ɗinki yake tafiya bayan horaswarki?Halima, how is the tailoring business going after your training?
HALIMA Yana tafiya sosai. Ina samun abokan ciniki ta waya kowace rana, kuma ina sayar musu da tufafi da nake ɗinka.It's going well. I get customers through my phone every day, and I sell them clothes that I sew.
IBRAHIM Ban gane ba, ta yaya kake yin talla a waya?I don't understand, how do you advertise on your phone?
HALIMA Ina ɗaukar hoton tufafin, sa'an nan na aika saƙo ga mutane don su gani. Amma wani lokaci manhajar tana da matsala kuma saƙon ba ya kai ga kowa.I take a photo of the clothes, then I send a message so people can see. But sometimes the app has a problem and the message doesn't reach everyone.
IBRAHIM To, na gane yanzu. Kuma yaya lissafinki? Kina samun riba?Okay, I understand now. And how are your accounts? Are you making a profit?
HALIMA Ina ajiye kuɗi kaɗan kowace mako, kuma za ni sayi sabuwar na'urar ɗinki idan na samu ajiya sosai. Na'urar nan da nake da ita yanzu, ba ta Halima da ta koyi ɗinki jiya ba, ta yi tsufa.I save a little money every week, and I will buy a new sewing machine once I have saved enough. This machine I have now, not the one Halima learned to sew with yesterday, is old.
IBRAHIM Ka sake fadi, nawa kike buƙata don sabuwar na'urar?Say it again, how much do you need for the new machine?
HALIMA Kusan naira dubu ashirin. Ina fatan zan samu ta kafin wata biyu.About twenty thousand naira. I hope to have it before two months.
IBRAHIM Madalla. Ni ma zan iya taimaka miki idan wata matsalar kuɗi ta taso. Ka'in a banki, akwai hanyoyin ajiya masu sauƙi.Good. I can also help you if a money problem comes up. At the bank, there are simple savings options.

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. What does Halima do to advertise her tailoring business?
  2. What problem does she sometimes have with her phone?
  3. What is Halima saving money for, and roughly how much does she need?
  4. What repair formula does Ibrahim use, and why?

Karatu — Sana'ar Sadiq (Reading: Sadiq's Trade)

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Small enterprise and mobile technology

Small home-based enterprises supported by mobile phones and messaging apps are common across many Hausa-speaking communities, though the specific tools, platforms, and business practices vary by region, generation, income level, and personal preference. Halima and Sadiq's situations represent plausible contemporary examples rather than a single model that applies to every entrepreneur.

Regional Variation — Review, not new material

As noted in Chapters 6 through 9, currency names, technology platforms, and workplace or educational terms can differ between Nigeria, Niger, and other Hausa-speaking regions. This chapter reuses examples from earlier chapters and does not introduce new regional labels.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Review: wrong gender on the possessive linker

Error: *Na'urar nan na Halima ce. (using the masculine linker na with the feminine noun na'ura)

Correction: Na'urar nan ta Halima ce. — Choose na or ta according to the gender of the noun being possessed, not the gender of the possessor.

Common Mistake — Review: dropping the subject marker before za

Error: *Za sayi sabuwar na'ura. (missing subject marker)

Correction: Za ta sayi sabuwar na'ura. — The future-time pattern requires a subject marker between za and the verb.

Common Mistake — Mixing domains without connecting language

Error: Listing unrelated facts about training, work, technology, and money in separate short sentences with no connection between them.

Correction: Use recycled connecting words and reference (pronouns, possessives, sequence words such as sa'an nan and kuma) to link ideas across domains into a coherent short passage, as modeled in the Halima and Sadiq readings.

  4. He is saving toward opening a bigger shop; his brother says it will take time, but Sadiq believes patience and business bring success.

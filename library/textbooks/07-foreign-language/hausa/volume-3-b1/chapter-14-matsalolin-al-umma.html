Sashe na 3 — Unit III: Bayani da Muhawara (Public Information and Discussion)
14

Matsalolin Al'umma

B1 Development · Unit III, Chapter 4 · Speak and Write Hausa: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse
Continuing Unit III — Community Issues. This chapter introduces new vocabulary for describing problems, causes, stakeholders, and possible responses; consolidates negation, prepositions/relational nouns, and focus/contrast with ne/ce from Chapters 11-13; and extends the opinion and moderating-tone formulas from Chapter 13 into describing causes and proposing responses.

Chapter Overview

Chapter 14 continues Unit III by moving from discussing opinions about a single announcement (Chapter 13) to describing a community problem more broadly (matsalolin al'umma): naming the problem, explaining its cause, identifying who is affected or responsible, and proposing a response. The opinion-expression and moderating-tone formulas from Chapter 13 (a ganina, ina ganin cewa, wataƙila, duk da haka) remain active and now support describing causes and proposing solutions rather than only discussing a single announcement. Negation, prepositions/relational nouns, and focus/contrast with ne/ce continue at the Consolidated level introduced across Chapters 11-13. Two new formulaic openers for describing cause and proposing a response, sanadin haka and mafita ita ce, are introduced as fixed communicative units.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use core vocabulary for community problems, causes, stakeholders, and responses accurately, including correct gender and plural forms.
  2. Name a community problem and identify who it affects using vocabulary from this chapter and recycled vocabulary from earlier chapters.
  3. Explain the cause of a problem using domin/saboda and the new formula sanadin haka.
  4. Use negation (ba... ba, babu) to say that a proposed cause or solution does not hold.
  5. Use a focus/contrast statement with ne/ce to identify who is most responsible or which response is most urgent, at the Consolidated level.
  6. Propose a response to a problem using the new formula mafita ita ce, and soften disagreement about a proposed response using wataƙila or duk da haka.
  7. Listen to and read short descriptions of community problems and answer factual and inferential questions.
  8. Take part in a discussion identifying a problem, its cause, and a possible response, responding respectfully to a counterpoint.
  9. Write a short organized paragraph describing a community problem, its cause, and a proposed response.

Communication Scenario

Aisha, Zainab, and Bashir, having discussed the water project in Chapter 13, now turn to a broader community problem: irregular rubbish collection in their neighborhood. They identify the problem, discuss its likely cause, name who is affected, and propose different responses, again using softening formulas so the discussion stays respectful.

  1. Domain: community problems, causes, stakeholders, and responses, building on the opinion and discussion domain from Chapter 13.
  2. Consolidated grammar: negation (ba... ba, babu), prepositions/relational nouns (domin, saboda), and focus/contrast (ne/ce), all at the Consolidated (C) level.
  3. New formulaic language: cause formula (sanadin haka) and response formula (mafita ita ce), alongside the continuing opinion and moderating-tone formulas from Chapter 13.
  4. All Chapter 1-13 vocabulary and grammar remain active and available throughout this chapter's tasks.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Tone and length in matsala/matsaloli and the digraph in rashin aikin yi

Practice the tone and vowel-length shift between matsala (singular) and matsaloli (plural), keeping the second vowel of matsala distinct in length from the corresponding vowel in the plural. Continue distinguishing the digraph ts in matsala and tasiri's single s from earlier ts practice, and keep the glottalized y in rashin aikin yi crisp and separate from the preceding vowel.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter continues consolidating negation, prepositions/relational nouns, and focus/contrast with ne/ce (all Consolidated (C) per the Volume III matrices) in the domain of community problems, and introduces two new formulaic openers, sanadin haka and mafita ita ce, as Formulaic (F) units, following the series' Formulaic Language Policy. The opinion and moderating-tone formulas from Chapter 13 remain active throughout.

14.1 Consolidation: naming a problem and who it affects

Language Building Block — Consolidated, new domain
Matsalar [gundumar/unguwar] ita ce ...
Matsalar unguwarmu ita ce ba a tattara shara akai-akai ba.
Our neighborhood's problem is that rubbish is not collected regularly.

Use the focus/contrast pattern with ne/ce, consolidated since Chapter 6, to name a problem clearly before discussing its cause.

14.2 Formulaic: explaining a cause

Language Building Block — Formulaic
sanadin haka shi ne ... / ... domin/saboda ...
Sanadin haka shi ne rashin isassun motocin tattara shara.
The cause of that is the lack of sufficient rubbish-collection vehicles.
Shara ba ta tattaru akai-akai, domin motocin hukuma kaɗan ne.
Rubbish isn't collected regularly, because the authority's vehicles are few.

Use sanadin haka ("the cause of that is...") as a fixed opener for stating a cause, alongside domin/saboda, consolidated since Chapters 6-12. Learn sanadin haka as a complete communicative unit; its internal grammar will not be broken down formally in this chapter.

14.3 Consolidation: disagreeing about a cause with negation

Language Building Block — Consolidated, new domain
ba sanadin ... ba ne / ba na jin cewa ... ba
Ba sanadin rashin kuɗi kaɗai ba ne; akwai kuma rashin tsari.
It isn't only a matter of lack of money; there is also poor planning.

Ba... ba negation, consolidated since Chapter 6, now supports disagreement about the cause of a problem rather than only disagreement about an opinion.

14.4 Formulaic: proposing a response

Language Building Block — Formulaic
mafita ita ce ...
A ganina, mafita ita ce hukuma ta ƙara motocin tattara shara.
In my opinion, the solution is for the authority to add more rubbish-collection vehicles.

Use mafita ita ce ("the solution is...") together with the opinion formula a ganina from Chapter 13 to propose a response to a problem as a fixed communicative unit.

14.5 Consolidation: focus/contrast with ne/ce on responsibility

Language Building Block — Consolidated
[Focused element] + ne/ce ...
Bashir: Hukumar birni ce ke da alhakin fara aiki. Zainab: Wataƙila haka ne, amma ƴan al'umma ne ma za su iya taimakawa da tsaftace unguwar kansu.
Bashir: It's the city authority that has the responsibility to start acting. Zainab: Maybe that's so, but it's community members who can also help by cleaning their own neighborhood.

Continue using focus/contrast with ne/ce at the Consolidated level to identify who bears the main responsibility, softened where useful with wataƙila from Chapter 13.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Aisha, Zainab, da Bashir suna tattaunawa a kan matsalar rashin tattara shara akai-akai a unguwarsu.

(Setting: Aisha, Zainab, and Bashir discuss the problem of irregular rubbish collection in their neighborhood.)

ZAINAB Matsalar unguwarmu ita ce ba a tattara shara akai-akai ba; ta zauna kwanaki kafin motar hukuma ta zo.Our neighborhood's problem is that rubbish isn't collected regularly; it sits for days before the authority's truck comes.
BASHIR Sanadin haka shi ne, a ganina, rashin isassun motocin tattara shara a hannun hukumar birni.The cause of that, in my opinion, is the lack of sufficient rubbish-collection vehicles in the city authority's hands.
AISHA Wataƙila haka ne a wani bangare, amma ba na jin cewa motoci kaɗai ne matsalar.Maybe that's so in one respect, but I don't think vehicles are the only problem.
BASHIR Me ya sa? Ba sanadin motoci kaɗai ba ne?Why is that? Isn't it only the vehicles' cause?
AISHA A'a. Domin wasu ƴan al'umma ma ba sa jiran motar hukuma; suna zuban shara a kowanne wuri.No. Because some community members don't wait for the authority's truck either; they dump rubbish anywhere.
ZAINAB Duk da haka, hukumar birni ce ke da babban alhaki, domin ita ce ta tsara tsarin tattara shara.Even so, it's the city authority that has the main responsibility, because it's the one that designed the rubbish-collection system.
BASHIR A ganina, mafita ita ce hukuma ta ƙara motoci tare da ƴan al'umma su ƙara hankali kan zuban shara.In my opinion, the solution is for the authority to add vehicles while community members pay more attention about dumping rubbish.
AISHA Ba matsala. Kowa ya ba da hujjarsa; mafita ɗaya kaɗai bata isa ba, sai duka bangarorin sun taimaka.No problem. Everyone gave their argument; one solution alone isn't enough — every side must help.

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. What problem does Zainab describe?
  2. What cause does Bashir give?
  3. What additional cause does Aisha add?
  4. What response do Bashir and Aisha eventually agree could help?

Writing Workshop

  1. Karanta wasiƙar ra'ayi da hankali. Reread the opinion letter and notice how it names the problem, gives a cause, acknowledges another view, proposes a response, and closes with a moderating formula.
  2. Tsarawa. Plan a short paragraph about a community problem familiar to you, noting the problem, one cause, one opposing view you will acknowledge respectfully, and one proposed response.
  3. Rubuta. Write 100-140 words in Hausa describing the problem, using at least one cause formula, one response formula, one negation or focus/contrast statement, and one moderating-tone formula.
  4. Duba jerin bincike. Check your draft against the checklist below.
  5. Rubuta siffa ta ƙarshe. Produce a clean final version.

Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

  • The paragraph clearly names a specific community problem.
  • At least one cause formula (sanadin haka or domin/saboda) appears.
  • At least one response formula (mafita ita ce) appears.
  • At least one negative construction (ba... ba or babu) or focus/contrast statement with ne/ce appears.
  • At least one moderating-tone formula (wataƙila or duk da haka) appears.
  • A different or opposing view about the cause or response is acknowledged respectfully.
  • Vocabulary and grammar remain within the cumulative Chapter 1-14 scope; no unscheduled forms appear.
  • Special characters (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ, ƴ) and apostrophe/glottal conventions are used accurately.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Sharing responsibility for community problems

In many Hausa-speaking communities, discussions of local problems often distribute responsibility across more than one party, such as an authority and the residents themselves, rather than assigning blame to a single actor. How responsibility is discussed can vary by relationship, setting, and the seriousness of the problem; treat the pattern in this chapter as one common and useful approach rather than the only acceptable one.

Regional Variation — Naming problems and proposing solutions

Sanadin haka and mafita ita ce are widely understood ways to introduce a cause or a response, but speakers in different regions, generations, and media contexts may prefer other expressions or add additional detail before proposing a solution. Treat the formulas in this chapter as a reliable, widely usable default rather than the only correct choice.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing sanadi (cause) with mafita (solution)

Error: *Sanadin haka shi ne hukuma ta ƙara motoci. (using sanadin haka to introduce a proposed response instead of a cause)

Correction: Mafita ita ce hukuma ta ƙara motoci. — Use sanadin haka only to introduce a cause, and mafita ita ce to introduce a response.

Common Mistake — Assigning all responsibility to one party without support

Error: *Hukuma ce kawai ke da alhakin duk matsalolin unguwar. (assigning total blame without a reason or acknowledgment of other views)

Correction: Hukuma ce ke da babban alhaki, domin ita ce ta tsara tsarin, amma ƴan al'umma su ma suna da nasu bangaren. — Support a responsibility claim with a reason and acknowledge other stakeholders, following the pattern modeled in the Model Dialogue.

Common Mistake — Dropping the closing ba when disagreeing about a cause

Error: *Ba sanadin rashin kuɗi kaɗai ba ne akwai kuma rashin tsari. (missing the closing ba after the negated clause)

Correction: Ba sanadin rashin kuɗi kaɗai ba ne; akwai kuma rashin tsari. — As consolidated since earlier chapters, the closing ba remains required, and a pause or punctuation mark can separate the negated clause from the following statement.

Tambayoyin fahimta na tattaunawa (Dialogue comprehension)

  1. Zainab describes irregular rubbish collection in their neighborhood.
  2. Bashir gives the cause as a lack of sufficient collection vehicles.
  3. Aisha adds that some community members also dump rubbish without waiting for the truck.
  4. They agree the authority should add vehicles while community members pay more attention to how they dispose of rubbish.

Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

(1) The speaker describes unemployment among young people. (2) The cause given is that few trades require the training many young people have received. (3) No, the speaker also thinks businesses have a role. (4) The proposed solution is combining training among businesses and the authority.

Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

  1. The writer describes poor cleanliness in the market, especially on weekends.
  2. The cause given is that many traders do not properly follow rubbish-disposal rules.
  3. The writer disagrees with those who say only the authority should solve it through more staff; the writer says traders also share responsibility.
  4. The writer proposes that the authority and traders cooperate, softening the closing with wataƙila and duk da haka.

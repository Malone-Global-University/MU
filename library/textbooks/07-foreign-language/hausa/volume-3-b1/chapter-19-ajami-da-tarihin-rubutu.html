Ajami da Tarihin Rubutu

B1–B2 Development · Unit IV, Chapter 4 · Speak and Write Hausa: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Overview

Chapter 19 continues Unit IV by turning from expressive style to writing-system heritage. Building on the cultural awareness developed in Chapter 18, this chapter builds receptive cultural literacy around Ajami, the Arabic-derived script historically and still used to write Hausa, alongside Boko, the modern Latin-based orthography used as the productive default throughout this series. No new grammar system is introduced this chapter; Ajami recognition is treated as receptive, comparative, cultural-literacy material, consistent with the Volume III Ajami Recognition/Script Heritage matrix, which remains Consolidated at C19 for general recognition while Specialized Ajami receptive literacy remains Receptive at this stage in the sequence.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify Ajami as a historically and culturally important Hausa writing tradition, distinct from Boko.
  2. Recognize that Ajami is based on the Arabic script and is written right to left.
  3. Compare a short Boko text with its Ajami equivalent and identify general correspondences without full productive command.
  4. Describe, in general terms, the historical, religious, literary, and scholarly contexts in which Ajami has been and continues to be used.
  5. Explain that Boko and Ajami exist together in Hausa-speaking communities rather than one replacing the other.
  6. Continue producing accurate, connected Hausa in Boko using Chapter 1-18 grammar and vocabulary.
  7. Discuss, at a general level, why a community might maintain more than one writing system for the same language.

Communication Scenario

Aisha visits her grandmother, who keeps an old notebook containing prayers and family notes written in Ajami. Aisha, who reads and writes only Boko, asks her grandmother about the notebook. Her grandmother explains what Ajami is, when she learned it, and why she still values it, even though Aisha's generation mostly reads and writes Hausa in Boko.

  1. Domain: writing-system heritage, family memory, and respectful cross-generational discussion, continuing Unit IV's themes of expression and connected discourse.
  2. Developed skill: recognizing Ajami as a script, comparing it with Boko, and discussing its cultural role receptively, consolidating the Ajami Recognition/Script Heritage matrix at C19.
  3. Recycled grammar in context: connected narration, reference tracking, and register/pragmatics, all Consolidated since Chapters 16-18.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Reading names for scripts and books aloud

Practice the vowel length in ajami and tarihi, and keep the special consonant in littafi clear. When reading aloud about Ajami and Boko, maintain natural Hausa phrase rhythm rather than pausing awkwardly at each script name, since these words function as ordinary nouns in the sentence.

Ajami and Writing Heritage

This chapter introduces no new grammar system. Ajami is taught as receptive, comparative, cultural-literacy content: learners recognize Ajami as a script, compare it in general terms with Boko, and discuss its historical and cultural role, but are not required to read or write Ajami productively. This is consistent with the Volume III Ajami Recognition/Script Heritage matrix (general recognition Consolidated at C19; Specialized Ajami receptive literacy remains Receptive at this stage) and the Dialect/Standard Hausa matrix's treatment of script heritage as a labeled, non-stigmatized tradition.

19.1 Introduced: what Ajami is

Ajami refers to the practice of writing Hausa using a version of the Arabic script, adapted with extra marks and letter combinations to represent Hausa sounds not found in Arabic. Ajami has a long history in Hausa-speaking communities, especially in religious education, poetry, historical record-keeping, and correspondence. Boko, the Latin-based script used throughout this textbook, became the dominant script for education, government, and media during the twentieth century, but Ajami did not disappear. Many communities, families, and religious scholars continue to use, read, and value Ajami today.

Language Note — Two scripts, one language

Boko and Ajami are two different ways of writing the same spoken language, Hausa. A word written in Boko and the same word written in Ajami represent the same sounds and the same meaning. Using two scripts for one language is sometimes called digraphia. This chapter treats digraphia as a normal, respected feature of Hausa literacy history, not as a sign that one script is more "real" Hausa than the other.

19.3 Formulaic: talking about scripts and heritage

  • Kakata ta san rubutun ajami sosai. My grandmother knows Ajami writing very well.
  • Muna koyon boko a makaranta, amma wasu suna koyon ajami a gida ko a makarantar allo. We learn Boko at school, but some people learn Ajami at home or at a Quranic school.
  • Ajami da boko dukansu rubutun Hausa ne. Ajami and Boko are both ways of writing Hausa.

    • These sentences recycle familiar predicate and comparison patterns from earlier chapters; no new sentence structure is introduced here.

    Model Dialogue

    Yanayin hali: Aisha ta ziyarci kakarta kuma ta lura da wani tsohon littafi da aka rubuta da ajami.

    (Setting: Aisha visits her grandmother and notices an old notebook written in Ajami.)

    AISHA Kaka, wane irin rubutu ne wannan? Ban gane haruffan ba.Grandmother, what kind of writing is this? I don't recognize the letters.
    KAKA Ana kiran wannan ajami, 'ya. Rubutun Hausa ne, amma da haruffan larabci.This is called Ajami, my child. It is Hausa writing, but with Arabic letters.
    AISHA To, ba boko ba ne kenan? Amma harshen guda ɗaya ne, ko ba haka ba?So it's not Boko then? But it's the same language, isn't it?
    KAKA Gaskiya kika faɗa. Hausa guda ɗaya ce; kawai muna da hanyoyi biyu na rubuta ta.You've spoken the truth. It's one Hausa language; we just have two ways of writing it.
    AISHA Ina kika koyi ajami, kaka?Where did you learn Ajami, Grandmother?
    KAKA Na koya a makarantar allo tun ina yarinya, don karatun addu'o'i da rubuce-rubucen iyali. Har yanzu ina son wannan gadon.I learned it at Quranic school when I was a girl, for reading prayers and family writings. I still cherish this heritage.

    Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

    1. What does the grandmother call the writing in the notebook?
    2. According to the grandmother, is Ajami a different language from Hausa, or a different way of writing the same language?
    3. Where and why did the grandmother learn Ajami?

    • Ajami is described accurately as a Hausa writing tradition based on the Arabic script, not as a separate language.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Ajami in religious and family life

    Ajami has long been associated with Islamic scholarship, Quranic education, poetry, and family record-keeping in many Hausa-speaking communities. This association is significant and widespread, but it does not mean Ajami is used only for religious purposes; historically it has also served correspondence, historical chronicles, and literary composition. Not every Hausa-speaking family maintains active Ajami literacy today, and that varies by region, generation, and educational background.

    Regional Variation — Script use across communities

    The balance between Boko and Ajami literacy differs across regions and generations. In some communities, Ajami remains actively taught and used; in others, it survives mainly in older documents, religious schools, or specialized scholarship. Boko remains the dominant script for government, education, and media across nearly all Hausa-speaking regions. Treat both scripts as part of a shared heritage rather than assuming uniform practice everywhere.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Treating Ajami and Boko as different languages

    Error: Assuming that a text written in Ajami is written in a different language from Hausa written in Boko.

    Correction: Remember that Ajami and Boko are two scripts for the same Hausa language, as shown in Section 19.1 and the Model Dialogue. The words and grammar are Hausa in both cases; only the script differs.

    Common Mistake — Describing Ajami as obsolete or purely historical

    Error: Stating that Ajami is no longer used or that it is only found in old books.

    Correction: Ajami continues to be read, written, and valued by many people today, particularly in religious, family, and scholarly contexts, as described in Section 19.1 and the Culture and Communication notes. Avoid presenting it as extinct or purely decorative.

    Common Mistake — Attempting full Ajami production at this stage

    Error: Trying to write original sentences in Ajami script as a required chapter task.

    Correction: This chapter's Ajami content is receptive and comparative only. Continue writing your own Hausa text in Boko, as in the Writing Workshop, while using this chapter's content to talk about Ajami accurately.

    Answer Key / Amsoshi

    Atisayen ƙa'ida (Script direction, Section 19.2)

    Ajami is read right to left; Boko is read left to right. Similarity: both represent the same spoken Hausa language and its sounds. Difference: they use different alphabets and different writing directions.

    Tambayoyin fahimta na tattaunawa (Dialogue comprehension)

    1. The grandmother calls the writing "ajami."
    2. Ajami is a different way of writing the same Hausa language, not a different language.
    3. She learned it at Quranic school as a girl, in order to read prayers and family writings.

    Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

    (1) The family has books written in Ajami, especially prayer books and books belonging to the speaker's grandparents. (2) The speaker themselves learned only Boko at school. (3) The speaker wants their child to understand that Hausa has two ways of writing it, not only one.

    Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

    1. Malam Sule teaches the children prayers and Ajami writing.
    2. At the other school in the morning, the children learn Boko for modern reading and writing.
    3. No, both scripts should be kept; Malam Sule states that both writing systems are useful and that one is not abandoned for the other.

    Sashi na B (Chapter Review Ajami and Boko)

    1. Sample: Ajami is a way of writing Hausa using an adapted form of the Arabic script. Boko is the modern Latin-based script used for most education, government, and media today. Both represent the same spoken Hausa language.
    2. Ajami is read right to left; Boko is read left to right.
    3. Sample: religious education (Quranic schools) and family record-keeping or correspondence.
    4. Sample: Ajami and Boko both represent the Hausa language; only the script and, in some cases, the learning context differ, so calling Ajami "a different language" would be inaccurate.

    Sashi na C (Sample writing)

    Ajami wani rubutu ne na Hausa da ake amfani da haruffan larabci wajen rubuta shi. Boko kuwa rubutu ne na zamani da ake koyarwa a makarantu. Kakannina sun san ajami sosai, amma ni na koyi boko ne kawai. Ko da yake haka ne, ina son sanin cewa Hausa tana da hanyoyi biyu na rubutu, kuma dukansu suna da muhimmanci.

    Other accurate, well-formed passages meeting the stated requirements are equally acceptable.

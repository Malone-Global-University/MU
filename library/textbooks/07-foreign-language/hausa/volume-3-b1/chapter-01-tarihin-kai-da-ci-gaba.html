Tarihin Kai da Ci Gaba

Opening Chapter, B1 Entry Point · Speak and Write Hausa: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Overview

Chapter 1 opens Volume III: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse. This chapter does not begin from zero. It carries forward the pronouns, TAM/aspect, negation, focus, and reference-tracking systems consolidated at the end of Volume II and puts them to work on a new communicative task: narrating a personal history. You will describe where you are from, how your life has changed, your education and work background, and your plans for continued growth, linking these ideas into connected paragraphs rather than isolated sentences. This chapter also reviews and consolidates independent pronouns and identity clauses at a fuller, more independent level, and begins the Unit I sequence that will be formally assessed in Chapter 5.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Introduce yourself with a connected account of background, origin, and current situation.
  2. Narrate change over time using completive forms together with the connectors sa'an nan, sannan, and daga baya.
  3. Describe your education and work history using consolidated TAM/aspect forms carried forward from Volume II.
  4. State plans for continued development using the future/intention expressions consolidated since Volume II.
  5. Ask and answer personal-history questions with a partner or instructor.
  6. Track reference to yourself and other people across a multi-sentence personal narration without unnecessary repetition.
  7. Ask for clarification or repetition when a personal-history conversation is not understood.
  8. Read and interpret a short written self-introduction or biographical note.
  9. Write a short, organized paragraph narrating personal background and development.
  10. Self-assess readiness to expand this narration into fuller connected discourse later in Unit I.

Communication Scenario

Halima has just joined a community adult-education program in her town. On the first evening, the instructor asks each new student to introduce themselves to the group: where they are from, what they have done so far in life, and what they hope to do next. Halima's classmate Sule, who joined the program the previous term, introduces himself first as a model, and then the instructor invites the new students, including Halima, to do the same. This chapter follows their introductions and asks you to prepare and deliver a similar one of your own.

  1. No new vocabulary domain beyond personal-history and education/work terms is introduced in this chapter; core greetings, identity clauses, and pronouns are carried forward from Volume II and expanded for independent use.
  2. No new grammar system is introduced. This chapter consolidates independent pronouns, subject-reference patterns, completive/perfective narration, and the future/intention expressions already active since Volume II, and begins scheduling reference tracking at the fuller Volume III level.
  3. This chapter opens Unit I, which will be formally assessed in Chapter 5.
  4. Vocabulary and grammar from Volume I and Volume II remain fully active and are expected to appear accurately throughout this chapter's tasks.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Tone and vowel length in personal-history vocabulary

This chapter's pronunciation focus consolidates tone and vowel-length control in a new but recycled-pattern vocabulary set. Pay particular attention to the long vowel in sana'a (trade, profession) and the falling tone on the second syllable of gogewa (experience), both of which affect intelligibility in a longer spoken introduction. As in Chapter 20 of Volume II, keep an even syllable rhythm across full sentences, and do not let tone or vowel length flatten toward the end of a long spoken self-introduction. Practice saying your name, place of origin, and one life event aloud as a single connected unit before moving to a full introduction.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new grammar system. It consolidates independent pronouns, subject-reference patterns, and completive narration at a fuller level of independence, and applies the future/intention expressions and connectors already active since Volume II to a new communicative task: connected personal narration.

1.1 Reviewing: Independent pronouns in extended self-reference

A personal introduction relies heavily on first-person reference, but a full introduction also refers to other people — parents, teachers, classmates — and must distinguish clearly between them. Review the independent pronouns and subject-reference forms before narrating your own history.

  • Ni Halima ce, kuma ina zaune a nan tun ƙuruciyata.I am Halima, and I have lived here since my childhood.
  • Iyayena sun yi ƙaura zuwa wannan gari kafin a haife ni.My parents moved to this town before I was born.

1.2 Reviewing: Narrating change with completive forms and connectors

Language Building Block — Sequencing a Personal History
na/ta + [completive verb] + [place/time], sa'an nan/sannan + [completive verb] + [next stage] daga baya, na/ta + [completive verb] + [later development]
Na yi karatun firamare a nan, sa'an nan na koma birni domin sakandare.
I did primary school here, then I moved to the city for secondary school.
Daga baya, na koma nan domin fara sana'ata.
Later, I came back here in order to start my trade.

As in Volume II, sa'an nan and sannan link two completed events in sequence, and daga baya introduces a further, later stage. These connectors are consolidated, not new, and are expected to appear accurately throughout this chapter's narration tasks.

1.3 Reviewing: Stating plans for continued development

  • Yanzu ina son ci gaba da karatuna, domin na sami ƙarin sana'a.Now I want to continue my studies, in order to gain further skills.
  • Watakila zan koma makaranta shekara mai zuwa, idan na sami kuɗi.Perhaps I will return to school next year, if I get the money.

The future/intention marker zan/za ta, the hedging expression watakila, and the condition connector idan are all recycled from Volume II and are used here to project a personal history forward into stated plans.

1.4 Introducing: Reference tracking in a personal narration

As your personal narration grows longer, avoid repeating your own name or another person's name in every sentence. Once you have been identified, continue with a pronoun or an omitted subject, exactly as practiced with the community group's name in Volume II.

  • Sule ya girma a wani ƙauye kusa da Kano. Ya yi karatun firamare a can, sa'an nan ya koma birni domin sana'a.Sule grew up in a village near Kano. He did primary school there, then he moved to the city for work."Sule" named once; "ya" (he) tracks the reference across two following clauses

Atisayen ƙa'ida — Grammar practice: Complete each sentence with the word or expression that fits.

  1. Na yi karatun firamare a garinmu, ___ (sa'an nan/domin) na koma birni.
  2. ___ (Daga baya/Idan) na koma gida, na fara sana'ata.
  3. ___ (Watakila/Tunda) zan ci gaba da karatu, idan na sami kuɗi.
  4. Aminu ya girma a birni. ___ (Aminu/Ya) yi karatun sakandare a can.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Dare na farko a wani shirin ilimin manya, malami ya tambayi ɗalibai sabbi da kansu su gabatar da tarihinsu ga ƙungiyar.

(Setting: The first evening of an adult-education program, the instructor asks the new students to introduce their personal history to the group.)

MALAMI Barka da zuwa. Kafin mu fara, ina son kowa ya gabatar da kansa a taƙaice. Sule, za ka fara?Welcome. Before we start, I would like everyone to introduce themselves briefly. Sule, will you begin?
SULE To, ni Sule ne. Na girma a wani ƙauye kusa da Kano, sa'an nan na koma birni domin sana'ar aikin gini.Alright, I am Sule. I grew up in a village near Kano, then I moved to the city for the building trade.
MALAMI Kai kaɗai ka koma birni, ko tare da iyalinka?Did you move to the city alone, or together with your family?
SULE Ni kaɗai na koma da farko, sa'an nan iyalina suka biyo daga baya. Yanzu ina son ci gaba da karatuna a wannan shiri.I moved alone at first, then my family followed later. Now I want to continue my studies in this program.
MALAMI Na gode, Sule. Halima, ke fa?Thank you, Sule. Halima, what about you?
HALIMA Ni Halima ce. Ina zaune a wannan gari tun ƙuruciyata, kuma na yi karatun firamare da sakandare a nan.I am Halima. I have lived in this town since my childhood, and I did both primary and secondary school here.
MALAMI Kai wuce sakandare, me kika yi?After secondary school, what did you do?
HALIMA Ban gama ba, don haka na fara sana'ar ɗinki. Yanzu watakila zan koma makaranta shekara mai zuwa, idan na sami kuɗi.I didn't finish, so I started tailoring. Now perhaps I will return to school next year, if I get the money.
MALAMI Da fatan za mu iya taimaka wa dukkanku ku cimma burinku.It is hoped we can help all of you reach your goals.

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. Where did Sule grow up, and what did he move to the city to do?
  2. Did Sule's family move with him or after him?
  3. Where did Halima do her schooling?
  4. Why did Halima start tailoring instead of continuing school?
  5. What condition does Halima attach to her plan to return to school?

Writing Workshop

  1. Karanta labarin da hankali. Reread Amina's biography and notice how it links birthplace, schooling, work, and future plans into one connected narration.
  2. Tsarawa. Plan a short narration of your own personal history (real or invented), from birthplace and upbringing through schooling, work, and one plan for continued development.

Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

  • The narration opens with a clear self-identification.
  • At least one completive-narration sequence uses sa'an nan or daga baya.
  • Education or work background is described accurately.
  • A future plan is expressed with zan/za ta or watakila.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Sharing personal history in group settings

Introducing oneself with a brief personal history is common in classroom, workplace, and community settings, but the level of detail considered appropriate varies. Some speakers readily mention family background and place of origin; others prefer to keep such details brief in a first meeting. Following the lead of the group or instructor, as Sule and Halima do in this chapter's dialogue, is a reasonable general strategy, though practices differ by community, setting, and relationship.

Regional Variation — Education and work vocabulary across contexts

Terms for school stages, trades, and qualifications can vary between Nigerian and Nigerien educational systems and between rural and urban contexts. This chapter uses widely understood general vocabulary; more specific institutional or regional terms are introduced for recognition in later chapters when the topic requires them.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Repeating a name instead of tracking reference

Error: *Aminu ya girma a birni. Aminu ya yi karatun sakandare a can. Aminu ya fara sana'ar kasuwanci. (repeating the full name in every sentence of a personal narration)

Correction: Aminu ya girma a birni, sa'an nan ya yi karatun sakandare a can, kuma daga baya ya fara sana'ar kasuwanci. — Once a person has been named, continue with a pronoun or an omitted subject, exactly as practiced with group and event names in Volume II.

Common Mistake — Using a future marker for a completed event

Error: *Zan yi karatun firamare a garinmu, sa'an nan zan koma birni. (using the future marker zan to narrate events that have already happened)

Correction: Na yi karatun firamare a garinmu, sa'an nan na koma birni. — A personal-history narration about the past uses completive forms; the future/intention marker zan/za ta is reserved for genuinely future plans, such as returning to school next year.

8

Fasaha da Sadarwa

B1 Development · Unit II, Chapter 3 · Speak and Write Hausa: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse
Unit II Continues — Systems consolidated further: imperfective/habitual and formulaic future TAM frames, object pronouns, possessive constructions, noun gender/plural as lexical entries, market/service repair formulas extended to digital-problem contexts.

Chapter Overview

Chapter 8 continues Unit II and moves the conversation from work and trade into technology and communication. You will learn to talk about phones, mobile credit, messages, the internet, and common digital problems, while consolidating the systems developed in Chapter 7. The imperfective/habitual frame and the formulaic future-time expression with za are now expected to be used with growing independence across a wider range of everyday situations, rather than only in fixed, tightly scaffolded frames. Object pronouns and possessive constructions continue as independently productive systems. Noun gender and plural continue to be recorded as lexical facts for every new noun, and the repair and negotiation formulas from Chapter 6 and Chapter 7 are recycled and extended into digital-problem contexts, such as a phone that will not turn on or a message that did not send. All vocabulary and grammar from Chapters 1 through 7 remain active.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify and use at least twelve new active vocabulary items related to phones, media, and digital communication.
  2. Describe a communication routine or problem using recycled location, possession, and quantity clauses.
  3. Use object pronouns with confidence to refer to devices, messages, and people already mentioned in a conversation.
  4. Use possessive constructions with na/ta to indicate ownership of a phone, account, or device.
  5. Use the imperfective/habitual frame across a fuller range of subject markers to describe communication routines.
  6. Use the formulaic future-time pattern za a/za ka/za ki/za mu + verb to talk about sending, calling, or fixing something soon.
  7. Record the gender and plural form of new technology-related nouns as part of learning them.
  8. Use repair and clarification formulas in a digital-problem context, such as asking someone to resend a message or explaining a network problem.
  9. Read a short passage about a communication problem and answer factual and inferential questions.
  10. Write a short paragraph describing a communication routine or problem, real or invented.

Communication Scenario

Halima calls her friend Ibrahim, but the call keeps dropping because the network is weak. She sends him a text message instead, but he says it did not arrive. Ibrahim explains that his phone has almost no credit and that he is having trouble connecting to the internet at home. Halima offers to send him some mobile credit and asks him to call her back once his phone is working again. Later, Halima tells her sister about the frustrating afternoon of trying to reach Ibrahim.

  1. New vocabulary domain: phones, mobile credit, messages, the internet, and digital communication problems.
  2. Consolidated grammar: fuller imperfective/habitual frame, the formulaic future-time expression, object pronouns, and possessive constructions.
  3. Continued system: noun gender and plural recorded as part of each new vocabulary entry.
  4. Extended pragmatic system: repair and clarification formulas applied to digital-problem contexts.
  5. All Chapter 1-7 vocabulary and grammar remain active and should be recycled throughout this chapter's tasks.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Consonant clusters in borrowed technology words

Several new words in this chapter, such as intanet and manhajar sadarwa, are borrowed or built from borrowed technology vocabulary and contain consonant patterns that differ from older, fully nativized Hausa words. This chapter does not introduce a new tone or vowel-length rule, but it does ask you to notice that borrowed words are pronounced according to Hausa syllable structure and rhythm, not according to their pronunciation in the source language, consistent with the borrowing policy established in the Hausa master instructions. Practice saying intanet and manhajar sadarwa slowly, breaking each into its Hausa syllables.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter consolidates (C) the systems developed in Chapters 6 and 7: the imperfective/habitual TAM frame is expected to be used with growing independence across a fuller range of subject markers, the formulaic future-time expression with za continues as a reliable high-frequency pattern, object pronouns and possessive constructions are expected to be produced independently rather than only in guided practice, noun gender/plural continues as a lexical-entry system, and repair/negotiation formulas extend into digital-problem contexts. No new predicate type, negative TAM paradigm, or focus structure is introduced in this chapter, consistent with the Volume III scope for Chapter 8.

8.1 Consolidating the imperfective/habitual frame

Language Building Block — Describing Communication Routines
ina/kana/tana/yana/muna/kuna/suna + [verbal noun]
Ina aika saƙonni ga abokaina kullum da yamma.
I send messages to my friends every evening.
Suna kiran junansu ta manhajar sadarwa kowace rana.
They call each other through the messaging app every day.

Chapter 7 introduced the full subject-marker set for this frame (ina/kana/tana/yana/muna/kuna/suna). This chapter asks you to use the frame independently, without a model sentence in front of you, to describe your own and other people's communication habits.

8.2 The formulaic future-time expression, continued

Language Building Block — Talking About What Will Happen Soon
za + [subject marker] + [verb]
Za ki aika mini kuɗin waya?
Will you send me some mobile credit?
Za a gyara hanyar sadarwa da wuri.
The network will be fixed soon.

This pattern remains formulaic and high-frequency, as in Chapter 7. It continues to be useful for near-term plans and promises, this time in the context of technology and communication, such as sending credit, calling back, or getting a device fixed. Full productive control of the future TAM system, including its negative and dependent forms, remains scheduled for later Unit II chapters.

8.3 Object pronouns, consolidated

  • Ban samu saƙonta ba.I did not receive her message.ta = feminine possessive on saƙo; object pronoun pattern recycled from Ch. 7
  • Halima ta kira shi sau biyu.Halima called him twice.shi = masculine object pronoun
  • Ibrahim bai amsa mini ba.Ibrahim did not answer me.mini = first-person object/benefactive pronoun, recycled from earlier chapters

Continue choosing the object pronoun according to the gender and number of the noun it replaces. In this chapter, object pronouns are used to talk about messages, calls, and devices that have already been mentioned, without repeating the full noun each time.

8.4 Possessive constructions with na/ta, continued

  • Wayar nan ta Halima ce.This phone is Halima's.ta (feminine possessive linker, agrees with waya)
  • Batirin nan na wayar Ibrahim ne.This battery belongs to Ibrahim's phone.na (masculine possessive linker, agrees with batir)

Continue linking a possessor to the thing possessed with na after a masculine noun and ta after a feminine noun. As in Chapter 7, the linker's gender depends on the noun being possessed, not on the possessor.

8.5 Noun gender and plural, continued

Continue recording gender and plural for every new noun, as shown in the Core Vocabulary table above. Some technology nouns, such as intanet and kuɗin waya, do not have a common plural form and should be learned as lexical facts.

8.6 Repair and clarification formulas, extended to digital problems

  • Ban gane ba, za ki iya sake aikawa?I didn't understand, can you send it again?
  • Kiran ya katse, za ka iya sake kiran ni?The call dropped, can you call me back?
  • To, na gane yanzu, matsalar ita ce hanyar sadarwa.Okay, I understand now, the problem is the network.

The confirmation formula from Chapter 7 (to, na fahimta/na gane yanzu) continues to be useful here. This chapter adds two formulas specific to digital problems: asking someone to resend a message and asking someone to call back after a dropped call.

Atisayen ƙa'ida — Controlled practice: Complete each sentence with the correct word or phrase.

  1. Ibrahim ___ (yana/ina) kira Halima kullum da yamma.
  2. Wayar nan ___ (ta/na) Halima ce.
  3. Halima ta kira ___ (shi/ta) sau biyu.
  4. ___ (Za a/Ina) gyara hanyar sadarwa da wuri.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Halima na ƙoƙarin isa ga Ibrahim ta waya, amma hanyar sadarwa ba ta da ƙarfi.

(Setting: Halima is trying to reach Ibrahim by phone, but the network is weak.)

HALIMA Ibrahim, kana ji ni? Kiran nan yana katsewa.Ibrahim, can you hear me? This call keeps dropping.
IBRAHIM A'a, ban ji ba sosai. Hanyar sadarwa a nan ba ta da ƙarfi.No, I can't hear well. The network here is weak.
HALIMA To, zan aika maka saƙo maimakon haka.Okay, I'll send you a message instead.
IBRAHIM (bayan mintuna biyar) Ban samu saƙonki ba tukuna. Wataƙila kuɗin wayata ya ƙare.(after five minutes) I haven't received your message yet. Maybe my mobile credit ran out.
HALIMA Ban gane ba, za ka iya bincika wayarka?I don't understand, can you check your phone?
IBRAHIM To, na gane yanzu. Batirin wayata kusan ya ƙare, kuma ba ni da kuɗin waya sosai.Okay, I understand now. My phone battery is almost dead, and I don't have much mobile credit.
HALIMA Kada ku damu. Za ni aika maka kuɗin waya yanzu.Don't worry. I'll send you mobile credit now.
IBRAHIM Na gode sosai. Za ka iya sake kiran ni bayan minti biyar?Thank you very much. Can you call me again after five minutes?
HALIMA Za a yi haka. Har sai lokacin, kada ku kashe wayar.It will be done. Until then, don't turn off the phone.

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. Why does Halima's first call to Ibrahim not work well?
  2. What two problems does Ibrahim have with his phone?
  3. What does Halima offer to do to help Ibrahim?
  4. What repair formula does Halima use when she doesn't understand, and why?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Mobile phones and daily communication

Mobile phones are a central part of daily communication in many Hausa-speaking communities, used for calls, messages, and increasingly for internet-based communication, but access to reliable networks, mobile credit, and electricity for charging devices varies widely by location, household resources, and infrastructure. Halima's and Ibrahim's situation represents one plausible experience rather than a description of every Hausa speaker's access to technology.

Regional Variation — Terms for mobile credit and messaging

The specific words and expressions used for mobile credit, data, and messaging apps can vary between Nigeria, Niger, and other Hausa-speaking regions, and can also change quickly as new services and brands become common. This chapter's vocabulary reflects widely understood general terms and should be treated as a stable, general starting point rather than the only possible way to talk about these topics.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Wrong gender on the possessive linker

Error: *Wayar nan na Halima ce. (using the masculine linker na with the feminine noun waya)

Correction: Wayar nan ta Halima ce. — Choose na or ta according to the gender of the noun being possessed, not the gender of the possessor.

Common Mistake — Dropping the subject marker before za

Error: *Za aika kuɗin waya. (missing subject marker)

Correction: Za ni aika kuɗin waya. — The future-time pattern requires a subject marker between za and the verb; za alone is not sufficient.

Rubutacciyar Hausa

B1–B2 Development · Unit IV, Chapter 2 · Speak and Write Hausa: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Overview

Chapter 17 continues Unit IV by turning from spoken narration to written Hausa genres. Building directly on the sequencing, comparison, negation, prepositions/relational nouns, and focus/contrast practiced in Chapter 16, this chapter teaches learners to shape that same connected discourse into four practical written forms: short messages, short reports, summaries, and short formal notes, alongside continued description writing. No new grammar system is introduced this chapter; instead, learners consolidate discourse-level reference tracking and apply Chapter 1-16 grammar and vocabulary to genre-appropriate organization, opening and closing conventions, and register choices, consistent with the Volume III matrices at C17.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify the organizational conventions of four written Hausa genres: the short message, the short report, the summary, and the short formal note.
  2. Write a short informal message requesting or sharing practical information.
  3. Write a short report describing a sequence of events using sequencing connectors from Chapter 16.
  4. Summarize a longer spoken or written passage in a small number of connected sentences without copying its wording.
  5. Write a short formal note using appropriate opening and closing conventions and a more formal register than a message to a friend.
  6. Track reference to people, places, and events across a written text without unnecessary repetition of names.
  7. Read and compare short examples of each genre and identify their defining features.
  8. Revise a written draft for organization, register, and accuracy using a genre-specific checklist.

Communication Scenario

Ibrahim, now settled into his new job in Legas, needs to communicate in writing for several everyday purposes: a quick message to Aisha about visiting Kano again, a short report to his new supervisor summarizing his first month of work, and a short formal note to a landlord about his rented room. Aisha helps him compare the three texts and notice how each one is organized differently.

  1. Domain: written Hausa genres — messages, reports, summaries, and short formal notes — continuing Unit IV's travel and work context from Chapter 16.
  2. Developed writing skill: organizing connected discourse into genre-appropriate written forms, consolidating the Writing Progression matrix at C17.
  3. Recycled grammar in written use: sequencing connectors, comparison, negation, prepositions/relational nouns, and focus/contrast with ne/ce, all Consolidated since Unit III or Chapter 16.
  4. All Chapter 1-16 vocabulary and grammar remain active and available throughout this chapter's tasks.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Tone and length in saƙo/saƙonni and wasiƙa/wasiƙu

Practice the vowel length shift between saƙo and its plural saƙonni, and between wasiƙa and wasiƙu. Keep the ejective ƙ clearly distinct from plain k in both words, since these two consonants are not interchangeable and carry different meanings in other word pairs you have studied.

Genre Conventions and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new grammar system. Instead, it teaches how to organize Chapter 1-16 grammar and vocabulary into four written genres, consolidating discourse-level reference tracking and genre-specific written conventions at C17, consistent with the Volume III Writing Progression and Orthography matrices.

17.1 Consolidated: the short message

Saƙo — Message

Sannu Aisha! Ina son sanar da ke cewa zan zo Kano a watan gobe don hutu. Zan zauna kwana biyar. Za mu iya haɗuwa? Sai an jima.
— Ibrahim

Hi Aisha! I want to let you know that I'll come to Kano next month for a holiday. I'll stay five days. Can we meet up? See you soon. — Ibrahim

A short message is brief, informal, and organized around a single practical purpose: a greeting, the key information, and a short closing. Messages often omit full sentences of background information that a report would include.

17.2 Developed: the short report

Rahoto — Report

Wannan rahoto ne kan watan farko na aikina a Legas. Da farko, na fara aiki ne a ma'aikatar sadarwa. Sannan, na koyi tsarin aiki daga abokan aikina. Bayan haka, na fara jagorantar ƙaramin aiki na kaina. A ƙarshe, shugabana ya ce nayi kyakkyawan aiki, kodayake har yanzu akwai abubuwan da zan koya.

This is a report on the first month of my work in Lagos. First, I started working in the communications department. Then, I learned the work system from my colleagues. After that, I began leading a small project of my own. Finally, my supervisor said I had done good work, although there are still things I need to learn.

A report states its purpose first, then presents events or findings in clear sequence using the connectors da farko, sannan, bayan haka, and a ƙarshe from Chapter 16, and often closes with an evaluation or assessment rather than a personal farewell.

17.3 Developed: the summary

Taƙaitawa — Summary

A cikin wasiƙar da muka karanta a Babi na 16, marubuciya ta bayyana yadda ta yi ƙaura daga ƙauyenta zuwa babban birni domin neman ilimi, ba domin gudu daga ƙauyen kawai ba. Ta gano cewa rayuwar birni ta bambanta da ta ƙauye, amma a ƙarshe ta fahimci cewa dangi da abokai su ne suka fi mahimmanci a ko'ina.

In the letter we read in Chapter 16, the writer explains how she migrated from her village to a big city to seek education, not simply to escape the village. She found that city life differs from village life, but in the end she realized that family and friends matter most wherever one is.

A summary restates the main ideas of a longer text in the summarizer's own words, using connected discourse rather than copying full sentences from the original. Use reference tracking (pronouns, marubuciya "the writer," etc.) rather than repeating a name in every sentence.

17.4 Developed: the short formal note

Wasiƙa — Short Formal Note

Ranar ___
Zuwa ga Mai Gida,
Ina rubuto muku ne domin sanar da ku cewa famfon ɗakina bai yi aiki sosai ba tun makon jiya. Zan yi farin ciki idan an gyara shi kafin ƙarshen wata. Na gode da fahimtarku.
Da girmamawa,
Ibrahim

Date ___
To the Landlord,
I am writing to inform you that the tap in my room has not been working well since last week. I would be glad if it could be fixed before the end of the month. Thank you for your understanding.
Respectfully,
Ibrahim

A short formal note uses a more formal opening (Zuwa ga..., "To..."), a respectful closing (Da girmamawa, "Respectfully"), and more formal vocabulary and pronoun choices than a message to a friend. Negation (ba... ba) and prepositions/relational nouns (tun, kafin) from earlier chapters appear naturally within the more formal register.

Atisayen ƙa'ida — Controlled practice: Identify which genre (saƙo, rahoto, taƙaitawa, or wasiƙa) each short description best matches.

  1. A short, friendly note telling a friend when you will visit.
  2. A four-stage account of your first week at a new job, ending in an evaluation.
  3. A few sentences restating the main ideas of a letter you read, in your own words.
  4. A respectful note to a landlord about a household problem.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Ibrahim yana nuna wa Aisha rubuce-rubucensa uku — saƙo, rahoto, da wasiƙa — kuma suna kwatanta yadda kowanne yake da tsari daban.

(Setting: Ibrahim shows Aisha three of his written texts — a message, a report, and a formal note — and they compare how each is organized differently.)

AISHA Na ga ka rubuta abubuwa da yawa. Me bambanci tsakanin saƙon nan da rahoton nan?I see you've written many things. What's the difference between this message and this report?
IBRAHIM Saƙon nan gajere ne kawai, don sanar da ke lokacin zuwana. Amma rahoton nan ya fi tsari; na yi amfani da da farko, sannan, bayan haka, da a ƙarshe don bayyana duk abin da ya faru a watan farko.This message is just short, to let you know when I'm coming. But this report is more organized; I used da farko, sannan, bayan haka, and a ƙarshe to explain everything that happened in the first month.
AISHA Na gani. Wasiƙar nan zuwa ga mai gida ma ta bambanta, ko ba haka ba?I see. This note to the landlord is different too, isn't it?
IBRAHIM I, ta fi girmamawa. Ban rubuta masa kamar yadda nake rubuta maka ba; na yi amfani da "Zuwa ga Mai Gida" da "Da girmamawa," ba kamar saƙon nan ba.Yes, it's more respectful. I didn't write to him the way I write to you; I used "To the Landlord" and "Respectfully," not like this message.
AISHA Za ka iya taƙaita rahoton nan a cikin jimloli biyu kawai?Can you summarize this report in just two sentences?
IBRAHIM Zan iya. A watan farko, na koyi tsarin aiki sannan na jagoranci ƙaramin aiki. A ƙarshe, shugabana ya yaba mini, ko da yake har yanzu akwai abubuwan da zan koya.I can. In the first month, I learned the work system and then led a small project. In the end, my supervisor praised me, even though there are still things I need to learn.
AISHA Ka gyara shi da kyau. Ina ganin ka fahimci bambancin salon rubutu sosai.You revised it well. I can see you understand the difference in writing style very well.

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. How does Ibrahim describe the difference between his message and his report?
  2. What two formal conventions does Ibrahim mention for his note to the landlord?
  3. How does Ibrahim summarize his report in two sentences?

Speaking Practice

Atisayen ƙa'ida — Controlled substitution

With a partner, take turns describing aloud, in one or two sentences, what genre (saƙo, rahoto, taƙaitawa, or wasiƙa) you would use for a given situation, and why.

Tattaunawa — Communicative task

In a group of two or three, role-play a conversation like Ibrahim and Aisha's, in which one person explains the differences between two written texts they have prepared, using comparison (fiye da, kamar, daidai da) to describe how the genres differ in tone and organization.

Madadin karatu kai tsaye — Solo alternative

Record yourself explaining, in your own words, the difference between a message to a friend and a formal note to someone you do not know well, giving one example of each.

Karatu — Rahoton Taro (Reading: A Meeting Report)

Writing Workshop

  1. Karanta samfurori da hankali. Reread the four genre samples in this chapter and notice their opening lines, closing lines, and level of formality.
  2. Zaɓi nau'i. Choose one genre (message, report, summary, or short formal note) and a realistic purpose for it, drawing on your own experience or an invented situation.
  3. Tsarawa. Plan your text's opening, main content in sequence, and closing, noting which recycled grammar (sequencing, comparison, negation, prepositions, or focus/contrast) you will include.
  4. Rubuta. Write 80-130 words in Hausa in your chosen genre, using appropriate opening and closing conventions and at least two recycled grammar points from Chapters 1-16.
  5. Duba jerin bincike. Check your draft against the checklist below.
  6. Rubuta siffa ta ƙarshe. Produce a clean final version.

Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

  • The text uses an opening and closing appropriate to its genre and register.
  • The content is organized clearly, using sequencing connectors where appropriate to the genre.
  • At least two recycled grammar points from Chapters 1-16 (comparison, negation, prepositions/relational nouns, or focus/contrast) appear and are used correctly.
  • Reference to people and places is tracked clearly, without unnecessary repetition of names.
  • If the text is a summary, it restates ideas in the writer's own words rather than copying full sentences from the original.
  • Vocabulary and grammar remain within the cumulative Chapter 1-17 scope; no unscheduled forms appear.
  • Special characters (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ, ƴ) and apostrophe/glottal conventions are used accurately.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Formality in written requests

Written requests to landlords, employers, or institutions in Hausa-speaking communities commonly use a more formal, respectful register than everyday messages between friends or family, including formal opening and closing conventions. How formal a note needs to be can vary by relationship, setting, and the individuals involved; treat this pattern as a common convention rather than a fixed universal rule.

Regional Variation — Written genre conventions

Specific opening and closing formulas for formal notes and reports can vary somewhat by region, institution, and medium (paper letter, text message, or email). The patterns taught in this chapter are broadly usable, but learners should expect some variation in workplaces, schools, or government offices, and should adapt their register to the specific audience and context.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using message-level informality in a formal note

Error: *Sannu mai gida, famfo ya lalace, ka gyara mana. (using an informal greeting and command form in a note to a landlord)

Correction: Zuwa ga Mai Gida, ina rubuto muku ne domin sanar da ku cewa famfon ɗakina bai yi aiki sosai ba... Da girmamawa. — Use a formal opening, a full explanatory sentence, and a respectful closing in a short formal note, rather than the abbreviated style of a message.

Common Mistake — Copying full sentences when summarizing

Error: Reproducing the original passage's exact sentences and calling the result a summary.

Correction: Restate the main ideas in new, shorter sentences using your own connected discourse, as in the Chapter 17 summary sample. A summary is judged by whether it captures the main ideas concisely in the summarizer's own words, not by how closely it matches the original wording.

Common Mistake — Omitting the report's evaluative closing

Error: Ending a report immediately after the last sequenced event, with no assessment or conclusion.

Correction: Close a short report with a brief evaluation or conclusion, as in A ƙarshe, shugabana ya ce nayi kyakkyawan aiki... — A report's closing typically does more work than a message's closing.

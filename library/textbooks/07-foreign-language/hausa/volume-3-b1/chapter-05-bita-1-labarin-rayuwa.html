Bita 1: Labarin Rayuwa

B1 Development · Unit I Integrated Assessment · Speak and Write Hausa: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse
Bita — This chapter is a formal Unit I assessment. No new vocabulary or grammar is introduced.

Chapter Overview

Chapter 5 is the Unit I assessment for Volume III. It does not introduce new vocabulary, grammar, or communicative functions. Instead, it asks you to bring together everything practiced in Chapters 1 through 4 — personal history and development, relationships and social interaction, town, village, and environment, and health and well-being — into a single connected narration. You will describe yourself, the people around you, the place where you live, and how you take care of your health, linking all four topics into one organized account rather than treating them as separate lists of facts.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Narrate personal background, a relationship, a place, and a health routine within one connected passage.
  2. Track reference to more than one person, place, or habit across an extended narration without unnecessary repetition.
  3. Use the comparative pattern ya/ta fi accurately to compare people, places, or habits.
  4. Use the "before and now" contrast (a da ... amma yanzu) to describe change over time in any of the four chapter domains.
  5. Integrate completive and habitual TAM forms accurately across a connected passage.
  6. Ask for clarification, repetition, or confirmation naturally within an extended conversation.
  7. Listen to a connected life narrative and identify how its parts relate to one another.
  8. Read a connected passage covering more than one Unit I topic and answer inferential questions about it.
  9. Write an organized paragraph of at least 150 words that integrates personal history, relationships, place, and health.
  10. Self-assess written and spoken work against the Unit I performance criteria.

Communication Scenario

At the end of Unit I, the instructor asks each learner to prepare a connected "life account" (labarin rayuwa) that draws on everything discussed in Chapters 1 through 4. Halima and Sule are asked to go first, as a model for the rest of the class. Halima begins with where she is from and how her family has changed, moves to her relationship with her older brother, describes her neighborhood, and finishes by explaining how her health habits have changed since she started university. Sule listens closely and asks her to clarify one detail about her brother before offering his own account, which follows the same pattern but compares his current city life to his life in his home village. This chapter asks you to prepare and perform a similar connected account.

  1. No new vocabulary domain is introduced in this chapter. Personal-history vocabulary from Chapter 1, relationship vocabulary from Chapter 2, place and environment vocabulary from Chapter 3, and health and routine vocabulary from Chapter 4 all remain active and are expected to appear accurately.
  2. No new grammar system is introduced. This chapter assesses the independent pronouns, subject-reference patterns, completive/habitual TAM forms, the comparative pattern ya/ta fi, the "before and now" contrast, and reference tracking already consolidated in Chapters 1 through 4 and Volume II.
  3. This chapter is the formal Unit I assessment point identified in the Volume III scope and sequence.
  4. Performance on this chapter should reflect cumulative control of Chapters 1-4 and Volume II material, not merely Chapter 4 alone.

Pronunciation Review

Sound Review — Tone, vowel length, and rhythm across a connected passage

This chapter introduces no new pronunciation feature. It asks you to maintain the tone and vowel-length control practiced in Chapters 1 through 4 across a longer, connected passage rather than in isolated sentences. Pay particular attention to keeping an even syllable rhythm as you move between topics — for example, from a sentence about your family to a sentence about your neighborhood to a sentence about your health — since speakers sometimes let rhythm and tone control slip at topic transitions. Special attention should continue on the special consonants ɓ, ɗ, and ƙ, and on the vowel-length contrasts already highlighted in earlier chapters.

Grammar Review and Integration

This chapter introduces no new grammar system. It integrates the identity and existential predicate patterns, the completive/habitual TAM forms, the comparative pattern, the "before and now" contrast, and reference tracking already consolidated in Chapters 1 through 4 and Volume II, and requires you to combine them across four topics in a single connected passage.

5.1 Reviewing: Combining topics in one connected passage

  • An haife ni a Kano, kuma yanzu ina zama a Kaduna tare da 'yar'uwata.I was born in Kano, and now I live in Kaduna with my sister.
  • Unguwarmu ta canja sosai tun lokacin da muka zauna a nan, kuma yanzu ina motsa jiki a filin wasa kusa da gida.Our neighborhood has changed a lot since we settled here, and now I exercise at the field near the house.

Notice how the second example moves from a place topic (Chapter 3) to a health topic (Chapter 4) using the connector kuma, exactly as practiced within single topics in earlier chapters. Moving between topics does not require a new connector system; the connectors already consolidated (kuma, amma, domin, sai) are sufficient.

5.2 Reviewing: The comparative pattern across topics

Language Building Block — Comparing Across Topics
[person/place/habit A] ya/ta fi [person/place/habit B] [adjective]
Birnin Kaduna ya fi ƙauyenmu girma, amma ƙauyenmu ya fi shi kwanciyar hankali.
Kaduna city is bigger than our village, but our village is calmer than it.
Yanzu ina motsa jiki fiye da yadda nake yi a ƙauye.
Now I exercise more than I did in the village.

The same comparative pattern used in Chapter 2 to compare people, in Chapter 3 to compare places, and in Chapter 4 to compare habits can compare any two items named earlier in a connected passage, including items from different chapters.

5.3 Reviewing: Reference tracking across an extended narration

In a passage that names several people, places, and habits, track each one clearly the first time it reappears, exactly as practiced separately in Chapters 2 through 4. Do not repeat a full name or noun once a pronoun has been established, and do not switch between two different pronouns for the same referent without a reason.

  • Ɗan'uwana Sule yana zaune a Kano. Shi ne ya taimaka mini na sami aiki a nan, kuma yanzu muna hira ta waya kowace mako.My brother Sule lives in Kano. He is the one who helped me find work here, and now we talk on the phone every week."Sule" named once, tracked with "shi"; later restated as "muna" (we) once the relationship is established

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: A ƙarshen darasi na farko, malami ya nemi Halima da Sule su gabatar da cikakken labarin rayuwarsu.

(Setting: At the end of Unit I, the instructor asks Halima and Sule to present a complete account of their lives.)

HALIMA An haife ni a Sokoto, amma yanzu ina zama a Kaduna tare da ɗan'uwana babba. A da, muna zama a ƙauye, amma iyayenmu sun canja zuwa birni domin aiki.I was born in Sokoto, but now I live in Kaduna with my older brother. Before, we lived in a village, but our parents moved to the city for work.
SULE Shin, ɗan'uwanki babba ne yake zama tare da ku a Kaduna, ko wani wuri dabam?Is it your older brother who lives with you in Kaduna, or somewhere else?
HALIMA I, shi ne, kuma shi ne ya taimaka mini na sami wurin karatu a nan. Unguwarmu a Kaduna ta fi ta Sokoto girma, amma na fi son unguwarmu ta Sokoto domin kwanciyar hankalinta.Yes, it's him, and he's the one who helped me find a place to study here. Our neighborhood in Kaduna is bigger than the one in Sokoto, but I prefer our Sokoto neighborhood for its calmness.
SULE Na fahimta. Yanzu, yaya lafiyarki tun bayan wannan canjin?I understand. Now, how has your health been since this change?
HALIMA A da, ba na yin motsa jiki sosai domin babu filin wasa kusa da gidanmu a ƙauye. Amma yanzu, akwai filin wasa kusa da unguwarmu a Kaduna, kuma ina motsa jiki kowace safiya. Cin abinci mai gina jiki tare da motsa jiki ya fi taimaka mini fiye da da.Before, I didn't exercise much because there was no field near our house in the village. But now, there's a field near our neighborhood in Kaduna, and I exercise every morning. Eating nutritious food together with exercising has helped me more than before.
SULE Wannan labari ne mai kyau. Yanzu ni ma zan gabatar da nawa. Ni kuwa, an haife ni a Kaduna, kuma dukan iyalina suna zama a nan tun farko...That's a good account. Now I will present mine too. As for me, I was born in Kaduna, and my whole family has lived here from the beginning...

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. Where was Halima born, and where does she live now?
  2. What does Sule ask Halima to clarify, and why?
  3. How does Halima compare her Kaduna neighborhood to her Sokoto neighborhood?
  4. How has Halima's health routine changed since moving to Kaduna?
  5. What connects Halima's account of family, place, and health into one narration?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Telling a connected life story

Presenting a personal life account that links family, place, and health is a familiar communicative task across many Hausa-speaking communities, but the order, emphasis, and detail included can vary by speaker, family, and setting. Some speakers foreground family relationships, others foreground place or occupation, and some prefer a compact account while others elaborate at length. Treating Halima's, Sule's, Aminu's, or Fatima's accounts as individual examples, rather than as a fixed template every Hausa speaker follows, remains the more accurate way to describe this variation, consistent with the culture guidance already introduced in Chapters 1 through 4.

Regional Variation — Migration and mobility in personal narratives

Movement between village and city, as in several of this chapter's passages, is common in many Hausa-speaking families but is not universal, and the reasons for such movement (work, education, family, or other factors) vary widely. This chapter's passages describe individual, plausible situations rather than a general claim about how Hausa-speaking families live.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Listing topics instead of connecting them

Error: *An haife ni a Kano. Ina da ɗan'uwa. Ina zama a Kaduna. Ina motsa jiki. (four disconnected simple sentences with no linking words or reference tracking)

Correction: An haife ni a Kano, kuma ina da ɗan'uwa wanda muke zama tare a Kaduna, inda nake motsa jiki kowace safiya. — Use the connectors and reference-tracking patterns already consolidated in Chapters 1 through 4 to link topics into one account, rather than presenting them as a list.

Common Mistake — Losing reference-tracking control over a longer passage

Error: *Halima ta canja unguwa. Bayan haka, ita ta fara aiki, sannan Halima ta fara motsa jiki. (switching back to the full name after a pronoun has already been established, without a reason)

Correction: Halima ta canja unguwa. Bayan haka, ta fara aiki, sannan ta fara motsa jiki. — Once "Halima" has been named and continued with ta, keep the pronoun consistently unless a genuine ambiguity requires repeating the name.

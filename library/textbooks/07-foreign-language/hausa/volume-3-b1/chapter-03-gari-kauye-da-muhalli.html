3

Gari, Ƙauye, da Muhalli

B1 Development · Speak and Write Hausa: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Overview

Chapter 3 continues Unit I by turning from people to place. You will describe the town or village where you live, compare it to another place, talk about infrastructure such as roads, water, and electricity, and discuss how a community has changed over time. This chapter carries forward the completive narration, comparative pattern, sequencing connectors, and reference-tracking systems introduced in Chapters 1 and 2 and applies them to a new communicative task: describing and comparing places rather than people.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe a town or village, including its size, layout, and general character.
  2. Name and describe common infrastructure: roads, water sources, electricity, and buildings.
  3. Compare two places using the consolidated comparative pattern.
  4. Describe how a place has changed, using a "before and now" contrast.
  5. Discuss environmental conditions and community concerns in simple terms.
  6. Ask a partner about their home town or village and respond to their answers.
  7. Track reference to more than one place across a connected passage.
  8. Ask for clarification or repetition when a description of a place is not understood.
  9. Read and interpret a short passage describing a place or a community change.
  10. Write a short, organized paragraph describing a place, its infrastructure, and a change it has undergone.

Communication Scenario

The following week, the instructor asks Halima and Sule to describe where they grew up and how it has changed. Sule, who grew up in a small village, describes how the roads and electricity supply have improved in recent years. Halima, who lives in a larger town, compares her neighborhood now to how it was when she was a child. As they talk, each asks the other to clarify a detail about the other's home area. This chapter follows their conversation and asks you to prepare a similar description of a place you know well.

  1. No new vocabulary domain beyond town, village, environment, and infrastructure terms is introduced in this chapter; relationship vocabulary from Chapter 2 and personal-history vocabulary from Chapter 1 remain active.
  2. No new grammar system is introduced. This chapter continues to consolidate independent pronouns, subject-reference patterns, and completive/habitual forms already active since Volume II, and extends the comparative pattern and reference tracking from Chapter 2 to descriptions of places.
  3. This chapter continues Unit I, which will be formally assessed in Chapter 5.
  4. Vocabulary and grammar from Chapters 1 and 2, and from Volume I and Volume II, remain fully active and are expected to appear accurately throughout this chapter's tasks.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Tone and vowel length in place vocabulary

This chapter's pronunciation focus continues consolidating tone and vowel length in a recycled-pattern vocabulary set. Pay particular attention to the vowel length in hanya (road) and the tone contrast between gari (town) and similarly shaped words, since confusing these can affect whether a listener understands you to be describing a town or something else entirely. As in Chapters 1 and 2, keep an even syllable rhythm across full sentences, especially when a sentence names more than one place and compares them.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new grammar system. It continues to consolidate independent pronouns, subject-reference patterns, and completive/habitual forms already active since Volume II, and extends the comparative pattern and reference-tracking work begun in Chapters 1 and 2 to passages describing two or more places.

3.1 Reviewing: Describing a place with identity and descriptive predicates

  • Garinmu babba ne, kuma yana da mutane da yawa.Our town is big, and it has many people.
  • Ƙauyenmu ƙarami ne, amma yana da kyakkyawan muhalli.Our village is small, but it has a beautiful environment.

The identity-predicate pattern with ne/ce and the existential pattern with yana da, both consolidated since Volume II, are recycled here to describe a place's size and character rather than a person's identity.

3.2 Reviewing: Comparing two places

Language Building Block — Comparing Two Places
[gari/ƙauye A] ya/ta fi [gari/ƙauye B] [adjective]
Garin Kano ya fi ƙauyenmu girma.
The city of Kano is bigger than our village.
Amma ƙauyenmu ya fi shi natsuwa.
But our village is calmer than it.

The comparative pattern ya/ta fi, already used in Chapter 2 to compare people (ya fi ni girma), is recycled here to compare places. As in Chapter 2, once the second place has been named, a pronoun such as shi or ita can replace it in a following sentence.

3.3 Reviewing: Describing change with "before and now"

  • A da, ba mu da wutar lantarki a ƙauyenmu.Before, we did not have electricity in our village.
  • Amma yanzu, mun samu ci gaba, kuma muna da wutar lantarki da hanyoyi masu kyau.But now, we have made progress, and we have electricity and good roads.

The time expression a da ("before, in the past") and the contrastive connector amma yanzu ("but now"), built from the sequencing pattern already practiced with amma daga baya in Chapter 2, allow a speaker to contrast an earlier state of a place with its present state using only completive and existential forms already consolidated.

3.4 Extending: Reference tracking with two or more places

When a passage describes or compares places, it usually names at least two. Track each place clearly with pronouns once each has been introduced, just as you tracked two people in Chapter 2.

  • Sule ya girma a wani ƙauye kusa da Kano. Ƙauyen yana da ƙananan gidaje, kuma yanzu yana da famfo da wutar lantarki.Sule grew up in a village near Kano. The village has small houses, and now it has a water pump and electricity."ƙauye" named once, then tracked with "yana" (it, habitual/existential subject marker)

Atisayen ƙa'ida — Grammar practice: Complete each sentence with the word or expression that fits.

  1. Garin Kano ___ (ya fi/ne) ƙauyenmu girma.
  2. ___ (A da/Yanzu), ba mu da hanyoyi masu kyau, amma yanzu mun samu ci gaba.
  3. Ƙauyenmu ƙarami ne, ___ (amma/domin) yana da kyakkyawan muhalli.
  4. Unguwarmu tana da sabbin gine-gine. ___ (Ita/Unguwarmu) ta canja sosai a cikin shekaru biyar.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: A cikin aji, malami ya nemi Sule da Halima su kwatanta gari ko ƙauyen da suka girma a ciki.

(Setting: In class, the instructor asks Sule and Halima to describe the town or village where they grew up.)

SULE Na girma a wani ƙauye kusa da Kano. Ƙauyenmu ƙarami ne, amma yana da kyakkyawan muhalli.I grew up in a village near Kano. Our village is small, but it has a beautiful environment.
HALIMA Shin, kuna da wutar lantarki a can?Do you have electricity there?
SULE A da, ba mu da ita, amma yanzu, mun samu ci gaba, kuma muna da wutar lantarki da hanyoyi masu kyau.Before, we did not have it, but now, we have made progress, and we have electricity and good roads.
HALIMA Ban gama fahimta ba. Ka ce hanyoyin sun inganta, ko wutar lantarki ce kawai?I didn't fully understand. Did you say the roads improved, or was it only the electricity?
SULE Su biyu, hanyoyi da wutar lantarki, sun inganta tare. Kai fa, ina kika girma?Both, roads and electricity, improved together. What about you, where did you grow up?
HALIMA Na girma a wata unguwa a Kano. Garin Kano ya fi ƙauyenku girma sosai, kuma unguwarmu tana da sabbin gine-gine yanzu.I grew up in a neighborhood in Kano. Kano is much bigger than your village, and our neighborhood has new buildings now.
SULE Amma ƙauyenmu ya fi garinku natsuwa, ba tare da hayaniya sosai ba.But our village is calmer than your town, without so much noise.
HALIMA Wannan gaskiya ne. Kowane wuri yana da nasa kyau da ƙalubale.That's true. Every place has its own good points and challenges.

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. Where did Sule grow up, and how does he describe it?
  2. What has changed in Sule's village, according to him?
  3. What does Halima ask Sule to clarify, and why?
  4. How does Halima describe her own neighborhood?
  5. What general statement does Halima make at the end of the conversation?

Speaking Practice

Reading Practice

Read the short passage below. Underline every infrastructure or environment expression, circle every comparative form, and put a box around every instance of pronoun reference tracking.

Karatu — Ƙauyen Garba da Birnin Ɗan'uwansa (Reading: Garba's Village and His Brother's City)

Garba yana zama a wani ƙauye kusa da Sakkwato, amma ɗan'uwansa Bello yana zama a birnin Sakkwato. Garba ya ce ƙauyensu ƙarami ne, kuma yana da kyakkyawan muhalli, ba tare da hayaniya sosai ba. Bello kuwa yana zama a wani gari mai girma, wanda ya fi ƙauyen Garba yawan mutane da gine-gine. A da, ƙauyen Garba ba shi da wutar lantarki, amma yanzu, sun samu ci gaba, kuma suna da ita da hanyoyi masu kyau. Duk da wannan ci gaba, Garba ya ce ya fi son ƙauyensu, domin yana da natsuwa fiye da birni. Bello kuwa ya ce garinsu yana da dama masu yawa na aiki da makaranta, kuma hakan ya sa shi son zama a can.

Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

  • The place being described is clearly identified.
  • Infrastructure or environmental features are described accurately.
  • A comparison between two places is made using ya/ta fi.
  • A "before and now" contrast appears where appropriate.
  • The second place is tracked afterward with a pronoun, not repeated by name.
  • Prior systems from Chapters 1-2 and Volume II (TAM/aspect, pronouns, connectors) remain accurate where they appear.
  • No grammar or vocabulary beyond the cumulative Chapter 1–3 scope appears.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Town and village life

Hausa-speaking communities live in a wide range of settings, from small villages to large cities such as Kano, Kaduna, and Sakkwato in Nigeria, and Zinder and Maradi in Niger. Infrastructure such as electricity, paved roads, and piped water varies considerably even within the same region, and access has been changing over time in many communities. Presenting one family's or one village's experience, as in Sule's and Garba's accounts, as an example rather than a universal description of "village life" or "city life" is a more accurate way to describe this variation.

Regional Variation — Naming towns, villages, and neighborhoods

The words used to describe administrative divisions, such as unguwa (neighborhood or ward), can carry different official or informal meanings depending on the country and local government context. This chapter uses general, widely understood vocabulary; more specific administrative terms are introduced for recognition in later chapters when the topic requires them.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Repeating a place name instead of tracking reference between two places

Error: *Ƙauyen Sule yana da famfo. Ƙauyen Sule yana da hanyoyi masu kyau yanzu. Ƙauyen Sule ya canja sosai. (repeating the full place name in every sentence of a passage about one place)

Correction: Ƙauyen Sule yana da famfo. Yana da hanyoyi masu kyau yanzu, kuma ya canja sosai. — Once a place has been named, continue with the pronoun ya/ta/yana/tana, exactly as practiced with people in Chapter 2.

Common Mistake — Placing the compared item incorrectly in the ya/ta fi pattern

Error: *Ƙauyenmu ya fi girma garin Kano. (placing the point of comparison, girma, before the thing being compared to)

Correction: Garin Kano ya fi ƙauyenmu girma. — The pattern is [subject] + ya/ta fi + [item compared to] + [quality]. The quality word always comes last.

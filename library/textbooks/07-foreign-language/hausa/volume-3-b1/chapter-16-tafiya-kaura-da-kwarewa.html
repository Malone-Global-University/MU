Sashe na 4 — Unit IV: Tafiya, Ƙaura, da Salon Magana (Travel, Migration, and Expression)
16

Tafiya, Ƙaura, da Ƙwarewa

B1–B2 Development · Unit IV, Chapter 1 · Speak and Write Hausa: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse
Opening Unit IV — Travel, Migration, and Experience. This chapter introduces new vocabulary for movement, relocation, and travel narration; develops connected narration and sequencing, comparison, and discourse-level reference tracking; and moves negation, focus/contrast with ne/ce, and prepositions/relational nouns into developed, more formal narrative use, building on Unit III.

Chapter Overview

Chapter 16 opens Unit IV by moving from discussing community-level issues (Unit III) to narrating personal experiences of movement: short trips, relocation, and cross-regional contact. Learners will sequence events using connectors such as da farko, sannan, bayan haka, and a ƙarshe, developed here beyond the introductory level established in Chapter 1 and practiced lightly since. Comparison is introduced for the first time (fiye da, kamar, daidai da) to support describing how one place, trip, or experience differs from another. Negation, prepositions/relational nouns, and focus/contrast with ne/ce, all Consolidated since Unit III, are now practiced in longer narrative and moderately formal passages, moving the more advanced, formal-register use of these systems from receptive recognition toward developed production, consistent with the Volume III matrices at C16.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use core vocabulary for travel, migration, and personal experience accurately, including correct gender and plural forms.
  2. Narrate a short sequence of travel or relocation events using da farko, sannan, bayan haka, and a ƙarshe.
  3. Compare two places, trips, or experiences using fiye da, kamar, and daidai da.
  4. Use negation, prepositions/relational nouns, and focus/contrast with ne/ce accurately within a longer narrative passage.
  5. Track reference to people and places across several connected sentences without unnecessary repetition.
  6. Listen to and read short travel narratives and answer factual, sequencing, and comparative questions.
  7. Take part in a discussion narrating and comparing travel or relocation experiences.
  8. Write a short organized narrative describing a trip or move, its sequence of events, and a comparison with another place or experience.

Communication Scenario

Aisha's cousin, Ibrahim, has recently moved from Kano to Legas (Lagos) for work, and Aisha, Zainab, and Bashir gather to hear about his trip and his early experience of the new city. Ibrahim narrates the sequence of his journey and compares life in Legas with life in Kano, while his friends ask questions and add their own travel experiences.

  1. Domain: travel, relocation, and personal experience narration, opening the new domain sequence of Unit IV.
  2. Developed grammar: connected narration and sequencing (da farko, sannan, bayan haka, a ƙarshe) and comparison (fiye da, kamar, daidai da), introduced or expanded in this chapter.
  3. Consolidated grammar in developed, narrative use: negation (ba... ba, babu), prepositions/relational nouns (daga, zuwa, tun, kafin), and focus/contrast (ne/ce).
  4. All Chapter 1-15 vocabulary and grammar remain active and available throughout this chapter's tasks.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Tone and length in tafiya/tafiye-tafiye and the glottalized ƙ in ƙaura/ƙwarewa

Practice the tone shift between tafiya (singular) and the reduplicated plural tafiye-tafiye, keeping each half of the reduplication clearly articulated rather than blurred into one word. Continue distinguishing the ejective ƙ in ƙaura and ƙwarewa from plain k, and keep the vowel length in baƙo/baƙi distinct and consistent.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops connected narration and sequencing beyond its introductory use, introduces comparison for the first time, and moves negation, prepositions/relational nouns, and focus/contrast with ne/ce into longer, more formal narrative passages, consistent with the Volume III matrices moving these systems from Consolidated conversational use toward Developed narrative and formal-register use at C16.

16.1 Developed: sequencing a narrative

Language Building Block — Developed
Da farko, ... Sannan, ... Bayan haka, ... A ƙarshe, ...
Da farko, na sayi tikiti a tashar mota. Sannan, na yi tafiya har kwana biyu. Bayan haka, na isa Legas da yamma. A ƙarshe, na sami wurin zama tare da wani ɗan'uwana.
First, I bought a ticket at the motor station. Then, I traveled for two days. After that, I arrived in Lagos in the evening. Finally, I found a place to stay with a relative.

Use these four connectors together as a set to organize a personal narrative into clear stages, going beyond the single-connector use introduced in Chapter 1.

16.2 Introduced: comparison with fiye da, kamar, and daidai da

Language Building Block — Introduced
X ya/ta fi Y girma/tsada/kyau, fiye da ... / X yana/tana kama da Y / X daidai da Y
Legas ta fi Kano girma, fiye da yadda nake tsammani.
Lagos is bigger than Kano, more than I expected.
Rayuwar birni tana kama da abin da nake tsammani, amma kuɗin gida ba daidai da na Kano ba.
City life is somewhat like what I expected, but rent isn't the same as in Kano.

Use fiye da after a comparative predicate to mean "more than," kamar to mean "like/similar to," and daidai da to mean "the same as." These patterns are new at this chapter and should be practiced in short controlled sentences before extended use.

16.3 Developed: negation in narrative

Language Building Block — Consolidated, developed narrative use
Ba... ba (within a longer narrative clause)
Ban san kowa a Legas ba tun farko, amma yanzu na sami abokai da yawa.
I didn't know anyone in Lagos at first, but now I have made many friends.

Ba... ba negation, consolidated since Chapter 6, now supports narrating a change of circumstance over time rather than only disagreement or refusal.

16.4 Developed: prepositions/relational nouns of movement and time

Language Building Block — Consolidated, developed narrative use
daga ... zuwa ... / tun ... / kafin ...
Na yi tafiya daga Kano zuwa Legas, tun bayan na kammala karatuna, kafin na fara aiki.
I traveled from Kano to Lagos, since finishing my studies, before starting work.

Combine daga... zuwa (source/destination), tun (since), and kafin (before) within a single narrative sentence to place events in relation to one another, extending the single-preposition use practiced in earlier chapters.

16.5 Developed: focus/contrast with ne/ce in narrative emphasis

Language Building Block — Consolidated, developed narrative use
[Focused element] + ne/ce ...
Aikin ne ya sa na yi ƙaura, ba domin son birni kawai ba.
It's work that made me migrate, not just because I liked the city.

Continue using focus/contrast with ne/ce to emphasize the true reason for an action within a narrative, combined with negation to rule out an alternative reason.

Atisayen ƙa'ida — Controlled practice: Complete each sentence with the correct word or phrase.

  1. ___ (Da farko/A ƙarshe), na sayi tikiti; ___ (sannan/kafin), na yi tafiya kwana biyu.
  2. Legas ta fi Kano girma, ___ (fiye da/kamar) yadda nake tsammani.
  3. Ban san kowa a Legas ___ (ba/ne) tun farko.
  4. Na yi tafiya ___ (daga/zuwa) Kano ___ (daga/zuwa) Legas.
  5. Aikin ___ (ne/ce) ya sa na yi ƙaura.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Ibrahim ya dawo Kano don ziyara bayan wata biyu a Legas; Aisha, Zainab, da Bashir suna tambayarsa game da tafiyarsa da sabuwar rayuwarsa.

(Setting: Ibrahim has returned to Kano for a visit after two months in Lagos; Aisha, Zainab, and Bashir ask him about his trip and new life.)

ZAINAB Ibrahim, yaya tafiyarka zuwa Legas ta kasance?Ibrahim, how was your trip to Lagos?
IBRAHIM Da farko, na sayi tikiti a tashar mota nan Kano. Sannan, na yi tafiya har kwana biyu. Bayan haka, na isa Legas da yamma, sannan a ƙarshe, na sami wurin zama tare da wani ɗan'uwana.First, I bought a ticket at the motor station here in Kano. Then, I traveled for two days. After that, I arrived in Lagos in the evening, and finally, I found a place to stay with a relative.
BASHIR Yaya Legas take kama da Kano?How is Lagos similar to Kano?
IBRAHIM Legas ta fi Kano girma, fiye da yadda nake tsammani. Rayuwar birni tana kama da abin da nake tsammani, amma kuɗin gida ba daidai da na Kano ba; ya fi tsada sosai.Lagos is bigger than Kano, more than I expected. City life is somewhat like what I expected, but rent isn't the same as in Kano; it's much more expensive.
AISHA Ka ji kaɗaici a can tun farko?Did you feel lonely there at first?
IBRAHIM Ban san kowa a Legas ba tun farko, amma yanzu na sami abokai da yawa a wurin aiki.I didn't know anyone in Lagos at first, but now I have made many friends at work.
ZAINAB Me ya sa ka yi ƙaura zuwa Legas maimakon neman aiki a nan Kano?Why did you migrate to Lagos instead of looking for work here in Kano?
IBRAHIM Aikin ne ya sa na yi ƙaura, ba domin son birni kawai ba. Na yi tafiya daga Kano zuwa Legas tun bayan na kammala karatuna, kafin na fara aiki na farko.It's work that made me migrate, not just because I liked the city. I traveled from Kano to Lagos after finishing my studies, before starting my first job.
BASHIR Wataƙila ni ma zan yi tunani kan hakan nan gaba.Maybe I'll also think about that in the future.

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. What four stages does Ibrahim describe in the sequence of his trip?
  2. How does Ibrahim compare Lagos to Kano in size and cost of living?
  3. Did Ibrahim feel lonely at first, and what changed?
  4. What does Ibrahim say was the true reason for his migration?

Writing Workshop

  1. Karanta wasiƙar ƙwarewa da hankali. Reread the migration letter and notice how it sequences events, compares two places, and closes with a reflection.
  2. Tsarawa. Plan a short narrative about a trip or move familiar to you, noting four stages, one comparison, and one true reason you will emphasize with focus/contrast.
  3. Rubuta. Write 100-140 words in Hausa narrating the trip or move, using all four sequencing connectors, at least one comparison, one negation, and one focus/contrast statement.
  4. Duba jerin bincike. Check your draft against the checklist below.
  5. Rubuta siffa ta ƙarshe. Produce a clean final version.

Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

  • The narrative clearly sequences a trip or move using all four connectors (da farko, sannan, bayan haka, a ƙarshe).
  • At least one comparison (fiye da, kamar, or daidai da) appears and is used correctly.
  • At least one negative construction (ba... ba or babu) appears, including the closing ba.
  • At least one focus/contrast statement with ne/ce appears with correct gender agreement.
  • Prepositions/relational nouns of movement or time (daga, zuwa, tun, kafin) are used accurately.
  • Vocabulary and grammar remain within the cumulative Chapter 1-16 scope; no unscheduled forms appear.
  • Special characters (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ, ƴ) and apostrophe/glottal conventions are used accurately.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Family and community ties across a move

Migration for work, study, or family reasons is common across Hausa-speaking communities, and it typically does not mean a break with home. Many migrants keep close contact with family, send remittances, and return for visits and celebrations, as reflected in Ibrahim's visit home. How closely ties are kept can vary by relationship, distance, and individual circumstances; treat this pattern as common and respectful rather than universal.

Regional Variation — Talking about distance and cost of living

Comparisons of distance, cost of living, and city life can differ noticeably depending on the specific places being compared and the speaker's own background. The comparison patterns in this chapter (fiye da, kamar, daidai da) are broadly usable across regions, but the specific vocabulary used to describe a place may include regionally marked terms that this course treats as receptive unless separately scheduled for production.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing fiye da with kamar

Error: *Legas ta fi Kano girma kamar yadda nake tsammani. (using kamar where a "more than" comparison is intended)

Correction: Legas ta fi Kano girma fiye da yadda nake tsammani. — Use fiye da after a comparative predicate (ya/ta fi...) to mean "more than," and reserve kamar for similarity.

Common Mistake — Sequencing connectors out of order

Error: *A ƙarshe, na sayi tikiti; da farko, na isa Legas. (reversing the natural order of narrated events)

Correction: Da farko, na sayi tikiti; a ƙarshe, na isa Legas. — Keep the four connectors in their natural sequence: da farko, sannan, bayan haka, a ƙarshe.

Common Mistake — Dropping the closing ba in a longer narrative clause

Error: *Ban san kowa a Legas tun farko, amma yanzu na sami abokai. (missing the closing ba after the negated clause)

Correction: Ban san kowa a Legas ba tun farko, amma yanzu na sami abokai. — As consolidated since Chapter 6 and reviewed in Chapter 14, the closing ba remains required even within a longer narrative sentence.

Answer Key / Amsoshi

  2. Legas ta fi Kano girma, fiye da yadda nake tsammani.
  3. Ban san kowa a Legas ba tun farko.
  4. Na yi tafiya daga Kano zuwa Legas.

Tambayoyin fahimta na tattaunawa (Dialogue comprehension)

  1. Ibrahim describes buying a ticket, traveling two days, arriving in the evening, and finding a place to stay.
  2. He says Lagos is bigger than Kano and more than he expected, and that rent is not the same as in Kano — it is more expensive.
  3. Yes, he felt lonely at first because he knew no one, but he later made many friends at work.
  4. He says it was work, not love of the city alone, that made him migrate.

Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

(1) The speaker describes buying a ticket and traveling to Kaduna, being met by a relative and going to their house, and returning to Kano after a week. (2) The speaker says Kaduna is similar to Kano in some ways, but houses are more spread out; the distance between houses is not the same as in Kano. (3) No, the speaker did not find the trip difficult, because it was a familiar route.

Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

  1. The writer describes family doubt about the move, leaving for education, realizing city life differs from village life, and concluding that family and friends matter most.
  2. The true reason was seeking better education, not simply escaping the village.
  3. The writer compares how much the city mixes with different kinds of people and how food costs more than expected.
  4. The writer concludes that family and friends are most important wherever one lives.

B1–B2 Development · Unit IV, Chapter 5 (Final) · Speak and Write Hausa: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Overview

Chapter 20 completes Unit IV and Volume III with the Final B2 Transition Project. Rather than introducing new grammar or vocabulary domains, this chapter asks learners to combine research, media input, discussion, and extended writing into a single connected-discourse project on a theme of personal or community significance. The chapter draws together the travel and migration narration of Chapter 16, the written genres of Chapter 17, the style and proverbs of Chapter 18, and the writing-heritage awareness of Chapter 19, while formally assessing the connected narration, reference tracking, and register/pragmatics systems that have been Consolidated across Unit IV. This chapter also serves as the Volume III capstone, in which the specialized, largely receptive strands introduced across Chapters 11-19 (advanced valency, rhetorical focus, advanced relational and negation frames, advanced regional/style analysis, advanced lexical choice, and ceremonial/professional formulas) reach their scheduled Assessed status at the general-recognition and appropriately controlled-production level defined by the Volume III matrices.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Select and narrow a connected-discourse project topic drawing on Chapters 16-19 themes.
  2. Gather information from at least one listening or reading source and integrate it into your own connected Hausa discourse.
  3. Organize an extended spoken or written text using connected narration, sequencing, and reference tracking developed since Chapter 16.
  4. Participate in a discussion that states an opinion, supports it with reasons, and responds respectfully to another viewpoint.
  5. Produce an extended piece of written Hausa in Boko that integrates description, narration, and explanation.
  6. Apply register-appropriate language, recycled proverbs or figurative expressions, and accurate special characters throughout the project.
  7. Reflect on your own progress across Volume III and identify strengths and areas for continued study.

Communication Scenario

A community learning center in your area is hosting an evening of short presentations by adult Hausa learners. Each participant chooses a topic connected to travel, migration, community life, or Hausa writing heritage, researches it briefly, and presents a short connected account to the group, followed by a discussion. You have been asked to prepare and deliver one such presentation, and to submit a written version afterward for the center's newsletter.

  1. Domain: connected discourse project work, drawing on travel/migration, written genres, style, and writing-heritage themes from Unit IV.
  2. Developed skill: organizing an extended spoken and written account, integrating a source, and participating in follow-up discussion, assessing the Final volume capstone and Unit IV connected-discourse systems.
  3. Recycled grammar in context: connected narration and sequencing, reference tracking, reasons and comparison, and register/pragmatics, all Consolidated since Chapter 16.
  4. All Chapter 1-19 vocabulary and grammar remain active and available throughout this chapter's project; no unscheduled grammar or productive Ajami writing is introduced.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Phrase-level flow in extended speech

When delivering an extended presentation, maintain natural phrase rhythm and pause at meaningful breaks rather than at every clause. Continue to monitor tone and vowel length in high-frequency words such as ra'ayi, tattaunawa, and bincike, and keep the special consonants in ƙarshe and taƙaitawa clear, since these words will recur throughout your presentation and discussion.

Connected Discourse Review

This chapter introduces no new grammar system. It reviews and formally assesses the connected-discourse tools developed across Chapters 16-19: sequencing and narration, reference tracking, reasons and comparison, and register-appropriate style, together with the receptive Ajami and script-heritage awareness built in Chapter 19. This is consistent with the Volume III Assessment Matrix's Unit III integrated assessment having already occurred at Chapter 15, and with the Final volume capstone reaching Assessed status at C20 across the connected-narration, reference-tracking, and register/pragmatics strands, together with the specialized advanced strands scheduled as Receptive through Chapters 11-15 and Developed/Consolidated through Chapters 16-19.

20.1 Review: organizing connected discourse

An effective presentation or extended written text is not simply a list of separate sentences. It uses sequencing words, reference tracking (so that pronouns and names are used clearly rather than repeated or omitted confusingly), and reasons or comparisons to connect ideas. Review the following connecting devices from earlier chapters before planning your project.

  • Da farko... sannan... daga baje... a ƙarshe... First... then... after that... finally... (sequencing a narrative or presentation)
  • Saboda haka... Therefore... (introducing a reason-based conclusion)
  • Idan aka kwatanta da... If compared with... (introducing a comparison)
  • Kamar yadda na ambata a baya... As I mentioned earlier... (tracking reference back to a prior point)

    • 20.2 Review: supporting an opinion in discussion

    During the discussion portion of your presentation, state your opinion clearly, support it with at least one reason, and respond respectfully if someone offers a different view, recycling the opinion and moderation language from Chapter 13.

    Language Note — Moderated disagreement

    Recall from Chapter 13 that disagreement in Hausa discussion is typically softened rather than stated bluntly. A phrase such as Na fahimci abin da kake nufi, amma... ("I understand what you mean, but...") acknowledges the other speaker before offering a different view. Use this pattern, or an equivalent recycled from Chapter 13, during your project discussion.

    20.3 Review: integrating a source respectfully

    When your presentation draws on something you read, heard, or learned from a family member or community member, briefly identify the source in general terms (for example, "in kaka ta ce," "a wata makala," or "wani malami ya bayyana cewa") rather than presenting the information as your own original claim. This recycles the source-attribution habits practiced with the news and interview material in Chapter 12.

    These review points recycle familiar structures from Chapters 12, 13, and 16-19; no new sentence pattern is introduced in this chapter.

    Model Dialogue

    Yanayin hali: Bayan gabatarwarsa a taron cibiyar koyo, Ibrahim yana amsa tambayoyi kuma yana tattaunawa da sauran ɗalibai.

    (Setting: After his presentation at the learning-center event, Ibrahim answers questions and discusses with other students.)

    ZAINAB Gabatarwarka ta yi kyau sosai, Ibrahim. Me ya sa ka zaɓi batun ƙaura?Your presentation was very good, Ibrahim. Why did you choose the topic of migration?
    IBRAHIM Na zaɓi wannan batu domin kakana ya taɓa yin ƙaura zuwa wata jiha don neman aiki, kuma na yi bincike a kan tarihinsa kafin in shirya wannan gabatarwa.I chose this topic because my grandfather once migrated to another state to look for work, and I researched his history before preparing this presentation.
    ZAINAB Na fahimci abin da kake nufi, amma ina ganin ya kamata mu tattauna dalilan da suke sa mutane ƙaura a yau, ba na baya kawai ba.I understand what you mean, but I think we should also discuss the reasons that cause people to migrate today, not only in the past.
    IBRAHIM Gaskiya ne. Idan aka kwatanta da zamanin kakana, yau da yawa mutane suna ƙaura don ilimi ko sabon aiki a birni.That's true. Compared with my grandfather's time, today many people migrate for education or a new job in the city.
    ZAINAB Saboda haka, ina tsammanin batun ƙaura ba zai taɓa ƙarewa a matsayin muhimmin bangare na rayuwar al'umma ba.Therefore, I think the topic of migration will never stop being an important part of community life.

    Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

    1. Why did Ibrahim choose migration as his presentation topic?
    2. What does Zainab suggest should also be discussed?
    3. According to Ibrahim, how do the reasons for migration today compare with his grandfather's time?

    Speaking Practice

    Atisayen ƙa'ida — Controlled substitution

    With a partner, take turns practicing the four connecting devices from Section 20.1 by inserting each one into a short sentence about your chosen project topic.

    Tattaunawa — Communicative task

    Deliver a two- to three-minute presentation on your chosen topic to a partner or small group, then answer at least two follow-up questions and respond to one differing opinion using moderated disagreement.

    Madadin karatu kai tsaye — Solo alternative

    Record your presentation on your own, then listen back and note one place where you used a connecting device successfully and one place you could improve.

    Reading Practice

    Read the short passage below. Underline every connecting device you recognize from Section 20.1, and note whether the passage is mainly narrating, comparing, or concluding at each point.

    Karatu — Rahoton Ɗalibi (Reading: A Student Report, Short Passage)

    Da farko, na yi tunanin batun aikina zai zama mai wahala, saboda ban san yadda zan tara bayanai ba. Sannan, na yi magana da kakata game da tarihin iyalinmu, kuma ta ba ni bayanai masu ban sha'awa. Daga baje, na rubuta tsarin gabatarwata bisa waɗannan bayanai. Idan aka kwatanta da yadda na fara, yanzu na fi fahimtar yadda ake shirya aiki mai haɗi cikin tsari. Saboda haka, na koyi cewa bincike da tsari suna taimakawa sosai wajen kammala aiki mai kyau.

    Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

    1. What difficulty did the student first anticipate?
    2. Who did the student consult, and what did that person provide?
    3. What conclusion does the student reach about research and organization?

    Writing Workshop

    1. Karanta misalai da hankali. Reread Sections 20.1-20.3 and the Model Dialogue.
    2. Tabbatar da batu da tushe. Confirm your project topic and the source or sources you identified in Guided Practice.
    3. Tsara rubutunka. Organize your written project into introduction, background, main points, and conclusion, using at least two connecting devices from Section 20.1.
    4. Rubuta. Write 150-200 words in Hausa, in Boko, presenting your chosen topic, integrating your source respectfully, and including at least one recycled proverb, idiom, or stylistic feature from Chapter 18 where natural.
    5. Duba jerin bincike. Check your draft against the checklist below.
    6. Rubuta siffa ta ƙarshe. Produce a clean final version suitable for submission to the newsletter scenario.

    Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

    • The project has a clear introduction, background, main points, and conclusion.
    • At least two connecting devices from Section 20.1 are used accurately.
    • Any information drawn from a source is attributed in general terms, not presented as invented fact.
    • Register is appropriate to a community presentation or newsletter piece, consistent with Chapters 13 and 17.
    • At least one recycled grammar point from Chapters 1-19 appears and is used correctly.
    • Special characters (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ, ƴ) and apostrophe/glottal conventions are used accurately in the Boko text.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Community presentation and shared learning

    Community learning events, family gatherings, and religious or civic occasions are common contexts for sharing personal histories, community observations, and short instructional talks in Hausa-speaking communities. Presentations of this kind commonly draw on family memory, proverbs, and communal experience, but their format and formality vary by setting, generation, and region, so avoid assuming one fixed style applies everywhere.

    Regional Variation — Discussion and presentation register

    The degree of directness expected during discussion and question periods differs across communities, age groups, and settings; some contexts favor visible deference to elders or senior participants, while others allow more direct peer exchange. The moderated-disagreement pattern in Section 20.2 is a widely usable default for this course, not the only acceptable style.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Presenting a list of facts without connection

    Error: Stringing together unconnected sentences about the topic without sequencing, comparison, or concluding language.

    Correction: Use the connecting devices reviewed in Section 20.1, such as da farko, sannan, idan aka kwatanta da, and saboda haka, to link ideas into a coherent account, as modeled in the Model Dialogue and Reading Practice passage.

    Common Mistake — Presenting a source's information as one's own claim

    Error: Reporting a fact learned from a relative, article, or teacher as though it were the presenter's own original statement.

    Correction: Briefly identify the source in general terms, as reviewed in Section 20.3 and modeled by Ibrahim's presentation, before stating what was learned.

    Common Mistake — Responding to disagreement too abruptly

    Error: Rejecting a differing opinion directly without acknowledgment, which can sound unnecessarily blunt in many Hausa discussion contexts.

    Correction: Use a moderated pattern such as Na fahimci abin da kake nufi, amma..., as reviewed in Section 20.2 and modeled in the Model Dialogue, before offering a different view.

    Answer Key / Amsoshi

    Tambayoyin fahimta na tattaunawa (Dialogue comprehension)

    1. Ibrahim chose migration because his grandfather once migrated to another state to look for work.
    2. Zainab suggests also discussing the reasons people migrate today, not only in the past.
    3. Ibrahim says that today many people migrate for education or a new job in the city, compared with his grandfather's time.

    Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

    (1) Zainab's topic is change in her town over ten years. (2) New roads and schools have been built. (3) She uses "saboda haka" ("therefore") to introduce her conclusion.

    Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

    1. The student anticipated difficulty because they did not know how to gather information.
    2. The student consulted their grandmother, who provided interesting information about the family's history.
    3. The student concludes that research and organization are very helpful for completing good work.

    Sashi na B (Chapter Review Connected Discourse)

    1. Da farko... sannan... daga baje... a ƙarshe... ("first... then... after that... finally..."); Saboda haka... ("therefore..."); Idan aka kwatanta da... ("if compared with..."); Kamar yadda na ambata a baya... ("as I mentioned earlier...").
    2. Sample: A speaker should briefly name the source in general terms, such as a family member or an article, rather than presenting the information as an original personal claim.
    3. Sample: Na fahimci abin da kake nufi, amma... ("I understand what you mean, but...").

    Sashi na C (Sample writing)

    Batuna shi ne yadda iyalina suka yi ƙaura zuwa birni shekaru da yawa da suka wuce. Da farko, kakana ya bar ƙauyenmu domin neman aiki, domin ba a sami isasshen aiki a wurin ba. Sannan, ya zauna a birni har sai da ya sami kuɗin gina gida. Kamar yadda babana ya taɓa gaya mini, "kowa ya san inda yake, amma ba kowa ke sanin inda zai kai ba." Idan aka kwatanta da yau, mutane da yawa suna yin irin wannan ƙaura don ilimi ko sabon aiki. Saboda haka, na koyi cewa tarihin iyali yana taimakawa wajen fahimtar canjin al'umma baki ɗaya.

    Other accurate, well-organized passages meeting the stated requirements are equally acceptable.

Sashe na 1 — Unit I: Kai da Rayuwa (Self and Life Experience)
4

Lafiya da Rayuwa Mai Kyau

B1 Development · Speak and Write Hausa: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Overview

Chapter 4 continues Unit I by turning from place to personal well-being. You will describe daily health routines, talk about access to care, discuss prevention, and explain personal choices that support good health. This chapter carries forward the identity and existential predicate patterns, completive/habitual narration, the comparative pattern, and reference-tracking systems introduced and practiced in Chapters 1 through 3, and applies them to a new communicative task: discussing health and well-being rather than people or places.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe a daily health routine, including sleep, food, exercise, and rest.
  2. Name and discuss common health concerns and ways of accessing care.
  3. Compare two habits or routines using the consolidated comparative pattern.
  4. Describe a change in a health habit, using a "before and now" contrast.
  5. Discuss simple prevention practices and personal health choices.
  6. Ask a partner about their health routine and respond to their answers.
  7. Track reference to more than one habit or person across a connected passage.
  8. Ask for clarification or repetition when a description of health or routine is not understood.
  9. Read and interpret a short passage describing a health routine or a change in habits.
  10. Write a short, organized paragraph describing a health routine, a comparison, and a change.

Communication Scenario

The following week, the instructor asks Halima and Sule to describe their health routines and how they have changed. Sule explains that he now sleeps earlier and walks to work instead of taking a motorcycle, and describes why. Halima compares her diet now with her diet a few years ago, and explains a small change she made after visiting a clinic. As they talk, each asks the other to clarify a detail about a routine or a reason. This chapter follows their conversation and asks you to prepare a similar description of your own health routine or one you know well.

  1. No new vocabulary domain beyond health, routine, prevention, and personal-choice terms is introduced in this chapter; place and infrastructure vocabulary from Chapter 3, relationship vocabulary from Chapter 2, and personal-history vocabulary from Chapter 1 remain active.
  2. No new grammar system is introduced. This chapter continues to consolidate independent pronouns, subject-reference patterns, and completive/habitual forms already active since Volume II, and extends the comparative pattern, the "before and now" contrast, and reference tracking from Chapters 2 and 3 to descriptions of health routines and personal choices.
  3. This chapter continues Unit I, which will be formally assessed in Chapter 5.
  4. Vocabulary and grammar from Chapters 1 through 3, and from Volume I and Volume II, remain fully active and are expected to appear accurately throughout this chapter's tasks.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Tone and vowel length in health vocabulary

This chapter's pronunciation focus continues consolidating tone and vowel length in a recycled-pattern vocabulary set. Pay particular attention to the tone contrast between lafiya (health/well-being) and words of similar shape, and to the vowel length in ciwo (illness, pain), since confusing these can affect whether a listener understands you to be describing good health or a health complaint. As in Chapters 1 through 3, keep an even syllable rhythm across full sentences, especially when a sentence names more than one habit or routine and compares them.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new grammar system. It continues to consolidate independent pronouns, subject-reference patterns, and completive/habitual forms already active since Volume II, and extends the comparative pattern and reference-tracking work begun in Chapters 1 through 3 to passages describing health routines and personal choices.

4.1 Reviewing: Describing a routine with existential and habitual predicates

  • Ina da lafiya lau, kuma ina motsa jiki kowace safiya.I am completely well, and I exercise every morning.
  • Yana da ciwon kai kaɗan, amma yana shan magani.He has a slight headache, but he is taking medicine.

The existential pattern with yana da, consolidated since Volume II and used in Chapter 3 to describe a place's features, is recycled here to describe a person's health condition or habit rather than a place's infrastructure.

4.2 Reviewing: Comparing two habits or routines

Language Building Block — Comparing Two Habits
[habit/person A] ya/ta fi [habit/person B] [adjective]
Cin abinci mai gina jiki ya fi cin abincin kwalta lafiya.
Eating nutritious food is healthier than eating fast food.
Sule ya fi ni yin motsa jiki.
Sule exercises more than I do.

The comparative pattern ya/ta fi, already used to compare people in Chapter 2 and places in Chapter 3, is recycled here to compare habits, foods, and routines. As in earlier chapters, once the second habit or person has been named, a pronoun can replace it in a following sentence.

4.3 Reviewing: Describing a health change with "before and now"

  • A da, ba na yin motsa jiki, kuma ina cin abinci mai yawan mai.Before, I did not exercise, and I ate food with a lot of oil.
  • Amma yanzu, na canja, kuma ina tafiya da ƙafa zuwa aiki kowace rana.But now, I have changed, and I walk to work every day.

The time expression a da and the contrastive connector amma yanzu, already recycled in Chapter 3 to contrast a place's past and present, allow a speaker to contrast an earlier habit with a present one using only completive and habitual forms already consolidated.

4.4 Extending: Reference tracking across a health narrative

When a passage describes a person's health routine and a change, it usually mentions the person and the habit more than once. Track each clearly with pronouns once each has been introduced, just as you tracked places in Chapter 3.

  • Halima ta je asibiti domin ciwon kai. Likitan ya ce ta rage barci kaɗan, kuma yanzu tana barci da wuri.Halima went to the hospital because of a headache. The doctor said she should reduce her sleep a little [i.e. adjust her sleep schedule], and now she sleeps early."Halima" named once, then tracked with "ta"; "likita" named once, then tracked with "likitan...ya"

Atisayen ƙa'ida — Grammar practice: Complete each sentence with the word or expression that fits.

  1. Cin abinci mai gina jiki ___ (ya fi/ne) cin abincin kwalta lafiya.
  2. ___ (A da/Yanzu), ba na motsa jiki, amma yanzu ina yi kowace rana.
  3. Yana da ciwon kai kaɗan, ___ (amma/domin) yana shan magani.
  4. Halima ta je asibiti. ___ (Ita/Halima) ta ce likita ya taimaka mata sosai.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: A cikin aji, malami ya nemi Sule da Halima su kwatanta yadda suke kula da lafiyarsu.

(Setting: In class, the instructor asks Sule and Halima to describe how they take care of their health.)

SULE A da, ba na yin motsa jiki, kuma ina cin abincin kwalta sosai. Amma yanzu, na canja.Before, I didn't exercise, and I ate a lot of fast food. But now, I have changed.
HALIMA Shin, me ya sa ka canja?What made you change?
SULE Na kan ji ciwon kai sosai, sai na je asibiti. Likita ya ce ni ya kamata na yi motsa jiki, kuma na rage cin abincin kwalta.I used to get headaches a lot, so I went to the hospital. The doctor said I should exercise, and I should reduce eating fast food.
HALIMA Ban gama fahimta ba. Ka ce likita ne ya faɗa maka, ko kai kanka ka gane?I didn't fully understand. Did you say it was the doctor who told you, or did you realize it yourself?
SULE Likita ne ya faɗa mini, kuma na fahimci cewa yana da gaskiya bayan na fara motsa jiki. Kai fa, yaya kike kula da lafiyarki?It was the doctor who told me, and I understood he was right after I started exercising. What about you, how do you take care of your health?
HALIMA Ni kuwa, ina cin abinci mai gina jiki fiye da da, kuma ina barci da wuri. Cin abinci mai gina jiki ya fi cin abincin kwalta lafiya.As for me, I eat more nutritious food than before, and I sleep early. Eating nutritious food is healthier than eating fast food.
SULE Gaskiya ne. Amma na fi ki yin motsa jiki, domin ina tafiya da ƙafa zuwa aiki kowace rana.That's true. But I exercise more than you, because I walk to work every day.
HALIMA Wannan gaskiya ne. Kowa yana da hanyarsa ta kula da lafiya, muddin tana da amfani.That's true. Everyone has their own way of taking care of their health, as long as it is beneficial.

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. What was Sule's health habit before, and what did he change?
  2. What made Sule change his habit?
  3. What does Halima ask Sule to clarify, and why?
  4. How does Halima describe her own health routine?
  5. What general statement does Halima make at the end of the conversation?

Garba ba ya yin motsa jiki sosai, amma ɗan'uwansa Bello yana yin motsa jiki kowace safiya. Garba ya ce shi, ba shi da lokaci, domin aikinsa yana ɗaukar sa'o'i da yawa. Bello kuwa yana tafiya da ƙafa zuwa aiki, kuma ya fi Garba ƙarfin jiki. A da, Bello shi ma ba ya motsa jiki, amma bayan ya ji ciwon baya sosai, sai ya je asibiti. Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

  • The health routine or habit being described is clearly identified.
  • A reason or cause is stated accurately.
  • A comparison between two habits or people is made using ya/ta fi.
  • A "before and now" contrast appears where appropriate.
  • The second person or habit is tracked afterward with a pronoun, not repeated by name.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Health, care, and access

Access to hospitals, clinics, and medicine varies considerably across Hausa-speaking communities, just as infrastructure access varied in Chapter 3, and has been changing over time. Some households rely on nearby clinics, others travel farther for care, and traditional and biomedical approaches to health sometimes coexist within the same family or community. Presenting Sule's, Halima's, Garba's, or Bello's routines and choices as individual examples, rather than as a universal description of "how Hausa speakers take care of their health," is a more accurate way to describe this variation.

Regional Variation — Talking about illness and treatment

Vocabulary and expressions for discussing illness, treatment, and recovery can vary by region, generation, and setting, and speakers may draw on Arabic-origin, English-origin, or locally rooted terms depending on the context and the type of care being discussed. This chapter uses general, widely understood vocabulary; more specialized or regionally marked medical terms are introduced for recognition in later chapters when the topic requires them.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Repeating a name instead of tracking reference in a health narrative

Error: *Halima ta je asibiti. Halima ta ji ciwon kai. Halima ta sha magani, kuma Halima ta warke. (repeating the full name in every sentence of a passage about one person)

Correction: Halima ta je asibiti domin ciwon kai. Ta sha magani, kuma ta warke. — Once a person has been named, continue with the pronoun ya/ta, exactly as practiced with people in Chapter 2 and places in Chapter 3.

Common Mistake — Placing the compared item incorrectly in the ya/ta fi pattern

Error: *Ni na fi lafiya Sule. (placing the point of comparison, lafiya, before the person compared to)

Correction: Ni na fi Sule lafiya. — The pattern is [subject] + ya/ta fi + [item compared to] + [quality]. The quality word always comes last, as already practiced in Chapters 2 and 3.

Tambayoyin fahimta na tattaunawa (Dialogue comprehension)

  1. Before, Sule did not exercise and ate a lot of fast food; he changed by starting to exercise and reducing fast food.
  2. Frequent headaches led Sule to visit the hospital, where a doctor advised him to exercise and reduce fast food.
  3. Halima asks Sule to clarify whether the doctor told him to change or whether he realized it himself; he confirms it was the doctor, and that he came to agree after exercising.
  4. Halima describes eating more nutritious food than before and sleeping early.
  5. Halima states that everyone has their own way of taking care of their health, as long as it is beneficial.

Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

(1) In the past, Amina did not exercise and ate a lot of fast food because she had no time. (2) Frequent stomach pain led her to visit the hospital, where a doctor advised changes. (3) She now walks to the market instead of riding, exercises each morning, and eats more nutritious food. (4) She says she still sometimes wants fast food, because nutritious food takes longer to prepare.

Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

  1. Garba does not exercise much, giving lack of time due to his job as the reason.
  2. Bello exercises every morning and walks to work; he is stronger than Garba.
  3. Bello changed his routine after severe back pain led him to the hospital, where a doctor advised exercise and walking.
  4. Garba says he plans to follow his brother's example, because he has realized health is more valuable than anything else.

  2. Sample: A da, ba na motsa jiki, amma yanzu ina yi kowace rana.

Sashi na C (Sample writing)

A da, ba na yin motsa jiki, kuma ina cin abincin kwalta sosai domin ba ni da lokaci. Amma bayan na ji ciwon kai sau da yawa, na je asibiti. Likita ya ce ni ya kamata na canja rayuwata. Yanzu, ina tafiya da ƙafa zuwa aiki kowace rana, kuma ina cin abinci mai gina jiki fiye da da. Cin abinci mai gina jiki ya fi cin abincin kwalta lafiya.

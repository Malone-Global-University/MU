Sashe na 2 — Unit II: Hulɗar Zamani (Modern Life and Interaction)
6

Ilimi da Horaswa

B1 Development · Unit II, Chapter 1 · Speak and Write Hausa: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse
Unit II Begins — New systems introduced: location/possession/quantity/routine clauses, early TAM/aspect frames, noun gender and plural as lexical entries, market/service repair formulas.

Chapter Overview

Chapter 6 opens Unit II of Volume III and shifts the conversation toward education and training. You will learn to talk about schools, courses, exams, skills, and digital learning, while building four new grammar systems: expressions of location and possession, quantity expressions, routine clauses, and the early stages of the imperfective/habitual and future TAM frames. You will also begin tracking noun gender and plural forms as lexical facts attached to individual words, and you will learn a small set of repair formulas useful in market, registration-desk, and service settings. All personal-history, relationship, place, and health vocabulary from Chapters 1 through 5 remains active and should appear naturally in your speaking and writing.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify and use at least twelve new active vocabulary items related to education, training, and digital learning.
  2. Express where something or someone is located using basic locative clauses with a and akwai.
  3. Express possession and non-possession using ina da and ba ni da.
  4. Ask and answer questions about quantity using nawa and yawa.
  5. Describe a routine activity using a habitual clause pattern.
  6. Recognize and begin producing the imperfective/progressive frame with ina/kana/tana + verbal noun.
  7. Record the gender and plural form of a new noun as part of learning that noun.
  8. Use at least two repair formulas to ask a clerk, instructor, or vendor to repeat, slow down, or clarify.
  9. Read a short passage about a course or exam and answer factual and inferential questions.
  10. Write a short paragraph describing your own education, training, or a skill you are learning.

Communication Scenario

Halima has decided to register for a computer skills course at a training center near her neighborhood in Kaduna. At the registration desk, she asks about the location of the classroom, the cost, the schedule, and what she needs to bring. The clerk, Malam Bello, answers her questions and explains the daily routine of the course. Halima does not understand one instruction the first time and asks him to repeat it more slowly. Later, she tells her brother Sule about the course and what she does there every day.

  1. New vocabulary domain: education, training, skills, exams, and digital learning.
  2. New grammar: location and possession clauses, quantity expressions, routine/habitual clauses, and the early imperfective/progressive TAM frame.
  3. New system: noun gender and plural recorded as part of each vocabulary entry.
  4. New pragmatic system: market/service repair formulas for requesting repetition or slower speech.
  5. All Chapter 1-5 vocabulary and grammar remain active and should be recycled throughout this chapter's tasks.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Vowel length in darasi, jarrabawa, and rajista

Several new words in this chapter contain vowel-length contrasts that affect meaning elsewhere in Hausa, even though this chapter does not assess a new tone or vowel-length rule. Practice darasi with a short first vowel, jarrabawa with careful attention to its four syllables, and rajista with a clear final syllable. Continue applying the tone and vowel-length control established in Chapters 1 through 5 as you produce these longer, multi-syllable education terms, since longer words make rhythm control more demanding.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces four new systems: location/possession/quantity/routine clauses, the early imperfective/progressive TAM frame, noun gender/plural as lexical entries, and market/service repair formulas. Each is presented for communicative use at the Introduced (I) level, consistent with the Volume III scope for Chapter 6.

6.1 Location: a and akwai

Language Building Block — Saying Where Something Is
[noun] yana/tana a [place] · Akwai [noun] a [place]
Ɗakin kwas yana a hawa na biyu.
The course room is on the second floor.
Akwai kwamfutoci a ɗakin darasi.
There are computers in the classroom.

Use yana/tana a when the subject is a specific, already-identified noun. Use akwai to introduce the existence of something without emphasizing a specific identity, similar to English "there is/are."

6.2 Possession: ina da and ba ni da

  • Ina da littafi da fensir don darasi.I have a book and a pencil for class.
  • Ba ni da kuɗin rajista tukuna.I don't have the registration money yet.ba + pronoun + da = negative possession

These formulaic possessive patterns already appear in fixed form. This chapter asks you to control them productively across the education domain rather than as isolated phrases.

6.3 Quantity: nawa and yawa

  • Nawa ne kuɗin kwas?How much is the course fee?
  • Akwai ɗalibai da yawa a darasin.There are many students in the class.

Nawa asks for a specific quantity or price; da yawa expresses "many" or "a lot" following the noun it describes.

6.4 Routine clauses and the early imperfective/habitual frame

Language Building Block — Describing a Routine
ina/kana/tana + [verbal noun] (imperfective/progressive/habitual)
Ina zuwa kwas kowace safiya, kuma ina koyon fasahar kwamfuta.
I go to the course every morning, and I am learning computer skills.
Malami yana koyar da darasi sau biyu a mako.
The teacher teaches the lesson twice a week.

This chapter introduces the ina/kana/tana + verbal noun frame as your first productive TAM/aspect system beyond completive forms. It expresses both an action in progress and a habitual routine, depending on context and time expressions such as kowace rana ("every day") or yanzu ("now"). Full negative and future TAM forms remain scheduled for Chapters 7-9 and are not assessed here.

6.5 Noun gender and plural as lexical entries

Beginning with this chapter, record the gender (masculine or feminine) and plural form of every new noun as part of learning that noun, as shown in the Core Vocabulary table above. Do not guess a plural pattern from a rule; learn each plural as a fact about that specific word. Some nouns, such as fasaha and rajista, have no common plural in ordinary use at this level and should be learned as singular-only for now.

6.6 Market/service repair formulas

  • Don Allah, ka/ki sake maimaita?Please, could you repeat that?
  • Ban gane sosai ba. Za ka/ki iya yin sannu-sannu?I didn't understand well. Can you speak more slowly?
  • Me ake nufi da "rajista"?What does "rajista" mean?

These formulas are useful whenever a clerk, vendor, or instructor speaks too quickly or uses an unfamiliar word. Use them at the registration desk in this chapter's scenario, and continue using them in later service and market contexts.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Halima ta je wurin rajista domin kwas na fasahar kwamfuta.

(Setting: Halima goes to the registration desk for a computer skills course.)

HALIMA Sannu, malam. Ina so na yi rajista domin kwas na fasahar kwamfuta.Hello, sir. I would like to register for the computer skills course.
MALAM BELLO Sannu. Kwas yana farawa mako mai zuwa. Ɗakin darasi yana a hawa na biyu, kuma akwai kwamfutoci goma a wajen.Hello. The course starts next week. The classroom is on the second floor, and there are ten computers there.
HALIMA Nawa ne kuɗin rajista?How much is the registration fee?
MALAM BELLO Kuɗin rajista dubu biyar ne, kuma za ka biya kuɗin makaranta kowane wata.The registration fee is five thousand, and you will pay school fees every month.
HALIMA Ban gane sosai ba. Za ka iya yin sannu-sannu?I didn't understand well. Can you speak more slowly?
MALAM BELLO To. Kuɗin rajista, dubu biyar. Sannan, kuɗin makaranta kowane wata.Okay. The registration fee, five thousand. Then, school fees every month.
HALIMA Na gode. Kuma, ina koyon fasahar kwamfuta sau nawa a mako?Thank you. And, how many times a week do I learn computer skills?
MALAM BELLO Ɗalibai suna zuwa sau uku a mako, kuma bayan wata uku, za ku sami takardar shaida.Students come three times a week, and after three months, you will receive a certificate.

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. What course does Halima want to register for?
  2. Where is the classroom, and what is in it?
  3. What does Halima ask Malam Bello to do, and why?
  4. How often do students attend the course, and what do they receive after three months?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Training centers and skill acquisition

Short-term training centers offering skills such as computer use, phone repair, tailoring, and trades are common in many Hausa-speaking urban and semi-urban areas, but the cost, format, and availability of such centers vary widely by city, region, and household resources. Halima's and Zainab's experiences represent individual, plausible situations rather than a description of how all learners in Hausa-speaking communities access training.

Regional Variation — Terms for fees and registration

Vocabulary and administrative procedures around school fees, registration, and certificates can differ between Nigerian and Nigerien educational contexts, and even between institutions within the same country. This chapter's vocabulary reflects a common, general pattern and should be treated as a starting point rather than a fixed nationwide standard.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing akwai and yana/tana a

Error: *Kwamfuta akwai a ɗakin darasi. (placing the specific subject before akwai)

Correction: Kwamfutar tana a ɗakin darasi. or Akwai kwamfuta a ɗakin darasi. — Use yana/tana a for a specific, already-identified noun, and place akwai at the start of the clause when introducing existence.

Common Mistake — Omitting the verbal noun after ina/kana/tana

Error: *Ina fasahar kwamfuta kowace rana. (missing the verbal noun "koyo")

Correction: Ina koyon fasahar kwamfuta kowace rana. — The imperfective/habitual frame requires a verbal noun after ina/kana/tana; the activity noun alone is not sufficient.

Salon Magana da Karin Magana

B1–B2 Development · Unit IV, Chapter 3 · Speak and Write Hausa: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Overview

Chapter 18 continues Unit IV by turning from written genres to expressive style. Building on the connected discourse and reference tracking developed in Chapter 17, this chapter introduces learners to a small, carefully controlled set of Hausa proverbs (karin magana) and figurative expressions, and develops recognition of how style and word choice shift with context. No new grammar system is introduced this chapter; proverbs and idioms are treated as formulaic, receptive-first material, consistent with the Volume III Register/Pragmatics/Culture matrix and the Focus/Emphasis matrix, both marked Consolidated at C18, with rhetorical and public-style emphasis remaining Receptive at this stage.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize a small set of common Hausa proverbs and state their general meaning in English.
  2. Distinguish a literal translation of a proverb from its intended, figurative meaning.
  3. Recognize simple figurative expressions and comparisons in spoken and written Hausa.
  4. Identify differences between plain, direct style and more vivid or figurative style in a text.
  5. Use one or two proverbs receptively in a listening or reading context without being required to produce new ones independently.
  6. Continue producing accurate, connected Hausa using Chapter 1-17 grammar and vocabulary, now enriched with occasional formulaic figurative language.
  7. Explain, in general terms, why a speaker might choose a proverb or figurative expression instead of a plain statement.
  8. Revise a short piece of writing to include one well-chosen formulaic expression appropriately.

Communication Scenario

Ibrahim's uncle, a respected elder in the family, uses proverbs frequently when giving advice. When Ibrahim mentions his new project at work, his uncle responds with a proverb rather than plain advice. Aisha later helps Ibrahim understand what the proverb means and why his uncle chose to speak that way instead of simply saying "be patient."

  1. Domain: expressive style, proverbs, and figurative language, continuing Unit IV's travel, work, and family contexts.
  2. Developed skill: recognizing and interpreting a small set of formulaic proverbs and figurative expressions, consolidating the Register/Pragmatics/Culture matrix at C18.
  3. Recycled grammar in context: comparison, negation, prepositions/relational nouns, and focus/contrast with ne/ce, all Consolidated since Unit III or Chapter 16-17.
  4. All Chapter 1-17 vocabulary and grammar remain active and available throughout this chapter's tasks; proverbs are introduced as formulaic, receptive-first units.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Rhythm and phrasing in fixed expressions

Proverbs and fixed expressions are often spoken with a distinctive rhythm and a slight pause between their two balanced halves, where the proverb has a two-part structure. Practice the vowel length in karin magana and hikima, and keep ƙ distinct from k in karin versus any similar-sounding forms you have studied.

Style, Idiom, and Proverb Patterns

This chapter introduces no new grammar system. Proverbs and figurative expressions are taught as formulaic units: learners recognize them, understand their general meaning, and may use one or two receptively, but are not required to generate new proverbs. This is consistent with the Volume III Focus/Emphasis matrix (Rhetorical focus and public-style emphasis remain Receptive at C18) and the Register/Pragmatics/Culture matrix (Consolidated at C18).

18.1 Formulaic: recognizing a proverb

A Hausa proverb usually states something concrete and then applies to a more general situation. The literal meaning and the intended meaning are often different. Do not translate a proverb word-for-word and expect the intended meaning to be obvious; instead, learn the general meaning alongside the literal wording.

Hakuri maganin duniya.

Literal: Patience is the medicine of the world.

General meaning: Patience helps a person deal with almost any problem in life.

Used when: giving comforting advice to someone facing a difficult or slow-moving situation.

Ba a hura wuta a bar dansa a ciki ba.

Literal: One does not light a fire and leave one's child inside it.

General meaning: A person does not deliberately create a situation that will harm their own family or dependents.

Used when: reassuring someone that a decision was made carefully, with their wellbeing in mind.

Kowa ya san ranarsa, ya hura wutarsa.

Literal: Whoever knows their day, lights their own fire.

General meaning: A person who understands their own situation or timing takes the necessary action for themselves.

Used when: encouraging someone to take responsibility for a decision or opportunity that is theirs to act on.

18.2 Formulaic: simple figurative comparison

  • Yana da hikima kamar dattijo. He has wisdom like an elder. (figurative: he is unusually wise for his age)
  • Zuciyarta ta yi sanyi kamar rana ta faɗi. Her heart went cold like the sun setting. (figurative: she suddenly felt discouraged or sad)

    • Figurative comparisons often use kamar ("like") to connect a concrete image to a feeling or quality, recycling the comparison structures from Chapter 16. Recognize the figurative meaning; do not analyze the grammar of kamar as a new structure, since it was introduced earlier.

    18.3 Introduced: plain style versus vivid style

    Compare the same idea expressed plainly and then more vividly:

  • Salo mai sauƙi: Ina son ya yi haƙuri. Plain style: I want him to be patient.
  • Salo mai kwatanci: Hakuri maganin duniya, don haka ya kamata ya jira. Vivid style: Patience is the medicine of the world, so he should wait.

    • Speakers and writers choose vivid, proverb-rich style in certain contexts — especially advice-giving by elders, public speaking, and storytelling — and plain style in others, such as service interactions or straightforward written reports like those in Chapter 17. Both styles are correct; the choice depends on context, relationship, and purpose.

    Model Dialogue

    Yanayin hali: Ibrahim ya gaya wa kawunsa game da sabon aikinsa. Kawunsa, wanda dattijo ne, ya yi amfani da karin magana maimakon shawara kai tsaye.

    (Setting: Ibrahim tells his uncle about his new project. His uncle, an elder, responds with a proverb instead of direct advice.)

    IBRAHIM Kawu, ina fara wani aiki na kaina, amma ina jin tsoro kadan.Uncle, I'm starting a project of my own, but I feel a little afraid.
    KAWU Ɗana, kowa ya san ranarsa, ya hura wutarsa. Kai ne ka san lokacinka.My son, whoever knows their day lights their own fire. You are the one who knows your time.
    IBRAHIM Na ji, amma ban fahimci ma'anar wannan karin maganar sosai ba.I hear you, but I don't fully understand the meaning of that proverb.
    KAWU Ma'anarsa ita ce, idan ka san cewa lokaci ya yi, kada ka jira wani ya yi maka aiki. Kai da kanka za ka fara.Its meaning is: if you know the time is right, don't wait for someone else to do the work for you. You yourself will begin.
    IBRAHIM To, na gode kawu. Amma me ya sa ba ka ce mini kawai "ka fara aikinka" ba?All right, thank you Uncle. But why didn't you just tell me "start your project"?
    KAWU Karin magana ya fi ƙarfin magana kai tsaye a wasu lokuta; yana ɗauke da hikimar dattawa, ba na ni kaɗai ba.A proverb is sometimes stronger than direct speech; it carries the wisdom of the elders, not just mine alone.

    Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

    1. What proverb does the uncle use, and what does it mean in general terms?
    2. Why does the uncle prefer to speak using a proverb rather than direct advice?
    3. How does Ibrahim show that he initially does not understand the proverb's figurative meaning?

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Proverbs, age, and authority

    In many Hausa-speaking families and communities, proverbs are commonly associated with the speech of elders and are often used to give advice with added weight and respect. This is a common and widely recognized pattern, but proverbs are not limited to elders; younger speakers, writers, broadcasters, and public figures also use them, and their frequency varies by individual, family, and setting.

    Regional Variation — Proverb wording and currency

    Some proverbs are widely known across Hausa-speaking regions, while others are more local, family-specific, or associated with a particular community. Wording can also vary slightly from one speaker or region to another while preserving the same general meaning. Treat the proverbs in this chapter as widely usable examples rather than the only correct wording.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Translating a proverb word-for-word

    Error: Assuming Ba a hura wuta a bar dansa a ciki ba is only about literal fires and children, and missing its figurative meaning.

    Correction: Learn the general, figurative meaning alongside the literal wording, as shown in the proverb cards in Section 18.1. A proverb's usefulness comes from its applied meaning, not its literal content.

    Common Mistake — Overusing proverbs in plain-style writing

    Error: Inserting two or three proverbs into a short, ordinary message or report, such as the Chapter 17 genres.

    Correction: Use at most one well-chosen proverb in a short passage, and reserve richer proverb use for storytelling, advice-giving, or public-style speech, where it is more naturally expected.

    Common Mistake — Inventing a new proverb

    Error: Creating an original "proverb-like" sentence and presenting it as a traditional Hausa proverb.

    Correction: Use only proverbs that have been taught or verified as authentic. Recognize that proverbs are inherited cultural material, not freely invented sentences.

    Answer Key / Amsoshi

    Atisayen ƙa'ida (Proverb-situation matching, Section 18.1)

    1. Hakuri maganin duniya.
    2. Ba a hura wuta a bar dansa a ciki ba.
    3. Kowa ya san ranarsa, ya hura wutarsa.

    Tambayoyin fahimta na tattaunawa (Dialogue comprehension)

    1. The uncle uses "Kowa ya san ranarsa, ya hura wutarsa," meaning that a person who knows their own timing should act on their own behalf rather than waiting for someone else.
    2. He explains that a proverb carries the accumulated wisdom of the elders and can sometimes be more persuasive than plain, direct advice.
    3. Ibrahim explicitly says he does not fully understand the proverb's meaning and asks his uncle to explain it.

    Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

    (1) The speaker's grandfather told them the proverb when they were young. (2) The general meaning is that parents do not deliberately do things that would harm their own children. (3) The speaker recalls the proverb when making decisions about their own family, and it helps guide their judgment.

    Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

    1. Aminu's friend uses "Hakuri maganin duniya," meaning that patience helps a person deal with almost any problem.
    2. No, Aminu did not quit that day; the negation Bai bar aikin ba ("He did not leave the job") signals this.
    3. The passage is mostly plain in style, with one proverb inserted at a key moment of advice, rather than dense figurative language throughout.

    Sashi na B (Chapter Review proverbs and style)

    1. Hakuri maganin duniya — patience helps with almost any problem; Ba a hura wuta a bar dansa a ciki ba — a person does not deliberately harm their own dependents; Kowa ya san ranarsa, ya hura wutarsa — a person who knows their timing should act for themselves.
    2. Sample: Plain style states an idea directly, such as Ina son ya yi haƙuri. Vivid style uses proverbs or figurative comparison, such as Hakuri maganin duniya, to express a similar idea more memorably.
    3. Sample: A person might use "Kowa ya san ranarsa, ya hura wutarsa" when encouraging a friend who keeps delaying a decision they know they are ready to make.
    4. The comparison likens a sudden feeling of discouragement to the sun setting, suggesting a sudden loss of warmth or hope.

    Sashi na C (Sample writing)

    Abokina yana son ya bar makarantarsa saboda jarabawa ta yi masa wahala. Na ce masa, "Hakuri maganin duniya, kada ka daina yanzu." Ya saurare ni, sannan ya yanke shawarar ci gaba. Bai bar makarantar ba, kuma yanzu yana kusa da kammalawa.

    Other accurate, well-formed passages meeting the stated requirements are equally acceptable.

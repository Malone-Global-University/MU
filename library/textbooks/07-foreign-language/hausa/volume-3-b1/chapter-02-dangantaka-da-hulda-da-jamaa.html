Dangantaka da Hulɗar Jama'a

B1 Development · Speak and Write Hausa: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Overview

Chapter 2 continues Unit I by turning from personal history toward the people in your life. You will discuss relationships — family, friends, neighbors, and colleagues — describe obligations you have toward others, talk about etiquette in different relationships, and learn how to disagree, apologize, and repair a misunderstanding without damaging a relationship. This chapter carries forward the completive narration, connectors, and reference-tracking systems introduced in Chapter 1 and applies them to a new communicative task: describing and discussing ongoing relationships rather than a one-time personal history.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe key relationships in your life, including family, friends, neighbors, and colleagues.
  2. Explain obligations and expectations within a relationship using consolidated TAM/aspect forms.
  3. Describe polite and impolite behavior appropriate to different relationships.
  4. State a disagreement respectfully and moderate its tone.
  5. Apologize and repair a misunderstanding in a social situation.
  6. Ask a partner about the people in their life and respond to their answers.
  7. Track reference to more than one person across a connected passage about relationships.
  8. Ask for clarification or repetition when a conversation about relationships is not understood.
  9. Read and interpret a short passage describing a relationship or a disagreement.
  10. Write a short, organized paragraph describing a relationship, an obligation, and how a disagreement was handled.

Communication Scenario

A week after their introductions in Chapter 1, Halima and Sule are paired for a class discussion activity. The instructor asks them to talk about the important people in their lives and about a time they disagreed with someone close to them but were able to resolve it. As they talk, Sule mentions a small misunderstanding with his older brother, and Halima describes her obligations to her aging grandmother. This chapter follows their conversation and asks you to prepare a similar discussion of your own relationships.

  1. No new vocabulary domain beyond relationships, obligation, etiquette, and disagreement/repair terms is introduced in this chapter; personal-history vocabulary from Chapter 1 and identity vocabulary from Volume II remain active.
  2. No new grammar system is introduced. This chapter continues to consolidate independent pronouns, subject-reference patterns, and completive/future forms already active since Volume II, and extends reference tracking to passages involving two or more people.
  3. This chapter continues Unit I, which will be formally assessed in Chapter 5.
  4. Vocabulary and grammar from Chapter 1, and from Volume I and Volume II, remain fully active and are expected to appear accurately throughout this chapter's tasks.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Tone and vowel length in relationship vocabulary

This chapter's pronunciation focus continues consolidating tone and vowel length in a recycled-pattern vocabulary set. Pay particular attention to the vowel length in maƙwabci (neighbor) and the tone contrast between hakki (obligation) and similar-sounding words, since confusing these can change the meaning of a sentence about duties and rights. As in Chapter 1, keep an even syllable rhythm across full sentences, especially when a sentence names more than one person and shifts between them.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new grammar system. It continues to consolidate independent pronouns, subject-reference patterns, and completive/future forms already active since Volume II, and extends the reference-tracking work begun in Chapter 1 to passages involving two or more people.

2.1 Reviewing: Describing obligations with hakki and wajibi

  • Hakkina ne in taimaka wa kakata, don haka nakan zo wurinta kowace mako.It is my duty to help my grandmother, so I usually come to her place every week.
  • Wajibi ne mu girmama manya a cikin iyali.It is obligatory for us to respect elders within the family.

The habitual marker nakan/kan and the identity-predicate pattern with ne/ce, both consolidated since Volume II, are recycled here to state general obligations rather than one-time actions.

2.2 Reviewing: Narrating and stating a disagreement

Language Building Block — Describing a Disagreement and Its Resolution
na/ta yi sabani da [person], domin [reason] amma daga baya, mun/muka yi sulhu
Na yi sabani da ɗan'uwana domin bai gaya mini gaskiya ba.
I had a disagreement with my brother because he did not tell me the truth.
Amma daga baya, mun yi sulhu, kuma muka fahimci juna.
But later, we reconciled, and we understood each other.

As with the sequencing pattern in Chapter 1, amma daga baya introduces a later, contrasting stage. Here it moves a narration from disagreement to reconciliation, using the same completive forms already consolidated in Volume II.

2.3 Reviewing: Apologizing and requesting forgiveness

  • Ina neman gafararka. Ban yi niyyar ɓata maka rai ba.I am asking for your forgiveness. I did not intend to upset you.
  • Ka yi haƙuri, wataƙila ban fahimce ka sosai ba.Please forgive me, perhaps I did not understand you well.

The formulaic apology ka/ki yi haƙuri and the negative completive pattern ban ... ba, both recycled from Volume II, combine here with the hedging expression wataƙila to soften an apology, consistent with the register practices introduced in Chapter 1.

2.4 Extending: Reference tracking with two or more people

When a passage describes a relationship, it usually names at least two people. Track each person clearly with pronouns or omitted subjects once each has been introduced, and be careful that the reader or listener can always tell who is doing what.

  • Sule yana da ɗan'uwa mai suna Bello. Bello ya fi shi girma, kuma sukan yi magana kowace rana ta wayar salula.Sule has a brother named Bello. Bello is older than him, and they usually talk every day by mobile phone."Sule" and "Bello" named once each; "shi" (him) and "sukan" (they, habitual) track them afterward

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: A cikin aji, malami ya haɗa Sule da Halima domin su tattauna game da dangantakarsu da mutanen kusa da su.

(Setting: In class, the instructor pairs Sule and Halima to discuss their relationships with the people close to them.)

SULE Ina da ɗan'uwa mai suna Bello, ya fi ni girma shekara biyar. Muna kusa sosai, amma mun taɓa yin sabani.I have a brother named Bello, he is five years older than me. We are very close, but we once had a disagreement.
HALIMA To, me ya faru?So, what happened?
SULE Na yi sabani da shi domin bai gaya mini gaskiya ba game da kuɗin da muka tara tare. Amma daga baya, mun yi sulhu, kuma muka fahimci juna.I had a disagreement with him because he did not tell me the truth about the money we had saved together. But later, we reconciled, and we understood each other.
HALIMA Ban gama fahimta ba. Ka ce, shi ne ya ɓoye maka gaskiya, ko kai ne?I didn't fully understand. Did you say he was the one who hid the truth from you, or was it you?
SULE A'a, shi ne. Amma na yi masa gafara domin ɗan'uwana ne kuma yana yi mini haƙuri sosai.No, it was him. But I forgave him because he is my brother and he apologized to me sincerely.
HALIMA Ni kuma ina da kaka, kuma hakkina ne in taimaka mata, domin ta tsufa yanzu. Ina zuwa wurinta kowace mako.I also have a grandmother, and it is my duty to help her, because she is old now. I go to her place every week.
SULE Wannan ladabi ne mai kyau. Ina tsammanin girmama manya wajibi ne a cikin kowace iyali.That is good courtesy. I think respecting elders is an obligation in every family.

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. Who is Bello, and what was the disagreement between him and Sule about?
  2. How was the disagreement between Sule and Bello resolved?
  3. What point does Halima ask Sule to clarify, and why?
  4. What obligation does Halima describe toward her grandmother?
  5. What general statement does Sule make about respecting elders?

Speaking Practice

With a partner, take turns naming one relationship in your life, one obligation you have within it, and one courtesy you show that person, using the sentence patterns from Sections 2.1 and 2.4.

Tattaunawa — Communicative task

With a partner, role-play the discussion scene from the Model Dialogue, but describe a real or invented disagreement you had with someone close to you and how it was resolved. Include at least one apology or forgiveness expression, one reference-tracking pronoun for a second person, and one moment where your partner asks you to clarify something.

Madadin karatu kai tsaye — Solo alternative

Reading Practice

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Obligation and etiquette within relationships

Obligations toward family members, especially elders, are commonly emphasized in Hausa-speaking communities, but the specific expectations vary by family, household structure, region, and generation. Some households place strong emphasis on regular visits and direct financial support; others express the same underlying value of care differently. Presenting one household's practice, as in Halima's account of visiting her grandmother, as a reasonable example rather than a universal rule is a more accurate way to describe this variation.

Regional Variation — Apology and disagreement styles

Directness in expressing disagreement and the specific phrasing used to apologize can vary across communities, generations, and settings. This chapter uses widely understood, generally polite expressions; more indirect or more direct styles, and community-specific formulas, are introduced for recognition in later chapters when the topic requires them.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Repeating a name instead of tracking reference between two people

Error: *Bello yana da ɗan'uwa mai suna Sule. Bello ya fi Sule girma. Bello da Sule sukan yi magana kowace rana. (repeating both full names in every sentence of a passage about two people)

Correction: Bello yana da ɗan'uwa mai suna Sule. Ya fi shi girma, kuma sukan yi magana kowace rana. — Once both people have been named, continue with pronouns, exactly as practiced with a single person in Chapter 1.

Common Mistake — Confusing hakki (a right or duty owed) with wajibi (an obligatory requirement)

Error: *Wajibi na ne in taimaka wa kakata. (using wajibi as though it took a possessive pronoun in the same way as hakki)

Correction: Hakkina ne in taimaka wa kakata. or Wajibi ne in taimaka wa kakata. — Hakki is typically possessed (hakkina, "my duty/right"), while wajibi functions as a predicate describing what is obligatory and does not normally take a possessive pronoun in the same way.

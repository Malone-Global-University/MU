A Kasuwa

At the Market · Speak and Write Hausa: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify common market goods and articles of clothing using correct gender and plural forms.
  2. Ask and state prices using Nawa ne…? and the Hausa currency systems of Nigeria and Niger.
  3. Request specific quantities, sizes, and colors of an item.
  4. Use direct-object pronouns in place of repeated noun objects.
  5. Recognize how a small set of high-frequency verbs change form before a noun object versus a direct-object pronoun.
  6. Evaluate and choose among two or more items using comparison-free descriptive language.
  7. Use demonstratives, adjectives, and linkers together in expanded descriptive phrases.
  8. Ask and answer information questions about goods using the relative-continuative forms introduced formulaically in Chapter 3 and expanded since Chapter 6.
  9. Complete a short purchase from greeting through payment with appropriate courtesy.

Communication Scenario

You need to outfit yourself for the coming weeks: a new piece of clothing, some household items, and a few things from the food stalls you have already learned about in Chapter 8. You head to Kasuwar Kurmi-style market in Kano — or, if you live elsewhere, to a market modeled on it — where vendors sell clothing, cloth, household goods, and produce side by side. To manage this well in Hausa, you need vocabulary for goods, sizes, and colors; you need to ask and understand prices; and you need a new grammatical tool that has been quietly waiting in the background since Chapter 3: a pronoun that can stand in for a noun you have already mentioned, so you do not have to repeat "the shirt" five times in one conversation.

  1. Until now, every object in your Hausa sentences has been a full noun: Ina son shinkafa (I want rice), Ina da littafi (I have a book). This chapter introduces direct-object pronouns — short forms that replace a noun object once it is clear from context, similar to how English replaces "the shirt" with "it." As you will see, a small number of common verbs change their final vowel or tone when a direct-object pronoun follows them. This is not random: it is a regular feature of Hausa verb behavior that you will meet in a carefully limited, verified form.
  2. Notice that the question Nawa ne wannan? (How much is this?) uses tools you already control — ne, a demonstrative — combined with a new question word, nawa. You do not need new grammar to ask a price; you need new vocabulary slotted into a pattern you already know. Watch for this throughout the chapter: much of what feels new is actually familiar grammar carrying new vocabulary.

Guda functions as a counter for individual items: guda biyu (two pieces/units), used after the noun and number when counting discrete objects, e.g., riga guda biyu (two gowns). Babba and karami show masculine, feminine, and plural agreement, reviewed in the grammar section below.

Language Note — babba and karami agreement

Unlike most color terms, babba (big) and karami (small) are true Hausa adjectives that agree with the noun they describe. The masculine and feminine singular forms are babba / babba (no change for many speakers in casual use, though some contexts use babba for masculine and a related form for feminine — this course treats babba as invariant for singular reference and reserves full adjective paradigms for later expansion), while the plural is manyan for "big" and kanana for "small." Learn the plural forms as fixed items now: riguna manyan (big gowns), riguna kanana (small gowns). The complete adjective agreement system continues to expand through Chapter 18.

Pronunciation and Boko

Tone and length in prices and quantities

Numbers and price vocabulary contain tone and length contrasts that affect intelligibility in a market setting, where mishearing a number has real consequences. Pay close attention to your instructor's or audio model's pronunciation of ɗari (hundred) and dubu (thousand), and practice the rhythm of combined numbers such as dubu biyu da ɗari biyar (2,500).

FeatureWhere it appearsGuidance
Vowel length in nawaprice questionsMaintain the vowel quality and length your model demonstrates; do not shorten it to match English "how."
Tone in ɗari versus dari-like wordsnumbersƊari uses the hooked ɗ and a specific tone pattern; never substitute plain d.

Object-conditioned pronunciation

As you will see in the grammar section, a small number of common verbs change their final vowel when followed directly by a pronoun object rather than a noun object or no object. This is a pronunciation and spelling matter as much as a grammar matter: the change is regular for the verbs taught here and must be reproduced accurately in both speech and writing.

Sound Focus — Final-vowel changes before pronoun objects

Listen carefully to the contrast between ina son riga (I want a gown — noun object) and ina son ta (I want it — pronoun object, referring to the feminine noun riga). In this case, the verb phrase itself does not change shape, but the object position is filled by a short pronoun instead of a noun. With certain verbs, such as saya (to buy), the form before a pronoun object shows a vowel change: sayi appears before a noun object in some constructions, while a distinct verified form is used before a direct pronoun object. This course teaches only the specific, verified forms presented in the grammar section — do not generalize a vowel change to a verb that has not been verified for you. Your instructor or audio model will demonstrate the correct pronunciation of each verified form.

Agreement and linker endings in connected speech

Review the linkers -n and -r from Chapter 4 as they appear in market descriptions: farashin rigar (the price of the gown — masculine linker on farashi, since farashi is masculine) versus girman tukunyar (the size of the pot). In connected speech, these endings blend smoothly into the following word; practice saying full phrases rather than isolated words.

Grammar Instruction

9.1 Direct-object pronouns

Purpose: replace a noun object that has already been mentioned, so that descriptions and transactions sound natural rather than repetitive.

Until now, every object in your Hausa sentences has been a full noun phrase. Hausa, like English, has a separate set of short pronoun forms used specifically as direct objects. These forms are not the same as the independent pronouns you learned in Chapter 3 (ni, kai, ke, shi, ita, mu, ku, su) — although several direct-object forms resemble the independent pronouns closely, others differ, and the position in the sentence is different: a direct-object pronoun follows the verb directly, taking the place of the noun object.

Person / Number / GenderDirect-object formEnglish equivalent
1st singularnime
2nd singular masculinekayou (m.)
2nd singular femininekiyou (f.)
3rd singular masculineshihim, it (masc. noun)
3rd singular femininetaher, it (fem. noun)
1st pluralmuus
2nd pluralkuyou (pl.)
3rd pluralsuthem

Notice that the third-person forms (shi, ta, su) agree with the grammatical gender or plurality of the noun being replaced — not with any property of a person, since these forms are routinely used to refer to objects, garments, and goods. Riga (gown) is feminine, so it is replaced by ta; wando (trousers) is masculine, so it is replaced by shi; a plural noun is replaced by su.

  • Ina son riga. Ina son ta.I want a gown. I want it. (riga is feminine → ta)
  • Ina son wando. Ina son shi.I want trousers. I want them/it. (wando is masculine → shi)
  • Ina son riguna. Ina son su.I want gowns. I want them. (plural → su)
  • Ka gan ni a kasuwa jiya.You saw me at the market yesterday. (recognition: gan form discussed below)
Language Note — direct-object pronouns are not independent pronouns moved into a new slot

It is tempting to assume that a direct-object pronoun is simply the independent pronoun placed after the verb. This is wrong for two of the eight forms: the independent second-person pronouns are kai (m.) and ke (f.), but the direct-object forms are ka and ki. The other six forms happen to be identical in shape to their independent counterparts (ni, shi, ita/ta, mu, ku, su — note that ita the independent pronoun corresponds to ta as a direct object, a further difference), but identical shape does not mean identical function. Always check this table rather than assuming the independent pronoun will work as an object.

9.2 Object-conditioned verb forms: saya and gani

Purpose: use the correct, verified form of two essential market verbs depending on what follows them.

Some high-frequency Hausa verbs change their final vowel or shape depending on whether they are followed by no object, a noun object, or a direct-object pronoun. This course introduces only two verbs for this contrast, using verified forms, and limits the comparison to what is needed for market communication. Do not extend this pattern to other verbs without verification — the full grade system that explains this behavior is reserved for advanced study.

Contextsaya (to buy)gani (to see)
No object / citation formsayagani
Before a noun objectsayi (e.g., sayi riga)gani (e.g., gani riga — recognition)
Before a direct-object pronounsayar (in some frames) / verb stem + pronoun — taught here as sayi + pronoun in the fixed frame belowgan (e.g., gan ta — recognition)

For this chapter's productive purposes, focus on one clear, verified contrast using saya in the imperative and statement frames you will use while shopping:

  • Zan sayi riga.I will buy a gown. (verb form before a noun object — future construction previewed formulaically; full future system in Chapter 14)
  • Zan sayi ta.I will buy it. (feminine noun replaced by pronoun; same verb form retained before the pronoun in this frame)
  • Na sayi wando jiya.I bought trousers yesterday. (formulaic completive, recognition — full system in Chapter 16)

The form gan (rather than gani) before a direct-object pronoun, as in Na gan ta a kasuwa (I saw her at the market), is presented here for recognition only. You are not required to produce this alternation productively in this chapter; you only need to recognize it in reading and listening. Productive use of additional object-conditioned forms is expanded in Chapters 13 and 16, as specified in the Grammar Progression Matrix.

Language Focus — A Hausa verb may change shape depending on its object

English speakers are used to a verb staying the same regardless of what follows it: "I buy," "I buy a shirt," "I buy it" all use the same word buy. Hausa does not work this way for every verb. Some verbs have one form when no object follows, a related but distinct form before a full noun object, and sometimes yet another form before a direct-object pronoun. This is a real, regular feature of the language — not an exception to memorize away. At this stage, you only need the specific, verified forms taught in this chapter. As the course progresses, you will learn more verbs and more contexts, always working from verified examples rather than guessing by analogy.

Language Building Block
subject-aspect form + verified verb form + object (noun or pronoun)
Zan sayi riga.
I will buy a gown. (noun object)
Zan sayi ta.
I will buy it. (direct-object pronoun, feminine)

The verb form sayi remains constant across both examples in this taught frame. Contrast this stability with the recognition-only alternation in gani / gan, where the form does change before a pronoun. The chapter restricts productive practice to the stable, verified pattern shown here.

9.3 Asking and stating prices

Purpose: ask the price of an item and state a price in response, using familiar grammar with new vocabulary.

The price question uses the question word nawa (how much/how many) together with ne, exactly as you used ne for identification in Chapters 1 through 3. No new sentence-building grammar is required.

  • Nawa ne wannan?How much is this?
  • Nawa ne rigar nan?How much is this gown?
  • Nawa ne farashin tukunyar?How much is the price of the pot?
  • Naira ɗari biyu ne.It is two hundred naira.
  • Naira dubu biyu da ɗari biyar ne.It is two thousand five hundred naira.
  • Yana da tsada.It is expensive. (formulaic continuative + da + quality, reviewed from Chapter 6)
  • Akwai rangwame?Is there a discount? (recycling akwai from Chapter 6)

The phrase yana da tsada recycles the continuative-plus-da possession pattern from Chapter 6 (continuative subject form + da + possessed item) but applies it to a quality noun (tsada, expensiveness) rather than an object — a natural Hausa way of saying something "has" a quality. This anticipates the fuller quality-expression system in Chapter 18 without requiring new grammar now.

9.4 Requesting quantities, sizes, and colors

Purpose: specify how much, what size, and what color of an item you want.

Quantity, size, and color phrases follow the noun, consistent with the descriptive word order you have used since Chapter 4.

  • Ina son riga ja, girma matsakaici.I want a red gown, medium size.
  • Ba ni da kwando guda biyu.Give me two baskets. (literally a request frame; recognition-supported, productive in controlled form)
  • Ina bukatar mita biyar na atamfa.I need five meters of printed cloth.
  • Akwai babba? Ina son karami.Is there a big one? I want a small one.
  • Ina son riguna manyan biyu.I want two big gowns.

Vocabulary note: bukata = need; ina bukatar… = I need… (a controlled formulaic frame related to the so constructions from Chapter 8). Ba ni da… here functions as a polite request frame ("give me…"), distinct from the negative possession pattern ba ni da (I do not have); context and intonation distinguish them, and your audio model will demonstrate the difference. This ambiguity is flagged for human review below.

9.5 Demonstrative and modifier review with new vocabulary

Purpose: combine demonstratives, adjectives, and linkers in fuller descriptive phrases about market goods.

This section recycles Chapter 2's demonstratives, Chapter 4's adjectives and linkers, and Chapter 6's demonstrative expansion, applying them to market vocabulary.

  • Wannan riga ce mai kyau.This is a good gown. (mai + quality noun, reviewed from Chapter 4)
  • Wancan zane ne mai launi ja.That is a wrapper cloth with a red color.
  • Farashin rigar nan ya yi tsada.The price of this gown is high. (recognition: ya yi formulaic, full completive Chapter 16)
  • Wadannan takalma sun fi girma.These shoes are bigger. (recognition only — comparison with fi is introduced productively in Chapter 18; included here as a glossed recognition item, not an assessment target)

9.6 Information questions about goods

Purpose: ask about the availability, price, and qualities of goods using relative-continuative forms already encountered.

Since Chapter 3, you have used relative-continuative forms formulaically in origin questions (Daga ina kake zuwa?). Chapters 6 and 9 expand this formulaic exposure to additional question contexts. The forms below follow the same internal pattern; you do not need new grammatical analysis, only new application.

  • Yaushe kake siyan riguna?When do you buy gowns? (recognition: verbal noun siyan, full verbal-noun system Chapter 7)
  • Wane irin zane kake nema?What kind of wrapper cloth are you looking for?
  • Me kike so?What do you want? (relative-continuative form kike, formulaic since Chapter 3)
Language Note — relative forms continue to appear in new questions

The forms kake, kike, yake, and take that you first met in Chapter 3's origin questions are relative-continuative subject forms. They appear whenever a continuative statement is questioned, focused, or follows a question word — which is exactly what happens in Me kike so? (What do you want?) and Wane irin zane kake nema? (What kind of cloth are you looking for?). You are not yet asked to derive these forms from first principles; you are asked to recognize the pattern and use the specific questions taught here. The complete relative-aspect system is consolidated in Chapters 10 and 17–19.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Zainab — wadda muka sani daga Babi na 8 — ta je kasuwa domin sayan riga da wasu kayan gida. Tana magana da Bashir, mai sayar da kaya.

(Setting: Zainab — whom we know from Chapter 8 — has gone to the market to buy a gown and some household goods. She is speaking with Bashir, a vendor.)

ZAINAB Sannu da aiki. Ina son riga, amma ban ga wadda nake so ba tukuna.Greetings on your work. I want a gown, but I have not yet seen the one I want.
BASHIR Sannu, madam. Akwai riguna da yawa a nan. Wace launi kike so?Hello, madam. There are many gowns here. What color do you want?
ZAINAB Ina son riga ja, ko wadda take da shuɗi.I want a red gown, or one that has blue.
BASHIR (yana nuna riguna biyu) Ga riga ja, kuma ga wannan mai shuɗi. Dukansu manyan ne. Akwai karami idan kina so.(showing two gowns) Here is a red gown, and here is this one with blue. Both are big. There is a small one if you want.
ZAINAB Ina son ta — wannan mai shuɗi. Nawa ne?I want it — this one with blue. How much is it?
BASHIR Naira dubu uku ne.It is three thousand naira.
ZAINAB Yana da tsada kaɗan. Akwai rangwame?It is a little expensive. Is there a discount?
BASHIR To, naira dubu biyu da ɗari biyar.All right, two thousand five hundred naira.
ZAINAB Madalla. Zan sayi ta. Kuma ina bukatar tukunya karami da kwando guda biyu.Excellent. I will buy it. I also need a small pot and two baskets.
BASHIR Ga tukunya, naira ɗari biyar. Kwando guda ɗaya, naira ɗari biyu — guda biyu, naira ɗari huɗu.Here is a pot, five hundred naira. One basket, two hundred naira — two of them, four hundred naira.
ZAINAB To. Jimla, nawa ne duka?All right. In total, how much is everything?
BASHIR Naira dubu uku, ɗari uku ne duka.It is three thousand three hundred naira in total.
ZAINAB Na gode. Ga kuɗin.Thank you. Here is the money.
BASHIR Na gode da ciniki. Sai an jima.Thank you for the business. See you again.

Vocabulary notes for the dialogue: sannu da aiki = greetings on your work (a greeting used toward someone actively working — receptive cultural note, see Culture section); mai sayar da kaya = vendor (literally "one who sells goods"); ban ga … ba tukuna = I have not seen … yet (recognition: negative completive, formulaic); dukansu = both of them; jimla = total, sum; duka = all, altogether; ciniki = business, trade, bargain; sai an jima = see you later (fixed farewell formula).

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. What item does Zainab go to buy first? What two colors does she mention as acceptable?
  2. Which gown does Zainab choose? Quote the direct-object pronoun she uses and explain why that pronoun was chosen.
  3. What was the original price of the gown? What price did Zainab and Bashir agree on?
  4. What two additional items does Zainab buy? What size pot does she request?
  5. Calculate: does Bashir's final total (naira dubu uku, ɗari uku) match the sum of the individual prices given in the dialogue? Show your work in Hausa numbers.
  6. Find one example of akwai in the dialogue and explain what it expresses.

Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

  • Each price question uses Nawa ne…? correctly.
  • Each price is written using the correct Hausa number pattern (ɗari, dubu, and combinations).
  • Direct-object pronouns match the gender or plurality of the noun they replace (shi for masculine, ta for feminine, su for plural).

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Markets across Hausa-speaking communities

Markets in Hausa-speaking regions take many forms. Kano's historic Kasuwar Kurmi is one of West Africa's oldest and largest markets, and large open markets of this kind remain central to commerce, social life, and craft production in many cities. At the same time, fixed-price supermarkets, shopping plazas, and growing digital commerce — online ordering and mobile-money payment — are increasingly common in urban centers across Nigeria and Niger. A learner should not assume that every Hausa-speaking shopper haggles over every purchase in an open-air stall; shopping practices vary by city, neighborhood, type of goods, and generation.

Open markets typically include sections organized by type of goods — cloth and clothing, household items, food, and so on — and vendors often specialize in one category. Greeting a vendor before beginning a transaction, as modeled in the dialogue above (Sannu da aiki, a greeting recognizing someone at work), remains a common courtesy across many settings, though the exact phrasing and expected exchange vary by region and relationship.

Culture in Context — Negotiation is contextual, not universal or compulsory

Price negotiation is a real and valued skill in many open-market settings, but it is not a fixed or compulsory ritual that occurs identically in every transaction. Some goods and some vendors have fixed prices; others expect a respectful back-and-forth, as Zainab and Bashir demonstrate in the model dialogue. Tourist guides sometimes present haggling as an exotic or mandatory performance — this course avoids that framing. A polite request such as Akwai rangwame? (Is there a discount?) is a normal, everyday question in many contexts, asked with the same straightforward tone as asking the price itself, not as a dramatic contest.

Regional Variation — Naira and CFA franc

This chapter's productive vocabulary and exercises use the Nigerian naira, consistent with the Kano-based standard used throughout this course. In Niger, the relevant currency is the CFA franc (FCFA), shared with several other West and Central African countries. Hausa speakers in Niger discussing prices will generally use francs rather than naira, and may use French-derived numerals or terms in commercial contexts alongside Hausa. This course does not require productive use of CFA franc vocabulary or French-influenced commercial terms at this level; if you travel or work in a Nigerien context, you will need additional region-specific vocabulary beyond this volume's scope.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using the independent pronoun as a direct object

Error: Ina son kai. (using the independent pronoun kai as a direct object)

Correction: Ina son ka. — The direct-object pronoun for second-person masculine is ka, not the independent pronoun kai. Always check the direct-object pronoun table in this chapter rather than assuming the independent pronoun works in every position. The same caution applies to second-person feminine: use ki, not ke, as a direct object.

Common Mistake — Choosing the wrong gender for a direct-object pronoun

Error: Ina son riga. Ina son shi. (using shi, masculine, to replace the feminine noun riga)

Correction: Ina son riga. Ina son ta. — Since riga is grammatically feminine, the correct pronoun is ta. The direct-object pronoun must match the grammatical gender of the noun it replaces, not any property of the speaker or listener. Before using shi, ta, or su, confirm the gender or plurality of the noun in question.

Common Mistake — Guessing a plural or gender for a new clothing or household noun

Error: Inventing a plural such as *rigaye for riga, or assuming tukunya is masculine because it refers to a large object.

Correction: The verified plural of riga is riguna; tukunya is feminine, as shown in the vocabulary table, regardless of the size or weight of the object it names. Continue the habit established in earlier chapters: check gender and plural from a verified source, never from size, shape, or assumed logic.

Common Mistake — Placing the size or color word before the noun

Error: Ina son ja riga. (placing the color before the noun, modeled on English word order)

Correction: Ina son riga ja. — Hausa descriptive words, including colors and size terms, typically follow the noun they describe, consistent with the adjective order taught since Chapter 4. Resist the instinct to translate English word order directly.

Common Mistake — Treating ɗari and dubu as ordinary nouns requiring a linker before the next number

Error: *ɗari na biyu for "two hundred" (inserting an unneeded linker)

Correction: ɗari biyu — The number word directly follows ɗari or dubu without a linker, the same way ashirin da biyu uses da only between a decade and a single unit (22), not between ɗari/dubu and a following multiplier. Review the patterns in the numbers vocabulary section above and practice with several examples before extending the pattern on your own.

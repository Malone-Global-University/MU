Sannu! First Encounters

Greetings, names, and introduction · Speak and Write Hausa: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Greet someone and say goodbye in common everyday situations.
  2. State your name and ask another person's name.
  3. Introduce yourself using basic identity statements.
  4. Use core courtesy expressions including na gode, don Allah, and yi haƙuri.
  5. Respond to simple questions about well-being.
  6. Recognize the independent pronouns ni, kai/ke, shi/ita, mu, ku, and su.
  7. Recognize that ne identifies masculine singular referents and ce identifies feminine singular referents.
  8. Identify the letters of Nigerian Standard Boko, including the special letters ɓ, ɗ, ƙ, and 'y.
  9. Begin to hear Hausa as a tonal language by distinguishing high and low tones in familiar words.

Communication Scenario

You have arrived at a community language exchange in Kano, northern Nigeria. People are settling in, meeting one another before the session begins. Names are exchanged, greetings offered, courtesy expressions shared. Everyone wants to know: What is your name? Are you well? Where are you from? You have never spoken a word of Hausa — but by the time this chapter ends, you will be able to handle that entire opening exchange.

  1. In English, how do you greet someone you have just met for the first time? Think about what you actually say versus what you might say to a friend. In many Hausa-speaking communities, a greeting is a sustained exchange, not a single word — it is part of the conversation itself.
  2. Hausa is spoken as a first language by tens of millions of people across northern Nigeria and southern Niger, and as a widely used regional lingua franca across West and Central Africa. The variety you will learn in this course is contemporary Standard Hausa based on educated Kano usage, written in the standard Latin-based script called Boko.

Pronunciation and Boko

Nigerian Standard Boko

Hausa has two writing systems in use today. Boko is the Latin-based standard used across Nigeria for education, publishing, broadcasting, and everyday writing. Ajami is an Arabic-script tradition with a long history; it remains in use for religious texts, poetry, and certain community contexts. This course teaches Boko as the productive writing system. You will read and write standard Boko from Chapter 1 onward.

The Boko alphabet contains most of the familiar Latin letters, plus four special letters that are unique to Hausa. These four must be learned, recognized, and written correctly from the start. They represent sounds that are genuinely different from any plain-letter equivalent, and they can change the meaning of a word entirely.

Sound Focus — What Makes the Hooked Letters Different

The hooked consonants ɓ, ɗ, and ƙ, and the apostrophe-y 'y, are made with a distinctive type of airstream that pulls inward rather than pushing outward from the lungs. Linguists call this an implosive for ɓ and ɗ, and the technical descriptions vary for ƙ and 'y. At this stage, you do not need the technical terms — you need to hear the contrast.

Listen carefully to your instructor, language partner, or audio model and notice that the hooked sounds feel slightly more inward or more held than their plain equivalents. With practice, your ear will learn to distinguish them reliably. The distinction matters: ba and ɓa are different words, as are da and ɗa. Your writing must reflect the correct letter.

The Five Vowels

Hausa has five vowel letters. Each represents a single, stable vowel quality. Unlike English vowels, which vary widely depending on neighboring sounds and spelling patterns, Hausa vowels are relatively consistent. However, Hausa also distinguishes short and long vowels — the same vowel held for a shorter or longer duration can make a real difference to meaning. Ordinary Boko does not mark this length difference in writing, but you will hear and practice it in every chapter.

Tone: Hausa as a Tonal Language

Sound Focus — Understanding Tone

Hausa is a tonal language. This means that the pitch on a syllable — whether it is spoken on a higher or lower musical note — is part of the word itself, just as the consonants and vowels are. Two words can have identical consonants and vowels but completely different meanings because they are spoken on different tones.

Hausa uses three tone levels: high, low, and falling (a syllable that starts high and drops). At this stage, focus on hearing and producing the contrast between high and low. Your instructor or audio materials will model each new word with its correct tone pattern. Do not guess the tone from the spelling — ordinary Boko leaves tone unmarked.

Tone is not the same as English sentence intonation, which rises and falls to signal questions or emotions. Hausa tone is a property of individual words and grammatical forms that must be learned alongside each item. You will return to tone in every chapter of this course.

  • Listen to your model carefully and repeat each word at the same pitch pattern.
  • Pay attention to tone even in short grammatical words like ne, ce, ni, and ba.
  • You will not be asked to write tone marks in ordinary Boko — but you will be asked to hear and produce tone accurately in spoken Hausa.

Vowel Length

In addition to tone, Hausa contrasts short and long vowels. A long vowel is held for roughly twice the duration of a short one. Ordinary Boko writing does not mark this difference, but it exists in spoken Hausa and affects word meaning. Your instructor or audio model will demonstrate vowel length. In this chapter, focus on hearing the difference. Productive control of vowel length develops over the first several chapters.

Writing Without Tone or Length Marks

Standard Boko spelling does not require tone marks or special length notation. In ordinary writing — texts, messages, exercises, and your written assignments — you will write words in standard Boko without adding any diacritics for tone or length. Tone and length will be modeled in pronunciation notes and heard in speech; they are not required in ordinary learner writing.

Grammar Note — Masculine and Feminine in the Second and Third Person

Hausa distinguishes masculine and feminine forms in the singular second person (kai / ke) and third person (shi / ita). When you say "you" to a man or boy, you use kai; to a woman or girl, ke. When you say "he," you use shi; "she" is ita.

This is grammatical gender — a property of the language system — not a statement about personality, importance, or social role. The plural forms mu, ku, and su do not distinguish masculine and feminine.

1.2 Identification with ne and ce

Purpose: identify who or what someone or something is.

Hausa uses the words ne and ce to identify people or things. They appear after the word being identified and express a kind of "this is / that is / I am / he is / she is" relationship. The choice between ne and ce depends on the grammatical gender of the noun or person being identified:

  • Use ne to identify a masculine singular person or thing.
  • Use ce to identify a feminine singular person or thing.

For the most common identity patterns in this chapter:

  • Sunana Musa ne.My name is Musa. (Musa is a masculine name.)
  • Sunana Hafsa ce.My name is Hafsa. (Hafsa is a feminine name.)
  • Ni ne Musa.I am Musa.
  • Ni ce Hafsa.I am Hafsa.
  • Shi ne Malam Bello.He is Malam Bello.
  • Ita ce Malama Kande.She is Malama Kande.
Grammar Note — ne and ce Are Not All-Purpose "Be" Words

Ne and ce identify a person or thing — they answer the question "Who is this?" or "What is this?" They are not general-purpose equivalents of every English use of the verb "be." Hausa expresses location ("The book is on the table"), existence ("There is water"), and possession ("She has a bag") through different constructions, which you will learn in Chapter 6. Do not use ne or ce for those meanings at this stage.

1.3 Name Questions

Purpose: ask for someone's name and state your own.

The most common ways to ask for and state a name in Hausa:

  • Sunanka nawa?What is your name? (asking a man)
  • Sunanki nawa?What is your name? (asking a woman)
  • Sunana Audu ne.My name is Audu.
  • Sunana Zainab ce.My name is Zainab.

nawa is the question word meaning "how much / how many / what" used here to ask for a name. Learn this phrase as a complete unit at this stage.

1.4 Basic Word Order

Purpose: build simple Hausa identity sentences correctly.

The default word order in a Hausa sentence is Subject — Verb or Predicate — Object or Complement. In the identity sentences of this chapter, this looks like:

  • Ni (subject) · ne (identifying particle) · Musa (name).I am Musa.
  • Sunana (subject) · Zainab (name) · ce (identifying particle).My name is Zainab.

Notice that in the Sunana… pattern, ne or ce comes after the name. In the Ni ne… pattern, it comes after the pronoun. Both are natural and common. Learn both as whole patterns — analyzing the exact position of every word comes later.

Language Focus

Language Focus — Three Things That Make Hausa Different from English

1. Hausa is a tonal language, and tone is not marked in ordinary Boko writing. Every word in Hausa has a tone pattern — a sequence of high and low pitches on its syllables — and that pattern is part of the word, like the consonants and vowels. Two words can look identical in writing but mean completely different things when spoken on different tones. English uses pitch as sentence intonation (rising for surprise, falling for statements), but English pitch does not change the identity of individual words the way Hausa tone does. You must learn Hausa through listening as well as reading, because the writing alone does not give you the complete picture.

2. Hausa uses ne and ce — not a single general "be" — for identification. When you say "I am Audu" or "She is the teacher" in English, you use the same word "be" in both. Hausa uses ne or ce for identification, and those words carry grammatical gender information. You will not find one Hausa word that covers every English use of "be," "have," "there is," and "is located at." Each of these meanings has its own Hausa expression, which you will learn over the first several chapters.

3. Grammatical gender is a property of the language, not of people or things. Hausa nouns and pronouns carry grammatical gender — roughly masculine or feminine. This is a grammatical system, not a social claim. The pronouns kai (you, masculine) and ke (you, feminine) are used according to the gender of the person being addressed, but for nouns, gender is a property you must learn with each word — it is not reliably predictable from meaning or spelling alone. The agreement between a noun's gender and the identifying particle (ne vs. ce) is one of the first patterns you will practice in this course.

Speaking Practice

Controlled Practice — Greeting Circuit

With a partner, take turns greeting each other using at least three different greetings from this chapter — for example, Sannu, Ina kwana?, and Ina lafia? — and respond appropriately to each one. Practice the full greeting-response pair, not just the greeting alone. Hausa greeting exchanges often include a follow-up, so model that two-turn rhythm from the start.

Communicative Task — First Meeting

Stand up and introduce yourself to three different classmates. For each interaction: (1) greet the person and respond to their greeting; (2) ask their name using the correct form for their gender (Sunanka nawa? or Sunanki nawa?); (3) state your own name with ne or ce; (4) close with a courtesy expression or farewell. Keep each exchange to four to six turns.

Independent Alternative

If you are studying without a partner: record yourself performing both sides of a complete first-meeting exchange — greeting, name question, name response with ne or ce, and farewell. Listen back. Ask yourself: Did I use the correct ne or ce? Did I model the expected greeting response rather than just the opening? Did I close the exchange naturally?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Haɗuwa da Juna: The Greeting Exchange

In many Hausa-speaking communities, greeting is one of the most important social acts. Arriving at a home, entering a shop, approaching a market stall, or beginning a conversation — each typically begins with an appropriate greeting and a response. The greeting is not merely a warm-up to the "real" conversation. It is part of the conversation, expressing care, respect, and acknowledgment of the other person.

In practice, greeting exchanges can be brief or extended depending on the relationship, age, status, setting, and time available. A quick Sannu, lafiya? on a busy street is perfectly natural. A longer exchange of several greetings — asking after health, after the family, after the work — is typical when meeting someone you know well, when meeting an elder, or when arriving at someone's home. Learn to read the context as your experience grows. This course builds the full range of greeting language progressively so that you can adapt to different situations.

Culture in Context — Titles and Forms of Address

Malam and Malama are among the most widely used respectful titles in Hausa-speaking communities. They apply to educated people, teachers, and community members generally deserving of respect, and are appropriate in a wide range of everyday situations. The titles Alhaji and Hajiya have religious significance — they mark a person who has made the Islamic pilgrimage to Mecca — but they are also used more broadly as marks of respect for older or distinguished individuals in many communities.

Which title is appropriate depends on the person, the relationship, the setting, and the community. Observe and adapt rather than applying one rule everywhere. As you build vocabulary and cultural knowledge across this course, you will learn more about how age, status, gender, and setting shape forms of address in Hausa.

Culture in Context — Islamic Greetings

The Arabic greeting Assalamu alaikum (Peace be upon you) and its response Wa alaikum assalam (And upon you peace) are widely used among Muslim speakers and are a normal part of daily life in many Hausa-speaking communities across Nigeria and Niger. You will encounter them frequently. These greetings are used in appropriate social and religious contexts between Muslim speakers; they are not interchangeable with all-purpose secular greetings in every setting. The fact that Hausa vocabulary includes many Arabic-origin words does not make every Hausa speaker Muslim, and not every speaker uses these expressions in every context. This course treats greetings as context-dependent and avoids presenting any one set of expressions as universal for all Hausa speakers everywhere.

Regional Note — Nigeria and Niger

The core variety in this course is contemporary Standard Hausa based on educated Kano usage, written in Nigerian Standard Boko. Hausa is also spoken widely across southern Niger, where it serves as a major lingua franca alongside French as the official language. Spellings and some vocabulary differ between Nigerian and Nigerien usage. The most visible orthographic difference is that the standard Nigerian letter 'y (apostrophe-y) corresponds to the letter ƴ in common Nigerien Hausa spelling. Both forms represent the same sound. In this course, you will learn Nigerian Standard Boko — including 'y — as your productive standard, and you will recognize the Nigerien form ƴ when it appears in labeled notes or readings.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake

Error: Using ne for a feminine name or ce for a masculine name — for example, writing Sunana Hafsa ne or Sunana Musa ce.

Correction: Ne is used after a masculine singular name or identity, ce after a feminine singular name or identity. Sunana Hafsa ce and Sunana Musa ne are the correct forms. When you learn a new person's name, also note whether it is masculine or feminine so that you can select the right particle.

Common Mistake

Error: Writing b instead of ɓ, d instead of ɗ, or k instead of ƙ because the plain letter is easier to type.

Correction: The four special Boko letters — ɓ, ɗ, ƙ, and 'y — are part of standard Hausa spelling and must be written correctly. Substituting a plain letter changes the word or makes it incorrect. Learn to type or write these characters from the start; they appear in common words you will use every day.

Common Mistake

Error: Treating ne and ce as general-purpose equivalents of English "be" and using them in sentences about location, such as Littafi ne kan tebur to mean "The book is on the table."

Correction: Ne and ce are used specifically for identification — stating what or who something or someone is. Location, existence, and possession are expressed through different Hausa constructions that you will learn in Chapter 6. At this stage, use ne and ce only for the identification patterns taught in this chapter.

Common Mistake

Error: Giving only the opening greeting without modeling the expected response — for example, saying Ina kwana? but not knowing what comes next when someone responds Lafiya lau.

Correction: Hausa greeting exchanges work in pairs: the greeting and the response both belong to the learner's repertoire. Practice the full exchange — Ina kwana? / Lafiya lau — as a single unit, the way a musician practices both notes of an interval. Knowing only the opening half of a greeting exchange leaves a gap in your conversational ability.

Common Mistake

Error: Using kai when addressing a woman, or ke when addressing a man.

Correction: Hausa distinguishes the second-person singular pronoun by gender: kai is used for a man or boy, ke for a woman or girl. Similarly, the name question Sunanka nawa? is addressed to a man and Sunanki nawa? to a woman. These forms are different words, and using the wrong one is a noticeable error.

Looking Ahead

Abin da ka/ki koyi yanzu — What you know now

You can greet someone in multiple ways appropriate to the time and setting, exchange names using ne and ce correctly, state who you or another person is, respond to questions about well-being, and say goodbye. You have been introduced to Nigerian Standard Boko — including its four special letters — and have begun to hear Hausa as a tonal language in which pitch is part of the meaning of words. These are genuine, usable conversational skills in Hausa.

Chapter 2: A Cikin Aji — In the Classroom

Now that you can introduce yourself, Chapter 2 gives you language for the classroom itself. You will learn to identify and ask about objects, count from 1 to 20, follow simple instructions, and ask for help when you do not understand. Along the way, you will meet Hausa grammatical gender and plural agreement fully for the first time, learn how demonstratives work, and practice the four special Boko letters in new vocabulary.

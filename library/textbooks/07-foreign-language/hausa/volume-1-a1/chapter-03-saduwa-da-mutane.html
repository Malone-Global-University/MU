Meeting People · Speak and Write Hausa: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Introduce another person using his or her name, origin, and profession.
  2. Ask and answer where someone comes from using the pattern Daga ina kake/kike zuwa? and Na fito daga…
  3. State a nationality and identify a language using common nationality and language terms.
  4. Say which languages you speak or understand using the formulaic continuative patterns Ina magana da… and Na fahimci…
  5. Name and ask about common professions, selecting masculine, feminine, and plural forms correctly.
  6. Use independent pronouns in contrastive and identifying functions.
  7. Form a simple negative identification sentence using ba … ba ne/ce.
  8. Count from 21 to 100 in Hausa.
  9. Pronounce r, l, w, and y with their Hausa values and read unfamiliar Boko words without applying English sound conventions.

Communication Scenario

A community language program in Kano has drawn participants from several countries and professional backgrounds. You arrive at a mixer before classes begin. You want to introduce yourself and a colleague, find out where people are from, ask about their languages and jobs, and make a few quick connections before the session starts. To do this well in Hausa, you need words for countries, languages, and professions — and you need the sentence patterns that put them together into natural questions and answers.

  1. When you ask someone where they are from in Hausa, the question looks slightly different from anything you have seen so far. The word that functions as the subject in an origin question is not quite the same form as the independent pronoun you would use in "I am a teacher." As you work through the grammar section, notice how the subject form changes depending on the type of sentence — and keep this question in mind: does the change affect meaning, or is it purely grammatical?
  2. Hausa uses two words where English uses one for the English verb "speak": context and construction matter. This chapter introduces the formula Ina magana da… as a complete communication pattern. You will not analyze every part of it yet — the full system behind it is introduced in Chapter 7. For now, learn it the same way you might learn a phrase in any language: as a meaningful whole that gets the job done.

Language Note — dan and 'yar in profession terms

Several Hausa profession terms use dan (son of, person of) for male reference and 'yar (daughter of, person of) for female reference, followed by a noun describing the domain. So dan kasuwa is literally "person of the market" (male), and 'yar kasuwa is "person of the market" (female). The plural yan kasuwa is a separate form. This pattern also appears in ɗan jarida (male journalist) and 'yar jarida (female journalist). Do not try to predict all Hausa profession terms from this pattern — it applies to some but not all professions. Learn each term as a lexical entry.

Pronunciation and Boko

r, l, w, and y in Hausa

These four consonants are written with letters that English speakers already know, but their Hausa sounds are not identical to their English values. Each one requires careful attention.

LetterHausa soundGuide for English speakersExample
rIn careful Hausa, a tap or flap of the tongue tip against the ridge behind the upper teeth — similar to the r in Spanish pero or the tt in American English butter when said quickly. In natural connected speech, Hausa r may vary. Do not substitute the English rhotic r as in red.Think of a single quick tap, not a continuous American-English r sound.direba (driver)
lA clear, light l with the tongue tip touching the ridge behind the upper teeth. More forward than the "dark" English l at the ends of words like full. Always produce this l clearly.Use the l from the beginning of let, never the thicker l from the end of pull.Larabci (Arabic)
wA rounded bilabial glide, similar to English w in wet. In Hausa, it is consistent and does not become silent as it can in some English spellings.Always pronounce w fully in Hausa words — it is never silent.yan kasuwa (traders)
yThe palatal glide as in English yes. In Hausa, plain y is distinct from 'y (implosive). Plain y is a smooth glide; 'y involves a catch of the breath.Use the y of English yes. Never confuse plain y with 'y in spelling or pronunciation.Najeriya (Nigeria)
Sound Focus — Reading Hausa r without English habits

English speakers often replace Hausa r with the American English rhotic sound — a sound produced with the tongue bunched or pulled back, which colors the whole vowel around it. This substitution is immediately noticeable in Hausa and affects intelligibility. The Hausa r in most careful speech is a brief tap: the tongue tip strikes the ridge behind the upper teeth once and releases. Practice by saying diri (ten cents) with a single flick, and then compare it to diri said with an American English r. The difference is clear. When you encounter r in Hausa words in this chapter, aim for the tap, not the English glide.

Reading unfamiliar Boko words

By now you have learned several principles of Hausa Boko spelling. Apply them to unfamiliar words using this checklist:

  1. Identify any hooked letters (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ) or the apostrophe consonant 'y — these are distinct Hausa sounds, not variations of the plain letter.
  2. Read c with its Hausa value (similar to English ch), not as English k or s.
  3. Read sh as a single sound, not as s + h separately.
  4. Read ts as a single glottalized sound, not as t + s.
  5. Read every vowel as its Hausa quality: a as in father; e as in bed; i as in feet; o as in go; u as in food. Do not apply English vowel shifts.
  6. Read r as a tap, not an English glide.
  7. Read w fully — it is never silent.
  8. Remember that tone and vowel length are present in the spoken word even when the written form does not show them. Your audio model or instructor will demonstrate both.
Pronunciation Practice — Tone in pronouns and short grammatical words

Several of the words introduced in this chapter are short: ni (I/me), kai (you, m.), ke (you, f.), shi (he/him), ita (she/her), mu (we/us), ku (you pl.), su (they/them), da (with), a (in/at), daga (from). Each of these has a specific tone — high or low — that is part of its identity. Ordinary Boko does not mark tone, but your ear needs to track it. In this chapter, focus on hearing the contrast between ni (I), said with a high tone, and the way tone shifts on pronouns when they appear in different grammatical positions. Your instructor or audio model will demonstrate. Do not let the shortness of these words lead you to rush past them — tone in short grammatical words contributes significantly to whether natural Hausa is intelligible.

Grammar Instruction

3.1 Independent pronouns — review and expansion

Purpose: use independent pronouns to identify, emphasize, and contrast people in personal-information exchanges.

You met several independent pronouns in Chapter 1. Here is the full set for this course. The label independent pronoun means this form stands on its own as a complete word — it is not the shorter form used as a subject alongside an aspect marker, which is a different system introduced later.

Person / Number / GenderHausaEnglish equivalent
1st singularniI, me
2nd singular masculinekaiyou (speaking to a male)
2nd singular femininekeyou (speaking to a female)
3rd singular masculineshihe, him
3rd singular feminineitashe, her
1st pluralmuwe, us
2nd pluralkuyou (plural or respectful singular)
3rd pluralsuthey, them

Independent pronouns are used in identification sentences, for emphasis, and in contrast. They are not the same as the shorter subject forms that appear with aspect markers — those forms are taught from Chapter 7 onwards. Do not use the independent pronoun as a general subject prefix before every verb.

  • Ni malami ne.I am a teacher. (male speaker)
  • Ni malama ce.I am a teacher. (female speaker)
  • Ita likita ce.She is a doctor.
  • Shi injiniya ne.He is an engineer.
  • Kai, wane ne?You — who are you? (to a male)
  • Ke, wace ce?You — who are you? (to a female)
Language Note — kai versus ke and the second-person distinction

Hausa distinguishes the gender of the person being addressed in the second-person singular. When you speak to a man, the independent pronoun is kai. When you speak to a woman, it is ke. This distinction also appears in commands: in Chapter 2, you saw Ka rubuta (write — to a male) versus Ki rubuta (write — to a female). English has no direct equivalent — "you" covers everyone — so this is an area that requires active attention. When you do not know the gender of the person you are addressing, or when addressing a group, use the second-person plural ku (or a respectful singular construction that uses plural address, discussed further in the culture section).

3.2 ne/ce with professions — review

Purpose: identify people by profession using the correct identification particle.

The identification particles ne and ce introduced in Chapters 1 and 2 apply directly to profession nouns. The particle must match the grammatical gender of the profession noun, not the biological sex of the person. For most profession nouns, the masculine and feminine forms are different words — so the form of the noun itself already signals gender. The particle then follows the noun's gender.

  • Malami ne.He is a teacher. (malami is masculine → ne)
  • Malama ce.She is a teacher. (malama is feminine → ce)
  • Likita ne.He is a doctor. (likita is masculine here → ne)
  • Likita ce.She is a doctor. (likita feminine in this context → ce)
  • Malamai ne.They are teachers. (plural → ne)

When a profession noun has the same form for masculine and feminine (such as likita or direba), the choice of ne or ce — along with the pronoun used — is the main signal of the person's gender. This is why the identification particle matters: removing it or choosing the wrong one creates a real ambiguity.

3.3 Negative identification: ba … ba ne/ce

Purpose: say that someone or something is not a particular person or thing.

To say that someone is not something, Hausa uses a discontinuous negative construction: ba appears before the noun or pronoun and ba ne or ba ce appears after it. Both elements are required — dropping either one produces an incomplete or incorrect sentence.

  • Ba malami ba ne.He is not a teacher. (masculine → ba ne at the end)ba + [noun] + ba ne
  • Ba malama ba ce.She is not a teacher. (feminine → ba ce at the end)
  • Ba likita ba ne.He is not a doctor.
  • Ba daga Kano ba ne.He is not from Kano.
  • Ba ni ba ne.It is not me. / That is not I.
  • Ba malamai ba ne.They are not teachers. (plural → ba ne)
Language Focus — Hausa negation uses two elements, not one

In English, negation typically works with one word: not. In Hausa, many negation patterns use two elements that surround the thing being negated. In this chapter's construction, ba comes before and ba ne or ba ce comes after. Think of the two ba elements as brackets that enclose the negated content. This discontinuous structure is a recurring feature of Hausa negation, and you will see it again in other contexts as the course progresses. The most important habit to build now: never use just one ba and assume the sentence is complete. The second element is not optional.

3.4 Asking where someone comes from

Purpose: ask and answer questions about origin using the formulaic patterns taught in this chapter.

The question Where are you from? requires a specific construction in Hausa that varies slightly depending on whom you are addressing. Each form below is a complete communication pattern. You do not need to analyze the subject word internally at this stage — the full grammar is taught in Chapters 7 and 16. Learn each as a whole question.

  • Q: Daga ina kake zuwa? · A: Na fito daga Kano.Where are you from? · I am from Kano.
  • Q: Daga ina kike zuwa? · A: Na fito daga Niamey.Where are you from? · I am from Niamey.

The words kake, kike, yake, and take are relative-continuative subject forms. They appear here because the question begins with a question word (daga ina — from where). The full system behind these forms is explained in Chapter 7 and reviewed in Chapter 16. For now, treat the complete question as a single usable pattern, the same way you would learn a fixed expression in any language.

3.5 Saying which languages you speak

Purpose: state which languages you speak or understand using two formulaic continuative frames.

Two formulaic patterns cover the most important language-ability statements at this level. Both are continuative constructions whose full grammar is introduced in Chapter 7. Learn them now as complete, usable expressions.

  • Ina magana da Hausa.I speak Hausa. (ongoing or general)
  • Ina magana da Turanci.I speak English.
  • Ina magana da Hausa da Turanci.I speak Hausa and English.
  • Yana magana da Faransanci.He speaks French.
  • Tana magana da Larabci.She speaks Arabic.
  • Na fahimci Hausa kaɗan.I understand Hausa a little.
  • Ban fahimci Jamusanci ba.I do not understand German.

Vocabulary note: kaɗan = a little, slightly; da kyau = well; sosai = very well, a lot. These degree adverbs can be added after a language name: Ina magana da Hausa da kyau (I speak Hausa well).

Language Note — ina, yana, tana: these are not the same word

The words ina, yana, and tana look similar but carry different information. Ina is the first-person singular continuative subject form ("I am…-ing"); yana is the third-person masculine singular continuative subject form ("he is…-ing"); tana is the third-person feminine singular continuative subject form ("she is…-ing"). These short words do the work that English does with both the pronoun and the auxiliary verb together: ina magana means "I speak / I am speaking" in one word. Do not add a separate independent pronoun (ni, shi, ita) in front of these forms in ordinary statements — the subject information is already built in. Placing an independent pronoun before the continuative form is possible for emphasis or contrast, but it is not the default and will be explained when relevant.

3.6 Introducing another person

Purpose: introduce a third person using name, origin, language, and profession.

When introducing someone else in Hausa, a natural pattern combines identification, origin, and an optional profession or language statement. The introduction does not require a special introductory verb at this stage — a simple identification and then further description works well.

  • Wannan Aminu ne. Malami ne. Ya fito daga Kano.This is Aminu. He is a teacher. He is from Kano.
  • Wannan Hauwa ce. Likita ce. Ta fito daga Niamey. Tana magana da Hausa da Faransanci.This is Hauwa. She is a doctor. She is from Niamey. She speaks Hausa and French.
  • Sunansa Suleiman ne. Injiniya ne daga Kamaru.His name is Suleiman. He is an engineer from Cameroon.
  • Sunanta Maryam ce. Ɗaliba ce. Tana zaune a Abuja.Her name is Maryam. She is a student. She lives in Abuja.

New vocabulary: sunansa = his name (literally "name-his"); sunanta = her name (literally "name-her"). These are possession constructions that will be analyzed fully in Chapter 4. For now, use sunansa … ne and sunanta … ce as fixed introduction frames.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Shirye-shiryen harshe a Kano. Bello, wanda ya fito daga Kano, yana magana da Amina, wadda ta fito daga Niamey, kafin darasi ya fara. Ana gabatar da Chukwuemeka kuma — ɗalibi daga Najeriya (Jihar Anambra) wanda yake koyon Hausa.

(Setting: A language program in Kano. Bello, who is from Kano, speaks with Amina, who is from Niamey, Niger, before the class begins. They also introduce Chukwuemeka — a student from Nigeria, Anambra State, who is learning Hausa.)

BELLO Sannu. Sunana Bello ne. Daga ina kike zuwa?Hello. My name is Bello. Where are you from?
AMINA Sannu, Bello. Sunana Amina ce. Na fito daga Niamey — a Nijar. Ina zaune a Kano yanzu. Kai kuwa, daga ina kake zuwa?Hello, Bello. My name is Amina. I am from Niamey — in Niger. I am living in Kano now. And you — where are you from?
BELLO Ni dan Kano ne — na fito daga nan. Ina magana da Hausa da Turanci. Kai kuwa, wane harshe kike magana da shi?I am a Kano person — I am from here. I speak Hausa and English. And you — what language do you speak?
AMINA Ina magana da Hausa da Faransanci. Na fahimci Turanci kaɗan. Sana'ata — malama ce a makaranta. Kai kuwa, menene sana'arka?I speak Hausa and French. I understand English a little. My profession — I am a teacher at a school. And you — what is your profession?
BELLO Ni ma'aikaci ne a gwamnati. (yana nuna ga Chukwuemeka) Kuma wannan abokinmu ne — Chukwuemeka. Ya fito daga Jihar Anambra, a Najeriya.I am a government worker. (pointing to Chukwuemeka) And this is our friend — Chukwuemeka. He is from Anambra State, in Nigeria.
AMINA Sannu, Chukwuemeka. Daga ina kake zuwa daidai?Hello, Chukwuemeka. Where exactly are you from?
CHUKWUEMEKA Sannu, Amina. Na fito daga Onitsha. Ina magana da Igbo da Turanci. Ba na magana da Hausa da kyau tukuna — ina koyo.Hello, Amina. I am from Onitsha. I speak Igbo and English. I don't speak Hausa well yet — I am learning.
AMINA Madalla! Hausa ba da wahala ba ce. Za ka iya koyo da sauri!Excellent! Hausa is not difficult. You will be able to learn quickly!

Vocabulary notes for the dialogue: yanzu = now; nan = here; wane harshe = which language; shi here = it (the language, object of magana da); sana'ata = my profession; sana'arka = your profession (masculine); menene = what is (it)?; gwamnati = government; abokinmu = our friend; daidai = exactly, precisely; tukuna = yet; ina koyo = I am learning; ba da wahala ba ce = it is not difficult (recognition only); za ka iya koyo da sauri = you will be able to learn quickly (recognition only; future and ability are taught in Chapters 13–14).

Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

  • Each identification sentence uses the correct particle: ne for masculine, ce for feminine, ne for plural.
  • Each profession noun has the correct masculine or feminine form for the person being described.
  • Each negative identification uses both ba elements: ba … ba ne or ba … ba ce.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Hausa as a language of wider communication

Hausa is one of the most widely spoken languages in Africa. It is the first language of ethnic Hausa communities concentrated primarily in northern Nigeria and southern Niger, but it functions far more broadly than that. Across a wide belt of West and Central Africa — including parts of Ghana, Cameroon, Chad, Sudan, Côte d'Ivoire, and beyond — Hausa serves as a language of trade, religious life, media, and cross-community communication for millions of people who grew up speaking other languages.

This means that the people you meet who speak Hausa are linguistically diverse. A Hausa speaker in Kano may also speak Arabic, Fulfulde, and English. A Hausa speaker in Accra may have grown up speaking Twi. A trader at a market in Niamey may use Hausa alongside Zarma, French, and Kanuri in a single day. When you ask someone Wane harshe kake magana da shi?, do not assume the answer will be only Hausa. Multilingualism — knowing and using several languages — is the norm in many of the communities where Hausa is used, not the exception.

This matters for language learners: the person you speak Hausa with may switch naturally between languages depending on topic, relationship, setting, and regional convention. That switching is a skilled, normal behavior — not a sign that someone does not speak Hausa properly. In this course, the core productive language is Standard Hausa based on educated Kano usage, but you will encounter the broader multilingual context through readings, dialogues, and cultural notes throughout the book.

Culture in Context — Language, ethnicity, nationality, and religion are not the same

It is important to separate four concepts that are sometimes confused when people discuss Hausa:

Language is what someone speaks. Anyone can learn and use Hausa regardless of background.

Ethnicity is a cultural and ancestral identity. Being ethnically Hausa involves specific cultural practices, kinship networks, and historical affiliations. Not every Hausa speaker is ethnically Hausa, and ethnic identity is self-defined.

Nationality is citizenship — which country a person holds a passport from. There are ethnic Hausa people in Nigeria, Niger, Ghana, Cameroon, Sudan, and many other countries. They are citizens of those different countries.

Religion is a personal or community belief system. Islam has historically been prominent in many Hausa-speaking communities, particularly in northern Nigeria and Niger, but Hausa-speaking communities also include Christians and people of other traditions. Do not assume a Hausa speaker's religion from their language or name. In this course, religious expressions and practices are discussed accurately where relevant, and religious diversity is represented across the chapter materials.

When you meet a Hausa speaker, the question Daga ina kake zuwa? asks about geography, not ethnicity, religion, or identity more broadly. Being respectful in this context means following the other person's lead about what aspects of their identity they choose to share.

Regional Variation — Nigerien Hausa and the question of origin

In Niger, Hausa is one of the official national languages alongside French, and the Hausa spoken there has some vocabulary, pronunciation, and spelling differences from Nigerian Standard Hausa. This course uses Nigerian Standard Boko as the productive orthography throughout. One difference you may encounter in Nigerien Hausa texts is the use of ƴ (a hooked y) in positions where Nigerian Boko writes 'y. For example, where Nigerian Boko writes 'yar kasuwa (female trader), a Nigerien text might write ƴar kasuwa. Both represent the same sound; the difference is orthographic convention by country. This course consistently uses the Nigerian form 'y. When you read Hausa material produced in Niger, recognize that ƴ and 'y are two spellings of the same consonant.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Omitting the second ba in a negative identification

Error: Ba malami ne. (omitting the second ba)

Correction: Ba malami ba ne. — Hausa negative identification requires both elements of the discontinuous construction. Ba malami ne is incomplete and sounds unnatural to a Hausa speaker. Think of the two ba elements as a pair — if you say one, the other must follow. A useful memory prompt: the negated word goes between the brackets; the brackets themselves are both parts of ba … ba ne/ce.

Common Mistake — Using the wrong origin subject form

Error: Ta fito daga Kano when referring to a male. Or: Na fito daga Kano when saying "she is from Kano."

Correction: Ya fito daga Kano (he is from Kano); Ta fito daga Kano (she is from Kano); Na fito daga Kano (I am from Kano). The first element — Na, Ya, Ta — carries both person and gender information. A quick check before speaking: who is the subject? First person → Na. Third person male → Ya. Third person female → Ta.

Common Mistake — Adding an independent pronoun before ina/yana/tana

Error: Ni ina magana da Hausa. (adding the independent pronoun ni before the continuative form)

Correction: Ina magana da Hausa. — The continuative subject form ina already encodes first-person singular. Adding ni before it in a plain statement is grammatically unusual and sounds awkward. Independent pronouns can appear before continuative forms for emphasis or contrast, but this is a specific communicative choice — not the default. At this stage, use the continuative form alone in straightforward statements.

Common Mistake — Using kake when speaking to a woman

Error: Daga ina kake zuwa? when addressing a female

Correction: Daga ina kike zuwa? — Hausa distinguishes the gender of the person being addressed in the second-person singular. Kake is used with a male addressee; kike is used with a female addressee. This is a straightforward one-letter difference (a vs. i) that significantly affects the naturalness and respect of the interaction. When you are unsure of gender, or addressing multiple people, the plural form is safer in many contexts.

Common Mistake — Assuming profession nouns always change for gender

Error: Inventing a feminine form like likita → *likitiya or direba → *direbiya

Correction: Some Hausa profession nouns — including likita (doctor) and direba (driver) — do not change their form for gender in everyday usage. The same noun is used for male and female referents, and the gender signal comes from the identification particle (ne or ce) and the surrounding pronouns. Do not create feminine forms by analogy. Always check the vocabulary table — or a dictionary — before producing a profession noun you have not seen verified.

Looking Ahead

Abin da ka/ki koya yanzu — What you know now

You can now introduce yourself and others, ask and state where someone is from, name common professions with their masculine and feminine forms, say which languages you speak, and count from 1 to 100 in Hausa. You have also added an important new grammatical tool: the negative identification pattern ba … ba ne/ce, which allows you to say what something or someone is not. And you have encountered your first formulaic continuative expressions — the ina / yana / tana magana da… patterns that will become much more familiar once the full continuative system is introduced in Chapter 7. Looking back: every greeting, identification sentence, gender agreement, and number from Chapters 1 and 2 is now required for the conversations you are having in Chapter 3. That cumulative building is what makes later chapters possible.

Babi na 4: Iyali da Abokai — Family and Friends

In Chapter 4, you move into the domain of family and relationships. You will learn how to describe relatives, state how people are connected, and give simple descriptions of people's qualities. The chapter introduces one of the most important structural tools in Hausa: the genitive linker. When you want to say "Amina's book" or "the teacher's classroom," Hausa uses a short linking word between the possessed noun and the possessor — and that word changes depending on the gender of the possessed noun, not the possessor. You will also begin working with introductory adjectives and quality expressions, including the widely used mai construction. Numbers already learned will return for talking about ages.

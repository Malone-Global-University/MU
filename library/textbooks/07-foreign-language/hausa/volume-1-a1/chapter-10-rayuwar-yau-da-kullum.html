Unit II Consolidation — No Major New Grammar

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Combine home, routine, food, and shopping language in a single connected exchange.
  2. Ask and answer follow-up questions across these four domains without switching to English.
  3. Resolve a simple everyday misunderstanding using polite clarification and repair language.
  4. Describe preferences and practical needs using so, the negative continuative, and quantity expressions.
  5. Sustain a short transaction from beginning to end, combining greeting, request, negotiation, and closing.
  6. Apply existence, nonexistence, possession with da, and location accurately within a longer passage.
  7. Select correct gender, plural, demonstrative, adjective, and linker agreement across all four units of vocabulary.
  8. Use direct-object pronouns and the object-conditioned verb forms taught in Chapter 9 in new transactional contexts.
  9. Form information questions using the relative-continuative patterns reviewed since Chapter 3.

Communication Scenario

A friend is visiting your home in Kano for the first time. Over the course of an afternoon, you show them around the house, discuss your daily schedule, decide what to cook for the evening meal, realize you are missing an ingredient, and go together to a nearby market stall to buy what you need. By the time your friend leaves, you have used language from four different chapters in one connected stretch of communication — which is, in fact, what real conversations require. This chapter does not introduce new grammar. Instead, it asks you to move fluidly between the systems you already control: existence and possession from Chapter 6, continuative routines from Chapter 7, food preferences and negation from Chapter 8, and market transactions and object pronouns from Chapter 9.

  1. As you read and listen to the materials in this chapter, keep a mental tally of which earlier chapter each sentence pattern comes from. This habit — noticing how the systems connect — is exactly the skill that will let you hold a real conversation rather than recite isolated sentences.
  2. Pay close attention to the misunderstanding-and-repair language introduced in this chapter. Knowing how to say "I don't understand," "can you repeat that," or "I meant something different" is just as important to fluency as knowing vocabulary, and it has not yet been the explicit focus of a chapter.

Pronunciation and Boko

Tone and length across connected phrases

Up to this point, you have practiced tone and vowel length mainly within single words, short phrases, and isolated sentences. This chapter asks you to maintain accurate tone and length while moving between several connected sentences — a more demanding but essential skill for real conversation.

Pronunciation Practice — Keeping tone steady across a sentence sequence

When speakers string several short statements together quickly — as happens naturally in a real exchange like the one in this chapter's dialogue — there is a tendency for English speakers to flatten Hausa tone into a single English-style intonation contour that rises and falls only at the boundaries of the whole utterance. Resist this tendency. Each Hausa word retains its own tone pattern even inside a longer phrase. Practice by reading the model dialogue aloud slowly at first, pausing briefly after each sentence to consciously reset to the next word's tone, then gradually increasing your speed while keeping the tone pattern of each word intact.

Number and price discrimination

Review the numbers 1–100 from Chapters 2 and 3, together with the price and quantity language from Chapter 9. In this chapter's listening passage, prices and quantities appear in a connected narrative rather than as isolated drill items — listen for them inside full sentences, not as a separate list.

Subject-form discrimination

This chapter recycles three subject-form systems you have already met: independent pronouns (Ch. 1), continuative subject forms (Ch. 7), and the formulaic relative-continuative forms used in information questions (Ch. 3, 6, 9). None of these is new, but distinguishing them quickly in connected speech takes practice.

Sound Focus — Distinguishing ina, kana, kina, yana, tana in rapid speech

These five continuative subject forms differ by only a vowel or a single consonant, and in natural speech they pass quickly. Ina (I), kana (you, m.), kina (you, f.), yana (he), and tana (she) all begin with a vowel-plus-nasal shape but carry distinct onsets and tone. Listen to the chapter's listening passages several times, each time focusing on identifying only the subject form before worrying about the rest of the sentence. This isolates the skill and builds confidence before you try to process a full sentence at natural speed.

Dictation in ordinary Boko

As in earlier chapters, dictation exercises in this chapter use unmarked ordinary Boko. Continue applying the reading checklist from Chapter 3: identify hooked letters and 'y before reading further, read c, sh, and ts with their correct Hausa values, and rely on your ear — not the spelling — for tone and length.

Hooked-letter accuracy

This chapter's vocabulary and dialogue contain frequent instances of ɗ, ƙ, ɓ, and 'y. As a final accuracy check for Unit II, proofread your own writing in the Writing Workshop section with particular attention to these four characters — they must never be replaced with a plain letter.

Grammar Instruction

This chapter introduces no new grammatical system. The sections below consolidate and recombine the systems taught in Chapters 6 through 9, with attention to how they interact in connected discourse.

10.1 Existence, nonexistence, location, and possession together

In a real description of a home, speakers move naturally between stating what is present (akwai), what is absent (babu), where something is located, and who possesses it. Chapter 6 introduced these separately. Here they appear combined in a single short passage.

  • Akwai ɗaki ɗaya da kicin a gidana.There is one bedroom and a kitchen in my house.
  • Babu gareji, amma akwai babban falo.There is no garage, but there is a large living room.
  • Kicin yana kusa da falo.The kitchen is near the living room.
  • Ina da firiji a kicin, amma babu na'urar wanki a gida.I have a refrigerator in the kitchen, but there is no washing machine at home.
Language Note — Choosing the right tool from Chapter 6

When you describe a real home, you will often need all four Chapter 6 tools in the same paragraph: akwai for what exists, babu for what does not, a location expression for where something is, and a continuative-plus-da construction for what a person possesses. None of these four substitutes for another. A common test: if the sentence answers "is there a…?", use akwai/babu. If it answers "where is…?", use a location expression. If it answers "who has…?", use possession with da. Practicing this decision quickly, rather than translating from English word by word, is the real skill this section reviews.

10.2 Continuative routines recombined with new time references

Chapter 7's continuative subject forms and verbal nouns return here in combination with the food and market vocabulary of Chapters 8 and 9 — for example, describing a daily shopping or cooking routine rather than a generic schedule.

  • Kowace rana, ina tafiya kasuwa da safe.Every day, I go to the market in the morning.ina + tafiya (verbal noun) + location + time
  • Yana saya kayan abinci kafin ya je aiki.He buys groceries before he goes to work.
  • Muna girki tare da yamma.We cook together in the evening.

Note that kafin ya je aiki uses a connector and a verb form that previews narrative sequencing developed further from Chapter 16 onward. Treat it here as a recognition-only formula meaning "before he goes to work."

10.3 Negative continuative and preference recombined

Chapter 8's negative continuative and so-constructions return here applied to both food and shopping contexts, since the same negation pattern works across domains.

  • Ba na son shinkafa da yamma; ina son ta da safe kawai.I don't like rice in the evening; I only like it in the morning.ta = direct-object pronoun referring to shinkafa (Ch. 9 review)
  • Ba mu da albasa a gida — dole ne mu je kasuwa.We don't have onions at home — we have to go to the market.
  • Ba ya son ya saya mai tsada sosai.He does not want to buy something very expensive.
Language Note — dole ne as a recognition preview

The expression dole ne (it is necessary / must) appears above as a formulaic recognition item. Necessity with dole becomes fully productive in Chapter 13. For this chapter, you may use dole ne mu je… ("we have to go…") as a fixed expression when a performance task calls for expressing urgency, but you are not expected to manipulate its grammar productively yet.

10.4 Direct-object pronouns and object-conditioned forms in new contexts

Chapter 9 introduced direct-object pronouns and a small set of object-conditioned verb forms for transaction verbs. This chapter recycles them in slightly expanded contexts — for instance, referring back to a food item already mentioned earlier in a conversation, several sentences later, rather than in the same sentence where it was introduced.

  • Ga albasa — kana son ta?Here is the onion — do you want it?ta refers back to albasa (feminine)
  • Ina nema shinkafa. Ba na ganin ta a kantin.I am looking for rice. I don't see it in the shop.
  • Ka saye su duka biyu?Did you buy both of them?su = plural direct-object pronoun; recognition-only completive form preview

The verb saye in the final example is the object-conditioned form of saya before a pronoun object, taught in Chapter 9. Its appearance with a completive subject form (ka) here is recognition-only; the general completive becomes productive in Chapter 16.

10.5 Information questions: ordinary and relative-continuative forms reviewed

Throughout Unit II, questions beginning with a question word have required a relative-continuative subject form rather than the ordinary continuative form. This chapter asks you to select the correct form across all four domains without a grammar label attached to remind you.

Ordinary statementInformation question (relative form)
Ina girki yanzu. (I am cooking now.)Me kake girki? (What are you cooking?)
Tana zuwa kasuwa. (She is going to the market.)Ina take zuwa? (Where is she going?)
Yana saya albasa. (He is buying onions.)Nawa yake biya? (How much is he paying?)
Language Building Block
domain 1 statement + domain 2 statement + connector + follow-up question
Akwai shinkafa a gida, amma ba mu da albasa. Don haka, sai mu je kasuwa. Me za mu saya a can?
There is rice at home, but we don't have onions. So, we must go to the market. What shall we buy there?

This Building Block does not present a single new pattern. It models how four already-taught systems — existence/possession (Ch. 6), connectors (amma, don haka — reviewed from Ch. 7), and a recognition-only future-style question (za mu saya, previewed productively in Ch. 14) — combine into one natural stretch of discourse. Use it as a model for your own connected speech and writing in this chapter's tasks.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Zainab tana karbar bakuwa, Aisha, a gidanta da yamma a Kano. Suna tattaunawa game da gida, abincin yamma, da abin da suka rasa, sannan su je kasuwa kusa da gida don sayan albasa.

(Setting: Zainab is hosting her friend Aisha at her home in the evening in Kano. They talk about the house, the evening meal, what they are missing, and then go to a nearby market to buy onions.)

ZAINAB Sannu, Aisha. Barka da zuwa. Ga falo, kuma can kicin yake.Hello, Aisha. Welcome. Here is the living room, and the kitchen is over there.
AISHA Na gode. Gidanki yana da kyau sosai! Akwai ɗakuna nawa a nan?Thank you. Your house is really nice! How many rooms are there here?
ZAINAB Akwai ɗaki uku da kicin ɗaya. Babu gareji, amma akwai lambu kadan a baya. Kowace rana, ina tashi da wuri, ina shan kofi a nan a falo.There are three rooms and one kitchen. There is no garage, but there is a small garden in the back. Every day, I get up early and drink coffee here in the living room.
AISHA Madalla. To, me kike girki da yamma yau?Excellent. So, what are you cooking this evening?
ZAINAB Ina son girki shinkafa da miyar albasa. Amma… ban gane ba — ina ganin ba mu da albasa a gida!I want to cook rice with onion stew. But… I don't understand — I see we don't have onions at home!
AISHA Babu damuwa. Akwai kasuwa kusa da gidanki, ko ba haka ba?No problem. There is a market near your house, isn't there?
ZAINAB I, akwai. Don haka, sai mu je can mu saya. Ki sake faɗa — kina son ki zo tare da ni?Yes, there is. So, we have to go there and buy some. Say that again — do you want to come with me?
AISHA To, na gane yanzu. I, ina son zuwa tare da ke. Mu tafi!Okay, I understand now. Yes, I want to come with you. Let's go!
(a kasuwa — at the market)
ZAINAB Sannu. Nawa kuɗin albasa?Hello. How much is the onion?
MAI SAYARWA Naira ɗari biyar ne na kwano ɗaya.It's five hundred naira for one bowl.
ZAINAB Ina son ina ɗaya da rabi, don Allah. Kuma akwai tumatir?I want one and a half, please. And do you have tomatoes?
MAI SAYARWA I, akwai. Wani abu kuma?Yes, there is. Anything else?
ZAINAB Shi ke nan. Na gode sosai.That's it. Thank you very much.

Vocabulary notes for the dialogue: Barka da zuwa = welcome; lambu = garden; da wuri = early; shan kofi = drinking coffee (verbal noun construction, Ch. 7); ko ba haka ba? = isn't that so?, right? (formulaic tag question); mai sayarwa = seller, vendor; kwano = bowl, basin (a common market measure); rabi = half.

Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

  • Each existence or nonexistence statement correctly uses akwai or babu, not the other.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Hosting and visiting in everyday life

Hosting a guest, as Zainab does in this chapter's dialogue, is a meaningful social event in many Hausa-speaking households, but the specific expectations vary by family, region, generation, and individual relationship. Offering something to drink or eat, showing a guest around, and accompanying a guest on an errand are common courtesies in many settings, but they are not rigid rules that every household follows in the same way. Avoid treating any single household's practice — including the one shown in this chapter — as representative of "how Hausa people host guests" in general.

The willingness to interrupt a planned activity (such as cooking) to solve a problem together, as Aisha does by offering to accompany Zainab to the market, reflects a value placed on companionship and mutual assistance in many communities, but again this is a tendency, not a universal script. Treat this dialogue as one plausible example among many.

Culture in Context — Repair and clarification as ordinary communication

Saying Ban gane ba ("I did not understand") or asking someone to repeat themselves is not a sign of weak language ability — it is a normal part of communication in any language, including for native speakers in noisy markets, fast conversations, or unfamiliar topics. As you continue through this course, build the habit of using repair expressions confidently rather than guessing silently or switching to English. Hausa speakers, like speakers of any language, generally respond warmly to a learner who asks for clarification clearly and politely.

Regional Variation — Market measures and currency reminders

This chapter's dialogue uses kwano (a bowl or basin) as a common informal measure for items like onions and tomatoes in many Nigerian markets — a practice that varies by region, vendor, and item. Some markets use weight-based measures instead, particularly for packaged or imported goods. As established in Chapter 9, this course distinguishes Nigerian naira from Nigerien CFA francs; all prices in this chapter are given in naira, consistent with the Kano-based setting of the dialogue and readings.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Negating akwai with ba instead of using babu

Error: Ba akwai shinkafa a gida.

Correction: Babu shinkafa a gida. — Hausa does not negate akwai by placing ba in front of it. Nonexistence has its own dedicated word, babu, which functions as the negative counterpart of akwai rather than as akwai plus a negative particle. If you find yourself wanting to negate akwai, stop and substitute babu instead.

Common Mistake — Doubling the object with both a noun and a pronoun

Error: Ina son shi albasa. (inserting a direct-object pronoun before the noun it is meant to replace)

Correction: A direct-object pronoun replaces the noun; it does not accompany it in the same clause. Say either Ina son albasa (using the full noun) or Ina son ta (using the pronoun, once albasa has already been mentioned), but not both together. Use the pronoun only after the noun it refers to has already been introduced in the conversation.

Common Mistake — Using a relative-continuative form in a plain statement

Error: Yana kake girki yanzu. (mixing a third-person ordinary form with a second-person relative form)

Correction: Yana girki yanzu. (He is cooking now — ordinary continuative, no question word present) or Me kake girki? (What are you cooking? — relative form, used because the sentence begins with a question word). Relative-continuative forms appear only when a question word, focus, or relative context requires them. A plain statement with no question word uses the ordinary continuative subject form that matches its own subject.

Common Mistake — Confusing possession with da and the independent pronoun as subject

Error: Mu da kuɗi sosai. (using the independent pronoun mu as the subject of a possession statement instead of the continuative subject form)

Correction: Muna da kuɗi sosai. (We have a lot of money.) Possession with da, as taught in Chapter 6, requires a continuative subject form — muna, not the independent pronoun mu — before da. The independent pronoun may appear additionally for emphasis (Mu kanmu, muna da kuɗi), but it cannot substitute for the continuative subject form.

Looking Ahead

Abin da ka/ki koya yanzu — What you know now

You have now completed Unit II. You can describe a home using existence, nonexistence, location, and possession; talk about daily routines using continuative subject forms and verbal nouns; express food preferences and negation; and complete a market transaction using quantities, prices, and direct-object pronouns. More importantly, this chapter has asked you to do something new with old material: combine all of these systems into one connected, natural stretch of communication, including the ability to signal confusion and request repair when something goes wrong. This combining skill — not just knowing individual patterns — is what moves you from reciting Hausa to actually using it.

Babi na 11: A Cikin Gari — Getting Around Town

In Chapter 11, you leave the home and the market behind for the wider city. You will learn to identify buildings and public services, ask where a place is, and — for the first time — give and follow real commands and directions using the affirmative singular imperative and subjunctive-based commands for groups or negative instructions. This is the first time imperatives become fully productive rather than fixed patterns, building directly on the formulaic commands you have used since Chapter 2. You will also begin a new unit, Unit III: Community and Travel, which carries your communication skills out into transportation, weather, health, work, and school over the next several chapters.

Abinci da Abin Sha

Food and Drink · Speak and Write Hausa: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common foods, drinks, and meals in Hausa.
  2. State what you want using so in affirmative and negative statements.
  3. Form negative continuative statements using the complete ba … ba pattern.
  4. Make polite requests for food and drink using don Allah and request frames.
  5. State a preference between two foods or drinks.
  6. Ask and answer simple questions about a meal, including basic quantities.
  7. Order a simple meal in a restaurant or home setting and respond to follow-up questions.
  8. Read and write a short food-related message, order, or meal description.

Communication Scenario

A small restaurant near the language program in Kano has become a regular meeting place after class. You and your classmates stop in most afternoons — sometimes to eat a full meal, sometimes only for tea or a cold drink. To take part naturally, you need to be able to say what you want, ask what is available, decline politely when something does not suit you, and talk about what you and your classmates like and dislike.

  1. In Chapter 7, you learned the continuative pattern for ongoing and regular activity — a subject form plus a verbal noun, such as ina cin abinci (I am eating / I eat). This chapter teaches the negative of that pattern. Hausa negation, as you saw briefly with ba … ba ne/ce in Chapter 3, again uses two parts that work together. Watch closely for both parts as you work through the grammar section.
  2. You will also meet so, a verb meaning "want" or "like," used with a verbal noun or a noun to say what someone wants or prefers. Like the continuative pattern itself, so follows the subject-form system you are already building — so this chapter is also a chance to practice and strengthen what you learned in Chapter 7, not just add something new.

Pronunciation and Boko

Tone in negative particles and subject forms

The word ba appears twice in the negative continuative pattern taught in this chapter, and both occurrences carry tone that is part of the construction's identity. Tone also distinguishes the continuative subject forms (ina, yana, tana, and so on, reviewed from Chapter 7) from their negative counterparts. Ordinary Boko does not mark this tone, so listen carefully to your instructor or audio model and imitate the contour of the whole phrase rather than reading the words in isolation.

Vowel length in food vocabulary

Several food and drink words in this chapter contain vowel-length distinctions that affect natural pronunciation, including miya, nama, and shinkafa. Vowel length is not marked in ordinary Boko, but it is part of how the word is recognized in speech. Practice each new food word aloud with your instructor or audio model rather than relying on the written form alone.

Hooked consonants in common food names

Notice the hooked ƙ in kaɗan is not present — but the hooked ɗ appears in kaɗan (a little) and dunƙun dawa contains ƙ. Keep these letters distinct from their plain counterparts in both reading and writing. A mispronounced or misspelled hooked letter can change a word's meaning entirely.

Polite request intonation

A polite request in Hausa, such as Ina so shinkafa, don Allah (I would like rice, please), typically uses a falling, even intonation rather than a sharp or clipped tone. Abrupt, flat delivery of a request can sound demanding even when the words themselves are polite. Practice requests with a warm, slightly rising-then-falling contour, especially on don Allah at the end of the sentence.

Pronunciation Practice — Negative ba versus identification ba

You met ba … ba ne/ce in Chapter 3 for negative identification. This chapter introduces a related but distinct pattern: ba + continuative subject form + verbal noun or complement + ba for negative ongoing or habitual statements. The two constructions share the discontinuous ba … ba shape, but the material inside the brackets differs — a noun-plus-particle in Chapter 3, a continuative statement here. Listen for which kind of sentence surrounds the ba … ba frame, and recognize that the function of the pattern depends on what it encloses.

Grammar Instruction

8.1 Review: continuative subject forms

Purpose: recall the continuative pattern from Chapter 7 before learning its negative.

In Chapter 7, you learned that Hausa expresses ongoing or regular activity with a continuative subject form plus a verbal noun: ina cin abinci (I am eating / I eat). The subject form carries person, number, and gender information. Review the forms you need for this chapter:

PersonContinuative subject formEnglish equivalent
1st singularinaI am…ing / I…
2nd singular masculinekanayou are…ing (to a male)
2nd singular femininekinayou are…ing (to a female)
3rd singular masculineyanahe is…ing
3rd singular femininetanashe is…ing
1st pluralmunawe are…ing
2nd pluralkunayou (pl.) are…ing
3rd pluralsunathey are…ing
  • Ina cin shinkafa.I am eating rice.
  • Tana sha shayi.She is drinking tea.
  • Muna son abinci.We want food.

Verbal nouns used in this chapter: ci (to eat) → verbal noun cin; sha (to drink) → verbal noun sha; so (to want/like) → verbal noun son. These verbal nouns have been verified individually and must be learned with their verbs; do not form a verbal noun by analogy.

8.2 The negative continuative

Purpose: say what is not happening or what someone does not regularly do.

The negative continuative uses the discontinuous pattern ba + continuative subject form + verbal noun or complement + ba. Both ba elements are required. Learn the complete negative pattern as a unit rather than treating the first ba as optional or interchangeable with the second.

  • Ba ni cin nama ba.I am not eating meat. / I do not eat meat.ba + ni + [verbal noun] + ba
  • Ba ya son shayi ba.He does not want tea.
  • Ba ta cin wake ba.She is not eating beans. / She does not eat beans.
  • Ba mu son miya mai zafi ba.We do not want spicy soup.

Notice that in the negative continuative, the first-person and second/third-person forms used after the initial ba are shortened compared to the plain affirmative continuative subject forms (ina → ni; yana → ya; tana → ta; muna → mu). This is a fixed feature of the negative pattern and must be learned as part of the construction — do not simply add ba … ba around the affirmative form unchanged.

Language Focus — Hausa negation is construction-specific

You have now met two different discontinuous negative patterns: ba … ba ne/ce for identification (Chapter 3) and ba + negative subject form + verbal noun + ba for the continuative (this chapter). These are not the same construction wearing different clothes — each has its own subject-form set and its own internal structure. Hausa does not have one all-purpose negative word placed before an affirmative sentence. As you continue through the course, expect each new aspect or sentence type to come with its own negative pattern, and learn each one as a complete construction rather than assuming the previous pattern transfers automatically.

Language Building Block
ba + negative subject form + verbal noun or complement + ba
Ba ni son kifi ba.
I do not want fish.
Ba ya cin nama ba.
He does not eat meat.

Present the complete negative pattern rather than treating ba as a single word placed before an affirmative sentence. Both elements of ba … ba are required; the construction is incomplete without the final ba.

8.3 So — wanting and liking

Purpose: state what you want, like, or do not want using the verb so.

So ("to want," "to like") follows the same continuative pattern as other verbs: continuative subject form + verbal noun son + the thing wanted. So can take a noun directly or a verbal noun expressing a wanted action.

  • Ina son shinkafa da miya.I want rice and soup.
  • Yana son ruwan sanyi.He wants cold water.
  • Muna son cin abinci yanzu.We want to eat now. (literally: we want eating food now)
  • Ba ta son shayi ba — tana son kofi.She does not want tea — she wants coffee.

Caution: Hausa so covers a range that English splits between "want" and "like." Context determines which English equivalent fits best; do not assume a one-to-one mapping with either English verb in every sentence.

8.4 Polite requests

Purpose: request food, drink, or service politely.

A polite request for food or drink commonly uses ina so ("I would like") followed by the item, and closes with don Allah ("please") when extra courtesy is appropriate. This is the same so construction from section 8.3, used in a service context.

  • Ina so shinkafa da miya, don Allah.I would like rice and soup, please.
  • Ina so kofi guda ɗaya, don Allah.I would like one cup of coffee, please.
  • Za mu so ruwa kaɗan, don Allah.We would like a little water, please. (recognition; za + so combination previewed; full future system in Ch. 14)

To decline an offer politely, use the negative continuative pattern from section 8.2 together with a courteous tone, optionally followed by an alternative request:

  • Na gode, amma ba ni son nama ba. Ina so kifi maimakon.Thank you, but I do not want meat. I would like fish instead.

Amma = but; maimakon = instead. Both are introduced here as useful connecting words for polite refusal and substitution.

8.5 Quantity expressions with food

Purpose: specify how much or how many of an item you want.

Quantity words such as kaɗan (a little) and da yawa (a lot) follow the noun they describe. Container words such as kwalba (bottle) and kofi (cup) combine with a linker and the contained item, following the genitive-linker pattern reviewed from Chapter 4.

  • Ina so ruwa kaɗan.I want a little water.
  • Ina so shinkafa da yawa.I want a lot of rice.
  • Kwalbar ruwa guda biyu, don Allah.Two bottles of water, please.kwalba + -r (linker) + ruwa + guda biyu

The linker form in kwalbar ruwa uses -r because kwalba is feminine, following the gender-sensitive linker pattern from Chapter 4. Verify the gender of any new container noun before attaching the linker.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Gidan abinci kusa da makarantar harshe a Kano, bayan azuzuwan rana. Amina da Bello sun zauna domin cin abincin rana. Mai gidan abinci, Hajiya Rabi, ta zo domin karɓar oda.

(Setting: A restaurant near the language school in Kano, after afternoon classes. Amina and Bello have sat down for lunch. The restaurant owner, Hajiya Rabi, comes to take their order.)

HAJIYA RABI Sannu, ku zo lafiya. Me kuke so a yau?Hello, welcome. What would you like today?
AMINA Sannu, Hajiya. Ina so shinkafa da miya, don Allah. Ina son miya mai daɗi, amma ba ni son ita mai zafi sosai.Hello, ma'am. I would like rice and soup, please. I want tasty soup, but I don't want it too spicy.
HAJIYA RABI To, ba damuwa. Muna da miyar kuka da miyar taushe — biyun ba su da zafi sosai. Kai kuwa, me kake so?All right, no problem. We have kuka soup and taushe soup — neither is very spicy. And you, what would you like?
BELLO Ina so tuwo da nama, don Allah. Amma ba ni cin naman alade — ina cin naman sa ko kaza kawai.I would like tuwo and meat, please. But I do not eat pork — I only eat beef or chicken.
HAJIYA RABI Mun fahimta. Za mu kawo maka naman sa. Kuma me kuke so ku sha?We understand. We will bring you beef. And what would you like to drink?
AMINA Ina so ruwan sanyi kaɗan, don Allah.I would like a little cold water, please.
BELLO Ni kuwa, ina so shayi. Ban son ruwan sanyi da abinci ba — ina son shayi mai zafi.As for me, I want tea. I don't like cold drinks with food — I like hot tea.
HAJIYA RABI To, zan kawo muku abinci da abin sha nan da nan. Na gode da zuwanku.All right, I will bring you the food and drinks shortly. Thank you for coming.
AMINA Na gode, Hajiya. Mun ji daɗin zuwa nan.Thank you, ma'am. We are glad to come here.

Vocabulary notes for the dialogue: ku zo lafiya = welcome (literally "may you come in peace"); me kuke so = what do you (pl./respectful) want (recognition; relative-continuative form, full grammar Ch. 7 and 16); to = okay, well; ba damuwa = no problem; miyar kuka, miyar taushe = named regional soup varieties; naman alade = pork; naman sa = beef; kaza = chicken; mun fahimta = we understand; zan kawo muku = I will bring you (recognition; future, Ch. 14); nan da nan = right away; mun ji daɗin zuwa nan = we are glad to come here (recognition expression).

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. What two food items does Amina order? What kind of soup does she ask for, and what does she say she does not want?
  2. Why does Bello ask specifically for beef or chicken? What does he say he does not eat?
  3. What drink does each person request? Find and quote both lines.
  4. Find one example of the negative continuative pattern in the dialogue spoken by Amina, and one spoken by Bello. Write both sentences and their English meanings.
  5. Find the sentence where Bello states a preference for hot tea over cold drinks with food. Write it in Hausa.

  1. Karanta samfuri. Reread Rubutu na 2 (the home food note) above. Underline every so statement. Circle every negative continuative sentence.
  2. Shirya bayananka. Decide on a short shopping note, meal description, or restaurant order to write. List the foods, drinks, and at least one preference and one thing you do not want.
  3. Rubuta farkon rubutunka. Write six to eight Hausa sentences. Include at least one affirmative continuative statement with so, one negative continuative statement, one polite request with don Allah, and one quantity expression.

Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

  • Each continuative statement uses the correct subject form for the speaker or subject (ina, yana, tana, and so on).
  • Each negative continuative uses both elements: ba + negative subject form + verbal noun + ba.
  • The negative subject form (ni, ya, ta, mu, ku, su) is used after the first ba, not the affirmative form.
  • Polite requests use ina so and include don Allah where appropriate.
  • Quantity words (kaɗan, da yawa) and container-plus-linker phrases are correctly formed.
  • Food and drink nouns are spelled correctly, with hooked letters preserved.
  • Each sentence ends with a full stop. Names are capitalized.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Shared meals and hospitality

Sharing food is an important part of social life in many Hausa-speaking communities, whether at home, at a roadside food stand, or in a sit-down restaurant. Offering food to a guest, and a guest accepting at least a small amount, is a common courtesy in many settings — though practices vary by household, region, religion, and personal circumstance, and no single rule describes every Hausa-speaking community. When declining food, a polite refusal that still expresses thanks (such as combining na gode with a negative continuative statement) tends to be better received than a blunt "no" alone.

Restaurant and food-stand settings range widely — from small family-run eateries to larger establishments in cities like Kano, Abuja, Niamey, and beyond. Menus, ordering customs, and payment practices differ by setting; this chapter's restaurant scenario reflects one common type of interaction, not a universal model.

Culture in Context — Dietary practices and accurate representation

Dietary practices among Hausa-speaking people vary by religion, household, region, and individual choice. Islam has historically been prominent in many Hausa-speaking communities, and many Muslim Hausa speakers do not eat pork (naman alade) for religious reasons, as reflected in Bello's line in the model dialogue. At the same time, Hausa-speaking communities include Christians, people of other traditions, and people with non-religious dietary preferences, so dietary choices should never be assumed automatically from someone's name, language, or region. This chapter's vocabulary and dialogue are designed to let learners state their own preferences accurately, whatever those preferences are.

Regional Variation — Named soups and regional dishes

Dishes such as miyar kuka (baobab-leaf soup) and miyar taushe (a pumpkin-and-vegetable-based soup) are widely known in northern Nigeria, but specific recipes, names, and availability vary by city, household, and country, including in southern Niger. This course introduces a small set of receptive dish names to support the model dialogue and readings; it does not attempt a complete regional culinary survey. When you travel or speak with Hausa speakers from different areas, expect to encounter dish names and preparation styles not covered here.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Omitting the second ba in the negative continuative

Error: Ba ni son shayi. (omitting the second ba)

Correction: Ba ni son shayi ba. — As with the negative identification pattern from Chapter 3, the negative continuative requires both elements of ba … ba. Dropping the final ba leaves the sentence incomplete and unnatural. Treat the two ba elements as a pair that must appear together.

Common Mistake — Using the affirmative subject form after the first ba

Error: Ba yana son nama ba. (using the affirmative yana instead of the negative form ya)

Correction: Ba ya son nama ba. — The negative continuative uses its own set of subject forms after the first ba (ni, ka, ya, ta, mu, ku, su), which differ from the affirmative continuative forms (ina, kana, yana, tana, muna, kuna, suna). Learn the negative forms as part of the construction rather than inserting the affirmative form into the negative frame.

Common Mistake — Confusing so with a literal English "like"

Error: Assuming so always means exactly "like" in the English sense, including for people.

Correction: So can express "want" or "like" depending on context, but its range of use does not map perfectly onto either English verb in every situation, and the course at this stage limits so to food, drink, and activity preferences. Use the example sentences in this chapter as your model for natural use, and do not extend so to untaught contexts without checking.

Common Mistake — Placing the quantity word before the noun

Error: Ina so kaɗan ruwa. (placing kaɗan before the noun)

Correction: Ina so ruwa kaɗan. — Quantity words such as kaɗan and da yawa follow the noun they describe in Hausa, unlike English "a little water," where the quantity word comes first. Practice the noun-plus-quantity order until it feels natural.

A Gidan Abinci — At the Restaurant

You are at a restaurant with a classmate. Complete both the oral and written components:

  • Oral component: With a partner (or recorded for solo study), role-play ordering a complete meal: two food items, one drink, and a quantity expression. Politely decline one item the server suggests and offer an alternative using maimakon. State one personal food or drink preference using so, and respond to one follow-up question without notes.
  • Written component: Write an 80-100-word Hausa note — either a message to a friend about meal plans or a description of your own food preferences. Include at least one affirmative continuative statement with so, one complete negative continuative sentence, one polite request with don Allah, and one quantity expression.

Evaluation criteria: correct continuative subject forms; complete negative continuative construction with correct negative subject forms; accurate use of so; appropriate polite request and refusal language; correct quantity-word order; accurate Boko spelling including hooked letters.

Atisayen D — So da fi so (sample answers)

  1. Ina fi son shayi da kofi. (formulaic fi so usage, recognition-supported)
  2. Ba ni son nama ba. Ina so kifi maimakon.
  3. Ina son miya mai zafi.
  4. Ba ya/tana son abin sha mai zaƙi ba. (choose ya for "he," tana affirmative form only used outside negative frame — accept Ba ya son…ba or Ba ta son…ba depending on chosen gender)

Tambayoyin fahimta na tattaunawa (Dialogue comprehension)

  1. Amina orders rice and soup. She asks for tasty soup and says she does not want it too spicy.
  2. Bello asks specifically for beef or chicken because he does not eat pork (naman alade).
  3. Amina: Ina so ruwan sanyi kaɗan, don Allah. Bello: Ni kuwa, ina so shayi.
  4. Amina: Ban son ruwan sanyi da abinci ba is Bello's line, not Amina's — correct attribution: Bello says Ban son ruwan sanyi da abinci ba — ina son shayi mai zafi. (I don't like cold drinks with food — I like hot tea.) Amina's negative continuative: implicit in declining excess spice is not a full negative continuative sentence in her lines; accept Bello's sentence as the clear example and note this for learner discussion.
  5. Ban son ruwan sanyi da abinci ba — ina son shayi mai zafi.

Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

  1. Hauwa wants: shinkafa, miya, kifi. She does not eat meat (nama).
  2. Hauwa says she wants fruit juice, but if it is unavailable, cold water is enough.
  3. The mother wants tasty rice and soup; she does not want it very spicy.
  4. The children prefer fruit ('ya'yan itace) instead of a lot of vegetables.
  5. Negative continuative examples: ba ni son nama ba (Rubutu 1, implied — accept close paraphrase); ba ta son ita mai zafi sosai (Rubutu 2, mother); ba su son kayan lambu da yawa ba (Rubutu 2, children). All negate continuative statements, not identification.
  6. Both readings mention a preference for fruit or fruit-related items and a dislike of excessive spice; either is an acceptable common fact.

Looking Ahead

Abin da ka/ki koya yanzu — What you know now

You can now name common foods, drinks, and meals; state what you want and like using so; politely request food and drink with ina so and don Allah; and — most importantly — form a complete negative continuative statement using ba plus the correct negative subject form plus a verbal noun plus the final ba. This negative pattern builds directly on the continuative system from Chapter 7 and gives you a second discontinuous negative construction alongside the ba … ba ne/ce pattern from Chapter 3. You can now talk about what is happening, what is not happening, what you want, and what you do not want — a significant expansion of your communicative range.

Babi na 9: A Kasuwa — At the Market

In Chapter 9, you will take the food and quantity vocabulary from this chapter into the market. You will learn to ask and state prices, request specific quantities and sizes, and complete a purchase courteously. The chapter introduces direct-object pronouns and shows how certain high-frequency verbs change their form before a noun object compared to a pronoun object — building on the verb and object foundations you have been developing since Chapter 7. Numbers will expand well beyond 100, and you will practice describing goods using the gender and plural agreement patterns you already control.

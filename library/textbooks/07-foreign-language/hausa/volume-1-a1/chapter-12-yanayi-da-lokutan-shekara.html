Yanayi da Lokutan Shekara

Weather and Seasons · Speak and Write Hausa: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe current weather conditions, including heat, cold, rain, wind, and dust.
  2. Ask about weather conditions in another place using Yanayi yana nawa a…? and related questions.
  3. Name and describe Hausa-region seasons accurately, without imposing a four-season model.
  4. Use the impersonal construction with ana to make general statements about weather and common activities.
  5. Use akwai and quality expressions to describe weather conditions that are not expressed with ana.
  6. Relate weather to clothing, travel plans, and activities using vocabulary and grammar from earlier chapters.
  7. Form a basic real condition with idan, linking a weather condition to a plausible result.
  8. Distinguish core weather and seasonal vocabulary as used in Nigeria and Niger, recognizing that the region's climate does not follow a temperate four-season pattern.

Communication Scenario

You are part of a small community network that includes friends and family in Kano, Niamey, and elsewhere. People in the group regularly check in about the weather before planning a trip, a market visit, or an outdoor event. This chapter prepares you to describe today's conditions where you are, ask what the weather is like somewhere else, and make a simple plan based on the forecast — for example, deciding whether to travel by car or to carry an umbrella.

  1. Hausa weather expressions do not use a single impersonal verb the way English uses "it" in "it is raining." Instead, Hausa draws on several different structures: some weather statements use the impersonal form ana, some use akwai ("there is/are"), and some use a quality word directly. As you move through the grammar section, notice which structure fits which kind of weather statement — and ask yourself why a single English pattern ("it is + adjective") does not transfer directly into Hausa.
  2. The Sahel and savanna regions where Hausa is widely spoken do not experience four temperate seasons. Instead, communities commonly distinguish a rainy season and a dry season, with further distinctions for cooler and hotter periods within the dry season. Keep this in mind as you learn the seasonal vocabulary — resist the instinct to map "spring," "summer," "autumn," and "winter" onto Hausa terms that mean something different.

Lokutan shekara — Seasons of the year

Hausa-speaking communities in Nigeria and Niger commonly organize the year around a rainy season and a dry season, with the dry season further divided into cooler and hotter stretches. This pattern reflects the Sahel and savanna climate of the region — it is not a four-season system like the one familiar from temperate climates.

HausaEnglishApproximate timing
daminarainy seasonroughly June through September in much of northern Nigeria and Niger
ranidry seasonthe broader dry period; sometimes used especially for the hot dry season
hunturucool dry season, "cold season"the cooler part of the dry season, often associated with the harmattan winds
bazarahot dry seasonthe hottest stretch, typically before the rains begin

Do not translate bazara as "spring" or hunturu as "winter" by reflex. These words name periods defined by local climate patterns, not by the temperate four-season calendar. Some learner materials map hunturu loosely onto "winter" because both refer to a cooler period, but the lived experience and surrounding cultural associations differ substantially. Treat each term on its own basis.

Regional Variation — Climate is not uniform across the Hausa-speaking world

The rainy-and-dry-season pattern described above fits much of northern Nigeria and southern Niger well, but it is not universal across every place where Hausa is spoken. Coastal and forest-zone areas farther south have different rainfall patterns, and Hausa speakers living outside West Africa may experience entirely different climates. When discussing weather and seasons, identify the place you are describing rather than assuming one climate pattern applies everywhere Hausa is used.

Pronunciation and Boko

Tone and length in weather vocabulary

Several weather words in this chapter are short and depend heavily on tone and vowel length for accurate pronunciation. Rana (sun/day) and ruwa (water, as in ruwan sama) both contain vowel-length and tone patterns that must be learned by listening rather than guessed from spelling. Pay particular attention to ƙura (dust): the hooked ƙ must be produced as a distinct ejective-like sound, clearly different from a plain k. Confusing ƙura with a plain-k pronunciation can affect intelligibility.

Sound Focus — ana versus similar short grammatical words

The impersonal form ana is short and can be confused with other short grammatical words you already know, such as akwai shortened in casual speech or the subject forms ina/yana/tana from Chapter 3 and Chapter 7. Listen carefully to the vowel quality and tone of ana as modeled by your instructor or audio source, and practice it in full phrases — ana ruwan sama — rather than in isolation. Producing ana clearly and distinctly from yana is important, since the two carry very different grammatical information: ana signals an impersonal or general statement, while yana signals "he is …-ing."

Clause-level intonation in conditional sentences

An idan-clause typically carries a rising or sustained intonation pattern through the condition, followed by a falling pattern through the result, similar to the way many languages intone conditional sentences. Practice this rhythm with the example sentences in the grammar section: pause briefly after the condition and let your pitch fall naturally across the result clause.

Reading a short forecast

Apply the Boko-reading principles from earlier chapters as you read weather reports in this chapter: distinguish hooked letters (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ) and 'y from their plain counterparts, read c, sh, and ts with their Hausa values, and remember that tone and vowel length are present in speech even though ordinary Boko does not mark them.

Grammar Instruction

12.1 The impersonal construction with ana

Purpose: make general statements about weather and common ongoing activities without naming a specific personal subject.

Hausa uses ana to express an unspecified, general, or impersonal subject — roughly comparable to English "it" in weather statements or "people" in general statements, though the match is not exact. Ana is related in form to the continuative subject forms you met in Chapter 7 (ina, yana, tana), but it does not refer to a specific person. Instead, it signals that the activity or condition is general, ongoing, or not attributed to a named subject.

  • Ana ruwan sama.It is raining. / There is rain (happening).
  • Ana ruwan sama a Kano yau.It is raining in Kano today.
  • Ba a ruwan sama a nan ba.It is not raining here. (negative impersonal — recognition only at this stage)
  • Ana sayar da kayan lambu a kasuwa.Vegetables are sold at the market. (general statement, recognition only)
Language Note — ana is not a weather-only word

Ana is a general impersonal construction used across many contexts, not a dedicated "weather verb." In this chapter, you will use it productively only for weather statements such as ana ruwan sama. You may also see ana in other general statements in readings or listening passages — these broader uses are for recognition only at this stage. The full range of impersonal and passive-like uses of ana is not a productive requirement of this chapter; focus your active production on weather.

12.2 Weather with akwai and quality words

Purpose: describe conditions that are not naturally expressed with ana, using akwai and quality constructions reviewed from earlier chapters.

Not every weather statement uses ana. Conditions expressed as the presence of something — heat, cold, wind, dust, cloud, haze — typically use akwai, which you learned in Chapter 6 for existence. Other statements use the mai-construction or yana da kyau / yana da muni ("it is good / it is bad," literally "it has goodness / it has badness") in a pattern that echoes the possession construction from Chapter 6.

  • Akwai zafi a yau.It is hot today. (literally "there is heat today")
  • Akwai sanyi sosai a safe.It is very cold in the morning.
  • Akwai iska mai ƙarfi.There is a strong wind. (mai ƙarfi = having strength)
  • Akwai ƙura a birnin Kano.There is dust in the city of Kano.
  • Babu ruwan sama a wannan lokaci.There is no rain at this time. (babu reviewed from Chapter 6)
  • Yanayi yana da kyau a yau.The weather is good today.
  • Yanayi yana da muni a Niamey.The weather is bad in Niamey.
Language Focus — Hausa weather uses several structures, not one impersonal verb

English speakers learning Hausa often look for a single equivalent of "it is raining" or "it is hot" that behaves like the English dummy subject "it" plus a weather verb. Hausa does not organize weather this way. Instead, different weather facts are expressed through different existing structures you already know:

  • ana + verbal noun, for an ongoing weather event such as rain (ana ruwan sama)
  • akwai + noun, for the presence of a condition such as heat, cold, wind, or dust (akwai zafi)
  • babu + noun, for the absence of a condition (babu ruwan sama)
  • yana da kyau / yana da muni, for an overall evaluation of the weather

Learning to select the right structure for the right kind of weather fact — rather than translating English "it is + adjective" word for word — is the central skill of this section.

Purpose: ask what the weather is like in another place using question patterns built on structures already taught.

Akwai zafi a can?Is it hot there? (literally "Is there heat there?")

The question Ya ke yanayi a Kano yau? contains a relative-continuative form related to the pattern you first met in Chapter 3 and practiced further in Chapters 6 and 9. Learn this complete question now as a formulaic pattern. Its full grammatical analysis is part of the relative-aspect consolidation in Chapters 16 and 19; do not attempt to build new sentences with this pattern by substituting words into its internal structure yet. The akwai, ana, and yana da kyau questions above are fully productive, since they follow grammar you have already studied.

Language Building Block
Akwai/Ana/Babu + weather noun + (a + place) + (time word)?
Akwai zafi a Kano yau?
Is it hot in Kano today?
A'a, babu zafi sosai. Akwai sanyi kaɗan da safe.
No, it is not very hot. There is a little cold in the morning.

Choose akwai for the presence of a condition, ana for an ongoing weather event such as rain, and babu to negate a condition's presence. The place phrase (a Kano) and time word (yau, da safe) can be added or omitted depending on context.

12.4 Real conditions with idan

Purpose: link a familiar weather condition to a plausible result using a basic real condition.

Hausa expresses a real condition — one describing a likely or general "if … then" relationship — with idan introducing the condition clause, followed by a result clause. At this stage, keep both clauses within grammar you have already studied: existence, possession, continuative statements, and fixed singular commands or formulaic suggestions are all available. Avoid productive future or subjunctive forms in the result clause unless the example is explicitly marked as formulaic.

  • Idan akwai ruwan sama, ba na fita.If it rains, I do not go out.idan + condition (akwai + noun), + result (negative continuative)
  • Idan akwai zafi sosai, muna shan ruwa da yawa.If it is very hot, we drink a lot of water.
  • Idan akwai hadari, kada ku fita waje.If there is a storm, do not go outside. (negative command, reviewed from Chapter 11)
  • Idan yanayi yana da kyau, za mu yi tafiya. (sample only)If the weather is good, we will travel. (formulaic — future za here is for recognition only; productive future begins in Chapter 14)
Language Note — keep the idan result clause inside taught grammar

It is natural to want to say "if it rains, I will stay home" using a future form, but the productive future construction with za is not introduced until Chapter 14. For this chapter, build idan sentences whose result clause uses continuative statements, existence and possession statements, or commands — all grammar you already control. One example above shows a formulaic future result clause labeled clearly as recognition-only; do not use that pattern as a model for new sentences of your own until Chapter 14 authorizes it.

12.5 Relating weather to clothing and activities

Purpose: connect a weather condition to a practical choice about clothing, travel, or activity, recycling vocabulary from Chapters 6, 9, and 11.

  • Akwai sanyi, don haka ina sa riga.It is cold, so I am wearing a gown.
  • Akwai zafi sosai, saboda haka muna zama a gida.It is very hot, so we are staying home.
  • Akwai ruwan sama, don haka ina ɗauke da laima.It is raining, so I am carrying an umbrella.
  • Yanayi yana da kyau, saboda haka muna shan iska a waje.The weather is good, so we are taking a stroll outside.

These sentences combine a weather statement, a connector (don haka or saboda haka), and a continuative statement describing an activity or clothing choice — all structures already in your productive repertoire. This is the core communicative pattern of the chapter: weather observation plus practical consequence.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Hauwa tana cikin Kano, Sadiq yana cikin Niamey. Suna magana ta waya kafin Sadiq ya yi tafiya zuwa Kano don ziyara.

(Setting: Hauwa is in Kano; Sadiq is in Niamey. They are speaking by phone before Sadiq travels to Kano for a visit.)

SADIQ Sannu, Hauwa. Ina son sani — akwai zafi a Kano yanzu?Hello, Hauwa. I want to know — is it hot in Kano right now?
HAUWA Sannu, Sadiq. Eh, akwai zafi sosai da rana, amma akwai sanyi kaɗan da dare. Ba ruwan sama a nan a wannan lokaci.Hello, Sadiq. Yes, it is very hot during the day, but there is a little cold at night. There is no rain here at this time.
SADIQ A nan Niamey kuma, ana ruwan sama kowace rana. Akwai danshi sosai. Ina ɗauke da laima ko'ina na je.Here in Niamey, it rains every day. It is very humid. I carry an umbrella everywhere I go.
HAUWA To, idan akwai zafi a Kano, kada ka kawo riga mai nauyi. Sa riga mai sauƙi kawai.Well, if it is hot in Kano, don't bring a heavy gown. Just wear a light gown.
SADIQ Na gode da shawarar. Idan ban kawo riga mai nauyi ba, zan kasance da kyau. (yana dariya)Thank you for the advice. If I don't bring a heavy gown, I will be fine. (laughing)
HAUWA Daidai. Kuma idan akwai ƙura, mu zauna a gida da safe. Mun fi son fita da yamma, lokacin da iska take kyautu.Right. And if there is dust, let's stay home in the morning. We prefer to go out in the evening, when the air is calmer.
SADIQ Madalla. Sai mu hadu da safiyar Litinin, in Allah Ya yarda.Excellent. Let's meet on Monday morning, God willing.

Vocabulary notes for the dialogue: ina son sani = I want to know; kowace rana = every day; ko'ina na je = everywhere I go (recognition only); mai nauyi = heavy; mai sauƙi = light, easy; shawara = advice; kasance da kyau = to be fine, to be well; kyautu = to be calm, gentle (here describing the wind); sai mu hadu = let's meet, see you (formulaic; full subjunctive system in later chapters); in Allah Ya yarda = God willing (a common, widely used courtesy expression; recognition only — not every speaker uses religious formulas, and their use is a matter of personal and community practice, not a required script).

Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

  • Each weather fact uses the correct structure: ana for an ongoing event such as rain; akwai/babu for the presence or absence of a condition; yana da kyau/muni for an overall evaluation.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Seasons in the Sahel and savanna

Much of northern Nigeria and southern Niger lies within the Sahel and savanna climate zones, where the year is shaped primarily by the alternation between a rainy season (damina) and a dry season, rather than by four distinct temperate seasons. The dry season itself includes a cooler stretch, often associated with the dust-laden harmattan winds that blow from the Sahara, and a hotter stretch before the rains return. These patterns affect agriculture, travel, clothing choices, and the timing of festivals and community events across the region.

This climate pattern is not identical everywhere Hausa is spoken. Areas farther south and other regions where Hausa functions as a language of wider communication may experience different rainfall and temperature patterns entirely. When you discuss weather and seasons in Hausa, anchor your description to a specific place rather than assuming one climate describes the whole Hausa-speaking world.

Culture in Context — Weather, clothing, and daily planning

Discussing the weather before a trip, an outdoor event, or a visit is a practical and socially natural conversation topic, much as it is in many parts of the world. Clothing choices in Hausa-speaking communities respond to both weather and social context: lighter fabrics and looser garments are common in hot conditions, while heavier wraps or layers may be added during the cooler dry-season mornings and evenings. These choices vary by region, occasion, personal preference, and individual household — there is no single uniform practice to memorize, but weather remains a genuinely practical factor in daily decisions about what to wear and when to travel.

Regional Variation — Naming the dry season

Speakers and regions vary somewhat in how precisely they distinguish rani, hunturu, and bazara. Some speakers use rani broadly for the entire dry season and reserve hunturu and bazara for more specific stretches within it; others draw the boundaries slightly differently depending on local climate experience. This course teaches all three terms as presented in the vocabulary section, but learners should expect some natural variation in how individual speakers apply them, just as English speakers vary in exactly when they say "fall" ends and "winter" begins.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using ana for every weather statement

Error: Ana zafi. (using ana for heat, by analogy with ana ruwan sama)

Correction: Akwai zafi. — Heat, cold, wind, and dust are expressed with akwai as conditions that are present, not with ana. Reserve ana for ongoing weather events such as rain. A useful check: if the English sentence could be rephrased as "there is [condition]," Hausa likely uses akwai.

Common Mistake — Translating "it is hot" with a personal pronoun subject

Error: *Shi yana zafi. (treating "it" as shi and weather as an ordinary continuative verb)

Correction: Akwai zafi. — English "it" in weather statements has no direct Hausa pronoun equivalent. Do not introduce shi as a dummy subject. Use akwai, ana, or yana da kyau/muni as appropriate, none of which require a personal pronoun subject for the weather itself.

Common Mistake — Mapping bazara and hunturu directly onto "spring" and "winter"

Error: Describing bazara as "spring, when flowers bloom and the weather turns mild" — importing temperate-climate associations.

Correction: Bazara refers to the hot dry season in the Sahel and savanna climate pattern, typically the hottest stretch of the year, before the rains begin. Hunturu refers to the cooler dry season, not a snowy winter. Learn each term through its actual climate meaning in context, not through a one-to-one English seasonal translation.

Common Mistake — Adding an unauthorized future form in an idan result clause

Error: Freely constructing new sentences such as *Idan akwai zafi, za mu je wurin shan ruwa. as if the future construction with za were already fully productive.

Correction: The productive future construction with za is introduced in Chapter 14. For this chapter's idan sentences, build the result clause using continuative statements, existence/possession statements, or commands you have already mastered, such as Idan akwai zafi, muna shan ruwa da yawa. One formulaic future example appears in this chapter for recognition only and should not be used as a model for new sentences yet.

Tambayoyin fahimta na tattaunawa (Dialogue comprehension)

  1. According to Hauwa, it is very hot in Kano during the day and a little cold at night; there is no rain at this time.
  2. According to Sadiq, it rains in Niamey almost every day and it is very humid.
  3. Hauwa advises Sadiq not to bring a heavy gown because it is hot in Kano; he should wear a light gown.
  4. Idan akwai ƙura, mu zauna a gida da safe.
  5. Example: Idan akwai zafi a Kano, kada ka kawo riga mai nauyi. Condition: it is hot in Kano. Result: do not bring a heavy gown.

Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

Kula na A: Hot, no rain, a little wind in the evening; recommended to wear a cap for the sun.

Kula na B: (1) Yes, it rains every day at this time. (2) Yes, very humid. (3) An umbrella.

Kula na C: (a) F — it is not very hot for the second speaker. (b) T. (c) F — the first speaker will bring a light gown, not a heavy one.

Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

  1. It is very hot in northern Nigeria, especially Kano and Katsina, with no rain during the day but a chance of clouds in the evening; people going out are advised to wear light clothing and drink plenty of water.
  2. The southern regions have strong rain and high humidity, unlike the dry, hot north.
  3. It is the rainy season (damina) in Kano; it rains almost every day, especially in the evening.
  4. The writer suggests November because the weather is good and there is not much rain at that time.
  5. Ana ruwan sama mai ƙarfi (Rubutu na 1) — describes an ongoing rain event. Ana ruwan sama kusan kowace rana (Rubutu na 2) — describes the recurring rain event of the rainy season.
  6. Akwai zafi sosai, akwai damar samun gajimare, akwai damshi sosai (Rubutu na 1) — presence of heat, possibility of clouds, presence of humidity. Akwai ƙwari da yawa (Rubutu na 2) — presence of insects as a result of the rain.

Looking Ahead

Abin da ka/ki koya yanzu — What you know now

You can now describe weather conditions using ana, akwai, and babu; evaluate the weather overall with yana da kyau and yana da muni; ask about conditions in another place; name the rainy and dry seasons accurately for the climate of the Hausa-speaking heartland; and link a weather condition to a practical result using your first real conditional sentences with idan. You have also continued to recycle existence and possession structures from Chapter 6, location vocabulary from Chapter 11, and clothing vocabulary from Chapter 9 — proof that each new chapter builds directly on what came before rather than starting over.

Babi na 13: Lafiya da Jiki — Health and the Body

In Chapter 13, you move from weather to the body and health. You will learn to name common body parts, describe simple symptoms, and ask for assistance. The chapter introduces two new practical tools: ability with iya (to express what someone can or cannot do) and necessity with dole (to express what someone must do). You will also take your first steps with indirect-object frames — saying that something happens to or for someone. The genitive linkers from Chapter 4 return as you talk about "my head," "her hand," and similar body-possession phrases, and the connectors don haka and idan from this chapter will continue to be useful as you explain symptoms and request help.

Rayuwa a Cikin Al'umma

Life in the Community · Speak and Write Hausa: Foundations of Practical Communication
Unit III Consolidation This chapter integrates and reviews all language from Chapters 11–14. No major new grammar is introduced.

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Navigate a community environment by asking for and giving directions, using imperatives, subjunctive commands, and negative commands from Chapter 11.
  2. Describe weather conditions and make conditional plans using idan constructions from Chapter 12.
  3. Explain a basic health need, state a symptom, and request assistance using ability, necessity, and body-reference language from Chapter 13.
  4. Discuss a work or school responsibility and state a future task using za and purpose expressions from Chapter 14.
  5. Combine all four communication areas into a single connected interaction, moving from one topic to the next naturally.
  6. Produce a connected 120–150-word practical account or plan drawing on vocabulary and grammar from Chapters 11–14.
  7. Sustain a five- to seven-minute guided practical interaction in Hausa, using repair strategies when necessary.
  8. Recognize appropriate ways to interact respectfully in public-service settings across varied community contexts.

Communication Scenario

Think of a busy morning in a Hausa-speaking city or town: you need to travel across the community, stop at a clinic or pharmacy, and still attend a work meeting or class later in the day. Weather may affect your route. You may need to ask strangers for directions, explain your situation to a receptionist, or send a message to your supervisor. This chapter brings together the four communication areas you have developed in Unit III — getting around town, describing the weather, talking about health, and discussing work and school — so that you can handle this kind of morning in Hausa.

  1. In a real community interaction, you rarely stay within one topic. A conversation about health may turn into a conversation about where the nearest pharmacy is, which may require directions and a weather-related comment about whether it is safe to walk. Notice how the four Unit III domains connect naturally, and practice moving between them fluidly rather than treating each domain as a separate, closed topic.
  2. This is a consolidation chapter: no major new grammatical system is introduced. Your work here is to strengthen, recombine, and deepen control of what you already know. Use the self-check grid below and the Unit III Review section to identify which areas need the most attention before moving into Unit IV.

Consolidation Note — Vocabulary recycling in this chapter

This chapter does not add a large new vocabulary domain. Instead, it asks you to use Unit III vocabulary in combined, realistic contexts — for example, using a health word (ciwon kai) inside a future sentence (ba zan iya zuwa aiki ba saboda ciwon kai) or placing a direction-giving sequence inside an idan condition (idan akwai zafi, kada ku bi tafarkin nan). This kind of cross-domain combination is what practical fluency looks like: real situations rarely stay neatly inside one topic area.

Pronunciation and Boko

Tone in commands, future forms, and multi-clause sentences

This chapter brings together tone patterns from three distinct grammatical areas you have studied: the imperative and subjunctive forms from Chapter 11, the future forms from Chapter 14, and the conditional idan sentences from Chapter 12. When these appear in a single connected exchange, keeping track of tone requires practice. Focus on producing each short grammatical word — kada, za, idan, domin — with its own accurate tone and length, rather than letting them blur into surrounding words.

Sound Focus — Vowel length in high-frequency service vocabulary

Several words central to public-service interactions contain vowel-length contrasts that are not visible in ordinary Boko but affect intelligibility. Pay particular attention to alƙawari (appointment) and dole (necessary, must): both have vowel-length patterns that should be learned by listening and imitation rather than assumed from spelling. The hooked letter ƙ in alƙawari is a reminder that this word contains a sound requiring special attention — do not substitute plain k. Similarly, bukatar contains a hooked ƙ and must be written and pronounced with it: ina bukatar taimako, not *ina bukatar taimako with a plain k.

Use this chapter's listening and speaking activities to consolidate your pronunciation of these forms in connected speech, not just in isolation.

Multi-clause phrasing and pausing

A connected five- to seven-minute interaction requires linking clauses naturally. Practice pausing at clause boundaries — after an idan condition, after a directional sequence, after stating a symptom before adding a request. Pausing purposefully at these points is not a sign of weakness; it is a feature of natural spoken Hausa. Speakers do not rush every clause together without breath or pause. Build the habit of pausing briefly at each clause boundary as you speak, especially in longer turns.

Dictation of directions and appointments

As part of the writing-system work in this chapter, practice writing a short set of spoken directions or an appointment confirmation from dictation. Your instructor or partner reads at a natural pace; you write in ordinary Boko without tone or vowel-length marks. Check your hooked letters, apostrophes, word spacing, and capitalization of place names carefully after each item.

Grammar Consolidation

No major new grammatical system is introduced in this chapter. The sections below review and integrate the four principal grammar areas of Unit III. For each area, a brief summary is followed by a set of combined-use practice exercises.

15.1 Commands and directions (Chapter 11 review)

Purpose: give clear, appropriate instructions for getting around using the right command form for each context.

Recall the three command patterns available to you:

PatternUseExample
Affirmative singular imperativeOne person, affirmativeTafi kai tsaye. (Go straight.)
Subjunctive-based plural / respectful commandGroup, or polite address to one personKu juyar da dama. (Turn right. — group/respectful)
Negative command with kadaAffirmative or group, negativeKada ka ƙetare a nan. (Don't cross here.)

Combined direction sequences should also include location vocabulary (kusa da, a gaban, bayan, a ɓangaren hagu/dama) and motion verbs (tafi, dawo, shiga, fita) reviewed from Chapter 11.

  • Fita daga nan, tafi kai tsaye, sannan ka juyar da hagu kusa da asibitin.Go out from here, go straight, then turn left near the hospital.
  • Kada ku shiga wannan titi — yana a rufe a yau.Don't enter this street — it is closed today.
  • Ku ƙetare gada, sannan ku juyar da dama a fitila ta biyu.Cross the bridge, then turn right at the second traffic light.

15.2 Weather, existence, and conditions (Chapter 12 review)

Purpose: describe conditions accurately and link them to practical decisions using idan.

The three-way distinction in weather structures remains central: ana for ongoing events such as rain, akwai for the presence of a condition, and babu for the absence of one. Combined with idan, these structures generate practical conditional plans.

  • Ana ruwan sama, saboda haka ina ɗauke da laima.It is raining, so I am carrying an umbrella.
  • Idan akwai hadari, dole ne mu zama a gida.If there is a storm, we must stay home. (combines Chapter 12 condition + Chapter 13 necessity)
  • Yanayi yana da kyau a yau, don haka zan iya tafiya da ƙafa.The weather is good today, so I will be able to travel on foot. (combines Chapter 12 + Chapter 13 ability + Chapter 14 future)
Language Focus — Combining idan, dole, iya, and za in one sentence

By this point in the course, you have four tools for expressing practical necessity, possibility, and plans: idan for real conditions, dole for necessity, iya for ability, and za for future intentions. These can work together within a single exchange, but each must still be produced with the correct subject-aspect form and word order. A common error is to build a perfectly correct idan clause and then lose the thread of the result clause. Practice writing out a complete idan sentence before speaking it, until the shape of both clauses feels automatic.

15.3 Health, ability, and necessity (Chapter 13 review)

Purpose: explain a health situation clearly enough to get appropriate assistance.

The productive health-communication framework from Chapter 13 consists of three layers that often appear together in a single exchange: a symptom statement, an ability or necessity statement, and a request for help.

  • Ina da ciwon kai sosai. Ba na iya aiki a yau.I have a bad headache. I cannot work today.
  • Tana da zazzaɓi. Dole ne ta je asibitin.She has a fever. She must go to the hospital.
  • Ina bukatar magani. Ina iya je kantin magani kusa da nan?I need medicine. Can I go to the pharmacy nearby? (self-directed ability question)
  • Kafin alƙawari nawa, dole ne in yi jiran kaɗan.Before my appointment, I must wait a little. (combines Chapter 13 necessity + Chapter 15 appointment vocabulary)

Reminder on dole ne: Dole ne is followed by a subject form in the subjunctive. This pattern was introduced formulaically in Chapter 13 and is practiced here in consolidation. Do not attach dole ne directly to a continuative form as though it were a modifier; it requires the subjunctive form of the verb.

15.4 Future plans and purpose (Chapter 14 review)

Purpose: discuss upcoming responsibilities and explain the purpose of an action.

Recall that future meaning is built with za and the appropriate subject form. The first-person singular zan and third-person masculine singular zai are the most frequently used forms and must be produced accurately in connected speech. Negative future uses a full discontinuous ba … ba pattern.

  • Zan je asibitin yau, sannan zan koma makaranta gobe.I will go to the hospital today, then I will return to school tomorrow.
  • Ba zan iya zuwa taro ba domin ina rashin lafiya.I will not be able to attend the meeting because I am ill. (combines Chapter 14 negative future + Chapter 13 health)
  • Muna zuwa ofis ɗin nan domin mu yi alƙawarin ganawa.We are going to that office in order to make an appointment.
  • Zai aika mana da sakonnin rubutu domin ya sanar da mu lokacin alƙawarin.He will send us a text message in order to let us know the appointment time. (recognition-only — complex indirect object; focus on the future and purpose structure)
Language Building Block — The Community Problem-Solving Frame
situation statement + idan / dole / iya + action + domin + purpose
Akwai ciwon kai. Dole ne in je kantin magani domin in sayi magani.
I have a headache. I must go to the pharmacy in order to buy medicine.
Ana ruwan sama, saboda haka ba zan iya tafiya da ƙafa ba. Zan ɗauki mota.
It is raining, so I will not be able to travel on foot. I will take a car.

This two-step frame — state the situation, then state what must or will happen and why — is the practical backbone of Unit III communication. Practise combining parts of this frame until moving from one unit's language to another feels natural.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Musa yana son je asibitin da ke cikin gari, amma bai san hanya ba. Yana tambayar Hajiya Rabi, dattijuwa da take zaune kusa da mashigin gida. Bayan wannan, Musa ya isa asibitin kuma yana magana da ma'aikaciyar reception.

(Setting: Musa wants to go to the hospital in town but does not know the way. He asks Hajiya Rabi, an older woman who lives near the entrance to his compound. After this, Musa arrives at the hospital and speaks with the receptionist.)

Part 1 — A ɗakin gidan (At the compound entrance)

MUSA Sannu, Hajiya. Ina lafia?Hello, Hajiya. How are you?
HAJIYA RABI Lafiya lau, Musa. Kai fa?Very well, Musa. And you?
MUSA Ni kuwa ina da ciwon kai da zazzaɓi kaɗan tun jiya. Dole ne in je asibitin yau. Ki iya gaya mani hanya, Hajiya?As for me, I have had a headache and a slight fever since yesterday. I must go to the hospital today. Can you tell me the way, Hajiya?
HAJIYA RABI Tabbas. Ka fita daga nan, ka tafi kai tsaye ta titi mai faɗi har ka isa wucin gadi. A can, ka juyar da hagu. Asibitin yana a gefen dama, kusa da makarantar firamare.Of course. Go out from here, go straight along the main road until you reach the junction. There, turn left. The hospital is on the right side, near the primary school.
MUSA Na gode sosai, Hajiya. Amma akwai iska mai ƙarfi a yau — yana da muni kaɗan. Shin dole ne in ɗauki mota?Thank you very much, Hajiya. But there is a strong wind today — it is a little unpleasant. Should I take a vehicle?
HAJIYA RABI Ba ruwan sama ba ne — iska kawai. Idan ka ji lafiya sosai, ka iya tafiya da ƙafa. Idan kana rauni, ka ɗauki achaba ko mota. Kada ka tsaya a rana da yawa, saboda zafin rana yana iya ƙara ciwon kai.It is not rain — just wind. If you feel well enough, you can walk. If you are weak, take a motorcycle taxi or a car. Don't stand in the sun for long, because the heat of the sun can make a headache worse.
MUSA Madalla. Zan tafi da ƙafa domin asibiti bai nisa ba. Sai da safe, Hajiya.Excellent. I will walk since the hospital is not far. Goodbye for now, Hajiya.
HAJIYA RABI Sai an jima. A sami lafiya da sauri.See you later. Get well soon.

Part 2 — A asibitin (At the hospital)

MUSA Sannu. Ina da alƙawari da likita. Sunana Musa Usman.Hello. I have an appointment with the doctor. My name is Musa Usman.
KWAƊAƊƊIYA Sannu, Musa. Na ga sunanka a jerin. Ku zauna, likita zai kira ka nan da ɗan lokaci.Hello, Musa. I see your name on the list. Please sit, the doctor will call you shortly.
MUSA Na gode. Shin zan iya sanar da likitan cewa ina da alƙawari domin cutar da ke kan kai da feverout?Thank you. Can I let the doctor know that I have an appointment for a headache and fever?
KWAƊAƊƊIYA Ee. Za mu rubuta matsalar a takardarka kafin ka shiga. Mene ne alamun ciwonka daidai?Yes. We will write the problem on your form before you go in. What exactly are your symptoms?
MUSA Ina da ciwon kai mai ƙarfi tun jiya da dare. Kuma akwai zazzaɓi kaɗan da safe. Ba na iya cin abinci da kyau.I have had a strong headache since last night. And there is a slight fever in the morning. I cannot eat well.
KWAƊAƊƊIYA Lafiya. Za mu sanar da likita. Sai a zauna da hankali.Fine. We will inform the doctor. Please wait patiently.

Vocabulary notes for the dialogue: tun jiya = since yesterday; titi mai faɗi = wide road, main road; wucin gadi = junction, crossroads; gefen dama = right side; rauni = weakness, feeling unwell; achaba = motorcycle taxi (colloquial; widely understood in northern Nigeria — a regional note); zafin rana = heat of the sun; ƙara = to add, to increase; bai nisa ba = it is not far (third-person masculine negative continuative of nisa, distance — recognition only); sai an jima = see you later (formulaic leave-taking); a sami lafiya = get well (formulaic; literally "may health be found"); jerin = list; ɗan lokaci = a short while; almura/takarda = form/paper; alamun ciwon = symptoms.

Reading

Rubutu na 1 — Sanarwar asibiti (A hospital notice)

Asibitin Tsakiyar Gari — Muhimman Bayanai

Masu alƙawari su isa asibitin awanni ɗaya kafin lokacin da aka tsara. Dole ne ku kawo takardun asusunka na lafiya. Idan kuna da ciwon ido ko ciwon kunnuwa, ku je ɗakin da yake a hagu na farko. Idan kuna da matsalar gaggawa, ku faɗa wa jami'in da ke zaune a ƙofar shiga. Kada ku kawo yara ƙanana a zauren jiran tare da marasa lafiya.

Words for recognition: masu alƙawari = those with appointments; aka tsara = was scheduled; asusun lafiya = health insurance/file; ciwon ido = eye problem; ciwon kunnuwa = ear problem; ɗakin da yake = the room that is (recognition-only relative); matsalar gaggawa = emergency; jami'i = officer, official; ƙofar shiga = entrance door; zauren jiran = waiting room; marasa lafiya = patients (literally "those without health").

Rubutu na 2 — Ranar da ba a mantawa da ita (A day to remember)

Safiyar Talata ta fara da wahala. Na tashi ne da ciwon kai da sanyi, kuma akwai iska mai ƙarfi a waje. Dole ne ni je asibitin domin alƙawarina da likita shi ne a karfe goma. Ban iya tafiya da ƙafa ba saboda rauni, don haka na ɗauki motar haya daga gida.

A titi, direba ya tambaye ni ina nufin. Na ce masa: "Asibiti ta tsakiya." Ya ce ba ya san hanyar daidai — saboda haka ni kaina na ba shi jagora: "Tafi kai tsaye ta babbar hanya, sannan ka juyar da dama a wucin gadi na uku. Asibiti tana a ɓangaren hagunka." Ya ce na gode, ya bi hanya.

Na isa asibitin da karfe tara da rabi. Na yi jiran kaɗan a zauren jiran. Likita ta ce dole ne in sha magani har kwanaki uku kuma ba zan iya zuwa aiki ba gobe. Na sanar da shugabana ta waya cewa ba zan iya iso ofis ba gobe domin rashin lafiya. Ya ce ya fahimci, ya gaya mani in huta da kyau.

Words for recognition: motar haya = taxi (hired vehicle); direba = driver; ina nufin = where I was heading (recognition only); ni kaina = I myself; jagora = guidance, directions; sha magani = to take medicine; kwanaki uku = three days; huta = to rest.

  4. Duba da jerin bincike. Check your draft against the editing checklist below before writing the final version.

Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

  • Each command uses the correct pattern: affirmative singular imperative, plural/respectful subjunctive, or negative with kada.
  • Each weather statement correctly uses ana, akwai, babu, or yana da kyau/muni — not a personal pronoun subject for weather.
  • Each dole ne statement is followed by a subjunctive subject form, not a continuative form.
  • Each future statement uses za plus the correct subject form; negative future uses full ba … ba.
  • Each purpose clause uses domin or an approved alternative and is followed by a grammatically appropriate verb form.
  • Connectors (don haka, saboda haka, amma, domin) are used to link ideas, not just to begin unrelated sentences.
  • Hooked letters (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ) and 'y are written correctly throughout, including in alƙawari, bukatar, ƙarfi, and any other words requiring them.
  • Place names and proper nouns are capitalized; sentences end with full stops.
  • The account reads as connected prose — ideas are linked, not just listed.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Respectful interaction in public services

Entering a public-service setting — a clinic, government office, bank, or pharmacy — in many Hausa-speaking communities typically calls for a greeting before stating your purpose. Jumping straight to a request without greeting can be perceived as abrupt or disrespectful, even when the setting is busy. The greeting need not be long: Sannu or a brief time-appropriate greeting followed by the request is often sufficient. In medical settings, where both the person seeking help and the staff member may be under pressure, a short respectful greeting can make the interaction noticeably smoother.

This is not a rigid rule that applies identically in every institution, city, country, or situation. Large urban hospitals, government offices, and private clinics each have their own pace and culture. What is consistent is the general principle that beginning with a greeting is socially expected and practically useful across a wide range of Hausa-speaking public-service contexts.

Culture in Context — Context-appropriate directness

The chapter's dialogue and reading texts model a level of directness appropriate for public-service and practical community interactions. Musa states his symptoms clearly to the receptionist, the narrator of Rubutu na 2 gives the driver precise directions, and characters ask directly for what they need. This level of directness is natural and appropriate in service contexts where clarity serves both the speaker and the person they are addressing.

The same directness would not always be appropriate with an elder, a high-status person, or in a more formal social context — in those cases, additional framing, respectful address forms, and a more gradual approach to the main point may be expected. The appropriate register depends on the relationship, the setting, and the community, not on a single universal rule about Hausa communication styles.

Regional Variation — Transport, health, school, and work settings

The vocabulary in this chapter reflects settings commonly found in northern Nigerian cities and towns, but the specifics of how public services are organized vary significantly: between Nigeria and Niger, between urban and rural areas, between public and private institutions, and between communities within the same city. Terms such as achaba (motorcycle taxi) are widely understood in northern Nigeria but may be less standard in other regions; terms for types of transport, types of healthcare provider, and institutional roles will differ. When you use Hausa in a real context, expect to encounter vocabulary and procedures that differ from what this textbook models, and be prepared to ask for clarification — kana nufin…? (do you mean…?) and don Allah, sake faɗa mani (please say that again) are practical tools in any setting.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using the continuative instead of the subjunctive after dole ne

Error: *Dole ne ina je asibitin. (using continuative ina after dole ne)

Correction: Dole ne in je asibitin. — Dole ne requires the subjunctive subject form (in, not ina). The subjunctive form for first-person singular is in; for second-person singular it is ka (masculine) or ki (feminine); for third-person masculine singular it is ya. Do not substitute the continuative forms you use for ongoing actions into this slot.

Common Mistake — Mixing up the singular imperative with the plural/respectful form

Error: Addressing one person respectfully with the singular affirmative imperative, or addressing a group with the singular form.

Correction: Use the plural/respectful subjunctive form (ku + verb) when addressing a group or when respect for one person is required. Use the singular affirmative imperative only for a direct command to one person in an informal or peer context. The negative command kada is followed by the appropriate subject form for the addressee: kada ka (singular masculine), kada ki (singular feminine), kada ku (plural or respectful).

Common Mistake — Omitting the second ba in a negative future

Error: *Ba zan iya zuwa taro. (omitting the final ba)

Correction: Ba zan iya zuwa taro ba. — The discontinuous ba … ba pattern is required for negation in future constructions. The second ba must appear at the end of the negated clause. This is a consistent reminder from Chapter 14 and from the general negation principle introduced in Chapter 8: Hausa negation is often a two-part construction, and omitting either part produces an incomplete sentence.

Common Mistake — Using purpose domin with the wrong verb form

Error: *Zan je asibitin domin ina sayen magani. (using continuative after purposive domin)

Correction: Zan je asibitin domin in sayi magani. — When domin introduces a purpose clause describing what the subject intends to do, the verb in the purpose clause typically uses the subjunctive form. The continuative is used for ongoing or regular activities, not for stating the intended purpose of a planned action. This pattern parallels the behavior of dole ne + subjunctive introduced in Chapter 13.

Looking Ahead

Abin da ka/ki koya a Sashe na 3 — What you have learned in Unit III

Over Chapters 11–15, you have built a practical toolkit for moving through a Hausa-speaking community. You can give and follow directions using three distinct command patterns; you can describe weather conditions accurately using the three-way ana/akwai/babu distinction; you can explain health situations and express necessity and ability; and you can state future plans and purposes using za and domin. You have also begun to combine these systems fluidly — a conditional weather sentence that leads to a health decision, a future plan that explains why you cannot go to work — and that combination is the foundation of practical A2 communication.

Sashe na 4 da Babi na 16: Jiya da Makon da Ya Gabata — Yesterday and Last Week

Unit IV begins a new chapter of the course. In Chapter 16, you will encounter the general completive subject-pronoun forms — the grammatical system Hausa uses to talk about events that have been completed. You have seen completive forms only in formulaic greetings until now; Chapter 16 makes them fully productive. You will learn to narrate what happened yesterday or last week, to ask questions about completed events using the relative completive form, and to say what did not happen using the negative completive construction. The connectors and time expressions you have built up across Units I–III will give you the tools to sequence completed events naturally. Everything you have learned about gender and plural agreement, object reference, and question formation will return as you work with this important new aspect system.

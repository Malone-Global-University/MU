Our First Conversations · Speak and Write Hausa: Foundations of Practical Communication
Unit I Consolidation Chapter — No major new grammar is introduced. This chapter integrates and assesses the language of Chapters 1–4.

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Sustain a two- to three-minute introductory conversation in Hausa, combining greetings, personal information, and questions.
  2. Introduce yourself and a third person using name, origin, language, and profession.
  3. Describe a family member using possessive linking and a personal quality.
  4. Identify classroom objects and ask for clarification using taught expressions.
  5. Use taught conversation-management expressions — asking for repetition, signaling understanding, and checking comprehension.
  6. Select the correct identification particle (ne or ce) in all taught contexts, including plural and negative identification.
  7. Apply masculine, feminine, and plural agreement in pronouns, ne/ce, demonstratives, and genitive linkers.
  8. Produce a 60–80-word personal profile in accurate standard Boko.
  9. Read a short personal message or profile and answer questions about it in Hausa.
  10. Count from 1 to 100 and use numbers in personal-information contexts such as age and class size.

Communication Scenario

The language program you joined in Chapter 3 has now completed its first week. On Friday afternoon, participants gather to share what they have learned, make new connections, and exchange contact details before the weekend. You want to manage a full conversation from beginning to end — greeting someone you have met before, asking about their family, introducing a mutual acquaintance, asking for clarification when something is unclear, and closing the exchange politely. To do this, you do not need any new grammar. You need to bring together everything from Chapters 1 through 4 and use it in a flowing, natural exchange.

  1. A real conversation does not move through one grammar point at a time. It moves back and forth between topics. As you work through this chapter, notice how the different sentence patterns from Chapters 1–4 fit together: a greeting leads to an identity exchange, which leads to an origin question, which leads to a family comment, which prompts a question about age or relationship. Practice connecting these moves fluently, not just performing them as isolated exercises.
  2. Conversation management is itself a skill. In this chapter you will practice the expressions that help you keep a conversation going when you do not catch something or need more time to respond — Mè ya faru?, Ka/ki maimaita, don Allah, Ban fahimta ba, and similar phrases. Using these well is a sign of communicative competence, not of weakness.

Language Note — Ban fahimta ba: another ba … ba construction

The expression Ban fahimta ba (I did not understand) uses the discontinuous ba … ba pattern you learned in Chapter 3 for negative identification. Here ba appears before the subject-plus-verb unit and again at the end. The form ban is a contraction of ba + first-person singular — a unit whose full analysis belongs in Chapter 16 when completive forms are taught. Learn Ban fahimta ba as a single formulaic expression for now. If you want to say "I don't understand" in an ongoing sense, Ban fahimci … ba (I don't understand …) — with a specific language or word following — is the natural pattern to use.

Pronunciation and Boko Review

Irin kira — Greeting rhythm and response timing

Earlier chapters introduced the individual sounds of Hausa — the five vowel qualities, tone awareness, vowel-length awareness, and Hausa-specific consonants. Chapter 5 focuses on something different: how sound works across a whole greeting exchange. In Hausa, greetings are not single sentences exchanged once. They are sequences with expected structure, rhythm, and timing.

A common greeting exchange in Hausa has the following shape: an opening salutation → an expected response → a question about well-being → an expected response → (optionally) further well-being questions → an exchange close before the main topic begins. Each step in this sequence has a characteristic rhythm. The response word is often short and rises slightly in pitch. The question phrase has a particular falling-then-rising movement. These patterns carry social meaning — they signal that you are engaging properly, that you respect the other person, and that you know the conventions of Hausa interaction.

When practicing the model dialogue and speaking tasks in this chapter, pay attention not only to the individual words but to the rhythm of the whole exchange: how quickly the response comes, how the pitch moves across the greeting phrase, and where the natural pauses fall.

Pronunciation Practice — Dictation in ordinary unmarked Boko

This chapter includes a dictation exercise that tests your ability to write accurate standard Boko from spoken input. When writing from dictation, apply everything you have learned about Hausa orthography:

  • Write every hooked letter correctly: ɓ, ɗ, ƙ. Do not substitute the plain letter.
  • Write 'y with its apostrophe where required — never as plain y.
  • Write sh and ts as single units — not as two separate letters that happen to appear next to each other.
  • Do not add tone marks or long-vowel marks to ordinary Boko writing. These marks are not part of standard spelling.
  • Capitalize names of people, languages, countries, and cities.
  • End each sentence with a full stop.

If a word contains ɗ, your only options are to write it with the hook or to write it incorrectly. There is no in-between. The same applies to ɓ, ƙ, and 'y. Practise writing these characters until they come naturally.

Pronunciation Practice — Tone in short grammatical words

Short words carry tone as reliably as long words — and getting them right matters for intelligibility. Focus on three sets of short words you have now used many times:

The independent pronouns: ni, kai, ke, shi, ita, mu, ku, su. Each of these has a specific tone that is part of its identity as a word. Practice them in identification sentences: Ni malami ne. Shi ɗalibi ne. Ita malama ce. Your instructor or audio model will demonstrate the tone of each pronoun.

The identification particles: ne and ce. These short words have characteristic tones that may shift slightly depending on what precedes them. For now, focus on hearing the difference between an identification sentence (Malami ne) and a negative identification sentence (Ba malami ba ne) — the rhythm and tone of the whole pattern changes.

The genitive linkers: -n and -r. These attach to the preceding noun and affect the rhythm of the noun phrase. Practice by saying a few possession phrases aloud at natural speed: sunan ɗalibi (student's name); mahaifiyar malama (teacher's mother).

Grammar Consolidation

No new grammatical system is introduced in Chapter 5. This section brings together the four main systems from Chapters 1–4 and shows how they work together in a single sustained exchange. Read each summary carefully — if anything feels unclear, return to the original chapter for fuller explanation before continuing.

5.1 The identification system: ne/ce and gender agreement

Purpose: identify people and objects accurately in a sustained exchange.

The particle ne is used with masculine singular nouns and with plurals. The particle ce is used with feminine singular nouns. The particle follows the noun or pronoun it identifies. Negative identification surrounds the noun with ba … ba ne or ba … ba ce.

  • Malami ne.He is a teacher. (masculine → ne)
  • Malama ce.She is a teacher. (feminine → ce)
  • Malamai ne.They are teachers. (plural → ne)
  • Ba malami ba ne.He is not a teacher.
  • Ba malama ba ce.She is not a teacher.
  • Wannan littafi ne.This is a book. (masculine → ne)
  • Wannan alƙalami ne ko fensir ne?Is this a pen or a pencil?
Language Building Block — The Identification System
noun or pronoun + ne (m./pl.) / ce (f.)
Shi ɗalibi ne.
He is a student.
Ita ɗaliba ce.
She is a student.
Su ɗalibai ne.
They are students.
Ba ɗalibi ba ne.
He is not a student.

The choice of ne or ce is controlled by the grammatical gender of the noun, not by the biological sex of the person or the last letter of the noun. When the noun does not change form for gender (such as likita or direba), the particle is the main gender signal.

5.2 The demonstrative system

Purpose: point out and identify specific people and objects.

Hausa demonstratives agree with the gender and number of the noun they refer to. You have learned near and far demonstratives across Chapters 1 and 2. Below is a reference summary.

Distance Masculine singular Feminine singular Plural
Near (this/these)wannanwannanwaɗannan
Far (that/those)wancanwaccanwaɗancan

Reminder: The near demonstrative wannan has the same form for masculine and feminine singular — gender is signaled by the ne/ce particle that follows the noun, not by the demonstrative itself. The far demonstrative distinguishes masculine (wancan) from feminine (waccan) in the singular.

Purpose: give personal information in sustained exchanges.

The following patterns were introduced in Chapter 3 as complete formulaic expressions. You have now had four chapters to practice them. Use them with confidence. The full continuative system that underlies ina, yana, tana is taught in Chapter 7.

The origin question takes the formulaic relative-continuative form: Daga ina kake zuwa? (to a male) / Daga ina kike zuwa? (to a female). These forms are taught as fixed patterns here. Their internal grammar is analyzed fully in Chapters 7 and 16.

5.4 The genitive linker system

Purpose: express possession and family relationships accurately.

The genitive linker agrees with the grammatical gender and number of the possessed noun — not with the possessor. Use -n with a masculine singular or plural possessed noun; use -r with a feminine singular possessed noun. The linker is attached to the end of the possessed noun, not to the possessor.

  • ɗan AminuAminu's son (ɗa + n → ɗan; masculine noun → -n)
  • 'yar HauwaHauwa's daughter ('ya + r → 'yar; feminine noun → -r)
  • sunan malamithe teacher's name (suna + n → sunan; masculine noun → -n)
  • mahaifiyar BelloBello's mother (mahaifiya + r → mahaifiyar; feminine noun → -r)
  • yaranmuour children (yara + n → yaran; + mu → yaranmu)
  • makarantar KanoKano school / a school in Kano (makaranta + r → makarantar; feminine noun → -r)
Language Focus — The linker agrees with the possessed noun, not the possessor

The single most important rule for genitive linkers in Hausa is this: the linker is determined by the gender of the noun being possessed, not by the gender of the person who owns it. So whether a female or a male person possesses something, the linker is still determined by the thing possessed.

Compare: sunan Fatima (Fatima's name) uses -n because suna (name) is masculine — even though Fatima is female. And mahaifiyar Bello (Bello's mother) uses -r because mahaifiya (mother) is feminine — even though Bello is male.

This is one of the patterns that most predictably causes errors for English-speaking learners, because English has no equivalent system. A useful strategy: before choosing the linker, identify the possessed noun and check its gender. Then apply -n for masculine/plural, -r for feminine.

5.5 Adjectives and mai quality expressions — review

Purpose: describe people and family members using quality expressions.

Chapter 4 introduced two ways to describe qualities in Hausa: true adjectives (which agree in gender and number with the noun they modify) and mai constructions (which use mai plus a noun to express a quality). Both are reviewed briefly here.

  • Yaro mai tsayi ne.The boy is tall. (mai + tsayi — noun meaning height/tallness)
  • Yarinya mai kyau ce.The girl is beautiful / good-looking.
  • Babba ne.He/it is big; He is older.
  • Ƙarama ce.She/it is small; She is younger.
  • Abokin da ya yi kyau.A good friend. (recognition only — relative form; for reference only)

Reminder from Chapter 4: Many English adjective meanings in Hausa are expressed through a mai construction or through a noun-based description rather than through a simple adjective. Do not invent an adjective in Hausa if what you have learned is a mai construction — use the construction you know.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Ƙarshen mako a shirye-shiryen harshe a Kano. Bello da Amina sun sadu da Sadiya — ɗaliba sabuwa wacce ta fito daga Jihar Sokoto. Suna yin tattaunawa game da iyalinta da asalinta kafin darasi na karshe ya fara.

(Setting: End-of-week gathering at the Kano language program. Bello and Amina meet Sadiya — a new student from Sokoto State. They make conversation about her family and background before the final session begins.)

BELLO Sannu da zuwa! Sunanka mene ne?Welcome! What is your name?
SADIYA Na gode. Sunana Sadiya ce. Na fito daga Jihar Sokoto — amma ina zaune a Kano yanzu. Ku kuwa?Thank you. My name is Sadiya. I am from Sokoto State — but I am living in Kano now. And you two?
AMINA Ni Amina ce. Na fito daga Niamey, a Nijar. Shi kuwa abokinmu Bello ne — dan Kano ne.I am Amina. I am from Niamey, in Niger. And he is our friend Bello — he is from Kano.
SADIYA Sannu, Amina! Sannu, Bello! Kina magana da Hausa da kyau, Amina.Hello, Amina! Hello, Bello! You speak Hausa well, Amina.
AMINA Na gode! Ina koyo. Harshen farkonmu Faransanci ne, amma ina son Hausa sosai.Thank you! I am learning. Our first language is French, but I really like Hausa.
BELLO (yana juya ga Sadiya) Kin zo kaɗai, ko kina da iyali a nan?(turning to Sadiya) Did you come alone, or do you have family here?
SADIYA Akwai yarona ɗaya a Kano — ɗana. Sunansa Usman ne. Ɗalibi ne a Jami'ar Bayero. Shi mai himma ne.I have one of my children in Kano — my son. His name is Usman. He is a student at Bayero University. He is hardworking / diligent.
AMINA Madalla! Nawa ne shekarunsa?Excellent! How old is he?
SADIYA Yana da shekaru ashirin da biyu. Ba yaro ba ne yanzu — matashi ne!He is twenty-two years old. He is not a boy any more — he is a young man!
BELLO Jami'ar Bayero! Ni ma, na yi karatu a can. Ɗaya daga cikin makarantun da suka fi kyau a Najeriya.Bayero University! I also studied there. It's one of the finest schools in Nigeria.
SADIYA To, lafiya. Yi haƙuri — ina son in tambayi wani abu. Me ke nufin "matashi"? Ban fahimta ba a farko.All right, fine. Excuse me — I want to ask something. What does "matashi" mean? I didn't understand it at first.
AMINA Matashi? Wato, mutumin da ba ya da shekarun yaro amma ba ya da shekarun babba kuma. Matashi ne — young man.Matashi? That is, a person who is no longer a child but not yet fully an adult. A matashi — a young man.
SADIYA Ee, na gane yanzu. Na gode, Amina!Yes, I understand now. Thank you, Amina!

Vocabulary notes: dan Kano = person from Kano / Kano native; ina son = I like / I love / I want (recognition — desire construction in Chapter 8); sosai = very much, a lot; kaɗai = alone; akwai yarona ɗaya = I have one child (literally "there is one of my children" — akwai + possession pattern); mai himma = hardworking, diligent; matashi = young man (m.), matashiya (f.); na yi karatu a can = I studied there (recognition only — completive form in Chapter 16); in tambayi = to ask / that I ask (recognition — subjunctive in Chapter 11); wani abu = something.

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  2. What does Amina say about her own language background? Find the relevant sentences in the dialogue.
  3. What is the name of Sadiya's son, what is he studying, and how old is he? Give all three facts in Hausa.
  4. Find the sentence where Sadiya says she didn't understand a word. What word didn't she understand, and how does Amina explain it?
  5. Find one example of a genitive linker in the dialogue. Write the phrase, identify the linker (-n or -r), and state why that linker was chosen.
  6. Find all examples of ne/ce used for identification in the dialogue. List them and note whether each uses ne or ce and why.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Greetings as social openings

You have now learned several Hausa greetings spread across four chapters. Chapter 5 is a good moment to step back and reflect on what greetings do in Hausa social interaction more broadly. In many Hausa-speaking communities, the greeting sequence at the beginning of an encounter is not a preliminary to the "real" conversation — it is a socially significant act in itself. Completing the greeting properly — giving the right salutation, receiving the right response, asking about well-being, and responding appropriately — signals that you are a respectful, socially aware interlocutor. Rushing through the greeting or skipping it entirely to get to the transactional purpose of a visit would often be noticed and could affect the tone of what follows.

This does not mean every Hausa greeting exchange lasts a long time. In a brief street encounter, the exchange may be just a word or two. In a more formal or socially significant meeting — visiting someone's home, making a request of an elder, or meeting someone for the first time in a professional context — the greeting may be longer and more elaborate. The appropriate length and form depends on the relationship, setting, age difference, social status, and community convention. As a learner, paying attention to how a greeting exchange unfolds — and following the lead of the more experienced speaker — is a practical and respectful strategy.

Culture in Context — Respectful address and the plural you

You have learned that Hausa has both a singular and a plural second-person pronoun: kai (male singular) / ke (female singular) versus ku (plural, or respectful singular). Across Chapters 1–5, you have seen the plural address form ku used in classroom commands (Ku zauna — sit down, everyone) and in the polite address form (Ku + subjunctive) for groups. The broader principle — that using plural forms can signal respect toward a single individual — is a feature of Hausa social practice that will become more prominent as you advance.

For now, the most important practical awareness is this: when speaking to someone significantly older than you, to a person of higher social status, or in a formal context, using the plural pronoun and plural command form for one person is often more appropriate than using the singular. This is not a fixed grammatical rule with no exceptions — it is a social convention that varies by community, relationship, and context. If in doubt, observe the conventions of the people around you and follow their lead.

Regional Variation — Names and naming practices

The names used in this textbook come from a variety of communities across the Hausa-speaking world. Several names in the dialogues and readings have Arabic origins that reflect the long history of Islamic scholarship and trade across northern West Africa — names like Maryam, Usman, Ibrahim, Fatima, and Zainab are common in both Nigerian and Nigerien Hausa-speaking communities. Other names are distinctively Hausa — Bello, Hauwa, Sadiya, Danladi, Hadiza. Others reflect the multilingual reality of the region — a name like Kwame signals Ghanaian origin, Chukwuemeka signals Igbo origin, and Ngozi is also an Igbo name.

Naming practices vary across communities and are influenced by religion, ethnic tradition, family heritage, and personal preference. Do not assume that a name identifies a person's religion or ethnic background with certainty. When you are introduced to a Hausa speaker, the polite approach is to use the name or title they offer, not to infer identity from its origin.

Common Mistakes — Unit I Review

This section reviews the most important patterns of error from Chapters 1–4, with emphasis on mistakes that are still common at this stage of the course.

Common Mistake — Choosing ne/ce based on the person's sex rather than the noun's gender

Error: Likita ne for a female doctor because "doctors sound masculine."

Correction: The particle must agree with the grammatical gender of the noun being used, not with assumptions about the referent. Likita is the same form for masculine and feminine, so the particle is your primary gender signal. A female doctor → Likita ce; a male doctor → Likita ne. Check the noun's gender first, then select the particle.

Common Mistake — Choosing the linker based on the possessor's gender

Error: mahaifir Ibrahim (Ibrahim's mother, trying to use -r because the mother is female)

Correction: Mahaifiyar Ibrahim. The linker agrees with the possessed noun. Mahaifiya (mother) is a feminine noun, so it always takes -r — regardless of who the possessor is. If you are talking about Ibrahim's mother, Bello's mother, or anyone's mother, the noun mahaifiya always carries the feminine linker -r. Check the noun, not the possessor.

Common Mistake — Writing plain letters instead of hooked letters

Error: Writing daliba instead of ɗaliba; writing kasa instead of ƙasa; writing yan instead of 'yan.

Correction: These are not optional decorations — they are different letters representing different sounds. ɗ, ƙ, ɓ, and 'y are each distinct Hausa consonants. Substituting the plain letter changes the word or produces a misspelling. In the dictation exercise and in all your writing tasks, treat these characters as non-negotiable. If your keyboard or text system makes them difficult to type, find a reliable method — character map, mobile keyboard input, or copy-paste from a reference sheet — and use it consistently.

Common Mistake — Using ina/yana/tana for completed events

Error: Ina fito daga Kano (intending "I came from Kano" as a completed trip)

Correction: The formulaic expression Na fito daga Kano is the pattern for stating where you are from. It uses Na — a completive subject form — not the continuative Ina. The continuative forms ina / yana / tana describe ongoing or general activities (speaking languages, living somewhere, happening now). Origin is expressed with Na / Ya / Ta fito daga… as a fixed expression, even though its full grammatical analysis belongs in Chapter 16. Use the pattern you have learned, not a literal substitution of ina.

Looking Ahead

Abin da ka/ki koya a Sashe na 1 — What you know after Unit I

You have completed Unit I. You can greet people appropriately, introduce yourself and others, exchange personal and family information, identify people and objects with correct gender agreement, express possession using the genitive linker, describe people with quality expressions, count to 100, and ask for clarification when you need it. You have learned to write standard Boko, including all the special characters of Hausa, and you have begun to develop the spoken fluency and conversation management skills of a real Hausa communicator. Everything in Unit I — every greeting, every linker, every identification sentence — remains active from here forward. Do not treat it as finished material.

Babi na 6: A Gida — Around the Home

Unit II opens with the home. Chapter 6 introduces the vocabulary of rooms, furniture, and household objects, and with it two new grammatical tools that significantly expand your ability to say what exists and what people have. Akwai (there is / there are) and babu (there is no / there are no) will let you describe what is present or absent in a space. And you will meet the Hausa way of expressing possession — not through an English-style verb meaning "have," but through a construction using da. These are not mere vocabulary additions. They are new structural tools that will remain active throughout the rest of the course, in every domain from food to health to market transactions. Alongside them, you will revisit gender and plural agreement, demonstratives, and the genitive linker in the new context of household description.

Al'adu da Bukukuwa

Culture and Traditions · Speak and Write Hausa: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe common celebrations and cultural events in Hausa-speaking communities, including their timing, activities, and significance.
  2. Compare two people, objects, or experiences using the Hausa comparison construction with fi and explain why this structure differs from English adjective comparisons.
  3. Express equality between two things using the appropriate Hausa equality construction.
  4. Express difference or contrast using selected Hausa difference constructions.
  5. Use frequency adverbs and expressions to describe how often something happens, from koyaushe (always) to ba … ba negative frames for "never."
  6. Expand your use of mai quality constructions and recognize true adjectives with their masculine, feminine, and plural agreement forms in the contexts taught.
  7. Discuss cultural practices respectfully, using qualifying language such as galibi (usually/mostly) and a wasu lokuta (sometimes/in some cases).
  8. Write a respectful 150–180-word comparison of two events or customs in standard Boko.

Communication Scenario

You have recently attended a large family celebration in a Hausa-speaking community and want to describe it to a friend from a different background. Your friend is curious and asks follow-up questions: How often do such events happen? How does this celebration compare with similar events they know? What makes it distinctive? This chapter gives you the language to answer those questions accurately, respectfully, and with appropriate nuance.

  1. Chapters 16 and 17 built your tools for narrating the past and planning the future. Chapter 18 gives you a third essential communicative tool: comparison and evaluation. Comparing is one of the most natural things conversation does — we compare places, foods, events, people, and experiences constantly. In Hausa, comparison uses a verb meaning "surpass" (fi) rather than English-style adjective endings (-er, more …). This is a fundamental structural difference, and this chapter teaches you to use it naturally rather than translating from English.
  2. Alongside comparison, this chapter expands two systems you have already met in earlier chapters: frequency expressions (introduced in Chapter 7 with daily routines) and quality constructions using mai (introduced in Chapter 4). Celebrations and cultural discussion are a natural domain for both — people talk about how often events occur and what qualities they prize in a celebration, a tradition, or a person.
  3. The cultural content of this chapter presents celebrations and traditions from Hausa-speaking communities in Nigeria and Niger as starting points for respectful inquiry and comparison. Culture is dynamic and internally varied. The chapter models the kind of qualified, specific language — "in many communities," "some families," "it varies" — that makes cultural discussion accurate rather than stereotyped.

Pronunciation and Boko

Tone and vowel length in comparison constructions

The comparison verb fi is a short, single-syllable word. In natural connected speech it runs closely with the subject form and quality word that surround it: Yā fi kyau (It is better / It surpasses in goodness) sounds like a single compact phrase, not three separate words. Listen carefully to how the tone of fi relates to what comes before and after it. The quality nouns used after fi — such as kyau, girma, yawa — have their own tones, and the phrase must be practiced as a unit. Your audio source or instructor is the required reference; do not infer tone from Boko spelling alone.

Sound Focus — True adjective agreement endings in connected speech

This chapter introduces three true adjectives with full masculine, feminine, and plural forms: babba / babba / manya; ƙarami / ƙarama / ƙanana; and sabo / sabuwa / sababbi. In connected speech, the feminine form ƙarama and the feminine form sabuwa end in a final -a that is long in some phonological environments and short in others — a distinction unmarked in ordinary Boko but significant for pronunciation. Practice these forms as complete phrases: liyafar babba (a big reception), al'adar sabuwa (a new tradition), bukukuwa manya (big celebrations). Listen to each as a connected phrase rather than as isolated words.

Sound Focus — Distinguishing ƙ in celebration vocabulary

Several important words in this chapter contain the hooked ƙ: ƙarama (small, f.), ƙanana (small, pl.), Ƙaramar Sallah (Eid al-Fitr), ƙoƙari (effort, if used), and baƙi (guests, pl. of bako). In each case, replacing ƙ with ordinary k is a spelling error and can alter meaning. Review the distinction between k and ƙ in these words before any dictation or writing task. When writing by hand or typing, verify that the correct Unicode character is used.

Reading cultural names and calendar terms aloud

This chapter contains several named events — Babban Sallah, Ƙaramar Sallah, suna, Kirsimeti — that learners may be encountering for the first time in Boko. Read these as Hausa words, not as approximations of their Arabic, English, or other source-language pronunciations. The vowels and consonants follow Hausa sound values. Your audio source should demonstrate each name in the context of a full sentence. Do not attempt to deduce tone from the source language.

Grammar Instruction

18.1 Comparison with fi: "A surpasses B in quality X"

Purpose: compare two people, objects, or experiences using the Hausa comparison verb fi.

English expresses comparison with adjective endings (-er) or with the word "more": "this celebration is bigger" or "this food is more delicious." Hausa comparison works differently: instead of modifying the adjective, Hausa uses a verb, fi, that means "to surpass" or "to exceed." The quality being compared is expressed as a noun, not as a modified adjective.

The basic comparison structure is:

Comparison with fi — Core pattern
Subject + completive subject form + fi + (second subject) + quality noun
Wannan biki yā fi wancàn girma.
This celebration is bigger than that one. (lit. "This celebration surpassed that one in greatness.")
When comparing A to B without mentioning B explicitly: Subject + completive subject form + fi + quality noun
Wannan biki yā fi kyau.
This celebration is better / more beautiful. (It surpasses in goodness.)

Study the following examples carefully. Notice that fi takes the completive subject form (yā, tā, mun, sun, etc.) in all standard comparison statements — it behaves like other verbs in the completive aspect in this construction.

  • Bikin aure yā fi suna girma.The wedding celebration is bigger than a naming ceremony.yā fi (3sg.m completive + fi) + suna (second subject) + girma (quality noun)
  • Liyafar ta fi dadi.The reception is more enjoyable / tastier.tā fi (3sg.f completive + fi) + dadi (quality noun: sweetness/pleasantness)
  • Waƙoƙin nan sun fi waƙoƙin can kyau.These songs are better than those songs.sun fi (3pl completive + fi) + waƙoƙin can (second subject) + kyau
  • Tufafin Sallah sun fi sababbi.The Eid clothes are newer.sun fi (3pl completive + fi) + sababbi (quality noun)
  • Wannan al'ada ta fi ta can tsufa.This custom is older than that one.tā fi (3sg.f) + ta can (that one, f.) + tsufa (oldness/age — quality noun)

Important: The quality word after fi is a noun in Hausa, not an adjective or adjective ending. Kyau = goodness/beauty; girma = greatness/size; dadi = sweetness/pleasantness; tsufa = oldness/age; yawa = numerousness/abundance. This is why the structure works differently from English "more beautiful" or "bigger." Flag for expert review: the tone patterns of fi constructions, particularly when a second subject is present, require specialist verification before audio production.

Language Focus — Why fi works the way it does

English speakers learning Hausa comparison often try to find an equivalent of "more" or "-er" to add to a word. That search leads to errors because Hausa encodes comparison in the verb, not in the quality word. When a Hausa speaker says yā fi kyau, they are not saying "it has more beauty" — they are saying "it surpassed in goodness." The completive subject form on fi is the same form you use for completed events: yā tafi (he went), yā fi kyau (it is better). This parallelism is useful: fi is a real Hausa verb, and it uses real Hausa verb grammar. Once you see that, comparison becomes a pattern rather than an exception.

One practical consequence: because fi is a verb, it agrees with its subject just as any Hausa verb does. A masculine singular subject takes yā fi; a feminine singular subject takes tā fi; a plural subject takes sun fi. Getting the subject agreement right is just as important in a comparison sentence as in any other Hausa sentence.

18.2 Negative comparison: "A is not as … as B"

Purpose: express that one thing does not surpass another in a given quality.

To express the idea that A is not better than, bigger than, or more [quality] than B, use the negative completive frame around fi:

  • Wannan biki bài fi wancàn girma ba.This celebration is not bigger than that one.bài fi … ba (negative completive 3sg.m wrapped around fi + quality)
  • Liyafar ban ta fi dadi ba.The reception is not more enjoyable (than expected / than before).ban tā fi dadi ba — negative completive 3sg.f; flag: confirm negative completive subject form for 3sg.f before audio
  • Waƙoƙin nan basu fi waƙoƙin can kyau ba.These songs are not better than those songs.

Flag for expert review: The negative completive subject forms used with fi (particularly 3sg.f ban tā fi vs. bà tā fi) require specialist verification. The forms in this chapter follow the general negative completive pattern established in Chapter 16; a specialist should confirm their accuracy in comparison contexts.

18.3 Equality: "A is the same as B" / "A is as … as B"

Purpose: express that two people, objects, or events are equal in a given respect.

Hausa uses several patterns to express equality. The most accessible for beginners are:

Pattern 1: daidai da — "equal to / the same as"

  • Bikin suna ya yi daidai da bikin aure a wajen baƙi.A naming ceremony is equal to a wedding in terms of guests.
  • Wannan kyautar ta yi daidai da waccan.This gift is the same (in value/size) as that one.tā yi daidai da (3sg.f completive + yi + daidai da)

Pattern 2: iri ɗaya ne/ce — "they are the same kind / identical type"

  • Al'adun biyu sun kasance iri ɗaya ne.The two customs are of the same type / are the same.
  • Tufafin biyu iri ɗaya ne.The two garments are the same.

Pedagogical note: Ya yi daidai da uses the completive of yi (to do/make) in an idiomatic construction meaning "to be equal to." This is introduced as a usable pattern; its internal grammar is not analyzed further at this stage. Flag for expert review: confirm that ya yi daidai da is the most natural and widely understood equality construction in Kano Hausa, or suggest a preferred alternative.

18.4 Difference and contrast: "A and B are different"

Purpose: express that two things are not the same, or to highlight a distinction.

  • Akwai bambanci tsakanin Babban Sallah da Ƙaramar Sallah.There is a difference between Eid al-Adha and Eid al-Fitr.akwai + bambanci + tsakanin … da … (between … and …)
  • Al'adun biyu sun bambanta.The two customs are different (from each other).sun bambanta — completive of bambanta (to differ, to be different)
  • Wannan al'ada ta bambanta da ta can.This custom is different from that one.
  • Su ne dabam — ba iri ɗaya ba ne.They are distinct — they are not the same kind.

The word tsakanin (between) is new in this chapter. It is followed by two nouns connected by da: tsakanin A da B (between A and B).

Pedagogical note on "never": Hausa has several ways to express "never," some of which involve taba (ever) inside a negative frame, and others which use specific aspect negations with frequency words. This chapter introduces the most common and widely recognized patterns. A Hausa-language specialist should verify the naturalness of these constructions before audio production.

18.6 Expanded mai quality constructions

Purpose: use mai constructions productively for a wider range of qualities, and understand when true adjectives are used instead.

You first met mai quality constructions in Chapter 4, where you learned that many English adjective meanings are expressed in Hausa through mai + a noun. This section expands that understanding in two directions: (1) more mai constructions for this chapter's cultural domain, and (2) the forms of true adjectives you need for comparison and description here.

The mai construction: mai is invariant — it does not change for masculine, feminine, or plural in the singular. In the plural, masu replaces mai.

Singular (m. or f.)maibiki mai girmaa big/important celebration (m.)
Singular (m. or f.)mailiyafa mai kyaua nice/beautiful reception (f.)
Pluralmasubukukuwa masu ban sha'awainteresting celebrations (pl.)
Pluralmasutufafi masu kyaunice clothes (pl.)

Flag for expert review: The rule that mai is invariant for singular gender and that masu is the plural form requires verification for the specific constructions in this chapter. Some authorities describe additional variation; a specialist should confirm the forms presented here against current Kano Standard Hausa.

True adjectives with masculine, feminine, and plural forms: The three adjectives below are the most important true adjectives in this chapter's cultural domain. They are genuinely common and high-frequency. Notice that each has three distinct forms that must be learned individually.

Masculine singular Feminine singular Plural (m./f.) English
babba babba manya big, great, elder
ƙarami ƙarama ƙanana small, little, young

Note on babba: The masculine and feminine singular forms of babba are identical (babba), but the plural manya is quite different and must be learned separately. The gender of the noun it describes determines the agreement, even though the adjective form does not change between masculine and feminine singular. Context and the noun's gender determine which agreement is intended. Flag for expert review: confirm the spelling and tone of manya and ƙanana in standard Kano Hausa.

Adjectives in Hausa typically follow the noun they describe and are connected to it by the appropriate genitive linker (-n for masculine singular or plural, -r for feminine singular), or they may occur as predicates. Both patterns are shown below:

  • liyafar babbathe big receptionf. noun with definite suffix + babba; note -r linker on liyafa + -r suffix merged in liyafar
  • bukukuwa manyabig celebrations (pl.)pl. noun + pl. adjective form
  • al'adar sabuwaa new customf. noun (al'ada) + f. adj. form (sabuwa)

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Bisa tana zaune da abokinta Ladi bayan sun dawo daga bikin suna. Suna tattauna al'adu da abubuwan da suka gani.

(Setting: Bisa is sitting with her friend Ladi after they have returned from a naming ceremony. They are discussing traditions and what they observed.)

LADI Bisa, na yi farin ciki sosai da bikin sunan yau. Yā fi bukukuwan da na yi a baya kyau, wallahi.Bisa, I enjoyed the naming ceremony today very much. It was better than the celebrations I attended before, truly.
BISA Gaskiyarki. Iyalin Alhaji Sani sun yi ƙoƙari sosai. Abincin ya fi na kullum dadi, kuma baƙin sun yi yawa.You're right. Alhaji Sani's family made a lot of effort. The food was more delicious than usual, and there were many guests.
LADI A ƙasar da na fito, muna yin biki iri ɗaya idan an haifi yaro. Amma al'adarmu ta bambanta da ta nan wani lokaci — galibi mukan yi bikin ne a ciki kawai, ba tare da kiɗa ba.In the region I come from, we hold a similar celebration when a child is born. But our tradition is sometimes different from the one here — we usually hold the celebration inside only, without music.
BISA Ga abin sha'awa! Nan a Kano, sau da yawa ana yin biki a fili — baƙi sun fi yawa a wajen fili. Kuma waƙoƙi da rawa su ne wani ɓangare muhimmi. Amma hakan ma ya bambanta daga iyali zuwa iyali — ba guda ɗaya ba ne.How interesting! Here in Kano, celebrations are often held in an open space — there are more guests at an outdoor venue. And songs and dancing are an important part. But that too varies from family to family — it is not one single way.
LADI Gaskiya. Wane ƙari ne? A gani, bikin sunan yau yā fi bikin aure ƙarami ne?True. Which is bigger, in your view — is a naming ceremony smaller than a wedding?
BISA Ya danganta. Wani lokaci bikin aure yā fi suna girma sosai — musamman idan ana tattare da dangi daga wurare masu nisa. Amma suna na iya zama babba ma, musamman ga ɗan fari. Al'ada ce ta musamman.It depends. Sometimes a wedding is much bigger than a naming ceremony — especially when relatives from far places are gathered together. But a naming can also be big, especially for a firstborn child. It is a special tradition.
LADI Na fahimta. Abin da ya fi muhimmanci shi ne zama tare da iyali da abokai, ko ba haka ba?I understand. The most important thing is being together with family and friends, isn't it?
BISA Lalle haka. Ba a yin biki ba tare da mutane ba.Indeed. You don't hold a celebration without people.

Vocabulary notes for the dialogue: ƙoƙari = effort, trying (m.; yi ƙoƙari = to make an effort); na kullum = of every day, ordinary, usual; a ƙasar da na fito = in the region/country I come from (fito = to come out of, to come from); a fili = in the open, outdoors (fili = open ground, m.); ɓangare = part, section (m.; pl. ɓangarori); hakan = that (thing), that (situation) — demonstrative pronoun; daga … zuwa … = from … to …; guda ɗaya = one single [way/thing]; ya danganta = it depends (danganta = to relate to, to depend on; also ya dogara); tattare = gathered together; wurare masu nisa = far places (nisa = distance, farness; masu nisa = that have distance); ɗan fari = firstborn son (lit. "son/child of first"); abin da ya fi muhimmanci = the most important thing (a superlative-type construction — see note below).

Grammar note — Superlative with fi: The expression abin da ya fi muhimmanci (the most important thing) uses fi in a relative construction: literally "the thing that surpasses in importance." Superlatives in Hausa are formed through comparison with fi rather than a separate superlative form. This construction is shown here as a recognition-only pattern — the full relative-clause analysis is deferred to Chapter 19. Learners may recognize and use it as a formula here.

Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

  • Every fi comparison uses the correct completive subject form that agrees with its subject (yā fi for m.sg., tā fi for f.sg., sun fi for pl., etc.).
  • The quality word after fi is a noun, not an adjective with an English-style ending — check: kyau not *kyaun or *morekyau.
  • Equality constructions use daidai da or iri ɗaya ne/ce correctly, not fi.
  • Every true adjective (babba/manya, ƙarami/ƙarama/ƙanana, sabo/sabuwa/sababbi) agrees with the noun it describes in number and gender.
  • mai is used for singular nouns (m. or f.); masu is used for plural nouns.
  • Frequency expressions are in natural positions — before or after the verb phrase — not attached to a noun as if they were adjectives.
  • Cultural claims are appropriately qualified — you do not write "Hausa people always …" without evidence that this is universal.
  • Hooked letters (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ) and 'y are used correctly throughout, including in ƙarami, ƙarama, ƙanana, Ƙaramar Sallah, and baƙi.
  • The text flows as a comparison or description, not as disconnected grammar sentences — there are connectors (kuma, amma, da farko, na biyu) between ideas.
  • Gender and plural agreement of pronouns, ne/ce, and demonstratives matches the nouns they refer to.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Sallah celebrations: Babban Sallah and Ƙaramar Sallah

In much of Hausa-speaking northern Nigeria and Niger, the two Eid celebrations — Eid al-Adha (locally called Babban Sallah, "the big Sallah") and Eid al-Fitr (locally called Ƙaramar Sallah, "the small Sallah") — are among the most significant recurring events in the year's calendar. Practices vary considerably by family, community, rural or urban setting, and economic circumstances. Common elements in many communities include communal prayer in an open-air space (filin salla), new or special clothing, visits to relatives, shared meals, and forms of charitable giving (sadaka).

The names Babban and Ƙaramar reflect a general perception that Eid al-Adha, with its association with animal sacrifice, tends to be a larger public event in many northern Nigerian communities — but this is a generalization, and individual families and communities vary. Not all Hausa-speaking people are Muslim; the chapter presents Sallah as a major community event in many Hausa-speaking contexts, not as a universal Hausa practice.

Culture in Context — The naming ceremony: Suna

The suna (naming ceremony) marks the formal introduction of a newborn into the community. In many Muslim Hausa families it takes place on the seventh day after birth, when the child is given their name before family and invited guests. The ceremony may involve prayers, the announcement of the child's name, hair-shaving (aski), and a shared meal. Gifts (kyaututtuka) and charitable giving (sadaka) are common components.

The suna is considered a significant social event, and attending one — even as an acquaintance of the family — is often seen as a gesture of support and respect. As with all cultural practices in this textbook, details vary by family, region, and community. The ceremony described above represents common elements in many Kano-area families but is not universal across all Hausa-speaking communities.

Culture in Context — How to compare cultures respectfully

Comparing cultural practices is a natural and important communicative skill, but it requires care. The model dialogue in this chapter demonstrates several strategies that make cultural comparison respectful and accurate:

  • Qualify claims: galibi (usually), wani lokaci (sometimes), a wasu lokuta (in some cases) signal that the speaker is not claiming a universal rule.
  • Acknowledge variation: ya danganta (it depends), ba guda ɗaya ba ne (it is not just one way) signal awareness that practices differ.
  • Speak from direct experience rather than generalizing: a ƙasar da na fito (in the region I come from) signals personal knowledge, not a claim about all speakers.
  • Avoid ranking: noting that one celebration is fi girma than another does not mean the smaller one is less important — the closing of the dialogue makes this explicit.

These strategies are not just cultural sensitivity — they are also linguistically accurate, since Hausa has specific tools (the frequency expressions and qualifiers in this chapter) to express these nuances.

Regional Variation — Celebration vocabulary and practice across Nigeria and Niger

Hausa-speaking communities in Niger use most of the same vocabulary for celebrations — suna, aure, biki, Sallah — but some practices and associated vocabulary reflect Niger's distinct cultural, linguistic, and political context. Written invitation cards may appear in both Hausa and French. The food and music at celebrations in Niamey or Zinder may differ in some ways from those in Kano or Sokoto. The Nigerien Hausa word for the Eid celebrations is the same as in Nigeria, but the Islamic calendar date calculations may result in Eid being celebrated on a different day in Niger than in Nigeria in some years. These are differences of context and detail, not of fundamentally different celebrations.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Translating "more" or "-er" into Hausa word by word

Error: *Wannan biki yā zama mafi kyau. or *Wannan biki ya fi m kore kyau. (attempts to add "more" to the adjective, or using the wrong structure)

Correction: Wannan biki yā fi kyau. — The Hausa comparison does not work by modifying the quality word. The quality word (kyau, girma, dadi) remains unchanged; it is fi that carries the comparative meaning. Note: mafi (the most…) exists in Hausa and is used in some superlative constructions — it may appear in texts learners encounter — but it is not taught productively at this stage. The productive comparison tool for this chapter is the pattern yā/tā/sun fi … [quality noun]. Use it consistently.

Common Mistake — Using the wrong subject form with fi

Error: *Wannan biki yana fi wancàn girma. (using the continuative subject form yana instead of the completive yā)

Correction: Wannan biki yā fi wancàn girma. — In the standard comparison construction, fi takes the completive subject form: yā fi (3sg.m), tā fi (3sg.f), sun fi (3pl). The continuative form yana/tana/suna is not used here. This is easy to confuse because comparison does not describe a completed event in the ordinary sense — but fi in a comparison is a fixed grammatical construction that takes the completive form. Learn it as a unit: yā fi + quality noun.

Common Mistake — Confusing mai (singular) and masu (plural)

Error: *Bukukuwa mai ban sha'awa. (using singular mai with a plural noun)

Correction: Bukukuwa masu ban sha'awa. — When the noun being qualified is plural, use masu, not mai. mai is for singular nouns (both masculine and feminine): biki mai ban sha'awa (a/the interesting celebration, sg.), liyafa mai kyau (a/the nice reception, sg.). masu is for plural nouns: bukukuwa masu ban sha'awa (interesting celebrations, pl.). Check the number of every noun before choosing between mai and masu.

Common Mistake — Using the same adjective form regardless of gender or number

Error: *al'ada ƙarami (using masculine form of adjective with a feminine noun) or *bukukuwa ƙarami (using singular form with plural noun)

Correction: al'adar ƙarama (f. sg.) / bukukuwa ƙanana (pl.) — The three true adjectives in this chapter (babba/manya, ƙarami/ƙarama/ƙanana, sabo/sabuwa/sababbi) have distinct forms for masculine singular, feminine singular, and plural. The gender of the noun determines which form you use. A masculine singular noun takes the masculine form; a feminine singular noun takes the feminine form; a plural noun takes the plural form, regardless of the gender of the singular. Check the noun's gender and number before selecting the adjective form.

Common Mistake — Making overly absolute cultural claims

Error: Writing or saying Hausawa koyaushe suna yin biki na kwanaki uku. (Hausa people always hold celebrations for three days.) as if this were a universal rule.

Correction: Use qualifying language: A wasu al'ummomi, galibi ana yin biki na kwanaki da yawa. (In some communities, celebrations are usually held for many days.) or Wani lokaci biki yana ɗaukar kwanaki uku ko huɗu. (Sometimes a celebration lasts three or four days.) — Cultural description requires the same precision as grammatical description. Practices vary by family, community, region, generation, religion, and individual preference. The frequency expressions in this chapter — galibi, wani lokaci, a wasu lokuta, da wuya — are not just vocabulary items; they are tools for describing culture accurately rather than stereotypically.

  4. Two ways weddings typically differ from naming ceremonies in Rubutu na 2: (1) Weddings are generally bigger, with more people invited and more food; (2) Weddings often take several days, while naming ceremonies are usually completed in a few hours.
  5. Equality construction: Daidai da bikin aure, bikin suna yana da muhimmanci sosai ga iyali — used to establish that despite the differences, a naming ceremony is equally important to a family.
  6. Two examples: bikin aure yā fi bikin suna girma a galibi and a wasu lokuta, yana da sauri. Qualified language is important because cultural practices vary by family, region, and circumstance; overgeneralizing creates stereotypes.

Bitar gaba ɗaya — Sashi na B (sample answers)

  1. (a) Koyaushe tana shirya abinci na musamman a bikin Sallah. (b) Liyafar tā fi ta kullum dadi. (c) Mu yi buki tare! (d) Sun aika kyaututtuka da yawa kafin bikin. (e) Ba za mu iya zuwa Asabar mai zuwa ba.
  2. (a) Agree: Eh, lalle yā fi. Abincin ya fi dadi kuma baƙin sun yi yawa. (b) Disagree: A gani nawa, ba haka ba ne. Bikin jiya ya yi daidai da yau — dukkansu sun fi kyau.
  3. Sample: A: Bikin aure yā fi bikin suna girma, ko ba haka ba? B: Galibi haka, amma a wasu lokuta bikin suna yana da girma ma — musamman ga ɗan fari. A: I, gaskiya. Dukkansu bukukuwa ne masu muhimmanci. B: Lalle haka.

Sashi na D — Rubutu (sample comparison article)

Bikin aure da bikin suna bukukuwa ne masu muhimmanci a al'ummar Hausa. Amma akwai bambance-bambance tsakaninsu. Da farko, bikin aure yā fi bikin suna girma — galibi ana gayyatar baƙi da yawa, kuma ana shirya abinci mai yawa. Bikin suna, a wasu lokuta, yana da sauri — ana yi shi cikin awa uku ko huɗu kawai. Na biyu, bikin aure yā fi lokaci — wani lokaci yana ɗaukar kwanaki da yawa. Amma daidai da bikin aure, bikin suna yana da muhimmanci sosai ga iyali — shi ne farkon rayuwar yaro a al'umma. Kuma dukkansu suna kawo farin ciki da murna. Al'adu ne masu ban sha'awa — kowane ɗaya yana da kyawunsa.

Other accurate, well-formed comparison texts are equally acceptable.

Looking Ahead

Abin da ka/ki koya yanzu — What you know now

Chapter 18 has given you the tools for comparison and cultural discussion. You can now compare two people, objects, or events using fi with the correct completive subject form and quality noun; express equality and difference using daidai da, iri ɗaya, bambanta, and dabam; describe how often events happen across the full frequency scale from koyaushe to never; and expand quality descriptions using mai/masu and the three new true adjectives with their masculine, feminine, and plural forms. You have also developed the important cultural-communication skill of qualifying claims rather than overgeneralizing. Combined with the completive narration of Chapter 16 and the planning and subjunctive tools of Chapter 17, you now have a substantial A2 communication toolkit — past, future, comparison, and cultural discussion are all within your reach.

Babi na 19: Ba da Labari — Telling Our Stories

Chapter 19 brings together everything from Unit IV and much of the course into its most challenging and rewarding task: connected personal narration. The central grammar of Chapter 19 is the consolidation of both completive and continuative constructions — general and relative forms — together with controlled productive introduction of habitual forms and the full range of clause connectors for cause, contrast, sequence, and result. You will also revisit comparison with fi, frequency expressions, direct- and indirect-object pronouns, and subjunctive sequencing from Chapters 17 and 18 — all in the service of telling a coherent story about a real or imagined experience. The writing target rises to 180–220 words of connected narrative. Chapter 19 is the final new-grammar chapter before the capstone review of Chapter 20.

Duniyata a Hausa

My World in Hausa · Speak and Write Hausa: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Sustain an unrehearsed, integrated conversation covering identity, daily life, a transaction, community navigation, completed experience, and future plans.
  2. Select correctly among continuative, habitual, future, completive, imperative, subjunctive, and relative forms according to meaning, question or focus status, and object context, without new instruction on any of these systems.
  3. Read a short practical or adapted newspaper-style article at the A2 level and answer comprehension and inference questions.
  4. Write a connected 220–300-word personal narrative and future-oriented reflection.
  5. Notice and repair your own communication errors, ask for clarification, and keep an interaction going when something goes wrong.
  6. Use greetings, forms of address, requests, invitations, and closings appropriate to a range of contexts and relationships.
  7. Demonstrate cumulative control of masculine, feminine, and plural agreement, noun plurals, and pronoun systems across all taught domains.
  8. Reflect on your own progress and identify strengths and areas for continued study beyond this volume.

Communication Scenario

A Hausa-speaking friend you met through your studies has invited you to a farewell gathering before you travel to a Hausa-speaking region for the first time. Over the course of the evening, you will greet new and familiar people, explain who you are and what brought you to Hausa, describe your daily life and routines, negotiate a small transaction, ask about getting around the city you plan to visit, talk about your health and well-being, discuss your work or studies and your plans, tell a story about something that happened to you, compare two experiences or customs, and close the evening with plans for staying in touch. This chapter does not teach a new grammatical system. Instead, it asks you to draw on everything from Chapters 1 through 19 at once, the way a real conversation actually unfolds — without warning about which topic or which grammar point is coming next.

  1. Communication repair — noticing an error, restating a sentence, or asking for clarification — is treated in this chapter as a proficiency skill in its own right, not as a sign of failure.
  2. No new vocabulary domain and no new grammatical system are introduced. Every construction in this chapter has appeared productively in an earlier chapter.
  3. The chapter's reading, listening, speaking, and writing tasks are each designed to require several communicative domains and several grammatical systems within a single passage or interaction, exactly as authentic A2 communication does.

Pronunciation and Boko

Cumulative tone and vowel-length control

By this stage, you have practiced tone and vowel length in isolated words, short phrases, question-and-answer exchanges, and connected narration. This chapter asks you to maintain that control across a single extended, multi-topic interaction — the most demanding pronunciation task of the course, because the aspect system shifts constantly as the topic shifts (identity, to routine, to a transaction, to a narrated story, and back again).

Sound Focus — Self-monitoring across a long interaction

As conversations grow longer, learners often let earlier pronunciation care slip in later sentences, particularly with tone in short grammatical words (subject-pronoun and aspect forms) and vowel length in high-frequency vocabulary. Practice noticing this tendency in your own speech: after every few sentences, briefly slow down and reset your attention to tone and length before continuing. This is a self-monitoring skill, not new pronunciation content.

Standard Boko spelling and paragraph conventions

Continue to apply Nigerian Standard Boko exactly as taught since Chapter 1: preserve ɓ, ɗ, ƙ, and 'y; use standard word division, capitalization, and punctuation; and leave ordinary writing unmarked for tone and vowel length. This chapter's writing task, at 220–300 words, is the longest connected composition of the course and should demonstrate consistent paragraph organization built up since Chapter 19.

Grammar Synthesis

This chapter introduces no new grammatical system. Its purpose is to require rapid, accurate, in-context selection among all major systems taught in Chapters 1–19, and to give you one integrated framework for thinking about how they work together.

Capstone Language Building Block

Ni ɗalibi ne. Kullum nakan yi karatun Hausa da yamma, amma jiya na fita da abokaina saboda muna bikin kammala jarabawa. Gobe kuma za mu tafi kasuwa mu sayi wasu kayan tafiya, don haka in yi shirye-shiryen tafiyata.
I am a student. I usually study Hausa in the evening, but yesterday I went out with my friends because we were celebrating finishing exams. Tomorrow we will go to the market and buy some travel items, so that I can prepare for my trip.

Notice how this single short passage moves through identification (ne), habitual, completive, cause (saboda), future (za), and a subjunctive purpose clause (in yi) — exactly the kind of shifting the final performance task will require.

20.2 Cumulative agreement review

Review masculine, feminine, and plural agreement across every domain where it has appeared: independent and subject pronouns, ne/ce, demonstratives, adjectives, genitive linkers -n/-r, and possessives. No new agreement pattern is introduced; the task is rapid, accurate selection when domains are mixed within one conversation or text.

20.3 Communication repair as a grammatical and pragmatic skill

Purpose: notice and correct your own errors, ask for clarification, and continue a conversation smoothly when communication breaks down.

Communication repair draws on constructions you already control: negation (ban gane sosai ba), ability (za ka iya maimaita), and simple discourse markers (wato, ina nufin). What is new in this chapter is not the grammar itself but the expectation that you deploy it naturally, in real time, as part of proficient communication — rather than treating a mistake as something to hide or a misunderstanding as a reason to give up.

  • Na tafi — ba, yi haƙuri, ina nufin za ni tafi gobe, ba jiya ba.I went — no, sorry, I mean I will go tomorrow, not yesterday.self-correction moving from completive to future after noticing the error
  • Yi haƙuri, ban gane sosai ba. Za ka iya maimaita, don Allah?Sorry, I didn't understand well. Could you repeat that, please?
  • Wato, ina nufin cewa muna bukatar tafiya da wuri, ba da rana ba.That is, I mean that we need to travel early, not in the daytime.

Pedagogical note: This section synthesizes existing grammar for a new pragmatic purpose. It does not introduce a new subject-aspect paradigm, and no assessment item in this chapter may require a construction beyond what Chapters 1–19 have taught.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Wata liyafar ban kwana kafin Sarah, ɗaliba Ba'amurkiya, ta tafi Kano don karatu. Ta sadu da wani sabon aboki, Bashir, a liyafar.

(Setting: A farewell party before Sarah, an American student, travels to Kano to study. She meets a new friend, Bashir, at the party.)

BASHIR Sannu! Ni Bashir ne. Kai fa, wanene?Hello! I'm Bashir. And you, who are you?
SARAH Sannu, Bashir. Ni Sarah ce, Ba'amurkiya ce. Ina koyon Hausa tun shekara guda, kuma zan tafi Kano makon gobe don ci gaba da karatu a can.Hello, Bashir. I'm Sarah, an American. I've been learning Hausa for a year, and I'm going to Kano next week to continue studying there.
BASHIR Wow, kina jin Hausa da kyau sosai! Yaya kika koya haka?Wow, you speak Hausa very well! How did you learn like that?
SARAH Na gode. To, kullum nakan yi karatu na sa'a ɗaya kowace safiya kafin aiki. Amma — yi haƙuri, ina nufin kafin makaranta, ba aiki ba, domin har yanzu ɗaliba ce.Thank you. Well, I usually study for one hour every morning before work. But — sorry, I mean before school, not work, since I'm still a student.
BASHIR Ba komai, na gane. To, ka gaya mini, me ya sa kika zaɓi Kano musamman?No problem, I understand. So tell me, why did you choose Kano specifically?
SARAH Domin Kano ita ce cibiyar Hausa ta gargajiya, kuma malamina ya ce yaren Kano ya fi sauƙin fahimta ga mai koyon Hausa. Bugu da ƙari, ina son ganin kasuwar Kurmi.Because Kano is the traditional center of Hausa, and my teacher said Kano Hausa is easier for a Hausa learner to understand. Also, I want to see the Kurmi market.
BASHIR Za ka iya — yi haƙuri, za ki iya samun taksi ko babur daga filin jirgin sama zuwa cikin gari. Amma idan kina son sauƙi, in ji abokina, ka kan gaya wa direban inda kake so kai tsaye.You can — sorry, you can get a taxi or motorbike from the airport into town. But if you want ease, according to my friend, you always tell the driver directly where you want to go.
SARAH Na gode da shawarar! Wata rana, na taɓa ɓacewa a wata sabuwar birni saboda ban tambayi wanda ya sani ba. Tun daga wannan lokaci, nakan tabbata na tambayi mutane hanya kafin in tafi.Thanks for the advice! One time, I once got lost in a new city because I didn't ask someone who knew. Since then, I always make sure I ask people directions before I go.
BASHIR Hakan hikima ce! To, lokacin da ka isa, idan ba ka da lafiya ko kana bukatar taimako, ka tuna cewa mutanen Kano suna taimakon baƙi sosai. Kuma kada ka manta, idan ba ka fahimci wani abu ba, kawai ka tambaya.That's wisdom! So, when you arrive, if you're not feeling well or need help, remember that the people of Kano help visitors a lot. And don't forget, if you don't understand something, just ask.
SARAH Zan tuna. To, bayan na dawo daga Kano a shekara mai zuwa, sai mu haɗu mu ba da labarunmu, ko?I'll remember. So, after I come back from Kano next year, let's meet up and share our stories, okay?
BASHIR Lallai! Sai mun sake haɗuwa. Allah ya kiyaye tafiyarki, Sarah.Certainly! Until we meet again. God protect your journey, Sarah.

Vocabulary notes for the dialogue: cibiyar = center, hub (linked form of cibiya, f.); bugu da ƙari = furthermore, in addition; babur = motorbike (m.; loanword); filin jirgin sama = airport (reviewed Ch. 19); baƙi = guests, strangers (pl.; reviewed Ch. 11); lallai = certainly, indeed.

Grammar note — Repair in the dialogue: Notice that both speakers self-correct naturally mid-sentence (Sarah on aiki/makaranta; Bashir on Za ka/za ki when he realizes he used the masculine form for a female addressee). Neither correction disrupts the conversation — this is the model of communication repair this chapter asks you to practice.

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. Why is Sarah traveling to Kano? Use a future-tense sentence from the dialogue in your answer.
  2. Find the moment where Sarah corrects herself. What was the error, and how did she fix it?
  3. Find the moment where Bashir corrects himself. What was the error, and how did he fix it?
  4. According to Bashir, what should Sarah do if she does not understand something in Kano?
  5. Find one habitual sentence in the dialogue. What repeated pattern does it describe?
  6. What do Sarah and Bashir plan to do after Sarah returns from Kano? Use the appropriate future or subjunctive form in your answer.

Rahoto — Sabon Kasuwar Ɗalibai a Jami'ar Bayero (Report: New Student Market at Bayero University)

Makon da ya gabata, an buɗe wata sabuwar kasuwa ta ɗalibai a harabar Jami'ar Bayero, Kano, domin taimaka wa ɗalibai su sami abinci da kayayyaki masu araha kusa da azuzuwansu. Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension and inference questions:

  1. What was opened at Bayero University, and for what stated purpose? Find the Hausa sentence.
  2. According to Fatima, what did students habitually have to do before the new market opened? What has changed?
  3. What are the stated hours of the new market?
  4. What complaint do some students have about the new market? What connector introduces the contrast with the general student reaction?

Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

  • The narrative has a clear opening orientation, a sequence of events, and a future-oriented closing reflection.
  • Habitual, completive, and continuative forms are each used correctly and for their appropriate function.
  • General and relative completive forms are correctly distinguished wherever a question, focus, or ordinary statement occurs.
  • At least one instance of natural self-correction or communication repair appears in the text.
  • Connectors of sequence, cause or result, and contrast are present and logically placed.
  • Masculine, feminine, and plural agreement is correct across pronouns, ne/ce, demonstratives, adjectives, and linkers.
  • Direct- and indirect-object pronouns are used correctly where objects are referred back to.

  • Paragraphing and punctuation organize the text clearly across its full 220–300-word length.

Speaking Practice

Atisayen ƙa'ida — Rapid aspect-switching drill

In pairs or small groups, recreate the situation of the model dialogue: one learner is about to travel to a Hausa-speaking region, and the others are friends at a farewell gathering. Warware matsala — Unplanned complication

Your instructor or partner introduces one unplanned complication partway through your conversation (for example: you realize you misunderstood a time, a plan changes, or you need to ask for repetition). Practice continuing the conversation smoothly, using communication-repair language rather than switching to English or stopping.

Madadin karatu kai tsaye — Solo alternative

Record yourself giving a 10–15-minute integrated monologue or simulated conversation (asking and answering your own questions in both roles) that moves through at least five communicative domains from the course. Afterward, review your recording and note at least one place where you could have repaired an error more naturally.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Hospitality toward newcomers and learners

Many Hausa-speaking communities place high value on welcoming visitors, newcomers, and language learners, and on offering practical help and guidance without being asked. This hospitality is expressed differently depending on setting, relationship, generation, and individual personality, and it should not be treated as a fixed script that every person will follow in the same way. A learner arriving in a Hausa-speaking community for the first time can reasonably expect helpfulness, but should also expect the same diversity of individual temperament found anywhere.

Culture in Context — Communication repair as a normal part of intercultural exchange

Making and correcting errors, asking for repetition, and checking understanding are normal, expected parts of communication between speakers of different first languages — and, indeed, between speakers of the same first language. Native and fluent speakers of Hausa routinely self-correct, clarify, and ask follow-up questions. A learner who does the same is participating in ordinary communication, not displaying weakness.

Regional Variation — Beyond this volume

This course has consistently used Kano-based Standard Hausa and Nigerian Standard Boko as its productive core, with selected receptive exposure to Niger and other regional variation. As you continue beyond this volume — through further study, travel, media, or direct interaction — expect to encounter a wider range of regional pronunciation, vocabulary, and pragmatic conventions than this beginner course could productively teach. This is a natural and welcome part of continued proficiency development, not a departure from what you have learned.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Avoiding repair instead of using it

Error: A learner notices a mistake mid-sentence, stops speaking entirely, and switches to English or falls silent.

Correction: Use a repair expression and continue in Hausa: Yi haƙuri, ina nufin ... — Treat the error as something to fix in the same breath, not as a reason to abandon the sentence. This chapter's dialogue models exactly this behavior.

Common Mistake — Reverting to one aspect for every situation

Error: *Na tafi Kano gobe. (using completive na with a future time word)

Correction: Zan tafi Kano gobe. — Under the pressure of an unrehearsed conversation, learners sometimes fall back on whichever aspect they feel most comfortable with, regardless of the time reference required. Let the time expression and communicative intent, not habit, determine the aspect.

Common Mistake — Losing gender agreement across a long interaction

Error: Using ne for a female referent partway through a long conversation after having correctly used ce earlier.

Correction: Self-monitor gender agreement throughout an extended interaction, not only in short, isolated exercises. If you notice the error, use a repair expression and correct it — this is itself good evidence of proficiency, not a sign of failure.

Common Mistake — Treating an inference question as a fact-finding question

Error: Answering an inference question (such as those in this chapter's reading task) only with information directly and explicitly stated in the text.

Correction: A2-level reading includes reasonable inference beyond directly stated facts. When a question asks you to infer, combine what the text states with reasonable background knowledge, and be prepared to explain your reasoning — this differs from simple comprehension, and both skills matter for continued proficiency growth.

  • Writing: Submit your 220–300-word connected written narrative and future-oriented reflection from the Writing Workshop.

Evaluation criteria: cumulative grammar control across continuative, habitual, future, completive, imperative, and subjunctive systems; correct general-versus-relative selection; accurate gender, plural, and pronoun agreement across domains; accurate connector use; evidence of communication repair; intelligible tone and vowel length; accurate ordinary Boko; a written narrative with a clear beginning, sequence, and future-oriented reflection; and culturally appropriate, comprehensible interaction throughout. Rubrics should reflect A2 expectations: comprehensible, task-fulfilling communication should not be penalized as though produced by an advanced speaker.

Answer Key / Amsoshi

Atisayen A — Zaɓi tsarin da ya dace (sample answers)

  1. Habitual — Kullum nakan yi wanka da sassafe a ranar Asabar. (or similar)
  2. Relative completive — Na ci shinkafa da miya jiya. (adjust to match the question word's requirement, i.e., na here answers a yes/no-style prompt; a direct me question requires na as the relative form, which coincides with 1sg general completive — flag: verify 1sg general/relative completive syncretism for accuracy before treating as a model answer)
  3. Imperative — Rufe ƙofa!
  4. Future — Zan kira iyalina bayan na isa.
  5. Continuative + completive — Ina zaune ina karatu, sai wayata ta yi ƙara.
  6. Subjunctive proposal — Mu ci abinci yanzu!

Atisayen B — Gyara kanka (sample answers)

  1. Na tafi — yi haƙuri, ina nufin, zan tafi makaranta gobe.
  2. Ina son kayan lambu, amma — yi haƙuri, ina nufin, ban ci su ba. (plural object requires plural pronoun su, not singular shi)
  3. Yarinya — yi haƙuri, ina nufin, yarinya ce.

Tambayoyin fahimta na tattaunawa (Dialogue comprehension)

  1. She is traveling to continue her studies: Zan tafi Kano makon gobe don ci gaba da karatu a can.
  2. Sarah says aiki (work) and corrects to makaranta (school), since she is still a student.
  3. Bashir begins with the masculine Za ka iya and corrects to the feminine za ki iya, addressing Sarah correctly.
  4. She should just ask (kawai ka tambaya) if she does not understand something.
  5. Tun daga wannan lokaci, nakan tabbata na tambayi mutane hanya kafin in tafi. — describes Sarah's habit of always asking for directions since getting lost once.
  6. They plan to meet up and share their stories after Sarah returns: sai mu haɗu mu ba da labarunmu (subjunctive sequencing after sai).

Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

(1) Yusuf first calls himself Halima's brother (ɗan'uwa) and corrects to brother-in-law (surukin). (2) The flight was delayed by two hours. (3) Kullum nakan yi mafarkin zuwa nan tun ina ƙarama — she always dreamed of coming since she was young. (4) Going to the market for traditional goods, then visiting a museum. (5) If she is not tired in the evening.

Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension and inference)

  1. A new student market was opened at Bayero University to help students access affordable food and goods near their classrooms: an buɗe wata sabuwar kasuwa ta ɗalibai a harabar Jami'ar Bayero, Kano, domin taimaka wa ɗalibai su sami abinci da kayayyaki masu araha kusa da azuzuwansu.
  2. Before the market opened, students had to travel far for everyday goods; now everything is close by.
  3. Eight in the morning to six in the evening.
  4. Some students find prices somewhat high compared to town markets; the contrast connector is kodayake.
  5. Likely to continue/expand — the article states the university plans to add more shops next year, suggesting institutional investment and permanence.
  6. Reasonable inference: convenience and lower transportation costs for vendors and students may be offset by higher campus rents or limited competition among sellers on campus; other reasonable, well-supported answers are acceptable.

Sashi na B — Nahawu (sample answers)

  1. (a) Kullum nakan tashi da wuri a lokacin ƙuruciyata. (b) Mun tafi wasan ƙwallon ƙafa a satin da ya gabata. (c) Za mu ziyarci kakanninmu wata mai zuwa. (d) Zauna nan! (e) Mu tafi kasuwa yanzu! (f) Muna cin abinci lokacin da wutar ta kashe.
  2. (a) Na tafi — yi haƙuri, zan tafi gobe. (b) Ita malami ne — yi haƙuri, ita malama ce. (c) Me kuka yi — ba, me kuka yi ke nan, ba mun yi ba.
  3. Sample: Mun isa gari, amma ba mu sami masauki ba nan take. Don haka, muka nemi wani otal, sannan muka sami wani mai kyau. A ƙarshe, muka huta.

Sashi na D — Rubutu (sample narrative)

Ni ɗalibi ne mai koyon Hausa tun shekara biyu. Kullum nakan yi karatu na mintuna talatin kowace safiya kafin in fita gida. Wata rana, na yanke shawarar shiga wani kulob na tattaunawa cikin Hausa a jami'a. Da farko, na yi tsoro sosai domin ban gane kalmomi da yawa ba. Amma sannan na sadu da wata Bahaushiya mai suna Zainab, kuma ta yi haƙuri ta yi min bayani a hankali. Muna magana, sai wani ɗalibi ya yi tambaya mai wahala, kuma na — yi haƙuri, ina nufin — na yi ƙoƙarin amsa, kodayake ban tabbata ba ko na yi daidai. Duk da haka, kowa ya yaba mini saboda na yi ƙoƙari. Tun daga wannan rana, nakan halarci kulob ɗin kowane mako. Gobe, in Allah ya yarda, zan gwada Hausa da wani sabon aboki daga Kano, kuma a shekara mai zuwa, ina fatan in ziyarci Najeriya don yin amfani da duk abin da na koya.

Other accurate, well-formed narratives that meet the stated requirements are equally acceptable.

A Wurin Aiki da Makaranta

Work and School · Speak and Write Hausa: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common occupations, workplaces, school subjects, and study-related activities in Hausa.
  2. Describe a work or school schedule using continuative forms and time expressions reviewed from Chapter 7.
  3. State future plans and predictions using the productive future construction with za.
  4. Negate future plans using the discontinuous pattern ba … ba with the appropriate future subject form.
  5. Express purpose — explaining why you will do something — using domin and its common alternatives.
  6. Review and apply ability with iya and necessity with dole from Chapter 13 in new work and school contexts.
  7. Use object pronouns reviewed from Chapter 9 in practical work and school sentences.
  8. Write a practical email-style message about duties and upcoming plans.

Communication Scenario

You have a colleague in another city who wants to understand what your work week looks like and what you have planned for the coming days. You also need to send a short written message to a supervisor or teacher explaining what you are working on and what you will do next. This chapter prepares you to describe your occupation, responsibilities, and schedule — and to talk about the future in Hausa for the first time as a fully productive skill.

  1. Up until now, every Hausa construction you have used productively has described something happening now, regularly, or as a general condition. In this chapter you add a genuinely new perspective: the ability to speak about what will happen. This requires a new subject-form set used together with the particle za. As you study this construction, notice that Hausa future meaning is built out of a subject form plus a particle, not out of a special ending attached to the lexical verb — a pattern quite unlike the way many European languages handle future tense.
  2. The concept of "purpose" — saying why you will do something — is closely tied to future and desired-action meaning. Hausa expresses purpose with a dedicated connector rather than with a relative clause or an infinitive construction as English often does. Learning to link an action to its purpose accurately is a key step toward producing connected, natural Hausa sentences and messages.

Domin and don can both introduce a purpose clause ("in order to …") or a reason clause ("because …"). Context and the clause that follows determine the meaning. This chapter focuses on the purpose use. The difference between domin and don is primarily one of register and speech rhythm rather than meaning; both are correct, and learners should expect to encounter either in authentic Hausa.

Pronunciation and Boko

Tone and length in future subject forms

The future construction introduces a new set of short subject forms. Several of these are phonetically similar to forms you already know from the continuative, and distinguishing them depends on tone and vowel quality rather than on spelling differences visible in ordinary Boko. For example, the first-person singular future marker is commonly contracted as zan, which must be clearly distinguished in speech from continuative ina or the independent pronoun ni. Listen carefully to your audio or instructor model for each future form and practice them in full phrases before moving to open production.

Sound Focus — Distinguishing future forms from similar short words

The future particle za and the subject forms that follow it are short, and they can run together in connected speech. When you practice sentences such as Za mu je makaranta gobe ("We will go to school tomorrow"), be careful to produce each element distinctly rather than merging za and mu into an unclear blur. This matters because changing the subject form changes the person, number, or gender of the statement entirely. Slow down slightly on future sentences until the forms feel secure, then build up speed.

The first-person singular future form zan is a contraction that must be recognized and used accurately — it is not simply za + ni inserted side by side. Similarly, the third-person masculine singular future form is commonly zai. Both of these contracted forms must be learned as units.

Multi-clause phrasing and purpose expressions

Sentences with domin join two clauses — an action and its purpose. In speech, a brief natural pause or a slight change in intonation typically marks the boundary between the main clause and the purpose clause. Practice the example sentences in the grammar section by speaking them aloud in full, noting where you would naturally breathe or pause. Do not treat the connector as a separate stressable word; it tends to flow on from the preceding clause without heavy stress.

Writing school and workplace vocabulary accurately

Several vocabulary items in this chapter contain letters that require careful attention in Boko. ɗalibi and ɗaliba each begin with hooked ɗ. The plural ɗalibai keeps the hook. ɗan kasuwa and 'yar kasuwa both require special characters: the ɗ in ɗan and the 'y in 'yar. Writing these as dan kasuwa or yar kasuwa with plain letters is a Boko error that must be avoided in all written work.

Writing Focus — Apostrophes and special characters in this chapter's vocabulary

Review the principle from Chapter 1: ordinary Boko uses four special characters — ɓ, ɗ, ƙ, and 'y — that must never be replaced with their plain-letter counterparts. This chapter's vocabulary is particularly rich in ɗ-initial words. When writing on paper, verify that the hook faces the correct direction. When typing, ensure your device supports Unicode and that you are not accidentally substituting a plain letter. The chapter review and writing workshop both include items specifically targeting these letters in the context of occupation and study vocabulary.

Grammar Instruction

14.1 The future construction with za — introducing productive future reference

Purpose: state what will happen or what someone plans to do, using the future particle za and the appropriate future subject form.

Until now, all the aspect constructions you have used productively have described situations happening now, regularly, or as a general state. In Chapter 14, you add the future: describing what has not yet happened but is planned, predicted, or expected. Hausa builds future meaning with the particle za followed by a short subject-form set. The lexical verb follows in its base form. This is the complete structure — there is no special future ending added to the verb itself.

The future subject forms follow za. Several contain contractions that must be learned as units:

Person / Gender Future subject form Example with verb je (to go) English
1st sg. zan Zan je makaranta gobe. I will go to school tomorrow.
2nd sg. za ka (m.) / za ki (f.) Za ka je ofis? Will you (m.) go to the office?
3rd sg. m. zai Zai je gona da wuri. He will go to the farm early.
3rd sg. f. za ta Za ta je asibiti yau. She will go to the clinic today.
1st pl. za mu Za mu yi taro gobe. We will hold a meeting tomorrow.
2nd pl. za ku Za ku rubuta jarrabawa jibi? Will you (pl.) write the exam the day after tomorrow?
3rd pl. za su Za su iso da wuri. They will arrive early.

Key contractions: zan is the standard contracted form for first-person singular (not za ni in careful standard usage). zai is the standard contracted form for third-person masculine singular. Both are in common use and must be learned as single units rather than as za + ni or za + shi placed separately.

Language Focus — Hausa future meaning comes from za + subject form, not from the verb

English speakers sometimes expect a "future tense" to show up as a change in the verb itself — the way English adds will as a separate word, or the way some languages change a verb ending. In Hausa, the lexical verb in a future sentence looks the same as it does in other constructions. What marks the future is the particle za plus the appropriate subject form that follows it. In Zan rubuta wasiƙa gobe ("I will write a letter tomorrow"), the verb rubuta carries no future marker at all; the future meaning comes entirely from zan.

This means that when you switch from a continuative sentence to a future sentence, only the subject-form element changes — the verb and time expression can remain the same. Compare: Ina rubuta wasiƙa (I am writing a letter) → Zan rubuta wasiƙa gobe (I will write a letter tomorrow). The difference is entirely in the subject form.

14.2 The negative future — ba … ba with future subject forms

Purpose: state what will not happen, using the discontinuous negative pattern ba … ba with the future subject form.

Just as the continuative and possession constructions have their own specific negative patterns, the future has its own too. The negative future uses the discontinuous ba … ba frame with the future subject form in its place. The structure is: ba + future subject form + verb + (object/complement) + ba.

  • Ba zan je ofis ba gobe.I will not go to the office tomorrow.ba + zan + je + ofis + ba
  • Ba zai zo taro ba.He will not come to the meeting.
  • Ba za mu rubuta jarrabawa ba a wannan sati.We will not write the exam this week.
  • Ba za ki iya zuwa ba yau?Will you (f.) not be able to come today?
Language Note — Both ba elements are required; omitting the second is an error

A very common mistake is to write or say only the first ba, by analogy with negation in some other languages. In the Hausa future negative, both parts of the frame are required: ba … ba wraps around the future subject form and the rest of the predicate. Ending the sentence without the second ba is incomplete — it leaves the negation frame open. Whenever you write a negative future sentence, make sure your sentence ends with ba (or has ba before any following time expression).

14.3 Expressing purpose with domin

Purpose: explain why an action will be done, linking a planned activity to its goal using domin.

Hausa uses domin (or its shorter form don) to introduce a purpose clause: the reason or goal that motivates an action. The main clause describes the action; domin then introduces the purpose. The verb in the purpose clause may be a continuative form or a verbal noun depending on context, but for this chapter, keep purpose clauses using the same verb constructions already in your productive repertoire.

  • Zan je makaranta gobe domin in yi jarrabawa.I will go to school tomorrow in order to take the exam.
  • Tana karanta littafi domin ta koyi Hausa.She is reading a book in order to learn Hausa.
  • Za mu tafi kasuwa don mu sayi kayan abinci.We will go to the market to buy food.
  • Yana aiki tuƙuru domin ya tara kudi.He is working hard in order to save money.
  • Malama ta rubuta takarda domin ta aika wa shugaba.The teacher wrote a document in order to send it to the director.

You will notice that the verb following domin in several examples above uses a short subject form that looks like a subjunctive — for example, in yi, ta koyi, mu sayi, ya tara. The full productive subjunctive system belongs to Chapters 11 and 17; treat the purpose-clause verb forms in these examples as formulaic patterns tied to specific sentences. Your task in this chapter is to use the domin connector productively to link an action to its purpose, not to construct purpose clauses with freely chosen new subjunctive forms.

Language Focus — Purpose in Hausa versus English infinitives

English speakers naturally want to translate a purpose clause with "to + verb": "I will go to school to take the exam." Hausa does not use an infinitive construction for this. Instead, it uses a connector (domin or don) followed by a full clause with its own subject reference. This means that even when the subject of the purpose clause is the same as the subject of the main clause, Hausa typically marks that subject reference inside the purpose clause rather than dropping it as English drops "I" in "I will go to school to take the exam." Treat each domin sentence as two connected clauses, not as a main verb plus a bare infinitive.

Language Building Block
future subject form + verb + (object) + domin/don + purpose clause
Zan rubuta wasiƙa gobe domin in sanar da shi.
I will write a letter tomorrow in order to inform him.
Ba zan rubuta wasiƙa ba yau — zan rubuta ta gobe.
I will not write the letter today — I will write it tomorrow.

The first sentence shows an affirmative future plus a purpose connector. The second contrasts a negative future with an affirmative alternative. Both patterns are fully productive in this chapter.

14.4 Reviewing ability and necessity in work and school contexts

Purpose: apply iya (ability) and dole (necessity) from Chapter 13 in new work and school sentences.

In Chapter 13, you learned to express ability with iya and necessity with dole in health contexts. Those same constructions now extend naturally to work and study responsibilities. The patterns remain identical; only the vocabulary changes. Review these frames and practice them with the new occupational and academic vocabulary in this chapter.

  • Yana iya yin lissafi sosai.He can do mathematics well.
  • Ba ta iya zuwa taro ba yau.She cannot come to the meeting today.
  • Dole in gama rahoton aiki kafin gobe.I must finish the work report before tomorrow.
  • Dole su zo makaranta da wuri.They must come to school early.
  • Zan iya aika da imel gobe safe.I will be able to send the email tomorrow morning.

Note the last example above: ability and future meaning can combine. Zan iya … means "I will be able to …" — the future subject form zan establishes future reference, and iya carries the ability meaning. This combination is fully natural and does not require any additional explanation beyond what you already know.

14.5 Object-pronoun review in work and school sentences

Purpose: apply direct-object pronouns from Chapter 9 in practical messages and schedules, using verified verb forms.

Work and school communication regularly involves referring back to people or things already mentioned — "I will send it tomorrow," "Did you read it?" These uses require the direct-object pronouns introduced in Chapter 9. The examples below recycle those pronouns in future and continuative sentences, using verbs whose object-conditioned forms are already in your repertoire.

  • Takarda tana nan — zan aike ta gobe.The document is here — I will send it tomorrow. (ta = it, referring to the feminine noun takarda)
  • Rahoton ya shiryu — malami zai karanta shi.The report is ready — the teacher will read it. (shi = it, masculine)
  • Ɗalibai suna nan — malama tana koyar da su.The students are here — the teacher (f.) is teaching them.
  • Wasiƙa ta zo — ka karanta ta?The letter has arrived — did you read it? (recognition-only completive; full productive completive in Chapter 16)

The last example introduces a completive verb form for recognition. Do not use the completive productively yet; Chapter 16 introduces it fully. All other sentences above are fully within your productive scope.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Layla ita ce malama a makaranta. Ibrahim abokin aikinta ne. Suna magana a farkon makon aiki game da shirye-shiryen mako mai zuwa.

(Setting: Layla is a teacher at a school. Ibrahim is her colleague. They are speaking at the beginning of the work week about plans for the coming week.)

IBRAHIM Sannu, Layla. Yaya aiki a wannan sati?Hello, Layla. How is work this week?
LAYLA Sannu, Ibrahim. Aiki yana da kyau, gode. Ɗalibaina suna yin jarrabawa jibi, don haka dole in shirya su da kyau a yau da gobe.Hello, Ibrahim. Work is good, thank you. My students have an exam the day after tomorrow, so I must prepare them well today and tomorrow.
IBRAHIM To, aiki mai muhimmanci. Kai kanka, za ka yi taro da shugaba a wannan sati?That is important work. What about you yourself — will you have a meeting with the director this week?
LAYLA A'a, ba zan yi taro ba wannan sati. Shugaba yana tafiya, ba zai zo makaranta ba har Laraba. Za mu yi taro a Alhamis domin mu tattauna jadawalin mako mai zuwa.No, I will not have a meeting this week. The director is travelling — he will not come to school until Wednesday. We will have a meeting on Thursday in order to discuss next week's schedule.
IBRAHIM Ai dai haka. Zan rubuta rahoton aiki gobe domin in gabatar da shi a taron Alhamis.I see. I will write the work report tomorrow in order to present it at the Thursday meeting.
LAYLA Kyakkyawa. Ɗan aiki — za ki iya taimaka min da lissafin ɗalibai a Talata? Ba ni da lokaci sosai a wannan rana.Excellent. A small favour — will you (f.) be able to help me with the students' marks on Tuesday? I do not have much time that day.
IBRAHIM I, zan iya. Ba da wahala. Za mu yi shi tare domin mu gama da wuri.Yes, I can. No problem. We will do it together so that we finish early.
LAYLA Na gode sosai, Ibrahim. Allah ya saka da alheri.Thank you very much, Ibrahim. May God reward you with goodness. (a widely used courtesy expression)

Vocabulary notes for the dialogue: mai muhimmanci = important (having importance); kai kanka = you yourself, what about you; har = until; tattauna = to discuss; gabatar da = to present (a report or proposal); ɗan aiki = a small favour, a little task; taimaka = to help; lissafin ɗalibai = students' marks / scores; ba da wahala = no problem, it is no trouble; tare = together; Allah ya saka da alheri = a common courtesy expression of blessing, used when thanking someone for a service or favour (recognition only — formulaic).

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. What is happening to Layla's students the day after tomorrow? What does this mean for her work today and tomorrow?
  2. Why will Layla not have a meeting this week?
  3. What will Layla and Ibrahim do on Thursday, and what is the purpose?
  4. Find the sentence in which Ibrahim explains why he will write a report tomorrow. Write it in Hausa.
  5. What does Layla ask Ibrahim for help with? When, and why does she need help?
  6. Find one example of a negative future sentence in the dialogue. Identify both ba elements.

Writing Workshop

  1. Karanta samfuri. Reread Rubutu na 1 (the work email) above. Underline every future sentence. Circle every use of domin or don. Put a box around any dole or iya expression. Note how the email is organized: greeting, main information, request, closing.
  2. Shirya bayananka. Choose a real or invented work or school situation. Decide: Who are you writing to? What is the subject? What will you do, and when? What is your purpose? What do you need from the reader?
  3. Rubuta farkon rubutunka. Write a short Hausa email or message of seven to nine sentences. Include: at least two future sentences, at least one negative future sentence, at least one domin or don purpose expression, and at least one use of dole or iya.
  4. Duba da jerin bincike. Check your draft against the editing checklist before writing the final version.
  5. Rubuta siffa ta ƙarshe. Produce a clean final version including a greeting, the body of your message, and a closing.

Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

  • Each future sentence uses za followed by the correct subject form for the person, number, and gender intended. The contracted forms zan (1st sg.) and zai (3rd sg. m.) are used where required.
  • Each negative future sentence contains both ba elements: ba before the future subject form and ba at (or near) the end of the clause.
  • Each domin or don sentence contains a main action clause and a purpose clause. The purpose clause is a complete clause, not a bare infinitive.
  • Any use of dole or iya follows the patterns established in Chapter 13.
  • Hooked letters (ɗ in ɗalibai, ɗan; 'y in 'yar kasuwa; ƙ where it appears) are written correctly.
  • Work and school vocabulary is spelled correctly; malami/malama, jarrabawa, jadawali, rahoton aiki, and similar words retain their standard Boko forms.
  • Each sentence ends with a full stop; the email or message includes a greeting and a closing.
  • Register is respectful where the addressee is a supervisor, teacher, or elder.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Work and school in Hausa-speaking communities

Work and school in Hausa-speaking communities in Nigeria and Niger take many forms. Northern Nigeria in particular has a long history of Islamic education through Qur'anic schools and later through Western-model government and private schools introduced during the colonial period; both traditions continue alongside each other today. Formal education is conducted in Hausa, English, and sometimes Arabic depending on the institution and level, and the relationship between these languages varies by school type, region, and household. Jami'ar Bayero (Bayero University Kano), founded in the 1970s, is a major centre for Hausa language and literary studies.

Work patterns are equally varied: urban professionals in cities like Kano, Zaria, Niamey, and elsewhere maintain office schedules that will feel familiar from many other contexts, while traders, farmers, craftspeople, and others organize their work around market cycles, seasonal rhythms, agricultural calendars, and community events. Do not assume that one occupational pattern or institutional structure represents all Hausa-speaking workplaces and schools.

Culture in Context — Respectful requests in professional settings

Workplace and school communication in Hausa typically begins with an appropriate greeting before moving to the purpose of the interaction — asking a colleague for help, requesting information from a supervisor, or addressing a teacher with a question. Jumping directly into a request without a greeting is considered abrupt in many Hausa-speaking settings, particularly when addressing someone older, more senior, or with whom you do not have a very familiar relationship. The dialogue in this chapter models this pattern: Ibrahim and Layla exchange greetings and inquire about each other's week before discussing plans. In written messages, even emails, a brief greeting and closing are standard.

The term alhaji or alhaja (one who has made the pilgrimage to Mecca) is used as a respectful title for many people in northern Nigerian and Nigerien Hausa-speaking communities. It may appear in names, in direct address, and in written correspondence. Using a title correctly shows awareness of the relationship and context; learners should treat titles as meaningful social information rather than decorative prefixes.

Regional Variation — Vocabulary for school and work across Nigeria and Niger

Several school and work terms show variation between Nigerian and Nigerien usage, partly because Nigerien Hausa has borrowed some administrative and educational vocabulary from French rather than from English. For example, the word for "school" may be makaranta across most of the Hausa-speaking world, but some administrative levels and types of institution may use different labels depending on the country's educational system. Similarly, titles and forms of address in professional settings may differ between the two countries' public-sector traditions. This chapter teaches the core Nigerian Standard Hausa forms; if you are working specifically in a Nigerien context, verify individual institutional terms with a speaker familiar with that setting.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Omitting the second ba in a negative future sentence

Error: *Ba zan je ofis gobe. (only one ba)

Correction: Ba zan je ofis ba gobe. — The Hausa negative future requires both elements of the ba … ba frame. The second ba closes the negative construction and must appear at or near the end of the predicate. A sentence ending with the lexical verb and no closing ba is incomplete. Every time you write a negative future sentence, check that ba appears both before the future subject form and after the verb (and object, if present).

Common Mistake — Using zan and zai interchangeably or writing za ni / za shi

Error: *Za ni je ofis. or *Za shi zo taro.

Correction: The standard contracted forms are zan (first-person singular, "I will") and zai (third-person masculine singular, "he will"). These are not written as two separate words za + ni or za + shi in standard Hausa; they are single forms that must be learned as units. Write and say zan and zai, not their uncontracted equivalents.

Common Mistake — Using a bare infinitive after domin by analogy with English

Error: *Zan je makaranta domin rubuta jarrabawa. (bare verbal noun without a subject reference in the purpose clause)

Correction: Zan je makaranta domin in rubuta jarrabawa. — Unlike English "to take the exam," Hausa purpose clauses introduced by domin typically contain a full clause with its own subject reference. The formulaic purpose-clause verb forms you have learned in this chapter (such as in rubuta, mu sayi, ta koyi) carry that subject reference. Do not use a bare verbal noun after domin as though it were an English infinitive.

Common Mistake — Writing ɗalibi or ɗan kasuwa with plain d or y

Error: *dalibi, *daliba, *dan kasuwa, *yar kasuwa

Correction: ɗalibi, ɗaliba, ɗan kasuwa, 'yar kasuwa — Both ɗ and 'y are required characters in Nigerian Standard Boko. Replacing them with plain d or y is a spelling error, not an acceptable shortcut. These characters represent distinct sounds in Hausa and must be preserved in all writing.

Common Mistake — Adding a future ending to the lexical verb

Error: *Zan jeshi or *zan rubuta-ko (inventing a future ending on the verb)

Correction: Zan je. / Zan rubuta. — The lexical verb in a Hausa future sentence carries no future marker of its own. The entire future signal comes from za plus the appropriate subject form. The verb itself appears in the same form it would take in other constructions. Do not add any suffix or ending to a verb to make it "future."

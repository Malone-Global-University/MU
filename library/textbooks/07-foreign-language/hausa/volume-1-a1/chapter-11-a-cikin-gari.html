Getting Around Town · Speak and Write Hausa: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common buildings, public services, and forms of transportation.
  2. Ask where a place is located using Ina … take?
  3. Ask for and give simple directions using affirmative singular commands.
  4. Give a command to a group or use a respectful subjunctive-based instruction.
  5. Give a negative command using kada.
  6. Use common motion verbs to describe going, coming, entering, and leaving.
  7. Use high-frequency prepositions and pronoun forms after prepositions to describe position and route.
  8. Read and follow simple public signs and route instructions in Boko.
  9. Pronounce imperative and subjunctive forms with attention to tone, and read place names and transportation vocabulary accurately.

Communication Scenario

You have just arrived in a new part of town and need to find your way to a bank, a market, and a friend's house before evening. A stranger on the street, a shopkeeper, and a taxi driver each give you part of the route. To follow their instructions — and to ask your own questions when you get lost — you need vocabulary for buildings, transportation, and direction, along with the sentence patterns Hausa uses to tell someone where to go and what to do.

  1. Until now, every command you have produced has used one fixed pattern (the singular imperative from Chapter 2) or the group form ku + verb. In this chapter, both patterns become fully productive, and you will also learn how to make a command negative. Notice that the negative command does not simply add "not" to the affirmative — it uses a different subject form entirely.
  2. Direction-giving in Hausa relies heavily on a small set of motion verbs — go, come, enter, leave, turn, continue — combined with location words you already know from Chapter 6. As you work through the grammar section, pay attention to how a sequence of short commands, rather than one long sentence, is the natural way to give directions in Hausa.

Pronunciation and Boko

Tone in imperative forms

Hausa imperative verbs carry their own tone pattern, separate from the tone of the citation form you learned in the vocabulary ledger. Several common motion verbs have a distinct, often low, tone in the imperative that must be learned by listening rather than guessed from spelling.

Hausa imperativeEnglishPronunciation note
Tafi!Go!verified low-high tone pattern; do not lengthen the final vowel
Zo!Come!single syllable; hold the vowel briefly, not extended
Juya!Turn!two syllables; stress falls naturally on the first
Tsaya!Stop!initial consonant cluster ts pronounced as one glottalized sound
Shiga!Enter!sh as one sound, not s + h
Pronunciation Practice — Command intonation versus statement intonation

A Hausa command typically carries a brisk, falling intonation contour distinct from an ordinary statement, although it is less dramatic than English command intonation can be. A polite or respectful command — for example, one using the plural ku form to address one person respectfully — is often delivered with a softer, more level contour and may be preceded by don Allah (please) or followed by a softening particle. Listen for the difference between a brisk Tafi dama! (Go right!) given quickly between friends and a more measured Don Allah, ku juya hagu (Please turn left) given to a stranger or an elder. Tone on individual words does not change, but the overall rhythm and pitch contour of the sentence does.

Place names and transportation vocabulary

Continue applying the Boko reading checklist from Chapter 3 to the place names and transportation words in this chapter. Pay particular attention to:

  1. Tsakanin — the consonant cluster ts at the start is one glottalized sound, not t followed by s.
  2. Masallaci — the doubled l sound is held briefly; do not shorten it to a single tap.
  3. Babur and banki — these are established loanwords with verified Hausa pronunciation; do not import the source-language pronunciation.
  4. Vowel length in kusa (near) versus kusurwa (corner) — these are related in form but distinct words; do not confuse them.
Sound Focus — Tone in subjunctive subject forms

The subjunctive subject forms introduced in this chapter (ka, ki, ku, and others) carry their own tone, distinct from similar-looking forms used elsewhere in the course. Because several of these short words resemble forms you already know from other constructions, careful listening is required to keep them distinct. Your instructor or audio model will demonstrate the tone of ku tafi (go, addressed respectfully or to a group) so that you can distinguish it by ear from forms with a similar spelling but a different grammatical function.

Grammar Instruction

11.1 The affirmative singular imperative — now productive

Purpose: give a direct command to one person.

In Chapter 2, you learned a small set of fixed singular commands for classroom use. In this chapter, the singular imperative becomes fully productive: you can form and use it with any high-frequency verb needed for giving directions and instructions. The singular imperative is used for direct commands to one person in a familiar or informal context.

Many common verbs form the singular imperative from a base form close to the citation form, often with a distinctive tone pattern. Because the imperative tone is not always predictable from the citation form's tone, each new imperative verb should be learned and verified individually rather than derived by a general rule.

  • Tafi dama!Go right!
  • Juya hagu!Turn left!
  • Tsaya nan!Stop here!
  • Shiga banki!Go into the bank! / Enter the bank!
  • Zo nan!Come here!

The singular imperative is limited to affirmative commands addressed to one person in a familiar context. It is not used for negative commands, for groups, or for respectful address to an elder or stranger — each of those situations uses a different pattern, introduced below.

11.2 Subjunctive commands for groups and respectful address — now productive

Purpose: give a command to more than one person, or address one person respectfully, using the subjunctive-based pattern.

In Chapter 2, you saw fixed ku + verb patterns for classroom instructions. The subjunctive subject-form system behind that pattern becomes productive here. The subjunctive subject form appears before the verb and carries the person, number, and (in the singular) gender information for the command.

Subject formUsed forExample
karespectful or formal command to one male, in approved contextsKa tafi dama. (Go right.)
kirespectful or formal command to one female, in approved contextsKi juya hagu. (Turn left.)
kucommand to a group, or respectful command to one personKu tsaya nan. (Stop here.)

Use ku for any command directed at more than one person, and also as a respectful way to address a single elder, stranger, or person of higher status — the same plural-for-respect pattern you encountered with pronouns in earlier chapters. The forms ka and ki appear in this course mainly in fixed, taught command frames; reserve ku as your default safe choice for an unfamiliar respectful context.

  • Ku shiga, don Allah.Please come in. (to a group, or respectfully to one person)
  • Ku juya dama a mahaɗar hanya.Turn right at the junction.
  • Ka tafi gaba sai ka isa banki.Go straight ahead until you reach the bank.
Language Focus — Imperative and subjunctive are not interchangeable by politeness alone

It is tempting to think of the singular imperative as "informal" and the subjunctive ku form as "formal," but the real distinction is about number and respect together, not politeness alone. The singular imperative is grammatically restricted to one addressee in an affirmative command; it cannot be used for a group regardless of how informal the setting is. The subjunctive-based ku form is required for any group, and it is also the safer respectful choice for one person whose relationship to you calls for distance — an elder, a stranger, or someone in an official role. Tone of voice and added words like don Allah (please) also affect politeness, but the choice of ka/ki versus ku versus the plain singular imperative is determined by grammar, not by how politely you say it.

11.3 Negative commands with kada

Purpose: tell someone not to do something.

Hausa forms a negative command with kada followed by the subjunctive subject form and the verb. This is a different construction from the affirmative imperative — you cannot simply add a negative word to the imperative form. The subject form changes for person, number, and gender, just as in the affirmative subjunctive commands above.

  • Kada ka tafi can.Don't go there. (to a male)
  • Kada ki juya hagu.Don't turn left. (to a female)
  • Kada ku shiga.Don't enter. (to a group, or respectfully to one person)
  • Kada a shiga.Do not enter. (impersonal sign form — recognition only at this stage)
Language Building Block
command + location or direction + next step
Ku tafi gaba, sa'an nan ku juya dama a kusurwa.
Go ahead, then turn right at the corner.
Kada ku tsaya nan; ku tafi can.
Don't stop here; go over there.

Choose the singular imperative for one familiar addressee, ka/ki/ku for respectful or plural address, and kada plus the subjunctive subject form for any negative command. Sequence two or three short commands with a connector such as sa'an nan (then) rather than building one long sentence.

11.5 Prepositions, location, and pronouns after prepositions

Purpose: describe position, route, origin, and destination using high-frequency prepositions and the correct pronoun form after them.

You have already used the basic locative a (in, at) since Chapter 6. This chapter adds kusa da (near), nesa da (far from), and tsakanin (between), and reviews daga (from) from Chapter 3. When a pronoun follows certain prepositions such as kusa da and nesa da, it takes the independent pronoun form you already know — Hausa does not require a separate prepositional pronoun set for these particular prepositions.

  • Bankin yana kusa da kasuwa.The bank is near the market.
  • Asibiti yana nesa da nan.The hospital is far from here.
  • Gidan abinci yana tsakanin banki da kasuwa.The restaurant is between the bank and the market.
  • Yana kusa da ita.It is near her/it. (independent pronoun ita after kusa da)

Not every Hausa preposition behaves identically with a following pronoun, and a small number of prepositions in later chapters require a different pattern. This chapter teaches only the high-frequency prepositions listed above; do not generalize their pronoun behavior to every preposition you encounter.

11.6 Asking where a place is located

Purpose: ask for the location of a place using a question pattern appropriate to this stage of the course.

To ask where something is located, Hausa uses the question word ina (where) together with a relative-continuative subject form, similar in structure to the origin question you learned in Chapter 3 with daga ina. Learn the complete question as a formulaic pattern; the full grammar of the relative form is consolidated later in the course.

Hausa questionUsed forEnglish meaning
Ina bankin yake?asking about a masculine-referenced place, e.g. bankiWhere is the bank?
Ina kasuwar take?asking about a feminine-referenced place, e.g. kasuwaWhere is the market?
  • Q: Ina bankin yake? · A: Yana kusa da kasuwa.Where is the bank? · It is near the market.
  • Q: Ina asibitin yake? · A: Yana tsakanin titi da kusurwa.Where is the hospital? · It is between the street and the corner.
Language Note — yake and take after ina

The forms yake and take in location questions are relative-continuative subject forms, the same family of forms you first met in Chapter 3 with kake and kike in origin questions and reviewed when asking about possession in Chapter 6. They are required here because the question word ina precedes the subject, just as a question word required a relative form in those earlier chapters. You are not yet asked to produce every relative form freely — only the specific location-question pattern shown here. The full system is consolidated in Chapters 16 and 19.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Aisha ta ɗauki tasi daga tashar mota zuwa wani sashe na gari da ba ta sani ba. Ta sauka kusa da kasuwa kuma ta tambayi wani mutum, Bashir, hanyar zuwa banki.

(Setting: Aisha has taken a taxi from the bus park to an unfamiliar part of town. She gets out near the market and asks a man, Bashir, the way to the bank.)

AISHA Don Allah, ina bankin yake?Excuse me, where is the bank?
BASHIR Yana kusa da nan. Ki tafi gaba, sa'an nan ki juya dama a mahaɗar hanya.It's near here. Go ahead, then turn right at the junction.
AISHA To, na gode. Kuma bayan mahaɗar hanya?All right, thank you. And after the junction?
BASHIR Bayan mahaɗar hanya, ki ci gaba kaɗan. Bankin yana tsakanin masallaci da gidan abinci. Kada ki juya hagu — can ne ofishin 'yan sanda, ba banki ba.After the junction, continue a little. The bank is between the mosque and the restaurant. Don't turn left — that's the police station, not the bank.
AISHA Na fahimta. Nesa ne daga tashar mota?I understand. Is it far from the bus park?
BASHIR A'a, ba nesa ba ne. Za ki iya tafiya da ƙafa cikin mintuna goma. Amma idan kina gajiya, akwai tasi da yawa a nan.No, it's not far. You can walk in ten minutes. But if you're tired, there are plenty of taxis here.
AISHA Na gode sosai, Bashir. Ina godiya.Thank you very much, Bashir. I'm grateful.
BASHIR Babu komai. Ku tafi lafiya!No problem. Go safely! (respectful/polite form of farewell)

Vocabulary notes for the dialogue: to = okay, all right; bayan = after, behind; ci gaba = continue (recognition phrase here; full verbal-noun analysis later); na fahimta = I understand; mintuna goma = ten minutes; gajiya = tiredness (used here in a recognition-only conditional frame, idan kina gajiya — full conditionals in Chapter 12); akwai … da yawa = there are many … (review from Chapter 6); ina godiya = I am grateful; babu komai = no problem, you're welcome; ku tafi lafiya = go safely (a fixed respectful farewell — recognition pattern).

  4. Is the bank far from the bus park? How does Aisha find out?
  5. What two transportation options does Bashir mention for reaching the bank?

Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

  • Each command uses the correct form for its addressee: singular imperative for one familiar person, ka/ki/ku for respectful or group address.
  • Every negative command uses kada plus the subjunctive subject form, not the singular imperative.
  • Direction and position words (dama, hagu, gaba, baya, kusa da, nesa da, tsakanin) are used accurately and match the intended meaning.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Finding your way without a fixed address system

In many cities and towns across northern Nigeria and Niger, as in much of West Africa, formal numbered street addresses are not always the primary way people locate places, although formal addressing systems exist and are expanding in many urban areas. Landmark-based directions — naming a market, a mosque, a particular shop, or a well-known building — are a common and effective way to describe location, and this chapter's vocabulary and direction patterns reflect that practice. This does not mean that formal addresses are absent; postal systems, street names, and numbered buildings exist in many cities, including Kano and Niamey, and are used in official, commercial, and postal contexts. Landmark-based direction-giving and formal addressing coexist, and a competent Hausa speaker moves between them depending on the situation and the listener's familiarity with the area.

Culture in Context — Asking for help on the street

Asking a stranger for directions in a Hausa-speaking community typically begins with a greeting or a polite opener such as don Allah (please) before the question itself, much as you saw modeled in the chapter dialogue. Responding helpfully to a lost traveler is generally regarded as an ordinary courtesy. The degree of detail offered, and whether someone simply points or walks partway with you, varies by individual, setting, and how busy the person is — there is no single fixed script that every Hausa speaker follows. Politeness markers and the choice between ka/ki and ku in a response often signal the degree of respect the speaker is extending, independent of the specific route information being given.

Regional Variation — Transportation vocabulary in Nigeria and Niger

Transportation infrastructure and the vocabulary surrounding it differ somewhat between Nigeria and Niger and across individual cities. Bas and tasi are widely recognized loanword terms, but local terms for shared taxis, minibuses, and motorcycle taxis vary by city and are sometimes known by city-specific names not covered in this introductory chapter. This course teaches the high-frequency, broadly recognized terms in the vocabulary list above as the productive core; learners traveling to a specific city should expect to encounter and learn additional local terms receptively.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using the singular imperative for a group

Error: Tafi dama! said to two or more people, or to an elder who should be addressed respectfully.

Correction: Ku tafi dama! — The plain singular imperative is restricted to one familiar addressee. Any group, and any person you wish to address respectfully, requires the subjunctive-based ku form. Before giving a command, check: is the addressee one familiar person, or does the situation call for ku?

Common Mistake — Negating the imperative directly

Error: *Ba tafi ba or *Kada tafi (attempting to negate the imperative form directly)

Correction: Kada ka tafi. — A negative command is built with kada plus the subjunctive subject form (ka, ki, ku), not by adding a negative word directly to the imperative verb. The verb form itself does not change to a negative form; the subject form changes from the imperative pattern to the subjunctive pattern.

Common Mistake — Confusing kusa da and nesa da

Error: Bankin yana nesa da kasuwa when the intended meaning is that the bank is near the market.

Correction: Bankin yana kusa da kasuwa. — Kusa da means "near," and nesa da means "far from." These two prepositions are opposite in meaning but easy to confuse because of their similar structure. Always pause and check which relationship you intend before choosing one.

Common Mistake — Omitting the relative form after ina in a location question

Error: *Ina banki yana? (using the ordinary continuative form yana instead of the required relative form)

Correction: Ina bankin yake? — When a question word like ina precedes the subject, Hausa requires the relative-continuative form (yake, not yana; take, not tana). This is the same pattern you learned for origin questions in Chapter 3. Check whether your sentence is an ordinary statement or a question beginning with a question word — the form of the subject word depends on it.

Common Mistake — Treating ka/ki as automatically more polite than ku

Error: Assuming ka or ki is always the "polite singular" form and avoiding ku for one person out of a belief that plural address is always for groups only.

Correction: In this course, ku is taught as the safe default for respectful address to one person of higher status, age, or unfamiliarity, alongside its use for groups. Ka and ki appear in specific taught command frames. When in doubt about which form fits a respectful one-person context, ku is the more reliable choice at this stage of the course.

Looking Ahead

Abin da ka/ki koya yanzu — What you know now

You can now name common buildings, public services, and forms of transportation; ask where a place is located; and give, follow, and negate directions using three distinct command patterns — the singular imperative, the respectful or plural subjunctive form, and the negative command with kada. You have also practiced motion verbs and the prepositions that describe position and route, and you have seen the relative-continuative subject forms first introduced in Chapter 3 return in a new context: location questions. Looking back: every gender agreement, existence and possession pattern from Chapter 6, and continuative form from Chapter 7 supports the direction-giving you are now doing in connected, communicative Hausa.

Babi na 12: Yanayi da Lokutan Shekara — Weather and Seasons

In Chapter 12, you move from navigating town to talking about the conditions around you. You will learn to describe current weather, ask about conditions elsewhere, and discuss seasonal patterns across different Hausa-speaking regions. The chapter introduces the impersonal construction with ana, used for general statements where no specific subject is named, and gives you your first basic real conditions with idan — the same word you encountered receptively in this chapter's reading passages. You will also see your first fixed future forms, which become fully productive in Chapter 14.

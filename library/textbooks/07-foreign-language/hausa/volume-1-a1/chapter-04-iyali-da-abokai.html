Iyali da Abokai

Family and Friends · Speak and Write Hausa: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify and describe immediate and extended family members using the correct Hausa kinship terms with their grammatical gender and plural forms.
  2. State relationships between people using possessive phrases with the genitive linker -n (masculine and plural) and -r (feminine).
  3. Use high-frequency possessive pronoun forms to express personal possession: na, ta, na (1sg); naka/taka (2sg m.); naki/taki (2sg f.); nasa/tasa (3sg m.); nata/tata (3sg f.); namu/tamu (1pl); naku/taku (2pl); nasu/tasu (3pl).
  4. Ask and state ages using numbers already learned.
  5. Describe people using introductory true adjectives with correct masculine, feminine, and plural agreement.
  6. Use introductory mai quality expressions to convey meanings that Hausa expresses through a noun construction rather than a standalone adjective.
  7. Produce a short connected oral and written presentation about a family or community network.
  8. Pronounce kinship terms accurately, paying particular attention to tone and vowel length in these high-frequency words, and write apostrophes correctly where standard Hausa spelling requires them.

Communication Scenario

After the first sessions of the Kano language program, participants are getting to know each other better. The topic of family comes up naturally — who lives with whom, how many siblings someone has, what relatives do for work, and what people are like as individuals. To join these conversations in Hausa, you need the vocabulary for family members and the grammatical tools that link people to each other. This chapter introduces both.

  1. When you say "Amina's book" in Hausa, you do not use an apostrophe-s the way English does. Instead, a short linking word is attached to the end of the possessed noun — the book — and this linker changes depending on the gender of the possessed noun, not of the possessor. This is the genitive linker, and it is one of Hausa's most important and frequently used grammatical tools. You will encounter it in every chapter from this point forward.
  2. English relies heavily on adjectives to describe people: "a tall woman," "a kind man," "a busy family." Hausa has true adjectives, but many English adjective meanings are expressed differently in Hausa — through noun-based constructions or through a form built with mai (having, possessing). This chapter introduces both types so you can build accurate descriptions from the start.
  3. Family is a domain where language and culture are inseparable. The vocabulary in this chapter reflects household structures and social relationships common in Hausa-speaking communities, which may differ from what you are used to. Kinship terms may be used more broadly in Hausa than in English, and some terms have no exact English equivalent. Pay attention to the cultural notes as well as the grammar.

Pronunciation and Boko

Tone and vowel length in kinship terms

Hausa kinship terms are among the highest-frequency words in the language, and they carry specific tone patterns that distinguish them from other words. Ordinary Boko does not mark tone, so you must learn each word's tonal shape through listening and imitation. The brief descriptions below give you a starting point; your audio model or instructor will demonstrate each word.

WordMeaningPronunciation guide (for listening focus)
ubafatherTwo short vowels; the word is said with level-high tone on both syllables in many contexts. Listen for how it sounds in a phrase like uba na (my father) where the linker follows.
uwamotherThe w glide is fully pronounced between the two vowels. Do not reduce this to one syllable. High-falling pattern in many utterances.
'ya'yachildrenBoth syllables begin with the implosive 'y — a catch of the breath unlike plain y. The vowel in each syllable is short. Practice the implosive by listening carefully and avoiding the smooth English y.
ɗasonThe hooked ɗ is an implosive — the airstream moves into the mouth rather than out of it at the moment of release. This is not the same sound as plain d. Listen to the contrast between ɗa (son) and da (with/and).
iyayeparentsThree syllables: i-ya-ye. The vowels are short. Give each syllable equal weight; do not stress the first syllable the way English would.
ƙaneyounger siblingThe hooked ƙ is an ejective — produced with a burst of air from the glottis. Contrast it with plain k in other words. The e at the end is pronounced clearly, not reduced to a schwa.
aboki / abokiyafriend (m./f.)The plural abokai ends in a diphthong: -ai. Pronounce both vowel sounds distinctly, not as a single English-style long vowel.
Sound Focus — Apostrophes in Hausa spelling: two different uses

This chapter introduces several words that use an apostrophe in their standard Boko spelling. It is important to distinguish two separate uses of the apostrophe in Hausa:

Use 1 — The implosive consonant 'y: When an apostrophe precedes y, it signals the distinct implosive consonant taught in Chapter 1. Words like 'ya'ya (children) and 'yan'uwa (siblings) both contain this consonant — twice, in the case of 'ya'ya. Each apostrophe-y in these words represents a single consonant, not a pause or a decoration.

Use 2 — A glottal stop or syllable boundary: The apostrophe in words like ma'aikaci (worker, from Chapter 3) and jami'i (official) marks a catch in the throat — a glottal stop — between two vowel sounds. This is not the same as 'y; it is a brief interruption of the vowel, distinct from the smooth vowel sequence in, say, uba or uwa.

When you read a Hausa word containing an apostrophe, ask: is it before y (→ implosive consonant), or between vowels (→ glottal stop)? Both are real sounds, and both must be written correctly. Never omit an apostrophe from standard Hausa spelling because it seems unnecessary.

Reading possessive phrases as connected units

In Hausa, a possessive phrase such as ɗan Amina (Amina's son) or uban Bello (Bello's father) is read as a connected unit — the linker (-n or -r) is attached to the end of the first word. In normal speech, the phrase flows without a pause between the possessed noun and its linker. Practice reading the following phrases aloud as single rhythmic units:

  • uban MusaMusa's father (father-of Musa)
  • uwar KandeKande's mother (mother-of Kande)
  • abokin BelloBello's friend (friend-of Bello)
  • abokiyar HauwaHauwa's friend (friend-of Hauwa)
  • 'ya'yan malamithe teacher's children (children-of teacher)
Pronunciation Practice — Consonant sequences in plural kinship forms

Several kinship plurals contain consonant sequences that do not appear in English words and may require extra practice. Give each consonant its full value:

  • 'yan'uwa (siblings) — begins with the implosive 'y, then a, then n, then 'u with another implosive, then wa.
  • ƙannenni (younger siblings) — the nn sequence is a genuine double nasal; hold it briefly. The hooked ƙ at the start requires the ejective articulation.
  • kakanni (grandparents) — three syllables; the double nn in the plural is held longer than a single n.
  • jikoki (grandchildren) — ends in -ki; pronounce this clearly, not as an English silent syllable.

If a consonant sequence feels difficult, slow it down syllable by syllable first, then build up to normal speed. Do not skip over consonants that seem unfamiliar.

Grammar Instruction

4.1 The genitive linker: introducing -n and -r

Purpose: express possession and relationship between nouns using the genitive linker that agrees with the possessed noun.

When you want to say that one thing belongs to or is associated with another — "Musa's father," "the teacher's classroom," "Amina's friend" — Hausa attaches a short linking element to the end of the first noun (the possessed noun, the thing that is owned or related). This element is called the genitive linker. It comes in two forms:

-n (masculine singular or plural possessed noun) -r (feminine singular possessed noun)
uban Musa
Musa's father		 uwar Kande
Kande's mother
abokin Bello
Bello's friend (m.)		 abokiyar Hauwa
Hauwa's friend (f.)
'ya'yan malami
the teacher's children		 makarantar yara
the children's school
sunan uba
the father's name		 sunar uwa
the mother's name

The key rule: The linker agrees with the possessed noun — the noun it attaches to — not with the possessor. If the possessed noun is masculine singular, use -n. If it is feminine singular, use -r. If the possessed noun is plural, use -n regardless of the singular gender.

Language Focus — The linker follows the possessed noun, not the possessor

This is the most important single fact about the genitive linker, and it is the point most likely to cause errors at first. Consider these two phrases:

  • uban Kande — Kande's father. The possessed noun is uba (father — masculine). Even though Kande is a girl's name, the linker is -n because uba is masculine.
  • uwar Musa — Musa's mother. The possessed noun is uwa (mother — feminine). Even though Musa is a boy's name, the linker is -r because uwa is feminine.

The possessor — the person who owns or relates — does not determine the linker. Only the possessed noun does. Whenever you form a genitive phrase, ask: What gender and number is the noun the linker attaches to? That is the only question that matters for choosing -n or -r.

When a noun ends in a vowel, the linker is added directly: uba → uban; uwa → uwar. When a noun ends in a consonant, the linker is added after the final consonant: aboki → abokin; abokiya → abokiyar; malami → malamin; malama → malamar. Noun-final vowels that change in the linked form must be learned with each noun — do not assume a universal rule.

Language Building Block
possessed noun + linker (-n / -r) + possessor

uwar Aminu
Aminu's mother — uwa (mother) is feminine → linker is -r
'ya'yan Aminu

Choose -n for masculine singular or any plural possessed noun. Choose -r for feminine singular possessed nouns. The possessor (Aminu) does not determine the choice.

4.2 Possessive pronoun forms

Purpose: express that something belongs to a particular person using possessive pronouns that follow the genitive linker.

When the possessor is a pronoun (my, your, his, her, our, their) rather than a noun, Hausa uses a set of possessive pronoun forms. These forms are built on the same linker system: na- for masculine or plural possessed nouns; ta- for feminine singular possessed nouns. The possessor is then encoded in the ending.

Person / Gender With masculine/plural noun (na- base) With feminine noun (ta- base) English
1st singularnawatawamy, mine
2nd singular masculinenakatakayour (m.), yours
2nd singular femininenakitakiyour (f.), yours
3rd singular masculinenasatasahis
3rd singular femininenatatataher, hers
1st pluralnamutamuour, ours
2nd pluralnakutakuyour (pl.), yours
3rd pluralnasutasutheir, theirs

The possessive pronoun follows the noun it modifies. The choice between the na- set and the ta- set is determined — just like the noun linker — by the gender of the possessed noun, not by the gender of the possessor.

  • uba nawamy father — uba is masculine → nawapossessed noun (m.) + na- possessive
  • uwa tawamy mother — uwa is feminine → tawapossessed noun (f.) + ta- possessive
  • aboki nasahis friend (m.) — aboki is masculine → nasa
  • abokiya tasahis friend (f.) — abokiya is feminine → tasa
  • 'ya'ya namuour children — 'ya'ya is plural → namu
  • ɗa nakiyour son (speaking to a female) — ɗa is masculine → naki
  • diya takiyour daughter (speaking to a female) — diya is feminine → taki
Language Note — Two ways to express possession: noun linker vs. possessive pronoun

Hausa offers two related structures for expressing possession. When the possessor is a noun (a person's name or a common noun), the genitive linker is attached to the possessed noun and the possessor noun follows: uban Musa (Musa's father). When the possessor is a pronoun (my, your, his, etc.), the possessive pronoun form follows the possessed noun directly: uba nawa (my father). Both structures position the possessed noun first — the opposite of English apostrophe-s order. This consistent ordering is a useful pattern to internalize: in Hausa, the thing possessed always comes first.

4.3 Adjectives: masculine, feminine, and plural agreement

Purpose: describe people and family members using true adjectives with correct gender and number agreement.

Hausa has a set of true adjectives. In a predicate (after ne/ce), the adjective typically follows the noun or pronoun that it describes. In an attributive phrase (before a noun, as in "a tall man"), the adjective follows the noun and is linked to it. This chapter focuses on the predicate use, which is more common at this level.

The adjective must agree in gender and number with the noun or pronoun it describes.

  • Musa tsawo ne.Musa is tall. (masculine subject → masculine adjective form)
  • Hauwa tsawanya ce.Hauwa is tall. (feminine subject → feminine adjective form)
  • Yara tsawaye ne.The children are tall. (plural subject → plural adjective form)
  • Uba babba ne.Father is big / Father is the senior one. (babba has the same form for m. and f. singular)
  • Ɗan'uwa ƙarami ne.The (younger male) sibling is small / young. (masculine → ƙarami)
  • Diya ƙarama ce.The daughter is small / young. (feminine → ƙarama)
  • 'Ya'ya ƙanana ne.The children are small / young. (plural → ƙanana)

babba: This adjective has the same form for masculine and feminine singular (babba). The plural attributive form is often manyan (as in mutane manyan — older people) or manya in predicate use. This is an irregular pattern — learn it as a lexical set rather than by applying a suffix rule.

4.4 mai quality expressions

Purpose: describe people's qualities using the invariant mai construction when no single adjective form exists in Hausa.

Many English adjectives — kind, hardworking, patient, happy — do not have direct one-word Hausa adjective equivalents. Instead, Hausa uses the word mai (literally "possessor of, one who has") followed by a noun that names the quality. The whole expression then functions like an adjective in a sentence.

The important feature of mai expressions for learners: mai does not change for gender or number. The same form is used for a man, a woman, and a group.

  • Uwa mai kirki ce.Mother is kind. (mai kirki invariant — no change for feminine)
  • Uba mai hankali ne.Father is wise/patient.
  • Abokin nawa mai aiki ne.My friend is hardworking. (male friend)
  • Abokiyar nawa mai aiki ce.My friend is hardworking. (female friend — note: mai aiki unchanged, but ce changes)
  • Abokai nawa mai aiki ne.My friends are hardworking. (plural — mai aiki unchanged, ne for plural)
  • Ɗa nawa mai farin ciki ne.My son is cheerful/happy.
Language Note — When Hausa does not use a single adjective

Hausa has fewer standalone adjectives than English. Where English uses a single descriptive word (kind, patient, busy), Hausa very often uses a mai construction built on a noun that names the relevant quality. For example, "kindness" in Hausa is kirki (a noun), and "kind" is expressed as mai kirki — "one who has kindness." This construction is not a workaround or a simplification: it is the natural, standard way to express these meanings in Hausa. Learning mai expressions is therefore not a preliminary step toward learning "real" adjectives — it is itself the correct and complete way to express those qualities. Do not try to create a Hausa adjective by analogy when a mai expression is what the language uses.

4.5 Asking and stating ages

Purpose: ask how old someone is and state ages using numbers already learned.

Age in Hausa is expressed using the word shekara / shekaru (year/years). The construction used here is formulaic — it employs a possession frame whose full grammar is taught in Chapter 6. Learn these patterns as complete communication expressions:

  • Yaya shekaru naka?How old are you? (asking a male) — literally "How-are years yours?"
  • Yaya shekaru naki?How old are you? (asking a female)
  • Yaya shekaru nasa?How old is he?
  • Yaya shekaru nata?How old is she?
  • Ina da shekaru talatin.I am thirty years old. (literally "I have years thirty")
  • Yana da shekaru ashirin da biyar.He is twenty-five years old.
  • Tana da shekaru arba'in da biyu.She is forty-two years old.
  • Yara suna da shekaru goma.The children are ten years old. (formulaic plural possession; full system in Chapter 7)

The number follows shekaru directly in age statements. Use the numbers from Chapters 2 and 3: units 1–20 from Chapter 2, decades and compounds 21–100 from Chapter 3. Numbers above 100 are not required for this chapter.

4.6 Introducing a third person — expanded

Purpose: introduce a family member or friend using possession, description, and age in connected sentences.

You learned in Chapter 3 how to introduce someone by name, origin, and profession. This chapter adds the tools to describe relationships, qualities, and ages in a connected presentation. The pattern below illustrates how these elements combine:

  • Wannan ubar nawa ce. Sunanta Halima ce. Malama ce. Tana da shekaru hamsin da uku. Mai hankali ce.This is my mother. Her name is Halima. She is a teacher. She is fifty-three years old. She is wise.
  • Wannan abokin nawa ne. Sunansa Dauda ne. Ya fito daga Nijar. Yana da shekaru ashirin da bakwai. Tsawo ne kuma mai kirki ne.This is my friend. His name is Dauda. He is from Niger. He is twenty-seven years old. He is tall and kind.

New connector: kuma = and (connecting two statements or adding information). This is the first connector introduced productively; others follow in later chapters. Kuma connects clauses and whole statements, not just single nouns — for nouns joined in a list, da (and, with) is used.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: A baya kadan bayan darasi na yau, Amina da Bello suna magana game da iyalansu. Amina tana nuna wa Bello hoto na iyalinta. (Setting: Shortly after today's class, Amina and Bello are talking about their families. Amina shows Bello a photo of her family.)

BELLO Wane hoto ne wannan, Amina?What photo is this, Amina?
AMINA Hoton iyali nawa ne. Wannan ubar nawa ne — sunansa Oumarou ne. Kuma wannan uwar nawa ce — sunanta Fatime ce. Malama ce, kamar ni.It is my family photo. This is my father — his name is Oumarou. And this is my mother — her name is Fatime. She is a teacher, like me.
BELLO Yaya shekaru nasu?How old are they?
AMINA Ubar nawa yana da shekaru sittin da biyu. Uwar nawa tana da shekaru hamsin da takwas. Manya ne, amma suna da lafiya.My father is sixty-two years old. My mother is fifty-eight years old. They are senior / older, but they are in good health.
BELLO Kana da 'yan'uwa?Do you have siblings?
AMINA I, ina da 'yan'uwa uku. Ɗan'uwa guda biyu da 'yar'uwa guda ɗaya. Wannan ɗan'uwana ne — sunansa Ali ne. Saurayi ne kuma mai aiki ne — jami'i ne a gwamnati. Kuma wannan 'yar'uwata ce — sunanta Rabi ce. Ƙarama ce — tana da shekaru goma sha tara. Yarinya ce tukuna, amma mai hankali sosai.Yes, I have three siblings. Two brothers and one sister. This is my brother — his name is Ali. He is a young man and hardworking — he is a government official. And this is my sister — her name is Rabi. She is young — she is nineteen years old. She is still a girl, but very sensible.
BELLO Kai kuwa? Iyalinka yaya suke?And you? What is your family like?
AMINA Na gode da tambayar. Iyali nawa — uba nawa, uwa nawa, da 'yan'uwa biyu — suna zaune a Niamey. Ni kaɗai nake zaune a Kano saboda aikin koyarwa. Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

  • Every genitive linker (-n or -r) agrees with the possessed noun, not the possessor.
  • Every possessive pronoun form (nawa / tawa, etc.) agrees with the gender and number of the possessed noun.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Family and household in Hausa-speaking communities

The word iyali in Hausa can refer to what English speakers call the nuclear family — parents and children — but it often encompasses a much wider circle. In many Hausa-speaking households, particularly in northern Nigeria and southern Niger, the residential compound (gida) may house multiple generations and branches of a family: grandparents, adult children with their own spouses and children, and sometimes younger relatives from the extended family who have come to live with the household for education, support, or social reasons.

Some kinship terms in Hausa reflect this wider social structure. Inna and innata are used for a father's other wives in a polygynous household — a social reality in many Hausa communities that has no standard English kinship term. Kawu (maternal uncle) and goggo (paternal aunt) have specific relational meanings that differ from the more generic English "uncle" and "aunt." Kinship terms may also be used as forms of address beyond their literal biological meaning — calling an older man baba or an older woman uwa can be a sign of respect and affection, not a statement of parentage.

When discussing family in Hausa, it is appropriate to ask about someone's relatives with genuine interest — questions about family health and well-being (Yaya iyalinku?) are common courtesy. However, detailed questions about household structure, finances, or personal family matters should follow the other person's lead. As in any social context, the right approach is to listen more than you ask and to let the conversation develop naturally.

Culture in Context — Age, seniority, and respect

Age carries significant social meaning in many Hausa-speaking communities. Older people are generally addressed with greater formality and deference, and seniority within a family affects how siblings relate to one another. The distinction between babba (older, senior) and ƙarami/ƙarama (younger, junior) is not merely descriptive — it reflects a social order in which older siblings have responsibilities toward younger ones and younger siblings show respect to elders.

Asking someone's age directly (Yaya shekaru naka?) is generally acceptable in casual introductions and educational settings, but the appropriate moment and relationship matter. Among strangers in a formal setting, age may be inferred from context rather than asked outright. When in doubt, follow the lead of the people around you.

The word tsoho / tsohuwa (old man / old woman) is descriptive, but using it directly to address an older person as a second-person term would generally be inappropriate — it is used in third-person descriptions only. Older people are typically addressed by name, title, or kinship term (baba, mama, kaka, or a professional title).

Regional Variation — Kinship terms across the Hausa world

Kinship vocabulary shows meaningful variation across Hausa-speaking regions, and some terms differ between northern Nigeria and southern Niger. In some communities, baba is used for "father" rather than or alongside uba; in others, mama is commonly used for mother in addition to or instead of uwa. The productive terms for this course are the Standard Hausa forms given in the vocabulary tables (uba, uwa), which are widely understood. When you encounter baba or mama in speech or reading, recognize them as common alternatives without treating them as errors — they are fully standard in many communities.

The distinction between paternal and maternal kinship (for example, kawu for maternal uncle versus baffa for paternal uncle) is a feature of traditional Hausa kinship terminology that is maintained in many communities, though usage varies by generation and region. This course teaches both receptively; learners are expected to recognize the distinction when they encounter it.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Choosing the linker based on the possessor's gender

Error: *uwan Musa (using -r because Musa is being described as having a mother, not because of any feature of the possessor)

Correction: uwar Musa — the linker agrees with uwa (mother — feminine), not with Musa (who is male). The question to ask is always: what gender is the noun the linker attaches to? Uwa is feminine → use -r. It does not matter that Musa is male. Similarly: uban Kande (Kande's father) uses -n because uba (father) is masculine, not because Kande is female.

Common Mistake — Using the wrong possessive base (na- vs ta-)

Error: *uba nata (using the ta- base because the possessor is female)

Correction: uba nasa (if the possessor is a male — e.g., his father) or uba nata (if the possessor is a female — e.g., her father). Wait — in this case the error is more subtle: uba is masculine, so the base is na-. The possessor's gender determines which person ending to use (-wa, -ka, -ki, etc.), but the na- vs. ta- choice is determined by the possessed noun. Uba (father) is masculine → always use the na- set: uba nawa (my father), uba naka (your father, m.), uba naki (your father, f.), uba nasa (his father), uba nata (her father). The ta- set would apply only to a feminine noun: uwa tawa (my mother), uwa taka (your mother, m.), etc.

Common Mistake — Making mai expressions agree for gender

Error: *mai kirkiya ce or *maikirkance ne (attempting to add gender endings to mai kirki)

Correction: mai kirki ce (she is kind) or mai kirki ne (he is kind). The expression mai kirki is invariant — it does not change for gender or number. Only the following identification particle (ne or ce) and the surrounding pronoun or noun carry gender information. Trying to add endings to mai expressions is never correct in standard Hausa.

Common Mistake — Writing 'ya'ya or 'yan'uwa without both apostrophes

Error: *ya'ya or *yanya instead of 'ya'ya; *yan'uwa instead of 'yan'uwa

Correction: Both words begin with the implosive consonant 'y, which requires the apostrophe to be written. In 'ya'ya, each syllable begins with 'y — both apostrophes must appear. In 'yan'uwa, the word begins with 'y — the initial apostrophe must appear. Omitting an apostrophe changes the word's written form and misrepresents its pronunciation. These are among the most common Hausa words for family — write them correctly every time.

Common Mistake — Using kuma to join nouns in a list

Error: *uba kuma uwa kuma 'ya'ya (using kuma to list nouns)

Correction: uba da uwa da 'ya'ya — when joining nouns or noun phrases in a list, Hausa uses da (and, with). Kuma connects clauses or statements: Tsawo ne kuma mai kirki ne (He is tall and he is kind). Think of da as joining things; kuma as joining statements. Mixing these up is a very common error for English speakers, because English "and" does both jobs equally well.

Bitar gaba ɗaya — Sashi na D (sample paragraph)

Ina da iyali kaɗan. Ubar nawa sunansa Musa ne. Yana da shekaru sittin. Malami ne kuma mai hankali ne sosai. Uwar nawa sunanta Asabe ce. Tana da shekaru hamsin da biyar. Ba malama ba ce — ma'aikaciya ce a asibiti. Tana da shekaru hamsin da biyar kuma mai farin ciki ce. Ina da 'yar'uwa guda ɗaya — sunanta Halima ce. Ita budurwa ce, tana da shekaru ashirin da uku. Ɗaliba ce a jami'a.

Looking Ahead

Abin da ka/ki koya yanzu — What you know now

You can now describe your family and social network in connected Hausa — naming relationships using genitive linker phrases, expressing possession with pronoun forms, giving ages, and characterizing people with adjectives and mai expressions. Two of Hausa's most productive tools — the genitive linker and the possessive pronoun system — are now part of your active repertoire, and you will use them in every chapter that follows. You have also expanded your connector inventory with kuma (and/also, joining clauses) alongside da (and, joining nouns). Looking back: every identification sentence, greeting, gender agreement, number, and origin expression from Chapters 1 through 3 is now woven into your family presentations. That cumulative build is what allows each chapter to go further than the one before.

Babi na 5: Tattaunawarmu ta Farko — Our First Conversations

Chapter 5 is the first cumulative review chapter of the course. No major new grammar system is introduced. Instead, you will bring together everything from Chapters 1 through 4 — greetings and identity, classroom language, personal information and origin, professions and languages, family and friendship, possession and description — into sustained conversations and a longer written profile. You will practice conversation-management strategies: how to ask for repetition, how to signal that you did not understand, and how to keep a conversation moving even when your language is not yet perfect. The chapter closes with a 60-80-word personal profile and a structured introductory conversation of two to three minutes — the first integrated speaking task of the course.

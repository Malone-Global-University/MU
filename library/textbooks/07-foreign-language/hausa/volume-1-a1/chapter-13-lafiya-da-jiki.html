Lafiya da Jiki

Health and the Body · Speak and Write Hausa: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify common body parts using correct gender and plural forms.
  2. Describe simple symptoms using sensation and possession-based expressions.
  3. Ask for medical or practical assistance using polite request frames.
  4. Express ability and inability with iya.
  5. Express necessity with dole.
  6. Talk about body-part possession using the genitive linkers -n and -r reviewed from Chapter 4.
  7. Use a small set of introductory indirect-object frames to say that something happens to or for someone.
  8. Understand and give simple practical advice in a clinic or pharmacy context.

Communication Scenario

You wake up not feeling well, and over the course of the day you need to explain the problem to a friend, visit a clinic, and pick up medicine at a pharmacy. This chapter prepares you to name body parts, describe what hurts or feels wrong, say what you can and cannot do because of how you feel, state what you must do (such as rest or see a doctor), and ask others for help in a clear, respectful way.

  1. Hausa does not use one general verb equivalent to English "to hurt" the way many learners expect. Symptom statements typically use sensation, possession, or body-part-as-subject constructions instead of a single dedicated "pain" verb. As you move through the grammar section, notice the structure each example actually uses rather than assuming a literal English template will transfer directly.
  2. Health scenarios in this chapter are designed purely for language practice. They are not medical advice, and the vocabulary here will not prepare you to diagnose or treat an illness — only to communicate clearly about a common, everyday health concern.

Pronunciation and Boko

Tone and length in body and symptom vocabulary

Several body-part words carry meaningful vowel length, including baya (back) and ƙafa (foot/leg). The hooked ƙ in ƙafa and ƙirji must remain clearly distinct from a plain k; confusing the two can change intelligibility, not just accent. Practice zazzaɓi carefully: it contains the hooked ɓ, which must never be replaced with a plain b.

Sound Focus — iya versus similar short words

Iya (to be able to) is short and should be practiced in full phrases, such as ina iya tafiya, rather than in isolation, so that its tone and vowel length are anchored to a meaningful pattern. Listen for the distinction between iya (ability) and unrelated short words you may already know from earlier chapters; do not rely on spelling alone to fix the pronunciation.

Request and advice intonation

Polite requests for help, such as don Allah ka taimaka mini, typically carry a gentle falling pattern across the final phrase rather than a sharp command-like rhythm. Practice the contrast between a flat command tone and a softer request tone using the example sentences in the grammar section.

Reading clinic and pharmacy notices

Apply the Boko-reading principles from earlier chapters as you read the clinic sign and appointment note in this chapter: distinguish hooked letters (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ) and 'y from their plain counterparts, and remember that tone and vowel length are present in speech even though ordinary Boko does not mark them.

Grammar Instruction

13.1 Describing symptoms: sensation, possession, and body-part constructions

Purpose: describe how you feel and what is wrong using structures already familiar from earlier chapters, rather than a single invented "pain" verb.

Hausa health expressions draw on more than one structure you have already studied. A common pattern uses the continuative subject form plus the verbal noun jin ("feeling," from the verb ji, "to feel") followed by the symptom: ina jin ciwo ("I feel pain," literally "I am feeling pain"). Another pattern uses possession with da, reviewed from Chapter 6: ina da mura ("I have a cold," literally "I am with a cold"). A third pattern names the body part directly as the location of the problem, often with a genitive linker: ciwon kai ("headache," literally "pain of head").

  • Ina jin ciwo a ciki.I feel pain in my stomach.continuative + jin (verbal noun) + ciwo + location
  • Ina jin zazzaɓi.I feel feverish.
  • Ina da mura.I have a cold.possession with da, reviewed from Chapter 6
  • Ina da ciwon kai.I have a headache.possession with da + ciwon (linked noun) + kai
  • Ina tari.I am coughing. (continuative with the noun tari functioning as the activity)
Language Focus — Hausa has no single verb for English "to hurt"

English speakers often look for one Hausa verb that behaves like "hurt" in "my head hurts." Hausa does not organize symptoms this way. Instead, you select among structures you already control:

  • jin + symptom, for a felt sensation (ina jin ciwo)
  • possession with da, for a named condition you currently have (ina da mura)
  • a linked noun phrase naming the affected body part (ciwon kai, ciwon ciki)

Choosing the right structure for the right kind of symptom statement is the central skill of this section, just as choosing among ana, akwai, and babu was the central skill for weather in Chapter 12.

13.2 Body-part possession with genitive linkers

Purpose: talk about someone's specific body part using the linkers -n and -r from Chapter 4.

To name a specific person's body part, link the body part to a possessor exactly as you learned in Chapter 4: a masculine or plural body-part noun takes -n, and a feminine body-part noun takes -r, with the linker agreeing with the possessed body part, not the possessor.

  • kan AuduAudu's headkai (masc.) + -n + Audu
  • ƙafar HauwaHauwa's footƙafa (fem.) + -r + Hauwa
  • cikinsahis stomachciki (masc.) + -n + pronominal possessor -sa (he/his)
  • hannuntaher handhannu (masc.) + -n + pronominal possessor -ta (she/her)

These pronominal possessive forms (-sa, -ta, and similar forms) were introduced for family possession in Chapter 4 and are simply applied here to body-part vocabulary. No new linker grammar is introduced; this section recycles and extends Chapter 4's system into a new vocabulary domain, as required by the spiral-review policy.

13.3 Ability with iya

Purpose: express what someone can or cannot do, often because of how they feel.

Iya follows a continuative subject form and is itself followed by a verbal noun or a verb phrase expressing the action someone is or is not able to do.

  • Ina iya tafiya yau.I am able to go today.
  • Ba ya iya tafiya yau, domin ciwon ƙafa.He cannot go today, because of foot pain.negative continuative + iya + verbal noun
  • Tana iya cin abinci, amma a hankali.She is able to eat, but slowly.
  • Ba mu iya kwana sosai domin tari.We cannot sleep well because of the cough.
Language Note — negating ability

To negate ability, use the negative continuative pattern from Chapter 8 around iya: ba ya iya..., ba ni iya..., and so on. Iya itself does not change; the negation is carried by the subject-form-plus-particle pattern you already control. Do not invent a separate negative form for iya alone.

13.4 Necessity with dole

Purpose: express what someone must do, especially as practical health advice.

Dole expresses necessity. It commonly appears as dole ne followed by a continuative subject form and a verbal noun, or directly before a continuative statement.

  • Dole ne ka huta.You must rest.dole ne + subjunctive-style command form (formulaic at this stage)
  • Dole ne in ga likita.I must see a doctor.
  • Dole ne mu sha magani kullum.We must take medicine every day.

The subject forms following dole ne in these examples (ka, in, mu) overlap with subjunctive-style forms that receive full productive treatment beginning in Chapter 11 and continuing through Chapter 17. Learn the complete dole ne pattern now as a fixed necessity construction; its full integration with the subjunctive system is consolidated later in the course.

13.5 Introductory indirect-object frames

Purpose: say that something happens to or for someone, using a small set of high-frequency verified frames.

Some common requests and statements in health contexts involve an indirect object — the person something is done to or for. At this introductory stage, learn the complete phrase mini ("to/for me") and maka/miki ("to/for you," masculine/feminine) as fixed indirect-object forms attached to a small set of verified verbs, rather than analyzing the full indirect-object system.

  • Don Allah ka taimaka mini.Please help me.taimaka (to help) + mini (to/for me)
  • Likita ya ba ni magani.The doctor gave me medicine. (recognition-only completive form; full completive system begins in Chapter 16)
  • Ina son ka taimaka mini da wannan.I want you to help me with this. (formulaic; full subjunctive sequencing in Chapter 17)
Language Note — indirect objects are introduced narrowly here

This chapter teaches only mini and maka/miki attached to the verb taimaka (to help) as a productive request frame. Other indirect-object forms and verb-conditioned alternations belong to the fuller treatment in Chapters 14, 16, and 17. Treat don Allah ka taimaka mini as a complete, reliable request pattern you can use immediately, without expecting to freely substitute every verb into the same frame yet.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Amina ta kira abokiyarta Zainab domin ba ta jin daɗi. Daga baya, Amina ta je asibiti don ganawa da likita.

(Setting: Amina calls her friend Zainab because she is not feeling well. Later, Amina goes to the clinic to see a doctor.)

ZAINAB Sannu, Amina. Lafiya lau?Hello, Amina. Are you well?
AMINA Sannu, Zainab. A'a, ba na jin daɗi sosai. Ina jin zazzaɓi, kuma ina da ciwon kai.Hello, Zainab. No, I do not feel well at all. I feel feverish, and I have a headache.
ZAINAB Yi haƙuri da haka. Kina iya tafiya asibiti yau?I'm sorry to hear that. Are you able to go to the hospital today?
AMINA Ina tunanin haka. Ba na iya zama a gida kawai; dole ne in ga likita.I am thinking the same. I cannot just stay home; I must see a doctor.
(A asibiti — At the hospital)
LIKITA Sannu. Ina jin ciwo a jikinki?Hello. Where do you feel pain in your body?
AMINA Ina jin ciwon kai sosai, kuma ina jin zazzaɓi tun jiya.I feel a strong headache, and I have felt feverish since yesterday.
LIKITA Na gane. Dole ne ki huta sosai, ki sha ruwa da yawa, kuma ki sha wannan magani kowace rana. Don Allah ki taimaka wa kanki ta hanyar zama a gida har sati guda.I understand. You must rest well, drink plenty of water, and take this medicine every day. Please help yourself by staying home for a week.
AMINA Na gode, likita. Akwai wani abu kuma da ya kamata na yi?Thank you, doctor. Is there anything else I should do? (formulaic recognition-only frame)
LIKITA A'a, abin da na ce ya wadatar. Idan ba ki ji daɗi bayan kwana uku, ki dawo nan.No, what I said is enough. If you do not feel better after three days, come back here.

Vocabulary notes for the dialogue: lafiya lau = a fixed greeting response meaning "completely well" (formulaic); ba na jin daɗi = I do not feel well (literally "I am not feeling sweetness"); tun jiya = since yesterday; na gane = I understand; ta hanyar = by means of, through; ya kamata = it is fitting/appropriate (formulaic; full modal analysis deferred); wadatar = to be sufficient; kwana uku = three days.

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. What two symptoms does Amina describe to Zainab?
  2. What does Zainab ask Amina, and how does Amina respond using iya?
  3. What three things does the doctor tell Amina she must do?
  4. Find the sentence in which the doctor gives a conditional instruction with idan. Write it in Hausa and explain the condition and result.
  5. Find one example of the indirect-object form mini or a related form in the dialogue, or note that it does not appear and explain why wa kanki is used instead.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Health care settings and access

Access to clinics, hospitals, and pharmacies varies widely across Hausa-speaking regions and depends on factors such as location, infrastructure, and household resources. Urban centers such as Kano and Niamey have a range of public and private health-care facilities, while rural communities may rely more heavily on community health workers, traveling clinics, or pharmacies for routine concerns. The vocabulary and scenarios in this chapter are designed for general communication practice and should not be treated as a complete or universal picture of health-care access in any single country or community.

Family members frequently play an active role in a sick person's care, including speaking on their behalf, accompanying them to appointments, and making decisions about rest and treatment, as modeled in this chapter's reading passage. This pattern is common but not universal, and it varies by family, age, and individual circumstance.

Culture in Context — Asking after someone's health

Greetings throughout the Hausa-speaking world commonly include a question about well-being, as introduced in Chapter 1 with expressions like lafiya? A genuine, more detailed health concern — like the one in this chapter's dialogue — is typically introduced after an initial greeting exchange rather than as the very first words of a conversation, reflecting the broader greeting norms covered earlier in the course. This is a common pattern rather than an invariable rule, and directness varies by relationship, urgency, and individual style.

Regional Variation — Health vocabulary across Nigeria and Niger

Core health vocabulary such as likita, asibiti, and magani is widely shared and understood across Hausa-speaking Nigeria and Niger. Specific institutional terms, such as names for types of clinics or insurance and payment systems, differ by country and are tied to each nation's health-care system rather than to the Hausa language itself. This course teaches general, widely applicable vocabulary and labels country-specific institutional terms separately when they arise in later, more advanced material.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Inventing a Hausa verb meaning "to hurt"

Error: *Kaina yana ciwo ni. (treating "hurt" as a verb that takes the body part as subject and the person as object, by direct translation from English)

Correction: Ina jin ciwon kai. — Use the jin + symptom pattern, or possession with da, or a linked noun phrase. Hausa does not have a direct equivalent of the English verb "to hurt" used this way; select one of the three taught structures instead.

Common Mistake — Using the independent pronoun instead of the indirect-object form

Error: *Don Allah ka taimaka ni. (using the independent pronoun ni where the indirect-object form is required)

Correction: Don Allah ka taimaka mini. — The verb taimaka requires the indirect-object form mini ("to/for me"), not the independent pronoun ni. Learn this complete request frame as a fixed unit at this stage.

Common Mistake — Mismatching the genitive linker with the body part's gender

Error: *ƙafan Sani (using the masculine/plural linker -n with the feminine noun ƙafa)

Correction: ƙafar Sani — Ƙafa is feminine singular, so it takes the feminine linker -r, regardless of the possessor's gender. The linker agrees with the possessed noun, exactly as taught in Chapter 4.

Common Mistake — Treating dole ne as interchangeable with iya

Error: Using dole ne and iya as if they expressed the same idea, such as saying *Dole ne in tafi, amma ba na iya without distinguishing necessity from ability clearly in context.

Correction: Dole ne expresses necessity ("must"), while iya expresses ability ("can/is able to"). A sentence can combine both ideas meaningfully, such as Dole ne in tafi, amma ba na iya tafiya domin ciwon ƙafa ("I must go, but I cannot go because of foot pain") — here the contrast is the point, not a confusion between the two words.

Tambayoyin fahimta na tattaunawa (Dialogue comprehension)

  1. Amina describes feeling feverish (zazzaɓi) and having a headache (ciwon kai).
  2. Zainab asks whether Amina is able to go to the hospital today; Amina responds that she cannot just stay home and must see a doctor, implying ability/inability through context and the following dole ne statement.
  3. The doctor tells Amina to rest well, drink plenty of water, and take the medicine every day.
  4. Idan ba ki ji daɗi bayan kwana uku, ki dawo nan. Condition: if she does not feel better after three days. Result: she should come back.
  5. Mini itself does not appear; the doctor uses taimaka wa kanki ("help yourself") instead, which is a different, reflexive-style phrase rather than the basic indirect-object frame taught in this chapter — students should note this as a recognition-only variation.

Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

Kula na A: Medicine for a headache; take two tablets morning and evening; best taken after eating.

Kula na B: (1) Stomach pain since morning. (2) Rest and drink hot water. (3) If the pain does not go away after two days.

Kula na C: (a) F — the pain is in the foot, not the hand. (b) T. (c) T.

Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

  1. Patients can come see a doctor from eight to ten in the morning; everyone must bring their hospital card.
  2. Patients with severe fever or severe pain should come immediately, without waiting for an appointment.
  3. The writer describes stomach pain that began the previous night.
  4. The writer's mother said he or she must rest at home until tomorrow.
  5. Dole ne kowa ya kawo katin asibiti (Rubutu na 1) — necessity of bringing a hospital card. Mahaifiyata ta ce dole ne na huta a gida har gobe (Rubutu na 2) — necessity of resting at home.
  6. Rubutu na 1: Idan kuna jin zazzaɓi mai ƙarfi ko kuna jin ciwo mai tsanani, ku zo nan take. Condition: severe fever or severe pain. Result: come immediately. Rubutu na 2: Idan har yanzu ina jin ciwo gobe, za mu je asibiti. Condition: still feeling pain tomorrow. Result: going to the hospital.

  1. (a) jin   (b) da   (c) Ciwon
  2. (a) kan Bashir   (b) ƙafar Maryam

Bitar gaba ɗaya — Sashi na D (sample paragraph)

Sannu. Yi haƙuri, ban iya zuwa makaranta ba yau. Ina jin ciwon ciki sosai tun jiya da dare. Ba na iya cin abinci sosai. Mahaifiyata ta ce dole ne na huta a gida yau. Idan ciwon bai tafi ba gobe, za mu je asibiti. Na gode da fahimtarka.

Looking Ahead

Abin da ka/ki koya yanzu — What you know now

You can now identify common body parts with correct gender and plural forms; describe symptoms using jin, possession with da, and linked noun phrases; express ability and inability with iya; express necessity with dole ne; link body parts to possessors using the genitive linkers from Chapter 4; and make a basic polite request for help using the indirect-object frame mini. You have also continued to recycle possession and existence structures from Chapter 6, negative commands from Chapter 11, and conditional idan sentences from Chapter 12 — proof that each new chapter builds directly on what came before rather than starting over.

Babi na 14: A Wurin Aiki da Makaranta — Work and School

In Chapter 14, you move from health to work and school. You will learn to discuss occupations, school subjects, duties, and schedules, and to talk about the future using the construction with za. The chapter introduces purpose expressions with domin, building directly on the cause-and-result connectors don haka and saboda haka from Chapter 12. The ability and necessity constructions from this chapter, iya and dole, return as you discuss what you can do and must do at work or school, and the introductory indirect-object frame you learned here expands into a fuller set of practical request and obligation patterns.

Shirye-shirye da Gayyata

Plans and Invitations · Speak and Write Hausa: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Make an invitation to do something together and respond to one — accepting, declining, or proposing an alternative time or place.
  2. Use subjunctive subject-pronoun forms to make proposals, suggestions, and desired-action statements, including in sequences where a subjunctive result clause follows a future or conditional premise.
  3. Consolidate the future construction with za, including its first-person singular contracted form, and produce negative future sentences with the complete discontinuous ba … ba construction.
  4. Contrast the three central aspect constructions — continuative, completive, and future — in a single connected exchange about what someone is doing now, what they did before, and what they plan to do.
  5. Agree on a time and place for an arrangement and revise the plan when circumstances change.
  6. Use object pronouns and indirect-object frames in invitation and arrangement contexts.
  7. Write an invitation and a confirming or declining reply of 130–160 words in standard Boko.

Communication Scenario

A friend contacts you to invite you to a gathering next weekend. You want to accept but need to check a detail about the time. You also have a suggestion about where to meet first. Later, a change in your work schedule forces you to ask about moving the arrangement to a different day. This chapter prepares you to navigate the full arc of that interaction — from the first invitation through negotiation and confirmation — in Hausa.

  1. In Chapter 14 you learned the future construction with za and its negative. Chapter 17 consolidates those forms and builds on them by adding the subjunctive, which you have met in two earlier contexts: the fixed ku-form commands of Chapter 2 and the subjunctive-based commands and negative commands of Chapter 11. This chapter brings the subjunctive into fully productive use for proposals, suggestions, and desired-action sequencing. The subjunctive is the natural grammar of saying "let's," "I want you to," "shall we," and similar inviting or planning expressions.
  2. The central communicative challenge of this chapter is choosing among three subject-aspect constructions according to meaning and context. When you say Yana tafiya, you describe an ongoing activity now. When you say Yā tafi, you report a completed event. When you say Za ya tafi, you express future intent or prediction. These three are not interchangeable, and this chapter gives you the tools to select among them accurately in the same conversation.

Pronunciation and Boko

Tone and vowel length in subjunctive subject-pronoun forms

The subjunctive subject-pronoun forms introduced in this chapter are short and carry distinctive tone and vowel length. Several of them look identical or very similar to other forms in print — a genuine challenge in unmarked Boko. Listening to your audio source and instructor is essential; do not attempt to infer the correct form solely from the written word.

Sound Focus — The first-person singular subjunctive: in

The first-person singular subjunctive is in (that I … / let me … / I should …). It is a single syllable with a short vowel. Compare: the first-person singular completive nā has a long vowel and different tone; the continuative ina has two syllables; the future particle zan precedes the verb in a different structural position. All four forms signal different relationships between the speaker and the action. The subjunctive in appears in contexts of purpose, proposal, or desired-action sequences — not in independent statements about what the speaker is doing or has done.

Sound Focus — The future particle za and its first-person contraction

You learned in Chapter 14 that the future construction uses za plus the appropriate subject form. The first-person singular combination contracts in natural speech to zan (I will …). The written forms you will encounter are: zan tafi (I will go), za ka tafi (you [m.] will go), za ta tafi (she will go), za mu tafi (we will go). Pay special attention to the contracted zan — it is not two words and must not be read as za + a separate element. Its tone and vowel length differ from the subjunctive form in and from the completive nā. Your audio source is the reference; verify before recording.

Invitation and response intonation

Invitations in Hausa, as in all spoken languages, carry a characteristic intonation that signals friendliness and openness rather than a simple command or statement. Practice the model dialogue's invitations — Mun gayyace ku zuwa liyafar and Za mu iya tafi tare? — with a rising or sustained pitch at the end that signals you are awaiting a response. A flat or sharply falling tone may make the same words sound like a statement rather than an invitation. The distinction is not marked in Boko but is a real feature of spoken Hausa; your instructor or audio source should model it for you.

Calendar dates and clock times in connected speech

When giving a specific time or date for an arrangement, the numbers and time words tend to run together quickly in natural speech. Practice the phrases a karfe biyu da rabi (at half past two), ran Laraba da yamma (Wednesday evening), and daga karfe uku zuwa biyar (from three to five o'clock) as single prosodic units — not word by word. Verify the pronunciation of cardinal numbers in these phrases against your audio source, since the tone and vowel length of numbers are not marked in ordinary Boko.

Grammar Instruction

17.1 The subjunctive: form, meaning, and use

Purpose: make proposals, suggestions, and desired-action statements; form the subjunctive base for sequencing and purpose clauses.

You have already used the subjunctive in two contexts without analyzing its full system:

  • In Chapter 2, the fixed expression ku + verb was introduced for group commands: Ku zauna (Sit down, everyone). That ku is the second-person plural subjunctive subject form.
  • In Chapter 11, subjunctive forms were used for commands to a group, negative commands with kada, and purpose expressions. Chapter 14 extended those into purpose clauses with domin.

Chapter 17 makes the subjunctive fully productive across all persons and genders for proposals, suggestions, desired actions, and clause sequencing. The subjunctive subject-pronoun forms are shown below, alongside the continuative and completive forms you already know, so you can see the full picture at once.

Person and gender Continuative (Ch. 7) Completive (Ch. 16) Subjunctive (Ch. 17) English equivalent (subjunctive)
1st sg. (I) ina nā in that I / let me / I should
2nd sg. masc. (you, m.) kana kā ka that you (m.) / you should
2nd sg. fem. (you, f.) kina kin ki that you (f.) / you should
3rd sg. masc. (he) yana yā ya that he / he should / let him
3rd sg. fem. (she) tana tā ta that she / she should / let her
1st pl. (we) muna mun mu let us / that we / we should
2nd pl. (you pl.) kuna kun ku that you all / you all should
3rd pl. (they) suna sun su that they / they should / let them

Several forms are identical in spelling to other forms (e.g., subjunctive ka and relative completive ka; subjunctive ta and relative completive ta); the distinction is tonal and must be confirmed. The subjunctive appears in the following high-frequency uses in this chapter. In each case, the subjunctive subject-pronoun form directly precedes the lexical verb.

17.2 Proposals and suggestions: "let's" and "shall we"

Purpose: propose a joint activity or invite someone to do something together.

The first-person plural subjunctive mu produces the Hausa equivalent of "let's" when followed by a verb. This is one of the most natural ways to extend an invitation or make a suggestion in everyday Hausa conversation:

  • Mu tafi gidan abinci.Let's go to a restaurant.mu (1pl subjunctive) + tafi (verb)
  • Mu zauna mu tattauna.Let's sit down and discuss it.mu + zauna, mu + tattauna — two subjunctive clauses in sequence
  • Mu sadu da karfe uku.Let's meet at three o'clock.
  • Mu canza lokaci zuwa Talata.Let's change the time to Tuesday.

To make the suggestion a question — "shall we?" — use rising intonation or add the question word ko at the end:

  • Mu tafi tare, ko?Shall we go together?
  • Mu sadu da safe, ko?Shall we meet in the morning?
Language Focus — The subjunctive as the grammar of cooperation

English expresses proposals and desired actions through several different structures: "let's go," "why don't we go," "shall we go," "I'd like you to go," "I want him to go." Hausa handles many of these with one systematic tool: the subjunctive subject-pronoun form plus verb. Understanding this helps you produce a wide range of social interactions — invitations, requests for someone to do something, suggestions, and sequences of actions — using one grammar pattern rather than several unconnected phrases. The key is selecting the right person form: mu + verb for joint proposals, ka/ki + verb after an expression of wanting or needing someone else to act, ya/ta + verb when the intended actor is a third person, and so on. Once you learn the system for proposals, the same forms work naturally in purpose clauses and sequencing, as Sections 17.3 and 17.4 show.

17.3 Desired-action frames: "I want you to …" and "I would like him to …"

Purpose: express what the speaker wants another person to do, using a matrix verb of wanting or wishing followed by a subjunctive clause.

When Hausa expresses a desire that someone else perform an action, the structure is: a matrix expression of wanting, wishing, or needing, followed by a subjunctive clause with the appropriate person form. The two most common matrix expressions at this stage are ina so (I want / I like) and ina fatan (I hope / I wish).

  • Ina so ka zo.I want you (m.) to come.ina so (I want) + ka (2sg.m subjunctive) + zo (verb)
  • Ina so ki sadu da ni jiya.I want you (f.) to meet me yesterday / I wanted you to meet me.Note: the subjunctive does not itself signal past or present; time reference comes from context and matrix verb tense.
  • Ina fatan za ku zo.I hope you all will come.ina fatan (I hope) + za ku (future 2pl) — here the future is used in the embedded clause because the event is future; the subjunctive may also appear here depending on the speaker's intent — flag for expert review
  • Muna so mu tafi tare.We want to go together.muna so (we want) + mu (1pl subjunctive) + tafi tare
  • Ina so ya kawo labarinsa.I want him to bring his news / report.

Pedagogical note: The interplay between future za and the subjunctive in purpose and embedded contexts is an area where scholarly descriptions of Hausa can differ. This chapter teaches the most frequent and unambiguous cases. Items marked "flag for expert review" above should be confirmed by a Hausa-language specialist before audio production and final publication.

17.4 Clause sequencing: subjunctive result clauses after future and conditional premises

Purpose: connect a future plan or conditional premise to its intended or expected outcome using a subjunctive result clause.

In a sequence of actions where the first clause is future, conditional, or expresses intent, subsequent clauses that describe the desired or intended result often use the subjunctive. This is a key construction for making multi-step plans:

  • Zan tafi kasuwa in saya kaya.I will go to the market to buy things / so that I can buy things.zan tafi (future) + in (1sg subjunctive) + saya kaya (purpose/result)
  • Za ku zo mu tattauna.You all will come and we will discuss it / Come so we can discuss it.za ku zo (future 2pl) + mu (1pl subjunctive) + tattauna
  • Zan kira ka in gaya maka lokaci.I will call you (m.) to tell you the time.zan kira ka (future + object pronoun) + in (1sg subjunctive) + gaya maka (tell you [indirect object])
  • Za ta zo mu tafi tare.She will come and we will go together.
  • Mu shirya yanzu mu isa a lokaci.Let's get ready now so we arrive on time.mu shirya (1pl subjunctive) + mu isa (1pl subjunctive) — two subjunctive clauses in sequence

Notice that in these sequences, the second clause does not need a new aspect marker or connector — the subjunctive subject form itself signals that this clause continues or completes the action introduced by the first clause. The structure is compact and natural. Compare the fuller connector-based alternative: Zan tafi kasuwa don in saya kaya (I will go to the market in order to buy things), where don makes the purpose relationship explicit.

17.6 Future consolidation: za and negative future

Purpose: review and consolidate the future construction from Chapter 14 alongside the subjunctive introduced above, and produce the negative future with the complete discontinuous ba … ba frame.

The complete future paradigm is reproduced here alongside the negative so you can compare both sets in one view. Study the first-person singular contracted form zan carefully — it is the most frequent future form in natural speech and the one most often misspelled or mis-analyzed.

Person and gender Affirmative future Negative future English
1st sg. zan (za ni → zan) ba zan … ba I will … / I will not …
2nd sg. masc. za ka ba za ka … ba you (m.) will … / will not …
2nd sg. fem. za ki ba za ki … ba you (f.) will … / will not …
3rd sg. masc. za ya ba za ya … ba he will … / will not …
3rd sg. fem. za ta ba za ta … ba she will … / will not …
1st pl. za mu ba za mu … ba we will … / will not …
2nd pl. za ku ba za ku … ba you all will … / will not …
3rd pl. za su ba za su … ba they will … / will not …

Reminder from Chapter 14: The negative future uses the same discontinuous ba … ba frame as all Hausa completive negation, but it wraps around the entire za + subject form construction: ba za ka zo ba (you will not come), not *za ba ka zo ba. The ba precedes za, and the closing ba follows the verb and any objects. Both parts are required.

  • Zan zo karfe huɗu.I will come at four o'clock.
  • Ba zan iya zo da safe ba.I will not be able to come in the morning.
  • Za mu sadu a gidan abinci.We will meet at the restaurant.
  • Ba za ta iya canza ranakun ba.She will not be able to change the dates.

17.7 Contrasting continuative, completive, and future in a single exchange

Purpose: select the correct aspect construction according to meaning and time reference within a connected conversation.

This section draws together the three central aspect constructions you have studied over Chapters 7, 16, and 14. Compare them directly using the same verb and a shared context:

ConstructionSubject form (3sg.m)HausaEnglish meaning
ContinuativeyanaYana shirya aiki.He is preparing / does prepare work. (ongoing / habitual)
CompletiveyāYā shirya aiki.He prepared / has prepared work. (completed)
Futureza yaZa ya shirya aiki.He will prepare work. (future)

A single conversation about a plan can move through all three:

Yanayin hali: Nasir yana magana da Aminu game da shirye-shirye

Nasir: Aminu, yana aiki yanzu, ko? (Is he working now?)
Aminu: A'a, yā gama aiki. Yanzu yana hutu. (No, he has finished work. Now he is resting.)
Nasir: To. Za ya iya zo liyafar gobe? (Will he be able to come to the reception tomorrow?)
Aminu: Ina tsammani. Zan gaya masa. (I think so. I will tell him.)
Nasir: Madalla. Mu tafi tare, ko? (Excellent. Shall we go together?)
Aminu: To, mu tafi tare. (Yes, let's go together.)

Notice that within six lines, Nasir and Aminu use yana (continuative) to describe what is happening now, yā gama (completive) to report something finished, za ya iya (future) to discuss what will happen, zan gaya (future) for a personal plan, and mu tafi (subjunctive) to make a joint proposal. This is the natural grammar of planning conversation.

Language Building Block
Invitation or proposal: mu + verb (+ ko? for a question)
Mu sadu da karfe uku, ko?
Shall we meet at three o'clock?
Desired action: ina so / ina fatan + subjunctive subject form + verb
Ina so ka zo da safe.
I want you (m.) to come in the morning.
Future + subjunctive sequence: za + subject form + verb + subjunctive subject form + verb
Zan zo in gaya maka.
I will come to tell you. (future premise + subjunctive result)

Select the subjunctive for proposals, desired actions, and sequenced results. Select the future (za) for independent predictions and plans. Do not use one as a substitute for the other; they occupy different positions and signal different relationships between the speaker and the action.

17.8 Object pronouns in invitation and arrangement contexts

Purpose: review direct-object and selected indirect-object pronouns in the context of making and confirming arrangements.

Invitation and arrangement conversations frequently involve referring back to people already mentioned, which requires object pronouns rather than repeated noun phrases. The direct-object pronouns were introduced in Chapter 9 and reviewed in Chapter 16. This section applies them in future and subjunctive contexts:

  • Zan gayyace shi zuwa liyafar.I will invite him to the reception.zan gayyace (future + object-conditioned verb form) + shi (3sg.m DO)
  • Za mu jira su a ƙofar.We will wait for them at the gate.
  • Zan kira ki gobe in gaya maki lokaci.I will call you (f.) tomorrow to tell you the time.zan kira ki (future + 2sg.f DO) + in gaya maki (subjunctive + indirect object)
  • Ya aika mana saƙo.He sent us a message.ya (completive relative or subjunctive — context clarifies) + aika + mana (indirect object pronoun: to us)

The indirect-object pronouns used above (maka = to you [m.], maki = to you [f.], mana = to us) were introduced in Chapter 13. This chapter reviews them in planning contexts where sending, telling, and calling another person are central actions.

Flag for expert review: The object-conditioned form of gayyata/gayyaci (invite) before a direct-object pronoun (shown above as gayyace) requires verification by a qualified Hausa-language specialist. The alternation is grade-related; use the forms in the vocabulary table as a guide and mark uncertain object-conditioned forms for correction.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Rakiya tana aika saƙo ga abokinta Musa ta wayar hannu. Suna tattauna shirye-shiryen biki.

(Setting: Rakiya is sending a message to her friend Musa by phone. They are discussing plans for a celebration.)

RAKIYA Sannu Musa. Ina fatan lafiya. Mun gayyace ku zuwa liyafar auren 'yar'uwata Hadiza. Za ta yi auren ran Asabar mako mai zuwa. Kuna iya zuwa?Hello Musa. I hope you are well. We have invited you all to the wedding reception of my sister Hadiza. She will have the wedding on Saturday next week. Are you all able to come?
MUSA Wa'alaikumus-salam, Rakiya! Madalla! Barka da auren Hadiza. Eh, ina so mu zo duka. Wane lokaci ne liyafar?And upon you peace, Rakiya! Wonderful! Congratulations on Hadiza's wedding. Yes, I want us all to come. What time is the reception?
RAKIYA Liyafar za ta fara da karfe uku da rana har da yamma. Wurin liyafar yana a unguwarmu — gidan iyalanmu ne. Za mu aika muku takardar gayyata da adireshi daidai.The reception will begin at three in the afternoon until evening. The reception venue is in our neighborhood — it is our family's house. We will send you all the invitation card with the exact address.
MUSA Nagode sosai! Amma yi haƙuri — ina da tambaya ɗaya. Nī da ɗan'uwana Haruna muna aiki har da tsakar rana ran Asabar. Za mu iya iso kimanin karfe huɗu. Shi zai dace da ku?Thank you very much! But excuse me — I have one question. My brother Haruna and I work until noon on Saturday. We will be able to arrive at around four o'clock. Will that suit you?
RAKIYA Eh, lalle ya dace! Kada ku damu — liyafar za ta ci gaba har dare. Zo ku sadu da abokai da dangi da yawa.Yes, of course that suits us! Don't worry — the reception will continue until evening. Come and meet many friends and relatives.
MUSA Madalla. Zan gaya wa Haruna yanzu. Za mu zo tare. Ina so ka gaya wa Hadiza gaishe ta daga gare mu.Excellent. I will tell Haruna now. We will come together. I want you to tell Hadiza [to receive] our greetings from us.
RAKIYA Zan gaya mata. Ta yi farin ciki da jin haka. Mun amince — Asabar da karfe huɗu. Sai an jima!I will tell her. She will be happy to hear that. We are agreed — Saturday at four o'clock. Until then!
MUSA To, mun amince. Sai Asabar!Yes, we are agreed. Until Saturday!

Vocabulary notes for the dialogue: auren 'yar'uwata = my sister's wedding (aure = marriage, wedding; 'yar'uwa = sister; f., lit. "daughter of the [same] mother"); barka da auren Hadiza = congratulations on Hadiza's wedding; takardar gayyata = invitation card (takarda = paper, card; f.); adireshi = address (loanword); lalle = certainly, of course; ci gaba = to continue, to go on (yana ci gaba = it is continuing); gaishe ta daga gare mu = greet her from us (gaishe = to greet; object-conditioned form — flag for review); Wa'alaikumus-salam = the standard Arabic-origin response to As-salamu alaykum, common among many Muslim Hausa speakers — not universal; Sai an jima = a farewell expression meaning "until we meet again" — flag for frequency in Kano Hausa.

Grammar notes for the dialogue: Kuna iya zuwa? — continuative-based ability question (from Ch. 13): kuna iya (2pl continuative + iya) + zuwa (verbal noun). Ina so mu zo duka — desired-action frame: ina so + mu (1pl subjunctive) + zo. Kada ku damu — negative subjunctive 2pl from Ch. 11 and Ch. 17. Zan gaya wa Haruna — future + indirect-object phrase. Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

  • Every proposal uses the correct first-person plural subjunctive form mu followed immediately by the verb — not a continuative or completive form.
  • Every desired-action frame uses ina so or ina fatan followed by the correct person-and-gender subjunctive form for the intended actor.
  • Every future sentence uses za + subject form (or the contracted zan for first-person singular).
  • Every negative future sentence includes both the initial ba before za and the closing ba after the verb. Neither element is missing.
  • Every negative subjunctive command uses kada + the correct person form + verb.
  • Time expressions are complete — they include a day, a time of day, or a clock time as appropriate to the context.
  • Object pronouns appear in the correct form where a noun referent has already been established (shi, ta, su, etc.).
  • Hooked letters (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ) and 'y are written correctly throughout, including in words such as ƙarshe, ƙofar, and 'yar'uwa.
  • The text flows as a connected message — invitation, reply, or arrangement — not as disconnected grammar sentences.
  • The opening and closing are appropriate for an informal message between friends.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Invitations and the language of social obligation

In many Hausa-speaking communities, being invited to a significant event — a wedding, a naming ceremony (suna), a graduation, or a reception — carries social weight. Accepting an invitation is often understood as an expression of care and support for the host family, not merely as a personal scheduling decision. When someone is genuinely unable to attend, declining is done with clear expressions of regret, acknowledgment of the event's importance, and often a separate gesture of goodwill such as sending greetings or a gift. The phrase nagode, amma … (thank you, but …) followed by an honest explanation is a natural and accepted way to begin a polite decline.

These norms vary by relationship, community, and setting — a formal institutional invitation differs from a casual text between friends. Neither context requires elaborate scripts, but both benefit from the learner's awareness that the language of invitation is socially meaningful beyond its grammatical content.

Culture in Context — Weddings and celebrations in Hausa-speaking communities

Weddings and family celebrations in Hausa-speaking northern Nigeria and Niger are among the most socially significant recurring events in community life. They may span several days and involve distinct stages with their own practices, music, dress, food, and gift-giving conventions. A formal wedding reception (liyafar aure) often takes place separately from the Islamic marriage contract (auren Fatiha) and may involve music, elaborate food preparation, and hosting of large numbers of guests. Practices differ considerably across urban and rural settings, socioeconomic backgrounds, families, and regions. This chapter uses the wedding as a communicative context, not as an opportunity to generalize about what Hausa weddings are always or necessarily like.

Regional Variation — Invitation and arrangement language across varieties

The invitation vocabulary and fixed expressions in this chapter are drawn from Kano-based Standard Hausa. In Niger, some of the same social events may use French-influenced terms alongside Hausa ones — for instance, réception alongside liyafa, or French-influenced date formats in written invitations. In communities using Hausa as an additional language, code-switching between Hausa, French, English, Fulfulde, or other languages is common in informal invitation messages. This chapter does not require productive mastery of these variants, but learners should expect to encounter them and recognize the Hausa elements accurately.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using the future instead of the subjunctive for proposals

Error: *Za mu tafi tare, ko? (using the future za mu tafi as an invitation or proposal)

Correction: Mu tafi tare, ko? — The future construction za mu tafi states a prediction or plan: "we will go together." The subjunctive mu tafi makes a proposal or invitation: "let's go together / shall we go together?" These are different speech acts. Using the future where a proposal is meant can sound like a statement of fact rather than an invitation, which may feel presumptuous rather than warm. Use mu + verb for invitations and proposals; add ko? or rising intonation to signal that you are asking rather than announcing.

Common Mistake — Omitting the closing ba in the negative future

Error: *Ba zan iya zo da safe. (missing the closing ba)

Correction: Ba zan iya zo da safe ba. — As with the negative completive (Chapter 16), the negative future requires both the initial ba and the closing ba. The initial ba comes before za; the closing ba comes after the lexical verb and any complement. Omitting either element leaves the sentence structurally incomplete. Check every negative sentence in your writing to confirm that both elements are present.

Common Mistake — Using an independent pronoun instead of a subjunctive subject form

Error: *Ina so shi ya zo. or *Ina so yana zo. (using an independent pronoun or a continuative form in the desired-action clause)

Correction: Ina so ya zo. — The desired-action frame requires the subjunctive subject form directly before the verb: ya zo, not shi ya zo or yana zo. The independent pronoun shi is not used as a subject form in this position; it functions as an object or emphatic pronoun. The continuative yana signals an ongoing action, not a desired future action. Both errors are common transfers from English word order and English present-tense framing. Use the appropriate subjunctive form: ya for third-person masculine singular, ta for third-person feminine singular, mu for first-person plural, and so on.

Common Mistake — Mixing aspect forms within a narrative sequence

Error: *Yā tafi kasuwa yanzu. Yana shirya aiki jiya. Za ya zo gobe. (using completive + time word "now," continuative + past time word, and future inconsistently)

Correction: The aspect form and the time expression must be compatible. The completive yā pairs with past time words (jiya, makon da ya gabata). The continuative yana pairs with now or habitual meaning (yanzu, frequency words). The future za ya pairs with future time words (gobe, mako mai zuwa). Mixing a completive form with yanzu, or a continuative with jiya, produces a contradiction. Always ask: is this event completed, ongoing, or future? Then select the matching subject form, and make sure any time word in the sentence reinforces the same time reference.

Common Mistake — Writing zan as two words

Error: *za ni tafi or *za n tafi (separating the contracted first-person singular future form)

Correction: Zan tafi. — In standard written Hausa, the first-person singular future is written as the single word zan, not as two words. This is a contracted form that has become standard. Writing it as za ni is not wrong in all contexts — some teaching materials present the uncontracted form — but zan is the more frequent form in natural speech and writing and is the course standard. Write it as one word, just as mun or kun are written as one word in the completive.

Looking Ahead

Abin da ka/ki koya yanzu — What you know now

Chapter 17 has consolidated the subjunctive as a productive system for proposals, desired actions, and sequenced results. You can now make and respond to invitations, negotiate times and places, express what you want someone else to do, and sequence a future plan across multiple clauses. You have also consolidated the future construction with za alongside its negative and reinforced the three-way contrast between continuative, completive, and future that is central to all Hausa conversation about time. The combination of these tools — aspect contrast, subjunctive for proposals and desires, future for predictions and plans, and connectors for sequencing — gives you the grammar of making arrangements in Hausa. Together with the completive narration of Chapter 16, you now have both a way to talk about the past and a way to plan the future.

Ayyukan Kullum

Daily Routines · Speak and Write Hausa: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use the productive continuative construction — a subject form plus a verbal noun — to describe activities happening now or regularly.
  2. Produce the continuative subject forms for all persons, numbers, and genders introduced in this chapter.
  3. Use the correct verbal noun for each high-frequency daily-activity verb taught here.
  4. Ask and answer questions about ongoing activities using the continuative.
  5. Tell and ask the time in Hausa using clock-hour expressions.
  6. Name the days of the week and the parts of the day.
  7. Use common frequency and sequence expressions to connect activities into a simple narrative of a day.
  8. Recognize the habitual -kan- form in listening and reading without yet producing it independently.
  9. Produce intelligible tone and rhythm across continuative subject forms and their verbal nouns.

Communication Scenario

You are getting to know a Hausa-speaking colleague, Bilkisu, who works at the same office in Kano. Over tea before the workday begins, she asks what your morning was like, and you want to describe what you do each day. You also want to ask about her schedule. To do this in Hausa, you need a new grammatical tool: the continuative construction, which combines a short subject form with a verbal noun to express what someone is doing now or does regularly. This chapter introduces that system fully and gives you the vocabulary to put it to use immediately.

  1. In Chapters 3 and 6, you encountered formulaic continuative expressions — Ina magana da… and ina zaune a… — without analyzing their internal structure. This chapter explains what those forms are and how they work, so that you can build new sentences freely rather than relying only on memorized phrases. The analysis reconnects to the formulas you already know: ina is the first-person singular continuative subject form, and what follows it is a verbal noun.
  2. Hausa does not distinguish a dedicated "present tense" from a "present progressive" the way English does. The continuative construction — Ina karatu — can mean "I am studying (right now)" or "I study (generally)." Context, time expressions, and frequency words carry that distinction. As you work through this chapter, pay close attention to how time expressions and frequency words shape meaning.

Time categories in Hausa may overlap with or reference Islamic prayer times, particularly Fajr (before dawn), Zuhr (midday), Asr (mid-afternoon), Maghrib (sunset), and Isha'i (night). Agogon lokaci — Telling the time

Clock time in Hausa uses the word ƙarfe (hour, o'clock) followed by the number. The question "What time is it?" is Nawa ne ƙarfe? or Wane lokaci ne? The answer uses the pattern Ƙarfe [number] ne.

HausaEnglish
Nawa ne ƙarfe?What time is it? (How many is the hour?)
Wane lokaci ne?What time is it? (What time is it?)
Ƙarfe ɗaya ne.It is one o'clock.
Ƙarfe biyu ne.It is two o'clock.
Ƙarfe takwas da rabi ne.It is half past eight. (rabi = half)
Ƙarfe goma da kwata ne.It is quarter past ten. (kwata = quarter)
Ƙarfe goma sha biyu ne.It is twelve o'clock.
Ƙarfe … da safe… o'clock in the morning
Ƙarfe … da yamma… o'clock in the afternoon/evening
Ƙarfe … da dare… o'clock at night

Note on ƙarfe: This word contains the hooked ƙ — a distinct Hausa consonant. Do not substitute plain k. The word originally referred to the sound of a struck bell or gong used to mark the hour. Practice the ƙ sound carefully whenever you say or write a time expression.

Pronunciation and Boko

Tone and length in continuative subject forms

The continuative subject forms introduced in this chapter are short but tonally significant. They carry person, number, and gender information in a single word, and their tone is part of that identity. The table below shows the forms with a guide to their tonal pattern. Sound Focus — Rhythm across the continuative construction

The continuative construction is a two-word unit: a short subject form followed by a verbal noun. In natural Hausa speech, these two words form a rhythmic pair — neither one is separately stressed in the English sense, and the subject form is not swallowed or reduced. When you say Ina karatu, both words receive their full sounds: i-na and then ka-ra-tu, with the rhythm flowing naturally across the phrase. English speakers sometimes reduce the subject form to something like a mumbled prefix. Resist this. The subject form carries grammatical information that Hausa listeners depend on — shortchanging it leads to misunderstanding.

Practice each pair as a single breath group: ina karatu, yana yin aiki, tana cin abinci, muna sauraro. Listen carefully to the rhythm before you speak, then reproduce it.

Verbal noun pronunciation

Verbal nouns are separate words with their own tone and vowel length. You cannot reliably predict the pronunciation of a verbal noun from the citation verb — learn each one as a new vocabulary item. Two patterns to note:

  1. karanta → karatu: The citation verb ends in -nta; the verbal noun ends in -tu. The vowel pattern shifts. Practice both words until the difference is automatic.
  2. sha → shayan: The citation verb is a single syllable; the verbal noun adds a suffix and becomes two syllables. The vowel in sha is short; the vowel in shay- is a diphthong. Do not pronounce them identically.
  3. barci → barci: Some verbal nouns share the same written form as the citation verb but may differ in tone. Follow the audio model.
  4. tashi → tashiwa: The verbal noun adds -wa suffix. The -wa is always pronounced fully — never drop it or reduce it to a murmur.
Pronunciation Practice — ƙ in time expressions

The word ƙarfe (o'clock, hour) begins with the ejective consonant ƙ — the same hooked letter you have been practicing since Chapter 1. In this consonant, the back of the tongue presses against the soft palate and the airstream is cut off briefly before releasing with a sharp burst. It sounds firmer and more abrupt than plain k. Every time expression you produce this chapter will begin with this sound: Ƙarfe ɗaya, Ƙarfe biyu, Ƙarfe goma sha biyar. Make the ƙ distinct. If you substitute plain k, a Hausa listener will hear a different word or assume you are not familiar with Boko. Consistent production of ƙ versus k is one of the most important orthographic and pronunciation habits of this course.

Grammar Instruction

7.1 The continuative construction — form

Purpose: describe activities that are happening now or that happen regularly, using the correct subject form for each person, number, and gender.

The Hausa continuative construction consists of two elements:

  1. A continuative subject form — a short word that encodes person, number, and grammatical gender.
  2. A verbal noun — the noun-form of the verb, which names the action.

The subject form comes first. The verbal noun follows immediately. Nothing separates them in a basic statement. Here is the complete set of productive continuative subject forms for this course:

Person / Number / Gender Continuative form English equivalent
1st singularinaI am (doing) / I (do)
2nd singular masculinekanayou are (doing) / you (do) — to a male
2nd singular femininekinayou are (doing) / you (do) — to a female
3rd singular masculineyanahe is (doing) / he (does)
3rd singular femininetanashe is (doing) / she (does)
1st pluralmunawe are (doing) / we (do)
2nd pluralkunayou all are (doing) / you all (do)
3rd pluralsunathey are (doing) / they (do)

Each form is a complete unit. Do not try to analyze it as a pronoun plus an ending at this stage. What matters for communication is choosing the right form for the right person and — crucially — the right gender: kana addresses a male, kina addresses a female; yana refers to a male, tana to a female.

Language Focus — The continuative subject form carries all the subject information

When you say Ina karatu, the word ina is doing the work of both the English pronoun "I" and the auxiliary "am." The continuative subject form already encodes first-person singular. Because of this, you do not normally add a separate independent pronoun (ni) in front of ina in a plain statement. Ni ina karatu is grammatically unusual and sounds emphatic or contrastive rather than ordinary. At this stage, use the continuative form alone in straightforward descriptions of what you or someone else is doing.

An independent pronoun can appear before the continuative form when you want to contrast or emphasize: Ni ina karatu, amma shi yana barci (I am studying, but he is sleeping). That contrastive use is natural — what you want to avoid is adding ni as a mechanical habit every time.

7.2 The continuative construction — meaning and use

Purpose: understand when the continuative expresses an ongoing action versus a regular habit, and how time expressions carry that distinction.

The Hausa continuative construction covers the communicative ground of both English "I am reading" and "I read (generally)." The construction itself is neutral between the two; time expressions, frequency words, and context determine which interpretation fits.

  • Ina karatu yanzu.I am studying right now. (yanzu = now → ongoing action at this moment)
  • Ina karatu kullum.I study every day. (kullum = every day → regular activity)
  • Yana yin aiki da safe.He works in the morning. / He is working in the morning. (context determines which)
  • Tana dafuwa yanzu.She is cooking right now.
  • Muna tafiya aiki Litinin.We go to work on Monday. / We are going to work on Monday.
  • Suna sauraro.They are listening. / They listen. (ongoing or regular, from context)

The key lesson: do not expect one Hausa form to map onto one English tense. Add a time expression whenever you need to specify whether you mean right now or regularly. When the context makes it clear, no time expression is needed.

7.3 Asking about ongoing activities

Purpose: form continuative questions using the correct subject form and question word.

To ask what someone is doing, combine the question word me or menene (what) with the continuative subject form. The word order places the question word at the start, followed by the continuative form and the context:

  • Me kake yi?What are you doing? (to a male)me + kake + yi — Note: kake is the relative-continuative form, required after me in this question type; see note below.
  • Me kike yi?What are you doing? (to a female)
  • Me yake yi?What is he doing?
  • Me take yi?What is she doing?
  • Me suke yi?What are they doing?
  • Ina kake aiki?Where do you work? (to a male)
  • Yaushe kake tafiya?When do you go? (to a male)
Language Note — kake, kike, yake, take: the relative-continuative forms

You first encountered kake and kike in Chapter 3, in origin questions like Daga ina kake zuwa? (Where are you from?). At that point, they were presented as fixed question patterns without full analysis. Now you can see the system they belong to: when a question word or focused element comes before the subject-aspect construction, Hausa uses a different set of subject forms — called relative-continuative forms — rather than the plain forms in the table above.

The relative-continuative forms for this chapter are: kake (2nd m. sg.), kike (2nd f. sg.), yake (3rd m. sg.), take (3rd f. sg.), muke (1st pl.), kuke (2nd pl.), suke (3rd pl.). For first-person singular, the relative-continuative form is nake. These forms appear in information questions beginning with me, ina, yaushe, and similar words. The full analysis of this system is developed further in later chapters. For now, treat each question pattern above as a complete communication formula and notice how these forms differ from the plain forms ina, kana, kina, yana, tana…

7.4 The continuative with location and time

Purpose: combine the continuative with location and time expressions from earlier chapters and this chapter.

Time and location expressions attach after the verbal noun. The basic word order is: continuative subject form + verbal noun + location or time.

  • Ina karatu a gida da safe.I study at home in the morning.
  • Yana yin aiki a ofis.He works at the office.
  • Tana tafiya kasuwa Talata.She goes to the market on Tuesday.
  • Muna cin abinci tare da iyali da dare.We eat with the family at night.
  • Suna hutawa Asabar da Lahadi.They rest on Saturday and Sunday.

7.5 Sequencing a routine with connectors

Purpose: use approved connectors to link continuative statements into a simple account of a day.

The connectors introduced in the vocabulary section — da farko, sa'an nan, bayan haka, a ƙarshe — attach at the start of a new statement and signal its place in a sequence. Each new statement then uses the continuative normally.

  • Da farko, ina tashiwa da ƙarfe shida da safe.First, I get up at six in the morning.
  • Sa'an nan, ina yin wanka.Then I bathe.
  • Bayan haka, ina cin abinci.After that, I eat.
  • A ƙarshe, ina tafiya aiki da ƙarfe takwas.Finally, I go to work at eight o'clock.

These connectors are a first introduction to the clause-sequencing system that will be developed throughout Units III and IV. Use them to organize spoken and written routines from this chapter onward.

7.6 Habitual meaning: recognition only

Purpose: recognize the habitual -kan- form in listening and reading; understand that it marks a specifically habitual (regularly repeated) meaning.

Hausa has a dedicated habitual construction that inserts -kan- between the subject form and the verbal noun. You may encounter it in listening passages or readings. Do not attempt to produce it independently at this stage — the full system is introduced in Chapter 19.

Continuative (productive)Habitual with -kan- (recognition only)English meaning
Ina karatu.Nakan karatu.I (habitually) study. / I always/regularly study.
Yana yin aiki.Yakan yin aiki.He habitually works.
Tana tafiya.Takan tafiya.She habitually goes.

If you see or hear nakan, kakan, yakan, takan, mukan, kukan, or sukan followed by a verbal noun, recognize this as a habitual form meaning the action is a regular, established habit. Do not confuse it with the plain continuative. The continuative is fully adequate for expressing regular activity with frequency words; the -kan- form adds an additional layer of "established habit" meaning. This chapter's productive requirement is the plain continuative — the habitual form is for recognition only.

Language Building Block
continuative subject form + verbal noun (+ time expression)

The subject form (ina, yana, tana…) carries person, number, and gender. The verbal noun names the action. A time expression specifies when. The three elements work together as a unit. Learn the correct verbal noun with every verb — do not form it by analogy.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Ofis a Kano, safe da wuri. Bilkisu — malama ce wacce tana koyarwa a makaranta ta gwamnati — ta zo ofis don wata taro kafin darasi ta fara. Tana magana da Usman, ɗan aiki a ofis ɗin. Su biyun suna sha shayi kafin su fara aiki.

(Setting: An office in Kano, early morning. Bilkisu — a teacher at a government school — has come to the office for a meeting before her class begins. She is speaking with Usman, an office worker. The two are having tea before starting work.)

USMAN Sannu, Bilkisu. Kin zo da wuri yau.Hello, Bilkisu. You have come early today.
BILKISU Eh, akwai taro da safe. Ina fita gida da ƙarfe shida kullum — makaranta tana da nisa.Yes, there is a meeting in the morning. I leave home at six every day — the school is far.
USMAN Ƙarfe shida! Da wuri sosai. Ni nakan tashi da ƙarfe bakwai. Sa'an nan ina yin wanka, ina cin abinci, bayan haka ina tafiya aiki da ƙarfe takwas da rabi.Six o'clock! Very early. I (habitually) get up at seven. Then I bathe, eat, then I go to work at half past eight.
BILKISU Haba! Ina a gida da ƙarfe biyar da rabi tukuna da ke nan. Da farko, ina yin sallah, sa'an nan ina dafa abinci. Bayan haka ina fitar da yara makaranta, a ƙarshe ina hawa mota.Goodness! I am still at home at half past five. First I pray, then I prepare food. After that I see the children off to school, and finally I get in the car.
USMAN Kai! Tana da aiki sosai. Ran Asabar kuma kina hutawa?Wow! That is very busy. And on Saturday do you rest?
BILKISU Kaɗan kaɗan. Asabar, muna tafiya kasuwa, ina saye abubuwa gida. Lahadi ne muna hutawa da gaske — suna karatu, ni kuma ina karanta littafi.A little. On Saturday we go to the market, I buy things for the home. Sunday is when we really rest — they study, and I read a book.
USMAN Muna da taro yanzu — amma bayan taro, wane aiki kike yi da safe a makaranta?We have a meeting now — but after the meeting, what do you do in the morning at school?
BILKISU Da farko ina koyarwa wa azuzuwa na ƙasa. Sa'an nan ina rubuta rahotanni. Da tsakar rana, ina cin abinci da abokan aiki. Sana'a mai daɗi, amma tana ɗaukar lokaci mai yawa!First I teach the lower classes. Then I write reports. At midday I eat with colleagues. It is a pleasant job, but it takes a lot of time!

Vocabulary notes for the dialogue: da wuri = early; tana da nisa = it is far (possession with da from Chapter 6); nakan tashi = I habitually get up (habitual form — recognition only); yin sallah = praying; fitar da yara makaranta = seeing the children off to school; hawa mota = getting in a car/vehicle; kai! = exclamation of surprise; da gaske = really, truly; littafi = book; azuzuwa na ƙasa = lower classes/grades; rahotanni = reports; mai daɗi = pleasant, enjoyable; ɗaukar lokaci mai yawa = taking a lot of time. Items marked (habitual form — recognition only) are for recognition; items involving da possession and akwai recycle Chapter 6 grammar.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Daily schedules and their variation

When you describe your daily routine to a Hausa speaker, you are entering a conversation that has many possible shapes. Daily schedules in Hausa-speaking communities — like anywhere in the world — vary enormously by occupation, family structure, location, religious practice, and personal habit. A schoolteacher in Kano, a farmer in rural Niger, a trader in Accra's Hausa quarter, and a university student in Kaduna will have daily rhythms that look very different from one another.

One variable that often appears in conversations about daily routine is the structure of Islamic prayers, which are observed five times each day by many (though not all) Hausa speakers. These prayer times — Fajr (pre-dawn), Zuhr (midday), Asr (mid-afternoon), Maghrib (sunset), and Isha'i (night) — often serve as natural anchors for scheduling. You may hear someone say they do something bayan sallah (after prayer) rather than naming a clock time. This is not unusual, and understanding it helps you interpret schedules accurately. The dialogues in this chapter reference prayer time without making it the only framework.

When asking someone about their routine, it is polite to show interest in what they describe rather than assuming your own schedule is the norm. Asking Kana/kina hutawa yaushe? (When do you rest?) or Yaushe kake/kike cin abinci? (When do you eat?) opens a genuine conversation about how another person organizes their day.

Culture in Context — Work, markets, and the rhythm of the week

Market days are an important organizing principle in many Hausa-speaking communities. In towns and cities across northern Nigeria and Niger, particular markets operate on specific days of the week, and some communities even take their name from their market day (a common pattern across West Africa). For traders and farmers, the market-day schedule structures the entire week — determining when goods are bought and sold, when travel happens, and when social contact is most dense.

For learners, this means that when you ask a Hausa speaker Yaushe kake tafiya kasuwa? (When do you go to the market?), the answer may be specific to the local market schedule rather than a general preference. Talata (Tuesday) might be the answer not because the person prefers Tuesdays, but because that is the day the market operates in their town.

Friday (Juma'a) holds special significance in many Muslim communities as the day of congregational prayer. In some settings, this affects working hours and the rhythm of business. The reading passage about Kwame reflects one version of this reality — he closes his shop early on Friday. This is a feature of many but not all businesses in Hausa-speaking areas, and it varies by community, individual practice, and context.

Regional Variation — Vocabulary for daily activities

Some daily-activity vocabulary differs between Nigerian and Nigerien Hausa, and between urban and rural registers. A few items to be aware of at this stage:

Cooking: The verb dafa abinci and its verbal noun dafuwa are widely used for cooking. You may also encounter girki (cooking, especially in certain contexts and regions) — this is listed as receptive for this chapter.

Going to work: Tafi aiki is the common productive expression. In some contexts, especially in Niger, you may encounter je aiki or similar constructions. This course uses tafi as the standard productive form.

Coffee versus tea: Both kofi (coffee) and shayi (tea) appear in this chapter's dialogue and reading. Tea — especially the sweetened, spiced Hausa tea or the West African ataya (Mauritanian-style gunpowder tea) — is widely consumed in northern Hausa-speaking communities. Coffee is present but less universally the morning default than it might be in other cultural contexts. Do not assume that "morning drink" means coffee.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using the citation verb instead of the verbal noun

Error: Ina karanta. (using the citation verb karanta instead of the verbal noun karatu)

Correction: Ina karatu. — In the continuative construction, the element after the subject form must be the verbal noun, not the citation form of the verb. The verbal noun for karanta (to read, to study) is karatu — a different word. This is not a small variation; using the citation verb here produces an unnatural or unclear sentence. The same applies throughout: Ina yin wanka (not Ina yi wanka); Ina tashiwa (not Ina tashi). Always learn the verbal noun alongside the verb.

Common Mistake — Wrong subject form for gender

Error: Yana karatu when referring to a female person, or Tana karatu when referring to a male person.

Correction: Yana karatu — for a male; Tana karatu — for a female. The third-person singular forms carry gender. Using the wrong one creates a genuine error that a Hausa speaker will notice, not just a stylistic preference. The same applies in the second person: kana when speaking to a male, kina when speaking to a female. Make a habit of asking: who is the subject, and what is their gender? Then select accordingly.

Common Mistake — Placing the time expression before the subject form

Error: Da safe ina karatu. (placing the time expression before the subject form as though time always comes first)

Correction: Ina karatu da safe. — In the standard continuative pattern, time expressions typically follow the verbal noun: subject form + verbal noun + time/location. The time expression can be moved to sentence-initial position for emphasis or contrast, but this is a specific communicative choice, not the default. When beginning a sequence (Da farko…), the connector is fronted — but what follows it is still the full continuative construction: Da farko, ina tashiwa.

Common Mistake — Adding an independent pronoun before a continuative form in plain statements

Error: Ni ina karatu da safe. as a neutral statement of routine

Correction: Ina karatu da safe. — The continuative subject form already encodes the first-person singular. Adding ni before ina in a plain statement is unusual and sounds contrastive or emphatic: "It is I who study in the morning (not someone else)." That emphasis may occasionally be exactly what you want, but it is not the default for describing a routine. Use the continuative form alone in ordinary descriptions.

Common Mistake — Writing karfe without the hooked ƙ

Error: karfe biyu (using plain k instead of hooked ƙ)

Correction: ƙarfe biyu — The word for "o'clock / hour" begins with ƙ, the ejective velar stop. Writing it with plain k is a Boko spelling error. Since time expressions appear in almost every routine description, this mistake is likely to appear many times if you are not careful. Check every time expression you write: the first letter of ƙarfe must always be the hooked form. If your keyboard does not produce ƙ easily, find a Unicode input method or character map — substituting k is not acceptable in Boko writing.

Common Mistake — Producing the habitual -kan- form independently

Error: Attempting to produce Nakan karatu, Yakan yin aiki, etc. as a productive form in written or spoken exercises in this chapter.

Correction: Use the plain continuative plus a frequency word: Ina karatu kullum. (I study every day.) — The habitual -kan- form is introduced for recognition only in this chapter. Its full production rules and contexts are taught in Chapter 19. Using it now without that foundation risks producing an incorrect or unnatural form. The plain continuative with kullum, koyaushe, or sau da yawa is fully adequate and natural for expressing regular habits at this level.

Looking Ahead

Abin da ka/ki koya yanzu — What you know now

This chapter introduced the most important grammatical tool of Unit II: the Hausa continuative construction. You can now describe ongoing and regular activities for any person, number, and gender using the correct subject form and verified verbal noun. You can tell and ask the time, name the days of the week, use frequency and sequence expressions to connect activities into a coherent routine, and recognize (but not yet produce) the habitual -kan- form. The continuative construction you have learned here also explains the formulaic expressions from Chapters 3 and 6 — Ina magana da…, ina zaune a…, yana zaune a… — which you now understand as continuative subject form plus verbal noun. Every chapter from here to Chapter 20 will use and recycle the continuative. It is the foundation of expressing what people do, and you will build on it in every remaining unit.

Babi na 8: Abinci da Abin Sha — Food and Drink

In Chapter 8, you move into the domain of food, drink, meals, and preferences. You will learn common food and drink vocabulary, how to make polite requests, and how to say what you want or do not want. Grammatically, Chapter 8 introduces the most important new element built on top of the continuative you now know: the negative continuative. Hausa negation in the continuative is a discontinuous construction with specific parts — learning it is the next major step in your grammar development. You will also encounter high-frequency constructions with so (to like, to want) and begin working with direct-object reference in fixed, high-frequency frames. The verbal nouns you have learned in this chapter will reappear throughout Chapter 8.

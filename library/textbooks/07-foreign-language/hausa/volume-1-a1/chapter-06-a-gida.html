A Gida

Around the Home · Speak and Write Hausa: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common rooms, areas, furniture, and household objects in Hausa, with their correct grammatical gender and plural forms.
  2. State that something exists or is present using akwai and that something is absent using babu.
  3. Say that someone has something using the construction continuative subject form + da + possessed item.
  4. Say that someone does not have something using the negative possession pattern ba shi da / ba ta da and related forms.
  5. Describe where people and objects are located using common prepositions and location expressions.
  6. Distinguish near and far objects using expanded masculine, feminine, and plural demonstratives.
  7. Review genitive linkers -n and -r with household nouns and possessors.
  8. Identify the tone and vowel-length patterns of akwai, babu, and common location words through listening and pronunciation practice.

Communication Scenario

A relative visiting from out of town wants to see your home before the family gathers for an occasion. You walk her through each room, point out where things are, explain what the household has and does not have, and answer her questions about the layout. To do this smoothly in Hausa, you need words for the spaces and objects around you — and three grammatical tools that English handles with one verb ("have," "there is," "there is not") but Hausa handles with three distinct constructions.

  1. English uses "there is" and "there are" for both existence and a rough idea of availability. Hausa keeps these meanings more precisely separate. When you want to say that something exists or is present in a place, you use akwai. When you want to say something is absent or unavailable, you use babu. These two words are complete predicate expressions — they do not require a separate verb alongside them. Pay attention as they appear in the vocabulary and grammar sections, and notice that their pronunciation is distinct enough that confusing them would significantly change your meaning.
  2. When you want to say that a specific person has something, Hausa does not use a verb "have" the way English does. Instead, it uses a continuative subject form followed by da (with) and the item. So "she has a table" is literally structured as "she-is with table." This chapter introduces the possession pattern as a complete communication frame. The full continuative system is treated in depth in Chapter 7 — but the possession construction with da is introduced here because you need it to describe a home.

Pronunciation and Boko

Tone and vowel length in akwai, babu, and possession frames

Two of the most important new words in this chapter — akwai and babu — have specific tone and vowel-length patterns that distinguish them from each other and from other short words. Ordinary Boko does not mark tone or vowel length, so your ear and your memory must carry this information.

WordApproximate pronunciation guideKey contrast
akwaiThe first syllable ak- carries a high tone; the second syllable -wai ends with a falling glide. The a of the first syllable is short. Think of a sharp, falling two-syllable word: AK-wai.Signals presence or existence. Mixing it up with babu produces the opposite meaning.
babuBoth syllables are low tone: ba-bu with even, unstressed quality. Neither syllable carries a high pitch. The vowels are short.Signals absence or nonexistence. The steady low tone distinguishes it from the single ba that begins a negation construction.
Pronunciation Focus — Distinguishing akwai, babu, and ba

English speakers sometimes blur three distinct Hausa words because they look short and simple on the page:

  • akwai — there is / there are (existence; two syllables with a distinctive falling second syllable)
  • babu — there is no / there are none (two syllables, both low tone, steady)
  • ba — the first element of a discontinuous negation pattern (single syllable; you have seen it in ba malami ba ne)

Mixing these up causes significant confusion. A sentence with akwai kujera a falo (there is a chair in the living room) means the opposite of babu kujera a falo (there is no chair in the living room). Ask your instructor or audio model to demonstrate each word in isolation and in a sentence, then practice repeating until the distinction is automatic.

Contrastive consonants in household vocabulary

Several household words contain the hooked consonants and apostrophe-consonant that Hausa distinguishes from plain letters. Review them in this new vocabulary:

ƙasanƙ — hooked k; produced at the back of the throat with a glottal catch, giving a sharp ejective quality distinct from plain kDo not write kasan.

Pronunciation Focus — Phrase rhythm in location expressions

Hausa location expressions are compound phrases that flow as a single rhythmic unit in natural speech: a cikin ɗaki, a saman tebur, kusa da zaure. English speakers may be tempted to pause between the preposition and the noun, or to stress the preposition heavily. In natural Hausa speech, the rhythm tends to carry the main stress forward onto the final noun. Practice each location expression as one continuous phrase, not as two or three separate words. Your instructor or audio model will demonstrate the natural phrasing. As you read and write, remember that location words like cikin, saman, and bayan already contain a built-in linker — they do not need a separate -n or -r added after them in ordinary use.

Accurate spelling of linked and unlinked forms

Chapter 4 introduced the genitive linkers -n (masculine/plural possessed noun) and -r (feminine possessed noun). In household vocabulary, linked phrases appear frequently:

  • ɗakin kwanabedroom — literally "room of sleeping"; ɗaki is masculine → linker -n joined to create ɗakin
  • kofar gidadoor of the house / front gate — kofa is feminine → linker -r joined to create kofar
  • lambun gidayard of the house — lambu is masculine → lambun
  • wutar lantarkielectricity — literally "fire of electricity"; wuta is feminine → wutar

When you encounter these compound nouns, identify whether the first noun is masculine or feminine so you know which linker form to expect. Do not guess — check the vocabulary table.

Grammar Instruction

6.1 Existence: akwai

Purpose: state that something is present or exists in a place or situation.

Akwai expresses existence or presence. It precedes the noun or noun phrase describing what exists. It does not change for gender, number, or any other grammatical category — one form covers all persons and all nouns. A location phrase can follow the noun to specify where something is, but it is optional.

  • Akwai tebur a falo.There is a table in the living room.
  • Akwai kujeru a zaure.There are chairs in the entrance hall. (kujeru is the plural of kujera)
  • Akwai gado a ɗakin kwana.There is a bed in the bedroom.
  • Akwai ruwa a gida.There is water at home / in the house.
  • Akwai wutar lantarki a nan.There is electricity here.
  • Akwai dakunan kwana biyu.There are two bedrooms.
Language Note — akwai does not conjugate

Akwai is an invariant word — it has one form for all situations. It does not change based on who or what exists, and it does not agree in gender or number. This is quite different from English "there is / there are," which at least varies between singular and plural. In Hausa, whether you say "there is one chair" or "there are fifty chairs," the word akwai stays the same. The number information comes from the noun and any numeral that follows.

6.2 Nonexistence: babu

Purpose: state that something is absent, unavailable, or does not exist.

Babu is the negative counterpart of akwai. Like akwai, it is invariant — one form for all situations. It precedes the noun phrase and, like akwai, can be followed by a location phrase.

  • Babu tebur a ɗakin girki.There is no table in the kitchen.
  • Babu kujera a nan.There is no chair here.
  • Babu firiji a gida.There is no refrigerator at home.
  • Babu wutar lantarki yanzu.There is no electricity right now.
  • Babu ruwa a ɗakin wanka.There is no water in the bathroom.
  • Babu dakunan kwana a gida.There are no bedrooms in the house. (e.g., a studio or office)
Language Focus — akwai and babu are not verbs

Neither akwai nor babu is an ordinary verb in Hausa. They are existential predicates — complete statements of presence or absence on their own. This means you do not add a subject-pronoun form before them in ordinary statements: Akwai tebur (there is a table) is complete; it does not need anything before akwai. You also do not negate akwai by placing ba … ba around it. To express nonexistence, Hausa simply uses the separate word babu instead. Think of akwai and babu as a matched positive-negative pair, each complete on its own.

6.3 Possession: continuative subject form + da

Purpose: say that a specific person has something, using the possession construction introduced in this chapter.

When you want to say that a particular person has something, Hausa uses a structure that can be understood as "person-is with item." The first element is a continuative subject form — a short word that carries person, number, and gender information along with a continuative (ongoing/present) meaning. This is followed by da (with) and then the possessed item.

The continuative subject forms for possession statements are introduced here as a complete communication pattern. The full continuative system — including its use with verbal nouns to describe actions — is the central grammar of Chapter 7. For now, concentrate on the possession meaning.

You (2nd sg. m.)kanaKana da tebur.You have a table. (to a male)
You (2nd sg. f.)kinaKina da kujera.You have a chair. (to a female)
He (3rd sg. m.)yanaYana da firiji.He has a refrigerator.
She (3rd sg. f.)tanaTana da taga biyu.She has two windows.
We (1st pl.)munaMuna da dakunan kwana uku.We have three bedrooms.
You (2nd pl.)kunaKuna da zaure.You (all) have an entrance hall.
They (3rd pl.)sunaSuna da gida babba.They have a big house.

The continuative forms introduced here — ina, kana, kina, yana, tana, muna, kuna, suna — are the general continuative subject-pronoun forms. They appear in Chapter 3 formulaically in expressions like Ina magana da… and Ina zaune a… You have already used them as complete patterns. Now they are presented as a system. Their full grammar — including their use with verbal nouns to describe actions — is the topic of Chapter 7.

Language Building Block
continuative subject form + da + possessed item
Ina da gado a ɗakin kwana.
I have a bed in the bedroom.
Tana da tebur da kujeru biyu.
She has a table and two chairs.
Muna da dakunan kwana uku a gida.
We have three bedrooms at home.

The continuative subject form carries the subject information — who has the item. The word da links the subject to the item possessed. Do not add a separate independent pronoun (ni, shi, ita) before the continuative form in a plain possession statement. The subject information is already built into the continuative form.

6.4 Negative possession

Purpose: say that a person does not have something.

The negative of the possession construction uses a discontinuous negative pattern that surrounds the continuative subject form. The structure is: ba + continuative subject short form + da + item + ba. The continuative form used inside the negative is a shorter version of the full continuative form. These are the forms used:

PersonNegative possession patternEnglish
IBa ni da…I do not have…
You (m.)Ba ka da…You do not have… (to a male)
You (f.)Ba ki da…You do not have… (to a female)
HeBa shi da…He does not have…
SheBa ta da…She does not have…
WeBa mu da…We do not have…
You (pl.)Ba ku da…You (all) do not have…
TheyBa su da…They do not have…
  • Ba shi da firiji.He does not have a refrigerator.
  • Ba ta da ɗakin wanka.She does not have a bathroom.
  • Ba mu da wutar lantarki a gida.We do not have electricity at home.
  • Ba ni da kujera a ɗakina.I do not have a chair in my room.
  • Ba su da talabijin.They do not have a television.
Language Note — ba … da versus babu

Hausa uses two different constructions to express the absence of something, and they are not interchangeable:

  • Babu tebur a falo. — There is no table in the living room. (general absence; no specific person's possession is in view)
  • Ba shi da tebur. — He does not have a table. (a specific person lacks the item)

Use babu when you are stating that something does not exist or is not present in a location. Use the ba + short form + da pattern when you are stating that a specific person does not possess something. In practice, both patterns may overlap in some contexts, but keeping them distinct will make your Hausa more natural and precise.

6.5 Location statements

Purpose: state where a person or object is located.

Location in Hausa is typically expressed using yana / tana / ina (the continuative forms) followed by a location phrase, or by placing a location phrase after the noun it refers to. Location phrases use the prepositions and location words introduced in the vocabulary section.

  • Tebur yana a cikin falo.The table is in the living room. (masculine noun → yana)
  • Kujera tana a zaure.The chair is in the entrance hall. (feminine noun → tana)
  • Gado yana a ɗakin kwana.The bed is in the bedroom.
  • Firiji yana a cikin ɗakin girki.The refrigerator is in the kitchen.
  • Fitila tana a saman tebur.The lamp is on the table. (feminine → tana)
  • Ina a gida.I am at home.
  • Yana a waje.He is outside.

In location statements, yana is used with masculine singular subjects (including masculine nouns used as subjects) and tana with feminine singular subjects. This is the same pattern as in the possession construction above, and it is the continuative form in its location use. Chapter 7 will show you how the same forms appear with verbal nouns to describe actions.

6.6 Demonstratives expanded — near and far

Purpose: point to near and far household objects using the correct masculine, feminine, and plural demonstrative form.

You met the demonstratives wannan and wancan in earlier chapters. Chapter 6 expands your demonstrative repertoire with all the gender and distance forms you need for household description.

DistanceMasculine singularFeminine singularPluralMeaning
Near (this)wannanwannanwaɗannanthis / these (near the speaker)
Far (that)wancanwaccanwaɗancanthat / those (further from the speaker)
  • Wannan ɗaki ne babba.This room is big. (ɗaki masculine → wannan, near)
  • Waccan kujera ce tsohuwa.That chair is old. (kujera feminine → waccan, far)
  • Waɗannan kujeru ne sabbin.These chairs are new. (plural → waɗannan)
  • Wancan gado ne ƙarami.That bed is small. (gado masculine, far → wancan)
  • Wannan taga ce mai kyau.This window is nice. (taga feminine, near → wannan)
  • Waɗancan dakunan suna da tagogi.Those rooms have windows. (plural, far → waɗancan)
Language Note — wannan is the same for masculine and feminine singular

The near demonstrative wannan has one form for both masculine and feminine singular nouns. The far demonstrative, however, does distinguish gender: wancan for masculine singular, waccan for feminine singular. When you use a far demonstrative, you must know whether the noun is masculine or feminine in order to choose correctly. The safest approach: check the gender of the noun in the vocabulary table before producing a far demonstrative. The plural forms waɗannan (near) and waɗancan (far) cover all nouns regardless of gender.

6.7 Genitive linker review with household nouns

Purpose: link household nouns to possessors using the correct genitive linker.

You learned the genitive linkers -n (masculine/plural possessed noun) and -r (feminine possessed noun) in Chapter 4. Household vocabulary provides many opportunities to use them:

  • ɗakin AminaAmina's room (ɗaki is masculine → ɗakin)
  • kofar gidathe door of the house / the front gate (kofa is feminine → kofar)
  • tebur ɗakin girkithe kitchen table (tebur is masculine → tebur + ɗakin with the linker on ɗaki)
  • kujerar BelloBello's chair (kujera is feminine → kujerar)
  • dakunan gidathe rooms of the house (dakuna is plural → linker -n: dakunan)
  • fitilun falothe lights of the living room (fitilu, plural, → fitilun)

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Hauwa ta isa gidan 'yar uwarta Rakiya a Kano, inda Rakiya ta koma kwanan nan. Suna zagayawa a cikin gidan tare.

(Setting: Hauwa has arrived at the home of her sister Rakiya in Kano, where Rakiya has recently moved. They are taking a tour of the house together.)

HAUWA Sannu, Rakiya! Barka da sabuwar gida!Hello, Rakiya! Congratulations on the new house!
RAKIYA Yauwa, na gode, Hauwa. Barka. Zo mu kalle gidan tare.Thank you, Hauwa. Welcome. Come, let us look around the house together.
HAUWA Ina ne wannan ɗaki na farko?What is this first room here?
RAKIYA Wannan zaure ne. A nan mutane sukan zauna. Akwai kujeru — waɗannan kujeru sabbin ne. Muna da tebur ɗaya kuma.This is the entrance hall. Here is where people sit. There are chairs — these chairs are new. We also have one table.
HAUWA Mai kyau! Akwai falo?Nice! Is there a living room?
RAKIYA I, akwai falo a nan — gefen zaure. Yana da talabijin da kujeru da kuma rediyo. Waccan fitila ce a saman — tana ba da haske da kyau.Yes, there is a living room here — beside the entrance hall. It has a television and chairs and also a radio. That lamp up above — it gives good light.
HAUWA Dakunan kwana nawa suke a gida?How many bedrooms are there in the house?
RAKIYA Akwai dakunan kwana biyu. Ɗakin kwana na farko yana da gado da kabad. Ɗakin kwana na biyu — wancan — yana da gado ɗaya. Ba mu da kujera a can tukuna.There are two bedrooms. The first bedroom has a bed and a wardrobe. The second bedroom — that one — has one bed. We do not have a chair there yet.
HAUWA Akwai ɗakin wanka?Is there a bathroom?
RAKIYA I, akwai ɗakin wanka a cikin gida. Akwai ruwa — alhamdulillah. Amma babu wutar lantarki yanzu — muna jira.Yes, there is a bathroom inside the house. There is water — thank God. But there is no electricity right now — we are waiting.
HAUWA Akwai lambun gida?Is there a yard?
RAKIYA I! Lambun gida yana a bayan gida. Babba ne. Muna son dasa itace a can.Yes! The yard is at the back of the house. It is big. We want to plant a tree there.
HAUWA Wannan gida mai kyau ne, Rakiya. Barka da zama!This house is beautiful, Rakiya. Congratulations on settling in!

Vocabulary notes for the dialogue: barka da sabuwar gida = congratulations on the new home (common greeting for a new house); zo = come (command); mu kalle = let us look (formulaic subjunctive pattern, recognition only; full subjunctive in Chapter 11); na farko = first; na biyu = second; kuma = also, and also; nawa = how many; a can = there (further away); tukuna = yet; amma = but; muna jira = we are waiting (formulaic continuative); muna son dasa = we want to plant (recognition only); itace = tree; barka da zama = congratulations on settling in.

Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

  • Every akwai sentence places the noun phrase after akwai with no extra subject word before it.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Housing and household arrangements in Hausa-speaking communities

There is no single "Hausa home." Housing across Hausa-speaking communities varies significantly by geography, socioeconomic situation, urban or rural setting, historical period, and household structure. In many parts of northern Nigeria and Niger, a traditional residential unit is the gida — a walled compound that may house an extended family across multiple dwellings, with shared open spaces, separate kitchen areas, and communal access points. A compound of this kind might have a zaure — the male reception room at the entrance — which serves as the primary space for welcoming visitors before they enter the domestic interior.

In urban settings including Kano, Kaduna, and Niamey, apartment buildings, bungalows, government housing estates, and rented rooms in subdivided compounds are all common. A student or young worker may live in a single room shared with a roommate, as described in the reading. A government official or business person may occupy a freestanding house with separate rooms for distinct purposes.

The vocabulary in this chapter covers a range of contexts rather than one idealized floor plan. Words like falo (a loanword used in many urban homes for a general living room) coexist with zaure (a term more tied to traditional compound architecture). Both are productive vocabulary in this course because learners are likely to encounter both. When you describe a home in Hausa, choose vocabulary that fits the actual space — do not impose one architectural model on every household.

Culture in Context — Hospitality, the zaure, and entering a home

Greeting before entering a home or compound, and being appropriately welcomed in, are important social practices in many Hausa-speaking communities. A visitor who arrives at a home typically calls out from the entrance — Sannu da gida! (Greetings to the household!) or Ko an ji? (Is anyone there? / May I come in?) — before entering. Entering without announcing yourself would be considered rude in most settings.

The zaure — the entrance hall or reception area — often functions as the first social space for visitors, particularly in compound-style housing. Depending on the household structure and community norms, guests may be received there before being invited further inside. These practices vary by household, region, community, and relationship between visitor and host. Do not treat any one set of rules as universal across all Hausa-speaking communities or contexts.

What remains consistent is that greetings and courtesy are expected at the threshold of any interaction — including entering a physical space. The expression Barka da zama (congratulations on settling in), used in the model dialogue, is a warm and common way to acknowledge someone who has recently moved into a new home.

Regional Variation — Housing vocabulary across Nigeria and Niger

Some household vocabulary differs between Nigeria and Niger, reflecting both linguistic variation and the influence of different colonial and administrative languages. In Nigerien Hausa contexts, French-origin terms for household items may be more common alongside Hausa terms — for example, frigidaire alongside firiji for refrigerator. This course uses the productive Hausa terms consistently; recognize that in Niger, a speaker may use a different vocabulary item for the same object. Similarly, architectural vocabulary for room types and compound structures may vary by region and community. When you encounter a Hausa speaker from a different setting, follow their lead on local terms and do not assume that the vocabulary in this textbook is the only valid set of options.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Adding a subject word before akwai or babu

Error: *Gida akwai tebur. or *Shi akwai tebur. (placing a noun or pronoun subject before akwai)

Correction: Akwai tebur a gida. — The word akwai begins the predicate directly. It does not need — and does not accept — a separate subject noun or pronoun placed before it in a basic existence statement. The location can follow the noun: Akwai tebur a gida (there is a table at home). If you want to say "there is a table" with reference to a room or place, put the place phrase after the noun.

Common Mistake — Using babu instead of ba … da for personal possession

Error: *Babu shi tebur. or *Babu Musa firiji. (using babu to say a person does not have something)

Correction: Ba shi da tebur. / Ba Musa da firiji. — When you want to say a specific person does not have something, use the discontinuous negative possession pattern: ba + short form + da + item. Babu states general absence from a location; ba shi da / ba ta da states that a person lacks something. Both are correct Hausa — they mean different things.

Common Mistake — Using the wrong continuative form for the subject

Error: *Yana da tebur when the subject is a woman. Or: *Tana da tebur when referring to a male subject.

Correction: Yana da tebur (he has a table / the table is there — masculine subject). Tana da tebur (she has a table — feminine subject). The continuative forms carry gender in the third person singular: yana for masculine, tana for feminine. This is the same distinction you have seen in ne and ce — Hausa marks gender in agreement consistently. Before producing a possession or location statement, identify the gender of the subject so you choose the correct form.

Common Mistake — Using wancan instead of waccan with a feminine noun

Error: *Wancan kujera ce. (using the masculine far demonstrative with a feminine noun)

Correction: Waccan kujera ce. — Kujera (chair) is feminine. The far demonstrative for feminine singular nouns is waccan, not wancan. The near demonstrative wannan is the same for both genders, but the far demonstrative is gender-sensitive. Check the gender of the noun before choosing between wancan and waccan. Plural nouns take waɗancan regardless of gender.

Common Mistake — Forgetting the linker in compound room names

Error: *ɗaki kwana or *kofa gida (omitting the genitive linker inside a compound noun)

Correction: ɗakin kwana (bedroom); kofar gida (front gate / house door). When a room name or compound noun consists of a head noun followed by another noun, the genitive linker joins them. The linker matches the gender of the head noun: ɗaki is masculine → ɗakin; kofa is feminine → kofar. These compound forms should be memorized as complete units, but knowing the underlying linker rule will help you recognize and check them.

Looking Ahead

Abin da ka/ki koya yanzu — What you know now

You can now name the rooms, furniture, and common objects in a Hausa-speaking home with their correct gender and plural forms. You have three new grammatical tools: akwai to say that something is present or exists; babu to say that something is absent; and the possession construction (continuative subject form + da) to say that a specific person has something. You have also practiced location expressions, expanded your demonstrative repertoire to include all gender and distance forms, and reviewed genitive linkers in household compound nouns. Looking back: the gender agreement you practiced with ne and ce in Chapters 1–3, the genitive linkers introduced in Chapter 4, and the demonstratives from Chapter 2 are all active now in household description — the systems are growing together.

Babi na 7: Ayyukan Kullum — Daily Routines

In Chapter 7, the continuative subject forms you introduced in this chapter — ina, kana, kina, yana, tana, muna, kuna, suna — will move to the center of attention. You have used them to express possession and location. Now you will use them with verbal nouns to describe actions: what someone is doing, what they do regularly, and when they do it. The verbal noun is the key to the Hausa continuative construction, and Chapter 7 will show you how to form and use the verbal nouns of common high-frequency verbs. You will also learn to tell the time, name the days of the week, and sequence a daily routine — building directly on the home-and-activity context of this chapter.

Jiya da Makon da Ya Gabata

Yesterday and Last Week · Speak and Write Hausa: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. State what you and others did yesterday or last week using general completive subject-pronoun forms.
  2. Sequence several completed events using narrative connectors such as da farko, bayan haka, sannan, and a ƙarshe.
  3. Say what did not happen using the negative completive construction with ba … ba.
  4. Ask information questions about completed events using relative completive subject-pronoun forms.
  5. Use direct-object pronouns with verified completive verb forms in high-frequency frames.
  6. Write a connected 130–160-word account of completed events using appropriate completive forms, negative completive where needed, and sequencing connectors.
  7. Distinguish the completive subject-pronoun forms from the continuative forms you have already studied, both in pronunciation and in meaning.

Communication Scenario

A friend you have not seen since last week asks you what you have been up to. You want to describe five or six things you did: where you went, what you ate, who you saw, what you bought at the market, whether you rested — and also one thing that did not happen. This chapter prepares you to tell that kind of story accurately in Hausa.

  1. In Chapters 3 and 7 you learned that Hausa subject forms carry aspect information — the form itself tells you whether an action is ongoing or being identified in a particular context. Chapter 16 introduces a new set of subject forms: the completive forms, which signal that an event is viewed as completed. Completive meaning is carried by the subject-pronoun form, not by an ending added to the lexical verb. This is a central difference from the way English "I walked," "she ate," or "they bought" signals completion through a past ending on the verb.
  2. Information questions about completed events often require a different subject-pronoun form — the relative completive — because the questioned element is extracted from its usual position in the sentence. You first saw the principle of ordinary-versus-relative forms applied to origin questions in Chapter 3 and to continuative questions in Chapters 6 and 9. This chapter makes the contrast fully explicit for completive contexts. Learn each question pattern as a complete meaningful construction before focusing on its internal structure.

Pronunciation and Boko

Tone and vowel length in completive subject-pronoun forms

The completive subject-pronoun forms are short words — often one syllable — and their tone and vowel length are crucial to correct meaning. Two pairs require special care from English-speaking learners.

Sound Focus — General completive versus continuative: nā / ina, mun / muna

The first-person singular general completive form nā (I completed …) must be distinguished from the first-person singular continuative ina (I am …-ing). Both are short and appear in the same sentence position before the lexical verb or verbal noun. The difference is carried by the form itself: nā tafi means "I went / I have gone," while ina tafiya means "I am going / I go." Similarly, the first-person plural completive mun differs from the continuative muna. Listen carefully to your audio source and your instructor for the verified tone pattern of each form.

The third-person masculine singular completive yā must also be distinguished from the continuative yana. The completive is a single syllable with a long high-tone vowel; the continuative has two syllables. These are not interchangeable: yā tafi means "he went," while yana tafiya means "he is going." The difference in meaning is real and important — choose the correct form for the time reference and viewpoint of your sentence.

Sound Focus — Distinguishing completive from subjunctive forms

Several completive and subjunctive forms look similar in print but differ in tone and vowel length. For example, the first-person singular subjunctive in (that I …) and the completive nā are clearly different in sound, but the third-person forms can feel similar to learners at first. Pay attention to the vowel: completive yā has a long vowel, while the subjunctive third-person form has a short one. As with all Hausa grammatical contrasts, verify by listening rather than by reading the Boko alone, since ordinary Boko does not mark the length distinction. Your instructor or audio source is the reference for tone and length throughout this chapter.

Tone in narrative: pausing at connectors

When narrating a sequence of events, the connectors da farko, sannan, bayan haka, and a ƙarshe function as natural pause points. Pause briefly after each connector and let your pitch drop slightly before beginning the next clause. Practice reading the narrative examples in Section 16.3 aloud, marking the pause points before you speak. This rhythm helps your listener track the sequence of events.

Vowel length in the negative completive

The negative completive construction involves a negative prefix that precedes the verb and a final ba that closes the construction. The negative prefix for the first-person singular is ban — a short form with a specific tone pattern. Do not confuse it with ba alone (which appears as the second element of many negations) or with the separate word ba meaning "not" in other contexts. Each element of the negative completive has a verified form; listen for both parts rather than focusing only on the initial ban.

Grammar Instruction

16.1 The general completive: what it is and how it works

Purpose: state who did what, framing the action as completed.

Hausa completive meaning is expressed through a dedicated set of subject-pronoun forms — not through an ending added to the lexical verb. Compare English: "I walked to the market" signals completion through the -ed ending on walk. Hausa signals completion through the subject form itself: nā tafi kasuwa. The verb tafi (go) does not change; the subject form nā carries the completive information.

The table below shows the general completive subject-pronoun forms alongside the continuative forms you already know. Both columns are provided so you can see the contrast clearly. Study both, then focus your active practice on the completive column for this chapter.

Person and gender Continuative (Ch. 7) General completive (Ch. 16) English equivalent
1st sg. (I) ina nā I … (completed)
2nd sg. masc. (you, m.) kana kā you … (completed, m.)
2nd sg. fem. (you, f.) kina kin you … (completed, f.)
3rd sg. masc. (he) yana yā he … (completed)
3rd sg. fem. (she) tana tā she … (completed)
1st pl. (we) muna mun we … (completed)
2nd pl. (you pl.) kuna kun you all … (completed)
3rd pl. (they) suna sun they … (completed)

Tone and vowel length note: The forms above are standard Kano-based Hausa. The tone and vowel length of each form must be verified through listening and audio modeling; the Boko spelling does not fully represent these distinctions. Flag for expert review: the precise tone pattern of each completive subject form as written in this table, and any variant forms used in different but closely related registers or teaching traditions, should be confirmed by a qualified Hausa-language specialist before audio production.

In the completive, the lexical verb typically follows the subject form directly. A noun object follows the verb. A time expression may appear at the start of the sentence or at the end:

  • Nā tafi kasuwa jiya.I went to the market yesterday.nā (1sg completive) + tafi (verb) + kasuwa (noun object) + jiya (time)
  • Yā ci abinci a gida.He ate food at home.yā (3sg.m completive) + ci (verb) + abinci (noun object) + a gida (location)
  • Tā sadu da abokin ta jiya da dare.She met her friend last night.
  • Mun gama aiki da yamma.We finished work in the evening.
  • Sun dawo gida da dare.They returned home at night.
  • Jiya, kā sha ruwa da yawa?Did you (m.) drink a lot of water yesterday? (yes/no question — rising intonation)
Language Focus — Completive meaning is in the subject form, not the verb

This is the single most important point in Chapter 16. When an English speaker hears "I went" or "she ate," the past-tense signal is the verb ending (went, ate). In Hausa, the lexical verb itself does not carry that signal. The form tafi is the same whether you say nā tafi (I went) or you will later say — in a future construction — that someone is going to go. What changes is the subject form: nā signals "I, completed"; the future subject form signals "I, future." Keep your attention on the subject-pronoun form whenever you want to convey completive meaning. If you use a continuative form instead (ina tafi), you say "I am going," not "I went." The two forms are not interchangeable.

16.2 The negative completive

Purpose: state that a completed action did not happen, using a construction-specific negative pattern.

Hausa negation is construction-specific. The completive has its own negative pattern, which is different from the continuative negation you learned in Chapter 8. The completive negative uses a discontinuous construction: a negative element that combines with or precedes the subject, followed by the verb, followed by a final ba that closes the construction. Both elements — the initial negative and the final ba — are required. Omitting the final ba produces an incomplete sentence.

Person and gender Negative completive form Example with yi English meaning
1st sg. (I) ban … ba Ban yi ba. I did not do it.
2nd sg. masc. (you, m.) bà ka … ba Bà ka yi ba. You (m.) did not do it.
2nd sg. fem. (you, f.) bà ki … ba Bà ki yi ba. You (f.) did not do it.
3rd sg. masc. (he) bài … ba Bài yi ba. He did not do it.
3rd sg. fem. (she) bà ta … ba Bà ta yi ba. She did not do it.
1st pl. (we) bà mu … ba Bà mu yi ba. We did not do it.
2nd pl. (you pl.) bà ku … ba Bà ku yi ba. You all did not do it.
3rd pl. (they) bà su … ba Bà su yi ba. They did not do it.

Flag for expert review: The precise written forms of the negative completive paradigm — particularly the representation of the initial negative element (whether ban vs. bà na, bài vs. bà ya, etc.) — vary somewhat across teaching traditions and reference grammars for Kano-based Hausa. The forms above represent the most commonly cited standard; a qualified Hausa-language specialist should verify and normalize these forms before final publication, and tone patterns for each negative form must be confirmed through audio review.

  • Ban tafi kasuwa ba jiya.I did not go to the market yesterday.
  • Bài ci komai ba a yamma.He did not eat anything in the evening.
  • Bà mu sadu da su ba.We did not meet them.
  • Bà ta dawo ba har dare.She did not return until nighttime.
  • Bà su gama aiki ba jiya.They did not finish work yesterday.
Language Note — Both parts of the negative are required

English speakers sometimes produce only the initial negative element and omit the closing ba: saying *Ban tafi kasuwa instead of Ban tafi kasuwa ba. This is incomplete. The closing ba is not optional; it is the second bracket of a discontinuous negative construction. Think of the two elements together as a frame around the verb phrase: [negative + subject] … verb … [ba]. Every completive negative sentence in this chapter must include the closing ba. Check for it every time you write a negative sentence in your revision checklist.

16.3 Sequencing completed events with narrative connectors

Purpose: connect a series of completed events into a clear, readable narrative using sequencing connectors.

The connectors in the vocabulary table above are placed at the beginning of a sentence or clause and followed by the completive subject form and verb. They do not change the form of the subject or verb; they simply signal the order of events to the listener or reader. Here is a short model narrative:

Labarin jiya na Musa (Musa's story of yesterday)

Jiya, Musa yā fara aiki da safe. Da farko, yā sha kofi kuma yā karanta labarun yau. Sannan yā tafi ofis ɗinsa. A ofis, yā yi ayyuka masu yawa. Bayan haka, yā fita kasuwa da rana. Yā saya kayan miya da kayan abinci. A ƙarshe, yā dawo gida da yamma. Yā ci abinci tare da iyalinsa. Ban sadu da shi ba a wannan rana — ban sani ba cewa yana can.

New words for recognition: ofis = office; labarun yau = today's news; kayan miya = ingredients for soup/stew; kayan abinci = food items; iyalinsa = his family; ban sani ba = I did not know.

Notice how each sentence begins with a connector or a time phrase, followed immediately by the completive subject form (yā) and the verb. The final sentence switches to the first person (ban sadu da shi ba) — a negative completive — to contrast the narrator's experience with Musa's.

16.4 The relative completive in information questions

Purpose: ask who, what, where, when, and how much about a completed event, using the relative completive subject-pronoun form that the question structure requires.

You have already seen the principle that ordinary and relative forms alternate in Hausa. In Chapter 3, you learned that origin questions use a relative-continuative pattern. In Chapter 9, direct-object questions in continuative contexts introduced you to a second instance of the same principle. Chapter 16 makes this explicit for the completive: when a question word (or a focused element) is placed in front of the subject-pronoun and verb, Hausa uses a distinct set of relative completive forms rather than the general completive forms from Section 16.1.

Language Building Block
General completive: completive subject form + verb + object or complement
Yā tafi kasuwa jiya.
He went to the market yesterday.
Ban tafi ba.
I did not go. (negative completive: ban … ba frames the verb)
Information question: question word + relative completive subject form + verb

Where did he go? (question word ina triggers the relative completive form ya)

Use the general completive (yā, tā, mun, etc.) in ordinary statements. Use the relative completive (ya, ta, muka, etc.) when a question word or focused element comes first. Never mix the two sets within the same clause.

Language Note — The general-relative distinction is not new, only the forms are new

You first encountered the general-versus-relative principle in Chapter 3, where origin questions used a relative-continuative form (Daga ina kake?). In Chapters 6 and 9 you saw the same principle applied to possession and object questions. Chapter 16 applies the same organizing principle to the completive. The alternation is consistent: whenever a question word or focused element precedes the subject-pronoun slot, Hausa selects the relative form of that aspect's subject-pronoun set. The completive relative forms are a new set of words to learn, but the grammatical rule governing when to use them is one you have been building since Chapter 3.

You learned direct-object pronouns in Chapter 9. This section reviews their forms and shows how they combine with completive constructions. Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Zainab ta dawo daga tafiya zuwa Kaduna. Abokinta Farida ta zo ziyartarta a gida. Suna zaune a ɗakin zama suna magana.

(Setting: Zainab has returned from a trip to Kaduna. Her friend Farida has come to visit her at home. They are sitting in the living room talking.)

FARIDA Sannu da zuwa, Zainab! Lafiya lau bayan tafiyar?Welcome back, Zainab! Are you well after the trip?
ZAINAB Eh, na gode, Farida. Lafiya. Nā dawo jiya da dare. Nā gaji sosai, don haka nā huta safe da daddawa.Yes, thank you, Farida. I'm well. I returned last night. I was very tired, so I rested this morning until late.
FARIDA Yaya Kaduna? Mē ki yi a can?How was Kaduna? What did you do there?
ZAINAB Kaduna tana da kyau. Da farko, nā sadu da danginmu a ran Litinin. Sannan mun tafi kasuwa tare. Nā saya rigunan biyu da kayan miya. Bayan haka, mun ziyarci gidan mahaifiyar ta abokin nawa — tā yi abinci mai dadi sosai!Kaduna was nice. First, I met our relatives on Monday. Then we went to the market together. I bought two gowns and some ingredients. After that, we visited my friend's mother's house — she made a very delicious meal!
FARIDA To! Ina ku zauna — a gidan danginku?Really! Where did you all stay — at your relatives' house?
ZAINAB Eh. Mun zauna a can kwanaki uku. Amma bà mu huta sosai ba saboda ayyuka da yawa. Nā yi taro da yawa a ofis ɗin ɗan'uwana.Yes. We stayed there for three days. But we did not rest much because of a lot of work. I had many meetings at my brother's office.
FARIDA Yaushe ki dawo daidai?Exactly when did you return?
ZAINAB Nā tashi daga Kaduna da yamma na Laraba. Nā isa Kano da dare. A ƙarshe nā isa gida bayan tsakar dare kaɗan.I departed from Kaduna on Wednesday evening. I arrived in Kano at night. Finally I got home a little after midnight.
FARIDA Subhanallah! Daidai. Yanzu hutu ne. (tana dariya)Goodness! Indeed. Now it is time to rest. (laughing)

Vocabulary notes for the dialogue: sannu da zuwa = welcome (lit. "greetings with [your] coming"); gaji = be tired; daddawa = a locust-bean condiment (here used in safe da daddawa, meaning "late in the morning," a common Hausa time expression — flag for review); ziyarci = to visit (a place or person; verbal noun: ziyara); mai dadi = delicious, sweet; kwanaki uku = three days; taro = meeting; ɗan'uwa = brother (lit. "son of the [same] mother"); tashi = to depart, to set off; isa = to arrive; tsakar dare = midnight; Subhanallah = an Arabic-origin exclamation expressing admiration or surprise, common among many Hausa speakers — not universal; Yaushe ki dawo? uses the relative completive ki because yaushe precedes the subject.

Grammar notes for the dialogue: Mē ki yi a can? — note the relative completive ki (2sg.f) following the question word mē (what). Ina ku zauna? — relative completive 2pl ku follows ina. Yaushe ki dawo daidai? — relative completive 2sg.f. These are natural patterns to notice and learn as complete questions; their internal analysis is part of what you are building in Section 16.4.

  2. Zaɓi rana. Choose a real or imagined day from last week. Note at least six events that happened on that day, in the order they occurred. Include at least one event that did not happen.

Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

  • Every completed event uses the correct general completive subject form — not a continuative form, not an independent pronoun.
  • Every negative completive sentence includes both the initial negative element (e.g., ban, bài, bà mu) and the closing ba.
  • Any information question in the text uses the relative completive form rather than the general completive form after the question word.
  • Direct-object pronouns appear in the correct form (e.g., shi, ta, su) and the preceding verb uses the appropriate object-conditioned form where required.
  • At least four narrative connectors are used and placed correctly at the start of a sentence or clause.
  • Hooked letters (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ) and 'y are written correctly wherever they appear, including in words such as ɗauka, ƙarfi, and a ƙarshe.
  • Person names and place names are capitalized correctly.
  • Each sentence ends with a full stop.
  • The text reads as a connected narrative, not as a numbered list of events.

Speaking Practice

Atisaye na ƙa'ida — Controlled practice: form drills

With a partner, take turns transforming sentences between continuative and completive. Partner A says a continuative sentence (e.g., Ina tafiya kasuwa); Partner B converts it to the completive and adds jiya (e.g., Nā tafi kasuwa jiya). Then switch. Do at least ten exchanges before moving to the next activity.

Tambayoyi da amsoshi — Information-question practice

Partner A tells a short story of three completed events (speaking spontaneously, not reading from notes). Partner B asks three information questions using different question words (ina, me, yaushe, nawa, wa). Partner A answers. Then switch roles. Pay attention to using the relative completive form in every question and listen for it in your partner's questions.

Labari na makon da ya gabata — Last-week narrative

Without notes, describe what you did last week in Hausa. Include at least five completed events in sequence, at least one negative (something you did not do), and a conclusion using a ƙarshe. Your partner listens and then asks two follow-up questions. Answer those questions spontaneously.

Madadin karatu kai tsaye — Solo alternative

Record yourself telling a 90-second account of a completed real or imagined day. Listen back and check: (1) Did you use completive forms, not continuative forms, for completed events? (2) Did any negative completive sentences include the closing ba? (3) Did you use at least three narrative connectors to sequence the events? Note any corrections and re-record one of the sequences.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Catching up after time apart

Exchanging accounts of what has happened since the last time two people met is a natural and valued part of social interaction in many Hausa-speaking communities, as it is in many parts of the world. Asking about someone's trip, health, household, or recent activities signals continued interest and care. These exchanges are often preceded by appropriate greetings and an inquiry after the other person's well-being before moving into the details of what happened. Practice the model dialogue's opening — sannu da zuwa followed by a health inquiry — as a social skill alongside the grammar of narrating events.

The length and depth of such exchanges vary by relationship, time available, and setting. A brief catch-up between colleagues at work may focus on a single key event; a visit between close friends or family members may involve a detailed account of several days. Neither is more or less authentically Hausa — the social context determines the expected depth of narration.

Culture in Context — Travel, family visits, and distances across the Hausa-speaking world

Travel between major Hausa-speaking cities — Kano, Kaduna, Katsina, Sokoto, Zaria in northern Nigeria; Niamey, Maradi, Zinder in Niger — is a common topic of everyday conversation. These cities are connected by road, air, and in some areas rail, and people regularly travel between them for trade, family visits, religious observances, and work. The distances involved mean that a trip may take several hours by road or a short flight by air, and the journey itself often features in the account of the trip. Vocabulary for departure (tashi), arrival (isa), and the road or route (hanya) appears naturally in completed-event narration about travel.

Regional Variation — Expressing completed events across Hausa varieties

The completive forms taught in this chapter are those of Kano-based Standard Hausa. Other Hausa-speaking communities and some learner materials may present slightly different forms or spellings for the completive paradigm — in particular, the written representation of tone differences that distinguish general completive from relative completive forms can vary across reference grammars and teaching traditions. If you encounter a form in authentic speech or reading that looks different from what you have learned here, note it as a potential regional or register variant rather than treating it as an error in either direction. The forms in this chapter are the productive standard for this course; expert review should confirm them before audio production.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using a continuative form for a completed event

Error: *Ina tafi kasuwa jiya. (using the continuative form ina with a past time word)

Correction: Nā tafi kasuwa jiya. — The continuative form ina signals an ongoing or regular activity ("I am going / I go"). A completed event in the past requires the completive form nā. Adding jiya (yesterday) or another past time word to a continuative form does not produce a past meaning in Hausa; it produces a contradiction or an odd sentence. Change the subject form, not just the time word.

Common Mistake — Omitting the closing ba in a negative completive

Error: *Ban tafi kasuwa. (omitting the closing ba)

Correction: Ban tafi kasuwa ba. — The negative completive is a discontinuous construction that requires both the initial negative element and the closing ba. Stopping after the verb leaves the sentence structurally incomplete. This is one of the most consistent errors English speakers make with Hausa negation. Use your editing checklist to check every negative completive sentence before submitting written work.

Common Mistake — Using the general completive form after a question word

Error: *Ina yā tafi? (using the general completive yā after a question word)

Correction: Ina ya tafi? — When a question word precedes the subject-pronoun form, use the relative completive form (ya for 3sg.m, not yā). The difference between yā and ya is tonal and/or in vowel length — it may not be visible in unmarked Boko, which is one reason this error is so easy to make in writing. In speech the distinction is real; your audio model is the reference. In writing, make a habit of checking: is there a question word before this subject form? If so, use the relative completive.

Common Mistake — Applying verb-object alternations by guessing

Error: Assuming that because gani becomes gan before a direct-object pronoun, other verbs follow the same pattern — for example, writing *dub shi instead of duba shi, or *say shi instead of sayi shi.

Correction: Do not generalize the object-conditioned alternation by analogy. The alternation is grade-related and applies differently to different verbs. This chapter teaches only the verified set in Section 16.5. For verbs not in that set, use the noun-object (citation) form and mark the sentence for later expert correction. Producing an incorrect form is worse than producing a slightly less natural form, because incorrect forms can genuinely impede comprehension.

Common Mistake — Adding a past-tense ending to the lexical verb

Error: *Nā tafiye or *Yā ciyed — adding an invented ending to the verb to signal past meaning.

Correction: The lexical verb does not carry completive information in Hausa. Do not add any suffix to the verb to make it past. The completive subject form (nā, yā, mun, etc.) is the only required signal of completed meaning. The verb form itself stays the same as in a continuative or other construction. This is a predictable transfer error from English, where past endings on verbs are required. Actively resist the instinct to mark the verb itself.

Looking Ahead

Abin da ka/ki koya yanzu — What you know now

You now have a working command of the general completive: you can state what you and others did, in the correct subject-pronoun form, with appropriate time expressions. You can negate a completed event using the full discontinuous ba … ba construction, sequence events with narrative connectors, and ask information questions about completed events using relative completive forms. You have also placed direct-object pronouns after verified completive verb forms. These are the tools of narrative — the ability to tell a story about what happened, to deny what did not happen, and to ask a follow-up question about the details. Together with the continuative forms from Chapter 7, the future forms from Chapter 14, and the ability and necessity expressions from Chapter 13, you can now refer to the past, the present, and the future in Hausa across a wide range of everyday situations.

Babi na 17: Shirye-shirye da Gayyata — Plans and Invitations

Chapter 17 builds directly on the completive you have just learned by adding its contrasting counterpart: the future and subjunctive constructions for planning and inviting. You will consolidate the future construction with za from Chapter 14 and develop the subjunctive for proposals, suggestions, and desired actions — including how to make and accept or decline an invitation politely. The chapter also builds clause sequencing, showing how a future or subjunctive result clause follows a completed or conditional premise. You will have opportunities to contrast Yā tafi (he went), Yana tafiya (he is going), and Za ya tafi (he will go) — the three most important aspect contrasts in the course — in a single connected exchange. Object-pronoun forms reviewed in Chapter 16 will recur in invitation contexts in Chapter 17.

In the Classroom · Speak and Write Hausa: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common classroom objects in Hausa, with their grammatical gender and plural forms.
  2. Ask what an object is using Mene ne? and Mece ce?, and answer with ne or ce according to gender.
  3. Point to nearby and far objects using the near demonstratives wannan (singular) and waɗannan (plural) and the far demonstratives wancan (singular) and waɗancan (plural).
  4. Understand that Hausa has grammatical gender — masculine and feminine — and that plural reference uses its own pattern.
  5. State that something is not what it appears using a simple ba … ba ne/ce negative, as introduced.
  6. Follow and give a small set of fixed classroom commands in both singular and group forms.
  7. Count from 1 to 20 in Hausa.
  8. Ask for clarification, repetition, and help using approved classroom expressions.
  9. Pronounce and write the Hausa letters c, sh, and ts with their Hausa sound values, and accurately type and handwrite ɓ, ɗ, ƙ, and 'y.

Communication Scenario

You have just started a Hausa class in Kano. The instructor holds up objects — a pen, a book, a chair — and asks the group to name them. You need to know the words, but you also quickly realize that knowing a Hausa noun means more than knowing its meaning: you need to know whether it is masculine or feminine, because that choice affects the word used to identify it. You also need strategies to slow the instructor down when things move too fast. By the end of this chapter, you will have the tools to navigate your first Hausa classroom.

  1. In English, you can say "this is a book" about any noun without changing the word "this." Hausa handles pointing differently — the word for "this" can depend on whether the noun is singular or plural, though not on its gender in the same way English gender languages do. As you work through the chapter, pay attention to how wannan and waɗannan are used and what triggers the switch between them.
  2. Hausa has two identification words where English has one: ne and ce. Both mean "is" in identification sentences, but they are not interchangeable. Think about what information the choice between them carries — you will find the answer in this chapter's grammar section.

Language Note — Gender is grammatical, not biological

Hausa grammatical gender — masculine and feminine — is a property of the noun itself. It does not directly describe people as male or female. Tebur (table) is feminine even though tables have no sex. Alkalami (pen) is masculine for the same arbitrary grammatical reason. You learn the gender of each noun when you learn the noun, just as you learn its meaning. Agreement — how the words around a noun change to match it — is the most reliable evidence of gender. Throughout this chapter, watch how ne and ce track the gender of the noun they identify.

Pronunciation and Boko

The Hausa values of c, sh, and ts

Three Hausa consonant letters frequently cause confusion for English speakers because their sound values differ from English conventions. Learn each one as a consistent, single Hausa sound.

Letter(s)Hausa soundApproximate guideExample wordMeaning
cA voiceless palatal affricate — roughly like English ch in church, but said further forward in the mouthThink of a sharp, clear ch — never like English k or sajiclassroom
shLike English sh in shipConsistent with Englishshidasix
tsA glottalized or ejective ts sound — the ts is said with a catch of the breath; not like the ts in English catsBegin with the ts of English pizza said emphatically, then add a slight glottal catchtsayastop
Sound Focus — c in Hausa Boko

The letter c in Hausa Boko never has the English value it has in words like cat (k) or city (s). In Standard Hausa, c represents a single consistent sound — a palatal affricate, similar to English ch but produced a little further forward in the mouth, with the blade of the tongue against the hard palate. When you see c in a Hausa word, always produce this one sound. Do not vary it according to English spelling conventions. The word aji (classroom) contains this sound at the start: pronounce it ch-ah-jee rather than k-ah-jee or s-ah-jee.

Reviewing the hooked consonants and 'y

Chapter 1 introduced the hooked letters ɓ, ɗ, ƙ, and the consonant 'y. These remain active throughout every chapter of this course. Practice writing and typing them again with this chapter's vocabulary.

Writing Practice — Hooked letters and 'y in Chapter 2 vocabulary

Each of the following words from this chapter contains a letter that requires special attention in writing. Practice each word carefully, making sure the hook, the tail, or the apostrophe is present in your written or typed output.

  • ɗalibi — the hooked ɗ is an implosive, produced differently from plain d; the hook is part of the standard spelling
  • ɗaya — the number "one"; again, ɗ with the hook
  • huɗu — the number "four"; the ɗ appears in the middle of the word
  • ƙofa — "door"; the hooked ƙ is an ejective, distinct from plain k
  • alkalami — "pen"; begins with al-, a common Arabic-origin prefix; note the plain k here, not hooked

If your keyboard or input method makes hooked letters difficult, do not substitute plain letters. Find the correct Unicode characters: ɓ (U+0253), ɗ (U+0257), ƙ (U+0199), and 'y (apostrophe U+2018 or straight apostrophe + y). Verify that your font renders each character correctly.

Short and long vowels — continuing awareness

Hausa contrasts short and long vowels in many words. Ordinary Boko does not mark this distinction in everyday writing, but it is real and affects meaning. In this chapter, listen carefully to how your instructor, audio model, or partner pronounces words like littafi and alkalami — the vowel lengths are set and must not be changed arbitrarily. Begin building the habit of noticing length as part of the pronunciation of each word you learn.

Pronunciation practice: Say each pair aloud and listen for the difference your instructor or audio model demonstrates. (Length is shown here in the pronunciation note only, not in ordinary Boko.)

  • alloPronunciation note: the first vowel is short; say al-lo, not a drawn-out first syllable
  • littafiPronunciation note: listen for the doubled consonant and the length of the vowels across the word
  • malamiPronunciation note: three syllables — ma-la-mi; contrast with malama (two syllables differ)

Tone discrimination — continuing awareness

Hausa tone is part of every spoken word, even when the written page gives you no marks to guide you. In this chapter, listen and imitate rather than analyzing tone technically. Two goals:

  1. Hear that littafi (book) and littattafai (books) have different tone patterns — even though both have the same base meaning.
  2. Notice that the identification particles ne and ce carry tones of their own. Your instructor's model or the audio will demonstrate this. Do not rush past these short words — they are tonally significant.

Grammar Instruction

2.1 Grammatical gender: masculine, feminine, and plural

Purpose: understand that every Hausa singular noun has a grammatical gender, and that plural nouns use a distinct pattern of agreement.

Hausa distinguishes three agreement categories in the noun system:

CategoryLabel used in this courseEvidence
Masculine singularm.Takes ne in identification sentences; masculine pronouns in later chapters
Feminine singularf.Takes ce in identification sentences; feminine pronouns in later chapters
Pluralpl.Takes ne in identification sentences; plural pronouns in later chapters

Notice immediately: plural nouns in Hausa take ne in identification sentences, just like masculine singular nouns do. This does not mean plurals are masculine — it means plural agreement is its own category, which happens to use the same identification particle as masculine singular in this construction. Watch for this throughout the course.

Language Note — Why spelling does not reliably predict gender

Many Hausa feminine nouns end in -a: tebur is an exception (it ends in -r but is feminine), and kujera ends in -a and is feminine. However, littafi (book) does not end in -a and is masculine, while takarda (paper) ends in -a and is feminine. The pattern -a → feminine is a useful tendency to notice, but it is not a rule you can apply blindly to a new noun. The safest approach is to learn the gender of each noun when you learn the word, and to let agreement in sentences confirm what you have learned. Do not infer gender from spelling alone.

2.2 Identification with ne and ce

Purpose: identify classroom objects and people using the appropriate identification particle.

In Chapter 1, you encountered ne and ce in personal introductions: Audu ne (Audu is a man's name, so ne); Fatima ce (Fatima is a woman's name, so ce). The same system applies to objects: you use ne with masculine nouns and plural nouns, and ce with feminine nouns.

  • Alkalami ne.It is a pen. / This is a pen. (alkalami is masculine → ne)
  • Littafi ne.It is a book. (littafi is masculine → ne)
  • Tebur ce.It is a table. (tebur is feminine → ce)
  • Kujera ce.It is a chair. (kujera is feminine → ce)
  • Littattafai ne.These are books. (plural → ne)
  • Kujeru ne.These are chairs. (plural → ne)

The noun comes first, then the particle. The particle is a short separate word — do not attach it to the noun without a space.

2.3 Asking what an object is: Mene ne? and Mece ce?

Purpose: ask the name of an unfamiliar object in a classroom setting.

To ask "What is this?" or "What is it?" in Hausa, the question changes depending on what kind of referent you expect:

  • Mene ne?What is it? — used when the referent is masculine or when you do not yet know the gender
  • Mece ce?What is it? — used when the referent is feminine

In practice, when pointing to an unknown object whose gender you have not yet learned, you will most often begin with Mene ne? as a default question. Once you learn the noun and its gender, you will know which form to use in future references.

  • Wannan mene ne?What is this? (pointing to something nearby; expecting a masculine or unknown referent)
  • Wannan mece ce?What is this? (pointing to something nearby; expecting a feminine referent)
  • A: Wannan mene ne? · B: Alkalami ne.A: What is this? · B: It is a pen.
  • A: Wannan mece ce? · B: Tebur ce.A: What is this? · B: It is a table.

2.4 Demonstratives: wannan, wancan, waɗannan, waɗancan

Purpose: point to nearby or distant objects in the singular and plural.

Hausa has two sets of demonstratives — one for nearby things and one for distant things — and each set has a singular and a plural form. Unlike the identification particles ne and ce, these demonstratives do not change for gender within each singular/plural distinction. The same singular form is used for masculine and feminine nouns alike.

DistanceSingular (m. and f.)PluralMeaning
Near (here)wannanwaɗannanthis / these
Far (there)wancanwaɗancanthat / those
  • Wannan alkalami ne.This is a pen. (singular, near)
  • Wannan tebur ce.This is a table. (singular, near — demonstrative unchanged for gender)
  • Waɗannan alkaluma ne.These are pens. (plural, near)
  • Wancan littafi ne.That is a book. (singular, far)
  • Waɗancan kujeru ne.Those are chairs. (plural, far)
Language Building Block
demonstrative + noun + ne/ce
Wannan alkalami ne.

Wannan tebur ce.

Waɗannan littattafai ne.

The demonstrative (wannan or waɗannan) is the same regardless of the noun's gender. What changes with gender is the identification particle: ne for masculine singular and all plurals; ce for feminine singular.

2.5 Noun plurals — learned as lexical forms

Purpose: use the correct plural of each classroom noun introduced in this chapter.

Hausa plural formation is highly varied. Some nouns change vowels inside the word, some add suffixes, some do both, and some are suppletive (completely different words). There is no single suffix that safely forms the plural of any new Hausa noun. For this reason, the course introduces each noun's plural alongside its singular, and asks you to learn both as part of the lexical entry.

Study the following singular-plural pairs from this chapter. Notice that the patterns differ across words:

2.6 Fixed classroom commands

Purpose: understand and follow classroom instructions in singular and group address.

Hausa commands are introduced in this chapter as fixed expressions — the full productive system of imperatives and subjunctive patterns is developed in Chapter 11. For now, learn the following commands as communication tools, without analyzing their internal grammar.

A command to one person uses a special singular imperative form. A command to a group — or to one person in a respectful register — uses the pattern ku + verb base. The word ku here is a formulaic marker of plural or respectful address; its full grammatical role is explained later in the course.

Singular (to one person)Group / Respectful (ku + …)Meaning
Karanta.Ku karanta.Read.
Rubuta.Ku rubuta.Write.
Saurara.Ku saurara.Listen.
Duba.Ku duba.Look. / Check.
Tsaya.Ku tsaya.Stop. / Stand up.
Zauna.Ku zauna.Sit down.
Maimaita.Ku maimaita.Repeat.
Buɗe littafi.Ku buɗe littafi.Open the book.
Rufe littafi.Ku rufe littafi.Close the book.

You can add don Allah (please) before or after a command to make it more courteous: Don Allah, ku saurara. (Please listen, everyone.)

Language Note — ku is not always the only difference

The ku before a group command in Hausa does more than signal plural address — it also signals a type of verb form (the subjunctive) that will be taught fully in Chapter 11. For now, treat ku + verb as an unanalyzed pattern meaning "everyone, please [do this]." Do not attempt to produce novel ku combinations beyond the examples in this chapter. When you reach Chapter 11, you will reconnect this familiar pattern to the full grammar. Using a command outside the taught list at this stage may produce an incorrect form, because some singular imperatives in Hausa are irregular.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Malama Aisha tana fara darasi a aji a Kano. Ɗalibai biyu — Bello da Zainab — suna zaune tare. Harshe: Hausa ta yau ta ma'aikata ta Kano.

(Setting: Teacher Aisha begins a lesson in a classroom in Kano. Two students — Bello and Zainab — are seated together. Language: contemporary educated Kano Hausa.)

MALAMA AISHA Sannu, ɗalibai. Barka da safiya.Hello, students. Good morning.
ƊALIBAI Yauwa, malama. Barka da yamma.Hello, teacher. (Response to morning greeting.)
MALAMA AISHA Ku saurara. Wannan mene ne?Everyone listen. What is this?

(Malama Aisha tana ɗaga alkalami. / Teacher Aisha holds up a pen.)

BELLO Alkalami ne.It is a pen.
MALAMA AISHA Nagode. Madalla. Kuma wannan mece ce?Thank you. Well done. And what is this?

(Malama Aisha tana ɗaga kujera. / Teacher Aisha lifts a chair slightly.)

ZAINAB Kujera ce.It is a chair.
MALAMA AISHA Daidai. Kujera ɗaya, kujeru biyu. Yanzu — Bello, rubuta "alkalami" a kan allo.Correct. One chair, two chairs. Now — Bello, write "alkalami" on the board.
BELLO Yi haƙuri, malama. Ban fahimta ba. Don Allah, sake faɗa.Sorry, teacher. I did not understand. Please say it again.
MALAMA AISHA To. Ka rubuta kalmar "alkalami" a kan allo, don Allah.All right. Please write the word "alkalami" on the board.
BELLO Na fahimta. Na gode.I understand. Thank you.
ZAINAB Malama, wannan mece ce? (tana nuna takarda)Teacher, what is this? (pointing to a piece of paper)
MALAMA AISHA Takarda ce. Kyau — kina tambayar tambayoyi na kyau.It is paper. Good — you are asking good questions.

Reading

Read the two short texts below. They are written for beginner learners of Hausa. As you read, look for classroom nouns, demonstratives, identification particles, and numbers.

Rubutu na 1 — Ajina (My classroom)

Wannan ajina ne. A cikin aji akwai kujeru ashirin da tebura biyu. Akwai littattafai da yawa a kan tebur. Malama tana da allo mai girma. Akwai alkaluma da yawa kuma — alkaluma ja, shudi, da rawaya.

Writing Workshop

  1. Karanta samfuri. Reread Rubutu na 1 (Ajina) above. Underline each classroom noun. Circle the identification particles ne or ce where they appear.
  2. Shirya tunaninka. Look around the room you are in — or imagine a classroom. List six objects using Hausa nouns from this chapter. Next to each, write its gender (m. / f.) and its plural form.
  3. Rubuta farkon rubutunka. Write six to eight Hausa sentences describing the objects in your space. Use wannan … ne/ce and waɗannan … ne as sentence frames. Include at least two plural nouns, at least one number, and at least one color.

  5. Rubuta siffa ta ƙarshe. Produce a clean final version for your portfolio or to share with a partner or instructor.

Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

  • Each singular noun is followed by the correct particle: ne for masculine, ce for feminine.
  • Each plural noun is followed by ne.
  • I used wannan with singular nouns and waɗannan with plural nouns.
  • Hooked letters (ɗ, ɓ, ƙ) and 'y are written correctly wherever they appear.
  • Numbers are spelled correctly as Hausa words.
  • Each sentence ends with a full stop.
  • I have not added tone marks or vowel-length marks to ordinary Boko writing.

Ƙarin aiki — Additional task: Write three classroom commands — one singular and one group form for each verb. Add don Allah to each one. Read them aloud to a partner, who must perform the action or say whether it was addressed to one person or the group.

Speaking Practice

Atisaye na ƙa'ida — Controlled practice

Pick up or point to five objects in the room. For each one, ask your partner Wannan mene ne? or Wannan mece ce? (choose based on the gender you have learned). Your partner gives a full-sentence answer using the correct particle. After five rounds, switch roles and move to far objects: Wancan mene ne?

Wasan "Ni ina zaton" — Guessing game

One person thinks of a classroom object from this chapter's vocabulary list. The other asks yes-no questions in English to narrow it down, but the answers must be in Hausa: I (yes) or A'a (no). When you think you know the object, make a guess in Hausa: Wannan alkalami ne? The other person confirms with a full sentence: I, alkalami ne. or A'a, ba alkalami ba ne. (No, it is not a pen.) — this negative form is new; use it receptively here and learn it fully in Chapter 3.

Wasan umarnai — Commands game

One person gives a classroom command from this chapter's table, choosing the singular or group form depending on how many people they are addressing. The other person or group performs the action. If the wrong form is used (for example, a singular command given to the whole class), the other participants point it out politely: Don Allah, ku… (Please, everyone…). This is practice — supportive correction is part of learning.

Madadin karatu kai tsaye — Solo alternative

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Classroom address and courtesy in Hausa-speaking settings

In many Hausa-speaking learning environments — whether in formal schools in northern Nigeria, community education settings, or informal study groups — greeting before the lesson is a natural and expected part of the interaction. A student who enters without greeting may be considered rude, regardless of how quickly the lesson needs to start. The forms of greeting vary by time of day, relationship, institution, and region.

The title malami (male teacher) or malama (female teacher) is widely used as both a professional title and a respectful form of address in northern Nigeria. Saying Malama Aisha or simply malama when addressing a female teacher is typically appropriate and respectful across a range of educational and community settings. These conventions are not uniform across all Hausa-speaking communities, and institutional expectations — in a university, a community center, or a quranic school — may differ.

Classroom interaction in Hausa-speaking settings can range from highly formal lecture-style instruction to warm and interactive sessions. The classroom language in this chapter represents a middle register suitable for educated adult learning environments in northern Nigeria. It is not the only register that exists, but it is a reliable and broadly appropriate model for a beginner learner.

Regional Variation — Classroom vocabulary across Nigeria and Niger

The word allo (blackboard, whiteboard) is the standard productive form in this course, used widely in northern Nigeria. You may also encounter the borrowed form bodi (from English "board") in informal usage in some Nigerian urban settings. This course uses allo throughout.

In Niger, the standard Hausa orthography uses ƴ (a hooked y with a descender) in some positions where Nigerian Boko writes 'y (an apostrophe followed by y). This course follows Nigerian Standard Boko throughout; 'y is the productive form. When you encounter Hausa texts from Niger, you may see ƴ in the same positions. Both represent the same speech sound. Neither form is more "correct" Hausa — they reflect different national orthographic conventions.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using ne with a feminine noun

Error: Wannan kujera ne. (using the masculine particle with a feminine noun)

Correction: Wannan kujera ce. — The identification particle must match the grammatical gender of the noun, not the English equivalent. Kujera is feminine, so it takes ce. Checking gender in the vocabulary table before producing an identification sentence is a good habit at this stage.

Common Mistake — Using wannan with a plural noun

Error: Wannan littattafai ne. (using the singular demonstrative with a plural noun)

Correction: Waɗannan littattafai ne. — The demonstrative changes from wannan to waɗannan when the noun is plural. This is a one-step check: singular noun → wannan; plural noun → waɗannan. Check the number of the noun before choosing the demonstrative.

Common Mistake — Guessing the plural with a universal suffix

Error: Forming a plural like kujerobi or littafiai by adding an invented ending

Correction: kujeru (chairs) and littattafai (books) — Hausa plural formation is not predictable from a single rule. Do not add -ai, -una, or any other ending to a new noun without checking its plural in the vocabulary list or a dictionary. Learn each plural as part of the noun's entry. Using a wrong plural is a significant error in Hausa because the plural form affects agreement words throughout the sentence.

Common Mistake — Replacing ɗ, ƙ, or 'y with a plain letter

Error: Writing daya instead of ɗaya, kofa instead of ƙofa, or y instead of 'y

Correction: ɗaya, ƙofa, 'y — These characters are part of the standard Nigerian Boko orthography. Omitting a hook or an apostrophe is a spelling error, not a stylistic choice. Daya and ɗaya are different — in Standard Hausa, the word for "one" is spelled with the hooked letter. If your keyboard or software makes hooked letters difficult, fix the input method; do not substitute plain letters.

Common Mistake — Assuming -a endings signal feminine gender

Error: Treating every noun ending in -a as feminine, or every noun not ending in -a as masculine

Correction: Check the vocabulary table. Taga (window) ends in -a and is feminine — that matches the pattern. But allo (blackboard) ends in -o and is masculine. And tebur (table) ends in -r and is feminine. Spelling does not reliably determine gender. The vocabulary table and agreement forms in context are your evidence. Learn gender with the noun, not from a spelling guess.

Looking Ahead

Abin da ka/ki koya yanzu — What you know now

You can now name classroom objects in Hausa with their gender and plural forms, point to singular and plural objects using wannan, wancan, waɗannan, and waɗancan, identify objects correctly with ne and ce, count from 1 to 20, give and follow basic classroom commands, and ask for help when you need it. You have also deepened your understanding of why Hausa gender matters in practice: the choice between ne and ce in identification sentences is your first active use of grammatical gender agreement. This principle — that words in a Hausa sentence must agree with the gender and number of the noun they relate to — will grow with you through every chapter that follows.

In Chapter 3, you move beyond the classroom and into conversations about people and places.

Hausa ta Ilimi da Bincike

C1–C2-oriented Specialization · Chapter 6 of 20, Opening Unit II · Speak and Write Hausa: Advanced Precision and Specialization
Unit II Opens — Academic and Research Hausa · New Vocabulary Domain, Consolidated Grammar

Chapter Overview

Chapter 6 opens Unit II, Fannonin Ƙwarewa (Domains of Expertise), by turning to academic and research-oriented Hausa. This chapter introduces no new grammar; all sentence patterns were already consolidated by the end of Volume IV and Unit I. What is new is a specialized vocabulary domain: the language of summaries, abstracts, arguments, and critiques. Amina, whose work on the proverb-based story in Chapter 3 and Chapter 5 sparked a deeper interest in Hausa oral literature, decides to write a short research note on regional variation in a familiar proverb. Ibrahim, Salamatu, and Zainab, continuing their mentoring roles from Unit I, form an informal peer-review circle to help her plan, draft, summarize, and critique her work.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify and use core academic vocabulary: bincike, taƙaitawa, hujja, sakamako, matsaya, and suka.
  2. Read a short research abstract and identify its topic, method, and main finding.
  3. Summarize a short research-oriented passage in your own words, using consolidated sentence patterns.
  4. State a position or claim and support it with evidence, using vocabulary from this chapter.
  5. Offer a respectful, constructive critique of another learner's short written work.
  6. Distinguish the register of an academic note from the rhetorical, literary, and media registers studied in Unit I.
  7. Complete an integrated listening, speaking, reading, and writing task built around a short research project.

Communication Scenario

After the cultural evening, Amina cannot stop thinking about the proverb she used in her story: “Haƙuri maganin duniya” (“Patience is the remedy for the world”). She notices that different people at the event stated the proverb slightly differently and explained its meaning in different ways. She decides to write a short research note describing what she observed and asks Ibrahim, Salamatu, and Zainab to read her draft, summarize it back to her, and offer a critique before she shares it more widely.

  1. Domain: reading and writing a short research-oriented note — abstract, argument, evidence, and critique.
  2. Target skill: using the new academic vocabulary domain within already-consolidated grammar patterns.
  3. Grammar status: identifying predicates, sequencing, reference tracking, and comparison remain at Consolidated status, as in Chapter 5; no new grammar is introduced.
  4. No unscheduled grammar beyond the Volume IV level, and no vocabulary domain beyond academic and research Hausa as scheduled for this chapter.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — The measured pace of academic register

No new pronunciation feature is introduced in this chapter. Learners instead apply consolidated tone and vowel-length control to a fifth delivery style: the measured, even pacing of academic explanation, distinct from the animated pacing of rhetoric (Chapter 2) and the brisk pacing of a media announcement (Chapter 4). Pay particular attention to the vowel length in bincike and the tone contrast between suka (critique) and words that begin with a similar syllable but carry a different tone pattern, since mishearing this word can change the meaning of a sentence from constructive feedback to something closer to an accusation.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new grammar. It applies the identifying-predicate pattern, sequencing connectors, reference tracking, and comparative patterns already consolidated through Chapter 5 to the new academic vocabulary domain.

6.1 Stating a research topic

A research note commonly opens by identifying its topic, recycling the identifying-predicate pattern used for speeches in Section 5.2.

  • Wannan bincike shi ne game da bambancin yadda ake faɗin wani karin magana a wurare daban-daban. This research is about the variation in how a particular proverb is stated in different places.

    • 6.2 Summarizing (taƙaitawa)

    Summarizing recycles the sequencing and reference-tracking patterns from Chapter 5's comparison sentences, applied to condensing a longer text into its main points.

  • Idan na taƙaita abin da na lura da shi, mutane biyu sun faɗi karin maganar iri ɗaya, amma na uku ya faɗi ta wata hanya dabam. If I summarize what I observed, two people stated the proverb the same way, but a third stated it differently.

    • 6.3 Stating a position and supporting it with evidence (hujja)

    Stating a position and giving evidence recycles the causal and explanatory patterns already consolidated in earlier volumes.

  • Matsayata ita ce cewa bambancin ba kuskure ba ne; hujjata ita ce cewa dukan sigogin nan uku suna da ma’ana ɗaya. My position is that the variation is not an error; my evidence is that all three versions share the same meaning.

    • 6.4 Offering a critique (suka)

    A respectful critique recycles the contrastive and comparative patterns from Section 5.5, applied to feedback on written work rather than to comparing genres.

  • Sukata ita ce: rubutunki yana da kyau, amma za a iya ƙara wani misali domin ƙarfafa hujjarki. My critique is: your writing is good, but you could add another example to strengthen your evidence.
    • Language Note — New vocabulary, familiar grammar

    Every sentence pattern in Sections 6.1–6.4 was already consolidated by the end of Chapter 5. The task in this chapter is to redeploy those patterns — identifying, summarizing, arguing, and critiquing — using the new academic vocabulary domain rather than to learn a new structure.

    Common learner error: Do not confuse suka (critique, a normal part of academic and peer-review discourse) with zargi (accusation). Using zargi where suka is meant can make constructive feedback sound like a personal accusation.

    Model Dialogue

    Yanayin hali: Amina ta kawo wa Ibrahim, Salamatu, da Zainab wani gajeren bincike game da karin maganar da ta yi amfani da ita a Babi na 5.

    (Setting: Amina brings Ibrahim, Salamatu, and Zainab a short research note about the proverb she used in Chapter 5.)

    AMINA Na fara wani ɗan bincike game da yadda mutane daban-daban suke faɗin karin maganar “Haƙuri maganin duniya.”I've started a small research project on how different people state the proverb “Haƙuri maganin duniya.”
    ZAINAB Mai ban sha’awa! Menene tambayar binciken?Interesting! What's the research question?
    AMINA Tambayar binciken ita ce: shin sigar karin maganar tana canzawa daga wuri zuwa wuri, kuma shin canjin yana shafar ma’anarta?The research question is: does the form of the proverb change from place to place, and does the change affect its meaning?
    IBRAHIM Ka taƙaita mana abin da ka lura da shi a daren nan na al’adu?Can you summarize what you observed on the night of the cultural evening?
    AMINA Idan na taƙaita abin da na lura da shi, mutane biyu sun faɗi karin maganar iri ɗaya, amma na uku ya faɗi ta wata hanya dabam.If I summarize what I observed, two people stated the proverb the same way, but a third stated it differently.
    SALAMATU Menene matsayarki game da wannan bambanci?What's your position on this variation?
    AMINA Matsayata ita ce cewa bambancin ba kuskure ba ne; hujjata ita ce cewa dukan sigogin nan uku suna da ma’ana ɗaya.My position is that the variation is not an error; my evidence is that all three versions share the same meaning.
    ZAINAB Zan iya ba ki suka kaɗan?May I offer you a little critique?
    AMINA Ee, don Allah.Yes, please.
    ZAINAB Sukata ita ce: rubutunki yana da kyau, amma za a iya ƙara wani misali domin ƙarfafa hujjarki.My critique is: your writing is good, but you could add another example to strengthen your evidence.
    AMINA Wannan shawara ce mai kyau. Zan ƙara wani misali daga inda kakata take zaune.That's good advice. I'll add another example from where my grandmother lives.

    Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

    1. What is Amina's research question?
    2. What did Amina observe about how the proverb was stated at the cultural evening?
    3. What is Amina's position, and what evidence does she give for it?
    4. What critique does Zainab offer, and how does Amina respond to it?
    5. Which vocabulary item from Section 6 introduces the idea of offering feedback respectfully?

    Speaking Practice

    Atisayen ƙa’ida — Controlled substitution

    With a partner, take turns stating a research topic using the pattern in Section 6.1, substituting a different everyday topic each time (a local custom, a recipe, a family tradition).

    Tattaunawa — Communicative task

    In a group of three or four, follow the Model Dialogue's structure: one person presents a short research topic and question aloud, a second summarizes what was said, a third asks for the presenter's position and evidence, and a fourth offers a respectful critique.

    Madadin karatu kai tsaye — Solo alternative

    Record yourself presenting Amina's research introduction from the Listening Practice aloud, then record yourself summarizing it in your own words in two or three sentences.

    Reading Practice

    Read the short abstract below, which Amina has drafted for her research note. Identify its topic, method, and finding.

    Karatu — Taƙaitawar Bincike (Reading: A Research Abstract)

    Wannan bincike yana nazarin yadda ake faɗin karin magana “Haƙuri maganin duniya” a tsakanin mutane daban-daban a cikin al’ummarmu. An lura da mutane uku a wani taro na al’adu; biyu sun yi amfani da sigar da aka fi sani, ɗayan kuma ya ƙara wata kalma ta musamman ga unguwarsa. Hujjar wannan bincike ita ce cewa bambancin kalmomi ba ya canza ma’anar asali ta karin maganar. Matsayar mai binciken ita ce cewa irin wannan bambanci abu ne mai kyau kuma yana nuna yadda al’adar magana take rayuwa da girma a cikin al’umma, maimakon kasancewa alama ta kuskure. Ana buƙatar ƙarin bincike domin fahimtar yadda wannan bambanci yake bambanta tsakanin yankuna daban-daban.

    Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

    1. What is the topic of this research abstract?
    2. How many people were observed, and what did the researcher notice about how they used the proverb?
    3. What is the stated evidence (hujja) for the researcher's claim?
    4. What is the researcher's position (matsaya) about the variation?
    5. Write a two-sentence summary of this abstract in your own words.

    1. Karanta misalai da hankali. Reread Sections 6.1–6.4, the Model Dialogue, and the Listening and Reading passages.
    2. Zaɓi wani abu na yau da kullum don yin bincike a kai. Choose a small, everyday topic you can observe or ask others about (a recipe, a greeting custom, a saying).
    3. Rubuta jumlar batun bincike da tambayar bincike. Write a topic sentence and a research question, following Section 6.1.
    4. Rubuta taƙaitaccen abin da ka/kika lura da shi. Write a short summary of what you observed, following Section 6.2.
    5. Rubuta matsayarka da hujjarka. State your position and give one piece of supporting evidence, following Section 6.3.
    6. Nemi ko rubuta wata suka mai ma’ana. Exchange your draft with a partner, or write your own critique of a model paragraph, following Section 6.4.
    7. Duba jerin bincike. Check your text against the checklist below.
    8. Rubuta siffa ta ƙarshe. Produce a clean final version of your short research note, then prepare to present it aloud, following the Speaking Practice instructions.

    Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

    • The topic sentence clearly states what the research note is about.
    • The research question is stated as a clear yes/no or open question.
    • The summary condenses the observation without adding new information.
    • The position is clearly distinguished from the evidence supporting it.
    • Any critique used is phrased respectfully, using suka rather than zargi.
    • Special characters (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ, ƴ) and apostrophe/glottal conventions are used accurately.
    • No unscheduled grammar beyond the Volume IV level is required to understand the text.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Proverbs as living, varying tradition

    Proverbs and sayings in Hausa-speaking communities, as in many oral traditions, are commonly restated with small variations depending on the speaker, family, or neighborhood, without this being considered an error. Treating such variation as evidence of a living tradition, rather than as a mistake to correct, reflects how many communities themselves understand their own proverbial speech.

    Regional Variation — Local wording of shared sayings

    The specific wording of a widely shared proverb can differ from one community, generation, or region to another across the Hausa-speaking world. Learners should treat Amina's small, informal observation as one example of this kind of variation, not as a comprehensive academic survey, and should recall the more systematic treatment of regional and generational variation scheduled for Chapter 11.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Confusing suka with zargi

    Error: Using zargi (accusation) when offering academic feedback, which can make a constructive comment sound like a personal attack.

    Correction: Use suka for critique in academic and peer-review contexts, as modeled in Section 6.4.

    Common Mistake — Blending summary and position

    Error: Writing a summary (taƙaitawa) that already includes the writer's opinion, so that the reader cannot tell what was observed from what the writer believes about it.

    Correction: Keep the summary in Section 6.2 purely descriptive, and state any position and evidence separately, as in Section 6.3.

    Common Mistake — Presenting a personal observation as a full study

    Error: Describing three casual observations at one event as though they were a complete, generalizable academic study.

    Correction: Use cautious, limited language, as Amina's abstract does, noting that further research is needed before drawing broader conclusions.

    Answer Key / Amsoshi

    Tambayoyin fahimta na tattaunawa (Dialogue comprehension)

    1. Amina's research question is whether the form of the proverb changes from place to place, and whether the change affects its meaning.
    2. Amina observed that two people at the cultural evening stated the proverb the same way, while a third stated it differently.
    3. Amina's position is that the variation is not an error; her evidence is that all three versions share the same meaning.
    4. Zainab suggests adding another example to strengthen the evidence; Amina agrees and says she will add an example from where her grandmother lives.
    5. The term suka introduces the idea of offering feedback respectfully.

    Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

    (1) Amina's research topic is the proverb “Haƙuri maganin duniya.” (2) Her research question is whether different people state the proverb the same way or whether there is variation. (3) She observed three people; two stated it the same way, and the third added an extra word. (4) Her preliminary finding is that even though the wording differed, the meaning stayed the same. (5) She hopes to continue the research to learn more about how her community uses proverbs.

    Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

    1. The abstract's topic is variation in how the proverb “Haƙuri maganin duniya” is stated across different people in the community.
    2. Three people were observed; two used the more familiar form, and one added a word specific to their neighborhood.
    3. The stated evidence is that the differences in wording do not change the proverb's original meaning.
    4. The researcher's position is that this kind of variation is a positive sign that the tradition is alive and growing, not a sign of error.
    5. Sample summary: A short study observed three people using a familiar proverb at a community event and found that, despite small differences in wording, the meaning stayed the same. The researcher argues that this variation reflects a living oral tradition rather than a mistake.

    Sashi na B (Grammar and Style)

    1. Sample: Wannan bincike shi ne game da yadda ake gaisuwa a lokutan asuba a unguwarmu.
    2. Sample: Idan na taƙaita abin da na karanta, marubucin ya ce ilimi yana da muhimmanci ga ci gaban al’umma, kuma ya bayar da misalai biyu na hakan.
    3. Sample: Matsayata ita ce cewa karatu yana taimaka wa yara girma da kyau; hujjata ita ce cewa yara masu yawan karatu suna samun kyakkyawan sakamako a makaranta.
    4. Sample: Sukata ita ce: bayaninka a fili yake, amma za a iya ƙara wata hujja domin ƙarfafa matsayarka.

    Sashi na C (Sample writing)

    Jumlar batu: Wannan bincike shi ne game da yadda iyalai daban-daban suke shirya kayan lefe kafin bikin aure.

    Taƙaitawa: Idan na taƙaita abin da na tambaya, iyalai biyu sun ce sun fi mayar da hankali kan tufafi, yayin da iyali na uku ya fi mayar da hankali kan kayan gida.

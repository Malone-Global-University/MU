Hausa ta Kasuwanci da Kuɗi

C1–C2-oriented Specialization · Chapter 7 of 20, Unit II continues · Speak and Write Hausa: Advanced Precision and Specialization
Unit II Continues — Business, Finance, and Enterprise Hausa · New Vocabulary Domain, Consolidated Grammar

Chapter Overview

Chapter 7 continues Unit II, Fannonin Ƙwarewa (Domains of Expertise), by turning from academic and research Hausa to the language of business, finance, and enterprise. This chapter introduces no new grammar; every sentence pattern needed was already consolidated by the end of Chapter 5. What is new is a specialized vocabulary domain: the language of planning, negotiating, reporting, and discussing profit and loss. Ibrahim, who mentioned in earlier chapters that he manages a small family business, brings a practical problem to the group: he wants to expand his shop and needs to plan an investment, negotiate with a supplier, and prepare a short report for his family. Amina, fresh from her research project in Chapter 6, Salamatu, and Zainab continue their peer-mentoring circle, this time helping Ibrahim think through his business plan.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify and use core business and finance vocabulary: kasuwanci, jari, riba, asara, hada-hadar kuɗi, shawarwari, tsari, and rahoto.
  2. Read a short business plan and identify its goal, required investment, and expected outcome.
  3. Describe a simple business plan using consolidated sentence patterns.
  4. Negotiate a price or condition politely, and respond to a counter-offer.
  5. Explain a profit or loss outcome and give a reason for it.
  6. Prepare a short spoken or written business report for a family or community audience.
  7. Distinguish the register of business negotiation from the academic register studied in Chapter 6.
  8. Complete an integrated listening, speaking, reading, and writing task built around a small business plan.

Communication Scenario

Ibrahim runs a small shop that his family has owned for years, selling fabric and household goods. He has been considering adding a second small stall to sell mobile-phone accessories, but he needs capital, a supplier who will give him a fair price, and a way to explain his plan clearly to his family before asking them to invest. He brings his idea to Amina, Salamatu, and Zainab, who take turns helping him plan, negotiate, and report on his idea.

  1. Domain: describing a business plan, negotiating price and terms, and reporting on profit, loss, and outcome.
  2. Target skill: using the new business and finance vocabulary domain within already-consolidated grammar patterns.
  3. Grammar status: identifying predicates, sequencing, reference tracking, and comparison remain at Consolidated status, as in Chapters 5 and 6; no new grammar is introduced.
  4. No unscheduled grammar beyond the Volume IV level, and no vocabulary domain beyond business, finance, and enterprise Hausa as scheduled for this chapter.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — The measured firmness of negotiation

No new pronunciation feature is introduced in this chapter. Learners instead apply consolidated tone and vowel-length control to a sixth delivery style: the calm, measured firmness of business negotiation, distinct from the academic evenness of Chapter 6 and the animated pacing of rhetoric in Chapter 2. Pay particular attention to the vowel length in riba and the tone contrast between riba (profit) and asara (loss), since these two words carry the entire meaning of a sentence about a business outcome and must not be confused in rapid speech.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new grammar. It applies the identifying-predicate pattern, sequencing connectors, reference tracking, and comparative patterns already consolidated through Chapter 5 to the new business and finance vocabulary domain.

7.1 Describing a business plan

A business plan commonly opens by identifying its goal, recycling the identifying-predicate pattern used for a research topic in Section 6.1.

  • Tsarina shi ne in buɗe ɗan kanti na biyu domin sayar da kayan wayar hannu. My plan is to open a second small stall to sell mobile-phone accessories.

    • 7.2 Negotiating price and terms (shawarwari)

    Negotiation recycles the sequencing and comparative patterns from Chapter 5, applied to proposing a price and responding to a counter-offer.

  • Idan zan sayi kaya da yawa daga wurinka, shin za ka rage min kuɗin kaɗan? If I buy a large quantity of goods from you, could you lower the price for me a little?

    • 7.3 Explaining profit and loss with a reason

    Explaining profit or loss recycles the causal and explanatory patterns already consolidated in earlier volumes, applied to a financial outcome.

  • Wata ta farko mun sami riba, domin kaya sun sayu da sauri; amma wata ta biyu mun yi asara kaɗan, domin sauran kaya sun tsaya a kanti. In the first month we made a profit, because the goods sold quickly; but in the second month we made a small loss, because the remaining goods stayed unsold in the shop.

    • 7.4 Presenting a short business report (rahoto)

    A short report recycles the summarizing pattern from Section 6.2, applied to condensing business activity into its main points for a family or community audience.

  • Idan na taƙaita rahoton wata ɗaya, mun kashe jari kaɗan, mun sami ɗan riba, kuma tsarina shi ne in ci gaba da wannan sana’a. If I summarize the one-month report, we spent a small amount of capital, we made a small profit, and my plan is to continue with this business.
    • Language Note — New vocabulary, familiar grammar

    Every sentence pattern in Sections 7.1–7.4 was already consolidated by the end of Chapter 5. The task in this chapter is to redeploy those patterns — identifying a plan, negotiating, explaining an outcome, and reporting — using the new business and finance vocabulary domain rather than to learn a new structure.

    Common learner error: Do not confuse riba (profit) with albashi ("salary/wage," not introduced as active vocabulary in this chapter). Profit describes what a business gains after costs; a salary is a fixed payment for labor, and the two should not be described with the same word.

    Model Dialogue

    Yanayin hali: Ibrahim ya kawo wa Amina, Salamatu, da Zainab tsarinsa na buɗe sabon kanti.

    (Setting: Ibrahim brings Amina, Salamatu, and Zainab his plan to open a new stall.)

    IBRAHIM Ina son in buɗe ɗan kanti na biyu domin sayar da kayan wayar hannu, amma ina bukatar shawarwarinku.I want to open a second small stall to sell mobile-phone accessories, but I need your advice.
    SALAMATU Menene tsarinka na jari? Nawa kake bukata don farawa?What's your capital plan? How much do you need to start?
    IBRAHIM Ina bukatar jari kaɗan don sayan kaya na farko, sannan zan yi shawarwari da mai kayayyaki domin samun farashi mai kyau.I need a small amount of capital for the first batch of goods, and then I'll negotiate with the supplier to get a good price.
    ZAINAB Idan zan sayi kaya da yawa daga wurinsa, shin zai rage min kuɗin kaɗan?If you're going to buy a large quantity of goods from him, would he lower the price for you a little?
    IBRAHIM Ee, na yi masa wannan tambaya, kuma ya ce zai iya rage min kaɗan idan na sayi kaya masu yawa a lokaci guda.Yes, I asked him that, and he said he could lower it for me a little if I bought a large quantity at once.
    AMINA Bayan wata guda, ta yaya kake tunanin sana’ar za ta kasance? Riba ko asara?After one month, how do you think the business will turn out? Profit or loss?
    IBRAHIM Ina fatan wata ta farko za mu sami riba, domin kayan wayar hannu suna da bukata sosai a unguwarmu.I hope in the first month we'll make a profit, because mobile-phone accessories are in high demand in our neighborhood.
    SALAMATU Mai kyau. Yanzu, ka shirya rahoto ga iyalinka kafin su bayar da jari?Good. Now, have you prepared a report for your family before they provide the capital?
    IBRAHIM Ba tukuna ba, amma zan taƙaita tsarina, kuɗin da nake bukata, da tsammanin ribata, in gabatar musu da shi a gida.Not yet, but I'll summarize my plan, the money I need, and my expected profit, and present it to them at home.
    ZAINAB Wannan tsari ne mai kyau. Za mu iya taimaka maka ka shirya rahoton.That's a good plan. We can help you prepare the report.

    Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

    1. What is Ibrahim's business plan?
    2. What does Ibrahim need capital for, and what negotiation has he already had with his supplier?
    3. Why does Ibrahim expect a profit in the first month?
    4. What does Ibrahim still need to do before his family provides the capital?
    5. Which vocabulary item from this chapter refers to a plan or arrangement?

    Karatu — Rahoton Kasuwanci (Reading: A Business Report)

    Wannan rahoto yana bayanin tsarina na buɗe ɗan kanti na biyu domin sayar da kayan wayar hannu a kusa da kantina na yadi. Ina bukatar jari kaɗan domin sayan kaya na farko, kuma na riga na yi shawarwari da mai kayayyaki, wanda ya amince ya rage min kuɗi idan na sayi kaya masu yawa a lokaci guda. Hujjar wannan tsari ita ce cewa kayan wayar hannu suna da bukata sosai a unguwarmu, kuma babu wani kanti mai sayar da irin wannan kaya kusa da mu. Matsayata ita ce cewa wannan sana’a za ta kawo riba a cikin watanni na farko, ko da yake yana yiwuwa mu sami ɗan asara a farkon lokacin sayar da kaya. Ina fatan iyalina za su amince su ba ni jarin da nake bukata domin fara wannan sabuwar sana’a.

    Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

    1. What is the goal of this business report?
    2. What has Ibrahim already negotiated with his supplier?
    3. What is the stated evidence (hujja) for Ibrahim's business plan?
    4. What is Ibrahim's position (matsaya) about the risk of an early loss?
    5. Write a two-sentence summary of this report in your own words.

    Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

    • The plan statement clearly states what the business is about.
    • The negotiation exchange is polite and includes a proposal and a response.
    • Profit and loss are described accurately, and riba and asara are not confused.
    • Any reason given for profit or loss is clear and relevant.
    • The report remains a concise summary and does not overstate certainty about the future.
    • Special characters (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ, ƴ) and apostrophe/glottal conventions are used accurately.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Family, trust, and small enterprise

    Small businesses in many Hausa-speaking communities are commonly family enterprises, where capital, labor, and decision-making are shared among relatives. Presenting a clear plan and report to family members before asking for investment, as Ibrahim does, reflects a widespread practice of building trust and shared understanding before committing shared resources, rather than a formality unique to any one family or region.

    Regional Variation — Negotiation styles across contexts

    The pace, directness, and formality of price negotiation can differ between a busy urban market, a small neighborhood shop, and a formal supplier relationship, and can vary across regions and generations. Learners should treat Ibrahim's negotiation with his supplier as one example of a business register, not as a single fixed script for all commercial contexts, and should recall that market-specific bargaining language was introduced separately in earlier volumes.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Confusing riba with albashi

    Error: Using riba (business profit) to describe a fixed salary or wage from employment.

    Correction: Use riba only for what a business gains after costs, as modeled in Section 7.3; a salary is a separate concept not introduced as active vocabulary in this chapter.

    Common Mistake — Overstating certainty in a report

    Error: Writing a business report that states future profit as a guaranteed fact rather than an expectation.

    Correction: Use cautious language, as Ibrahim's report does, distinguishing a hoped-for outcome from a certainty, and acknowledging the possibility of an early loss.

    Common Mistake — Skipping the reason for profit or loss

    Error: Stating that a business made a profit or loss without giving any reason, leaving the explanation incomplete.

    Correction: Always pair a profit or loss statement with a brief reason, as modeled in Section 7.3.

    Answer Key / Amsoshi

    Tambayoyin fahimta na tattaunawa (Dialogue comprehension)

    1. Ibrahim's business plan is to open a second small stall to sell mobile-phone accessories, in addition to his existing fabric and household-goods shop.
    2. He needs capital for his first batch of goods; his supplier has agreed to lower the price if Ibrahim buys a large quantity at once.
    3. He expects a profit in the first month because mobile-phone accessories are in high demand in his neighborhood.
    4. He still needs to prepare a report for his family before they provide the capital.
    5. The term tsari refers to a plan or arrangement.

    Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

    (1) Ibrahim's existing business is a shop selling fabric and household goods. (2) He wants to add a stall selling mobile-phone accessories. (3) The supplier agreed to lower the price if Ibrahim buys a large quantity at once. (4) He expects a profit in the first month because of high demand for phone accessories in his neighborhood. (5) He says he will not worry if the second month shows a small loss, because business requires patience.

    Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

    1. The report's goal is to plan opening a second stall selling mobile-phone accessories near Ibrahim's existing fabric shop.
    2. Ibrahim has already negotiated with his supplier, who agreed to lower the price for a large quantity purchased at once.
    3. The stated evidence is that phone accessories are in high demand in the neighborhood and no nearby shop sells them.
    4. Ibrahim's position is that the business will likely bring a profit within the first few months, though a small early loss is possible.
    5. Sample summary: Ibrahim plans to open a phone-accessories stall near his fabric shop, having already negotiated a better price with his supplier for larger orders. He expects an eventual profit despite the possibility of a small loss at the start.

    Sashi na B (Grammar and Style)

    1. Sample: Tsarina shi ne in fara ɗan kantin sayar da abinci a kusa da makaranta.
    2. Sample: Idan na sayi kaya da yawa daga wurinki, shin za ki rage min kuɗin kaɗan? — Ee, zan iya rage maka kaɗan idan ka sayi akalla apka biyar.
    3. Sample: Wata ta farko mun yi asara kaɗan, domin ba mutane da yawa suka sani game da kantina ba tukuna.
    4. Sample: Idan na taƙaita rahoton wata ɗaya, mun kashe jari kaɗan, kaya sun sayu sannu a hankali, kuma tsarina shi ne in yi ƙarin talla domin jama’a su san kantina.

    Sashi na C (Sample writing)

    Jumlar tsari: Tsarina shi ne in buɗe ɗan kantin ɗinki a unguwarmu domin sana’ar iyalina ta ƙaru.

    Shawarwari: Idan zan sayi injin ɗinki daga wurinka, shin za ka rage min kuɗin kaɗan tunda zan sayi guda biyu a lokaci guda?

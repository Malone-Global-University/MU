Chapter Overview

Chapter 5 introduces no new grammar or core vocabulary. It is the Unit I integrated assessment, bringing together the four style domains developed across Chapters 1–4: precision and revision in speech and writing, rhetoric and public style, literature and oral narrative, and media and digital style. Amina, Ibrahim, Salamatu, and Zainab meet to prepare a single connected assignment for the community organization Salamatu has been supporting since Chapter 4, one that requires each of them to draw on a different style register they have practiced this unit. The chapter's listening, speaking, reading, and writing tasks are drawn entirely from vocabulary and grammar already consolidated through Volume IV and Chapters 1–4, and its Practical Performance Task functions as the graded Unit I assessment.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify which of the four Unit I style domains — precision/revision, rhetoric, literature/oral narrative, or media/digital style — a given passage represents, and explain why.
  2. Compare and contrast the tone, structure, and purpose of a persuasive speech, a proverb-based narrative, and a public social media post.
  3. Revise a piece of your own writing for accuracy, cohesion, and register, applying precision skills from Chapter 1.
  4. Produce a short passage of ceremonial, explanatory, or advisory rhetoric appropriate to a public occasion.
  5. Interpret a short proverb or oral narrative excerpt and explain its meaning and communicative function.
  6. Draft an audience-appropriate social media post and a respectful public comment reply.
  7. Complete an integrated listening, speaking, reading, and writing task that demonstrates control of all four Unit I style domains together.

Communication Scenario

The community organization Salamatu has been helping has asked for a small package of materials for an upcoming cultural evening: a short welcome speech, a proverb-based story to open the program, a printed program note explaining the evening's purpose, and a social media announcement with a comment reply. Amina, Ibrahim, Salamatu, and Zainab divide the work, each drawing on a different style register they have practiced this unit, then review one another's drafts together before submitting the finished package.

  1. Domain: reviewing and producing a connected set of texts across precision/revision, rhetoric, literature/oral narrative, and media/digital style.
  2. Target skill: recognizing and producing register-appropriate Hausa across four related but distinct public-communication genres.
  3. Grammar status: every system in this chapter remains at Consolidated status; no new grammar or vocabulary is introduced, consistent with the Volume V Assessment Matrix entry for Chapter 5.
  4. No unscheduled grammar beyond the Volume IV level, and no style domain beyond the four established in Chapters 1–4.

Pronunciation Review

Sound Focus — Matching delivery to genre

No new pronunciation feature is introduced in this chapter. Learners instead review how consolidated tone, vowel-length, and phrase-rhythm control support four different delivery styles practiced this unit: the more animated pacing of persuasive rhetoric (Chapter 2), the slower, deliberate delivery of a proverb or folktale (Chapter 3), the flatter, even pacing of a broadcast-style announcement (Chapter 4), and the careful, unhurried pacing appropriate to reading revised writing aloud (Chapter 1). Learners should be able to demonstrate at least two of these delivery styles during the Speaking Assessment.

Grammar and Style Review

This chapter introduces no new grammar. It consolidates and integrates the identifying-predicate patterns, sequencing connectors, reference tracking, and comparative and contrastive patterns already established across Volume IV and Chapters 1–4, applying them across four connected text types.

5.1 Revising for precision (Chapter 1 review)

Revision means checking accuracy, cohesion, and register without changing the communicative intent of a passage, as established in Chapter 1.

  • Bayan na sake duba rubutuna, na gyara jimlolin da ba su da tsari, domin su fi bayyana ma’ana. After reviewing my writing again, I corrected the poorly organized sentences, so that they express the meaning more clearly.

    • 5.2 Framing persuasive and ceremonial rhetoric (Chapter 2 review)

    Ceremonial and advisory speech opens by naming the occasion and its significance, recycling the identifying-predicate pattern also used in Chapter 4's post framing.

  • Wannan jawabi shi ne na maraba da mu ga wannan taro na al’adu. This speech is my welcome to this cultural gathering.

    • 5.3 Introducing a proverb-based narrative (Chapter 3 review)

    Introducing a proverb before a story recycles the framing and explanatory patterns consolidated in Chapter 3.

  • Kamar yadda ake cewa, “Haƙuri maganin duniya,” wannan labari zai nuna ma’anar wannan magana. As it is said, “Patience is the remedy for the world,” this story will show the meaning of that saying.

    • 5.4 Announcing and inviting for a public audience (Chapter 4 review)

    Announcing practical details and inviting a broad audience recycles the patterns consolidated in Chapter 4.

  • Za a gudanar da wannan maraice na al’adu a ranar Asabar, kuma muna gayyatar kowa da kowa ya zo tare da iyalinsa. This cultural evening will be held on Saturday, and we invite everyone to come together with their family.

    • 5.5 Comparing style across the four domains

    Comparing the tone and structure of these four text types recycles the contrastive and comparative patterns consolidated across Unit I.

  • Jawabi yana amfani da kalmomi masu ƙarfi domin shawo kan masu sauraro; amma labarin gargajiya yana amfani da salon hankali da natsuwa; sannan sanarwar shafin al’umma tana buƙatar taƙaitaccen bayani mai tsari. A speech uses strong words to persuade the audience; but a traditional story uses a calm, thoughtful style; while a community-page announcement requires brief, organized information.
    • Language Note — Integration, not new grammar

    Every pattern reviewed in this chapter was already consolidated by the end of Chapter 4. What is new is the requirement to move between four related registers within a single connected task, using the same underlying grammar — identifying predicates, sequencing, reference tracking, and comparison — adjusted for purpose and audience.

    Common learner error: Do not use the same register for all four text types in the performance task. A welcome speech, a proverb-based story, a program note, and a social media post each call for a different balance of formality, warmth, and brevity, as shown in Sections 5.1–5.5.

    Model Dialogue

    Yanayin hali: Amina, Ibrahim, Salamatu, da Zainab suna raba aikin shirya kayan aiki ga maraice na al’adu na ƙungiyar al’umma.

    (Setting: Amina, Ibrahim, Salamatu, and Zainab are dividing the work of preparing materials for the community organization's cultural evening.)

    ZAINAB Muna da abubuwa hudu da za mu shirya: jawabin maraba, labarin karin magana, taƙaitaccen bayani na shirin, da sanarwar shafin al’umma.We have four things to prepare: a welcome speech, a proverb story, a short program note, and a community page announcement.
    IBRAHIM Zan iya rubuta jawabin maraba, domin na saba da salon jawabi tun a Babi na 2.I can write the welcome speech, since I'm familiar with the style of a speech from Chapter 2.
    AMINA To, zan ɗauki labarin karin magana. Zan yi amfani da abin da muka koya a Babi na 3 game da tatsuniya da ma’anarta.Alright, I'll take the proverb story. I'll use what we learned in Chapter 3 about folktales and their meaning.
    SALAMATU Zan rubuta sanarwar shafin al’umma da amsa ga sharhi, kamar yadda muka yi a Babi na 4.I'll write the community page announcement and comment reply, as we did in Chapter 4.
    ZAINAB Ni kuwa zan duba dukan rubuce-rubucen domin daidaito da tsari, kamar yadda muka koya a Babi na 1.And I'll review all the writing for accuracy and organization, as we learned in Chapter 1.
    IBRAHIM Wannan jawabi shi ne na maraba da mu ga wannan taro na al’adu.This speech is my welcome to this cultural gathering.
    AMINA Kyakkyawa. Kamar yadda ake cewa, “Haƙuri maganin duniya,” labarina zai nuna ma’anar wannan magana.Good. As it is said, “Patience is the remedy for the world,” my story will show the meaning of that saying.
    SALAMATU Za a gudanar da wannan maraice na al’adu a ranar Asabar, kuma muna gayyatar kowa da kowa ya zo tare da iyalinsa.This cultural evening will be held on Saturday, and we invite everyone to come together with their family.
    ZAINAB Idan na kwatanta abubuwan da muka rubuta guda huɗu, na ga cewa kowanne yana da salon kansa: jawabi mai ƙarfi, labari mai natsuwa, sanarwa mai taƙaitawa, da bayanin shiri mai tsari.Comparing the four things we've written, I see that each has its own style: a forceful speech, a calm story, a brief announcement, and an organized program note.
    SALAMATU Wannan shi ne abin da muka koya a wannan sashe: kalma ɗaya za ta iya canza salo dangane da mai ita da wanda ake yi wa.This is what we've learned in this unit: the same idea can change style depending on who says it and who it's for.

    Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

    2. Who takes responsibility for each item, and which earlier chapter does each task recall?
    3. What is Zainab's role in the group's process?
    4. What proverb does Amina plan to use, and what does she say it means?
    5. What comparison does Zainab draw among the four finished texts?

    1. Karanta misalai da hankali. Reread Sections 5.1–5.5, the Model Dialogue, and the Listening and Reading Assessment passages.
    2. Zaɓi maraice ko taron da za a rubuta masa kayan aiki. Choose a real or imagined cultural or community event.

    6. Kwatanta salon nau’o’in nan uku. Write two or three sentences comparing the tone of the three texts, following Section 5.5.

    Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

    • The welcome-speech opening names the occasion and uses a ceremonial, respectful tone.
    • The proverb-story introduction names a proverb and states what the story will show.
    • The announcement includes practical details and a welcoming, public-appropriate tone; the comment reply is respectful and avoids unconfirmed promises.
    • The comparison sentences accurately describe how tone differs across the three text types.
    • Feedback language, if used, is respectful and constructive, following the Chapter 1 mentoring register.
    • Special characters (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ, ƴ) and apostrophe/glottal conventions are used accurately.
    • No unscheduled grammar beyond the Volume IV level is required to understand the texts.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Cultural evenings and community memory

    Community and cultural evenings that combine welcome speeches, storytelling, shared food, and public announcements are a common way for Hausa-speaking communities, among many others, to mark occasions of cultural memory, education, and family connection. Such events often bring together several genres of public language within a single occasion, exactly as this chapter's four style domains do.

    Regional Variation — Program structure across communities

    The specific order, length, and formality of a cultural evening's program can vary between communities, generations, and regions across the Hausa-speaking world. Learners should treat the program note in this chapter as one common, widely usable structure, remaining open to variation encountered elsewhere, as discussed further in Chapter 11's treatment of regional and generational variation.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Flattening all four texts into one register

    Error: Writing the welcome speech, proverb introduction, announcement, and program note all in the same tone, which erases the register distinctions built across Unit I.

    Correction: Match each text's tone to its purpose and audience, as modeled in Sections 5.1–5.5.

    Common Mistake — Skipping the revision step

    Error: Submitting a first draft of the performance task without checking accuracy, cohesion, or register, treating revision as optional.

    Correction: Apply the Chapter 1 revision process to every text in the package before finalizing it, following Section 5.1.

    Common Mistake — Treating the comparison task as optional

    Error: Producing the four texts without explaining how their styles differ, missing the chapter's core integrative goal.

    Correction: Include explicit comparison sentences, as modeled in Section 5.5 and required in the Practical Performance Task.

    2. Ibrahim takes the welcome speech (Chapter 2), Amina takes the proverb story (Chapter 3), Salamatu takes the announcement and comment reply (Chapter 4), and Zainab reviews all the writing for accuracy and organization (Chapter 1).

    Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

    (1) The speaker welcomes the audience to a cultural evening. (2) The community organization organized it, to remember the community's history and bring families together. (3) The evening will include hearing a proverb story, discussing its meaning, and sharing food together. (4) The speaker hopes everyone will enjoy the gathering and learn something new about their culture. (5) This passage represents the rhetoric/public-style domain (Chapter 2), because it opens with a ceremonial welcome and uses persuasive, warm language appropriate to a public occasion.

    Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

    1. The program note states three purposes: remembering community history, teaching children about proverbs and folktales, and bringing families together in celebration.
    2. The program's four parts, in order: welcome speech, proverb story from an elder, discussion of the story's meaning, and shared food.
    3. An elder woman from the neighborhood will tell the proverb story.
    4. This program note most closely resembles the media/digital and practical-announcement style from Chapter 4, because it lists practical details and purposes in an organized, informational way, though it also draws on the literary domain from Chapter 3 in describing the proverb story.
    5. Sample summary: A community cultural evening will open with a welcome speech and a proverb story told by an elder, followed by a discussion of its meaning and a shared meal. The evening aims to preserve community history and bring families together.

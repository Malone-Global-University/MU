Fassara, Taƙaitawa, da Sharhi

Unit III Continues — Translation, Summary, and Commentary · Mediation Across Audiences, Not New Grammar

Chapter Overview

Chapter 13 continues Unit III by turning from script and history to the practical work of mediating meaning across languages and audiences. Building directly on the identifying, reported-speech, comparison, and clarification patterns consolidated through Chapters 11 and 12, this chapter develops translation, summary, and commentary as communicative tasks in their own right. No new grammar is introduced. Zainab has been asked by a community cultural center to help prepare a short bilingual handout based on the group's visit to Malam Nasiru, and Amina, Ibrahim, and Salamatu help her translate, summarize, and annotate the material for different audiences — English-speaking visitors, Hausa-speaking elders, and younger Hausa-English bilingual readers. The chapter treats translation and summary as acts of judgment and register choice, not as mechanical word-for-word substitution.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Translate a short Hausa passage into natural English and a short English passage into natural standard Hausa, without word-for-word calquing.
  2. Summarize a Hausa text in Hausa and in English, preserving its main ideas in fewer words.
  3. Identify at least one point where literal translation would distort meaning, and explain why.
  4. Comment, in standard instructional Hausa, on a register or word choice made in a translation or summary.
  5. Distinguish an audience that needs more cultural explanation from one that needs less, and adjust a text accordingly.
  6. Use the identifying-predicate, reported-speech, comparison, and clarification-question patterns from Chapters 11 and 12 to discuss translation choices.
  7. Ask a respectful clarifying question about a translation or summary decision.
  8. Complete listening, speaking, reading, and writing tasks that integrate translation, summary, and commentary with vocabulary and grammar already consolidated through Chapter 12.

Communication Scenario

Encouraged by their visit to Malam Nasiru in Chapter 12, Zainab is asked by the local cultural center to help prepare a short bilingual handout about Ajami and community writing history, to be shared with visitors during a heritage weekend. She brings the draft to Amina, Ibrahim, and Salamatu, and the four of them work through translating a short Hausa paragraph into English for visitors who do not read Hausa, summarizing the same paragraph in Hausa for a community newsletter with limited space, and adding a short commentary note explaining one word choice that does not translate directly. They discuss how the same content must be adjusted for different audiences without losing its accuracy or respect for the subject.

  1. Domain: translation, summary, and commentary as communicative tasks applied to community and cultural content.
  2. Target skill: producing an accurate translation and a shorter summary of the same text, and commenting on one register or word choice, for two different audiences.
  3. Grammar status: no new productive grammar; all sentence patterns used for discussion remain at Consolidated status from Chapters 1–12.
  4. Vocabulary status: a small set of clearly labeled translation- and mediation-related terms is introduced as productive vocabulary; the cultural-heritage vocabulary from Chapter 12 is recycled, not retaught.
  5. The chapter does not treat translation as simple word substitution, and does not present one audience's needs as more important or more "correct" than another's.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Reading a translated pair aloud with matching intonation

No new sound is introduced in this chapter. Learners practice reading a Hausa sentence and its English translation aloud in succession, paying attention to keeping natural Hausa tone, vowel length, and the special consonants ɓ, ɗ, and ƙ consistent even when the English translation uses a different sentence rhythm. This guards against learners unconsciously flattening Hausa intonation to match English sentence stress when moving between the two languages in the same task. Continue producing tone and vowel length according to the standard model consolidated since Volume I.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new grammar. It applies the identifying-predicate, reported-speech, comparison, and clarification-question patterns consolidated through Chapter 12 to the new task of discussing translation, summary, and commentary choices.

13.1 Identifying what kind of text or task something is

Recycles the identifying-predicate pattern from Section 11.1/12.1 to name a text type or task.

  • Wannan taƙaitaccen sigar labarin ne, an rage shi don ya dace da sararin jaridar unguwa. This is a summarized version of the article, shortened to fit the space in the community newsletter.

    • 13.2 Reporting what someone said about a translation choice

    Recycles the reported-speech pattern from Section 11.2/12.2, here applied to quoting a translation decision.

  • Zainab ta ce, “Ban fassara wannan kalma kai tsaye ba, don ba za ta yi ma’ana ga masu karatu na Turanci ba,” watau ta zaɓi kalmar da ta fi dacewa. Zainab said, "I didn't translate this word literally, because it wouldn't make sense to English-reading readers," meaning she chose the word that fit better.

    • 13.3 Comparing two versions of the same text for different audiences

    Recycles the comparison pattern from Section 11.3/12.3 to note a difference between versions without ranking one as better in an absolute sense.

  • Sigar Turanci da sigar Hausa dukansu suna ɗauke da ma’ana ɗaya, sai dai kowanne yana da salon da ya dace da masu karatunsa. The English version and the Hausa version both carry the same meaning, but each has a style suited to its own readers.

    • 13.4 Asking a clarifying question about a translation or summary decision

    Recycles the clarification-question pattern from Section 11.4/12.4.

  • Zainab, me ya sa kika zaɓi wannan kalma maimakon fassara ta kai tsaye? Zainab, why did you choose this word instead of translating it literally?
    • Language Note — Analysis and judgment, not new structure

    Every pattern in Sections 13.1–13.4 was consolidated by Chapter 12. The new task is applying these familiar patterns to a new kind of content: identifying a text type, reporting a translation decision, comparing two audience-adjusted versions, and asking about a translation choice. Learners are not expected to master formal translation theory — only to discuss translation, summary, and commentary decisions accurately in standard instructional Hausa.

    Common learner error: Do not assume that a "correct" translation must match the source sentence word for word. Describe translation choices in terms of meaning and audience, as in Section 13.2 and 13.3.

    Model Dialogue

    Yanayin hali: Zainab ta kawo wa Amina, Ibrahim, da Salamatu daftarin takardar da za a raba wa baƙi a bikin al’adu na gida, tare da neman taimakonsu wajen fassarawa da taƙaitawa.

    (Setting: Zainab brings Amina, Ibrahim, and Salamatu a draft handout to be shared with visitors at the local heritage festival, asking for their help translating and summarizing it.)

    ZAINAB Cibiyar al’adu ta roƙe ni in shirya wata gajeriyar takarda game da ziyararmu zuwa wurin Malam Nasiru, domin baƙin da ba su fahimci Hausa ba.The cultural center asked me to prepare a short handout about our visit to Malam Nasiru, for visitors who don't understand Hausa.
    IBRAHIM Wannan taƙaitaccen sigar labarin ne, an rage shi don ya dace da sararin jaridar unguwa, ko?This is a summarized version of the article, shortened to fit the space in the community newsletter, right?
    ZAINAB A’a, wannan ba taƙaitawa ba ce kawai; ina bukatar in fassara ta zuwa Turanci ma, don baƙin bikin da yawa Turanci suke amfani da shi.No, this isn't just a summary; I also need to translate it into English, since many festival visitors use English.
    SALAMATU Zainab, me ya sa kika zaɓi wannan kalma maimakon fassara ta kai tsaye?Zainab, why did you choose this word instead of translating it literally?
    ZAINAB Ban fassara wannan kalma kai tsaye ba, don ba za ta yi ma’ana ga masu karatu na Turanci ba. Sun bukaci ɗan bayani na ƙari domin su fahimci muhimmancin Ajami a tarihinmu.I didn't translate this word literally, because it wouldn't make sense to English-reading readers. They need a bit of additional explanation to understand the importance of Ajami in our history.
    AMINA To, don haka sigar Turanci da sigar Hausa dukansu suna ɗauke da ma’ana ɗaya, sai dai kowanne yana da salon da ya dace da masu karatunsa.So then, the English version and the Hausa version both carry the same meaning, but each has a style suited to its own readers.
    IBRAHIM Zan iya taimaka maki wajen taƙaita sigar Hausar don jaridar unguwa, in rage ta zuwa jimloli uku kawai.I can help you summarize the Hausa version for the community newsletter, cutting it down to just three sentences.
    ZAINAB Madalla, kuma bari mu rubuta ɗan sharhi guda, mu bayyana wa masu karatu dalilin da ya sa muka zaɓi wannan salon fassara.Wonderful, and let's write one short commentary note, explaining to readers why we chose this style of translation.

    Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

    1. What has the cultural center asked Zainab to prepare, and for whom?
    2. What two separate tasks does Zainab say the project requires, according to her second turn?
    3. Why did Zainab avoid a literal translation of one word or phrase?
    4. What does Amina conclude about the relationship between the Hausa and English versions?
    5. What does the group decide to add to the handout, beyond the translation and the summary?

    Aikin fahimta — Recognition: Reread the Model Dialogue and mark each line as identification, reported speech, comparison, or clarification question, using Sections 13.1–13.4.

    Aikin zaɓi — Selection: Given six short translation pairs (Hausa and English), some faithful to meaning and some distorted by literal word-for-word substitution, identify which pairs preserve meaning accurately.

    Aikin taƙaitawa — Summary: Read the short Hausa paragraph below and write a two-sentence Hausa summary and a separate two-sentence English summary of the same content.

    Aikin sharhi — Commentary: Choose one word or phrase from the paragraph below that would not translate well word for word, and write one sentence, using the pattern in Section 13.2, explaining your translation choice.

    Sigar da za a Taƙaita da Sharhanta — Passage for Summary and Commentary

    Ziyarar da muka kai wa Malam Nasiru ta koya mana cewa Ajami da Boko dukansu muhimman kayan aiki ne wajen adana tarihin Hausa, kuma babu wanda ya fi wani daraja. Malam Nasiru ya nuna mana yadda ake karanta rubutun Ajami daga dama zuwa hagu, kuma ya bayyana yadda malamai suka daɗe suna amfani da shi wajen rubuta addini, ilimi, da waƙoƙin gargajiya. Wannan gogewar ta sa mu ƙara godiya ga wadanda suka adana wannan al’ada har zuwa yau.

    Gloss: Our visit to Malam Nasiru taught us that Ajami and Boko are both important tools for preserving Hausa history, and that neither is more valuable than the other. Malam Nasiru showed us how Ajami writing is read from right to left, and explained how scholars have long used it to write religious material, education, and classical poetry. This experience made us more grateful to those who have preserved this tradition to this day.

    Reading Practice

    Read the short bilingual-style handout draft below, prepared for heritage-weekend visitors. Identify the purpose, key points, and intended audience of each version.

    Karatu — Takardar Baƙi: Ajami da Tarihin Rubutu (Reading: Visitor Handout: Ajami and Writing History)

    Sigar Hausa (don jaridar unguwa): Kafin Boko, Ajami shi ne babban rubutun da malamanmu suka yi amfani da shi wajen adana addini, ilimi, da waƙoƙin gargajiya. Har yanzu ana girmama wannan gado, kuma masana suna ci gaba da nazarinsa. Ziyarar da muka kai wa Malam Nasiru ta nuna mana muhimmancin wannan al’ada.

    English version (for visitors): Before the Latin-based Boko script became common, Ajami — a Hausa writing tradition adapted from Arabic script — was the primary way local scholars recorded religious teaching, education, and classical poetry. This heritage is still respected today, and researchers continue to study it. A visit to a local manuscript keeper showed our group why this tradition still matters.

    Ɗan sharhi — Short commentary: The word "Ajami" is kept as a proper name in both versions rather than translated, because no single English word captures it accurately; a short explanatory phrase is added instead so English-reading visitors understand what it refers to without renaming the tradition itself.

    Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

    1. What is the intended audience of each of the two main versions?
    2. What information appears in both versions?
    3. What does the commentary note explain, and why was this choice made?
    4. According to the handout, why do researchers continue to study Ajami even though Boko is now common?
    5. Write a three-sentence summary, in English, comparing the Hausa and English versions of the handout.

    Writing Workshop

    1. Karanta misalai da hankali. Reread Sections 13.1–13.4, the Model Dialogue, and the Reading Practice handout.
    2. Zaɓi gajeriyar sigar Hausa da za ka/kika yi aiki da ita. Choose a short Hausa passage (the Guided Practice paragraph or one of your own, three to five sentences).
    3. Rubuta fassarar Turanci. Translate the passage into natural English, avoiding literal word-for-word substitution.
    4. Rubuta taƙaitaccen sigar Hausa. Write a two-sentence Hausa summary of the same passage.
    5. Rubuta ɗan sharhi. Write one sentence, following Section 13.2, explaining a word or phrase you chose not to translate literally.
    6. Rubuta jimlar kwatanci. Write one sentence, following Section 13.3, comparing your English and Hausa versions without ranking either.
    7. Haɗa dukkan sassan zuwa gajeriyar takarda guda ɗaya. Combine the translation, summary, and commentary into one short organized handout, similar in structure to the Reading Practice sample.
    8. Duba jerin bincike. Check your text against the checklist below, then produce a clean final version.

    Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

    • The English translation reads naturally and is not a word-for-word calque of the Hausa.
    • The Hausa summary is shorter than the original passage while preserving its main ideas.
    • The commentary sentence identifies a specific word or phrase and explains the reasoning behind the translation choice.
    • The comparison sentence does not present either version as inherently better than the other.
    • Hausa sentences use standard instructional Hausa with accurate special characters and punctuation.
    • No new grammar beyond Chapter 12 is required to understand the text.
    • The handout reads as one coherent, organized document, not disconnected fragments.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Translation as mediation, not substitution

    Community translation and interpretation work, such as preparing bilingual materials for a cultural festival, commonly requires mediators to make judgment calls about which words to translate directly, which to keep as proper names or loanwords, and where additional explanation is needed. This kind of mediation is a valued skill in many multilingual Hausa-speaking communities, where individuals often move between Hausa, English, French, Arabic, and other languages depending on context, audience, and setting.

    Regional Variation — Audience and language contact vary by setting

    The languages a Hausa-speaking community mediates between depends heavily on region and context: in Nigeria, English is frequently the second language of wider communication, while in Niger, French plays a comparable role in many public and educational settings. This chapter's scenario, focused on English-speaking visitors, is illustrative rather than universal; learners should expect that translation and commentary tasks in other Hausa-speaking contexts may involve different language pairs.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Translating word for word

    Error: Substituting each Hausa word with its closest English equivalent in the same order, producing an unnatural or inaccurate English sentence.

    Correction: Translate for meaning and natural expression, as modeled in the Reading Practice handout, adjusting word order and structure as needed for the target language.

    Common Mistake — Treating a summary as a shortened literal translation

    Error: Producing a "summary" that simply repeats the first few sentences of the original rather than condensing its main ideas.

    Correction: Identify the main ideas of the whole passage first, then express them concisely in your own words, as modeled in the Guided Practice summary task.

    Common Mistake — Ranking one language version as more correct

    Error: Implying that the Hausa version or the English version of a translated text is the "real" or superior one.

    Correction: Describe both versions as carrying the same meaning with styles suited to different audiences, as modeled in Section 13.3 and Amina's line in the Model Dialogue.

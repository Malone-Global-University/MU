Unit IV Assessment — Final Capstone of Volume V

Chapter Overview

Chapter 20 is the final advanced capstone of Volume V. It introduces no new grammar paradigm and no new vocabulary domain. Instead, it draws together the extended writing project planned in Chapter 16, drafted and revised in Chapter 17, presented and defended in Chapter 18, and assembled into a portfolio in Chapter 19, asking learners to complete one integrated performance across specialized reading, speaking, writing, interpretation, and mediation. Amina, Ibrahim, Salamatu, and Zainab return to Malama Hadiza and Malam Sani for a final mentoring session in which each learner presents their finished portfolio, mediates a short passage for a new audience, and reflects on the full arc of the project and the volume.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Present a completed portfolio and defend its choices in connected, register-appropriate Hausa.
  2. Interpret a specialized text and mediate its content for an audience unfamiliar with the original source.
  3. Summarize, translate, and comment on a passage while preserving its meaning and adjusting its register.
  4. Sustain connected narration, comparison, and reference tracking across an extended spoken and written performance.
  5. Respond to unscripted follow-up questions on one's own project with clarification and repair strategies.
  6. Evaluate one's own overall development across Volume V and identify a specific direction for continued study.
  7. Complete the final integrated capstone assessment for Volume V, drawing on every system consolidated through Chapter 19.

Communication Scenario

Malama Hadiza and Malam Sani invite Amina, Ibrahim, Salamatu, and Zainab for one final session before the cultural center's project is formally closed. Each learner presents their assembled portfolio, then takes on a short mediation task: reading a specialized passage related to the project and explaining its content, in their own words, to a visitor who was not part of the earlier sessions. Malama Hadiza and Malam Sani ask unscripted follow-up questions, and each learner closes by reflecting honestly on their growth across the whole volume and naming one goal for continued study beyond this course.

  1. Domain: capstone performance — portfolio defense, mediation, and self-directed reflection across the full volume.
  2. Target skill: sustaining connected, register-appropriate Hausa across a long, partly unscripted performance that integrates reading, speaking, writing, and mediation.
  3. Grammar status: no new productive grammar; every pattern consolidated through Chapter 19 is drawn on as needed, with no single pattern isolated for new teaching.
  4. Vocabulary status: no new productive vocabulary domain; a small number of already-active mediation and reflection terms from Chapters 13 and 19 are recycled.
  5. This chapter is the Volume V final capstone and is formally assessed in full.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Sustained control across a long, partly unscripted performance

No new sound is introduced in this final chapter. The pronunciation focus is sustaining tone, vowel length, and special-consonant control (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ) across a longer, mixed performance that moves between prepared presentation, live mediation of a new passage, and unscripted question-and-answer exchange. Pay particular attention to maintaining clear phrase-level intonation when shifting from rehearsed material to spontaneous response, since pacing often changes noticeably at that point.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new grammar. It draws on the full range of connected narration, sequencing, reasoning, comparison, reference-tracking, translation/mediation, and presentation-and-defense patterns consolidated through Chapter 19, applying them together in one integrated capstone performance.

20.1 Presenting and defending a completed portfolio

Recycles Chapter 18 presentation-and-defense patterns and Chapter 19 item-selection and comparison patterns to introduce the portfolio as a whole and justify its overall shape.

  • Fayil ɗina ya ƙunshi tsari, daftari, da gabatarwa, kuma na tsara su bisa ga tsarin da ya nuna yadda aikina ya bunƙasa daga farko zuwa ƙarshe. My portfolio contains a plan, a draft, and a presentation, and I arranged them in an order that shows how my work developed from start to finish.

    • 20.2 Mediating a specialized passage for a new audience

    Recycles Chapter 13 translation-and-commentary patterns to summarize and explain a text's content for someone who has not encountered it before, adjusting register for a listener rather than a reader.

  • Wannan rubutu yana magana ne a kan tarihin al’adarmu, kuma zan taƙaita muku ma’anarsa domin ku fahimci abin da yake nufi ba tare da karanta shi da kanku ba. This text is about the history of our tradition, and I will summarize its meaning for you so that you understand what it means without reading it yourselves.

    • 20.3 Responding to an unscripted follow-up question

    Recycles Chapter 18 clarification-and-repair patterns to handle a question that was not anticipated, buying time politely and answering with reasoned support.

  • Wannan tambaya ba ta samu shiri ba, amma bari in yi ƙoƙarin bayyana dalilin da ya sa na zaɓi wannan hanya maimakon wata. This question was not prepared for, but let me try to explain the reason I chose this approach rather than another.

    • 20.4 Reflecting on development across the whole volume and naming a future goal

    Extends Chapter 19's self-reflection pattern from a single project to the full volume, and introduces a forward-looking goal beyond the course.

  • A duk fannin karatuna a wannan littafi, na ga ci gaba mafi girma a fannin fassara da tattaunawa mai zurfi, kuma manufa ta gaba ita ce in ci gaba da yin magana da mutanen da suka fi ni ƙwarewa. Across my whole course of study in this book, I have seen my greatest progress in translation and in-depth discussion, and my future goal is to keep speaking with people more advanced than me.
    • Language Note — Integration, not innovation

    Every pattern in Sections 20.1–20.4 was consolidated by an earlier chapter of Volume V. What is new is the task: combining presentation, mediation, unscripted response, and volume-level reflection into a single sustained performance. No system beyond what is scheduled through Chapter 20 in the Volume V matrices appears in this chapter.

    Common learner error: Do not treat mediation as word-for-word translation. Follow Section 20.2: summarize and explain meaning for the listener's benefit rather than reproducing the source sentence by sentence.

    Model Dialogue

    Yanayin hali: Amina, Ibrahim, Salamatu, da Zainab sun taru a taron ƙarshe da Malama Hadiza da Malam Sani domin gabatar da fayilolinsu da fassara wa wata baƙuwa da ta ziyarci cibiyar al’adu.

    (Setting: Amina, Ibrahim, Salamatu, and Zainab have gathered for a final session with Malama Hadiza and Malam Sani to present their portfolios and mediate for a visitor to the cultural center.)

    MALAM SANI Wannan ita ce ranar ƙarshe. Kowanne daga cikinku zai gabatar da fayil ɗinsa, sannan ya fassara ɗan gajeren rubutu domin baƙuwarmu, wadda ba ta ji labarin aikinku ba tun farko.This is the final day. Each of you will present your portfolio, then mediate a short text for our visitor, who has not heard about your project from the beginning.
    AMINA Fayil ɗina ya ƙunshi tsari, daftari, da gabatarwa, kuma na tsara su bisa ga tsarin da ya nuna yadda aikina ya bunƙasa daga farko zuwa ƙarshe.My portfolio contains a plan, a draft, and a presentation, and I arranged them in an order that shows how my work developed from start to finish.
    MALAMA HADIZA Yanzu, Amina, ki fassara wa baƙuwarmu abin da sashinki na littafin yake magana a kai, ba tare da karanta duk kalma ɗaya bayan ɗaya ba.Now, Amina, mediate for our visitor what your section of the booklet is about, without reading every single word one after another.
    AMINA Wannan rubutu yana magana ne a kan tarihin al’adarmu, kuma zan taƙaita muku ma’anarsa domin ku fahimci abin da yake nufi ba tare da karanta shi da kanku ba.This text is about the history of our tradition, and I will summarize its meaning for you so that you understand what it means without reading it yourselves.
    BAƘUWA Na gode. Yanzu ina son sanin dalilin da ya sa kuka zaɓi wannan taken maimakon wani.Thank you. Now I would like to know the reason you chose this theme instead of another one.
    IBRAHIM Wannan tambaya ba ta samu shiri ba, amma bari in yi ƙoƙarin bayyana dalilin da ya sa mun zaɓi wannan hanya maimakon wata: mun ga cewa tarihin al’adarmu ba a rubuta shi sosai a littattafan makaranta.This question was not prepared for, but let me try to explain the reason we chose this approach rather than another: we saw that the history of our tradition is not written about much in school textbooks.
    SALAMATU A duk fannin karatuna a wannan littafi, na ga ci gaba mafi girma a fannin tsara aiki da magana a gaban jama’a.Across my whole course of study in this book, I have seen my greatest progress in organizing work and speaking in front of a group.
    ZAINAB Ni kuwa, na fi ganin ci gabana a fannin fassara da tattaunawa mai zurfi, kuma manufa ta gaba ita ce in ci gaba da yin magana da mutanen da suka fi ni ƙwarewa a Hausa.As for me, I see my progress most in translation and in-depth discussion, and my future goal is to keep speaking with people more advanced than me in Hausa.
    MALAM SANI Mun yi alfahari da ku duka huɗu. Kun tafi daga gabatar da kanku a farkon wannan littafi, zuwa fassara ma’anar rubutu na musamman a yau.We are proud of all four of you. You have gone from introducing yourselves at the beginning of this book, to mediating the meaning of a specialized text today.

    Sigar don Kwatanci — Weak and Strong Mediation

    Fassara mara ƙarfi: Wannan rubutu labari ne game da tarihi kuma yana da kyau.

    Fassara mai ƙarfi: Wannan rubutu yana bayyana yadda al’ummarmu ta kiyaye tarihinta ta hanyar baka tsawon shekaru, kafin a fara rubuta shi a littattafai, kuma yana taimaka wa mai karatu ya fahimci muhimmancin adana wannan tarihin a yau.

    Gloss: Weak mediation: "This text is a story about history and it is good." Strong mediation: "This text explains how our community preserved its history orally for years before it began to be written in books, and it helps the reader understand the importance of preserving this history today."

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Mediating for a visitor with care and respect

    When learners mediate specialized or historical material for a visitor unfamiliar with the topic, it is common and respectful to summarize with accuracy and without exaggeration, checking that the listener has understood before moving on; this reflects a broader pattern in many Hausa-speaking educational and community settings, where explaining unfamiliar material to a newcomer is treated as a shared responsibility rather than a formal test.

    Regional Variation — Closing ceremonies and final reflections vary by setting

    The informal final session modeled in this chapter suits a small mentoring group at a cultural center. In some schools, universities, or professional programs, a final capstone may instead involve a panel of evaluators, a formal written report, or a public exhibition, while still serving the same underlying goal of demonstrating integrated skill and reflecting honestly on growth.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Translating word for word instead of mediating

    Error: Reading a passage aloud sentence by sentence in the original language and then repeating it in English without adjusting for the listener's needs.

    Correction: Follow Section 20.2: summarize the meaning and explain its significance in the learner's own words, adjusted for a listener who was not part of the original project.

    Common Mistake — Freezing on an unscripted question

    Error: Falling silent for a long time or abandoning the answer entirely when a question was not anticipated.

    Correction: Follow Section 20.3: use a polite time-buying phrase, then attempt a reasoned answer rather than abandoning the exchange.

    Common Mistake — Vague volume-level reflection

    Error: Summarizing progress across the whole volume only as "I got better at Hausa," without naming a specific skill or a specific future goal.

    Correction: Follow Section 20.4: name one specific area of greatest progress and one specific, realistic goal for continued study.

    Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

    (1) Each student presented a portfolio and mediated a short text. (2) Amina mediated her text's meaning without reading it word for word. (3) Ibrahim explained the reason for their theme choice in response to the unscripted question. (4) Salamatu's progress is in organizing work and public speaking; Zainab's is in translation and deep discussion, and her goal is to keep studying Hausa with more advanced speakers.

    Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

    1. Before Boko, the Hausa community relied on oral tradition and Ajami to preserve its history.
    2. Scholars and prominent families were responsible for teaching children before modern schools were established.
    3. The students chose to investigate this history to understand how their community has changed and how it continues to preserve what it values across generations.
    4. Sample mediation: Before modern schools existed, Hausa communities kept their history alive through spoken storytelling and the Ajami writing tradition, relying on scholars and family elders to pass this knowledge to children. Today's students chose to study this history to understand how their community has changed while still valuing what it has long considered important.

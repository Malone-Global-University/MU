Ajami, Tarihi, da Rubutun Gargajiya

C1–C2-oriented Specialization · Chapter 12 of 20, continuing Unit III · Speak and Write Hausa: Advanced Precision and Specialization
Unit III Continues — Ajami, History, and Classical Writing · Receptive Script Literacy, Not Productive Ajami Writing

Chapter Overview

Chapter 12 continues Unit III by turning from spoken variation to written heritage. Building on the script-heritage awareness established earlier in the series, this chapter deepens learners' receptive literacy around Ajami, the historically and culturally important Arabic-derived Hausa writing tradition, and around classical and historical Hausa texts more broadly. As in Chapter 11, no new productive grammar is introduced, and Ajami production is not required. Amina, Ibrahim, Salamatu, and Zainab visit Malam Nasiru, a respected community elder and manuscript keeper, who shows them examples of Ajami writing and explains its place alongside Boko in Hausa history. The chapter treats Ajami as a living part of cultural and scholarly life, not as an obsolete curiosity, while keeping Boko as the productive default for the course.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify Ajami as a historically and culturally significant Hausa writing tradition, distinct from but related to Boko.
  2. Recognize that Ajami is written right to left and compare this visually with left-to-right Boko text.
  3. Describe, in standard instructional Hausa, the historical, religious, scholarly, and literary contexts in which Ajami has been and continues to be used.
  4. Compare a short labeled Ajami example with its Boko transliteration and identify at least two points of correspondence.
  5. Discuss classical and historical Hausa writing (proverbs, praise poetry, chronicles) as part of a living literary heritage.
  6. Explain why Ajami literacy remains receptive and cultural at this stage of the course rather than a required productive skill.
  7. Ask respectful clarifying questions about unfamiliar Ajami symbols or historical references.
  8. Complete listening, speaking, reading, and writing tasks that integrate this receptive script and history focus with vocabulary and grammar already consolidated through Chapter 11.

Communication Scenario

After their conversations with Hajiya Ta’alatu and Fatima in Chapter 11, the group grows curious about how Hausa was written before Boko became the everyday standard. Zainab arranges a visit to Malam Nasiru, an elder in the community known for keeping old notebooks and manuscripts written in Ajami. He shows them a page of Ajami script, explains its right-to-left direction, and reads aloud a short proverb recorded in both Ajami and Boko. The group discusses what they saw afterward, comparing the two writing systems and reflecting on why both matter to Hausa history and identity.

  1. Domain: Ajami script recognition, history, and classical Hausa writing, treated receptively and culturally.
  2. Target skill: recognizing Ajami as a script and discussing its history and use, without being required to write it productively.
  3. Grammar status: no new productive grammar; all sentence patterns used for discussion remain at Consolidated status from Chapters 1–11.
  4. Vocabulary status: a small set of clearly labeled Ajami- and history-related terms is introduced as receptive vocabulary only; no item is assessed for production.
  5. The chapter does not present Ajami as obsolete, purely religious, or inferior to Boko, and does not present Boko as having replaced Ajami's cultural value.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Reading classical and formulaic phrases aloud

No new sound is introduced in this chapter. Learners practice reading aloud short proverbs and classical-style phrases with careful attention to tone, vowel length, and the special consonants ɓ, ɗ, and ƙ already consolidated, since proverbs and traditional sayings are often spoken slowly and deliberately, giving learners a useful model for controlled, careful pronunciation. Continue producing tone and vowel length according to the standard model consolidated since Volume I; this chapter asks for careful, expressive reading of familiar sound patterns, not any new articulation.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new grammar. It applies the identifying-predicate, reported-speech, comparison, and clarification-question patterns consolidated through Chapter 11 to the new task of discussing script, history, and classical writing.

12.1 Identifying a script or text as historical

Recycles the identifying-predicate pattern from Section 11.1 to name what a piece of writing is.

  • Wannan rubutu Ajami ne, tsohon rubutun Hausa da aka rubuta shi dama zuwa hagu. This writing is Ajami, the old Hausa script that is written from right to left.

    • 12.2 Reporting what an elder or scholar explained

    Recycles the reported-speech pattern from Section 11.2, here applied to quoting Malam Nasiru's explanation.

  • Malam Nasiru ya ce, “Ajami da Boko dukansu hanyoyi ne na rubuta Hausa,” watau babu wanda ya fi wani muhimmanci. Malam Nasiru said, "Ajami and Boko are both ways of writing Hausa," meaning neither is more important than the other.

    • 12.3 Comparing Ajami and Boko respectfully

    Recycles the comparison pattern from Section 11.3 to note a difference without ranking one script as better.

  • Ajami da Boko dukansu na Hausa ne, sai dai kowanne yana da tarihinsa da yanayin amfaninsa. Both Ajami and Boko belong to Hausa; each simply has its own history and context of use.

    • 12.4 Asking a clarifying question about an unfamiliar symbol or reference

    Recycles the clarification-question pattern from Section 11.4.

  • Malam, me ake nufi da wannan alama a rubutun Ajami? Ban taɓa ganin irinta ba. Sir, what does this symbol in the Ajami writing mean? I've never seen one like it.
    • Language Note — Analysis and recognition, not new structure

    Every pattern in Sections 12.1–12.4 was consolidated by Chapter 11. The new task is applying these familiar patterns to a new kind of content: identifying, quoting, comparing, and asking about script and historical writing. Learners are not expected to write in Ajami — only to discuss it accurately in standard instructional Hausa using Boko.

    Common learner error: Do not assume that because Boko is the productive default for this course, Ajami is a lesser or outdated script. Describe the relationship between the two neutrally, as in Section 12.3.

    Model Dialogue

    Yanayin hali: Zainab ta shirya ziyarar zuwa gidan Malam Nasiru, wani dattijo a unguwar da aka sani da ajiye tsoffin rubuce-rubuce na Ajami. Ya nuna musu wata shafa ta rubutun Ajami tare da bayani.

    (Setting: Zainab has arranged a visit to the home of Malam Nasiru, a neighborhood elder known for keeping old Ajami manuscripts. He shows them a page of Ajami writing and explains it.)

    ZAINAB Malam, na gode da ka yarda mu zo mu ga tsoffin littattafanka.Sir, thank you for allowing us to come see your old notebooks.
    MALAM NASIRU Da fatan alheri. Ga shafa guda, an rubuta ta da Ajami, wanda kakannina suka rubuta shekaru da yawa da suka wuce.You're most welcome. Here is one page, written in Ajami, which my ancestors wrote many years ago.
    IBRAHIM Wannan rubutu Ajami ne, tsohon rubutun Hausa da aka rubuta shi dama zuwa hagu, ko ba haka ba?This writing is Ajami, the old Hausa script written from right to left, isn't it?
    MALAM NASIRU Daidai ne. Ajami ya samo asali ne daga rubutun Larabci, kuma malamai da manazarta sun daɗe suna amfani da shi wajen rubuta addini, tarihi, da waƙoƙin gargajiya.That's correct. Ajami originates from Arabic script, and scholars and researchers have long used it to write religious, historical, and classical poetic material.
    SALAMATU Malam, me ake nufi da wannan alama a rubutun Ajami? Ban taɓa ganin irinta ba.Sir, what does this symbol in the Ajami writing mean? I've never seen one like it.
    MALAM NASIRU Wancan alama tana wakiltar wani sauti da ba ya nan a rubutun Larabci na asali, sai dai malaman Hausa suka daidaita shi don ya dace da harshenmu. Kada ku damu da ƙware da rubuta Ajami yanzu; abin da ke da muhimmanci a wannan mataki shi ne ku fahimci muhimmancinsa.That symbol represents a sound that isn't in the original Arabic script, but Hausa scholars adapted it to suit our language. Don't worry about mastering Ajami writing right now; what matters at this stage is understanding its importance.
    AMINA Malam Nasiru ya ce, “Ajami da Boko dukansu hanyoyi ne na rubuta Hausa,” watau babu wanda ya fi wani muhimmanci.Malam Nasiru said, "Ajami and Boko are both ways of writing Hausa," meaning neither is more important than the other.
    ZAINAB Ajami da Boko dukansu na Hausa ne, sai dai kowanne yana da tarihinsa da yanayin amfaninsa.Both Ajami and Boko belong to Hausa; each simply has its own history and context of use.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Ajami as living heritage, not a relic

    Ajami has long served Hausa-speaking communities for religious instruction, scholarship, historical record-keeping, and classical poetry, and continues to hold scholarly, religious, and family significance today. Presenting Ajami only as something "used long ago" would understate its ongoing cultural and scholarly life; many families, religious scholars, and researchers continue to read, preserve, and study Ajami manuscripts.

    Regional Variation — Ajami practice across communities

    The prevalence and use of Ajami varies across Hausa-speaking regions and communities; some families and religious schools maintain stronger living traditions of Ajami literacy than others. This chapter's example, centered on one elder's personal manuscripts, is illustrative rather than exhaustive; learners should expect variation in how communities relate to Ajami today.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Treating Ajami as outdated or purely religious

    Error: Describing Ajami as an old script no longer relevant, or as used only for religious purposes.

    Correction: Describe Ajami as a script with ongoing historical, scholarly, religious, literary, and family significance, as modeled in the Culture in Context note and the Model Dialogue.

    Common Mistake — Attempting to write in Ajami for this chapter's tasks

    Error: Trying to produce Ajami script in the Writing Workshop or Performance Task instead of writing in Boko.

    Correction: Complete all productive writing tasks in standard Boko, as consolidated through Chapter 11; this chapter requires only recognition and discussion of Ajami, not its production.

    Common Mistake — Assuming Boko replaced Ajami entirely

    Error: Concluding that because Boko is now the everyday standard, Ajami is no longer used or read by anyone.

    Correction: Recognize, as the Reading Practice article explains, that scholars, religious teachers, and some families continue to read and study Ajami manuscripts alongside the widespread use of Boko.

Chapter Overview

Chapter 3 continues Unit I without introducing new grammar or a new vocabulary domain. Building on the rhetorical-purpose work consolidated in Chapter 2, learners now turn from public speech to literary and oral tradition, examining how proverbs, short folktales, and oral narrative conventions carry meaning through consolidated vocabulary and grammar. Zainab, having successfully delivered her farewell remarks with the team's help, is now invited to a community storytelling evening and asked to prepare something to share. Amina, Ibrahim, and Salamatu return to help her recognize and interpret proverbial and narrative structures so she can participate with confidence.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify a proverb (karin magana), a short folktale (tatsuniya), and a narrative frame in a spoken or written Hausa passage.
  2. Explain, in Hausa, the likely meaning or lesson of a familiar proverb using consolidated vocabulary and grammar.
  3. Recognize the typical opening and closing formulas of Hausa oral storytelling.
  4. Follow the sequence of events in a short oral narrative and summarize it accurately.
  5. Compare how a proverb is used literally versus how it is applied figuratively in conversation.
  6. Share a short proverb or story with a partner or group using the storytelling register established in this chapter.

Communication Scenario

Zainab has been invited to a community storytelling evening organized by a neighbor's family, where guests are expected to share a proverb or a short story. She feels comfortable with everyday conversation but unsure how to recognize or interpret the proverbs and narrative patterns she has heard growing up. Amina, Ibrahim, and Salamatu meet with her to walk through a familiar proverb, a short folktale, and the formulas that open and close Hausa oral narration, so she can choose something appropriate to share.

  1. Domain: a small mentoring session preparing for a community storytelling evening.
  2. Target skill: recognizing and interpreting proverbial and narrative traditions using vocabulary and grammar already consolidated.
  3. Grammar status: every system in this chapter remains at Consolidated status; no new grammar, vocabulary domain, or discussion move is introduced.
  4. No unscheduled grammar beyond the Volume IV level, and no literary category beyond proverb, folktale, and oral narrative frame.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Consolidated rhythm and pacing in oral storytelling

No new pronunciation feature is introduced in this chapter. Learners instead apply consolidated tone, vowel-length, and phrase-rhythm control to notice how oral narration uses pacing and repetition differently from ordinary conversation: opening and closing formulas are often delivered with a distinctive, slightly sing-song rhythm, and proverbs are frequently spoken slowly and deliberately for emphasis. Reading the model dialogue and the sample tatsuniya aloud supports this recognition.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new grammar. It consolidates narrative sequencing, predicate types, and reference tracking from Volume IV, applying them to the task of following, interpreting, and retelling proverbs and short oral narratives.

3.1 Introducing a proverb

Speakers often introduce a proverb with a short framing phrase before stating it, recycling reported-speech and identifying patterns.

  • Kamar yadda tsofaffi ke cewa, "Kowa ya san gidansa, kafin ya san na kowa." As the elders say, "Everyone knows their own house before they know everyone else's."

    • 3.2 Explaining a proverb's meaning

    Explaining the lesson of a proverb recycles the identifying-predicate pattern from Chapter 2's naming-the-purpose sentences.

  • Ma'anar wannan karin magana ita ce, ya kamata mutum ya fahimci al'amuransa kafin ya shiga na wasu. The meaning of this proverb is that a person should understand their own affairs before getting involved in others'.

    • 3.3 Opening a folktale

    Hausa oral narration conventionally opens with a fixed call-and-response formula, recycled here as a formulaic unit rather than a new grammar point.

  • Gizo, Gizo, ba ni labari! Storyteller, storyteller, tell me a story! (traditional opening call)

    • 3.4 Sequencing events in narration

    Following the order of events in a folktale recycles the sequencing connectors already consolidated in earlier narration chapters.

  • Da farko, gizo ya tafi kasuwa; sannan ya sadu da kura; a ƙarshe, dukansu suka koma gida tare. First, the spider went to market; then he met the hyena; finally, they both returned home together.

    • 3.5 Closing a folktale

    Oral narration also conventionally closes with a fixed formula marking the end of the story, again treated as a recycled formulaic unit.

  • Kuma su zauna lafiya. Nan aka kammala tatsuniya. And they lived peacefully. Here the story ends.
    • Language Note — Interpretation is not new grammar

    Recognizing and interpreting a proverb or folktale does not require any grammar form beyond what Volume IV already scheduled. What changes is the task applied to consolidated tools: identifying-predicate patterns for stating meaning, sequencing connectors for following narrative order, and fixed formulaic units for opening and closing oral narration.

    Common learner error: Do not translate a proverb word-for-word and assume the literal meaning is the intended lesson. Always state the figurative meaning separately, as shown in Section 3.2.

    Model Dialogue

    Yanayin hali: Amina, Ibrahim, da Salamatu suna taimaka wa Zainab wajen shirya wani abu don raba a wani taron ba da labari na al'umma.

    (Setting: Amina, Ibrahim, and Salamatu are helping Zainab prepare something to share at a community storytelling evening.)

    ZAINAB An gayyace ni zuwa wani taron ba da labari, amma ban san wace irin karin magana ko tatsuniya zan zaɓa ba.I've been invited to a storytelling evening, but I don't know which proverb or folktale to choose.
    IBRAHIM Za ki iya farawa da karin magana mai sauƙi. Misali, "Kowa ya san gidansa, kafin ya san na kowa."You could start with a simple proverb. For example, "Everyone knows their own house before they know everyone else's."
    ZAINAB Na fahimci kalmomin, amma ban tabbata da ma'anarta ba.I understand the words, but I'm not sure of its meaning.
    SALAMATU Ma'anarta ita ce, ya kamata mutum ya fahimci al'amuransa kafin ya shiga na wasu. Ba magana ce game da gidaje kai tsaye ba.The meaning is that a person should understand their own affairs before getting involved in others'. It isn't literally about houses.
    ZAINAB To, na fahimta yanzu. Amma ina so in kawo wata gajeriyar tatsuniya kuma, ba karin magana kaɗai ba.Alright, I understand now. But I'd also like to bring a short folktale, not just a proverb.
    AMINA Ki tuna, kowace tatsuniya tana da wata hanyar farawa, kamar "Gizo, Gizo, ba ni labari!" da kuma hanyar kammalawa a ƙarshe.Remember, every folktale has an opening formula, like "Storyteller, storyteller, tell me a story!" and a way of closing at the end.
    ZAINAB Da farko, gizo ya tafi kasuwa; sannan ya sadu da kura; a ƙarshe, dukansu suka koma gida tare. Zan iya kammala da "kuma su zauna lafiya."First, the spider went to market; then he met the hyena; finally, they both returned home together. I can close with "and they lived peacefully."
    IBRAHIM Wannan tsari ya yi kyau sosai. Ki bi wannan jerin abubuwa daidai kamar yadda kika faɗa, kuma masu sauraro za su fahimta cikin sauƙi.That structure works very well. Follow this sequence of events exactly as you've said it, and the audience will understand easily.
    SALAMATU Kuma ki tuna, karanta a hankali, musamman lokacin da za ki faɗi karin maganar, domin hakan yana ba ta ƙarfi.And remember, speak slowly, especially when you say the proverb, because that gives it strength.

    Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

    1. What problem does Zainab identify at the start of the conversation?
    2. What proverb does Ibrahim suggest, and what does Salamatu say it actually means?
    3. What opening formula does Amina remind Zainab about?
    4. What three events does Zainab sequence in her folktale?
    5. What advice does Salamatu give about delivery?

    Listening Practice

    Ask your instructor, a partner, or a text-to-speech tool to read the following passage aloud, twice. Answer the questions after listening.

    Sauraro — Tatsuniyar Gizo da Kura (Listening: The Folktale of the Spider and the Hyena)

    "Gizo, Gizo, ba ni labari! Da farko, gizo ya tafi kasuwa domin sayen abinci, amma kuɗinsa bai isa ba. Sannan ya sadu da kura a hanya, kuma kura ta ba shi shawarar su yi aiki tare domin samun ƙarin kuɗi. Gizo ya yarda, amma cikin zuciyarsa yana son ya ci amanar kura ya ɗauki dukkan riba shi kaɗai. A ƙarshe, lokacin da suka kai kasuwa, kura ta fahimci nufin gizo, kuma ta ƙi ba shi kome sai dai abin da ya cancanta. Daga wannan lokaci, mutane suka faɗi cewa, "Wanda ya yi zamba, ba ya cin moriyar zamanta." Kuma su zauna lafiya. Nan aka kammala tatsuniya."

    Kula na sauraro — Questions: (1) What opening formula begins the story? (2) What problem does the spider have at the market? (3) What does the hyena suggest, and what does the spider secretly plan? (4) What happens at the end, and what proverb-like lesson does the story leave? (5) How does the story close?

    Rubutun sauraro don malami, abokin karatu, ko amfani da rubutun lantarki. Duba amsoshi a sashi na amsoshi.

    Reading Practice

    Read the short passage below. Identify the opening formula, the sequence of events, the closing formula, and the proverb-like lesson at the end.

    Karatu — Tatsuniyar Zomo da Kunkuru (Reading: The Folktale of the Hare and the Tortoise)

    Gizo, Gizo, ba ni labari!

    Da farko, zomo da kunkuru sun yi jayayya kan wanda ya fi sauri. Sannan suka yanke shawarar yin tsere domin tabbatar da gaskiya. Zomo, saboda yana da tabbaci da kansa, ya tsaya ya huta a tsakiyar hanya. Kunkuru kuwa ya ci gaba da tafiya a hankali, ba tare da tsayawa ba. A ƙarshe, kunkuru ya isa wurin da aka nufa kafin zomo ya farka daga hutunsa. Daga wannan lokaci, mutane suka faɗi cewa, "Jinkiri jinkirin dawaki, ba jinkirin kwana biyu ba." Kuma su zauna lafiya. Nan aka kammala tatsuniya.

    Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

    1. What opening formula begins this folktale?
    2. List the three main events in order.
    3. What does the hare do that costs him the race?
    4. What proverb-like lesson closes the story, and what does it mean?
    5. Write a two-sentence summary of the folktale in your own words.

    Writing Workshop

    1. Karanta misalai da hankali. Reread Sections 3.1–3.5, the Model Dialogue, and the Reading Practice passage.
    2. Zaɓi karin magana ko tatsuniya. Choose a proverb you already know, or use one of the folktales from this chapter.
    3. Rubuta ma'ana ko taƙaitawa. If you chose a proverb, write one sentence stating its figurative meaning. If you chose a folktale, write a short summary using sequencing connectors.
    4. Ƙara buɗewa da rufewa. If summarizing a folktale, add an appropriate opening and closing formula.
    5. Duba jerin bincike. Check your text against the checklist below.
    6. Rubuta siffa ta ƙarshe. Produce a clean final version, then prepare to present it aloud, following the Speaking Practice instructions.

    Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

    • The proverb's figurative meaning is stated separately from its literal wording, if a proverb was chosen.
    • The folktale summary follows a clear sequence using da farko, sannan, a ƙarshe, if a folktale was chosen.

    • Feedback language, if used, is respectful and constructive, following the Chapter 1 mentoring register.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Proverbs and storytelling in daily life

    Proverbs are woven into everyday Hausa conversation, often used to comment indirectly on a situation, offer advice, or settle a disagreement without stating an opinion too bluntly. Oral storytelling traditions, including tatsuniya performed for children and adults alike, commonly use fixed call-and-response opening formulas and a recognizable closing line, and are found across community gatherings, family settings, and radio or media programs.

    Regional Variation — Proverb and formula variation

    The specific wording of proverbs, and the exact opening or closing formulas used in oral narration, can vary between communities, families, and regions across the Hausa-speaking world. Learners should treat the examples in this chapter as common, widely recognized forms, remaining open to variation encountered in other communities, as introduced in the Chapter 13 discussion of regional and register variation in Volume IV.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Treating a proverb's literal meaning as its lesson

    Error: Assuming a proverb about houses, animals, or objects is only about those literal things, missing the figurative lesson the proverb is meant to convey.

    Correction: Always state the figurative meaning explicitly and separately from the literal wording, as in Section 3.2.

    Common Mistake — Omitting the opening or closing formula

    Error: Retelling a folktale as a plain sequence of events without the conventional opening call or closing line, which can make the retelling sound incomplete to a Hausa-speaking audience.

    Correction: Include a recognizable opening formula and closing line, as modeled in Sections 3.3 and 3.5.

    Common Mistake — Disordering the sequence of events

    Error: Summarizing a folktale's events out of order, or omitting the sequencing connectors that make the order clear to a listener.

    Correction: Use da farko, sannan, a ƙarshe to mark the beginning, middle, and end of the narrative, as in Section 3.4.

    Answer Key / Amsoshi

    Tambayoyin fahimta na tattaunawa (Dialogue comprehension)

    1. Zainab does not know which proverb or folktale to choose for the storytelling evening.
    2. Ibrahim suggests "Kowa ya san gidansa, kafin ya san na kowa"; Salamatu explains it means a person should understand their own affairs before getting involved in others'.
    3. Amina reminds Zainab of the opening formula "Gizo, Gizo, ba ni labari!"
    4. Zainab sequences: the spider going to market, meeting the hyena, and the two returning home together.
    5. Salamatu advises speaking slowly, especially when delivering the proverb, to give it strength.

    Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

    (1) The story opens with "Gizo, Gizo, ba ni labari!" (2) The spider does not have enough money to buy food at the market. (3) The hyena suggests they work together to earn more money, while the spider secretly plans to keep all the profit for himself. (4) At the end, the hyena realizes the spider's intention and refuses to give him more than his fair share; the lesson is that someone who cheats does not enjoy the benefit of their cheating. (5) The story closes with "Kuma su zauna lafiya. Nan aka kammala tatsuniya."

    Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

    1. The story opens with "Gizo, Gizo, ba ni labari!"
    2. The three main events are: the hare and tortoise arguing over who is faster, agreeing to race, and the tortoise continuing steadily while the hare stops to rest.
    3. The hare stops to rest in the middle of the race, confident in his own speed.
    4. The story closes with the saying that slow and steady progress is not the same as taking two days' worth of delay; the lesson is that steady effort can outperform overconfidence.
    5. Sample summary: A hare and a tortoise argue over who is faster and agree to race. The overconfident hare stops to rest, allowing the steady tortoise to win.

    Sashi na B (Grammar)

    1. Sample: Kamar yadda tsofaffi ke cewa, "Hakuri maganin duniya."
    2. Sample: Ma'anar wannan karin magana ita ce, mutum ya kamata ya yi haƙuri domin cimma nasara a rayuwa.
    3. Sample: Gizo, Gizo, ba ni labari!
    4. Sample: Da farko, kura ta yi yunwa; sannan ta nemi abinci a gari; a ƙarshe, ta sami abinci daga wurin wani manomi.

    Sashi na C (Sample writing)

    Karin magana: "Hannu ɗaya ba ya ɗaukar jinka." Ma'anarta ita ce, aiki mai girma yana bukatar hadin kan mutane fiye da mutum ɗaya.

    Tatsuniya (taƙaitawa): Gizo, Gizo, ba ni labari! Da farko, zomo ya sami gonar kayan lambu; sannan kura ta zo ta so ta ci daga gonar ba tare da izini ba; a ƙarshe, zomo ya kama kura tare da taimakon abokansa. Daga wannan lokaci, mutane suka faɗi cewa, kowa ya girmama abin wani. Kuma su zauna lafiya. Nan aka kammala tatsuniya.

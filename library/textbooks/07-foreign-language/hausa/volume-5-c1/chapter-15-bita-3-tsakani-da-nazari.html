Bita 3: Tsakani da Nazari

C1–C2-oriented Specialization · Chapter 15 of 20, Unit III Assessment · Speak and Write Hausa: Advanced Precision and Specialization
Unit III Assessment — Integrating Variation, Ajami, Translation, Commentary, and Advanced Discussion

Chapter Overview

Chapter 15 is the Unit III integrated assessment. It does not introduce new vocabulary or grammar. Instead, it brings together the four skills developed across Chapters 11 through 14 — comparing regional and generational variation, engaging receptively with Ajami and historical Hausa writing, translating and commenting on texts for different audiences, and managing disagreement and consensus-building — into a single connected task. The heritage-weekend planning storyline concludes this unit as Amina, Ibrahim, Salamatu, and Zainab prepare a final integrated packet for the cultural center: a short bilingual commentary, a translated excerpt, a note on regional variation, and a written record of how the planning committee resolved its disagreement. Every language pattern used in this chapter was already consolidated by Chapter 13, and every communicative task was already modeled in Chapters 11 through 14.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify and label regional, generational, and register-based variation in a short Hausa text or exchange.
  2. Recognize Ajami as a Hausa writing tradition and describe, in general terms, how it relates to Boko.
  3. Translate a short passage for a specified audience and explain a register choice made in translation.
  4. Summarize and comment on a text, distinguishing the text's content from your own commentary.
  5. Restate two competing positions fairly, compare them without declaring either simply correct, and propose a compromise.
  6. Integrate listening, speaking, reading, and writing tasks that combine variation awareness, Ajami literacy, translation and commentary, and disagreement management.
  7. Complete a cumulative performance task assessing all systems scheduled through Chapter 14, using only grammar and vocabulary consolidated by Chapter 13 plus the small metalinguistic sets introduced in Chapters 11 through 14.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new grammar. It requires independent, combined use of the identifying-predicate, reported-speech, comparison, and clarification-question patterns from Sections 11.1–13.4, and the disagreement, restatement, and compromise patterns from Sections 14.1–14.4, across four connected tasks.

15.1 Describing variation without ranking one form as more correct

Recycles the comparison pattern (Sections 11.3/12.3/13.3/14.3) to note a difference between how two speakers of different generations or regions might describe the same event, without presenting either as more correct.

  • Yadda kakan Ibrahim yake kwatanta bikin ya bambanta da yadda ɗan uwansa mai shekaru ashirin yake yin haka, amma dukansu suna magana ne a kan biki guda. The way Ibrahim's grandfather describes the festival differs from the way his twenty-year-old cousin does, but both are talking about the same event.

    • 15.2 Introducing Ajami for a general audience

    Recycles the identifying-predicate pattern to state a fact about Ajami's status as a Hausa writing tradition, at the level of general awareness developed in Chapter 12.

  • Ajami rubutun Hausa ne wanda aka samo daga haruffan Larabci, kuma Boko rubutun zamani ne da ake amfani da shi a makarantu a yau. Ajami is a Hausa writing tradition drawn from Arabic letters, and Boko is the modern writing system used in schools today.

    • 15.3 Translating and commenting on a register choice

    Recycles the translation-and-commentary pattern from Section 13.1–13.4 to explain, in a short sentence, why a particular register was chosen for a specified audience.

  • Na zaɓi kalmomi masu sauƙi domin baƙi da yawa ba su saba da Hausa sosai ba. I chose simple words because many visitors are not very familiar with Hausa.

    • 15.4 Recording how a disagreement was resolved

    Recycles the reported-speech and compromise patterns from Sections 14.2 and 14.4 to summarize, in writing, how two positions were reconciled.

  • Bayan an maimaita ra’ayin Malama Hadiza da na Malam Sani, an amince da matsakaicin shawarar da Salamatu ta bayar. After Ms. Hadiza's and Mr. Sani's views were restated, the compromise Salamatu proposed was accepted.
    • Language Note — An assessment of combination, not of new structure

    Every pattern in Sections 15.1–15.4 was consolidated by Chapter 14. The assessment task is combining them accurately within a single connected packet: describing variation fairly, introducing Ajami accurately, translating and explaining a register choice, and recording a disagreement's resolution. No section requires a form, structure, or vocabulary item that has not already appeared in Chapters 1–14.

    Common learner error: Do not treat this chapter as an opportunity to try new vocabulary or grammar "because it is an assessment." Use only the forms consolidated through Chapter 14; accuracy with familiar material is the goal, not novelty.

    Model Dialogue

    Yanayin hali: Amina, Ibrahim, Salamatu, da Zainab suna tattaunawa a gaban taron ƙarshe na kwamitin, suna shirya fayil ɗin ƙarshe domin bikin al’adu.

    (Setting: Amina, Ibrahim, Salamatu, and Zainab discuss, ahead of the committee's final meeting, how to prepare the final packet for the heritage festival.)

    IBRAHIM Ina tsammani ya kamata mu fara da bayanin bambancin tsara, tunda kakannin baƙi da yaransu za su fahimci bikin daban-daban.I think we should start with the note on generational variation, since visitors' grandparents and their children will understand the festival differently.
    ZAINAB Na yarda, amma kada mu ce salon kaka ne kawai daidai, ko na yaro ne kawai daidai — dukansu hanyoyi ne masu inganci.I agree, but let's not say the grandparent's style is the only correct one, or the child's is — both are valid ways.
    SALAMATU Bayan haka, sai mu rubuta gajeriyar sakwa game da Ajami, don baƙi masu sha’awar tarihi su fahimci cewa Hausa tana da al’adar rubutu fiye da Boko kaɗai.After that, we should write a short note about Ajami, so visitors interested in history understand that Hausa has a writing heritage beyond Boko alone.
    AMINA To, ni zan fassara saƙon maraba domin baƙi, sannan in rubuta gajeren sharhi kan dalilin da ya sa na zaɓi kalmomi masu sauƙi.All right, I'll translate the welcome message for visitors, then write a short comment on why I chose simple words.
    IBRAHIM Kuma kada mu manta mu rubuta yadda aka warware sabanin Malama Hadiza da Malam Sani, don kwamitin gaba su ga yadda muka yi.And let's not forget to write how Ms. Hadiza's and Mr. Sani's disagreement was resolved, so future committees can see how it was handled.
    SALAMATU Zan rubuta cewa bayan an maimaita ra’ayinsu, an amince da matsakaicin shawara ta raba taron gida biyu.I'll write that after their views were restated, a compromise splitting the event into two parts was accepted.
    ZAINAB Sannan mu duba jerin bincike gaba ɗaya kafin mu miƙa fayil ɗin ga kwamiti.Then let's check the full review checklist before we hand the packet to the committee.

    Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

    1. Which four components does the group agree to include in the final packet?
    2. What caution does Zainab give about describing generational variation?
    3. Why does Salamatu say the note on Ajami will be useful to visitors?
    4. What two tasks does Amina take responsibility for?
    5. What does Ibrahim want the packet to preserve for future committees?

    Sigar don Kwatanci da Sharhi — Passage for Comparison and Commentary

    A bikin al’adu, dattawa da yawa suna gaisuwa da jimloli masu tsawo, suna tambayar lafiyar iyali gaba ɗaya kafin fara magana. Yara da matasa, a wasu lokuta, suna amfani da gajeriyar gaisuwa kaɗan sannan su ci gaba da tattaunawa. Dukansu salailan gaisuwa suna nuna girmamawa, sai dai kowanne yana bin al’adar zamaninsa.

    Gloss: At the cultural festival, many elders greet with longer phrases, asking about the whole family's health before beginning to talk. Young people, at times, use a shorter greeting and then continue the conversation. Both greeting styles show respect, but each follows the custom of its generation.

    • The disagreement record accurately reflects both original positions and the compromise reached.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — A packet that honors multiple perspectives

    Preparing a single document that fairly represents generational variation, historical writing traditions, translation choices, and a resolved disagreement reflects a broader value in many Hausa-speaking community settings: public materials that will reach a mixed audience are expected to acknowledge more than one legitimate way of experiencing or describing a shared event or tradition.

    Regional Variation — This packet models one approach among several

    The specific balance struck in this packet — how much space is given to elders' versus younger speakers' perspectives, or how simple the translated welcome message is kept — reflects choices appropriate to this particular cultural center and audience. Other communities or events might reasonably make different choices about emphasis, length, or register while still following the same underlying principles of fairness and clarity.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Ranking one generation's or region's Hausa as more correct

    Error: Describing an elder's or a younger speaker's way of talking about the festival as the "real" or "proper" way, and the other as a deviation.

    Correction: Use the comparison pattern in Section 15.1 to note a difference without ranking either form, as modeled in the Reading Practice packet.

    Common Mistake — Treating Ajami as merely historical or decorative

    Error: Introducing Ajami as an outdated curiosity rather than a living, respected part of Hausa literacy heritage.

    Correction: Follow the accurate, respectful framing in Section 15.2 and the Reading Practice packet.

    Common Mistake — Omitting the reason for a translation choice

    Error: Providing a translation without any commentary explaining why particular words or a particular register were chosen.

    Correction: Always pair a translation with a short commentary sentence, as in Section 15.3 and Amina's task in the Model Dialogue.

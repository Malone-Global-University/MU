Tattaunawa Mai Zurfi

C1–C2-oriented Specialization · Chapter 14 of 20, continuing Unit III · Speak and Write Hausa: Advanced Precision and Specialization
Unit III Continues — Advanced Discussion and Negotiation · Managing Disagreement, Not New Grammar

Chapter Overview

Chapter 14 continues Unit III by turning from mediation and translation to the practical work of managing complex spoken exchange: disagreement, consensus-building, and formal negotiation. Building directly on the identifying, reported-speech, comparison, and clarification patterns consolidated through Chapters 11 through 13, this chapter develops advanced discussion as a communicative task in its own right. No new grammar is introduced. The heritage-weekend handout that Zainab, Amina, Ibrahim, and Salamatu prepared in Chapter 13 has drawn attention from the cultural center's planning committee, and the four friends are invited to a planning meeting where committee members disagree about how the festival program should be organized. The chapter treats disagreement as an ordinary, manageable part of communication, and consensus-building as a skill of listening, restating, and proposing rather than simply winning an argument.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Express respectful disagreement with a proposal or opinion in standard instructional Hausa.
  2. Restate another speaker's position accurately before responding to it.
  3. Propose an alternative or compromise that acknowledges more than one point of view.
  4. Report what two or more people said about a disputed topic, distinguishing their positions.
  5. Compare competing proposals without declaring one participant simply right or wrong.
  6. Ask a clarifying question to make sure a disagreement is understood correctly before responding.
  7. Use the identifying-predicate, reported-speech, comparison, and clarification-question patterns from Chapters 11 through 13 to manage a multi-person discussion.
  8. Complete listening, speaking, reading, and writing tasks that integrate disagreement and consensus-building with vocabulary and grammar already consolidated through Chapter 13.

Communication Scenario

The cultural center's planning committee liked the bilingual handout Zainab, Amina, Ibrahim, and Salamatu prepared in Chapter 13, and has invited the four of them to a planning meeting for the heritage weekend. At the meeting, two committee members disagree: one wants the schedule to center on formal presentations by scholars like Malam Nasiru, while another wants more time set aside for informal storytelling and open conversation with visitors. Zainab, Amina, Ibrahim, and Salamatu listen to both positions, restate them to make sure they understand correctly, and help propose a schedule that includes both formal and informal elements, without declaring either committee member's original idea wrong.

  1. Domain: disagreement, restatement, and consensus-building applied to community event planning.
  2. Target skill: understanding two competing positions accurately, restating each fairly, and proposing an alternative that respects both.
  3. Grammar status: no new productive grammar; all sentence patterns used for discussion remain at Consolidated status from Chapters 1–13.
  4. Vocabulary status: a small set of clearly labeled discussion- and negotiation-related terms is introduced as productive vocabulary; the cultural-heritage and translation vocabulary from Chapters 12 and 13 is recycled, not retaught.
  5. The chapter does not present either committee member's original proposal as more correct, and treats disagreement as a normal, manageable part of planning together.

Core Vocabulary

As in Chapters 11 through 13, this chapter's new productive vocabulary is small and metalinguistic — words for talking about the process of discussing and resolving disagreement, rather than a new topical domain. The cultural-heritage and translation vocabulary from Chapters 12 and 13 (Ajami, tarihi, fassara, taƙaitawa, sharhi) is recycled here as background context for the planning meeting, not retaught as new vocabulary.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Intonation of respectful disagreement

No new sound is introduced in this chapter. Learners practice the phrase-level intonation used when introducing a disagreement respectfully, such as before amma ("but") in the formulaic connector na yarda, amma… ("I agree, but…"). A falling, measured tone before amma signals that a considered contrast is coming, rather than an abrupt contradiction. Continue producing tone, vowel length, and the special consonants ɓ, ɗ, and ƙ according to the standard model consolidated since Volume I, with particular attention to keeping disagreement sound calm and measured rather than clipped or rushed.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new grammar. It applies the identifying-predicate, reported-speech, comparison, and clarification-question patterns consolidated through Chapter 13 to the new task of managing disagreement, restatement, and consensus-building.

14.1 Identifying a position or a proposal

Recycles the identifying-predicate pattern from Section 11.1/12.1/13.1 to name a position as belonging to a particular speaker.

  • Wannan shawarar taron ce, ba ta kwamitin gudanarwa ba kaɗai. This is the meeting's proposal, not only the planning committee's.

    • 14.2 Reporting what each side said, before responding

    Recycles the reported-speech pattern from Section 11.2/12.2/13.2, here applied to restating a disputed position accurately before answering it.

  • Malama Hadiza ta ce ya kamata mu mai da hankali kan gabatarwar malamai, yayin da Malam Sani ya ce ya kamata a bar lokaci mai yawa domin zumunta da baƙi. Ms. Hadiza said we should focus on scholars' presentations, while Mr. Sani said plenty of time should be left for socializing with visitors.

    • 14.3 Comparing two proposals without ranking either as simply right

    Recycles the comparison pattern from Section 11.3/12.3/13.3 to note a difference between positions without presenting one as inherently correct.

  • Ra’ayin Malama Hadiza da na Malam Sani dukansu suna da inganci, sai dai kowanne yana mai da hankali kan wani muhimmin al’amari daban. Ms. Hadiza's view and Mr. Sani's view are both sound, but each focuses on a different important consideration.

    • 14.4 Asking a clarifying question before responding to a disagreement

    Recycles the clarification-question pattern from Section 11.4/12.4/13.4.

  • Malam Sani, kana nufin a bar lokaci fiye da rabin taron domin zumunta, ko wani abu ne dabam? Mr. Sani, do you mean leaving more than half the event for socializing, or something different?
    • Language Note — Managing discussion, not new structure

    Every pattern in Sections 14.1–14.4 was consolidated by Chapter 13. The new task is applying these familiar patterns to a new kind of content: identifying whose proposal is whose, restating two competing positions fairly, comparing them without declaring a winner, and asking a clarifying question before responding. Learners are not expected to master formal negotiation theory — only to manage a respectful disagreement accurately in standard instructional Hausa.

    Common learner error: Do not respond to a disagreement by simply restating your own opinion louder or more firmly. Restate the other speaker's position first, as in Section 14.2, before offering a comparison or a middle-ground proposal.

    Model Dialogue

    Yanayin hali: An gayyaci Amina, Ibrahim, Salamatu, da Zainab zuwa taron shirya bikin al’adu, inda Malama Hadiza da Malam Sani, mambobin kwamiti, suke da sabani a kan tsarin shirin.

    (Setting: Amina, Ibrahim, Salamatu, and Zainab are invited to the heritage-festival planning meeting, where committee members Ms. Hadiza and Mr. Sani disagree about the program's structure.)

    HADIZA Ina ganin ya kamata mu mai da hankali kan gabatarwar malamai kamar Malam Nasiru, domin baƙi su koyi tarihin da ya dace.I think we should focus on presentations by scholars like Malam Nasiru, so visitors learn accurate history.
    SANI Ni kuwa, ina ganin ya kamata a bar lokaci mai yawa domin zumunta da baƙi, domin haka ne suke tuna bikin.As for me, I think plenty of time should be left for socializing with visitors, since that's how they remember the event.
    ZAINAB Malam Sani, kana nufin a bar lokaci fiye da rabin taron domin zumunta, ko wani abu ne dabam?Mr. Sani, do you mean leaving more than half the event for socializing, or something different?
    SANI A’a, ba dole ba ne fiye da rabi; ina nufin a tabbatar akwai isashen lokaci, ba kawai mintuna kaɗan a ƙarshe ba.No, it doesn't have to be more than half; I mean making sure there's enough time, not just a few minutes at the end.
    AMINA To, Malama Hadiza ta ce ya kamata mu mai da hankali kan gabatarwar malamai, yayin da Malam Sani ya ce ya kamata a bar lokaci mai yawa domin zumunta da baƙi. Na fahimci dukansu daidai?So, Ms. Hadiza said we should focus on scholars' presentations, while Mr. Sani said plenty of time should be left for socializing with visitors. Have I understood both of you correctly?
    HADIZA Haka ne, kin fahimta daidai.Yes, that's right, you've understood correctly.
    IBRAHIM Ra’ayin Malama Hadiza da na Malam Sani dukansu suna da inganci, sai dai kowanne yana mai da hankali kan wani muhimmin al’amari daban.Ms. Hadiza's view and Mr. Sani's view are both sound, but each focuses on a different important consideration.
    SALAMATU Ina da matsakaicin shawara: za mu iya raba taron gida biyu — sa’a ɗaya domin gabatarwar Malam Nasiru, sannan sauran lokacin domin zumunta da tattaunawa cikin walwala.I have a middle-ground proposal: we could split the event into two parts — one hour for Malam Nasiru's presentation, and then the rest of the time for socializing and relaxed conversation.
    HADIZA Na yarda, amma ina son a tabbatar gabatarwar ba za a yanke ta gajarce ba don rashin lokaci.I agree, but I want to make sure the presentation isn't cut short for lack of time.
    SANI Ni ma na yarda da wannan shawarar; ina ganin za ta gamsar da bukatunmu biyu.I also agree with this proposal; I think it will satisfy both our needs.

    Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

    1. What does Malama Hadiza propose the festival should focus on, and why?
    2. What does Malam Sani propose instead, and why?
    3. What does Zainab do before responding to Malam Sani's position?
    4. How does Amina check that she has understood both committee members correctly?
    5. What compromise does Salamatu propose, and how do Hadiza and Sani respond to it?

    Writing Workshop

    1. Karanta misalai da hankali. Reread Sections 14.1–14.4, the Model Dialogue, and the Reading Practice meeting notes.
    2. Zaɓi sabani na yau da kullum. Choose a small, realistic disagreement between two people (planning an event, choosing a topic, dividing a task).
    3. Rubuta ra’ayin kowanne bangare. Write one sentence stating each side's position, following Section 14.1.
    4. Rubuta jimlar ba da rahoto. Write one sentence reporting both positions together, following Section 14.2.
    5. Rubuta jimlar kwatanci. Write one sentence comparing the two positions without ranking either, following Section 14.3.
    6. Rubuta tambayar bayyanawa. Write one clarifying question you would ask before responding, following Section 14.4.
    7. Rubuta matsakaicin shawara. Write one or two sentences proposing a compromise that respects both positions.
    8. Duba jerin bincike. Check your text against the checklist below, then produce a clean final version.

    Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

    • Each side's position is stated clearly and fairly, without exaggeration.
    • The reported-speech sentence accurately represents both positions.
    • The comparison sentence does not declare either position simply right or wrong.
    • The clarifying question is genuinely useful for understanding the disagreement better.
    • The proposed compromise acknowledges something valuable in both original positions.
    • Hausa sentences use standard instructional Hausa with accurate special characters and punctuation.
    • No new grammar beyond Chapter 13 is required to understand the text.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Disagreement as a normal part of community planning

    In many Hausa-speaking community settings, open disagreement in a planning meeting is treated as an ordinary and constructive part of reaching a good outcome, not as a breakdown in relationships. Restating another person's position respectfully before responding, as Amina does in the Model Dialogue, is a widely valued way of showing that a speaker has been heard, and often makes a group more willing to consider a compromise.

    Regional Variation — Styles of disagreement vary by setting and relationship

    How directly disagreement is expressed can vary by region, age difference, and the relationship between speakers; some community and generational contexts favor more indirect approaches to disagreement, especially across age or status differences, while peer discussions among younger community members, as modeled in this chapter, may be somewhat more direct. Learners should treat the Model Dialogue as one illustrative pattern rather than the only acceptable style of disagreement.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Responding before restating the other position

    Error: Immediately stating one's own opinion in response to a disagreement, without first showing that the other speaker's position has been understood.

    Correction: Restate the other speaker's position, as Amina does in the Model Dialogue, before offering a comparison or a proposal.

    Common Mistake — Declaring one position simply correct

    Error: Describing one committee member's proposal as "right" and the other as "wrong," rather than recognizing that both may reflect valid concerns.

    Correction: Use the comparison pattern in Section 14.3 to note that both positions are sound but focus on different considerations.

    Common Mistake — Proposing a compromise that ignores one side entirely

    Error: Offering a "middle-ground" proposal that actually adopts only one speaker's position in full.

    Correction: Follow Salamatu's model, which explicitly preserves an element of both Hadiza's and Sani's original concerns.

    Answer Key / Amsoshi

    Tambayoyin fahimta na tattaunawa (Dialogue comprehension)

    1. Malama Hadiza proposes focusing on scholars' presentations so that visitors learn accurate history.
    2. Malam Sani proposes leaving plenty of time for socializing with visitors, because that is what makes the event memorable for them.
    3. Zainab asks Malam Sani a clarifying question to make sure she understands exactly how much time he means before responding.
    4. Amina restates both Hadiza's and Sani's positions together and explicitly asks whether she has understood both of them correctly.
    5. Salamatu proposes splitting the event into two parts: one hour for the presentation and the remaining time for socializing; both Hadiza and Sani accept this proposal.

    Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

    (1) Hadiza and Sani initially disagreed about how to allocate the meeting's time between formal presentations and informal socializing. (2) The group restated each person's position to make sure they understood the disagreement correctly before responding. (3) Salamatu proposed splitting the event into two parts, one hour for the presentation and the rest of the time for socializing. (4) The meeting concluded with the whole committee in agreement, after Hadiza and Sani both accepted the compromise.

    Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

    1. Malama Hadiza's original position was to focus on scholars' presentations, so that visitors would learn accurate history.
    2. Malam Sani's original position was to leave plenty of time for socializing with visitors, since that is what helps them remember the event.

    4. The final proposal split the event into two parts, one hour for Malam Nasiru's presentation and the rest of the time for socializing; both committee members accepted it.
    5. Sample summary: Hadiza and Sani initially disagreed about how to structure the festival's schedule, one favoring formal presentations and the other favoring informal socializing.

Unit IV Concludes — Extended Writing: Advanced Portfolio

Chapter Overview

Chapter 19 brings together the planning notes from Chapter 16, the drafted-and-edited booklet section from Chapter 17, and the presentation-and-defense materials from Chapter 18 into a single, organized advanced portfolio. No new grammar paradigm and no new vocabulary domain appears here beyond a small set of portfolio-assembly terms; the connected narration, sequencing, reasoning, comparison, and reference-tracking patterns consolidated through Chapter 15, together with the planning, drafting, and presentation vocabulary from Chapters 16–18, are now applied to selecting, arranging, and reflecting on a body of work. Amina, Ibrahim, Salamatu, and Zainab assemble their individual and shared materials into a portfolio for Malama Hadiza and Malam Sani, showing the full arc of the project from first plan to final defense.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Select representative materials from a completed project to include in a portfolio.
  2. Arrange planning, drafting, and presentation materials in a coherent, labeled sequence.
  3. Write a short self-commentary explaining the purpose and value of each portfolio item.
  4. Compare an early draft with its final version and describe what changed and why.
  5. Describe, in connected discourse, one's own development in genre, register, and revision skill across the project.
  6. Evaluate one's own work honestly, identifying strengths and areas for continued growth.
  7. Complete a full portfolio-assembly cycle as the penultimate capstone of the extended writing sequence begun in Chapter 16.

Communication Scenario

With the commemorative booklet printed and the presentation to the cultural center behind them, Amina, Ibrahim, Salamatu, and Zainab meet with Malama Hadiza and Malam Sani to assemble an advanced portfolio documenting the whole project. Each contributor selects their planning notes, draft, revision, and presentation materials, arranges them in order, and writes a short self-commentary explaining what each item shows about their growth. Malama Hadiza and Malam Sani ask each learner to compare an early draft with the final version and to reflect honestly on what they would still like to improve.

  1. Domain: extended writing and reflection — selecting, arranging, and commenting on a body of finished work.
  2. Target skill: assembling a coherent portfolio and describing one's own development across genre, register, and revision.
  3. Grammar status: no new productive grammar; connected narration, sequencing, reasoning, comparison, and reference-tracking patterns from Chapters 11–18 are consolidated and applied to self-reflective commentary.
  4. Vocabulary status: a small portfolio-assembly vocabulary set is introduced as the chapter's only new productive vocabulary.
  5. This chapter is not formally assessed; it prepares the Chapter 20 final capstone.
Kalmomin Fayil da Nazarin Kai — Portfolio and Self-Reflection Vocabulary

Core Vocabulary

This chapter introduces one small productive vocabulary set: the metalanguage needed to select, arrange, and comment on finished work in a portfolio. Like the planning terms from Chapter 16, the drafting terms from Chapter 17, and the presentation terms from Chapter 18, these terms describe the assembly process itself and will be recycled through the remainder of Unit IV.

Cumulative recycling: This chapter recycles the eight planning terms from Chapter 16, the eight drafting-and-editing terms from Chapter 17, the eight presentation-and-defense terms from Chapter 18, connected narration and sequencing markers, reason and comparison patterns, and reference-tracking devices consolidated by Chapter 15. Only the eight portfolio-and-reflection terms above are new productive vocabulary.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Sustaining clear delivery in reflective, evaluative speech

No new sound is introduced in this chapter. The pronunciation focus is discourse-level: maintaining tone, vowel length, and special-consonant control (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ) across the more measured, reflective pace typical of self-commentary and comparison, where speakers often pause between points to weigh their words. Continue applying phrase-level intonation to distinguish a straightforward statement of fact from a more tentative, self-evaluative remark.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new grammar. It consolidates the connected narration, sequencing, reasoning, comparison, and reference-tracking patterns built through Chapter 15, together with the planning, drafting, and presentation vocabulary from Chapters 16–18, applying them to selecting, arranging, and reflecting on a completed body of work.

19.1 Selecting and introducing a portfolio item

Recycles topic-introduction and reason patterns to name a chosen item and state briefly why it was selected as representative of the project.

  • Na zaɓi wannan misali na farko domin yana nuna yadda na fara tsara sashina kafin in fara rubutu. I chose this first example because it shows how I began organizing my section before I started writing.

    • 19.2 Comparing a draft with its final version

    Recycles comparison patterns (Chapters 13–15) to describe specific differences between an early draft and the finished text, and to explain the reasoning behind each change.

  • Idan aka kwatanta daftarina na farko da na ƙarshe, za a ga cewa na canza tsarin jumloli domin su fi sauƙin fahimta ga mai karatu. If my first draft is compared with the final one, you will see that I changed the sentence structure so it would be easier for a reader to understand.

    • 19.3 Describing one's own development across the project

    Recycles connected narration and sequencing to trace growth from the planning stage (Chapter 16) through drafting and revision (Chapter 17) to presentation and defense (Chapter 18).

  • Daga tsarin da na yi a Babi na 16, zuwa daftarin da na gyara a Babi na 17, har zuwa gabatarwar da na yi a Babi na 18, na ga cewa ci gabana ya bayyana musamman a fannin tsara jimloli da amsa tambayoyi. From the plan I made in Chapter 16, to the draft I revised in Chapter 17, to the presentation I gave in Chapter 18, I can see that my progress shows especially in organizing sentences and answering questions.

    • 19.4 Naming an area for continued improvement honestly

    Recycles polite self-evaluative language to state, without excessive self-criticism, one specific area the learner intends to keep working on.

  • Abin da zan ci gaba da inganta shi ne saurin amsa tambayoyi ba tare da yin shiru mai tsawo ba kafin in fara magana. What I will continue to improve is answering questions quickly, without a long silence before I begin speaking.
    • Language Note — Reflective language, not new grammar

    Every pattern in Sections 19.1–19.4 was consolidated by Chapter 15 or introduced as vocabulary in Chapters 16–18. What is new is the task: using these familiar patterns to select representative work, compare early and final versions, narrate one's own development, and name an honest area for growth. Chapter 20 will draw on this portfolio, together with every skill practiced across the volume, for the final capstone.

    Common learner error: Do not describe an area for improvement using only vague self-criticism such as "my Hausa is not good." Follow Section 19.4: name one specific, concrete skill and describe it without exaggeration in either direction.

    Model Dialogue

    Yanayin hali: Amina, Ibrahim, Salamatu, da Zainab suna zama tare da Malama Hadiza da Malam Sani domin tsara fayil ɗin ƙwarewarsu.

    (Setting: Amina, Ibrahim, Salamatu, and Zainab are sitting with Malama Hadiza and Malam Sani to assemble their portfolios.)

    MALAMA HADIZA Yau za mu tsara fayil ɗin ƙwarewarku. Ku zaɓi mafi kyau daga tsarinku, daftarinku, da gabatarwarku, sannan ku rubuta sharhin kai a kan kowanne.Today we will assemble your portfolios. Choose the best from your plans, your drafts, and your presentations, then write a self-commentary on each.
    AMINA Na zaɓi tsarin da na yi a Babi na 16 a matsayin misali na farko, domin yana nuna yadda na fara tunani game da sashina kafin in rubuta ko da kalma ɗaya.I chose the plan I made in Chapter 16 as my first example, because it shows how I began thinking about my section before I wrote even a single word.
    MALAM SANI Yanzu, ku kwatanta daftarinku na farko da na ƙarshe. Me ya canza, kuma me ya sa kuka canza shi?Now, compare your first draft with the final one. What changed, and why did you change it?
    IBRAHIM Idan aka kwatanta daftarina na farko da na ƙarshe, za a ga na ƙara wata hujja ta biyu domin ta ƙarfafa amsata ga tambayoyin da za a iya yi mani.If my first draft is compared with the final one, you will see that I added a second piece of evidence to strengthen my answer to any questions I might be asked.
    SALAMATU Ni kuwa, daga tsarina a Babi na 16, zuwa daftarina a Babi na 17, har zuwa gabatarwata a Babi na 18, na ga ci gabana musamman a fannin sauya jimloli daga rubutu zuwa magana.As for me, from my plan in Chapter 16, to my draft in Chapter 17, to my presentation in Chapter 18, I have seen my progress especially in turning written sentences into spoken ones.
    MALAMA HADIZA Wannan tsari mai kyau ne. Zainab, kina da abin da za ki ci gaba da inganta?That's a good arrangement. Zainab, do you have something you will continue to improve?
    ZAINAB I, abin da zan ci gaba da inganta shi ne saurin amsa tambayoyi ba tare da yin shiru mai tsawo ba, kamar yadda ya faru lokacin gabatarwar da muka yi.Yes, what I will continue to improve is answering questions quickly, without a long silence, as happened during our presentation.
    MALAM SANI Wannan sharhi ne mai gaskiya kuma mai amfani. Yanzu ku tsara fayil ɗinku bisa ga tsari, daftari, sannan gabatarwa, tare da sharhin kai a gaban kowane misali.That is an honest and useful reflection. Now arrange your portfolio by plan, then draft, then presentation, with a self-commentary in front of each example.

    Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

    1. Which item does Amina choose as her first portfolio example, and why?
    2. What does Ibrahim say changed between his first and final draft?
    3. Where does Salamatu say her progress is most visible?
    4. What area for continued improvement does Zainab name, and how does she connect it to Chapter 18?

    Sigar don Kwatanci — Weak and Strong Self-Commentary

    Sharhi mara ƙarfi: Na canza sashina domin ya fi kyau yanzu.

    Sharhi mai ƙarfi: Na canza sashina ta hanyar ƙara hujja ta biyu da gajarta jimloli masu tsawo, domin mai karatu ya fi fahimtar mahangata cikin sauƙi.

    Gloss: Weak commentary: "I changed my section because it is better now." Strong commentary: "I changed my section by adding a second piece of evidence and shortening long sentences, so that a reader would understand my point more easily."

    Writing Workshop

    1. Tattara abubuwan aikinka. Gather your plan (Chapter 16), draft and final version (Chapter 17), and presentation materials (Chapter 18).
    2. Zaɓi mafi kyau. Select the item from each stage that best represents your work, following Section 19.1.
    3. Rubuta sharhin kai. Write a short self-commentary for each selected item, explaining why you chose it.
    4. Kwatanta daftari da na ƙarshe. Write two or three sentences comparing your first draft with your final version, following Section 19.2.
    5. Rubuta labarin ci gabanka. Write a short connected paragraph narrating your development from planning through presentation, following Section 19.3.
    6. Rubuta abin da za ka ci gaba da inganta. Write one honest, specific sentence naming an area for continued growth, following Section 19.4.
    7. Duba jerin bincike. Check your portfolio against the checklist below before moving on to Chapter 20.

    Jerin binciken fayil — Portfolio checklist:

    • The portfolio includes one representative item from planning, drafting, and presentation.
    • Each item has a short self-commentary explaining why it was selected.
    • The draft-to-final comparison names at least one specific change and its reason.
    • The development narrative connects the plan, draft, and presentation in a clear sequence.
    • The area for continued improvement is specific and stated without excessive self-criticism.
    • Hausa sentences use standard instructional Hausa with accurate special characters and punctuation.
    • No grammar or vocabulary beyond what has been consolidated through Chapter 18 (plus the eight new portfolio-and-reflection terms) is required to understand the portfolio.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Honest self-evaluation within a mentoring relationship

    When a mentor and learner review a body of work together, as Malama Hadiza and Malam Sani do here, it is common for the learner to be asked to name their own areas for growth before the mentor offers feedback; this practice treats the learner as a capable evaluator of their own progress and keeps the relationship collaborative rather than one-directional.

    Regional Variation — Portfolio and self-assessment practices vary by setting

    The informal, spoken self-commentary modeled in this chapter suits a small mentoring group. In some schools, universities, or professional settings, portfolios may instead require written self-assessment forms, formal rubrics, or a structured interview, while still serving the same underlying goal of documenting growth and encouraging honest reflection.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Choosing an item without explaining why

    Error: Including a portfolio item with no self-commentary, leaving the reader to guess why it was selected.

    Correction: Follow Section 19.1: state specifically what the item shows about the writer's work or growth.

    Common Mistake — Vague, non-specific self-criticism

    Error: Naming an area for improvement only as a general feeling, such as "my Hausa is not good enough," without naming a specific skill.

    Correction: Follow Section 19.4: name one concrete, specific skill, such as answering questions more quickly, rather than a vague overall judgment.

    Common Mistake — Comparing drafts without naming the reason for a change

    Error: Stating only that something changed between draft and final version, without explaining why the change was made.

    Correction: Follow Section 19.2: name both the change and the reasoning behind it, as modeled in the Model Dialogue.

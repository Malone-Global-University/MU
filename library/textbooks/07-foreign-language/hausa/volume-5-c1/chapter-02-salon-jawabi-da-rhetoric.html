Salon Jawabi da Rhetoric

C1–C2-oriented Specialization · Chapter 2 of 20, Unit I Continues · Speak and Write Hausa: Advanced Precision and Specialization
Unit I Continues — No New Grammar or Vocabulary Domain · Consolidation Chapter

Chapter Overview

Chapter 2 continues Unit I without introducing new grammar or a new vocabulary domain. Building on the precision and revision skills consolidated in Chapter 1, learners now examine how word choice, structure, and tone shape a speaker's or writer's effect on an audience across four rhetorical purposes: persuasive, ceremonial, explanatory, and advisory discourse. Zainab, having revised her notice with Amina, Ibrahim, and Salamatu's help, is now asked to draft brief remarks for an upcoming office gathering. The mentoring team returns to help her recognize how the same underlying content changes shape depending on whether the goal is to persuade, to mark an occasion, to explain, or to advise.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Distinguish persuasive, ceremonial, explanatory, and advisory purposes in a short Hausa text or spoken passage.
  2. Identify the word choices and structural devices that make a passage persuasive, ceremonial, explanatory, or advisory.
  3. Compare two versions of the same content written for different rhetorical purposes.
  4. Analyze a short speech or notice and explain, in Hausa, which rhetorical purpose it serves and how.
  5. Produce a short spoken or written passage matched to a specified rhetorical purpose.
  6. Give and receive feedback on rhetorical effectiveness using the respectful, mentoring register established in Chapter 1.

Communication Scenario

Zainab's revised notice was well received, and she has now been asked to prepare brief remarks for a farewell gathering for a retiring colleague. She drafts a version that explains the colleague's history but is unsure whether it sounds appropriately ceremonial. Amina, Ibrahim, and Salamatu return to help her compare her draft against three other short passages, each written for a different rhetorical purpose, so she can hear how persuasive, ceremonial, explanatory, and advisory discourse each sound different even when discussing the same person and event.

  1. Domain: a short mentoring session comparing four rhetorical purposes applied to the same office event.
  2. Target skill: recognizing and producing persuasive, ceremonial, explanatory, and advisory discourse using vocabulary and grammar already consolidated.
  3. Grammar status: every system in this chapter remains at Consolidated status; no new grammar, vocabulary domain, or discussion move is introduced.
  4. No unscheduled grammar beyond the Volume IV level, and no rhetorical category beyond persuasive, ceremonial, explanatory, and advisory discourse.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Consolidated phrase-level intonation across rhetorical purposes

No new pronunciation feature is introduced in this chapter. Learners instead apply consolidated tone, vowel-length, and phrase-rhythm control to notice how delivery shifts with rhetorical purpose: ceremonial remarks tend toward slower, more measured phrasing, while persuasive remarks often carry more emphatic stress on key words. Reading each of the four sample passages aloud, and comparing how they feel to say, supports this recognition.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new grammar. It consolidates predicate types, focus and emphasis, coordination and subordination, and register control from Volume IV, applying them to the task of shaping the same content for four different rhetorical purposes.

2.1 Persuasive framing

A persuasive passage foregrounds a recommendation and gives a reason, recycling focus and reason-giving patterns already consolidated.

  • Ina ganin ya kamata mu goyi bayan wannan shiri, domin zai amfane mu duka. I believe we should support this plan, because it will benefit all of us.

    • 2.2 Ceremonial framing

    A ceremonial passage marks the occasion directly and uses respectful, warm language, recycling greeting and blessing formulas from earlier volumes.

  • A yau, mun taru domin girmama shekaru masu yawa na hidimarsa; muna godiya sosai. Today, we have gathered to honor his many years of service; we are deeply grateful.

    • 2.3 Explanatory framing

    An explanatory passage states facts in order, without urging a particular reaction, recycling sequencing and reporting patterns.

  • Ya fara aiki a wannan ofis shekaru goma sha biyar da suka wuce, sannan ya rike mukamai daban-daban. He began working at this office fifteen years ago, and then he held various positions.

    • 2.4 Advisory framing

    An advisory passage offers guidance using softened, suggestion-style language, recycling the qualifying patterns from Chapter 18 of Volume IV.

  • Idan za ku ci gaba da wannan aiki, ina ba da shawarar ku ci gaba da hulɗa da tsofaffin abokan aiki. If you will continue this work, I suggest you keep in touch with former colleagues.

    • 2.5 Naming the rhetorical purpose

    A reviewer names which purpose a passage serves, using a simple identifying predicate.

  • Wannan sakin layi bayani ne kawai; bai ƙunshi wata shawara ko rarrashi ba. This paragraph is purely explanation; it does not contain any advice or persuasion.
    • Language Note — Rhetoric is not new grammar

    Shaping a passage for a rhetorical purpose does not require any grammar form beyond what Volume IV already scheduled. What changes is which consolidated tools a writer selects: reason-giving and recommendation for persuasion, respectful and warm formulas for ceremony, ordered factual statement for explanation, and softened suggestion for advice.

    Common learner error: Do not mix advisory softening (ina ba da shawarar…) into a ceremonial passage, where it can sound oddly tentative. Match the framing device to the purpose, as shown in Sections 2.1–2.4.

    Model Dialogue

    Yanayin hali: Amina, Ibrahim, da Salamatu suna taimaka wa Zainab wajen shirya jawabin ban kwana domin wani tsohon ma’aikaci.

    (Setting: Amina, Ibrahim, and Salamatu are helping Zainab prepare farewell remarks for a retiring colleague.)

    ZAINAB Na rubuta wani abu, amma ina jin ba shi da salon bikin ban kwana; ya fi kama da bayani kawai.I have written something, but I feel it does not have the style of a farewell ceremony; it sounds more like a plain explanation.
    SALAMATU Wannan sakin layi bayani ne kawai; bai ƙunshi wata shawara ko rarrashi ba. Don bikin ban kwana, kina bukatar kalmomin girmamawa da godiya.This paragraph is purely explanation; it does not contain any advice or persuasion. For a farewell ceremony, you need words of honor and gratitude.
    ZAINAB To, zan iya cewa, "A yau, mun taru domin girmama shekaru masu yawa na hidimarsa; muna godiya sosai."Alright, I could say, "Today, we have gathered to honor his many years of service; we are deeply grateful."
    IBRAHIM Hakan ya fi dacewa. Idan kina son kawo shawara ga sabbin ma’aikata a ƙarshen jawabin, za ki iya amfani da salo mai laushi, misali "ina ba da shawarar."That fits much better. If you want to bring in advice for the new staff at the end of the speech, you can use a softer style, for example "I suggest."
    ZAINAB Ina ganin ya kamata mu goyi bayan sabbin shirye-shiryensa ma, domin hakan zai kara nuna godiyarmu.I think we should also support his new plans, because that would further show our gratitude.
    AMINA Wannan jimla ta fi kama da rarrashi fiye da bikin ban kwana; ki yi hankali kada ki cakuɗa dukkan salo huɗu a sakin layi ɗaya.That sentence sounds more like persuasion than farewell ceremony; be careful not to mix all four styles into one paragraph.
    ZAINAB Na fahimta. Zan raba jawabin: bayani a farko, sannan girmamawa, sannan in kammala da shawara guda ɗaya kawai.I understand. I will divide the speech: explanation first, then honor, and then I will close with just one piece of advice.
    SALAMATU Wannan tsari yana da kyau sosai; jawabinki zai fi tasiri haka.That structure is very good; your speech will be more effective this way.

    Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

    1. What problem does Zainab identify in her first draft?
    2. What rhetorical purpose does Salamatu say a farewell ceremony requires?
    3. What softer style does Ibrahim suggest for advice at the end of a speech?
    4. What mistake does Amina warn Zainab against?
    5. How does Zainab decide to structure her final speech?

    Speaking Practice

    Atisayen ƙa’ida — Controlled substitution

    With a partner, take turns converting a short explanatory sentence about an office event into a persuasive, then a ceremonial, then an advisory version.

    Tattaunawa — Communicative task

    In a group of three, follow the Model Dialogue: one person drafts remarks, one identifies which rhetorical purpose is missing or mismatched, and the group agrees on a final structure combining explanation, ceremony, and a single piece of advice.

    Madadin karatu kai tsaye — Solo alternative

    Record yourself delivering the same short announcement three times: once persuasively, once ceremonially, and once as plain explanation, noticing how your word choice and delivery change each time.

    Writing Workshop

    1. Karanta misalai da hankali. Reread Sections 2.1–2.5, the Model Dialogue, and the Reading Practice passage.
    2. Zaɓi taron ku. Choose a real or imagined office event (a farewell, an anniversary, a project launch) to write about.
    3. Rubuta gajerun sakin layi guda huɗu. Write one short paragraph for each rhetorical purpose: explanatory, ceremonial, persuasive, and advisory, all about the same event.
    4. Zaɓi tsari na ƙarshe. Decide how to combine two or three of these paragraphs into a single short, coherent speech or notice, without mixing purposes within a single paragraph.
    5. Duba jerin bincike. Check your combined text against the checklist below.
    6. Rubuta siffa ta ƙarshe. Produce a clean final version, then prepare to present it aloud, following the Speaking Practice instructions.

    Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

    • Each paragraph clearly serves one rhetorical purpose: persuasive, ceremonial, explanatory, or advisory.
    • No paragraph mixes advisory softening into ceremonial language, or persuasive urging into plain explanation.
    • The combined text moves through its chosen purposes in a logical, natural order.
    • Feedback language, if used, is respectful and constructive, following the Chapter 1 mentoring register.
    • Special characters (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ, ƴ) and apostrophe/glottal conventions are used accurately.
    • No unscheduled grammar beyond the Volume IV level is required to understand the text.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Farewell and honor in the workplace

    Marking a colleague's retirement or departure with public words of honor and gratitude is a common professional practice in many Hausa-speaking workplaces. Such remarks typically combine a brief factual account of the person's service with warm, respectful language, and often close with a forward-looking word of encouragement for those who remain.

    Regional Variation — Length and formality of ceremonial remarks

    The expected length and degree of formality for farewell or anniversary remarks can vary between institutional, community, and media contexts across Hausa-speaking regions. Learners should treat the short model in this chapter as one common professional standard, adapting length and formality to the specific occasion and audience, as introduced in the Chapter 13 discussion of regional and register variation in Volume IV.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Mixing rhetorical purposes within one paragraph

    Error: Combining ceremonial gratitude, persuasive urging, and advisory suggestion within a single sentence or paragraph, which can confuse the audience about the passage's purpose.

    Correction: Keep each paragraph focused on one rhetorical purpose, as in Sections 2.1–2.4, and use paragraph breaks to move between purposes.

    Common Mistake — Using advisory softening in ceremonial contexts

    Error: Using a tentative phrase such as ina ba da shawarar when the moment calls for direct, confident honor or gratitude, which can sound oddly hesitant.

    Correction: Reserve softened advisory language for genuine suggestions, and use direct, warm language such as muna godiya sosai for ceremonial purposes.

    Common Mistake — Treating explanation as inherently persuasive

    Error: Assuming that stating facts in order automatically persuades the audience, and omitting an explicit recommendation when persuasion is actually the goal.

    Correction: If the purpose is persuasion, include a clear recommendation and reason, as in ina ganin ya kamata… domin…, rather than relying on facts alone.

    Answer Key / Amsoshi

    Tambayoyin fahimta na tattaunawa (Dialogue comprehension)

    1. Zainab feels her draft sounds like plain explanation rather than a farewell ceremony.
    2. Salamatu says a farewell ceremony requires words of honor and gratitude, not plain explanation.
    3. Ibrahim suggests softened advisory language such as "ina ba da shawarar" for advice at the end.
    4. Amina warns Zainab against mixing all four rhetorical styles into a single paragraph.
    5. Zainab decides to structure her speech as explanation first, then honor, then a single piece of advice at the end.

    Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

    (1) The event is a ten-year departmental anniversary, opened with ceremonial gratitude toward everyone who helped reach that milestone. (2) The explanatory facts given are that the department started with five staff and has grown to fifty. (3) The persuasive claim is that continuing the department's spirit of teamwork is the secret to its future success. (4) New staff are advised to learn from more experienced colleagues and to take time building relationships. (5) The speech closes with confident optimism that the next ten years will be even better.

    Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

    1. The explanatory sentence states that the committee started the program three months ago and has since cleaned ten locations in the neighborhood.
    2. The ceremonial sentence expresses gratitude to everyone who gave their time and effort to the project.
    3. The persuasive sentence claims that all residents should join the program because a clean neighborhood brings health benefits to everyone.
    4. The advisory sentence suggests that new participants come on Saturday morning to meet the group before work begins.
    5. Sample summary: A community committee describes its three-month-old neighborhood cleanup program and thanks its volunteers. It encourages all residents to join and advises newcomers to meet the group before their first Saturday session.

    Sashi na C (Sample writing)

    Bayani: Aliyu ya fara aiki a sashenmu shekaru goma da suka wuce, sannan ya rike mukamai uku daban-daban kafin ya kai matsayin da yake yanzu.

    Bikin girmamawa: A yau, mun taru domin girmama shekaru goma na hidimarsa; muna godiya sosai ga duk abin da ya bayar.

    Rarrashi: Ina ganin ya kamata mu bi tafarkinsa na kwazo da haɗin kai, domin hakan ne ya kawo mana wannan nasara.

    Shawara: Ga sababbin ma’aikata, ina ba da shawarar ku nemi shawarwarinsa kafin ya bar mu.

    Other accurate paragraphs that clearly match each rhetorical purpose, without mixing purposes within a single paragraph, are equally acceptable.

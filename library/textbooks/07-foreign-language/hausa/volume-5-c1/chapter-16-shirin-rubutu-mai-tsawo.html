Unit IV Begins — Extended Writing: Planning, Evidence, Audience, and Style

Chapter Overview

Chapter 16 opens Unit IV by turning from short integrated tasks toward planning a single piece of extended writing. Learners do not encounter new grammar or a new vocabulary domain in this chapter; instead, the connected narration, sequencing, reasoning, comparison, and reference-tracking patterns consolidated through Chapter 15 are drawn together as planning tools for a long-form project. The heritage-weekend storyline continues as the cultural center invites Amina, Ibrahim, Salamatu, and Zainab to write a short commemorative booklet about the festival for future volunteers and visitors. Before any drafting begins, the group must decide on a purpose, an audience, a structure, and the kind of evidence the booklet will use — the planning work this chapter teaches.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. State the purpose and intended audience of an extended piece of Hausa writing in one or two clear sentences.
  2. Outline a long-form text into labeled sections using connected sequencing language.
  3. Identify what kind of evidence, example, or personal experience will support each planned section.
  4. Compare two possible ways of organizing the same material and give a reason for preferring one.
  5. Track a consistent set of references (people, places, and events) across a multi-section plan without confusing them.
  6. Choose a register appropriate to a stated audience and justify that choice.
  7. Produce a complete written plan for an extended project, combining listening, speaking, reading, and writing.

Communication Scenario

The heritage weekend was a success, and the cultural center's director asks the planning committee to turn the final packet from Chapter 15 into something longer: a short commemorative booklet that future volunteers can use to understand why the festival matters, how it is organized, and what past disagreements taught the committee. Before writing a single paragraph, Amina, Ibrahim, Salamatu, and Zainab meet to plan the booklet together — deciding who will read it, what sections it needs, what evidence or stories belong in each section, and how to keep names, places, and events consistent from one section to the next.

  1. Domain: extended writing project planning — purpose, audience, structure, evidence, and reference tracking.
  2. Target skill: producing a complete, organized plan for a longer text before drafting begins.
  3. Grammar status: no new productive grammar; connected narration, sequencing, reasoning, comparison, and reference-tracking patterns from Chapters 11–15 are consolidated and applied to planning.
  4. Vocabulary status: a small planning-and-composition vocabulary set is introduced as the chapter's only new productive vocabulary.
  5. This chapter is not formally assessed; it prepares the drafting, revision, and editing work of Chapter 17.
Kalmomin Shirin Rubutu — Writing-Planning Vocabulary

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Reading a plan aloud with clear section breaks

No new sound is introduced in this chapter. The pronunciation focus is discourse-level: producing a brief pause and a falling intonation contour at the end of each labeled section when reading a plan aloud, so a listener can follow where one section ends and the next begins. Continue applying the tone, vowel-length, and special-consonant control (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ) consolidated since Volume I within each section of connected speech.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new grammar. It consolidates the connected narration, sequencing, reasoning, comparison, and reference-tracking patterns built through Chapter 15, applying them specifically to the task of planning — rather than yet drafting — an extended piece of writing.

16.1 Stating purpose and audience

Recycles the identifying-predicate and reason patterns to name why a text is being written and for whom, before any content is drafted.

  • Maƙasudin ƙasidar shi ne a bai wa sabbin masu ba da agaji labarin yadda bikin ya samo asali. The booklet's purpose is to give new volunteers the story of how the festival came to be.
  • Masu karatunmu galibi sababbi ne, don haka za mu yi amfani da salo mai sauƙi. Our readers are mostly newcomers, so we will use a simple style.

    • 16.2 Outlining sections with sequencing language

    Recycles connected sequencing markers (na farko, na biyu, bayan haka, a ƙarshe) to lay out a multi-part structure before drafting.

  • Sashi na farko zai bayyana tarihin bikin; na biyu zai kwatanta yadda ake shirya shi a yau; a ƙarshe, sashi na uku zai ba da shawarwari ga masu shirya nan gaba. The first section will explain the festival's history; the second will describe how it is organized today; finally, a third section will offer advice to future organizers.

    • 16.3 Matching evidence to each planned section

    Recycles the reason and comparison patterns to state what kind of evidence, example, or personal experience will support a given section, and to compare two possible choices.

  • Za mu iya amfani da labarin kakan Ibrahim a matsayin shaida ga sashin tarihi, ko kuma mu yi amfani da rubutun kwamitin na farko; na farko zai fi ba da zafi da rai ga labarin. We could use Ibrahim's grandfather's story as evidence for the history section, or we could use the committee's original written record; the first would give the account more warmth and life.

    • 16.4 Tracking references across a multi-section plan

    Recycles pronoun-reference and noun/name repetition patterns from Chapters 1–15 to keep people, places, and events consistent as the plan moves from section to section.

  • A sashi na farko za mu kira ta "Malama Hadiza," kuma za mu ci gaba da amfani da wannan suna a sauran sassan, ba tare da canza shi zuwa wata siga ba. In the first section we will call her "Malama Hadiza," and we will keep using this same name in the remaining sections, without changing it to another form.
    • Language Note — Planning language, not drafting language

    Every pattern in Sections 16.1–16.4 was consolidated by Chapter 15. What is new is the task: using these familiar patterns to produce a plan — a document about a future text — rather than the text itself. Chapter 17 will use this plan to draft, revise, and edit the actual booklet.

    Common learner error: Do not begin drafting full paragraphs of the booklet in this chapter. The performance task requires a plan: purpose, audience, section outline, evidence notes, and a reference list, not finished prose.

    Model Dialogue

    Yanayin hali: Amina, Ibrahim, Salamatu, da Zainab suna taro don shirya tsarin ƙasidar tunawa da bikin al’adu, kafin fara rubutu.

    (Setting: Amina, Ibrahim, Salamatu, and Zainab meet to plan the structure of the festival commemorative booklet, before beginning to write.)

    AMINA Kafin mu rubuta kome, ya kamata mu yanke shawara a kan maƙasudin ƙasidar da kuma wanda za mu yi wa magana.Before we write anything, we should decide on the booklet's purpose and who we are speaking to.
    IBRAHIM Ina ganin maƙasudin shi ne mu taimaki sabbin masu ba da agaji su fahimci bikin, don haka masu karatunmu sabbi ne, ba ƙwararrun kwamiti ba.I think the purpose is to help new volunteers understand the festival, so our readers are newcomers, not committee experts.
    SALAMATU To, in haka ne, sai mu raba ƙasidar zuwa sassa uku: tarihin bikin, tsarin shiryawa a yau, da shawarwari ga masu shirya nan gaba.All right, if that's so, let's divide the booklet into three sections: the festival's history, how it is organized today, and advice for future organizers.
    ZAINAB Don sashin tarihi, za mu iya amfani da labarin kakan Ibrahim a matsayin shaida, domin ya fi rubutun kwamiti tsohuwa rai da zafi.For the history section, we can use Ibrahim's grandfather's story as evidence, because it has more life and warmth than the old committee record.
    IBRAHIM Na yarda, amma ya kamata mu tabbata mun kira shi da suna guda a duk sassan, kada mu canza masa suna daga sashi zuwa sashi.I agree, but we should make sure we call him by the same name throughout all the sections, and not change his name from one section to another.
    AMINA Domin sashin shawarwari, za mu yi amfani da abin da muka koya daga sabanin Malama Hadiza da Malam Sani a Babi na 14.For the advice section, we will use what we learned from Ms. Hadiza's and Mr. Sani's disagreement in Chapter 14.
    SALAMATU Kafin mu ci gaba, bari mu rubuta ƙa’idar rubutun a takarda ɗaya, don kada mu manta wani sashi ko wata shaida.Before we go on, let's write the outline on a single sheet, so we don't forget a section or a piece of evidence.

    Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

    1. What does the group decide is the booklet's purpose and intended readers?
    2. Into how many sections do they divide the booklet, and what is each about?
    3. What evidence does Zainab propose for the history section, and why?
    4. What caution does Ibrahim give about naming him consistently across sections?
    5. Why does Salamatu want to write the outline down before continuing?

    Reading Practice

    Read the draft writing plan below, prepared by Amina, Ibrahim, Salamatu, and Zainab for the commemorative booklet.

    Karatu — Ƙa’idar Rubutun Ƙasidar (Reading: Draft Writing Plan for the Booklet)

    Maƙasudi da masu karatu: Taimaka wa sabbin masu ba da agaji su fahimci bikin al’adu; masu karatu sabbi ne, don haka salo mai sauƙi ne za a yi amfani da shi.

    Sashi na 1 — Tarihin bikin: Shaida: labarin kakan Ibrahim game da yadda bikin ya fara shekaru da yawa da suka wuce.

    Sashi na 2 — Tsarin shiryawa a yau: Shaida: rubutun kwamiti na yanzu game da jerin shirye-shirye da kwanan wata.

    Sashi na 3 — Shawarwari ga masu shirya nan gaba: Shaida: abin da aka koya daga warware sabanin Malama Hadiza da Malam Sani.

    Jerin sunaye da za a bi: Malama Hadiza, Malam Sani, kakan Ibrahim — kada a canza waɗannan sunaye a kowane sashi.

    Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

    1. Who are the booklet's intended readers, and what does that mean for its style?
    2. What evidence is planned for each of the three sections?
    3. Which names must remain consistent across the whole booklet?
    4. Why might it matter that Section 1 and Section 3 both draw on real experiences rather than only official records?
    5. Write a three-sentence summary, in English, of the complete writing plan.

    Writing Workshop

    1. Karanta misalai da hankali. Reread Sections 16.1–16.4, the Model Dialogue, and the Reading Practice plan.
    2. Zaɓi maƙasudi da masu karatu. Choose the purpose and intended readers for your own extended-writing project, following Section 16.1.
    3. Zana tsarin sassa. Outline three or more sections for your project, following Section 16.2.
    4. Zaɓi shaida ga kowane sashi. For each section, note what evidence, example, or experience will support it, following Section 16.3.
    5. Rubuta jerin sunaye da wurare. List the names of people, places, and events you will refer to, and commit to using the same form throughout, following Section 16.4.
    6. Haɗa dukkan sassan zuwa ƙa’ida guda. Combine all the parts into one organized writing plan, following the model in the Reading Practice section.
    7. Duba jerin bincike. Check your plan against the checklist below before moving on to Chapter 17.

    Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

    • The purpose and intended readers are stated clearly in one or two sentences.
    • The outline has at least three clearly labeled sections in a logical order.
    • Each section has at least one planned piece of evidence, example, or experience.
    • A reference list of names, places, or events is included, with one consistent form for each.
    • The plan does not yet contain full drafted paragraphs; it remains a plan.
    • Hausa sentences use standard instructional Hausa with accurate special characters and punctuation.
    • No grammar or vocabulary beyond what has been consolidated through Chapter 15 (plus the eight new planning terms) is required to understand the plan.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Planning as a shared, collaborative step

    Deciding on purpose, audience, and structure as a group before any drafting begins reflects a common expectation in many Hausa-speaking institutional and community settings: a document intended for a shared audience, such as a cultural center's volunteers, is often planned collectively so that everyone involved agrees on its purpose and content before one person begins to write.

    Regional Variation — This planning process is one workable model

    The specific way this committee plans its booklet — three sections, a shared reference list, evidence drawn from both personal story and committee record — suits this particular cultural center and audience. Other institutions, writing teams, or individual writers might reasonably plan differently, with more or fewer sections, different kinds of evidence, or a different balance of formal and personal material, while still following the same underlying planning principles.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Drafting full paragraphs instead of planning

    Error: Writing complete, polished paragraphs of the booklet during the planning stage, before purpose, audience, and structure have been agreed upon.

    Correction: Follow the staged planning process in Sections 16.1–16.4 and the Writing Workshop; save full drafting for Chapter 17.

    Common Mistake — Leaving out evidence for a planned section

    Error: Listing a section title with no note on what evidence, example, or experience will actually support it.

    Correction: Assign at least one concrete piece of evidence to every section, as modeled in the Reading Practice plan.

    Common Mistake — Letting a name or reference shift across sections

    Error: Referring to the same person by a title in one section and a first name in another, causing confusion for readers.

    Correction: Record a single consistent form for each name or reference at the planning stage, following Section 16.4.

    Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

    (1) The booklet's purpose is to help new volunteers understand the festival; its readers are newcomers, so a simple style will be used. (2) The three sections are the festival's history, how it is organized today, and advice for future organizers. (3) Ibrahim's grandfather's story will support the history section. (4) Amina states that each person should be called by the same name in every section, without changing it from section to section.

    Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

    1. The booklet's readers are new volunteers, which means the booklet will use a simple style.
    2. Section 1 uses Ibrahim's grandfather's story; Section 2 uses the current committee's schedule record; Section 3 uses what was learned from resolving Hadiza and Sani's disagreement.
    3. The names Malama Hadiza, Malam Sani, and Ibrahim's grandfather must remain consistent throughout the booklet.
    4. Personal experience can make history and advice sections feel authentic and memorable, complementing the accuracy of official records.
    5. Sample summary: The plan states that the booklet is meant to help new volunteers understand the festival in simple language. It outlines three sections — history, current organization, and advice for the future — each supported by a specific piece of evidence. It also fixes one consistent name for each person mentioned across all sections.

    Sashi na B (Grammar and Style)

    1. Sample: Maƙasudin wannan rubutu shi ne bayyana wa sababbin ma’aikata yadda ake gudanar da aikin; masu karatu sabbi ne.

Sashi na 3 — Shawarwari ga sababbin ɗalibai: shaida, abin da ɗalibai na baya suka koya daga kurakuransu. Jerin sunaye: Malam Bello (tsohon shugaba), za a yi amfani da wannan suna a duk sassan.

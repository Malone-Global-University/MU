Unit IV Continues — Extended Writing: Drafting, Revision, and Editing

Chapter Overview

Chapter 17 takes the writing plan produced in Chapter 16 and turns it into a full draft. Learners do not encounter a new grammar paradigm or a new vocabulary domain here either; the connected narration, sequencing, reasoning, comparison, and reference-tracking patterns consolidated by Chapter 15, and the planning vocabulary introduced in Chapter 16, are now applied to drafting complete paragraphs, then revising and editing them. Amina, Ibrahim, Salamatu, and Zainab use their approved plan to write the commemorative booklet's three sections, read each other's drafts, and apply a shared set of revision and editing steps before the booklet is considered ready for Chapter 18.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Turn a section of an approved writing plan into a complete draft paragraph.
  2. Maintain the purpose, audience, structure, and reference choices set in a prior plan while drafting.
  3. Identify specific content, organization, and consistency problems in a draft using a shared checklist.
  4. Distinguish revision (content and organization) from editing (spelling, punctuation, and mechanics).
  5. Give and respond to written feedback on a partner's draft respectfully and specifically.
  6. Produce a revised, edited paragraph that corrects identified problems without introducing new content beyond the plan.
  7. Complete a full drafting-revision-editing cycle for one section of an extended text.

Communication Scenario

With the writing plan from Chapter 16 approved by the cultural center's director, Amina, Ibrahim, Salamatu, and Zainab now divide the drafting work: each will write one section of the commemorative booklet, then the group will read every draft together, note problems, and revise and edit before submitting a finished booklet. Zainab drafts the history section using Ibrahim's grandfather's story; Salamatu drafts the section on how the festival is organized today; Ibrahim drafts the advice section using the lesson from the Chapter 14 disagreement. Amina reads all three drafts against the plan and leads the group through revision and editing.

  1. Domain: extended writing — drafting from a plan, giving and receiving feedback, revising, and editing.
  2. Target skill: producing a complete, plan-consistent draft, then improving it through a structured revision-and-editing cycle.
  3. Grammar status: no new productive grammar; connected narration, sequencing, reasoning, comparison, and reference-tracking patterns from Chapters 11–16 are consolidated and applied to drafting and revision.
  4. Vocabulary status: a small drafting-and-editing vocabulary set is introduced as the chapter's only new productive vocabulary.
  5. This chapter is not formally assessed; it prepares the oral defense and presentation work of Chapter 18.
Kalmomin Rubutu da Gyara — Drafting and Editing Vocabulary

Core Vocabulary

This chapter introduces one small productive vocabulary set: the metalanguage needed to draft, revise, and edit an extended text. Like the planning terms from Chapter 16, these terms describe the writing process itself and will be recycled through the rest of Unit IV.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Reading a full draft aloud for a revision check

No new sound is introduced in this chapter. The pronunciation focus is discourse-level: reading an entire drafted paragraph aloud at a steady pace, with a brief pause at each sentence boundary, so that a listening partner can catch organizational or reference problems by ear before looking at the page. Continue applying tone, vowel-length, and special-consonant control (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ) consolidated since Volume I across the full length of a drafted paragraph, not only in short model sentences.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new grammar. It consolidates the connected narration, sequencing, reasoning, comparison, and reference-tracking patterns built through Chapter 15, together with the planning vocabulary from Chapter 16, applying them to drafting full paragraphs and then revising and editing them.

17.1 Turning a plan entry into a drafted paragraph

Recycles connected sequencing and reason patterns to expand a one-line plan entry (a section title plus its assigned evidence) into a complete paragraph with an opening statement, supporting detail, and a closing sentence.

  • Kamar yadda muka rubuta a shirin, sashi na farko zai bayyana tarihin bikin ta hanyar labarin kaka; don haka, sai in fara da labarin yadda kakan Ibrahim ya fara halartar bikin tun yana yaro. As we wrote in the plan, the first section will explain the festival's history through the grandfather's story; so, I will begin with the story of how Ibrahim's grandfather first attended the festival as a boy.

    • 17.2 Keeping a draft consistent with its plan

    Recycles comparison patterns to check a drafted paragraph against its plan entry and identify where the draft has drifted from the agreed purpose, audience, or evidence.

  • Daftarin Salamatu ya yi kyau, amma ta ƙara wani labari da ba a tsara shi ba a shirin; ya kamata mu tambaye ta ko za mu daidaita sashin da shirin ko mu sabunta shirin. Salamatu's draft is good, but she added a story that was not planned in the outline; we should ask her whether we should align the section with the plan or update the plan.

    • 17.3 Giving and receiving specific feedback

    Recycles reason patterns and polite disagreement forms (Chapter 14) to state a specific problem in a partner's draft and suggest a correction without discouraging the writer.

  • Ra’ayina shi ne sashinka ya yi kyau ƙwarai, amma jumla ta biyu ba ta bayyana dalilin canjin tsari ba; za a iya ƙara jumla guda don bayyana hakan. My feedback is that your section is very good, but the second sentence doesn't explain the reason for the change in schedule; one more sentence could be added to make that clear.

    • 17.4 Distinguishing revision from editing

    Recycles the plan-versus-draft distinction from Chapter 16 to separate two kinds of correction: gyara of content and organization, and gyaran rubutu of spelling, punctuation, and special characters.

  • Da farko, mu duba ko kowane sashi yana bin shirinmu; bayan haka ne kawai za mu duba rubutu don neman kurakuran haruffa da alamomin rubutu. First, let's check whether each section follows our plan; only afterward will we check the writing for spelling and punctuation mistakes.
    • Language Note — Drafting language, not new grammar

    Every pattern in Sections 17.1–17.4 was consolidated by Chapter 15 or introduced as vocabulary in Chapter 16. What is new is the task: using these familiar patterns first to expand a plan into full paragraphs, and then to talk about a draft as an object that can be checked, discussed, and improved. Chapter 18 will use the finished, edited booklet as the basis for an oral research presentation.

    Common learner error: Do not revise for spelling and punctuation before checking whether the draft still matches its plan. Content and organization problems should be resolved first; mechanical editing comes last, as modeled in Section 17.4.

    Model Dialogue

    Yanayin hali: Amina, Ibrahim, Salamatu, da Zainab sun rubuta daftarin sassan ƙasidar, kuma yanzu suna karanta su tare don gyara da bita.

    (Setting: Amina, Ibrahim, Salamatu, and Zainab have written drafts of the booklet's sections, and now they read them together to revise and edit.)

    ZAINAB Na gama daftarin sashin tarihi; na fara da labarin kakan Ibrahim yadda muka tsara a shirin.I've finished the draft of the history section; I started with Ibrahim's grandfather's story, as we planned.
    AMINA Ra’ayina shi ne kyakkyawan farawa ne, amma sashin ya ƙare kwatsam; ba mu san dalilin da ya sa bikin ya ci gaba har yau ba.My feedback is that it's a good start, but the section ends abruptly; we don't learn why the festival has continued to this day.
    ZAINAB Gaskiya ne. Zan ƙara jumla ɗaya a ƙarshe don haɗa tarihin da yadda ake ci gaba da bikin a yau.That's true. I'll add one more sentence at the end to connect the history to how the festival continues today.
    SALAMATU Daftarina na sashi na biyu ya yi daidai da shirinmu, amma na lura da wasu kurakuran haruffa guda biyu.My draft of the second section matches our plan, but I noticed two spelling mistakes.
    IBRAHIM Mu bar gyaran rubutu zuwa ƙarshe; da farko, sai mu tabbata kowane sashi ya bi tsari da shirinmu.Let's leave the proofreading for last; first, let's make sure every section follows our plan and structure.
    AMINA Daidai. Ibrahim, sashinka na shawarwari ya yi kyau, amma ban ga sunan Malama Hadiza a ciki ba, ko da yake shirin ya ce za a ambace ta.Right. Ibrahim, your advice section is good, but I don't see Ms. Hadiza's name in it, even though the plan says she will be mentioned.
    IBRAHIM Kin gaskata; na manta in ambace ta da suna. Zan daidaita sashin da shirin kafin mu ci gaba zuwa gyaran rubutu.You're right; I forgot to mention her by name. I will align the section with the plan before we move on to proofreading.

    Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

    1. What problem does Amina identify in Zainab's history-section draft, and how does Zainab plan to fix it?
    2. What kind of problem does Salamatu report in her own draft, and what does the group decide to do about it first?
    3. What content problem does Amina find in Ibrahim's advice-section draft?
    4. What order does Ibrahim propose for checking the drafts, and why?

    Guided Practice

    Aikin fahimta — Recognition: Reread the Model Dialogue and mark each line as drafting, content feedback, plan-consistency feedback, or a note about editing order, using Sections 17.1–17.4.

    Aikin zaɓi — Selection: Given four short feedback comments, sort each into "revision" (content/organization) or "editing" (spelling/punctuation), following Section 17.4.

    Aikin kwatanci — Comparison: Read the two versions of the same paragraph below and write one or two sentences explaining what changed between the first draft and the revised version, following Section 17.2.

    Sigar don Kwatanci — Draft and Revised Paragraph

    Daftari na farko: Kakan Ibrahim ya fara halartar bikin tun yana yaro. Ya taimaka wajen shirya wurin taro kowace shekara.

    Sigar da aka gyara: Kakan Ibrahim ya fara halartar bikin tun yana yaro, kuma tun daga wannan lokacin, ya taimaka wajen shirya wurin taro kowace shekara. Saboda wannan gudunmawa, bikin ya ci gaba da girma har zuwa yau.

    Gloss: Draft: "Ibrahim's grandfather first attended the festival as a boy. He helped organize the gathering place every year." Revised: "Ibrahim's grandfather first attended the festival as a boy, and from that time on, he helped organize the gathering place every year. Because of this contribution, the festival has continued to grow to this day."

Tun daga wannan lokacin, ya taimaka wajen shirya wurin taro kowace shekara, tare da wasu mutanen ƙauyen.     Writing Workshop

    1. Karanta shirin da aka amince da shi. Reread the writing plan you completed in Chapter 16.
    2. Rubuta daftarin sashi guda. Draft one full paragraph for one section of your plan, following Section 17.1.
    3. Kwatanta daftari da shirin. Compare your draft against the plan entry it comes from and note any drift in purpose, audience, or evidence, following Section 17.2.
    4. Nemi ra’ayin abokin karatu. Exchange drafts with a partner and give each other specific, respectful feedback, following Section 17.3.
    5. Gyara abin da ya shafi tsari da abun ciki. Revise your draft's content and organization first, before checking spelling or punctuation, following Section 17.4.
    6. Yi bitar rubutu. Proofread your revised draft for spelling, punctuation, and special characters.
    7. Duba jerin bincike. Check your final version against the checklist below before moving on to Chapter 18.

    Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

    • The draft covers exactly one section from the approved plan, with no unplanned content added.
    • Every name, place, or event uses the single consistent form recorded in the plan's reference list.
    • At least one piece of feedback from a partner or reader has been addressed.
    • Content and organization were revised before spelling and punctuation were edited.
    • The paragraph has a clear opening statement, supporting detail, and a closing sentence.
    • Hausa sentences use standard instructional Hausa with accurate special characters and punctuation.
    • No grammar or vocabulary beyond what has been consolidated through Chapter 16 (plus the eight new drafting-and-editing terms) is required to understand the draft.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Feedback as a collective responsibility

    When a document is meant to represent a shared community effort, such as a cultural center's commemorative booklet, it is common for a small group to read every draft together rather than leaving each writer to revise alone; feedback in this setting is generally understood as a shared responsibility to the finished document, not as personal criticism of the individual writer.

    Regional Variation — Feedback style varies by relationship and setting

    The direct but warm feedback style modeled in this chapter's dialogue suits a small, familiar committee. In other institutions, workplaces, or more formal writing teams, feedback might be delivered more indirectly, in writing rather than aloud, or through a designated editor rather than an open group discussion, while still serving the same underlying goal of improving the shared text.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Editing spelling before revising content

    Error: Correcting spelling and punctuation in a draft before checking whether the draft still matches its plan and reads as an organized paragraph.

    Correction: Follow the order in Section 17.4: check content and organization first, then proofread for mechanics.

    Common Mistake — Adding unplanned content while drafting

    Error: Introducing a new story, example, or claim while drafting that was not assigned to that section in the Chapter 16 plan.

    Correction: Check new content against the plan, as in the Model Dialogue; either align the draft with the plan or explicitly update the plan first, following Section 17.2.

    Common Mistake — Giving vague feedback

    Error: Telling a partner only that a draft is "good" or "not good" without identifying a specific sentence, problem, or missing element.

    Correction: Name the specific sentence or element affected and suggest a concrete fix, as modeled in Section 17.3.

    Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

    1. The festival originally took place in a small field near a river.
    2. Ibrahim's grandfather helped organize the gathering place every year, from the time he first attended as a boy.
    3. The festival grew from a small local gathering into today's larger festival, which now draws visitors from different parts of the region.
    4. The sentence connecting the grandfather's early contribution to the festival's continued growth today was added to fix the abrupt ending.
    5. Sample summary: This revised section traces the festival from its small beginnings near a river to its current scale, crediting Ibrahim's grandfather's long-term contribution. It closes by framing the festival as the result of sustained community effort rather than a sudden creation.

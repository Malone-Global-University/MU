Unit III Opens — Law, Rights, and Responsibility

Chapter Overview

Chapter 11 opens Unit III by turning from Unit II’s public-information domains toward law, rights, and responsibility. Learners will discuss legal and civic vocabulary, personal and community rights, obligations, and the kind of careful, qualified claims that legal and civic discussion requires. This chapter introduces three new systems at the Introduced level: expanded TAM/aspect and negation (dependent, conditional, and prohibitive forms building on the imperfective, habitual, and negative frames consolidated in Chapter 10), prepositions and relational nouns (for expressing relationships such as “according to,” “on behalf of,” and “in accordance with”), and focus and contrast in answers (for distinguishing what is a right from what is an obligation, or what one party claims from what another party claims). These three systems develop across Chapters 11–14 and reach full consolidation and assessment at Chapter 15, the Unit III assessment.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name and briefly describe a basic personal or civic right and a basic civic obligation, using accurate vocabulary.
  2. State a condition under which a right or obligation applies, using a newly introduced dependent/conditional TAM form.
  3. Express a prohibition or a required action, using a newly introduced imperative/prohibitive TAM form appropriate to a legal or civic context.
  4. Use a small set of relational nouns and prepositions, such as those meaning “according to,” “on behalf of,” and “in accordance with,” to introduce a source, an authority, or a standard.
  5. Use a focus/contrast pattern to distinguish a right from an obligation, or one party’s claim from another’s, in a short answer.
  6. Record the gender and plural of new legal and civic nouns as part of active vocabulary learning.
  7. Discuss, carefully and without overstating certainty, a simple legal or civic situation, in speech and in writing.

Communication Scenario

Following the special radio program prepared in Chapter 10, a listener named Ibrahim calls the station with a question about his rights as a tenant after a dispute with his landlord over a repair. Sani, now hosting a call-in segment, brings in a community legal-aid volunteer, Malam Bello, to explain, in plain terms, what a tenant’s rights and a landlord’s obligations generally are, what conditions change the answer, and what Ibrahim should and should not do next. Malam Bello is careful throughout to distinguish a general legal principle from advice about Ibrahim’s specific case, and to state conditions clearly rather than giving an unqualified answer.

  1. Domain: discussing legal and civic vocabulary, rights, obligations, and careful, appropriately qualified claims in a call-in or community-advice setting.
  2. Developed skill: naming a right and an obligation, stating a condition, expressing a prohibition or requirement, introducing a source or standard with a relational noun/preposition, and contrasting a right with an obligation using a focus pattern.
  3. New grammar introduced: expanded TAM/aspect and negation (dependent/conditional and imperative/prohibitive forms, Introduced), prepositions and relational nouns (Introduced), and focus/contrast in answers (Introduced). All three develop through Chapters 12–14 and are consolidated and assessed at Chapter 15.
  4. No unscheduled grammar beyond the Introduced level for these three systems, no invented legal rule, no dialect mixture, and no productive Ajami writing is introduced in this chapter. Legal information is presented as general community knowledge, not as professional legal advice.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Careful, measured delivery in qualified claims

Legal and civic explanation often depends on a careful, measured phrase rhythm, since a qualified claim (“this is generally true, but it depends on…”) is easy to misread as an unqualified one if delivered too quickly. Pay attention to a slight pause before and after a conditional clause introduced by idan (“if”), and to level, unhurried tone on words such as haƙƙi and alhaki, which are easily confused if tone and vowel length are rushed. No new consonant or vowel-length contrast is introduced; this chapter continues to apply tone and rhythm control already built across Chapters 1–10 to a legal/civic register that requires visibly marked conditional and contrastive phrasing.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces three systems at the Introduced level, consistent with the Volume IV matrices: expanded TAM/aspect and negation, prepositions and relational nouns, and focus/contrast in answers. All three build directly on the imperfective/progressive/habitual TAM frame and negative TAM pattern consolidated in Chapter 10, and all three develop through Chapters 12–14 before formal consolidation and assessment at Chapter 15.

11.1 Conditional clauses with idan

Use idan (“if”) to introduce a condition under which a right, obligation, or rule applies. This is the first formally taught dependent/conditional TAM form in the series; treat it as an Introduced pattern to be developed further in Chapters 12–14.

  • Idan mai gida bai gyara ba, mai haya na da haƙƙin yin ƙara. If the landlord does not make the repair, the tenant has the right to file a complaint.
  • Idan an sanya hannu kan yarjejeniya, dukkan bangarorin biyu suna da alhaki. If an agreement has been signed, both parties have responsibilities.

    • 11.2 Prohibition and requirement with legal/civic verbs

    Use the prohibitive form (kada + verb) to state what someone should not do, and a simple obligation frame (dole or ya kamata + verb) to state what someone is required or expected to do. These forms are Introduced here and developed further across Unit III.

  • Kada mai gida ya fitar da mai haya ba tare da sanarwa ba. The landlord should not evict the tenant without notice.
  • Dole mai haya ya biya haya a kan lokaci, in ba haka ba yarjejeniyar na iya karyewa. The tenant must pay rent on time, otherwise the agreement may be broken.

    • 11.3 Prepositions and relational nouns: gwargwadon, bisa, madadin

    Use a small set of relational nouns and prepositions to introduce a source, standard, or authority behind a claim: bisa (“according to, based on”), gwargwadon (“in accordance with, to the extent of”), and madadin (“on behalf of, in place of”).

  • Bisa yarjejeniyar, mai gida ne ke da alhakin gyara rufin gida. According to the agreement, it is the landlord who is responsible for repairing the roof.
  • Malam Bello na magana madadin ƙungiyar bayar da shawarar shari’a. Malam Bello is speaking on behalf of the legal-aid organization.

    • 11.4 Focus and contrast: distinguishing a right from an obligation

    Use the focus particle ne/ce directly after the contrasted element to state precisely which party has a right and which has an obligation, correcting a possible misunderstanding.

  • Mai haya ne ke da haƙƙin buƙatar gyara, ba mai gida ba. It is the tenant who has the right to request the repair, not the landlord.
  • Alhakin biyan haya na mai haya ne, ba na mai gida ba. The responsibility to pay rent belongs to the tenant, not to the landlord.
    • Language Note — A general principle versus advice about one case

    Just as Chapter 9 distinguished a suspected cause from a confirmed one, legal and civic language distinguishes a general principle (“a landlord is generally responsible for structural repairs”) from advice about a specific dispute (“whether that applies to Ibrahim’s situation depends on his agreement”). Use idan-clauses (Section 11.1) to mark conditions, and avoid stating a general principle as if it automatically resolved a specific case.

    Common learner error: Do not drop the condition marked by idan when repeating a rule in a new sentence; doing so turns a conditional claim into an unqualified one. Idan mai gida bai gyara ba, mai haya na da haƙƙin yin ƙara (conditional) is not the same claim as the unqualified Mai haya na da haƙƙin yin ƙara (stated as if always true regardless of circumstance).

    Model Dialogue

    Yanayin hali: Ibrahim ya kira gidan rediyo domin tambaya kan haƙƙinsa a matsayin mai haya, kuma Sani ya gayyaci Malam Bello domin ba da shawara.

    (Setting: Ibrahim calls the radio station to ask about his rights as a tenant, and Sani invites Malam Bello to give advice.)

    IBRAHIM Sannu. Mai gidana bai gyara rufin ɗakina ba tsawon watanni biyu. Ina da haƙƙin yin wani abu?Hello. My landlord hasn't repaired the roof of my room for two months. Do I have the right to do something?
    MALAM BELLO Sannu Ibrahim. Bisa ƙa’idar gama-gari, idan mai gida bai gyara wani muhimmin lahani ba bayan an sanar da shi, mai haya na da haƙƙin yin ƙara.Hello Ibrahim. According to the general rule, if the landlord has not repaired an important defect after being notified, the tenant has the right to file a complaint.
    SANI Ban ji sarai ba. Za ka iya maimaita sharaɗin da ka ambata?I didn't hear clearly. Can you repeat the condition you mentioned?
    MALAM BELLO Tabbas. Ina cewa, sharaɗin shi ne cewa dole a fara sanar da mai gida a rubuce ko a baki, sannan a jira ɗan lokaci kafin a ce haƙƙin ƙara ya kama.Of course. I'm saying that the condition is that the landlord must first be notified, in writing or verbally, and then a reasonable time must be allowed to pass before the right to complain applies.
    IBRAHIM To, na sanar da shi ta baki makonni shida da suka wuce. Wanene ke da alhakin biyan kuɗin gyara, ni ko shi?Well, I notified him verbally six weeks ago. Who is responsible for paying for the repair, me or him?
    MALAM BELLO Gwargwadon abin da yarjejeniyarku ta ce, amma bisa ƙa’idar gama-gari, mai gida ne ke da alhakin gyaran rufi, ba mai haya ba, sai dai idan yarjejeniyar ta ce akasin haka.It depends on what your agreement says, but according to the general rule, it is the landlord who is responsible for roof repairs, not the tenant, unless the agreement says otherwise.
    IBRAHIM Kai, ban gane ba. Za ka iya sake bayyana abin da ya kamata in yi yanzu?Oh, I don't understand. Can you explain again what I should do now?
    MALAM BELLO Ina nufin cewa, ya kamata ka sanar da mai gida a rubuce yanzu, ka ajiye kwafin sakon, kuma kada ka daina biyan haya, domin hakan na iya raunana haƙƙinka. Idan bai amsa cikin makonni biyu ba, za ka iya neman shawarar ƙungiyarmu madadin kanka.I mean that you should notify the landlord in writing now, keep a copy of the message, and you should not stop paying rent, because that could weaken your right. If he does not respond within two weeks, you can seek our organization's advice on your behalf.

    Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

    1. What condition does Malam Bello state before a tenant's right to complain applies?
    2. Who does Malam Bello say is generally responsible for the roof repair, and what qualifies that answer?
    3. What two things does Malam Bello advise Ibrahim to do, and what does he advise against?
    4. What relational noun/preposition does Malam Bello use to introduce the agreement as a standard?

    Writing Workshop

    1. Karanta misalai da hankali. Reread Sections 11.1–11.4 and the Model Dialogue.
    2. Zaɓi batu. Choose a real or invented legal or civic situation (a tenancy dispute, a workplace obligation, or a community rule).
    3. Tsara sashi uku. Plan three short parts: a condition under which a right or obligation applies, a prohibition or requirement, and a focus/contrast statement distinguishing one party's right from another's obligation.
    4. Rubuta taƙaitaccen bayani. Draft an 80–120 word explanation in Boko, using the conditional, prohibitive/requirement, and focus/contrast patterns accurately.
    5. Duba jerin bincike. Check your draft against the checklist below.
    6. Rubuta siffa ta ƙarshe. Produce a clean final version suitable for submission.

    Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

    • The explanation states at least one condition clearly, using idan.
    • At least one prohibition or requirement is stated using kada or dole/ya kamata.
    • At least one focus/contrast statement clearly distinguishes a right from an obligation, or one party's claim from another's.
    • At least one relational noun or preposition (bisa, gwargwadon, or madadin) is used correctly.
    • New nouns are recorded with correct gender and plural where relevant.
    • Special characters (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ, ƴ) and apostrophe/glottal conventions are used accurately.
    • The explanation does not state a general legal principle as if it automatically applied without qualification.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Community legal-aid and dispute resolution

    Across Hausa-speaking communities, disputes such as the tenancy question in this chapter are addressed through a range of channels, including formal courts, customary or religious dispute-resolution processes, community elders, local government offices, and, increasingly, community radio call-in programs and legal-aid volunteers. Learners should not assume that one process, such as a single call-in program or a single court system, is the only or most authoritative avenue everywhere Hausa is spoken.

    Regional Variation — Legal and civic terminology

    Terms for tenancy, agreements, and dispute resolution can vary between Nigeria, Niger, and other Hausa-speaking regions, and legal systems themselves differ by country. The terms used in this chapter represent a common, broadly understandable instructional standard; learners should expect real terminology and procedures to vary by jurisdiction and should not treat this chapter as a substitute for professional legal advice.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Stating a general principle as if it applied without condition

    Error: Saying Mai haya na da haƙƙin yin ƙara as an unqualified fact, without the condition that the landlord was notified and given time to respond.

    Correction: Keep the idan-clause attached to the claim (Section 11.1), as in Idan mai gida bai gyara ba bayan an sanar da shi, mai haya na da haƙƙin yin ƙara, so the condition is not lost.

    Common Mistake — Confusing a right with an obligation

    Error: Describing the tenant's right to request a repair and the landlord's obligation to make it as if they were the same kind of claim, without distinguishing who holds which.

    Correction: Use the focus/contrast pattern (Section 11.4) to state precisely which party has the right and which has the obligation, as Malam Bello does in the Model Dialogue.

    Common Mistake — Treating community legal-aid advice as a final legal ruling

    Error: Presenting a legal-aid volunteer's general explanation as though it were a binding decision that resolves a specific dispute.

    Correction: Distinguish general community legal-aid guidance from a formal legal ruling, and note, as Malam Bello does, that the outcome depends on the specific agreement or case.

    Answer Key / Amsoshi

    Tambayoyin fahimta na tattaunawa (Dialogue comprehension)

    1. The landlord must have been notified of the defect and must have failed to repair it after a reasonable time has passed.
    2. Malam Bello says the landlord is generally responsible for roof repairs, unless the agreement states otherwise.
    3. He advises Ibrahim to notify the landlord in writing and keep a copy, and advises against stopping rent payments.
    4. He uses bisa (“according to”) and gwargwadon (“depending on, in accordance with”) to introduce the agreement as a standard.

    Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

    (1) The listener called because his landlord had not repaired an important defect. (2) The volunteer states that the landlord must have been notified and given time to respond before the right to complain applies. (3) The answer depends on what each person's specific agreement says. (4) The volunteer advises the tenant not to stop paying rent while waiting for the repair.

    Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

    1. The landlord is generally responsible for major repairs, unless the agreement states otherwise.
    2. The tenant must have notified the landlord and allowed a reasonable amount of time to pass without repair.
    3. The notice advises the tenant not to stop paying rent without seeking advice, because doing so could weaken the tenant's right.

    Sashi na B (Grammar)

    1. Sample: Idan mai gida bai gyara muhimmin lahani ba, mai haya na da haƙƙin yin ƙara. Idan an sanya hannu kan yarjejeniya, bangarorin biyu suna da alhaki.
    2. Sample: Kada mai gida ya fitar da mai haya ba tare da sanarwa ba. Dole mai haya ya sanar da mai gida a rubuce kafin ya ɗauki mataki.
    3. Sample: Mai haya ne ke da haƙƙin buƙatar gyara, ba mai gida ba. Mai gida ne ke da alhakin gyaran rufi, ba mai haya ba.
    4. Sample: Legal and civic claims are usually stated with conditions because the same general rule can apply differently depending on the specific agreement, timing, or circumstances of a case; stating an absolute rule could mislead someone whose situation does not meet the stated condition.

    Sashi na C (Sample writing)

    A unguwarmu, akwai ƙa’idar cewa idan mai gida bai gyara wani muhimmin lahani ba bayan an sanar da shi, mai haya na da haƙƙin yin ƙara. Duk da haka, kada mai haya ya daina biyan haya yayin da ake jiran gyara, domin hakan na iya raunana haƙƙinsa. Bisa yarjejeniyar kowane mutum, mai gida ne yawanci ke da alhakin gyaran manyan lahani, ba mai haya ba. Idan babu gyara bayan wani lokaci mai ma’ana, mai haya na iya neman shawarar ƙungiyar bayar da shawarar shari’a.

    Other accurate, well-organized explanations that correctly use a condition, a prohibition or requirement, and a focus/contrast statement are equally acceptable.

Rahoto da Bincike

B2–C1 Development · Unit I, Chapter 4 of 5 · Speak and Write Hausa: Advanced Functional Hausa

Chapter Overview

Chapter 4 continues Unit I by moving from the oral presentation skills of Chapter 3 into summarizing information, comparing sources, and writing a short evidence-based report. Building on the professional greetings, identity clauses, and update patterns from Chapter 1, the formal opening and requirement patterns from Chapter 2, and the signaling and example-giving language of Chapter 3, this chapter applies those same consolidated predicate, pronoun, and TAM systems to the demands of gathering information from more than one source, comparing what each source says, and organizing findings into a short written report. No new grammar paradigm is introduced. Instead, learners practice attributing information to a source, noting agreement or disagreement between sources, and drawing a brief conclusion, preparing them for the Unit I integrated assessment in Chapter 5.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify the standard parts of a short evidence-based report in Hausa.
  2. Attribute a piece of information to the source that provided it.
  3. Compare what two sources say about the same topic, noting agreement or disagreement.
  4. Summarize information from more than one source in your own words.
  5. Draw a brief, well-supported conclusion from compared information.
  6. Prepare and write a short evidence-based report on a familiar professional or institutional topic.
  7. Distinguish reporting language from the presentation-signaling language of Chapter 3.

Communication Scenario

After Amina's presentation in Chapter 3, her supervisor asks her to write a short report comparing how two sources describe employee response to the new personal-records update procedure: the official attendance log from the records office and informal feedback collected from colleagues at the staff meeting. Amina must attribute each piece of information to its source, note where the two sources agree and where they differ, and close with a brief, well-supported conclusion.

  1. Domain: summarizing information, comparing sources, and writing a short evidence-based report.
  2. Developed skill: organizing written information for a reader and recycling greetings, identity clauses, and institutional and presentation vocabulary established in Chapters 1-3.
  3. Recycled grammar in context: core predicate types, independent pronouns, and completive/future TAM forms, all Consolidated by the end of Volume III.
  4. No unscheduled TAM paradigm, dialect mixture, or productive Ajami writing is introduced in this chapter.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Tone and length in comparison vocabulary

A written report is usually read silently, but learners should still be able to read bincike, tushe, yarjejeniya, and sabani aloud with clear tone and vowel length, since these words will also be used in speaking practice this chapter. Pay particular attention to the vowel length difference between tushe ("source") and shorter, unrelated words with a similar consonant shape, and practice a steady, even rhythm when reading a comparison sentence aloud, since a report sentence is typically longer than a presentation-signaling sentence.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new grammar system. It reviews and extends the core predicate types, independent pronouns, and completive/future TAM forms consolidated across Volumes I-III, applying them to attribution, comparison, and conclusion-drawing in a short written report, consistent with the Volume IV Assessment Matrix's Chapter 4 status of Consolidated (C) for these systems.

4.1 Review: attributing information to a source

Attributing information to a source recycles basic nominal and completive-TAM predicates already consolidated, applied to a new communicative purpose.

  • A cewar tushen bayanai na ofishin rijista, kowa ya cika fom ɗin kafin ranar ƙarshe. According to the records office source, everyone completed the form before the deadline.
  • Ma'aikata da yawa sun ce sun gamsu da sabon tsarin. Many employees said they were satisfied with the new procedure.
  • Bisa ga bayanan da aka tattara, ba kowa ba ne ya cika fom ɗin da wuri. Based on the information gathered, not everyone completed the form early.

    • 4.2 Review: comparing two sources

    Comparing what two sources say recycles familiar negation and contrast patterns already consolidated, without introducing a new construction.

    Language Note — Noting agreement and disagreement between sources

    A useful pattern for comparing sources in a report is: Tushen bayanai guda biyu sun yi daidai a kan cewa kowa ya cika fom ɗin, amma sun bambanta a kan lokacin da aka cika shi. ("The two sources agree that everyone completed the form, but they differ on the time it was completed.") This recycles the familiar completive-TAM pattern joined by amma ("but"), a coordinating word already consolidated, rather than introducing any new structure.

    4.3 Review: drawing a brief conclusion

    A report typically closes with a short conclusion supported by the evidence already presented. Recall formulas such as Daga wannan bincike, za mu iya cewa... ("From this inquiry, we can say that...")

    These review points recycle familiar structures from Volumes I-III and Chapters 1-3; no new sentence pattern is introduced in this chapter.

    Model Dialogue

    Yanayin hali: Amina tana tattaunawa da shugabanta, Malama Hadiza, game da rahoton da take shiryawa a kan sabon tsarin sabunta bayanan sirri.

    (Setting: Amina is discussing with her supervisor, Malama Hadiza, the report she is preparing about the new personal-records update procedure.)

    HADIZA Amina, ka gama tattara bayanai daga ofishin rijista da kuma daga ma'aikata?Amina, have you finished gathering information from the records office and from the employees?
    AMINA I, na gama. A cewar tushen bayanai na ofishin rijista, kowa ya cika fom ɗin kafin ranar ƙarshe.Yes, I have finished. According to the records office source, everyone completed the form before the deadline.
    HADIZA To, mene ne bayanin da ma'aikata suka bayar?Well, what information did the employees give?
    AMINA Ma'aikata da yawa sun ce sun gamsu da sabon tsarin, amma wasu sun ce ba su san ranar ƙarshe da kyau ba.Many employees said they were satisfied with the new procedure, but some said they did not know the deadline well.
    HADIZA Don haka akwai sabani tsakanin tushen bayanai biyu?So there is a discrepancy between the two sources?
    AMINA I. Tushen bayanai guda biyu sun yi daidai a kan cewa kowa ya cika fom ɗin, amma sun bambanta a kan ko an sanar da kowa ranar ƙarshe.Yes. The two sources agree that everyone completed the form, but they differ on whether everyone was informed of the deadline.
    AMINA Daga wannan bincike, za mu iya cewa sabon tsarin ya yi nasara, amma sadarwa game da ranar ƙarshe na buƙatar ƙarin bayani.From this inquiry, we can say that the new procedure succeeded, but communication about the deadline needs more clarity.
    HADIZA Kammalawa ce mai kyau. Rubuta rahoton kamar yadda muka tattauna.That is a good conclusion. Write the report as we have discussed.

    Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

    1. What two sources does Amina compare for her report?
    2. Where do the two sources agree?
    3. Where do the two sources disagree?

    Guided Practice

    Aikin fahimta — Recognition: Read the Model Dialogue again and underline each attribution phrase (a cewar, ma'aikata sun ce) and the sentence that states the point of agreement and the point of disagreement.

    Aikin zaɓi — Selection: Choose the more appropriate comparison phrase (agreement or disagreement) to complete each of three short source-comparison sentences.

    Aikin cikawa — Completion: Complete three short sentences: one attributing information to a source, one comparing two sources, and one drawing a conclusion, following the patterns in Sections 4.1-4.3.

    Aikin sauyawa — Transformation: Rewrite Amina's comparison sentence using a different pair of sources and a different point of agreement or disagreement, keeping the same connecting structure.

    Writing Workshop

    1. Karanta misalai da hankali. Reread Sections 4.1-4.3 and the Model Dialogue.
    2. Tabbatar da tushen bayanai. Decide on two familiar sources of information for your report on a workplace or institutional topic.
    3. Tsara rahotonka. Plan an attribution for each source, a statement of agreement, a statement of disagreement, and a conclusion.
    4. Rubuta. Write a 60-90 word report in Hausa, in Boko, attributing information to each source and drawing a brief conclusion, following the model in the Reading Practice section.
    5. Duba jerin bincike. Check your draft against the checklist below.
    6. Rubuta siffa ta ƙarshe. Produce a clean final version suitable for submission.

    Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

    • The report attributes information clearly to each source.
    • At least one point of agreement and one point of disagreement between the sources are stated.
    • The report closes with a brief, well-supported conclusion.
    • At least one recycled TAM form is used correctly.
    • Special characters (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ, ƴ) and apostrophe/glottal conventions are used accurately in the Boko text.
    • The report is organized clearly, moving from sources to comparison to conclusion.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Reporting information carefully

    In many Hausa-speaking professional and institutional settings, a report that clearly names its sources and avoids overstating a conclusion is generally viewed as more trustworthy than one that presents a single opinion as established fact. Careful attribution, such as a cewar ("according to"), is a useful habit across workplace, civic, and media contexts, though expectations for report length and tone can vary by institution.

    Regional Variation — Reporting conventions

    Expectations for how formally a workplace report should be written, and how much detail it should include, vary across institutions, regions, and generations; some settings favor a brief bulleted summary, while others expect a fuller narrative report. The pattern shown in this chapter is a widely usable model for this course, not the only acceptable style.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Presenting information without naming its source

    Error: Stating information as if it were simply known, without identifying which source it came from.

    Correction: Use a clear attribution phrase such as a cewar or ma'aikata sun ce, as modeled in Section 4.1.

    Common Mistake — Ignoring disagreement between sources

    Error: Reporting only the information that supports one conclusion and omitting a source that disagrees.

    Correction: Note both agreement and disagreement between sources, as modeled in Section 4.2 and the Model Dialogue.

    Common Mistake — Drawing a conclusion the evidence does not support

    Error: Closing the report with a conclusion that goes beyond what the compared sources actually show.

    Correction: Base the conclusion directly on the points of agreement and disagreement already stated, using a formula such as Daga wannan bincike, za mu iya cewa...

    Sashi na C (Sample writing)

    Rahoto Kan Halartar Taro. Tushen bayanai: jerin sa hannu da kuma ra'ayin ma'aikata. A cewar jerin sa hannu, kashi tasa'in cikin ɗari na ma'aikata sun halarci taron. Amma wasu ma'aikata sun ce ba a tuna musu taron da wuri ba. Tushen bayanai guda biyu sun yi daidai a kan cewa yawancin ma'aikata sun halarta, amma sun bambanta a kan ko an isar da sanarwa cikin lokaci. Daga wannan bincike, za mu iya cewa halartar ta yi kyau, amma tsarin tunatarwa na buƙatar gyara.

    Other accurate, well-organized reports meeting the stated requirements are equally acceptable.

Jawabi da Gabatarwa

B2–C1 Development · Unit I, Chapter 3 of 5 · Speak and Write Hausa: Advanced Functional Hausa

Chapter Overview

Chapter 3 continues Unit I by moving from the written institutional register of Chapter 2 into structured oral presentation. Building on the professional greetings, identity clauses, and update patterns from Chapter 1 and the formal opening, requirement, and clarification patterns from Chapter 2, this chapter applies those same consolidated predicate, pronoun, and TAM systems to the demands of preparing, delivering, and responding to a short spoken presentation. No new grammar paradigm is introduced. Instead, learners practice organizing information for an audience, signaling structure aloud, giving examples, and handling questions, preparing them for the evidence-based reporting of Chapter 4 and the Unit I integrated assessment in Chapter 5.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify the standard parts of a short structured oral presentation in Hausa.
  2. Use signaling language to introduce a topic, move between points, and reach a conclusion.
  3. Give a concrete example to support a general statement while speaking.
  4. Prepare and deliver a short presentation on a familiar professional or institutional topic.
  5. Ask and answer a clarifying question from an audience member.
  6. Distinguish the pacing and structure of a prepared presentation from the pacing of ordinary conversation.
  7. Respond politely to a question that interrupts or challenges a point.

Communication Scenario

Amina's office has asked her to give a short presentation at the next staff meeting, explaining the new personal-records update procedure she learned about from the notice in Chapter 2. She must organize her explanation clearly, give at least one concrete example so her colleagues understand exactly what to do, and be ready to answer questions from the audience, including one colleague who is unsure whether the deadline applies to part-time staff.

  1. Domain: structured oral presentation, explanation with examples, and audience question-and-answer.
  2. Developed skill: organizing spoken information for an audience and recycling greetings, identity clauses, and institutional vocabulary established in Chapters 1-2.
  3. Recycled grammar in context: core predicate types, independent pronouns, and completive/future TAM forms, all Consolidated by the end of Volume III.
  4. No unscheduled TAM paradigm, dialect mixture, or productive Ajami writing is introduced in this chapter.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Pacing and emphasis in prepared speech

A prepared presentation is typically delivered more slowly and deliberately than casual conversation, with a brief pause at the end of each main point to let listeners absorb it. Practice saying maƙasudi, gabatarwa, and tambaya with clear tone and vowel length, and give slight extra stress to signaling words such as na farko ("firstly") and a ƙarshe ("finally") so that listeners can track the structure of your talk by ear.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new grammar system. It reviews and extends the core predicate types, independent pronouns, and completive/future TAM forms consolidated across Volumes I-III, applying them to the sequencing and signaling language of a structured oral presentation, consistent with the Volume IV Assessment Matrix's Chapter 3 status of Consolidated (C) for these systems.

3.1 Review: signaling the structure of a talk

A structured presentation typically marks its main sections with simple sequencing words, recycling basic nominal and verbal predicates already consolidated.

  • Na farko, zan yi bayani game da sabon tsari. Firstly, I will explain the new procedure.
  • Na biyu, zan ba da misali domin ku fahimta sosai. Secondly, I will give an example so that you understand well.
  • A ƙarshe, zan amsa tambayoyinku. Finally, I will answer your questions.

    • 3.2 Review: giving an example to support a point

    Introducing an example recycles completive and future TAM forms already consolidated, applied to a new communicative purpose.

    Language Note — Introducing a supporting example

    A useful pattern for introducing an example in a presentation is: Misali, idan ka cika fom ɗin kafin ranar ƙarshe, za a sabunta bayanan ka nan take. ("For example, if you complete the form before the deadline, your records will be updated right away.") This recycles the familiar conditional-like sequencing and future-TAM pattern rather than introducing any new structure.

    3.3 Review: handling a question from the audience

    When a listener asks a clarifying question, a speaker should acknowledge it politely before answering. Recall formulas such as Tambaya ce mai kyau. Bari in bayyana. ("That is a good question. Let me clarify.")

    These review points recycle familiar structures from Volumes I-III and Chapters 1-2; no new sentence pattern is introduced in this chapter.

    Model Dialogue

    Yanayin hali: Amina tana gabatar da jawabi a taron ma'aikata game da sabon tsarin sabunta bayanan sirri, kuma wani abokin aikinta, Bello, ya yi mata tambaya.

    (Setting: Amina is giving a presentation at the staff meeting about the new personal-records update procedure, and her colleague, Bello, asks her a question.)

    AMINA Sannu, ma'aikata. Na farko, zan yi bayani game da sabon tsarin sabunta bayanan sirri.Hello, colleagues. Firstly, I will explain the new personal-records update procedure.
    AMINA Misali, idan ka cika fom ɗin kafin ranar ƙarshe, za a sabunta bayanan ka nan take.For example, if you complete the form before the deadline, your records will be updated right away.
    BELLO Ina jin daɗi, amma ina da tambaya: shin wannan bukata ta shafi ma'aikata masu aikin ɗan lokaci ma?Excuse me, but I have a question: does this requirement apply to part-time employees as well?
    AMINA Tambaya ce mai kyau. Bari in bayyana: i, ta shafi kowa, ko cikakken lokaci ne ko ɗan lokaci.That is a good question. Let me clarify: yes, it applies to everyone, whether full-time or part-time.
    AMINA Na biyu, zan ba da wani misali domin ku fahimta sosai. Sannan, a ƙarshe, zan amsa sauran tambayoyinku.Secondly, I will give another example so that you understand well. Then, finally, I will answer your remaining questions.
    BELLO Na gode, Amina. Bayaninki a sarari yake.Thank you, Amina. Your explanation is clear.

    Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

    1. What is Amina presenting about?
    2. What question does Bello ask?
    3. How does Amina respond to Bello's question?

    Writing Workshop

    1. Karanta misalai da hankali. Reread Sections 3.1-3.3 and the Model Dialogue.
    2. Tabbatar da maƙasudi. Decide on a familiar workplace or institutional topic for your presentation outline.
    3. Tsara jawabinka. Plan an opening, at least two main points, one example, and a conclusion.
    4. Rubuta. Write a 60-90 word presentation outline in Hausa, in Boko, using at least two signaling phrases and one example, following the model in the Reading Practice section.
    5. Duba jerin bincike. Check your draft against the checklist below.
    6. Rubuta siffa ta ƙarshe. Produce a clean final version suitable for use as speaking notes.

    Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

    • The outline opens with a brief greeting and states the purpose of the talk.
    • At least two signaling phrases (such as na farko, na biyu, a ƙarshe) organize the outline.
    • At least one concrete example supports a main point.
    • At least one recycled TAM form is used correctly.
    • Special characters (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ, ƴ) and apostrophe/glottal conventions are used accurately in the Boko text.
    • The outline closes with a polite conclusion and readiness for questions.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Structuring a spoken presentation

    A structured Hausa presentation in a workplace or institutional setting commonly opens with a brief greeting to the audience before moving into the topic, as in Sannu, ma'aikata. Clear sequencing words help listeners follow the talk, and a speaker who pauses to invite questions at the end is generally seen as respectful of the audience's understanding, though the degree of formality can vary by workplace and setting.

    Regional Variation — Presentation style

    Expectations for presentation length, formality, and audience interaction vary across institutions, regions, and generations; some settings favor a highly structured, uninterrupted talk followed by questions, while others are comfortable with brief clarifying questions during the talk itself. The pattern shown in this chapter is a widely usable model for this course, not the only acceptable style.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Presenting without signaling structure

    Error: Moving from point to point without any sequencing language, leaving listeners unsure where one point ends and the next begins.

    Correction: Use clear signaling phrases such as na farko, na biyu, and a ƙarshe, as modeled in Section 3.1.

    Common Mistake — Giving a point without a supporting example

    Error: Stating a general point without any concrete illustration, leaving the audience uncertain how it applies in practice.

    Correction: Follow a general point with a concrete example, as modeled in Section 3.2 and the Model Dialogue.

    Common Mistake — Responding abruptly to an audience question

    Error: Answering a question without any acknowledgment, which can sound dismissive.

    Correction: Acknowledge the question politely before answering, using a formula such as Tambaya ce mai kyau. Bari in bayyana.

    Answer Key / Amsoshi

    Tambayoyin fahimta na tattaunawa (Dialogue comprehension)

    1. Amina is presenting about the new personal-records update procedure.
    2. Bello asks whether the requirement applies to part-time employees as well.
    3. Amina thanks him for the question and clarifies that it applies to everyone, whether full-time or part-time.

    Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

    (1) Every employee must complete the personal-records update form before Friday. (2) If an employee completes the form early, their records are updated right away. (3) Yes, the requirement applies to part-time employees as well as full-time employees.

    Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

    1. The presentation outline is about the new personal-records update procedure.
    2. The example states that completing the form early leads to records being updated right away.
    3. At the closing, the speaker expresses thanks and readiness to answer questions.

    Sashi na B (Chapter Review Presentation and Examples)

    1. Sample: Na farko, zan yi bayani game da sabon tsarin biyan albashi. Misali, idan an biya ka da wuri, za ka iya duba shi a manhajar banki nan take. A ƙarshe, ina shirye in amsa tambayoyinku.
    2. Sample: Tambaya ce mai kyau. Bari in bayyana. ("That is a good question. Let me clarify.")
    3. Sample: A structured oral presentation typically opens with a brief greeting and statement of purpose, moves through main points marked by sequencing words, supports at least one point with a concrete example, and closes with thanks and an invitation for questions.

    Sashi na C (Sample writing)

    Sannu, ma'aikata. Na farko, zan yi bayani game da sabon tsarin ba da hutu. Kowa dole ne ya mika bukatarsa kwana bakwai kafin ranar hutu. Misali, idan ka mika bukatarka da wuri, za a amsa maka cikin kwana biyu. Na biyu, zan bayyana yadda ake cika fom ɗin bukatar hutu. A ƙarshe, ina shirye in amsa duk wata tambaya da kuke da ita. Na gode da lokacinku.

    Other accurate, well-organized presentation outlines meeting the stated requirements are equally acceptable.

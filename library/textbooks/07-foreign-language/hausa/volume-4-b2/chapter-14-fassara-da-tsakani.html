Unit III Continues — Mediation and Translation

Chapter Overview

Chapter 14 continues Unit III by turning from Chapter 13’s treatment of regional and national variation toward mediation and translation: summarizing, paraphrasing, translating short passages, and mediating meaning between a Hausa-speaking audience and an audience that relies on English, or between two Hausa speakers whose usage differs regionally. Learners will practice restating the same information in different words and for different audiences while preserving meaning, register, and nuance. This chapter continues developing, at the Developed level, the three systems introduced at the Introduced level in Chapter 11 and developed further in Chapters 12–13: expanded TAM/aspect and negation (conditional and prohibitive/requirement forms), prepositions and relational nouns, and focus/contrast in answers. All three reach full consolidation and assessment at Chapter 15, the Unit III assessment.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Summarize the main point of a short Hausa passage or spoken message in your own words.
  2. Paraphrase a sentence or short passage while preserving its meaning and register.
  3. State a condition under which a translation or summary must be checked or clarified, extending the conditional (idan) pattern from Chapters 11–13 to mediation contexts.
  4. Express a caution about translating too literally or a recommendation to verify meaning, extending the prohibitive/requirement forms from Chapters 11–13.
  5. Use the relational nouns and prepositions from Chapters 11–13 (bisa, gwargwadon, madadin) to introduce the basis, source, or substitute used in a translation or mediated message.
  6. Use a focus/contrast pattern to distinguish what a source text says from what a mediator adds, omits, or rephrases.
  7. Mediate a short message between two audiences (for example, a Hausa speaker and an English speaker, or a native and a learner of Hausa) with reasonable accuracy and appropriate register.

Communication Scenario

Amina, who discussed regional word choice with her colleague Ibrahim in Chapter 13, is asked to help a visiting English-speaking consultant understand a short notice written in Hausa about an upcoming community meeting. Rather than translating word for word, Amina summarizes the key points, checks with Ibrahim about one phrase she is unsure how to render, and explains to the consultant, in English, what the notice says and what it does not say, careful not to add information that was not in the original.

  1. Domain: summarizing a short Hausa text, paraphrasing for clarity, checking uncertain wording with a colleague, and mediating the content accurately for someone who does not read Hausa.
  2. Developed skill: stating a condition under which a translation should be verified, cautioning against literal or careless translation, introducing the basis for a rendering with a relational noun/preposition, and distinguishing what the source text says from what the mediator infers or adds, using focus.
  3. Continued grammar development: expanded TAM/aspect and negation (conditional and prohibitive/requirement forms), prepositions and relational nouns, and focus/contrast in answers (Developed, continuing from Chapters 11–13, reaching full consolidation at Chapter 15).
  4. No unscheduled grammar beyond the Developed level for these three systems, no requirement to produce a full professional translation beyond the chapter's scope, and no implication that mediation and translation are simple word-for-word substitution.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Clear phrasing when restating a message

This chapter continues the phrase-rhythm and pacing awareness scheduled since earlier volumes, applying it explicitly to summarizing and mediating a message aloud. When restating a message for someone who did not hear the original, slow slightly and place a brief pause after the summarizing phrase (for example, a taƙaice, “in short”) before the restated content, so the listener can distinguish the summary from a direct quotation. As in Chapters 11–13, maintain measured rhythm when qualifying a claim about what a source text does or does not say. No new consonant or vowel-length contrast is introduced in this chapter.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter continues developing, at the Developed level, the three systems introduced in Chapter 11 and developed in Chapters 12–13: expanded TAM/aspect and negation, prepositions and relational nouns, and focus/contrast in answers, consistent with the Volume IV matrices. This chapter applies these systems to summarizing, paraphrasing, translating, and mediating short passages, continuing toward full consolidation and assessment at Chapter 15.

14.1 Conditional clauses with idan in mediation contexts

Continue using idan to introduce a condition under which a translation, summary, or mediated message must be checked, clarified, or handled with particular care, just as Chapters 11–13 used it for legal, cultural, and regional-variation conditions.

  • Idan ba ka tabbata da ma’anar wata kalma ba, ya kamata ka tambayi wanda ya fi sani. If you are not sure of a word's meaning, you should ask someone who knows better.
  • Idan kana taƙaita saƙo don wani da bai ji na asali ba, kula kada ka bar wani muhimmin bayani. If you are summarizing a message for someone who did not hear the original, be careful not to leave out important information.

    • 14.2 Respectful caution and recommendation about translation and mediation

    Use kada to caution against translating too literally or adding unstated information, and ya kamata or dole to recommend verification, continuing the pattern from Chapters 11–13 in a mediation context.

  • Kada ka fassara kalma-kalma idan hakan zai canja ma’anar saƙon asali. Do not translate word-for-word if doing so will change the meaning of the original message.
  • Dole ne mai tsakani ya tabbatar da ma’ana kafin ya isar da saƙo ga wanda ba ya jin Hausa. A mediator must confirm the meaning before conveying the message to someone who does not speak Hausa.

    • 14.3 Prepositions and relational nouns: introducing the basis of a rendering

    Continue using bisa, gwargwadon, and madadin from Chapters 11–13 to introduce the basis, source, or substitute wording used in a translation, summary, or mediated message.

  • Bisa abin da aka rubuta a sanarwar, taron zai fara ne da ƙarfe goma na safe. Based on what was written in the notice, the meeting will begin at ten in the morning.
  • Madadin fassara kalma ɗaya bisa ɗaya, mai fassara ya zaɓi bayyana ma’anar gaba ɗaya. Instead of translating one word at a time, the translator chose to convey the overall meaning.

    • 14.4 Focus and contrast: distinguishing the source text from the mediator's rendering

    Continue using the focus particle ne/ce to state precisely what the source text says, as distinct from what the mediator infers, adds, or leaves out.

  • Abin da sanarwar ta faɗa ke nan, ba abin da na tsammani ba ne. That is exactly what the notice says, not what I assumed.
  • Taƙaitawata ce ta ƙunshi wannan bayani, ba a asalin rubutun ba ne a sarari. It is my summary that includes this detail, not the original text explicitly.
    • Language Note — Summary versus source, and translation versus interpretation

    Just as Chapter 13 distinguished a regional label from a correctness judgment, this chapter's language distinguishes what a source text explicitly states from what a mediator infers, adds for clarity, or omits for brevity. Use the focus pattern from Section 14.4 together with phrases such as a asalin rubutun (“in the original text”) or a taƙaitawata (“in my summary”) to keep these two layers clearly separated for the audience.

    Common learner error: Do not present a paraphrase or summary as if it were a direct quotation of the source. A taƙaice, sanarwar ta ce... (“in short, the notice says...,” clearly marked as a summary) is not the same as quoting the exact original wording without indicating that it has been condensed or reworded.

    Model Dialogue

    Yanayin hali: Amina tana taimaka wa wani baƙo mai magana da Ingilishi ya fahimci sanarwar taron al’umma da aka rubuta da Hausa, kuma ta tambayi Ibrahim game da wata kalma da ba ta tabbata da ma’anarta ba.

    (Setting: Amina is helping an English-speaking visitor understand a community-meeting notice written in Hausa, and she asks Ibrahim about a word whose meaning she is not sure of.)

    AMINA Ibrahim, ina son taƙaita wannan sanarwa don baƙon nan, amma ban tabbata da ma’anar wannan kalma ba. Me take nufi a nan?Ibrahim, I want to summarize this notice for the visitor, but I'm not sure of the meaning of this word. What does it mean here?
    IBRAHIM Bisa yadda aka saba amfani da ita a irin wannan rubutu, tana nufin za a tattauna kasafin kuɗi ne, ba wai an riga an yanke shawara ba.Based on how it's typically used in this kind of text, it means the budget will be discussed, not that a decision has already been made.
    AMINA To, kada in ce wa baƙon cewa an riga an yanke shawara, saboda hakan zai canja ma’anar saƙon asali.So I shouldn't tell the visitor that a decision has already been made, because that would change the meaning of the original message.
    IBRAHIM Daidai ne. Ya kamata ka taƙaita abin da sanarwar ta faɗa, ba abin da kake tsammani ba.Exactly right. You should summarize what the notice actually says, not what you assume.
    AMINA Zan gaya wa baƙon, a taƙaice, cewa za a tattauna kasafin kuɗi da lokacin taro, ba tare da ƙarin bayani da ba a rubuta ba.I will tell the visitor, in short, that the budget and the meeting time will be discussed, without adding information that was not written.
    IBRAHIM Kyakkyawan tsari ne. Idan baƙon yana da wata tambaya bayan hakan, za mu iya bincika sanarwar tare.That's a good approach. If the visitor has another question after that, we can look at the notice together.
    AMINA Na gode. Ba na son in fassara kalma-kalma in kuma ɓata ma’ana.Thank you. I don't want to translate word-for-word and end up distorting the meaning.
    IBRAHIM Madadin fassara kalma ɗaya bisa ɗaya, mafi kyau shi ne isar da ma’ana gaba ɗaya, kamar yadda kika yi yanzu.Instead of translating one word at a time, it's better to convey the overall meaning, as you've just done.

    Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

    1. What does Amina ask Ibrahim about at the start of the conversation?
    2. According to Ibrahim, what does the word actually mean in this notice?
    3. What does Amina decide not to tell the visitor, and why?
    4. What approach does Ibrahim recommend instead of word-for-word translation?

    Writing Workshop

    1. Karanta misalai da hankali. Reread Sections 14.1–14.4 and the Model Dialogue.
    2. Zaɓi rubutu. Choose a real or invented short Hausa notice, message, or passage (60–100 words) to summarize or mediate.
    3. Tsara sashi uku. Plan three short parts: a condition under which the rendering should be verified, a respectful caution or recommendation about literal translation, and a focus/contrast statement distinguishing the source text from your summary.
    4. Rubuta taƙaitaccen bayani. Draft an 80–120 word summary or mediated explanation in Boko, using the conditional, prohibitive/requirement, and focus/contrast patterns accurately.
    5. Duba jerin bincike. Check your draft against the checklist below.
    6. Rubuta siffa ta ƙarshe. Produce a clean final version suitable for submission.

    Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

    • The summary states at least one condition clearly, using idan.
    • At least one caution or recommendation about translation is stated using kada or dole/ya kamata.
    • At least one focus/contrast statement clearly distinguishes the source text from the summary or mediated rendering.
    • At least one relational noun or preposition (bisa, gwargwadon, or madadin) is used correctly to introduce the basis of the rendering.
    • New nouns are recorded with correct gender and plural where relevant.
    • Special characters (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ, ƴ) and apostrophe/glottal conventions are used accurately.
    • The summary does not add information absent from the source text or omit information necessary for the audience's understanding.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Mediation across languages and communities

    Many Hausa speakers regularly mediate between Hausa and English, French, Arabic, or another regional language, whether formally as translators and interpreters or informally for family members, colleagues, or visitors. Careful mediation preserves not only literal meaning but also tone, intention, and appropriate register, and requires humility about what one is certain of versus uncertain of, as Amina models by checking with Ibrahim rather than guessing.

    Regional Variation — Mediating across Hausa varieties

    Mediation is sometimes needed between two Hausa speakers from different regions or countries, not only between Hausa and another language, as Chapter 13 illustrated. The same care about verifying meaning, avoiding assumptions, and distinguishing source from restatement applies whether the mediation crosses languages or crosses varieties of Hausa.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Translating word-for-word

    Error: Rendering each Hausa word with its most common English equivalent regardless of context, producing a translation that distorts or obscures the intended meaning.

    Correction: Use madadin fassara kalma ɗaya bisa ɗaya ("instead of translating word by word," Section 14.3) to signal a shift toward conveying overall meaning, as Ibrahim recommends in the Model Dialogue.

    Common Mistake — Adding unstated information to a summary

    Error: Including an assumption, inference, or personal opinion in a summary as though it were part of the original source text.

    Correction: Use the focus/contrast pattern (Section 14.4) to separate what the source states from what the mediator infers, as in Abin da sanarwar ta faɗa ke nan, ba abin da na tsammani ba ne.

    Common Mistake — Guessing at an uncertain meaning instead of verifying it

    Error: Proceeding with a translation or summary despite being unsure of a key word's meaning, risking a distorted message.

    Correction: Use a conditional clause (Section 14.1) together with ya kamata ka tambayi wanda ya fi sani ("you should ask someone who knows better"), as Amina does with Ibrahim in the Model Dialogue.

    Answer Key / Amsoshi

    Tambayoyin fahimta na tattaunawa (Dialogue comprehension)

    1. Amina asks Ibrahim about the meaning of a word in the notice that she is unsure how to render.
    2. Ibrahim explains that the word means the budget will be discussed, not that a decision has already been made.
    3. Amina decides not to tell the visitor that a decision has already been made, because that would change the meaning of the original message.
    4. Ibrahim recommends conveying the overall meaning rather than translating word-for-word.

    Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

    (1) A mediator should not assume every word has only one fixed translation. (2) A mediator must confirm the meaning before conveying the message to someone who does not speak Hausa. (3) A mediator must keep what the text explicitly says separate from their own assumptions. (4) A good summary should give the listener full understanding without adding or removing information.

    Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

    1. The notice addresses the annual budget and road-maintenance plans.
    2. The summary cautions against adding a mention of a leadership election, since the notice does not mention this topic.
    3. The summary uses Bisa abin da sanarwar ta faɗa ("based on what the notice says") to mark what comes directly from the source.

    Sashi na B (Grammar)

    1. Sample: Idan ba ka tabbata da ma’anar kalma ba, ya kamata ka tambaya. Idan kana taƙaita saƙo, kula kada ka bar muhimman bayani.
    2. Sample: Kada ka fassara kalma-kalma idan hakan zai ɓata ma’ana. Dole ne ka tabbatar da ma’ana kafin ka isar da saƙo.

    4. Sample: Word-for-word translation can distort meaning because words often carry different senses depending on context, and a literal rendering may ignore idiomatic or cultural meaning. A good translator conveys the overall message rather than substituting each word mechanically.

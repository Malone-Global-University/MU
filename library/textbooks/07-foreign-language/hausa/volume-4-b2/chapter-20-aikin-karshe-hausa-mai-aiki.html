Chapter Overview

Chapter 20 is the final chapter of Volume IV and does not introduce new grammar, vocabulary domains, or discussion moves. Instead, it brings together the professional communication of Chapters 1–5, the media, civic, economic, and public-information work of Chapters 6–10, the legal, cultural, regional, and mediation work of Chapters 11–15, and the narrative, analytical, debate, and proposal work of Chapters 16–19 into a single assessed capstone project. Amina, Ibrahim, and Salamatu's scheduling-consultation trial — discussed in the Chapter 18 staff meeting and presented as a proposal in Chapter 19 — now reaches its final stage: reporting on the completed trial, addressing questions and concerns from across the office and the community, and recommending what should happen next. Learners complete a parallel project of their own choosing, demonstrating control of the full range of systems scheduled across Volume IV.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Report on the outcome of a completed plan or trial, combining narrative sequencing (Chapter 16) with analytical summary (Chapter 17).
  2. Support a recommendation with reasons, evidence, and a qualified acknowledgment of limitations, drawing on the debate language of Chapter 18.
  3. Restate a proposal's original purpose and plan for an audience that has followed the project from an earlier chapter, without assuming unstated background.
  4. Respond to a range of questions and challenges across professional, civic, media, and analytical registers.
  5. Produce a single connected written project (a report with a recommendation) and present it orally with a closing statement of next steps.
  6. Self-assess overall command of the vocabulary, grammar, pronunciation, and register scheduled across Volume IV.

Communication Scenario

Three months have passed since the Chapter 19 presentation. The scheduling-consultation trial is complete. The office manager asks Amina, Ibrahim, and Salamatu to report on the results to the same staff-and-community gathering, recommend whether the practice should continue, and answer questions — including one from a colleague who was skeptical from the start. Because the audience includes people who missed earlier meetings, the three colleagues must again restate the original purpose and plan before reporting the outcome, exactly as the Chapter 19 Common Mistake note advised.

  1. Domain: a final project report and recommendation, combining narrative outcome, evidence-based reasoning, and a closing recommendation with next steps.
  2. Target skill: integrating reporting, analysis, qualified argument, and proposal-closing language from across the volume into one connected project.
  3. Grammar status: every system listed in the Volume IV scope reaches Assessed status in this chapter; no new grammar, vocabulary domain, or discussion move is introduced.
  4. No unscheduled grammar beyond the Chapter 19 level, and no project stage beyond restating background, reporting outcome, supporting a recommendation, answering questions, and closing with next steps.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Consolidated presentation pacing across a full project

No new pronunciation feature is introduced in this final chapter. Learners instead apply, across a longer connected presentation, the phrase-level pacing, turn-boundary pausing, and rising-intonation question invitation (tambaya?) practiced in Chapters 16–19, along with the tone, vowel-length, and special-consonant control (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ, ƴ, ts, sh) scheduled as Assessed across the whole volume. Presenters should mark the boundary between reporting the outcome and stating the recommendation with the same pause-and-pitch-drop pattern used between proposal stages in Chapter 19.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new grammar. It consolidates, at Assessed level, the full range of systems scheduled across Volume IV: predicate types, noun gender/plural behavior, negation, question formation, coordination and subordination, pronoun and TAM/aspect systems, focus structures, prepositions and relational nouns, and the discourse-level connector, paragraph, discussion, and proposal-staging language built in Chapters 16–19.

20.1 Restating background for a returning audience

Because some listeners attended the Chapter 19 presentation and some did not, the report opens by briefly restating the original purpose before moving to the outcome, using the same manufar wannan shawarwari ita ce pattern from Chapter 19, now in the past-oriented frame of a completed project.

  • Kamar yadda muka bayyana a taron da ya gabata, manufar shawarwarinmu ita ce mu gwada tuntuɓar ma’aikata kan jadawali na tsawon watanni uku. As we explained at the previous gathering, the purpose of our proposal was to try consulting employees about the schedule for three months.

    • 20.2 Reporting the outcome

    The report states what was found, using bayan gwajin, mun gano cewa ("after the trial, we found that"), followed by connected clauses recycling the Chapter 16 sequencing and Chapter 17 support-with-examples pattern.

  • Bayan gwajin, mun gano cewa yawancin ma’aikata sun gwammace sabon jadawalin, kuma rashin fahimta kan lokutan aiki ya ragu sosai. After the trial, we found that most employees preferred the new schedule, and misunderstanding about work hours decreased significantly.

    • 20.3 Supporting a recommendation

    A recommendation is stated with shawarwarinmu ita ce ("our recommendation is"), supported by a reason, and, where appropriate, qualified with the Chapter 18 conceding language so that a limitation is acknowledged rather than hidden.

  • Shawarwarinmu ita ce mu ci gaba da tsarin, domin sakamakon ya tabbatacce ne kuma yawancin ma’aikata sun goyi baya. Duk da haka, ba koyaushe ba ne kowa ya gamsu gaba ɗaya, don haka za mu ci gaba da sauraron ra’ayoyi. Our recommendation is to continue the arrangement, because the result is confirmed and most employees support it. Even so, that does not mean everyone is entirely satisfied, so we will keep listening to opinions.

    • 20.4 Answering a skeptical question

    The final report anticipates and answers a genuinely skeptical question, recycling the challenge-and-response pattern from Chapter 18 and the audience-question pattern from Chapter 19, now applied to a real rather than hypothetical objection.

  • Ina ganin akwai matsala a nan: ashe ba a fara wannan tsari ba tare da tabbacin zai yi nasara ba? — Kina da gaskiya cewa ba mu da cikakken tabbaci a farko, amma sakamakon gwajin ya nuna mana hanya madaidaiciya. I see a problem here: wasn't this arrangement started without certainty it would succeed? — You are right that we did not have full certainty at the start, but the trial's results have shown us the right path.

    • 20.5 Closing the final project

    The project closes with next steps and a courtesy formula, combining the Chapter 19 closing pattern with the greetings/politeness system from Chapters 1–2.

  • A taƙaice dai, matakan gaba sun haɗa da tabbatar da sabon tsarin a hukumance da sanar da dukkan ma’aikata sakamakon. Na gode kan halartar ku. In short, the next steps include formally confirming the new arrangement and informing all employees of the result. Thank you for attending.
    • Language Note — From plan to outcome

    Chapter 19 organized purpose, plan, anticipated challenge, and next steps for a proposal that had not yet been tried. Chapter 20 reorganizes the same stages around a project that has already happened: purpose is restated rather than newly announced, the plan becomes a reported outcome, the anticipated challenge becomes a real question that must be answered honestly, and the next steps close a finished cycle rather than opening one. No new connector, discussion move, or sentence pattern is required to make this shift; only the time reference and the content change.

    Common learner error: Do not report only positive results and omit the acknowledged limitation. A C1-level report that hides a known weakness is less credible, not more persuasive; use the Chapter 18 qualifying/conceding language to name a limitation honestly, as in duk da haka, ba koyaushe ba ne kowa ya gamsu gaba ɗaya.

    Yanayin hali: Amina, Ibrahim, da Salamatu suna gabatar da rahoto na ƙarshe kan gwajin tuntuɓar jadawali ga taron ma’aikata da al’umma.

    IBRAHIM Bayan gwajin, mun gano cewa yawancin ma’aikata sun gwammace sabon jadawalin, kuma rashin fahimta kan lokutan aiki ya ragu sosai.After the trial, we found that most employees preferred the new schedule, and misunderstanding about work hours decreased significantly.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Closing a project with accountability

    Returning to a staff-and-community audience with a final report, rather than simply letting a trial arrangement continue unannounced, is a way of showing accountability to the people affected by a decision. Acknowledging a real limitation honestly, as in duk da haka, ba koyaushe ba ne kowa ya gamsu gaba ɗaya, is generally received as a sign of trustworthiness in Hausa professional and community settings, rather than as a weakness in the presentation.

    Regional Variation — Closing courtesy formulas

    The closing courtesy formula na gode kan halartar ku ("thank you for attending") is broadly understood across Hausa-speaking regions, but some settings extend the closing with additional blessings or respect-marked address for elders and officials present. Learners should adapt the length and formality of the closing to the specific gathering, as introduced in the Chapter 13 discussion of regional and register variation.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Reporting only success

    Error: Presenting a final report that lists only positive outcomes and gives no acknowledged limitation, making the report sound unrealistic.

    Correction: Include at least one honest qualification using Chapter 18 conceding language, as in duk da haka, ba koyaushe ba ne…

    Common Mistake — Skipping the restated background

    Error: Moving directly to the outcome without restating the original purpose, leaving new listeners unable to follow the report.

    Correction: Open with a brief restatement, as in Kamar yadda muka bayyana a taron da ya gabata, manufar shawarwarinmu ita ce…

    Common Mistake — Avoiding a skeptical question

    Error: Answering only friendly, easy questions and ignoring or deflecting a genuinely skeptical one.

    Correction: Acknowledge the skeptical point directly before responding, as in Kina da gaskiya cewa ba mu da cikakken tabbaci a farko, amma…

    Sample closing next-steps statement: A taƙaice dai, matakan gaba sun haɗa da naɗa ƙarin shugabannin sashi a wannan wata, sannan fara tsarin lada watan gobe. Na gode kan halartar ku.

    Other accurate, well-organized final reports that restate purpose, report a specific outcome, support a recommendation with an honest qualification, respond to a genuine question, and close with concrete next steps are equally acceptable.

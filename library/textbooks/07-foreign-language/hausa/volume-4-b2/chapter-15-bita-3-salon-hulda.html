Unit III Assessment — Integrating Chapters 11–14

Chapter Overview

Chapter 15 is the Unit III integrated assessment. It does not introduce new grammar. Instead, it brings together the four communicative domains of Unit III — law, rights, and responsibility (Chapter 11); religion, culture, and respectful register (Chapter 12); regional Hausa and variation (Chapter 13); and mediation and translation (Chapter 14) — into a single set of listening, speaking, reading, and writing tasks. This chapter also formally consolidates and assesses the three systems that have run through the unit: the conditional (idan) and prohibitive/requirement (kada; dole/ya kamata) TAM forms, prepositions and relational nouns (bisa, gwargwadon, madadin, and related forms), and focus/contrast in answers (ne/ce). Per the Volume IV matrices, all three systems move from Developed to Consolidated and Assessed status at this chapter.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use conditional (idan) clauses accurately and independently across legal, cultural, regional, and mediation contexts.
  2. Use kada and dole/ya kamata to caution against and recommend courses of action across the same four contexts.
  3. Use prepositions and relational nouns (bisa, gwargwadon, madadin, and related forms) to introduce a basis, standard, or substitute in formal and semi-formal discussion.
  4. Use the focus particle ne/ce to distinguish, contrast, and correct information precisely.
  5. Discuss a right, obligation, or rule (Chapter 11), a culturally or religiously marked practice (Chapter 12), a regional variation (Chapter 13), and a mediated or translated message (Chapter 14) within a single connected task.
  6. Complete an integrated listening, speaking, reading, and writing performance that draws on all four chapters without introducing unscheduled grammar.

Communication Scenario

Amina and Ibrahim, whose workplace conversations have run through Chapters 11–14, are asked to prepare a short joint briefing for new colleagues about how their office handles matters that touch on rights and responsibilities, cultural and religious sensitivity, regional differences among staff and clients, and the mediation of documents and messages between Hausa and English. Rather than treating these as four separate topics, they must show how the same habits of care — stating conditions clearly, cautioning against careless assumptions, naming the basis for a decision, and distinguishing what a source says from what a person infers — apply across all four situations.

  1. Domain: an integrated workplace briefing that touches on rights/responsibility, respectful cultural/religious register, regional variation among colleagues and clients, and mediation/translation of documents and messages.
  2. Assessed skill: accurate, independent use of conditional clauses, cautions/recommendations, prepositions/relational nouns, and focus/contrast across all four Unit III domains.
  3. Grammar status: the conditional and prohibitive/requirement TAM forms, prepositions and relational nouns, and focus/contrast in answers reach full Consolidated and Assessed status in this chapter, per the Volume IV matrices.
  4. No unscheduled grammar beyond Consolidated status for these three systems, and no new topic introduced that was not already scheduled in Chapters 11–14.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Consolidated phrase rhythm across registers

This chapter assesses, rather than introduces, phrase-rhythm and pacing control. Learners should be able to shift pacing appropriately between a careful legal or civic explanation (Chapter 11), a respectful cultural or religious reference (Chapter 12), a comment on regional pronunciation (Chapter 13), and a mediated summary (Chapter 14), without collapsing all four into one flat delivery. No new consonant or vowel-length contrast is introduced in this chapter.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter formally consolidates and assesses the three systems developed across Chapters 11–14: expanded TAM/aspect and negation (conditional and prohibitive/requirement forms), prepositions and relational nouns, and focus/contrast in answers. No new grammatical forms are introduced. The sections below review each system across all four Unit III domains and provide consolidated practice.

15.1 Conditional clauses with idan across Unit III domains

Review the conditional pattern as it has applied to rights and obligations (Ch. 11), respectful cultural and religious reference (Ch. 12), regional variation (Ch. 13), and mediation (Ch. 14).

  • Idan wani ba ya sane da haƙƙinsa, ya kamata ya tuntuɓi mai ba da shawara kan shari’a. If someone is not aware of their right, they should consult a legal advisor.
  • Idan baƙo ba ya tabbata ko wata magana tana da alaƙa da addini, sai ya tambaya cikin ladabi. If a visitor is not sure whether a remark is religiously connected, they should ask politely.
  • Idan wata kalma ta bambanta daga yanki zuwa yanki, kada a ɗauka cewa fom ɗaya kawai ne daidai. If a word differs from region to region, one should not assume only one form is correct.
  • Idan mai tsakani bai tabbata da ma’anar kalma ba, sai ya nemi tabbaci kafin ya isar da saƙo. If a mediator is not sure of a word's meaning, they should seek confirmation before conveying the message.

    • 15.2 Respectful caution and recommendation across Unit III domains

    Review kada and dole/ya kamata as they have applied across the same four domains.

  • Kada ma’aikaci ya ɗauka cewa duk abokan aikinsa suna da irin wannan haƙƙi, sai an bincika ƙa’idar hukuma. An employee should not assume all colleagues have the same right, until the official rule has been checked.
  • Dole ne a nuna girmamawa ga al’adar wani ko da ba ta yi daidai da tawa ba. One must show respect for someone else's custom even if it is not the same as one's own.
  • Kada a ce wani yanki ne kawai ke magana da Hausa “daidai,” domin dukkan bambance-bambance suna da inganci. One should not say only one region speaks Hausa "correctly," since all varieties have validity.
  • Dole ne mai tsakani ya raba abin da rubutun ya faɗa a sarari daga abin da shi kansa yake tsammani. A mediator must keep what the text explicitly says separate from their own assumptions.

    • 15.3 Prepositions and relational nouns across Unit III domains

    Review bisa, gwargwadon, and madadin as they have introduced a basis, standard, or substitute across law, culture, region, and mediation.

  • Bisa dokar da ke akwai, kowane ma’aikaci na da haƙƙin hutu na shekara. Based on the existing law, every employee has a right to annual leave.
  • Gwargwadon al’adar iyali, ana iya gaisawa daban-daban a lokutan biki. Depending on a family's custom, greetings may differ on festival occasions.
  • Madadin ɗauka cewa yanki ɗaya ne ma’auni na daidai, ya fi kyau a fahimci kowace magana bisa yanayinta. Instead of assuming one region is the standard of correctness, it is better to understand each form of speech on its own terms.

    • 15.4 Focus and contrast across Unit III domains

    Review the focus particle ne/ce as it has distinguished a rule from a practice, an observation from a judgment, and a source text from a mediator's rendering.

  • Doka ce ta bayyana haƙƙin, ba al’adar ofis kawai ba ce. It is the law that specifies the right, not merely office custom.
  • Bambancin lafazi ne kawai, ba kuskure ba ne. It is only a pronunciation difference, not an error.
    • Language Note — One set of habits, four domains

    Chapters 11–14 taught four different topics, but they share one underlying discipline: state the condition, caution against a careless assumption, name the basis for a claim, and keep separate what is established from what is inferred. This chapter assesses whether learners can apply that shared discipline flexibly, moving between a legal question, a cultural or religious reference, a regional comparison, and a mediated message within the same conversation or text.

    Common learner error: Do not use only one of the four systems (for example, only idan) throughout a task while avoiding the others. The assessed performance requires evidence of all four: a condition, a caution or recommendation, a preposition/relational noun, and a focus/contrast statement.

    Model Dialogue

    Yanayin hali: Amina da Ibrahim suna shirya taƙaitaccen jawabi ga sababbin ma’aikata game da yadda ofishinsu ke kula da al’amuran haƙƙi, girmamawa ga al’ada da addini, bambancin yanki, da fassara/tsakani tsakanin harsuna.

    (Setting: Amina and Ibrahim are preparing a short briefing for new employees about how their office handles matters of rights, respect for culture and religion, regional variation, and mediation/translation between languages.)

    IBRAHIM Amina, don jawabinmu, ya kamata mu fara da haƙƙin ma’aikata. Bisa dokar ofis, kowa na da haƙƙin sanin dalilin duk wata shawara da ta shafe shi.Amina, for our briefing, we should start with employee rights. Based on office policy, everyone has the right to know the reason for any decision that affects them.
    AMINA Daidai ne. Kuma idan wani sabon ma’aikaci bai tabbata da wannan haƙƙin ba, ya kamata ya tambayi ɓangaren albarkatun ɗan adam.Right. And if a new employee is not sure of this right, they should ask the human resources department.
    IBRAHIM Sannan, game da girmamawa ga al’ada da addini, kada mu ce akwai hanya ɗaya kawai ta nuna girmamawa, domin gwargwadon iyali da yanki, hanyoyin sun bambanta.Then, about respect for culture and religion, we shouldn't say there's only one way to show respect, since depending on family and region, the ways differ.
    AMINA Gaskiya ne. Bambancin al’ada ne kawai, ba wai wani ba daidai ba ne. Kamar yadda muka tattauna a Babi na 13, bambancin yanki ma haka yake.True. It's only a cultural difference, not that one is incorrect. As we discussed in Chapter 13, regional variation is the same.
    IBRAHIM To, sai mu ƙara wani sashi game da fassara. Idan wani takarda ya zo daga Ingilishi zuwa Hausa, dole ne mai tsakani ya tabbatar da ma’ana kafin isar da saƙo.Then let's add a section about translation. If a document comes from English to Hausa, the mediator must confirm the meaning before conveying the message.
    AMINA Kuma madadin fassara kalma ɗaya bisa ɗaya, ya kamata a bayyana ma’anar gaba ɗaya, kamar yadda muka koya a Babi na 14.And instead of translating word by word, one should convey the overall meaning, as we learned in Chapter 14.
    IBRAHIM Kyakkyawan tsari ne. Jawabinmu zai nuna cewa dukkan waɗannan al’amura suna buƙatar irin wannan hankali: bayyana sharaɗi, gargaɗi kan zato, da nuna tushen kowace magana.That's a good plan. Our briefing will show that all these matters require the same care: stating a condition, cautioning against assumption, and showing the basis for each statement.
    AMINA Abin da muke shiryawa jawabi ne game da hanyar aiki, ba jerin dokoki masu wuya ba ne.What we are preparing is a briefing about a way of working, not a list of difficult rules.

    Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

    1. What right does Ibrahim say every employee has, and on what basis?
    2. What point do Amina and Ibrahim agree on regarding ways of showing respect?
    3. What must a mediator do before conveying a translated message, according to Ibrahim?
    4. What single underlying habit do Amina and Ibrahim say connects all four topics of their briefing?

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — One habit of mind across many domains

    Across Chapters 11–14, the same underlying habit has appeared in different settings: stating conditions clearly, cautioning against careless assumptions, naming the basis for a claim, and keeping observation separate from judgment. This habit supports respectful, accurate communication whether the topic is a legal right, a religious or cultural practice, a regional variety of Hausa, or a translated message. Recognizing this shared discipline helps learners transfer what they have learned to new topics not explicitly covered in the course.

    Regional Variation — Consolidated awareness

    As in Chapter 13, regional and national variation in Hausa remains a matter of description, not correctness. This chapter's tasks assess whether learners can discuss such variation respectfully and accurately, using the same conditional, cautionary, prepositional, and focus/contrast tools used for other Unit III topics.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Treating one Unit III domain as more important than the others

    Error: Producing a task that addresses only one of the four Unit III topics (for example, only mediation) while ignoring the others, even though the assessed task requires integration.

    Correction: Address all four topics — rights/responsibility, culture/religion, region, and mediation — at least briefly, using the shared grammar tools from Sections 15.1–15.4.

    Common Mistake — Presenting a custom, region, or rule as universal

    Error: Stating that a religious practice, regional form, or workplace custom applies to everyone without qualification.

    Correction: Use gwargwadon or a conditional clause to show that the practice may vary, as in Gwargwadon bukatar kowane ma’aikaci, ofishi na iya samar da sauƙi na musamman.

    Common Mistake — Omitting the focus/contrast statement

    Error: Making a claim (about a rule, a custom, a regional form, or a translated passage) without distinguishing it clearly from a related but different idea, leaving the listener or reader uncertain what is established fact.

    Correction: Add a focus/contrast sentence such as Bambancin al’ada ne kawai, ba wai wani ba daidai ba ne to make the distinction explicit.

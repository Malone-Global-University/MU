B2–C1 Development · Chapter 8 of 20, Unit II Chapter 3 of 5 · Speak and Write Hausa: Advanced Functional Hausa

Chapter Overview

Chapter 8 continues Unit II by moving from civic and public life toward economics and business communication. Learners will discuss markets, trade, finance, business plans, and risk, while continuing to develop the imperfective/progressive/habitual TAM frame and the negative TAM pattern opened in Chapter 6 and continued in Chapter 7. Both systems remain at the Introduced/Developed level in this chapter and will continue developing through Chapter 9 before consolidating fully at Chapter 10, the Unit II assessment. Noun gender and plural tracking continues for new business and finance vocabulary, and market/service repair formulas recur here in the context of a small-business discussion about risk and planning.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe current business or market activity using the imperfective TAM frame to talk about what a business is doing right now.
  2. Describe recurring economic patterns, such as market days or seasonal price changes, using the habitual TAM frame.
  3. State what a business plan does not yet guarantee, or what a risk has not yet been resolved, using the negative TAM pattern.
  4. Record the gender and plural form of new economic and business-related nouns as part of active vocabulary learning.
  5. Ask a business partner or trader for clarification or repetition, recycling market/service repair formulas in a finance context.
  6. Discuss, in simple terms, a small business plan or a financial risk, in speech and in writing.

Communication Scenario

Sani, who discussed the radio report with Amina in Chapter 6, is considering opening a small shop with his cousin, Bilkisu. Bilkisu explains what she is currently doing to prepare, what tends to happen every year around market season, and what the plan does not yet cover, such as a loan that has not been approved. Sani asks for clarification twice, using the same repair formulas from earlier chapters.

  1. Domain: discussing a small business plan, market activity, or financial risk with a partner, family member, or colleague.
  2. Developed skill: describing ongoing business preparation and recurring market patterns, qualifying what a plan does not yet cover, and requesting repetition from a business partner.
  3. Continued grammar development: imperfective/progressive/habitual TAM frames and negative TAM patterns (Introduced/Developed, continuing from Chapters 6–7), and gender/plural tracking for new nouns (Introduced/Developed).
  4. No unscheduled focus construction, prepositional system, dialect mixture, or productive Ajami writing is introduced in this chapter.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Numbers and figures in fluent speech

Business and financial conversation frequently includes numbers, prices, and quantities embedded in fast, connected speech. Pay attention to tone and vowel length in number words as they combine with nouns for money and goods, and to the phrase-final tone pattern that signals the end of a stated amount before the speaker continues. No new consonant or vowel-length contrast is introduced; this chapter continues to apply tone and rhythm control already built across Chapters 1–7 to a business register that includes numerical detail.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter continues developing the imperfective/progressive/habitual TAM frame and the negative TAM pattern introduced in Chapter 6 and continued in Chapter 7, still at the Introduced/Developed level, consistent with the Volume IV Verb/TAM/Aspect Matrix. This chapter adds explicit practice with the habitual meaning for recurring market and seasonal patterns. Both systems continue through Chapter 9 and are formally assessed in Chapter 10.

8.1 Imperfective and habitual meaning in business contexts

Use the imperfective TAM frame to describe a business action currently underway, and the habitual frame to describe something that recurs regularly, such as a market day or a seasonal change in prices.

  • Bilkisu na shirya takardun bashi domin buɗe kanti. Bilkisu is preparing loan documents in order to open the shop.
  • Farashin kayan lambu na canzawa kowace shekara a lokacin damina. The price of vegetables changes every year during the rainy season.

    • 8.2 Negative TAM patterns with business claims

    Use the negative TAM pattern to state that a business plan does not yet cover something, or that a loan or approval has not yet been finalized.

  • Bankin bai amince da bashin ba tukuna. The bank has not yet approved the loan.
  • Shirin bai game da hayar wurin kanti ba, saboda har yanzu ba mu san farashi ba.The plan does not cover the shop's rent yet, because we still do not know the price.
    • Language Note — A profit expected versus a profit confirmed

    Just as Chapter 7 distinguished a stated right from a currently available service, business language distinguishes a hoped-for or planned profit (riba) from a profit that has actually been confirmed. Use the imperfective TAM frame to describe what a business is currently doing to try to earn a profit, and the negative TAM pattern to state clearly that a result, such as loan approval or confirmed earnings, has not yet occurred. Do not present an expected outcome as if it were already achieved.

    Common learner error: Do not use the completive/perfective TAM (introduced in Volume III and consolidated through Chapter 5) to describe a business plan that is still in progress or only partially arranged. Bilkisu ta buɗe kanti ("Bilkisu opened the shop," completed) is not the same claim as Bilkisu na shirya buɗe kanti ("Bilkisu is preparing to open the shop," ongoing).

    Model Dialogue

    Yanayin hali: Sani ya je gidan Bilkisu domin tattaunawa game da shirin buɗe kantin sayar da kaya tare.

    (Setting: Sani visits Bilkisu's house to discuss the plan to open a shop together.)

    SANI Sannu, Bilkisu. Yaya shirin kantin yake tafiya?Hello, Bilkisu. How is the shop plan going?
    BILKISU To. Ina shirya takardun bashi domin mu sami kuɗin fara kasuwanci.Well. I'm preparing loan documents so we can get money to start the business.
    SANI Ban ji sarai ba. Za ki iya maimaita wancan sashe?I didn't hear clearly. Can you repeat that part?
    BILKISU Tabbas. Ina cewa, ina shirya takardun bashi a banki, domin mu sami kuɗin da za mu fara kasuwanci da su.Of course. I said, I'm preparing loan documents at the bank, so we can get the money to start the business with.
    SANI To, shin bankin ya amince da bashin?Well, has the bank approved the loan?
    BILKISU Bankin bai amince da bashin ba tukuna, amma suna nazarin takardunmu yanzu.The bank has not yet approved the loan, but they are reviewing our documents now.
    SANI Kai, ban gane ba. Za ki iya sake bayyana wannan?Oh, I don't understand. Can you explain that again?
    BILKISU Ina nufin cewa, har yanzu ba mu san ko za su ba mu bashin ba, amma yakamata mu san amsa nan da wata ɗaya. Idan sun amince, za mu iya hayar wurin kanti nan take.I mean that we still don't know whether they will give us the loan, but we should know the answer within a month. If they approve, we can rent the shop location right away.

    Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

    1. What is Bilkisu currently doing to prepare for the business?
    2. What has the bank not yet done, according to Bilkisu?
    3. What is the bank currently doing with their documents?
    4. What will Sani and Bilkisu be able to do if the loan is approved?

    Writing Workshop

    1. Karanta misalai da hankali. Reread Sections 8.1–8.2 and the Model Dialogue.
    2. Zaɓi kasuwanci. Choose a real or invented small business (shop, food stall, service, or trade).
    3. Tsara sashi uku. Plan three short parts: what you are currently doing to prepare, a recurring market or seasonal pattern relevant to the business, and one detail the plan does not yet cover.
    4. Rubuta taƙaitaccen bayani. Draft an 80–120 word explanation in Boko, using imperfective, habitual, and negative TAM frames accurately.
    5. Duba jerin bincike. Check your draft against the checklist below.
    6. Rubuta siffa ta ƙarshe. Produce a clean final version suitable for submission.

    Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

    • The explanation clearly states what you are currently doing to prepare the business.
    • At least one recurring or habitual detail uses the habitual TAM frame correctly.
    • At least one unconfirmed or not-yet-provided detail uses the negative TAM pattern correctly.
    • New nouns are recorded with correct gender and plural where relevant.
    • Special characters (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ, ƴ) and apostrophe/glottal conventions are used accurately.
    • The explanation does not present an expected profit or approval as an already-confirmed result.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Small business and family cooperation

    Small businesses across Hausa-speaking communities are often started through cooperation between relatives, neighbors, or trusted partners, reflecting varied local financing practices, including formal bank loans, informal savings groups, and family contributions, depending on the country, region, and individual circumstances. Learners should not assume that one financing method, such as a bank loan, is the only or most common route to starting a business everywhere Hausa is spoken.

    Regional Variation — Business and market terms

    Terms for shops, loans, and financial institutions can vary between Nigeria, Niger, and other Hausa-speaking regions, as well as between urban and rural commercial contexts. The terms used in this chapter represent a common, broadly understandable instructional standard; learners should expect some local variation in business and banking terminology when working with real institutions.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Treating a planned profit as an already-confirmed result

    Error: Assuming that because a business plan projects a profit (riba), the profit has already been earned or the loan has already been approved.

    Correction: Distinguish a planned or hoped-for outcome from a confirmed result, and use the negative TAM pattern (Section 8.2) to state clearly what has not yet been confirmed.

    Common Mistake — Confusing a one-time transaction with a recurring market pattern

    Error: Using the imperfective TAM frame alone to describe a pattern, such as a seasonal price change, that recurs on a regular schedule.

    Correction: Use the habitual TAM frame (Section 8.1) for patterns that recur regularly, such as Farashin kayan lambu na canzawa kowace shekara, reserving the imperfective for a single action currently in progress.

    Common Mistake — Overgeneralizing one bank's decision to all financing options

    Error: Concluding that a business cannot proceed at all because one bank has not yet approved a loan.

    Correction: State precisely what one institution has not yet decided, as Bilkisu does in the Model Dialogue, rather than making a broader negative claim than the evidence supports.

    Answer Key / Amsoshi

    Tambayoyin fahimta na tattaunawa (Dialogue comprehension)

    1. Bilkisu is preparing loan documents at the bank so they can get money to start the business.
    2. The bank has not yet approved the loan.
    3. The bank is currently reviewing their documents.
    4. If the loan is approved, they can rent the shop location right away.

    Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

    (1) Bilkisu is preparing loan documents so they can get money to start the business. (2) They study the recurring seasonal change in vegetable prices during the rainy season. (3) The bank has not yet approved the loan. (4) They do not yet know whether they will get the shop they want.

    Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

    1. Sani and Bilkisu are currently preparing to open a new shop in the neighborhood.
    2. They study the recurring yearly change in prices during the rainy season, to decide the best time to start the business.
    3. The notice does not confirm whether the bank has approved the loan or when the shop will open; if the loan is approved, the shop will open within two months.

    Sashi na B (Grammar)

    1. Sample: Ana yin kasuwa a garinmu kowace ranar Talata. Farashin shinkafa na hauhawa kowace shekara a lokacin damina.
    2. Sample: Shirin bai game da hayar wurin kanti ba tukuna. Har yanzu bankin bai amince da bashin ba.
    3. Sample: A planned business outcome is something the owners hope or expect will happen, marked with words like shiri or riba, while a confirmed result is something the negative or imperfective TAM frame shows has actually happened or is happening now. Business language keeps these separate so that partners know what is expected versus what has actually occurred.

    Sashi na C (Sample writing)

    Ni da ɗan'uwana muna shirin buɗe kantin sayar da tufafi a kasuwarmu. Muna nazarin farashin kayan sawa, wanda ke canzawa kowace shekara kafin idi. Muna kuma neman bashi domin mu sami kuɗin fara kasuwanci. Har yanzu bankin bai amince da bashin ba tukuna, kuma ba mu tabbatar da ranar buɗe kanti ba. Idan bashin ya samu amincewa, za mu buɗe kantin nan da wata biyu.

    Other accurate, well-organized explanations that correctly distinguish ongoing, recurring, and unconfirmed details are equally acceptable.

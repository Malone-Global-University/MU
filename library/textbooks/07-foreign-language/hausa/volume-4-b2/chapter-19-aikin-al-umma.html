Unit V Begins — Community Project Communication

Chapter Overview

Chapter 19 builds directly on Chapter 18. There, learners moved the Chapter 16 sequencing tools and the Chapter 17 main-idea/support/conclusion structure into the turn-by-turn moves of a controlled formal discussion — stating a position, qualifying, conceding, challenging, responding, and closing. This chapter takes those same discussion tools and applies them to a longer, staged task: planning, explaining, revising, and presenting a community-oriented proposal. No new connector words or discussion-move phrases are introduced; instead, learners recombine the sequencing, comparison, qualifying, conceding, and closing language from Chapters 16–18 into the stages of a written proposal and its oral presentation. Following the staff meeting in Chapter 18, Amina, Ibrahim, and Salamatu are asked by the office manager to turn their agreed one-month scheduling-consultation trial into a short written proposal, then present it at a wider community and staff gathering, continuing the workplace storyline toward the final C1 functional project of Chapter 20.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Plan a short community-oriented proposal using the main-idea/support/conclusion structure from Chapter 17.
  2. Explain a proposal's purpose, reasoning, and expected outcome in organized spoken and written Hausa.
  3. Anticipate and address a likely challenge within the proposal itself, using the qualifying and conceding language from Chapter 18.
  4. Revise a draft proposal for clarity, organization, and accuracy using a structured checklist.
  5. Present a proposal orally to a group, inviting and responding to questions.
  6. Close a proposal presentation with a clear statement of next steps.

Communication Scenario

After the staff meeting in Chapter 18, the office manager asks Amina, Ibrahim, and Salamatu to turn their agreed one-month scheduling-consultation trial into a short written proposal for a combined staff-and-community gathering, since the office serves the surrounding community directly. The three colleagues must plan the proposal together, write it out, revise it, and then present it aloud to a wider audience that was not part of the original discussion — which means restating the position, the qualifying limit, the anticipated challenge, and the agreed trial in a form that a new audience can follow without having heard Chapter 18's discussion.

  1. Domain: a short community-oriented proposal, written and then presented orally, building on the Chapter 18 structured discussion.
  2. Target skill: planning, explaining, revising, and presenting a proposal, then closing with next steps.
  3. Grammar status: the Chapter 16 connectors, Chapter 17 main-idea/support/conclusion structure, and Chapter 18 qualifying/conceding/challenging language all move toward Consolidated in this chapter; the staged proposal genre itself is newly Introduced.
  4. No unscheduled grammar beyond the Chapter 18 level, and no proposal stage beyond planning, explaining, anticipating a challenge, revising, presenting, and closing with next steps.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Presentation pacing and audience-question intonation

When presenting a proposal aloud to a group, each stage — purpose, plan, anticipated challenge, and next steps — is typically marked by a brief pause and a slight drop in pitch at its close, which helps a listening audience track where one stage ends and the next begins, since the audience cannot see paragraph breaks the way a reader can. The phrase tambaya? ("questions?"), used to invite audience questions, is spoken with a light rising intonation and a longer pause afterward than an ordinary yes/no question, giving listeners time to respond. No new consonant or vowel-length feature is introduced in this chapter; this continues the phrase-level pacing and turn-boundary pausing practiced in Chapters 16–18.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter consolidates the Chapter 16 connectors, the Chapter 17 main-idea/support/conclusion structure, and the Chapter 18 qualifying/conceding/challenging language, and introduces the staged structure of a community-oriented proposal: stating purpose, explaining the plan, anticipating a challenge, inviting questions, and closing with next steps. No new connector words or discussion-move phrases are introduced.

19.1 Stating the purpose

A proposal opens by naming its purpose directly, using manufar wannan shawarwari ita ce ("the purpose of this proposal is"), which functions like the main-idea sentence from Chapter 17 but is framed for an audience that has not seen the earlier discussion.

  • Manufar wannan shawarwari ita ce mu gwada tuntuɓar ma’aikata kan jadawali sau ɗaya a wata, na tsawon watanni uku. The purpose of this proposal is to try consulting employees about the schedule once a month, for three months.

    • 19.2 Explaining the plan

    After stating the purpose, the proposal explains the plan step by step, recycling the Chapter 16 sequencing connectors (sannan, sai, bayan, kafin) and stating the expected outcome with ana sa ran ("it is expected that").

  • Da farko, za mu tattara ra’ayoyin ma’aikata a rubuce; sannan, mu tsara jadawalin gwaji; bayan haka, mu fara gwajin na tsawon watanni uku. First, we will gather employees' opinions in writing; then, we will design the trial schedule; after that, we will begin the trial for three months.
  • Ana sa ran wannan zai rage rashin fahimta a kan jadawali kuma ya ƙara yarda a tsakanin ma’aikata. It is expected that this will reduce misunderstanding about the schedule and increase agreement among employees.

    • 19.3 Anticipating a challenge

    A well-organized proposal names a likely question or objection before the audience raises it, using mai yiwuwa wani ya tambaya ("someone might well ask"), followed by a qualifying or conceding response drawn from Chapter 18.

  • Mai yiwuwa wani ya tambaya ko tuntuɓar akai-akai zai ɗauki lokaci mai yawa. Ba koyaushe ba ne haka zai kasance, domin za mu iyakance tuntuɓar zuwa sau ɗaya a wata kawai. Someone might well ask whether regular consultation will take a great deal of time. That will not always be the case, because we will limit consultation to just once a month.

    • 19.4 Inviting and responding to questions

    An oral presentation pauses after the plan and anticipated challenge to invite questions with tambaya?, and responds to a genuine audience question using the same challenge-and-response pattern practiced in Chapter 18.

  • Tambaya? — Ina ganin akwai matsala a nan: shin ma’aikatan da ba su son gwajin fa? — Kina da gaskiya kan wannan, don haka za mu tattara ra’ayoyin kowa, kuma ba za mu tilasta kowa ba. Questions? — I see a problem here: what about employees who don't want the trial? — You are right about that, so we will gather everyone's opinions, and we will not force anyone.

    • 19.5 Closing with next steps

    A proposal closes not with a general summary alone, but with concrete next steps, introduced by matakan gaba sun haɗa da ("the next steps include").

  • A taƙaice dai, matakan gaba sun haɗa da: tattara ra’ayoyin ma’aikata a wannan mako, fara gwajin a farkon watan gobe, sannan mu sake tattaunawa bayan watanni uku. In short, the next steps include: gathering employees' opinions this week, starting the trial at the beginning of next month, and then discussing again after three months.
    • Language Note — From discussion to proposal

    Chapter 18 distributed the position-challenge-concession-closing structure across several speakers in a single meeting. Chapter 19 takes the outcome of that discussion and reorganizes it for a new, wider audience: one presenter states the purpose, explains the plan, anticipates the same kind of challenge that arose naturally in the meeting, and closes with next steps rather than a closing statement alone. The underlying connectors, qualifying language, and challenge-and-response pattern do not change; what changes is that they are now organized as a planned, one-directional presentation rather than a live back-and-forth discussion.

    Common learner error: Do not present a proposal as though the audience already knows the background ("Kamar yadda muka riga muka yarda…" without explanation). A proposal presented to a new audience must restate the purpose and plan in full, even if the presenters already discussed it among themselves.

    Model Dialogue

    Yanayin hali: Amina, Ibrahim, da Salamatu suna gabatar da shawarwarinsu ga wani taro na ma’aikata da al’umma kan gwajin tuntuɓar jadawali.

    (Setting: Amina, Ibrahim, and Salamatu present their proposal to a joint staff-and-community gathering about the scheduling-consultation trial.)

    AMINA Manufar wannan shawarwari ita ce mu gwada tuntuɓar ma’aikata kan jadawali sau ɗaya a wata, na tsawon watanni uku.The purpose of this proposal is to try consulting employees about the schedule once a month, for three months.
    IBRAHIM Da farko, za mu tattara ra’ayoyin ma’aikata a rubuce; sannan, mu tsara jadawalin gwaji; bayan haka, mu fara gwajin.First, we will gather employees' opinions in writing; then, we will design the trial schedule; after that, we will begin the trial.
    SALAMATU Mai yiwuwa wani ya tambaya ko tuntuɓar akai-akai zai ɗauki lokaci mai yawa. Ba koyaushe ba ne haka zai kasance, domin za mu iyakance tuntuɓar zuwa sau ɗaya a wata kawai.Someone might well ask whether regular consultation will take a great deal of time. That will not always be the case, because we will limit consultation to just once a month.
    AMINA Tambaya?Questions?
    ƊAN AL'UMMA Ina ganin akwai matsala a nan: shin ma’aikatan da ba su son gwajin fa?I see a problem here: what about employees who don't want the trial?
    IBRAHIM Kina da gaskiya kan wannan, don haka za mu tattara ra’ayoyin kowa, kuma ba za mu tilasta kowa ba.You are right about that, so we will gather everyone's opinions, and we will not force anyone.
    SALAMATU Bugu da ƙari, za mu sanar da kowa da sakamakon gwajin bayan watanni uku, kafin a yanke shawara ta dindindin.Furthermore, we will inform everyone of the trial's results after three months, before a permanent decision is made.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Presenting a plan to a wider audience

    When a proposal that began among a small group of colleagues is presented to a wider staff-and-community audience, speakers commonly restate the background in full rather than assuming prior knowledge, since many listeners are hearing the plan for the first time. Anticipating a likely question before it is asked, as in mai yiwuwa wani ya tambaya, is valued in many Hausa community and workplace settings because it shows the presenters have considered the audience's concerns in advance, which builds trust before the discussion even opens.

    Regional Variation — Shared proposal structure, varied presentation style

    The purpose-plan-anticipated-challenge-next-steps structure introduced in this chapter is broadly shared across Hausa-speaking workplaces, schools, and community associations in Nigeria, Niger, and other regions, but the degree of formality in the opening address, the use of respectful titles for elders or officials present, and the amount of time given to open questions varies by setting. Learners should adjust these elements to the specific audience and occasion.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Assuming the audience already knows the background

    Error: Beginning a presentation with "Kamar yadda muka riga muka yarda…" ("as we already agreed…") when the audience was not part of the earlier discussion.

    Correction: Restate the purpose and plan in full at the opening, as in Manufar wannan shawarwari ita ce…, even if the presenters already discussed it privately.

    Common Mistake — Listing steps without a plan structure

    Error: Naming several actions without sequencing connectors, leaving the audience unsure what happens first, next, and last.

    Correction: Use da farko, sannan, bayan haka to organize the plan clearly, as in Da farko, za mu tattara ra’ayoyin ma’aikata… sannan, mu tsara jadawalin gwaji…

    Common Mistake — Closing without concrete next steps

    Error: Ending a proposal with only a general summary sentence and no concrete next actions, leaving the audience unsure what will actually happen next.

    Correction: Add a closing list of next steps, introduced with matakan gaba sun haɗa da, as in A taƙaice dai, matakan gaba sun haɗa da tattara ra’ayoyin ma’aikata a wannan mako…

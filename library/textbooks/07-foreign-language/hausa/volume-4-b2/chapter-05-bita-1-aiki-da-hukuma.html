Bita 1: Aiki da Hukuma

B2–C1 Development · Unit I Assessment, Chapter 5 of 5 · Speak and Write Hausa: Advanced Functional Hausa

Chapter Overview

Chapter 5 is the Unit I integrated assessment. It does not introduce new vocabulary domains, grammar systems, or sentence patterns. Instead, it draws together the workplace communication of Chapter 1, the official documents and institutional messages of Chapter 2, the oral presentation skills of Chapter 3, and the source comparison and report writing of Chapter 4 into a single connected scenario. Learners will move through a workplace situation that requires them to greet and update a colleague, interpret and respond to an institutional notice, deliver a short structured presentation, and compare two sources of information to reach a supported conclusion, all in one continuous professional episode. Performance in this chapter is evaluated against the consolidated (C) status of the predicate, pronoun, and TAM systems recorded for Chapter 5 in the Volume IV matrices, and it prepares learners for the shift into Unit II beginning with Chapter 6.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Combine greetings, workplace updates, and task negotiation from Chapter 1 with formal institutional interpretation from Chapter 2 in a single connected exchange.
  2. Deliver a short structured presentation using the signaling and example-giving language of Chapter 3.
  3. Attribute information to two sources, compare them, and draw a supported conclusion, as in Chapter 4.
  4. Recognize how professional, institutional, presentational, and report-writing language combine within one workplace episode.
  5. Complete an integrated listening, speaking, reading, and writing performance task that draws on all four chapters of Unit I.
  6. Identify areas of strength and areas needing further review before moving into Unit II.

Communication Scenario

It is the end of the month at Amina's workplace. Her supervisor, Malama Hadiza, asks Amina to update her on the personal-records project, respond to a new institutional notice about a scheduling change, present a short summary of the project's progress to the office team, and finally deliver the report comparing the records-office and employee sources that she prepared in Chapter 4. The episode moves through all four communicative domains of Unit I in sequence, exactly as they would occur together in a real workplace day.

  1. Domain: integrating workplace updates, institutional notices, presentations, and report writing into one connected episode.
  2. Developed skill: moving fluidly between register and task type within a single professional interaction, while recycling greetings, identity clauses, and institutional, presentation, and reporting vocabulary from Chapters 1-4.
  3. Recycled grammar in context: core predicate types, independent pronouns, and completive/future TAM forms, all Consolidated and formally Assessed (A) at this chapter.
  4. No unscheduled TAM paradigm, dialect mixture, or productive Ajami writing is introduced in this chapter.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Tone and length across registers

As this chapter moves between a casual workplace update, a formal notice, a structured presentation, and a written report, practice adjusting pace and rhythm to the register of each segment: a slightly quicker, informal rhythm for the update with Hadiza, a more measured pace for reading the institutional notice, clear phrase-final tone for signaling moves in the presentation, and a steady, even rhythm for the report comparison, as introduced in Chapter 4. No new sound or vowel-length contrast is introduced; this is a consolidation of the tone and vowel-length control built across Chapters 1-4.

    Language Note — Combining registers within one episode

    A single connected workplace episode, like Amina's day in this chapter, moves between an informal update, a formal institutional response, a structured presentation, and a written report, without introducing any new sentence pattern. What changes across these segments is register and text type, not the underlying grammar, which remains the completive/future TAM, independent pronoun, and predicate-type system consolidated since Volume III.

    These review points recycle familiar structures from Volumes I-III and Chapters 1-4; no new sentence pattern is introduced in this chapter.

    Model Dialogue

    Yanayin hali: Rana ce ta ƙarshen wata a wurin aikin Amina. Ta sadu da Malama Hadiza domin sabunta ta game da aikin, ta amsa wa saƙon hukuma, ta gabatar da taƙaitaccen rahoto ga tawagar, sannan ta gabatar da cikakken rahoton kwatancen tushen bayanai.

    (Setting: It is the end of the month at Amina's workplace. She meets Malama Hadiza to update her on the project, responds to an institutional message, presents a brief summary to the team, and delivers the full source-comparison report.)

    HADIZA Ina kwana, Amina. Ina ci gaban aikin sabunta bayanan sirri?Good morning, Amina. How is the progress on the personal-records update task?
    AMINA Ina kwana, Malama Hadiza. Aikin ya kusa gama. Amma na karɓi wani saƙo daga hukuma game da taron yau.Good morning, Malama Hadiza. The task is nearly finished. But I received a message from the institution about today's meeting.
    HADIZA To, mene ne saƙon ya ce?Well, what does the message say?
    AMINA Bisa ga saƙon hukuma, an canja ranar taro zuwa ranar Alhamis, don haka za mu gabatar da rahoton a ranar Alhamis maimakon yau.According to the institutional message, the meeting date has been changed to Thursday, so we will present the report on Thursday instead of today.
    HADIZA Da kyau. Amma bari mu fara da taƙaitaccen gabatarwa ga tawagar yanzu.Good. But let's begin with a brief presentation to the team now.
    AMINA To. Yanzu, bari in bayar da misali guda: mun tattara bayanai daga ofishin rijista da kuma daga ma'aikata game da sabon tsarin.All right. Now, let me give one example: we gathered information from the records office and from the employees about the new procedure.
    AMINA A cewar tushen bayanai na ofishin rijista, kowa ya cika fom ɗin kafin ranar ƙarshe. Ma'aikata da yawa sun ce sun gamsu, amma wasu sun ce ba su san ranar ƙarshe da kyau ba.According to the records office source, everyone completed the form before the deadline. Many employees said they were satisfied, but some said they did not know the deadline well.
    AMINA Tushen bayanai guda biyu sun yi daidai a kan cewa kowa ya cika fom ɗin, amma sun bambanta a kan ko an sanar da kowa ranar ƙarshe. Daga wannan bincike, za mu iya cewa sabon tsarin ya yi nasara, amma sadarwa game da ranar ƙarshe na buƙatar ƙarin bayani.The two sources agree that everyone completed the form, but they differ on whether everyone was informed of the deadline. From this inquiry, we can say that the new procedure succeeded, but communication about the deadline needs more clarity.
    HADIZA Kyakkyawan gabatarwa da kammalawa. Za mu gabatar da cikakken rahoton a ranar Alhamis kamar yadda saƙon hukuma ya nuna.A fine presentation and conclusion. We will present the full report on Thursday as the institutional message indicates.

    Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

    1. What task update does Amina give Hadiza at the start of the exchange?
    2. What did the institutional message change, and what is the effect on the presentation plan?
    3. What example does Amina give in her brief presentation to the team?
    4. Where do the two sources agree and disagree, and what conclusion does Amina draw?

    Writing Workshop

    1. Karanta misalai da hankali. Reread Sections 5.1-5.4 and the Model Dialogue.
    2. Tsara sassan hudu. Plan four short connected segments: a workplace update, a response to an institutional message, a brief presentation summary, and a report comparison with conclusion, all on one familiar workplace topic.
    3. Rubuta kowane sashe. Draft each of the four segments, recycling the appropriate patterns from Chapters 1-4.
    4. Haɗa sassan. Combine the four segments into one connected 100-150 word episode, in Boko, similar in structure to Amina's day.
    5. Duba jerin bincike. Check your draft against the checklist below.
    6. Rubuta siffa ta ƙarshe. Produce a clean final version suitable for submission.

    Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

    • The episode includes all four Unit I segments: update, institutional response, presentation, and report.
    • Each segment uses register appropriate to its communicative purpose.
    • The report segment attributes information to two sources, states agreement and disagreement, and closes with a supported conclusion.
    • Recycled TAM forms, predicate types, and independent pronouns are used correctly throughout.
    • Special characters (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ, ƴ) and apostrophe/glottal conventions are used accurately in the Boko text.
    • The episode reads as one connected sequence rather than four disconnected texts.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Moving between registers in one workday

    In many Hausa-speaking professional and institutional settings, a single workday commonly requires moving smoothly between an informal update to a colleague, a formal response to institutional correspondence, a structured presentation to a team, and a carefully attributed written report. Being able to shift register appropriately within one connected episode, as Amina does in this chapter, is itself a valued professional skill, though the specific expectations for tone and formality can vary by institution and region.

    Regional Variation — Scheduling and notice conventions

    How institutional notices announce schedule changes, and how much advance notice is customary, vary across institutions, regions, and sectors. The pattern shown in this chapter is a widely usable model for this course, not the only acceptable convention.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Using the same register across all four segments

    Error: Speaking or writing the institutional response, the presentation, and the report all in the same informal register used for the initial workplace update.

    Correction: Shift register appropriately for each segment, as modeled in Sections 5.1-5.4 and the Model Dialogue.

    Common Mistake — Losing the connection between segments

    Error: Writing four disconnected texts instead of one continuous workplace episode.

    Correction: Use brief transitional phrases, as Hadiza and Amina do in the Model Dialogue, to link the update, the institutional response, the presentation, and the report into one sequence.

    Common Mistake — Overstating the report conclusion

    Error: Drawing a conclusion in the final segment that goes beyond what the compared sources actually show, as cautioned in Chapter 4.

    Correction: Base the conclusion directly on the stated points of agreement and disagreement, using a formula such as Daga wannan bincike, za mu iya cewa...

    Tambayoyin fahimta na tattaunawa (Dialogue comprehension)

    1. Amina tells Hadiza that the personal-records update task is nearly finished.
    2. The institutional message changes the meeting date to Thursday, which also moves the full report presentation to Thursday.
    3. Amina gives the example that they gathered information from the records office and from the employees about the new procedure.
    4. The two sources agree that everyone completed the form; they disagree on whether everyone was informed of the deadline. Amina concludes the new procedure succeeded but communication about the deadline needs more clarity.

    Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

    (1) Amina told Hadiza the records task is nearly finished. (2) The institutional message changed the meeting date to Thursday. (3) The sources agree that everyone completed the form but disagree on when everyone was informed of the deadline. (4) The conclusion is that the new procedure succeeded, but communication needs more clarity.

    Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

    1. The notice announces that the department meeting date has changed to Thursday because of an important institutional meeting.
    2. The report excerpt cites the records office and the employees as its two sources.
    3. The sources agree that everyone completed the form but disagree on whether everyone was informed of the deadline in time; the conclusion is that the new procedure succeeded but communication needs more clarity.

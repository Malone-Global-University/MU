Unit III Continues — Regional Hausa and Variation

Chapter Overview

Chapter 13 continues Unit III by turning from Chapter 12’s treatment of religion and culture toward Hausa itself: the language's regional, national, and community variation. Learners will discuss how vocabulary, pronunciation, and everyday expressions differ between Nigerian and Nigerien Hausa, and among urban, rural, media, and household contexts, while continuing to state such differences as labeled variation rather than as errors or as evidence that one variety is more correct than another. This chapter continues developing, at the Developed level, the three systems introduced at the Introduced level in Chapter 11 and developed further in Chapter 12: expanded TAM/aspect and negation (conditional and prohibitive/requirement forms), prepositions and relational nouns, and focus/contrast in answers. All three continue to develop in Chapter 14 and reach full consolidation and assessment at Chapter 15, the Unit III assessment.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe a regional or national difference in Hausa vocabulary, pronunciation, or usage using clearly labeled, non-stigmatizing terms.
  2. State a condition under which a regionally marked form is expected or understood, extending the conditional (idan) pattern from Chapters 11–12 to language-variation contexts.
  3. Express a respectful caution or recommendation about word choice across regions, extending the prohibitive/requirement forms from Chapters 11–12.
  4. Use the relational nouns and prepositions from Chapters 11–12 (bisa, gwargwadon, madadin) to introduce the regional or national source of a word or expression.
  5. Use a focus/contrast pattern to distinguish how one region or country uses a form differently from another, without ranking varieties.
  6. Record the gender and plural of new vocabulary-variation nouns as part of active vocabulary learning.
  7. Discuss, respectfully and with appropriate qualification, regional and national variation in Hausa, in speech and in writing.

Communication Scenario

Amina, whose visit to the naming ceremony was described in Chapter 12, meets a new colleague, Ibrahim, who grew up in Niger before moving to Nigeria for work. During a conversation about a form Ibrahim is filling out, Amina notices that he uses a few words she has not heard before. Curious rather than corrective, she asks him about the difference, and Ibrahim explains which words are Nigerien, which are Nigerian, and when each is more likely to be understood, careful not to suggest that either country's Hausa is more correct than the other's.

  1. Domain: describing a regional or national vocabulary, pronunciation, or usage difference, asking respectfully about an unfamiliar form, and distinguishing one country's or region's usage from another's without ranking them.
  2. Developed skill: stating a condition under which a regionally marked form is expected, expressing a respectful caution about word choice across contexts, introducing a form's regional or national source with a relational noun/preposition, and contrasting one region's usage with another's using focus.
  3. Continued grammar development: expanded TAM/aspect and negation (conditional and prohibitive/requirement forms), prepositions and relational nouns, and focus/contrast in answers (Developed, continuing from Chapters 11–12, reaching full consolidation at Chapter 15).
  4. No unscheduled grammar beyond the Developed level for these three systems, no requirement to produce any single regional variety beyond the productive standard, and no implication that one country's or region's Hausa is more correct, more authentic, or more prestigious than another's.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Listening for regionally marked pronunciation

This chapter continues the receptive regional-pronunciation awareness scheduled since earlier volumes, applying it explicitly to discussion of variation itself. Learners are not asked to imitate a specific regional accent, but should practice recognizing that the same word may be pronounced with small differences in vowel length or tone emphasis depending on the speaker's background, without treating any single pronunciation as the only correct one. As in Chapters 11–12, maintain a slightly slower, more measured rhythm when qualifying a claim about regional usage, and pause briefly before a contrastive phrase such as a Nijar (“in Niger”) or a Najeriya (“in Nigeria”). No new consonant or vowel-length contrast is introduced in this chapter.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter continues developing, at the Developed level, the three systems introduced in Chapter 11 and developed in Chapter 12: expanded TAM/aspect and negation, prepositions and relational nouns, and focus/contrast in answers, consistent with the Volume IV matrices. This chapter applies these systems to describing regional and national variation in Hausa, and continues toward full consolidation and assessment at Chapter 15.

13.1 Conditional clauses with idan in language-variation contexts

Continue using idan to introduce a condition under which a regionally marked word or pronunciation is expected or understood, just as Chapters 11–12 used it for legal and cultural conditions.

  • Idan kana magana da wanda ya girma a Nijar, wataƙila zai yi amfani da wata kalma dabam. If you are speaking with someone who grew up in Niger, they might use a different word.
  • Idan kana cikin babban gari, mutane sukan fahimci kalmomin gwamnati da sauri. If you are in a large city, people tend to understand government-related words quickly.

    • 13.2 Respectful caution and recommendation about word choice

    Use kada to caution against assuming a word is understood everywhere, and ya kamata or dole to recommend checking word choice, continuing the pattern from Chapters 11–12 in a language-variation context.

  • Kada ka ɗauka cewa kowace kalma da ka sani a garinku ana amfani da ita a ko’ina. Do not assume that every word you know from your hometown is used everywhere.
  • Ya kamata ka tambaya idan wata kalma ba a saba ji ba a wani yanki. You should ask if a word is unfamiliar in another region.

    • 13.3 Prepositions and relational nouns: introducing the regional or national source of a form

    Continue using bisa, gwargwadon, and madadin from Chapters 11–12 to introduce the regional or national source, standard, or alternative for a word or expression.

  • Bisa yadda ake magana a Nijar, ana amfani da wata kalma dabam don “mota.” According to how people speak in Niger, a different word is used for “car.”
  • Madadin kalmar da aka saba amfani da ita a birni, wasu a ƙauye na amfani da wata dabam. Instead of the word commonly used in the city, some in rural areas use a different one.

    • 13.4 Focus and contrast: distinguishing one region's or country's usage from another's

    Continue using the focus particle ne/ce to state precisely which region or country uses a given form, avoiding the implication that one usage is more correct than another.

  • A Najeriya ake amfani da wannan kalma, ba a Nijar ba. It is in Nigeria that this word is used, not in Niger.
  • Lafazin garinsu ne ke haka, ba na ko’ina ba. It is their hometown's way of speaking that is like that, not everywhere's.
    • Language Note — A regional label versus a correctness judgment

    Just as Chapter 12 distinguished a family or community practice from a claim about all Hausa speakers, this chapter's language distinguishes a regional or national label from a judgment about correctness. Use a Nijar, a Najeriya, a birni, or a ƙauye together with the focus pattern from Section 13.4 to mark a usage as regionally specific, and avoid words such as “wrong” or “bad Hausa” when describing a variety you do not personally use.

    Common learner error: Do not state that a regionally marked word or pronunciation is incorrect Hausa. A Nijar ake amfani da wannan kalma (“this word is used in Niger,” appropriately labeled) is not the same claim as the judgmental Wannan ba daidai ba ne (“this is not correct,” applied to a legitimate regional form).

    Model Dialogue

    Yanayin hali: Amina da Ibrahim, sabon abokin aikinta wanda ya girma a Nijar, suna magana ne kan wata takarda da Ibrahim ke cikawa, kuma Amina ta lura da wasu kalmomi da ba ta saba ji ba.

    (Setting: Amina and Ibrahim, her new colleague who grew up in Niger, are discussing a form Ibrahim is filling out, and Amina notices some words she has not heard before.)

    AMINA Ibrahim, na lura kana amfani da wata kalma dabam don wannan sashi na takardar. Me yasa?Ibrahim, I noticed you use a different word for this section of the form. Why is that?
    IBRAHIM Ah, wannan kalma ce da muke amfani da ita a Nijar. Bisa yadda muka koya can, ba haka ake kira ba a nan Najeriya, amma dukkanmu Hausawa ne.Ah, that's a word we use in Niger. Based on how we learned it there, it's not called that here in Nigeria, but we're all Hausa speakers.
    AMINA Ban gane ba sarai. Wanne ne daidai, naka ko nawa?I don't quite understand. Which one is correct, yours or mine?
    IBRAHIM Babu wanda ya fi daidai. A Nijar ake amfani da nawa, a Najeriya kuma ake amfani da naki, kuma dukkansu Hausa ne kawai bambancin yanki ne.Neither is more correct. Mine is used in Niger, and yours is used in Nigeria, and both are simply Hausa — it's just a regional difference.
    AMINA To, kada in ɗauka cewa kalmar da na sani ana amfani da ita ko’ina, ko?So, I shouldn't assume that a word I know is used everywhere, right?
    IBRAHIM Daidai ne. Ya kamata ka tambaya idan kalma ba a saba ji ba, musamman idan kana magana da wanda ya girma a wani yanki ko wata ƙasa dabam.Exactly right. You should ask if a word is unfamiliar, especially if you're speaking with someone who grew up in a different region or country.
    AMINA Akwai wasu bambance-bambance na lafazi ma, ko?Are there some pronunciation differences too?
    IBRAHIM I, akwai. Gwargwadon inda mutum ya girma, tsawon wasu wasula na iya bambanta kaɗan, amma fahimta ba ta rasa ba idan an saurara da kyau.Yes, there are. Depending on where a person grew up, the length of some vowels can differ slightly, but understanding isn't lost if you listen carefully.
    AMINA Na gode da bayanin. Yanzu na fahimci cewa bambanci ba yana nufin kuskure ba ne.Thank you for the explanation. Now I understand that a difference doesn't mean a mistake.

    Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

    1. Where did Ibrahim learn the word Amina noticed?
    2. According to Ibrahim, is one word more correct than the other?
    3. What does Ibrahim recommend Amina do when she encounters an unfamiliar word?
    4. What kind of variation does Ibrahim say can depend on where a person grew up?

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Hausa as a language spoken across borders

    Hausa is spoken across northern Nigeria, Niger, and other parts of West Africa, as well as in diaspora communities. Speakers move between countries, regions, cities, and villages for work, education, family, and trade, and encounter word choices, pronunciations, and expressions that differ from their own without those differences indicating an error. Learners should approach unfamiliar regional forms with curiosity, as Amina does in the Model Dialogue, rather than assuming their own variety is the only correct standard.

    Regional Variation — Nigeria, Niger, and beyond

    Vocabulary, pronunciation, and some expressions differ between Nigerian and Nigerien Hausa, and between urban and rural communities within each country. This chapter's productive standard remains the instructional model used throughout this course; regional forms introduced here are for recognition and respectful discussion rather than required production, unless a later specialized scope assigns them productively.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Describing a regional form as incorrect

    Error: Saying Wannan ba daidai ba ne ("This is not correct") about a word or pronunciation that is simply used in a different region or country.

    Correction: Use a regional label together with the focus pattern (Section 13.4), as in A Nijar ake amfani da wannan kalma, ba a Nijeriya ba, so the statement describes a regional difference rather than a correctness judgment.

    Common Mistake — Assuming one's own usage is universal

    Error: Assuming that a word or pronunciation familiar from one's own town or region is used identically everywhere Hausa is spoken.

    Correction: Use kada ka ɗauka ("do not assume") together with a conditional clause (Section 13.1), and ask a speaker from a different area, as Amina does in the Model Dialogue.

    Common Mistake — Treating national variation as a strict boundary

    Error: Presenting Nigerian and Nigerien Hausa as two completely separate languages, or implying that speakers from one country cannot understand the other.

    Correction: Present national variation as differences within a shared language, as Ibrahim does in the Model Dialogue, noting that mutual understanding remains possible with attentive listening.

    Answer Key / Amsoshi

    Tambayoyin fahimta na tattaunawa (Dialogue comprehension)

    1. Ibrahim learned the word in Niger, where he grew up.
    2. No; Ibrahim says neither word is more correct, since each is used in its respective country.
    3. Ibrahim recommends that Amina ask when she encounters an unfamiliar word, especially when speaking with someone from a different region or country.
    4. Ibrahim says vowel length can differ slightly depending on where a person grew up, though understanding is not lost with careful listening.

    Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

    (1) Hausa is spoken in Nigeria, Niger, and other West African countries. (2) Traveling to Niger, one will hear some words and pronunciations that differ from what is common in Nigeria. (3) Whether a person grew up in a city or a village affects pronunciation differences. (4) A person should ask if they don't understand a word, and should avoid assuming that the word they know is the only correct one.

    Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

    1. A traveler moving between Nigeria and Niger might notice different words and pronunciations from what they are used to.
    2. The notice states "A Najeriya ake amfani da wasu kalmomi, a Nijar kuma ake amfani da wasu dabam," using the focus particle ake to mark each country's usage without ranking either.
    3. A learner should ask when they encounter an unfamiliar word and should avoid assuming their own word is the only correct one.

    Sashi na B (Grammar)

    1. Sample: Idan ka je Nijar, za ka ji wasu kalmomi dabam. Idan mutum ya girma a ƙauye, lafazinsa na iya bambanta kaɗan da na birni.
    2. Sample: Kada ka ɗauka cewa kalmarka ita ce kaɗai daidai. Ya kamata ka tambaya idan wata kalma ba a saba ji ba.

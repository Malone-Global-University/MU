Unit IV Concludes — Structured Debate

Chapter Overview

Chapter 18 builds directly on Chapter 17. There, learners organized connected sentences into an analytical paragraph with a main idea, supporting reasons and comparisons, and a conclusion, using the Chapter 16 sequencing, reason/plan, and comparison connectors at a developing level of independence. This chapter takes that same main-idea-and-support skill into spoken and written argument. Learners will state a position, qualify a claim so it is not overstated, concede a point an opponent has raised, challenge a claim they disagree with, and respond to a challenge without abandoning their position. No new connector words are introduced; instead, learners recombine the sequencing, reason/plan, comparison, and paragraph-organization tools from Chapters 16 and 17 into the turn-by-turn structure of a controlled formal discussion. The newsletter piece that Amina, Ibrahim, and Salamatu wrote in Chapter 17 becomes the starting point for a short staff meeting in which colleagues discuss whether the office should extend its new policy further, continuing the workplace storyline toward the community project of Chapter 19.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. State a position on a familiar workplace or community issue using the main-idea patterns from Chapter 17.
  2. Qualify a claim so that it is not presented as an absolute or universal fact.
  3. Concede a valid point raised by someone who disagrees, without abandoning your own position.
  4. Challenge a claim respectfully, using reasons and comparisons from Chapters 16 and 17.
  5. Respond to a challenge by restating, adjusting, or defending your position.
  6. Participate in a short structured discussion that moves from position, to challenge and concession, to a closing statement.

Communication Scenario

After the newsletter piece from Chapter 17 is published, the office manager calls a short staff meeting to discuss a follow-up question: should the office extend its new consultation policy to cover scheduling decisions as well as rights decisions? Amina supports the idea, Ibrahim is cautiously in favor but wants limits, and Salamatu raises a concern about how it would work in practice. The three colleagues must state their positions, respond to one another's reasons, concede fair points, and reach a closing statement to bring back to the office manager — without simply repeating the newsletter's conclusions.

  1. Domain: a short structured workplace discussion, building on the Chapter 17 analytical paragraph.
  2. Target skill: stating a position, qualifying, conceding, challenging, and responding, then closing a discussion.
  3. Grammar status: the Chapter 16 connectors and Chapter 17 main-idea/support/conclusion structure move toward Consolidated in this chapter; qualifying, conceding, and challenging language is newly Introduced.
  4. No unscheduled grammar beyond the Chapter 17 level, and no debate move beyond stating, qualifying, conceding, challenging, responding, and closing.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Intonation for agreement, concession, and challenge

In spoken discussion, a rising-then-falling contour on na yarda ("I agree") followed by a brief pause before amma signals that a concession is about to be qualified, so listeners expect a "but" clause rather than full agreement. A challenge introduced with ina ganin akwai matsala a nan is typically spoken more slowly and evenly, without a rising or aggressive pitch, which keeps the challenge respectful rather than confrontational. No new consonant or vowel-length feature is introduced in this chapter; this continues the phrase-level pacing and paragraph-boundary pausing practiced in Chapters 16 and 17.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter consolidates the Chapter 16 connectors and Chapter 17 main-idea/support/conclusion structure, and introduces the turn-by-turn moves of a controlled formal discussion: stating, qualifying, conceding, challenging, responding, and closing. No new connector words are introduced.

18.1 Stating a position

A discussion turn opens the same way an analytical paragraph does — with a clear statement of the speaker's position, often using a ganina or matsayina shi ne ("my position is").

  • Matsayina shi ne ya kamata ofishi ya faɗaɗa tuntuɓar ma’aikata zuwa batutuwan jadawali. My position is that the office should extend consultation of employees to scheduling matters.
  • A ganina, wannan zai kawo ƙarin gamsuwa a wurin aiki. In my opinion, this will bring greater satisfaction at work.

    • 18.2 Qualifying a claim

    A speaker who does not want to overstate a claim uses a qualifying phrase such as ba koyaushe ba ne ("it is not always the case") or names a limit with iyaka, so the claim remains defensible.

  • Ba koyaushe ba ne tuntuɓar ma’aikata ke kawo sauri wajen yanke shawara, amma yawanci tana kawo yarda mai kyau. Consulting employees does not always bring faster decisions, but it usually brings good agreement.
  • Ina goyon bayan wannan ra’ayin, amma da iyaka: ya kamata a tuntuɓi ma’aikata kafin manyan canje-canje kawai, ba ƙananan al’amura ba. I support this idea, but with a limit: employees should be consulted before major changes only, not small matters.

    • 18.3 Conceding a point

    Conceding uses na yarda, amma… to accept part of what another speaker said before restating or adjusting one's own position. Concession is not surrender; it is followed by amma or duk da haka and a continued point.

  • Na yarda da Salamatu cewa jadawali ya fi wuya a canza fiye da ƙa’idar haƙƙi, amma duk da haka, ina ganin ya kamata mu gwada tuntuɓar ma’aikata a kan jadawali na wata guda kafin mu yanke shawara ta ƙarshe. I agree with Salamatu that scheduling is harder to change than the rights policy, but even so, I think we should try consulting employees on scheduling for one month before making a final decision.

    • 18.4 Challenging a claim

    A challenge is introduced respectfully with ina ganin akwai matsala a nan ("I see a problem here") and supported with a reason or comparison, not a personal criticism.

  • Ina ganin akwai matsala a nan: jadawali na canzawa kowane mako, don haka tuntuɓar ma’aikata a kai a kai zai ɗauki lokaci fiye da yadda muke tsammani. I see a problem here: the schedule changes every week, so consulting employees regularly will take more time than we expect.

    • 18.5 Responding to a challenge and closing the discussion

    A response to a challenge either adjusts the original position with a qualifying phrase, or defends it with a further reason, and the discussion closes with a summarizing sentence, often introduced by a taƙaice dai, that states what the group has agreed on.

  • Kina da gaskiya kan lokaci, don haka za mu iya iyakance tuntuɓar zuwa sau ɗaya a wata maimakon kowane mako. You are right about the time issue, so we could limit consultation to once a month instead of every week.
  • A taƙaice dai, mun yarda cewa ya kamata a gwada tuntuɓar ma’aikata a kan jadawali sau ɗaya a wata, na tsawon watanni uku, kafin a yanke shawara ta dindindin. In short, we agree that consulting employees about scheduling should be tried once a month, for three months, before a permanent decision is made.
    • Language Note — From paragraph to discussion turn

    Chapter 17 organized a main idea, support, and conclusion into a single written paragraph produced by one writer. Chapter 18 distributes that same structure across several speakers: one person states a position, another qualifies or concedes, a third challenges, and the position is defended or adjusted before the group reaches a shared closing statement. The connectors and reference-tracking skills do not change; what changes is that they now operate across turns and across speakers rather than within one writer's paragraph.

    Common learner error: Do not treat conceding a point as the end of a turn ("Na yarda da Salamatu" and stopping there). A concession must be followed by amma or duk da haka and a continued point, or it will sound like the speaker has abandoned their position entirely.

    Model Dialogue

    Yanayin hali: Amina, Ibrahim, da Salamatu suna tattaunawa a taron ma’aikata kan ko ya kamata ofishi ya faɗaɗa tuntuɓar ma’aikata zuwa batutuwan jadawali.

    (Setting: Amina, Ibrahim, and Salamatu discuss at a staff meeting whether the office should extend consultation to scheduling matters.)

    AMINA Matsayina shi ne ya kamata ofishi ya faɗaɗa tuntuɓar ma’aikata zuwa jadawali, kamar yadda muka yi wa ƙa’idar haƙƙi.My position is that the office should extend consultation to scheduling, as we did with the rights policy.
    IBRAHIM Ina goyon bayan wannan ra’ayin, amma da iyaka: ya kamata mu tuntuɓi ma’aikata kan manyan canje-canjen jadawali kawai.I support this idea, but with a limit: we should consult employees on major schedule changes only.
    SALAMATU Ina ganin akwai matsala a nan: jadawali na canzawa kowane mako, don haka tuntuɓa akai-akai zai ɗauki lokaci sosai.I see a problem here: the schedule changes every week, so consulting regularly will take a lot of time.
    AMINA Na yarda da Salamatu cewa jadawali ya fi wuya a canza fiye da ƙa’idar haƙƙi, amma duk da haka, ina ganin ya kamata mu gwada tuntuɓa a kan jadawali na wata guda kafin mu yanke shawara ta ƙarshe.I agree with Salamatu that scheduling is harder to change than the rights policy, but even so, I think we should try consulting on scheduling for one month before making a final decision.
    SALAMATU Kina da gaskiya kan iyakance shi zuwa gwaji, don haka za mu iya iyakance tuntuɓar zuwa sau ɗaya a wata maimakon kowane mako.You are right about limiting it to a trial, so we could limit consultation to once a month instead of every week.
    IBRAHIM Bugu da ƙari, za mu iya sanar da ma’aikata cewa gwajin zai ɗauki watanni uku kafin a yanke shawara ta dindindin.Furthermore, we could inform employees that the trial will last three months before a permanent decision is made.
    AMINA A taƙaice dai, mun yarda cewa ya kamata a gwada tuntuɓar ma’aikata a kan jadawali sau ɗaya a wata, na tsawon watanni uku, kafin a yanke shawara ta dindindin.In short, we agree that consulting employees about scheduling should be tried once a month, for three months, before a permanent decision is made.
    SALAMATU Wannan zai zama abin da za mu kai wa shugaban ofishi.That will be what we bring to the office manager.

    Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

    1. What is Amina's original position?
    2. What limit does Ibrahim propose?
    3. What problem does Salamatu raise as a challenge?
    4. What does the group finally agree on, and for how long?

    Guided Practice

    Aikin fahimta — Recognition: Reread the Model Dialogue and label each turn as stating, qualifying, challenging, conceding, responding, or closing.

    Aikin zaɓi — Selection: Choose the correct phrase (matsayina shi ne, ba koyaushe ba ne, na yarda, amma, ina ganin akwai matsala a nan, duk da haka, a taƙaice dai) to complete six turns drawn from a short model discussion.

    Aikin tsari — Ordering: Arrange six scrambled discussion turns (position, challenge, concession, qualified response, further point, closing statement) into a coherent structured discussion.

    Aikin sake rubutu — Rewriting: Rewrite a turn that concedes a point but stops abruptly, adding a qualifying continuation with amma or duk da haka.

    Writing Workshop

    1. Karanta misalai da hankali. Reread Sections 18.1–18.5, the Model Dialogue, and the Reading Practice passage.
    2. Zaɓi batu. Choose a familiar issue with two reasonable sides (a family, community, school, or workplace question, including the office scheduling issue if you wish).
    3. Tsara muhawara. Plan a short written discussion: one position statement, one qualifying sentence, one challenge, one concession with a continuation, and one closing statement.
    4. Rubuta daftarin farko. Draft a 120–160 word written discussion in Boko, either as a short dialogue or as a single analytical account of the disagreement.
    5. Duba jerin bincike. Check your draft against the checklist below.
    6. Rubuta siffa ta ƙarshe. Produce a clean final version, then write a separate one-sentence statement of what was finally agreed.

    Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

    • The discussion opens with a clear position statement.
    • At least one claim is qualified with ba koyaushe ba ne or a stated iyaka.
    • At least one challenge is raised respectfully, supported by a reason or comparison.
    • At least one concession uses na yarda, amma or duk da haka and continues with a point, rather than stopping abruptly.
    • Reference to people, positions, and the issue itself is tracked without unnecessary repetition.
    • The discussion closes with a summarizing statement, ideally introduced with a taƙaice dai.
    • Special characters (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ, ƴ) and apostrophe/glottal conventions are used accurately.
    • No unscheduled grammar beyond the Chapter 17 level is required to understand the discussion.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Disagreeing while preserving relationship

    Phrases such as na yarda, amma and ina ganin akwai matsala a nan allow a speaker to disagree with a colleague's idea while still acknowledging what is reasonable in it. In many Hausa professional and community settings, structured disagreement of this kind — concede first, then add a further point — is valued because it keeps working relationships intact even when people do not fully agree, which matters more in ongoing colleague or family relationships than in a one-time transaction.

    Regional Variation — Shared discussion structure, varied formality

    The specific level of formality in a structured discussion — how directly a challenge is raised, how much a speaker softens disagreement with elders or supervisors — varies across Hausa-speaking workplaces, schools, and communities in Nigeria, Niger, and other regions. The position-challenge-concession-closing structure introduced in this chapter is broadly shared, but learners should adjust directness and softening language to the setting and the people involved.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Treating concession as full surrender

    Error: Saying "Na yarda da Salamatu" and stopping there, which makes it sound as though the speaker has abandoned their entire position.

    Correction: Follow the concession with amma or duk da haka and a continued point, as in Na yarda da Salamatu cewa jadawali ya fi wuya a canza… amma duk da haka, ina ganin ya kamata mu gwada…

    Common Mistake — Challenging without a reason

    Error: Raising a challenge with only ina ganin akwai matsala a nan and no supporting reason, which leaves the challenge unclear and hard to respond to.

    Correction: Follow the challenge with a specific reason or comparison, as in Ina ganin akwai matsala a nan: jadawali na canzawa kowane mako, don haka…

    Common Mistake — Ending a discussion without a closing statement

    Error: Stopping a discussion right after the last challenge or concession, with no sentence stating what the group agreed on.

    Correction: Add a closing statement, often introduced with a taƙaice dai, that states the shared conclusion, as in A taƙaice dai, mun yarda cewa ya kamata a gwada tuntuɓar ma’aikata…

    Answer Key / Amsoshi

    Tambayoyin fahimta na tattaunawa (Dialogue comprehension)

    1. Amina's original position is that the office should extend consultation to scheduling, as it did with the rights policy.
    2. Ibrahim proposes limiting consultation to major schedule changes only.
    3. Salamatu challenges that the schedule changes weekly, so regular consultation would take a lot of time.
    4. The group agrees to try consulting employees about scheduling once a month, for three months, before making a permanent decision.

    Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

    (1) The speaker's original position is that the school should lengthen the break between lessons. (2) Another parent challenges that a longer break would make lessons end too late. (3) The speaker concedes that this is a fair concern but continues by proposing to shorten the break instead of canceling the idea entirely. (4) The committee agrees to try a short five-minute extra break for one month.

    Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

    1. The chairwoman's original position is that the market should open one hour earlier than it currently does.
    2. The trader challenges that opening earlier would increase lighting and security costs.
    3. The chairwoman concedes that the concern is valid, but continues by proposing to test the idea with only a few stalls first, before expanding it to everyone.
    4. Sample summary: The traders' association agreed to test earlier opening hours with just ten stalls for two months. After the trial, they will discuss the results before deciding whether to expand it further.

    Sashi na B (Grammar)

    1. Sample: Matsayina shi ne ya kamata makaranta ta ba da ƙarin lokacin karatu a gida.
    2. Sample: Ba koyaushe ba ne ƙarin aikin gida ke inganta koyo, don haka akwai iyaka kan yawan da ya kamata a bayar.
    3. Sample: Ina ganin akwai matsala a nan: yara da yawa ba su da lokaci saboda taimakon iyali bayan makaranta.
    4. Sample: Na yarda da wannan damuwar, amma duk da haka, za mu iya bayar da ƙaramin aikin gida kawai kwana biyu a mako.
    5. Sample: A taƙaice dai, mun yarda a gwada ƙaramin aikin gida na kwana biyu a mako na tsawon wata guda.

    Sashi na C (Sample writing)

    Matsayina shi ne ya kamata iyalinmu su fara raba aikin gida daidai gwargwado a tsakanin kowa. Ba koyaushe ba ne aiki ke rabuwa daidai a yanzu, kuma wannan yana damuna. Kanina ya ce ina ganin akwai matsala a nan: kowa yana da lokaci daban-daban saboda makaranta da aiki. Na yarda da wannan damuwar, amma duk da haka, ina ganin za mu iya rarraba aiki bisa lokacin da kowa ke da shi, ba daidai adadi ba. Bugu da ƙari, za mu iya sake tattaunawa a ƙarshen wata guda don ganin ko tsarin yana aiki. A taƙaice dai, iyalinmu ya yarda a gwada sabon tsarin rabon aiki na wata guda, sannan a sake dubawa.

    Sample closing statement: A taƙaice dai, iyalin ya yarda a gwada sabon tsarin rabon aiki na tsawon wata guda kafin yanke shawara ta ƙarshe.

    Other accurate, well-organized discussions that state a position, qualify a claim, raise and answer a respectful challenge, and close with a genuine shared statement are equally acceptable.

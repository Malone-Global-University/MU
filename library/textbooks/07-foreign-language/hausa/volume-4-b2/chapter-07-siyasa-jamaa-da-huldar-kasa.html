Sashe na 2 — Unit II: Jama’a da Bayanai (Public Life and Information)
7

Siyasa, Jama’a, da Hulɗar Ƙasa

B2–C1 Development · Chapter 7 of 20, Unit II Chapter 2 of 5 · Speak and Write Hausa: Advanced Functional Hausa

Chapter Overview

Chapter 7 continues Unit II by moving from media analysis toward civic and public life. Learners will discuss government services, public policy, participation in civic life, and civic responsibility, while continuing to develop the imperfective/progressive/habitual TAM frame and the negative TAM pattern first introduced in Chapter 6. Both systems remain at the Introduced/Developed level in this chapter and will continue developing through Chapters 8 and 9 before consolidating fully at Chapter 10, the Unit II assessment. Noun gender and plural tracking continues for new civic vocabulary, and market/service repair formulas from earlier volumes recur here in the context of asking a government office or official for clarification.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe government services and public policies using the imperfective TAM frame to talk about what is currently being done.
  2. Describe recurring civic events, such as elections or public meetings, using the habitual TAM frame.
  3. State what a policy or service does not yet guarantee or provide, using the negative TAM pattern.
  4. Record the gender and plural form of new civic and government-related nouns as part of active vocabulary learning.
  5. Ask a government office or official for clarification or repetition, recycling market/service repair formulas in a civic context.
  6. Discuss, in simple terms, a civic responsibility or form of public participation, in speech and in writing.

Communication Scenario

Amina visits the local government office to ask about a new civic registration program mentioned in the news report from Chapter 6. A civil servant explains what the office is currently doing, what happens every year around election time, and what the program does not yet cover. Amina asks for clarification twice, using the same repair formulas she and Sani used when discussing the radio report.

  1. Domain: asking about and discussing a government service, public program, or civic responsibility at a local office or in conversation with a colleague.
  2. Developed skill: describing ongoing government action and recurring civic events, denying or qualifying what a program covers, and requesting repetition from an official.
  3. Continued grammar development: imperfective/progressive/habitual TAM frames and negative TAM patterns (Introduced/Developed, continuing from Chapter 6), and gender/plural tracking for new nouns (Introduced/Developed).
  4. No unscheduled focus construction, prepositional system, dialect mixture, or productive Ajami writing is introduced in this chapter.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Careful, deliberate register in official speech

Civil servants and officials speaking in a professional capacity often use a slower, more deliberate pace than casual conversation, with clear phrase-final tone to signal the end of one point before moving to the next. This supports careful listening for lists of steps, requirements, or exceptions, all of which are common in civic and official contexts. No new consonant or vowel-length contrast is introduced; this chapter continues to apply tone and rhythm control already built across Chapters 1–6 to a careful, official register.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter continues developing the imperfective/progressive/habitual TAM frame and the negative TAM pattern introduced in Chapter 6, still at the Introduced/Developed level, consistent with the Volume IV Verb/TAM/Aspect Matrix. This chapter adds explicit practice with the habitual meaning for recurring civic events. Both systems continue through Chapters 8 and 9 and are formally assessed in Chapter 10.

7.1 Imperfective and habitual meaning in civic contexts

Use the imperfective TAM frame to describe a government action currently underway, and the habitual frame to describe something that recurs regularly, such as an election or a yearly public meeting.

  • Gwamnati na fara sabon shirin rajistar zaɓe. The government is starting a new voter-registration program.
  • Ana yin zaɓe a ƙasar nan kowace shekara huɗu. Elections are held in this country every four years.

    • 7.2 Negative TAM patterns with civic claims

    Use the negative TAM pattern to state that a government program does not yet cover something, or that a step has not yet been taken.

  • Sabon shirin bai game da mutanen da ba su cika shekara ashirin ba. The new program does not cover people who are not yet twenty years old.
  • Har yanzu ba a buɗe ofishin rajista a wannan yankin ba. The registration office has not yet opened in this area.
    • Language Note — A right versus a service actually provided

    Just as Chapter 6 distinguished an attributed claim from a confirmed fact, civic language distinguishes a stated right (haƙƙi) from a service that is actually and currently provided (using the imperfective) or not yet provided (using the negative TAM pattern). A law may state that a right exists without a corresponding service yet being available everywhere. Keep this distinction in mind when discussing government programs.

    Common learner error: Do not use the completive/perfective TAM (introduced in Volume III and consolidated through Chapter 5) to describe a civic program that is still ongoing or only partially implemented. Gwamnati ta buɗe ofishin rajista ("The government opened the registration office," completed) is not the same claim as Gwamnati na buɗe ofisoshin rajista ("The government is opening registration offices," ongoing).

    Model Dialogue

    Yanayin hali: Amina ta je ofishin gwamnati domin tambaya game da sabon shirin rajistar zaɓe da aka ji labarinsa a rediyo.

    (Setting: Amina goes to the government office to ask about the new voter-registration program mentioned on the radio.)

    AMINA Sannu. Ina so in san game da sabon shirin rajistar zaɓe.Hello. I would like to know about the new voter-registration program.
    MA’AIKACI To. Gwamnati na fara shirin ne domin ƙara yawan masu rajista kafin zaɓe.Well. The government is starting the program in order to increase the number of registered voters before the election.
    AMINA Ban ji sarai ba. Za ka iya maimaita wancan sashe?I didn't hear clearly. Can you repeat that part?
    MA’AIKACI Tabbas. Ina cewa, ana yin rajista domin duk wanda ya cika shekara ashirin ya iya jefa ƙuri’a a zaɓe.Of course. I said, registration is being done so that everyone who is twenty years old can vote in the election.
    AMINA To, shin shirin ya game da mutanen da suke zaune a ƙauyuka kuma?Well, does the program cover people who live in villages too?
    MA’AIKACI Har yanzu ba a buɗe ofishin rajista a wasu ƙauyuka ba, amma ana shirin buɗe su nan gaba.The registration office has not yet opened in some villages, but there are plans to open them in the future.
    AMINA Kai, ban gane ba. Za ka iya sake bayyana wannan?Oh, I don't understand. Can you explain that again?
    MA’AIKACI Ina nufin cewa, a yanzu, mutanen ƙauye za su iya zuwa babban gari domin yin rajista, har sai an buɗe ofisoshi a ƙauyukansu.I mean that, for now, village residents can come to the main town to register, until offices are opened in their own villages.

    Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

    1. Why does the government say it is starting the voter-registration program?
    2. What does the civil servant say about the minimum age for registering?
    3. What has not yet happened in some villages?
    4. What can village residents do in the meantime?

    Writing Workshop

    1. Karanta misalai da hankali. Reread Sections 7.1–7.2 and the Model Dialogue.
    2. Zaɓi shiri. Choose a real or invented government service or civic program (health, education, registration, or a public utility).
    3. Tsara sashi uku. Plan three short parts: what the government is currently doing, who is eligible or what happens regularly, and one detail the program does not yet cover.
    4. Rubuta taƙaitaccen bayani. Draft an 80–120 word explanation in Boko, using imperfective, habitual, and negative TAM frames accurately.
    5. Duba jerin bincike. Check your draft against the checklist below.
    6. Rubuta siffa ta ƙarshe. Produce a clean final version suitable for submission.

    Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

    • The explanation clearly states what the government is currently doing.
    • At least one recurring or habitual detail uses the habitual TAM frame correctly.
    • At least one unconfirmed or not-yet-provided detail uses the negative TAM pattern correctly.
    • New nouns are recorded with correct gender and plural where relevant.
    • Special characters (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ, ƴ) and apostrophe/glottal conventions are used accurately.
    • The explanation does not turn a stated right into a service that is confirmed as already available everywhere.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Civic participation across Hausa-speaking communities

    Civic participation, including voting, attending public meetings, and contacting local officials, takes different practical forms across the many countries and regions where Hausa is spoken, reflecting different national institutions, election systems, and levels of rural and urban infrastructure. Learners should not assume that one country's civic procedures apply everywhere, and should treat differences in access, such as the distance to a registration office, as a common and reasonable topic of everyday civic conversation rather than a sign of dysfunction.

    Regional Variation — Terms for government offices and officials

    Titles for local government offices and officials can vary between Nigeria, Niger, and other Hausa-speaking regions, as well as between urban and rural administrative structures. The terms used in this chapter represent a common, broadly understandable instructional standard; learners should expect some local variation in official titles when interacting with real institutions.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Treating a stated right as a service already received

    Error: Assuming that because a law states a right (haƙƙi) exists, the corresponding service is already available everywhere, as Amina initially assumes about village registration.

    Correction: Distinguish a stated right from a currently available service, and use the negative TAM pattern (Section 7.2) to state clearly what is not yet available.

    Common Mistake — Confusing a one-time action with a recurring civic event

    Error: Using the imperfective TAM frame alone to describe an event, such as an election, that recurs on a regular schedule.

    Correction: Use the habitual TAM frame (Section 7.1) for events that recur regularly, such as Ana yin zaɓe kowace shekara huɗu, reserving the imperfective for a single action currently in progress.

    Common Mistake — Overgeneralizing one office's limitation to the whole program

    Error: Concluding that a program does not exist at all because one office or village has not yet implemented it.

    Correction: State precisely what has not yet happened in a specific place, as the civil servant does in the Model Dialogue, rather than making a broader negative claim than the evidence supports.

    Answer Key / Amsoshi

    Tambayoyin fahimta na tattaunawa (Dialogue comprehension)

    1. The government says it is starting the program to increase the number of registered voters before the coming election.
    2. The civil servant says anyone who is twenty years old can register to vote.
    3. The registration office has not yet opened in some villages.
    4. Village residents can travel to the main town to register in the meantime.

    Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

    (1) The government is starting the program to increase the number of registered voters before the coming election. (2) Anyone who is twenty years old is eligible to register. (3) The passage does not confirm when registration offices will open in some villages. (4) Village residents can travel to the main town to register for now.

    Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

    1. The government is currently starting a new voter-registration program across the state.
    2. Anyone who is twenty years old has the right to register in order to vote in the coming election.
    3. The notice does not confirm that registration offices are open in every village; in the meantime, residents of villages without an office can travel to the main town to register.

    Sashi na B (Grammar)

    1. Sample: Ana yin zaɓen kansila kowace shekara biyar. Ana gudanar da babban taron jama’a kowane wata a garinmu.
    2. Sample: Shirin bai game da mutanen da ba su cika shekara ashirin ba. Har yanzu ba a buɗe sabon asibiti a wannan yankin ba.
    3. Sample: A stated civic right is something a law says a person is entitled to, marked with words like haƙƙi, while a currently available service is something the imperfective TAM frame shows is actually being provided right now. Civic language keeps these separate so that residents know what they are entitled to versus what they can actually access today.

    Sashi na C (Sample writing)

    Ma’aikatar ilimi na fara sabon shirin rajistar yara don shiga makarantar firamare. Duk yaron da ya cika shekara shida yana da haƙƙin shiga makaranta kyauta. Ma’aikatan gwamnati na aiki a manyan garuruwa, kuma ana yin rajista kowace shekara kafin fara zangon karatu. Amma har yanzu ba a buɗe rassan rajista a dukan ƙauyuka ba. Iyayen da ke zaune a irin waɗannan ƙauyukan za su iya zuwa babban gari domin yin rajista.

    Other accurate, well-organized explanations that correctly distinguish ongoing, recurring, and unconfirmed details are equally acceptable.

Rubutun Ilimi

B2–C1 Development · Chapter 17 of 20, Unit IV Continues · Speak and Write Hausa: Advanced Functional Hausa
Unit IV Continues — Academic and Analytical Writing

Chapter Overview

Chapter 17 builds directly on Chapter 16. There, learners met sequencing connectors (sannan, sai, bayan, kafin, tun), reason/plan connectors (saboda, domin, tunda), comparison connectors (fiye da, kamar), and basic reference-tracking strategies at the Introduced level, and used them to narrate how the office's policy on employee rights developed over time. This chapter develops the same three connector systems and reference tracking to a fuller, more independent level of control, and adds a new communicative target: organizing connected sentences into an analytical paragraph that states a main idea, supports it with reasons and examples, and closes with a conclusion. Learners will summarize a short text, respond to a prompt in writing, and produce a short analytical paragraph, continuing the Amina, Ibrahim, and Salamatu storyline as the office now prepares a short written report reflecting on its policy history for a staff newsletter.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use sequencing, reason/plan, and comparison connectors with greater independence across longer stretches of connected writing and speech.
  2. Track reference to people, places, and events across a full paragraph without unnecessary repetition or ambiguity.
  3. Identify the main idea of a short Hausa text and state it in a single sentence.
  4. Summarize a short text in two to three sentences, in your own words.
  5. Organize a short paragraph with a clear main idea, supporting reasons or examples, and a conclusion.
  6. Write a short analytical response (four to six sentences) to a direct question about a text or situation.

Communication Scenario

Salamatu, having heard the full story of the office's rights policy in Chapter 16, suggests that Amina and Ibrahim write a short piece for the staff newsletter explaining the policy's history and why it matters. Their editor asks for a piece with a clear main idea, supported by reasons and a brief comparison of past and present, ending with a short concluding thought — not simply a retelling of events. Amina and Ibrahim must now organize what they narrated aloud in Chapter 16 into a written paragraph that makes an argument, not just a timeline.

  1. Domain: a short analytical paragraph and a written response for a workplace newsletter, building on the Chapter 16 narrative.
  2. Target skill: stating a main idea, organizing supporting reasons and comparisons, summarizing, and writing a short analytical response.
  3. Grammar status: sequencing, reason/plan, and comparison connectors, along with reference tracking, move from Introduced toward Developed in this chapter; paragraph organization for analytical writing is newly Introduced.
  4. No unscheduled grammar beyond the Developed level for the three connector systems and reference tracking, and no paragraph-organization demand beyond main idea, support, and conclusion.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Pausing at paragraph boundaries

When reading an analytical paragraph aloud, learners should mark a longer pause and a fuller pitch fall at the end of the main-idea sentence, a lighter pause between supporting reasons, and a clear final fall at the concluding sentence. This helps a listener hear the paragraph's structure — main idea, support, conclusion — rather than one long unbroken string of clauses. No new consonant or vowel-length feature is introduced in this chapter; this is a continuation of the phrase-level pacing practiced in Chapter 16.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops the Chapter 16 connector systems and reference tracking toward independent control, and introduces paragraph-level organization for analytical writing. No new connector words are introduced.

17.1 Stating a main idea

An analytical paragraph opens with a sentence that states its main idea clearly, before any narrative detail is given. This sentence is often signaled with a ganina ("in my opinion") when the main idea is a personal judgment, or stated as a plain fact when it is not.

  • A ganina, canjin da ofishinmu ya yi kan haƙƙin ma’aikata babban ci gaba ne. In my opinion, the change our office made regarding employee rights is a major step forward.
  • Babban ra’ayin wannan rahoto shi ne cewa sauraron ma’aikata ya kawo sauyi mai kyau ga ofishi. The main idea of this report is that listening to employees brought a positive change to the office.

    • 17.2 Supporting a main idea with reasons and comparison

    Once the main idea is stated, supporting sentences use the Chapter 16 connectors — especially saboda, domin, bugu da ƙari, and fiye da/kamar — to give reasons, add a further point, and compare past and present in support of that idea.

  • Na yi imani da haka saboda ma’aikata da yawa yanzu sun fi jin daɗin aiki fiye da yadda suke a da. I believe this because many employees now enjoy their work more than they used to.
  • Bugu da ƙari, tunda ofishi ya fara tuntuɓar ma’aikata kafin yanke kowace shawara, an rage rikici a wurin aiki. Furthermore, since the office started consulting employees before every decision, conflict at work has decreased.

    • 17.3 Concluding a paragraph

    A concluding sentence, often introduced by a taƙaice dai, restates the main idea in different words rather than simply stopping the narrative.

  • A taƙaice dai, sauraron ma’aikata da girmamawa ga bambancinsu sun kawo sauyi mai amfani ga kowa. In short, listening to employees and respecting their differences brought a change that benefits everyone.

    • 17.4 Summarizing a text

    To summarize, state only the most important main idea and one or two key supporting points, in your own words, without repeating full sentences from the original or adding new information.

  • A taƙaice, labarin ya nuna yadda ofishi ya canza ƙa’idarsa saboda ma’aikata sun tayar da tambaya, kuma yanzu ana kula da bambancin yanki fiye da da. In short, the account shows how the office changed its policy because employees raised a question, and it now attends to regional variation more than before.

    • 17.5 Reference tracking across a paragraph

    A full analytical paragraph is longer than the two- or three-sentence examples in Chapter 16, so learners must track reference across five or more sentences. Once the office, the policy, or a person has been introduced, use a pronoun, a subject marker, or a short form such as ofishin or ƙa’idar instead of the full description, returning to a fuller noun phrase only when introducing a new element (for example, moving from the office's policy to employees' reactions).

    Language Note — From story to argument

    Chapter 16 asked learners to tell what happened, in order. Chapter 17 asks learners to use some of that same material to support a point. The events do not change, but their purpose does: instead of narrating for its own sake, the writer selects and orders events, reasons, and comparisons to support a stated main idea. This is the central skill of academic and analytical writing.

    Common learner error: Do not open an analytical paragraph with narrative detail ("Da farko, ofishinmu bai da ƙa’ida...") before stating the main idea. State the main idea first, then use the narrative and connectors from Chapter 16 as support.

    Model Dialogue

    Yanayin hali: Amina, Ibrahim, da Salamatu suna tattaunawa kan yadda za su tsara gajeren rahoto game da tarihin ƙa’idar ofishi don jaridar ma’aikata.

    (Setting: Amina, Ibrahim, and Salamatu discuss how to organize a short report on the office's policy history for the staff newsletter.)

    SALAMATU Kafin mu fara rubutu, me ya kamata mu ce shi ne babban ra’ayin rahotonmu?Before we start writing, what should we say is the main idea of our report?
    AMINA A ganina, babban ra’ayin shi ne cewa sauraron ma’aikata ya kawo sauyi mai kyau ga ofishi.In my opinion, the main idea is that listening to employees brought a positive change to the office.
    IBRAHIM Na yarda da haka. Yanzu, wace dalilai za mu bayar domin tallafawa wannan ra’ayin?I agree with that. Now, what reasons should we give to support this idea?
    AMINA Za mu iya cewa, saboda ma’aikata sun tayar da tambaya, sai aka fara nazari, sannan aka canza ƙa’idar.We can say that, because employees raised a question, a review began, and then the policy changed.
    SALAMATU Bugu da ƙari, za mu iya kwatanta yadda ofishi yake yau da yadda yake a da, ko ba haka ba?Furthermore, we could compare how the office is today with how it was before, couldn't we?
    IBRAHIM Daidai ne. Yau, ofishi na kula da bambancin yanki fiye da yadda ya kasance shekaru biyar da suka wuce. Wannan zai zama misali mai kyau.Exactly. Today, the office attends to regional variation more than it did five years ago. That will make a good example.
    SALAMATU Kuma yaya za mu kammala rahoton?And how should we conclude the report?
    AMINA A taƙaice dai, za mu iya cewa sauraron ma’aikata da girmamawa ga bambancinsu sun kawo sauyi mai amfani ga kowa.In short, we could say that listening to employees and respecting their differences brought a change that benefits everyone.
    IBRAHIM Wannan zai zama kammalawa mai kyau. Yanzu za mu iya tsara rahoton daga babban ra’ayi, zuwa dalilai, zuwa kammalawa.That will make a good conclusion. Now we can organize the report from main idea, to reasons, to conclusion.

    Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

    1. What does Amina propose as the report's main idea?
    2. What two events do Amina and Ibrahim give as reasons supporting the main idea?
    3. What comparison does Salamatu suggest adding?
    4. How do the three colleagues plan to conclude the report?

    Guided Practice

    Aikin fahimta — Recognition: Reread the Model Dialogue and label each sentence as main idea, reason, comparison, or conclusion.

    Aikin zaɓi — Selection: Choose the correct connector or phrase (a ganina, saboda, bugu da ƙari, fiye da, a taƙaice dai) to complete six sentences drawn from a short model paragraph.

    Aikin tsari — Ordering: Arrange five scrambled sentences (main idea, two reasons, a comparison, a conclusion) into a well-organized paragraph.

    Aikin taƙaitawa — Summarizing: Read a six-sentence passage and write a two-sentence summary in your own words.

    Speaking Practice

    With a partner, take turns stating a main idea about something familiar (a class, a habit, a routine), then adding one reason using saboda or domin.

    Tattaunawa — Communicative task

    With a partner, orally construct a four-part mini analytical statement (main idea, reason, comparison, conclusion) about a change you have both noticed in a familiar place or organization.

    Madadin karatu kai tsaye — Solo alternative

    Record yourself presenting a short analytical statement (five to six sentences) with a main idea, two supporting reasons, one comparison, and a conclusion, about a topic of your choice.

    Writing Workshop

    1. Karanta misalai da hankali. Reread Sections 17.1–17.5, the Model Dialogue, and the Reading Practice passage.
    2. Zaɓi babban ra’ayi. Choose a main idea about a change you know well (a family, community, school, or workplace change, including the one you narrated in Chapter 16 if you wish).
    3. Tsara sakin layi. Plan a paragraph: one main-idea sentence, two to three supporting sentences (using at least one reason/plan connector and one comparison), and one concluding sentence.
    4. Rubuta daftarin farko. Draft a 100–150 word analytical paragraph in Boko.
    5. Duba jerin bincike. Check your draft against the checklist below.
    6. Rubuta siffa ta ƙarshe. Produce a clean final version, then write a separate two-sentence summary of your own paragraph.

    Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

    • The paragraph opens with a clear main-idea sentence, not with narrative detail.
    • At least one reason/plan connector (saboda, domin, or tunda) supports the main idea.
    • At least one comparison (fiye da or kamar) supports the main idea.
    • At least one sequencing connector from Chapter 16 is used naturally if narrative detail is included.
    • Reference to people, places, or events is tracked without unnecessary repetition across the whole paragraph.
    • The paragraph closes with a concluding sentence, ideally introduced with a taƙaice dai.
    • Special characters (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ, ƴ) and apostrophe/glottal conventions are used accurately.
    • No unscheduled grammar beyond the Developed level of this chapter's connector and reference systems is required to understand the paragraph.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Stating an opinion respectfully in writing

    Phrases such as a ganina ("in my opinion") allow a writer to state a view clearly while marking it as a personal judgment rather than an unquestionable fact. In many Hausa professional and educational settings, this kind of clear but modest framing is valued: the writer takes a position and supports it, but leaves room for readers, colleagues, or elders to hold a different view.

    Regional Variation — Shared analytical tools, varied local content

    The specific topics learners choose to analyze — a family, a school, a market, a workplace — will differ widely across Hausa-speaking regions, but the main-idea, reason, comparison, and conclusion structure introduced in this chapter is a broadly shared way of organizing analytical writing across educational contexts in Nigeria, Niger, and other Hausa-speaking areas.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Opening with narrative instead of a main idea

    Error: Beginning an analytical paragraph with "Da farko..." and a full timeline of events before stating any main idea.

    Correction: State the main idea first, as in A ganina, canjin da ofishinmu ya yi babban ci gaba ne, then use the Chapter 16 narrative material as supporting detail.

    Common Mistake — Summarizing by copying full sentences

    Error: Producing a "summary" that repeats whole sentences from the original passage rather than restating the main idea in the learner's own words.

    Correction: Identify only the main idea and one or two key supporting points, and restate them briefly and independently, as in the model summary in Section 17.4.

    Common Mistake — Ending without a conclusion

    Error: Stopping a paragraph after the last supporting detail, with no sentence that restates the main idea.

    Correction: Add a concluding sentence, often introduced with a taƙaice dai, that restates the main idea in different words, as in A taƙaice dai, sauraron ma’aikata da girmamawa ga bambancinsu sun kawo sauyi mai amfani ga kowa.

    Answer Key / Amsoshi

    Tambayoyin fahimta na tattaunawa (Dialogue comprehension)

    1. Amina proposes that listening to employees brought a positive change to the office.
    2. They cite employees raising a question, which led to a review, and then a policy change.
    3. Salamatu suggests comparing how the office is today with how it was before.
    4. They plan to conclude by saying that listening to employees and respecting their differences brought a change that benefits everyone.

    Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

    (1) The headteacher's main idea is that the change in teaching methods is a major step forward. (2) The first reason is that children have gotten better results since the new method began. (3) The additional point is that parents began attending meetings to share their views with teachers, which had not happened before. (4) The headteacher concludes that the new teaching method and parental cooperation brought a good change to the whole school.

    Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

    1. The writer's main idea is that building the main road near the town is the chief reason the market grew.
    2. The reasons given are that people from other towns started coming to shop after the road was built, and traders began offering a wider range of goods to satisfy new customers.
    3. The writer compares today's market, twice as large, to how it was twenty years ago.
    4. Sample summary: The market grew mainly because a new road brought in outside customers, prompting traders to expand what they sold. As a result, the market is now twice the size it was twenty years ago.

    Sashi na B (Grammar)

    1. Sample: A ganina, koyon sabon harshe yana da amfani sosai ga rayuwar mutum.
    2. Sample: Na yi imani da haka saboda yana buɗe wa mutum sabbin dama. Bugu da ƙari, mutumin da ya koyi harshe biyu yana fahimtar duniya fiye da wanda ya san harshe ɗaya kawai.
    3. Sample: A taƙaice dai, koyon sabon harshe babban jari ne ga kowa.
    4. Sample: Ofishinmu ya canza ƙa’idarsa bara. Sannan, ta fara aiki daga watan Janairu. Yanzu, ma’aikata na bin sabuwar ƙa’idar duk lokacin aiki.

    Sashi na C (Sample writing)

    A ganina, buɗe ƙaramin kanti a gidanmu babban ci gaba ne ga iyalinmu. Na yi imani da haka saboda tun bayan da muka fara sayar da kayan gona a kasuwa maimakon amfani da su a gida kawai, kuɗin shigarmu ya ƙaru. Bugu da ƙari, yanzu iyalinmu na siyar da kayayyaki iri-iri fiye da yadda muke yi shekaru biyar da suka wuce. Duk da haka, kamar yadda kakana ya koya mana, muhimmancin taimakon juna a cikin iyali bai canza ba. A taƙaice dai, kantin ya kawo sauyi mai kyau, amma bai canza dabi’unmu na kirki ba.

    Sample two-sentence summary: Buɗe kanti ya ƙara wa iyali kuɗin shiga da kayayyaki iri-iri. Duk da haka, iyalin ya ci gaba da riƙe al’adar taimakon juna.

    Other accurate, well-organized paragraphs that state a main idea, support it with reasons and comparison, track reference clearly, and conclude appropriately are equally acceptable.

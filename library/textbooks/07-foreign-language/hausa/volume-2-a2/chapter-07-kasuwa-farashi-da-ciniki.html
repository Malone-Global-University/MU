Kasuwa, Farashi, da Ciniki

Markets, Prices, and Bargaining · Speak and Write Hausa: Expanding Foundations for Independent Use

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common market goods, containers, and bargaining-related vocabulary in Boko.
  2. Ask and answer questions about price using nawa ("how much").
  3. Continue using existence, possession, and quantity expressions (akwai, yana/tana da, da yawa, kaɗan) in a market context.
  4. Use object pronouns after common market verbs such as siya (buy) and sayar da (sell).
  5. Expand negative statements across existence, possession, and continuous/habitual frames introduced in Chapter 6.
  6. Politely bargain, accept, decline, and confirm a purchase.
  7. Read a short price list and a market-related message.
  8. Write a short paragraph comparing what is available in two markets or stalls.

Communication Scenario

Bala goes to Kasuwar Kurmi to buy ingredients for Hajiya Talatu's kitchen, following up on the invitation from Chapter 6. He needs to check prices, ask what is available, buy several items, and politely bargain with the sellers. This chapter continues Unit II by extending the existence, possession, and quantity clauses from Chapter 6 into more independent market interactions, and by introducing object pronouns and price questions with nawa.

  1. New vocabulary in this chapter centers on markets, goods, containers, and prices.
  2. New grammar introduces price questions with nawa and object pronouns used with common market verbs.
  3. The akwai/yana-tana da system and the ina/kana/tana + verbal noun frame from Chapter 6 continue to develop and now cover a wider set of verbs, including siya and sayar da.
  4. All vocabulary and grammar from Chapters 1–6 remain available for recycling.
  5. Communication repair remains an active, expected part of every interaction.

Pronunciation and Boko Focus

7.1 Tone contrast: farashi versus other similar words

Practice the tone pattern of farashi (price) carefully, keeping the syllables even and avoiding English stress transfer onto the first syllable. Also review the vowel length in kuɗi (money), which contrasts with shorter vowels in nearby words.

Sound Focus — The special consonant ɗ in kuɗi and rangwame-related vocabulary

Continue practicing the implosive ɗ in kuɗi. This sound is produced further back and with a distinct implosive quality compared to an ordinary English "d." Say kuɗi slowly, then at a natural pace, and compare it to gida (house) from earlier chapters, which uses an ordinary "d."

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter extends the existence, possession, and continuous/habitual frames from Chapter 6 into market interactions, and introduces price questions with nawa and object pronouns used with market verbs. All forms below build directly on the subject-marker system introduced in Chapter 6.

7.2 Asking price: nawa

Nawa asks "how much" or "how many." It typically appears at the end of a question about price or quantity.

  • Shinkafa nawa ne kilo ɗaya?How much is one kilo of rice?
  • Wannan buhu, nawa ne?This sack, how much is it?
  • Tulu biyu na mai, nawa ne duka?Two tins of oil, how much is that altogether?
Language Note — Nawa and the copula ne

Nawa combines with the identifying copula ne (recycled from Chapter 1) in most price questions. The pattern is: [item] + nawa + ne. This is the same identifying-clause pattern used for basic identity statements, now applied to price.

7.3 Object pronouns with market verbs

To avoid repeating a noun, use an object pronoun after verbs such as siya (buy) and sayar da (sell). The forms below attach directly after the verb.

Independent PronounObject FormExample
ni (I)niYa sayar mini da shi. (he sold it to me)
kai (you, m.)kaZan siya maka shi. (recognition: future, glossed)
shi (he/it, m.)shiIna son sayan shi. (I want to buy it)
ita (she/it, f.)taNa siya ta jiya. (recognition: past, glossed)
su (they)suIna son sayan su duka. (I want to buy all of them)

At this stage, object pronouns are productive only in the present-based frames already active from Chapter 6 (ina son + verbal noun). Examples using the future or past marker are included for recognition and glossed accordingly; they are not yet productive targets.

7.4 Expanded negation with akwai, yana/tana da, and the continuous frame

Negation forms introduced in Chapter 6 (babu, ba ... da) now extend to a wider range of market contexts, including negating the continuous/habitual frame.

  • Babu shinkafa mai arha yau.There isn't cheap rice today.
  • Ba mu da isasshen kuɗi domin buhu biyu.We don't have enough money for two sacks.
  • Ba na siyan nama mai tsada.I'm not buying expensive meat.
Language Building Block
price question + existence/possession clause + negation + bargaining formula
Shinkafa nawa ne kilo ɗaya? Akwai mai arha, amma ba ni da kuɗi mai yawa. Yi mini rangwame, don Allah.
How much is one kilo of rice? There is a cheap kind, but I don't have a lot of money. Please give me a discount.

Atisayen A — Cika gibi (Fill in the blank): Complete each sentence with the correct price question, existence/possession clause, or negation.

  1. Wannan tulu na mai, ___ ne?
  2. ___ shinkafa mai arha a kasuwa yau, amma ___ nama.
  3. Ni ___ (ba ... da) kuɗi mai yawa domin buhu biyu.
  4. Ina son siyan ___ (object pronoun for "su," referring to 'ya'yan itace).

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Bala ya je Kasuwar Kurmi domin sayan kayan girki domin Hajiya Talatu.

(Setting: Bala goes to Kasuwar Kurmi to buy cooking ingredients for Hajiya Talatu.)

BALA Sannu. Shinkafa nawa ne kilo ɗaya?Hello. How much is one kilo of rice?
MAI SAYARWA Sannu, ɗan'uwa. Kilo ɗaya, naira ɗari biyar ne.Hello, brother. One kilo is five hundred naira. (recognition: number-price formula, glossed)
BALA Ya yi tsada sosai. Akwai wani irin mai arha?That's too expensive. Is there a cheaper kind?
MAI SAYARWA Akwai, amma ba shi da kyau sosai. Wannan mai kyau ne, don haka farashinsa haka yake.There is, but it isn't very good. This one is good quality, so its price is like that. (recognition: possessive suffix "farashinsa," glossed)
BALA To, yi mini rangwame, don Allah. Ina son siyan buhu, ba kilo ɗaya kaɗai ba.Okay, please give me a discount. I want to buy a sack, not just one kilo.
MAI SAYARWA To, domin kai, buhu ɗaya, naira dubu goma ne kawai.Okay, for you, one sack is only ten thousand naira. (recognition: number formula, glossed)
BALA Yi haƙuri, ka maimaita farashin, don Allah? Ban ji sarai ba.Sorry, could you repeat the price, please? I didn't hear it clearly.
MAI SAYARWA Naira dubu goma, don buhu ɗaya.Ten thousand naira, for one sack.
BALA Mai kyau. Ina kuma son sayan tulu biyu na mai da kayan lambu kaɗan.Good. I also want to buy two tins of oil and a little vegetables.
MAI SAYARWA Babu matsala. Akwai kayan lambu da yawa a can, amma ba ni da mai a yau.No problem. There are plenty of vegetables over there, but I don't have oil today.
BALA Babu matsala. Sai na gode.No problem. Thank you, then.

Vocabulary notes for the dialogue: naira ɗari biyar, naira dubu goma = five hundred naira, ten thousand naira (recognition, number-price formulas built on recycled numerals, not assessed for production as full sentences); farashinsa = its price (recognition, possessive-suffix form; the productive possessive system for nouns is scheduled later in the volume).

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. What does Bala first ask about, and how does the seller respond?
  2. Why does Bala ask for a cheaper option?
  3. What does Bala decide to buy instead of just one kilo?
  4. What does Bala do when he doesn't hear the price clearly?
  5. What is unavailable at the end of the dialogue?

Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

  • At least one price question or statement with nawa appears accurately.
  • At least one existence or possession clause (akwai, yana/tana da, or negative) appears accurately.
  • At least one negation form from Chapter 6 or 7 is used correctly.
  • At least one object pronoun is used correctly with a market verb.
  • Prior systems (identity clauses, pronouns, continuous/habitual frame, quantity words) remain accurate where they appear.
  • No grammar beyond this chapter's scope appears (no productive future za, no productive past na/ka/ta/mu/ku/sun, no productive possessive-suffix system).
  • Special characters (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ) and the apostrophe/glottal convention are used accurately.

Speaking Practice

With a partner, take turns pointing to pictures or objects and asking Nawa ne? Practice answering with recycled numbers and quantity words.

Ciniki — Bargaining role-play

Role-play a market interaction as in the Model Dialogue. Ask about price, ask for a discount, and use at least one moment of communication repair.

Madadin karatu kai tsaye — Solo alternative

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Bargaining as a communicative skill

Polite bargaining is a normal and expected part of many market interactions in Hausa-speaking communities, though the degree of bargaining, tone, and expectations vary by market, item, region, and relationship between buyer and seller. Bargaining is generally a friendly, respectful exchange rather than a confrontation.

Regional Variation — Currency and measurement terms

Currency names, common measurement units (such as tulu and buhu), and typical market prices vary between Nigerian and Nigerien Hausa-speaking communities, and change over time. This course presents forms most useful for general communication in Kano-based standard usage; other regional currency and measurement terms are equally legitimate and should be adjusted for local context.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Placing nawa incorrectly

Error: *Nawa shinkafa ne? (placing nawa before the noun)

Correction: Shinkafa nawa ne? — In price questions, the item being priced normally comes first, followed by nawa ne.

Common Mistake — Using an independent pronoun instead of an object form

Error: *Ina son sayan shi ita. (mixing an object form with an unneeded independent pronoun)

Correction: Ina son sayan ta. — Use only the object form after the verbal noun; do not add a second independent pronoun for the same object.

Answer Key / Amsoshi

Tambayoyin fahimta na tattaunawa (Dialogue comprehension)

  1. Bala asks the price of one kilo of rice; the seller says it is five hundred naira.
  2. He asks because he thinks the price is too expensive.
  3. Bala decides to buy a whole sack instead of one kilo.
  4. Bala politely asks the seller to repeat the price.
  5. Oil is unavailable at the end of the dialogue.

Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

(1) The speaker goes to the market every Tuesday. (2) They usually buy rice and vegetables, and buy meat only when it is cheap. (3) The price of rice is too expensive today. (4) Because they don't have much money this week, they buy only a small amount, though the seller gives them a small discount.

Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

  1. Rice is five hundred naira per kilo, soup is three hundred naira per tin, and vegetables are one hundred naira for a small amount.
  2. Meat is missing today; fish is available instead.
  3. They should ask directly for a discount.
  4. The seller asks customers to come in the morning, because they do not have much time in the evening.

Sashi na B (Chapter Review grammar)

  1. Sample: Kayan lambu nawa ne? Akwai kifi da yawa a kanti. Ina son siyan su. (Other accurate sentences are acceptable.)
  2. Babu nama mai arha a kasuwa.
  3. Ina son siyan shi.

Sashi na C (Sample writing)

A Kantin Amina, akwai shinkafa da kayan lambu da yawa, amma babu nama a yau. Farashin shinkafa, kilo ɗaya, naira ɗari biyar ne. A wani kanti kusa, farashinsa ya yi tsada sosai, don haka ba ni da isasshen kuɗi domin siyan can. Ina son siyan kayan lambu daga Kantin Amina, don akwai su da yawa kuma mai arha ne.

Other accurate, well-formed passages meeting the stated requirements are equally acceptable.

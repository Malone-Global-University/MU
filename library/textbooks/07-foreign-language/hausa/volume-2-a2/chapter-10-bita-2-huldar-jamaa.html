Unit II Assessment · Speak and Write Hausa: Expanding Foundations for Independent Use

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Integrate the food, cooking, and hospitality vocabulary and patterns from Chapter 6 with the market, transport, and health domains that follow.
  2. Complete an independent market negotiation combining prices, quantities, and comparison, as developed in Chapter 7.
  3. Handle a transport and directions interaction, including delay and sequencing language, as developed in Chapter 8.
  4. Describe symptoms, give and receive advice, and use early continuous/habitual TAM frames, as developed in Chapter 9.
  5. Use existence, possession, relational-noun, object-pronoun, and negation patterns accurately across all four domains.
  6. Use communication-repair strategies naturally and appropriately in an unrehearsed interaction.
  7. Read and interpret a multi-domain community text combining service, market, transport, and health information.
  8. Write a connected paragraph that draws on vocabulary and grammar from at least two of the four Unit II domains.
  9. Demonstrate integrated listening, speaking, reading, and writing performance appropriate to the A2 range.

Communication Scenario

A week has passed since Bala's visit to the clinic in Chapter 9. He is feeling better and spends a busy Saturday running several errands: buying food for a family meal, bargaining at the market, catching transport across town, and stopping by the pharmacy to finish his course of medicine. This chapter does not introduce new vocabulary or grammar. Instead, it brings together the food, market, transport, and health/advice systems built across Chapters 6 through 9 into a single integrated unit of formal assessment.

  1. No new productive vocabulary or grammar is introduced in this chapter.
  2. All vocabulary and grammar from Chapters 6–9 are treated as active and expected for accurate use.
  3. Vocabulary and grammar from Chapters 1–5 remain available for recycling and are expected in supporting positions.
  4. Communication repair remains an active, expected, and assessed part of every interaction.
  5. Performance across listening, speaking, reading, and writing is formally evaluated in this chapter.

Pronunciation Review

This chapter does not introduce a new sound. Review the following features, all active since earlier chapters in this unit, and be ready to demonstrate them in the Speaking Assessment.

Sound Focus — Reviewing ɓ, ɗ, ƙ, and vowel length across the unit

Practice the implosive ɓ in zazzaɓi (fever) and baƙi (guests), the hooked ɗ in words recycled from earlier volumes, and the ejective ƙ in alƙawari (appointment) and farashi-related market vocabulary. Continue attending to vowel length contrasts in high-frequency words such as ciwo (pain) and kasuwa (market), and to even syllable rhythm across all four domains without English-style stress transfer.

Grammar and Sentence Pattern Review

No new grammar is introduced in this chapter. The exercises below integrate the existence/possession, relational-noun, object-pronoun, continuous/habitual, and negation systems developed across Chapters 6–9 and assess them together, as scheduled by the Volume II matrices at Chapter 10.

10.1 Integrated review: existence, possession, and relational nouns

  • Akwai kayan lambu da yawa a kasuwa yau.There are many vegetables at the market today.
  • Tasha tana kusa da kasuwa.The station is near the market.
  • Yana da ciwon kai bayan tafiya mai jinkiri.He has a headache after the delayed trip.

10.2 Integrated review: continuous/habitual and negation

  • Ba ni da isasshen kuɗi domin abincin yau.I don't have enough money for today's food.
  • Ina sayen kayan abinci kullum ranar Asabar.I buy groceries every Saturday, habitual.
  • Babu mota a tasha yanzu, don haka muna jira.There is no vehicle at the station now, so we are waiting.
Language Building Block — Integrated Unit II Frame
location/existence clause + price or symptom clause + advice or negotiation frame + repair request
Kasuwa tana kusa da tashar mota. Farashin kayan lambu ya yi tsada yau, kuma ina jin ciwon kai kaɗan. Ya kamata in huta bayan siyayya, don Allah ka maimaita farashin?
The market is near the bus station. The price of vegetables is high today, and I feel a slight headache. I should rest after shopping — could you repeat the price, please?

Atisayen Bita — Cika gibi (Review fill-in-the-blank): Complete each sentence using a pattern from Chapters 6–9.

  1. ___ (akwai) kayan lambu mai yawa a kasuwa. (There is/are)
  2. Mota ___ (existence, negative) a tasha yanzu.
  3. Ina ___ (habitual, to buy) abinci kullum da safe.
  4. Ya kamata ___ (ki, to a woman) sha ruwa mai yawa bayan tafiya.

Integrated Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Bala yana kasuwa domin siyan kayan abinci, sannan ya je tasha domin komawa gida.

(Setting: Bala is at the market buying groceries, then goes to the station to return home.)

MAI SAYARWA Sannu, me kake bukata yau?Hello, what do you need today?
BALA Ina son kayan lambu domin abincin dare. Nawa ne kilo ɗaya?I want vegetables for dinner. How much is one kilo?
MAI SAYARWA Farashi ya yi tsada kaɗan yau, amma zan iya rangwame maka kaɗan.The price is a bit high today, but I can give you a small discount.
BALA Yi haƙuri, ban ji sarai ba. Za ka maimaita farashin, don Allah?Sorry, I didn't hear clearly. Could you repeat the price, please?
MAI SAYARWA Naira ɗari biyar kilo ɗaya.Five hundred naira per kilo.
BALA To, na gode. Yanzu ina bukatar tafiya tasha domin komawa gida. Tasha tana nesa da nan?Alright, thank you. Now I need to go to the station to return home. Is the station far from here?
MAI SAYARWA A'a, tana kusa da kasuwa, amma akwai jinkiri a tashoshi da yawa yanzu.No, it's near the market, but there is delay at many stations right now.
BALA Na gode da bayanin. Ina jin ciwon kai kaɗan bayan siyayya, don haka zan huta a gida da zarar na dawo.Thank you for the information. I feel a slight headache after shopping, so I will rest at home as soon as I get back.

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. What does Bala want to buy, and for what occasion?
  2. What does Bala do when he doesn't hear the price clearly?
  3. What price does the seller give for one kilo?
  4. What does Bala learn about the station?
  5. What symptom does Bala mention at the end, and what does he plan to do about it?

Writing Assessment

  1. Karanta allon sanarwa da hankali. Reread the community notice board text and notice how it links market, transport, and health information using existence, possession, and relational-noun patterns.
  2. Tsarawa. Plan a short paragraph describing a Saturday that includes at least two of the four Unit II domains (food/hospitality, market, transport, health/advice).
  3. Rubuta. Write 100–130 words in Hausa. Include at least one existence/possession clause, one relational noun of place, one continuous/habitual frame, one negation, and one use of a formulaic advice or bargaining expression.
  4. Duba jerin bincike. Check your draft against the checklist below.
  5. Rubuta siffa ta ƙarshe. Produce a clean final version.

Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

  • At least two Unit II domains (food, market, transport, health/advice) appear connected in the paragraph.
  • At least one existence or possession clause (akwai, yana/tana da, or negative) appears accurately.
  • At least one relational noun of place (kusa da, gaba da, etc.) is used correctly.
  • At least one continuous or habitual frame is used correctly, with kullum if habitual.
  • At least one negation form from Chapters 6–9 is used correctly.
  • A formulaic advice or bargaining expression (ya kamata ka/ki..., a price question, or a polite request) appears naturally.
  • Prior systems (identity clauses, pronouns, numerals, time expressions) remain accurate where they appear.
  • No grammar beyond Chapters 1–9 appears (no productive future za, no productive past na/ka/ta/mu/ku/sun, no productive possessive-suffix system).
  • Special characters (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ) and the apostrophe/glottal convention are used accurately.

Speaking Assessment

Atisayen ƙa'ida — Cross-domain substitution drill

With a partner, take turns building sentences that link two domains, for example a market clause followed by a health clause, using the Integrated Unit II Frame as a model.

Rana Ɗaya a Birni — A Day in Town role-play

Role-play an unrehearsed sequence: bargain briefly at a market stall, ask about a delayed vehicle at a station, and mention a minor symptom with a piece of advice, as in the Integrated Dialogue. Include at least one moment of communication repair.

Madadin karatu kai tsaye — Solo alternative

Record yourself narrating a Saturday that includes at least two Unit II domains, describing what you bought or needed, how you traveled, and how you felt.

Culture and Communication Review

Culture in Context — Errands, hospitality, and health as connected community life

Across many Hausa-speaking communities, a single day may naturally combine market shopping, hosting or preparing for guests, arranging transport, and attending to health needs, often with flexible timing and mutual accommodation among family and neighbors. No single sequence of errands should be treated as the only typical pattern of daily life.

Regional Variation — Reviewing market, transport, and clinic terminology

As noted in Chapters 7 through 9, terms for market stalls, transport hubs, and health facilities vary between Nigerian and Nigerien Hausa-speaking communities and between urban and rural settings. This course continues to present Kano-based standard forms as the productive default; other regional terms remain equally legitimate.

Common Mistakes Review

Common Mistake — Confusing existence and possession across domains

Error: *Akwai ciwon kai. when describing one's own headache (using the impersonal existence frame instead of personal possession)

Correction: Ina da ciwon kai. — Use akwai for general existence ("there is/are"), and yana/tana/ina da for personal possession of a symptom, item, or quality, as reviewed in Chapters 6 and 9.

Common Mistake — Omitting relational nouns when locating a place

Error: *Tasha kasuwa. (juxtaposing two nouns without a relational noun)

Correction: Tasha tana kusa da kasuwa. — A relational noun such as kusa da (near) is required to connect two locations, as established in Chapter 8.

Answer Key / Amsoshi

Atisayen Bita (Grammar, Section 10)

  1. Akwai kayan lambu mai yawa a kasuwa.
  2. Mota babu a tasha yanzu. (also acceptable: Babu mota a tasha yanzu.)
  3. Ina sayen abinci kullum da safe.
  4. Ya kamata ki sha ruwa mai yawa bayan tafiya.

Tambayoyin fahimta na tattaunawa (Dialogue comprehension)

  1. Bala wants to buy vegetables for that evening's dinner.
  2. Bala politely asks the seller to repeat the price.
  3. The seller gives a price of five hundred naira per kilo.
  4. Bala learns that the station is near the market, but there is delay at many stations.
  5. Bala mentions a slight headache and plans to rest at home once he returns.

Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

(1) The speaker went to the market to buy food for guests coming that evening. (2) The price of vegetables was a bit high, but the speaker bought what was needed. (3) There was delay at the station, so the speaker waited a while. (4) After returning home, the speaker rested because of a slight headache before starting to cook for the guests.

Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

  1. The market is open Monday through Saturday, from seven in the morning until six in the evening.
  2. There is delay at the central station this week due to road repair; passengers should use the second station near the main market.
  3. Clinic staff are available until six in the evening.
  4. Someone who feels unwell while shopping should rest before returning home.

Sashi na B (Chapter Review grammar)

  1. Sample: Kasuwa tana kusa da tasha. (Other accurate sentences are acceptable.)
  2. Babu mota a tasha yanzu.
  3. Ina sayen kayan abinci kullum ranar Asabar.

Sashi na C (Sample writing)

A ranar Asabar, na je kasuwa domin siyan kayan abinci domin baƙi da za su zo gida. Kasuwa tana kusa da tashar mota. Farashin kayan lambu ya yi tsada kaɗan, amma na sayi abin da nake bukata. Bayan siyayya, na je tasha, amma babu mota na ɗan lokaci domin jinkiri. Ina jin ciwon kai kaɗan, don haka ya kamata in huta kafin in fara girki domin baƙin.

Other accurate, well-formed passages meeting the stated requirements are equally acceptable.

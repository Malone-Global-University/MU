Sashe na 4 — Unit IV: Shirye-shirye da Ra'ayi (Plans and Perspectives)
18

Matsala da Magani

Problems and Solutions · Speak and Write Hausa: Expanding Foundations for Independent Use

Chapter Overview

Chapter 18 continues Unit IV. In Chapter 17 you used tunda, idan, fiye da, and kamar to state and justify a preference, and continued tracking a named person across connected sentences. This chapter keeps those same systems active and applies them to a new communicative task: describing a problem clearly, proposing one or more solutions, and negotiating what to do next. No new grammar system is introduced. Instead, the same reason, condition, comparison, and reference-tracking forms continue developing toward consolidation, alongside a small set of new problem-and-solution vocabulary and the verb warware ("to solve, to untangle").

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe a familiar problem clearly using akwai matsala (there is a problem) and related expressions.
  2. Propose a solution using magani (solution, remedy) and the verb warware (to solve).
  3. Give a reason for a proposed solution using domin and tunda, continuing from Chapters 16–17.
  4. State a condition under which a different solution would be needed, using idan.
  5. Compare two possible solutions using fiye da and kamar.
  6. Offer a tentative suggestion using the new formulaic expression watakila (maybe, perhaps).
  7. Negotiate next steps and reach or defer a decision politely.
  8. Track a problem, person, or solution across several connected sentences without unnecessary repetition.
  9. Read and write a short, organized paragraph describing a problem and proposing a solution.
  10. Identify and self-correct common Unit IV errors in problem-solution language.

Communication Scenario

Amina and Bala's community group, last seen choosing a wedding venue in Chapter 17, now faces a new problem: the community hall they eventually agreed on has a leaking roof, and the wedding is only two weeks away. Over the course of this chapter, you will help describe the problem, propose solutions, and negotiate what the group should do.

  1. No new vocabulary domain beyond problems, solutions, and negotiation is required for this chapter.
  2. Reason and condition connectors (tunda, idan) and comparison expressions (fiye da, kamar) from Chapters 16–17 remain developing, not yet fully consolidated, and continue to appear in this chapter.
  3. Reference tracking across connected sentences, introduced in Chapter 16 and practiced in Chapter 17, is expected to continue without additional explanation of the underlying rule.
  4. Vocabulary and grammar from Units I, II, and III remain fully active and are expected to appear accurately.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Tone and vowel length in matsala, warware, and gaggawa

Pay attention to the tone pattern and vowel length of this chapter's key words: matsala, warware, and gaggawa. Note the doubled consonant in gaggawa, which must be held slightly longer than a single consonant, and the repeated syllable in warware, which should keep an even rhythm rather than speeding up on the second half of the word. As in earlier chapters, keep an even syllable rhythm across full sentences such as A takaice, akwai matsala kuma muna bukatar warware ta da sauri. This review consolidates pronunciation features already active since earlier chapters; no new sound or contrast is introduced here.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter continues developing the four systems active since Chapter 16 — reason and condition, comparison, reference tracking, and controlled reading/writing — and applies them to problems and solutions. No new connector or comparison expression is introduced. Completive/perfective, negation, focus, and prepositional systems from Units I–III remain fully active.

18.1 Describing a problem with akwai matsala

  • Akwai matsala a zauren al'umma: rufin yana yoyo.There is a problem at the community hall: the roof is leaking.
  • Matsalar ita ce, bikin zai yi kwana goma sha huɗu kacal.The problem is that the celebration is only fourteen days away.

Akwai matsala introduces the existence of a problem, continuing the existential pattern from earlier chapters. Matsalar ita ce... ("the problem is that...") is a useful frame for stating the specific nature of a problem before proposing a solution.

18.2 Continuing: Reason and condition with tunda and idan

Language Building Block — Proposing and Justifying a Solution
na gwammace / za mu iya + [solution], domin/tunda + [reason] idan + [condition clause], za mu warware matsalar ta wata hanya
Za mu iya gyara rufin da sauri, domin muna da lokaci kaɗan kacal.
We can fix the roof quickly, because we have very little time.
Idan ba a gama gyaran nan da nan, za mu warware matsalar ta wata hanya, kamar neman wani wuri na ɗan lokaci.
If the repair isn't finished right away, we'll solve the problem another way, such as finding a temporary place.

As in Chapters 16–17, tunda introduces a reason the listener already accepts, while idan introduces a genuine condition. Continue to use these connectors carefully, since they remain developing forms at this stage.

18.3 Continuing: Comparison with fiye da and kamar, and the new formulaic watakila

  • Neman ma'aikacin gyara rufi da sauri ya fi jira fiye da amfani.Finding a roof repairman quickly is more useful than waiting.
  • Watakila mu tambayi maƙwabta idan suna da wani wurin da za mu yi bikin.Maybe we should ask the neighbors if they have a place where we could hold the celebration.watakila = "maybe," softens a suggestion offered under uncertainty

Watakila ("maybe, perhaps") is a new formulaic expression that introduces a tentative suggestion without committing fully to it. It is introduced here as a formulaic opener, not as a fully analyzed grammar system, and pairs naturally with a following clause stating the suggestion.

18.4 Continuing: Reference tracking while negotiating a solution

When a problem or a proposed solution has already been named, later sentences should continue with a pronoun, an omitted subject, or a short agreement/disagreement expression rather than restating the full problem or solution.

  • Bala ya ba da shawarar neman ma'aikacin gyara rufi. Ya ce zai iya zuwa gobe da safe, kuma ba zai ɗauki lokaci mai tsawo ba.Bala suggested finding a roof repairman. He said he can come tomorrow morning, and it won't take a long time."Bala" named once; "ya" (he) tracks the reference afterward

Atisayen ƙa'ida — Grammar practice: Complete each sentence with the word or expression that fits.

  1. Akwai ___ (matsala/magani) a zauren al'umma, saboda rufin yana yoyo.
  2. ___ (Tunda/Idan) muna da ɗan lokaci kaɗan, dole mu yanke shawara da sauri.
  3. Gyara rufin ya fi neman wani wuri ___ (kamar/fiye da) sauƙi.
  4. ___ (Watakila/Maimakon) mu tambayi maƙwabta, don kada mu ɓata lokaci.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Amina da Bala sun gano cewa rufin zauren al'umma yana yoyo, kuma suna tattaunawa da Musa a kan yadda za a warware matsalar.

(Setting: Amina and Bala have discovered that the community hall's roof is leaking, and they are discussing with Musa how to solve the problem.)

AMINA Musa, akwai matsala. Rufin zauren al'umma yana yoyo, kuma bikin zai yi kwana goma sha huɗu kacal.Musa, there's a problem. The community hall's roof is leaking, and the wedding is only fourteen days away.
MUSA Wannan abin gaggawa ne. Ba mu da lokacin jira. Wace shawara kake da ita, Bala?This is an urgent matter. We don't have time to wait. What suggestion do you have, Bala?
BALA Na gwammace mu nemi ma'aikacin gyara rufi da sauri, domin gyara rufin ya fi neman wani wuri sauƙi.I prefer that we quickly find a roof repairman, because fixing the roof is easier than finding another place.
AMINA Idan ma'aikacin bai samu ba nan da nan, watakila mu tambayi maƙwabta idan suna da wani wuri.If a repairman isn't found right away, maybe we should ask the neighbors if they have another place.
MUSA Shawarar ka da kyau, amma mu tsai da hankali kafin mu yanke shawara. Bala, za ka iya kiran ma'aikacin yau?Both suggestions are good, but let's stay calm before we decide. Bala, can you call the repairman today?
BALA Zan yi haka. Idan ya amsa da sauri, ba za mu bukaci shawarar Amina ba.I'll do that. If he responds quickly, we won't need Amina's suggestion.
MUSA A takaice, mu jira sakamakon kiran Bala, sa'an nan mu tuntuɓi maƙwabta idan ya zama dole.In short, let's wait for the result of Bala's call, then contact the neighbors if it becomes necessary.

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. What is the problem Amina describes at the start?
  2. What solution does Bala propose, and why does he prefer it?
  3. What alternative does Amina suggest, and with what condition?
  4. What does Musa ask Bala to do?
  5. What is the group's plan, stated at the end?

Saƙo — Matsalar Titi a Unguwarmu (Message: The Road Problem in Our Neighborhood)

Writing Workshop

  1. Karanta saƙon da hankali. Reread the road-problem message and notice how it links a problem, a reason, a comparison, and a condition around a proposed course of action.
  2. Tsarawa. Plan a short paragraph describing a problem you know about (personal, family, or community), with at least one proposed solution, one reason, one comparison, and one condition.
  3. Rubuta. Write 120–150 words in Hausa. Include a problem statement with akwai matsala or matsalar ita ce, a proposed solution with magani or warware, one reason with domin or tunda, one comparison with fiye da or kamar, one condition with idan, and at least one instance of reference tracking.
  4. Duba jerin bincike. Check your draft against the checklist below.

Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

  • A problem is clearly stated with akwai matsala or matsalar ita ce.
  • A solution is proposed using magani, warware, or a related expression.
  • A reason is expressed with domin or tunda.
  • A comparison is expressed with fiye da or kamar.
  • A condition is expressed with idan.
  • At least one named person or problem is tracked afterward with a pronoun or omitted subject, not repeated by name.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Negotiating solutions calmly

When a problem arises within a family or community group, many Hausa speakers favor phrases such as tsai da hankali ("stay calm, settle your mind") and a takaice ("in short") to keep negotiation focused and unhurried, even when a matter is genuinely urgent. This preference for composure during negotiation is common but not universal, and direct, fast-moving problem-solving is also appropriate depending on the relationship, setting, and severity of the problem.

Regional Variation — Problem-solving vocabulary

Some communities and registers use additional expressions for describing urgency, proposing solutions, or deferring a decision beyond matsala, magani, and watakila. This course presents the productive default forms for this level; further regional and formal-register vocabulary is introduced for recognition in later volumes.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing magani (solution) with its health-domain meaning

Error: *Magani na matsalar shine likita. (using magani as if it could only mean medicine, producing an unclear sentence)

Correction: Maganin matsalar shine mu nemi ma'aikacin gyara rufi. — Magani means "solution" or "remedy" in a general sense, and in the problem-solution domain it should be followed by a clause or noun phrase naming the actual solution, not treated as automatically referring to medicine.

Common Mistake — Repeating a name instead of tracking reference

Error: *Bala ya kira ma'aikaci. Bala ya bayyana matsalar. Bala ya jira amsa. (repeating the name in every sentence)

Correction: Bala ya kira ma'aikaci. Ya bayyana masa matsalar, sa'an nan ya jira amsa. — Once a person has been named, continue with a pronoun, an omitted subject, or a connector, as is natural in connected Hausa narration.

Tambayoyin fahimta na tattaunawa (Dialogue comprehension)

  1. The roof of the community hall is leaking, and the wedding is only fourteen days away.
  2. Bala proposes quickly finding a roof repairman, because fixing the roof is easier than finding another place.
  3. Amina suggests asking the neighbors for another place, on the condition that a repairman isn't found right away.
  4. Musa asks Bala to call the repairman today.
  5. They will wait for the result of Bala's call, then contact the neighbors if necessary.

Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

(1) Halima discovers there is no water in their house since early morning. (2) Sule thinks the problem might be bigger because the neighbors also have no water. (3) If the problem isn't solved by evening, they will need to buy water from elsewhere. (4) Halima compares that buying water is more expensive than tap water.

Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

  1. The neighborhood is facing a badly damaged road after last week's rain.
  2. Some residents prefer to raise money themselves because waiting for the government could take a long time.
  3. If the road isn't repaired within a month, the problem will worsen, especially during rain.
  4. The neighborhood leaders have decided to call a meeting to discuss solutions together.

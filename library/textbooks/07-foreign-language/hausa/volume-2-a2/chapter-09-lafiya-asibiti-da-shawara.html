Lafiya, Asibiti, da Shawara

Health, Clinic, and Advice · Speak and Write Hausa: Expanding Foundations for Independent Use

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common body parts, symptoms, and clinic-related vocabulary in Boko.
  2. Describe how you feel using existence and possession expressions (akwai, yana/tana da) applied to symptoms.
  3. Continue using relational nouns and prepositions of place from Chapter 8 to describe the location of a clinic or pharmacy.
  4. Use early continuous/habitual TAM frames to describe ongoing symptoms and regular health habits.
  5. Use object pronouns with health-related verbs in present-based frames.
  6. Extend negation to symptoms, ability, and advice statements.
  7. Give and receive simple practical advice using a formulaic advice frame.
  8. Read a short clinic notice and a set of health advice statements.
  9. Write a short paragraph describing symptoms and the advice received.

Communication Scenario

After the delayed trip home in Chapter 8, Bala wakes up the next morning feeling unwell. He decides to visit a nearby clinic, describe his symptoms to the nurse, and follow her advice. This chapter continues Unit II by extending the existence, possession, relational-noun, and negation patterns developed so far into the health domain, and by introducing early continuous/habitual TAM frames for describing ongoing symptoms and routines.

  1. New vocabulary in this chapter centers on the body, symptoms, the clinic, and medicine.
  2. New grammar introduces early continuous/habitual frames and a formulaic advice structure.
  3. The akwai/yana-tana da system, relational nouns of place, object pronouns, and negation from Chapters 6–8 continue to develop and now cover the health domain.
  4. All vocabulary and grammar from Chapters 1–8 remain available for recycling.
  5. Communication repair remains an active, expected part of every interaction.

Pronunciation and Boko Focus

9.1 Tone and vowel length: zazzaɓi and ciwo

Practice the reduplicated word zazzaɓi (fever) carefully, paying attention to the doubled consonant and to the implosive ɓ, reviewed from earlier chapters. Also practice the vowel length in ciwo (pain), which contrasts with the shorter vowel in related forms you will meet in later volumes.

Sound Focus — The implosive ɓ in zazzaɓi and gajiya's vowel rhythm

Continue practicing the implosive ɓ, reviewed from earlier chapters, in zazzaɓi (fever). The sound is produced with a slight inward pull of air, distinct from an ordinary English "b." Also practice the smooth vowel rhythm of gajiya (tiredness), keeping even syllable timing rather than English-style stress on the first syllable.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter continues the existence, possession, relational-noun, object-pronoun, and negation patterns from Chapters 6–8, and introduces early continuous/habitual TAM frames together with a formulaic advice structure. All forms below build directly on the subject-marker system introduced in Chapter 6.

9.2 Describing symptoms with akwai and yana/tana da

Symptoms are commonly described using the same existence and possession patterns used for objects and people in earlier chapters.

  • Ina jin zazzaɓi tun jiya.I have felt feverish since yesterday.
  • Yana da ciwon kai.He has a headache.
  • Tana da tari da gajiya.She has a cough and fatigue.
Language Note — Ciwo combined with body-part nouns

Ciwo (pain) combines directly with a body-part noun to name a specific ache, as in ciwon kai (headache) and ciwon ciki (stomachache). This follows the same possessive-relational pattern reviewed with other noun combinations in earlier chapters. Do not translate these compounds word-for-word without the accompanying example.

9.3 Early continuous/habitual frames for symptoms and routines

The continuous frame using ina/yana/tana + verbal noun, reviewed from Chapter 6, now extends to describing ongoing symptoms. A habitual sense is expressed with the same frame plus a frequency word such as kullum.

SubjectContinuous FrameExample
ni (I)ina + verbal nounIna jin ciwon ciki. (I am feeling stomach pain)
shi (he)yana + verbal nounYana huta kullum bayan aiki. (He rests every day after work, habitual)
ita (she)tana + verbal nounTana shan magani kullum da safe. (She takes medicine every morning, habitual)
mu (we)muna + verbal nounMuna son ganin likita. (recognition: desire frame, glossed)

Object pronouns remain productive only in the present-based frames already active, as in Chapter 8. The frame combined with kullum is productive for habitual meaning; future and past forms remain for recognition only and are glossed accordingly.

9.4 The formulaic advice frame: ya kamata ka/ki...

Advice is given using the formulaic expression ya kamata ka/ki... (you should...), followed by a verb. Learners use this as a memorized communicative unit at this stage; its internal grammar is not analyzed until a later volume.

  • Ya kamata ka huta yau.You should rest today.
  • Ya kamata ki sha ruwa mai yawa.You should drink plenty of water.
  • Ya kamata ka ga likita idan zazzaɓi bai tafi ba.You should see a doctor if the fever doesn't go away.

9.5 Negation with symptoms, ability, and advice

Negation forms from Chapters 6–8 (babu, ba ... da, negative continuous/habitual) now extend to symptoms, ability, and advice statements.

  • Ba ni da lafiya sosai yau.I am not very well today.
  • Ba na iya cin abinci da kyau.I am not able to eat well.
  • Babu magani a gida domin ciwon kai.There is no medicine at home for a headache.
Language Building Block
symptom clause + existence/possession clause + advice frame + repair request
Ina jin zazzaɓi da ciwon kai tun jiya. Ba ni da magani a gida. Ya kamata ka gaya mini abin da zan yi, don Allah?
I have felt feverish with a headache since yesterday. I don't have medicine at home. Could you tell me what I should do, please?

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Bala ya je asibiti domin ba shi da lafiya bayan tafiyarsa ta jiya.

(Setting: Bala goes to the clinic because he is unwell after yesterday's trip.)

MA'AIKACIYA Sannu. Me ke damun ka yau?Hello. What is bothering you today?
BALA Sannu. Ina jin zazzaɓi da ciwon kai tun jiya da yamma.Hello. I have felt feverish with a headache since yesterday evening.
MA'AIKACIYA Kana da ciwon ciki ko tari kuma?Do you have stomach pain or a cough too?
BALA A'a, babu tari, amma ina jin gajiya sosai.No, there's no cough, but I feel very tired.
MA'AIKACIYA To. Ya kamata ka huta kwana biyu, kuma ka sha ruwa mai yawa. Ga magani domin zazzaɓi.Alright. You should rest for two days, and drink plenty of water. Here is medicine for the fever.
BALA Na gode. Yaya zan sha maganin, kowace rana?Thank you. How should I take the medicine, every day?
MA'AIKACIYA I, ka sha shi kullum da safe da kuma da yamma, har kwana biyar.Yes, take it every day in the morning and evening, for five days. (recognition: extended dosage instruction, glossed)
BALA Yi haƙuri, za ki maimaita, don Allah? Ban ji sarai ba.Sorry, could you repeat that, please? I didn't hear it clearly.
MA'AIKACIYA Ka sha magani kullum, safe da yamma. Idan zazzaɓi bai tafi ba bayan kwana biyu, ya kamata ka koma asibiti.Take the medicine every day, morning and evening. If the fever doesn't go away after two days, you should come back to the clinic.
BALA Na gode sosai. Za ni gida yanzu domin huta.Thank you very much. I'm going home now to rest. (recognition: future frame, glossed)

Vocabulary notes for the dialogue: har kwana biyar = for five days (recognition, extended duration expression built on recycled numerals, not assessed for production this chapter); za ni gida = I'm going home (recognition, future frame, glossed to support comprehension of the closing line only).

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. What two symptoms does Bala describe first?
  2. Does Bala have a cough?
  3. What advice does the nurse give Bala?
  4. What does Bala do when he doesn't hear the dosage instructions clearly?
  5. What should Bala do if the fever does not go away after two days?

Writing Workshop

  1. Karanta sanarwar asibiti da hankali. Reread the clinic notice and notice how it combines symptom vocabulary, existence/possession clauses, and the advice frame.
  2. Tsarawa. Plan a short paragraph describing a time you or someone you know felt unwell and the advice that was given.
  3. Rubuta. Write 90–120 words in Hausa. Include at least one symptom expression, one existence/possession clause, one continuous/habitual frame, one negation, and one use of ya kamata ka/ki....
  4. Duba jerin bincike. Check your draft against the checklist below.
  5. Rubuta siffa ta ƙarshe. Produce a clean final version.

Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

  • At least one symptom expression (ina jin ciwo, ina jin zazzaɓi, or a ciwon + body-part compound) appears accurately.
  • At least one existence or possession clause (akwai, yana/tana da, or negative) appears accurately.
  • At least one continuous or habitual frame is used correctly, with kullum if habitual.
  • At least one negation form from Chapters 6–9 is used correctly.
  • The advice frame ya kamata ka/ki... is used at least once, correctly matched to the addressee's gender.
  • Prior systems (identity clauses, pronouns, relational nouns of place, object pronouns, price questions with nawa) remain accurate where they appear.
  • No grammar beyond this chapter's scope appears (no productive future za, no productive past na/ka/ta/mu/ku/sun, no productive possessive-suffix system).
  • Special characters (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ) and the apostrophe/glottal convention are used accurately.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Clinics, pharmacies, and home remedies

In many Hausa-speaking communities, a person who feels unwell may consult a nearby clinic, a pharmacist, or a trusted family member, and may also use a home remedy alongside or before seeking formal medical care. Describing symptoms clearly and asking for advice is a normal part of these interactions across settings, and no single approach should be treated as the only legitimate response to illness.

Regional Variation — Names for clinics and health workers

Terms for clinics, hospitals, and health workers, as well as typical appointment practices, vary between Nigerian and Nigerien Hausa-speaking communities and between urban and rural settings. This course presents forms most useful for general communication in Kano-based standard usage; other regional terms are equally legitimate and should be adjusted for local context.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing yana da and yana jin for symptoms

Error: *Yana da jin ciwon kai. (mixing the possession frame with the sensation frame)

Correction: Yana jin ciwon kai. or Yana da ciwon kai. — Use jin + symptom for a sensation ("he feels..."), or yana/tana da + symptom noun for possession ("he has..."), but do not combine both frames in the same clause.

Common Mistake — Wrong gender agreement in ya kamata ka/ki

Error: *Ya kamata ka huta, said to a woman (using the masculine form ka when addressing a female addressee)

Correction: Ya kamata ki huta. — The pronoun following ya kamata must agree with the gender of the person being addressed: ka for a male addressee, ki for a female addressee.

Tambayoyin fahimta na tattaunawa (Dialogue comprehension)

  1. Bala describes feeling feverish with a headache since the previous evening.
  2. No, Bala does not have a cough.
  3. The nurse advises Bala to rest for two days, drink plenty of water, and take medicine morning and evening.
  4. Bala politely asks the nurse to repeat the dosage instructions.
  5. Bala should return to the clinic if the fever does not go away after two days.

Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

(1) The speaker first noticed a fever and a headache. (2) The speaker went to the clinic the following morning. (3) The nurse advised resting for two days and drinking plenty of water. (4) The speaker feels a little better today but is still resting at home until tomorrow.

Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

  1. The notice mentions fever, headache, and cough.
  2. No, an appointment is not required for emergencies.
  3. The clinic operates from eight in the morning until six in the evening.
  4. Someone unwell at night should go to the main hospital in the town center.

Sashi na B (Chapter Review grammar)

  1. Sample: Ina jin ciwon kai. Ba ni da lafiya sosai. Ya kamata ka huta yau. (Other accurate sentences are acceptable.)
  2. Ba ni da lafiya sosai yau.
  3. Tana shan magani kullum da safe.

Sashi na C (Sample writing)

Jiya da safe, na fara jin ciwon kai da gajiya sosai. Ba ni da lafiya, don haka na je asibiti kusa da gidana. Ma'aikaciyar jinya ta ce ya kamata na huta kwana biyu kuma in sha ruwa mai yawa. Ta ba ni magani, kuma ina shan sa kullum, safe da yamma. Babu tari, amma ina jin gajiya har yanzu. Ya kamata na koma asibiti idan ban ji daɗi ba bayan kwana biyu.

Other accurate, well-formed passages meeting the stated requirements are equally acceptable.

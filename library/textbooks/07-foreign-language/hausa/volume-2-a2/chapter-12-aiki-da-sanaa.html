Aiki da Sana'a

Work and Occupations · Speak and Write Hausa: Expanding Foundations for Independent Use

Chapter Overview

Chapter 12 continues Unit III by moving from the classroom of Chapter 11 into the workplace. You will talk about jobs, skills, schedules, duties, and workplace needs, using the same systems introduced in Chapter 11 — focus with ne/ce, prepositions and relational nouns, negation and TAM patterns for obligation and inability, and basic sentence-linking with kuma, amma, and domin — and applying them productively to a new domain. No new grammar system is introduced in this chapter; instead, the Chapter 11 systems are developed further through additional contexts, as scheduled by the Volume II matrices at Chapter 12. Everything from Chapters 1–11 remains active and continues to appear throughout.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify and use vocabulary for jobs, workplaces, skills, schedules, and duties.
  2. Ask and answer questions about occupation, workplace, and daily work tasks.
  3. Use ne/ce focus constructions to emphasize a specific piece of information about work.
  4. Use prepositions and relational nouns to express time, cause, and comparison in workplace contexts.
  5. Form negative statements and questions about ability, obligation, and past inability related to work tasks.
  6. Link two short sentences into one connected sentence using kuma, amma, or domin.
  7. Describe a job, a workplace duty, or a scheduling problem in a short spoken and written passage.
  8. Read a short job advertisement or workplace notice and identify key details.
  9. Recognize that occupational identity and workplace norms vary across Hausa-speaking communities.

Communication Scenario

Bala's classmate Hauwa is a tailor who also cleans offices part-time, and she is thinking about applying for a new job at a shop near the learning center. Over the course of the week, Hauwa discusses her current work schedule with a co-worker, asks about the requirements for the new job, and explains to Bala why she is considering the change. Throughout the week she uses the systems introduced in Chapter 11: emphasizing exactly what she means with focus structures, connecting her reasons with relational nouns, and explaining what she can, cannot, and must do at work.

  1. New vocabulary in this chapter is drawn from the work and occupations domain.
  2. No new grammar system is introduced in this chapter; the Chapter 11 systems (focus with ne/ce, expanded prepositions and relational nouns, negation/TAM for obligation and inability, and sentence-linking with kuma/amma/domin) continue to develop.
  3. Vocabulary and grammar from Chapters 1–11 remain active and are expected to appear accurately.
  4. Communication repair remains an active and expected part of every interaction.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Apostrophe/glottal conventions in sana'a and ma'aikaci

Practice the glottal stop marked by the apostrophe in sana'a (occupation) and ma'aikaci (worker), keeping the two syllables on either side of the glottal stop clearly separated rather than gliding through them as a single English-style sound. Pay attention to vowel length in alhaki (duty), and continue building even syllable rhythm across longer compound expressions such as ma'aikacin gwamnati, giving each syllable comparable weight.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter does not introduce a new grammar system. Instead, it develops the four systems introduced in Chapter 11 — focus with ne/ce, prepositions and relational nouns, negation/TAM patterns for obligation and inability, and sentence-linking — through additional contexts drawn from work and occupations, consistent with the Developed (D) status of these systems at Chapter 12 in the Volume II matrices.

12.1 Focus with ne and ce in workplace contexts

The same focus pattern from Chapter 11 continues here: the emphasized element comes first, followed by ne (masculine/plural) or ce (feminine).

  • Dinki ne sana'ata.Tailoring is my occupation. (emphasis on "tailoring")[focused noun] + ne + [rest of clause]
  • Hauwa ce ta fi kowa gogewa a shagon.It's Hauwa who has more experience than anyone at the shop.
  • Ranar Litinin ne nake aiki daga safe.It's on Monday that I work from morning.

As in Chapter 11, use ne/ce when answering a question word directly, especially wa (who), me/mece (what), and yaushe (when), so that the answer sounds sharp and natural rather than a full restated sentence.

12.2 Prepositions and relational nouns: time, cause, and comparison at work

  • Kafin fara aiki, ina share ma'aikata.Before starting work, I clean the office.
  • Ban samu albashi mai kyau ba saboda alhakina ba ya da yawa.I don't get a good salary because my responsibilities aren't many.
  • Wannan sana'a ta fi wancan alhaki.This occupation has more responsibility than that one.
  • Ina son sana'ar dinki fiye da tsabta.I like tailoring more than cleaning.
  • Zan nemi aiki wajen shagon kayan lantarki maimakon shagon abinci.I will look for work at the electronics shop instead of the food shop.

The relational noun wajen (recycled from Chapter 8) and the newly active maimakon continue to precede the noun phrase they connect to, following the same word-order pattern as kafin and bayan.

12.3 Negation and TAM: obligation, ability, and inability at work

PatternMeaningExample
Ba na iya...I cannot / am not able to...Ba na iya yin dinki da kyau ba tukuna. — I can't sew well yet.
Bai kamata...bashould not...Bai kamata ki bar aiki kafin lokacin hutu ba. — You shouldn't leave work before break time.
Dole ne ka/ki...must, have to...Dole ne ka kai rahoto ga shugaba kowace rana. — You must give a report to the boss every day.
Ban taɓa...baI have never...Ban taɓa rasa aiki ba. — I have never lost a job.

These patterns are the same forms introduced in Chapter 11, Section 11.3, now applied to workplace situations. As before, most negative statements use the frame ba... ba surrounding the predicate, while dole ne expresses obligation without negation.

12.4 Linking two ideas: kuma, amma, and domin

The same three linking words from Chapter 11 continue to connect short sentences about work.

  • Ina dinki da kyau, kuma ina son koyon sabuwar fasaha.I sew well, and I want to learn a new skill.
  • Na yi aiki tuƙuru, amma ban samu albashi mai kyau ba.I worked hard, but I didn't get a good salary.
  • Ina son sabon aiki domin lokacin aiki ya fi dacewa.I want the new job because the work hours suit me better.

Kuma (and), amma (but), and domin (because) remain the only productive linking words at this stage, as established in Chapter 11.

Language Building Block — Focus + Reason Frame (recycled from Chapter 11)
[focused element] + ne/ce + clause + domin/saboda + reason
Sabon aiki ne nake so, domin albashinsa ya fi na yanzu.
It's the new job that I want, because its salary is better than my current one.

Atisayen ƙa'ida — Grammar practice: Complete each sentence using a pattern from Chapters 11–12.

  1. ___ (focus, ne/ce) ta fi kowa fasaha wajen dinki — Hauwa ko Bala? Choose one and write a full focused sentence.
  2. Ban iya ___ (kammala) aikin ba domin lokaci bai isa ba.
  3. ___ (dole ne) ku kai rahoto ga shugaba kafin ƙarshen mako.
  4. Wannan sana'a ___ (fi) wancan alhaki.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Hauwa tana magana da abokin aikinta, Musa, a lokacin hutu na aiki.

(Setting: Hauwa is talking with her co-worker Musa during a work break.)

MUSA Sannu Hauwa, na ji za ki nemi sabon aiki. Gaskiya ne?Hello Hauwa, I heard you're going to look for a new job. Is that true?
HAUWA I, gaskiya ne. Shagon kayan lantarki ne nake son yin aiki a wurin, maimakon nan.Yes, it's true. It's the electronics shop where I want to work, instead of here.
MUSA Don me? Ba ka son aikin nan?Why? Don't you like the work here?
HAUWA Ina son aikin, amma albashi ne matsalar. Ban samu albashi mai kyau ba, domin alhakina ya yi yawa fiye da abin da nake samu.I like the work, but salary is the problem. I don't get a good salary, because my responsibilities are more than what I earn.
MUSA Yi haƙuri, ban ji sarai ba — albashi ne matsalar, ko lokacin aiki ne?Sorry, I didn't hear clearly — is salary the problem, or is it the work hours?
HAUWA Albashi ne. Kafin in nemi sabon aiki, dole ne in tabbatar da fasahata ta dinki tana da kyau, domin wannan shago yana bukatar gogaggen ma'aikaci.It's the salary. Before I apply for the new job, I must make sure my sewing skill is good, because that shop needs a skilled worker.
MUSA To, na tabbata za ki iya. Ke ce mafi gogewa a nan.Well, I'm sure you can. You're the one with the most experience here.

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. Where does Hauwa want to work instead of her current job?
  2. What problem does Hauwa say she has with her current job?
  3. Why does Musa ask Hauwa to repeat herself?
  4. What must Hauwa make sure of before applying for the new job?
  5. What does Musa say about Hauwa's experience?

Speaking Practice

Atisayen ƙa'ida — Focus substitution drill

With a partner, take turns asking and answering questions about work using ne/ce focus, for example: "Wace sana'a ce ta fi ka/ki gogewa?" (Which occupation do you have the most experience in?)

Tattaunawa — Job change role-play

Role-play an unrehearsed conversation with a co-worker about considering a new job: state what the problem with the current job is, describe one duty or skill required, and explain your plan. Include at least one moment of communication repair.

Madadin karatu kai tsaye — Solo alternative

Record yourself describing your own work or a job you would like to have: what your duties are, what you must do, and what you cannot yet do.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Skilled trades and diverse occupations

Occupations such as tailoring, trading, government service, teaching, and skilled crafts are all common and respected forms of work across Hausa-speaking communities, and combining more than one source of income, as Hauwa does, is a familiar pattern for many workers. No single occupation, whether agricultural, artisanal, professional, or civil-service, should be treated as more authentically Hausa than another.

Regional Variation — Workplace terms

Some workplace vocabulary varies between Nigerian and Nigerien Hausa-speaking communities, and terms for certain job titles or employment sectors may differ depending on local administrative and economic structures. This course presents the Kano-based standard forms above as the productive default; other regional terms remain equally legitimate and are noted for recognition.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Wrong focus particle for gender

Error: *Sana'a ne mai wahala. (using ne with a feminine noun)

Correction: Sana'a ce mai wahala. — Use ce when the focused noun is feminine, as with sana'a, and ne for masculine or plural reference.

Common Mistake — Confusing obligation and ability

Error: *Ba na iya kai rahoto kowace rana. when the intended meaning is "I must give a report every day" (using inability instead of obligation)

Correction: Dole ne in kai rahoto kowace rana. — Use ba na iya only for genuine inability, and dole ne for obligation, as introduced in Chapter 11 and reinforced here.

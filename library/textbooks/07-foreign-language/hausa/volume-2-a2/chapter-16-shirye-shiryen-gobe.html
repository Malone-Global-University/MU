Sashe na 4 — Unit IV: Shirye-shirye da Ra'ayi (Plans and Perspectives)
16

Shirye-shiryen Gobe

Plans and Intentions · Speak and Write Hausa: Expanding Foundations for Independent Use

Chapter Overview

Chapter 16 opens Unit IV. Building on the education, work, money, and narration domains you consolidated in Chapters 11 through 15, you will now talk about the future: plans, arrangements, hopes, and the constraints that shape them. Future and intention expressions with za and niyya, first introduced in Chapters 6 through 10, are now consolidated and expected to support normal chapter production. This chapter develops four systems that will remain active through the rest of the unit: connected narration and sequencing at a broader discourse level, reasons and comparisons that justify a plan, reference tracking so that a person or plan can be discussed across several sentences without needless repetition, and integrated reading and writing in a controlled, organized register.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe future plans, arrangements, hopes, and intentions using consolidated za and niyya patterns.
  2. Give a reason for a plan using domin and the new connector tunda (since, given that).
  3. State a condition or constraint on a plan using the new connector idan (if).
  4. Make a simple comparison between two plans or options using fiye da (more than) and kamar (like, as).
  5. Track a single person or plan across several connected sentences without repeating the name or noun unnecessarily.
  6. Read and write a short, organized paragraph describing plans, reasons, and constraints.
  7. Identify and self-correct common Unit IV errors in future-time and conditional language.
  8. Complete an integrated listening, speaking, reading, and writing task about personal or community plans.

Communication Scenario

Bala got the new position discussed in Chapter 15's assessment. Now his family, his coworkers, and his community group are all making plans of their own: for the coming month, for a wedding, for a community project, and for his own first months on the job. Over the course of this chapter, you will help narrate and discuss these plans, giving reasons, naming conditions, and comparing options.

  1. No new vocabulary domain beyond plans, arrangements, hopes, and constraints is required for this chapter.
  2. Future and intention expressions from Chapters 6–10 are treated as consolidated and should appear accurately without additional explanation.
  3. The new connectors idan, tunda, and the comparison expressions fiye da and kamar are introduced as developing (not yet fully consolidated) forms.
  4. Vocabulary and grammar from Units I, II, and III remain fully active and are expected to appear accurately.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Tone and vowel length in shiri, niyya, and buri

Pay attention to the tone pattern and vowel length of this chapter's key nouns: shiri, niyya, and buri. Listen for the doubled consonant in niyya, which is pronounced with a clearly lengthened y-glide rather than a single, quick sound. As in earlier chapters, practice the even syllable rhythm of full sentences such as Na yi niyyar zuwa makaranta, resisting the English habit of stressing only one syllable in a long word. This review consolidates pronunciation features already active since earlier chapters; no new sound or contrast is introduced here.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter consolidates future and intention expressions active since Chapter 6, and develops four systems scheduled for Unit IV: connected narration and sequencing at a broader level, reasons and comparisons for plans, reference tracking across discourse, and controlled organization in reading and writing. Completive/perfective, negation, focus, and prepositional systems from Units I–III remain fully active.

16.1 Consolidation: Future and intention with za and niyya

  • Za mu yi bikin aure a wata mai zuwa.We are going to hold the wedding celebration next month.
  • Na yi niyyar fara sabon aikina da kyau.I intend to start my new job well.

These patterns were introduced in Chapters 6–10 and are now expected to appear accurately without further explanation.

16.2 New: Reason and condition with tunda and idan

Language Building Block — Reason and Condition
tunda + [known fact], [plan clause] idan + [condition clause], [result/plan clause]
Tunda na sami sabon aiki, za mu iya biyan kuɗin makaranta.
Since I got a new job, we will be able to pay the school fees.
Idan an gama gina gida, za mu ƙaura a wata mai zuwa.
If the house-building is finished, we will move next month.

Tunda introduces a reason the listener already knows or accepts, similar to "since" in English. Idan introduces a condition, similar to "if." Both are new, developing connectors for this chapter; they are not yet expected to be fully consolidated, so occasional hesitation or repair is normal.

16.3 New: Simple comparison with fiye da and kamar

  • Wannan shirin ya fi ɗayan fiye da kyau.This plan is better than the other one.lit. "this plan exceeds the other in goodness"
  • Muna shirin yin aiki kamar yadda muka tsara.We plan to work just as we arranged.

Comparison in Hausa is often built on the verb fi ("to exceed") together with fiye da ("more than"), rather than an adjective ending as in English. Kamar introduces similarity or manner.

16.4 New: Reference tracking across connected sentences

When a person, plan, or place has already been named, Hausa speakers usually continue with a pronoun or simply omit the subject where the verb form makes the subject clear, rather than repeating the full noun in every sentence.

  • Bala ya sami sabon aiki. Ya yi murna sosai, kuma ya gaya wa iyalinsa nan take.Bala got a new job. He was very happy, and he told his family right away."Bala" named once; "ya" (he) tracks the reference in later clauses

Avoid repeating Bala in every sentence of a connected passage; doing so sounds unnatural and childlike in Hausa, just as constant name repetition would in English.

Atisayen ƙa'ida — Grammar practice: Complete each sentence with the connector or comparison expression that fits.

  1. ___ (tunda/idan) an gama aiki da wuri, za mu iya hutawa.
  2. Wannan gidan ya fi wancan ___ (kamar/fiye da) girma.
  3. ___ (idan/tunda) kuɗi ya isa, za mu sayi sabon abu.
  4. Ta shirya komai ___ (kamar/fiye da) yadda ta yi niyya.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Bala yana tattaunawa da 'yar uwarsa, Amina, kan shirye-shiryen bikin auren danginsu.

(Setting: Bala is discussing plans for their relative's wedding celebration with his sister, Amina.)

AMINA Bala, mun kusa fara shirin bikin auren ɗan'uwanmu. Me kake tunani?Bala, we're about to start planning our cousin's wedding celebration. What are you thinking?
BALA Tunda na sami sabon aiki, zan iya taimakawa da kuɗi. Ina da niyyar ba da gudummawa mai kyau.Since I got a new job, I can help with money. I intend to make a good contribution.
AMINA Mai kyau sosai! To, idan kowa ya ba da gudummawarsa a kan lokaci, za mu iya yin bikin a wata mai zuwa.Very good! Well, if everyone gives their contribution on time, we can hold the celebration next month.
BALA Wannan shirin ya fi shirin bara kyau, domin mun fara wuri-wuri a wannan karon.This plan is better than last year's plan, because we've started early this time.
AMINA Gaskiya ne. Amma akwai kalubale guda: wurin bikin karami ne fiye da yadda muke bukata.That's true. But there is one challenge: the venue is smaller than what we need.
BALA Kada ki damu. Idan wurin bai isa ba, za mu nemi wani babban wuri kamar filin makaranta.Don't worry. If the venue isn't enough, we'll look for a bigger place like the school field.
AMINA Na yarda. Zan tuntuɓi shugaban makarantar gobe, kuma zan sanar da ke sakamakon nan take.I agree. I'll contact the school head tomorrow, and I'll let you know the result right away.

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. Why can Bala help with money for the wedding celebration?
  2. Under what condition can the celebration happen next month?
  3. Why does Bala say this year's plan is better than last year's?
  4. What challenge does Amina mention?
  5. What does Bala suggest as a solution, and what comparison does he use?

Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

  • A future or intention expression (za or niyya) appears accurately.
  • A reason is expressed with tunda or domin.
  • A condition is expressed with idan.
  • A comparison is expressed with fiye da or kamar.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Planning as a shared, communal activity

Family and community plans, such as weddings and development projects, are frequently discussed and decided collectively across many Hausa-speaking communities, though individual and small-family planning is equally common, particularly in urban and diaspora contexts. Neither approach should be treated as more traditional or more modern; the appropriate approach depends on the event, the institution, and the people involved.

Regional Variation — Expressing conditions and hopes

Some communities favor alternative conditional expressions or additional politeness markers when discussing hopes and future plans, and formal register vocabulary for community planning exists beyond what has been presented in this chapter. This course presents the productive default forms for this level; further regional and formal register vocabulary is introduced for recognition in later volumes.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing idan and tunda

Error: *Idan na sami sabon aiki, na taimaka wa iyalina. (used to describe an already-known fact as if it were an uncertain condition)

Correction: Tunda na sami sabon aiki, zan taimaka wa iyalina. — Use tunda for a fact the speaker already knows to be true, and reserve idan for a genuine condition that may or may not happen.

Common Mistake — Repeating a name instead of tracking reference

Error: *Amina ta yi shiri. Amina ta tuntuɓi shugaban makaranta. Amina ta sanar da Bala. (repeating the name in every sentence)

Correction: Amina ta yi shiri. Ta tuntuɓi shugaban makaranta, sa'an nan ta sanar da Bala. — Once a person has been named, continue with a pronoun, an omitted subject, or a connector, as is natural in connected Hausa narration.

  2. Wannan gidan ya fi wancan girma. (fiye da also acceptable: ...ya fi wancan fiye da girma in extended form)

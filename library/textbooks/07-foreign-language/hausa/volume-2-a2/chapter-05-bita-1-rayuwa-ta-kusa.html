Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Combine identity, family, household, routine, time, and obligation vocabulary from Chapters 1–4 in a single connected performance.
  2. Demonstrate accurate, controlled use of ne/ce identity clauses across all four topic domains reviewed in this unit.
  3. Demonstrate accurate use of independent pronouns and subject-reference forms in identity, family, household, and routine statements.
  4. Demonstrate accurate basic negation with ba ... ba across the topics of Chapters 1–4.
  5. Use greetings, household vocabulary, chore expressions, time expressions, days of the week, and frequency words together in one extended interaction.
  6. Ask for clarification, repetition, or confirmation naturally within a longer, multi-topic exchange.
  7. Read and interpret a connected text that draws on vocabulary and structures from every chapter in Unit I.
  8. Write an integrated paragraph describing yourself, your household, and your weekly routine.

Communication Scenario

Hajiya Talatu has invited you to a small gathering with several neighbors from your street. Over the course of the visit, you introduce yourself again to someone new, talk about your family and household, describe your weekly routine, and help settle a small mix-up about the day and time of a future gathering. This chapter introduces no new grammar. It is a consolidation chapter: every language system below was already introduced in Chapters 1 through 4 and is now assessed together, in combination, rather than one domain at a time.

  1. No new vocabulary domain, grammar system, or Boko convention is introduced in this chapter.
  2. All active vocabulary and grammar from Chapters 1–4 remain available for recycling without re-teaching, consistent with the Volume II Scope and Sequence.
  3. Communication repair remains an active, expected part of every interaction.

Pronunciation and Boko Review

Bita: haruffa na musamman a fadin Sashe I — Review: special characters across Unit I

Across Chapters 1–4, you have practiced the hooked consonants ɓ, ɗ, and ƙ, as well as the apostrophe/glottal convention in words such as Jumma'a. This chapter does not introduce a new sound or symbol; it asks you to apply all of these accurately at once, across a longer stretch of connected speech and writing.

Sound Focus — Rhythm across a longer turn

When you combine several sentences from different topic domains — for example, moving from an identity statement to a household description to a routine statement — practice keeping natural Hausa phrase rhythm throughout, rather than pausing heavily at each sentence boundary as an English speaker might. Continue to observe the vowel-length distinctions already highlighted in rana, mako, and other high-frequency words from Chapters 1–4.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns Review

This chapter introduces no new grammatical system. It consolidates the identity clauses (ne/ce), independent pronouns, subject-reference forms, and basic negation reviewed across Chapters 1 through 4, and requires you to apply them together across identity, family, household, and routine topics in a single connected performance.

5.1 Consolidation: identity clauses across all four domains

The same ne/ce agreement pattern applies whether the subject is a person, a household item, a chore, or a day of the week. Compare examples drawn from each chapter of this unit.

  • Ni malami ne.I am a teacher. (Ch. 1 domain: identity)
  • Wannan ɗan'uwana ne.This is my brother. (Ch. 2 domain: family)
  • Ɗakin nan lalace ne.This room is damaged. (Ch. 3 domain: housing)
  • Yau Litinin ne.Today is Monday. (Ch. 4 domain: time)

5.2 Consolidation: pronouns and subject-reference across connected turns

In extended speech, the same subject is often referred to more than once. Continue to use independent pronouns and subject-reference forms consistently, without switching unnecessarily between a name and a pronoun within the same short passage.

  • Ni Bala ne. Ina zama a wannan gida tare da iyalina. Kowace rana, ina tsaftace ɗaki na kafin zuwa aiki.I am Bala. I live in this house with my family. Every day, I clean my room before going to work.

Notice that once the subject is established with Ni Bala ne, the remaining sentences continue with the pronoun-based subject-reference form ina rather than repeating the name.

5.3 Consolidation: basic negation across topics

  • Ni ba malami ba ne, ma'aikacin banki ne.I am not a teacher, I am a bank worker.
  • Ɗakin nan ba lalace ba ne.This room is not damaged.
  • Ba na zuwa aiki ranar Lahadi.I don't go to work on Sunday.
Language Building Block
identity clause + family/household reference + frequency word + routine statement + negation
Ni Amina ce. Ina zama a wannan unguwa tare da iyalina. Ɗakin girki na gidanmu sabo ne. Kowace rana, ina tsaftace gida kafin zuwa makaranta. Ranar Lahadi ba kasafai ba ne ina aiki, kuma wannan ba ranar makaranta ba ce.
I am Amina. I live in this neighborhood with my family. Our house's kitchen is new. Every day, I clean the house before going to school. On Sunday I rarely work, and this is not a school day.

Atisayen A — Haɗa jimloli (Combine sentences): Using the pattern above, combine an identity clause, a family or household reference, a frequency word, and a negation into one short connected passage of your own.

  1. State who you are using ne/ce.
  2. Add one household or family reference.
  3. Add one routine statement with a frequency word.
  4. Add one negative sentence about something you do not do, or a day that is not a workday.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: A wurin taron unguwa a gidan Hajiya Talatu, kuna saduwa da wani sabon maƙwabci, Malam Sani, kuma kuna tattaunawa game da kanku, iyalinku, gidanku, da jadawalinku.

(Setting: At a neighborhood gathering at Hajiya Talatu's house, you meet a new neighbor, Malam Sani, and talk about yourself, your family, your house, and your schedule.)

MALAM SANI Sannu. Ban san ku ba tukuna. Wane ne sunanku?Hello. I don't know you yet. What is your name?
BALA Sunana Bala. Ni ma'aikacin banki ne, kuma ina zama a wannan unguwa tare da iyalina.My name is Bala. I am a bank worker, and I live in this neighborhood with my family.
MALAM SANI To, nawa ne yawan iyalinku?Okay, how many people are in your family?
BALA Yi haƙuri, ka maimaita, don Allah? Ban gane tambayar ba sosai.Sorry, could you repeat that, please? I didn't quite catch the question.
MALAM SANI Na tambaya: mutane nawa ne a cikin iyalinku?I asked: how many people are in your family?
BALA To, na gane yanzu. Muna da mutum biyar: ni, matata, da yara uku. Muna zama a gida mai ɗaki uku.Okay, I understand now. We are five people: me, my wife, and three children. We live in a house with three rooms.
MALAM SANI Wannan gida ba sabo ba ne, ko?This house is not new, is it?
BALA A'a, ba sabo ba ne, amma yana da kyau. Ɗakin girki kaɗai ke da lalace kaɗan.No, it is not new, but it is in good condition. Only the kitchen is a little damaged.
MALAM SANI Kai kuma, yaya jadawalin ku a mako?And you, how is your weekly schedule?
BALA Kowace rana, ina farka da safe, sa'an nan na tsaftace gida kafin zuwa aiki. Ranar Lahadi ba kasafai ba ne ina aiki, don haka wannan ita ce ranar hutu na.Every day, I wake up in the morning, then I clean the house before going to work. I rarely work on Sunday, so that is my rest day.
MALAM SANI Mai kyau. Za mu sake haɗuwa a wannan unguwa, in Allah ya yarda.Good. We will meet again in this neighborhood, God willing.

Vocabulary notes for the dialogue: mutum biyar = five people (recognition; numbers beyond the small counting set already active are treated formulaically here and are not a new productive system); in Allah ya yarda = God willing (recognition; a common formula presented as one practice among several, per the master instructions on religion and cultural representation, not a required personal statement).

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. What is Bala's occupation?
  2. How many people are in Bala's family, and who are they?
  3. Is Bala's house new? What condition is it in?
  4. What does Bala do every day before going to work?
  5. Which day is Bala's rest day, and why?

Sauraro — Gidan Hauwa (Hauwa's Household)

"Sunana Hauwa. Ni ɗiyar Malam Ibrahim ce. Muna zama a gida mai ɗaki huɗu tare da kakata. Ɗakin kwana na ba shi da lalace, amma bandakin gidanmu lalace ne kaɗan. Reading

Read the short community notice below, which combines identity, family, household, and schedule information in the style of Chapters 1 through 4. Underline every identity clause (ne/ce) and circle every frequency word.

Sanarwar Unguwa — Neighborhood Notice

Wannan sanarwa ce ga dukan iyalan unguwarmu. Kowace rana Asabar, mu ke tsaftace titunan unguwa tare, kuma wannan aiki ne na kowa, ba na mutum ɗaya ba. Wanda ba shi da lokaci ranar Asabar zai iya zuwa ranar Lahadi da safe maimakon haka. Iyalan da suke da yara ƙanana ba dole ba ne su halarta, amma za mu yi murna da ganin ku. Gidan taron mu shi ne gidan Hajiya Talatu, wanda ke da ɗaki huɗu kusa da makaranta.

New words for recognition: sanarwa = notice, announcement; titunan = streets (pl.); maimakon haka = instead; halarta = to attend; murna = joy, gladness.

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. Who is this notice addressed to?
  2. What activity happens every Saturday?
  3. What alternative is offered to someone who cannot attend on Saturday?
  4. Whose house is the meeting house, and how is it described?

Writing Workshop

  1. Karanta sanarwar unguwa da hankali. Reread the neighborhood notice and notice how it combines identity, household, and schedule information smoothly in one text.
  2. Tsarawa. Plan a short integrated paragraph about yourself: who you are, who is in your household, one description of your house or a room in it, and your weekly routine including one rest day.
  3. Rubuta. Write 90–120 words in Hausa. Include at least two identity clauses (ne/ce), one family or household reference, one frequency word, one day of the week, and one negation.
  4. Duba jerin bincike. Check your draft against the checklist below.
  5. Rubuta siffa ta ƙarshe. Produce a clean final version.

Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

  • Ne and ce are used correctly according to gender/number of the subject, across at least two different topic domains.
  • At least one family or household reference from Chapters 2–3 appears accurately.
  • At least one frequency word and one day of the week from Chapter 4 appear accurately.
  • Negation with ba ... ba is correctly formed.
  • Pronoun and subject-reference forms are used consistently once a subject is introduced.
  • No grammar beyond Volume I appears (no future za, no habitual -kan-, no productive focus construction).
  • Special characters (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ) and the apostrophe/glottal convention are used accurately if included.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Neighborhood gatherings and shared responsibility

Neighborhood gatherings and shared tasks, such as the street-cleaning activity in the reading passage, are a common feature of community life in many Hausa-speaking neighborhoods, though the specific form these gatherings take varies widely by city, neighborhood, and household. Not every community organizes shared tasks the same way, and participation expectations differ by household and individual circumstance.

Regional Variation — Describing household size and composition

Ways of describing household size, composition, and living arrangements can vary between urban and rural settings and between Nigerian and Nigerien Hausa-speaking communities. This course presents forms most useful for general communication in Kano-based standard usage.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Switching subject-reference forms unnecessarily

Error: *Ni Bala ne. Bala yana zama a gida. (switching back to the name instead of continuing with a pronoun)

Correction: Ni Bala ne. Ina zama a gida. — Once a subject is introduced, continue with the appropriate pronoun-based subject-reference form rather than repeating the name in every sentence.

Common Mistake — Forgetting negation agreement across domains

Error: *Wannan gida ba sabo ne. (omitting the second ba)

Correction: Wannan gida ba sabo ba ne. — The two-part negation frame ba ... ba applies the same way whether the topic is identity, family, housing, or routine; do not drop the second ba in any domain.

Answer Key / Amsoshi

Atisayen A (Grammar synthesis, Section 5.3)

Answers will vary. A sample acceptable response: Ni Bala ne. Ina zama a gida tare da iyalina. Sau da yawa, ina taimaka wa matata a gida. Ba na aiki ranar Lahadi. Other accurate, well-formed passages meeting the four required elements are equally acceptable.

Tambayoyin fahimta na tattaunawa (Dialogue comprehension)

  1. Bala is a bank worker.
  2. There are five people in Bala's family: Bala, his wife, and three children.
  3. Bala's house is not new, but it is in good condition; only the kitchen is a little damaged.
  4. Every day, Bala wakes up in the morning and cleans the house before going to work.
  5. Bala's rest day is Sunday, because he rarely works that day.

Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

(1) Hauwa is the daughter of Malam Ibrahim. (2) She lives with her grandmother in a house with four rooms. (3) The bathroom is a little damaged. (4) She helps her mother in the kitchen every day before going to school. (5) Sunday is the family's rest day.

Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

  1. The notice is addressed to all families in the neighborhood.
  2. Every Saturday, neighbors clean the neighborhood streets together.
  3. Someone who cannot attend on Saturday may come on Sunday morning instead.
  4. The meeting house is Hajiya Talatu's house, described as having four rooms near the school.

Sashi na B (Chapter Review grammar)

  1. Sample: Ni Bala ne. Ɗakin girki na gidanmu ba lalace ba ne. (Other accurate sentences meeting both requirements are acceptable.)
  2. (a) Ni ɗalibi ne. (b) Wannan ɗakin girki ne. (c) Yau Jumma'a ce.
  3. Amina malama ce. Tana zama a Kano. Tana tsaftace ɗaki kowace rana.

Sashi na C (Sample writing)

Ni Amina ce. Ni malama ce, kuma ina zama a wannan unguwa tare da mijina da yarana biyu. Gidanmu yana da ɗaki uku; ɗakin kwana ba lalace ba ne, amma bandaki yana bukatar gyara. Kowace rana, ina farka da safe, sa'an nan na tsaftace gida kafin zuwa makaranta. Sau da yawa, muna dafa abinci tare da yamma. Ranar Lahadi ba kasafai ba ne ina aiki, don haka wannan ita ce ranar hutu na iyali.

Other accurate, well-formed paragraphs meeting the stated requirements are equally acceptable.

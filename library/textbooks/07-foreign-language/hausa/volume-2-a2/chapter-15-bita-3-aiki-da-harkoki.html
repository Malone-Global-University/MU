Chapter Overview

Chapter 15 is the Unit III integrated assessment. It does not introduce new vocabulary or a new grammar system. Instead, it brings together everything developed since Chapter 11 — education and learning (Ch. 11), work and occupations (Ch. 12), money, banking, and forms (Ch. 13), and narrating yesterday (Ch. 14) — and evaluates your control of the four systems that have been active throughout the unit: focus with ne/ce, prepositions and relational nouns, negation and TAM patterns for obligation and inability, and sentence-linking with kuma, amma, and domin. This chapter also reaches back to material from Unit I and Unit II, since all prior chapters remain active. Consistent with the Assessment Matrix for Volume II, Chapter 15 marks the formal Unit III integrated assessment point.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Review and demonstrate accurate use of education, work, money/banking, and narration vocabulary from Chapters 11–14.
  2. Demonstrate accurate use of ne/ce focus constructions across a range of Unit III topics.
  3. Demonstrate accurate use of prepositions and relational nouns of place, time, and cause.
  4. Demonstrate accurate use of negation and obligation/inability patterns (dole ne, ban iya...ba, bai kamata...ba, ban taɓa...ba).
  5. Link connected sentences using kuma, amma, and domin across topics of work, money, and narration.
  6. Complete an integrated listening, speaking, reading, and writing performance task combining education, work, documentation, and narration.
  7. Identify and self-correct common Unit III errors before the performance task.
  8. Reflect on progress toward independent, familiar-domain communication at the A2–B1 boundary.

Communication Scenario

Bala, whose eventful day was narrated in Chapter 14, has now decided to apply for a new position at a community organization. Over the course of this unit review, he prepares his application paperwork at the bank, discusses his study and work history with a friend, narrates recent events from his week to a interviewer, and explains a scheduling problem that arose along the way. Each task below asks you to bring together vocabulary and grammar from Chapters 11 through 14, and from earlier units where relevant, to help Bala — or to narrate your own comparable experience.

  1. No new vocabulary or grammar system is introduced in this chapter.
  2. All tasks integrate the Chapter 11 systems (focus, prepositions/relational nouns, negation/obligation, and linking) as they have developed through Chapters 12, 13, and 14.
  3. Vocabulary and grammar from Units I and II, and from Chapters 11–14, remain fully active and are expected to appear accurately.
  4. Communication repair remains an active and expected part of every interaction.

Pronunciation Review

Sound Focus — Consolidating tone, vowel length, and special consonants across Unit III

Before the performance task, review tone and vowel length in the words most likely to appear across your responses: work and education vocabulary such as aiki and karatu, banking vocabulary such as kuɗi and asusu, and narration vocabulary such as lamari and sa'an nan. Pay particular attention to ɓ, ɗ, and ƙ in words like ɗan'uwa, ƙasa, and ɓoye, and to maintaining even syllable rhythm across full sentences rather than compressing syllables as in English. This review consolidates pronunciation features already active since earlier chapters; no new sound or contrast is introduced here.

Grammar Systems Review

This chapter introduces no new grammar system. It consolidates the four systems developed since Chapter 11 — focus with ne/ce, prepositions and relational nouns, negation/TAM for obligation and inability, and sentence-linking — consistent with their Assessed (A) status at Chapter 15 in the Volume II matrices. Completive/perfective TAM forms from earlier Volume II chapters remain fully active throughout.

15.1 Review: Focus with ne and ce

  • Malaminmu ne ya ba mu jarrabawa mai wahala.It's our teacher who gave us a difficult exam.
  • Rasit ɗin ce take a cikin jaka.It's the receipt that is in the bag.

Remember: ne follows masculine or plural focused nouns; ce follows feminine focused nouns, as reinforced in Chapters 13 and 14.

15.2 Review: Prepositions and relational nouns

  • Na cika takardar bisa ga umarnin ma'aikacin banki.I filled out the form according to the bank officer's instructions.
  • Akwai bambanci tsakanin aikin ofis da aikin gona.There is a difference between office work and farm work.

15.3 Review: Negation, obligation, and inability

PatternMeaningExample
Ban iya...baI could not...Ban iya kai aikin gida a kan lokaci ba. — I could not turn in the homework on time.
Dole ne na...I had to...Dole ne na sake cika takardar. — I had to fill out the form again.
Bai kamata...bashould not have...Bai kamata mu manta da takardun ba. — We shouldn't have forgotten the documents.
Ban taɓa...baI had never...Ban taɓa yin aiki a banki ba. — I had never worked at a bank.

15.4 Review: Linking with kuma, amma, domin

  • Na yi karatu da yawa, kuma na ci jarrabawar.I studied a lot, and I passed the exam.
  • Na nemi aiki, amma ban samu ba tukuna.I looked for work, but I haven't found any yet.
  • Na je banki domin in buɗe asusu.I went to the bank in order to open an account.
Language Building Block — Unit III Integration Frame
[sequencing word], [focused element] + ne/ce + clause + amma/domin + [obligation or inability pattern]
Da farko, malamin ne ya ba mu aikin, amma ban iya kammala shi a kan lokaci ba.
At first, it was the teacher who gave us the assignment, but I couldn't finish it on time.

Atisayen ƙa'ida — Grammar practice: Complete each sentence using a pattern reviewed in this chapter.

  1. ___ (focus, ne/ce) ta taimake ni cika takardar banki — 'yar uwata ko abokina?
  2. Ban iya ___ (biya) kuɗin makaranta a kan lokaci ba.
  3. ___ (dole ne) mu kai rahoto ga manaja kafin ƙarshen mako.
  4. Na yi aiki tuƙuru, ___ (linking word) ban gaji ba.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Bala yana tattaunawa da aboki, Ibrahim, kan aikace-aikacen sabon aiki.

(Setting: Bala is discussing his application for a new job with his friend Ibrahim.)

IBRAHIM Sannu, Bala. Na ji kana neman sabon aiki. Yaya al'amura suke tafiya?Hello, Bala. I heard you're looking for a new job. How are things going?
BALA Da farko, na cika takardu da yawa, kuma na kai su ga ma'aikatar. Sa'an nan aka gayyace ni don tattaunawa.At first, I filled out many forms, and I submitted them to the office. Then I was invited for an interview.
IBRAHIM Mai kyau! Me ya sa ka zaɓi wannan sana'ar?That's great! What made you choose this occupation?
BALA Ilimin da na samu a makaranta ne ya taimake ni in fahimci wannan fanni sosai. Ban taɓa yin irin wannan aikin ba, amma ina son koyo.It's the education I got in school that helped me understand this field well. I've never done this kind of work before, but I want to learn.
IBRAHIM Ban gane ba sarai — akwai matsala kafin ka kai takardun?I don't quite understand — was there a problem before you submitted the documents?
BALA I, gaskiya ne. Bai kamata in jira har ranar ƙarshe ba, don haka dole ne na yi gaggawa zuwa banki domin samun takardar shaidar asusu.Yes, that's true. I shouldn't have waited until the last day, so I had to rush to the bank to get my account statement.
IBRAHIM Daga baya fa, me ya faru?And afterward, what happened?
BALA Ma'aikacin banki ne ya taimake ni cikin sauri, kuma na kai duk takardu a kan lokaci. A ƙarshe, na ji daɗi domin na kammala aikace-aikacen daidai.It was the bank officer who helped me quickly, and I submitted all the documents on time. In the end, I felt good because I completed the application properly.

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. What did Bala do first regarding the job application?
  2. Why did Bala choose this occupation?
  3. What problem occurred before Bala submitted the documents?
  4. Who helped Bala at the bank?
  5. How did Bala feel at the end, and why?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Applying for work and school opportunities

Application and documentation processes for jobs, schooling, and financial services vary across Hausa-speaking communities and institutions, ranging from informal community recommendations to formal written procedures. Both approaches are common and legitimate, and neither should be treated as more modern or more traditional than the other; the appropriate approach depends on the institution and context involved.

Regional Variation — Institutional vocabulary

Some institutional and documentation vocabulary differs between Nigerian and Nigerien contexts, and additional formal register vocabulary exists beyond what has been presented across Chapters 11–14. This course presents the productive default forms for this level; further institutional and formal register vocabulary is introduced for recognition in later volumes.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Overusing focus where a plain statement is expected

Error: *Ni ne na je banki jiya. (used as a plain, unemphasized statement)

Correction: Na je banki jiya. — Reserve ne/ce focus for answering a specific question or highlighting one detail in contrast to another, as reinforced since Chapter 11; do not use it for every ordinary statement.

Common Mistake — Confusing dole ne and ban iya...ba

Error: *Dole ne na gyara keke, amma na kasa. (mixing an obligation pattern with an unpracticed form for "I failed")

Correction: Dole ne na gyara keke, amma ban iya ba. — Use ban iya...ba, the pattern taught since Chapter 11, to express inability, rather than an untaught alternative.

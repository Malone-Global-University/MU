Labari na Jiya

Narrating Yesterday · Speak and Write Hausa: Expanding Foundations for Independent Use

Chapter Overview

Chapter 14 continues Unit III by moving from the banking and documentation focus of Chapter 13 into connected narration of past events. You will tell short, connected accounts of things that happened yesterday or recently, using the same systems developed since Chapter 11 — focus with ne/ce, prepositions and relational nouns, negation and TAM patterns for obligation and inability, and sentence-linking with kuma, amma, and domin — together with the completive/perfective forms active since Chapter 1, now organized into sequence with a small set of new sequencing expressions. No new grammar system is introduced in this chapter; instead, the Chapter 11 systems continue to develop, consistent with their Developed (D) status at Chapter 14 in the Volume II matrices, while sequencing words are added as vocabulary that supports narration. Everything from Chapters 1–13 remains active and continues to appear throughout.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify and use vocabulary for sequencing and narrating a short account of past events.
  2. Tell a connected account of something that happened yesterday or recently, using completive/perfective forms already active since earlier chapters.
  3. Use ne/ce focus constructions to emphasize a specific detail within a narrated account.
  4. Use prepositions and relational nouns to express time, cause, and sequence within a narrative.
  5. Use negation and obligation/inability patterns to explain what did not happen, what someone had to do, or what someone could not do.
  6. Link sentences within a narrative using kuma, amma, or domin, and order events using sequencing expressions such as da farko, sa'an nan, and a ƙarshe.
  7. Describe a personal event from the recent past in a short spoken and written narrative.
  8. Read a short narrated account and identify the sequence and reasons behind the events.
  9. Recognize that storytelling and personal narration styles vary across Hausa-speaking communities and settings.

Communication Scenario

Bala had an eventful day yesterday: he went to the bank to help Hauwa, but along the way his motorcycle broke down, he was late to work, and he had to explain what happened to his manager. That evening, he calls his sister to tell her the whole story, and the next day, a coworker asks him what took him so long. Throughout the chapter, Bala uses the systems developed since Chapter 11 — emphasizing exactly what went wrong with focus structures, explaining causes and sequence with relational nouns and time expressions, and describing what he could not do and what he had to do instead.

  1. New vocabulary in this chapter is drawn from the narration and sequencing domain.
  2. No new grammar system is introduced in this chapter; the Chapter 11 systems (focus with ne/ce, expanded prepositions and relational nouns, negation/TAM for obligation and inability, and sentence-linking with kuma/amma/domin) continue to develop, applied to narration.
  3. Vocabulary and grammar from Chapters 1–13 remain active and are expected to appear accurately, including the completive/perfective TAM forms first introduced in early chapters.
  4. Communication repair remains an active and expected part of every interaction.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Vowel length and rhythm in lalace, lamari, and sequencing expressions

Practice even syllable rhythm across lalace (broke down) and lamari (event), keeping each syllable clear rather than compressing them as in English. Pay attention to the vowel length distinction in sa'an nan, where the glottal-adjacent syllable and the following long vowel must both be held accurately. Continue to practice natural phrase-level intonation across a full sequencing chain such as da farko... sa'an nan... daga baya... a ƙarshe, since narration relies on this rhythm to signal that events are connected.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter does not introduce a new grammar system. Instead, it develops the four systems introduced in Chapter 11 and reinforced in Chapters 12 and 13 — focus with ne/ce, prepositions and relational nouns, negation/TAM patterns for obligation and inability, and sentence-linking — through the domain of narrating past events, consistent with the Developed (D) status of these systems at Chapter 14 in the Volume II matrices. The completive/perfective TAM forms used for narration were introduced in early Volume II chapters and are recycled here in connected sequence rather than taught as new material.

14.1 Ordering events: sequencing expressions

Sequencing expressions are vocabulary items, not a new grammar system. They combine with the completive/perfective forms already active since earlier chapters to organize a narrative into a clear order.

  • Da farko, na tashi da wuri.First, I got up early.
  • Sa'an nan na hau keke domin in je banki.Then I got on the motorcycle to go to the bank.
  • Daga baya, keken ya lalace a tsakiyar hanya.Later, the motorcycle broke down in the middle of the road.
  • A ƙarshe, na kai wurin aiki da makara.In the end, I arrived at work late.

Sequencing expressions normally begin the sentence, followed by a comma in writing and a brief pause in speech, before the completive verb form.

14.2 Focus with ne and ce in a narrative

The same focus pattern continues here, now used to highlight one specific detail within a story rather than the whole event.

  • Keken ne ya lalace, ba motar ba.It was the motorcycle that broke down, not the car.[focused noun] + ne + [rest of clause] + ba... ba (contrast)
  • A tsakiyar hanya ne ya faru.It was in the middle of the road that it happened.
  • Manajana ce ta fahimta lokacin da na bayyana mata.It's my manager who understood when I explained to her.
  • Gaggawa ce ta sa na kira ta.It was an emergency that made me call her.

As before, use ne/ce when answering a question word directly, especially me ya faru (what happened) and me ya sa (what made/caused), so the answer highlights the key detail sharply.

14.3 Prepositions and relational nouns: time and cause in narration

  • Kafin na kai banki, keken ya lalace.Before I reached the bank, the motorcycle broke down.
  • Na tsaya saboda na ji wani sauti mai ban tsoro.I stopped because I heard a frightening sound.
  • Na yi bayani ga manajana bisa ga abin da ya faru.I gave an explanation to my manager according to what happened.
  • Na yi awa ɗaya tsakanin gyara keke da tafiya da ƙafa.I spent one hour between fixing the motorcycle and walking.

The relational nouns kafin, saboda, bisa ga, and tsakanin, all recycled from Chapters 8, 11, and 13, continue to follow the same word order in narration as in other domains.

14.4 Negation and TAM: what did not happen, and what had to be done

Dole ne na...I had to...Dole ne na tafi da ƙafa har wurin aiki. — I had to walk all the way to work.

These patterns are the same forms introduced in Chapter 11, Section 11.3, and reinforced in Chapter 13, now applied to explaining what could not, did not, and had to happen during a narrated event.

14.5 Linking ideas across a narrative: kuma, amma, and domin

The same three linking words continue to connect the parts of a narrative.

  • Na tashi da wuri, kuma na shirya don tafiya banki.I got up early, and I got ready to go to the bank.
  • Na yi ƙoƙarin gyara keke, amma bai yi aiki ba.I tried to fix the motorcycle, but it didn't work.
  • Na kira manajana domin in ba ta bayani.I called my manager to give her an explanation.

Kuma (and), amma (but), and domin (because/in order to) remain the only productive linking words at this stage, as established in Chapter 11.

Language Building Block — Sequence + Focus + Reason Frame
[sequencing word], [focused element] + ne/ce + clause + saboda/domin + reason
Daga baya, keken ne ya lalace, saboda na yi amfani da shi da yawa.
Later, it was the motorcycle that broke down, because I had used it a lot.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Bala yana bayyanawa 'yar'uwarsa, Amina, abin da ya faru masa jiya.

(Setting: Bala is explaining to his sister, Amina, what happened to him yesterday.)

AMINA Sannu, ɗan'uwa. Na ji ka makara zuwa aiki jiya. Me ya faru?Hello, brother. I heard you were late to work yesterday. What happened?
BALA I, gaskiya ne. Da farko, na tashi da wuri domin in taimaki Hauwa a banki. Sa'an nan keken ya lalace a tsakiyar hanya.Yes, that's true. First, I got up early to help Hauwa at the bank. Then the motorcycle broke down in the middle of the road.
AMINA Kai! Me ya sa ya lalace? Ban gane ba.Oh no! What made it break down? I don't understand.
BALA Yi haƙuri, bari in bayyana sarai. Ban taɓa amfani da shi da yawa kamar haka ba, don haka na yi tsammani shi ya sa ya lalace.Sorry, let me explain clearly. I had never used it that much before, so I think that's what caused it to break down.
AMINA To, daga baya fa, me ka yi?Okay, so afterward, what did you do?
BALA Ban iya gyara keke da kaina ba, don haka na kira manajana domin in ba ta bayani. Dole ne na tafi da ƙafa har wurin aiki.I couldn't fix the motorcycle myself, so I called my manager to explain to her. I had to walk all the way to work.
AMINA Ita ce ta fahimta?Did she understand?
BALA I, manajana ce ta fahimta, amma ta ce bai kamata in yi latti sau na biyu ba. A ƙarshe, na kai wurin aiki, kuma na cika aikina.Yes, my manager understood, but she said I shouldn't be late a second time. In the end, I got to work, and I completed my tasks.

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. What was Bala doing early in the morning, according to the story?
  2. What happened to the motorcycle, and where?
  3. Why does Bala think the motorcycle broke down?
  4. What did Bala have to do after he could not fix the motorcycle himself?
  5. How did the story end?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Storytelling and everyday narration

Narrating the events of one's day is a common and valued part of everyday conversation in many Hausa-speaking communities, whether shared with family in the evening, with coworkers the next day, or with friends over the phone. Styles of narration vary: some speakers favor brief, efficient accounts, while others favor longer, more detailed storytelling with more elaborate sequencing. Neither style should be treated as more correct or more authentically Hausa than the other.

Regional Variation — Narrative connectors

Some sequencing and connecting expressions vary between Nigerian and Nigerien Hausa-speaking communities, and additional narrative connectors exist beyond the four presented in this chapter. This course presents the Kano-based standard forms above as the productive default for narration at this level; other regional or more advanced connectors remain equally legitimate and are introduced for recognition in later volumes.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Wrong focus particle for gender in narration

Error: *Hanya ne ta toshe. (using ne with a feminine noun)

Correction: Hanya ce ta toshe. — Use ce when the focused noun is feminine, as with hanya, and ne for masculine or plural reference, consistent with the pattern reinforced in Chapter 13.

Common Mistake — Stringing events without sequencing words

Error: *Na tashi. Na je banki. Keken ya lalace. (three unconnected sentences with no signal of order)

Correction: Da farko, na tashi. Sa'an nan na je banki. Daga baya, keken ya lalace. — Use sequencing expressions to make the order of events clear to the listener or reader, rather than relying on sentence order alone.

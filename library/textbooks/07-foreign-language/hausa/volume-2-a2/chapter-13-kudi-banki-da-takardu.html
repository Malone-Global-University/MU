Kuɗi, Banki, da Takardu

Money, Banking, and Forms · Speak and Write Hausa: Expanding Foundations for Independent Use

Chapter Overview

Chapter 13 continues Unit III by moving from the workplace of Chapter 12 into money, banking, and basic documentation. You will read forms, discuss payments, accounts, receipts, and everyday paperwork, using the same systems developed since Chapter 11 — focus with ne/ce, prepositions and relational nouns, negation and TAM patterns for obligation and inability, and sentence-linking with kuma, amma, and domin — and applying them productively to a new domain. No new grammar system is introduced in this chapter; instead, the Chapter 11 systems are developed further, consistent with their Developed (D) status at Chapter 13 in the Volume II matrices. Everything from Chapters 1–12 remains active and continues to appear throughout.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify and use vocabulary for money, banking, payments, accounts, and basic forms.
  2. Ask and answer questions about prices, balances, deposits, and withdrawals.
  3. Use ne/ce focus constructions to emphasize a specific piece of information about money or documents.
  4. Use prepositions and relational nouns to express time, cause, and comparison in banking and payment contexts.
  5. Form negative statements and questions about ability, obligation, and past inability related to money and forms.
  6. Link two short sentences into one connected sentence using kuma, amma, or domin.
  7. Describe a payment, an account problem, or a documentation task in a short spoken and written passage.
  8. Read a short bank form or receipt and identify key details.
  9. Recognize that banking access and payment habits vary across Hausa-speaking communities.

Communication Scenario

Hauwa has decided to open a savings account so she can put aside part of her salary from the electronics shop. Over the course of the week, she visits a bank branch to ask about requirements, fills out a form with a bank officer's help, and later explains to Bala why she chose that particular bank instead of another one. Throughout the week she uses the systems developed since Chapter 11: emphasizing exactly what she means with focus structures, connecting her reasons with relational nouns, and explaining what she can, cannot, and must do regarding money and paperwork.

  1. New vocabulary in this chapter is drawn from the money, banking, and forms domain.
  2. No new grammar system is introduced in this chapter; the Chapter 11 systems (focus with ne/ce, expanded prepositions and relational nouns, negation/TAM for obligation and inability, and sentence-linking with kuma/amma/domin) continue to develop.
  3. Vocabulary and grammar from Chapters 1–12 remain active and are expected to appear accurately.
  4. Communication repair remains an active and expected part of every interaction.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Vowel length and glottal conventions in asusu, ajiya, and rasit

Practice the vowel length distinction in asusu (account), where the doubled written vowel corresponds to a longer held sound rather than two separate vowel articulations. Continue to keep the glottal-adjacent syllables of ajiya distinct, and pay attention to the borrowed-word stress pattern in rasit, which does not follow English stress placement. Maintain even syllable rhythm across compound expressions such as ma'aikacin banki.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter does not introduce a new grammar system. Instead, it develops the four systems introduced in Chapter 11 and reinforced in Chapter 12 — focus with ne/ce, prepositions and relational nouns, negation/TAM patterns for obligation and inability, and sentence-linking — through additional contexts drawn from money, banking, and forms, consistent with the Developed (D) status of these systems at Chapter 13 in the Volume II matrices.

13.1 Focus with ne and ce in banking contexts

The same focus pattern continues here: the emphasized element comes first, followed by ne (masculine/plural) or ce (feminine).

  • Wannan banki ne na fi so.This is the bank I prefer. (emphasis on "this bank")[focused noun] + ne + [rest of clause]
  • Asusun ajiya ce nake son buɗewa.It's a savings account that I want to open.
  • Ranar Talata ne zan je banki.It's on Tuesday that I will go to the bank.
  • Ma'aikacin banki ne ya taimake ni cika takardar.It's the bank officer who helped me fill out the form.
  • Ba kuɗi da yawa ne nake ajiyewa ba, sai kaɗan-kaɗan.It isn't a lot of money that I'm saving, just little by little.

As before, use ne/ce when answering a question word directly, especially wace banki (which bank), me (what), and yaushe (when), so that the answer sounds sharp and natural rather than a full restated sentence.

13.2 Prepositions and relational nouns: time, cause, and comparison with money

  • Kafin buɗe asusu, dole ne a cika takarda.Before opening an account, one must fill out a form.
  • Ban samu rasit ba saboda na'urar tana da matsala.I didn't get a receipt because the machine had a problem.
  • Wannan banki ya fi wancan sauƙi bisa ga abin da nake ji.This bank is easier than that one, according to what I've heard.
  • Akwai bambanci tsakanin ajiya da bashi.There is a difference between savings and debt.
  • Zan biya bashi na fiye da yadda na yi tunani.I will pay more debt than I thought.

The relational noun tsakanin (recycled from Chapter 8) and the newly active bisa ga continue to precede the noun phrase they connect to, following the same word-order pattern as kafin and wajen.

13.3 Negation and TAM: obligation, ability, and inability with money and forms

PatternMeaningExample
Ba na iya...I cannot / am not able to...Ba na iya fitar da kuɗi ba tukuna, domin lamba ta ɓace. — I can't withdraw money yet, because the PIN is lost.
Bai kamata...bashould not...Bai kamata ku sa hannu ba tukuna, ku fara karanta takardar. — You shouldn't sign yet, read the form first.
Dole ne ka/ki...must, have to...Dole ne ki kawo katin banki don tantancewa. — You must bring the bank card for verification.
Ban taɓa...baI have never...Ban taɓa samun bashi ba. — I have never had a debt.

These patterns are the same forms introduced in Chapter 11, Section 11.3, now applied to banking situations. As before, most negative statements use the frame ba... ba surrounding the predicate, while dole ne expresses obligation without negation.

13.4 Linking two ideas: kuma, amma, and domin

The same three linking words continue to connect short sentences about money and paperwork.

  • Na cika takardar, kuma na sa hannu a ƙasa.I filled out the form, and I signed at the bottom.
  • Na je banki da wuri, amma ma'aikatan sun makara.I went to the bank early, but the workers were late.
  • Zan buɗe asusun ajiya domin ina son tanadin gaba.I will open a savings account because I want to plan ahead.

Kuma (and), amma (but), and domin (because) remain the only productive linking words at this stage, as established in Chapter 11.

Language Building Block — Focus + Reason Frame (recycled from Chapter 11)
[focused element] + ne/ce + clause + domin/saboda + reason
Wannan banki ne na zaɓa, domin yana kusa da gida.
It's this bank that I chose, because it's close to home.

Atisayen ƙa'ida — Grammar practice: Complete each sentence using a pattern from Chapters 11–13.

  1. ___ (focus, ne/ce) ta fi sauƙi wajen buɗe asusu — wannan banki ko wancan? Choose one and write a full focused sentence.
  2. Ban iya ___ (fitar da) kuɗi ba domin lamba ta ɓace.
  3. ___ (dole ne) ku kawo takardar shaida kafin buɗe asusu.
  4. Wannan banki ___ (fi) wancan sauƙi.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Hauwa tana magana da ma'aikacin banki a lokacin da take son buɗe asusun ajiya.

(Setting: Hauwa is speaking with a bank officer while trying to open a savings account.)

HAUWA Sannu. Ina son buɗe asusun ajiya. Me nake bukata?Hello. I want to open a savings account. What do I need?
MA'AIKACI Sannu. Dole ne ki kawo takardar shaida, kuma dole ne ki cika wannan takarda.Hello. You must bring an identification document, and you must fill out this form.
HAUWA To. Ga takardar shaidata. Ina kuma so in san ko wannan banki ne ya fi sauran sauƙi wajen fitar da kuɗi.Alright. Here is my identification. I also want to know if this bank is easier than others for withdrawing money.
MA'AIKACI I, gaskiya ne. Kina iya fitar da kuɗi a kowace rana, amma dole ne ki tuna lambarki.Yes, that's true. You can withdraw money every day, but you must remember your PIN.
HAUWA Yi haƙuri, ban ji sarai ba — lamba ce kuke nufi, ko katin banki?Sorry, I didn't hear clearly — do you mean a PIN, or a bank card?
MA'AIKACI Duka biyun. Bai kamata ki ba kowa lambarki ba, domin katin banki da lambar suna tafiya tare.Both. You shouldn't give anyone your PIN, because the bank card and the PIN go together.
HAUWA Na gane. Bayan na cika takardar, zan sa hannu a ƙasa, ko ba haka ba?I understand. After I fill out the form, I will sign at the bottom, right?
MA'AIKACI Daidai ne. Bayan haka, za a ba ki rasit da katin banki bayan kwana biyu.Exactly right. After that, you'll be given a receipt, and the bank card after two days.

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. What two things does the bank officer say Hauwa must bring or do?
  2. What does Hauwa want to know about the bank?
  3. Why does Hauwa ask the officer to repeat herself?
  4. What does the officer say Hauwa should not do with her PIN?
  5. What will Hauwa receive, and when?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Formal and informal saving practices

While formal bank accounts, as in this chapter, are common and increasingly accessible across Hausa-speaking communities, many people also use informal community savings arrangements, mobile-money services, and family-based saving practices alongside or instead of banks. No single saving method should be treated as more modern, more responsible, or more authentically Hausa than another.

Regional Variation — Banking and payment terms

Some banking and payment vocabulary varies between Nigerian and Nigerien Hausa-speaking communities, and terms for mobile-money services or specific banking products may differ depending on local providers and regulations. This course presents the Kano-based standard forms above as the productive default; other regional terms remain equally legitimate and are noted for recognition.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Wrong focus particle for gender

Error: *Takarda ne mai muhimmanci. (using ne with a feminine noun)

Correction: Takarda ce mai muhimmanci. — Use ce when the focused noun is feminine, as with takarda, and ne for masculine or plural reference.

Common Mistake — Confusing obligation and ability

Error: *Ba na iya kawo takardar shaida. when the intended meaning is "I must bring an identification document" (using inability instead of obligation)

Correction: Dole ne in kawo takardar shaida. — Use ba na iya only for genuine inability, and dole ne for obligation, as introduced in Chapter 11 and reinforced here.

Sake Haɗuwa da Mutane

Reconnecting and Social Identity · Speak and Write Hausa: Expanding Foundations for Independent Use

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Greet someone you already know and someone you are meeting for the first time, using appropriately different opening exchanges.
  2. Reintroduce yourself and expand on your identity: name, origin, language background, and current activity.
  3. Ask about and describe relationships between people, including how two people know each other.
  4. Talk about your neighborhood or community and ask someone about theirs.
  5. Ask for clarification, repetition, or confirmation when you do not catch what someone said.
  6. Use masculine, feminine, and plural agreement correctly across pronouns, ne/ce, and simple noun phrases in identity statements.
  7. Read and write a short paragraph reintroducing yourself to a community or group.
  8. Identify which Volume I systems this chapter recycles and which new vocabulary domains it adds.

Communication Scenario

Months after finishing your first course of study, you attend a community gathering — perhaps a neighborhood event, a language-exchange meetup, or a family friend's visit — where you see people you met briefly before and meet several new ones. Over the course of the gathering, you greet an old acquaintance warmly, reintroduce yourself to someone new, explain a little more about who you are and where you come from than you could before, ask how two other guests know each other, and talk about your neighborhood and community. This chapter does not introduce a new grammatical system. Instead, it reactivates and expands the greetings, identity clauses, and pronoun systems from Volume I so that you can use them with more confidence, more detail, and more natural variety.

  1. Every grammar point in this chapter was already introduced in Volume I. Its purpose here is review, expansion of vocabulary, and more independent use — not new instruction.
  2. New vocabulary in this chapter focuses on relationships, reconnection, and community/neighborhood life.
  3. Communication repair — asking someone to repeat or clarify something — is treated from the start of this volume as a normal, expected part of every conversation.

Pronunciation and Boko

Bita: sauti da tsawon wasali — Review: sounds and vowel length

Before this volume introduces any new sound feature, take a moment to reactivate the tone and vowel-length awareness you built in Volume I. Say the following word pairs aloud, listening for the vowel-length contrast that distinguishes their meaning.

Sound Focus — Reactivating vowel length in new vocabulary

This chapter's new words include several with long vowels that carry meaning, including maƙwabci (neighbor) and al'umma (community). As in Volume I, standard Boko spelling does not mark vowel length, so rely on listening models, your instructor, or a Hausa speaker for the accurate pronunciation of each new word, and continue to treat vowel length as a real feature of speech even when spelling does not show it.

Bita: haruffan Boko na musamman — Review: special Boko characters

Continue to write ɓ, ɗ, ƙ, and 'y exactly as taught in Volume I. This chapter's new vocabulary includes maƙwabci (with ƙ), so pay particular attention to producing and typing this letter correctly, and never substitute the plain letter k.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new grammatical system. Its purpose is to reactivate and expand your control of Volume I's greetings, identity clauses (ne/ce), independent pronouns, and subject-reference patterns, so that you can use them with new vocabulary and slightly more independence.

1.1 Review: identity clauses with ne and ce

Recall from Volume I that Hausa identity statements use ne after a masculine or plural subject and ce after a feminine subject. This agreement pattern does not change; this chapter simply gives you more vocabulary to use it with.

  • Ni abokin makarantar Amina ne.I am Amina's schoolmate. (male speaker)
  • Ni abokiyar makarantar Amina ce.I am Amina's schoolmate. (female speaker)
  • Su maƙwabtanmu ne.They are our neighbors.

1.2 Review: independent pronouns and subject reference

Review the independent pronouns from Volume I (ni, kai, ke, shi, ita, mu, ku, su) and the corresponding subject-reference forms you already use with the general completive (na, ka/kika, ya/ta, mun, kun, sun). This chapter's dialogue and tasks will require you to select the correct form rapidly as speakers change.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Wata liyafar unguwa. Bala ya sadu da Amina, tsohuwar abokiyar makarantarsa, kuma daga baya ya sadu da wani sabon maƙwabci, Ibrahim.

(Setting: A neighborhood gathering. Bala meets Amina, his old schoolmate, and later meets a new neighbor, Ibrahim.)

BALA Amina! Dogon lokaci! Ba mu haɗu ba tun muna makaranta!Amina! Long time! We haven't met since school!
AMINA Bala! Na tuna ka sosai! Yaya kake jin daɗi?Bala! I remember you well! How have you been?
BALA Ina lafiya, na gode. Yanzu ina zaune a unguwa mai suna Fagge. Kai fa, ina kuke zaune yanzu?I'm fine, thank you. Now I live in a neighborhood called Fagge. And you, where do you live now?
AMINA Ni ma a Fagge nake zaune, tsawon shekara biyu yanzu! Ba mu san haka ba tukuna.I also live in Fagge, for two years now! We didn't know that yet.
(Ibrahim, a neighbor Bala does not yet know, joins them.)
AMINA Bala, wannan Ibrahim ne, maƙwabcina. Ibrahim, wannan Bala ne, tsohon abokin makarantata.Bala, this is Ibrahim, my neighbor. Ibrahim, this is Bala, my old schoolmate.
IBRAHIM Sannu, Bala. Yi haƙuri, ka maimaita sunanka, don Allah? Ban gane sosai ba.Hello, Bala. Sorry, could you repeat your name, please? I didn't catch it well.
BALA Ba komai. Ni Bala ne. Ni ma sabo ne a wannan unguwa; mun ƙaura zuwa nan makon da ya gabata.No problem. I'm Bala. I am also new to this neighborhood; we moved here last week.
IBRAHIM To, barka da zuwa! Ni da Amina maƙwabta ne tun tsawon lokaci. Al'ummarmu tana da kyau sosai.Well, welcome! Amina and I have been neighbors for a long time. Our community is very good.

Vocabulary notes for the dialogue: Fagge = a real neighborhood name (proper noun); ƙaura = to move/relocate (verb, recognition here, active vocabulary in a later chapter); barka da zuwa = welcome (formulaic, reviewed from Vol. I greetings).

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. How do Bala and Amina know each other?
  2. Where do Bala and Amina each live now, and for how long has Amina lived there?
  3. Who is Ibrahim, and how does Amina introduce him to Bala?
  4. Find the moment Ibrahim asks for repetition. What exact phrase does he use?
  5. Is Bala new or established in the neighborhood? Find the sentence that tells you.

Reading

Read the short community profile below. Underline every identity clause (ne/ce) and circle every relationship word.

Bayanin Al'umma — Unguwar Fagge (Community Profile: Fagge Neighborhood)

Fagge unguwa ce a birnin Kano, kuma mutane da yawa suna zaune a can tsawon shekaru masu yawa. Wasu maƙwabta sun san juna tun suna yara, wasu kuma sabbi ne, sun ƙaura kwanan nan. Al'ummar Fagge tana da kasuwanni, makarantu, da masallatai kusa da juna. Mazauna unguwar suna taimakon juna, kuma galibi suna gaisawa da sabbin maƙwabta da kyakkyawar murya.

New words for recognition: mazauna = residents (pl.); masallatai = mosques (pl.); murya = voice.

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. Where is Fagge, and how long have people lived there?
  2. What is the difference between the two kinds of neighbors described?
  3. What does the community have, according to the passage?
  4. How do residents treat new neighbors?

Writing Workshop

  1. Karanta bayanin al'umma da hankali. Reread the Fagge community profile and notice how it introduces the place, describes relationships, and closes with a general observation.
  2. Tsarawa. Plan a short paragraph reintroducing yourself to a community: your name, one relationship (a friend, schoolmate, or neighbor), how long you have lived somewhere or known someone, and one detail about your neighborhood or community.
  3. Rubuta. Write 60–90 words in Hausa. Include at least two identity clauses (ne/ce), one relationship word, one negation, and one communication-repair expression used naturally (for example, imagining someone asked you to repeat something).
  4. Duba jerin bincike. Check your draft against the checklist below.
  5. Rubuta siffa ta ƙarshe. Produce a clean final version.

Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

  • Ne and ce are used correctly according to gender/number of the subject.
  • At least one relationship word from this chapter's vocabulary appears.
  • Negation with ba ... ba is correctly formed if used.
  • Hooked letters (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ) and 'y are used correctly.
  • No grammar beyond Volume I appears (no future za, no habitual -kan-, no relative forms).

Speaking Practice

Atisayen ƙa'ida — Reintroduction drill

With a partner, take turns reintroducing yourselves as if you had met briefly a long time ago. Use Dogon lokaci! and at least one relationship or community word.

Sabon maƙwabci — New-neighbor role-play

In groups of three, recreate the situation of the model dialogue: two people who already know each other introduce a third person who is new. Include at least one moment of asking for repetition.

Madadin karatu kai tsaye — Solo alternative

Record yourself greeting an imagined old acquaintance, reintroducing yourself, and describing your neighborhood, playing both roles of the conversation.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Extended greetings on reconnection

When people who know each other meet again after time apart, greetings in many Hausa-speaking communities are often more extended than a first meeting, including exclamations like Dogon lokaci! and multiple rounds of health and family inquiries. This warmth varies by relationship, generation, and individual personality and should not be treated as a fixed script.

Regional Variation — Neighborhood and community terms

Unguwa is the standard term for neighborhood in Nigerian urban contexts such as Kano. Community organization and terminology can differ somewhat in rural areas and across the border in Niger; this course continues to use Kano-based usage as its productive standard.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Dropping gender agreement when vocabulary is new

Error: *Ni abokiyar makarantar Amina ne. (using masculine ne with feminine self-reference)

Correction: Ni abokiyar makarantar Amina ce. — New vocabulary does not change the agreement rule; apply ne/ce exactly as reviewed in Section 1.1.

Common Mistake — Avoiding repair language out of politeness

Error: A learner who does not catch a name or word stays silent rather than asking for repetition.

Correction: Use Ka/Ki maimaita, don Allah? — asking for repetition is normal and expected, not impolite.

Answer Key / Amsoshi

Atisayen A (Grammar synthesis, Section 1.2)

  1. Ita Fatima ce.
  2. Su maƙwabta ne.
  3. Na tuna ki daga makaranta.
  4. Ba abokiyar aikina ba ce.

Tambayoyin fahimta na tattaunawa (Dialogue comprehension)

  1. Bala and Amina know each other from school (abokin/abokiyar makaranta).
  2. Both now live in Fagge; Amina has lived there two years.
  3. Ibrahim is Amina's neighbor; she introduces him as maƙwabcina.
  4. Ibrahim says Yi haƙuri, ka maimaita sunanka, don Allah?
  5. Bala is new; he says Ni ma sabo ne a wannan unguwa; mun ƙaura zuwa nan makon da ya gabata.

Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

(1) Zainab asks whether they have ever met before. (2) Zainab and Ibrahim have known each other for three years. (3) The other speaker has lived there for one month. (4) She is happy because she does not yet know many people in the neighborhood.

Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

  1. Fagge is a neighborhood in Kano; many people have lived there for many years.
  2. Some neighbors have known each other since childhood; others are new and have recently moved in.
  3. Markets, schools, and mosques close to one another.
  4. Residents help each other and generally greet new neighbors warmly.

Sashi na B (Chapter Review grammar)

  1. (a) Ita maƙwabciyata ce. (b) Su abokaina ne. (c) Ni abokinsa ne.
  2. Ba shi abokin aikina ba ne.

Sashi na C (Sample writing)

Ni Hauwa ce. Ina zaune a wata unguwa mai suna Sabon Gari, tsawon shekara ɗaya yanzu. Zainab maƙwabciyata ce, kuma mun san juna tun na zo nan. Ba mu haɗu ba tun mako biyu, don haka na yi farin ciki da ganin ta a liyafar. Al'ummarmu tana da kyau, kuma mutane suna taimakon juna sosai.

Other accurate, well-formed paragraphs meeting the stated requirements are equally acceptable.

Makaranta da Koyo

Education and Learning · Speak and Write Hausa: Expanding Foundations for Independent Use

Chapter Overview

Chapter 11 opens Unit III and moves you from the everyday-life domains of Unit II into the world of school, study, and learning. You will talk about classes, exams, study plans, and the challenges that come with learning something new — while your Hausa itself keeps growing. This chapter introduces four new systems: fuller negation and TAM patterns for talking about obligation and inability, prepositions and relational nouns that connect ideas about time, cause, and comparison, focus expressions with ne/ce that let you emphasize exactly what you mean, and simple ways of linking two ideas together in one sentence. Everything from Chapters 1–10 remains active and continues to appear throughout.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify and use vocabulary for classes, exams, study plans, and learning difficulties.
  2. Ask and answer questions about school subjects, schedules, and performance.
  3. Use ne/ce focus constructions to emphasize a specific piece of information in an answer.
  4. Use an expanded set of prepositions and relational nouns to express time, cause, and comparison.
  5. Form negative statements and questions about ability, obligation, and past inability using patterns developed in this chapter.
  6. Link two short sentences into one connected sentence using a basic coordinating or subordinating word.
  7. Describe a personal study plan or a learning challenge in a short spoken and written passage.
  8. Read a short school notice or study schedule and identify key details.
  9. Recognize Ajami as a historical Hausa writing tradition distinct from Boko.

Communication Scenario

Bala has decided to continue his studies in the evenings after work. On his first week back at the community learning center, he registers for a class, talks with a classmate about the exam schedule, and asks his teacher for advice about a subject he finds difficult. Throughout the week he uses the systems introduced in this chapter: emphasizing exactly what he means with focus structures, connecting his reasons with relational nouns, and explaining what he can and cannot yet do.

  1. New vocabulary in this chapter is drawn from the education and learning domain.
  2. New grammar in this chapter includes focus with ne/ce, an expanded set of prepositions and relational nouns, further negation and TAM patterns, and basic sentence-linking.
  3. Vocabulary and grammar from Chapters 1–10 remain active and are expected to appear accurately.
  4. Communication repair remains an active and expected part of every interaction.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — The digraph ts and vowel length in jarrabawa and ɗalibi

Practice the hooked ɗ in ɗalibi (student), keeping the implosive quality distinct from a plain English "d." Pay attention to the long vowel in the second syllable of jarrabawa (exam), which distinguishes it clearly from shorter, unrelated words. Continue building even syllable rhythm across longer words such as shirin karatu, giving each syllable comparable weight rather than stressing one syllable the way English often does.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces four connected systems: focus with ne/ce, an expanded set of prepositions and relational nouns, further negation and TAM patterns for obligation and inability, and a first step into linking two clauses together. Each system is introduced at the Developed (D) level, consistent with the Volume II matrices at Chapter 11, and will continue to expand through Chapter 15.

11.1 Focus with ne and ce

Hausa uses the particles ne (for masculine/plural reference) and ce (for feminine reference) to emphasize exactly which piece of information answers a question. The focused element usually comes first in the sentence, followed by ne/ce.

  • Malami ne ya ba ni littafin.It was the teacher who gave me the book. (emphasis on "the teacher")[focused noun] + ne + [rest of clause]
  • Jarrabawa ce ta sa ni tsoro.It's the exam that makes me afraid. (emphasis on "the exam"; ce because jarrabawa is feminine)
  • Ranar Litinin ne aka fara aji.It was on Monday that class started.

Use ne/ce when answering a question word directly, especially wa (who), me/mece (what), and yaushe (when), to give a sharp, natural-sounding answer rather than a full restated sentence.

11.2 Expanded prepositions and relational nouns: time, cause, and comparison

  • Kafin jarrabawa, ina karatu kowace rana.Before the exam, I study every day.
  • Ban fahimci darasin ba saboda wahala.I didn't understand the lesson because of the difficulty.
  • Wannan darasi ya fi wancan wahala.This lesson is more difficult than that one. (comparison with fi)
  • Ina bukatar taimako fiye da yadda nake tunani.I need help more than I thought.

Relational nouns and prepositions continue to precede the noun or clause they connect to, as established in Chapter 8. Kafin and bayan may introduce either a noun phrase or a short clause.

11.3 Negation and TAM: obligation, ability, and inability

PatternMeaningExample
Ba na iya...I cannot / am not able to...Ba na iya amsa tambayar ba. — I can't answer the question.
Bai kamata...bashould not...Bai kamata ka bar aji da wuri ba. — You shouldn't leave class early.
Dole ne ka/ki...must, have to...Dole ne ki yi karatu kafin jarrabawa. — You must study before the exam.
Ban taɓa...baI have never...Ban taɓa kasa jarrabawa ba. — I have never failed an exam.

These patterns build on the negation system developed in Chapters 6–9. Notice that most negative statements continue to use the frame ba... ba surrounding the predicate, while dole ne expresses obligation without negation.

11.4 Linking two ideas: kuma, amma, and domin

You may now connect two short sentences into one longer sentence using a small set of familiar linking words in a new, more deliberate way.

  • Ina son darasin lissafi, kuma ina son darasin Hausa.I like the math lesson, and I like the Hausa lesson.
  • Na yi karatu da yawa, amma ban fahimci komai ba.I studied a lot, but I didn't understand anything.
  • Ina bukatar taimako domin darasin ya yi wahala.I need help because the lesson is difficult.

This is a first, controlled step toward the fuller coordination and subordination system that continues developing through Chapter 15. Only kuma (and), amma (but), and domin (because) are productive at this stage.

Language Building Block — Focus + Reason Frame
[focused element] + ne/ce + clause + domin/saboda + reason
Jarrabawar lissafi ce ta ba ni wahala, domin ban yi cikakken karatu ba.
It's the math exam that gave me trouble, because I didn't study thoroughly.

Atisayen ƙa'ida — Grammar practice: Complete each sentence using a pattern from this chapter.

  1. ___ (focus, ne/ce) ya sa ni farin ciki — jarrabawa ko hutu? Choose one and write a full focused sentence.
  2. Ban iya ___ (amsa) tambayar ba domin ba ta fahimta.
  3. ___ (dole ne) ku yi karatu kafin jarrabawar gobe.
  4. Darasin kimiyya ___ (fi) darasin tarihi wahala.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Bala yana magana da abokin karatunsa, Hauwa, kafin fara aji.

(Setting: Bala is talking with his classmate Hauwa before class begins.)

HAUWA Sannu Bala, yaushe ne jarrabawar lissafi?Hello Bala, when is the math exam?
BALA Ranar Alhamis ce jarrabawar. Ina jin tsoro kaɗan, domin darasin ya yi wahala.The exam is on Thursday. I'm a little afraid, because the lesson is difficult.
HAUWA Ni ma ban fahimci komai ba a farko, amma malami ne ya taimake ni sosai.I didn't understand anything at first either, but it was the teacher who helped me a lot.
BALA Ina bukatar shirin karatu mai kyau kafin ranar Alhamis. Dole ne in yi karatu kowace safe.I need a good study plan before Thursday. I have to study every morning.
HAUWA Yi haƙuri, ban ji sarai ba — kowace safe ne, ko kowace rana?Sorry, I didn't hear clearly — every morning, or every day?
BALA Kowace safe ne, kafin in tafi aiki. Kuma za mu iya yin karatu tare bayan aji yau, saboda ni ba na iya amsa dukkan tambayoyin da kaina.Every morning, before I go to work. And we can study together after class today, because I can't answer all the questions by myself.
HAUWA To, mun yarda. Tare mun fi ƙarfi.Alright, agreed. Together we are stronger.

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. When is the math exam?
  2. Who helped Hauwa understand the lesson at first?
  3. What does Bala say he must do every morning?
  4. Why does Bala ask Hauwa to repeat herself?
  5. Why does Bala want to study together with Hauwa after class?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Learning across the lifespan

Community learning centers, evening classes, and continuing education programs are common across Hausa-speaking communities, and adult learners returning to study while working is a familiar and respected pattern rather than an exception. Educational settings range from government schools and universities to religious schools and community-run programs, and no single type of institution should be treated as the only authentic form of Hausa education.

Regional Variation — School vocabulary

Some school-related terms vary between Nigerian and Nigerien Hausa-speaking communities, and English-origin words such as maki (grade/mark) are common in Nigerian educational contexts due to the English-medium school system, while other regions may favor different terms. This course presents the Kano-based standard forms above as the productive default; other regional terms remain equally legitimate and are noted for recognition.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Wrong focus particle for gender

Error: *Jarrabawa ne ta sa ni tsoro. (using ne with a feminine noun)

Correction: Jarrabawa ce ta sa ni tsoro. — Use ce when the focused noun is feminine, as with jarrabawa, and ne for masculine or plural reference.

Common Mistake — Confusing obligation and ability

Error: *Ba na iya karatu kafin jarrabawa. when the intended meaning is "I must study before the exam" (using inability instead of obligation)

Correction: Dole ne in yi karatu kafin jarrabawa. — Use ba na iya only for genuine inability, and dole ne for obligation, as introduced in Section 11.3.

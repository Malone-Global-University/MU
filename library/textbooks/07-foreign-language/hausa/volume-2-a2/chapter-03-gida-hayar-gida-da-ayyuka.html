Gida, Hayar Gida, da Ayyuka

Housing and Household Tasks · Speak and Write Hausa: Expanding Foundations for Independent Use

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name the rooms and basic parts of a Hausa home and describe where things are located.
  2. Describe housing situations, including renting, owning, and sharing a home.
  3. Describe household chores and repairs using familiar verbs and nouns.
  4. Ask about and report a housing problem or repair need to a landlord, neighbor, or worker.
  5. Use location expressions to describe where a room, object, or problem is found.
  6. Continue to ask for clarification, repetition, or confirmation when communication breaks down.
  7. Apply ne/ce identity clauses, independent pronouns, and subject-reference forms accurately with housing and chore vocabulary.
  8. Read and write a short description of your own home and its household tasks.

Communication Scenario

After learning about Ibrahim's family in Chapter 2, you are now looking for a place to live near his neighborhood. You visit a house that is available to rent, walk through its rooms with the landlord, and later discover a small problem with the house that needs fixing. As in Chapters 1 and 2, this chapter introduces no new grammatical system. It expands your identity-clause, pronoun, and location vocabulary from Volume I into the domain of housing, rooms, chores, and repairs.

  1. Every grammar point in this chapter continues to review and extend Volume I structures — no new aspect, tense, or clause type is introduced.
  2. New vocabulary in this chapter focuses on rooms, housing terms, chores, and repair vocabulary.
  3. Communication repair remains an active, expected part of every interaction in this volume.

Pronunciation and Boko

Bita: haruffan Boko na musamman a kalmomin gida — Review: special Boko characters in housing vocabulary

This chapter's vocabulary continues to use the hooked letter ƙ (as in ƙofa) alongside the digraphs ts and sh (as in tsaftacewa and share, shara). Continue to write these characters and digraphs exactly as taught, without substituting plain letters.

Sound Focus — Vowel length in ƙofa and taga

The words ƙofa (door, gate) and taga (window) contain vowel-length distinctions not shown in ordinary Boko spelling. Practice these words aloud with a listening model, paying attention to natural rhythm rather than reading each syllable as though it carried equal weight.

Bita: jimlar "ya/ta lalace" — Review: the "it is broken" frame

The formulaic frame ya lalace / ta lalace uses the same subject-reference forms reviewed in Chapters 1 and 2 (ya for masculine subjects, ta for feminine subjects). Practice the frame with different housing nouns: famfo ya lalace (the tap is broken), ƙofa ta lalace (the door is broken).

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

As in Chapters 1 and 2, this chapter introduces no new grammatical system. It continues to expand your control of Volume I's identity clauses (ne/ce), independent pronouns, subject-reference patterns, and basic negation, now applied to housing, rooms, chores, and repairs.

3.1 Review and expansion: identity clauses with housing vocabulary

Identity clauses with ne/ce work the same way with housing vocabulary as they did with family vocabulary in Chapter 2. Agreement continues to depend on the gender and number of the subject.

  • Wannan ɗakin kwana ne.This is a bedroom.
  • Wannan ƙofa ce.This is a door.
  • Wannan gida na haya ne.This is a rented house.

3.2 Location review: describing where something is

This chapter recycles the basic location vocabulary reviewed in Chapters 1 and 2, applying it now to rooms and objects in the home. Location statements continue to use the subject-reference forms already active from Volume I.

  • Kicin yana kusa da zauren gida.The kitchen is near the sitting room.
  • Bandaki yana bayan ɗakin kwana.The bathroom is behind the bedroom.
  • Famfo yana cikin kicin.The tap is in the kitchen.

Prepositions such as kusa da (near), bayan (behind), and cikin (inside) are treated here formulaically as recognized frames; a fuller system of prepositions and relational nouns is not scheduled until later in this volume.

3.3 The formulaic frame: ya/ta lalace (it is broken)

Treat ya lalace / ta lalace as a fixed descriptive frame rather than analyzing its internal verb structure, consistent with the age-pattern approach in Chapter 2.

  • Famfo ya lalace, don haka ba mu da ruwa.The tap is broken, so we don't have water.
  • Taga ta lalace tun mako biyu.The window has been broken for two weeks.
Language Building Block
noun (room/object) + ne/ce OR yana/tana + location word / OR ya/ta lalace
Gidanmu gida ne mai ɗakuna uku: ɗakin kwana, kicin, da zauren gida. Bandaki yana kusa da ɗakin kwana. Famfo na bandaki ya lalace, don haka muna kiran mai gyara gobe.
Our house is a house with three rooms: a bedroom, a kitchen, and a sitting room. The bathroom is near the bedroom. The bathroom's tap is broken, so we are calling a repair worker tomorrow.

3.4 Review: basic negation with housing statements

  • Ba mu da wutar lantarki a yau.We don't have electricity today.
  • Wannan ba ɗakin kwana ba ne, kicin ne.This is not a bedroom, it is a kitchen.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Kuna ziyarar wani gida na haya kusa da unguwar Ibrahim. Mai gidan yana nuna muku ɗakuna, kuma daga baya kuna tattaunawa game da wata matsala a gidan.

(Setting: You are visiting a rented house near Ibrahim's neighborhood. The landlord shows you the rooms, and later you discuss a problem with the house.)

MAI GIDA Barka da zuwa. Wannan gida ne mai ɗakuna uku: ɗakin kwana, kicin, da zauren gida.Welcome. This is a house with three rooms: a bedroom, a kitchen, and a sitting room.
BALA Na gode. Ina bandaki, don Allah?Thank you. Where is the bathroom, please?
MAI GIDA Bandaki yana kusa da ɗakin kwana, a bayan gida.The bathroom is near the bedroom, at the back of the house.
BALA Yi haƙuri, ka maimaita, don Allah? Ban gane inda bandaki yake ba.Sorry, could you repeat that, please? I didn't catch where the bathroom is.
MAI GIDA Ba komai. Bandaki yana nan, kusa da ɗakin kwana — ka duba, ƙofar bandaki tana nan.No problem. The bathroom is here, near the bedroom — look, the bathroom door is right here.
BALA To, na gane yanzu. Nawa ne kuɗin haya kowane wata?Okay, I understand now. How much is the rent each month?
MAI GIDA Kuɗin haya ba shi da yawa sosai. Amma dole ne in gaya maka: famfo na kicin ya lalace, kuma mai gyara zai zo mako mai zuwa.The rent isn't very much. But I have to tell you: the kitchen tap is broken, and a repair worker will come next week.
BALA Babu matsala. Kuma wa ke tsaftace gidan waje?No problem. And who cleans the outer yard?
MAI GIDA Kowane mai haya na tsaftace gefensa, kuma muna raba ɗaukar shara tsakanin gidaje.Every renter cleans their own side, and we share taking out the trash among the households.

Vocabulary notes for the dialogue: mai haya = renter, tenant (parallel to mai gida, "owner of house"; recognition here, active later); tsakanin = between, among (recognition; a fuller preposition system is not scheduled until later in this volume); gefensa = his/her side (recognition).

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. How many rooms does the house have, and what are they?
  2. Where is the bathroom located?
  3. Why does Bala ask the landlord to repeat himself?
  4. What problem does the landlord mention, and when will it be fixed?
  5. How is cleaning the outer yard divided among the households?

Sanarwar Hayar Gida — Housing Rental Notice

Akwai gida na haya kusa da kasuwa. Gidan gida ne mai ɗakuna uku: ɗakin kwana biyu da kicin ɗaya. Bandaki yana bayan gida, kuma akwai famfo mai kyau a cikin kicin. Kuɗin haya ba shi da yawa, kuma mai gida yana zaune kusa da gidan. Duk wanda ke son duba gidan, ya tuntuɓi mai gidan da wayar salula.

Writing Workshop

  1. Karanta sanarwar hayar gida da hankali. Reread the housing rental notice and notice how it names rooms, states the rent situation, and gives contact information.
  2. Tsarawa. Plan a short paragraph describing your own home: how many rooms it has, at least one location statement, at least one chore, and one repair need (real or imagined).
  3. Rubuta. Write 60–90 words in Hausa. Include at least two identity clauses (ne/ce), one location expression, one chore or repair expression, and one negation.
  4. Duba jerin bincike. Check your draft against the checklist below.
  5. Rubuta siffa ta ƙarshe. Produce a clean final version.

Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

  • Ne and ce are used correctly according to gender/number of the subject.
  • At least one location expression uses a reviewed frame (kusa da, bayan, cikin) accurately.
  • At least one chore or repair word or phrase from this chapter's vocabulary appears.
  • Negation with ba ... ba is correctly formed if used.
  • The hooked letter ƙ and the digraphs ts/sh are used correctly, especially in housing words.
  • No grammar beyond Volume I appears (no future za, no habitual -kan-, no productive focus construction).

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Compound living and shared responsibility

Housing in many Hausa-speaking communities often takes the form of a compound (gida) shared by several households or extended family units, with shared outer yards and negotiated responsibilities for cleaning and upkeep. This arrangement varies by region, income level, and urban or rural setting, and should not be treated as the only legitimate housing pattern.

Regional Variation — Housing vocabulary

Some housing and repair vocabulary, particularly terms drawn from English contact (such as famfo), may vary in pronunciation or preferred form between Nigerian and Nigerien Hausa-speaking communities. This course presents forms most useful for general communication in Kano-based standard usage.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing ya lalace with an identity clause

Error: *Famfo lalace ne. (treating the "broken" frame like an identity clause with ne)

Correction: Famfo ya lalace. — Hausa expresses "it is broken" with the subject-reference form plus lalace, not with ne/ce. Keep the two patterns separate, just as with the age pattern in Chapter 2.

Common Mistake — Dropping the hooked letter in ƙofa

Error: *kofa written with a plain k.

Correction: ƙofa — the hooked ƙ marks a distinct sound from plain k and must always be written accurately.

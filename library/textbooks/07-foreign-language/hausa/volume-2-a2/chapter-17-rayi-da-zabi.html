Sashe na 4 — Unit IV: Shirye-shirye da Ra'ayi (Plans and Perspectives)
17

Ra'ayi da Zaɓi

Opinions and Choices · Speak and Write Hausa: Expanding Foundations for Independent Use

Chapter Overview

Chapter 17 continues Unit IV. In Chapter 16 you consolidated future and intention expressions and began developing reason and condition connectors, comparison expressions, and reference tracking in the domain of plans. This chapter keeps those same four systems active and applies them to a new communicative task: stating a preference, comparing two or more possibilities, and giving a simple reason for a choice. No new grammar system is introduced. Instead, tunda, idan, fiye da, and kamar continue developing toward consolidation, alongside a small set of new opinion-and-preference vocabulary and the verb gwammace ("to prefer").

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. State a personal preference using na fi so (I like more) and na gwammace (I prefer).
  2. Compare two or more options using fiye da and kamar, continuing from Chapter 16.
  3. Give a simple reason for a preference or choice using domin and tunda.
  4. State a condition that would change a choice using idan.
  5. Ask someone else's opinion and respond to it appropriately.
  6. Track a person, option, or choice across several connected sentences without unnecessary repetition.
  7. Read and write a short, organized paragraph stating and justifying a preference.
  8. Identify and self-correct common Unit IV errors in preference and comparison language.

Communication Scenario

Amina and Bala's community group, first introduced in Chapter 16, must now choose between two possible venues for the wedding celebration: the school field they discussed, or a community hall recently offered by a neighbor. Different family members have different opinions. Over the course of this chapter, you will help state, compare, and justify these opinions.

  1. No new vocabulary domain beyond opinions, preferences, and choices is required for this chapter.
  2. Reason and condition connectors (tunda, idan) and comparison expressions (fiye da, kamar) from Chapter 16 remain developing, not yet fully consolidated, and continue to appear in this chapter.
  3. Reference tracking across connected sentences, introduced in Chapter 16, is expected to continue without additional explanation of the underlying rule.
  4. Vocabulary and grammar from Units I, II, and III remain fully active and are expected to appear accurately.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Tone and vowel length in ra'ayi, zaɓi, and gwammace

Pay attention to the tone pattern and vowel length of this chapter's key words: ra'ayi, zaɓi, and gwammace. Note the glottal stop marked by the apostrophe in ra'ayi, which must be pronounced as a distinct break between the two syllables, not glided over as in English "reign." As in earlier chapters, keep an even syllable rhythm across full sentences such as A ganina, na fi so mu zaɓi filin makaranta. This review consolidates pronunciation features already active since earlier chapters; no new sound or contrast is introduced here.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter continues developing the four systems introduced in Chapter 16 — reason and condition, comparison, reference tracking, and controlled reading/writing — and applies them to opinions and choices. No new connector or comparison expression is introduced. Completive/perfective, negation, focus, and prepositional systems from Units I–III remain fully active.

17.1 Stating a preference with na fi so and na gwammace

  • Na fi son zama a rumfar makaranta.I prefer staying at the school hall (lit. "I exceed in wanting").
  • Na gwammace mu zaɓi wurin da yake kusa da gida.I prefer that we choose the place near the house.

Na fi so and na gwammace are both used to state a preference between options. Na gwammace is often followed directly by a clause describing the preferred action, while na fi so is often followed by a verbal noun.

17.2 Continuing: Reason and condition with tunda and idan

Language Building Block — Justifying a Preference
na gwammace / na fi so + [option], domin/tunda + [reason] idan + [condition clause], zan canza ra'ayina
Na gwammace rumfar makaranta, domin tana da wuri mai yawa.
I prefer the school hall, because it has plenty of space.
Idan an sami kuɗi mai yawa, zan canza ra'ayina in zaɓi babban wuri.
If a lot of money is found, I will change my opinion and choose a bigger place.

As in Chapter 16, tunda introduces a reason the listener already accepts, while idan introduces a genuine condition. Continue to use these connectors carefully, since they remain developing forms at this stage.

17.3 Continuing: Comparison with fiye da and kamar, and maimakon

  • Rumfar makaranta ta fi zauren al'umma fiye da girma.The school hall is bigger than the community hall.
  • Maimakon mu je zauren al'umma, mun gwammace mu je rumfar makaranta.Instead of going to the community hall, we prefer to go to the school hall.maimakon = "instead of," pairs naturally with gwammace

Maimakon ("instead of") is a new relational expression that pairs naturally with gwammace when rejecting one option in favor of another. It is introduced here as a formulaic pairing, not as a fully analyzed grammar system.

17.4 Continuing: Reference tracking in opinion exchanges

When a speaker's opinion has already been stated, later sentences should continue with a pronoun, an omitted subject, or a short agreement/disagreement expression rather than restating the full opinion.

  • Amina ta gwammace zauren al'umma. Ta ce yana kusa da gidanta, kuma ba ta son tuki mai nisa.Amina prefers the community hall. She says it is close to her house, and she doesn't want to drive far."Amina" named once; "ta" (she) tracks the reference afterward

Atisayen ƙa'ida — Grammar practice: Complete each sentence with the word or expression that fits.

  1. Na ___ (gwammace/fi so) mu zaɓi rumfar makaranta.
  2. ___ (Tunda/Idan) filin makaranta ya fi girma, zan zaɓe shi.
  3. Zauren al'umma ya fi rumfar makaranta ___ (kamar/fiye da) kusa.
  4. ___ (Maimakon/Idan) mu yi jayayya, bari mu tambayi kowa ra'ayinsa.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Amina da Bala suna tattaunawa da wani ɗan uwansu, Musa, kan zaɓin wurin bikin aure.

(Setting: Amina and Bala are discussing the choice of wedding venue with their relative, Musa.)

MUSA Ina ra'ayinku a kan wurin bikin? Zauren al'umma ko filin makaranta?What is your opinion about the venue? The community hall or the school field?
BALA A ganina, na fi son filin makaranta, domin yana da wuri mai yawa.In my view, I prefer the school field, because it has plenty of space.
AMINA Ni kuwa, na gwammace zauren al'umma, tunda yana kusa da gidan iyayenmu.As for me, I prefer the community hall, since it is close to our parents' house.
MUSA Duk zaɓukan biyu suna da kyau. Wanne ne ya fi ɗayan fiye da dacewa a ganinku?Both choices are good. Which one is more suitable in your view?
BALA Idan yawan baƙi zai yi yawa, filin makaranta ya fi dacewa, saboda girmansa.If the number of guests will be large, the school field is more suitable, because of its size.
AMINA Na yarda da dalilinka. Amma maimakon mu yi jayayya, bari mu tambayi sauran iyalinmu ra'ayinsu.I agree with your reason. But instead of arguing, let's ask the rest of our family for their opinion.
MUSA Wannan tunani ne mai kyau. Zan tattauna da kowa, kuma in gaya muku sakamakon gobe.That's a good idea. I'll talk with everyone, and I'll tell you the result tomorrow.

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. Which venue does Bala prefer, and why?
  2. Which venue does Amina prefer, and why?
  3. Under what condition does Bala say the school field is more suitable?
  4. What does Amina suggest instead of arguing?
  5. What will Musa do, and when will he report back?

Speaking Practice

Atisayen ƙa'ida — Controlled substitution drill

With a partner, take turns stating a preference between two familiar options (two foods, two routes, two days), giving one reason with domin or tunda.

Tattaunawa — Communicative task

Role-play a conversation in which two partners disagree about a choice (a venue, a plan, a purchase), each giving a reason and a comparison, and reach an agreement or agree to ask a third person, using at least one moment of communication repair.

Madadin karatu kai tsaye — Solo alternative

Record yourself stating a personal preference in at least six connected sentences, including one reason, one condition, and one comparison, while tracking yourself or another person across the sentences without repeating names unnecessarily.

Saƙo — Ra'ayin Kwamitin Makaranta (Message: School Committee's Opinion)

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Expressing disagreement respectfully

When family or community members hold different opinions, many Hausa speakers favor phrases such as a ganina ("in my view") and na yarda da dalilinka ("I agree with your reason") to soften disagreement and keep discussion cooperative, though direct disagreement is also common and appropriate depending on the relationship and setting. Neither style should be treated as more correct; both are ordinary parts of respectful discussion.

Regional Variation — Preference vocabulary

Some communities and registers use additional preference verbs and formal expressions of opinion beyond gwammace and fi so. This course presents the productive default forms for this level; further regional and formal-register vocabulary is introduced for recognition in later volumes.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing gwammace and fi so structure

Error: *Na gwammace zuwan makaranta. (treating gwammace as if it required the same verbal-noun structure as fi so)

Correction: Na gwammace in je makaranta. / Na fi son zuwa makaranta. — Gwammace is typically followed by a subjunctive-like clause, while fi so is typically followed by a verbal noun.

Common Mistake — Repeating a name instead of tracking reference

Error: *Musa ya tambaya. Musa ya saurari kowa. Musa ya gaya wa Amina. (repeating the name in every sentence)

Correction: Musa ya tambaya. Ya saurari kowa, sa'an nan ya gaya wa Amina. — Once a person has been named, continue with a pronoun, an omitted subject, or a connector, as is natural in connected Hausa narration.

  2. Maimakon mu yi jayayya, bari mu tambayi kowa.

Aikin gyara maimaici (Reference-tracking correction)

Sample: Musa ya tambaya. Ya saurari kowa, sa'an nan ya gaya wa Amina da Bala sakamakon. (Other natural revisions that reduce repetition are acceptable.)

Aikin kwatanta (Comparison practice)

Sample: Filin makaranta ya fi zauren al'umma girma. / Muna son zaɓin da ya fi dacewa kamar yadda muka tattauna. (Other accurate comparisons are acceptable.)

Atisayen ƙa'ida (Grammar practice, Section 17)

  1. Na gwammace mu zaɓi rumfar makaranta.
  2. Tunda filin makaranta ya fi girma, zan zaɓe shi. (idan also acceptable depending on intended meaning)
  3. Zauren al'umma ya fi rumfar makaranta fiye da kusa. (kamar acceptable in a different sentence frame)
  4. Maimakon mu yi jayayya, bari mu tambayi kowa ra'ayinsa.

Tambayoyin fahimta na tattaunawa (Dialogue comprehension)

  1. Bala prefers the school field because it has plenty of space.
  2. Amina prefers the community hall because it is close to their parents' house.
  3. Bala says the school field is more suitable if the number of guests will be large.
  4. Amina suggests asking the rest of the family for their opinion instead of arguing.
  5. Musa will talk with everyone and report the result tomorrow.

Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

(1) Halima prefers the small car because she doesn't want to spend a lot on fuel. (2) Sule prefers the big car because they have four children and travel long distances. (3) Sule compares the big car as more useful than the small one for a large family. (4) The family will ask an expert before choosing.

Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

  1. The school committee is discussing the choice of a new textbook.
  2. Many teachers prefer the new textbook because it contains examples more suited to life today.
  3. Some parents prefer the old textbook because students are already familiar with it.
  4. Instead of deciding right away, the committee will wait for all parents' opinions before next Friday.

Other accurate, well-formed passages meeting the stated requirements are equally acceptable.

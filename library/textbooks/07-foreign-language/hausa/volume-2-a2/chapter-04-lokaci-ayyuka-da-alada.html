4

Lokaci, Ayyuka, da Al'ada

Time, Routines, and Habits · Speak and Write Hausa: Expanding Foundations for Independent Use

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Tell time and refer to parts of the day, days of the week, and simple time expressions.
  2. Describe a daily or weekly schedule using familiar verbs and time words.
  3. Talk about how often something happens using frequency words.
  4. Describe recurring household and personal obligations.
  5. Ask someone about their own schedule, habits, and free time.
  6. Continue to ask for clarification, repetition, or confirmation when communication breaks down.
  7. Apply ne/ce identity clauses, independent pronouns, and subject-reference forms accurately with time and routine vocabulary.
  8. Read and write a short description of your own weekly schedule and habits.

Communication Scenario

Having settled into the rented house from Chapter 3, you are now getting to know your new neighbor, Hajiya Talatu. She asks about your daily schedule, and you compare notes about chores, work, and free time during the week. As in Chapters 1 through 3, this chapter introduces no new grammatical system. It expands your identity-clause, pronoun, and subject-reference vocabulary from Volume I into the domain of time, schedules, and habits.

  1. Every grammar point in this chapter continues to review and extend Volume I structures — no new aspect, tense, or clause type is introduced.
  2. New vocabulary in this chapter focuses on time expressions, days of the week, routines, and frequency words.
  3. Communication repair remains an active, expected part of every interaction in this volume.

Pronunciation and Boko

Bita: haruffan Boko na musamman a kalmomin lokaci — Review: special Boko characters in time vocabulary

This chapter's vocabulary continues to use the apostrophe/glottal convention, as in Jumma'a. Continue to write this character exactly as taught, without substituting a plain vowel sequence or dropping the glottal mark.

Sound Focus — Vowel length in rana and mako

The words rana (day) and mako (week) contain vowel-length distinctions not shown in ordinary Boko spelling. Practice these words aloud with a listening model, paying attention to natural rhythm rather than reading each syllable as though it carried equal weight.

Bita: kwanakin mako — Review: pronouncing the days of the week

The days of the week are Arabic-contact vocabulary integrated into Hausa. Practice each day name aloud with a listening model, paying particular attention to the glottal stop in Jumma'a and the natural stress pattern of Laraba and Alhamis.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

As in Chapters 1 through 3, this chapter introduces no new grammatical system. It continues to expand your control of Volume I's identity clauses (ne/ce), independent pronouns, subject-reference patterns, and basic negation, now applied to time, schedules, and habits.

4.1 Review and expansion: identity clauses with time vocabulary

Identity clauses with ne/ce work the same way with time vocabulary as they did with housing vocabulary in Chapter 3. Agreement continues to depend on the gender and number of the subject.

  • Yau Litinin ne.Today is Monday.
  • Wannan lokacin hutu ne.This is free time.
  • Jumma'a rana ce mai muhimmanci.Friday is an important day.

4.2 Review: subject-reference forms with routine statements

This chapter recycles the subject-reference forms reviewed in Chapters 1 through 3, applying them now to daily routines. Reference statements continue to use the same pronoun and subject-marker patterns already active from Volume I.

  • Ni na farka da safe kowace rana.I wake up in the morning every day.
  • Ita ta na zuwa aiki da rana.She goes to work during the day.
  • Mu muna hutu ranar Lahadi.We rest on Sunday.

Frequency words such as kowace rana (every day), sau da yawa (often), wani lokaci (sometimes), and ba kasafai ba (rarely) are treated here formulaically as recognized frames attached to already-active subject-reference patterns; a fuller system of TAM/aspect marking is not scheduled until later in this volume.

4.3 The formulaic frame: stating a schedule

Treat frequency-word placement as a fixed pattern (frequency word plus already-known subject-reference statement) rather than analyzing it as new grammar, consistent with the age-pattern approach in Chapter 2 and the lalace frame in Chapter 3.

  • Kullum ina tsaftace ɗaki na kafin zuwa aiki.I always clean my room before going to work.
  • Sau da yawa muna dafa abinci tare da yamma.We often cook food together in the evening.
Language Building Block
frequency word + pronoun/subject-marker + known verb/predicate
Kowace rana, ina farka da safe, sa'an nan na tsaftace ɗaki na. Sau da yawa, ina zuwa aiki da rana, kuma da yamma ina hutu tare da iyalina. Ranar Lahadi ba kasafai ba ne ina aiki.
Every day, I wake up in the morning, then I clean my room. I often go to work during the day, and in the evening I rest with my family. On Sunday I rarely work.

4.4 Review: basic negation with time and habit statements

  • Ba na zuwa aiki ranar Lahadi.I don't go to work on Sunday.
  • Wannan ba lokacin hutu ba ne, lokacin aiki ne.This is not free time, it is work time.

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Kuna hira da makwabciyarku, Hajiya Talatu, game da jadawalin makonku da ayyukanku na yau da kullum.

(Setting: You are chatting with your neighbor, Hajiya Talatu, about your weekly schedule and daily routines.)

HAJIYA TALATU Barka da rana. Yaya jadawalin ku a wannan mako?Good afternoon. How is your schedule this week?
BALA Lafiya lau. Kowace rana, ina farka da safe, sa'an nan na tsaftace ɗaki na kafin zuwa aiki.Fine. Every day, I wake up in the morning, then I clean my room before going to work.
HAJIYA TALATU To, wane lokaci ka ke zuwa aiki?Okay, what time do you go to work?
BALA Yi haƙuri, ki maimaita, don Allah? Ban gane tambayar ba sosai.Sorry, could you repeat that, please? I didn't quite catch the question.
HAJIYA TALATU Na tambaya: wane karfe ka ke zuwa aiki da safe?I asked: what time do you go to work in the morning?
BALA To, na gane yanzu. Ina zuwa aiki da safe, kuma ina dawowa gida da yamma.Okay, I understand now. I go to work in the morning, and I return home in the evening.
HAJIYA TALATU Kai kuma, kana da lokacin hutu a wannan mako?And you, do you have free time this week?
BALA Ranar Lahadi ba kasafai ba ne ina aiki, don haka wannan ita ce ranar hutu na.I rarely work on Sunday, so that is my rest day.
HAJIYA TALATU Ni ma, sau da yawa ina hutu ranar Lahadi. Amma ranar Jumma'a, ina zuwa masallaci da safe.Me too, I often rest on Sunday. But on Friday, I go to the mosque in the morning.

Vocabulary notes for the dialogue: wane karfe = what time (recognition here, formulaic frame; a fuller clock-time system is not analyzed further in this chapter); dawowa = to return (recognition); masallaci = mosque (recognition; religiously marked vocabulary, presented as one common practice among several, per the master instructions on religion and cultural representation).

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. What does Bala do every day before going to work?
  2. Why does Bala ask Hajiya Talatu to repeat her question?
  3. Which day is Bala's rest day, and why?
  4. What does Hajiya Talatu do on Friday mornings?
  5. How often does Hajiya Talatu rest on Sunday?

Writing Workshop

  1. Karanta jadawalin aiki da hankali. Reread the weekly work schedule notice and notice how it names days, states work times, and marks rest days.
  2. Tsarawa. Plan a short paragraph describing your own weekly schedule: what time you wake up, which days you work or study, at least one frequency word, and one rest day.
  3. Rubuta. Write 60–90 words in Hausa. Include at least two identity clauses (ne/ce), one frequency word, one routine or chore expression, and one negation.
  4. Duba jerin bincike. Check your draft against the checklist below.
  5. Rubuta siffa ta ƙarshe. Produce a clean final version.

Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

  • Ne and ce are used correctly according to gender/number of the subject.
  • At least one frequency word (kowace rana, sau da yawa, wani lokaci, ba kasafai ba, kullum) is used accurately.
  • At least one day of the week appears correctly spelled.
  • Negation with ba ... ba is correctly formed if used.
  • The apostrophe/glottal convention in Jumma'a is used correctly, if included.
  • No grammar beyond Volume I appears (no future za, no habitual -kan-, no productive focus construction).

Speaking Practice

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — The rhythm of the week

In many Hausa-speaking communities, Friday (Jumma'a) carries particular significance for Muslim residents, often shaping midday schedules and business hours, while other households organize their week around different religious, educational, or civic rhythms. This varies by community, religion, and individual household, and should not be treated as a universal pattern for all Hausa speakers.

Regional Variation — Clock time and schedules

Ways of expressing clock time and describing work schedules may vary somewhat between Nigerian and Nigerien Hausa-speaking communities, as well as between urban and rural settings. This course presents forms most useful for general communication in Kano-based standard usage.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Dropping the glottal mark in Jumma'a

Error: *Jummaa or *Jumma written without the apostrophe.

Correction: Jumma'a — the apostrophe marks a glottal stop that is a real part of the word's pronunciation and must always be written accurately.

Common Mistake — Treating frequency words as verbs

Error: *Kullum na. (using a frequency word alone as if it were a complete predicate)

Correction: Kullum ina tsaftace ɗaki na. — Frequency words like kullum attach to an already-known subject-reference statement; they do not replace the verb or predicate. Keep the two parts together, just as with the lalace frame in Chapter 3.

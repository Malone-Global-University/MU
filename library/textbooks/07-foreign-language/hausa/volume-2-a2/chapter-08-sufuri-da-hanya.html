Sufuri da Hanya

Transport and Routes · Speak and Write Hausa: Expanding Foundations for Independent Use

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common forms of local transport and route-related vocabulary in Boko.
  2. Ask for and give simple directions using relational nouns and prepositions of place.
  3. Continue using existence, possession, and quantity expressions (akwai, yana/tana da) in a transport context.
  4. Describe delays and sequence a short trip using recycled time expressions and connectors.
  5. Use object pronouns with transport-related verbs in present-based frames.
  6. Extend negation to location, direction, and delay statements.
  7. Politely ask a driver or fellow passenger for help, clarification, or repetition.
  8. Read a short transport schedule and a set of route directions.
  9. Write a short paragraph describing how to get from one place to another.

Communication Scenario

After finishing his shopping at Kasuwar Kurmi in Chapter 7, Bala needs to get himself and his purchases back home. He must find the right vehicle, ask the driver about the route and the fare, and manage a delay along the way. This chapter continues Unit II by extending the existence, possession, and quantity clauses developed so far into location and direction language, and by introducing prepositions and relational nouns for handling transport and travel sequencing.

  1. New vocabulary in this chapter centers on transport, routes, and directions.
  2. New grammar introduces relational nouns and prepositions of place, along with connectors for sequencing a trip.
  3. The akwai/yana-tana da system, object pronouns, and negation from Chapters 6–7 continue to develop and now cover location and travel verbs.
  4. All vocabulary and grammar from Chapters 1–7 remain available for recycling.
  5. Communication repair remains an active, expected part of every interaction.

Pronunciation and Boko Focus

8.1 Tone contrast: hanya versus haya

Practice the tone and vowel quality of hanya (road, way) carefully, and compare it with the similar-looking word haya (rent), reviewed from Chapter 3. These words are distinguished by tone and by the medial consonant cluster, not by stress placement as in English.

Sound Focus — The digraph ts and the special consonant ƙ in tasha and tuƙa

Continue practicing the digraph ts, reviewed from earlier chapters, in words like tasha (station). Also practice the ejective ƙ in tuƙa (to drive), which is produced with a sharper, more forceful release than an ordinary English "k." Say tuƙa slowly, then at a natural pace, and compare it to kuɗi (money) from Chapter 7, which uses an ordinary "k."

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter extends the existence, possession, object-pronoun, and negation patterns from Chapters 6–7 into location and direction language, and introduces prepositions and relational nouns for handling transport and travel sequencing. All forms below build directly on the subject-marker system introduced in Chapter 6.

8.2 Relational nouns of place: gaba, baya, dama, hagu

Hausa expresses many spatial relations using a relational noun followed by da and the reference point, rather than a single one-word preposition. This is the same relational pattern used for possession-like relations and now applies to direction.

  • Tashar bas tana gaba da kanti.The bus station is ahead of the stall.
  • Mota tana baya da makaranta.The car is behind the school.
  • Ku juya hagu a tashar.Turn left at the station.
Language Note — Relational nouns versus single-word prepositions

Gaba, baya, dama, and hagu function as relational nouns rather than fixed prepositions. They combine with da to introduce the reference point, following the same pattern reviewed with kusa da and nesa da. Do not translate these word-for-word as single English prepositions without the accompanying example.

8.3 Object pronouns with transport verbs

Object pronouns from Chapter 7 continue to be productive in present-based frames, now with verbs such as hau (board) and sauka daga (get off from).

8.4 Negation with location, direction, and delay

Negation forms from Chapters 6–7 (babu, ba ... da, negative continuous/habitual) now extend to location, direction, and delay statements.

  • Babu mota kai tsaye zuwa gari.There isn't a direct vehicle to town.
  • Ba mu da lokaci mai yawa domin jinkiri.We don't have much time because of the delay.
  • Ba na tuƙa da dare.I don't drive at night.
Language Building Block
direction/location clause + existence/possession clause + negation + repair request
Tashar bas tana gaba da kanti. Akwai mota zuwa gari, amma babu wanda ke kai tsaye. Za ka iya maimaita sunan tashar, don Allah?
The bus station is ahead of the stall. There is a vehicle to town, but there isn't one that goes directly. Could you repeat the name of the station, please?

Atisayen A — Cika gibi (Fill in the blank): Complete each sentence with the correct relational noun, existence/possession clause, or negation.

  1. Tashar keke tana ___ da makaranta. (behind)
  2. ___ mota kai tsaye zuwa kasuwa yau, amma ___ bas mai yawa.
  3. Mu ___ (ba ... da) kuɗin sufuri mai yawa domin tafiya mai nisa.
  4. Ina son hawan ___ (object pronoun for "ta," referring to mota).

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Bala ya isa tashar bas domin komawa gida bayan siyayya a Kasuwar Kurmi.

(Setting: Bala arrives at the bus station to go home after shopping at Kasuwar Kurmi.)

BALA Sannu. Ina tashar bas zuwa Sabon Gari?Hello. Where is the bus station to Sabon Gari?
DIREBA Sannu, ɗan'uwa. Tana gaba da kanti, kusa da hanya babba.Hello, brother. It's ahead of the stall, near the main road.
BALA Na gode. Akwai mota kai tsaye zuwa can?Thank you. Is there a vehicle that goes directly there?
DIREBA Babu kai tsaye a wannan lokaci, akwai cuwa-cuwa a hanya. Amma ina son sauka a wata tasha, sannan ka hau wani bas.There isn't a direct one at this time, there is traffic on the road. But I'd suggest getting off at another station, then you board another bus. (recognition: suggestion framing, glossed)
BALA To, mai kyau. Nawa ne kuɗin sufuri zuwa can?Okay, good. How much is the fare there?
DIREBA Naira ɗari uku ne, saboda jinkiri a yau.It's three hundred naira, because of the delay today. (recognition: number-price formula, glossed)
BALA Yi haƙuri, za ka maimaita sunan tashar da zan sauka a can, don Allah? Ban ji sarai ba.Sorry, could you repeat the name of the station where I should get off, please? I didn't hear it clearly.
DIREBA Tashar Kofar Ruwa. Ba ta da nisa da nan.Kofar Ruwa Station. It isn't far from here.
BALA Na gode sosai. Ina son hawan ta yanzu.Thank you very much. I want to board it now.
DIREBA Babu matsala. Ku hau, don Allah.No problem. Please board.

Vocabulary notes for the dialogue: naira ɗari uku = three hundred naira (recognition, number-price formula built on recycled numerals, not assessed for production as a full sentence); ina son sauka... sannan ka hau = suggestion framing built on the present-based frame already active, glossed to highlight its function as advice rather than a new productive pattern.

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. What does Bala first ask the driver?
  2. Why is there no direct vehicle to Sabon Gari today?
  3. What does the driver suggest Bala do instead?
  4. What does Bala do when he doesn't hear the station name clearly?
  5. Where does Bala decide to get off?

Reading

Hanyar Zuwa Tasha da Bayanin Lokaci — Route Directions and Schedule Note

Barka da zuwa Tashar Kofar Ruwa! Domin isa nan daga kasuwa, ku bi hanya babba, sannan ku juya dama a kanti. Tashar tana kusa da makaranta, kuma tana baya da babban banki. Writing Workshop

  1. Karanta bayanin hanya da hankali. Reread the route directions and notice how they combine direction expressions, existence/possession clauses, and negation with a travel purpose.
  2. Tsarawa. Plan a short paragraph describing how to get from one place to another that you know well.
  3. Rubuta. Write 90–120 words in Hausa. Include at least one relational-noun direction expression, one existence/possession clause, one negation, and one object pronoun.
  4. Duba jerin bincike. Check your draft against the checklist below.
  5. Rubuta siffa ta ƙarshe. Produce a clean final version.

Jerin binciken gyara — Editing checklist:

  • At least one direction or location expression with a relational noun (gaba da, baya da, kusa da, nesa da) appears accurately.
  • At least one existence or possession clause (akwai, yana/tana da, or negative) appears accurately.
  • At least one negation form from Chapters 6–8 is used correctly.
  • At least one object pronoun is used correctly with a transport verb.
  • Prior systems (identity clauses, pronouns, continuous/habitual frame, price questions with nawa) remain accurate where they appear.
  • No grammar beyond this chapter's scope appears (no productive future za, no productive past na/ka/ta/mu/ku/sun, no productive possessive-suffix system).
  • Special characters (ɓ, ɗ, ƙ) and the apostrophe/glottal convention are used accurately.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Shared transport and flexible routes

In many Hausa-speaking communities, local transport often involves shared vehicles that fill before departing, and routes and fares can shift with traffic, time of day, and demand. Asking a driver or fellow passenger for the current route, fare, or expected delay is a normal and expected part of everyday travel, not a sign of unfamiliarity.

Regional Variation — Names for vehicles and stations

Terms for local transport, station names, and typical fares vary between Nigerian and Nigerien Hausa-speaking communities, and between cities. This course presents forms most useful for general communication in Kano-based standard usage; other regional terms are equally legitimate and should be adjusted for local context.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Omitting da after relational nouns of place

Error: *Tashar tana gaba kanti. (omitting da before the reference point)

Correction: Tashar tana gaba da kanti. — Relational nouns of place such as gaba and baya require da before the reference point, just as kusa da and nesa da do.

Common Mistake — Confusing dama and hagu with gaba and baya

Error: *Ku juya gaba a tashar. (using gaba where a side-direction word is needed)

Correction: Ku juya hagu a tashar. — Use dama (right) or hagu (left) for turning instructions; reserve gaba and baya for describing relative position, not the direction of a turn.

  2. There is no direct vehicle because of traffic on the road.
  3. The driver suggests Bala get off at another station and board a different bus.
  4. Bala politely asks the driver to repeat the station name.
  5. Bala decides to get off at Tashar Kofar Ruwa.

Kula na sauraro (Listening responses)

(1) The bus station is near the speaker's house, ahead of the stall. (2) Traffic on the road sometimes causes a delay. (3) Because there was no direct vehicle today, the speaker had to get off at another station and board a different bus. (4) The speaker does not like the delay, but had no other choice today.

Tambayoyin karatu (Reading comprehension)

  1. Travelers should follow the main road, then turn right at the stall.
  2. The station is near the school and behind the main bank.
  3. The bus to Sabon Gari runs every half hour.
  4. A traveler who wants a direct trip should come in the morning.

Aikin Ƙarshe: Hulɗa Mai Zaman Kanta

Final B1 Transition Project · Speak and Write Hausa: Expanding Foundations for Independent Use

Chapter Overview

Chapter 20 closes Unit IV and closes Volume II. No new grammar system and no new vocabulary domain are introduced in this chapter. Instead, this chapter brings together the reason, condition, comparison, and reference-tracking systems developed across Chapters 16–19, together with the fuller TAM/aspect, negation, focus, and prepositional systems consolidated earlier in Units II and III, in one integrated familiar-domain project. You will plan an event, discuss and revise the plan, compare options, give reasons, attach conditions, announce the outcome to a group, and narrate how the plan came together — combining listening, speaking, reading, and writing in a single connected task. This chapter formally assesses the systems scheduled across Chapters 16 through 19 and marks your transition point from Volume II toward Volume III: Independent Communication.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Plan and discuss an event or arrangement using future/intention expressions and connected sentence sequencing developed since Chapter 16.
  2. State a reason for a decision using domin and tunda.
  3. Attach a condition to a plan using idan.
  4. Compare two options, plans, or arrangements using fiye da and kamar.
  5. Announce a decision or change to a group using ana sanar da, a sani cewa, and an canza.
  6. Close a message or presentation politely using da fatan.
  7. Track a named person, place, or event across several connected sentences without unnecessary repetition.
  8. Narrate, in connected form, how a plan was made, discussed, revised, and finally decided.
  9. Complete an integrated listening, speaking, reading, and writing project that draws on vocabulary and grammar from across Volume II.
  10. Self-assess readiness to move from Volume II to Volume III: Independent Communication.

Communication Scenario

Amina, Bala, and Musa's community group has now announced the change of venue for their celebration and posted the news at the mosque and the market. With the event only weeks away, the group must finish planning the celebration itself: deciding on food, arranging contributions, comparing options for entertainment, and preparing a final announcement and a short account of how the planning went. This final project follows their planning from the first meeting to the finished event, and asks you to complete a similar project of your own.

  1. No new vocabulary domain is introduced in this chapter. All vocabulary is drawn from Chapters 1–19, with heaviest reliance on Chapters 14–19.
  2. No new grammar system is introduced. This chapter formally assesses tunda, idan, fiye da, kamar, reference tracking, and the announcement expressions introduced in Chapters 16–19, alongside TAM/aspect, negation, and focus systems consolidated in Units II and III.
  3. This chapter functions as the Volume II capstone: performance here provides the primary evidence for readiness to begin Volume III.
  4. Vocabulary and grammar from Units I, II, and III remain fully active and are expected to appear accurately throughout the project.

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — Rhythm and tone across a full connected narration

This chapter's pronunciation focus is consolidation rather than a new sound or contrast. As your sentences grow longer and more connected — moving from a single announcement sentence to a full multi-sentence narration of how a plan developed — keep the even syllable rhythm already practiced since earlier chapters, and continue to observe tone and vowel length in high-value words such as tunda, idan, fiye da, sanarwa, and da fatan. Pay particular attention to phrase-final rhythm: connected Hausa narration should not speed up or flatten in tone toward the end of a long sentence. Practice reading your final written project aloud at a controlled, even pace before recording your speaking task.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new grammar system. It integrates and formally assesses the four systems developed since Chapter 16 — reason and condition, comparison, reference tracking, and announcement/update language — together with the TAM/aspect, negation, and focus systems consolidated across Units II and III.

20.1 Reviewing: Connecting a full planning narration

A connected narration of a plan typically moves through several stages: an initial idea or need, a discussion, one or more reasons, a condition attached to the plan, a comparison between options, a decision, and an announcement. Each stage can be linked using the connectors already active since Chapter 16, without introducing any new connecting device.

  • Da farko, mun yanke shawarar shirya biki, domin al'umma tana son yin bikin murnar kammala gyaran zauren.At first, we decided to organize a celebration, because the community wanted to celebrate finishing the hall repair.
  • Sai muka tattauna zaɓuɓɓuka, tunda ba mu tabbata ko za mu sami isasshen kuɗi ba.Then we discussed the options, since we weren't sure we would have enough money.

20.2 Reviewing: Condition and comparison in decision-making

Language Building Block — Weighing Two Options for a Plan
idan + [condition clause], za mu zaɓi + [option A] [option A] ya/ta fi [option B] + [quality] fiye da/kamar yadda muke tsammani
Idan mutane da yawa sun bayar da gudummawa, za mu zaɓi shirya babban abinci ga kowa.
If many people contribute, we will choose to prepare a big meal for everyone.
Waƙa da rawa sun fi sauƙi shiryawa fiye da babban abinci, amma abinci ya fi muhimmanci kamar yadda dattawa suka ce.
Music and dancing are easier to arrange than a big meal, but food is more important, as the elders said.

As in Chapters 17–18, idan introduces a genuine future condition, and fiye da and kamar compare two options against each other or against an expectation. These forms are expected to be used accurately in this chapter's project, consistent with their developing (not yet fully consolidated) status noted since Chapter 16.

20.3 Reviewing: Announcing the final decision

  • Ana sanar da al'umma cewa an gama shirin bikin, kuma za a gudanar da shi ranar Asabar mai zuwa a makarantar al'umma.It is announced to the community that the celebration plan is finished, and it will be held next Saturday at the community school.
  • A sani cewa duk wanda yake son bayar da gudummawa, ya kai ta ga kwamitin gudanarwa kafin ranar Alhamis.Be it known that anyone who wishes to contribute should bring it to the organizing committee before Thursday.

These announcement expressions, introduced in Chapter 19, are used here to close a planning narration with a clear, public decision, often followed by da fatan as a polite closing wish.

20.4 Reviewing: Reference tracking across a long narration

The longer a narration becomes, the more important reference tracking is. Once a group, event, or plan has been named, continue referring to it with a pronoun, an omitted subject, or a natural connector, rather than repeating the full name in every sentence.

  • Kwamitin gudanarwa ya fara tattaunawa a kan bikin makonni uku da suka wuce. Sun tattauna zaɓuɓɓuka da dama, sai suka zaɓi mafi kyawun shirin, kuma yanzu suna shirin sanar da al'umma.The organizing committee started discussing the celebration three weeks ago. They discussed several options, then chose the best plan, and now they are preparing to inform the community."Kwamitin gudanarwa" named once; "sun/suna" (they) track the reference across three following clauses

Model Dialogue

Yanayin hali: Makonni bayan sanarwar farko, Amina, Bala, da Musa suna gama shirin bikin murnar gyaran zauren al'umma, kuma suna shirin sanar da al'umma ƴan sa'o'i kafin ranar biki.

(Setting: Weeks after the first announcement, Amina, Bala, and Musa are finishing the plan for the celebration marking the community hall repair, and are preparing to inform the community a few hours before the event.)

AMINA Mun tattauna da dattawa jiya, kuma sun ce abinci ya fi muhimmanci fiye da waƙa da rawa a wannan biki.We discussed with the elders yesterday, and they said food is more important than music and dancing at this celebration.
BALA To, idan haka ne, za mu tara gudummawar abinci daga gidaje da dama, tunda kwamitin ba shi da isasshen kuɗi shi kaɗai.Well, if that's the case, we'll gather food contributions from several households, since the committee alone doesn't have enough money.
MUSA Zan tuntuɓi masu waƙa domin su yi ƴan waƙoƴi kaɗan kawai, kamar yadda muka yi shekarar bara.I'll contact the musicians so they play just a few songs, as we did last year.
AMINA Yanzu bari mu rubuta sanarwar ƙarshe. Ana sanar da al'umma cewa an gama shirin bikin, kuma za a fara da karfe biyu na yamma.Now let's write the final announcement. It is announced to the community that the celebration plan is finished, and it will begin at two in the afternoon.
BALA A sani cewa duk wanda ya kawo gudummawar abinci, ya kai ta zauren al'umma kafin karfe goma na safe.Be it known that anyone bringing a food contribution should bring it to the community hall before ten in the morning.
MUSA Da fatan kowa zai zo da murna, bayan dukkan wahalar da muka sha wajen shirya wannan biki.It is hoped everyone will come joyfully, after all the trouble we went through to organize this celebration.
AMINA Mun yi shi tare, kuma mun koyi abubuwa da yawa game da yadda al'umma take taimakon kanta.We did it together, and we learned many things about how the community helps itself.

Tambayoyin fahimta — Comprehension questions:

  1. What did the elders decide was more important, and why does that matter for planning?
  2. What condition leads the group to gather food contributions from households?
  3. How does Musa compare this year's music plan to last year's?
  4. What are the two pieces of information in the final announcement?
  5. What does Musa hope for, and what reason does he give?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Collective planning and shared decision-making

Community events such as celebrations, school functions, and youth projects are often planned through rounds of discussion involving several stakeholders — organizing committees, elders, teachers, or youth leaders — rather than a single decision-maker. Consulting elders or respected community members before finalizing a plan, as seen in this chapter's dialogue and reading, reflects a common but not universal pattern of shared decision-making; the degree of consultation and the specific people consulted vary by community, setting, and the nature of the event.

Regional Variation — Event planning language across contexts

The planning and announcement vocabulary in this chapter reflects a general, widely usable register suitable for community, family, and school contexts. More formal or institutional planning language, used for example in government, religious, or large-scale civic contexts, draws on a wider and more specialized vocabulary that is introduced for recognition in later volumes.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing tunda (accepted reason) and idan (future condition)

Error: *Tunda mutane da yawa sun bayar da gudummawa, za mu iya shirya babban biki. (using tunda to describe a condition that has not yet happened)

Correction: Idan mutane da yawa sun bayar da gudummawa, za mu iya shirya babban biki. — Tunda introduces a reason already accepted as true; idan introduces a genuine future condition. A plan that depends on something not yet decided needs idan, not tunda.

Common Mistake — Losing reference tracking in a long narration

Error: *Kwamitin gudanarwa ya tattauna shirin. Kwamitin gudanarwa ya zaɓi ranar biki. Kwamitin gudanarwa ya rubuta sanarwar ƙarshe. (repeating the full committee name in every sentence of a longer narration)

Correction: Kwamitin gudanarwa ya tattauna shirin, sa'an nan ya zaɓi ranar biki, kuma daga baya ya rubuta sanarwar ƙarshe. — As a narration grows longer, reference tracking becomes more, not less, important. Continue with a pronoun, an omitted subject, or a connector once the subject is established.

Across Unit IV, you developed reason and condition connectors, comparison expressions, announcement and update language, and reference tracking across connected discourse, applying these systems to plans, opinions, problems, community news, and, in this chapter, a full integrated planning project. Together with the vocabulary, TAM/aspect, negation, focus, and prepositional systems consolidated in Units I–III, these systems complete the developmental arc of Volume II: Expanding Foundations for Independent Use.

Mai Zuwa — Coming Up

Volume III: Independent Communication builds on the foundation completed here, developing fuller connected discourse, richer TAM/aspect and focus control, expanded vocabulary domains, and longer spoken and written texts, so that learners can discuss experiences, plans, problems, opinions, and community topics with increasing independence in connected Hausa.

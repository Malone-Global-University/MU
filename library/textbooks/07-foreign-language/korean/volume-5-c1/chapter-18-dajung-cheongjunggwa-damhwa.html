Chapter 18 다중 청중 담화
Multi-Audience Communication

Chapter Overview

In Chapter 17 you learned to edit a single text for one audience. In this chapter you take one underlying message and learn to reshape it for several different audiences at once — a general public reader, a specialist or expert, a fellow learner or newcomer, and a professional colleague — without changing the underlying facts. This is a core C2-oriented skill: recognizing that the same information demands different vocabulary density, sentence complexity, honorific weight, and explanatory scaffolding depending on who is listening or reading. No new grammar, particle, or speech-level system is introduced here; instead, you will consciously redeploy systems already active in your Korean to serve different readers and listeners.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, learners will be able to:

  • identify what a specific audience needs more or less explanation of, given their background;
  • adapt vocabulary density, sentence length, and explanatory scaffolding to a named audience;
  • adjust honorific weight and address terms appropriately across public, expert, learner, and professional audiences;
  • produce three audience-specific versions of the same core content;
  • explain, in Korean, why a specific adaptation choice suits a given audience;
  • deliver a short spoken explanation of the same information twice, adjusted for two different listeners.

Opening Communication Scenario

당신은 한 공공 기관의 홍보실에서 일하며, 새로운 대중교통 요금 개편안에 대해 설명해야 합니다. 같은 내용을 (1) 일반 시민을 위한 안내문, (2) 관련 분야 전문가를 위한 보고서, (3) 한국어를 배우는 외국인 학습자를 위한 쉬운 설명으로 각각 다시 구성해야 합니다.

(You work in the public relations office of a public institution and must explain a new public transit fare restructuring plan. You must reshape the same content into (1) a notice for the general public, (2) a report for field experts, and (3) an easy explanation for foreign learners of Korean.)

Hangeul, Orthography, and Digital Focus

This chapter's orthographic focus is formatting choices that signal audience: bullet points, bolded key terms, and short paragraphs for public notices versus dense, fully punctuated prose paragraphs with footnote-style citations for expert reports. Digital tasks include reformatting the same paragraph into a bulleted public notice and a continuous-prose expert paragraph, and typing a short simplified caption suitable for a learner-facing infographic.

Grammar, Style, and Sentence Patterns Principal Focus

Principal scheduled systems for Chapter 18: adaptation across public, expert, learner, and professional audiences. No new grammar, particle, or speech-level system is introduced. Instead, this chapter reorganizes previously activated systems — speech level, noun modification, nominalization, connectors, and vocabulary strata — around the single variable of audience.

1. Sentence length and embedding by audience

Compare how the same fact can be expressed with heavy noun-modifying embedding (suited to expert readers) or split into two short sentences (suited to the general public or learners).

Expert version (embedded)Public version (split)
기존 요금 체계의 한계를 보완하기 위해 도입된 이번 개편안은 이용 거리와 시간대에 따라 요금을 차등 적용하는 방식을 채택하였다.이번 개편안은 기존 요금 체계의 문제를 보완하기 위해 마련되었습니다. 이제 이용 거리와 시간대에 따라 요금이 달라집니다.

2. Terminology substitution

Practice replacing Sino-Korean technical compounds with everyday paraphrase, and vice versa, while preserving accuracy.

example: 전문가용 — "차등 요금제" (tiered/differentiated fare system) → 대중용 — "거리와 시간에 따라 다르게 매기는 요금" (a fare that is set differently according to distance and time)
The underlying policy is identical; only the density and technicality of the wording changes.

3. Honorific and address adjustment

Review how the same information shifts speech level and address terms: an internal professional memo may use plain or -습니다 style with colleague-level distance, a public notice uses deferential -습니다 with respectful distance from an unspecified public, and a learner-facing explanation may use -아요/어요 for warmth and accessibility, without becoming inappropriately casual.

4. Scaffolding for learners and non-specialists

Use analogy (비유), concrete examples (예시), and explicit definition of technical terms the first time they appear, reserving unexplained technical density for the expert version only.

Speaking Practice

  • Role-play: One partner explains a chosen topic (e.g., a new library policy) to a "general public" listener; the other then re-explains the same topic to a "specialist" listener, adjusting vocabulary and tone aloud.
  • Short presentation: Prepare a one-minute explanation of a topic from an earlier chapter, then deliver it twice — once for a Korean-learner audience, once for a professional audience — noting aloud what changed.
  • Solo alternative: Record yourself explaining the same fact three times, addressing an imagined child, an imagined colleague, and an imagined expert, then compare your word choices.

Writing Workshop

Task: Choose one piece of factual content (a policy, a scientific finding, or an institutional announcement — your own invention or one provided by an instructor). Produce:

  1. A public-facing notice version (80–120 words) using accessible vocabulary, short sentences, and deferential -습니다 tone;
  2. An expert/professional version (100–150 words) using precise terminology, embedded noun-modifying clauses, and appropriate citation-style hedging;
  3. A learner-facing version (80–120 words) for intermediate Korean learners, using simpler grammar, glossed technical terms, and at least one analogy;
  4. A short written note (2–3 sentences) explaining the single most important adaptation you made for each audience.

Success criteria: all three versions convey the same underlying facts accurately; each version is clearly distinguishable in vocabulary density, sentence complexity, and tone; no version contains information the others lack; adaptation choices are justified by audience need rather than arbitrary variation.

Culture and Communication

South Korean public institutions, media outlets, and academic publishers frequently produce parallel versions of the same announcement for different audiences — a short 보도자료 (press release) for general media, a technical 보고서 (report) for oversight bodies, and increasingly, plain-language or multilingual versions for residents with limited Korean proficiency. This layered communication practice reflects an institutional norm of audience awareness rather than any single cultural rule, and its specific conventions vary by institution, sector, and era.

Common Mistakes

  • Changing the facts, not just the register: learners sometimes drop or distort actual content when simplifying for a public or learner audience, rather than only adjusting vocabulary and sentence structure.
  • Over-simplifying the expert version: some learners accidentally soften the expert version too, losing the precision and terminology that specialist readers expect and need.
  • Mismatched honorific weight: using casual -아요/어요 forms for a formal public notice, or an overly stiff -습니다 tone for a friendly learner-facing explanation, when the audience calls for the opposite.

Chapter Summary

This chapter asked learners to hold one set of facts steady while reshaping vocabulary density, sentence complexity, honorific tone, and explanatory scaffolding for four different audience types: general public, expert, learner, and professional. No new grammar system was introduced; instead, learners practiced deliberately redeploying already-active systems — noun modification, connectors, vocabulary strata, and speech level — according to audience need, a skill central to C2-oriented mediation.

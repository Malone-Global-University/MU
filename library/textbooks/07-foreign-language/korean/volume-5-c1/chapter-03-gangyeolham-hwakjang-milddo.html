Continuing Volume V · Speak and Write Korean: Mastery, Precision, Rhetoric, and Specialized Expression

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Compress a sentence into a denser nominalized form appropriate to formal written registers, without losing its original meaning.
  2. Expand a compressed or clipped sentence into a fuller, more explicit form appropriate to spoken explanation or careful writing.
  3. Choose between a compressed and an expanded version of the same content according to genre, audience, and purpose.
  4. Control rhetorical pacing across a passage by varying sentence length and density for emphasis, transition, and closure.
  5. Identify nominalized structures in an authentic-style formal text and restate their meaning in fuller, plainer sentences.
  6. Revise a piece of your own writing by tightening some sentences and expanding others for a more deliberate rhetorical effect.

This chapter continues Unit I: 정확성, 문체, 그리고 매개 (Precision, Style, and Mediation). It builds on Chapter 2's argument architecture and introduces no new endings, particles, or predicate systems beyond nominalization patterns already active from Volume IV. Its new material is stylistic control: when to compress and when to expand already-known Korean for rhetorical effect.

발음과 억양 — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation and Delivery Focus: Pacing Dense and Loose Sentences Aloud

Compressed, nominalized sentences and expanded, explanatory sentences are delivered differently aloud. This chapter introduces no new phonology; it reviews how experienced speakers slow down and add internal pauses when reading dense, nominalized writing aloud, and how they use a faster, more connected rhythm for looser, expanded explanation.

Pronunciation Note — Reading dense prose aloud

A heavily nominalized sentence packs several ideas into a single noun phrase, so a reader who wants to be understood aloud typically adds a short internal pause after each major noun phrase rather than reading straight through at a uniform pace. An expanded, explanatory sentence, by contrast, is usually read with fewer internal pauses and a more even, connected rhythm, since the ideas are already spread across separate clauses.

Practice: read the following compressed sentence aloud, pausing briefly after each bracketed noun phrase, and then read its expanded version aloud at a more even pace, noticing how differently the two versions feel to deliver.

  • [통근 시간 감소로 인한] [피로도 완화]가 [생산성 향상]으로 이어진다.The fatigue relief resulting from reduced commuting time leads to increased productivity.
  • 통근 시간이 줄어들면 피로가 줄어들고, 그 결과 생산성이 높아진다.If commuting time decreases, fatigue decreases, and as a result, productivity increases.
압축과 확장 — Compression and Expansion Architecture

Compression and Expansion: Nominalization, Density, and Rhetorical Pacing

This chapter's principal system is stylistic control over sentence density rather than new sentence-level grammar. The nominalizing patterns below are already active from Volume IV; what is new is using them deliberately to compress or expand the same content, and pacing a passage by alternating dense and loose sentences for effect.

1. Compressing a clause into a nominalized noun phrase

A clause built on an action or descriptive verb can be compressed into a noun phrase using already-active nominalizers such as -음/-기 and derived nouns such as 완화, 향상, and 감소. This packs a full proposition into a single noun phrase that can then serve as a subject or object of a further sentence.

  • 통근 시간이 줄어들면 피로가 줄어든다. → 통근 시간 감소로 인한 피로도 완화If commuting time decreases, fatigue decreases. → the fatigue relief resulting from reduced commuting time

2. Expanding a nominalized phrase back into full clauses

The reverse move restores a dense noun phrase to full clauses with subjects, connectors, and predicates, making the underlying causal or logical relationship explicit rather than implicit in the noun phrase itself.

  • 통근 시간 감소로 인한 피로도 완화가 생산성 향상으로 이어진다. → 통근 시간이 줄어들면 피로가 줄어들고, 그 결과 생산성이 높아진다.The fatigue relief resulting from reduced commuting time leads to increased productivity. → If commuting time decreases, fatigue decreases, and as a result, productivity increases.

3. Trimming excess wording without losing meaning

Concision also means removing redundant modifiers, repeated subjects, and filler connectors that do not add information, using already-active omission and ellipsis patterns.

  • 저는 개인적으로 제 생각에는 이 정책이 좀 문제가 있다고 봅니다. → 이 정책에는 문제가 있다고 봅니다.Personally, in my own opinion, I think this policy has a bit of a problem. → I think this policy has a problem.

4. Expanding a thin claim with supporting elaboration

Expansion is not padding: it adds genuine supporting detail, using already-active connectors such as -기 때문에 and -(으)므로, so that a compressed claim becomes fully supported for a reader or listener who needs more context.

  • 재택근무는 효과적이다. → 재택근무는 통근 시간을 줄여 주고 피로도를 낮춰 주기 때문에 효과적이다.Remote work is effective. → Remote work is effective because it reduces commuting time and lowers fatigue.

5. Pacing a passage by alternating density

A skilled writer varies sentence density deliberately: a short, plain sentence after a dense, nominalized one can provide emphasis or a moment of rest for the reader, while a dense sentence after several plain ones can signal a formal summary or a turning point.

  • 통근 시간 감소로 인한 피로도 완화는 업무 몰입도 향상으로 이어진다. 결국 사람들은 더 잘 일한다.The fatigue relief resulting from reduced commuting time leads to improved work engagement. In the end, people work better.

[동사/형용사 절] ↔ [명사구 압축형] : 압축 = 명사화 + 군더더기 제거 / 확장 = 절 복원 + 근거 보완

Grammar Note — Structure, not new endings

Every nominalizer, connector, and structure in this chapter's examples — -음/-기 nominalization, derived nouns such as 완화 and 향상, and connectors such as -기 때문에 — is already active from earlier volumes. The new skill is choosing, sentence by sentence, whether to compress toward a dense noun phrase or expand toward a full explanatory clause, and pacing a passage by alternating the two.

Writing Workshop: Revising for Density and Pacing

Take the five-part argument paragraph you wrote in Chapter 2, or write a new short argumentative paragraph (5–7 sentences), and revise it deliberately for density and pacing.

  • 압축할 문장 고르기. Choose one or two sentences that would read more effectively in a formal document if compressed into a nominalized form.
  • 명사화하기. Rewrite those sentences using nominalization, trimming redundant wording as you go.
  • 확장할 문장 고르기. Choose one sentence that a reader unfamiliar with the topic would need expanded with more explicit support.
  • 풀어서 설명하기. Rewrite that sentence with full clauses and an added connector such as -기 때문에 to make the reasoning explicit.
  • 호흡 조정하기. Read the revised paragraph aloud and adjust so that at least one short, plain sentence follows a dense, nominalized one for emphasis.

    체크리스트 — Checklist:

    • At least one sentence is compressed into a nominalized form appropriate to formal writing.
    • At least one sentence is expanded with an explicit connector to support a reader who needs more context.
    • Redundant wording, such as repeated first-person framing, has been trimmed where it added no information.
    • The paragraph includes at least one short, plain sentence following a denser one for rhetorical effect.
    • The compressed and expanded versions communicate the same underlying meaning.
    • Register remains consistently formal throughout; no unmarked mixing with everyday register.
    • Spacing, particles, and Hangeul spelling are accurate, with no romanization.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Density as a marker of genre, not formality alone

    In South Korean professional and academic settings, a heavily nominalized, compressed style is closely associated with written reports, official documents, and academic abstracts, while a more expanded, connective style is expected in spoken presentations and explanations aimed at a general audience. Choosing the wrong density for a genre — for example, submitting a report written in loose, spoken-style sentences, or presenting orally in dense, nominalized prose — can make a text or talk seem underprepared even when its content is sound.

    Regional and Generational Note — Tolerance for density varies by field

    Some professional and academic fields favor very dense, nominalized writing as a marker of expertise, while others, including many public-facing and journalistic contexts, actively favor plainer, more expanded sentences for accessibility. Learners should observe the conventions of any specific field or publication rather than assuming maximal density is always the more prestigious choice.

    Common Mistakes to Avoid

    Common Mistake — Compressing until meaning is lost

    Error: Nominalizing a clause so heavily that the relationship between its parts, such as cause and result, becomes ambiguous or disappears entirely.

    Correction: Compress wording and redundant framing, but keep the logical relationship between ideas recoverable, using a connecting noun such as ~로 인한 or ~에 따른 when a causal link must remain visible.

    Common Mistake — Using compressed written style in live speech

    Error: Reading a heavily nominalized, report-style sentence aloud in a presentation without adjusting it, leaving listeners unable to follow the packed noun phrases in real time.

    Correction: Expand nominalized report language into fuller spoken clauses before presenting it aloud, reserving the compressed form for the written document itself.

    Common Mistake — Expanding until a passage becomes padded

    Error: Adding connectors, restatements, or qualifying phrases that repeat the same point without adding new supporting information, producing 군더더기 rather than genuine elaboration.

    Correction: Expand only where a reader or listener genuinely needs more support, and remove any added sentence that does not introduce new information.

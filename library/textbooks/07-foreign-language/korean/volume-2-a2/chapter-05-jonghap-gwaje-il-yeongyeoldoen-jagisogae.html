종합 과제 1: 연결된 자기소개Unit I Assessment: A Connected Self-Introduction

Combining self-description, relationships, habits, and past narrative into one connected personal and social profile · Speak and Write Korean: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

This is an assessment chapter. No major new grammar, particle, or speech-level system is introduced. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Combine self-description, topic chains, and Hangeul-only reading from Chapter 1 with relationship and address vocabulary from Chapter 2.
  2. Describe habits, ongoing activity with -고 있다, and expanded descriptive predicates from Chapter 3 as part of a longer connected profile.
  3. Narrate a short sequence of past events using sequence connectors, -기 전에/-(으)ㄴ 후에, and past noun-modifying forms from Chapter 4.
  4. Produce one connected spoken and written self-introduction that moves smoothly from identity, to relationships, to habits, to a short past narrative.
  5. Maintain consistent everyday polite -아요/어요 speech level, accurate spacing, and accurate particle use across a longer connected text.
  6. Read and listen to connected self-introductions that combine all four chapters' systems without introducing new grammar.
  7. Self-assess readiness for Unit II using the Unit I checklist provided in this chapter.

Communication Scenario

The study group's meeting with 수진 has arrived. Each member of the group will give a short spoken self-introduction and submit a written version for a shared class booklet. 지은 reminds everyone that a good introduction is not four separate paragraphs about identity, relationships, habits, and the past — it is one connected profile where those parts flow into each other naturally. 라일라 decides to use everything she has practiced since Chapter 1: her topic chain from her first self-description, the relationship and address terms from Chapter 2, her daily routine and descriptive language from Chapter 3, and the short biography she wrote in Chapter 4.

  1. This chapter introduces no new grammar, particle, speech-level, or honorific system. Every pattern used here was activated in Chapters 1 through 4.
  2. The task of this chapter is synthesis: combining four separate chapters' systems into one connected, well-organized spoken and written performance.
  3. Assessment in this chapter focuses on cumulative accuracy, cohesion between sections, and independent control — not on introducing anything unfamiliar.

발음 — Pronunciation Consolidation

Pronunciation Consolidation

This chapter introduces no new sound-change rule. It consolidates control of the sound changes practiced across Chapters 1 through 4, including liaison across batchim before vowel-initial endings, past-tense liaison with 았/었, and stable pronunciation of high-frequency particles and endings.

Pronunciation Note — Reading a longer connected passage aloud

When reading or speaking a longer connected text such as this chapter's self-introduction, maintain the same syllable timing and liaison patterns you used in short sentences. Do not slow down and separate syllables at sentence boundaries; instead, use natural pauses at commas and full stops while keeping each sentence's internal liaison smooth.

저는 요즘 한국어를 배우고 있어요 · 가족들과 자주 연락해요 · 어릴 때 부산에서 서울로 이사했어요

Grammar Synthesis

This chapter introduces no new grammar system. It reviews how the systems from Chapters 1 through 4 combine into one connected text, and it practices the transitions between sections.

Review: Moving between sections smoothly

A connected profile does not simply list four separate topics. Each section should lead naturally into the next. The table below shows one way to move from identity, to relationships, to habits, to past narrative, using only systems already activated.

TransitionFunctionExample
저는 [이름]이에요/예요.opens with identity (Ch. 1)저는 라일라예요.
저는 가족이 있어요. / 제 [관계]는 ...moves into relationships (Ch. 2)제 언니는 회사원이에요.
요즘 저는 ...moves into current habits (Ch. 3)요즘 저는 한국어를 배우고 있어요.
저는 [장소]에서 태어났어요. 그 다음에 ...moves into past narrative (Ch. 4)저는 시카고에서 태어났어요. 그 다음에...

These transitions are not a new grammar system; they are a review strategy for organizing already-known sentence patterns into one text.

Review: Cumulative accuracy checkpoints

  • 제 동생은 학생이고, 저는 회사원이에요. My younger sibling is a student, and I am an office worker. noun modifier + 의 control (Ch. 2) recycled with -고 coordination
  • 저는 매일 운동하고 있어요. 그리고 주말에는 가족을 만나요. I am exercising every day. And on weekends I see my family. frequency adverb + -고 있다 (Ch. 3)
  • 고등학교를 졸업한 후에 이 학교에 입학했어요. After graduating from high school, I entered this school. -(으)ㄴ 후에 + past noun-modifying pattern (Ch. 4)
Grammar Note — Why this chapter adds nothing new

Assessment chapters in this volume are designed to test independent control of previously activated systems, not to introduce new material under time pressure. If a sentence in your profile requires a grammar point not yet covered — for example, quoted speech or the passive voice — restate the idea using a system from Chapters 1 through 4 instead of reaching for an untaught form.

Practice — Combining sections

  1. Write one sentence that introduces your identity (Chapter 1 pattern).
  2. Write one sentence that introduces a family member or friend using a title or kinship term (Chapter 2 pattern).
  3. Write one sentence using -고 있다 to describe something you are currently doing (Chapter 3 pattern).
  4. Write one sentence using a sequence connector or -기 전에/-(으)ㄴ 후에 to describe a past event (Chapter 4 pattern).
  5. Combine your four sentences with one transition phrase from the table above between each pair.

Model Dialogue

Setting: 수진 asks 라일라 to introduce herself at the study group meeting, combining everything from identity to past narrative.

수진 라일라 씨, 자기소개 좀 해 주시겠어요? Layla, could you introduce yourself?
라일라 네, 안녕하세요. 저는 라일라예요. 저는 이 학교 학생이고, 전공은 한국어예요. Yes, hello. I'm Layla. I'm a student at this school, and my major is Korean.
라일라 제 가족은 미국에 살아요. 언니가 한 명 있는데, 언니는 회사원이에요. My family lives in the U.S. I have one older sister, and she's an office worker.
라일라 요즘 저는 한국어를 열심히 배우고 있어요. 그리고 매일 아침에 운동하고 있어요. These days I'm studying Korean hard. And I'm exercising every morning.
라일라 저는 시카고에서 태어났어요. 열 살 때 여기로 이사했어요. 이사하기 전에는 한국어를 전혀 몰랐어요. I was born in Chicago. I moved here when I was ten. Before moving, I didn't know any Korean at all.
라일라 이 학교에서 처음 만난 친구가 지은 씨예요. 정말 좋은 친구예요. The first friend I met at this school was Jieun. She's a really good friend.
수진 소개 정말 잘 들었어요. 만나서 반가워요, 라일라 씨. That was a great introduction. Nice to meet you, Layla.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — What belongs in a connected self-introduction

As noted in Chapter 4, self-introductions in many South Korean school and community settings often move beyond name and current activity to include brief biographical and relational information. A connected introduction that moves from identity, to family, to daily life, to a short personal history reflects a common structure for this kind of spoken and written self-presentation, though the exact content and order vary by setting, relationship, and personal preference.

Language Note — Cohesion over accumulation

A common beginner tendency is to produce four unconnected paragraphs rather than one cohesive text. Reusing topic chains and omission from Chapter 1, rather than repeating 저는 at the start of every single sentence, helps the profile read as connected narration rather than a checklist of unrelated facts.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Mixing speech levels across sections

Error: Beginning the identity section in everyday polite -아요/어요 and switching to plain or formal endings in the past-narrative section without a reason to do so.

Correction: Keep everyday polite -아요/어요 consistent across all four sections in this chapter's task, since no new speech level has been activated yet.

Common Mistake — Treating the four sections as unrelated lists

Error: 저는 라일라예요. 저는 학생이에요. 저는 언니가 있어요. 저는 운동을 해요. (four disconnected sentences with no transitions)

Correction: Use transition phrases and topic-chain omission, as in 저는 라일라예요. 이 학교 학생이고, 언니가 한 명 있어요. 요즘 운동도 하고 있어요., so the sections read as one connected profile.

Common Mistake — Reaching for untaught grammar to sound more advanced

Error: Attempting to use quoted speech, the passive voice, or formal -습니다 endings because they sound more sophisticated.

Correction: Express the same idea using a Chapter 1–4 pattern instead. This chapter assesses independent control of what has already been taught, not early use of unscheduled systems.

기분과 감정 이야기Feelings and Reactions

Emotion vocabulary and reason clauses with feelings · Speak and Write Korean: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe your own and other people's feelings using common emotion vocabulary, such as 기쁘다, 슬프다, 화나다, 걱정되다, and 긴장되다.
  2. Explain the reason for a feeling using the connector -아서/어서.
  3. Ask someone how they feel and respond appropriately in everyday polite speech.
  4. Continue using the comparison pattern 보다/더 from Chapter 12 to compare feelings or reactions.
  5. Continue using noun-modifying descriptive forms to describe a person's emotional state.
  6. Understand a short conversation and a short written message about feelings and their causes.
  7. Write a short paragraph describing a recent feeling and its reason.
  8. Complete a practical task: explaining how you feel about a recent event and why.

Communication Scenario

A few days later, 라일라 texts 지영 about her upcoming Korean class presentation. She feels nervous, and 지영 checks in on how she is doing. As they talk, 지영 also shares some good news that made her happy, and 라일라 asks why. Together they practice describing feelings and explaining the reasons behind them.

  1. This chapter continues Unit III, extending the description of people from Chapters 11 and 12 into feelings and emotional reactions.
  2. New productive systems in this chapter are limited to emotion vocabulary and the reason connector -아서/어서 used specifically to explain feelings.
  3. Kinship terms, comparison with 보다/더, closeness vocabulary, and noun-modifying descriptive forms continue to be used throughout.

발음 — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus

This chapter introduces no new sound-change rule. It reviews the ㅡ deletion pattern that appears in 기쁘다 and 슬프다 when they combine with the polite ending, and reviews liaison in 걱정돼서 and 긴장돼서 before the following syllable.

Pronunciation Note — 기뻐요 and 슬퍼요

Both 기쁘다 and 슬프다 drop the stem vowel ㅡ before the polite ending, giving 기뻐요 and 슬퍼요 rather than a form built directly from 쁘/프 plus 아요. Practice the phrases below at a natural pace.

기뻐요 · 슬퍼요 · 걱정돼서 · 긴장돼서 · 화나서

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces emotion vocabulary and extends the reason connector -아서/어서, already recycled from Chapter 11, specifically to explaining feelings. It also reviews comparison with 보다/더 and noun-modifying descriptive forms.

New: describing feelings with 기분이 + descriptive verb

Many feelings can be described using the noun 기분 together with 이/가 and a descriptive verb such as 좋다 or 나쁘다, or with a specific emotion verb used on its own.

KoreanMeaning
기분이 좋아요.I'm in a good mood.
기분이 나빠요.I'm in a bad mood.
저는 기뻐요.I'm glad.
저는 긴장돼요.I'm nervous.

Only these core emotion words are activated productively this chapter. Additional feeling vocabulary remains receptive until a later chapter schedules it.

Building Block

[reason clause] + -아서/어서 + [feeling]

This pattern reuses the connector -아서/어서 from Chapter 11 to state the reason for a feeling. The first clause gives the cause; the second clause names the emotion.

발표가 있어서 긴장돼요.

I'm nervous because I have a presentation.

New: 왜 그래요? and checking on someone's feelings

  • 라일라 씨, 왜 그래요? Laila, what's wrong? fixed question, everyday polite
  • 발표가 있어서 좀 긴장돼요. I'm a little nervous because I have a presentation. -아서/어서 + feeling verb

Review: comparison with 보다/더

  • 저는 언니보다 더 긴장돼요. I'm more nervous than my older sister. recycled 보다/더 from Chapter 12

Review: noun-modifying descriptive forms -(으)ㄴ

  • 지영 씨는 신난 목소리로 전화했어요. Jiyoung called me with an excited voice. recycled -(으)ㄴ from Chapter 11

Practice — Building feeling and reason sentences

  1. Write a sentence stating your current mood using 기분이 좋아요/나빠요 or a specific emotion word.
  2. Write a sentence explaining a feeling with a reason using -아서/어서.
  3. Write one sentence asking a friend 왜 그래요? and a possible response.

Model Dialogue

Setting: 지영 calls 라일라 in the evening before her Korean class presentation.

지영 라일라 씨, 목소리가 좀 이상해요. 왜 그래요? Laila, your voice sounds a bit strange. What's wrong?
라일라 내일 발표가 있어서 좀 긴장돼요. I'm a little nervous because I have a presentation tomorrow.
지영 아, 그래요? 저도 발표 전에는 항상 긴장돼요. 괜찮아질 거예요. Oh, really? I always get nervous before presentations too. You'll be okay.
라일라 고마워요. 그런데 지영 씨는 오늘 기분이 좋아 보여요. Thanks. By the way, you seem to be in a good mood today.
지영 네, 오늘 좋은 소식을 들어서 아주 기뻐요. Yes, I heard some good news today, so I'm very glad.
라일라 정말요? 무슨 소식이에요? Really? What's the news?
지영 다음 달에 오빠가 한국에 와서 신나요. My brother is coming to Korea next month, so I'm excited.

내용 이해 — Comprehension questions:

  1. Why does 라일라 feel nervous?
  2. How does 지영 respond to 라일라's nervousness?
  3. Why is 지영 in a good mood, and why is she excited?

Writing Workshop

This chapter's writing task is a short paragraph describing a recent feeling and its cause: 요즘 제 기분 이야기하기 ("Talking About My Mood These Days").

  1. State your current mood. Use 기분이 좋아요/나빠요 or a specific emotion word.
  2. Give a reason. Use -아서/어서 to explain why you feel that way.
  3. Add a second feeling and reason. Describe at least one other feeling from your day or week with its cause.
  4. Compare your feelings. Use 보다 and 더 to compare how you feel now with how you felt before, or compare yourself to a friend or family member.
  5. Describe someone else briefly. Use a noun-modifying descriptive phrase from Chapter 11 to describe a person connected to your story.
  6. Revise. Check your draft against the checklist below.

체크리스트 — Editing checklist:

  • Current mood is stated clearly.
  • At least one feeling is explained with -아서/어서.
  • At least one comparison sentence with 보다/더 is used correctly.
  • At least one noun-modifying descriptive phrase is used correctly.
  • Speech level is consistent everyday polite -아요/어요 throughout.
  • Only Hangeul is used; no romanization appears in the final draft.
  • No grammar or vocabulary beyond Chapter 13 appears productively.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Checking on someone's mood

Asking 왜 그래요? or noticing a change in someone's tone of voice is a common way of showing care in everyday South Korean interactions among friends and classmates. It is generally seen as a caring gesture rather than an intrusive question.

Culture in Context — Sharing good and difficult news

Among close friends, sharing both good news, such as a family visit, and stressful situations, such as an upcoming presentation, is a common way of maintaining closeness. This chapter's dialogue reflects that everyday pattern rather than a formal or public disclosure of feelings.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Confusing 기쁘다 and 기분이 좋다

Error: Using 기뻐요 and 기분이 좋아요 as if they always mean exactly the same thing in every context.

Correction: 기쁘다 usually describes a stronger, often event-triggered joy, while 기분이 좋다 describes a general good mood. Both are acceptable in this chapter, but learners should notice the difference in later readings.

Common Mistake — Dropping the reason clause with -아서/어서

Error: Saying 긴장돼요. 발표가 있어요. as two separate sentences when a single reason-and-feeling sentence is more natural.

Correction: Combine the two ideas with -아서/어서: 발표가 있어서 긴장돼요.

Common Mistake — Misspelling 기뻐요 and 슬퍼요

Error: Writing 기쁘어요 or 슬프어요 instead of the correct contracted forms.

Correction: The stem vowel ㅡ is deleted before the polite ending: 기뻐요, 슬퍼요.

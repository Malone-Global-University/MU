여행과 특별한 경험Travel and Special Experiences

Talking about things you have done, things you want to do, and telling a connected story · Speak and Write Korean: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Talk about things you have done using the experience pattern -아/어 본 적이 있다.
  2. Ask other people about their past experiences and respond naturally, including saying you have never done something.
  3. Express a wish to do something using -고 싶다.
  4. Express intention or a plan in progress using -(으)려고 하다.
  5. Organize a personal story into a connected narrative paragraph with a beginning, a middle, and an end.
  6. Use travel and experience vocabulary to describe a trip, an activity, and a reaction to it.
  7. Maintain consistent everyday polite -아요/어요 speech level and accurate Hangeul spelling across a longer piece of connected writing.

This chapter opens Unit IV, 경험, 의견, 그리고 프로젝트 (Experiences, Opinions, and Projects). It introduces experience, desire, and intention as productive systems and expands narrative writing beyond single sentences into connected paragraphs.

Communication Scenario

Since 민수's visit in Chapter 15, 라일라 and 지영 have been talking about travel. 지영's brother 민수 lives abroad, and hearing about his trips has made 라일라 curious about her own travel plans. In this chapter, 라일라 and 지영 talk about places they have already been, a place 라일라 has always wanted to visit, and a trip 지영 is now planning. Their conversation moves from past experience to present desire to future intention — and by the end of the chapter, 라일라 turns her own travel story into a short written narrative.

  1. This chapter recycles description, comparison, feelings, and scheduling language from Unit III (Chapters 11-15).
  2. This chapter introduces the experience pattern -아/어 본 적이 있다, the desire pattern -고 싶다, and the intention pattern -(으)려고 하다 as new productive systems.
  3. This chapter also introduces narrative paragraph organization: sequencing events, expressing a reaction, and closing a short personal story.

발음 — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus

This chapter recycles previously introduced sound changes and adds no new sound-change rule. Two items deserve attention because they appear frequently in this chapter's new grammar patterns.

Pronunciation Note — 본 적이 있어요 as one breath group

The experience pattern -아/어 본 적이 있어요 is usually produced as a single connected breath group rather than four separate words. The batchim in 적이 liaises smoothly into the following syllable: 저기 is pronounced closer to jeo-gi-like flow than as two separated blocks. Practice saying the whole phrase quickly and smoothly before breaking it into parts for analysis.

Pronunciation Note — Intonation in wishes and intentions

Statements with -고 싶어요 typically fall gently at the end, similar to other everyday polite statements. Statements with -(으)려고 해요 also fall, but speakers often place slightly more stress on the verb stem before -(으)려고 when the intention is new or notable information, as in 저는 이번 여름에 제주도에 가려고 해요.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces three connected systems for talking about time relative to now: what you have experienced, what you want, and what you plan to do — plus how to organize these into a connected narrative.

1. Experience: -아/어 본 적이 있다/없다

Grammar Note — Form, Meaning, and Use

Form: verb stem + -아/어 보다 (in its past connective form -아/어 봐) + 본 적이 있다/없다. In practice, learners attach -아/어 본 적이 있어요 (or 없어요) directly to the verb stem, following the same vowel-harmony pattern used for -아/어요.

Meaning: this pattern expresses whether the speaker has ever had the experience of doing something, not simply whether an action happened in the past. It answers "have you ever...?" rather than "did you...?"

Use: use this pattern to ask about or share life experiences, especially travel, food, and activities that are notable because they happened at least once, not because of when they happened.

Dictionary FormStem Type-아/어 본 적이 있어요 Form
가다ㅏ-final가 본 적이 있어요
먹다consonant-final, dark vowel먹어 본 적이 있어요
타다ㅏ-final타 본 적이 있어요
살다ㅏ-final, ㄹ stem살아 본 적이 있어요

  • 외국에서 살아 본 적이 없어요. I have never lived abroad. 살다 stem + 아 본 적이 없어요
  • 한 번도 등산해 본 적이 없어요. I've never gone hiking, not even once. 한 번도 + 등산하다 stem + 여 본 적이 없어요
Common Mistake — Confusing experience with simple past

Error: Using simple past 갔어요 when the intended meaning is "have you ever been," producing an ambiguous or incomplete question such as 제주도에 갔어요? when a specific past visit is not what is being asked.

Correction: Use the experience pattern when asking about life experience in general: 제주도에 가 본 적이 있어요?

2. Desire: -고 싶다

Grammar Note — Form, Meaning, and Use

Form: verb stem + -고 싶다. In everyday polite speech, this becomes -고 싶어요. This pattern is only used with action verbs, since descriptive verbs already express states directly.

Meaning: expresses the speaker's own wish or desire to do something. Using -고 싶다 to describe someone else's wish (rather than asking about it as a question) requires an evidential ending not yet active in this volume; this chapter limits productive use to first-person statements and second-person questions.

Use: use -고 싶어요 to express personal goals, travel wishes, and preferences about future activities.

  • 언젠가 유럽에 가고 싶어요. I want to go to Europe someday. 가다 stem + 고 싶어요
  • 라일라 씨는 어디에 가고 싶어요? Where do you want to go, Laila? second-person question with -고 싶어요

3. Intention: -(으)려고 하다

Grammar Note — Form, Meaning, and Use

Form: vowel-final stem + -려고 하다; consonant-final stem + -으려고 하다. In everyday polite speech: -(으)려고 해요.

Meaning: expresses a plan or intention that is more concrete than a simple wish but not yet a firm commitment. It signals that the speaker has decided to try to do something.

Use: use -(으)려고 해요 for stating plans that are underway or fairly settled, distinct from -고 싶어요 (a wish) and from -기로 하다 (a firm decision, scheduled for Chapter 18).

Dictionary FormStem Type-(으)려고 해요 Form
가다vowel-final가려고 해요
먹다consonant-final먹으려고 해요
준비하다vowel-final준비하려고 해요
  • 저는 이번 여름에 제주도에 가려고 해요. I'm planning to go to Jeju Island this summer. 가다 stem + 려고 해요
  • 여행 가기 전에 계획을 세우려고 해요. I'm planning to make a plan before I go on the trip. 세우다 stem + 려고 해요
Building Block — Combining experience, desire, and intention

[experience -아/어 본 적이 있다/없다] + [desire -고 싶다] + [intention -(으)려고 하다]

A natural travel conversation often moves through all three systems in sequence: first establishing what has already happened, then what the speaker wants, then what the speaker plans to actually do about it.

저는 제주도에 가 본 적이 없어요. 그래서 언젠가 가고 싶어요. 사실 이번 여름에 가려고 해요.

I've never been to Jeju Island. So I want to go someday. Actually, I'm planning to go this summer.

4. Narrative Paragraph Organization

Writing Note — Sequencing a personal story

A narrative paragraph in this chapter should use sequencing adverbs to organize events clearly: 먼저 (first), 그다음에 (next), and 마지막으로 (lastly), often closing with 결국 (in the end) or a reaction using 기억에 남아요 (it remains in my memory) or 잊을 수 없어요 (I can't forget it). This is the first chapter in which learners are expected to connect more than two or three sentences into a single organized paragraph rather than a list of separate sentences.

Practice — Combining the new systems

  1. Write one sentence about a place you have been, using -아/어 본 적이 있어요.
  2. Write one sentence about a place you have never been, using -아/어 본 적이 없어요.
  3. Write one sentence expressing a travel wish using -고 싶어요.
  4. Write one sentence expressing a travel plan using -(으)려고 해요.

Model Dialogue

Setting: 라일라 and 지영 are talking about travel after hearing about 민수's trips abroad.

지영 라일라 씨, 제주도에 가 본 적이 있어요? Laila, have you ever been to Jeju Island?
라일라 아니요, 한 번도 가 본 적이 없어요. 그런데 언젠가 꼭 가고 싶어요. No, I've never been there, not even once. But I really want to go someday.
지영 저는 작년에 가 봤어요. 바다도 예쁘고 산도 정말 특별해요. I went last year. The sea is pretty, and the mountains are really special.
라일라 와, 좋았겠어요! 저는 사실 이번 여름에 여행을 가려고 해요. Wow, that must have been nice! Actually, I'm planning to go on a trip this summer.
지영 정말요? 어디에 가려고 해요? Really? Where are you planning to go?
라일라 아직 정하지 않았지만, 제주도에 가고 싶어요. 계획을 세워야 해요. I haven't decided yet, but I want to go to Jeju Island. I need to make a plan.
지영 좋은 생각이에요! 제가 여행지를 몇 개 추천해 줄까요? That's a good idea! Shall I recommend a few destinations?

내용 이해 — Comprehension questions:

  1. Has 라일라 ever been to Jeju Island? Has 지영?
  2. What does 라일라 say she wants to do, and what is she actually planning to do?
  3. What does 지영 offer to do at the end of the conversation?

Writing Workshop

This chapter's writing task is a short narrative paragraph: 특별한 경험 ("A Special Experience").

  1. Choose an experience. Think of a real or imagined trip or activity you have done. State that you have done it using -아/어 본 적이 있어요.
  2. Sequence the events. Describe at least three things that happened, using 먼저, 그다음에, and 마지막으로 to organize them in order.
  3. Add a reaction. Describe how the experience felt, using vocabulary such as 특별하다, 놀랍다, or 기억에 남다.
  4. Close with a wish or plan. End the paragraph with a sentence using -고 싶어요 or -(으)려고 해요 that connects the past experience to the future.
  5. Revise. Check your draft against the checklist below.

체크리스트 — Editing checklist:

  • An experience is described using -아/어 본 적이 있어요.
  • At least three events are sequenced using 먼저, 그다음에, and 마지막으로.
  • A reaction to the experience is included.
  • The paragraph closes with -고 싶어요 or -(으)려고 해요.
  • Speech level is consistent everyday polite -아요/어요 throughout.
  • Only Hangeul is used; no romanization appears in the final draft.
  • No grammar or vocabulary beyond Chapters 1 through 16 appears productively.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Domestic travel and 제주도

Jeju Island is one of the most commonly discussed domestic travel destinations in South Korea, known for its volcanic landscape, coastline, and distinct regional character. Many South Koreans have visited Jeju at some point, which is part of why it appears so naturally in everyday conversations about travel experience and future plans.

Culture in Context — Sharing travel stories

In casual South Korean conversation, sharing a travel story often moves quickly from what happened to how the listener reacts and what the listener might want to do themselves, as in this chapter's dialogue. Responding to someone's travel story with a reaction such as 와, 좋았겠어요! ("Wow, that must have been nice!") is a common and expected part of maintaining the conversation.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Using -고 싶다 for someone else's stated plan

Error: Using -고 싶어요 to describe another person's plan as if it were a directly known personal wish, such as 지영 씨는 제주도에 가고 싶어요. stated as a flat fact rather than a question or reported information.

Correction: Ask the person directly with -고 싶어요? or report what they said using a quotation pattern introduced in a later chapter: 지영 씨는 제주도에 가고 싶어요?

Common Mistake — Mixing up desire and intention

Error: Using -(으)려고 해요 to express a vague wish that is not actually a concrete plan, such as saying 아프리카에 가려고 해요. when the speaker has no real plan yet, only a wish.

Correction: Use -고 싶어요 for a wish without a concrete plan, and reserve -(으)려고 해요 for an actual intended plan: 아프리카에 가고 싶어요.

Common Mistake — Writing events without sequencing words

Error: Listing several events as disconnected sentences with no sequencing words, making the order of events unclear, such as 항구에 갔어요. 배를 탔어요. 섬에 도착했어요. with no connective structure.

Correction: Use 먼저, 그다음에, and 마지막으로 to make the sequence explicit: 먼저 항구에 갔어요. 그다음에 배를 탔어요. 마지막으로 섬에 도착했어요.

Section D — Sample "A Special Experience" paragraph

작년 겨울에 친구와 함께 산에 가 봤어요. 태어나서 처음으로 등산해 봤어요. 먼저 아침 일찍 숙소에서 나왔어요. 그다음에 산을 올라가면서 사진을 많이 찍었어요. 마지막으로 정상에서 정말 놀라운 경치를 봤어요. 힘들었지만 특별한 하루였어요. 결국 그 경험은 제 기억에 오래 남았어요. 저는 다른 산에도 또 가고 싶어요.

Check that: an experience is described with -아/어 본 적이 있어요, events are sequenced with 먼저/그다음에/마지막으로, a reaction is included, the paragraph closes with -고 싶어요 or -(으)려고 해요, and no structures beyond Chapter 16 appear productively.

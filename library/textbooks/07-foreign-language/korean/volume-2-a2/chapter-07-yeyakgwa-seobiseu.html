예약과 서비스Appointments and Services

Negotiating times, making and changing appointments, and using service-encounter language · Speak and Write Korean: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Make an appointment by phone or message, stating the service you need and a preferred day and time.
  2. Propose, accept, or decline a specific time using patterns such as -(으)ㄹ 수 있어요? and -는 게 어때요?.
  3. Change or cancel an existing appointment politely.
  4. Use 에게/한테 productively to say who receives an action, such as calling, telling, or giving something to someone.
  5. Interact in a service encounter at a place such as a clinic, hair salon, or restaurant, including waiting, confirming, and following up.
  6. Read and listen to short exchanges about appointments and services.
  7. Write a short message requesting, confirming, or changing an appointment.

Communication Scenario

Chapter 6 solved 라일라's boiler, window, and noise problems. Now that her apartment feels more settled, 라일라 needs to take care of several appointments: a haircut before the new semester starts, a check-up at a nearby clinic, and dinner reservations for a friend's birthday. Each of these requires proposing a time, responding to the other person's availability, and sometimes changing plans at the last minute. This chapter builds on the request and softening patterns from Chapter 6 and expands 에게/한테 — until now mostly recognized in passing — into a system 라일라 can use on her own.

  1. This chapter introduces time-negotiation language and the productive use of 에게/한테.
  2. Everyday polite speech level, softening, and the request pattern -아/어 주세요 continue from Chapters 1 through 6.
  3. Assessment in this chapter focuses on making, confirming, and changing an appointment clearly and politely.

발음 — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus

This chapter reinforces native-Korean hour counting with 시 and reviews liaison before vowel-initial particles, using appointment vocabulary. It introduces no new sound-change rule.

Pronunciation Note — 시 with native Korean numbers

Hours are counted with the native Korean number series plus 시, so 한 시, 두 시, 세 시, 네 시 use the native forms 한, 두, 세, 네 rather than the Sino-Korean numbers used for minutes. Minutes use Sino-Korean numbers plus 분, as in 세 시 십오 분. Learners commonly try to use Sino-Korean numbers for the hour as well; this is a fixed pattern rather than a free choice.

오후 세 시에 예약할 수 있어요? · 다음 주 화요일이 어때요? · 직원한테 물어봤어요

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter activates two new productive systems: time-negotiation patterns for proposing, accepting, and declining appointment times, and the productive use of 에게/한테 to mark the recipient of an action. Requests, softening, and everyday polite speech level continue from earlier chapters.

New: proposing a time with -(으)ㄹ 수 있어요? and -는 게 어때요?

To ask whether someone is free at a certain time, use the recycled ability pattern -(으)ㄹ 수 있어요? as a negotiation question. To suggest a time more gently, use -는 게 어때요? ("how about…?").

PatternExampleGloss
-(으)ㄹ 수 있어요?화요일 오후에 올 수 있어요?Can you come Tuesday afternoon?
-는 게 어때요?수요일 세 시에 하는 게 어때요?How about doing it Wednesday at three?
다른 시간 있어요?그 시간은 안 돼요. 다른 시간 있어요?That time doesn't work. Do you have another time?
Building Block

[time expression] + 에 + [predicate stem]-(으)ㄹ 수 있어요?

The time expression takes 에 as in earlier chapters; the verb stem adds -(으)ㄹ 수 있어요? to ask about possibility rather than general ability.

오전 열 시에 갈 수 있어요?

Can you go at ten in the morning?

New: 에게/한테 as a productive recipient particle

에게/한테 marks the person who receives an action, such as a phone call, a message, information, or an object. 한테 is more common in everyday speech; 에게 appears more often in writing. Until this chapter, 에게/한테 has mostly been recognized in dialogues and readings. From this chapter forward, learners are expected to produce it independently.

  • 친구한테 전화했어요. I called my friend. 한테 + 전화하다 (recipient of the call)
  • 직원한테 물어봤어요. I asked the staff member. 한테 + 물어보다
  • 저한테 알려 주세요. Please let me know. 저한테 (formal 저 + 한테) + 알려 주다
  • 집주인한테 문자를 보냈어요. I sent the landlord a text message. recycles 집주인 from Chapter 6
Grammar Note — 에게/한테 versus 에

에게/한테 is used only for people and animals, marking the recipient of an action such as calling, telling, giving, or sending. 에 marks locations and times, and is not used for people in this way. Compare 학교에 가요 ("I go to school," a place) with 친구한테 가요 ("I go to my friend," a person). Do not substitute 에 for 에게/한테 when the target of the action is a person.

Review: polite requests with -아/어 주세요

  • 예약 좀 확인해 주세요. Please check the reservation. softening 좀 + -아/어 주세요, recycled from Chapter 6
  • 예약을 바꿔 주시겠어요? Could you change the reservation? softened request with -아/어 주시겠어요

Review: softening a change request

Softening openers such as 죄송하지만 remain important when asking to change or cancel a plan.

  • 죄송하지만, 예약을 미룰 수 있어요? I'm sorry, but could I postpone the appointment? softening opener + -(으)ㄹ 수 있어요?

Practice — Building time-negotiation sentences

  1. Choose a service (haircut, clinic visit, dinner reservation) and propose a specific day and time.
  2. Ask whether the other person is available at that time using -(으)ㄹ 수 있어요?.
  3. If the first time does not work, suggest another time using -는 게 어때요? or 다른 시간 있어요?.
  4. Add one sentence using 에게/한테 to say who you contacted or who should contact you.

Model Dialogue

Setting: 라일라 calls a hair salon to make an appointment before the semester starts.

직원 안녕하세요, ○○ 미용실입니다. 무엇을 도와드릴까요? Hello, this is ○○ Hair Salon. How can I help you?
라일라 안녕하세요. 머리를 자르고 싶은데, 예약할 수 있어요? Hello. I'd like to get a haircut — can I make an appointment?
직원 네, 가능해요. 언제가 편하세요? Yes, that's possible. When is convenient for you?
라일라 이번 주 금요일 오후 세 시에 갈 수 있어요? Can I come this Friday at three in the afternoon?
직원 죄송하지만 그 시간은 예약이 다 찼어요. 네 시는 어때요? I'm sorry, but that time is fully booked. How about four?
라일라 네, 네 시 좋아요. 그럼 제 전화번호로 문자를 보내 주시겠어요? Sure, four works. Then could you send a text to my phone number?
직원 네, 예약 확인 문자를 손님한테 보내 드릴게요. Yes, I'll send a confirmation text to you.
라일라 감사합니다. 금요일에 뵐게요. Thank you. I'll see you Friday.

Writing Workshop

This chapter's writing task is a short message requesting or changing an appointment: 예약 메시지 ("An Appointment Message").

  1. Open politely. Greet the reader and identify yourself if needed.
  2. State the purpose. Say what service or plan you want to arrange.
  3. Propose a time. Use -(으)ㄹ 수 있어요? or -는 게 어때요?.
  4. Use 에게/한테. Include at least one sentence naming who you are contacting or who should contact you.
  5. Add a softened change request, if needed. Use 죄송하지만 to ask about postponing or canceling.
  6. Close politely. Thank the reader and ask for confirmation.
  7. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist below.

체크리스트 — Editing checklist:

  • The message names a specific service or plan (haircut, clinic visit, reservation, etc.).
  • At least one time-negotiation pattern (-(으)ㄹ 수 있어요? or -는 게 어때요?) is used.
  • At least one 에게/한테 phrase is used correctly to mark a recipient.
  • Speech level is consistent everyday polite -아요/어요 throughout.
  • Only Hangeul is used; no romanization appears in the final draft.
  • No grammar, particle, or vocabulary item beyond Chapter 7 appears in the text.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Booking appointments by phone and app

In South Korea, appointments for clinics, hair salons, and restaurants are commonly made by phone, but booking through a mobile app or website is increasingly common as well, especially among younger customers and in larger cities. Confirmation texts, like the one in the Model Dialogue, are a widely used way for businesses to remind customers of their appointment time.

Culture in Context — Queue numbers and waiting

Many clinics, government offices, and busy service counters in South Korea use a numbered ticket system, often called 번호표, to manage waiting customers in order. Announcements calling the next number are common in these settings, and asking a staff member how long the wait will be is a normal and polite question, not an imposition.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Using 에 instead of 에게/한테 for people

Error: 친구에 전화했어요. (using the location/time particle 에 with a person)

Correction: Use 친구한테 전화했어요. 에게/한테 marks a person as the recipient of an action; 에 is reserved for places and times.

Common Mistake — Sino-Korean numbers for the hour

Error: 삼 시에 만나요. (using the Sino-Korean number 삼 with 시)

Correction: Use the native Korean number: 세 시에 만나요. Hours use native Korean numbers; only minutes use Sino-Korean numbers.

Common Mistake — Proposing a time with no softening after a refusal

Error: Responding to a declined time with only 그럼 언제요?, which can sound abrupt.

Correction: Soften the follow-up, as in 아, 그러면 다른 시간 있어요?, to keep the exchange polite.

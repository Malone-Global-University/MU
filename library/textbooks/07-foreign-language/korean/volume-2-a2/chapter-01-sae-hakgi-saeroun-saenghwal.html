새 학기, 새로운 생활A New Term and Current Life

Reintroducing yourself with fuller detail, linking ideas into topic chains, and reading connected Hangeul without romanization · Speak and Write Korean: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe your current life — your school, program, living situation, and schedule — in fuller connected sentences than in Volume I.
  2. Identify and produce a topic chain: several sentences in a row about the same topic, using 은/는 once and omitting the subject afterward.
  3. Recognize and use expanded time and frequency words such as 요즘, 최근에, 아직, and 벌써.
  4. Connect ideas smoothly using the connectors reviewed from Volume I: 그리고, 그래서, 하지만, 그런데.
  5. Read a connected paragraph-length Hangeul text about someone's current life without romanization support.
  6. Introduce yourself and your current life to a partner or class, maintaining everyday polite -아요/어요 speech level throughout.
  7. Write a short organized paragraph describing your present situation, using topic chains and connectors accurately.

Communication Scenario

It is the first week of a new term. 라일라 has just finished Volume I of her Korean course and is now meeting a new classmate, 민호, in a slightly more advanced class. Instead of the short, simple self-introductions from the beginning of Volume I, 라일라 and 민호 now describe their current lives — school, living situation, daily schedule, and near-term plans — in longer, connected Korean.

  1. This chapter reviews and expands identity, routine, and existence patterns from Volume I rather than introducing an entirely new grammar system.
  2. The chapter's principal new focus is the topic chain: linking several sentences about the same person or topic without repeating the topic particle in every sentence.
  3. Hangeul remains the only script used for Korean text. No romanization appears in this chapter except where explicitly noted as pronunciation support.

발음 — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus

This chapter does not introduce a new sound-change rule. It develops the batchim, liaison, and intonation targets from Volume I into longer, connected stretches of speech, since this chapter's sentences are noticeably longer than typical Volume I sentences.

Pronunciation Note — Reading a topic chain aloud

When several sentences in a row share the same topic, Korean speakers keep a light, connected rhythm across the sentences rather than pausing sharply after every one, especially when the topic particle appears only in the first sentence and is then omitted. Practice reading the sentences below as one connected unit, paying attention to liaison between 기숙사에 and the following syllable, and to the batchim in 생활 and 시간표.

저는 요즘 기숙사에서 살아요 · 아직 좀 낯설지만 점점 적응하고 있어요 · 시간표도 벌써 익숙해졌어요

New focus: The topic chain

Building Block — Topic Chain

[Topic]은/는 + [sentence 1]. + [sentence 2, no repeated subject]. + [sentence 3, no repeated subject].

Once a topic is introduced with 은/는, Korean speakers usually do not repeat the subject in the sentences that follow, as long as the topic remains clear from context. Repeating 저는 in every sentence sounds unnatural and overly emphatic in connected speech or writing.

저는 신입생이에요. 기숙사에서 살아요. 아직 학교가 좀 낯설어요.

I'm a new student. (I) live in the dormitory. (Things are) still a little unfamiliar at school.

Compare an unnatural repeated version with a natural topic chain:

  • 저는 신입생이에요. 저는 기숙사에서 살아요. 저는 아직 학교가 낯설어요. Unnatural: repeats 저는 in every sentence.
  • 저는 신입생이에요. 기숙사에서 살아요. 아직 학교가 낯설어요. Natural: 저는 appears once; the topic carries across the chain.
Grammar Note — When to repeat 은/는

Repeat 은/는 when the topic changes, when you want to contrast two topics, or when enough sentences have passed that the topic needs to be reestablished for clarity. Within a single short paragraph about one person, one 은/는 at the beginning is usually enough.

Expanded time adverbs

These adverbs typically appear near the beginning of a sentence and help situate a topic chain in time.

  • 요즘 많이 바빠요.I'm very busy these days.
  • 최근에 기숙사로 이사했어요.I recently moved into the dormitory.
  • 아직 이름을 다 못 외웠어요.I still haven't memorized all the names.
  • 벌써 3주가 지났어요.Three weeks have already passed.

Practice — Building a topic chain

Combine each set of short sentences into a natural topic chain by removing unnecessary repetition of 저는.

  1. 저는 2학년이에요. 저는 전공이 한국어예요. 저는 한국어를 좋아해요.
  2. 저는 요즘 바빠요. 저는 동아리에 들었어요. 저는 매일 연습해요.
  3. 저는 기숙사에 살아요. 저는 룸메이트가 있어요. 저는 룸메이트와 잘 지내요.

Model Dialogue

Setting: The first week of the new term. 라일라 meets a new classmate, 민호, before class.

민호 안녕하세요. 저는 민호예요. 처음 뵙겠습니다. Hello. I'm Minho. Nice to meet you.
라일라 안녕하세요. 저는 라일라예요. 반가워요. Hello. I'm Layla. Nice to meet you too.
민호 요즘 생활이 어때요? 벌써 적응했어요? How's life these days? Have you already gotten used to things?
라일라 아직 조금 낯설어요. 저는 이번 학기에 기숙사로 이사했어요. 룸메이트가 있고 방이 편해요. It's still a bit unfamiliar. I moved into the dormitory this term. I have a roommate, and the room is comfortable.
시간표도 좀 바뀌었어요. 그래서 요즘 정신이 없어요. 하지만 새 동아리에 들어서 재미있어요. My schedule also changed a bit. So I've been quite busy/disorganized lately. But I joined a new club, so it's fun.
민호 저도 그래요. 우리 이번 학기에 같은 반이니까 서로 도와요. Same here. Since we're in the same class this term, let's help each other out.
라일라 네, 좋아요. 잘 지내 봐요! Yes, sounds good. Let's do well together!

Writing Workshop

This chapter's writing task is a short paragraph, 나의 새 학기 ("My New Term"), describing your current life as a connected topic chain.

  1. Introduce your topic. Use 은/는 once to introduce yourself with one identity detail (year, major, or role).
  2. Describe your living situation. Use 에서 살아요 or 에 살아요 without repeating the subject.
  3. Add a feeling or change. Use a descriptive verb such as 낯설다, 편하다, or 힘들다, and a time adverb such as 요즘 or 아직.
  4. Connect two ideas. Use -지만 or -고 to link a difficulty and a positive point.
  5. Close with a plan or hope. Use -고 싶어요 or -(으)ㄹ 거예요 from Volume I.
  6. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist below.

체크리스트 — Editing checklist:

  • 은/는 appears once at the start of the topic chain and is not repeated unnecessarily.
  • The paragraph includes identity, living situation, a feeling or change, and a closing plan.
  • At least one -지만 or -고 sentence connects two ideas.
  • Speech level is consistent everyday polite -아요/어요 throughout.
  • Only Hangeul is used; no romanization appears in the final draft.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — 선배 and 후배 relationships

In many South Korean schools, workplaces, and organizations, the terms 선배 (senior) and 후배 (junior) mark a relationship based on when someone joined a school, class, or organization, not necessarily age. A 선배 often offers practical guidance to a 후배 during a transition such as the start of a new term, though the closeness of this relationship varies by school, department, and individual personality.

Language Note — Why topic chains matter

Learners who repeat 저는 in every sentence are understandable, but their Korean can sound stiff or overly emphatic to a Korean listener. Learning to build a topic chain is one of the clearest ways to sound more natural at this stage, without learning any new grammar forms — it simply changes how existing forms are combined across sentences.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Repeating 저는 in every sentence

Error: 저는 신입생이에요. 저는 기숙사에 살아요. 저는 아직 낯설어요.

Correction: 저는 신입생이에요. 기숙사에 살아요. 아직 낯설어요. — once the topic is established, omit 저는 in the following sentences unless you are contrasting or reestablishing the topic.

Common Mistake — Placing time adverbs in the wrong position

Error: 기숙사로 이사했어요 최근에.

Correction: 최근에 기숙사로 이사했어요. — time adverbs such as 최근에, 요즘, 아직, and 벌써 typically appear near the beginning of the sentence, not at the end.

Common Mistake — Overusing 그리고 to connect every idea

Error: 낯설어요. 그리고 재미있어요. (when the relationship is actually a contrast)

Correction: 낯설지만 재미있어요. — choose -지만 or 하지만 when the two ideas contrast, rather than defaulting to 그리고 for every connection.

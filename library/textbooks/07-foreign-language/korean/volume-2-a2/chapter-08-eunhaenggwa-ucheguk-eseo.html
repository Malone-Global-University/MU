은행과 우체국에서At the Bank and Post Office

Handling transactions, filling out forms, and using quantity and money expressions · Speak and Write Korean: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. State a banking or postal task, such as opening an account, exchanging money, or sending a package.
  2. Ask for and give the information required to fill out a simple form, using -이/가 어떻게 되세요? and related patterns.
  3. Use Sino-Korean numbers together with 원 to state and confirm amounts of money.
  4. Describe quantities and weights using expanded counters relevant to packages and mail.
  5. Say what is needed for a transaction using -이/가 필요하다.
  6. Interact politely at a bank or post office counter, including waiting, confirming information, and following up.
  7. Read and listen to short exchanges about banking and postal transactions.
  8. Write a short form-style message stating a transaction request and personal information.

Communication Scenario

라일라's appointments from Chapter 7 are settled, and her weekend is now free for two errands she has been putting off: opening a Korean bank account so her scholarship stipend can be deposited directly, and sending a birthday package to her family back home. Both tasks require her to state a clear purpose, provide personal information clearly and in order, and understand and produce numbers for money, weight, and quantity. This chapter builds on the request and time-negotiation language from Chapter 7 and introduces form-filling language and money/quantity expressions that 라일라 can use at any counter-service transaction.

  1. This chapter introduces form-filling language (asking for and giving personal information) and the productive use of Sino-Korean numbers with 원 and expanded counters for packages and quantities.
  2. Everyday polite speech level, softened requests, and 에게/한테 continue from Chapters 1 through 7.
  3. Assessment in this chapter focuses on completing a bank or post office transaction clearly, accurately, and politely.

발음 — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus

This chapter reinforces Sino-Korean number pronunciation with 원, including liaison across large numbers, and reviews the batchim behavior of 원 before a following vowel-initial particle. It introduces no new sound-change rule.

Pronunciation Note — Large Sino-Korean numbers with 원

Money amounts use Sino-Korean numbers, read in blocks of four digits (만, 억, and so on) rather than blocks of three as in English. 오만 원 ("fifty thousand won") is read as 오 (5) + 만 (10,000), not as separate thousands. When 원 follows a syllable ending in a consonant, such as 칠천 원, keep the batchim clearly released before pronouncing 원 so the amount stays intelligible. Learners commonly try to group Korean numbers the way English groups thousands; this leads to hesitation with amounts above 9,999.

오만 원이에요 · 이 소포는 칠천 원이에요 · 신분증이 있어야 해요

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter activates two new productive systems: form-filling language for requesting and giving personal information, and money/quantity expressions using Sino-Korean numbers with 원 and expanded counters. Requests, softening, and 에게/한테 continue from earlier chapters.

New: asking for information with -이/가 어떻게 되세요?

To politely ask for a piece of information such as a name, address, or phone number, use [noun] + 이/가 어떻게 되세요? This pattern is more polite than a direct question with 뭐예요? and is standard at counters, offices, and other formal service settings.

PatternExampleGloss
이름이 어떻게 되세요?성함이 어떻게 되세요?What is your name? (formal alternative with 성함)
주소가 어떻게 되세요?주소가 어떻게 되세요?What is your address?
전화번호가 어떻게 되세요?전화번호가 어떻게 되세요?What is your phone number?
Building Block

[noun] + 이/가 + 어떻게 되세요?

The noun to be requested takes 이/가; 어떻게 되세요? literally asks "how does it become," functioning as a polite request for information rather than a direct question.

생년월일이 어떻게 되세요?

What is your date of birth?

New: stating a requirement with -이/가 필요하다

To say that something is needed for a transaction, use [noun] + 이/가 필요하다. 필요하다 is a descriptive verb and follows the same everyday polite pattern as other descriptive verbs.

  • 계좌를 만들려면 신분증이 필요해요. You need identification to open an account. 신분증 + 이 + 필요하다 (recognition-only 으려면 clause, glossed)
  • 소포를 보내려면 봉투가 필요해요. You need an envelope to send a package. 봉투 + 가 + 필요하다
  • 이거 필요해요? Do you need this? 쓰다 form with pronoun subject omitted

The connector -(으)려면 ("if you want to...") appears above only as a glossed, formulaic frame around 필요하다 sentences. It is not an independent productive target of this chapter and will be developed in a later chapter.

New: money and quantity with Sino-Korean numbers

Money amounts, weights in grams and kilograms, and fees all use Sino-Korean numbers. Practice reading amounts in blocks of 만 (ten-thousand) rather than English-style thousands.

  • 오만 원 넣고 싶어요. I'd like to deposit fifty thousand won. 오만 (Sino-Korean) + 원 + 넣다
  • 이 소포는 이 킬로그램이에요. This package is two kilograms. 이 (Sino-Korean) + 킬로그램
  • 배송비는 칠천 원이에요. The shipping fee is seven thousand won. 칠천 (Sino-Korean) + 원
Grammar Note — 어떻게 되세요? versus 뭐예요?

Both 어떻게 되세요? and 뭐예요? can ask for information, but 어떻게 되세요? is the more polite and standard choice at banks, offices, and other formal counters, since it avoids a bare, direct question about the other person's personal details. Compare the more casual 이름이 뭐예요? with the more polite 이름이 어떻게 되세요?, which is expected in the service encounters of this chapter.

Review: 에게/한테 with transaction verbs

  • 직원한테 신분증을 보여 줬어요. I showed the staff member my ID. recycled from Chapter 7
  • 가족한테 소포를 보내요. I'm sending a package to my family. 한테 + 보내다

Review: polite requests with -아/어 주세요

  • 신청서 좀 작성해 주세요. Please fill out the application form. softening 좀 + -아/어 주세요, recycled from Chapters 6-7

Practice — Building form-filling and money sentences

  1. Choose a task (opening an account, exchanging money, sending a package) and state it clearly.
  2. Give or ask for one piece of personal information using -이/가 어떻게 되세요?.
  3. Say what is required for the task using -이/가 필요하다.
  4. State an amount of money or a weight using a Sino-Korean number.

Writing Workshop

This chapter's writing task is a short form-style message stating a transaction request and personal information: 거래 신청 메시지 ("A Transaction Request Message").

  1. Open politely. Greet the reader and identify yourself if needed.
  2. State the purpose. Say what banking or postal task you want to complete.
  3. Give personal information. State at least two pieces of information (name, address, phone number) as if completing a form.
  4. State a requirement. Use -이/가 필요하다 to say what is needed for the task.
  5. Give an amount. Use a Sino-Korean number with 원, 그램, or 킬로그램.
  6. Close politely. Thank the reader and ask for confirmation if needed.
  7. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist below.

체크리스트 — Editing checklist:

  • The message names a specific banking or postal task.
  • At least two pieces of personal information are stated clearly and in order.
  • At least one -이/가 필요하다 sentence is used correctly.
  • At least one Sino-Korean number with 원, 그램, or 킬로그램 is used correctly.
  • Speech level is consistent everyday polite -아요/어요 throughout.
  • Only Hangeul is used; no romanization appears in the final draft.
  • No grammar, particle, or vocabulary item beyond Chapter 8 appears in the text.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Banking as a newcomer in South Korea

Foreign residents in South Korea, including students, generally need a valid form of identification, such as a passport or Alien Registration Card, to open a bank account, and some banks may also ask for a document confirming enrollment or address. Many everyday banking tasks, such as transfers and balance checks, are now commonly handled through mobile banking apps, but opening a new account in person at a branch, as in this chapter, remains a common first step.

Culture in Context — Sending mail internationally

South Korea's postal service, 우체국, handles both domestic and international mail, and staff routinely weigh packages and quote a shipping fee and estimated delivery time on the spot. Registered mail, called 등기, is a common option for important documents or valuable packages and provides tracking, though it is not required for every shipment.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Grouping Sino-Korean numbers like English thousands

Error: Hesitating over 오만 원 by trying to build it from "fifty" and "thousand" separately, the way English does.

Correction: Read Korean numbers in blocks of 만 (ten-thousand): 오만 is simply 5 × 10,000. Practice common amounts as whole units rather than translating digit-by-digit from English.

Common Mistake — Using 뭐예요? instead of 어떻게 되세요? at a counter

Error: 이름이 뭐예요? spoken to a customer by bank or post office staff, which can sound too direct for the setting.

Correction: Use 이름이 어떻게 되세요? or 성함이 어떻게 되세요? in formal service settings; save 뭐예요? for casual, informal contexts.

Common Mistake — Omitting 이/가 필요하다's subject particle

Error: 신분증 필요해요. without a particle, which sounds incomplete in careful speech.

Correction: Include the subject particle: 신분증이 필요해요. Omission is common in fast casual speech but should not be the default in this chapter's controlled practice.

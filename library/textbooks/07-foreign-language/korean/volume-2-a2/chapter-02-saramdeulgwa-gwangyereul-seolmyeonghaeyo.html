사람들과 관계를 설명해요Relationships and Social Roles

Expanding titles and address terms, reviewing kinship vocabulary, and using noun modifiers and 의 to describe the people around you with precision · Speak and Write Korean: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe the people in your life — family, classmates, and school relationships — using an expanded set of titles and address terms.
  2. Use 선배, 후배, and 동기 accurately to describe relationships based on when someone joined a school or organization.
  3. Attach a noun modifier — a descriptive verb form placed directly before a noun, such as 친한 친구 — to describe a person or thing more precisely in a single phrase.
  4. Control the possessive/associative particle 의, including when it may be omitted in casual speech and when it should be kept in careful or written Korean.
  5. Continue building topic chains from Chapter 1 while introducing a second person into the description.
  6. Read a short connected passage that introduces several people and their relationships to one another, without romanization.
  7. Introduce a friend, classmate, or family member to a partner or class using noun modifiers, titles, and 의 accurately.

Communication Scenario

라일라 and 민호 are now comfortable describing their own lives, but a new classmate has just joined their study group, and 라일라 wants to introduce him to a friend. To do this clearly, she needs more than the basic titles from Volume I — she needs to describe how people relate to one another: who is older in the program, who is a close friend, and whose notebook belongs to whom.

  1. This chapter reviews kinship terms and titles from Volume I and expands them into fuller relationship description.
  2. The chapter's two principal new focuses are noun modifiers built from descriptive verbs, and controlled use of the possessive/associative particle 의.
  3. Topic chains from Chapter 1 continue to recycle here, now often describing a second person rather than only the speaker.

발음 — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus

This chapter does not introduce a new sound-change rule. It develops liaison across the possessive particle 의 and across noun-modifier endings such as -은, -는, and -인, since these endings frequently attach directly to a following noun with no pause.

Pronunciation Note — 의 in connected speech

In careful or formal speech, 의 is pronounced close to its spelling. In fast, casual connected speech, many speakers pronounce possessive 의 closer to e, especially between two nouns such as 친구의 이름. This chapter's productive target remains the standard spelled form; the pronunciation note is for listening recognition, not a spelling change.

제 친구의 이름은 지은이에요 · 조용한 성격이지만 아주 재미있는 사람이에요 · 저는 그 친구와 아주 친해요

New focus: Noun modifiers from descriptive verbs

Building Block — Descriptive Noun Modifier

[descriptive verb stem] + -은/-ㄴ + [noun]

A descriptive verb can attach directly before a noun to modify it, similar to an adjective placed before a noun in English. Stems ending in a consonant take -은; stems ending in a vowel take -ㄴ. This is a new ending, not a repetition of -아요/어요.

친한 친구, 조용한 사람, 재미있는 사람*

a close friend; a quiet person; an interesting/fun person

*재미있다 and other predicates ending in 있다/없다 use -는 rather than -은/-ㄴ; this pattern is previewed here and developed further in later chapters. For this chapter, treat 재미있는 and 없는 forms as fixed vocabulary items rather than a rule to generalize.

조용하다조용하-조용한조용한 사람
착하다착하-착한착한 후배
똑똑하다똑똑하-똑똑한똑똑한 학생

힘들다 drops its ㄹ before -은, giving 힘든 rather than 힘들은. This ㄹ-irregular pattern is reviewed here from Volume I and will be developed further in later chapters.

Compare a two-sentence description with a single noun-modifier phrase:

  • 그 사람은 조용해요. 그 사람은 좋은 친구예요. Two separate sentences: workable but less efficient.
  • 그 사람은 조용한 사람이지만 좋은 친구예요. One connected sentence using a noun modifier and -지만.

New focus: The possessive/associative particle 의

Building Block — 의

[noun 1] + 의 + [noun 2]

의 marks a possessive or associative relationship between two nouns, roughly "noun 1's noun 2." In casual speech, 의 is frequently dropped between two nouns when the relationship is clear from context, especially with personal pronouns such as 제 (my, from 저 + 의) and 제 친구. In careful speech and in writing, 의 is more often kept, and it is required in some fixed expressions.

제 친구의 이름, 학교의 위치, 친구 이름 (의 dropped, casual)

my friend's name; the school's location; friend's name (casual, no 의)

Grammar Note — 저의 → 제; 나의 → 내

저 + 의 contracts to 제, and 나 + 의 contracts to 내, in ordinary speech and writing. The uncontracted forms 저의 and 나의 exist but sound stiff or overly formal in everyday conversation. Learners should produce 제 친구, not 저의 친구, in this chapter's speaking and writing tasks.

  • 제 친구의 이름은 지은이에요.My friend's name is Jieun.
  • 이건 제 동생 가방이에요.This is my younger sibling's bag. (의 dropped, natural in speech)
  • 우리 학교의 위치가 편해요.Our school's location is convenient. (의 kept, more formal-sounding)

Model Dialogue

Setting: 라일라 introduces her close friend 지은 to 민호 before class.

Writing Workshop

This chapter's writing task is a short paragraph, 제 친구 소개 ("Introducing My Friend"), describing a friend or family member using noun modifiers, a title or kinship term, and 의.

  1. Introduce the person. State their name and your relationship to them, using a title, kinship term, or relationship word such as 친구, 선배, 후배, or 동기.
  2. Add a possessive detail. Use 의 (or its contracted form 제) at least once to connect two nouns, such as a name and a detail about them.
  3. Describe their personality. Use at least one noun-modifier phrase built from a descriptive verb, such as 조용한 사람 or 착한 친구.
  4. Add a connecting idea. Use -지만 or -고 to link two personality traits or one trait and one activity.
  5. Close with a feeling. Use 좋아요, 재미있어요, or a similar closing statement about the relationship.
  6. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist below.

체크리스트 — Editing checklist:

  • At least one noun-modifier phrase (-은/-ㄴ + noun) appears and is correctly formed from the stem.
  • 의 or its contracted form (제/내) is used correctly at least once.
  • The paragraph includes the person's relationship to you, a personality description, and a closing feeling.
  • Speech level is consistent everyday polite -아요/어요 throughout.
  • Only Hangeul is used; no romanization appears in the final draft.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Titles instead of pronouns

Korean speakers commonly address and refer to people by title, name plus 씨, or kinship term rather than by a second-person pronoun such as 당신, which can sound distant, formal, or even confrontational in ordinary conversation. When introducing someone, using their name with an appropriate title, as in the Model Dialogue, is the more natural default in most everyday and school contexts.

Language Note — 의 is not always optional

While 의 is frequently dropped between two ordinary nouns in casual speech, it is generally kept in formal writing, in longer noun phrases where dropping it would create ambiguity, and in certain fixed expressions. Learners should not assume 의 can always be omitted; this chapter's target is recognizing both patterns, not defaulting to always dropping it.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Using the dictionary form before a noun

Error: 조용하다 사람이에요.

Correction: 조용한 사람이에요. — a descriptive verb must take the noun-modifying ending -은/-ㄴ before a noun; the dictionary form -다 cannot attach directly to a following noun.

Common Mistake — Using 저의 in casual speech

Error: 저의 친구예요. (in an ordinary conversational context)

Correction: 제 친구예요. — use the contracted form 제 in everyday speech and writing; reserve 저의 for unusually formal or careful contexts.

Common Mistake — Confusing 선배/후배 with age

Error: Assuming a 선배 must always be older than a 후배 in every case.

Correction: 선배 and 후배 are based on when someone joined a school, class, or organization, not strictly on age. A 후배 can occasionally be older than a 선배, though this is less common.

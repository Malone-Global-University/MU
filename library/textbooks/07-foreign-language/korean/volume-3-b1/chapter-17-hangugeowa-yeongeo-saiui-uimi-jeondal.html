한국어와 영어 사이의 의미 전달Mediation Between Korean and English

Summarizing, paraphrasing, and explaining meaning across two languages, using quotation, register, and source-based writing systems already active in this course · Speak and Write Korean: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Explain the meaning of a Korean sentence or short passage to an English-speaking listener without translating word for word.
  2. Explain the meaning of an English sentence or short passage to a Korean-speaking listener in natural, level-appropriate Korean.
  3. Use -다고 하다 and related indirect-quotation patterns to report what a Korean or English source said, rather than quoting it directly.
  4. Signal a paraphrase explicitly using frames such as 쉽게 말하면 and 다시 말해서.
  5. Identify expressions, structures, or cultural references that do not transfer directly between Korean and English, and explain them instead of forcing a literal equivalent.
  6. Mediate a short exchange in which one participant does not share the other's language.

This chapter introduces no major new grammar system. It consolidates indirect quotation (introduced in Chapter 4 and developed through Chapters 6–15), source-based writing, and register control, and applies them to a new communicative task: mediating meaning between Korean and English. Two new paraphrase-signaling frames — 쉽게 말하면 and 다시 말해서 — are introduced as vocabulary-level expressions rather than new grammatical systems.

Opening Scenario

지영, 민준, 라일라, and 수아 are asked to help at a community event where Korean-speaking grandparents and English-speaking grandchildren attend a shared workshop. Several grandparents do not speak English confidently, and several grandchildren do not speak Korean confidently. The four students are asked not to translate every sentence, but to mediate — to make sure each side understands the meaning, the tone, and the intention of what the other side is saying, even when an exact word-for-word version would sound strange or would miss the point. The instructor reminds them that mediation is a skill of its own: it requires understanding both languages well enough to explain meaning, not just to exchange words.

  1. You will learn how to summarize and paraphrase across Korean and English rather than translating literally.
  2. You will practice reporting what someone said using indirect quotation instead of quoting them directly.
  3. You will mediate a short bilingual exchange for two people who do not share a language.

발음 — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation — Formal spoken-mediation rhythm

When mediating for two listeners, speakers often slow down, add brief pauses between clauses, and place slightly stronger stress on the connector that introduces a paraphrase, such as 쉽게 말하면 or 다시 말해서. This is not a new sound-change rule; it is a pacing strategy that helps the listener recognize that a restatement, not a new idea, is coming.

Practice reading the model dialogue below aloud, pausing briefly before each paraphrase frame and giving it slightly more emphasis than the surrounding words.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new grammar system. It reviews and applies indirect quotation, register control, and source-attribution patterns already active since Chapters 4, 6, and 13, in a new communicative context: mediating meaning between Korean and English.

1. Review — Indirect Quotation for Reporting What Someone Said (Ch. 4, Ch. 6–15)

Review — -다고 하다 and related forms

When mediating, you almost always report what someone said rather than quoting them directly. Use -다고 하다 (statements), -냐고 하다 (questions), and -라고 하다 (commands/requests) to convey the content of what was said without claiming to reproduce the exact words.

  • 할머니께서 오늘 날씨가 좀 춥다고 하셨어요.Grandmother said that the weather is a bit cold today.plain form + -다고 하셨어요 (honorific past)
  • 손자가 이 단어가 무슨 뜻이냐고 물어봤어요.The grandson asked what this word means.

In mediation, indirect quotation is often more useful than direct quotation, because it lets you adjust wording, add context, or soften a phrase while still faithfully conveying the meaning.

2. Signaling a Paraphrase: 쉽게 말하면 / 다시 말해서

New expressions — Marking a restatement clearly

쉽게 말하면 ("to put it simply") and 다시 말해서 ("in other words") signal to the listener that what follows restates, rather than adds to, the previous idea. These are vocabulary-level discourse frames, not a new grammatical system — they attach to a full sentence that follows, using forms you already control.

  • 이 표현은 직역하기 어려워요. 쉽게 말하면, 두 사람 사이에 오래 쌓인 정 같은 감정이에요.This expression is hard to translate literally. To put it simply, it's a feeling like a long-accumulated bond between two people.
  • 할아버지께서는 손녀가 자주 연락하기를 바라신대요. 다시 말해서, 자주 전화해 달라는 뜻이에요.Grandfather said he hopes his granddaughter contacts him often. In other words, it means he's asking her to call more often.

3. Review — Source-Based Writing and Register Control (Ch. 6, Ch. 13)

Review — attributing and adjusting register when mediating

Mediation also requires register judgment: a grandparent's formal, deferential Korean may need to become relaxed, friendly English for a grandchild, and vice versa. Continue to use the source-attribution patterns from Chapter 13 (-에 따르면, -다고 해요) when explaining who said what, and adjust the surrounding register to fit the listener, not the original speaker.

  • 할머니 말씀에 따르면, 이 음식은 명절에만 만드는 특별한 음식이래요.According to what grandmother said, this food is a special dish made only for holidays.
Building Block — The mediation frame

[hear/read source language] → [report content with -다고/냐고/라고 하다] → [쉽게 말하면 / 다시 말해서 + plain restatement] → [add context if the expression resists direct equivalence]

Use this four-step frame whenever you mediate. Do not skip the restatement step even when a literal version is possible — mediation is about meaning, not word-for-word exchange.

Model Dialogue

수아 mediates between her grandmother, who speaks mostly Korean, and her cousin Ben, who speaks mostly English, at the community workshop.

Context: community workshop, mediated three-way exchange. 수아 speaks Korean to her grandmother and reports meaning in English to Ben, and vice versa. Everyday polite style with her grandmother; friendly informal English with Ben (represented here in English).

할머니 벤이 오늘 왜 이렇게 조용해? 무슨 일 있어? Why is Ben so quiet today? Is something wrong?
수아 할머니께서 벤이 오늘 왜 이렇게 조용하냐고 물어보세요. 무슨 일 있냐고요. (to Ben, in Korean, before switching) Grandmother is asking why you're so quiet today. She's asking if something is wrong.
수아 turns to Ben and continues in English.
수아 (EN) "할머니 said she noticed you've been quiet — she wants to know if everything's okay." Mediated rendering: keeping the concern but softening the directness for Ben.
벤 (EN) "Tell her I'm just a little tired from the flight, nothing serious." Ben's response in English.
수아 할머니, 벤이 비행기 타고 오느라고 좀 피곤한 거래요. 심각한 일은 아니래요. Grandmother, Ben says he's just a bit tired from the flight. He says it's nothing serious.
할머니 아이고, 그랬구나. 그럼 오늘은 일찍 쉬라고 해. Oh, I see. Then tell him to rest early today.
수아 (EN) "She says that makes sense, and she wants you to get some rest early tonight." 쉽게 말하면 rendering — 수아 conveys warmth and permission, not a literal command.
벤 (EN) "That's really kind of her. Thank her for me." Ben's reply.
수아 할머니, 벤이 정말 감사하대요. 할머니가 참 다정하시다고요. Grandmother, Ben says thank you so much. He says you're very warm and kind.

Writing Workshop

This chapter's writing task asks you to write a short mediation reflection, following the pattern practiced in 라일라's reading passage above.

  1. Choose a mediation moment. Think of a real or imagined situation where you conveyed meaning between Korean and English, or between two people who did not share a language.
  2. Describe what was said. Report it indirectly, using -다고/냐고/라고 하다.
  3. Explain what a literal version would miss. Identify the tone, intention, or cultural context behind the words.
  4. Signal your paraphrase. Use 쉽게 말하면 or 다시 말해서 to introduce your restatement of the meaning.
  5. Reflect. Add one or two sentences on why mediating meaning matters more than exchanging words exactly.
  6. Revise. Check your draft against the checklist below.

체크리스트 — Editing checklist:

  • The reported speech uses -다고/냐고/라고 하다 correctly.
  • At least one paraphrase is signaled with 쉽게 말하면 or 다시 말해서.
  • The paragraph explains meaning or intention, not only literal wording.
  • Register is consistent and appropriate to who is speaking to whom.
  • Only Hangeul is used; no romanization appears.
  • Spacing and particles are accurate throughout.
  • No grammar or vocabulary beyond Volume III, Chapters 1–17, appears productively.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Mediation is a relationship skill, not just a language skill

In many Korean-speaking families with members who have different levels of English or Korean proficiency, a younger bilingual family member often serves informally as a mediator between generations. This role carries real responsibility: mediating well means preserving warmth, concern, and respect, not only content. Treat this role as a skill worth developing carefully, not as a simple substitute for a dictionary.

Culture in Context — Some meaning resists direct translation

Terms such as 정, 눈치, and 한 are frequently discussed as difficult to translate because they describe culturally shaped emotional or relational concepts rather than simple objects or actions. Explaining such terms through context and example, rather than forcing a single English word, is a legitimate and often necessary mediation strategy.

Regional Awareness — Mediating across generations and backgrounds

As in Chapter 16, remember that a grandparent's Korean may reflect regional or generational patterns, and a grandchild's Korean may reflect a diaspora or heritage-learner background. Mediating meaning sometimes also means mediating between different varieties of Korean, not only between Korean and English. Treat all of these varieties with respect, as established in Chapter 16.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Translating word for word and losing the intended meaning

Error: Rendering "밥 먹었어?" only as "Did you eat rice?" without conveying that it functions as an expression of care.

Correction: 쉽게 말하면, 이건 식사 여부를 묻는 것이 아니라 걱정을 표현하는 인사예요. ("To put it simply, this isn't really asking whether you ate — it's a greeting that expresses concern.")

Common Mistake — Quoting directly instead of reporting indirectly

Error: 할머니가 "오늘 좀 피곤해."라고 말했어요 used awkwardly as a mediated report, mixing direct quotation into a mediation context where indirect reporting would flow more naturally.

Correction: 할머니께서 오늘 좀 피곤하시다고 하셨어요. ("Grandmother said she's a bit tired today.") — indirect quotation, adjusted register and honorifics.

Common Mistake — Treating an untranslatable term as untranslatable and stopping there

Error: Saying only "there's no English word for this" and moving on, without attempting to explain the concept.

Correction: 정확히 대응하는 단어는 없지만, 쉽게 말하면 오랫동안 서로를 아끼면서 쌓인 마음이라고 설명할 수 있어요. ("There's no exact equivalent, but to put it simply, it can be explained as a feeling built up over time from caring about each other.")

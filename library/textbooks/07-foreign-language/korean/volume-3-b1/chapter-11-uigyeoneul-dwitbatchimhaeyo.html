의견을 뒷받침해요Supporting an Opinion

Stance, evidence, concession, and expanded use of -지만/-는데 · Speak and Write Korean: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use vocabulary for stating an opinion, giving a reason, and supporting a position with evidence.
  2. State a personal stance clearly using expressions such as 제 생각에는 and -다고 생각하다.
  3. Give a reason for an opinion using 왜냐하면 ~ 때문이다 and 그 이유는 ~이다, building on the reason/result systems from Chapter 3.
  4. Use -지만 and -는데 in their expanded, argumentative function — acknowledging an opposing point before returning to your own position — rather than only their earlier simple-contrast and backgrounding functions.
  5. Recognize and use basic concessive language (물론 ~지만) to show that you have considered another viewpoint before disagreeing with it.
  6. Read a short opinion paragraph and identify its stance, its reasons, and any concession it makes.
  7. Write a short, organized opinion paragraph that states a position, gives at least one reason, and acknowledges a counterpoint before restating the position.
  8. Discuss an opinion aloud with a partner, stating a stance and responding respectfully to a differing view.

This chapter opens Unit III. It introduces stance-marking and evidence-marking vocabulary as new productive systems, and it expands the already-activated connectors -지만 and -는데 (first introduced in Chapter 3 and recycled since) into their argumentative, concession-marking function. It does not introduce new sentence-final speech levels or new honorific systems.

Opening Scenario

Since the district office's new online system in Chapter 10, 지영, 민준, 라일라, and 수아 have kept talking about it — but now the conversation has turned into a real disagreement. 민준 thinks the online system should completely replace paper applications, since it is faster and more convenient. 지영 agrees that it is convenient, but she worries that older residents who are not comfortable with computers will be left behind. Their instructor asks them to write and discuss their own opinions on the issue, making sure each person gives at least one clear reason and acknowledges the other side before returning to their own position.

  1. You will build vocabulary for stating an opinion and supporting it with a reason.
  2. You will learn to state a personal stance and to introduce a reason using new expressions built on familiar grammar.
  3. You will expand -지만 and -는데 into their concessive, argumentative use — acknowledging one side of an issue before stating your own view.

발음 — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation — Intonation in concessive and argumentative sentences

When a sentence acknowledges a counterpoint before disagreeing, such as 편리하지만 문제가 있어요, the clause before -지만 is typically spoken with a slight rise and a brief pause, signaling that more is coming. The clause after -지만 then carries the main point and receives stronger stress. Practice this rise-pause-emphasis pattern with the example sentences in this chapter before moving to your own opinions.

Also practice the rhythm of 물론 ~ 지만 sentences, where 물론 is read with a falling, matter-of-fact tone that signals genuine acknowledgment rather than sarcasm.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces stance-marking and reason-marking expressions as new productive systems, and it expands the already-familiar connectors -지만 and -는데 into an argumentative, concessive function used to support an opinion.

1. Stating a Stance

Grammar — 제 생각에는 and -다고 생각하다

제 생각에는 ("in my opinion") is a fixed expression that opens a sentence stating a personal view. It is typically followed by a full sentence ending in -아요/어요, -ㄴ/는 것 같아요 (recognition, recycled receptively), or -습니다 in more formal contexts.

-다고 생각하다 ("to think that ~") attaches to a full sentence and is treated as a formulaic pattern at this stage: learners should be able to use it with familiar sentence types (-다, -ㄴ다, 이다 → -이라고) without a full analysis of Korean's indirect quotation system, which is introduced formally in Chapter 4 and developed further in later volumes.

  • Descriptive verb: 편리하다 → 편리하다고 생각해요.
  • Action verb (present): 필요하다 → 필요하다고 생각해요.
  • Copula: 문제이다 → 문제라고 생각해요.
  • 제 생각에는 온라인 시스템이 아주 편리해요. In my opinion, the online system is very convenient. 제 생각에는 (stance marker) + 편리하다 (descriptive verb) + -아요/어요
  • 저는 종이 신청서가 아직 필요하다고 생각해요. I think that paper applications are still necessary. 필요하다 + -다고 생각하다 (formulaic stance pattern)

2. Giving a Reason

Grammar — 왜냐하면 ~ 때문이다 and 그 이유는 ~이다

왜냐하면 ~ 때문이다 ("this is because ~") builds directly on the 때문에 pattern already familiar from earlier chapters, but frames it as a second sentence that explains the sentence before it, rather than a subordinate clause within one sentence. 왜냐하면 opens the explanation, and 때문이다 (or the polite 때문이에요) closes it.

그 이유는 ~이다 ("the reason is that ~") is a second common frame for introducing a reason, often used in slightly more formal or written opinion paragraphs.

  • 저는 온라인 시스템에 반대해요. 왜냐하면 나이 든 주민들이 컴퓨터에 익숙하지 않기 때문이에요. I am opposed to the online system. This is because older residents are not familiar with computers. 반대하다 + -아요/어요; 왜냐하면 ~ 때문이에요 (reason frame)
  • 저는 이 정책에 찬성해요. 그 이유는 시간이 많이 절약되기 때문이에요. I am in favor of this policy. The reason is that a lot of time is saved. 찬성하다 + -아요/어요; 그 이유는 ~ 때문이에요

3. Expanding -지만 and -는데 into Concession

Grammar — Concession: acknowledging a counterpoint before disagreeing

-지만 and -는데 were first introduced in Chapter 3 for simple contrast and background information. In this chapter, they take on an argumentative function: the first clause concedes a point the reader or listener might make, and the second clause states the writer's actual position. 물론 ("of course") often opens the concessive clause to make the acknowledgment explicit and genuine rather than dismissive.

  • 물론 [concession] -지만, [main position].
  • [concession] -는데, [main position]. (slightly softer, more conversational than -지만)

This is the same connector, used for a new communicative purpose: earlier chapters used it to contrast two facts; this chapter uses it to build a persuasive argument that treats an opposing view fairly before responding to it.

  • 물론 온라인 시스템이 편리하지만, 모든 사람에게 편리한 것은 아니에요. Of course the online system is convenient, but it isn't convenient for everyone. 물론 (concessive marker) + 편리하다 + -지만 (concession); main position follows
  • 종이 신청서가 시간이 더 걸리는데, 나이 든 분들에게는 더 편해요. Paper applications do take more time, but they are more comfortable for older people. 걸리다 + -는데 (concession, conversational); main position follows
Building Block — The supported-opinion frame

[stance: 제 생각에는 ~ / 저는 ~다고 생각해요] + [reason: 왜냐하면 ~ 때문이에요 / 그 이유는 ~이에요] + [concession: 물론 ~지만 / ~는데] + [restated position]

This frame gives a reliable structure for a short opinion paragraph: state your view, support it with a reason, acknowledge a counterpoint, and return to your position.

-지만날씨가 춥지만 나갔어요.편리하지만 문제가 있어요.

Model Dialogue

지영 and 민준 continue their conversation about the district office's new online application system, each supporting their opinion with a reason.

Context: 지영's apartment, after class. Everyday polite style, informal but respectful discussion between friends.

민준 저는 온라인 신청 시스템이 정말 좋다고 생각해요. 왜냐하면 구청에 직접 가지 않아도 되기 때문이에요. I really think the online application system is good. This is because you don't have to go to the district office in person.
지영 물론 편리하지만, 모든 사람에게 편리한 건 아니에요. Of course it's convenient, but it isn't convenient for everyone.
민준 그게 무슨 뜻이에요? What do you mean by that?
지영 제 생각에는 나이 든 주민들은 컴퓨터에 익숙하지 않기 때문에 오히려 더 불편할 수 있어요. In my opinion, older residents may actually find it more inconvenient, because they aren't familiar with computers.
민준 그 이유는 이해가 되는데, 그래도 시스템이 전면적으로 바뀌어야 한다고 생각해요. I understand that reason, but I still think the system should be changed completely.
지영 저는 전면적으로 바꾸는 것보다 단계적으로 바꾸는 게 더 좋다고 생각해요. I think it would be better to change it gradually rather than completely.
민준 단계적으로 바꾸는 것도 좋은 방법인 것 같아요. Changing it gradually also seems like a good approach.

Writing Workshop

This chapter's writing task asks you to write a short, organized opinion paragraph that states a position, gives at least one reason, acknowledges a counterpoint, and restates your position.

  1. Choose a topic. Pick a familiar issue with two reasonable sides — for example, whether all applications should move online, whether class attendance should be required, or a similar everyday topic.
  2. State your stance. Open with 제 생각에는 ~ or a sentence using -다고 생각해요.
  3. Give a reason. Use 왜냐하면 ~ 때문이에요 or 그 이유는 ~이에요.
  4. Acknowledge a counterpoint. Use 물론 ~지만 or ~는데 to fairly state a reasonable objection.
  5. Restate your position. Close with a sentence that returns clearly to your stance.
  6. Revise. Check your draft against the checklist below.

체크리스트 — Editing checklist:

  • The paragraph follows the supported-opinion frame: stance, reason, concession, restated position.
  • A stance expression (제 생각에는 or -다고 생각하다) is used correctly.
  • A reason-marking expression (왜냐하면 ~ 때문이다 or 그 이유는 ~이다) is used correctly.
  • A concessive use of -지만 or -는데 fairly acknowledges an opposing point.
  • Speech level is consistent throughout (everyday polite, unless your instructor assigns formal style).
  • Only Hangeul is used; no romanization appears.
  • Spacing and particles are accurate throughout.
  • No grammar or vocabulary beyond Volume III, Chapters 1–11, appears productively.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Acknowledging the other side before disagreeing

In many Korean conversational and written contexts, directly contradicting someone without first acknowledging their point can come across as abrupt or dismissive, even among friends discussing an everyday topic. Opening a disagreement with 물론 ~지만 ("of course ~, but ~") signals that the speaker has genuinely considered the other view before offering a different one, and this pattern is common well beyond formal debate.

Culture in Context — Compromise as a valued conversational outcome

In everyday Korean discussion, a conversation that ends in an explicit compromise, such as 민준 and 지영's agreement on a gradual rollout, is often considered a successful and satisfying outcome in itself, rather than a sign that neither party won the argument.

Register Awareness — Opinion language shifts with formality

The stance and reason expressions in this chapter (제 생각에는, 왜냐하면 ~ 때문이에요) work well in everyday polite conversation and informal writing. In more formal or academic contexts, later volumes will introduce more formal equivalents; for now, keep these expressions in the everyday polite register modeled in this chapter.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Stating an opinion with no reason

Error: Writing only 저는 온라인 시스템이 좋다고 생각해요. and stopping there.

Correction: Add a reason: 저는 온라인 시스템이 좋다고 생각해요. 왜냐하면 시간을 절약할 수 있기 때문이에요. A supported opinion always includes at least one reason in this chapter's tasks.

Common Mistake — Using -지만 to reject the counterpoint instead of acknowledging it

Error: Writing 편리하지만 그건 틀렸어요. ("It's convenient, but that's wrong"), which dismisses the point rather than genuinely acknowledging it.

Correction: Use -지만 to fairly state the other side before responding with your own reasoning: 물론 편리하지만, 모든 사람에게 편리한 것은 아니에요.

Common Mistake — Mixing everyday polite and formal endings within one opinion paragraph

Error: Writing 저는 찬성해요. 그 이유는 시간이 절약되기 때문입니다., mixing -아요/어요 and -습니다 style in the same paragraph.

Correction: Choose one speech level for the whole paragraph: 저는 찬성해요. 그 이유는 시간이 절약되기 때문이에요., unless your instructor specifically assigns formal style throughout.

In this chapter, it introduces a concession — a point the writer agrees is true — before the writer's own argued position, giving it a persuasive, argumentative function rather than a purely descriptive one.

제 생각에는 수업 출석이 꼭 필요해요. 왜냐하면 직접 들으면 더 잘 이해할 수 있기 때문이에요. 물론 온라인 수업도 편리하지만, 질문을 바로바로 하기가 더 어려워요. 그래서 저는 출석이 여전히 중요하다고 생각해요.

Check that: the paragraph follows the stance–reason–concession–restated position frame, uses a correct stance expression and reason-marking expression, and uses -지만 or -는데 concessively rather than dismissively.

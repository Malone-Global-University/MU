공식 이메일과 문의Formal Email and Inquiries

Formal writing, honorific address, and respectful requests · Speak and Write Korean: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use vocabulary for formal correspondence, inquiries, and email conventions to write and interpret a formal Korean email.
  2. Recognize and use the standard structural conventions of a formal Korean email, including greeting, self-identification, purpose statement, body, and closing.
  3. Use the honorific marker -(으)시- productively with common verbs when referring respectfully to the recipient of a formal email, extending the honorific awareness recycled from earlier volumes.
  4. Use honorific address terms and titles appropriately in formal written correspondence, including 귀하, 선생님, and job-title-plus-님 patterns.
  5. Continue using formal -습니다/-습니까 style, and the formal request patterns from Chapter 8 (-아/어 주시겠습니까, -아/어 주시기 바랍니다), now in a written email genre.
  6. Read a short formal email or inquiry and identify its purpose, its request, and its expected response.
  7. Write a complete formal email making an inquiry or a respectful request, using appropriate structure, honorific address, and closing conventions.
  8. Discuss the differences between an everyday polite message to a friend and a formal email to an institution or unfamiliar person.

This chapter continues the formal-register and formal-request systems from Chapters 6 through 8, now applied to written email correspondence. It introduces the honorific marker -(으)시- as a new productive system and introduces formal email structure and honorific address terms as new productive systems.

라일라's residence registration renewal from Chapter 8 has hit a snag: the confirmation notice mentioned a missing document, but the notice did not explain exactly which one. 발음 — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation — The honorific marker -(으)시- in connected speech and reading

When the honorific marker -(으)시- attaches to a verb stem ending in a consonant, the linking vowel 으 appears and is fully pronounced, as in 읽으시다 → 읽으세요. When the stem ends in a vowel, 시 attaches directly, as in 가시다 → 가세요. Practice noticing which pattern applies as you read formal email vocabulary such as 계시다, 바쁘시다, and 보내시다.

Also practice the rhythm of longer formal closing lines such as 확인 부탁드립니다 and 감사드립니다, which combine several Sino-Korean syllables. Read them slowly in syllable groups before increasing speed, keeping the batchim of each syllable connected smoothly into the next.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces the honorific marker -(으)시- as a new productive system, introduces honorific address terms for formal writing, teaches the standard structure of a formal Korean email, and continues the formal request patterns from Chapter 8 in this new written genre.

1. The Honorific Marker -(으)시-

Grammar — Subject honorification with -(으)시-

The honorific marker -(으)시- attaches to a verb stem to show respect toward the subject of the sentence — that is, toward the person being talked about or written to. In a formal email, the subject is very often the reader, so this marker appears constantly when describing what the recipient has done, is doing, or should do.

  • Consonant-final stem: stem + -으시-, e.g. 받다 → 받으시다.
  • Vowel-final stem: stem + -시-, e.g. 보다 → 보시다.
  • The honorific stem then takes ordinary endings: 받으세요, 받으셨습니다, 받으실 수 있습니다.

Some very common verbs have irregular honorific forms rather than adding -(으)시- to the plain stem: 있다 → 계시다 (to be, honorific), 먹다/마시다 → 드시다 (to eat/drink, honorific), 자다 → 주무시다 (to sleep, honorific). This chapter activates 계시다 productively because of its frequency in formal email closings; the remaining irregular honorific verbs are introduced for recognition only at this stage.

  • 이메일을 받으셨습니까? Did you receive the email? (honorific, respectful of the reader) 받다 + -으시- (honorific) + -었습니까 (past formal question)
  • 바쁘시겠지만 확인해 주시면 감사하겠습니다. I imagine you are busy, but I would appreciate it if you could check. 바쁘다 + -시- (honorific) + -겠지만 (concession); 확인하다 + -아/어 주시면 (conditional formal request)
  • 담당자님께서 자리에 계시면 문의드리고 싶습니다. If the person in charge is available, I would like to make an inquiry. 담당자님 + 께서 (honorific subject particle); 계시다 (honorific existential verb) + -(으)면 (condition)

Note the honorific subject particle 께서, the honorific counterpart of 이/가, used with an honored subject such as 담당자님 or 선생님. It regularly co-occurs with -(으)시- on the verb.

2. Honorific Address Terms in Formal Writing

Grammar — 귀하, 선생님, and title + 님 in formal correspondence

Formal Korean emails typically open with an honorific address term rather than a plain name or a casual greeting. Three common patterns are:

  • 귀하 — a formal written term meaning roughly "you" or "dear," used mainly in official letters, notices, and highly formal correspondence when the writer does not know the reader personally.
  • 선생님 — literally "teacher," but widely used as a respectful address term for any adult whose exact title is unknown, including in formal email.
  • job title + 님 — such as 담당자님 ("the person in charge," honorific) or 교수님 ("professor," honorific), used when the recipient's role is known.

Avoid addressing an unfamiliar recipient by first name alone in a formal email; this reads as overly casual and disrespectful in this register.

3. Formal Email Structure

Building Block — The formal email frame

[greeting: 안녕하십니까/안녕하세요] + [self-identification: 저는 ~입니다] + [purpose statement: 다름이 아니라 ~] + [body: request or inquiry, often with -(으)시-, -아/어 주시겠습니까?, or -아/어 주시기 바랍니다] + [closing: 감사드립니다/수고하십시오]

This frame gives a reliable structure for writing a formal Korean email from opening to closing, regardless of the specific topic.

  • 안녕하십니까? 저는 지난주에 등록증을 신청한 라일라입니다. Hello. I am 라일라, who applied for a registration card last week. formal greeting + self-identification with -입니다
  • 다름이 아니라, 확인 과정에서 어떤 서류가 부족한지 여쭤보고 싶어서 문의드립니다. The reason I am writing is that I would like to ask which document is missing during the confirmation process. 다름이 아니라 (formal opening expression); 여쭤보다 (humble verb) + -고 싶다 (desire, recycled)

4. Continued Formal Requests in Email Closings

Grammar — -아/어 주시겠습니까? and -아/어 주시기 바랍니다 in email bodies and closings

The formal request patterns introduced in Chapter 8 continue to apply directly to email writing. A polite request in the body of an email typically closes with one of these patterns, often followed by a brief thanks line.

  • 필요한 서류를 알려 주시겠습니까? — "Could you let me know the documents I need?"
  • 가능하시면 이번 주 안에 회신해 주시기 바랍니다. — "If possible, please reply within this week."
  • 번거롭게 해 드려 죄송하지만, 확인 부탁드립니다. — "I'm sorry for the trouble, but I would appreciate your checking on this."

라일라 드림

Hello. I am 라일라, who applied for a renewal of my residence registration last week.

The reason I am writing is that I received a text message yesterday saying that document confirmation is being delayed, but I could not tell exactly which document was missing, so I am writing to inquire. I submitted both the application form and a copy of my ID at the time of submission.

I'm sorry for the trouble, but could you please check what document is missing? If possible, please reply within this week.

Thank you for taking the time despite being busy.

Sincerely, 라일라

수고하십시오.

According to our check, the signature was missing from the application form, which delayed the document confirmation. After signing the application form, please scan it and send it again as an attached file.

Writing Workshop

This chapter's writing task asks you to produce a complete formal email making an inquiry or a respectful request, demonstrating control of formal email structure, honorific address, the honorific marker -(으)시-, and the Chapter 8 formal request patterns.

  1. Choose a formal-email situation. Pick a plausible scenario: following up on a delayed document, asking a professor a question, inquiring about a service, or requesting information from an office.
  2. Draft the greeting and self-identification. Open with 안녕하십니까 or 안녕하세요, then identify yourself with 저는 ~입니다.
  3. State your purpose. Use 다름이 아니라 or a similar formal opening expression to introduce your reason for writing.
  4. Write the body. Explain the situation clearly, using at least one verb marked with -(으)시- referring respectfully to the reader, and make your request using -아/어 주시겠습니까? or -아/어 주시기 바랍니다.
  5. Close politely. End with a thanks line such as 감사드립니다 or 수고하십시오.
  6. Revise. Check your draft against the checklist below.

체크리스트 — Editing checklist:

  • The email follows the formal email frame: greeting, self-identification, purpose, body, closing.
  • An honorific address term (귀하, 선생님, or title + 님) is used appropriately.
  • At least one verb is correctly marked with -(으)시- when referring to the reader.
  • The draft uses -습니다/-ㅂ니다 style consistently throughout, with no everyday polite endings.
  • At least one formal request pattern from Chapter 8 is used correctly.
  • Only Hangeul is used; no romanization appears.
  • Spacing and particles are accurate throughout.
  • No grammar or vocabulary beyond Volume III, Chapters 1–9, appears productively.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Why formal emails open with self-identification

Formal Korean emails to institutions, offices, and unfamiliar recipients typically open by stating who the writer is and how they are connected to the situation, even when this information appears elsewhere (such as a student ID or application number). This convention helps the recipient quickly place the request in context and reflects the broader Korean pragmatic pattern of establishing relationship and context before making a request.

Culture in Context — Softening a request with an apology phrase

Phrases such as 번거롭게 해 드려 죄송하지만 ("I'm sorry for the trouble, but...") are common in formal Korean requests, even when the request itself is entirely reasonable and the recipient's job is to respond to such inquiries. This softening convention signals awareness of the reader's time and effort rather than genuine apology for wrongdoing.

Institutional Awareness — Email conventions vary by institution and context

The exact greeting, closing, and level of formality in a Korean email can vary depending on the institution, the recipient's role, and the existing relationship between writer and reader. A first-time inquiry to an unfamiliar office typically calls for more formal language than a follow-up message to someone the writer has already corresponded with. Always adjust register to the specific relationship and context.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Omitting the honorific marker -(으)시- when referring to the reader

Error: Writing 이메일을 받았습니까? when respectfully asking whether the reader received an email.

Correction: Use the honorific form: 이메일을 받으셨습니까?, adding -(으)시- before the past-tense ending whenever the subject is the honored reader.

Common Mistake — Applying -(으)시- to the writer's own actions

Error: Writing 제가 이메일을 보내셨습니다, incorrectly adding the honorific marker to describe the writer's own action.

Correction: The honorific marker -(으)시- shows respect toward the subject of the sentence, not toward the writer describing their own actions. Use the plain form for the writer's actions: 제가 이메일을 보냈습니다. Reserve -(으)시-, humble verbs like 드리다 and 여쭤보다, and honorific address terms for the recipient and for third parties being respected.

Common Mistake — Opening a formal email with a casual greeting

Error: Opening a formal email to an unfamiliar office with 안녕! or a first-name-only address.

Correction: Open with 안녕하십니까? or 안녕하세요? followed by an honorific address term such as 담당자님 or 귀하, as modeled in this chapter.

1. Sample answer: 가다 → 가시다; 읽다 → 읽으시다; 쓰다 → 쓰시다; 앉다 → 앉으시다; 듣다 → 들으시다.

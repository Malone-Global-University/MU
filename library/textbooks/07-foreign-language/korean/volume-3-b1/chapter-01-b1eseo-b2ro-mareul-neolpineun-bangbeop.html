B1에서 B2로: 말을 넓히는 방법From B1 to B2 Expression

Reviewing discourse control from Volume II, expanding sentences into connected paragraphs, tightening cohesion across ideas, and resetting register for more independent B2-oriented communication · Speak and Write Korean: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Review and consolidate the core discourse tools from Volume II — connectors, tense and modality control, and speech-level consistency — as a foundation for B2 communication.
  2. Expand short, separate sentences into longer, well-organized paragraphs using cohesive devices.
  3. Identify and correct common cohesion problems, such as disconnected sentences, unclear reference, and repeated subjects.
  4. Recognize when a communicative situation calls for a register shift, and begin adjusting between everyday polite and more careful, deliberate style.
  5. Read and interpret a short multi-paragraph text and identify its organizing structure.
  6. Produce a connected, multi-paragraph piece of writing on a personal or familiar topic.
  7. Speak in longer, more connected turns rather than isolated sentences, using pauses and connectors naturally.

This chapter is a discourse-review and expansion chapter. It introduces no major new particle, honorific, or tense system beyond cohesion and paragraph-level connectors. Its purpose is to reset the register and structural expectations of the volume before Chapter 2 begins new B2-oriented content.

발음과 말하기 초점 — Pronunciation and Speech Focus

Pronunciation and Speech Focus

This chapter introduces no new sound-change rule. Instead, it reviews how intonation and pausing support connected speech, since connected paragraphs and longer spoken turns depend on prosody as much as grammar.

Speech Note — Pausing at connectors, not at random points

In connected Korean speech, pauses tend to occur after connectors such as 그런데, 그래서, and -는데, not in the middle of a phrase. A learner who pauses mid-phrase to search for the next word can sound more disconnected than one who pauses briefly right after a natural connector. Practice reading the model dialogue aloud, pausing only where a comma or connector appears.

Speech Note — Falling intonation at the end of a completed idea

When one idea is complete and a new one begins, a slightly stronger falling intonation at the sentence-final ending signals the boundary to a listener. This becomes especially useful in longer turns and presentations, where a listener needs clear signals for where one point ends and the next begins.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns Review

This chapter's principal focus is discourse review, paragraph expansion, cohesion, and register reset. It introduces no major new particle, honorific, or tense system; it develops learners' control of connecting sentences that they can already form correctly.

1. From Sentence to Paragraph

Review Note — What makes a paragraph, not just a list of sentences

A paragraph is not simply several correct sentences placed next to each other. It has one guiding topic (주제), an order that a listener or reader can follow (차례로), and connectors that show how each sentence relates to the one before it. Compare a disconnected list to a connected paragraph on the same content.

  • 저는 대학교에서 일해요. 저는 한국어를 배워요. 저는 새 동네에 살아요. I work at a university. I study Korean. I live in a new neighborhood. disconnected — three separate facts, no relationship shown
  • 저는 대학교에서 일하고 있는데, 요즘 한국어도 배우고 있어요. 그리고 최근에 새 동네로 이사해서 새로운 생활에 적응하고 있어요. I'm working at a university, and these days I'm also learning Korean. And I recently moved to a new neighborhood, so I'm adjusting to a new life. -는데 (background) + 그리고 + -아/어서 (reason) connect the same facts into one flowing account

2. Cohesion Devices: Connectors, Reference, and Repetition Control

Review Note — Three tools for cohesion

Connectors (그리고, 그래서, 하지만, 그런데, -고, -아/어서, -지만, -는데) show the logical relationship between ideas. Reference uses omission or a demonstrative such as 그 or 이 instead of repeating a full noun phrase. Repetition control avoids restating the same subject or object in every sentence when the topic is already established and can be omitted.

Overusing full noun phrases where omission or a connector would be more natural is one of the clearest signs of B1-level writing that has not yet expanded into B2 cohesion.

  • 저는 어제 친구를 만났어요. 저는 친구하고 영화를 봤어요. 저는 친구하고 저녁도 먹었어요. I met a friend yesterday. I watched a movie with the friend. I also ate dinner with the friend. repetition of 저는 and 친구 — flat, list-like
  • 저는 어제 친구를 만나서 같이 영화를 보고, 저녁도 먹었어요. I met a friend yesterday, and we watched a movie together and also ate dinner. -아/어서 (reason/sequence) + -고 (coordination) + omission of repeated subject and object

3. Register Reset: Everyday Polite Toward More Deliberate Style

Review Note — Preparing for register expansion

Volume II established everyday polite -아요/어요 as the productive baseline. Volume III will gradually introduce more careful, deliberate registers for news, institutions, formal writing, and academic contexts, beginning in Chapter 6. This chapter does not yet introduce -습니다 as a new productive system; instead, it asks learners to notice that the same content can sound more organized and deliberate even within -아요/어요, simply through better cohesion, clearer topic sentences, and more careful word choice such as 결론적으로 or 다시 말해서.

Building Block — A cohesive paragraph frame

[topic sentence] + [supporting detail with a connector] + [example or elaboration] + [closing or summarizing sentence]

A well-organized paragraph opens with a topic sentence, develops it through one or two connected supporting sentences, may add an example with 예를 들면, and closes with a brief summarizing or concluding sentence, often introduced with 결론적으로 or 그래서.

저는 요즘 말하기 연습에 집중하고 있어요. 예를 들면, 매일 아침에 혼자 한국어로 그날 계획을 이야기해 봐요. 처음에는 어색했지만, 지금은 훨씬 자연스러워졌어요. 결론적으로, 짧게라도 매일 말하는 연습이 큰 도움이 되는 것 같아요.

These days I'm focusing on speaking practice. For example, every morning I try talking through that day's plan alone in Korean. At first it felt awkward, but now it feels much more natural. In conclusion, I think practicing speaking every day, even briefly, seems to help a lot.

Practice — Expanding sentences

  1. Take three short separate sentences about your weekend and connect them into one flowing sentence using -고 or -아/어서.
  2. Write a two-sentence pair in which the second sentence omits a repeated subject that is clear from context.
  3. Write one sentence using 예를 들면 to give an example of a habit or routine.
  4. Write one closing sentence for a short paragraph using 결론적으로.

Model Dialogue

Setting: 지영's discussion group, first session. 지영 asks 라일라 to describe her week, first as separate sentences and then as a connected account.

지영 라일라 씨, 이번 주에 뭐 했어요? 문장 세 개로 짧게 말해 보세요. Laila, what did you do this week? Try saying it briefly in three sentences.
라일라 저는 회사에 갔어요. 저는 친구를 만났어요. 저는 집에서 쉬었어요. I went to work. I met a friend. I rested at home.
지영 좋아요. 이제 그 세 문장을 하나의 이야기처럼 연결해 보세요. Good. Now try connecting those three sentences into one flowing story.
라일라 음... 저는 이번 주에 회사에 갔는데, 일이 많아서 좀 바빴어요. 그래서 주말에 친구를 만나서 스트레스를 풀었어요. Um... I went to work this week, and I was a bit busy because there was a lot of work. So I met a friend over the weekend and relieved stress.
지영 훨씬 자연스러워졌어요. 마지막 문장도 연결해 볼까요? That sounds much more natural. Shall we connect the last sentence too?
라일라 네. 친구를 만난 후에는 집에서 조용히 쉬었어요. 결론적으로, 바쁘지만 나름 균형 잡힌 한 주였던 것 같아요. Yes. After meeting my friend, I rested quietly at home. In conclusion, I think it was a busy but fairly balanced week.
지영 바로 그거예요. 문장을 연결하니까 훨씬 더 하나의 이야기처럼 들려요. Exactly. Now that the sentences are connected, it sounds much more like one connected story.

Writing Workshop

This chapter's writing task is a short two-paragraph reflection: 한국어 공부와 나의 변화 ("Studying Korean and My Own Change"), modeled on the Reading Practice passage above.

  1. Draft separate sentences. Write four to six short, separate sentences about how your Korean (or another skill) has changed over time.
  2. Choose a topic sentence. Decide which idea should open your first paragraph.
  3. Connect with -는데, -지만, or -아/어서. Combine your separate sentences into two or three connected sentences.
  4. Add an example. Use 예를 들면 to illustrate one point with a specific detail.
  5. Remove repeated subjects. Check each sentence and omit a subject where it is already clear from context.
  6. Write a second paragraph about your next goal. Use 다시 말해서 to restate your goal more precisely.
  7. Close with 결론적으로. Write one closing sentence that sums up your reflection.
  8. Revise. Check your draft against the checklist below.

체크리스트 — Editing checklist:

  • Each paragraph has one clear topic sentence.
  • At least three different connectors are used across the two paragraphs.
  • At least one repeated subject or object has been omitted where natural.
  • At least one discourse expression (예를 들면, 다시 말해서, or 결론적으로) appears.
  • Speech level is consistent everyday polite -아요/어요 throughout.
  • Only Hangeul is used; no romanization appears in the final draft.
  • Spacing and particles are accurate throughout.
  • No grammar or vocabulary beyond Volume II and this chapter appears productively.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Storytelling and connected speech in everyday conversation

In everyday South Korean conversation, recounting one's day or week as a flowing, connected account — rather than a list of disconnected facts — is generally seen as more engaging and easier to follow. Listeners often respond with backchanneling sounds such as 네 or 그래서요? to encourage the speaker to continue, which supports longer connected turns.

Culture in Context — Discussion groups and peer feedback

Language exchange and study groups, like the one 지영 leads in this chapter, are a common and appreciated way for Korean learners and speakers alike to practice speaking and get direct, friendly feedback. Giving feedback on organization and flow, not only on grammar accuracy, is a valued part of these sessions.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Treating a paragraph as a list of separate sentences

Error: Writing several grammatically correct sentences in a row with no connectors, so the writing sounds like a checklist rather than a connected account.

Correction: Use connectors such as 그리고, 그래서, -고, or -아/어서 to link related sentences, following the Building Block paragraph frame above.

Common Mistake — Repeating the subject in every sentence

Error: Repeating 저는 or a full noun phrase in every sentence of a paragraph, even when the topic is already clear.

Correction: Omit the subject once it has been established, as Korean naturally allows, and rely on context and verb endings to carry the meaning forward.

Common Mistake — Overcorrecting into overly formal style

Error: Assuming that "sounding more advanced" means switching to -습니다 or borrowing academic-sounding vocabulary before it has been scheduled.

Correction: Keep everyday polite -아요/어요 as the productive speech level in this chapter. More formal registers are introduced later in the volume, once particular genres call for them.

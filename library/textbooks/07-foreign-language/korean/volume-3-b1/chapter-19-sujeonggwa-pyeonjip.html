수정과 편집Revision and Editing

Reviewing your own written and spoken Korean for cohesion, spacing, particles, style, and accuracy, using systems already active in this course · Speak and Write Korean: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify weaknesses in your own earlier Korean writing, including gaps in cohesion, spacing errors, particle misuse, and register inconsistency.
  2. Revise a paragraph so that topic continuity and cross-sentence cohesion are clearer, using topic marking, omission, and connectors already active in this volume.
  3. Correct common spacing errors around nouns, particles, predicates, endings, and bound nouns.
  4. Distinguish appropriate particle choices in context, especially 은/는 versus 이/가 and 에 versus 에서, and correct misuse.
  5. Recognize and correct unintended register shifts within a single piece of writing.
  6. Use a structured self-editing checklist to revise a full piece of writing before presenting it as a finished text.

This chapter introduces no new grammar system. It reviews cohesion, spacing, particle function, and register control from across Volume III and applies them to a new task: revising and editing a learner's own prior work. This chapter is a preparation chapter for Chapter 20, the final B2 portfolio.

Opening Scenario

지영, 민준, 라일라, and 수아 are preparing their portfolios for the end of Volume III. Their instructor asks each of them to choose one piece of earlier writing — an opinion paragraph, a formal email, or a presentation script — and revise it before including it in the final portfolio. 라일라 decides to revise the presentation script she wrote for Chapter 18, on part-time work among university students. Reading it again, she notices that one paragraph jumps between ideas without a clear connector, that she has occasionally dropped a required space before a particle, and that one sentence slips into a more casual tone than the rest of the script. The instructor reminds the class that revision is not about starting over — it is about reading carefully, identifying specific problems, and fixing them one at a time.

  1. You will learn how to read your own Korean writing critically for cohesion, spacing, particles, and register.
  2. You will practice correcting common errors in each of these areas using a structured checklist.
  3. You will revise a full piece of your own earlier writing in preparation for your Chapter 20 portfolio.

발음 — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation — Reading your own writing aloud to catch errors

Reading a written draft aloud, at a natural but unhurried pace, often reveals problems that silent reading misses: a missing particle, an awkward run of clauses, or a sentence that no longer sounds like the rest of the register. This is not a new sound-change rule; it is a proofreading strategy that draws on the syllable timing, phrase intonation, and rhythm control built throughout this course.

Practice reading the revision examples below aloud twice: once as originally written, and once in the corrected form. Notice how the corrected version reads more smoothly and predictably.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new grammar system. It reviews cohesion, spacing, particle function, and register control from across Volume III, and applies them to the task of revising a learner's own writing.

1. Cohesion — Connecting Sentences Clearly

Review — connectors and topic continuity (Ch. 2–4, 11, 14)

A paragraph feels disconnected when sentences sit side by side with no signal of how they relate. Review the connectors already active in this volume — 그래서, 하지만, 그런데, -아서/어서, -지만, -(으)니까, -는데 — and check that each sentence boundary in your draft uses one when a relationship needs to be signaled.

Before — disconnected아르바이트를 하는 학생이 많다. 생활비가 부족하다.
After — connected생활비가 부족해서 아르바이트를 하는 학생이 많다.

Also check topic continuity: once a topic is established with 은/는, later sentences about the same topic can often omit the subject rather than repeating it, as reviewed since Chapter 1.

2. Spacing — Checking High-Frequency Spacing Points

Review — spacing around particles, predicates, and bound nouns (Ch. 1–18)

When editing, check spacing at four points that account for most beginner-through-intermediate spacing errors: between a noun and its particle (no space), between a predicate stem and its ending (no space), around bound nouns such as 것 and 수 (a space before the bound noun), and around quotation and attribution phrases such as -다고 하다.

Before — spacing error학생 들이 아르바이트 를 많이 해요.
After — corrected학생들이 아르바이트를 많이 해요.

3. Particle Review — Common Confusions in Editing

Review — 은/는 vs. 이/가; 에 vs. 에서 (Ch. 1, Ch. 6–13)

When editing, check whether 은/는 is marking an established topic and 이/가 is marking new or focused information, rather than treating the two as interchangeable. Also check whether 에 (location of existence, destination, or time) has been used where 에서 (location of an action) is required, or the reverse.

  • 도서관에서 공부해요.I study at the library. (action location — 에서)
  • 도서관에 책이 많아요.There are many books at the library. (existence location — 에)

4. Register Consistency — Catching Unintended Shifts

Review — maintaining one speech level across a text (Ch. 6, 9, 17, 18)

A formal email, presentation script, or academic paragraph should maintain one consistent speech level throughout, usually -습니다 style or careful -아요/어요 style, not a mixture of the two. When editing, scan every sentence-final ending in a formal text and confirm that none has drifted into a casual or plain form.

Before — register slip발표를 시작하겠습니다. 이 주제는 진짜 재밌어.
After — consistent register발표를 시작하겠습니다. 이 주제는 정말 흥미롭습니다.
Building Block — The four-pass revision routine

[Pass 1: cohesion — check connectors and topic continuity] → [Pass 2: spacing — check particles, endings, bound nouns] → [Pass 3: particles — check 은/는 vs. 이/가 and 에 vs. 에서] → [Pass 4: register — confirm one consistent speech level throughout]

Work through your draft four separate times, once per pass, rather than trying to catch every error at once. This mirrors the structured checklist you will use in the Writing Workshop below.

Practice — Finding and fixing errors

  1. Add a connector to join these two sentences naturally: 아르바이트 시간이 너무 많다. 성적이 나빠질 수 있다.
  2. Correct the spacing: 저는 발표 를 준비 하고 있어요.
  3. Choose the correct particle: 도서관(에/에서) 자전거 도로에 대한 자료가 많다.
  4. Identify and correct the register slip: 이상으로 발표를 마치겠습니다. 질문 있으면 물어봐.

Writing Workshop

This chapter's writing task asks you to revise a complete piece of your own earlier writing from this volume, following the four-pass routine practiced above.

  1. Choose your text. Select one full piece of writing from Chapters 1–18: an opinion paragraph, a formal email, or a presentation script.
  2. Pass 1 — cohesion. Read through and add or correct connectors so that sentence relationships are clear. Check topic continuity and omission.
  3. Pass 2 — spacing. Check spacing around every particle, ending, and bound noun in the text.
  4. Pass 3 — particles. Check every instance of 은/는 vs. 이/가 and 에 vs. 에서 for accuracy.
  5. Pass 4 — register. Confirm that every sentence-final ending matches the same speech level throughout.
  6. Produce a final version. Write out the fully revised text as your final version, ready for the Chapter 20 portfolio.

체크리스트 — Editing checklist:

  • Every sentence connects clearly to the one before it, using a connector or appropriate omission.
  • Spacing around particles, predicate endings, and bound nouns is accurate throughout.
  • 은/는 and 이/가 are each used correctly for topic or new/focused information.
  • 에 and 에서 are each used correctly for existence/destination or action location.
  • One speech level is maintained consistently from beginning to end.
  • Only Hangeul is used; no romanization appears.
  • No grammar or vocabulary beyond Volume III, Chapters 1–19, appears productively.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Peer feedback and revision culture

In many Korean academic and workplace settings, drafts are commonly reviewed by peers, colleagues, or supervisors before a final version is submitted, and constructive feedback is generally offered directly but respectfully, often softened with expressions like 좋은 피드백이에요 or 이 부분은 이렇게 하면 더 좋을 것 같아요.

Culture in Context — Consistency as a marker of care

Maintaining a single, consistent register throughout a formal document or presentation is often read as a sign of careful preparation. An unintended shift into casual language partway through a formal text can be noticed by readers or listeners as a lapse in attention, even when the content itself is accurate.

Regional Awareness — Editing conventions vary by setting

The exact conventions for marking up a draft, the strictness applied to spacing rules, and the directness of peer feedback can vary by school, workplace, and generation. The routine in this chapter reflects a common, broadly usable approach to self-editing formal Korean writing.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Trying to fix everything in a single read-through

Error: Reading a draft once and attempting to catch cohesion problems, spacing errors, particle mistakes, and register shifts all at the same time, which causes many errors to be missed.

Correction: Use the four-pass routine from this chapter — one focused read-through per category — rather than one unfocused read-through.

Common Mistake — Treating 은/는 and 이/가 as always interchangeable

Error: 아르바이트가 좋은 경험이 돼요. used as the opening statement of a paragraph introducing a new topic, without first establishing it as a topic.

Correction: 아르바이트는 좋은 경험이 될 수 있습니다. ("Part-time work can be a good experience.") — 은/는 introduces the topic clearly at the start of a paragraph.

Common Mistake — Letting register drift in a long text

Error: Beginning a formal email with -습니다 style and drifting into -아요/어요 or casual endings by the final paragraph.

Correction: Check the sentence-final ending of every sentence in a formal text against the register established in the opening line, and correct any that do not match.

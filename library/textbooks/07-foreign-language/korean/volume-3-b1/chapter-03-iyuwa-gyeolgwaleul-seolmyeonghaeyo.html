이유와 결과를 설명해요Causes and Results

Developing -아서/어서 and -(으)니까 in depth, together with result language and explanation · Speak and Write Korean: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Distinguish the reason connector -아서/어서 from the reason connector -(으)니까, and choose correctly between them according to context.
  2. Explain a cause and its result across two or more connected sentences, rather than stating a bare fact.
  3. Use result-focused expressions such as 그 결과 and 그래서 그런지 to connect a cause to its outcome in extended explanation.
  4. Recognize why certain verbs and endings cannot combine with -아서/어서 (such as commands and suggestions), and use -(으)니까 instead in those contexts.
  5. Read a short explanatory passage and identify how the writer connects causes to results.
  6. Write a short explanation of why something happened, using at least two different reason connectors accurately.
  7. Speak about a cause-and-result situation from your own life or observation in an extended turn.

This chapter's principal new systems are the expanded contrast between -아서/어서 and -(으)니까, and result-focused explanatory language. It recycles the narrative frame from Chapter 2 — background clauses with -는데, the ongoing aspect -고 있다, and time clauses — while adding the ability to explain why events happened.

Communication Scenario

The discussion group meets for a third session. 라일라's narrative about moving and starting Korean impressed the group, but 지영 noticed that when 라일라 tries to explain *why* something happened — why she felt lonely, why she finally started studying, why her coworkers seemed distant at first — she tends to simply list two facts next to each other without connecting them. 지영 introduces a closer look at reason connectors: -아서/어서 for straightforward cause-and-effect, and -(으)니까 for reasons that lead to a suggestion, request, or realization. She also shows how 그 결과 and similar expressions can tie a whole explanation together.

  1. This chapter builds directly on Chapter 2's narrative frame: background clauses, ongoing aspect, and time clauses.
  2. This chapter's new focus is the deepened contrast between -아서/어서 and -(으)니까, plus result language such as 그 결과.
  3. The chapter's task is to explain a cause-and-result situation clearly, choosing the correct reason connector for each context.

발음과 말하기 초점 — Pronunciation and Speech Focus

Pronunciation and Speech Focus

This chapter introduces no new sound-change rule. The speech focus continues from Chapter 2, now applying intonation patterns to reason clauses.

Speech Note — A brief lift after -아서/어서 and -(으)니까

Like the background use of -는데 practiced in Chapter 2, a reason clause ending in -아서/어서 or -(으)니까 is typically spoken with a slight rise or level pitch, signaling that the result or conclusion is still coming. A falling pitch at this point would sound as though the speaker had stopped mid-thought.

Speech Note — Stress on the cause, not the connector

When explaining a cause, natural speech usually places slightly more emphasis on the content word describing the cause (for example, 바빠서 or 시간이 없으니까) rather than on the ending itself. Practice keeping the connector light and quick while giving the reason itself a touch more weight.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter's principal focus is explaining causes and results: deepening the contrast between -아서/어서 and -(으)니까, and adding result-focused discourse expressions. No new particle, honorific, or speech-level system is introduced.

1. Reason with -아서/어서

Grammar Note — -아서/어서 for direct cause and effect

-아서/어서 connects a cause directly to its natural result. The clause before -아서/어서 and the clause after it are understood as closely, almost automatically, linked — the second clause is a natural consequence of the first, not a separate decision made because of it.

Attach -아서 or -어서 according to the final vowel of the stem, following the same vowel-harmony pattern as -아요/어요. Past tense is not marked on the clause before -아서/어서; the tense of the whole sentence is carried by the final verb.

-아서/어서 cannot be followed by a command (-(으)세요) or a suggestion (-(으)ㅂ시다) in the second clause. For those, -(으)니까 is required instead.

  • 일이 너무 많아서 스트레스를 많이 받았어요. Because there was too much work, I got very stressed. -아서 (direct cause) + natural consequence
  • 한국어 수업이 재미있어서 계속 다니고 싶어졌어요. Because the Korean class was fun, I came to want to keep attending. -어서 (direct cause) linking interest to a resulting desire

2. Reason with -(으)니까

Grammar Note — -(으)니까 for reasons leading to a decision or realization

-(으)니까 also gives a reason, but the relationship between the two clauses is looser than with -아서/어서: the speaker presents the first clause as a basis for a decision, request, suggestion, command, or new realization in the second clause. Unlike -아서/어서, -(으)니까 can attach after the past tense marker and can be followed by commands and suggestions.

Attach -니까 after a vowel-final stem and -으니까 after a consonant-final stem. 있다/없다 and past-tense stems always take -으니까 in the form 있으니까, 없으니까, 했으니까.

  • 시간이 없으니까 나중에 다시 이야기합시다. Since there's no time, let's talk again later. -으니까 (reason for a suggestion) + -(으)ㅂ시다
  • 처음에는 잘 못했으니까 자신감이 별로 없었어요. Since I wasn't good at it at first, I didn't have much confidence. past stem + -으니까 (reason for a realization/state)
Comparing the two: a quick contrast

바빠서 못 갔어요 presents being busy as the direct, natural cause of not going — a simple cause-and-effect statement. 바쁘니까 못 가요 presents being busy as the speaker's stated basis for a decision or for informing the listener, and could be followed naturally by a suggestion such as 다음에 갑시다 ("let's go next time"). Both are correct; they simply frame the connection between cause and result differently.

3. Result Language: Tying an Explanation Together

Grammar Note — Discourse expressions for results

그 결과 ("as a result") opens a new sentence that states the outcome of what was just described, and is common in more organized or written explanation. 그래서 그런지 ("maybe that's why, perhaps as a result") softens a result, suggesting the speaker is not entirely certain of the connection. 그러다 보니(까) ("as I kept doing that, and so") describes a result that emerged gradually from a repeated or ongoing action, and often follows a clause with -고 있었다 from Chapter 2's narrative frame.

  • 매일 조금씩 한국어를 공부했어요. 그 결과, 지금은 뉴스를 어느 정도 이해할 수 있어요. I studied Korean a little every day. As a result, I can now understand the news to some degree. 그 결과 (formal result marker) opening a new sentence
  • 그때 매일 늦게까지 일하고 있었는데, 그러다 보니까 건강이 나빠졌어요. At that time I was working late every day, and as I kept doing that, my health got worse. -고 있었는데 (Ch.2 ongoing background) + 그러다 보니까 (gradual result)
Building Block — A cause-and-result explanation frame

[background situation, recycled from Ch.2] + [direct cause with -아서/어서] + [decision or realization with -(으)니까] + [result marker: 그 결과 / 그래서 그런지 / 그러다 보니까] + [closing reflection]

An explanation typically opens with background, states a direct cause, shows a decision that followed from a reason, names the eventual result explicitly, and closes with a brief reflection.

그 당시에 저는 새 프로젝트를 맡고 있었는데, 자료가 너무 많아서 정리하기가 힘들었어요. 시간이 부족하니까 팀원들한테 도움을 요청하기로 했어요. 다 같이 나눠서 일하니까 훨씬 수월했어요. 그 결과, 예정보다 일찍 프로젝트를 끝낼 수 있었어요. 지금 생각해 보면, 그때 혼자 버티지 않고 도움을 요청한 게 정말 다행이었어요.

At that time I was handling a new project, and because there was too much material, it was hard to organize. Since there wasn't enough time, I decided to ask my team members for help. Since we divided the work and did it together, it was much smoother. As a result, I was able to finish the project earlier than planned. Looking back now, I think it was really fortunate that I asked for help instead of enduring it alone.

Model Dialogue

Setting: 지영's discussion group, third session. 라일라 explains why her feelings about learning Korean changed over time.

지영 지난번에 한국어 수업을 듣기 전에는 배울 생각이 없었다고 했죠? 왜 마음이 바뀌었어요? Last time you said you didn't plan to learn before taking the Korean class, right? Why did your mind change?
라일라 사실 처음에는 회사 일 때문에 너무 바빠서 한국어를 배울 시간이 전혀 없다고 생각했어요. Actually, at first, because of work I was so busy that I thought I had no time at all to learn Korean.
지영 그런데 결국 수업을 듣게 됐잖아요. 이유가 뭐예요? But you ended up taking the class after all. What was the reason?
라일라 동료들하고 대화가 안 통하니까 너무 답답했어요. 그래서 뭐라도 해야겠다고 생각했어요. Since I couldn't communicate with my coworkers, it was really frustrating. So I thought I had to do something.
지영 수업을 듣고 나서 어땠어요? How was it after you started the class?
라일라 매주 조금씩 배우다 보니까 자신감이 조금씩 생겼어요. 그래서 그런지 회사에서도 예전보다 덜 외로웠어요. As I kept learning a little each week, I gradually gained confidence. Maybe that's why I felt less lonely at work than before.
지영 포기하고 싶었던 적은 없었어요? Was there ever a time you wanted to give up?
라일라 단어가 너무 어려워서 몇 번 포기하고 싶었어요. 하지만 여기까지 왔으니까 조금만 더 버텨 보자고 생각했어요. 그 결과, 지금은 이렇게 여러분 앞에서 발표도 할 수 있게 됐어요. Because the vocabulary was so hard, I wanted to give up a few times. But since I'd come this far, I thought I'd try to hold out just a bit more. As a result, I've now become able to present in front of everyone like this.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Framing effort and results

In South Korean conversation, describing a difficult period and the eventual positive result — as in this chapter's readings — is a common way to talk about personal growth, whether in a discussion group, a job interview, or a casual conversation with friends. Naming the specific turning point or effort behind a result, rather than simply stating the outcome, is often seen as a mark of thoughtful self-reflection.

Culture in Context — 덕분에 and acknowledging help

The expression 덕분에 ("thanks to") is frequently used to credit another person, a place, or a circumstance for a positive result, and doing so is generally viewed favorably in South Korean conversation, whether the credited cause is a person, an institution, or even a small everyday circumstance like a friendly café. This differs from 때문에, which is more neutral or negative in tone and is not typically used to thank someone directly.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Attaching a command or suggestion after -아서/어서

Error: Producing an incorrect sentence such as *시간이 없어서 빨리 가세요, attaching a command directly after -아서/어서.

Correction: Use -(으)니까 whenever the second clause is a command, suggestion, or request: 시간이 없으니까 빨리 가세요.

Common Mistake — Adding past tense before -아서/어서

Error: Producing an incorrect form such as *바빴어서 못 갔어요, marking past tense on the clause before -아서/어서.

Correction: -아서/어서 does not take a separate past-tense marker on the first clause; past tense is carried by the final verb only: 바빠서 못 갔어요.

Common Mistake — Treating -아서/어서 and -(으)니까 as fully interchangeable

Error: Using whichever reason connector comes to mind first, without considering whether the second clause states a natural effect or a decision, request, or realization.

Correction: Check the second clause: if it states a command, suggestion, or clear personal decision, -(으)니까 is usually the better fit; if it states a direct, natural consequence, -아서/어서 usually fits better.

Model Dialogue — Comprehension

  2. Sample sentence with -(으)니까: 동료들하고 대화가 안 통하니까 너무 답답했어요 — the reason leads to her realization/frustration and eventual decision to act.
  3. 라일라 names being able to present in front of the group as the result, introduced by 그 결과.

Listening comprehension

  • Sitting all day for work caused 민준's health to decline (앉아서 일하다 보니까 몸이 안 좋아졌다).
  • He decided to walk every morning; the reason is introduced with -(으)니까 (병원에 가 보니까 의사가 운동을 하라고 했다).
  • The result named at the end is that he became much healthier, introduced by 그 결과.

Reading comprehension

  1. The writer's first impression was that the city felt a little frightening because people seemed too busy.
  2. Sample sentence: 사장님이 항상 반갑게 인사해 주니까 마음이 조금씩 편해졌어요 — this reason leads to the writer's growing sense of comfort.

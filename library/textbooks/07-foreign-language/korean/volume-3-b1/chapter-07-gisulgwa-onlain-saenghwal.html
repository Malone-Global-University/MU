기술과 온라인 생활Technology and Online Life

Digital vocabulary, online register boundaries, and loanwords · Speak and Write Korean: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use everyday digital and technology vocabulary to describe devices, apps, connectivity problems, and online activity.
  2. Recognize how loanwords are adapted into Korean spelling and pronunciation, and use common technology loanwords accurately.
  3. Distinguish everyday polite -아요/어요 messaging from more clipped or informal online writing conventions, without adopting the latter as your own default style.
  4. Continue developing formal -습니다/-습니까 recognition and controlled use, now applied to app instructions, error messages, and formal digital notices.
  5. Continue developing source-framing patterns (에 따르면, -다고 보도했다, -라고 밝히다) and recognized passive-form verbs, now applied to technology-related reporting.
  6. Read a short technology notice, app description, or online article and identify its main point and register.
  7. Write a short message or short online post appropriately matched to its intended audience and setting.
  8. Discuss technology habits and online life using -아요/어요 style while correctly labeling casual online forms you encounter as receptive only.

This chapter continues developing the formal style, source-framing, and passive-recognition systems introduced in Chapter 6, now in a technology and online-life domain. It also introduces new productive vocabulary for devices, connectivity, and digital communication, along with receptive recognition of common online abbreviations and casual internet-register forms, which remain labeled and non-productive.

지영's discussion group meets again, and this week 민준 arrives frustrated: his phone froze during an important video call, and he could not find the right words to explain the problem to the building's IT help desk. 수아 shows the group a message from a class group chat that is full of abbreviations she does not fully understand, and 라일라 has found a short online article about a new public Wi-Fi service that just launched in her neighborhood. The group agrees to spend this session learning to talk about technology clearly — and to recognize, without imitating, the more casual shorthand that appears in group chats and online posts.

  1. You will build vocabulary for devices, apps, connectivity, and common technical problems, so that you can explain and request help clearly.
  2. You will notice how Korean online writing sometimes shortens or reshapes ordinary polite forms, and you will learn to recognize this without adopting it as your own default style.
  3. You will continue practicing the formal and source-framing systems from Chapter 6, now applied to app instructions, error messages, and short technology reports.

발음 — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation — Loanwords and Korean syllable adaptation

Technology loanwords such as 스마트폰, 와이파이, and 업데이트 follow Korean syllable structure rather than their original English pronunciation. Consonant clusters that are not permitted in a single Korean syllable are broken apart with an inserted vowel, and word-final consonants that Korean batchim cannot represent are replaced with an appropriate syllable, most often built on 으 or 이.

Practice reading these words with their Korean syllable timing rather than reaching back toward the English original: 스마트폰 (seu-ma-teu-pon), 업데이트 (eop-de-i-teu), 다운로드 (da-un-lo-deu). Read each word aloud evenly, syllable by syllable, before increasing speed.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops three systems introduced in Chapter 6 — formal -습니다/-습니까 style, source framing, and recognized passive-form verbs — by applying them to technology notices and short reports. It also introduces a new receptive-only system: recognizing where online writing departs from standard spacing, punctuation, and speech-level conventions, so that you can read casual messages without adopting their shortcuts yourself.

1. Formal Style in App Instructions and Error Messages

Grammar — -습니다/-ㅂ니다 in digital instructions and notices

Korean apps, websites, and devices routinely use formal -습니다/-ㅂ니다 style for instructions, confirmations, and error messages, since this language is addressed to an unspecified general user rather than to one known person. This is the same speech level you recognized in Chapter 6's news and public-notice texts, now appearing in a new domain.

Dictionary form-아요/어요 (everyday polite)-습니다/-ㅂ니다 (formal, in-app)

발생하다발생해요발생했습니다

  • 오류가 발생했습니다. 다시 시도해 주십시오. An error has occurred. Please try again. 발생하다 + -았습니다 (past formal); 시도하다 + -아 주십시오 (formal polite request)
  • 현재 서버에 연결되어 있지 않습니다. You are not currently connected to the server. 연결되다 (passive-form verb) + -어 있지 않습니다 (formal negative state)

As in Chapter 6, you are not expected to produce -습니다/-습니까 as your everyday default. Recognize it fluently in app and device language, and use it in the controlled writing task later in this chapter.

2. Source Framing in Technology Reporting

Grammar — Extending 에 따르면, -다고 보도했다, and -라고 밝히다 to technology topics

The same source-framing patterns from Chapter 6 appear constantly in technology news: companies announce updates, officials explain outages, and users report problems. Continue watching for these three patterns, now inside a technology domain:

  • 에 따르면 — "according to ~"
  • -다고 보도했다/보도되었다 — "(a news outlet) reported that ~"
  • -라고 밝혔다 — "(someone) stated/revealed that ~"
  • 회사 관계자에 따르면, 이번 업데이트는 이달 말에 배포될 예정입니다. According to a company official, this update is scheduled to be distributed at the end of this month. 에 따르면 (source marker) + -(으)ㄹ 예정입니다 (formal scheduled future)
  • 한 매체는 서비스 장애의 원인이 아직 밝혀지지 않았다고 보도했습니다. One media outlet reported that the cause of the service outage had not yet been revealed. -다고 보도했습니다 (news-report attribution, formal)
  • 고객센터 담당자는 "문제는 곧 해결됩니다"라고 밝혔습니다. The customer service representative stated, "The problem will be resolved soon." -라고 밝혔습니다 (direct-quote attribution, formal)

3. Recognized Passive-Form Verbs in Technology Contexts

Grammar — 연결되다, 해결되다, and related passive-form verbs (recognition and limited controlled use)

As in Chapter 6, these passive-form verbs remain a small recognized set, now expanded with two technology-relevant items. Full production of the passive and causative systems remains scheduled for Chapter 8.

  • 연결되다 — to be connected (vs. 연결하다, to connect)
  • 끊기다 — to be cut off / disconnected (vs. 끊다, to cut/disconnect something)
  • 해결되다 — to be resolved (vs. 해결하다, to resolve)
  • 복구되다 — to be restored (recycled from Ch. 6)

4. Recognizing Online Register Boundaries

Register Awareness — Online writing is not a separate grammar, but it does shift conventions

Casual Korean online writing — in group chats, comments, and social posts among friends — often shortens words, drops spacing, uses consonant-only abbreviations such as ㅇㅋ or ㄱㅅ, and marks laughter with repeated ㅋ. These are real, common patterns, but they belong to a specific casual register among people who already know each other well.

This chapter asks you only to recognize these forms in context, glossed where necessary. Your own productive writing in this chapter — messages, notices, and posts — should remain in accurate, properly spaced everyday polite -아요/어요 style or, where scheduled, formal -습니다/-ㅂ니다 style. Do not imitate consonant-abbreviation forms or drop spacing in your own writing tasks.

Building Block — The technology-notice frame

[source marker: 에 따르면 / 관계자는] + [reported content with -다고/-라고 + 하다/보도하다/밝히다, in formal -습니다 past or present] + [optional passive-form verb: 연결되다 / 해결되다 / 복구되다]

This is the same news-sentence frame from Chapter 6, now applied to app updates, outages, and public technology announcements.

Writing Workshop

This chapter's writing task asks you to produce two short pieces that demonstrate register control: a formal technology notice, and an everyday polite message to a friend about the same topic.

  1. Choose a technology situation. Pick a small, plausible scenario: an app update, a connectivity problem, a new online service, or a account/security notice.
  2. Draft the formal notice. Write three to four sentences in -습니다/-ㅂ니다 style describing what happened or what is planned, including at least one source-framing expression and one recognized passive-form verb from this chapter or Chapter 6.
  3. Draft the everyday message. Write two to three sentences in everyday polite -아요/어요 style, as if texting a friend about the same situation, using accurate spacing and no consonant-abbreviation forms.
  4. Compare. Underline the words or endings that changed between the two versions and note, in English, why each change was necessary.
  5. Revise. Check both drafts against the checklist below.

체크리스트 — Editing checklist:

  • The formal notice uses -습니다/-ㅂ니다 style consistently throughout, not mixed with -아요/어요.
  • The formal notice includes at least one source-framing expression, attributed clearly.
  • The formal notice includes at least one recognized passive-form verb, used correctly.
  • The everyday message uses -아요/어요 style consistently and contains no consonant-abbreviation or casual online shorthand.
  • Only Hangeul is used in both drafts; no romanization appears.
  • Spacing and particles are accurate throughout both drafts.
  • No grammar or vocabulary beyond Volume III, Chapters 1–7, appears productively.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Formality in Korean digital services

Korean apps, banking services, and customer-support systems typically default to formal -습니다/-습니까 style because they address an unknown general public rather than a specific familiar person. This mirrors the same public-address logic you learned in Chapter 6 for news and notices, and it explains why even a friendly-sounding app can suddenly switch to formal language in its error messages and confirmations.

Culture in Context — Group chats and register-switching among Korean speakers

Korean speakers often move fluidly between highly casual, abbreviation-heavy chat language among close friends and much more careful, standard writing in academic, professional, or public contexts. Recognizing this range — without assuming that the casual end is "normal Korean" for every situation — is an important part of reading real, everyday Korean texts.

Regional and Generational Awareness — Online abbreviations change quickly

Online abbreviations and slang such as ㅇㅋ, ㄱㅅ, or 인싸 change quickly across generations and online communities, and they are not universal across all Korean speakers or all platforms. Treat this chapter's small receptive set as an introduction to a general pattern, not as a permanent or complete list.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Using casual online abbreviations in formal or academic writing

Error: Writing ㅇㅋ, ㄱㅅ, or dropped spacing in a message to an instructor, a customer service center, or in this chapter's Writing Workshop notice.

Correction: Reserve these forms for recognition only. Use full, properly spaced -아요/어요 or -습니다/-ㅂ니다 in your own productive writing, matched to the setting.

Common Mistake — Mixing formal and everyday polite endings within one notice

Error: Writing a technology notice that switches between -습니다 and -아요/어요 within the same passage.

Correction: As in Chapter 6, choose one speech level for the whole passage and keep it consistent throughout.

Common Mistake — Mispronouncing loanwords with English stress and rhythm

Error: Pronouncing 스마트폰 or 업데이트 with English syllable stress and consonant clusters instead of even Korean syllable timing.

Correction: Practice loanwords with even Korean syllable timing, as modeled in the Pronunciation Focus, rather than reaching back toward the English original.

  4. These forms are appropriate only among close friends in casual settings; a professor or a customer service center expects standard spacing, complete words, and an appropriately polite or formal speech level.

1. Sample answer: 앱이 멈춰요 → 앱이 멈춥니다; 비밀번호를 잊어버렸어요 → 비밀번호를 잊어버렸습니다; 화면이 안 나와요 → 화면이 나오지 않습니다.

2. Sample answer: 회사에 따르면, 다음 주에 새 업데이트가 배포될 예정입니다. / 담당자는 "문제는 오늘 안에 해결됩니다"라고 밝혔습니다.

3. Sample answer: Formal notices address an unknown general public and must remain accurate and universally readable; casual abbreviations assume shared context and familiarity that a formal audience does not have, and they can appear unprofessional or unclear.

Section D — Sample response pair

Formal notice: 서비스 제공업체에 따르면, 오늘 오전 일부 이용자의 계정 연결이 끊겼다고 합니다. 담당팀은 원인을 조사하고 있으며, 오늘 저녁까지 복구될 예정이라고 밝혔습니다.

Everyday message: 오늘 앱이 갑자기 끊겼어요. 저녁까지는 고쳐진대요. 조금만 기다려요!

Check that: the formal notice stays in -습니다/-ㅂ니다 style throughout and includes source framing and a passive-form verb, while the everyday message stays in -아요/어요 style with accurate spacing and no abbreviations.

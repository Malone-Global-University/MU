Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Assemble a coherent B2 portfolio that draws together revised writing, a spoken presentation, and reflective commentary from across Volume III.
  2. Present a short, well-organized spoken piece that narrates, explains, compares, or argues a position using systems consolidated throughout the volume.
  3. Respond to follow-up questions on your own portfolio content in appropriately formal spoken Korean.
  4. Write a reflective self-assessment that identifies specific strengths and remaining challenges in your own Korean, using accurate and specific language rather than vague generalizations.
  5. Maintain consistent register, accurate spacing, correct particle use, and cohesive discourse across every portfolio component.
  6. Evaluate your own portfolio against a stated rubric before submitting it as your Volume III capstone.

This chapter introduces no new grammar system. It is the Volume III capstone: it consolidates and synthesizes systems built across all twenty chapters — Hangeul literacy, particles, predicate morphology, speech levels, honorifics, connectors, noun modification, quotation, register control, and the four skills — into one integrated independent-communication portfolio at the B2 level.

발음 — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation — Presenting with steady rhythm and clear sentence-final endings

This chapter introduces no new sound-change rule. It draws together the syllable timing, phrase intonation, and sentence-ending prosody built across the volume and applies them to sustained, five-minute presentational speech. Presentations flow more clearly when speakers slow down slightly at connectors such as 그래서 and 하지만, and pause briefly at the end of each sentence rather than rushing into the next one.

Before recording or delivering your presentation, practice reading your script aloud at least twice, listening for any sentence-final ending that sounds rushed, mismatched in register, or unclear.

Portfolio Synthesis and Review

This chapter introduces no new grammar system. It reviews and synthesizes cohesion, particles, predicate morphology, speech levels, honorifics, connectors, and register control from across Volume III, and applies all of them together to the capstone task of building and presenting a B2 portfolio.

1. Synthesis Review — Systems Your Portfolio Must Demonstrate

Review — the systems consolidated across Volume III

Your portfolio should show control of the systems this volume has built and reviewed, including:

  • topic and focus marking with 은/는 and 이/가, and topic continuity across sentences (Ch. 1, 11, 19);
  • location and action particles 에 and 에서 (Ch. 6–13, 19);
  • connectors and clause linkage such as -아서/어서, -지만, -(으)니까, -는데 (Ch. 3, 11, 14, 19);
  • noun-modifying clauses for description and explanation (across the volume);
  • indirect quotation and reported speech for summarizing others' views (Ch. 4, 17);
  • comparison and evidence language for supporting a position (Ch. 11–13);
  • one consistent speech level throughout each formal text or presentation (Ch. 6, 9, 17–19); and
  • accurate Hangeul spelling and spacing throughout (Ch. 1–19).

Nothing here is new. The task in this chapter is to combine these systems fluently in one integrated body of work rather than to learn anything additional.

2. Presentation Structure — Organizing a B2 Spoken Presentation

Review — presentation structure and source integration (Ch. 18)

A well-organized B2 presentation typically follows an introduction–body–conclusion structure: an opening that states the topic and its importance, a body that presents two or three main points with supporting evidence or examples, and a conclusion that summarizes the position and, where appropriate, suggests an implication.

Weaker — no clear structure도서관이 좋아요. 사람들이 많이 가요. 책도 많고 조용해요.
Stronger — structured with a connector오늘은 지역 도서관이 늘어난 이유에 대해 말씀드리겠습니다. 먼저 이용자 수가 증가했기 때문에 도서관이 늘었습니다.

3. Responding to Questions — Extended Q&A in Formal Register

Review — clarification, repair, and formal register in discussion (Ch. 14, 18)

When answering a follow-up question, maintain the same speech level used in the presentation itself. Use softening and repair strategies such as 좋은 질문이네요, 다시 한번 설명하자면, or 제가 정확히 이해했는지 확인하고 싶은데요 rather than shifting into a casual tone simply because the exchange feels less scripted.

  • 좋은 질문이네요. 다시 한번 설명하자면, 이용자 수가 늘어난 것이 가장 큰 이유입니다.That's a good question. To explain it again, the increase in the number of users is the biggest reason.

4. Reflective Self-Assessment — Writing Specific, Accurate Reflection

Review — specific self-description over vague generalization

A strong reflective paragraph identifies specific systems, not vague feelings. Compare a vague reflection with a specific one.

Vague저는 한국어를 많이 배웠어요. 더 열심히 할 거예요.

Model Dialogue

수아 delivers part of her presentation to her classmates, and 민준 asks a follow-up question.

Context: a classroom presentation followed by a brief question-and-answer exchange. Formal deferential register throughout, as appropriate to a presentation setting.

수아 오늘은 우리 동네 작은 도서관이 늘어난 이유에 대해 발표하겠습니다. 먼저 이용자 수가 최근 몇 년 동안 계속 증가했기 때문에 지역 도서관에 대한 관심도 커졌습니다. Today I will present on the reasons small neighborhood libraries have increased. First, because the number of users has continued to increase in recent years, interest in local libraries has also grown.
수아 둘째, 시에서 지역 주민들을 위한 문화 공간을 늘리려고 하기 때문에 도서관을 새로운 장소에 만들고 있습니다. 마지막으로, 조사에 따르면 많은 주민들이 도서관을 단순한 독서 공간이 아니라 만남의 장소로도 생각한다고 합니다. Second, because the city is trying to increase cultural spaces for local residents, it is building libraries in new locations. Finally, according to a survey, many residents reportedly think of the library not simply as a reading space but also as a meeting place.
수아 정리하면, 이용자 증가, 시의 정책, 그리고 주민들의 인식 변화가 함께 작용해서 지역 도서관이 늘어났다고 볼 수 있습니다. 이상으로 발표를 마치겠습니다. To summarize, increased users, city policy, and changing resident perceptions can be seen as working together to increase local libraries. This concludes my presentation.
(정적 — a brief pause, then 민준 raises his hand.)
민준 좋은 발표 감사합니다. 질문이 하나 있는데요, 조사는 어디에서 하신 건가요? Thank you for the good presentation. I have one question — where was the survey conducted?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Presentations and Q&A in Korean academic and professional settings

In many Korean classroom, academic, and workplace settings, a presentation is commonly followed by a short question-and-answer period in which the presenter is expected to maintain the same formal register used during the presentation itself, even when responding spontaneously.

Culture in Context — Self-reflection as part of formal learning

Written self-assessment is a common closing element in many Korean educational contexts, where learners are asked to identify specific strengths and areas for continued growth rather than offering only general praise or general criticism of their own work.

Regional Awareness — Portfolio and presentation conventions vary by setting

The exact format of a portfolio, the length of a presentation, and the formality expected during Q&A can vary by school, workplace, and context. The routine in this chapter reflects a common, broadly usable approach to closing an independent-communication course with an integrated capstone task.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Shifting to casual register during Q&A

Error: Delivering a presentation in -습니다 style, then answering a question with 그건 제가 잘 몰라요.

Correction: 그 부분은 제가 정확히 확인하지 못했습니다. — maintain the same formal register established at the start of the presentation.

Common Mistake — Writing a vague self-assessment

Error: 저는 한국어를 잘하게 됐어요. 계속 열심히 할게요. as the entire self-assessment.

Correction: Name a specific system, such as connectors, 은/는과 이/가의 구별, or 격식체 유지, and describe concretely how it has changed and what still needs practice.

Common Mistake — Presenting without a clear connector structure

Error: Listing several points with no connecting language, so that the presentation sounds like an unordered list rather than a structured argument.

Correction: Use ordering connectors such as 먼저, 둘째, and 마지막으로, and close with a summarizing connector such as 정리하면.

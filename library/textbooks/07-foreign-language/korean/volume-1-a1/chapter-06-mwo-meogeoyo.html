뭐 먹어요?Food, Drink, and Basic Actions

Your first full set of action-verb tools: the present polite ending, the object particle, and short negation · Speak and Write Korean: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Conjugate action verbs into the present polite -아요/어요 form.
  2. Mark the object of a sentence with the particle 을/를.
  3. Say what you like using 좋아해요.
  4. Negate simple action-verb sentences with short negation 안.
  5. Name common foods and drinks and order or talk about them in simple sentences.
  6. Read and write new batchim patterns that appear in this chapter's food vocabulary.
  7. Hold a short exchange about what someone eats, drinks, and likes.

Communication Scenario

It is lunchtime at Kim Seongsaengnim's school, and the class is at a small campus cafeteria, 학생 식당. Everyone is deciding what to eat and drink, comparing what they like, and telling each other what they do and do not want.

  1. To talk about eating, drinking, and liking things, you need your first action verbs — words like 먹다 (to eat) and 마시다 (to drink) — conjugated into a form people actually use in conversation.
  2. By the end of this chapter you will be able to say what you eat, drink, like, and do not eat, using complete polite sentences built from real action verbs rather than the copula and existence patterns you have used so far.

발음 — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus

This chapter does not introduce a new sound-change rule. It gives you controlled practice reading and producing the batchim and vowel-contraction patterns that appear in this chapter's action verbs.

Pronunciation Note — Vowel contraction in -아요/어요

When a verb stem ends in the vowels ㅏ or ㅗ, the ending contracts rather than simply attaching. 가다 becomes 가요, not 가아요. 오다 becomes 와요, not 오아요. 마시다 becomes 마셔요, not 마시어요. These contractions are pronunciation and spelling facts together — say and write the contracted form, not the full uncontracted one.

Read the following words aloud, paying attention to the double batchim in 읽어요 and the liaison that carries its second consonant into the following syllable.

먹어요 · 마셔요 · 가요 · 와요 · 읽어요 · 봐요 · 사요 · 좋아해요

받침 점검: in 읽어요, the batchim ㄺ carries its second consonant ㄱ into the following syllable, so the word is pronounced closer to 일거요 in connected speech. You will study this liaison pattern formally in a later chapter; for now, simply imitate the pronunciation as modeled.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

문법 1 — The present polite ending -아요/어요

Communicative purpose: talk about actions happening now, habitually, or in the near future, in ordinary everyday polite speech.

Every dictionary-form verb ends in -다. Remove -다 to find the stem, then attach -아요 or -어요 depending on the stem's last vowel.

Stem's last vowelEndingExample
ㅏ or ㅗ-아요가다 → 가요
any other vowel-어요먹다 → 먹어요
  • 저는 밥을 먹어요. I eat rice / a meal. 저는 [topic] + 밥을 [object] + 먹어요 [verb]
  • 물을 마셔요. I drink water.
  • 학교에 가요. I go to school.
Grammar Note — This is not the copula you already know

Since Chapter 1 you have used 이에요/예요 to say what something is. The ending in this chapter, -아요/어요, attaches to action-verb stems to say what someone does. The two systems look similar because both end in polite 요, but they attach to different kinds of words and serve different functions. Do not attach -아요/어요 to a noun, and do not attach 이에요/예요 to an action-verb stem.

문법 2 — The object particle 을/를

Communicative purpose: mark the thing that receives the action of a transitive verb.

을 attaches to a noun ending in a consonant (batchim); 를 attaches to a noun ending in a vowel.

  • 빵을 먹어요. I eat bread. 빵 ends in batchim ㅇ → 을
  • 커피를 마셔요. I drink coffee. 커피 ends in a vowel → 를

In natural spoken Korean, 을/를 is sometimes dropped. This chapter's written and careful spoken practice keeps it, following the productive standard established in the Korean Language-Specific Master Instructions.

문법 3 — 좋아해요 ("to like")

Communicative purpose: say what you like.

좋아하다 behaves like an ordinary action verb and takes the object particle, not a subject-marking pattern.

  • 저는 과일을 좋아해요. I like fruit.
  • 커피를 좋아해요? 차를 좋아해요? Do you like coffee? Do you like tea?

문법 4 — Short negation with 안

Communicative purpose: say that you do not do something.

Place 안 directly before the conjugated verb.

  • 고기를 안 먹어요. I don't eat meat.
  • 우유는 안 마셔요. I don't drink milk.

This negation pattern applies to action verbs, as practiced here. You already know a separate way to negate the copula (아니에요, Chapter 2) and existence (없어요, Chapter 3); do not mix those patterns with 안.

Language Building Block — All four patterns together
저는 [음식]을/를 좋아해요. [음식]을/를 먹어요. [음식]은/는 안 먹어요.
Preference sentence + affirmative action sentence + negated action sentence
저는 과일을 좋아해요. 사과를 먹어요. 고기는 안 먹어요.
I like fruit. I eat apples. I don't eat meat.

Practice — Building action sentences

  1. Conjugate 읽다, 보다, and 사다 into the polite present form.
  2. Write one sentence with 을/를 saying what you eat for breakfast.
  3. Write one sentence with 안 saying something you don't drink.
  4. Write one sentence with 좋아해요 saying what food you like.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Nurul and Minjun are getting lunch together at the campus cafeteria, 학생 식당.

누룰 민준 씨, 뭐 먹어요? Minjun, what are you eating?
민준 저는 라면을 먹어요. 누룰 씨는요? I'm eating ramyeon. What about you, Nurul?
누룰 저는 밥하고 김치를 먹어요. 저는 고기를 안 먹어요. I'm eating rice and kimchi. I don't eat meat.
민준 아, 그래요? 저는 채소를 안 좋아해요. 고기를 좋아해요. Oh, really? I don't like vegetables. I like meat.
누룰 뭐 마셔요? 저는 물을 마셔요. What are you drinking? I'm drinking water.
민준 저는 커피를 마셔요. 저는 커피를 아주 좋아해요. I'm drinking coffee. I really like coffee.

내용 이해 — Comprehension questions:

  1. What does Minjun eat for lunch?
  2. What does Nurul eat, and what does she avoid?
  3. What does Minjun say he likes and dislikes?
  4. What does each person drink?

Writing Workshop

This chapter's writing task is a short food diary entry, 오늘 뭐 먹어요?, describing what you eat, drink, and like in one day.

  1. State what you eat and drink. Use 을/를 with 먹어요 and 마셔요 for at least two different foods and two different drinks.
  2. State a preference. Use 좋아해요 to say what food or drink you like.
  3. State something you avoid. Use 안 with an action verb to say something you don't eat or drink.
  4. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist below.

체크리스트 — Editing checklist:

  • Every action verb is correctly conjugated into -아요/어요, with vowel contractions applied where required (가요, 와요, 마셔요).
  • 을/를 is chosen correctly based on whether the noun ends in batchim or a vowel.
  • 안 is placed directly before the verb, not before the object.
  • 은/는 and 이/가 from earlier chapters are still used correctly where they appear.
  • No sentence relies on grammar not yet activated (Chapters 7 and beyond).

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — 밥 as "rice" and "meal"

The word 밥 literally means cooked rice, but it is also used broadly to mean "a meal" in everyday speech, since rice has traditionally been a central part of a standard Korean meal. Asking 밥 먹었어요? ("Have you eaten?") is a common, friendly way of checking in with someone — you will study this specific past-tense greeting formula more formally once past tense is introduced in Chapter 16.

Culture in Context — Sharing food

In many everyday Korean dining situations, side dishes such as 김치 are placed in the center of the table and shared rather than individually plated. This is a common practical and social pattern in casual meals among family, friends, and classmates, though restaurant and household customs vary.

Regional and Diaspora Note — Food vocabulary abroad

Korean diaspora communities sometimes use adapted or additional food vocabulary shaped by locally available ingredients. The vocabulary in this chapter reflects everyday South Korean usage; later volumes will address more specialized and regional food terminology.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Using the copula ending on an action verb

Error: 저는 밥 먹이에요.

Correction: 저는 밥을 먹어요. — Action verbs take -아요/어요, not 이에요/예요. Keep the two ending systems separate.

Common Mistake — Forgetting vowel contraction

Error: 학교에 가아요.

Correction: 학교에 가요. — When the stem already ends in ㅏ or ㅗ, the ending contracts; do not write both vowels separately.

Common Mistake — Placing 안 in the wrong position

Error: 고기 안을 먹어요.

Correction: 고기를 안 먹어요. — 안 goes directly before the conjugated verb, never attached to the object.

Common Mistake — Choosing the wrong object particle

Error: 빵를 먹어요.

Correction: 빵을 먹어요. — 빵 ends in batchim ㅇ, so it takes 을, not 를.

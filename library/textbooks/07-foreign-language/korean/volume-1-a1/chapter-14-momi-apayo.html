몸이 아파요Health, Needs, and Reasons

Giving reasons with -아/어서, expressing obligation with 해야 해요, telling someone not to do something with -지 마세요, and describing symptoms and needs · Speak and Write Korean: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe basic symptoms and parts of the body using everyday polite Korean.
  2. Give a reason for a situation or request using -아/어서.
  3. Express obligation or necessity using 해야 해요.
  4. Tell someone not to do something using -지 마세요.
  5. Write and understand short health-related messages, such as a note to a teacher or a message to a friend.
  6. Combine reason, obligation, and prohibition language with ability and request patterns from Chapter 13.
  7. Maintain accurate Hangeul, spacing, particles, and everyday polite speech level while working with health, needs, and reasons.

Communication Scenario

라일라 woke up this morning with a headache and a sore throat. She needs to tell her Korean class instructor that she cannot come to class today, explain why, and say what she needs to do instead — rest and see a doctor. She also wants to ask her classmate 수진 not to worry and not to visit her apartment, since she does not want 수진 to catch her cold. To do this, 라일라 needs a way to give a reason, state an obligation, and tell someone not to do something — language that Chapter 13's ability and request patterns do not yet provide on their own.

  1. This chapter introduces reason with -아/어서, obligation with 해야 해요, and prohibition with -지 마세요, along with vocabulary for symptoms, body parts, and health needs.
  2. Every task below builds on identity, family, dates, food, routines, location, shopping, plans, directions, and ability/request language already active from Chapters 1–13.
  3. By the end of this chapter you will be able to explain why you cannot do something, say what you need to do, and tell someone what not to do, all in everyday polite Korean.

발음 — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus

This chapter's main pronunciation target is the batchim linking that occurs when 아파요 and 아파서 follow the stem 아프-, and the pronunciation of the doubled consonant in 아파요's related forms as they combine with endings introduced in this chapter.

Pronunciation Note — Linking with 아/어서 and 해야 해요

When a consonant-final stem is followed by -아/어서, the batchim links forward, so 아파서 and 피곤해서 are pronounced as smooth, connected syllables rather than as separated words. This is the same liaison pattern practiced since Chapter 8, now applied to this chapter's reason clauses. The phrase 해야 해요 is also pronounced as a single connected phrase, with the two 해 syllables kept distinct but linked smoothly in natural speech.

Read the following aloud, listening for the linked sound between each syllable.

머리가 아파서 · 피곤해서 · 쉬어야 해요 · 가지 마세요

받침 점검: continue distinguishing 아/어서 reason clauses from the -아요/어요 polite ending introduced in Chapter 6, as covered in this chapter's grammar section.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

문법 1 — Giving a reason: -아/어서

Communicative purpose: explain why something is happening or why you are making a request.

-아/어서 attaches to a predicate stem to connect a reason to a result. Choose 아서 or 어서 the same way you would choose between -아요 and -어요, following the vowel pattern first practiced in Chapter 6. Unlike the everyday polite ending, -아/어서 connects two clauses and does not itself carry a separate speech-level ending; the final verb of the sentence carries the speech level.

Dictionary formVowel pattern-아/어서 form
아프다ㅡ stem아파서
피곤하다하다 verb피곤해서
가다ㅏ stem가서
  • 머리가 아파서 오늘 학교에 못 가요. My head hurts, so I can't go to school today. 아프다 → 아파서
  • 피곤해서 일찍 자야 해요. I'm tired, so I need to go to bed early. 피곤하다 → 피곤해서
Grammar Note — -아/어서 versus 그래서

Chapter 11 introduced 그래서 to connect two separate sentences with "so." -아/어서 instead attaches directly to a predicate stem to join a reason and a result within one sentence, as in 머리가 아파서 못 가요. The meaning is similar, but -아/어서 creates a single connected sentence rather than two separate ones.

문법 2 — Obligation: 해야 해요

Communicative purpose: say that something is necessary or that you must do it.

Obligation is expressed with -아/어야 하다, most often heard as 해야 해요 for 하다 verbs. The pattern attaches to a predicate stem using the same vowel choice as -아/어서, followed by 야 해요.

Dictionary formVowel pattern-아/어야 해요 form
쉬다ㅣ stem쉬어야 해요
가다ㅏ stem가야 해요
먹다consonant-final먹어야 해요
  • 오늘은 집에서 쉬어야 해요. I need to rest at home today.
  • 약을 먹어야 해요. I need to take medicine.

문법 3 — Prohibition: -지 마세요

Communicative purpose: politely tell someone not to do something.

-지 마세요 attaches to a predicate stem to tell someone not to do an action. It builds on the polite instruction ending -(으)세요 from Chapter 12, but reverses the meaning to a negative instruction. The stem does not change according to vowel pattern; only -지 is added before 마세요.

Dictionary form-지 마세요 form
가다가지 마세요
걱정하다걱정하지 마세요
오다오지 마세요
  • 걱정하지 마세요. Please don't worry.
  • 오늘 제 방에 오지 마세요. 감기가 있어요. Please don't come to my room today. I have a cold.
Grammar Note — -지 마세요 versus 안 and 못

Chapter 6 introduced 안 for simple negation, and Chapter 13 introduced 못 for inability. -지 마세요 is different from both: it is a polite instruction telling someone else not to do something, not a statement about the speaker's own action. Compare 안 가요 ("I'm not going"), 못 가요 ("I can't go"), and 가지 마세요 ("Please don't go").

Language Building Block — Explaining a health problem and a need
[증상]이/가 아파서 [행동]아/어야 해요. [상대방]이/가 걱정하지 마세요.
A reason clause with -아/어서, followed by an obligation with 해야 해요, followed by a prohibition with -지 마세요
머리가 아파서 오늘 쉬어야 해요. 걱정하지 마세요.
My head hurts, so I need to rest today. Please don't worry.

Model Dialogue

Setting: 라일라 calls 수진 to explain that she is sick and cannot meet today.

수진 여보세요? 라일라 씨, 오늘 학교에 왜 안 왔어요? Hello? Layla, why didn't you come to school today?
라일라 머리가 아프고 목도 아파서 오늘 못 갔어요. My head hurts and my throat hurts too, so I couldn't go today.
수진 감기예요? 병원에 가야 해요. Is it a cold? You should go to the hospital.
라일라 네, 이따가 병원에 가서 약을 받아야 해요. 지금은 집에서 쉬어야 해요. Yes, I need to go to the hospital later and get medicine. Right now I need to rest at home.
수진 알겠어요. 제가 오늘 라일라 씨 집에 갈까요? Got it. Should I come to your place today?
라일라 아니요, 오지 마세요. 감기가 있어서 걱정돼요. 걱정하지 마세요, 저는 괜찮아요. No, please don't come. I have a cold and I'm worried about you. Don't worry, I'm okay.
수진 네, 그럼 푹 쉬세요. 빨리 나으세요. Okay, then get plenty of rest. Get well soon.

Writing Workshop

This chapter's writing task is a short absence message, 결석 메시지 ("Absence Message"), combining a reason sentence with -아/어서, an obligation sentence with 해야 해요, and at least one prohibition or reassurance with -지 마세요.

  1. Greet your reader. Use a greeting or address term from earlier chapters, such as 선생님 or a friend's name.
  2. Explain your symptom and reason. Use -아/어서 to connect a symptom to why you cannot come.
  3. State what you need to do. Use 해야 해요.
  4. Add a reassurance or prohibition. Use -지 마세요 to tell your reader not to worry or not to visit.
  5. Close politely. Use a closing expression appropriate to everyday polite speech.
  6. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist below.

체크리스트 — Editing checklist:

  • Every sentence uses only Hangeul and only systems activated through Chapter 14.
  • Speech level is consistent everyday polite -아요/어요, -아/어서, 해야 해요, and -지 마세요 throughout.
  • -아/어서 is chosen correctly based on the vowel pattern of the stem.
  • 해야 해요 correctly follows the -아/어야 pattern for the verb used.
  • -지 마세요 is attached directly to the stem without a vowel change.
  • Spacing between nouns, particles, and predicates follows the patterns modeled in Chapters 1–13.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Reporting an absence

In many South Korean schools and workplaces, it is common and expected to notify an instructor or supervisor directly, briefly and politely, when illness prevents attendance, often by message rather than only by phone call; a short message explaining the reason and expressing an intention to make up missed work, such as 지훈's message above, is a typical and courteous approach.

Culture in Context — 빨리 나으세요

The expression 빨리 나으세요 ("Get well soon") is a common and warm response to hearing that someone is sick, and it is common courtesy in many everyday South Korean conversations to reassure a sick friend that they should rest and not to worry about obligations while they recover.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Wrong vowel pattern for -아/어서

Error: 아프다 → 아프서

Correction: 아파서 — the ㅡ in 아프다 drops and combines with 아, following the same contraction pattern practiced with -아요/어요 since Chapter 6.

Common Mistake — Treating -아/어서 as a full sentence ending

Error: 머리가 아파서. (stopping the sentence there)

Correction: 머리가 아파서 학교에 못 가요. — -아/어서 connects a reason to a result clause; the sentence should continue to a final predicate that carries the speech level.

Common Mistake — Confusing -지 마세요 with 안 or 못

Error: 저는 오늘 안 가요. (when the intended meaning is telling someone else not to come)

Correction: 오지 마세요. — -지 마세요 is a polite instruction directed at the listener, while 안 marks the speaker's own negation.

Common Mistake — Missing 야 in the obligation pattern

Error: 쉬어 해요.

Correction: 쉬어야 해요. — the obligation pattern requires 야 between the -아/어 form of the stem and 해요.

가족이 있어요Family and Possession/Existence

있어요/없어요, the subject particle 이/가, the connector 하고, and batchim in depth · Speak and Write Korean: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. State that people, animals, and things exist or are present using 있어요.
  2. State that people, animals, and things do not exist or are absent using 없어요.
  3. Say that you have or do not have family members using the possession construction 저는 [family member]이/가 있어요/없어요.
  4. Use the subject particle 이/가 as the standard particle before 있어요 and 없어요.
  5. Link two nouns together using the connector 하고 to mean "and."
  6. Name core family members in Korean using the standard vocabulary for everyday polite conversation.
  7. Recognize and produce Hangeul syllable blocks containing batchim, including common final consonants that appear in family vocabulary.
  8. Ask and answer simple questions about family using 있어요? and 없어요?.

Communication Scenario

The class is now in its third session. The instructor, Kim Jisu, has asked everyone to bring a photo of one or two people they care about — a family member, a close friend, or a pet. Around the room, students share their photos with a partner. Layla shows a photo of her older sister and her dog. Tom pulls up a picture of his parents on his phone. Nurul describes her large family from memory.

  1. In Chapter 1 you introduced yourself by name. In Chapter 2 you described who people are and what role they hold. This chapter asks a different kind of question: not "Who is that person?" but "Do you have family? Who is there in your life?" Answering this question requires a new Korean predicate — the existential — and a new particle choice. These tools let you say what exists or what someone possesses, which is an essential everyday function.
  2. By the end of this session you will be able to say "I have an older sister and a younger brother," "There is no cat — there is a dog," and "Do you have family in Korea?" You will also read and write Hangeul words with batchim more confidently, since family vocabulary in Korean is full of syllable blocks with final consonants.

Korean sibling terms encode both the speaker's gender and the relative age of the sibling. A female speaker calls her older sister 언니 and her older brother 오빠; a male speaker uses 누나 for an older sister and 형 for an older brother. Younger siblings of either gender are called 동생, or specifically 남동생 (younger brother) or 여동생 (younger sister). These terms reflect the speaker's position in the family, not just the sibling's role.

한글 — Writing System

Hangeul — Batchim in Depth

Chapters 1 and 2 introduced batchim as a concept. This chapter develops it systematically, because every major word in today's vocabulary — family terms, key predicates, and the chapter's core constructions — contains final consonants. Being able to read and write batchim accurately is not a bonus skill; it is a basic requirement for Korean literacy.

받침이란? — What is batchim?

Every Korean syllable block contains at least an initial consonant and a vowel. Some blocks also contain a final consonant — called batchim (받침), literally "support" or "base." The batchim sits below the initial consonant and vowel in the block. Without batchim, the block is open (CV). With batchim, the block is closed (CVC).

Syllable Block Anatomy
Open block: [initial consonant] + [vowel]  →  가 (ga), 나 (na), 저 (jeo)
Closed block: [initial consonant] + [vowel] + [batchim] → CVC shape
한 (han)   국 (guk)   형 (hyeong)   딸 (ttal)
Each syllable ends with a consonant that sits beneath the vowel.

Script Note — Liaison: what happens when batchim meets a vowel

When a syllable block ending in batchim is followed by a syllable beginning with the silent ㅇ (used as a placeholder in vowel-initial syllables), the batchim consonant "moves" to that next syllable. This is called liaison or resyllabification. The spelling does not change; only the pronunciation shifts.

  • 있어요 is spelled 있 + 어요 but pronounced i-sseo-yo (three syllables: 이 + 써 + 요) — the ㅆ shifts forward.
  • 형이 (subject form of 형) is spelled 형 + 이 but pronounced hyeong-i (two syllables) — the ㅇ batchim /ŋ/ stays in place and the particle 이 follows cleanly.
  • 딸이 (subject form of 딸) is pronounced tta-ri — the ㄹ batchim moves into the vowel-initial particle syllable.

Liaison applies to particles, endings, and any following vowel-initial syllable. Reading Korean accurately depends on recognizing when a consonant belongs to the preceding syllable in spelling but sounds as if it begins the following one.

Script Note — Double batchim: 없어요

The word 없 contains a double batchim: the two letters ㅄ sit together in the final position. When a double batchim appears before a following consonant or at the end of a phrase, only one of the two is pronounced. For ㅄ, the ㅂ is pronounced. Before a vowel-initial syllable (such as the 어 of 없어요), the second consonant of the pair — ㅅ — moves forward and becomes the initial consonant of the next syllable. The result: 없어요 is pronounced eop-sseo-yo. This pattern appears in a small number of very common Korean words. For now, focus on 없어요 as a whole. The double-batchim system will be reviewed in later chapters with additional vocabulary.

Pronunciation

받침의 일곱 가지 소리 — The seven final sounds

Korean has many batchim spelling possibilities, but only seven distinct final sounds. No matter which consonant letter appears in the batchim position, it will be pronounced as one of these seven:

Final soundCommon spellings that produce itExample wordPronunciation
/k/ ㄱ, ㄲ, ㅋ 가족 ga-jok (final ㄱ unreleased)
/n/ ㄴ 남편 nam-pyeon (final ㄴ = nasal /n/)
/t/ ㄷ, ㅅ, ㅆ, ㅈ, ㅊ, ㅌ, ㅎ 있 it (before consonant; before vowel: liaison)
/l/ ㄹ 딸 ttal (final ㄹ = lateral /l/)
/m/ ㅁ 남 nam (final ㅁ = nasal /m/)
/p/ ㅂ, ㅍ, ㅄ (before consonant) 없다 eop-da (final ㅄ → /p/ before consonant)
/ŋ/ ㅇ 형 hyeong (final ㅇ = /ŋ/ as in "sing")
Pronunciation Note — Unreleased final stops

When the final sounds /k/, /t/, and /p/ appear at the end of a phrase or before a consonant-initial syllable, they are not released — the mouth closes into position but the air is not expelled. This is very different from English, where final stops are usually either released with a puff of air or followed by a vowel. The Korean unreleased stop may feel like the sound simply stops, or like a brief silence where the mouth is closed. English speakers sometimes add a vowel sound after these finals, producing something like "gajok-uh" instead of "gajok." Training yourself to stop cleanly at the final consonant is one of the most important pronunciation habits for Korean learners.

강세 없는 한국어 — Korean without word stress

English uses stress to distinguish syllables within a word. Korean does not organize words by stress in the same way. Each syllable receives roughly equal weight, which gives Korean its characteristic even, rhythmic quality. When reading family vocabulary aloud, resist the impulse to stress one syllable over others. Practice 어머니 as three even syllables (eo-meo-ni), 아버지 as three even syllables (a-beo-ji), and 남동생 as four even syllables (nam-dong-saeng).

Grammar Instruction

3.1 있어요 and 없어요 — Existence and possession

Purpose: state that someone or something exists, is present, or is possessed — and deny it.

Korean has a dedicated pair of predicates for existence and possession. 있어요 says that something exists or that someone has something. 없어요 is its negative: something does not exist or is not possessed.

These predicates are not the same as the copula 이에요/예요 from Chapters 1 and 2. The copula identifies or classifies (X is Y). 있어요 and 없어요 describe existence or possession (X exists / I have X).

  • 언니가 있어요. I have an older sister. / There is an older sister. 언니 (vowel-final) + 가 + 있어요
  • 남동생이 없어요. I don't have a younger brother. / There is no younger brother. 남동생 (consonant-final ㅇ) + 이 + 없어요
  • 강아지가 있어요. There is a dog. / I have a dog. 강아지 (vowel-final ㅣ) + 가 + 있어요
  • 고양이가 없어요. There is no cat. / I don't have a cat.
Grammar Note — Topic and possession: 저는 [noun]이/가 있어요

To say what you have or don't have, add a topic-marked phrase before the existence construction. The topic 저는 frames the sentence ("as for me…") and the noun + particle + 있어요/없어요 gives the content.

  • 저는 형이 있어요. — As for me, I have an older brother.
  • 저는 여동생이 없어요. — As for me, I don't have a younger sister.
  • 저는 가족이 있어요. — I have family. / My family exists.

The topic 저는 is often omitted once the context is established. If the conversation makes clear that you are talking about yourself, you can simply say 형이 있어요 or 여동생이 없어요.

3.2 Subject particle 이/가 — Introduction

Purpose: mark the subject or focus of a sentence, especially with 있어요 and 없어요.

You have already seen the topic particle 은/는 from Chapter 2, and the brief appearance of 이/가 before 아니에요. This chapter makes 이/가 a productive system — as the standard particle used with 있어요 and 없어요.

The form of the particle follows the same rule as 이에요/예요 and 은/는: look at the final sound of the preceding noun.

이 — after a consonant

이

Used after any noun ending in a consonant (batchim).

남동생이 있어요.
남동생 ends in ㅇ (consonant) → 이

형이 없어요.
형 ends in ㅇ (consonant) → 이

가 — after a vowel

가

Used after any noun ending in a vowel (no batchim).

언니가 있어요.
언니 ends in ㅣ (vowel) → 가

아내가 있어요.
아내 ends in ㅔ (vowel) → 가

Grammar Note — 은/는 versus 이/가: a first look

Both 은/는 and 이/가 appear on nouns in Korean sentences, and English-speaking learners frequently ask which to use. This is one of the most important distinctions in Korean grammar, and it takes time to develop through practice. For now, learn these foundational patterns:

  • 은/는 marks the topic — what the sentence is about. It often means "as for X…" and can signal contrast. You used it in Chapters 1 and 2 with identity sentences: 저는 학생이에요.
  • 이/가 is the standard particle with 있어요 and 없어요. In this construction, it marks what exists or is possessed.

Do not use 은/는 before 있어요/없어요 in ordinary statements about possession. The pattern is: 저는 (topic) + [noun]이/가 + 있어요/없어요.

3.3 하고 — Connecting nouns

Purpose: link two or more nouns with "and."

하고 connects nouns. It attaches directly to the first noun (with no space), and the second noun follows. 하고 does not change based on the final sound of the preceding noun.

  • 언니하고 남동생이 있어요. I have an older sister and a younger brother. 언니 + 하고 + 남동생 + 이 + 있어요
  • 강아지하고 고양이가 있어요. There is a dog and a cat. / I have a dog and a cat. 강아지 + 하고 + 고양이 + 가 + 있어요
  • 아버지하고 어머니가 있어요. I have a father and a mother. / My father and mother are here.
  • 형하고 누나가 없어요. I don't have an older brother or an older sister. 형 + 하고 + 누나 + 가 + 없어요
Grammar Note — Particle placement with 하고

When 하고 connects two nouns before 있어요/없어요, the particle 이/가 attaches to the last noun in the list. The first noun takes 하고; the particle goes on the final noun.

  • 언니하고 남동생이 있어요. — 남동생 takes 이 (consonant-final).
  • 아버지하고 어머니가 있어요. — 어머니 takes 가 (vowel-final).

하고 itself can also appear on each noun in a longer list, though this sounds more emphatic and is less common in natural everyday speech. At this level, use 하고 once between two nouns.

3.4 있어요? — Asking about existence and possession

Purpose: ask whether something or someone exists or is possessed.

Korean questions in the polite -아요/어요 style are formed simply by raising the intonation at the end of a statement. The word order does not change. A question mark in writing signals the rising intonation.

  • 가족이 있어요? Do you have family? / Is there family? Same structure as the statement — intonation rises at the end
  • 형이 있어요? Do you have an older brother?
  • 강아지가 있어요? Do you have a dog?
  • 네, 있어요. Yes, I do. / Yes, there is.
  • 아니요, 없어요. No, I don't. / No, there isn't.
Language Building Block
저는 [noun A]하고 [noun B]이/가 있어요.
Topic (저는) + connector list (A하고 B) + subject particle + existential predicate
저는 언니하고 남동생이 있어요.
I have an older sister and a younger brother.

Model Dialogue

Setting: The classroom before the lesson begins. Layla and Tom are talking while Nurul looks for her seat. Kim Seongsaengnim has placed family photo cards on each desk as a conversation prompt. Layla has a photo of her sister and her dog on the table.

TOM 레일라 씨, 가족이 있어요? Layla, do you have family?
LAYLA 네, 있어요. 저는 언니가 있어요. 이분이 제 언니예요. Yes, I do. I have an older sister. This person is my older sister.
TOM 아, 반갑네요. 남동생이나 여동생도 있어요? Oh, nice. Do you also have a younger brother or younger sister?
LAYLA 아니요, 없어요. 언니만 있어요. 그리고 강아지도 있어요! No, I don't. I only have my older sister. And I also have a dog!
TOM 강아지요? 귀여워요! 톰 씨는요? A dog? How cute! What about you, Tom?

(레일라 씨가 강아지 사진을 보여줘요. — Layla shows a photo of her dog.)

TOM 저는 부모님이 있어요. 아버지하고 어머니가 계세요. 그리고 형이 있어요. 형은 결혼했어요. I have parents. My father and mother are here. And I have an older brother. My older brother is married.
LAYLA 형이 있어요? 좋겠네요. 저는 오빠가 없어요. You have an older brother? That must be nice. I don't have an older brother.
NURUL 안녕하세요! 저는 가족이 많아요. 부모님하고 오빠하고 남동생이 있어요. 그리고 고양이도 있어요. Hello! I have a lot of family. I have parents, an older brother, and a younger brother. And I also have a cat.
TOM 와, 가족이 많네요! Wow, you have a big family!
KIM SEONGSAENGNIM 자, 여러분! 가족이 있어요? 가족이 몇 명이에요? Now, everyone! Do you have family? How many people are in your family?
NURUL 저는 다섯 명이에요! My family has five people!
TOM 저는 네 명이에요. My family has four people.
LAYLA 저는 세 명이에요. 부모님하고 저하고 언니예요! My family has three people — my parents, me, and my older sister!
Dialogue Note — 계세요: an honorific form for 있어요

Tom uses 계세요 when speaking about his parents: 아버지하고 어머니가 계세요. This is the honorific equivalent of 있어요 and is used when the subject of the existence sentence is someone the speaker respects — typically a senior family member, teacher, or other person deserving of honorification. 계세요 is for recognition only at this stage. In your own sentences, use 있어요 as the standard polite form. The honorific system will be developed fully in later chapters.

Writing Workshop

This chapter's writing tasks develop the ability to write a family introduction using the structures and vocabulary of this chapter.

  1. Plan your family introduction. Before writing, decide: Who is in your family? Do you have siblings, parents, children, or pets? How many people in total? Which vocabulary from this chapter will you use? If you prefer not to write about your own family, create a fictional family for a character you name.
  2. Write a basic family introduction. Write four to six sentences using the following pattern:
    • Sentence 1: Greeting with 안녕하세요? and name.
    • Sentence 2: Nationality.
    • Sentence 3: At least one thing you have, using [noun]이/가 있어요.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Korean kinship terms encode relationship, not just biology

Korean sibling terms are not simply labels for biological siblings. They encode the speaker's own gender relative to the sibling's age, which means the same sibling has a different name depending on who is speaking. A woman calls her older brother 오빠; a man calls his older brother 형. The same biological brother carries two different Korean names depending on the speaker's gender. This system reflects a broader characteristic of Korean kinship language: the terms locate the speaker within a relational structure rather than simply describing the other person in isolation.

In everyday speech among younger Koreans in peer settings, these terms extend beyond biological family. 오빠 and 언니 may be used to address close older friends. 형 and 누나 function similarly among men. These extended uses reflect social closeness and familiarity rather than literal kinship. This is receptive cultural information at this stage — your productive vocabulary should focus on using the terms accurately for actual family members in everyday polite conversation.

Culture in Context — Talking about family in Korean conversation

Questions about family come up naturally and often in Korean social conversation, including in early acquaintance. Asking 가족이 있어요? ("Do you have family?") or 형제가 있어요? ("Do you have siblings?") is a common way of getting to know someone. This openness about family information reflects conversational norms in many Korean-speaking contexts where family background, household composition, and relationship status are considered appropriate early-conversation topics.

How you answer is up to you. Learners should feel free to give simplified or fictional answers in practice tasks if they prefer not to share personal family details. What matters for language development is that you can ask and answer these questions with accurate, natural Korean.

Regional and Diaspora Note — Family terms across Korean-speaking communities

The family terms taught in this chapter represent the contemporary South Korean standard. Diaspora communities and heritage speakers may use alternative terms for some relationships, or may mix Korean and the local language's kinship vocabulary. In some diaspora contexts, 아빠 and 엄마 are used alongside or instead of 아버지 and 어머니 even in semi-formal contexts, reflecting the informalization of address norms over generations and across communities. This course uses 아버지 and 어머니 as the standard polite forms, while 아빠 and 엄마 are labeled as informal. Neither is incorrect — their appropriateness depends on context, relationship, and community.

Culture in Context — 계세요: honoring elders in language

Tom's use of 계세요 in the model dialogue is worth brief cultural attention. Korean marks social relationships not only through address terms but through the predicates themselves. The choice between 있어요 (neutral polite) and 계세요 (honorific) when speaking about someone's existence or presence encodes respect for the person being referred to. Using 있어요 about one's own parents in a conversation with a peer is not rude, but speakers who want to signal proper respect for their parents will often use 계세요. This is receptive cultural awareness for now — your productive grammar remains 있어요 and 없어요. The honorific system, including 계세요, becomes productive in later chapters.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using 은/는 instead of 이/가 before 있어요/없어요

Error: 저는 언니는 있어요.

Correction: 저는 언니가 있어요. — The topic particle 은/는 marks what the sentence is about. The particle immediately before 있어요/없어요 is 이/가, which marks what exists or is possessed. The pattern is: topic (저는) + [what is possessed]이/가 + 있어요.

Common Mistake — Wrong particle form (이 vs. 가)

Error: 형가 있어요. (형 ends in consonant ㅇ)

Correction: 형이 있어요. — Apply the same rule you use for 은/는 and for the copula: consonant-final nouns take 이; vowel-final nouns take 가. 형 ends in the consonant ㅇ (pronounced /ŋ/), so the particle is 이.

Common Mistake — Adding a space before 하고

Error: 언니 하고 남동생이 있어요. (space before 하고)

Correction: 언니하고 남동생이 있어요. — 하고 attaches directly to the preceding noun with no space. It behaves like a particle in this respect. Writing a space before 하고 is a very common error among beginners and is incorrect in standard Korean spacing.

Common Mistake — Putting 이/가 on the wrong noun in a 하고 list

Error: 언니가 하고 남동생이 있어요. (particle on the first noun)

Correction: 언니하고 남동생이 있어요. — When two nouns are connected with 하고 before 있어요/없어요, the particle goes on the last noun in the list. The first noun simply takes 하고.

Common Mistake — Using the wrong sibling term for your gender

Risk: A female speaker says 형이 있어요 to mean "I have an older brother."

Guidance: 형 is used by male speakers to refer to an older brother. A female speaker uses 오빠 for her older brother. Similarly, 누나 (older sister, male speaker) and 언니 (older sister, female speaker) must be matched to the speaker's gender. At this stage, the key pairs to learn are: female speaker = 오빠 (older brother), 언니 (older sister); male speaker = 형 (older brother), 누나 (older sister).

Chapter Review — Section B

  1. 형: batchim ㅇ → /ŋ/; 딸: batchim ㄹ → /l/; 남편: 남 batchim ㅁ → /m/, 편 batchim ㄴ → /n/; 가족: batchim ㄱ → /k/ (unreleased); 있어요: batchim ㅆ → /t/ in isolation, but liaison applies before 어요 → sounds as 이써요; 없어요: double batchim ㅄ → /p/ before consonant, liaison before 어요 → 업써요; 아들: batchim ㄹ → /l/.

  3. (a) 형하고 남동생이 있어요. (b) 어머니하고 아버지가 있어요. (c) 고양이하고 강아지가 있어요.
  4. Liaison is the process by which a batchim consonant moves to the next syllable when that syllable begins with the silent ㅇ (a vowel-initial syllable). 있어요 is written 있 + 어요, but the ㅆ moves into the following syllable because 어 begins with silent ㅇ. It is pronounced 이써요 (three syllables: 이 + 써 + 요).

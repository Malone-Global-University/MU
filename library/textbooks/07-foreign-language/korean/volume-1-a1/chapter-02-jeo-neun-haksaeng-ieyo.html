저는 학생이에요Identity and Classroom Roles

Topic particles, the negative copula, demonstratives, and complete basic Hangeul · Speak and Write Korean: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify yourself and others using 은/는 and the copula 이에요/예요 with nouns for roles and nationalities.
  2. Deny or correct identity statements using the negative copula 아니에요.
  3. Use the demonstratives 이, 그, and 저 to point to nearby, mid-distance, and far people or things.
  4. Refer to people with 이분, 그분, and 저분 as polite third-person forms.
  5. Build basic noun phrases by combining a demonstrative, a noun, and a topic or subject particle.
  6. Use the address title 씨 correctly when referring to or addressing another person.
  7. Complete the basic Hangeul consonant set, including the remaining lax, tense, and aspirated consonants.
  8. Read and write short sentences using the full set of basic Hangeul letters introduced so far.

Communication Scenario

It is the first full session of the Korean language course. The instructor, Kim Jisu, has written a name tag system on the board: each person's name followed by their role — student, teacher, or guest — in Korean. Students are now introducing themselves and pointing out their classmates to each other.

  1. If someone asked you "Who is that person?" while pointing across the room, what information would you need to give? In Chapter 1 you learned to say your own name. This chapter gives you the tools to introduce and identify other people, to point toward things and people at a distance, and to correct a mistaken assumption about identity.
  2. By the end of today's session, you will be able to say not only "I am a student" but also "That person is the teacher," "This is not a book — it's a notebook," and "Layla is not Korean; she is Egyptian." These identity and classification statements are among the most frequently used building blocks in Korean conversation.

씨 follows the name it attaches to, with a space: 레일라 씨, 김민수 씨. It is an everyday polite title — respectful without being overly formal — and suits most peer or semi-formal situations. Avoid using it with professional titles (선생님, 교수님) or with one's own name.

Script Note — Tense consonants are doubled in writing, not in sound length

Tense consonants such as ㄲ, ㄸ, ㅃ, ㅆ, and ㅉ are written by doubling the base letter. This doubling is a spelling convention, not a signal that the sound is held for twice as long. The actual phonetic difference between lax, tense, and aspirated consonants involves muscle tension in the throat, airflow, and voice quality — not duration. The romanizations "kk," "tt," "pp," "ss," "jj" may suggest length to English readers, but they are approximations. Focus on the feeling of tightness in the throat for tense consonants, and on the burst of air for aspirated ones.

Pronunciation

The three-way consonant contrast in full

Korean organizes most obstruent consonants into three-way sets: lax, tense, and aspirated. Chapter 1 introduced this concept. With the new letters in this chapter, the complete picture is:

LaxTenseAspiratedPlace of articulation
ㄱ ㄲ ㅋ velar (back of mouth)
ㄷ ㄸ ㅌ alveolar (tongue tip behind upper teeth)
ㅂ ㅃ ㅍ bilabial (both lips)
ㅈ ㅉ ㅊ palato-alveolar (tongue toward palate)
ㅅ ㅆ — alveolar fricative (no aspirated partner)
Pronunciation Note — Why this contrast matters for meaning

The lax-tense-aspirated distinction creates meaningful contrasts in Korean. The words 달 (moon), 딸 (daughter), and 탈 (mask) are three completely different words whose only phonetic difference is the initial consonant. The same applies to 불 (fire), 뿔 (horn/antler), and 풀 (grass / glue). English-speaking learners hear lax consonants as somewhat voiced (like English "b," "d," "g") and aspirated consonants as voiceless with a puff of air (like English "p," "t," "k"). Tense consonants have no English equivalent: they are produced with a catch in the throat, more muscular tension, and no burst of air. Practice all three within the same session so your ear trains on the contrast, not just the individual sounds.

Batchim in this chapter's vocabulary

Several new words contain batchim (final consonants) that are useful to note:

  • 책 chaek — the batchim is ㄱ; in isolation the final ㄱ is unreleased (you close but do not release the velar stop)
  • 학생 haksaeng — the word has two syllable blocks: 학 (batchim ㄱ) and 생 (batchim ㅇ, pronounced /ŋ/)
  • 가방 gabang — the final ㅇ is /ŋ/ as in "sing"; the first syllable 가 has no batchim
  • 칠판 chilpan — two batchim: ㄹ in 칠 and ㄴ in 판; ㄹ in final position is a lateral /l/ sound

Grammar Instruction

2.1 Topic particle 은/는 — Extended use

Purpose: mark the topic of a wider range of sentences, moving beyond self-introduction.

In Chapter 1, you used 저는 and 제 이름은 as the only topic-marked phrases. This chapter expands topic-marking to any noun: people, objects, and roles.

  • 이분은 선생님이에요. This person is a teacher. 이분 + 은 (consonant-final: 분 ends in ㄴ) + 선생님 + 이에요
  • 이 책은 제 책이에요. This book is my book. 이 책 + 은 (consonant-final: 책 ends in ㄱ)
  • 저는 미국 사람이에요. I am American. 저 + 는 (vowel-final: 저 ends in ㅓ)
  • 의자는 교실에 있어요. The chair is in the classroom. 의자 + 는 (vowel-final: 자 ends in ㅏ)
Grammar Note — 은/는 for contrast

Beyond marking the topic, 은/는 also signals contrast — often implying "but as for X..." This contrastive use will become important when you are denying or correcting a statement. For now, recognize that when two topic-marked phrases appear together, one often implicitly contrasts with the other:

저는 학생이에요. 선생님은 김 선생님이에요.

I am a student. The teacher is Teacher Kim.

The second 은/는 implicitly sets up a contrast: "I am not the teacher — the teacher is someone else."

2.2 아니에요 — The negative copula

Purpose: deny or correct an identity or classification.

To say that something or someone is not something, use 아니에요. It is the negative counterpart of 이에요/예요 and does not change based on the preceding noun.

  • 저는 선생님이 아니에요. 학생이에요. I am not a teacher. I am a student. 선생님 + 이 + 아니에요 — the particle 이/가 appears before 아니에요
  • 이것은 책이 아니에요. 공책이에요. This is not a book. It's a notebook.
  • 저는 미국 사람이 아니에요. 영국 사람이에요. I am not American. I am British.
  • 그분은 학생이 아니에요. That person is not a student.
Grammar Note — The particle before 아니에요

Before 아니에요, the noun that is being negated takes the particle 이 (after a consonant) or 가 (after a vowel). This is the subject/focus particle 이/가 — which you will study fully in Chapter 3. For now, treat the pattern [noun + 이/가 + 아니에요] as the standard way to negate a copular sentence:

  • 선생님이 아니에요 — 선생님 ends in consonant ㅁ → 이 아니에요
  • 의자가 아니에요 — 의자 ends in vowel ㅏ → 가 아니에요

Do not add 이/가 before 이에요/예요. The particle appears only in negated sentences.

2.3 이/그/저 — Demonstratives

Purpose: point to nearby, mid-distance, or distant people and objects.

Korean has a three-way demonstrative system. Each demonstrative is used before a noun as a modifier and reflects the physical or conversational distance of the referent.

이
Near the speaker
this / these
그
Near the listener or mentioned
that / those
저
Far from both parties
that over there
  • 이 가방은 제 가방이에요. This bag is my bag. 이 (near speaker) + 가방 + 은 + 제 + 가방 + 이에요
  • 그 책은 뭐예요? What is that book (by you)?
  • 저 분은 선생님이에요. That person over there is the teacher.
  • 이것은 연필이에요. This is a pencil. 이것 (standalone form of 이 + object) + 은 + 연필 + 이에요
Grammar Note — 저 the demonstrative vs. 저 the pronoun

Korean has two words written and pronounced the same way — 저 — with completely different meanings. One is the polite first-person pronoun "I/me" (Chapter 1). The other is the demonstrative meaning "that over there." Context always makes clear which is intended: 저는 학생이에요 uses 저 as "I," while 저 분은 선생님이에요 uses 저 as the demonstrative modifier "that over there." In speech, the demonstrative is often said with a slight lengthening or with a gesture pointing into the distance, but the word itself is the same. Learners quickly sort these out through context and practice.

2.4 이분/그분/저분 — Polite third-person referentials

Purpose: refer to or introduce a third person with appropriate courtesy.

분 is a respectful word meaning "person." When combined with the demonstratives, it produces polite ways to refer to people without using their names:

  • 이분은 제 친구 레일라 씨예요. This person is my friend Layla. 이분 (this person, polite) + 은 + 제 친구 레일라 씨 + 예요
  • 그분은 한국 사람이에요? Is that person Korean?
  • 저분이 선생님이에요. That person over there is the teacher.
Language Focus

Polite vs. plain forms for "person." The plain noun for person is 사람. It is used in neutral contexts: 이 사람 (this person) is fine among close friends or peers. 이분, 그분, and 저분 use 분 — a more respectful word — and are appropriate when introducing or referring to someone in polite company. When in doubt in early-level Korean, use 이분/그분/저분 rather than 이 사람/그 사람/저 사람 to avoid unintended bluntness.

2.5 뭐예요? — What is it?

Purpose: ask for the identity or name of an object or person.

The question 뭐예요? ("What is it?" / "What is this?") uses the question word 뭐 (what) followed directly by the copula ending 예요?. It is one of the most useful and frequent question frames at this level.

  • 이것이 뭐예요? What is this?
  • 그것이 뭐예요? What is that?
  • 이분이 누구예요? Who is this person? 누구 = who (question word for people)

The question words 뭐 (what) and 누구 (who) appear in the same position in the sentence as the word that would answer the question. There is no movement of question words to the front of the sentence in Korean, as there is in English. This is a fundamental structural difference you will practice throughout the course.

Language Building Block
[Topic]은/는 [Noun]이에요/예요. → [Topic]은/는 [Noun]이/가 아니에요. [Noun]이에요/예요.
Positive identity → Negative correction + Positive restatement
저는 한국 사람이 아니에요. 미국 사람이에요.
I am not Korean. I am American.

Model Dialogue

Setting: The language center classroom, just before the lesson begins. Layla and Tom are seated next to each other. A new student, Nurul, has arrived. The instructor, Kim Jisu, begins to take attendance. There is also a guest lecturer standing at the front of the room.

LAYLA 안녕하세요? 저는 레일라예요. 이분은 제 친구 톰 씨예요. Hello. I am Layla. This person is my friend Tom.
NURUL 안녕하세요, 레일라 씨. 반가워요. 저는 누룰이에요. Hello, Layla. Nice to meet you. I am Nurul.
TOM 누룰 씨, 반가워요. 저는 톰이에요. 미국 사람이에요. Nurul, nice to meet you. I am Tom. I am American.
NURUL 저도 반가워요. 저는 말레이시아 사람이에요. Nice to meet you too. I am Malaysian.

(누룰 씨가 교실 앞을 가리켜요. — Nurul points to the front of the classroom.)

NURUL 저분은 선생님이에요? Is that person over there the teacher?
LAYLA 아니요, 저분은 선생님이 아니에요. 손님이에요. 선생님은 김 선생님이에요. No, that person over there is not the teacher. She is a guest. The teacher is Teacher Kim.

(김 선생님이 교실에 들어오세요. — Kim Seongsaengnim enters the classroom.)

KIM SEONGSAENGNIM 안녕하세요, 여러분! 저는 김지수예요. 선생님이에요. 이분은 박 교수님이에요. 오늘 특별 손님이에요. Hello, everyone! I am Kim Jisu. I am your teacher. This person is Professor Park. Today she is a special guest.
STUDENTS 안녕하세요, 선생님! 안녕하세요, 교수님! Hello, Teacher! Hello, Professor!
KIM SEONGSAENGNIM 자, 이것은 뭐예요? Now, what is this?
TOM 책이에요! It's a book!
KIM SEONGSAENGNIM 맞아요! 이것은 책이에요. 그러면 이것은요? That's right! This is a book. Then what about this?
NURUL 공책이에요! It's a notebook!
KIM SEONGSAENGNIM 네, 맞아요. 공책이에요. 책이 아니에요! Yes, that's right. It's a notebook. Not a book!

내용 이해 — Comprehension questions:

  1. Who introduces Tom to Nurul, and what does she say about him?
  2. What nationality does Nurul say she is?
  3. Who is the special guest, and what title does Kim Seongsaengnim use for her?
  4. How does Layla correct Nurul's mistaken assumption about the guest lecturer? Write the correcting sentence she uses.
  5. In the vocabulary drill at the end, why does Kim Seongsaengnim say 공책이에요. 책이 아니에요! rather than 책이 아니에요. 공책이에요? Does the order matter? What is the difference in emphasis?
Grammar Note — 여러분: addressing a group

Kim Seongsaengnim opens the lesson with 여러분, which means "everyone" or "all of you." It is a natural, polite way to address a group in Korean, used in classroom, public, and presentational contexts. It is a fixed formula for now — you will see the internal structure more clearly in later chapters when you study plural reference and address conventions in depth.

Writing Workshop

체크리스트 — Editing checklist:

  • Every Hangeul syllable block has both a consonant and a vowel in the correct positions.
  • I chose 이에요 or 예요 correctly based on the final sound of each noun.
  • In negative sentences, I used [noun + 이/가 + 아니에요] — not 아니에요 directly after the noun alone.
  • I chose 이 (consonant-final) or 가 (vowel-final) correctly before 아니에요.
  • I attached the topic particle 은/는 directly to the noun with no space.
  • I used 저 / 제 (not 나 / 내) for polite first-person reference.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Titles and roles in Korean address

Korean often reflects a person's role or position through the words used to address or refer to them. When Kim Jisu is introduced as the teacher, the students' greeting shifts from 안녕하세요? to 안녕하세요, 선생님! — the title replaces, or is added to, the greeting. This reflects a broader feature of Korean social language: role-marking words such as 선생님, 교수님, 사장님 (company president), and 의사 선생님 (doctor) are used both as nouns ("a teacher") and as address forms ("Teacher, ...").

The suffix 님 signals that the role carries social or professional respect. It appears on many common titles and is absent from informal or peer-level references. You do not add 님 to your own title — it is directed outward, toward the person you are addressing or describing respectfully.

Culture in Context — 씨: a versatile everyday courtesy title

The title 씨 follows a person's name with a space and is one of the most useful address conventions for adult learners. It is roughly equivalent to "Mr." or "Ms." in formality but is neither gender-specific nor excessively formal — it fits peer-level polite interaction comfortably. 씨 is typically added to either a full name (레일라 아마드 씨) or a given name (레일라 씨), but not to a family name alone and never to one's own name. Using 씨 with a professional title such as 선생님 is inappropriate — the title itself already conveys the necessary respect. In professional or institutional settings, people are often addressed by their job title rather than by 씨.

Culture in Context — Nationality and identity in Korean conversation

In Korean, nationality is often expressed through the pattern [country name] + 사람. This is a neutral descriptive formula and carries no cultural loading in itself. Learners should be aware, however, that questions about nationality, background, and home country come up naturally and early in Korean social interaction — particularly with new acquaintances. It is common and considered friendly to ask 어느 나라 사람이에요? ("Which country are you from?") in a getting-to-know-you context. This kind of directness reflects common conversational norms in many Korean-speaking contexts, though individual preferences vary. Knowing how to answer these questions confidently, clearly, and in polite Korean is a practical early skill.

Regional and Diaspora Note — Korean-speaking communities worldwide

The students in today's dialogue include learners from Malaysia, Egypt, and the United States — a reflection of the genuine diversity of people who learn and use Korean around the world. It is worth noting that Korean is not only spoken in South Korea and North Korea: there are significant communities of Korean speakers in China (the Joseonjok community), Central Asia (Korean Koryo-saram communities), Japan (Zainichi Korean communities), the United States, Canada, Australia, and many other countries. The histories, varieties, and community contexts of these diaspora communities differ considerably. This course teaches contemporary South Korean Standard Korean as the shared productive foundation, while recognizing and respecting the breadth of global Korean-speaking life.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Omitting 이/가 before 아니에요

Error: 저는 선생님 아니에요. (no particle before 아니에요)

Correction: 저는 선생님이 아니에요. — The negative copula 아니에요 requires the particle 이 or 가 on the noun immediately before it. 선생님 ends in consonant ㅁ, so the particle is 이. Missing this particle is one of the most common early errors in Korean.

Common Mistake — Using 가 instead of 이 (or vice versa) before 아니에요

Error: 이것은 책가 아니에요. (책 ends in consonant ㄱ but 가 is used)

Correction: 이것은 책이 아니에요. — After a consonant-final noun, the particle is 이. After a vowel-final noun, it is 가. Apply the same vowel-harmony logic you use for 은/는 and for 이에요/예요.

Common Mistake — Confusing 저 the pronoun with 저 the demonstrative

Confusion: A learner writes 저 가방 meaning "my bag" when they intended the polite first-person meaning.

Clarification: 저 가방 means "that bag over there" (demonstrative + noun). "My bag" in polite Korean is 제 가방, using 제 (the possessive form of 저, the pronoun). The polite pronoun "I" is 저 used as subject or topic (저는), while the possessive is 제 (제 책, 제 친구). The demonstrative 저 only appears directly before a noun: 저 분, 저 가방.

Common Mistake — Using 이 사람/그 사람 when 이분/그분 is more appropriate

Risk: Using 이 사람은 선생님이에요 when addressing or discussing someone present in a polite context.

Guidance: 이 사람 is grammatically correct but may sound abrupt or even slightly rude in formal or semi-formal situations. Use 이분은 선생님이에요 when introducing or referring to someone present in polite company, especially someone senior or unfamiliar. Among close peers, 이 사람 is natural; in classroom or professional settings, 이분 is the safer choice.

Common Mistake — Attaching 씨 to one's own name

Error: 저는 레일라 씨예요. (using 씨 in self-introduction)

Correction: 저는 레일라예요. — 씨 is a courtesy title used when addressing or referring to another person. It is never used by a speaker about themselves, just as an English speaker would not say "I am Ms. Layla" in most everyday contexts.

안녕하세요? 처음 만나요First Meetings

Greetings, names, and your first Hangeul · Speak and Write Korean: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Greet someone and take leave using common everyday polite Korean expressions.
  2. Introduce yourself using 저는 [name]이에요/예요 in a polite, everyday context.
  3. Ask and answer the question "What is your name?" using 이름이 뭐예요?
  4. Use 저 and 제 correctly as the polite first-person forms.
  5. Use basic courtesy expressions, including thank you, sorry, and you're welcome.
  6. Recognize and name the basic components of Hangeul: vowels and consonants as individual letters (jamo), and their combination into syllable blocks.
  7. Read and write the five simple Korean vowels and the first set of Korean consonants.
  8. Identify how Korean syllable blocks are structured.

Communication Scenario

You have just arrived for the first session of a Korean language course at a community language center. Before the class starts, the instructor has asked everyone to greet and introduce themselves to at least two other participants — in Korean. Some people seem as new to the language as you are. The instructor will also introduce herself.

  1. Think about the last time you met a stranger in a slightly formal context, such as starting a new class or attending a workshop. What did you say first? Was the opening word a greeting or your name?
  2. In Korean, there is one everyday polite greeting that fits almost any situation where you are meeting someone for the first time: 안녕하세요? By the end of this chapter, you will say it naturally, introduce yourself, and begin reading and writing in Hangeul — the Korean writing system you will use from this chapter forward.

한글 — Writing System

Hangeul

Korean is written in Hangeul (한글), a phonetic alphabet invented in 1443 under King Sejong. You will read and write exclusively in Hangeul throughout this course. Romanization — the practice of representing Korean sounds in the Roman alphabet — may appear temporarily to support pronunciation, but it will never replace Hangeul as your reading and writing system.

Script Note — Hangeul is not a syllabary; it is an alphabet arranged into syllable blocks

Unlike Chinese characters or Japanese hiragana, Hangeul letters represent individual sounds — vowels and consonants — exactly as a Roman-alphabet language does. However, Korean letters are not written in a row from left to right one letter at a time. Instead, they are packaged together into syllable blocks: each block is one syllable, and a block normally contains two or three letters arranged into a compact square unit.

This means the word 한국어 (Korean language) is written as three syllable blocks — 한, 국, 어 — each of which is made up of individual Hangeul letters. Understanding this structure is the key to reading and writing Korean.

음절 블록 — How syllable blocks are built

Every Korean syllable is written as a compact block. The block always has at least one consonant and one vowel. When a syllable begins with a vowel sound, the consonant ㅇ serves as a silent placeholder in the initial position.

Script Note — The silent ㅇ in initial position

The consonant ㅇ has two jobs in Korean. When it appears at the beginning of a syllable block (in the initial consonant position), it is silent — it simply marks that the syllable starts with a vowel sound, since no Korean syllable block can begin with a vowel letter alone. When ㅇ appears at the end of a syllable block (as the final consonant, called the batchim), it is pronounced as the /ŋ/ sound, like the "ng" at the end of the English word "song." Both uses of ㅇ appear in the very first word you are learning: 안녕하세요.

Answer: 안녕하세요 contains five syllable blocks: 안 · 녕 · 하 · 세 · 요. The blocks 안 and 녕 each have a final consonant (batchim); the remaining blocks do not.

Pronunciation

Korean vowels — five stable sounds

The five simple vowels introduced in this chapter each have a steady, pure quality. Unlike many English vowels, Korean vowels do not glide or shift in quality as they are held. Practice each one as a single, stable sound.

  • ㅏ /a/ — similar to the "a" in "father," but short and pure; lips open wide아 — ah
  • ㅓ /ʌ/ — a sound with no close English equivalent; the mouth is slightly open, the tongue mid-low and central; romanized as "eo" but not pronounced like the letters "e" and "o" in English어 — a sound between "uh" and "aw"
  • ㅗ /o/ — similar to the "o" in "more," but without the glide at the end; lips rounded오 — oh (pure, no glide)
  • ㅜ /u/ — similar to "oo" in "moon," but with less lip-rounding than English우 — oo
  • ㅡ /ɯ/ — no equivalent in English; a back vowel made with the tongue pulled back and lips unrounded; romanized "eu"으 — a flat, unrounded back vowel
  • ㅣ /i/ — similar to the "ee" in "see," but shorter이 — ee
Pronunciation Note — ㅓ and ㅡ are frequently mispronounced

Two Korean vowels cause consistent trouble for English speakers. The vowel ㅓ is romanized as "eo," which leads learners to pronounce it as two separate sounds — "e" plus "o." It is actually a single vowel, roughly in the range between English "uh" (as in "cup") and "aw" (as in "law"), with neither glide nor transition. The vowel ㅡ is romanized as "eu," but again this is a single sound: an unrounded back vowel with no English equivalent. Placing a pencil lightly between your teeth and making an "ee" sound while gradually pulling your tongue back will get you closer to ㅡ. Practice both vowels in isolation before using them in words.

The first consonants

Several consonants in this chapter behave predictably for English speakers:

  • ㄴ — like English "n"나 — na
  • ㅁ — like English "m"마 — ma
  • ㅎ — like English "h," but the sound sometimes weakens or disappears between vowels in natural speech하 — ha

Three consonants require more care:

  • ㄹ — this consonant has no direct English equivalent; at the beginning of a syllable it is produced with a light flap of the tongue tip, similar to the "r" in Spanish para; at the end of a syllable it becomes a lateral /l/ sound. In English approximation, beginners may hear it as something between "r" and "l."리을 — listen for the flap, not a rolling "r" or hard "l"
  • ㅇ (final) — at the end of a syllable block, this is the /ŋ/ sound, like "ng" in "song"앙 — a + ng
  • ㅇ (initial) — silent; simply holds the initial consonant position when the syllable begins with a vowel sound아 — the ㅇ is not pronounced
Pronunciation Note — Sentence-final intonation in greetings

안녕하세요? is written with a question mark, and it can be said with a slight rise in pitch at the end — making it feel like a genuine inquiry about the other person's wellbeing. However, it is equally natural to say it with a falling or level intonation as a simple greeting. Both are correct. The question mark in writing signals the greeting's origin as a courtesy question ("Are you at peace?"), not a strict requirement to raise your voice at the end.

Grammar Instruction

1.1 저는 [이름]이에요/예요 — Introducing yourself

Purpose: state your name using a complete, polite everyday sentence.

The pattern 저는 [이름]이에요/예요 combines three parts: the topic 저 (polite "I"), the topic particle 는, and the copular predicate 이에요 or 예요 attached after your name.

  • 저는 사라예요. Jeoneun Sarajeyo. — I am Sara. [topic] 저 + 는 + [name] 사라 + [copula] 예요
  • 저는 유수프예요. Jeoneun Yusupeyeyo. — I am Yusuf.
  • 저는 민준이에요. Jeoneun Minjunieyo. — I am Minjun. [topic] 저 + 는 + [name] 민준 + [copula] 이에요
Grammar Note — 이에요 versus 예요: which ending to use

The copula has two forms in everyday polite Korean, and the choice depends entirely on the final sound of the noun it follows.

  • If the preceding syllable ends in a consonant (has a batchim), use 이에요: 민준 ends in ㄴ, so → 민준이에요.
  • If the preceding syllable ends in a vowel (no batchim), use 예요: 사라 ends in ㅏ, so → 사라예요.

This is not an irregular rule — it is one of the most systematic patterns in Korean. Many endings in Korean change their shape depending on whether the preceding syllable ends in a consonant or a vowel. You will encounter this pattern throughout the course.

1.2 저 and 제 — Polite first-person forms

Purpose: refer to yourself appropriately in polite Korean.

Korean distinguishes two registers of first-person reference. In everyday polite Korean — the style you are learning — use 저 as the subject/topic form and 제 as the possessive form.

  • 저는 학생이에요. I am a student. 저 = polite "I" as topic
  • 제 이름은 사라예요. My name is Sara. 제 = polite "my"
Grammar Note — 저 and 나: register matters from day one

Korean has two sets of first-person forms: 저/제 (polite) and 나/내 (plain/casual). In this course, you will use 저/제 as your default, because the productive style you are building is everyday polite Korean. The plain forms 나/내 are used among close friends, in casual situations, and in speech directed to people younger than oneself. They will be introduced with their full social context at the appropriate stage. For now, 저/제 is always the safe, appropriate choice.

1.3 Topic particle 는/은 — Marking what you are talking about

Purpose: mark the topic of a sentence.

Korean marks the topic of a sentence with the particle 은 or 는. Like the copula endings above, the choice between them depends on whether the topic noun ends in a consonant or a vowel:

  • 저는 사라예요. I am Sara. 저 ends in vowel (ㅓ) → 는
  • 제 이름은 민준이에요. My name is Minjun. 이름 ends in consonant (ㅁ) → 은
Grammar Note — 은/는 is a topic marker, not simply a subject marker

The particle 은/는 is often introduced in textbooks as "the subject marker," but this description is inaccurate and will cause confusion in later chapters. 은/는 marks the topic of the sentence — the thing you are making a statement about — which is a different grammatical role from the subject. Topics can be subjects, objects, or other elements, depending on context. Korean has a separate particle for marking subjects, which you will study in Chapter 3. For now, focus on using 저는 and 제 이름은 as topic-marked frames for self-introduction.

Language Focus

Particles attach to nouns. In Korean, grammatical particles like 은/는 attach directly to the end of the noun — there is no space between the noun and its particle. So it is always written 저는, not 저 는. This is one of the fundamental spacing rules in Korean writing, and it applies to all particles throughout the language. Getting this habit right from the very start will serve you well across the entire course.

Model Dialogue

Setting: The entrance of a community language center. First session of a Korean course. Layla, an Egyptian student, meets Tom, an American student. Their instructor, Kim Seongsaengnim, then greets them both.

LAYLA 안녕하세요? 저는 레일라예요. Hello. I am Layla.
TOM 안녕하세요. 저는 톰이에요. 처음 만나요. Hello. I am Tom. Nice to meet you (for the first time).
LAYLA 반가워요, 톰 씨. Nice to meet you, Tom.
TOM 저도 반가워요. 이름이 뭐예요? Nice to meet you too. What is your name?
LAYLA 저는 레일라예요. I am Layla.

(김 선생님이 오세요. — Kim Seongsaengnim arrives.)

KIM SEONGSAENGNIM 안녕하세요? 저는 김지수예요. 선생님이에요. Hello. I am Kim Jisu. I am the teacher.
LAYLA & TOM 안녕하세요, 선생님! 처음 만나요. Hello, teacher! Nice to meet you.
KIM SEONGSAENGNIM 반가워요. 이름이 뭐예요? Nice to meet you. What is your name?
LAYLA 저는 레일라예요. I am Layla.
KIM SEONGSAENGNIM 레일라 씨, 반가워요. 그럼, 교실에서 봐요. Layla, nice to meet you. Well then, see you in the classroom.
LAYLA & TOM 네, 선생님. 또 봐요. Yes, teacher. See you again.

내용 이해 — Comprehension questions:

  1. What is the teacher's name? What two things does she tell the students about herself?
  2. How does Tom signal warmth when meeting Layla the first time? Which expression does he use?
  3. When Layla addresses Tom directly, she uses 톰 씨. What do you think 씨 might mean? (You will study it formally in the next chapter.)
  4. In the farewell exchange, what does 또 봐요 mean? Which character uses it?
  5. The instructor says 그럼, 교실에서 봐요. Which part of this sentence can you guess from context, even if you have not studied all the words?
Grammar Note — 저도: adding 도 to mean "too" or "also"

Tom says 저도 반가워요 — "I am also glad to meet you" or "Nice to meet you too." The particle 도 attaches to a noun and means "also" or "too." It replaces, rather than joins, other particles such as 는. So where 저는 means "I (as topic)," 저도 means "I too." You will study 도 systematically in Chapter 7; for now, recognize it as a useful and very common particle.

Reading Note — 선생님 as a respectful title

선생님 literally means "teacher" and functions as a respectful title in Korean, used for teachers, certain medical professionals, and other figures of expertise or authority. When addressing an instructor, students typically say 선생님 — with or without the family name — rather than using the instructor's given name. Like all Korean titles, 선생님 is never used by the teacher about herself in self-introduction: Kim Jisu would say 저는 김지수예요, not a form that attaches 선생님 to her own name.

Writing Workshop

체크리스트 — Editing checklist:

  • I wrote Hangeul syllable blocks correctly — vowels in the correct position relative to consonants.
  • I attached the topic particle 은/는 directly to the noun with no space.
  • I chose 이에요 or 예요 correctly based on whether the name ends in a consonant or vowel.
  • I used 저 (not 나) as the polite first-person subject.
  • I used 제 (not 내) when meaning "my."
  • I used an appropriate greeting at the start of my introduction.
  • I included a first-meeting expression to close my introduction.
  • I ended each sentence with a period (.) or question mark (?) as appropriate.

Move around the room and introduce yourself to four different people, as if you had all just arrived at the language center for the first time. For each person:

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — 안녕하세요? is a question that became a greeting

안녕하세요? is built from the word 안녕, which means "peace," "wellbeing," or "safety," and the formal verb ending that turns it into a polite question: "Are you at peace?" or "Are you well?" Over time it became the standard everyday polite greeting for virtually any situation — morning, afternoon, or evening; first meeting or daily encounter; formal or casual context. Unlike the Japanese greeting はじめまして, which is reserved for first meetings only, 안녕하세요? can be used every time you see someone.

The word 안녕 on its own also appears in farewell expressions (안녕히 가세요, 안녕히 계세요), and among close friends and family members, simply saying 안녕! — without the polite ending — functions as a casual "hi" or "bye." You will see this distinction more clearly once you study speech levels later in the course.

Culture in Context — Names and family names in Korean

Korean names are typically written and spoken family name first, given name second: in the name 김지수, 김 is the family name and 지수 is the given name. In everyday spoken Korean, people are often addressed by their full name plus a courtesy title, by their family name plus a title, or — in close relationships — by their given name with an appropriate level marker. Using only a given name without any marker is common among close friends and between people with a clear age or seniority relationship, but it is not the appropriate default for new acquaintances. Titles and address terms are an important system in Korean that this course will develop gradually.

Culture in Context — A diverse world of Korean speakers

Korean is spoken by people in South Korea, North Korea, and diaspora communities across East Asia, the Americas, Europe, Central Asia, and beyond. The speakers in today's listening and dialogue sections come from a variety of backgrounds — reflecting the genuine diversity of people who learn, use, and are connected to Korean around the world. This course teaches contemporary Standard Korean as used in South Korea as the productive foundation, but Korean-speaking communities are varied in their histories, regional backgrounds, and language practices. The course will introduce you to that variation respectfully and with clear labeling as you progress.

Regional Variation — The word 안녕 across varieties

This course teaches contemporary Standard Korean as used in South Korea. While 안녕하세요? is understood and used across all Korean-speaking contexts, speakers from different regions of South Korea may have distinct intonation patterns, vocabulary choices, and even greeting conventions in casual speech. The Gyeongsang region (in the southeast), the Jeolla region (in the southwest), and the Jeju island variety all have recognizable spoken characteristics. These regional differences will appear in the course as receptive and cultural content, clearly labeled, as your proficiency develops. The productive standard you are building now will serve as a shared foundation understood across all these communities.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using 이에요 after a vowel-final name

Error: 저는 사라이에요. (using 이에요 after 사라, which ends in a vowel)

Correction: 저는 사라예요. — After a syllable ending in a vowel, use 예요, not 이에요. The rule is consistent: consonant-final → 이에요; vowel-final → 예요.

Common Mistake — Writing a space between a noun and its particle

Error: 저 는 톰이에요. (space between 저 and 는)

Correction: 저는 톰이에요. — In Korean, particles attach directly to the noun they follow with no space. This is one of the core spacing rules of Korean and applies to all particles throughout the language.

Common Mistake — Using 나 instead of 저 in polite speech

Error: 나는 사라예요. (using the casual 나 in an everyday polite introduction)

Correction: 저는 사라예요. — The polite first-person form is 저. Using 나 in a polite context — especially with a teacher or new acquaintance — sounds abrupt and inappropriately casual in Korean.

Common Mistake — Placing a vowel letter without a consonant in initial position

Error: Writing a vowel such as ㅏ alone without the silent ㅇ as the initial consonant placeholder

Correction: Every Korean syllable block must have a letter in the initial consonant position. When a syllable begins with a vowel sound, write the silent ㅇ in initial position: 아, not the bare jamo ㅏ. A bare vowel jamo written alone is not a valid syllable block in Korean.

Common Mistake — Treating ㄹ like English "r" or "l"

Error: Pronouncing 레일라 with an English rolling "r" at the start of 레, or with a hard English "l."

Correction: The Korean consonant ㄹ in syllable-initial position is a light flap of the tongue tip — closer to the "r" sound in Spanish para than to English "r" or "l." Avoid adding friction or rolling the sound. Your tongue taps once, briefly, just behind the upper front teeth.

Dialogue — Comprehension

  1. The teacher's name is Kim Jisu (김지수). She tells them her name and that she is the teacher (선생님이에요).
  2. Tom uses 처음 만나요 — the expression for meeting someone for the very first time.
  3. 씨 is a courtesy title attached after a name, used when addressing or referring to someone politely. It will be studied fully in Chapter 2.
  4. 또 봐요 means "see you again." Layla and Tom use it together at the end of the exchange.
  5. The phrase 교실에서 봐요 can be guessed from context as "see you in the classroom" — learners may recognize 봐요 from 또 봐요 and connect 교실 with the classroom setting.

Chapter Review — Section A

  1. 안녕하세요? · 처음 만나요 · 반가워요 · 안녕히 가세요 · 안녕히 계세요 · 또 봐요 · 고마워요 · 천만에요
  2. 처음 만나요 specifically signals a first meeting; 반가워요 expresses warmth and gladness at the encounter and can be used more broadly. The two are often said together at first meetings.
  3. 저 is the polite "I" used as subject or topic (example: 저는 학생이에요); 제 is the polite "my" (example: 제 이름은 사라예요).
  4. 이름이 뭐예요?
  5. 다시 한 번 해 주세요 (please repeat) and 천천히 말해 주세요 (please speak slowly).

Chapter Review — Section B

  1. 아 이 우 오 으 이 → as syllable blocks: 아 이 우 오 으 이
  2. A syllable block is a compact written unit in Korean containing at least one consonant and one vowel. Every block must have a letter in the initial consonant position because Korean syllable blocks cannot begin with a bare vowel letter; ㅇ serves as the silent placeholder when the syllable begins with a vowel sound.
  3. (a) 저는 나타샤예요. (샤 ends in vowel) (b) 저는 민준이에요. (준 ends in consonant) (c) 이분은 선생님이에요. (님 ends in consonant)
  4. 은/는 marks the topic of a sentence — the thing being commented on — which may or may not be the grammatical subject; Korean has a separate particle for marking subjects.
  5. (a) → 저는 레일라예요. (b) → 저는 톰이에요. (no space between noun and particle)

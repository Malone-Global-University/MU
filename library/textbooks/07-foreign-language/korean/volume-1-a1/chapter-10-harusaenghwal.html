Chapter Objectives

This is an assessment chapter. No new productive grammar is introduced. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Combine identity, family, date, food, routine, location, and shopping language from Chapters 1 through 9 in a single connected exchange.
  2. Read a connected multi-part passage describing a person's day, using only systems activated through Chapter 9.
  3. Write a short, organized account of a typical day that integrates vocabulary and grammar from every chapter in Unit II.
  4. Complete a spoken interaction that moves naturally between introducing people, describing routines, giving locations, and completing a shopping exchange.
  5. Self-assess your accuracy in Hangeul, spacing, particles, predicate endings, native and Sino-Korean numbers, and register across the full range of Unit I and Unit II material.
  6. Identify which Unit II systems still need review before beginning Unit III.

Communication Scenario

Layla has been asked to record a short video message for a language exchange partner in Korea, introducing herself and describing a typical day — where she studies, what she eats, and how she shops for small things near campus. To do this well, she needs to bring together everything she has learned since Chapter 1, not just the most recent shopping language from Chapter 9.

  1. This chapter introduces no new grammar, particles, endings, counters, or vocabulary domains. Every task below uses only systems already activated in Chapters 1 through 9.
  2. The chapter is organized as a review and synthesis: it revisits identity and classroom language (Chapters 1–2), family and existence (Chapter 3), numbers and dates (Chapter 4), food and actions (Chapter 6), routines (Chapter 7), location (Chapter 8), and shopping and quantities (Chapter 9).
  3. By the end of this chapter you will be able to produce a single connected spoken and written account of a typical day, drawing accurately on all of Unit II.

발음 — Pronunciation and Hangeul Review

Pronunciation and Hangeul Review

This chapter introduces no new sound-change rule. It gives you controlled practice distinguishing three number-related contrasts you have studied separately, side by side, since accurate control of all three is required for the tasks in this chapter.

Review Note — Three number-related contrasts
  • Sino-Korean numbers (일, 이, 삼...) for dates, phone numbers, and prices with 원.
  • Native Korean numbers (하나, 둘, 셋...) for counting people and objects with counters like 개, 명, 병, 잔.
  • The shortened forms 한, 두, 세, 네, 스무 used directly before a counter.

Read the following aloud, paying attention to which number system each phrase requires.

구월 십오 일 · 두 명 · 삼천 원 · 세 개 · 오후 다섯 시

받침 점검: continue to link batchim smoothly before vowel-initial particles and endings (예: 학생이에요, 집에서), as practiced since Chapter 3. No new batchim rule is assessed in this chapter.

Grammar and Sentence Pattern Review

This is a consolidation section, not new instruction. It regroups the sentence patterns from Chapters 1 through 9 that this chapter's tasks require you to combine. No pattern below is new.

복습 1 — Identity, existence, and family

Communicative purpose: introduce yourself and describe who and what you have.

  • 저는 학생이에요. 저는 라일라예요. I am a student. I am Layla.
  • 저는 언니가 한 명 있어요. I have one older sister.

복습 2 — Dates, food, and routine

Communicative purpose: describe a typical day in time order.

  • 저는 아침에 커피를 마셔요. In the morning I drink coffee.
  • 저는 매일 학교에서 공부해요. I study at school every day.

복습 3 — Location and existence

Communicative purpose: say where people, places, and objects are.

  • 도서관은 학교 안에 있어요. The library is inside the school.
  • 저는 편의점에서 물을 사요. I buy water at the convenience store.

복습 4 — Shopping and quantity

Communicative purpose: complete a simple transaction.

  • 사과 세 개 주세요. 전부 얼마예요? Please give me three apples. How much altogether?
Language Building Block — A day in one connected account
저는 [이름]이에요/예요. 저는 [가족/소유]. 저는 [시간]에 [장소]에서 [행동]. 저는 [장소]에서 [수량] [물건]을/를 사요.
Identity → possession/family → routine with time and location → shopping, all in the same speech level
저는 라일라예요. 저는 언니가 한 명 있어요. 저는 아침에 학교에서 공부해요. 저는 편의점에서 물 두 병을 사요.
I'm Layla. I have one older sister. I study at school in the morning. I buy two bottles of water at the convenience store.
Grammar Note — Keeping speech level consistent across a longer account

When combining sentences from several chapters into one connected account, keep every sentence in the everyday polite -아요/어요 style you have used since Chapter 1. Mixing in an untaught speech level, even one you may have heard elsewhere, is outside this volume's scope through Chapter 10.

Writing Workshop

This chapter's writing task is a short integrated self-introduction, 저의 하루 ("My Day"), combining identity, family, a date, a routine, a location, and a shopping request into one organized text.

  1. Introduce yourself. Give your name and a copula sentence stating who you are (Ch. 1–2).
  2. Describe your family. Use 있어요/없어요 and a counter for people where relevant (Ch. 3, Ch. 9).
  3. State a date. Use Sino-Korean numbers for month and day (Ch. 4).
  4. Describe a routine. Use a time expression, 에, and an -아요/어요 verb (Ch. 6–7).
  5. Give a location. Use 에 or 에서 with a place noun (Ch. 8).
  6. Add a shopping sentence. Use a native number, a counter, and 주세요 or 사요, plus a total price (Ch. 9).
  7. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist below.

체크리스트 — Editing checklist:

  • Every sentence uses only Hangeul and only systems activated through Chapter 9.
  • Speech level is consistent everyday polite -아요/어요 throughout.
  • Native Korean numbers are used with counters; Sino-Korean numbers are used with dates and 원.
  • Particles (은/는, 이/가, 을/를, 에, 에서, 도, 하고) are used correctly for each sentence's function.
  • Spacing between nouns, particles, and predicates follows the patterns modeled in Chapters 1–9.
  • The text moves in a clear order: identity, family, date, routine, location, shopping.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — A typical day across contexts

The daily rhythm described in this chapter's readings — studying at school, doing homework at a library, and making small purchases at a convenience store — reflects common patterns among many students in South Korea, though individual schedules, campuses, and routines vary widely.

Culture in Context — Self-introductions in language exchange

Self-introductions in a language exchange setting typically move from identity and family to daily routine, since this order gives a new partner a clear, organized picture of who you are before more detailed conversation begins, as modeled in this chapter's dialogue.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Mixing native and Sino-Korean numbers across sentences

Error: 오늘은 아홉 월 십오 일이에요.

Correction: 오늘은 구월 십오 일이에요. — dates always use Sino-Korean numbers, even though this chapter also reviews native numbers for counters.

Common Mistake — Dropping the connected account back into isolated sentences

Error: A series of unconnected sentences with no time or location markers linking them.

Correction: Use time expressions (아침에, 저녁에), 그리고, and consistent subject reference (저는) to connect sentences into one account, as modeled in the Building Block pattern.

Common Mistake — Reintroducing yourself with 이/가 instead of 은/는

Error: 제가 라일라예요. as the opening line of a self-introduction.

Correction: 저는 라일라예요. — an opening self-introduction typically frames the speaker as the topic with 은/는, as taught in Chapter 2.

Common Mistake — Using a counter with the wrong number system

Error: 물 이 병 주세요.

Correction: 물 두 병 주세요. — counters take native Korean numbers, not Sino-Korean numbers, as reviewed above.

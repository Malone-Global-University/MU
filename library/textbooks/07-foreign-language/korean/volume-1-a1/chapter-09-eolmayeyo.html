얼마예요?Shopping and Quantities

Counting with native Korean numbers, using the counters 개, 명, 병, and 잔, requesting items with 주세요, and asking and understanding prices · Speak and Write Korean: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Count people and objects using native Korean numbers from 1 to 99.
  2. Attach the counters 개, 명, 병, and 잔 to native Korean numbers to count objects, people, bottles, and cups/glasses.
  3. Ask how much something costs using 얼마예요? and understand answers using Sino-Korean numbers.
  4. Make a polite request for an item or quantity using 주세요.
  5. Read simple price labels and shopping signs.
  6. Combine quantity, counter, and price language to complete a simple shopping transaction in connected polite Korean.

Communication Scenario

Layla and Minjun stop at a small campus convenience store on their way back from the library. Layla wants to buy a few drinks and snacks for her roommates, but she needs to ask how many items cost, count out the right quantity, and make her request politely to the clerk.

  1. Korean uses two number systems. This chapter activates the native Korean number system, which is used with counters for people and everyday objects, alongside the Sino-Korean numbers you already use for prices, dates, and phone numbers.
  2. By the end of this chapter you will be able to ask a price, count out a quantity with the correct counter, and request an item politely, using only the systems activated through Chapter 9.

발음 — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus

This chapter does not introduce a new sound-change rule. It gives you controlled practice with the shortened native numbers before counters, and with distinguishing native numbers (하나, 둘, 셋...) from the Sino-Korean numbers you already use for prices (일, 이, 삼...).

Pronunciation Note — Native versus Sino-Korean numbers

Native numbers (하나, 둘, 셋) and Sino-Korean numbers (일, 이, 삼) sound completely different from each other. Mixing them up is a common beginner error, not a matter of accent. Listen for whether a number is followed by a counter like 개 or 명 (native) or by 원 (Sino-Korean).

Read the following aloud, paying attention to the shortened forms before counters.

한 개 · 두 개 · 세 개 · 네 개 · 한 명 · 두 명 · 한 병 · 두 병 · 한 잔 · 두 잔

받침 점검: 몇 개 links smoothly in fast speech; the final ㅊ of 몇 is pronounced as an unreleased ㄷ-family sound before a consonant-initial counter. Simply imitate the pronunciation as modeled; the formal rule is not assessed at this level.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

문법 1 — Native Korean numbers with counters

Communicative purpose: count people, objects, bottles, and drinks accurately.

To count something in everyday Korean, use a native Korean number followed directly by a counter. The numbers 하나, 둘, 셋, 넷, and 스물 shorten to 한, 두, 세, 네, and 스무 immediately before a counter.

NumberShortened form+ counterMeaning
하나한한 개one (object)
둘두두 명two (people)
셋세세 병three (bottles)
넷네네 잔four (cups)
  • 사과 세 개 주세요. Please give me three apples. 사과 [noun] + 세 개 [number + counter] + 주세요 [request]
  • 친구가 두 명 있어요. I have two friends.
  • 물 한 병 주세요. Please give me one bottle of water.
Grammar Note — Word order with counters

The counted noun comes first, followed by the number and counter together, and then the rest of the sentence: 사과 세 개 ("apples, three of them"), not 세 개 사과. Do not attach a particle between the noun and the number-counter phrase.

문법 2 — Asking "how many?" with 몇

Communicative purpose: ask for a quantity before stating or receiving one.

몇 ("how many") is followed directly by a counter, just like a number.

  • 사과 몇 개 있어요? How many apples do you have?
  • 가족이 몇 명이에요? How many people are in your family? recycled from Chapter 3 family vocabulary

문법 3 — Asking price with 얼마예요? and Sino-Korean numbers

Communicative purpose: ask and understand the cost of an item.

Prices in Korean use Sino-Korean numbers (일, 이, 삼...), the same system you already use for dates and phone numbers, followed by the currency word 원. The question word for price is 얼마, used with the copula.

  • 이거 얼마예요? How much is this?
  • 천 원이에요. It's 1,000 won.
  • 사과 세 개, 전부 얼마예요? Three apples — how much altogether?
Language Building Block — Count, then ask, then request
[물건] [숫자][단위] 있어요/주세요? / [물건] 얼마예요?
Counting pattern contrasted with the price-asking pattern
사과 세 개 주세요. 전부 얼마예요?
Please give me three apples. How much is that altogether?

문법 4 — Requests with 주세요

Communicative purpose: politely ask for an item, a quantity, or a service.

주세요 attaches directly to a noun phrase, usually a noun and a number-counter combination, to make a polite request. The particle 을/를 is often omitted here in everyday spoken requests.

  • 빵 두 개 주세요. Please give me two pieces of bread.
  • 물 한 병 주세요. Please give me one bottle of water.
Grammar Note — 주세요 is a fixed request pattern for now

At this stage, treat 주세요 as a fixed polite request pattern attached after a noun phrase. Its internal grammar — how it relates to the verb 주다 and the honorific ending system — is developed formally in a later chapter. You may use it productively for requests now.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Layla and Minjun stop at the campus convenience store on the way back from the library.

레일라 이 사과 얼마예요? How much are these apples?
점원 한 개에 천 원이에요. 1,000 won per one.
레일라 그럼 사과 세 개 주세요. 그리고 물 두 병도 주세요. Then please give me three apples. And please give me two bottles of water too.
점원 네, 여기 있어요. 전부 오천 원이에요. Sure, here you are. It's 5,000 won altogether.
Minjun picks up a loaf of bread near the register.
민준 저는 이 빵 한 개 주세요. Please give me one of this bread.
점원 빵은 이천오백 원이에요. The bread is 2,500 won.

  2. What two items does Layla buy, and how many of each?
  3. How much does Layla pay altogether?
  4. What does Minjun buy, and how much is it?

Writing Workshop

This chapter's writing task is a short shopping note, 쇼핑 목록과 가격 ("Shopping List and Prices"), listing items you want to buy with their quantities and prices, plus one polite request.

  1. List three items. Write each item with a native Korean number and the correct counter (개, 명, 병, or 잔 as appropriate).
  2. State a price for each item. Use Sino-Korean numbers and 원.
  3. Write a total. Use 전부 and 얼마예요? or a total price statement.
  4. Add one request sentence. Use 주세요 with a noun and a number-counter phrase.
  5. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist below.

체크리스트 — Editing checklist:

  • Native Korean numbers (하나, 둘, 셋...) are used with counters, with 1–4 and 20 correctly shortened.
  • Sino-Korean numbers (일, 이, 삼...) are used with 원 for prices.
  • The correct counter (개, 명, 병, 잔) is chosen for each item.
  • 주세요 is attached directly after a noun and number-counter phrase.
  • Earlier systems (은/는, 이/가, 을/를, 에, 에서, -아요/어요, position nouns) are still used correctly where they appear.
  • No sentence relies on grammar not yet activated (Chapter 10 and beyond).

Speaking Practice

통제 연습 — Controlled practice: Number and counter match

Working with a partner or alone, take turns saying a quantity aloud using the correct shortened number and counter: 3 apples (사과), 2 people (친구), 4 bottles (물), 1 cup (커피).

의사소통 과제 — Communicative task: 편의점 역할극

Work with a partner. One person plays the customer and one plays the clerk. The customer asks the price of two or three items, states a quantity with 주세요, and asks for the total with 전부 얼마예요? The clerk answers each price using Sino-Korean numbers and 원. Switch roles and repeat with different items.

  • 이거 얼마예요?
  • 사과 두 개 주세요.
  • 전부 얼마예요?
  • 물 몇 병 있어요?

혼자 하는 연습 — Solo alternative

Record yourself playing both roles of a short shopping exchange: ask the price of two items, request a quantity of each with 주세요, and state the total. Listen back and check your native versus Sino-Korean number choice against the Grammar section.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Convenience stores as everyday spaces

Convenience stores such as 편의점 are extremely common across South Korea and are used for quick everyday purchases, small meals, and even paying certain bills, making the shopping language in this chapter broadly useful in daily life.

Culture in Context — Two number systems in daily use

Using native Korean numbers for counting objects and people, and Sino-Korean numbers for prices, dates, and phone numbers, is not optional stylistic variation — both systems are used constantly and interchangeably within a single shopping conversation, as shown in this chapter's dialogue.

Regional and Diaspora Note — Currency and pricing vary

Prices and currency shown in this chapter reflect South Korean won and typical campus-area pricing for illustration only. Actual prices vary by store, region, and time, and diaspora Korean-speaking communities outside South Korea often shop using local currencies while still using Korean number and counter vocabulary in conversation.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Using Sino-Korean numbers with counters

Error: 사과 삼 개 주세요.

Correction: 사과 세 개 주세요. — counters like 개, 명, 병, and 잔 take native Korean numbers, not Sino-Korean numbers.

Common Mistake — Using native Korean numbers with 원

Error: 하나 천 원이에요.

Correction: 천 원이에요. — 원 (currency) always takes Sino-Korean numbers, and does not need a separate native-number word in front of it.

Common Mistake — Forgetting to shorten the number before a counter

Error: 하나 개 주세요.

Correction: 한 개 주세요. — 하나, 둘, 셋, 넷, and 스물 must shorten to 한, 두, 세, 네, and 스무 directly before a counter.

Common Mistake — Putting the number-counter phrase before the noun

Error: 세 개 사과 주세요.

Correction: 사과 세 개 주세요. — the counted noun comes first, followed by the number and counter together.

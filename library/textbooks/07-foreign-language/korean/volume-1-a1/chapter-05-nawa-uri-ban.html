An integrated performance chapter combining identity, classroom roles, family and existence, and numbers and dates · Speak and Write Korean: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

This chapter introduces no new grammar. By the end of it, you will be able to:

  1. Introduce yourself and identify others using the copula and topic particle 은/는, combined without hesitation.
  2. Describe your family using 있어요/없어요 and the particle 이/가.
  3. State today's date, your birthday, and other important dates using Sino-Korean numbers and the particle 에.
  4. Combine identity, family, and date information into a single connected spoken and written self-introduction.
  5. Read and write Hangeul syllable blocks from all four chapters fluently, without sounding out each block letter by letter.
  6. Recognize and correct the most common errors from Chapters 1 through 4 in your own speech and writing.
  7. Complete an integrated performance task — presenting yourself and your class — using only the systems activated so far.

Communication Scenario

Kim Seongsaengnim's class is putting together a class booklet — 우리 반 소개 ("Introducing Our Class") — that every student will contribute a page to. Each page needs a short self-introduction: name, role, family, and an important date. Before students write their pages, the class reviews everything from the first four chapters together.

  1. This chapter does not teach anything new. Instead, it asks you to combine what you already know — from Chapter 1's greetings and copula, Chapter 2's identity and classroom language, Chapter 3's family and existence sentences, and Chapter 4's numbers and dates — into longer, connected Korean.
  2. By the end of this chapter you will have written and spoken a complete self-introduction that a real classmate could read and understand, and you will have practiced catching and correcting the mistakes that beginning learners make most often at this stage.

Grammar and Pattern Review

This chapter introduces no new grammar. The four patterns below are the productive core of Unit I. Each is shown once more, followed by a combined pattern that draws on all four at once.

복습 1 — Identity: 은/는 + 이에요/예요 / 아니에요

Review purpose: state who someone is or is not.

  • 저는 지나예요. I am Jina.
  • 저는 선생님이 아니에요. 학생이에요. I am not a teacher. I am a student.

복습 2 — Family and existence: 이/가 있어요/없어요

Review purpose: state who or what exists in your life.

  • 저는 언니가 있어요. I have an older sister.
  • 남동생이 없어요. I don't have a younger brother.

복습 3 — Dates and time: [month] [date]에

Review purpose: state when something happens.

  • 제 생일은 유월 오 일이에요. My birthday is June 5th.
  • 시월 십오 일에 시험이 있어요. There is a test on October 15th.
Language Building Block — Combining all three
저는 [이름]이에요/예요. [가족]이/가 있어요/없어요. 제 생일은 [month] [date]이에요.
Identity sentence + existence sentence + date sentence, in sequence, with no connector required between them
저는 누룰이에요. 언니가 있어요. 제 생일은 시월 이십삼 일이에요.
I'm Nurul. I have an older sister. My birthday is October 23rd.
Grammar Note — You are not learning a new connector here

Notice that the Language Building Block above simply places three complete sentences next to each other. You already know how to connect nouns with 하고 from Chapter 3, but connecting full sentences with words like 그리고 and 그래서 is not scheduled until Chapter 11. For now, short separate sentences are the correct and natural way to build a connected self-introduction — do not try to invent a sentence connector on your own.

Writing Workshop

This chapter's writing task is your contribution to 우리 반 책, the class booklet. It combines everything from Chapters 1 through 4 into one connected piece of writing.

  1. Greet the reader and introduce yourself. Use the greeting and identity patterns from Chapters 1 and 2. State your name and your role (student).
  2. Describe your family. Use 있어요/없어요 and 이/가 from Chapter 3 to say who is and is not in your family. Use 하고 to connect at least two family members in one sentence.
  3. State an important date. Give your birthday, or another meaningful date, using the month-then-date order and the particle 에 where it belongs, following Chapter 4.
  4. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist below before submitting it for the class booklet.

체크리스트 — Editing checklist:

  • Every sentence ends with the correct form of the copula, 있어요/없어요, or another verb already studied — not a form from a later chapter.
  • 은/는 and 이/가 are used correctly, not interchangeably by guesswork.
  • Family members are connected with 하고 where two or more appear in one sentence.
  • Sino-Korean numbers are spelled correctly, including 유월 or 시월 if either month is used.
  • Month comes before the date, and 에 appears after the date but not after 오늘.
  • Each syllable block is written as a complete, self-contained block.
  • No sentence relies on grammar, particles, or endings not yet activated (Chapters 6 and beyond).

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Self-introductions as a social ritual

A short structured self-introduction — name, role, and often family or hometown — is a common opening ritual in Korean-speaking classrooms, workplaces, and many first-meeting situations. The class booklet task in this chapter reflects a genuinely useful, everyday communicative skill, not an artificial classroom exercise: being able to introduce yourself smoothly in a short, organized way is valued across many social and professional settings.

Culture in Context — What people share, and what they don't

Sharing basic family information, such as whether one has older or younger siblings, is common in everyday introductions, since Korean kinship terms like 언니, 오빠, 누나, and 형 are relative to the speaker's age and gender and often come up naturally in conversation. More personal or sensitive family information is generally shared only as a relationship develops, just as in most communities; a class-booklet introduction stays at the level of general, comfortable detail.

Regional and Diaspora Note — Introductions beyond the classroom

The self-introduction pattern practiced in this chapter is widely usable, but the amount of detail expected, and which details are offered first, can vary somewhat by setting, generation, and community, including among diaspora and heritage Korean speakers. As your Korean develops in later volumes, you will learn how self-introductions expand and adjust for more formal, professional, and public contexts.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

The errors below are drawn from Chapters 1 through 4. Watch for them in your own performance task.

Common Mistake — Confusing 은/는 and 이/가 by guesswork

Error: 저가 학생이에요.

Correction: 저는 학생이에요. — Use 은/는 to introduce yourself as a topic, as taught in Chapters 1 and 2. Save 이/가 for existence sentences and other contexts practiced in Chapter 3, such as 언니가 있어요.

Common Mistake — Omitting 하고 between family members

Error: 아버지 어머니가 있어요.

Correction: 아버지하고 어머니가 있어요. — When listing two nouns together, connect them with 하고 rather than simply placing them side by side.

Common Mistake — Reversing month and date order

Error: 오 일 유월이에요.

Correction: 유월 오 일이에요. — Month always comes before the date in Korean, the reverse of common American English order.

Common Mistake — Adding 에 after 오늘, or leaving it off a specific date

Error: 오늘에 생일이에요. / 시월 십오 일 시험이 있어요.

Correction: 오늘 생일이에요. (no 에 after 오늘) / 시월 십오 일에 시험이 있어요. (에 required after a specific date). This distinction was introduced in Chapter 4 and applies whenever you state a specific date.

Common Mistake — Reaching for grammar you haven't studied yet

Risk: Trying to connect sentences with a full connector like 그리고 or 그래서, or trying to use past tense for a birthday that already happened this year, before either system is scheduled.

Guidance: Use short, separate sentences, as shown in the Language Building Block earlier in this chapter. Sentence connectors are introduced in Chapter 11, and past tense is introduced in Chapter 16. Simple, correct Korean within your current scope is always stronger than an attempted shortcut into unstudied grammar.

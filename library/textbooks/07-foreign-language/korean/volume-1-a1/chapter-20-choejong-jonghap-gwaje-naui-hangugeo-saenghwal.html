Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Introduce yourself and describe your identity, family, and daily routine in connected polite Korean.
  2. Talk about food, shopping, and everyday transactions using appropriate particles, counters, and prices.
  3. Give and understand directions, describe locations, and talk about plans and abilities.
  4. Describe past events, personal experience, arrangements, and future intentions.
  5. State an opinion, make a comparison, and connect ideas using -지만 and -고.
  6. Read and listen to connected beginner-level Korean texts on familiar topics without routine romanization.
  7. Produce a organized spoken and written portfolio that demonstrates accurate Hangeul, spacing, particles, predicate endings, and everyday polite speech level across the full range of Volume I.

Communication Scenario

It is the last day of the term. 라일라 and 수진's class is holding a small closing presentation where each student shares a short portfolio about their life in Korean: who they are, what they do, what they have experienced, and what they think about learning Korean so far. 선생님 asks each student to prepare a short spoken and written self-portrait using everything the class has learned this term.

  1. This chapter introduces no new productive grammar, particles, endings, or vocabulary systems.
  2. Every task below draws only on systems already activated in Chapters 1–19: identity, family, dates, food, routines, location, shopping, plans, directions, ability, requests, health, reasons, past events, experience, wishes, arrangements, opinions, and comparison.
  3. The chapter's purpose is synthesis and demonstration, not new instruction. Treat this chapter as evidence of what you can now do in Korean.

발음 — Pronunciation Review

Pronunciation Review

This chapter reviews rather than introduces pronunciation targets. Read the following aloud, checking batchim, liaison, and the ㅂ irregular pattern reviewed in Chapter 19.

Pronunciation Note — Reading a connected paragraph aloud

When reading a longer connected paragraph, pay attention to liaison across word boundaries (a batchim linking into the next syllable's silent ㅇ), the smooth attachment of connective endings such as -지만 and -고 without a pause, and consistent sentence-final intonation for statements versus questions. These are the same targets introduced in Chapters 1–19; this chapter asks you to apply them together in longer stretches of speech.

안녕하세요 · 잘 지냈어요 · 한국어가 재미있어요 · 발음이 어렵지만 계속 공부하고 싶어요

Grammar Note — Combining systems in one paragraph

A strong A2-oriented paragraph does not use every pattern from every chapter at once. Instead, it selects a handful of relevant systems and connects them naturally, the way the sample self-portrait in the Model Dialogue and Reading Practice sections below does. Aim for accuracy and natural connection over maximum density of grammar points.

Model Dialogue

Setting: The closing class presentation. 라일라 shares her portfolio self-introduction aloud before the class.

선생님 라일라 씨, 이번 학기 마지막 발표를 시작해 주세요. Layla, please begin this term's final presentation.
라일라 안녕하세요. 저는 라일라예요. 미국 사람이고 지금 한국어를 배우고 있어요. Hello. I'm Layla. I'm American, and I'm learning Korean now.
저는 보통 아침 일곱 시에 일어나고 매일 학교에 가요. 저녁에는 한국어 숙제를 해요. I usually get up at seven in the morning and go to school every day. In the evening, I do Korean homework.
지난달에 제주도에 가 봤어요. 정말 좋았어요. 제 생각에는 제주도가 서울보다 더 아름다워요. Last month, I went to Jeju Island. It was really good. In my opinion, Jeju is more beautiful than Seoul.
한국어는 어렵지만 재미있어요. 발음이 제일 어렵지만 저는 계속 공부하고 싶어요. Korean is difficult, but it's interesting. Pronunciation is the most difficult, but I want to keep studying.
선생님 아주 잘했어요! 다음 학기에도 계속 열심히 공부하세요. Very well done! Please keep studying hard next semester too.
라일라 네, 감사합니다. 다음 학기에는 부산에도 가 보려고 해요. Yes, thank you. Next semester, I'm planning to go to Busan too.

Writing Workshop

This chapter's writing task is your final portfolio paragraph, 나의 한국어 생활 ("My Life in Korean"), combining identity, routine, an experience, an opinion, and a future plan.

  1. Introduce yourself. Give your name and one identity detail using 이에요/예요.
  2. Describe a routine. Use -아요/어요 to describe one daily habit.
  3. Narrate a past experience. Use -았/었어요 or -아/어 본 적이 있어요.
  4. Share an opinion. Use 제 생각에는, with a comparison or superlative if natural.
  5. Connect two ideas. Use -지만 or -고 at least once.
  6. Close with a future plan. Use -(으)ㄹ 거예요, -고 싶어요, or -(으)려고 해요.
  7. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist below.

체크리스트 — Editing checklist:

  • Every sentence uses only Hangeul and only systems activated through Chapter 19.
  • The paragraph includes identity, routine, a past experience, an opinion, and a future plan.
  • At least one sentence uses -지만 or -고 correctly.
  • Speech level is consistent everyday polite throughout.
  • Spacing between nouns, particles, and predicates follows the patterns modeled in Chapters 1–19.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Closing a course respectfully

At the end of a class term, it is common in many South Korean educational settings to thank the instructor and classmates, briefly reflect on what was learned, and express intention to continue studying. Simple closing language such as 감사합니다 and 계속 열심히 공부하겠습니다 (a slightly more formal form you will encounter productively in later volumes) reflects this pattern, though individual classrooms and instructors vary in how formal or informal a closing feels.

Language Note — What "A2-oriented" means for this portfolio

An A2-oriented portfolio does not require flawless, native-like Korean. It shows that a learner can combine familiar vocabulary and grammar to communicate connected, practical information about themselves, their routines, their experiences, and their opinions, with generally accurate Hangeul, spacing, and everyday polite speech level. Occasional hesitation or simple errors are a normal part of this stage.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Mixing speech levels within one portfolio

Error: 저는 학생이에요. 어제 학교에 갔다.

Correction: 저는 학생이에요. 어제 학교에 갔어요. — this volume's productive speech level is everyday polite -아요/어요 throughout; do not switch to plain written forms mid-paragraph.

Common Mistake — Overloading one sentence with every pattern

Error: a single sentence trying to combine identity, routine, past tense, opinion, and future plan all at once, producing an unnatural run-on sentence.

Correction: distribute these functions across several connected sentences, as modeled in the Reading Practice passage, using -지만 and -고 to join only two closely related ideas at a time.

Common Mistake — Forgetting particles under time pressure

Error: 저 제주도 가 봤어요.

Correction: 저는 제주도에 가 봤어요. — even in a fast presentation, keep the topic particle 는 and the destination particle 에; omission is more forgivable in casual spoken Korean than in a prepared portfolio task.

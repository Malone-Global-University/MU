오늘 몇 월 며칠이에요?Numbers, Dates, and Time

Sino-Korean numbers, dates, the question word 몇, the time particle 에, and continued Hangeul block fluency · Speak and Write Korean: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Count using the Sino-Korean number system from 1 to 100.
  2. State the current month and day of the month using Sino-Korean numbers.
  3. Ask and answer "What month is it?" and "What is today's date?" using 몇 월 and 며칠.
  4. Say the names of the twelve months in Korean.
  5. Say the days of the week in Korean.
  6. Attach the particle 에 to a date or day to mean "on" or "in" a specific time.
  7. Ask "when" something happens using 언제.
  8. Read and write Hangeul syllable blocks containing the number-related batchim and vowel patterns introduced in this chapter with increased fluency.

Communication Scenario

Kim Seongsaengnim begins class by pointing to the calendar on the wall. It is almost the end of the month, and several important dates are coming up: a class trip, someone's birthday, and the deadline for a project. The class needs a shared way to talk about when things happen.

  1. In Chapter 3 you learned to say who exists in your life. This chapter gives you the tools to say when things happen. Dates and time expressions depend on a number system you have not yet studied — the Sino-Korean numbers — and a small set of question words that Korean speakers use constantly in daily life.
  2. By the end of this session you will be able to say "Today is October 15th," ask "What is today's date?", and say "The trip is on the 20th." You will also continue building fluency with Hangeul syllable blocks, since number words and date words introduce several new combinations of vowels and batchim.

Core Vocabulary

한자어 숫자 — Sino-Korean numbers 1–10

Korean has two number systems. This chapter introduces the Sino-Korean system, which is used for dates, months, years, minutes, prices, phone numbers, and most counting above native-number limits. The native Korean number system (used for age and for counting objects with counters like 개 and 명) is introduced in Chapter 9. Learning both systems at once causes confusion, so this chapter focuses only on Sino-Korean numbers.

한글 — Writing System

Hangeul — Building Block Fluency

By this point you can identify jamo, construct syllable blocks, and read words containing batchim. This chapter does not introduce new letters. Instead, it develops fluency — the ability to read and write number and date vocabulary quickly and accurately, without sounding out each block letter by letter.

Script Note — 십일 is two blocks, not one

A common early mistake is trying to squeeze a two-digit number into a single syllable block. 십일 (11) is written as two separate blocks — 십 and 일 — placed side by side, just as "ten" and "one" are separate English words placed next to each other. The same is true for every combined number: 이십오 (25) is three blocks: 이 + 십 + 오. Do not attempt to merge the batchim of one block into the initial consonant of the next; each syllable block is self-contained.

이 과의 불규칙 형태 — This chapter's irregular forms

유월 and 시월 look irregular in writing because the expected forms 육월 and 십월 are difficult to pronounce smoothly — the batchim ㄱ or ㅂ would collide awkwardly with the following ㅇ-initial syllable. Korean spelling reflects the simplified pronunciation that resulted from this collision. Practice writing and reading these two words specifically, since they do not follow the regular number + 월 pattern.

Pronunciation

숫자 연음 — Liaison in combined numbers

When a number ending in batchim is followed by a vowel-initial syllable, liaison applies exactly as you studied in Chapter 3. This happens constantly in number sequences.

WrittenPronouncedWhat happens
십일 si-bil batchim ㅂ of 십 moves onto 일
십육 sim-nyuk nasalization: ㅂ + ㄴ-like onset produces ㅁ; a special case covered receptively here
일월 i-rwol batchim ㄹ of 일 moves onto 월
며칠이에요 myeo-chi-ri-e-yo batchim ㄹ of 며칠 moves onto 이에요

The sound change in 십육 (16) is more complex than ordinary liaison and is included here for recognition only. You are not expected to derive this pattern yourself yet; simply learn 십육 as sim-nyuk.

Pronunciation Note — Keeping number rhythm even

English speakers often stress the first syllable of a multi-digit number ("TWEN-ty-five"). Korean numbers keep each syllable at roughly even weight: i-sib-o, not I-sibo. This matters especially when giving dates and phone numbers, where clear, evenly paced syllables help the listener catch each digit accurately.

Grammar Instruction

4.1 몇 월이에요? — Asking and stating the month

Purpose: ask what month it is and state a month using the copula you already know.

몇 means "what" or "how many" and requires a number-based answer. Placed before 월, it asks specifically about the month. The answer uses the copula 이에요/예요 from Chapter 1, attached to the month word.

  • 몇 월이에요? What month is it? 몇 + 월 + 이에요 (question)
  • 시월이에요. It's October. 시월 (consonant-final ㄹ) + 이에요
  • 오늘은 사월이에요. Today is April. 오늘 (topic) + 사월 + 이에요

4.2 며칠이에요? — Asking and stating the date

Purpose: ask and state the day of the month.

며칠 is a fixed question word meaning "which day of the month." It is not built from 몇 + 일 in ordinary speech and should be learned as a single unit. Dates are given as [Sino-Korean number] + 일 ("day"), plus the copula.

  • 오늘 며칠이에요? What is today's date?
  • 십오 일이에요. It's the 15th. 십오 (15) + 일 (day) + 이에요
  • 오늘은 시월 십오 일이에요. Today is October 15th. month + date, in that order — the reverse of English
Grammar Note — Month-then-day order and full-date sentences

Korean states dates in the order year → month → day when all three appear, and month → day when giving a shorter date, as in 시월 십오 일 ("October 15th," literally "October 15 day"). This is the reverse of the common American English order (month, day) but matches many international date conventions. At this level you will typically give month and day together, without the year.

  • 오늘은 시월 십오 일이에요. — Today is October 15th.
  • 생일이 유월 삼 일이에요. — My birthday is June 3rd.

4.3 무슨 요일이에요? — Days of the week

Purpose: ask and state the day of the week.

무슨 means "what kind of" and precedes 요일 in the standard question. Day names attach directly to the copula, just like months and dates.

  • 오늘 무슨 요일이에요? What day of the week is it today?
  • 금요일이에요. It's Friday.
  • 내일은 토요일이에요. Tomorrow is Saturday. 내일 (tomorrow, receptive item) + 은 + 토요일 + 이에요

4.4 시간의 에 — The particle 에 with time expressions

Purpose: mark a specific point in time — a date, day, or month — using the particle 에.

You will see the particle 에 again in Chapter 8 marking location. In this chapter, 에 attaches to a time expression to mean "on," "in," or "at" that time. It does not change form based on the preceding sound.

  • 금요일에 만나요. Let's meet on Friday. 금요일 + 에 (time marker)
  • 시월 십오 일에 시험이 있어요. There is a test on October 15th. date + 에 + 시험이 있어요 (existential, from Ch.3)
  • 유월에 한국에 가요. I'm going to Korea in June. 유월 + 에 (time); note this differs from 한국에 (destination), a use of 에 introduced fully in Chapter 8
Grammar Note — When 에 does not appear

Certain very common time words — including 오늘 (today), 내일 (tomorrow, receptive), and 어제 (yesterday, receptive; formally activated in Chapter 16) — do not take 에 in ordinary usage. You say 오늘 만나요 ("Let's meet today"), not 오늘에 만나요. Specific calendar dates, days of the week, and months do take 에: 금요일에, 시월에, 십오 일에. This distinction must simply be learned item by item rather than derived from a single rule.

4.5 언제예요? — Asking "when"

Purpose: ask a general question about timing without specifying month, day, or day of the week.

  • 생일이 언제예요? When is your birthday?
  • 시험이 언제예요? When is the test?
  • 시월 십오 일이에요. It's October 15th.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Kim Seongsaengnim stands beside the wall calendar. The class trip and a vocabulary test are both coming up, and everyone wants to know the exact dates.

KIM SEONGSAENGNIM 여러분, 오늘 며칠이에요? Everyone, what's today's date?
TOM 시월 십오 일이에요. It's October 15th.
KIM SEONGSAENGNIM 맞아요! 그리고 무슨 요일이에요? That's right! And what day of the week is it?
NURUL 목요일이에요. It's Thursday.
KIM SEONGSAENGNIM 네, 목요일이에요. 자, 여행이 언제예요? 레일라 씨? Yes, Thursday. Now, when is the trip? Layla?
LAYLA 여행은 시월 이십 일에 있어요. 월요일이에요. The trip is on October 20th. It's a Monday.
TOM 시험은요? 시험도 이십 일이에요? What about the test? Is the test also on the 20th?
KIM SEONGSAENGNIM 아니요, 시험은 이십 일이 아니에요. 시험은 십팔 일에 있어요. 수요일이에요. No, the test is not on the 20th. The test is on the 18th. It's a Wednesday.

Writing Workshop

This chapter's writing task develops the ability to write and read a short personal calendar.

  1. Choose four dates. Pick four dates that matter to you: your birthday, a family member's birthday, a holiday, or any other memorable date. If you prefer, invent dates for a fictional character.
  2. Write each date in full Korean. For each date, write a complete sentence stating what the date is and, if relevant, what happens on it, following the pattern [event]이/가 [month] [date]에 있어요 or [사람]의 생일은 [month] [date]이에요.
  3. Include the day of the week for at least two dates. Use 무슨 요일이에요? as your own prompt, and answer with the correct day.
  4. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist below before submitting.

체크리스트 — Editing checklist:

  • Sino-Korean numbers are spelled correctly, including 유월 and 시월 where relevant.
  • Month comes before the date, in that order.
  • The particle 에 appears after specific dates, days of the week, and months — not after 오늘.
  • Each syllable block is written as a complete, self-contained block.
  • I used 이에요/예요 correctly based on the final sound of the preceding word.
  • Each sentence ends with a period or question mark.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Two number systems, one everyday life

Korean speakers move fluently between the Sino-Korean number system taught in this chapter and the native Korean system introduced later in the course, choosing the correct one automatically depending on what is being counted: dates and money use Sino-Korean numbers, while age and most counted objects use native Korean numbers. This dual system can feel unfamiliar to English speakers, who use a single number system for everything. Rather than trying to find a single unifying rule, it is more effective to learn which system applies to which everyday function, one function at a time, as this course introduces them.

Culture in Context — Talking about dates in daily life

Asking about dates, schedules, and birthdays is an ordinary part of daily conversation in Korean-speaking contexts, appearing in classrooms, workplaces, and casual conversation alike. Calendar dates are frequently written in a numeric year-month-day format (for example, 2026.10.15) on forms, receipts, and digital calendars, which is worth recognizing even though this chapter focuses on spoken and written full-word dates.

Regional and Diaspora Note — Additional calendars

Alongside the standard solar calendar taught in this chapter, the traditional lunar calendar remains culturally significant for certain holidays and, in some families, for tracking birthdays and ancestral observances. Lunar calendar dates are a specialized topic addressed in later volumes; the productive system in this chapter is limited to the solar calendar, which is the standard for everyday scheduling.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Reversing month and date order

Error: 십오 일 시월이에요. (date before month)

Correction: 시월 십오 일이에요. — Korean states the month before the date, the reverse of common American English order. Always give the larger unit of time first: month, then date.

Common Mistake — Using 육월 and 십월 instead of the irregular forms

Error: 육월이에요. (for June) / 십월이에요. (for October)

Correction: 유월이에요. / 시월이에요. — June and October are the two irregular months. Every other month follows the regular [number] + 월 pattern.

Common Mistake — Adding 에 after 오늘

Error: 오늘에 시험이 있어요.

Correction: 오늘 시험이 있어요. — 오늘 does not take 에 in ordinary usage. Specific dates, days of the week, and months do: 시월 십오 일에, 금요일에.

Common Mistake — Treating 며칠 as 몇 + 일

Error: Pronouncing or analyzing 며칠 as if it were built the same way as 몇 월.

Correction: 며칠 is a fixed question word for "which day of the month" and should be learned and spelled as a single unit, distinct from 몇 월 (what month) and 몇 일, which is not the standard question form.

Common Mistake — Splitting a two-digit number across syllable blocks incorrectly

Risk: Merging the batchim of one number block into the next block when writing by hand or typing.

Guidance: Each number word remains a separate, complete syllable block. 이십오 (25) is always three distinct blocks — 이, 십, 오 — never combined into fewer blocks, even though liaison changes how they sound when spoken.

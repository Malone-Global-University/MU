Headline style, framing, source voice, and report structure · Speak and Write Korean: Professional, Academic, Media, and Institutional Korean

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize the compressed grammar of Korean news headlines and expand a headline into a full sentence.
  2. Identify the basic structure of a short news report: lead, background, detail, and source attribution.
  3. Distinguish a reporter's own voice from quoted or attributed material within a news article.
  4. Recognize simple framing choices — which details a report foregrounds or omits — without asserting bias where none is demonstrated.
  5. Use reporting and attribution language such as -다고 밝히다, -다고 전했다, and -(으)로 알려지다 to summarize what a source said.
  6. Write a short, accurately sourced news-style summary of a familiar event, and discuss it in formal spoken register.

This chapter opens Unit II, 미디어와 공공 쟁점 (Media and Public Issues). It introduces headline style, framing, source voice, and report structure as scheduled productive systems, while continuing to consolidate the register discipline, institutional language, comparison, and argumentation activated in Chapters 1–5.

Opening Scenario

수아 and 민준's class moves from Unit I's analytical writing into a new domain: Korean news media. 박 교수님 brings in three short items from a campus newsletter and a local news site — a headline, a short breaking-news notice, and a longer report about a public transportation change. Before discussing the content, she asks the class to notice something about the language itself: headlines drop particles and endings that full sentences require, reports move in a predictable order from lead to background to detail, and almost every claim in a report is attached to a source. Today's goal is not to master journalism, but to read Korean media the way an informed reader does — noticing structure and sourcing, not just content.

  1. You will learn to expand a compressed Korean headline into a complete sentence.
  2. You will learn the typical structure of a short news report and the language used to attribute information to a source.
  3. You will read and discuss a real-style report, then write and present your own short news-style summary.

발음 — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation — Headline rhythm and broadcast delivery

Korean broadcast news is read with a distinct, evenly paced rhythm and a slight downward intonation at the end of each reported clause, even before the sentence is grammatically complete. When reading a headline aloud, group the compressed noun phrase as a single rhythmic unit rather than pausing between every word, since headlines are read as a single idea rather than a list.

When reading attribution phrases such as -다고 밝혔다 or -(으)로 알려졌다 aloud, keep the pitch measured and slightly lowered, distinct from the more animated pitch used for direct quotation within a report.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces headline style, source-attribution reporting language, and report structure as new productive systems. It builds directly on the register discipline (Ch. 1), and the quotation and reported-speech systems already active from prior volumes, expanding them into a genre-specific discourse pattern.

1. Headline Style: Compressed Noun-Phrase Sentences

Grammar — expanding a Korean headline

Korean headlines commonly omit particles, drop the copula, and use a nominalized or truncated predicate rather than a full sentence-final ending. A headline is not ungrammatical; it follows its own compressed register, and a competent reader must be able to expand it mentally into a full sentence.

  • 캠퍼스 셔틀버스 요금 인상 예고Headline: Campus shuttle bus fare increase forecastheadline: [topic] [event noun] — no particle, no sentence-final ending
  • 학교 측은 캠퍼스 셔틀버스 요금을 인상할 예정이라고 예고했다.Expanded: The school has announced that it plans to raise the campus shuttle bus fare.expanded: 학교 측은[topic] + 요금을[object] + 인상할 예정이라고[quoted clause] + 예고했다[predicate]

Expansion typically requires: (1) supplying an omitted subject or agent, often an institution rather than a person; (2) restoring the object particle; and (3) converting a bare noun into a full reporting predicate such as -다고 밝혔다 or -다고 예고했다.

2. Source Attribution: Reporting What a Source Said

Grammar — attribution predicates

A responsible news report attaches nearly every claim to an identified source. The following reporting predicates attach to an indirect quotation clause ending in -다고/-라고, consistent with the indirect quotation system already active from prior volumes.

  • 시청은 내년부터 요금이 오른다고 밝혔다.The city office stated that the fare will rise starting next year.-다고 밝히다 = to state/disclose that ~
  • 관계자는 정확한 인상 폭은 아직 정해지지 않았다고 전했다.An official relayed that the exact size of the increase has not yet been decided.-다고 전하다 = to relay/convey that ~
  • 요금 인상은 다음 달 발표될 것으로 알려졌다.It is known/reported that the fare increase will be announced next month.-(으)ㄹ 것으로 알려지다 = it is reported/known that ~ (source often unnamed or institutional)

3. Report Structure: Lead, Background, Detail, Source

Grammar and discourse — the short news-report frame

A short Korean news report typically opens with a lead sentence stating the main event, follows with background explaining why the event is happening or relevant, adds supporting detail, and closes by attributing the key claim to a named or institutional source. This order is a discourse convention, not a grammatical rule, but recognizing it helps a reader locate the most important information quickly.

4. Reporter's Voice versus Quoted Material

Grammar — distinguishing narration from quotation

A report mixes the reporter's own descriptive narration (plain or formal written endings, no attribution) with quoted or paraphrased material (marked by -다고/-라고 plus a reporting predicate, or by direct quotation marks). A critical reader must be able to separate what the reporter is asserting directly from what a quoted source is asserting.

  • 시는 어제 오전 관련 계획을 공개했다.The city released the related plan yesterday morning. [reporter's own narration]
  • 한 시 관계자는 "주민들의 의견을 충분히 반영했다"고 말했다.A city official said, "We fully reflected residents' opinions." [direct quotation, attributed]
Building Block — The short report frame

[리드: 핵심 사건] + [배경: 왜 / 언제부터] + [세부 사항] + [출처: -다고 밝히다/전하다/알려지다]

리드 = lead; 배경 = background; 세부 사항 = detail; 출처 = source attribution

시청은 내년 3월부터 캠퍼스 셔틀버스 요금을 인상한다고 밝혔다. 이번 조치는 유가 상승과 운영 적자를 반영한 것으로 알려졌다. 정확한 인상 폭은 다음 달 회의에서 결정될 예정이다. 시 관계자는 "이용객 부담을 최소화하는 방향으로 검토하고 있다"고 전했다.

The city office announced that the campus shuttle bus fare will rise starting next March. This measure is reported to reflect rising fuel prices and an operating deficit. The exact size of the increase is expected to be decided at next month's meeting. A city official relayed, "We are reviewing this in a way that minimizes the burden on riders."

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Source attribution as a professional norm

In Korean news writing, as in many journalistic traditions, careful attribution of claims to named or institutional sources is treated as a basic professional standard rather than an optional courtesy. A report that makes claims with no attributed source at all is often read as less credible, whatever its content.

Culture in Context — Reading for framing, not assuming bias

Recognizing that a report emphasizes certain details and omits others is a normal, useful reading skill. It is different from concluding that a specific outlet or article is biased, which requires comparing coverage or citing specific evidence rather than general impressions. Learners should practice describing framing choices concretely rather than making broad claims about an outlet's reliability.

Regional Awareness — Campus newsletters as a controlled genre

The campus-newsletter and local-news style used in this chapter's model texts is a simplified, illustrative version of the genre, useful for building the underlying skills. Professional Korean news media — national broadcasters, major newspapers, and specialized outlets — vary in register, house style, and editorial stance, and a learner moving to authentic materials should expect more density and variation.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Treating a headline as a complete grammatical sentence

Error: Trying to conjugate or add particles directly onto headline-style phrasing as if it were a normal sentence, or assuming a headline is grammatically incomplete or wrong.

Correction: Recognize headline compression as its own register. Expand the headline into a full sentence separately, rather than treating the headline itself as something to correct.

Common Mistake — Blurring the reporter's voice with the source's words

Error: Presenting a source's quoted claim as if it were the reporter's own confirmed fact, or vice versa.

Correction: Keep attribution predicates such as -다고 밝혔다, -다고 전했다, and quotation marks clearly attached to the source, and use plain descriptive predicates only for what the reporter is asserting directly.

Common Mistake — Calling a report biased without evidence

Error: Labeling a report as 편향된 보도 based on a general impression rather than specific details of emphasis, omission, or word choice.

Correction: Support any claim of framing or bias with a specific detail — a foregrounded fact, an omitted perspective, or a loaded word choice — rather than an unsupported general judgment.

Section D — Sample response

Check that: the headline is compressed, the lead expands it with a reporting predicate, background and detail are clearly separated, at least one source is quoted or paraphrased with attribution, and the reporter's own narration is distinguishable from the source's words.

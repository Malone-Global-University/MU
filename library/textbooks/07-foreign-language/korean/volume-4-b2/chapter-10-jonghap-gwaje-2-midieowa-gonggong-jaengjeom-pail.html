Consolidating Chapters 6–9 · Speak and Write Korean: Professional, Academic, Media, and Institutional Korean

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Assemble a short media and public-issue dossier that integrates media literacy, formal spoken interaction, careful-claim hedging, and rights/obligations language from Chapters 6–9.
  2. Read a short news-style report and a related general notice, and correctly identify framing, source voice, careful claims, and any stated obligations, rights, or conditions.
  3. Listen to a short interview-style exchange and identify stance, clarification, and institutional vocabulary.
  4. Write a structured dossier entry that summarizes a public issue, attributes claims to sources, and states any relevant rights, obligations, or restrictions with appropriate hedging.
  5. Present and discuss the dossier orally, responding to follow-up questions in formal register.
  6. Maintain consistent formal register, accurate Hangeul and spacing, and appropriate source caution throughout, without introducing any new productive grammar system.

This is a Unit II assessment and synthesis chapter. It introduces no new productive grammar, particle, honorific, or vocabulary system. It consolidates media literacy and report structure (Chapter 6), formal spoken interaction (Chapter 7), careful-claim hedging and institutional vocabulary (Chapter 8), and rights/obligations and form-reading language (Chapter 9) into one integrated performance task.

Opening Scenario

수아 and 마이클 have been asked by 박 교수님 to prepare a short "미디어와 공공 쟁점 파일" (media and public-issue dossier) as their Unit II project: a compact packet that reads like something a careful, informed citizen might assemble when trying to understand a public issue from several angles. Their dossier must draw on a news-style report, connect it to a related general notice or regulation, and present their own findings both in writing and aloud, fielding questions the way 수아 practiced in the Chapter 7 interview unit. 박 교수님 reminds them that a good dossier does not simply repeat what a source said — it distinguishes what is reported from what is confirmed, hedges appropriately, and never treats a right or obligation as unconditional when the source itself attaches a condition.

  1. You will review and connect the media, interview, civic-issue, and legal/administrative language from Chapters 6–9.
  2. You will read a short report and a related notice, then listen to a short interview-style exchange.
  3. You will write a structured dossier entry and present it aloud, responding to follow-up questions.

발음 — Pronunciation Consolidation

Pronunciation Consolidation

Pronunciation — Reading a mixed-genre dossier aloud

A dossier presentation typically moves between report-style sentences, hedged claims, and formal rights/obligations statements within the same short talk. Keep the even, unhurried pacing used for formal endings such as -습니다 and -아/어서는 안 됩니다 consistent across all three sections, rather than speeding up on familiar material and slowing only on new-sounding vocabulary.

As in Chapter 9, pause briefly after 단, whenever it introduces a condition or exception, so a listener can register that the preceding statement is about to be narrowed.

Grammar Consolidation

This chapter introduces no new grammar. It requires learners to combine, in a single connected task, the report-structure and framing language of Chapter 6, the formal spoken interaction patterns of Chapter 7, the careful-claim hedging of Chapter 8, and the obligation/right/prohibition/condition patterns of Chapter 9.

1. Review — Careful Claims (Chapter 8)

Review — hedged reporting

Recall the difference between a confirmed fact and a reported or apparent claim.

  • 시 당국은 새로운 규정을 검토하고 있는 것으로 알려졌다.The city authorities are known to be reviewing a new regulation.~것으로 알려지다 = reported, not confirmed by the writer
  • 주민들은 이 계획에 대해 우려를 표하고 있는 것으로 보인다.Residents appear to be expressing concern about this plan.~것으로 보인다 = writer's cautious observation, not a flat assertion

2. Review — Rights, Obligations, and Conditions (Chapter 9)

Review — bounded rights and obligations

Recall that a right, obligation, or prohibition in a real document is almost never unconditional.

  • 주민은 공청회에 참석할 권리가 있다. 단, 사전 등록을 마친 경우에 한하여 발언권이 주어진다.Residents have the right to attend the public hearing. However, the right to speak is given only to those who have completed advance registration.단, ~에 한하여 = the right to attend is broad, but the right to speak is narrowly bounded
Building Block — The dossier sentence

[보도/출처 표시] + [쟁점 진술: 조심스러운 표현] + [관련 권리/의무/제한 + 조건]

보도/출처 표시 = attributing the claim to a source; 쟁점 진술 = the hedged statement of the issue; 권리/의무/제한 + 조건 = any bounded right, obligation, or restriction connected to the issue

한 지역 신문에 따르면 시 당국은 소음 규정을 강화하는 것으로 알려졌다. 이 규정에 따르면 주민은 야간 공사에 대해 민원을 제기할 권리가 있다. 단, 공사 허가서에 명시된 예외 시간대는 이 조항이 해당하지 않는다.

According to one local newspaper, the city authorities are reportedly strengthening noise regulations. Under this regulation, residents have the right to file a complaint about nighttime construction. However, this clause does not apply to exception time periods specified in the construction permit.

Practice — Combining Systems

  1. Write one sentence attributing a claim to a source, using a hedging pattern from Chapter 8.
  2. Write one sentence stating a right or obligation connected to that same issue, using a pattern from Chapter 9.
  3. Attach a condition or exception to your Item 2 sentence using 단, or ~에 한하여.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Public hearings and citizen participation

Public hearings and comment periods on local issues such as construction noise are a common feature of civic life in many South Korean municipalities, as in many countries. Careful readers and speakers distinguish between what officials have confirmed, what is still under review, and what rights or obligations are already fixed by existing regulation, rather than assuming an outcome before it is announced.

Culture in Context — Media caution and institutional response

Local news coverage of an unresolved public issue often quotes residents' concerns alongside a cautious institutional response, reflecting a broader pattern in which officials avoid confirming outcomes before a formal decision, a practice found across many countries' public communication norms, not only in Korea.

Important Boundary — This chapter is not legal or journalistic advice

The dossier task in this chapter is a language-learning exercise in combining report structure, hedged claims, and rights/obligations language. It is not training in professional journalism, law, or public administration, and learners facing an actual public issue should consult a qualified professional or official institutional resource.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Converting a reported claim into a confirmed fact

Error: Summarizing 주민들은 우려를 표하고 있는 것으로 보인다 as 주민들은 우려를 표했다, dropping the hedge and stating it as settled fact.

Correction: Keep the hedge when the source itself is uncertain: 주민들은 우려를 표하고 있는 것으로 보인다.

Common Mistake — Dropping the exception when summarizing a right

Error: Writing 주민은 언제든지 민원을 제기할 권리가 있다 when the source text attaches an exception for permitted construction hours.

Correction: Restate the exception: 주민은 민원을 제기할 권리가 있다. 단, 공사 허가서에 명시된 예외 시간대는 해당하지 않는다.

Common Mistake — Mixing casual and formal register within the dossier

Error: Writing the report summary in formal -습니다 style but the rights/obligations memo in casual -아요/어요 style within the same dossier entry.

Correction: Maintain one consistent formal register across all parts of the dossier, as modeled in the Model Dossier Excerpt above.

Answer Key

Model Dossier Excerpt — Structure Analysis

  1. The first sentence, 한 지역 신문 보도에 따르면..., attributes the information to a source using Chapter 6 report-structure language.
  2. The two hedged claims are marked by 것으로 알려졌다 (residents' complaints are increasing — reported, not confirmed) and 것으로 보이나...것으로 보인다 (the city is reviewing the plan and no final decision has been made — the writer's cautious observation).
  3. The right-statement sentence uses -(으)ㄹ 권리가 있다; it is bounded by the exception for construction-permit exception hours and emergency work, marked by 단,.
  4. The obligation-statement sentence uses 준수할 의무가 있다; violating it can result in a fine (벌금이 부과될 수 있다).

Listening comprehension

  • The proposal is still at the review stage; no final decision has been made.
  • The final decision is expected to be announced after next month's meeting.
  • Residents currently have the right to file a complaint with the responsible department, but this right does not apply during exception time periods specified in the construction permit.
  • Sample clarification phrase: 간단히 설명해 주시겠습니까? ("Could you briefly explain...?").

Reading comprehension

  1. The source of the complaints is nighttime construction near an apartment complex; the report hedges residents' claims about sleep disruption with 주장하는 것으로 보도되었다 (reported as claiming), rather than stating it as settled fact.
  2. The report hedges the authorities' next steps with 검토하고 있으며...결정되지 않은 것으로 보인다, indicating review is ongoing and no decision is confirmed.
  3. Residents have the right to file a complaint about nighttime construction noise; in return, the responsible department has an obligation to review received complaints within seven days.
  4. The construction company must comply with set noise standards; violating this may result in a fine.
  5. The stated exception covers time periods specified in the construction permit and work classified as emergency construction; these are excluded from the general rule.
  6. The notice clarifies the concrete right, obligation, and time frame (seven days, specific exceptions) that the report leaves general and uncertain.

Rights, obligations, forms, restrictions, and boundaries · Speak and Write Korean: Professional, Academic, Media, and Institutional Korean

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize and use general vocabulary for rights, obligations, and restrictions in everyday legal and administrative contexts.
  2. Read and complete common general-purpose forms and notices with accurate register and formatting.
  3. Express what a person is permitted to do, required to do, and prohibited from doing, using level-appropriate structures.
  4. State the boundaries or conditions attached to a right or obligation without overstating what a document actually permits.
  5. Read a short lease-related notice or general administrative text and identify the parties, rights, obligations, and restrictions it describes.
  6. Write and speak about a general administrative situation while distinguishing what is required, permitted, and restricted.

This chapter continues Unit II, 미디어와 공공 쟁점 (Media and Public Issues), extending the institutional vocabulary and careful-claim language from Chapter 8 into general legal and administrative contexts. It introduces rights/obligations vocabulary and form-reading conventions as new productive systems, while continuing to consolidate careful-claim hedging and institutional vocabulary from Chapter 8. This chapter addresses general-purpose legal and administrative language for everyday situations. It does not provide legal advice, and it does not attempt specialized legal, contractual, or professional legal training.

Opening Scenario

수아 is helping a new international student, 마이클, read the standard lease notice attached to his housing contract near campus. 마이클 is confused: some lines describe what he must do, some describe what he is allowed to do, and some describe what he is not allowed to do, but the formal, deferential style makes it hard for him to tell which is which. 박 교수님 explains that general legal and administrative Korean marks obligation, permission, and prohibition with specific, recognizable patterns, and that careful readers pay close attention to conditions and exceptions attached to each one — a right is rarely unconditional, and an obligation often has a stated boundary. Today's goal is to learn the vocabulary and sentence patterns needed to read, discuss, and write about general rights, obligations, restrictions, and their boundaries — not to interpret an actual legal document professionally.

  1. You will learn vocabulary for rights, obligations, forms, and restrictions in general administrative Korean.
  2. You will learn to identify obligation, permission, and prohibition patterns and the conditions attached to them.
  3. You will read a general lease-related notice, then write and speak about a general administrative situation of your own.

발음 — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation — Formal document style read aloud

When formal clauses such as -아/어서는 안 됩니다 are read aloud, they are typically produced with even, unhurried pacing and a clear boundary before the negative ending, rather than being rushed together with the preceding clause. This helps the listener register the prohibition clearly.

The discourse marker 단, is usually followed by a short pause in careful reading, since it signals that a condition or exception is about to be introduced. Skipping this pause can cause a listener to miss that a restriction has just been qualified.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces obligation, permission, prohibition, and condition/exception patterns as new productive systems, applied to general legal and administrative contexts. It builds on the institutional vocabulary and careful-claim hedging from Chapter 8, since rights and obligations in real documents are almost always bounded by conditions rather than absolute.

1. Stating an Obligation

Grammar — obligation patterns

General administrative Korean states an obligation using -아/어야 하다 (from earlier volumes) or the more formal -(으)ㄹ 의무가 있다, which names the obligation as a noun.

  • 임차인은 매달 정해진 날짜에 임대료를 내야 한다.The tenant must pay the rent on the set date each month.-아/어야 하다 = ordinary obligation, familiar from earlier volumes
  • 신청자는 기한 내에 서류를 제출할 의무가 있다.The applicant has an obligation to submit the documents within the deadline.-(으)ㄹ 의무가 있다 = formal, names the obligation explicitly

2. Stating a Right or Permission

Grammar — right and permission patterns

A right or permission is stated with -(으)ㄹ 권리가 있다, or with 허용하다/허용되다 when describing what a rule or institution permits.

  • 임차인은 계약 기간 중 계약 해지를 요구할 권리가 있다.The tenant has the right to request termination of the contract during the contract period.-(으)ㄹ 권리가 있다 = formal right-statement pattern
  • 사전 동의를 받은 경우에 한하여 전대가 허용된다.Subletting is permitted only in cases where prior consent has been obtained.허용되다 (passive) + ~에 한하여 = permission explicitly bounded by a condition

3. Stating a Prohibition

Grammar — prohibition patterns

General documents often use the more formal -아/어서는 안 되다 for prohibition, alongside 금지하다 for naming what is prohibited as an institutional act.

  • 임차인은 임대인의 동의 없이 구조를 변경해서는 안 된다.The tenant must not alter the structure without the landlord's consent.-아/어서는 안 되다 = formal prohibition, stronger register than -지 마세요
  • 이 규정은 상업적 용도로의 사용을 금지한다.This regulation prohibits use for commercial purposes.금지하다 = institutional/regulatory prohibition as a transitive verb

4. Attaching a Condition or Exception

Grammar — condition and exception markers

Rights, obligations, and prohibitions in real documents are rarely unconditional. Careful reading and writing identify the condition or exception attached to each one, often marked by 단, or ~에 한하여.

  • 계약은 자동으로 연장된다. 단, 계약 만료 한 달 전까지 해지 의사를 통보한 경우는 예외로 한다.The contract is automatically extended. However, a case in which notice of termination is given up to one month before the contract expires is treated as an exception.단, ~ = introduces the exception to the preceding rule
  • 보증금 반환은 원상 복구가 확인된 경우에 한하여 이루어진다.Return of the deposit takes place only in the case that restoration to the original condition has been confirmed.~에 한하여 = restricts the right to a specific bounded condition
Building Block — The bounded right or obligation

[권리/의무/금지 진술] + [단, / ~에 한하여: 조건 또는 예외] + [해당 여부 명시]

권리/의무/금지 진술 = statement of the right, obligation, or prohibition; 조건/예외 = the condition or exception that bounds it; 해당 여부 = whether it applies to a given case

임차인은 계약 기간 중 시설 수리를 요구할 권리가 있다. 단, 임차인의 고의나 과실로 발생한 고장은 이 조항이 해당하지 않는다.

The tenant has the right to request repair of facilities during the contract period. However, this clause does not apply to a malfunction caused by the tenant's intent or negligence.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — General administrative documents in daily life

Everyday life in South Korea, as in many countries, involves regularly reading and signing general administrative documents such as lease notices, membership agreements, and institutional forms. These documents typically state obligations, rights, and restrictions in a formal, deferential register, and careful readers pay close attention to conditions and exceptions rather than assuming a right or obligation is unconditional.

Culture in Context — Written notice and documentation practices

Many general administrative processes in South Korea, such as terminating a lease or requesting a refund, expect written notice within a stated deadline rather than a verbal request alone. This reflects a broader institutional preference for documented communication in formal and administrative contexts, a pattern that appears in many countries' administrative systems, not only in Korea.

Important Boundary — This chapter is not legal advice

The vocabulary and patterns in this chapter support general reading, writing, and speaking about everyday administrative situations. They are not a substitute for professional legal advice, and learners facing an actual legal or contractual situation should consult a qualified professional or official institutional resource rather than relying on this chapter alone.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Treating a right as unconditional

Error: Writing 임차인은 언제든지 계약을 해지할 권리가 있다 without mentioning any condition, when the source document attaches a notice period.

Correction: State the condition explicitly, as in 임차인은 계약 만료 한 달 전까지 통보한 경우에 한하여 계약을 해지할 권리가 있다.

Common Mistake — Using casual prohibition in a formal document

Error: Writing 이거 하지 마세요 in a formal notice or administrative context.

Correction: Use the more formal prohibition pattern, as in 사전 동의 없이 이를 해서는 안 됩니다.

Common Mistake — Ignoring the exception marked by 단,

Error: Summarizing a rule without mentioning the exception that follows 단, in the source text, which changes what the rule actually covers.

Correction: Always check for and state a 단, clause, since it typically narrows or modifies the preceding statement rather than simply adding extra information.

  • Users have the right to extend the loan period once, but this is not allowed if another user has reserved the material.
  • Damaging or losing materials is prohibited; violating this makes the user responsible for the cost of the material.
  • Materials in special collections may be subject to separate regulations.

Reading comprehension

  1. Residents are obligated to comply with dormitory regulations; violating them can result in a warning or removal from the dormitory.
  2. Residents have the right to request repairs from the responsible department for facility problems in common spaces.
  3. Long-term lodging of outside visitors without prior permission is prohibited, but short-term visits limited to one day are permitted.
  4. Residents who wish to move out during the contract period must give written notice to the responsible department at least two weeks in advance; failing to meet this deadline may mean part of the deposit is not returned.
  5. The sentence beginning 단, 하루에 한하여 방문객의 단기 방문은 허용된다 introduces an exception allowing short-term, one-day visits despite the general prohibition on lodging outside visitors.
  6. Both texts state a right or rule and then immediately narrow it with a condition or exception, rather than presenting it as absolute.

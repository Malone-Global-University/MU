인터뷰와 공개 토론Interviews and Public Discussion

Formal spoken Korean, turn-taking, stance-marking, and clarification · Speak and Write Korean: Professional, Academic, Media, and Institutional Korean

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize the formal spoken register used in interviews and public discussion, distinct from the written report register of Chapter 6.
  2. Take and yield conversational turns appropriately in a formal spoken exchange, using standard turn-taking language.
  3. Mark your own stance clearly — agreement, partial agreement, reservation, or disagreement — without abandoning politeness or register.
  4. Ask for clarification and repair misunderstandings in a formal spoken exchange.
  5. Answer interview-style questions with organized, appropriately hedged spoken responses.
  6. Participate in a short formal discussion or mock interview, maintaining consistent register and clear turn management throughout.

This chapter continues Unit II, 미디어와 공공 쟁점 (Media and Public Issues), moving from the written report register of Chapter 6 into formal spoken interaction. It introduces turn-taking, stance-marking, and clarification language as new productive systems, while continuing to consolidate register discipline, source attribution, and report structure from Chapters 1–6.

Opening Scenario

수아 and 민준's class is invited to observe — and then take part in — a recorded roundtable discussion for the campus radio station about the shuttle-bus fare increase covered in last week's news writing. 박 교수님 explains that reading a report and speaking about it in a live discussion require different skills: a discussion moves in real time, speakers must claim and yield the floor, ask for clarification when something is unclear, and mark their own stance without simply repeating what a report already said. Today's goal is to move from analyzing media as a reader to participating in formal spoken exchange as a speaker — asking follow-up questions, taking a position, and staying in register under the pressure of a live conversation.

  1. You will learn the language of formal turn-taking: claiming the floor, yielding it, and inviting another speaker to continue.
  2. You will learn to mark stance — agreement, partial agreement, reservation, and disagreement — in formal spoken Korean.
  3. You will practice asking for clarification and repairing a misunderstanding, then take part in a short mock interview or roundtable discussion.

발음 — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation — Turn-claiming rhythm and stance intonation

In formal spoken discussion, a speaker claiming the floor often uses a brief rising-then-level pitch on an opening phrase such as 저도 한 말씀 드리자면, signaling "I would like to add something" before the moderator or another speaker has fully finished. This differs from an interruption, which typically breaks in on a falling pitch mid-clause.

Stance-marking phrases such as 저는 다소 이견이 있습니다 are typically delivered with a measured, slightly lowered pitch, distinct from the more animated pitch used for enthusiastic agreement such as 전적으로 동의합니다. Practice contrasting these two intonation patterns aloud.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces formal spoken turn-taking, stance-marking, and clarification language as new productive systems. It builds directly on the register discipline (Ch. 1) and the source-attribution and quotation systems consolidated in Chapter 6, expanding them into live, interactive spoken discourse.

1. Claiming and Yielding the Floor

Grammar — turn-taking language

Formal spoken discussion in Korean uses set phrases to request, claim, and yield the floor, keeping the exchange orderly without requiring interruption. These phrases attach to the formal deferential style already active from Chapter 1.

  • 저도 한 말씀 드려도 될까요?May I also say something?floor-request: -아/어도 될까요 + 한 말씀 드리다
  • 이어서 말씀드리자면,Continuing on from that, ...floor-claim connector: turn-initial framing phrase
  • 네, 여기까지만 말씀드리겠습니다.Yes, I'll stop here for now.floor-yield: signals the speaker is finished

2. Marking Stance: Agreement, Partial Agreement, and Reservation

Grammar — stance-marking predicates and hedges

A formal spoken discussion requires a speaker to mark their own stance clearly but without excessive bluntness. The following patterns move from strong agreement to clear disagreement, each appropriate to different degrees of confidence and social distance.

  • 말씀하신 부분에 전적으로 동의합니다.I fully agree with the point you made.동의하다 + 전적으로 (fully) = strong agreement
  • 그 점에는 일부 동의하지만, 다른 측면도 고려해야 할 것 같습니다.I partly agree with that point, but I think other aspects should also be considered.일부 동의하다 + -지만 = partial agreement with a following contrast
  • 저는 그 부분에 대해 다소 이견이 있습니다.I have somewhat differing views on that point.이견이 있다 = softened disagreement
  • 지금 단계에서는 판단을 유보하고 싶습니다.At this stage, I would like to reserve judgment.판단을 유보하다 = declining to commit to a position yet

3. Asking for Clarification and Repairing Misunderstanding

Grammar — clarification requests and repair

When a speaker's meaning is unclear, formal spoken Korean uses set clarification requests rather than simply guessing or ignoring the gap. These requests remain in the deferential or polite register throughout.

  • 죄송하지만, 다시 한번 말씀해 주시겠어요?I'm sorry, but could you say that once more?-아/어 주시겠어요 = polite request for repetition
  • 정확히 어떤 뜻으로 말씀하신 건지 여쭤봐도 될까요?May I ask exactly what you meant by that?여쭤보다 (humble: to ask) + -아/어도 될까요
  • 제가 이해하기로는 ~라는 말씀이신 것 같은데, 맞을까요?As I understand it, I believe you mean ~ — is that right?clarification-check: restating for confirmation

4. Moderator Language: Directing and Closing a Topic

Grammar and discourse — moderator turn-management

A moderator or host uses a distinct set of directive phrases to allocate turns, redirect a discussion back to the topic, and close a segment. These phrases remain within the formal deferential register even when redirecting a speaker.

  • 이 부분에 대해 다른 의견 있으신 분 계신가요?Is there anyone with a different opinion on this point?
  • 시간 관계상 이 주제는 여기서 마무리하겠습니다.Due to time constraints, I'll wrap up this topic here.
Building Block — The stance-marked turn

[발언권 신호] + [입장 표현: 동의/일부 동의/이견/유보] + [근거 한 문장] + [발언권 마무리]

발언권 신호 = floor signal; 입장 표현 = stance expression; 근거 = one supporting reason; 발언권 마무리 = turn closer

저도 한 말씀 드리자면, 말씀하신 부분에 일부 동의하지만 요금 인상이 학생들에게 미치는 부담도 함께 고려해야 한다고 생각합니다. 이 점만 말씀드리고 마치겠습니다.

If I may add something, I partly agree with the point you made, but I think the burden the fare increase places on students should also be considered. I'll close with just that point.

  3. Write a clarification request you could use if a panelist's answer was too vague.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Softened disagreement in public discussion

In many formal Korean discussion settings, direct disagreement is typically softened with phrases such as 이견이 있다 (to have a differing view) rather than a blunt 반대하다 (to oppose), especially toward someone of higher status, an institutional representative, or in a broadcast setting. This softening is a register choice, not a sign that the speaker lacks a firm position.

Culture in Context — The moderator's role in managing turns

A moderator in a formal Korean discussion is expected to actively allocate turns, request clarification on behalf of the audience, and close topics when time is limited. Speakers generally wait for or explicitly request the floor rather than interjecting freely, which differs from the more overlapping turn-taking style common in some informal conversations.

Regional Awareness — Broadcast versus everyday spoken register

The formal spoken register modeled in this chapter — measured pacing, deferential endings, explicit turn-taking phrases — is typical of broadcast interviews, public panels, and formal meetings. Everyday spoken Korean among peers is generally faster, more elliptical, and less reliant on explicit turn-taking language; a learner should not assume this chapter's register applies to casual conversation.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Interrupting instead of claiming the floor

Error: Breaking into another speaker's turn mid-sentence without a floor-claiming phrase, which can read as rude in a formal discussion setting.

Correction: Use a floor-claiming phrase such as 저도 한 말씀 드려도 될까요? and wait for a natural pause or the moderator's cue before speaking.

Common Mistake — Disagreeing too bluntly in formal register

Error: Using a blunt phrase such as 그건 틀렸어요 in a formal panel or interview setting.

Correction: Use a softened stance marker such as 그 부분에 대해 다소 이견이 있습니다, which expresses clear disagreement while remaining in register.

Common Mistake — Guessing instead of asking for clarification

Error: Responding to an unclear point by guessing at its meaning rather than checking, which can lead to talking past the other speaker.

Correction: Use a clarification request such as 정확히 어떤 뜻으로 말씀하신 건지 여쭤봐도 될까요? before responding to the point.

Listening comprehension

  • Panelist 1 fully agrees with the policy, reasoning that it will solve the problem of limited seating during exam periods.

  • The moderator asks Panelist 2 to specify exactly what the mentioned "safety issue" refers to.
  • Panelist 2 clarifies that the concern is the safety of students alone late at night.

Reading comprehension

  1. The moderator's opening line allocates the floor to the Student Support Office official first; the official's response begins with 네, 말씀드리겠습니다.
  2. The student representative claims the floor with 저도 한 말씀 드려도 될까요? and marks a stance of empathizing with the goal while raising a concern — closer to partial agreement with a reservation about implementation.
  3. The moderator asks the student representative to explain more specifically what "불편" (inconvenience) refers to; the representative clarifies it means difficulty carrying a reusable cup during a rushed morning commute.
  4. The official partially agrees with the student's point but adds the limiting condition that disposable cups will still be available for a fee for takeout orders, so she does not expect major inconvenience.
  5. The moderator's closing phrase is 시간 관계상 이 주제는 여기서 마무리하겠습니다, signaling that the segment is ending due to time constraints.

Looking Ahead

지금 할 수 있는 것 — What you can do now

You can claim and yield the floor in a formal spoken discussion, mark your own stance clearly across a range from strong agreement to reservation or disagreement, ask for and provide clarification when meaning is unclear, and participate in a short formal interview or discussion while maintaining consistent register.

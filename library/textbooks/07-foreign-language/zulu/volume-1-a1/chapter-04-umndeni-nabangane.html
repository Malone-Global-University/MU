Umndeni nabangane

Family and Friends · Speak and Write Zulu: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify and name immediate family members and close friends in isiZulu.
  2. State and ask about relationships using possessive pronominal forms for my, your, his or her, our, and their.
  3. Ask and state ages using the number expressions introduced in Chapters 1 through 3.
  4. Express basic possession using active na-based constructions.
  5. Give a simple description of a person using a limited set of canonical adjective stems with the correct adjective concord.
  6. Recognize the class 1a/2a noun pair and describe how it works alongside the class 1/2 pair already introduced.
  7. Apply possessive concords controlled by the possessed noun for active human-class nouns.
  8. Distinguish the alveolar click q from the dental c and the lateral x already introduced.
  9. Recognize and produce the digraphs hl and dl in family vocabulary.
  10. Write a short connected paragraph about important people in your life using standard conjunctive isiZulu orthography.

Communication Scenario

Your isiZulu class has just finished its third session in Durban. Your classmate Sipho has pulled out his phone to show you a family photo taken at a recent gathering in uMlazi. He points to each face and tells you who the person is, how old they are, and something about what they are like. You want to do the same — to talk about the people who matter most to you. The language of family is among the most personally meaningful a learner can acquire, and it will recur in almost every chapter that follows.

  1. In English, write the names of four people in your family or social circle. Next to each name, note one word you would use to describe them. Keep this list — you will use it in the Writing Workshop later.
  2. In isiZulu, the possessive phrase works differently from English. Predict whether the possessive word comes before or after the noun. Then compare your prediction with the Grammar section.

Pronunciation and Tone

The alveolar click: q

In this chapter you meet the third basic click type in isiZulu. You already know the dental click c from Chapter 2 and the lateral click x from Chapter 3. This chapter introduces the alveolar or postalveolar click, written q. It appears in important vocabulary including mqatha (strong) and in other common words you will encounter throughout the course.

Pronunciation Focus — How to produce the q click

For the q click, the tongue tip or blade makes firm contact further back in the mouth than for c, approximately at the alveolar ridge or just behind it. The back of the tongue simultaneously seals against the velum. When the tongue tip releases, a sharp popping sound is produced. The release feels more percussive than the dental c.

Practise in this order:

  1. Make the back-of-mouth seal silently.
  2. Place the tongue tip firmly at the alveolar ridge.
  3. Release the tip quickly while holding the back seal, producing the click.
  4. Let a vowel follow: qa, qi, qu.
  5. Move to real words: listen to your instructor or audio model and repeat qatha, qala, qeda.

Do not substitute an English k or kw for q. The click is produced by a lingual mechanism, not by the lips or a plosive airflow from the lungs. As with all clicks, listen before you produce, and work from syllable to word to phrase.

The digraphs hl and dl

Two consonant combinations that appear in family and kinship vocabulary are hl and dl. Both are lateral fricatives and require care.

Pronunciation Focus — hl and dl

hl is a voiceless lateral fricative. Place the tongue tip behind the upper teeth as if to make an l, but allow air to escape at the sides without vibrating the vocal cords. The sound is similar to the Welsh ll in Llandudno. It is not the same as English hl pronounced as two separate sounds.

dl is the voiced counterpart of hl. The tongue position is the same, but the vocal cords vibrate. Think of it as a voiced hl.

These sounds appear in words such as uhlelo (plan; schedule) and udlala (he/she plays). In this chapter, pay attention to the hl sequence in adjective forms and family vocabulary as your instructor or audio model demonstrates them.

Tone in possessive phrases

Tone in isiZulu is a property of the word and the phrase as a whole, not of individual syllables in isolation. Possessive phrases, such as ubaba wami (my father), have a characteristic tonal shape that differs from the two words spoken in isolation. Listen for the phrase melody rather than trying to mark individual syllables. Your goal at this stage is accurate imitation of verified whole-phrase models. Specific tonal patterns are not written in standard isiZulu spelling.

Grammar Instruction

4.1 Class 1a and class 2a nouns

Purpose: recognize and use the second important pair of human noun classes and understand how they relate to class 1/2.

In Chapter 2 you learned class 1 and class 2, the pair that includes most common human nouns with the prefixes umu- (singular) and aba- (plural). IsiZulu has a second human-noun pair called class 1a and class 2a. These classes contain personal names, many kinship terms, and selected other human nouns.

Language Building Block — Class 1a and class 2a
Class 1a: u- + noun stem   |   Class 2a: o- + noun stem
Class 1a singular uses the prefix u-. Class 2a plural uses the prefix o-. These are distinct from the umu-/aba- prefixes of class 1/2.
ubaba → obaba   |   umama → omama   |   ugogo → ogogo
father → fathers   |   mother → mothers   |   grandmother → grandmothers

Class 1a and class 2a nouns behave similarly to class 1 and class 2 for the purposes of agreement. The key difference is in the noun prefix itself. Both pairs use the same subject concord for singular and plural.

Class Prefix Subject concord (singular) Subject concord (plural) Example noun
1 / 2 umu- / aba- u- ba- umfundi / abafundi
1a / 2a u- / o- u- ba- ubaba / obaba

The singular subject concord is u- for both class 1 and class 1a nouns. The plural subject concord is ba- for both class 2 and class 2a nouns. This means the verb agreement you have already practised carries over directly to kinship nouns. What is new here is the noun prefix shape.

  • Ubaba uyafunda. u-baba + u-ya-fund-a Father is studying. / My father is studying.
  • Omama bafunda isiZulu. o-mama + ba-fund-a The mothers are studying isiZulu.
  • Ugogo uhlala eThekwini. u-gogo + u-hlab-a eThekwini Grandmother lives in Durban.

4.2 Possessive pronominal forms

Purpose: express whose something is using the correct possessive pronominal form, which is controlled by the possessed noun's class.

In English, possessives come before the noun: my mother, her friend. In isiZulu, the possessive form follows the noun and is controlled by the class of the noun being possessed, not the class of the possessor.

Grammar Note — Possessive concord agrees with the possessed noun

In isiZulu, the possessive is formed from a concord that matches the class of the noun that is possessed, followed by the possessor. This means "my father" and "my friend" will have different possessive forms because ubaba and umngane belong to different classes.

For human classes (class 1/2 and 1a/2a), the possessive concord for a singular possessed noun is wa-, and for a plural possessed noun it is ba-. These are then combined with the pronominal possessor stem.

The table below shows the active possessive pronominal forms for singular (class 1/1a) possessed nouns. Plural forms (class 2/2a) follow the same pattern with ba- as the base.

Person Possessive form (class 1/1a singular) Example English
1st singular (my) wami ubaba wami my father
2nd singular (your) wakho umama wakho your mother
3rd singular (his / her) wakhe umngane wakhe his / her friend
1st plural (our) wethu umndeni wethu our family
2nd plural (your, plural) wenu ugogo wenu your grandmother (speaking to more than one person)
3rd plural (their) wabo ubhuti wabo their brother

For plural possessed nouns (class 2 or 2a), the base changes from wa- to ba-: obaba bami (my fathers), abangane bakhe (his/her friends). The pronominal stems remain the same. You are not yet expected to produce all these plural forms independently; focus on singular possessed nouns at this stage and learn the plural examples by recognition.

  • ubaba wami u-baba + wa-mi my father
  • umama wakho u-mama + wa-kho your mother
  • umngane wakhe u-mngane + wa-khe his friend / her friend
  • umndeni wethu u-mndeni + we-thu our family (note vowel change: wa + ethu → wethu)
Grammar Note — Vowel coalescence in possessive forms

When the possessive concord base wa- combines with a possessor stem beginning with e-, the two vowels merge: wa + ethu becomes wethu, not waethu. Similarly, wa + enu becomes wenu. This is a regular spelling rule in isiZulu, not an exception. Write the merged form in standard text; use the unmerged analysis only in grammar explanations.

4.3 Possession with na-based constructions

Purpose: state what someone has using the appropriate na-based construction for human classes.

To say that someone has something, isiZulu uses a construction built on na- (with; and; having), combined with a subject concord. For the first and second persons, the form combines the subject concord directly with na- and the possessed noun.

Language Building Block — Having something
Subject concord + na + noun
The subject concord fuses with na- to produce a single word. The noun that follows is the thing possessed.
Nginabangane.  |  Unemindeni.  |  Sinabantwana.
I have friends.  |  He/she has a family.  |  We have children.

The age expression Uneminyaka e___ uses this same construction: una- + iminyaka (years) = uneminyaka. The e- of iminyaka fuses with the final -a of una-, producing une-. This is the same vowel coalescence you saw in possessive forms. Treat the age formula as a learned construction for now; you will see this pattern again throughout the course.

  • Uneminyaka engamashumi amathathu. une- + iminyaka + e + amashumi amathathu He/she is thirty years old.
  • Sinabantwana ababili. sina- + abantwana ababili We have two children.

4.4 Adjective concords for description

Purpose: describe people using canonical adjective stems with the correct class 1/1a adjective concord.

IsiZulu has a limited set of canonical adjective stems. These stems require a concord prefix controlled by the noun they describe. For class 1 and class 1a singular nouns, the adjective concord is om- or om(u)- depending on the first consonant of the stem. For class 2 and class 2a plural nouns, the concord is aba-.

The adjective follows the noun it describes.

Language Building Block — Adjective phrase
Noun (class 1/1a) + adjective concord + adjective stem
For class 1/1a singular: the concord prefix is om- or um-, which combines with the adjective stem. For class 2/2a plural: the concord prefix is aba-. The adjective follows the noun it modifies.
umngane omude  |  ubaba omkhulu  |  abangane abahle
a tall friend  |  a big/great father  |  beautiful friends
Adjective stem Class 1/1a form (singular) Class 2/2a form (plural) Example
-de (tall)omudeabadeumfundi omude / abafundi abade
-fushane (short)omfushaneabafushaneingane emfushane (receptive; class 9 form)
-khulu (big; old)omkhuluabakhuluumuntu omkhulu / abantu abakhulu
-ncane (small; young)omncaneabancaneumngane omncane / abangane abancane
-hle (beautiful)omuhleabahleugogo omuhle / ogogo abahle
-qatha (strong)omqathaabaqathaubaba omqatha / obaba abaqatha

The forms in the table represent the main productive pattern for this chapter. Notice that some stems cause the concord prefix to appear as om- (before stems beginning with a consonant cluster or nasal) and omu- (before stems beginning with a single consonant). These surface differences are phonologically conditioned and will become more familiar through practice. Do not try to derive every form from a rule at this stage; work from the verified examples in the table and practise them in full noun phrases.

  • umngane omude u-mngane + o-mu-de a tall friend
  • ugogo omuhle u-gogo + o-mu-hle a beautiful grandmother
  • Ubaba wami omqatha uyasebenza. u-baba wa-mi o-m-qatha + u-ya-sebenz-a My strong father is working.
  • Abangane bethu abahle bayafunda. aba-ngane be-thu aba-hle + ba-ya-fund-a Our beautiful friends are studying.
Grammar Note — Do not place adjectives before the noun

A common error at this stage is to place the adjective before the noun as in English: *omude umngane. In isiZulu the adjective follows the noun and takes its own concord prefix. The noun comes first, the adjective concord plus stem comes second. This ordering applies consistently across the course. Practise each adjective phrase as a complete unit: umngane omude, not *omude umngane.

Language Focus

Three systems are active in this chapter and they work together: class 1a/2a nouns behave like class 1/2 for verb agreement; possessive forms are controlled by the possessed noun's class; and adjective concords are also controlled by the noun's class. This means that once you know a noun's class, you have the key to all three types of agreement. A noun's class is therefore the single most important piece of information about any isiZulu noun.

A second key principle: in isiZulu, the possessive follows the noun, and so does the adjective. This is the opposite of English word order in both cases. Make this your default expectation from the very start: noun first, then everything that qualifies or modifies it.

4.5 Numbers reviewed: counting people

Purpose: use numbers up to 100 in age expressions and to state how many people are in a group.

You have now seen numbers from 0 to 100. When counting people, the number is placed after the noun it quantifies. Full numeral agreement across all noun classes will be developed progressively; at this stage, focus on using the decade forms correctly in age expressions and in simple quantity statements involving human nouns.

  • Sinabantwana abathathu. We have three children.
  • Uneminyaka engamashumi amahlanu. He/she is fifty years old.
  • Nginabangane abahlanu. I have five friends.

The number forms shown here use the class 2 concord aba- with the number stem for human plurals, as in abantwana abathathu. This is a specific instance of numeral agreement. The full numeral agreement system across all noun classes is introduced gradually; you are not expected to derive every class's numeral form independently at this stage.

Model Dialogue

Isimo: Ngemuva keklasi, uSipho noLindiwe baxoxa ngomndeni wabo. USipho ufake izithombe zomndeni wakhe kukheli lakhe. ULindiwe akaziwa ezinye izihlobo zikasSipho. Ubudlelwane: izinhlobo zebanga lokuqala.
Setting: After class, Sipho and Lindiwe are talking about their families. Sipho has pulled up family photos on his phone. Lindiwe does not yet know Sipho's relatives. Relationship: classmates who are getting to know each other.

SIPHO Lindiwe, ngifuna ukukubonisa umndeni wami. Lindiwe, I want to show you my family. [ngifuna + ukukubonisa is introduced here as a formulaic expression; the full grammar of infinitives with object concords is developed in Chapter 8]
LINDIWE Yebo, ngicela ukubona! Yes, please let me see!

(USipho ubonisa isithombe sokuqala, esinabantu ababili. — Sipho shows the first photo, which has two people in it.)

SIPHO Lona ngubaba wami. Ubaba wami omqatha futhi omude. Uneminyaka engamashumi amahlanu. This is my father. My father is strong and tall. He is fifty years old.
LINDIWE Ohhh, ubaba wakho omuhle! Usebenzani? Oh, your father is handsome! What does he do for work?
SIPHO Uyathisha. Lo ogezolwandle ngumalume wami. He is a teacher. The one in the blue shirt is my maternal uncle. [ogezolwandle = blue/sea-coloured; receptive only — do not produce independently]

(USipho ubonisa isithombe sesibili, esinomuntu wesifazane omkhulu. — Sipho shows a second photo, which has an older woman in it.)

SIPHO Lona ngugogo wami. Ugogo wami omuhle futhi omqatha. Uneminyaka engamashumi ayisikhombisa nane. This is my grandmother. My grandmother is beautiful and strong. She is seventy-four years old.
LINDIWE Maye! Nawe uneminyaka emingaki? Wow! And how old are you?
SIPHO Mina? Uneminyaka engamashumi amabili nambili. Nawe? Me? I am twenty-two years old. And you?
LINDIWE Nami uneminyaka engamashumi amabili. Nginabangane abaningi lapha eThekwini, kodwa umndeni wami uhlala eGoli. I am also twenty years old. I have many friends here in Durban, but my family lives in Johannesburg. [kodwa = but; eGoli is the common name for Johannesburg]
SIPHO Umndeni wakho mkhulu? Is your family big?
LINDIWE Cha, omncane. Nginaye ubaba, umama, nobhuti wami omncane. Baphila kahle bonke. No, it is small. I have father, mother, and my little brother. They are all well. [baphila kahle bonke = they are all well; receptive at this stage]
SIPHO Kuhle lokho. Nginomndeni omkhulu — nginabafowethu ababili, izingane zakabhuti, nabangane abaningi. Bafana nabangane. That is good. I have a big family — I have two brothers, the children of my older relatives, and many friends. They feel like friends. [formulaic expression; enjoy for meaning]

Several expressions in this dialogue, including kodwa (but), baphila kahle (they are well), and bonke (all), contain grammar or vocabulary beyond the current chapter scope. They are included as recognition items to make the conversation sound natural. Focus your productive study on the items in the Grammar section.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • Possessive forms follow the noun: ubaba wami, not *wami ubaba.
  • The possessive form matches the class of the possessed noun, not the possessor.
  • Adjective phrases follow the noun: ubaba omqatha, not *omqatha ubaba.
  • The adjective concord prefix matches the class of the noun it describes.
  • Verb words are written as single conjunctive units without spaces or hyphens.
  • The age formula is written as Uneminyaka e___ with no extra spaces inside the verb word.
  • Class 1a nouns use the prefix u- (singular) and o- (plural), not umu- or aba-.
  • Each sentence begins with a capital letter and ends with a full stop.
  • No analytical hyphens appear in the ordinary writing of any verb or possessive form.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Family and household in isiZulu-speaking communities

In many isiZulu-speaking households, the concept of family, rendered as umndeni, often extends well beyond the nuclear unit of two parents and their children. Grandparents, aunts, uncles, cousins, and community members may be part of daily household life, share childcare responsibilities, and be addressed with kinship terms that signal closeness and respect rather than biological relationship alone. An uncle may be addressed as ubaba omncane (little father, or younger father), and an aunt as umama omncane, acknowledging their role as parental figures.

Kinship terms frequently function as forms of respectful address beyond their literal meanings. Ugogo (grandmother) or umkhulu (grandfather) may be used to address any elder with warmth and respect, not only one's biological grandparents. Context determines the meaning, and learners should not assume a biological relationship is always implied.

Household structures vary widely across isiZulu-speaking communities, including those in KwaZulu-Natal, Gauteng, and urban areas throughout South Africa. Rural and urban households may differ significantly in size, composition, and social organization. Avoid assuming that any single household pattern is universal.

Culture in Context — Names, clan names, and respectful address

IsiZulu-speaking communities have a rich tradition of personal names, surnames, and clan names called izithakazelo. Clan names are praise names associated with one's patrilineal ancestry and carry significant identity and social meaning. Knowing a person's clan name is often a way of showing respect and recognizing their heritage.

Personal names in isiZulu frequently carry meaning: names like Siyabonga (we are grateful), Nompumelelo (mother of success), and Zinhle (beautiful things) are chosen deliberately. Do not assume that an isiZulu name is merely decorative or that you can assign cultural meaning to a name without knowledge of that individual's family and context.

The izithakazelo require careful specialist treatment. Do not attempt to produce or teach clan-name sequences without qualified isiZulu-language review.

Regional Note — Kinship terms across isiZulu-speaking communities

Kinship terminology varies across regions and communities within isiZulu-speaking South Africa. The terms in this chapter represent broadly used Standard isiZulu forms. Communities in KwaZulu-Natal, Gauteng, and peri-urban areas may use additional terms, locally preferred forms, or code-switched equivalents for some kinship roles.

For example, the widely used informal terms ubhuti (brother; from English brother) and usisi (sister) are common across urban communities in South Africa among isiZulu, isiXhosa, and other speakers, and reflect multilingual contact. These are the forms introduced in this chapter because of their widespread productive use. Verify the forms preferred by any specific community you are connecting with.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Placing the possessive before the noun

Error: Writing *wami ubaba to mean "my father," by direct transfer from English word order.

Correction: In isiZulu, the possessive form follows the noun: ubaba wami. This is the opposite of English order. Every time you want to say "my ___" or "your ___" in isiZulu, think: noun first, possessive form second. Adjectives follow the same principle.

Common Mistake — Making the possessive agree with the possessor instead of the possessed noun

Error: Trying to change the possessive concord based on who is doing the possessing rather than what is being possessed. For example, using *abangane wami instead of abangane bami (my friends, where abangane is class 2 plural).

Correction: The possessive concord is controlled entirely by the class of the noun being possessed. Abangane (friends) is class 2, so the possessive base is ba-, giving abangane bami. Singular nouns like umngane (friend, class 1) take wa-, giving umngane wami. The identity of the possessor — whether "I," "you," or "they" — does not change the concord base; only the stem after it changes.

Common Mistake — Placing the adjective before the noun

Error: Writing *omude umngane (intended meaning: a tall friend), placing the adjective phrase before the noun as in English.

Correction: In isiZulu, the adjective phrase follows the noun: umngane omude. The adjective concord prefix must also match the class of the noun. Both the position and the concord are different from English. Practise each adjective phrase as a complete unit.

Common Mistake — Using umu- prefix for class 1a nouns

Error: Writing *umumama or *umumkhulu by applying the class 1 prefix umu- to class 1a kinship nouns.

Correction: Class 1a nouns use the prefix u- (singular): umama, ugogo, umkhulu, ubaba. Their plurals use o-: omama, ogogo, omkhulu, obaba. The class 1 prefix umu- belongs to a different group of human nouns such as umuntu and umfundi. Learning the noun prefix correctly from the start prevents persistent concord errors later.

Common Mistake — Writing spaces or hyphens inside verb words

Error: Writing *u ne minyaka or *u-ne-minyaka with spaces or hyphens inside the possession construction.

Correction: The na-based possession construction produces a single conjunctive verb word in standard isiZulu. Write uneminyaka as one unbroken word. Analytical hyphens appear only in grammar explanations inside this textbook, never in ordinary writing. Preserving standard word boundaries is one of the most important orthographic habits to develop from the very beginning of the course.

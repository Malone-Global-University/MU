Izithuthi, izindlela nezindawo zomphakathi

Transport, Directions, and Public Places · Speak and Write Zulu: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common forms of transport and key public places in and around a South African city using productive vocabulary from established noun classes, with particular attention to class 5/6 and class 7/8 transport nouns and class 3/4 and class 9/10 place nouns at a receptive level.
  2. Ask for directions in a public setting using the question frame Ngifika kanjani e___? (How do I get to ___?) and the established polite opening Uxolo, ngicela usizo. (Excuse me, I would like some help.)
  3. Give simple directions using the command forms from Chapter 11 combined with directional adverbs, including phambili (forward / straight ahead), ekhohlo (to the left), kwesokudla (to the right), emuva (behind / back), and lapha (here).
  4. State your intended destination and mode of transport using the motion verb -hamba (to go / to travel) and the locative patterns consolidated in Chapters 11 and 13, combined with the future tense from Chapter 12.
  5. Ask about and state the price and availability of transport using the established frames Kubiza malini? (How much does it cost?) and Ikhona i___? (Is there a ___?) recycled from earlier chapters.
  6. Use the ability frame ngikwazi uku- and angikwazi uku- from Chapter 12 in transport and navigation contexts, explaining what you can or cannot do or find.
  7. Recognize class 3/4 place nouns such as umgwaqo (road / street) and umtapo (library) at a receptive level and begin to use selected high-frequency class 3/4 nouns in simple sentences with supported subject-concord guidance.
  8. Read a simple public transport notice, a hand-drawn direction card, and a short dialogue about getting around in isiZulu.
  9. Write a short set of directions from one place to another, or a brief message explaining how you will travel to a destination.
  10. Distinguish between asking where something is (Ikuphi i___?) and asking how to get there (Ngifika kanjani e___?), and use both frames in realistic navigation exchanges.

Communication Scenario

It is Saturday morning in Durban. Lwazi, visiting from Pietermaritzburg for the weekend, is standing outside the Victoria Street Market and needs to reach the beachfront taxi rank to catch a minibus taxi back to the city centre. He does not know this part of Durban well. He stops a passer-by and asks for help. The passer-by gives him directions in isiZulu, tells him which taxi to take, and explains roughly how long it will take. Lwazi thanks her and sets off. Later in the day, he needs to find a pharmacy. He stops a street vendor and asks again. This time the vendor tells him to walk to the next corner, turn left, and look for the large pharmacy near the shopping centre.

  1. Think about the last time you had to ask someone for directions in any language. What information did you need: the name of a place, the direction to walk, the mode of transport, or the distance? In English, list the three most useful pieces of information when you are lost in an unfamiliar city.
  2. You already know the polite request opener Uxolo, ngicela usizo and the motion verb -hamba. How do you think these would appear in a request for directions? Write one sentence in isiZulu that uses either of these, or explain in English how they might function.

Pronunciation and Tone

Direction adverbs and their stress patterns

The direction adverbs in this chapter are mostly two- or three-syllable words with consistent penultimate stress. Practise each one aloud, making sure that the second-to-last syllable is slightly lengthened at a phrase boundary:

pham-bi-li  |  e-mu-va  |  e-kho-hlo  |  kwe-so-ku-dla  |  e-du-ze  |  ku-de

Note that kwesokudla (to the right) has four syllables and may feel longer than the others. Break it as kwe-so-ku-dla. The final cluster -dla is a single syllable: the dl is a lateral affricate produced in one motion, not two separate sounds. Do not insert a vowel between the d and the l.

Pronunciation Focus — The lateral affricate dl in direction vocabulary

The cluster dl appears in several important words in this chapter: kwesokudla (to the right) and the verb root -dla (to eat), which you know from earlier chapters. The sound is produced by placing the tongue against the upper gum ridge (alveolar ridge) as if starting a d, then releasing air over the sides of the tongue as in a lateral sound, all in a single movement. It is not the same as English "dl" in a word like "handlebars," where the two sounds are in different syllables.

A common learner error is to replace the dl with simply an l, producing something like *kwesokula. Another error is to separate the sounds into two syllables: *kwe-so-ku-de-la. Practise: say dla as a single syllable several times, then place it at the end of kwesoku-. A qualified isiZulu speaker or verified audio resource should be used to confirm your model before independent practice.

Transport loanwords and their isiZulu syllable structure

Most transport vocabulary in this chapter consists of loanwords adapted into isiZulu phonology. These words follow standard isiZulu syllable patterns even though they originated in English or Afrikaans. Each syllable in an isiZulu word ends in a vowel, and consonant clusters that would exist in the source language are broken up by inserted vowels. Compare the English and isiZulu forms:

Practise each loanword at an even syllable pace. Avoid shortening them back toward the English source pronunciation. When you say ithekisi, all four syllables should be distinct, with the penultimate ki slightly lengthened at a phrase boundary.

The question word kanjani

The question word kanjani (how?) contains four syllables: ka-nja-ni. The second syllable nja is a palatal nasal followed by a vowel. The nj cluster functions as a single prenasalized consonant: do not break it into two sounds or insert a pause between the nasal and the j. Practise: ka-nja-ni with a slightly lengthened penultimate syllable -nja- when it falls before a phrase boundary. The full frame Ngifika kanjani e___? has a rising intonation at the end, which signals a genuine open question.

Grammar Instruction

14.1 Asking where something is and how to get there

Purpose: use two distinct question frames to find a place and to ask for directions to it.

English uses "where is" for both location and direction in casual conversation, but isiZulu uses two distinct frames depending on what you need to know. The frame Ikuphi i___? asks where something is located. The frame Ngifika kanjani e___? asks how to reach it, requesting the route rather than just the location. Both frames are productive at this stage and essential for independent navigation.

Language Building Block — Location vs. directions
Ikuphi i___?   |   Ngifika kanjani e___?
Ikuphi uses the class 5 subject concord i- with -kuphi (where?). The question asks for a location. Ngifika kanjani uses the first-person -fika (to arrive / to reach) with kanjani (how?). This question asks for a method or route. For the second frame, insert the locative form of the destination in the blank after e-: e.g. Ngifika kanjani eposini? = How do I get to the post office?
Ikuphi ibhange?  |  Ngifika kanjani ebhange?
Where is the bank?  |  How do I get to the bank?
  • Ikuphi isitolo esikhulu? Where is the large shop / shopping centre?
  • Ngifika kanjani esiteshini sethekisi? How do I get to the taxi rank?
  • Ikuphi iposi? Where is the post office?
  • Ngifika kanjani esibhedlela? How do I get to the hospital?
  • Ikuphi imakethe? Where is the market?

14.2 Giving directions using command forms and direction adverbs

Purpose: combine the positive command forms from Chapter 11 with directional adverbs to give clear, useful directions in isiZulu.

Giving directions in isiZulu uses the same command-form verb structure you practised in Chapter 11 and applied to health instructions in Chapter 13. A direction consists of one or more commands in sequence, each specifying a movement, a turn, or a landmark. The key new element in this chapter is the set of directional adverbs that follow the command verb. These adverbs are invariant, meaning they do not change for person, class, or tense. They simply follow the command verb and locate the movement in space.

PhendukaekhohloPhenduka ekhohlo.Turn left.
PhendukakwesokudlaPhenduka kwesokudla.Turn right.

HambaeduzeHamba eduze.Go nearby / Keep it close.
KhwelaibhasiKhwela ibhasi.Board the bus. / Get on the bus.

Directions are typically given as a short sequence of two to four commands. Connect them with the conjunction bese (and then) or futhi (and also) for a more natural flow, or simply list them in order without a connector, which is equally natural in spoken isiZulu direction-giving.

  • Hamba phambili. Phenduka kwesokudla emgwaqweni wokuqala. Ibhange likhona lapho. Go straight ahead. Turn right at the first road. The bank is there.
  • Hamba phambili, bese uphenduka ekhohlo. Isitolo sikhona eduze. Go straight ahead, then turn left. The shop is nearby.
  • Khwela ithekisi eliya eThekwini. Ehla esiteshini sesitimela. Board a taxi going to Durban. Get off at the train station.
  • Hamba phambili izigidi ezimbili. Phenduka ekhohlo. Iposi likhona eceleni kwesikole. Go straight ahead two blocks. Turn left. The post office is there next to the school.

Note on bese: The connector bese (and then; after that) links sequential actions. When it introduces the following verb, that verb takes a participial-like form in natural isiZulu: bese uphenduka (and then you turn). This combined form is introduced here receptively within natural direction-giving sequences. Your productive target is to use bese before a full present-tense verb form, which is a common and natural pattern that does not require full analysis of the participial system at this stage.

Practice — Giving directions

Write a two- or three-step direction sequence for each situation using command forms and direction adverbs.

  1. Tell someone to go straight ahead, turn right, and stop at the clinic.
  2. Tell someone to board the bus, go to the city centre, and get off at the market.
  3. Tell someone the pharmacy is nearby, to the left of the bank.

14.3 Stating destination and mode of transport using motion verbs and the future tense

Purpose: combine the motion verb -hamba, transport vocabulary, and the future marker -zo- from Chapter 12 to state where you are going and how you will get there.

When you want to explain your travel plans, you need three pieces of information in isiZulu: how you will travel, where you will go, and when. The motion verb -hamba (to go / to travel) serves as the main action verb. The destination is expressed using a locative, which you have been building across Chapters 7, 11, and 13. The future marker -zo- places the whole plan in future time.

Language Building Block — Travel plan statement
Ngi-zo-hamba nge-[transport] e-[destination locative] ngo-[time].
The subject concord ngi- carries the first-person meaning. The future marker -zo- slots in before the verb root. The transport is expressed using nge- + transport noun (by bus: ngebhasi; by taxi: ngethekisi; by train: ngezitimela; on foot: ngezinyawo). The destination uses the locative prefix e- plus the place noun in its locative form. The time reference recycles patterns from Chapter 8.
Ngizohamba ngebhasi eThekwini ngo-8 ekuseni.
I will travel by bus to Durban at 8 in the morning.

  • USipho uzohamba ngezinyawo esitolo. Akukude. Sipho will walk to the shop. It is not far.
  • Sizohamba ngebhasi eThekwini ngoMgqibelo. We will travel by bus to Durban on Saturday.
  • Uzohamba ngemoto eposini namuhla ntambama. She will travel by car to the post office this afternoon.
  • Ngizohamba ngezinyawo ebhange. Ikhona eduze. I will walk to the bank. It is nearby.

Practice — Travel plans

Write a complete travel plan sentence for each cue. Include the mode of transport, the destination in locative form, and a time expression.

  1. You will travel by taxi to the hospital tomorrow morning at 9.
  2. Nomvula will walk to the pharmacy. It is nearby.
  3. We will travel by bus to the market on Saturday.
  4. They (plural) will travel by car to the school this afternoon. (Use babo for they, receptive subject concord ba-.)

14.4 Asking about transport cost and availability

Purpose: apply the established cost-question frame and the existence question to transport and navigation encounters.

At a taxi rank or bus stop, the two most practical questions are how much the fare is and whether a particular service goes to your destination. Both questions recycle frames you already know. The cost question Kubiza malini? (How much does it cost?) comes from Chapter 6. The existence or destination check uses the copulative verb -ya (to go to / to go towards), which you know as the present-tense active marker and also as a motion verb meaning to go to a specific place.

  • Kubiza malini ithekisi eliya eThekwini? How much does a taxi to Durban cost?
  • Ibhasi liya esiteshini sesitimela? Does the bus go to the train station?
  • Ithekisi likhona elikhona eliya emaketheni? Is there a taxi that goes to the market? (receptive combined form; use Ikhona ithekisi elikhona eliya emaketheni? or the simpler Ithekisi liya emaketheni? productively)
  • Kubiza malini ibhasi elikhona elikhona elikhona elikhona elikhona elikhona elikhona elikhona elikhona? (avoid; simply ask:) Kubiza malini ibhasi? = How much does the bus cost?

Note on transport-noun concords in fare questions: In the question Ibhasi liya e___? the class 5 subject concord for ibhasi is li- (not i-), because class 5 nouns use li- as their subject concord in predicates. Similarly, ithekisi liya uses li-. These concords are presented here receptively within formulaic transport expressions. You do not yet need to generate them independently from scratch: learn the whole expressions Ibhasi liya e___? and Ithekisi liya e___? as productive units at this stage, and understand that the li- concord belongs to the class 5 noun class. Full class 5 concord production is developed progressively in later chapters.

Language Focus

This chapter integrates three systems in a navigation context: the location-question and direction-question frames are new productive items; the command forms for giving directions transfer directly from Chapter 11 and Chapter 13; and the travel plan statements combine the future tense from Chapter 12 with locatives from Chapters 7, 11, and 13. The cost question and transport availability checks recycle frames from Chapter 6. The genuinely new productive grammar in this chapter is the frame Ngifika kanjani e___?, the direction adverbs, the boarding and alighting verbs -khwela and -ehla, and the transport-means expressions using nge- plus a transport noun. Keep your productive energy on these new items while letting the recycled systems carry the rest of each exchange.

Model Dialogue

Isimo: ULwazi umi ngaphandle kweVictoria Street Market eThekwini. Ufuna ukuya esiteshini sethekisi ukuze abone ithekisi elikhona elikhona elikhona elikhona elikhona elikhona elikhona elikhona elikhona elikhona elikhona elikhona elikhona elikhona elikhona. Aphenduke kumuntu odlulayo, uNokukhanya, acele usizo. Ubudlelwane: izihambeli ezingaxhumene; izinga lokuhlonipha elilungele inkulumo yemphakathi phakathi kwezinye izifiki.
Setting: Lwazi is standing outside the Victoria Street Market in Durban. He needs to get to the taxi rank to find a taxi back to the city centre. He approaches a passer-by, Nokukhanya, and asks for help. Relationship: strangers; respectful but approachable register appropriate to a public encounter between adults.

ULWAZI Sawubona. Uxolo, ngicela usizo. Hello. Excuse me, I would like some help.
UNOKUKHANYA Yebo, ngikusize kanjani? Yes, how can I help you?
ULWAZI Ngifika kanjani esiteshini sethekisi? Ngifuna ithekisi eliya emzini waseThekwini. How do I get to the taxi rank? I want a taxi that goes to the Durban city centre.
UNOKUKHANYA Kulungile. Hamba phambili emgwaqweni. Phenduka kwesokudla emgwaqweni wokuqala. Isiteshi sethekisi sikhona lapho, eduze nesitolo esikhulu. All right. Go straight ahead on the road. Turn right at the first road. The taxi rank is there, near the large shop.
ULWAZI Ngiyabonga. Kude yini? Thank you. Is it far?
UNOKUKHANYA Cha, akukude. Uhamba ngezinyawo izigidi ezimbili kuphela. Lihamba imizuzwana eyishumi noma eyishumi nantlanu. No, it is not far. You walk only two blocks. It takes ten or fifteen minutes.
ULWAZI Kulungile. Uma ngifinyelele esiteshini, ngifika kanjani ngesitimela emzini waseThekwini? All right. When I reach the rank, how do I get by taxi to the Durban city centre? (Note: Lwazi uses ngesitimela loosely here to mean by transport; a qualified speaker would clarify that the taxi rank is for taxis, not trains.)
UNOKUKHANYA Khwela ithekisi elikhomba "City Centre." Kubiza amarandi ambalwa. Ungaceli indlela ekhona, bangakusiza bona. Board a taxi that shows "City Centre." It costs a few rand. You can also ask for directions there; they can help you.
ULWAZI Ngiyabonga kakhulu! Hamba kahle. Thank you very much! Go well.
UNOKUKHANYA Sala kahle. Uhambe kahle! Stay well. Travel well!

Several expressions in this dialogue are receptive at this stage: Ngikusize kanjani (how can I help you, with object concord -ku-), eduze nesitolo esikhulu (near the large shop, with the copulative ne- connector), izigidi ezimbili (two blocks), imizuzwana eyishumi (ten minutes), Uma ngifinyelele (when I reach, conditional), elikhomba (that shows, relative clause), and amarandi ambalwa (a few rand). Focus your productive study on the direction-request frames, direction commands, and travel plan statements identified in the Grammar section.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. ULwazi ufuna ukuphi? Yiliphi izithuthi afuna ukukhwela zona? (Where does Lwazi want to go? Which transport does he want to board?)
  2. UNokukhanya unika uLwazi izinyathelo ezingaki zokukhomba indlela? Bhala izinyathelo ngesiZulu. (How many direction steps does Nokukhanya give Lwazi? Write the steps in isiZulu.)
  3. Isiteshi sethekisi sikude yini? UNokukhanya uthi kuthatha isikhathi esingakanani ukuhamba ngezinyawo? (Is the taxi rank far? How long does Nokukhanya say it takes to walk?)
  4. UNokukhanya uthi uLwazi akenze ini esiteshini? (What does Nokukhanya tell Lwazi to do at the rank?)
  5. Yiliphi igama lokukhulumisana elisetshenziswa ukusho "hamba kahle" kumuntu ohambayo? Bhala izinhlobo ezimbili ezisetshenziswayo kulesi sahluko. (Which farewell expression is used to say "travel well" to someone leaving? Write both forms used in this dialogue.)

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — The minibus taxi in South African public life

The minibus taxi (ithekisi) is the dominant form of public transport for the majority of South Africans and is particularly important in KwaZulu-Natal and Durban. Minibus taxis are privately owned vehicles, typically sixteen-seater minibuses, that operate on established routes from taxi ranks (iziteshi zamathekisi). They are generally cheaper and more flexible than buses or trains, and they depart when full rather than on a fixed timetable. Interaction at a taxi rank involves a degree of directness: passengers call out their destination, drivers or assistants call out destinations to attract passengers, and boarding and alighting are communicated quickly using simple commands. The ability to name your destination clearly in isiZulu and to confirm the route with the driver is genuinely useful in KwaZulu-Natal.

Fares are typically paid to the driver's assistant (ikhondukheta, from English "conductor"), who may also call out the route to attract passengers. In some contexts, the driver's assistant will be the one to answer your destination question. Knowing the direct question Ithekisi liya e___? (Does this taxi go to ___?) and the fare question Kubiza malini? (How much does it cost?) will serve you in these interactions.

Culture in Context — Asking for directions in public

In many isiZulu-speaking communities, stopping a stranger to ask for directions is a common and entirely acceptable social interaction, but the way you open the conversation matters. Beginning with Uxolo (Excuse me; literally: pardon) or Sawubona (Hello) before making your request is expected. Moving directly into a question without any social acknowledgment is considered abrupt and can feel rude, particularly to older speakers. A brief greeting before the request signals that you recognize the other person and value their help, even in a quick transactional exchange.

It is also culturally common for a person giving directions to offer more information than you asked for, including personal observations, additional landmarks, or general reassurance about the route. This extended help is a sign of goodwill, not a sign that the directions are complex. Respond with thanks and a positive closing such as Ngiyabonga kakhulu (Thank you very much) or Hamba kahle (Go well).

Regional Note — Transport and place-name variation across KwaZulu-Natal

Place names in KwaZulu-Natal and the wider South Africa often exist in both an isiZulu form and an English or Afrikaans form, and speakers move between both depending on context. The city of Durban is widely called eThekwini in isiZulu, which is its official name in the isiZulu language; it is also called Durban in English. Pietermaritzburg is referred to as eMgungundlovu in isiZulu. In public transport contexts, drivers and conductors may call out destinations using isiZulu place names, English names, or both. Familiarity with common isiZulu place names for the areas you intend to visit is practical preparation for real travel in KwaZulu-Natal.

Transport vocabulary also shows some regional variation. In some areas, the word i-taxi is used instead of ithekisi in casual speech. The term ibhasi consistently refers to a full-size bus rather than a minibus. In Johannesburg and other cities, isiZulu speakers navigate alongside Sesotho, Xhosa, and Zulu taxi-rank cultures that overlap in vocabulary. The forms in this chapter represent standard productive isiZulu as used in KwaZulu-Natal public contexts.

Culture in Context — Travel farewells and good-luck expressions

The two standard farewells Hamba kahle (Go well) and Sala kahle (Stay well) carry a subtle but important distinction: hamba kahle is said to the person who is leaving; sala kahle is said by the person who is leaving to those staying behind. Getting these the right way around shows cultural literacy and a command of the pragmatics that extends beyond simple vocabulary. In a direction-giving interaction, the person who gave you directions will typically say Hamba kahle to you as you leave, since they are remaining and you are going. You may respond with Sala kahle.

For a traveller setting off on a longer journey, a speaker might also add Uhambe kahle (May you travel well), which uses the subjunctive-like form uhambe to convey a wish or blessing rather than a direct command. This distinction between a direct command and a wish is receptive at this stage; learn Uhambe kahle as a formulaic travel blessing and use it appropriately in context without analyzing the full subjunctive system.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing Ikuphi and Ngifika kanjani

Error: Using *Ikuphi ikhliniki? when you actually want to know how to get to the clinic, and using *Ngifika kanjani ikhliniki? when you simply want to know where the clinic is located.

Correction: Ikuphi ikhliniki? asks where the clinic is located. The answer will be a place description: "It is on the left, near the market." Ngifika kanjani ekhliniki? asks how to reach the clinic and expects a set of directions in response. Note also that Ngifika kanjani uses the locative form of the destination after it: ekhliniki, not ikhliniki. The bare noun form after Ngifika kanjani is a common error. If you need location information only, use Ikuphi. If you need route information, use Ngifika kanjani e___ with the locative.

Common Mistake — Adding a subject concord to direction commands

Error: Writing *Uphambili or *Uphenduka ekhohlo instead of Hamba phambili or Phenduka ekhohlo, by adding a subject concord prefix to a command form.

Correction: This is the same error identified in Chapters 11 and 13 in the contexts of movement commands and health instructions. In direction-giving, command forms must be used without a subject concord. Phenduka ekhohlo is the correct form for "Turn left." *Uphenduka ekhohlo would be understood as a statement meaning "He/she turns to the left," not as an instruction. Review the command-form table in the Grammar section of this chapter if this pattern is not yet automatic: a command uses only the verb root plus the final vowel.

Common Mistake — Using a bare noun instead of a locative after Ngifika kanjani

Error: Writing *Ngifika kanjani ibhange? instead of Ngifika kanjani ebhange?, by omitting the locative prefix on the destination noun.

Correction: The frame Ngifika kanjani e___ requires a locative form after the e-. The destination is a place, and places in isiZulu are expressed in locative form when they function as destinations or locations. Ngifika kanjani ebhange (How do I get to the bank) uses the locative ebhange, not the bare noun ibhange. If you are uncertain about the locative form of a particular place noun, use the vocabulary table in this chapter and in Chapters 7, 11, and 13, which provide the locative forms you need at this stage.

Common Mistake — Writing nge- and the transport noun as two separate words

Error: Writing *nge ibhasi or *nge ithekisi instead of ngebhasi or ngethekisi, by inserting a space between the instrumental prefix and the noun.

Correction: The instrumental prefix nge- (by means of; using) is a bound morpheme: it attaches directly to the noun stem without a space. When it combines with a noun beginning with i-, the prefix nge- replaces or merges with the initial vowel: ibhasi → ngebhasi; ithekisi → ngethekisi. Write these as single continuous words. This is the same agglutinative principle that applies to all other verb words and locatives in isiZulu: they are written without internal spaces.

Common Mistake — Confusing Hamba kahle and Sala kahle

Error: Saying Sala kahle to someone who is leaving, or saying Hamba kahle to the person who is staying behind.

Correction: Hamba kahle (Go well) is said to the person departing, by the person who is staying. Sala kahle (Stay well) is said by the person departing, to the person who is staying. In a direction-giving encounter, you are the one leaving: as you walk away, the person who helped you will say Hamba kahle, and you can respond with Sala kahle or Ngiyabonga kakhulu. Getting this distinction right is a small but important marker of cultural fluency.

  • Passage 2 — Isiteshi: Destinations: eThekwini (Durban), eMlazi; Mode: taxi (amathekisi); Time: ngo-2 ntambama for eMlazi taxis; city taxis departing now.
  • Passage 3 — UMthokozisi: Destination: ekhliniki (clinic), then home; Mode: taxi (ngethekisi) to clinic, bus (ngebhasi) home; Time: ngo-9 ekuseni to clinic, ngo-4 ntambama pick-up.

Bonus: Accept akukude with a correct explanation (it is not far / the negative copulative of distance) and a well-formed new sentence using it, e.g., Ibhange akukude. Ngizohamba ngezinyawo.

Reading — Comprehension

  1. Umuntu uphenduka ekhohlo ngemuva kwesikole. Iphisi lomphakathi likhona eceleni kwendawo yokuthenga encane. (Turn left after the school; next to a small shopping area.)
  2. Ibhasi 32. Umuntu wehla emgwaqweni weSixth Avenue. (Bus 32; alight at Sixth Avenue.)
  3. ULwazi wahamba ngethekisi. Kubize amarandi ayisikhombisa. (By taxi; seven rand.)
  4. ULwazi uzohamba ngethekisi ekhliniki kusasa. Uzobiza uThabo uma ephuma ekhliniki. (By taxi to the clinic tomorrow; will call after leaving the clinic.)
  5. Accept any two future-tense sentences from the message, e.g., Ngizofika ekhaya ngo-6 ntambama. and Kusasa ngizohamba ngethekisi ekhliniki.

Chapter Review — Ingxenye A

  1. ibhasi / amabhasi (class 5/6); ithekisi / amathekisi (class 5/6); isitimela / izitimela (class 7/8); imoto / izimoto (class 9/10); ikhemisi / amakhemisi (class 5/6); iposi / amaposi (class 5/6); umtapo / imitapo (class 3/4, receptive class); imakethe / amakethe (class 5/6); umgwaqo / imigwaqo (class 3/4, receptive class); ibhayisikili / amabhayisikili (class 5/6).
  2. phambili = forward / straight ahead; ekhohlo = to the left; kwesokudla = to the right; emuva = behind / back; eduze = near / nearby; kude = far; eceleni = next to / beside; khona = there / present / existing.
  3. Hamba kahle is said to the person leaving, by the person staying. Sala kahle is said by the person leaving, to the person staying.
  4. Accept any two correct examples, e.g., ngebhasi (by bus) and ngethekisi (by taxi). Other correct answers: ngesitimela (by train), ngemoto (by car), ngezinyawo (on foot), ngebhayisikili (by bicycle).

Chapter Review — Ingxenye B

  1. (a) Ikuphi iposi? / Ngifika kanjani eposini? (b) Ikuphi isiteshi sesitimela? / Ngifika kanjani esiteshini sesitimela? (c) Ikuphi ikhliniki? / Ngifika kanjani ekhliniki?
  2. Accept any three well-formed direction steps using command forms (no subject concord prefix) and correct direction adverbs, e.g.: Hamba phambili. Phenduka kwesokudla. Ibhange likhona lapho.
  3. (a) Ngizohamba ngethekisi ekhliniki kusasa ekuseni ngo-8. (b) UNomvula uzohamba ngebhasi emaketheni ngoMgqibelo. (c) Sizohamba ngezinyawo ebhange. Akukude.
  4. Corrections: (a) Ngifika kanjani ebhange? — Use the locative form after Ngifika kanjani. (b) Phenduka kwesokudla. — Command form has no subject concord prefix. (c) Ngizohamba ngethekisi ekhliniki. — Write nge- fused with the transport noun as one word; no space between nge- and ithekisi.

Cumulative Review — Correct answers

Uxolo / kanjani / Ngifika kanjani / Hamba / Phenduka / lapho / yini / akukude / Hamba (said to UNokukhanya who is staying) / Sala (said by UNokukhanya as the staying person)

Note on the final farewell pair: ULwazi is leaving, so he says Hamba kahle to UNokukhanya only if UNokukhanya is also leaving. In this encounter, ULwazi is the one walking away; the correct forms are ULwazi says Ngiyabonga. Sala kahle! and UNokukhanya says Hamba kahle! or Uhambe kahle! Accept both orderings if a learner explains the logic correctly.

Looking Ahead

Okungakwenzeka manje — What you can do now

You can ask where a public place is and how to get there, give a sequence of direction steps using command forms and directional adverbs, state your destination and mode of transport using the future tense and nge- transport expressions, ask about transport fares and availability, and close a navigation interaction with culturally appropriate farewells. The command forms from Chapter 11, the future tense from Chapter 12, and the locative system developed across Chapters 7, 11, and 13 now work together in travel and navigation contexts. Chapter 15 will use this foundation to expand into descriptions of community roles, work, and school life, introducing more class 3/4 noun vocabulary productively and extending subject-concord control to third-person singular and plural forms for a wider range of noun classes.

Isahluko 15: Emphakathini, emsebenzini nasesikoleni

Chapter 15 addresses community life, work, and school. You will describe what people do for work, what their roles are in the community, and what happens at school, using stabilized concord patterns and a broader range of noun classes. Class 3/4 nouns, which appeared receptively in this chapter in words such as umgwaqo and umtapo, will be developed more actively as you encounter vocabulary for jobs and community roles. Motion verbs and locatives from Chapters 7, 11, 13, and 14 return as you describe where people work and go. Time expressions from Chapter 8, possession from Chapter 9, and the command and future forms from Chapters 11 and 12 all continue to appear in increasingly integrated practical communication.

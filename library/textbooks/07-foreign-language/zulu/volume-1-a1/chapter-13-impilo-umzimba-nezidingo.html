Impilo, umzimba nezidingo

Health, the Body, and Needs · Speak and Write Zulu: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common body parts using active vocabulary from established noun classes, focusing on class 3/4 and class 9/10 body-part nouns at a receptive level and class 5/6 and class 7/8 body-part nouns at a productive level.
  2. Describe common symptoms and physical feelings using the productive expression ubuhlungu-based pain statements and the formulaic frame ngiziwa ngi- (I feel) for describing how someone feels.
  3. State what someone is unable to do because of illness using the angikwazi uku- frame consolidated from Chapter 12, now applied in a health context.
  4. Ask for and offer help in a health setting using established cela and ngicela frames extended to clinic, pharmacy, and community health contexts.
  5. Make a simple appointment at a clinic or health facility using the positive future marker -zo- from Chapter 12 to state when someone will come and what they need.
  6. Use command and request patterns from Chapter 11 in a health-instruction context, including simple instructions given by a health worker.
  7. Recognize the class 9/10 subject concord i- and zi- in supported health vocabulary sentences, building toward fuller class 9/10 concord control in Chapter 14.
  8. Distinguish between expressing a current physical state and stating a future plan using the contrast between present-tense and future-tense verb forms.
  9. Read a simple clinic notice, a medication instruction label, and a short health dialogue in standard isiZulu.
  10. Write a short message describing a health problem and arranging a time to visit a clinic or health worker.

Communication Scenario

It is Monday morning in Durban. Nomvula did not go to the beach on Saturday because she felt unwell. She sends a message to Zinhle explaining what is wrong and asking what to do. Zinhle suggests she go to the community health clinic nearby. Nomvula calls the clinic to ask about visiting times and is told to come in the afternoon. When she arrives, a health worker asks her what symptoms she has, for how long she has been feeling unwell, and what medicine she has taken. Nomvula must answer clearly in isiZulu. At the end of the visit, the health worker gives her three short instructions for taking her medication.

  1. In English, list the three most important things you would want to tell a doctor or health worker if you were unwell. Then predict which isiZulu patterns from earlier chapters might appear in those statements.
  2. Think about how the angikwazi uku- frame from Chapter 12 could be used to describe what you are unable to do when you are sick. Write one example sentence in isiZulu or explain the idea in English.

Pronunciation and Tone

Body-part nouns and their syllable patterns

Many body-part nouns introduced in this chapter contain prenasalized consonants that were first practiced in Chapters 2 through 4. Words such as indlebe (ear), impumulo (nose), and intamo (neck) each begin with a nasal-consonant cluster in which the nasal and the following consonant are produced in a single breath unit, not as two separate sounds. The initial in- or im- is the class 9 noun prefix, not a standalone syllable that you can pause after.

Pronunciation Focus — Prenasalized onsets in body-part nouns

Practise the following words by dividing them into syllables and saying each syllable at an even pace. Remember that the prenasalized onset counts as a single consonant, so the first syllable of the word includes both the nasal and the following consonant together:

  1. in-dle-be — three syllables; the ndl is one onset, not two
  2. im-pu-mu-lo — four syllables; the mp is one prenasalized onset
  3. in-ta-mo — three syllables; the nt is one prenasalized onset

A common learner error is to insert a short vowel sound between the nasal and the following consonant, producing something like *i-ni-dlebe. Keep the prenasalized onset fused. Use a qualified speaker or verified audio to confirm your production before treating your own model as correct.

The ngi- pain frame and vowel quality

The productive pain frame Ngibuhlungu e___ begins with the first-person subject concord ngi-. The vowel in ngi- is a short, high front vowel, similar to the vowel in English "key" but shorter and without the glide at the end. Do not reduce it to a schwa. In fast connected speech, the -i may shorten further before the following syllable, but it should remain clearly a high vowel. Practice: ngi-bu-hlu-ngu, four syllables at an even pace, with the penultimate syllable -hlu- receiving slight lengthening at a phrase boundary.

Class 5/6 locatives for body parts

The locative form ekhanda (on the head, at the head) and esiswini (in the stomach) illustrate two different locative patterns for body-part nouns. The form ekhanda follows the e-… pattern without the -ini suffix because ikhanda ends in a vowel preceded by a nasal, producing an irregular locative surface form. The form esiswini shows the e-…-ini pattern with a vowel change: the final -u of isisu becomes -w- before -ini. Both of these locatives should be learned as whole units. Do not attempt to derive the irregular forms mechanically at this stage.

Health instruction intonation

Instructions given by a health worker or pharmacist in isiZulu are typically delivered as a sequence of short commands, exactly like the direction sequences practiced in Chapter 11. Each command is a complete verb-root plus final-vowel phrase with no subject concord. In a health context, these commands tend to be delivered at a steady, clear pace with a slightly falling contour, signaling care rather than urgency. Practise reading the following sample instructions aloud as a connected sequence without pausing overly long between steps:

Phumula. Natha amanzi amaningi. Thatha ipilisi elilodwa ekuseni nelilodwa ntambama.

Rest. Drink plenty of water. Take one tablet in the morning and one in the afternoon.

Grammar Instruction

13.1 Expressing pain and physical symptoms

Purpose: describe where something hurts and how someone feels using productive pain expressions and the angizizwa kahle frame.

IsiZulu has several ways to express pain and discomfort. The most straightforward approach for this stage uses the class 14 noun ubuhlungu (pain) inside a formulaic predicate, or the verb -zizwa (to feel oneself; to feel) in a negative or modified form. A third productive route uses the -no- possession frame, which you already know from earlier chapters, to say that someone has a certain condition.

Language Building Block — Pain and discomfort expressions
Ngibuhlungu e[locative body part].   |   Angizizwa kahle.   |   Nginomkhuhlane.
Ngibuhlungu + locative = I hurt at / in [body part]. The locative positions the pain. Angizizwa kahle uses the negative present of -zizwa (I feel) with the adverb kahle (well): I do not feel well. Nginomkhuhlane uses the -no- possession frame: I have a cold / flu. All three frames are productive at this stage.
Ngibuhlungu ekhanda.  |  Angizizwa kahle namuhla.  |  Nginomkhuhlane.
I have a headache. (literally: I am painful at the head)  |  I do not feel well today.  |  I have a cold / flu.

The expression Ngibuhlungu is a copulative predicate in which the subject concord ngi- combines with the class 14 noun stem -buhlungu in a way that is best treated as a productive formulaic frame at this stage. The locative that follows names the location of the pain. Use the locatives from the vocabulary table for body parts you know; for other body parts, use a pointing gesture and the phrase Ngibuhlungu lapha (I am in pain here) as a reliable alternative.

  • Ngibuhlungu ekhanda namuhla. I have a headache today.
  • Ngibuhlungu esifubeni. I have chest pain. / My chest hurts.
  • Ngibuhlungu esiswini. I have stomach pain. / My stomach hurts.
  • Angizizwa kahle. Ngiyagula. I do not feel well. I am sick.
  • Ngineshansela nokubuhlungu ekhanda. I have a headache and head pain. (the two expressions reinforce each other in natural speech)

13.2 Inability due to illness: angikwazi uku- in health contexts

Purpose: explain what you or someone else cannot do because of illness using the angikwazi uku- frame from Chapter 12, now applied to health-related actions.

The ability frame ngikwazi uku- and its negative counterpart angikwazi uku- were introduced in Chapter 12 for leisure contexts. In a health context, angikwazi uku- is one of the most natural and frequently needed expressions: it allows you to explain to a health worker, a friend, or an employer exactly what you are not able to do because of how you feel. The structure is identical to what you already know.

  • Angikwazi ukuhamba kahle. Ngibuhlungu umlenze. I cannot walk well. My leg hurts.
  • Angikwazi ukudla namuhla. Angizizwa kahle. I cannot eat today. I do not feel well.
  • Angikwazi ukuphefumula kahle. I cannot breathe well.
  • Angikwazi ukuya emsebenzini namuhla. Ngiyagula. I cannot go to work today. I am sick.
  • Angikwazi ukulala. Ngibuhlungu kakhulu. I cannot sleep. I am in a lot of pain.

13.3 Making a simple appointment using the future tense

Purpose: arrange a time to visit a clinic or health worker using the positive future marker -zo- consolidated from Chapter 12.

Arranging a health appointment requires you to state when you will come, where you will go, and what you need. All of these use the future tense you already know from Chapter 12. The new element in this chapter is combining the future with a health location locative from Chapter 11 and a need expressed through ngidinga uku- (I need to) or ngicela ___ (I would like).

Language Building Block — Simple appointment statement
Ngizofika + time expression + location locative. Ngidinga + uku- + verb root + -a.
The first sentence states when and where you will arrive using the future -zo-. The second states what you need, using ngidinga (I need) with the class 15 infinitive. Both sentences together form a complete appointment request. Ngidinga is a new productive verb at this stage; its present-tense form follows the standard present pattern you already know.
Ngizofika ekhliniki ngo-2 ntambama. Ngidinga ukukhuluma nodokotela.
I will arrive at the clinic at 2 in the afternoon. I need to speak with a doctor.

13.4 Health instructions using command forms

Purpose: recognize and produce simple health instructions using the positive command forms from Chapter 11, now applied to medication, rest, and self-care contexts.

A health worker or pharmacist giving instructions in isiZulu uses the same positive command forms you learned in Chapter 11 for directions. The verb root plus the final vowel -a gives a singular command; adding -ni gives a plural command. In a health context, the most productive commands are phumula (rest), natha (drink), thatha (take), yima (stop), and buka (look), all of which follow the same pattern you already know.

The negative command form Musa uku- from Chapter 11 applies here as well. Common health-related negative instructions include Musa ukudla ukudla okunoketshezi. (Do not eat fatty food.) and Musa ukuphuza utshwala. (Do not drink alcohol.) These are receptive at this stage; recognize and understand them from a health worker's instructions without needing to produce them independently.

  • Phumula izinsuku ezimbili. Natha amanzi amaningi. Rest for two days. Drink a lot of water.
  • Thatha ipilisi elilodwa ekuseni nalilodwa ntambama. Take one tablet in the morning and one in the afternoon.
  • Dla ukudla okunempilo. Phumula kahle ebusuku. Eat healthy food. Rest well at night.
  • Vula umlomo. Ngizokhangela amazinyo akho. Open your mouth. I will check your teeth. (receptive possessive; focus on the command)
Language Focus

This chapter consolidates three key systems in a new domain: the pain and symptom expressions are new productive vocabulary; the inability frame angikwazi uku- is a direct transfer from Chapter 12; and the appointment statements combine the future tense from Chapter 12 with the locatives and motion verbs from Chapter 11. Health instructions bring back the command forms from Chapter 11 in a new context. The goal of this chapter is integration rather than new grammar burden: you are using systems you already know to communicate in a health setting. The genuinely new productive items are the pain frame Ngibuhlungu e___, the general ill-health expression Angizizwa kahle, and the need verb ngidinga uku-. Keep your productive energy on these three additions and their natural combination with what came before.

Model Dialogue

Isimo: UNomvula ufike ekhliniki yomphakathi eThekwini. Uxoxa noGcwele (ugcwele wokuqala ukubona iziguli), owuzwa izimpawu zakhe wamnikeza iseluleko. Ubudlelwane: isiguli nomanesi wezempilo emphakathini; izinga lokuhlonipha eliphakathi, elilungele endaweni yezempilo.
Setting: Nomvula has arrived at a community clinic in Durban. She speaks with Gcwele (a nurse who first assesses patients), who hears her symptoms and gives her advice. Relationship: patient and community health nurse; respectful but approachable register appropriate to a clinic setting.

UGCWELE Sawubona. Ngingakusiza? Hello. Can I help you?
UNOMVULA Yebo, ngiyabonga. Angizizwa kahle namuhla. Ngibuhlungu ekhanda futhi nginomkhuhlane. Yes, thank you. I do not feel well today. I have a headache and I have a cold / flu.
UGCWELE Ngiyaxolisa ukuzwa lokho. Ugule izinsuku ezingaki? I am sorry to hear that. For how many days have you been sick?
UNOMVULA Ngigula izinsuku ezimbili. NgoMgqibelo ngangifuna ukuya ebhishini kodwa angikwazi ukuhamba. Ngibuhlungu kakhulu. I have been sick for two days. On Saturday I wanted to go to the beach but I could not walk. I am in a lot of pain.
UGCWELE Kulungile. Kukhona okunye okukuphazamisayo? Ngibuhlungu esifubeni? Noma esiswini? All right. Is there anything else bothering you? Pain in the chest? Or in the stomach?
UNOMVULA Cha, isiswi asibuhlungu. Kodwa ngiyaphoxeka kancane ekuseni. No, the stomach is not painful. But I feel a little nauseous in the morning.
UGCWELE Ngiyaqonda. Uthatha umuthi manje? I understand. Are you taking any medicine now?
UNOMVULA Cha. Angithathanga lutho. Ngidinga amapilisi. No. I have not taken anything. I need tablets.
UGCWELE Kulungile. Ngikunika amapilisi awumbili namuhla. Thatha elilodwa ekuseni nelilodwa ntambama namanzi amaningi. Phumula izinsuku ezimbili. Uma ungezwa ngcono ngoMsombuluko, buya lapha. All right. I will give you two tablets today. Take one in the morning and one in the afternoon with plenty of water. Rest for two days. If you are not feeling better by Monday, come back here.
UNOMVULA Ngiyabonga kakhulu! Ngizophumula ekhaya. Uma ngingezwa ngcono, ngizofika futhi ngoMsombuluko. Thank you very much! I will rest at home. If I do not feel better, I will come back on Monday.
UGCWELE Kulungile. Khumbula: phumula, natha amanzi, thatha amapilisi ngesikhathi. Sala kahle! All right. Remember: rest, drink water, take the tablets on time. Stay well!

Several expressions in this dialogue are receptive at this stage: Ngangifuna (I wanted, past tense of -funa), Uma ungezwa ngcono (if you do not feel better, conditional), Ngikunika (I am giving you, with object concord), ngesikhathi (on time), and Angithathanga lutho (I have not taken anything, negative perfect). These are provided as natural clinical input. Focus your productive study on the pain expressions, the inability frame, the appointment language, and the health instruction commands identified in the Grammar section.

Writing Workshop

In this chapter you will write a short message describing a health problem and arranging a clinic visit. Follow the full planning, drafting, and revision sequence below.

  1. Choose a health situation. Decide what health problem you or an imagined person has. Your situation must involve at least two symptoms, include a statement of how long the person has been unwell, and lead to an appointment at a clinic or a visit to a pharmacy.
  2. Describe the symptoms. Write two or three sentences using Ngibuhlungu e___, Angizizwa kahle, or Ngino___. Include at least one sentence using angikwazi uku- to explain what you cannot do because of the illness.
  3. State how long you have been unwell. Use the duration expression Ngigula izinsuku ___ with a number recycled from earlier chapters.
  4. Plan your clinic visit. Write two sentences arranging when you will go and what you need. Use the positive future marker -zo- and the need expression ngidinga uku-. Include the location using a locative form.
  5. Give or include health instructions. End your message with two or three short commands that someone has given you or that you are following, such as instructions for taking medicine, resting, or drinking water.
  6. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist before writing your final version.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • Pain expressions use Ngibuhlungu e___ with a body-part locative spelled correctly from the vocabulary table.
  • The general ill-health expression Angizizwa kahle is spelled as one continuous verb word without internal spaces.
  • The inability frame uses angikwazi uku- followed correctly by the class 15 infinitive with uku-.
  • Future-tense forms use -zo- in the correct slot without the present-tense -ya- marker appearing in the same verb word.
  • The duration expression includes a correct number from Chapters 1 through 12.
  • Locative forms for health locations use the patterns established in Chapters 11 and 13.
  • Command forms for health instructions use only the verb root plus the final vowel, with no subject concord prefix.
  • Each sentence begins with a capital letter and ends with appropriate punctuation.
  • No analytical hyphens appear inside ordinary verb words.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Healthcare settings in South Africa

South Africa has both public and private healthcare systems, and the experience of seeking care differs significantly between them. Public clinics (ikhliniki) serve the majority of South Africans and are often the first point of contact for common ailments. They are generally staffed by nurses and community health workers who speak isiZulu in KwaZulu-Natal and Durban, making isiZulu a genuinely functional language in those health settings. Doctors are available at hospitals and larger clinics. In townships and rural areas, a community health worker may make home visits and is an important link between households and formal care.

Private healthcare, including private hospitals and specialist clinics, operates separately. Many South Africans in working and middle-class contexts navigate both systems depending on urgency, cost, and availability. Pharmacies (amakhemisi), including large chain pharmacies at shopping centres, are accessible to many people for over-the-counter medicine and advice for minor ailments. The pharmacist (umphathi wekhemisi, receptive) is a commonly consulted resource before a full clinic visit.

Culture in Context — Traditional medicine and umuthi

The word umuthi in isiZulu can refer to medicine in general, to pharmaceutical medication, or specifically to traditional medicine prepared by a traditional healer (isangoma or inyanga). The meaning depends entirely on context. In a clinic or pharmacy setting, umuthi most often refers to pharmaceutical medicine. When discussing health in broader community or cultural contexts, it may refer to plant-based or traditional preparations. Both systems coexist in many communities, and many isiZulu-speaking South Africans may consult both a clinic and a traditional healer for the same illness at different points.

As a learner, treat umuthi as a general and neutral word for medicine and be aware that its meaning is context-dependent. Do not assume that mentioning umuthi in a health conversation implies anything beyond medicine in general.

Regional Note — Language in health settings across KwaZulu-Natal

In KwaZulu-Natal and in communities with significant isiZulu-speaking populations in Johannesburg, Durban is a city where isiZulu functions fully in public health settings. Clinic notices, patient intake forms, and health education materials are often available in isiZulu alongside English and other official languages. A learner who uses isiZulu in a clinic setting will generally be understood and appreciated for the effort. Health workers in KwaZulu-Natal public clinics routinely communicate in isiZulu with patients, and standard productive phrases from this chapter are directly applicable in real settings.

In other South African cities, isiZulu may be less dominant in health signage even when the patient population speaks it. In Johannesburg, multilingual settings with Sesotho, Zulu, Xhosa, and English all present are common. The vocabulary in this chapter remains useful across settings, but the dominant administrative language of a health facility may vary.

Culture in Context — Expressing illness and seeking help

Describing illness in isiZulu-speaking communities is shaped by the same cultural norms that govern other requests for help. A greeting before explaining the problem is expected. Moving directly into describing symptoms without any social preamble can feel abrupt. A brief Sawubona followed by Angizizwa kahle (I do not feel well) or Ngidinga usizo (I need help) is the natural opening for a health encounter with a stranger or a health worker you do not know personally.

It is also culturally common to downplay symptoms, particularly among older isiZulu speakers, who may minimize pain or discomfort when speaking to a health authority. As a learner, you can speak directly about symptoms without concern about being too forward; the productive expressions in this chapter are appropriately factual and clear. Being specific about what hurts and for how long is helpful to any health worker and is not considered impolite.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Omitting the locative suffix in pain expressions

Error: Writing *Ngibuhlungu isisu instead of Ngibuhlungu esiswini, by using the bare noun form instead of the locative form after the pain expression.

Correction: The pain frame Ngibuhlungu e___ requires a locative, not a bare noun. The locative positions the pain at or in the body part. Ngibuhlungu esiswini means "I have stomach pain" because the locative esiswini means "in the stomach." If you use the bare noun isisu, the sentence becomes grammatically incomplete. Learn the locative forms for frequently used body parts in this chapter as whole units and use them directly after Ngibuhlungu.

Common Mistake — Separating angizizwa into two words

Error: Writing *angi zizwa kahle or *angi-zizwa kahle with a space or an analytical hyphen between the negative prefix and the rest of the verb word.

Correction: The full negative verb word angizizwa is written as a single unbroken unit. The negative prefix angi- and the verb root -zizwa with its reflexive element are all fused into one conjunctive word. This is the same orthographic principle that applies to all other verb words in isiZulu, including ngiyahamba and ngizohamba. Write Angizizwa kahle as two words only because kahle (well) is a separate adverb, not part of the verb word itself.

Common Mistake — Using the present tense instead of the future for appointment statements

Error: Writing *Ngiyahamba ekhliniki kusasa when the intended meaning is "I will go to the clinic tomorrow," and expecting the present-tense form to convey a future appointment.

Correction: While present-tense forms can sometimes imply future meaning in colloquial contexts in some languages, the productive target in isiZulu at this stage is to use the explicit future marker -zo- whenever you are describing a plan or appointment in the future. Write Ngizohamba ekhliniki kusasa to mean "I will go to the clinic tomorrow." Reserve the present-tense forms for what is happening now or habitually. Using the future marker removes ambiguity and is the expected form in a clinical or planning context.

Common Mistake — Confusing umuthi and amapilisi

Error: Using umuthi when the specific meaning of "tablets" or "pills" is needed, or using amapilisi in a context where the general word for medicine is more appropriate.

Correction: Umuthi is the general word for medicine of any kind, including pharmaceutical and traditional preparations. Amapilisi refers specifically to tablets or pills. If a health worker gives you tablets, you have received amapilisi. If someone asks whether you are taking medicine generally, the question uses umuthi. In this chapter, use amapilisi when you are specifically requesting or referring to tablets, and umuthi when the general category of medicine is meant. Both words will continue to appear in later chapters.

Common Mistake — Adding a subject concord to health instruction commands

Error: Writing *Uphumula izinsuku ezimbili instead of Phumula izinsuku ezimbili, by adding the third-person subject concord u- to a command.

Correction: This is the same error identified in Chapter 11 for direction commands, and it reappears naturally in a health context. A positive command consists only of the verb root plus the final vowel -a, with no subject concord. *Uphumula is a present-tense statement meaning "he/she is resting," not an instruction to rest. A health worker instructing a patient says Phumula izinsuku ezimbili (Rest for two days) without any prefix. Review the command-form table in this chapter if this pattern is not yet automatic.

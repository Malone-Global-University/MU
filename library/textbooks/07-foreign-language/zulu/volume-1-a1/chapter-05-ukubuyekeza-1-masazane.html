Ukubuyekeza 1: Masazane

Unit I Assessment, Let Us Get to Know Each Other · Speak and Write Zulu: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

This chapter introduces no new grammar system. By the end of it, you will be able to:

  1. Greet one person and a group, respond appropriately, and take leave, using register suited to the situation.
  2. Introduce yourself and another person, and exchange personal information about identity, origin, and languages.
  3. Identify classroom participants and use supported identity copulatives accurately.
  4. Describe family members and friends, using possessive pronominal forms and a small set of adjective concords.
  5. State and ask about ages, and use the active na-based possession construction.
  6. Apply active subject concords for classes 1, 2, 1a, and 2a accurately in connected speech and writing.
  7. Distinguish the dental, alveolar, and lateral clicks, and recycle the consonant and vowel targets of Chapters 1 through 4.
  8. Interpret a short personal profile and a short connected listening passage.
  9. Write a connected, multi-sentence personal introduction using standard conjunctive orthography.
  10. Repair a simple misunderstanding using a clarification strategy.

Communication Scenario

Your isiZulu class in Durban is holding a small welcome gathering, umcimbi wokwamukela, for new students joining the program. You will meet several people you have not spoken with before. Some are fellow students, some are instructors, and one is a visiting relative of your classmate Sipho. Over the course of the gathering, you will need to greet people appropriately, introduce yourself, ask where someone is from, find out about their family, and keep the conversation going when you do not understand something the first time. This is the moment where everything from Chapters 1 through 4 comes together into one continuous interaction, rather than four separate skills.

  1. Before you begin, look back through your notes from Chapters 1 through 4. Write down one expression from each chapter that you feel confident using without checking the book.
  2. Predict which part of a first conversation, greeting, self-introduction, asking about origin, or asking about family, feels least automatic to you yet. You will return to this prediction at the end of the chapter.

Pronunciation and Orthography Review

The three click types together

Chapters 2, 3, and 4 introduced the three basic isiZulu click letters one at a time. This chapter asks you to distinguish all three in connected, mixed practice rather than in isolation.

LetterTypeReview word
cdentalicici (earring)
xlateralixoxo (frog)
qalveolar/postalveolaromqatha (strong, class 1)
Pronunciation Focus — Mixed click discrimination

Listen to a partner, instructor, or verified audio model say a short list mixing words with c, q, and x. Before producing the words yourself, focus only on identifying which click you heard. Once you can identify the three reliably, move to production, saying each word slowly and comparing the place of contact in your own mouth against what you learned in Chapters 2 through 4.

This chapter does not introduce click accompaniments such as aspiration, voicing, or nasalization. Continue practising the plain form of each click type.

Recycled consonants and vowels

Review the following targets from earlier chapters in connected phrases rather than isolated drills:

  • b versus bh Recycled from Chapter 2; compare ubaba with a word containing bh from your earlier notes.
  • hl and dl Recycled from Chapter 4; practise in uhlala and similar verbs.
  • Five full vowels and final vowels Recycled from Chapter 1; do not reduce any vowel to an English schwa.
  • Penultimate lengthening Recycled from Chapters 1 through 4; listen for it at the end of each phrase, not each isolated word.

Orthography checkpoint

Before moving into the integrated tasks, confirm that you can apply the following standard writing conventions without hesitation:

  • Verb words are written as single conjunctive units, with no spaces or hyphens inside them.
  • Nouns are written with their full augment and class prefix, for example umngane, not *mngane.
  • Possessive forms follow the noun they belong to: ubaba wami, not *wami ubaba.
  • Sentences begin with a capital letter and end with appropriate punctuation.
  • Tone marks do not appear in ordinary writing.

No new grammar system appears in this chapter. The systems below are drawn together because they regularly interact in real conversation. Each box gives a compact reminder; consult the relevant chapter for full explanation if a pattern feels unfamiliar.

  • Uneminyaka engamashumi amabili. He/she is twenty years old.

5.5 Adjective concords for description

Purpose: review describing a person with the active class 1/1a and class 2/2a adjective forms, recycled from Chapter 4.

  • abangane abahle beautiful friends
Language Focus

The single thread connecting Chapters 1 through 4 is the noun class. Once you identify a noun's class, you can predict its subject concord, its possessive form, and its adjective concord. This chapter does not add a new class or a new concord type. It asks you to apply the classes and concords you already know, classes 1, 2, 1a, and 2a, fluently and in combination, inside one continuous conversation rather than one isolated exercise at a time.

A second thread is word order. Possessives and adjectives both follow the noun they relate to. Keep this order automatic as your sentences grow longer in this chapter's tasks.

Integrated grammar practice

Combine two or more systems in a single sentence, as you will need to in real conversation.

  1. Write one sentence that uses both a possessive form and an adjective phrase about the same person.
  2. Write one sentence that uses the na-based construction together with an age expression.
  3. Write one sentence that uses a subject concord with a class 1a noun and an identity copulative together.

Integrated Dialogue

Isimo: Kumcimbi wokwamukela abafundi abasha, uThandeka uhlangana noMnumzane Khumalo, umalume kaSipho ovela eGoli. Akakaze bahlangane ngaphambili. Ubudlelwane: ukwaziswa kwabantu abasha, inhlonipho efanele.
Setting: At the welcome gathering for new students, Thandeka meets Mr Khumalo, Sipho's uncle who has come from Johannesburg. They have not met before. Relationship: a first introduction requiring appropriate respect.

THANDEKA Sawubona, baba. Hello, sir. [baba used here as a respectful address, recycled from Chapter 4's culture note]
MNU. KHUMALO Sawubona, ntombazane. Unjani? Hello, young woman. How are you?
THANDEKA Ngiyaphila, ngiyabonga. Wena unjani? I am well, thank you. How are you?
MNU. KHUMALO Nami ngiyaphila. Ungubani igama lakho? I am also well. What is your name?
THANDEKA NginguThandeka. Ngiyafunda isiZulu lapha. Uvelaphi, baba? I am Thandeka. I am studying isiZulu here. Where are you from, sir?
MNU. KHUMALO Ngivela eGoli, kodwa umndeni wami uhlala lapha eThekwini. Ngingumalume kaSipho. I am from Johannesburg, but my family lives here in Durban. I am Sipho's uncle.
THANDEKA Ohhh, uSipho ungumngane wami! Sifunda isiZulu ndawonye. Oh, Sipho is my friend! We study isiZulu together.
MNU. KHUMALO Kuhle lokho. USipho ungumfundi omqatha. Nawe uneminyaka emingaki? That is good. Sipho is a hardworking student. And how old are you?
THANDEKA Uxolo, awungiphindeli? Angizwanga kahle. Sorry, could you repeat that? I did not hear well. [clarification strategy, recycled from Chapter 2]
MNU. KHUMALO Ngithe, uneminyaka emingaki? I said, how old are you?
THANDEKA Ngingu, uxolo, nginee, uneminyaka engamashumi amabili nambili. Nginabazali ababili nodadewethu omncane. I am, sorry, I mean, I am twenty-two years old. I have two parents and my younger sister. [self-correction modeled naturally; nginabazali and udadewethu are receptive items for this chapter]
MNU. KHUMALO Kuhle. Sengikujabulele ukukwazi, Thandeka. Hamba kahle. Good. I have been glad to know you, Thandeka. Go well. [formulaic closing expression; receptive at this stage]
THANDEKA Sala kahle, baba. Stay well, sir.

Notice that Thandeka uses a clarification request, Uxolo, awungiphindeli?, when she does not understand the first time. This kind of repair strategy is essential for real conversation and was introduced receptively in Chapter 2. A few expressions, including nginabazali and Sengikujabulele ukukwazi, are included as recognition items to keep the conversation natural; they are not productive targets of this chapter.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Ubani uMnumzane Khumalo kuSipho? (Who is Mr Khumalo to Sipho?)
  2. Uvelaphi uMnumzane Khumalo? (Where is Mr Khumalo from?)
  3. UThandeka wenzani lapho engazwanga kahle? (What does Thandeka do when she does not hear well?)
  4. Uneminyaka emingaki uThandeka? (How old is Thandeka?)

Reading

Read the following short profile written for the welcome gathering's program booklet. All text is in Standard isiZulu. Read the passage aloud twice before answering the questions.

Owesabaza — Profile: uZandile Mthembu

Sawubona! NginguZandile Mthembu. Ngivela eMlazi, kodwa manje ngihlala eThekwini ngenxa yomsebenzi wami. Ngingumfundi wesiZulu lapha eklasini. Ngiyajabula ukufunda ulimi lwami ngendlela enzulu.

Umndeni wami omncane kodwa uyajabula. Nginaye umama wami, uNompumelelo, noyise wami, uMandla. Umama wami omuhle futhi omqatha. Uneminyaka engamashumi amane. Ubaba wami uyathisha esikoleni esiseduze.

Nginaye nodadewethu omncane, uLindiwe. Uneminyaka eyishumi nesishiyagalombili. Naye uyafunda isiZulu, kodwa kwelinye iklasi.

Nginabangane abaningi eklasini, kanti umngane wami omkhulu kakhulu nginguThandeka. Sibangane isikhathi eside. Ngiyathanda ukuhlangana nabantu abasha emicimbini efana nalo.

The expressions ngendlela enzulu (in a deep way), ngenxa yomsebenzi (because of work), noyise wami (and my father, an alternative form), and kwelinye iklasi (in another class) extend beyond the productive scope of Chapters 1 through 4. Read for overall meaning using vocabulary and patterns you know, and treat unfamiliar forms as recognition items, consistent with the recognition-before-production principle that governs this series.

  3. Uneminyaka emingaki udadewabo kaZandile? (How old is Zandile's younger sister?)
  4. Ubani umngane kaZandile omkhulu kakhulu? (Who is Zandile's closest friend?)

Writing Workshop

In this chapter, you will write a connected personal introduction that draws on everything from Chapters 1 through 4. This is your first opportunity to combine greeting, identity, origin, and family information into a single piece of organized writing.

  1. Plan. On a sheet of paper, note the following in English: your name, where you are from, where you live now, one or two languages you speak or study, two or three family members or friends with one description and an age for each, and one closing courtesy expression.
  2. Study the model. Reread Owesabaza: uZandile Mthembu in the Reading section. Notice that the writer opens with a greeting and her name, states origin and residence, introduces family members with possessive forms and descriptions, and closes with a friend and a courtesy statement. Your paragraph will follow a similar shape.
  3. Draft. Write seven to ten connected sentences in isiZulu. Include a greeting, your name using an identity copulative, your origin and current residence, at least one language statement, at least two possessive pronominal forms, at least one adjective phrase with the correct concord, at least one age expression, and at least one na-based sentence.
  4. Add a repair strategy. Include one sentence showing that you can ask for clarification or repetition, for example Ngicela ukuphinda, even though no one has spoken to you in writing. Frame it as something you might say if someone reads your introduction aloud and you want to check that your own pronunciation is being understood.
  5. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist below before writing your final version.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • Possessive forms follow the noun and match the possessed noun's class, not the possessor.
  • Adjective phrases follow the noun and use the correct class 1/1a or class 2/2a concord.
  • The na-based construction and the age formula are written as single conjunctive words.
  • Class 1a nouns use u-/o-, not umu-/aba-.
  • Verb words contain no internal spaces or analytical hyphens.
  • Every sentence begins with a capital letter and ends with appropriate punctuation.
  • The paragraph reads as one connected introduction, not a list of disconnected facts.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: chain introductions

In a group of three or more, the first person greets the group and gives a full self-introduction, including origin and one family detail. The second person greets the first person by name, then gives their own introduction. Continue around the group. Each person must use at least one possessive form and one age expression.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: the welcome gathering

Working with a partner you have not yet practised with, role-play meeting for the first time at umcimbi wokwamukela. Greet each other appropriately for your assigned relationship, perhaps stranger to stranger, student to instructor, or younger person to elder. Exchange names, origin, residence, and at least one piece of family or friend information. If you do not understand something, use a clarification strategy rather than guessing. Close the conversation with an appropriate leave-taking.

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself delivering a ninety-second to two-minute self-introduction as though meeting your isiZulu class for the first time. Include a greeting, your name, origin, residence, at least one language, at least two family or friend descriptions with possessive forms and adjectives, one age expression, and a closing courtesy phrase. Listen back once for content and once for pronunciation, checking clicks, vowel quality, and verb-word boundaries.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Meeting someone for the first time

A first meeting in many isiZulu-speaking contexts typically opens with a clear greeting before any other communicative purpose, whether asking a question, making a request, or beginning small talk. Skipping the greeting, or rushing past it, can feel abrupt regardless of the speaker's intention. This expectation recurs throughout the chapters you have completed and remains relevant in every subsequent unit.

Age and apparent social position can shape word choice, for instance whether a younger person addresses an older person with a kinship term such as baba or mama rather than a name alone. These choices vary by individual relationship, setting, and community, including across KwaZulu-Natal, Gauteng, and other South African isiZulu-speaking communities, so observation and verification with a qualified speaker remain valuable habits for a learner to build early.

Culture in Context — Repairing a misunderstanding

Asking for repetition or clarification is a normal, expected part of conversation, not a sign of failure. The expression Uxolo, awungiphindeli?, used by Thandeka in this chapter's dialogue, is one polite way to request repetition. Self-correction, as Thandeka also demonstrates when she restarts her sentence about her age, is likewise a normal feature of real spoken communication in any language, including isiZulu.

Common Mistakes Revisited

Common Mistake — Treating possessive and adjective order as optional

Error: Reverting to English word order under the pressure of real-time conversation, for example saying *wami umngane instead of umngane wami when speaking quickly.

Correction: The noun-first order does not change under conversational pressure. If you notice yourself reverting to English order, slow down and rebuild the phrase: noun, then possessive or adjective. This habit becomes more automatic with the kind of connected, integrated practice this chapter provides.

Common Mistake — Confusing class 1/2 and class 1a/2a noun prefixes under pressure

Error: Applying umu- to a class 1a kinship noun in the middle of a longer spoken turn, for example saying *umumama while also tracking several other pieces of information.

Correction: Both classes share the same subject concords, u- and ba-, which is why this error often appears only in the noun prefix itself, not in the surrounding verb. Review the class 1a/2a noun list in this chapter's Vocabulary Review section before extended speaking practice.

Common Mistake — Guessing instead of requesting repetition

Error: Answering a question that was not fully understood, which can lead to a confusing or irrelevant response and stall the conversation.

Correction: Use a clarification strategy such as Uxolo, awungiphindeli? or Ngicela ukuphinda rather than guessing. Asking for repetition keeps the conversation accurate and is a normal, expected part of real communication at every proficiency level.

Looking Ahead

Okungakwenzeka manje — What you can do now

You can manage a complete first conversation from greeting to leave-taking, introduce yourself and ask about another person's identity and origin, describe family members and friends using possessive and adjective concords, state and ask about age, and recover from a misunderstanding using a clarification strategy. You have also consolidated the three basic click types and the consonant and vowel targets introduced across Chapters 1 through 4. This integrated control, more than any single new form, is what marks the end of Unit I.

Isahluko 6: Ekhaya nasendaweni

Chapter 6 opens Unit II, Impilo yansuku zonke, and introduces two new noun-class pairs, classes 7/8 and 9/10, alongside existential and locative constructions for describing a home and neighborhood. You will learn to say what is present in a place, where things are located, and whether a service or place is nearby. The human noun classes, subject concords, and possessive patterns you have just consolidated in this chapter will continue to recur as you apply them to new, nonhuman vocabulary.

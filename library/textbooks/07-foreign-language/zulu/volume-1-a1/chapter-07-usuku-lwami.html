Usuku lwami

Daily Routine · Speak and Write Zulu: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Tell time in isiZulu using hours and common time-of-day expressions.
  2. Describe a weekday and a weekend routine using familiar present-tense verbs.
  3. Ask and answer questions about when and how often activities occur.
  4. Sequence daily activities using common connecting expressions.
  5. Form and use the present negative for the first and second persons.
  6. Form and use the present negative for active human and nonhuman noun classes.
  7. Recognize and use class 15 infinitives with uku- as citation forms and activity nouns.
  8. Distinguish the present positive from the present negative in natural contrast.
  9. Recognize the rhythm of positive and negative verbs, including the negative final vowel -i.
  10. Write a sequenced paragraph describing a typical day using standard conjunctive orthography.

Communication Scenario

You have moved into a shared house near campus in Durban, and your housemate, Nokuthula, is comparing schedules with you so the two of you can plan a weekly grocery run and figure out who is home when. She wants to know what time you wake up, what you do before class, and what you do not do during the week that you save for weekends. You realize that to compare routines honestly, you need to talk about what you do, and just as importantly, what you do not do.

  1. In English, write a short list of three things you do every weekday morning and one thing you never do on a weekday.
  2. In isiZulu, saying what you do not do requires changing the verb in more than one place. Before reading the Grammar section, guess whether you think the change happens only at the beginning of the verb, only at the end, or in both places.

Pronunciation and Tone

Positive and negative rhythm

This chapter introduces a new listening target: the contrast between the rhythm of a positive present verb and a negative present verb. The two patterns differ not only in their opening sound but in their closing vowel, and this affects the whole phrase melody.

Pronunciation Focus — Hearing positive and negative verbs

Compare ngiyasebenza (I work) with angisebenzi (I do not work). Notice three differences as you listen:

  1. The positive form begins with the subject concord ngi-; the negative form begins with a- followed by the subject concord.
  2. The positive form ends in -a; the negative form ends in -i.
  3. The overall phrase has a different shape because of the added negative material at the front and the changed final vowel.

Practise in pairs: say the positive form, then the corresponding negative form, paying attention to the change at both ends of the word. Work from a verified audio model or a qualified speaker whenever possible, since the negative final vowel carries meaningful tone information that text alone cannot fully represent.

Final -a versus negative -i

Every regular present positive verb you have learned so far ends in the final vowel -a. Beginning in this chapter, you will also produce verbs ending in -i, which signals present negative meaning. Train your ear to listen for the final vowel as a grammatical signal, not just as a sound at the end of a word.

Positive (-a)Negative (-i)English
ngiyafundaangifundiI study / I do not study
uyasebenzaakasebenzihe/she works / he/she does not work
siyadlaasidliwe eat / we do not eat

Aspirated ph, th, and kh

Several high-frequency verbs in this chapter contain aspirated consonants you began noticing in earlier chapters: ph in ukupheka (to cook) and ukuphumula (to rest), and kh appears in related vocabulary such as ngokuhambisana. Continue to distinguish these aspirated sounds, produced with a clear puff of air, from their unaspirated counterparts p, t, and k.

A puff of air after the consonant release is the key cue. Hold a hand or a strip of paper near your mouth while practising pheka versus an unaspirated p sound to feel the difference.

Syllabic parsing of long negative verbs

Negative verb forms can grow longer than their positive counterparts because of the added negative subject marker. Practise reading negative verbs by syllable rather than letter by letter, for example: a-ka-se-be-nzi. Reading by syllable will help you keep vowels full and avoid collapsing the word into an English-like cluster of consonants.

Penultimate lengthening in schedule statements

When you state a time or a sequence of activities, listen for the lengthening of the second-to-last syllable at the end of each phrase, as in ekuseeni spoken naturally within Ngivuka ekuseni. This lengthening is a feature of connected speech, not of the isolated word, and it helps a listener identify where one phrase ends and the next begins.

Grammar Instruction

7.1 Class 15 infinitives with uku-

Purpose: recognize the infinitive as the verb's citation form and use it productively to name an activity.

Every isiZulu verb has a citation form built with the prefix uku-. This prefix marks class 15, the class of infinitival nouns. You have already seen infinitives in vocabulary lists since Chapter 3, such as ukufunda (to study) and ukusebenza (to work). In this chapter, the infinitive becomes an active grammatical target in its own right.

Language Building Block — The infinitive
uku- + verb root + final vowel -a
The class 15 prefix uku- attaches directly to the verb root, which ends in the final vowel -a in its citation form.
uku-vuk-a  |  uku-geza  |  uku-lal-a
to wake up  |  to wash  |  to sleep

Because the infinitive is itself a class 15 noun, it can be the subject of a sentence, just like any other noun. When it functions this way, it takes the class 15 subject concord ku- in the following verb.

  • Ukuvuka ekuseni kuyangikhathaza. uku-vuk-a + ku-ya-ngi-khathaz-a Waking up early bothers me. (lit. the waking-up in the morning, it bothers me)
  • Ukusebenza kuhle. uku-sebenz-a + ku-hle Working is good.

You are not yet expected to produce sentences with the infinitive as subject independently. Focus your productive practice on recognizing and using the infinitive as the dictionary form of a verb, and on recognizing it as a noun when it appears this way in reading or listening.

7.2 Present negative: first and second persons

Purpose: say what you, or the person you are speaking to, do not do, using the correct negative subject marker and final vowel.

You already know that the present positive verb begins with a subject concord and ends in -a. The present negative changes the verb in two places at once: a negative element is added before the subject concord, and the final vowel changes from -a to -i.

Grammar Note — Negation changes both ends of the verb

A common assumption among English-speaking learners is that negation adds one word meaning "not," similar to English not or don't. In isiZulu, present negation is a two-part change: a negative marker appears near the beginning of the verb, and the final vowel changes from -a to -i. Both changes are required together. A verb with only the front change, or only the final-vowel change, is not a complete or correct negative form.

For the first and second persons, the negative marker a- combines with the personal subject concord. The table below shows the active forms for this chapter.

Person Positive subject concord Negative form Example English
Ingi-angi-Angifundi.I do not study.
you (singular)u-awu-Awusebenzi.You do not work.
wesi-asi-Asidli.We do not eat.
you (plural)ni-ani-Anihlanzi.You (plural) do not clean.

Notice that the second-person singular negative awu- involves a small vowel change rather than a simple a- prefix: a- plus u- becomes awu-. This is a regular spelling pattern, not an exception. Write the merged form in standard text.

  • Angivuki ekuseni ngempela sonto. a-ngi-vuk-i + ekuseni + ngempela sonto I do not wake up early on weekends.
  • Awuphuzi ikhofi ebusuku, akunjalo? a-u-phuz-i (awuphuzi) + ikhofi + ebusuku You do not drink coffee at night, right?
  • Asisebenzi ngeSonto. a-si-sebenz-i + ngeSonto We do not work on Sundays.

7.3 Present negative: active noun classes

Purpose: say what a third-person human subject, or a subject from an active nonhuman noun class, does not do.

The third-person and noun-class negative pattern follows the same two-part principle: an a- element combines with the subject concord, and the final vowel becomes -i. For classes 1, 1a, 7, and 9, which are active in this chapter, the negative subject markers are shown below.

Class Positive subject concord Negative form Example English
1 / 1au-aka-Ubaba akasebenzi ngeSonto.Father does not work on Sunday.
2 / 2aba-aba-Abafundi abafundi ebusuku.The students do not study at night.
7si-asi-Isikole asivulwa ngeSonto.The school does not open on Sunday. (receptive structure; for recognition)
9i-ayi-Ingane ayilali ekuseni.The child does not sleep in the morning.

The third-person singular class 1/1a negative marker aka- does not simply add a- to the positive concord u-; it uses the distinct form aka-. This is a single learned pattern rather than a regular combination, so commit it to memory as a whole unit. The other classes shown here follow the more regular pattern of a- plus the positive subject concord.

  • Umama akapheki namuhla. a-ka-phek-i Mother is not cooking today.
  • Abangane bami abahambi ngoMgqibelo. aba-hamb-i My friends do not travel on Saturdays.
  • Ingane ayidli ushukela kaningi. a-yi-dl-i The child does not eat sugar often.
Language Focus

Present negation in isiZulu is a coordinated two-part system, not a single inserted word. A negative marker appears at the front of the verb, fused with the subject concord, and the final vowel changes from -a to -i. The exact shape of the front marker depends on the person or noun class, with class 1/1a singular taking the special form aka-. Holding both ends of the verb in mind at once, rather than treating negation as one quick fix, is the single most useful habit to build in this chapter.

7.4 Time, frequency, and sequence

Purpose: state when and how often an activity happens, and connect activities in a natural sequence.

Time and frequency expressions normally appear at the end of the clause or, for emphasis, at the beginning. Sequence is built using simple connectors rather than a complex grammatical structure at this stage.

  • Ngivuka ngehora lesithupha ekuseni. I wake up at six o'clock in the morning.
  • Kuqala ngiyageza, bese ngiyadla. First I wash, and then I eat.
  • Ngiyasebenza njalo ngemini, kodwa angisebenzi ngempela sonto. I work every weekday, but I do not work on the weekend.

The connector bese (and then) is a formulaic sequencing word at this stage; its full grammatical behavior connects to subjunctive and dependent verb forms developed later in the course. For now, use it productively to link two activities in order.

Model Dialogue

Isimo: UNokuthula noThandeka bahlala endlini eyodwa eThekwini. Bafuna ukuhlanganisa izinhlelo zabo zansuku zonke. Ubudlelwane: abahlali abahlanganyele indlu.
Setting: Nokuthula and Thandeka share a house in Durban. They want to compare their daily schedules. Relationship: housemates.

NOKUTHULA Thandeka, uvuka ngasiphi isikhathi ekuseni? Thandeka, what time do you wake up in the morning?
THANDEKA Ngivuka ngehora lesithupha, kodwa angivuki kanjalo ngempela sonto. Ngempela sonto, ngivuka ngehora lesishiyagalombili. I wake up at six o'clock, but I do not wake up like that on weekends. On weekends, I wake up at eight o'clock.
NOKUTHULA Kuhle lokho. Mina angisebenzi ngoMsombuluko, kodwa ngiyafunda ekuseni. Uyasebenza nini wena? That's good. I do not work on Mondays, but I study in the morning. When do you work?
THANDEKA Ngisebenza njalo, kusukela ngoMsombuluko kuya kuLwesihlanu. Angisebenzi ngempela sonto. I work regularly, from Monday to Friday. I do not work on the weekend. [kusukela...kuya = from...to; formulaic at this stage]
NOKUTHULA Upheka njalo, noma uthenga ukudla? Do you cook regularly, or do you buy food?
THANDEKA Ngipheka kaningi ntambama, kodwa angipheki ngeSonto. NgeSonto, umama wami uyapheka, futhi ngiya ekhaya. I cook often in the evening, but I do not cook on Sunday. On Sunday, my mother cooks, and I go home.
NOKUTHULA Mina angipheki kahle. Ngithanda ukuhamba ngiyodla phandle ntambama. I do not cook well. I like to go out and eat in the evening. [ngiyodla phandle = formulaic, "go and eat out"; receptive at this stage]
THANDEKA Kulungile. Kuqala ngiyahlanza ikhishi ngeSonto, bese ngiyaphumula. Wena uhlanza nini? Okay. First I clean the kitchen on Sunday, and then I rest. When do you clean?
NOKUTHULA Ngihlanza ngoMgqibelo emini. Asihlanzi kabili ngesonto elilodwa, akunjalo? I clean on Saturday afternoon. We do not clean twice in one week, do we? [akunjalo = isn't that so?; formulaic tag question]
THANDEKA Cha, akudingeki. Kulungile, sesivumelene. No, it is not necessary. Good, we have agreed. [akudingeki, sesivumelene = receptive expressions; enjoy for meaning]

Some expressions in this dialogue, including kusukela...kuya, ngiyodla phandle, akudingeki, and sesivumelene, contain grammar or vocabulary beyond the current chapter scope. They are included as recognition items to make the conversation sound natural. Focus your productive study on the items in the Grammar section.

Writing Workshop

In this chapter you will write a short sequenced paragraph describing a typical weekday and how it differs from your weekend. Follow the full planning-drafting-revision sequence.

  1. Plan. Return to the list you made in the Communication Scenario: three weekday morning activities and one thing you never do on a weekday. Add two more details: one weekend activity, and one thing you do not do on weekends that you do during the week.
  2. Study the model. Read the passage Usuku lukaZandile in the Reading section once more. Notice how the writer: (a) states when she wakes up, (b) sequences morning activities using bese, (c) contrasts weekday and weekend using positive and negative verbs, and (d) closes with a reflection. Your paragraph will follow a similar structure.
  3. Draft. Write six to eight connected sentences in isiZulu about your typical day. Include: at least two time expressions, at least two present negative verbs (one for a person, one for a noun class if possible), one sequencing word such as kuqala or bese, and a clear contrast between weekday and weekend activities.
  4. Check your negatives. For each negative sentence, confirm that the verb has both the front negative marker and the final vowel -i.
  5. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist below before writing your final version.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • Every negative verb has both the front negative marker and the final vowel -i.
  • The class 1/1a third-person negative marker is written as aka-, not as a- plus u-.
  • Verb words are written as single conjunctive units without spaces or hyphens.
  • Time and frequency expressions appear naturally, usually at the end of the clause.
  • At least one sentence uses kuqala or bese to sequence activities.
  • Day names are capitalized correctly.
  • Each sentence begins with a capital letter and ends with a full stop.
  • No analytical hyphens appear in the ordinary writing of any verb form.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: positive to negative drill

Your partner says a positive sentence about a daily activity. You respond with the negative form of the same sentence, changing only what is necessary. Switch roles after six rounds.

Example: Partner says Ngiyasebenza ngoMsombuluko. You say: Angisebenzi ngoMsombuluko.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: comparing schedules

For at least two of your answers, include something you do not do, using a correctly formed present negative verb.

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself describing a sixty- to ninety-second account of your typical weekday, then your typical weekend. Use at least three time expressions, two sequencing words, and three present negative sentences. Listen back and check your negative verb endings against the Grammar section tables.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Daily schedules across South African communities

Daily schedules among isiZulu-speaking South Africans vary widely depending on occupation, location, household structure, and individual circumstance. A learner who commutes long distances between a township and a city center, for example, may have a very different morning routine from someone who works close to home. In and around Durban and in Gauteng's urban centers, commuting time often shapes when people wake up and how the day is structured.

Many households also coordinate schedules around shared responsibilities, including childcare, eldercare, and household tasks. Avoid assuming that any single daily pattern, whether urban or rural, professional or informal, represents all isiZulu-speaking households. The routines presented in this chapter are illustrative examples, not a universal template.

Culture in Context — Sunday and the weekend

For many isiZulu-speaking families, Sunday, iSonto, carries particular significance as a day for rest, family time, religious observance, or household gatherings, though practices vary considerably by family, faith tradition, and region. Some households treat Saturday as a day for errands, cleaning, or social activities, while Sunday is reserved differently. Avoid presenting one fixed weekend pattern as standard for every household or community.

Regional Note — Time expressions in everyday speech

The clock-time expressions introduced in this chapter follow Standard isiZulu hour-based forms. In fast, informal speech, especially in urban centers such as Durban and Johannesburg, speakers may shorten these expressions or mix in English or Afrikaans-derived time vocabulary, particularly with numbers. The standard forms taught here remain widely understood and are the appropriate productive target for this course. Verify any locally preferred variant with a qualified speaker if you are learning to communicate with a specific community.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Changing only the front of the verb

Error: Writing *angifunda instead of angifundi, adding the negative marker at the front but leaving the final vowel as -a.

Correction: Present negation requires changes at both ends of the verb: the negative marker at the front, and the final vowel -i at the end. A verb with only one of these two changes is incomplete. Check every negative verb you write for both features before treating it as finished.

Common Mistake — Changing only the final vowel

Error: Writing *ngifundi instead of angifundi, changing the final vowel but forgetting the negative marker at the front.

Correction: The form *ngifundi is missing the negative marker a- that must appear before the subject concord. Both changes work together to signal negation; neither one alone is sufficient. Practise the complete pattern as a single unit rather than as two separate optional steps.

Common Mistake — Treating aka- as a- plus u-

Error: Writing *awusebenzi when intending "he/she does not work," by mechanically combining a- with the class 1 positive concord u-.

Correction: The correct third-person singular form for class 1 and class 1a is akasebenzi, using the distinct negative marker aka-. The form awu- is reserved for the second-person singular ("you do not"). Confusing these two markers is a common and understandable error, since both involve the letter sequence a plus a vowel-initial element. Learn aka- for "he/she does not" as a separate, memorized form.

Common Mistake — Treating -ya- as required in every present sentence

Error: Writing *Ngiyavuka ngehora lesihlanu ekuseni when a short form would be more natural because a time expression follows the verb.

Correction: As you learned in Chapter 3, the long form with -ya- typically occurs when nothing follows the verb, while the short form is typical when a time, place, or other element follows. Ngivuka ngehora lesihlanu ekuseni is the more natural short-form choice here. Continue to listen for this pattern across positive and negative sentences alike.

Common Mistake — Writing spaces inside negative verb words

Error: Writing *a ngi fundi or *a-ngi-fundi with spaces or hyphens inside the negative verb.

Correction: The complete negative verb is written as one unbroken conjunctive word: angifundi. Analytical hyphens appear only in grammar explanations inside this textbook, never in ordinary writing. This is the same orthographic principle you practised with possessive and positive verb forms in earlier chapters.

  1. (a) Angifundi ekuseni.   (b) Awusebenzi ngoLwesihlanu.   (c) Umama akapheki ntambama.   (d) Abafundi abafundi.   (e) Ingane ayilali emini.

Looking Ahead

You can tell time, describe a weekday and weekend routine, and ask when and how often activities happen. You can sequence activities using kuqala and bese, and, most importantly, you can now say what you and others do not do, using the present negative for both persons and active noun classes. These negative patterns, along with the long-short present contrast from Chapter 3, will be recycled and expanded in every chapter that follows, including the next chapter's focus on food, requests, and quantities.

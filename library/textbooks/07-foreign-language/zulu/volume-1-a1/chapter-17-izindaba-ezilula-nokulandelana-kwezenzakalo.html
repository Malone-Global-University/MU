Izindaba ezilula nokulandelana kwezenzakalo

Simple Narratives and Sequencing · Speak and Write Zulu: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Connect two or more past-time events using the sequencing connectors kuqala (first), ngemva kwalokho (after that), and ekugcineni (finally, in the end).
  2. Recycle the past-tense forms from Chapter 11 productively inside a short connected narrative, rather than in isolated single sentences.
  3. Use the time expressions ekuseni (in the morning), emini (at midday), and ntambama (in the afternoon) to anchor events in a daily sequence, recycling routine vocabulary from Chapter 8.
  4. Use the connector bese (and then) to link two closely following actions within a single narrative turn.
  5. Ask and answer simple sequencing questions such as Kwenzekeni kuqala? (What happened first?) and Kwenzekeni ngemva kwalokho? (What happened after that?).
  6. Narrate a short personal account of three to five past events in a coherent order, orally and in writing.
  7. Read a short narrative built from sequencing connectors and past-tense verbs, and identify the order of events.
  8. Write a short paragraph narrating a sequence of past events using time expressions and sequencing connectors.
  9. Recognize, without full analysis, a small number of narrative-tense verb forms that some speakers use in storytelling, understanding that this chapter's productive narration continues to rely on the past tense already learned.

Communication Scenario

It is Tuesday evening, and Zanele is telling her friend Thandeka about her school day, which included the group project her teacher, uThisha Mchunu, assigned in Chapter 16. Zanele wants to explain, in order, what happened that morning, at midday, and in the afternoon, so that Thandeka can understand the whole story rather than a jumble of separate events. Later, Zanele's uncle asks her to retell the same story more briefly, using only the most important moments, first, next, and last.

  1. Think of three things that happened to you yesterday, in the order they happened. In English, write each one as a short sentence.
  2. In English, words such as "first," "then," and "finally" help a listener follow a story in order. Write down any isiZulu words you already know, or have guessed, that might do the same job.

Pronunciation and Tone

Rhythm in connected narration

A short spoken narrative in isiZulu is not simply a chain of separate, evenly stressed sentences. Each clause keeps its own penultimate lengthening, the pattern you have practised since Chapter 1, but a fluent narrator lets the voice fall gently at the end of each event and then rise slightly at the start of the next, similar to how a fluent English storyteller signals continuation with intonation rather than a full stop.

Practise saying Kuqala, ngahamba esikoleni. with a slight forward lean in the voice, as though more of the story is still coming, then Ekugcineni, ngajabula kakhulu. with a clear falling close, signalling the end of the account.

Pronunciation Focus — Connector placement and phrasing

The connectors kuqala, ngemva kwalokho, and ekugcineni are usually pronounced as their own short phrase, followed by a brief pause, before the verb that follows. Practise Kuqala, [pause] ngafika esikoleni. rather than running the connector directly into the verb without any separation. This short pause helps a listener track each new step in the sequence.

The connector bese, by contrast, is normally pronounced closely joined to the words that follow it, without a pause, since it links two actions that belong to the same narrative moment.

Reviewing past-tense stress patterns

Before continuing, review the past-tense forms you learned in Chapter 11, since this chapter relies on them heavily. Confirm that you place the natural penultimate lengthening correctly in forms such as ngahamba and ngafika, rather than transferring English stress patterns onto the isiZulu word.

Grammar Instruction

17.1 Reviewing the past tense for narration

Purpose: recycle the past-tense verb forms from Chapter 11 productively, this time inside a connected sequence of events rather than in single isolated sentences.

In Chapter 11, you learned to report a single past event using the subject concord plus the past-tense marker, such as ngahamba (I went) or wafika (he/she arrived). This chapter does not introduce a new tense. Instead, it teaches you to string several of these familiar past-tense forms together into a short, ordered account, using the sequencing connectors introduced in this chapter.

Language Building Block — Reviewing the past tense
[subject concord]-a-[verb stem]-a
This is the same past-tense pattern from Chapter 11. No new verb morphology is introduced in this chapter. The new work is connecting several past-tense clauses in order, using kuqala, ngemva kwalokho, bese, and ekugcineni.
Ngahamba esikoleni ekuseni.
I went to school in the morning.

17.2 Sequencing with kuqala, ngemva kwalokho, and ekugcineni

Purpose: connect three or more past events in order, using a clear opening, middle, and closing connector.

A short isiZulu narrative typically opens an account with kuqala, moves to further events with ngemva kwalokho, and closes with ekugcineni. Each connector normally begins its own clause, followed by a comma in writing and a brief pause in speech.

Language Building Block — A three-step narrative
Kuqala, [past-tense clause]. Ngemva kwalokho, [past-tense clause]. Ekugcineni, [past-tense clause].
Each connector opens a new clause. The past-tense verb form inside each clause follows the pattern you already know from Chapter 11. The connectors carry the order of events, so the verb forms themselves do not need to change to show sequence.
Kuqala, ngafika esikoleni. Ngemva kwalokho, sasebenza ngephrojekthi. Ekugcineni, sajabula kakhulu.
First, I arrived at school. After that, we worked on the project. Finally, we were very happy.
  • Kuqala, ngavuka ekuseni. First, I woke up in the morning.
  • Ngemva kwalokho, ngadla ukudla kwasekuseni. After that, I ate breakfast.
  • Ekugcineni, ngahamba esikoleni. Finally, I went to school.

Practice — Sequencing with the three main connectors

Put the following events in a natural order using kuqala, ngemva kwalokho, and ekugcineni.

  1. sajabula (we were happy) / safika esikoleni (we arrived at school) / sasebenza ngephrojekthi (we worked on the project) →
  2. wadla ukudla kwasekuseni (he/she ate breakfast) / wavuka (he/she woke up) / wahamba emsebenzini (he/she went to work) →

17.3 Linking two close actions with bese

Purpose: connect two actions that happen close together in time, without opening a new full sentence for each one.

The connector bese links two actions that follow each other closely, similar to English "and then" inside the same breath. Unlike kuqala, ngemva kwalokho, and ekugcineni, which usually open their own clause, bese joins two verbs within a single narrative turn, and is commonly followed by a verb form recycled from the present tense used with a past-time meaning in this fixed frame. At this stage, treat bese as a formulaic connector for recognition and limited production, joining two familiar past-tense-meaning clauses.

Language Building Block — Linking with bese
[clause], bese [clause].
Bese links two closely following actions. Learn short bese phrases as fixed units at this stage, without analyzing the internal verb form in detail. Full analysis of narrative-sequence verb forms is developed in Volume II.
Sahlangana nabangane, bese sadlala ibhola.
We met with friends, and then we played ball.
  • Ngafika ekhaya, bese ngalungisa ikhishi. I arrived home, and then I tidied the kitchen.
  • Wavula incwadi, bese wafunda. He/She opened the book, and then read.

17.4 Asking and answering sequencing questions

Purpose: ask what happened at each stage of an account, and answer using the connector that matches the question.

To check the order of a story, ask Kwenzekeni kuqala? (What happened first?), Kwenzekeni ngemva kwalokho? (What happened after that?), or Kwenzekeni ekugcineni? (What happened finally?). Answer with the matching connector and a past-tense clause.

  • Kwenzekeni kuqala? — Kuqala, sahlangana nothisha. What happened first? — First, we met with the teacher.
  • Kwenzekeni ekugcineni? — Ekugcineni, sagcina umsebenzi wethu. What happened finally? — Finally, we finished our work.

Note on scope: This chapter's productive narration relies entirely on the past-tense forms already active since Chapter 11, joined by the sequencing connectors taught here. A small number of alternative narrative-tense verb forms exist in fuller isiZulu storytelling and are receptive only at this stage; full narrative-tense analysis is developed in Volume II and Volume III.

Language Focus

This chapter's central grammatical development is discourse-level sequencing: connecting familiar past-tense clauses with kuqala, ngemva kwalokho, bese, and ekugcineni, supported by the time expressions ekuseni, emini, and ntambama. No new verb tense is introduced. The command and request forms from Chapter 16 continue to recur in this chapter's dialogue and reading, showing how earlier systems remain active alongside new ones.

Model Dialogue

Isimo: Ntambama, ekhaya, uZanele uxoxela umngane wakhe uThandeka ngosuku lwakhe esikoleni. Ngemva kwalokho, umalume kaZanele umcela ukuba aphinde indaba ngokufushane.
Setting: In the afternoon, at home, Zanele tells her friend Thandeka about her day at school. Afterward, Zanele's uncle asks her to retell the story briefly.

UTHANDEKA Sawubona, Zanele! Kwenzekeni namuhla esikoleni? Hello, Zanele! What happened today at school?
UZANELE Sawubona, Thandeka. Kwaba usuku olude, kodwa olujabulisayo. Kuqala, safika esikoleni ekuseni. Hello, Thandeka. It was a long day, but a pleasant one. First, we arrived at school in the morning.
UTHANDEKA Kwenzekeni ngemva kwalokho? What happened after that?
UZANELE Ngemva kwalokho, uThisha Mchunu wasinika imiyalo yephrojekthi. Sahlala phansi, bese saqala umsebenzi. After that, Uthisha Mchunu gave us instructions for the project. We sat down, and then we began the work.
UTHANDEKA Nenzeni emini? What did you do at midday?
UZANELE Emini, ngaya ehhovisi lesikole ukuze ngicele incwadi entsha. UNkosazana Dube wangisiza. At midday, I went to the school office to request a new book. Miss Dube helped me.
UTHANDEKA Kwenzekeni ekugcineni? What happened finally?
UZANELE Ekugcineni, sagcina iphrojekthi, bese sajabula kakhulu. Ntambama, ngabuyela ekhaya, ngashanela ikhishi, njengoba umama ecelile. Finally, we finished the project, and then we were very happy. In the afternoon, I returned home, and I swept the kitchen, as my mother had requested.
Ngemva kwalokho, kufika umalume kaZanele. — After that, Zanele's uncle arrives.
UMALUME Ngizwile ukuthi unendaba emnandi. Ngicela uyiphinde ngokufushane. I heard you have a good story. Please retell it briefly.
UZANELE Kulungile, Malume. Kuqala, safika esikoleni. Ngemva kwalokho, saqala iphrojekthi. Ekugcineni, sayigcina, saze sajabula. All right, Uncle. First, we arrived at school. After that, we began the project. Finally, we finished it, and we were very happy.
UMALUME Indaba enhle leyo. Ngiyabonga ngokuyixoxa kahle. That is a good story. Thank you for telling it well.

Two expressions in this dialogue extend slightly beyond full productive analysis at this stage: saze sajabula (we even became happy, an intensifying connector) and njengoba umama ecelile (as my mother had requested, a subordinate clause with a perfect-like form) are receptive. Focus your productive study on the sequencing connectors, the time expressions, and the past-tense clauses identified in the Grammar section.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Kwenzekeni kuqala, ngokusho kukaZanele? (What happened first, according to what Zanele says?)
  2. UZanele wenzeni emini esikoleni? (What did Zanele do at midday at school?)
  3. Kwenzekeni ekugcineni ephrojekthini? (What happened finally with the project?)
  4. UZanele wenzeni ntambama, ngemva kokubuyela ekhaya? (What did Zanele do in the afternoon, after returning home?)

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Sharing the day's events with family

In many isiZulu-speaking households, recounting the events of the day to family members in the evening is a valued daily practice, connecting family members and reinforcing a sense of shared life. As seen in this chapter's dialogue, Zanele's telling of her day to Thandeka, and again more briefly to her uncle, reflects this everyday storytelling habit rather than a formal or unusual event.

Culture in Context — Retelling a story briefly for an elder

Notice that Zanele retells her story more briefly and directly when asked by her uncle, an elder relative, compared with the fuller version she gave her friend Thandeka. Adjusting the length and detail of an account according to the listener's relationship and status is a common communicative skill in isiZulu-speaking communities, connecting to the respect-language principles introduced in Chapter 1 and Chapter 16.

Regional Note — Variation in everyday sequencing words

Alongside kuqala, ngemva kwalokho, and ekugcineni, speakers across different regions may use additional or slightly different connecting words in everyday storytelling. This textbook establishes the three connectors above as the primary productive forms for Volume I, since they are widely understood, and introduces additional narrative connectors receptively as they arise in later chapters and volumes.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using the present tense inside a past-time narrative

Error: Writing *Kuqala, ngiyahamba esikoleni. to mean "First, I went to school," by keeping the present-tense form ngiyahamba instead of switching to the past tense.

Correction: A past-time narrative requires the past-tense form throughout. Kuqala, ngahamba esikoleni. (First, I went to school.) is correct. Review the past-tense pattern from Chapter 11 if this form feels unfamiliar.

Common Mistake — Omitting the comma pause after an opening connector

Error: Writing *Kuqala ngafika esikoleni without any separation between the connector and the clause that follows.

Correction: In writing, place a comma after an opening connector such as kuqala, ngemva kwalokho, or ekugcineni, matching the brief pause used in speech: Kuqala, ngafika esikoleni. (First, I arrived at school.)

Common Mistake — Using ekugcineni to open a narrative

Error: Writing *Ekugcineni, safika esikoleni. Kuqala, sajabula., by placing ekugcineni before the first event and kuqala after the last one, reversing the natural order of the connectors.

Correction: Keep kuqala at the opening of the account and ekugcineni at the closing: Kuqala, safika esikoleni. Ekugcineni, sajabula. (First, we arrived at school. Finally, we were happy.)

Common Mistake — Overusing bese for every connection

Error: Writing *Safika bese sasebenza bese sajabula bese sabuya., stringing an entire narrative together with only bese, instead of using the fuller connectors for major steps.

Correction: Reserve bese for two closely following actions within a single narrative moment, and use kuqala, ngemva kwalokho, and ekugcineni for the larger steps of the account: Kuqala, safika. Ngemva kwalokho, sasebenza, bese sajabula. Ekugcineni, sabuya.

Answer Key

Practice — Sequencing with the three main connectors

  1. Kuqala, safika esikoleni. Ngemva kwalokho, sasebenza ngephrojekthi. Ekugcineni, sajabula.
  2. Kuqala, wavuka. Ngemva kwalokho, wadla ukudla kwasekuseni. Ekugcineni, wahamba emsebenzini.

Dialogue — Comprehension

  1. Kuqala, uZanele nabangane bakhe bafika esikoleni ekuseni.
  2. Emini, wahamba ehhovisi lesikole ukuze acele incwadi entsha.
  3. Ekugcineni, baqeda iphrojekthi, bajabula kakhulu.
  4. Ntambama, wabuyela ekhaya, washanela ikhishi, njengoba umama ecelile.

Listening

  • Passage 1 — Sipho's day: first, he woke up; after that, he ate breakfast and went to work; finally, he returned home in the afternoon and was happy.
  • Passage 2 — The class project: first, the students arrived in class; after that, the teacher gave instructions and they began working; finally, they finished the work and were very happy.

Bonus: Accept any correct sentence built on a connector identified in the passages, e.g., Ngemva kwalokho, baqala ukusebenza.

Reading — Comprehension

  1. Kuqala, abafundi bangena ekilasini, bahlala ezihlalweni zabo.
  2. Ekuseni, wadla ukudla kwasekuseni nomndeni wakhe.
  3. Wahlangana nabangane bakhe emsangweni wesikole.
  4. Ekugcineni, waxoxela umalume indaba yakhe, wajabula naye.

Chapter Review — Ingxenye A

  1. kuqala = first; ngemva kwalokho = after that; bese = and then; ekugcineni = finally.
  2. ekuseni = in the morning; emini = at midday; ntambama = in the afternoon; ebusuku = at night.
  3. Ngemva kwalokho usually opens its own clause and marks a clear new step in the account, while bese links two actions closely together within the same narrative moment, without opening a fully separate clause.

Chapter Review — Ingxenye B

  1. Kuqala, wavuka. Ngemva kwalokho, wahamba emsebenzini. Ekugcineni, wajabula.
  2. (a) Sample answer: Ekuseni izolo, ngavuka, ngadla ukudla kwasekuseni. (b) Sample answer: Ekugcineni kosuku lwami izolo, ngajabula kakhulu.
  3. Corrections: (a) Kuqala, ngahamba esikoleni. — Use the past tense throughout a past-time narrative. (b) Kuqala, safika esikoleni. Ekugcineni, sajabula. — Keep kuqala at the opening and ekugcineni at the closing.

Looking Ahead

Okungakwenzeka manje — What you can do now

You can connect several past events into a short, ordered account, using kuqala, ngemva kwalokho, bese, and ekugcineni, supported by the time expressions ekuseni, emini, and ntambama. These sequencing tools, together with the past-tense forms from Chapter 11 and the command and request patterns from Chapter 16, give you the ability to tell a coherent short story about your own life. Chapter 18 will build on your growing reading and writing skills, introducing practical notices and short messages so you can read everyday public texts and write your own.

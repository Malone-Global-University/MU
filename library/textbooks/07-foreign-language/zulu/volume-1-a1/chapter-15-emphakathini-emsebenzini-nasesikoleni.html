Emphakathini, emsebenzini nasesikoleni

Community, Work, and School · Speak and Write Zulu: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common occupations and community roles using productive vocabulary, with attention to class 1/2 human nouns and the class 9/10 and class 7/8 patterns already stabilized in earlier chapters.
  2. Describe what a person does for work using the frame Ngu___ (He/She is a ___) and the verb phrase usebenza njenge___ (works as a ___).
  3. Extend subject concord control to third-person singular and plural forms across the noun classes active in this volume, building on the first and second-person concords consolidated in Chapters 1 through 14.
  4. Use the class 3/4 noun pattern productively, including umsebenzi (work, job), umuzi (homestead, household), and umgwaqo (road), which appeared receptively in Chapter 14.
  5. Describe school life using vocabulary for subjects, classrooms, and daily school routines, recycling time expressions from Chapter 8 and locatives from Chapters 7, 11, 13, and 14.
  6. Ask and answer questions about someone else's occupation, workplace, or school using the question word ini (what) and the established locative and possessive patterns.
  7. Describe simple community roles and institutions, including induna (community leader), umphathi (manager, supervisor), and usomabhizinisi (businessperson), at a productive or receptive level as indicated.
  8. Read a short community notice and a simple biography describing someone's work and daily routine.
  9. Write a short paragraph describing your own work or studies, or those of someone you know, using third-person subject concords accurately.
  10. Distinguish between describing a permanent role using the copulative ngu- and describing an ongoing activity using the present tense verb, applying both correctly in community and workplace contexts.

Communication Scenario

Lwazi has returned to Pietermaritzburg after his weekend in Durban and is now telling his cousin Zanele about the people in his neighbourhood. Zanele is a final-year student at a local high school and is curious about what different community members do for work. As they walk to the local shop, Lwazi points out a teacher heading into the school, a nurse leaving the clinic, and a shopkeeper opening his store. Later, Zanele describes her own school day and her plans to study further after she finishes matric. The two of them also talk about their uncle, who works as a taxi driver, and their aunt, who is a community health worker.

  1. Think about three people you know well: a family member, a friend, and a neighbour. In English, write one sentence describing what each of them does for work or study.
  2. You already know the pattern Ngi-... for talking about yourself. How do you think isiZulu might change the beginning of a sentence to talk about someone else, such as "he" or "she"? Write down your guess, or explain in English what you expect to be different.

Pronunciation and Tone

The syllable cluster in umsebenzi

The word umsebenzi (work, job) appears throughout this chapter and is worth practising carefully. It breaks into five syllables: u-mse-be-nzi, or more naturally in connected speech, um-se-be-nzi. The consonant cluster nz in the final syllable is a single prenasalized consonant, similar to the nj cluster in kanjani from Chapter 14. Practise the final syllable nzi as one smooth unit, without a pause between the nasal and the following consonant.

um-se-be-nzi  |  um-su-thi-like pattern  |  u-mu-zi  |  i-si-fu-ndo

Notice that the penultimate syllable, be, is where the natural lengthening falls when the word occurs at the end of a phrase, as in Ngiya emsebenzini. (I am going to work.)

Pronunciation Focus — Third-person subject concords and vowel contact

Several third-person subject concords in this chapter begin with a vowel, such as u- (he/she) and ba- (they, class 2). When a vowel-initial concord meets a verb root that also begins with a vowel, a small adjustment in pronunciation naturally occurs, and you may notice a slight glide sound between the two vowels in fluent speech. At this stage, you are not required to analyze or produce these glide adjustments productively. Simply pronounce each element clearly and at a natural pace, and trust that comprehension will remain clear even if your pronunciation is not perfectly fluent at first.

Focus your practice on keeping the subject concord u- distinct and audible at the beginning of third-person sentences, since dropping it entirely is a common early error that can obscure meaning.

Stress in compound occupation and school terms

Compound terms such as umsebenzi wezempilo yomphakathi (community health worker) and umsebenzi wasekhaya (housework) are long, but each word within the compound keeps its own penultimate lengthening. Practise each word separately first, then join them at a natural conversational pace, allowing a very brief break between words rather than rushing the whole phrase as a single unit.

Grammar Instruction

15.1 Third-person subject concords for people

Purpose: extend subject concord control from first and second person to third person, so that you can talk about other people's work, studies, and daily activities.

Throughout Chapters 1 through 14, you have used the first-person subject concord ngi- (I) and, receptively, second-person forms. This chapter activates the third-person singular subject concord u- (he, she) and the third-person plural subject concord ba- (they, for class 1/2 human nouns) for full productive use. These concords attach to the verb in the same slot you already know from the verb template: subject concord, followed by tense marker where needed, followed by the verb root and final vowel.

Language Building Block — Third-person human subject concords
u- (he/she)   |   ba- (they, class 2)
The concord u- replaces ngi- when the subject is a single other person. The concord ba- replaces ngi- when the subject is more than one person. These concords occupy the same subject-concord slot in the verb template that you have used since Chapter 1, immediately before the tense marker or verb root.
Usebenza ehhovisi.  |  Basebenza esikoleni.
He/She works at an office.  |  They work at a school.
  • Uthisha usebenza esikoleni. The teacher works at a school.
  • Udadewethu ufunda enyuvesi. My sister studies at university.
  • Umalume usebenza njengomshayeli wethekisi. My uncle works as a taxi driver.
  • Othisha bafika esikoleni ekuseni. The teachers arrive at school in the morning.
  • Abafundi bafunda izibalo nesiNgisi. The students study mathematics and English.

Note on plural subject nouns and the ba- concord: When the subject noun is already plural, such as othisha (teachers) or abafundi (students), the verb still takes the concord that matches the noun class, which for class 1a/2a and class 1/2 plural human nouns is ba-. This agreement pattern, where the verb concord matches the noun class of the subject rather than simply meaning "they" in the abstract, is the beginning of full concord propagation, which is developed extensively in Volume II. At this stage, learn ba- as the concord for plural human subjects.

15.2 Identifying someone's occupation with the copulative ngu-

Purpose: use the copulative prefix ngu- to identify who someone is or what their occupation is, distinguishing this permanent-identity frame from the ongoing-activity frame with -sebenza.

isiZulu distinguishes between stating what someone is, using a copulative construction, and describing what someone does as an activity, using a full verb. To identify a person's occupation as a category, use the copulative prefix ngu- directly before the occupation noun, without a separate verb. This pattern is similar to the identification pattern you learned in Chapter 3 for introducing family members.

Language Building Block — Occupation identification with ngu-
Ngu-[occupation noun].
The copulative prefix ngu- attaches directly to a class 1a/2a noun or certain class 1/2 nouns to identify who or what someone is. No separate verb is required. This is the same structural pattern used in Chapter 3 for statements such as Ngumama wami (She is my mother).
Ubaba ungudokotela.
My father is a doctor.
  • Ungumhlengikazi. She is a nurse.
  • Umalume ungumshayeli. My uncle is a driver.
  • Ubabekazi ungumthengisi. My aunt is a vendor / shopkeeper.
  • Nguthisha wesiZulu. He/She is an isiZulu teacher.

Note on ungu- versus ngu-: When the third-person subject concord u- combines with the copulative ngu-, the two elements join to form ungu-, as in Ungumhlengikazi (She is a nurse). When there is no separate subject concord needed, such as in a short answer to "Who is this?", the bare form ngu- is used, as in Nguthisha. (He/She is a teacher.) Both forms are productive at this stage; use ungu- when a subject noun or pronoun context requires the third-person concord, and use the bare ngu- form in short identifying answers.

15.3 Class 3/4 nouns in productive use

Purpose: use class 3/4 nouns, including umsebenzi and umuzi, productively in sentences, building on the receptive exposure to this class in Chapter 14.

Class 3/4 nouns take the singular prefix um- and the plural prefix imi-. You encountered this pattern receptively in Chapter 14 with words like umgwaqo (road) and umtapo (library). This chapter activates class 3/4 for full productive use, centred on umsebenzi (work, job) and umuzi (homestead, home).

Singular Plural Locative English
umsebenziimisebenziemsebenziniwork / job, jobs / tasks, at work
umuziimiziemzinihomestead / home, homes, at home
umgwaqoimigwaqoemgwaqweniroad, roads, on the road (recycled from Chapter 14)
umtapoimitapoemtapwenilibrary, libraries, at the library (recycled from Chapter 14)

The class 3 subject concord for these nouns, when they themselves are the subject of a sentence, is u-, the same form as the third-person human singular concord. This overlap is a helpful coincidence for beginners: you already know how to use u- as a subject concord from Section 15.1, and class 3 nouns use the identical form.

  • Umsebenzi wami uqala ngo-8 ekuseni. My work starts at 8 in the morning.
  • Umuzi wethu ukude nesikole. Our home is far from the school.
  • Ngiya emsebenzini ngethekisi. I go to work by taxi.
  • Ngihlala emzini omkhulu nomndeni wami. I live in a large homestead with my family.

15.4 Describing school life and daily routines

Purpose: combine school vocabulary, time expressions from Chapter 8, and third-person subject concords to describe a typical school day.

Describing school life recycles the daily-routine patterns from Chapter 8 and applies them to a third person, such as a sibling, cousin, or friend who is still at school. A simple school-day description follows the pattern: subject, verb of arrival or activity, location, time.

Language Building Block — School day description
U-[name] u-fika e-sikoleni ngo-[time]. U-funda i-[subject].
The third-person concord u- carries the subject. The verb -fika (to arrive) recycles from Chapter 5. The locative esikoleni uses the established e-...-ini pattern. The verb -funda (to study, to learn) introduces the subject being studied, often a class 7 or class 8 noun.
UZanele ufika esikoleni ngo-7:30. Ufunda izibalo nesiNgisi.
Zanele arrives at school at 7:30. She studies mathematics and English.
  • Umfowethu ufunda ebangeni leshumi. My brother is in grade ten (literally: studies in grade ten).
  • Abafundi bafika esikoleni ngo-7:30 ekuseni. The students arrive at school at 7:30 in the morning.
  • Uthisha wami wesiZulu uthanda umsebenzi wakhe. My isiZulu teacher likes his/her work.
  • Ngemva kwesikole, ngiyafunda ekhaya. After school, I study at home.

Practice — School life description

Write a short school-day description for the cue provided, using the pattern above.

  1. uThemba, arrives at school at 8:00, studies mathematics.
  2. your friend, is in grade eleven, likes English.
  3. abafundi, arrive at school at 7:00, study isiZulu and biology.

15.5 Asking about someone else's work or studies

Purpose: ask questions about a third person's occupation, workplace, or place of study, using established and newly activated question frames.

To ask what someone does for work, use Wenzani? (What does he/she do?), which combines the third-person concord u-, the verb -enza (to do), and the question word -ni (what). To ask where someone works or studies, use the formulaic frames Usebenzelaphi? and Ufunda kuphi?

Question English Notes
Wenzani?What does he/she do?occupation question
Usebenzelaphi?Where does he/she work?workplace question, formulaic
Ufunda kuphi?Where does he/she study?study-location question
Ungudokotela yini?Is he/she a doctor?yes/no occupation question, recycled -yini pattern
  • Wenzani umalume wakho? — Ungumshayeli. What does your uncle do? — He is a driver.
  • Usebenzelaphi udadewenu? — Usebenza ehhovisi. Where does your sister work? — She works at an office.
  • Ufunda kuphi umfowenu? — Ufunda esikoleni esikhulu. Where does your brother study? — He studies at a large school.
Language Focus

This chapter's central grammatical development is the extension of subject concords into the third person, together with the productive activation of class 3/4 nouns. Keep two frames clearly distinct: the copulative ngu- frame for identifying who someone is by occupation, and the verb-based -sebenza and -funda frames for describing ongoing work or study activity. The question frames Wenzani?, Usebenzelaphi?, and Ufunda kuphi? are learned here as productive formulaic units; their internal structure, including the applicative-like ending on -sebenzela, will be analyzed explicitly when verb extensions are activated in Volume III.

Model Dialogue

Isimo: ULwazi noZanele bahamba ngasesitolo emphakathini wasePietermaritzburg. ULwazi ubuya eThekwini ngempelasonto. UZanele ungumfundi wasesikoleni, usebanga leshumi nambili. Ubudlelwane: bangamashona omndeni, bakhulukumana ngokukhululekile.
Setting: Lwazi and Zanele are walking near the shop in their Pietermaritzburg neighbourhood. Lwazi has just returned from Durban over the weekend. Zanele is a school student in grade twelve. Relationship: cousins, speaking comfortably and informally with each other.

ULWAZI Sawubona Zanele! Unjani? Hello Zanele! How are you?
UZANELE Ngikhona, ngiyabonga. Wena unjani? Ubuye kanjani eThekwini? I am well, thank you. How are you? How was your trip back from Durban?
ULWAZI Ngikhona kahle. Bheka lowo muntu, uphuma esikoleni. Ngubani lowo? I am fine. Look at that person coming out of the school. Who is that?
UZANELE Nguthisha wami wesiZulu. Usebenza kanzima futhi uthanda umsebenzi wakhe. That is my isiZulu teacher. He works hard and likes his work.
ULWAZI Kuhle. Wena, usebanga bani manje? That's good. What grade are you in now?
UZANELE Ngisebanga leshumi nambili. Ngifunda izibalo, isiNgisi, nesiZulu. Ngizoqeda umatikuletsheni kulo nyaka. I am in grade twelve. I study mathematics, English, and isiZulu. I will finish matric this year.
ULWAZI Uzokwenzani ngemva kwesikole? What will you do after school?
UZANELE Ngifuna ukuya enyuvesi. Umalume, wena umkhumbula, usebenza njengomshayeli wethekisi, kodwa ufuna ngibe udokotela. I want to go to university. Uncle, you remember him, works as a taxi driver, but he wants me to become a doctor.
ULWAZI Nobabekazi usebenza njengomsebenzi wezempilo yomphakathi, akunjalo? And Aunt works as a community health worker, doesn't she?
UZANELE Yebo, uyasiza abantu emphakathini wonke. Umsebenzi wakhe uqala ekuseni kakhulu, cishe ngo-6. Yes, she helps people across the whole community. Her work starts very early, around 6.
ULWAZI Umndeni wethu unemisebenzi ehlukene kakhulu. Ngiyabonga ngokungitshela, Zanele. Ngiyabuya emzini manje. Our family has very different jobs. Thank you for telling me, Zanele. I am heading home now.
UZANELE Kulungile. Hamba kahle! All right. Go well!

Several expressions in this dialogue are receptive at this stage: Ubuye kanjani (how was your trip back, recent past), Ngizoqeda (I will finish, future with a new root), umkhumbula (you remember him, object concord), ngibe (that I become, subjunctive-like form), akunjalo (isn't that so, tag question), and unemisebenzi ehlukene (has different jobs, possessive verb with relative). Focus your productive study on the occupation identification, third-person subject concords, and class 3/4 nouns identified in the Grammar section.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Ngubani othisha kaZanele? Wenzani, futhi umuzwa kanjani ngomsebenzi wakhe? (Who is Zanele's teacher? What does he do, and how does he feel about his work?)
  2. Uzanele usebanga bani, futhi ufunda yiziphi izifundo? (What grade is Zanele in, and which subjects does she study?)
  3. Umalume kaZanele wenzani, futhi ufunani kuye? (What does Zanele's uncle do, and what does he want for her?)
  4. Ubabekazi kaZanele usebenza njengani, futhi umsebenzi wakhe uqala nini? (What does Zanele's aunt work as, and when does her work start?)

Isaziso somphakathi — Community notice

Osizayo emphakathini wethu:
Sinabantu abaningi abasiza emphakathini wethu njalo nsuku zonke. Udokotela Nkosi usebenza ekhliniki, esuka ngo-8 ekuseni kuya ngo-4 ntambama. Umhlengikazi Zulu usiza abagula emakhaya abo, ehamba ngemoto. Uthisha Mchunu ufundisa izibalo esikoleni esikhulu. Umlimi Ndlovu unikeza imifino emphakathini nsuku zonke ngoLwesihlanu. Uma udinga usizo, ungabuza umuntu ohlala eduze — bonke laba bantu bayasebenza kanzima ukusiza umphakathi wethu.

Umlando omfushane kaNokwanda — A short biography of Nokwanda

UNokwanda uneminyaka engamashumi amabili nambili ubudala. Usebanga lokugcina enyuvesi, efunda ukuba ngudokotela. Umsebenzi wakhe wesikole uqala ekuseni futhi uphela ntambama kakhulu, ngoba ufunda izifundo eziningi kanye nokusebenza esibhedlela. Uhlala emzini omncane eduze nenyuvesi, kodwa uvakashela umndeni wakhe ekhaya njalo ngempelasonto. Ubaba kaNokwanda ungumthengisi, kanti umama wakhe usebenza njengothisha wesikole samabanga aphansi. Bobabili bayaziqhenya ngoNokwanda, ngoba usebenza kanzima kakhulu futhi uthanda umsebenzi wakhe wesikole.

Several expressions in these texts are receptive: abasiza (those who help, relative clause), abagula (the sick, relative clause), unikeza (provides, gives, applicative-like), lokugcina (the last, ordinal-like form), eziningi (many, relative concord), and bayaziqhenya (they are proud, reflexive). Read for overall meaning using the vocabulary and grammar you know.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  2. UNokwanda ufunda ukuba yini enyuvesi? (What is Nokwanda studying to become at university?)
  3. Ubaba noMake bakaNokwanda benzani? (What do Nokwanda's father and mother do?)

Umsebenzi A (Self-description): Write a paragraph of five to seven sentences describing your own work or studies. Include your occupation or grade level using ngu- or a school-life statement, your workplace or school using a locative, at least one time reference, and a sentence describing whether you like your work or studies.

Umsebenzi B (Description of another person): Write a paragraph of five to seven sentences describing the work or studies of a family member or friend, using third-person subject concords throughout. Include their occupation or grade level, their workplace or school, at least one time reference, and a sentence describing their attitude toward their work or studies.

  1. Plan your content. Decide whether you are writing about yourself or another person. List the key facts you want to include: occupation or grade, workplace or school, a time reference, and an opinion or attitude statement.
  2. Write using active vocabulary. Use only occupation nouns, school vocabulary, and grammar patterns from this chapter and from earlier chapters. Do not attempt structures beyond the active scope.
  3. Check your subject concords. If writing about another person, confirm that every verb uses the third-person concord u- or ba-, not the first-person ngi-.
  4. Check your occupation identification. Confirm that you have used ngu- or ungu- correctly to identify an occupation, and that you have not confused this with the ongoing-activity verb -sebenza.
  5. Check your locatives. Confirm that workplace and school locations use the correct locative form from the vocabulary table, such as ehhovisi, esikoleni, or emsebenzini.
  6. Revise and write your final version. Check your draft against the editing checklist below before writing or typing the final version.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • Third-person subject concords u- and ba- are used correctly and consistently, without slipping back into ngi- when describing another person.
  • The copulative ngu- or ungu- is used for occupation identification, not confused with the verb -sebenza.
  • Class 3/4 nouns such as umsebenzi and umuzi use the correct singular, plural, and locative forms.
  • Workplace and school locatives use the correct forms from the vocabulary table.
  • Each sentence begins with a capital letter and ends with appropriate punctuation.
  • No analytical hyphens appear inside ordinary verb words or compound nouns.
  • Occupation nouns preserve the correct class prefix, particularly for class 1a/2a nouns such as uthisha and udokotela.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Community roles and mutual support

In many isiZulu-speaking communities, occupations are understood not only as individual work but also as a contribution to the wellbeing of the wider community, referred to as umphakathi. Roles such as teachers, nurses, community health workers, and small business owners are often spoken about with particular respect, because their work is seen as directly benefiting neighbours and extended family, not only the individual worker. When introducing someone's occupation, it is common and appropriate to add a short comment about how their work helps others, as seen in this chapter's reading passage about community helpers.

This community-oriented framing reflects a broader cultural value often summarized by the concept of ubuntu, an idea you will encounter in more depth in later volumes. For now, simply notice that occupation descriptions in isiZulu often include a brief statement of purpose or benefit, not just a job title.

Culture in Context — Respect for teachers and elders in education

Teachers, referred to as othisha in the plural, are generally addressed and discussed with a notable degree of respect in isiZulu-speaking communities. It is common to refer to a teacher by the title uthisha followed by their surname, rather than by a first name, even outside the classroom. This pattern extends the respect-language principles introduced in Chapter 1 and developed further in Chapter 19. When describing your own teachers in writing or speech, using the title-plus-surname pattern, such as uThisha Mchunu, is both natural and appropriate.

Regional Note — Occupation vocabulary in urban and rural contexts

Some occupation terms in this chapter, such as umlimi (farmer), are more commonly discussed in rural and peri-urban KwaZulu-Natal communities, while terms like umphathi (manager) and usomabhizinisi (businessperson) appear more frequently in urban contexts such as Durban and Pietermaritzburg. Both sets of vocabulary are part of standard productive isiZulu and are useful regardless of where you plan to use the language, since most learners will encounter both rural and urban contexts in South Africa.

Additionally, many professional titles in modern isiZulu, such as udokotela and usomabhizinisi, are established loanwords or loanword-based formations integrated into standard noun classes. This reflects the ongoing evolution of isiZulu vocabulary in professional and educational contexts, which you will study in more depth in later volumes.

Culture in Context — Talking about future plans and education

Discussions about a young person's future studies or career, as seen in this chapter's dialogue between Lwazi and Zanele, are a common and valued topic of conversation in isiZulu-speaking families. Family members, particularly uncles and aunts, often express clear hopes for a younger relative's future occupation, as Zanele's uncle does in the dialogue. Responding to such expectations with respect, even when your own plans differ, is considered good manners. A simple acknowledgment such as Ngiyabonga ngokungikhathalela (Thank you for caring about me) is a useful phrase to recognize this kind of family support, and will be developed further in later chapters.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using ngi- instead of u- or ba- for third-person subjects

Error: Writing *Ngisebenza ehhovisi to mean "He/She works at an office," by leaving the first-person concord ngi- in place instead of switching to the third-person concord.

Correction: When the subject of the sentence is someone other than the speaker, the subject concord must change to match. Usebenza ehhovisi (He/She works at an office) uses u-. Basebenza ehhovisi (They work at an office) uses ba-. Always check who the true subject of the sentence is before choosing the subject concord, especially when translating directly from an English sentence that begins with "he" or "she."

Common Mistake — Confusing ngu- identification with -sebenza activity

Error: Writing *Usebenza udokotela instead of Ungudokotela, by attempting to use the activity verb -sebenza where the identification copulative ngu- is required.

Correction: To identify what someone is as an occupation category, use the copulative ngu- or ungu-: Ungudokotela (He/She is a doctor). To describe the activity of working, use -sebenza: Usebenza esibhedlela (He/She works at a hospital). The two frames can combine in one sentence, as in Ungudokotela, usebenza esibhedlela (He/She is a doctor, working at a hospital), but they are not interchangeable on their own.

Common Mistake — Omitting the locative on workplace and school nouns

Error: Writing *Usebenza ihhovisi or *Ufunda isikole instead of Usebenza ehhovisi or Ufunda esikoleni, by omitting the locative prefix on the destination or location noun.

Correction: Just as with the destination nouns in Chapter 14, workplace and school nouns must appear in locative form when they express where an action takes place. Usebenza ehhovisi (He/She works at an office) and Ufunda esikoleni (He/She studies at school) both require the locative form. Review the vocabulary tables in this chapter and in Chapters 7, 11, 13, and 14 for the correct locative form of each place noun.

Common Mistake — Using the wrong plural concord for class 1a/2a occupation nouns

Error: Writing *Othisha isebenza esikoleni instead of Othisha basebenza esikoleni, by using a singular-style concord with a plural class 1a/2a subject.

Correction: Plural class 1a/2a nouns, such as othisha (teachers), take the plural human concord ba-, the same concord used for class 1/2 plural nouns like abafundi (students). Othisha basebenza esikoleni (The teachers work at school) is the correct form. Do not treat the plural prefix o- on the noun itself as sufficient; the verb still requires its own matching concord.

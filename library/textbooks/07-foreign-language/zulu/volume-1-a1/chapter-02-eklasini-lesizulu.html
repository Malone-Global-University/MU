Eklasini lesiZulu

In the isiZulu Classroom · Speak and Write Zulu: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify teachers, classmates, and other people in a classroom by name and role.
  2. Ask who a person is using Ubani lowo? and answer with a noun copulative identity statement.
  3. State a person's role or occupation using the class 1 copulative pattern.
  4. Name common classroom objects with supported reference to their noun class.
  5. Follow common classroom instructions given by your teacher.
  6. Ask for repetition or clarification in isiZulu.
  7. Count people and objects from 0 to 20.
  8. Recognize that isiZulu groups nouns into classes, and that human nouns belong to classes 1/2 and 1a/2a.
  9. Produce singular and plural noun forms for active human-class nouns.
  10. Demonstrate awareness of the dental click c in selected classroom vocabulary.
  11. Write simple identity statements using standard conjunctive isiZulu spelling.

Communication Scenario

It is the second meeting of your isiZulu class at a community learning center in Durban. The teacher, Nkosi Dlamini, is welcoming three new students who joined late. He points to people around the room and names their roles. Then he counts the students, writes on the board, holds up objects, and gives instructions in isiZulu. You want to understand what is happening, identify the people around you, and begin to participate. The classroom is where language learning starts, and the words you learn here will help you in every chapter that follows.

  1. Before you open the vocabulary section, think about what you would want to say about another person in a classroom. Make a list of three things: their name, their role, and one fact about them. You will use these ideas in the writing workshop at the end of the chapter.
  2. In English, we say "He is a student" or "She is a teacher." Does isiZulu work the same way? Make a prediction, then compare it with what you learn in the Grammar section.

Pronunciation and Orthography

The dental click: c

isiZulu has three click types, each represented by a different letter in the alphabet: c, q, and x. In Chapter 1 you were introduced to the idea that these letters do not represent English sounds. This chapter introduces the dental click, written c, through vocabulary you will use in the classroom.

The dental click is made by placing the tip or blade of your tongue just behind your upper front teeth, forming a seal, and then pulling the tongue sharply downward and backward while releasing the seal. The result is a sharp clicking sound. It is not a hiss, a "ts" sound, or a letter name from the English alphabet.

Pronunciation Focus — Producing the dental click c

Follow these steps with a qualified speaker or a verified audio model:

  1. Position: Rest the tip of your tongue lightly behind your upper front teeth, forming a gentle seal across the front of your mouth.
  2. Press: Create a light suction by pressing your tongue firmly against the teeth so that no air can pass.
  3. Release: Pull your tongue sharply downward and away from your teeth. The "pop" you produce is the dental click.
  4. Combine: Now add the vowel that follows. For ca, release the click and immediately pronounce a as in the isiZulu vowel you learned in Chapter 1.

The click is produced at the front of the mouth. It is a quick, sharp sound, not a drawn-out hiss. Practice the isolated click first, then combine it with a vowel: ca, ce, ci, co, cu. Then practice it inside a word.

Audio is essential for click practice. A text description alone is not sufficient. If no qualified speaker is available, this target should be marked for audio and specialist support during production.

The dental click c appears in some common isiZulu words you will encounter in the classroom and beyond. At this stage, your goal is to recognize the letter c as a click, to attempt the click sound with support, and to avoid substituting an English "c" or "s" sound in its place.

The clicks q and x will be introduced in Chapters 3 and 4. Each click also has accompaniment variants, including aspirated, voiced, and nasalized forms, which are introduced gradually as you encounter them in productive vocabulary. Do not attempt to learn all click accompaniments at once.

Plain click versus click accompaniments: first awareness

The plain dental click, written simply as c, is different from clicks with accompaniments such as ch (aspirated), gc (voiced), and nc (nasalized). At this stage, you need to:

  • Recognize that c alone represents the plain click.
  • Notice that combinations such as ch, gc, and nc are related but different sounds.
  • Aim to produce the plain click consistently before moving to accompanied forms.

High and low tone in phrases: first imitation

In Chapter 1 you learned that isiZulu is a tonal language in which high and low pitch distinguish words and grammatical patterns. In this chapter, begin listening for tone differences in the classroom instruction phrases. Notice in particular that the instruction Lalela and the response Ngiyezwa have distinct pitch patterns. Your goal at this stage is imitation of whole phrases, not analysis of individual tones.

Pronunciation Focus — Tone and phrase-final lengthening

In Chapter 1 you were introduced to tone as pitch rather than stress. In isiZulu, the second-to-last syllable (the penultimate syllable) is often lengthened when a word or phrase comes at the end of an utterance. You can hear this in common single-word commands spoken by themselves.

Listen to your instructor or audio model say Lalela in isolation. The middle syllable, le, may sound noticeably longer than when the same word appears in the middle of a longer sentence. This phenomenon is called penultimate lengthening. It is a feature of isiZulu prosody rather than a change in the word's underlying form. Do not add extra letters when you write the word; the standard spelling remains the same.

Orthography: full noun forms and augments

Every isiZulu noun in standard spelling consists of an augment (a short initial vowel), a class prefix, and a stem. In your vocabulary lists, the full standard form is always shown. When a grammar explanation shows the internal structure, it uses hyphens for clarity, for example:

  • umfundi u- (augment) + mu- (class 1 prefix) + -fundi (stem) student; learner
  • abafundi a- (augment) + ba- (class 2 prefix) + -fundi (stem) students; learners

The hyphenated analysis is only for learning. When you write isiZulu, always write the complete word without hyphens: umfundi, not *um-fundi. Analytical hyphens are never used in ordinary isiZulu writing.

Grammar Instruction

2.1 Noun classes in isiZulu: an introduction

Purpose: understand what a noun class is and why it matters for everything you will learn in isiZulu.

isiZulu does not have masculine and feminine grammatical gender like French or Spanish. Instead, it organizes all nouns into groups called noun classes. Each class has its own prefix for the singular and a different prefix for the plural. When you learn a noun in isiZulu, you need to learn its class so that other words in the sentence can agree with it.

Noun classes are numbered. In this chapter you will work productively with classes 1 and 2 (for most human nouns) and classes 1a and 2a (for personal names, kinship terms, and some other human nouns). These are the most important classes for talking about people.

Classes 1/2: most human nouns (singular/plural)
Class Augment + Prefix Example noun English
Class 1 (singular) u(mu)- umfundi student (one)
Class 2 (plural) a(ba)- abafundi students (more than one)
Class 1 (singular) u(mu)- umuntu person (one)
Class 2 (plural) a(ba)- abantu people (more than one)
Classes 1a/2a: personal names, kinship terms, some human nouns
Class Augment + Prefix Example noun English
Class 1a (singular) u- uThemba Themba (a personal name)
Class 2a (plural) o- oThemba Themba and others; the Thembas
Class 1a (singular) u- uthisha teacher (one)
Class 2a (plural) o- othisha teachers (more than one)
Grammar Note — Why do noun classes matter?

In isiZulu, when you use a noun in a sentence, many other words in that sentence must match the noun's class. This is called concord or agreement. For example, the word for "this" changes depending on which noun you are pointing to, and the verb changes too depending on whether the subject is class 1 or class 2.

You are not expected to control every concord pattern yet. In this chapter you will learn: the class 1 and 2 subject concords used in identity statements, and the near demonstratives for classes 1 and 2. Think of concord as a gradually expanding skill, not something you must master all at once.

2.2 Subject concords for classes 1, 1a, 2, and 2a

Purpose: identify the short marker that connects a class 1 or class 2 noun to its verb.

In isiZulu, the verb always carries information about its subject. The part of the verb that shows the subject's class is called the subject concord. For human nouns, the subject concords are:

Class Subject concord Used for
Class 1 / 1a (singular human) u- one person: umfundi, uthisha, uNandi
Class 2 / 2a (plural human) ba- more than one person: abafundi, othisha

You have already met these concords embedded in Chapter 1 greetings, without yet knowing the term. In Sawubona, the element u- refers to the one person you are greeting. In Sanibonani, the element replaced involves plural reference. Now you are beginning to understand why.

2.3 Identity and classification: noun copulatives

Purpose: state who a person is and what role they have, using the isiZulu identity construction.

In English, we use the verb "to be" to make identity and classification statements: "She is a teacher," "He is a student." isiZulu does not have a single word for "is" or "are" that works the same way. Instead, isiZulu uses a noun copulative: a construction in which the class prefix combines with the predicate noun to make a statement of identity or classification.

For class 1 human subjects, the construction uses the subject concord u- attached directly to the predicate noun:

Language Building Block
Subject noun + u- + predicate noun
The copulative prefix u- matches the class 1 subject and introduces the identity or role being stated. No separate "is" word is needed.
UNandi uthisha.  |  UThemba umfundi.
Nandi is a teacher.  |  Themba is a student.
  • UNandi uthisha. u-Nandi (class 1a subject) + u- (copulative prefix) + thisha (predicate noun stem) Nandi is a teacher.
  • UThemba umfundi. u-Themba (class 1a subject) + u- (copulative prefix) + mfundi (predicate noun stem) Themba is a student.
  • UMnu. Dlamini uthisha. u-Mnu. Dlamini (class 1a subject) + u- + thisha Mr. Dlamini is a teacher.
  • Ngumfundi. ngu- (first person copulative) + mfundi I am a student.

The first-person copulative uses ngu- rather than u-. Ngumfundi means "I am a student." This is a formulaic productive form that you should learn now; the full analysis of first- and second-person copulatives is scheduled for Chapter 3. For now, learn Ngumfundi and Nguthisha (I am a teacher) as useful identity statements.

Grammar Note — Why does the predicate noun look different?

You may have noticed that in UNandi uthisha, the word uthisha looks the same as in the vocabulary table. But in UThemba umfundi, the predicate appears as umfundi. What is happening?

The copulative prefix is u- for class 1 and 1a subjects. When this u- combines with a predicate noun that itself begins with u- (like umfundi), the two vowels merge into one. The result sounds and is written as a single u. So u + umfundi becomes umfundi in the copulative sentence, while the class 1a noun uthisha (which begins differently) combines as u + thisha to give uthisha. These changes happen naturally and consistently according to vowel patterns in isiZulu. You will meet similar patterns throughout the course.

Practice — Identity statements

Write an isiZulu identity statement for each person. Use the noun copulative pattern you have just learned. Write the full word without hyphens.

  1. Sipho is a student. (Use uSipho)
  2. Nokuthula is a teacher. (Use uNokuthula)
  3. Say: "I am a student."
  4. Say: "I am a teacher."
  5. Mr. Nkosi is a teacher. (Use uMnu. Nkosi)

2.4 Asking who someone is: Ubani lowo?

Purpose: ask for a person's identity and respond correctly.

To ask "Who is that person?" use the question:

  • Ubani lowo? Who is that? Who is that person?
  • Ubani igama lakho? What is your name? (lit. What is your name?) — from Chapter 1, recycled here

A complete exchange looks like this:

  • Ubani lowo? Who is that?
  • Nguye uNomsa. Ungumfundi. That is Nomsa. She is a student.

The form nguye means "that one is" or "it is him/her" and is a formulaic third-person copulative. Learn it now as a useful response form. Its full analysis is scheduled for Chapter 3.

2.5 Near demonstratives for classes 1 and 2

Purpose: point to people near you using the correct demonstrative form.

A demonstrative is the word used to point to something: "this" or "that" in English. In isiZulu, demonstratives agree with the class of the noun they refer to. The near demonstrative (meaning "this one here") for classes 1 and 2 is introduced in this chapter:

Class Near demonstrative Example English
Class 1 (singular human) lo umfundi lo this student (here)
Class 2 (plural human) laba abafundi laba these students (here)

The demonstrative typically follows the noun it modifies in isiZulu:

  • Umfundi lo nguSipho. umfundi lo = this student + ngu- + Sipho This student is Sipho.
  • Abafundi laba baseklasini. abafundi laba = these students + ba- (class 2 subject concord) + seklasini These students are in the classroom. [formulaic — locative constructions are introduced in Chapter 6]
  • Uthisha lo nguNokuthula. uthisha lo = this teacher + ngu- + Nokuthula This teacher is Nokuthula.

The full demonstrative system in isiZulu is extensive. Each noun class has near, middle-distance, and far demonstrative forms, and there are further distinctions in isiZulu that do not map neatly onto English "this" and "that." In this chapter, you work only with the near demonstratives for classes 1 and 2. Further demonstrative forms are introduced as each new noun class enters productive use.

2.6 Counting people: numbers with human nouns

Purpose: state how many students or teachers are in the room using simple number expressions.

In Chapter 1 you learned numbers 0 to 10. This chapter extends that range to 20 and gives you practice counting people in the classroom context. At this stage, you will use number words in standalone counting expressions rather than in fully analyzed noun-number agreement patterns, which are introduced starting in Chapter 4.

  • Kunabafundi abaningaki eklasini? How many students are there in the class? [formulaic question — recognize and use as a whole]
  • Kunabafundi abayishumi nambili. There are twelve students. [formulaic with number — recognize as a whole at this stage]

A simpler approach for counting people uses standalone numbers:

  • Uthisha — munye. Teacher — one.
  • Abafundi — abaningi. Students — many.

Practice the numbers 11 to 20 by counting physical objects around you. Count classroom chairs, books, and windows, saying each number aloud as you point to each object.

Language Focus

The noun class is the foundation of isiZulu grammar. Every sentence you produce in isiZulu will be shaped by the class of the noun you are talking about. You have now taken your first step into this system by learning two things: that human nouns belong primarily to classes 1/2 and 1a/2a, and that the class 1 subject concord u- appears both in demonstratives and in identity statements.

Two principles to hold onto from this chapter: first, in isiZulu you say "who is that?" not "who are they?" because the demonstrative and copulative both agree with the singular or plural of the noun. Second, you do not need a separate word for "is" in identity statements, because the copulative prefix carries that meaning inside the predicate noun itself.

Model Dialogue

Indawo: Iklasi lesiZulu, isentheni yomphakathi eThekwini. Abafundi abathathu basanda kujoyina. UNkosi Dlamini uyabamukela futhi ababonisa izinto eklasini. Izingane zamfundi: uSipho, uNomsa, no-Ayesha. Ithisha nguNkosi Dlamini.
Setting: An isiZulu class at a community center in Durban. Three new students have just joined. Mr. Dlamini welcomes them and shows them things in the classroom. The students are Sipho, Nomsa, and Ayesha. The teacher is Mr. Dlamini.

NKOSI DLAMINI Sanibonani, abafundi! Ngiyajabula ukukwamukela. Hello, students! I am happy to welcome you. [Ngiyajabula ukukwamukela is a formulaic welcome — learn as a whole expression]
ABAFUNDI Sanibona, Nkosi Dlamini! Hello, Mr. Dlamini!

(UNkosi Dlamini ubheka uSipho — Mr. Dlamini looks at Sipho.)

NKOSI DLAMINI Ubani igama lakho? What is your name?
SIPHO Igama lami nguSipho. My name is Sipho.
NKOSI DLAMINI Sawubona, Sipho. Ungumfundi. Hello, Sipho. You are a student.

(UNkosi Dlamini ubheka uNomsa — Mr. Dlamini looks at Nomsa.)

NKOSI DLAMINI Ubani igama lakho? What is your name?
NOMSA Igama lami nguNomsa. Ngiyajabula ukubekhona. My name is Nomsa. I am happy to be here. [formulaic — learn as a whole]

(UNkosi Dlamini ubheke ibhodi — Mr. Dlamini looks at the board.)

NKOSI DLAMINI Lalelani. Lena yibhodi. Lo ngumfundi — lo nguSipho. Laba ngabafundi. Listen (everyone). This is a board. This person is a student — this is Sipho. These are students. [ngabafundi — plural copulative form, receptive only at this stage]
AYESHA Ake uphinde? Angizwanga kahle. Could you say that again? I didn't hear clearly. [formulaic — learn as a whole]
NKOSI DLAMINI Kulungile. Phinda nami. Lena — yibhodi. That's fine. Repeat after me. This — is a board.
ABAFUNDI Lena yibhodi. This is a board.
NKOSI DLAMINI Kuhle. Manje, bhekani lapha. Ngingabala abafundi. Munye, nambili, nantathu — abafundi abathathu. Kulungile? Good. Now, look here (everyone). I am going to count the students. One, two, three — three students. Is that right? [Ngingabala is beyond current scope — receptive only]
ABAFUNDI Kulungile! That's right! / Correct!

Several expressions in this dialogue, including Ngiyajabula ukukwamukela, Ngingabala, and plural copulative forms such as ngabafundi, are beyond the current chapter's productive scope. They appear here as natural classroom language. Focus your active study on: the identity statements using u- + noun, the demonstrative forms lo and laba, classroom instructions, and the name exchange from Chapter 1.

Writing Workshop

In this chapter you will write a short connected set of identity statements about people in your real or imagined isiZulu classroom. This is your first isiZulu writing task involving complete sentences in Standard isiZulu orthography.

  1. Choose your people. Identify three people from your real class, or invent three people for an imagined classroom. Give each person a name and a role: teacher or student. Keep a note of these for your draft.
  2. Study the model. Read the passage Iklasi lethu lesiZulu in the Reading section. Notice how the writer: (a) introduces the class and the teacher, (b) mentions each student using Kukhona umfundi … and then gives a copulative identity statement, and (c) closes with a feeling about the class. Your writing will follow a similar structure at a simpler level.
  3. Draft. Write four to six sentences about your real or imagined classroom. Include: a statement identifying the teacher (Ithisha nguX), at least two identity statements about students using the noun copulative, and one sentence using a near demonstrative (Umfundi lo nguX).
  4. Add a count. Write one sentence stating how many students are in your classroom. Use a number from the vocabulary table.
  5. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist below before writing your final version.

Uhlelo lokubheka — Editing checklist:

  • Personal names begin with a capital letter: uSipho, not *usipho.
  • Each sentence begins with a capital letter and ends with a full stop.
  • Nouns are written as complete words without internal hyphens: umfundi, not *um-fundi.
  • The identity copulative is attached directly to the predicate noun: uthisha, not *u thisha with a space.
  • Demonstrative forms match the noun: lo with singular humans, laba with plural humans.
  • Numbers are written in full isiZulu words, not as numerals, in the sentence.
  • I have not used a separate isiZulu word for "is" — the copulative prefix does that work.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Respectful teacher-learner interaction

In many South African educational settings, the relationship between teachers and learners is shaped by conventions of respect. Addressing a teacher by their first name without a title is often considered impolite. In isiZulu-medium classrooms and in many multilingual South African schools, teachers may be addressed as Nkosi (a respectful title for a man), Nkosazane or Nkosikazi (for a woman), or by the abbreviations familiar from English, such as Mnu. (Mnumzane, Mr.) and Nksz. (for a woman). These practices vary by institution, region, and community.

Some teachers prefer to create a more informal, conversational classroom atmosphere, especially in adult language learning contexts. The best approach is to observe the conventions of your specific classroom and community rather than assuming one universal rule applies everywhere.

Culture in Context — Multilingual classrooms in South Africa

South Africa has eleven official languages. Many learners in isiZulu classrooms speak two, three, or more languages fluently and move between them in daily life. Your isiZulu class may include people whose home language is isiZulu, English, Sesotho, isiXhosa, Afrikaans, or another language. This diversity is a strength, not a complication.

IsiZulu-medium instruction does not require learners to suppress their other languages. Comparison with your home language, asking questions in English when isiZulu vocabulary is not yet available, and using context clues across languages are all normal parts of language learning. What matters in the classroom is engaging honestly with isiZulu structures as you encounter them, especially noun classes and concord, which differ from most European languages learners may already know.

Regional Note — Classroom and educational terminology

Educational vocabulary in South Africa draws on several languages. The word ithisha (teacher) is a widely used borrowing, as is iklasi (class). Some isiZulu speakers use more established isiZulu-origin terms, and vocabulary for newer technology in particular varies by speaker, region, and institution. Terms such as ikhompyutha (computer) and ifoni (phone) are broadly understood but their precise form and noun class may differ slightly across sources. Always verify current usage with a qualified specialist during production review.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Inserting "is" as a separate word

Error: Writing *USipho is umfundi or *USipho yena umfundi by inserting an English verb or pronoun to fill in what feels like a gap.

Correction: isiZulu identity statements do not use a separate word for "is." The copulative prefix is attached directly to the predicate noun: USipho ungumfundi — or more simply for a direct statement, USipho umfundi. The meaning "is" is carried inside the construction. Avoid inserting English words or invented isiZulu words to fill this position.

Common Mistake — Inserting a hyphen inside a written noun or verb word

Error: Writing *um-fundi or *a-ba-fundi in ordinary text because the grammar lesson showed internal analysis using hyphens.

Correction: Hyphens only appear in grammar explanations to show structure. In ordinary isiZulu writing, words are written as complete units: umfundi, abafundi, uthisha. This is called conjunctive spelling, and it is a feature of standard written isiZulu. Never use analytical hyphens in a learner writing task.

Common Mistake — Using the wrong demonstrative for plural nouns

Error: Writing *abafundi lo (using the singular demonstrative with a plural noun) instead of abafundi laba.

Correction: Demonstratives in isiZulu agree with the class and number of the noun they refer to. The singular human demonstrative is lo; the plural is laba. Since abafundi is class 2 (plural), the correct demonstrative is laba: abafundi laba. Every time you use a demonstrative, ask: is this noun singular or plural? Then choose the matching form.

Common Mistake — Treating "c" as an English letter sound

Error: Pronouncing c in an isiZulu word as the English "s" or "k" sound, because those are the sounds that "c" commonly represents in English words.

Correction: In isiZulu, c always represents the dental click, a sound made by pulling the tongue down from behind the upper front teeth. It is neither "s" nor "k." Substituting an English sound for the click makes isiZulu words difficult or impossible to understand. Use the step-by-step guidance in the Pronunciation section and listen to a qualified speaker until you can produce and recognize the click consistently.

Common Mistake — Confusing class 1a and class 1 noun prefixes

Error: Writing *umThemba (adding the class 1 prefix um-) when the correct form for a personal name is uThemba (class 1a prefix u-).

Correction: Personal names and a number of human nouns including uthisha belong to class 1a, which uses only u- as its prefix (no m element). Common nouns such as umfundi belong to class 1, which uses umu- (shortened to um- before most consonants). This distinction matters because it affects the copulative and concord forms. Pay attention to which prefix a noun takes when you first learn it.

Looking Ahead

Okwenziwa manje — What you can do now

You can identify people in a classroom by name and role using noun copulative identity statements. You can ask who someone is, respond with a near demonstrative, count people and objects from 0 to 20, follow and give basic classroom instructions, and write simple sentences in standard isiZulu orthography without internal hyphens. You have also taken the first step into the isiZulu noun-class system: you now know that human nouns belong primarily to classes 1/2 and 1a/2a, and that the class 1 subject concord u- is the foundation of identity statements in isiZulu.

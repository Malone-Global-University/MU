Imiyalo, izicelo, nokuqala kwesibopho

Commands, Requests, and Subjunctive Beginnings · Speak and Write Zulu: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Form the singular imperative for common verbs, using the bare verb stem, such as Hamba! (Go!) and Vula! (Open!).
  2. Form the plural imperative by adding the ending -ni, such as Hambani! (Go! to more than one person) and Vulani! (Open!).
  3. Recognize the small set of irregular imperative forms, including Woza! (Come!) and Yiza! (Come!, an alternative form), and use them productively.
  4. Make polite requests using the frame Ngicela u- plus a verb in the subjunctive form ending in -e, such as Ngicela uvule ifasitela (Please open the window), distinguishing this softened request from the direct imperative.
  5. Form basic negative commands, or prohibitives, using Musa uku- for one person and Musani uku- for more than one person, recycling the negative patterns introduced in Chapters 8 and 13.
  6. Recognize and use as formulaic units a small set of subjunctive-purpose expressions built with ukuze plus a subjunctive verb, such as ukuze ngifunde (so that I may study), without full analysis of the subjunctive system.
  7. Give and follow classroom, workplace, and household instructions using vocabulary and command forms active in this chapter.
  8. Read a short set of classroom and workplace notices written using the imperative and polite-request forms.
  9. Write a short set of instructions or a polite request note for a classroom, workplace, or household situation.
  10. Distinguish, in speech and writing, between a direct command, a softened request with ngicela, and a negative command, choosing the appropriate form for the social situation.

Communication Scenario

It is Monday morning at the school where Zanele's isiZulu teacher, uThisha Mchunu, is preparing his class for a group project. He gives the students a series of instructions for organizing their desks, opening their books, and working together quietly. After class, Zanele stops by the school office to ask the office assistant, uNkosazana Dube, for a favour, and is careful to phrase her request politely rather than as a direct command. Later that afternoon, at home, Zanele's mother gives her a list of household instructions before leaving for work, including one gentle prohibition about the stove. That evening, Zanele's uncle reminds her, using a short subjunctive-purpose phrase, why she is studying so hard this term.

  1. Think of three instructions a teacher, parent, or supervisor commonly gives. In English, write each one as a short command.
  2. In English, "please" softens a command into a request. What word or phrase do you expect isiZulu to use for a similar softening effect? Write down your guess, or explain what you already know.

Pronunciation and Tone

Intonation for direct commands

isiZulu commands are typically spoken with a falling intonation on the final syllable, similar to the falling intonation used for confident statements. This differs from the rising intonation you practised for yes/no questions in earlier chapters. Practise Hamba!, Vula!, and Thula! with a clear, firm fall on the final vowel, rather than the gentle rise used when asking Uyahamba? (Are you going?).

When a command is softened by Ngicela, the overall sentence intonation becomes more level and conversational, closer to an ordinary statement than to a sharp command. Compare the firm fall of Vala umnyango! (Close the door!) with the more even, polite intonation of Ngicela uvale umnyango. (Please close the door.)

Pronunciation Focus — The -ni ending in plural imperatives

The plural imperative ending -ni is added directly to the singular imperative form, and it always carries a light stress of its own, distinct from the penultimate lengthening pattern you have practised elsewhere. In a word like Hambani!, the natural phrase-final lengthening still falls on the penultimate syllable, which is -ba-, not on the final -ni. Practise saying Ham-BA-ni with a clear, slightly lengthened middle syllable, keeping the final -ni short and crisp.

Take care not to drop the final -ni in fast speech, since doing so removes the only signal that a command is directed at more than one person.

The irregular imperatives Woza and Yiza

Most isiZulu imperatives are formed by using the bare verb stem, but the verb meaning "to come" is irregular. Both Woza! and Yiza! are heard across isiZulu-speaking communities, and this chapter treats Woza! as the primary productive form, with Yiza! introduced for recognition and limited production. Practise the initial w and y sounds clearly, since confusing these two irregular forms with a regular verb stem is a common early error.

Grammar Instruction

16.1 The singular imperative

Purpose: give direct instructions to one person, using the bare verb stem, in classroom, workplace, and household contexts.

The singular imperative in isiZulu is formed, for most verbs, by using the bare verb stem with its final vowel -a, with no subject concord attached. This is the simplest verb form you have encountered so far, since it contains no subject marker at all. The identity of the person being addressed is understood from the situation, exactly as in the English command "Sit down!"

Language Building Block — Singular imperative
[verb stem]-a!
The singular imperative uses the bare verb stem plus the final vowel -a, with no subject concord. This is the same verb root you already know from other tense forms, but without the subject-concord and tense-marker slots you practised in Chapters 1 through 15.
Vula incwadi yakho!
Open your book!
  • Hlala phansi! Sit down!
  • Bhala igama lakho. Write your name.
  • Lalela kahle. Listen carefully.
  • Woza lapha. Come here.

Practice — Singular imperatives

Give a singular command using the cue provided.

  1. (open) + the window →
  2. (write) + your name →
  3. (sit down) →
  4. (come) + here →

16.2 The plural imperative with -ni

Purpose: give the same direct instructions to more than one person, by adding the ending -ni to the singular imperative form.

To address a command to more than one person, add -ni to the end of the singular imperative form. This is one of the simplest and most regular endings in the entire verb system, and it applies consistently across almost all verbs.

Language Building Block — Plural imperative
[verb stem]-a-ni!
The plural imperative adds -ni directly after the final vowel -a of the singular imperative. No subject concord is used, exactly as in the singular form. The -ni ending alone signals that the command is directed at more than one listener.
Vulani izincwadi zenu!
Open your books! (to more than one person)
  • Hlalani phansi! Sit down! (to more than one person)
  • Thulani, ngicela. Be quiet, please. (to more than one person)
  • Phendulani imibuzo. Answer the questions. (to more than one person)
  • Wozani, sesizoqala. Come, we are about to begin. (to more than one person)

Note on classroom instructions: Because a teacher is almost always addressing more than one student, most of the classroom commands you will hear and give in isiZulu use the plural imperative with -ni. Notice this in the model dialogue for this chapter, where uThisha Mchunu addresses the whole class.

Practice — Plural imperatives

Change each singular command to a plural command.

  1. Vula! →
  2. Bhala! →
  3. Lalela! →
  4. Woza! →

16.3 Softened requests with ngicela

Purpose: make a request sound polite rather than blunt, using the frame ngicela plus a subjunctive verb form ending in -e.

A direct imperative is appropriate between family members, close friends, or in a classroom where the teacher has authority to give commands. In many other situations, particularly with someone you do not know well, someone older than you, or someone in a formal or public-service role, a softened request is more appropriate. isiZulu softens a request using Ngicela (literally, "I request" or "I ask") followed by a verb ending in -e instead of -a. This -e ending is your first formulaic contact with the subjunctive mood, which will be analyzed fully in Volume II and Volume III.

Language Building Block — Polite request with ngicela
Ngicela u-[verb stem]-e.
Ngicela combines the first-person concord ngi- with the verb root -cela (to request). It is followed by the subject concord u- and a verb ending in -e rather than the ordinary final vowel -a. Learn this whole frame as a fixed pattern at this stage, without analyzing why the final vowel changes.
Ngicela uvule ifasitela.
Please open the window. (literally: I request that you open the window.)
  • Ngicela uvale umnyango. Please close the door.
  • Ngicela ubhale igama lakho lapha. Please write your name here.
  • Ngicela ulinde umzuzwana. Please wait a moment.
  • Uxolo, ngicela ungisize. Excuse me, please help me.
  • Ngicela amanzi. May I have some water, please.

Note on scope: This chapter introduces the Ngicela u-[verb]-e frame as a productive formulaic pattern for common, high-frequency requests. The full subjunctive mood, including its use in negative forms, other persons, and other functions beyond polite requests, is developed productively beginning in Volume II. At this stage, learn each request as a complete unit rather than building new subjunctive forms freely.

Practice — Polite requests

Turn each direct command into a polite request using ngicela.

  1. Vula ifasitela! →
  2. Bhala igama lakho! →
  3. Linda umzuzwana! →

16.4 Negative commands with Musa uku- and Musani uku-

Purpose: tell someone not to do something, using the formulaic prohibitive frames Musa uku- for one person and Musani uku- for more than one person.

To tell someone not to do something, isiZulu uses the negative command word Musa (for one person) or Musani (for more than one person), followed by the infinitive form of the verb, which begins with uku-. You have already seen the infinitive prefix uku- in vocabulary items such as ukusebenza and in the Class 15 noun pattern named in the master instructions. This chapter activates it productively inside the negative command frame.

Language Building Block — Negative commands
Musa uku-[verb].  |  Musani uku-[verb].
Musa (singular) and Musani (plural) are fixed negative-command words. They are followed directly by the infinitive form of the verb, marked by the prefix uku-. No subject concord or final-vowel change is required beyond the infinitive form itself.
Musa ukuvula isitofu ngingekho.
Don't turn on the stove while I'm not here.
  • Musa ukuhamba! Don't go!
  • Musani ukuthetha ekilasini. Don't talk in class. (to more than one person)
  • Musa ukucima ilambu. Don't switch off the light.

Note: The verb -thetha (to talk, to chat) appears here as a receptive addition drawn from common classroom instructions. Focus productive attention on Musa uku- and Musani uku- as fixed frames, and recycle verbs you already know inside them.

Practice — Negative commands

Give a negative command using the cue provided.

  1. (don't, sg.) + go →
  2. (don't, pl.) + open the door →
  3. (don't, sg.) + switch off the light →

16.5 Formulaic subjunctive-purpose expressions with ukuze

Purpose: recognize and use, as fixed formulaic units, a small set of purpose expressions built with ukuze plus a subjunctive verb ending in -e, without full analysis of the subjunctive system.

You saw a subjunctive-like form receptively in Chapter 15, in the sentence Umalume ufuna ngibe udokotela (Uncle wants me to become a doctor). This chapter introduces the connector ukuze (so that, in order that), which is commonly followed by a verb ending in -e, the same subjunctive ending you practised with ngicela in Section 16.3. At this stage, learn each ukuze expression as a complete formulaic phrase rather than constructing new ones freely.

Language Building Block — Formulaic purpose expressions
... ukuze ngi-[verb]-e.
Ukuze introduces a purpose clause. The verb that follows it ends in -e, the same ending used in the subjunctive forms you met in Section 16.3. Learn common ukuze phrases as whole units for recognition and limited, formulaic production; full productive control of the subjunctive is developed in Volume II and Volume III.
Ngifunda kanzima ukuze ngiphase.
I study hard so that I may pass.
  • Ngisebenza kanzima ukuze ngithole imali. I work hard so that I earn money. (receptive/formulaic)
  • Ufunda ukuze abe ngudokotela. He/She studies so that he/she may become a doctor. (receptive/formulaic)
  • Woza masinyane ukuze singaphuzi. Come quickly so that we are not late. (receptive/formulaic)
Language Focus

This chapter's central grammatical development is the command system: the singular imperative, the plural imperative with -ni, the softened request frame Ngicela u-[verb]-e, and the negative command frames Musa uku- and Musani uku-. The ukuze purpose expressions in Section 16.5 are introduced only as formulaic recognition items; do not attempt to build new subjunctive forms beyond the fixed patterns practised here. Full subjunctive analysis, including its use in purpose clauses, conditionals, and polite requests beyond ngicela, is developed productively in Volume II and Volume III.

Model Dialogue

Isimo: Kusekuseni ngoMsombuluko esikoleni. UThisha Mchunu unikeza abafundi bakhe imiyalo yephrojekthi yeqembu. Ngemva kwekilasi, uZanele uya ehhovisi lesikole ukuze acele usizo kuNkosazana Dube. Ntambama, ekhaya, umama kaZanele umnika imiyalo engaphambi kokuya emsebenzini.
Setting: It is Monday morning at school. Uthisha Mchunu gives his students instructions for a group project. After class, Zanele goes to the school office to ask Nkosazana Dube for help. In the afternoon, at home, Zanele's mother gives her instructions before leaving for work.

UTHISHA Sanibonani, bafundi! Hlalani phansi, ngicela. Hello, students! Sit down, please. (to the whole class)
ABAFUNDI Sawubona, Thisha. Hello, Teacher.
UTHISHA Vulani izincwadi zenu ekhasini leshumi. Bhekani isithombe esikhulu. Open your books to page ten. Look at the large picture.
UTHISHA Namuhla sizosebenza ngamaqembu. Lalelani kahle, ngizonichazela umyalo. Today we will work in groups. Listen carefully, I will explain the instruction to you.
UTHISHA Bhalani izisho ezinhlanu, kodwa musani ukukhuluma isiNgisi. Sisebenzisa isiZulu kuphela. Write five sentences, but don't speak English. We use only isiZulu.
USIPHO Thisha, ngicela uphinde umyalo. Angizwanga kahle. Teacher, please repeat the instruction. I did not hear it well.
UTHISHA Kulungile. Bhalani izisho ezinhlanu ngesiZulu, ngamunye ngamunye ekwabelweni kwenu. All right. Write five sentences in isiZulu, each in your own section.
Ngemva kwekilasi, esikoleni. Uzanele uya ehhovisi lesikole. — After class, at school. Zanele goes to the school office.
UZANELE Uxolo, Nkosazana Dube. Ngicela ungisize. Excuse me, Miss Dube. Please help me.
UNKOSAZANA DUBE Kulungile, Zanele. Wenzani? All right, Zanele. What are you doing? / What do you need?
UZANELE Ngicela incwadi entsha, ngoba yami ilahlekile. May I have a new book, please, because mine is lost.
UNKOSAZANA DUBE Kulungile. Linda umzuzwana, ngizokulanda enye. All right. Wait a moment, I will fetch you another one.
Ntambama, ekhaya. Umama kaZanele unikeza uZanele imiyalo. — In the afternoon, at home. Zanele's mother gives Zanele instructions.
UMAMA Zanele, ngiyaphuma manje, ngiya emsebenzini. Shanela ikhishi, ngicela. Zanele, I am leaving now, I am going to work. Sweep the kitchen, please.
UMAMA Futhi, musa ukuvula isitofu ngingekho. Also, don't turn on the stove while I'm not here.
UZANELE Kulungile, Mama. Ngizokwenza njalo. All right, Mother. I will do so.
UMALUME (ngobusuku) Zanele, ngiyabona ufunda kanzima namuhla. (that evening) Zanele, I see you are studying hard today.
UZANELE Yebo, Malume. Ngifunda kanzima ukuze ngiphase iphrojekthi yesiZulu. Yes, Uncle. I am studying hard so that I pass the isiZulu project.

Several expressions in this dialogue extend beyond full productive analysis at this stage: ngizonichazela (I will explain to you, object concord and future), angizwanga (I did not hear, negative past), ekwabelweni (in the section, passive-like noun), ngizokulanda (I will fetch you, object concord and future), and ekwabelweni kwenu together are receptive. Focus your productive study on the imperative forms, the ngicela request frame, the negative commands, and the formulaic ukuze phrase identified in the Grammar section.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Uthisha Mchunu unika abafundi imiyalo mini ekilasini? (What instructions does Uthisha Mchunu give the students in class?)
  2. Uzanele ucelani kuNkosazana Dube, futhi kungani? (What does Zanele request from Nkosazana Dube, and why?)
  3. Umama kaZanele umnika imiyalo mini engaphambi kokuya emsebenzini? (What instructions does Zanele's mother give her before going to work?)
  4. Kungani uZanele efunda kanzima namuhla, ngokusho kwakhe? (Why is Zanele studying hard today, according to what she says?)

Reading

Read the following two texts. The first is a set of classroom notices posted on the wall. The second is a short household instruction list left on the kitchen table. Read each text aloud twice before answering the comprehension questions.

Izaziso zasekilasini — Classroom notices

Imithetho yekilasi:
Ngenani ekilasini ngesikhathi. Hlalani ezihlalweni zenu. Vulani izincwadi zenu ekuqaleni kwekilasi. Ngicela nilalele lapho uthisha ekhuluma. Musani ukudla ekilasini. Musani ukukhuluma uma omunye umfundi ekhuluma. Uma unombuzo, ngicela uphakamise isandla sakho.

Uhlu lwemiyalo ekhaya — A household instruction list

Sipho, ngiyaphuma manje. Ngicela wenze lezi zinto ngaphambi kokuba ngibuye: shanela ikhishi, hlanza izitsha, futhi cima wonke amalambu ohlala ekamelweni lakho. Musa ukuvula isitofu, futhi musa ukuphuma endlini ngaphandle kokutshela ugogo. Uma udinga usizo, ngicela ubize ugogo, uhlala eduze. Ngizobuya ngo-5 ntambama. Ngiyabonga, Mama.

Several expressions in these texts are receptive: uma unombuzo (if you have a question, conditional), uphakamise (raise, subjunctive form), ngaphandle kokutshela (without telling, infinitive with negation), and uhlala eduze (who lives nearby, relative-like construction). Read for overall meaning using the vocabulary and grammar you know.

  1. Yimuphi umyalo abafundi okumele bawulandele uma bengenile ekilasini? (What instruction must students follow when they enter the classroom?)
  2. Yimuphi umyalo omubi (owenqatshelwe) obekwe ekilasini mayelana nokudla? (What prohibition is set in the classroom regarding eating?)
  3. USipho unemiyalo emingaki emqondweni endlini, futhi yimiphi eyodwa yayo? (How many household instructions does Sipho have, and what is one of them?)
  4. Uma uSipho edinga usizo endlini, kumele enzeni? (If Sipho needs help at home, what must he do?)

Writing Workshop

In this chapter you will write a short set of instructions or a polite request note for a classroom, workplace, or household situation. Choose one of the two tasks below.

Isikhungo sokukhethwa — Choose your writing task:

Umsebenzi A (Instruction list): Write a list of five to seven instructions that a teacher, parent, or supervisor might give, using at least three singular imperatives, one plural imperative, and one negative command with Musa uku- or Musani uku-.

Umsebenzi B (Polite request note): Write a short note of four to six sentences requesting help or a favour from someone in a position of respect, such as a teacher, office assistant, or elder relative. Use Uxolo as an opener, at least two Ngicela request frames, and one formulaic ukuze phrase explaining your purpose.

  1. Plan your content. Decide whether you are writing an instruction list or a request note. List the key instructions or requests you want to include.
  2. Write using active vocabulary. Use only command verbs, request frames, and grammar patterns from this chapter and from earlier chapters. Do not attempt structures beyond the active scope.
  3. Check your imperative forms. Confirm that singular commands use the bare verb stem, and that plural commands add -ni correctly.
  4. Check your request frame. If writing a request note, confirm that every Ngicela phrase is followed by a verb ending in -e, not -a.
  5. Check your negative commands. Confirm that Musa uku- or Musani uku- is followed by the infinitive form of the verb.
  6. Revise and write your final version. Check your draft against the editing checklist below before writing or typing the final version.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • Singular imperatives use the bare verb stem, with no subject concord.
  • Plural imperatives add -ni correctly.
  • The Ngicela u-[verb]-e frame is used correctly for polite requests, with the correct -e ending.
  • Negative commands use Musa uku- or Musani uku- followed by the infinitive.
  • Any ukuze phrase is used as a whole formulaic unit, matching the patterns studied in this chapter.
  • Each sentence begins with a capital letter and ends with appropriate punctuation, including an exclamation mark for direct commands where natural.
  • No analytical hyphens appear inside ordinary verb words or compound nouns.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: command chain

Your partner gives you a singular command from this chapter's vocabulary table. Perform the action, or mime it, and then give the plural version of the same command back to your partner. After five rounds, swap roles.

Example: Partner says "Vula!" You mime opening something, then say: Vulani!

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: polite request role-play

Partner A plays a school office assistant, teacher, or shopkeeper. Partner B approaches with a request, opening with Uxolo and using at least two Ngicela frames from this chapter. Partner A responds with Kulungile or a follow-up question such as Wenzani? Swap roles after three exchanges.

Useful checking expressions: Ngiyaqonda. (I understand.)  |  Uxolo, ngicela uphinde. (Sorry, please repeat.)  |  Kulungile. (All right.)

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself giving a short set of instructions, sixty to ninety seconds long, as if you were a teacher, parent, or supervisor. Include at least three singular commands, one plural command, one negative command, and one polite request using Ngicela. Listen back and check that your imperative and request forms are accurate.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — When a direct command is appropriate

In many isiZulu-speaking households and classrooms, a direct imperative between a parent and child, or a teacher and a class of students, is entirely normal and is not considered rude. The directness of the imperative reflects an established relationship of care and authority, rather than harshness. However, the same directness used toward a stranger, an elder outside the family, or a person in a formal service role would generally be considered inappropriate, and a softened request with Ngicela is expected instead.

Notice, in this chapter's dialogue, that uThisha Mchunu gives direct plural commands to his class, while Zanele uses Ngicela when addressing the office assistant, uNkosazana Dube, whom she does not know as closely.

Culture in Context — Softening requests to elders and strangers

Opening a request with Uxolo (excuse me, sorry) before making it is a widely valued politeness strategy in isiZulu-speaking communities, particularly when addressing someone older, someone in authority, or someone you are meeting for the first time. Combining Uxolo with a Ngicela request, as in Uxolo, ngicela ungisize, signals both respect for the listener's time and recognition of their position. This pattern connects to the respect-language principles introduced in Chapter 1 and developed further in Chapter 19.

Regional Note — Woza and Yiza across communities

Both Woza! and Yiza! are widely understood across isiZulu-speaking communities as commands meaning "Come!", though speakers and regions may show a preference for one form in everyday speech. This textbook treats Woza! as the primary productive form for consistency, while introducing Yiza! for recognition, since you are likely to hear both forms used naturally in different contexts.

Culture in Context — Instructions as an expression of care

As seen in this chapter's dialogue, household instructions given before a parent leaves for work, such as Zanele's mother's reminder about the stove, are commonly understood as an expression of care and responsibility rather than as mere rule-giving. Responding to such instructions with a simple acknowledgment such as Kulungile, Mama (All right, Mother) shows respectful acceptance. This pattern of instruction and acknowledgment appears throughout isiZulu-speaking family life and will continue to appear in later chapters.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Adding a subject concord to the imperative

Error: Writing *Uvula ifasitela! to mean "Open the window!", by adding the subject concord u- to the verb, as if forming an ordinary statement.

Correction: The imperative uses the bare verb stem with no subject concord at all. Vula ifasitela! (Open the window!) is correct. Adding u- changes the sentence into a statement, such as "He/She opens the window," rather than a command.

Common Mistake — Dropping the -ni ending in plural commands

Error: Writing *Vula izincwadi zenu! to address a whole class, instead of Vulani izincwadi zenu!, by leaving the command in its singular form.

Correction: Any command directed at more than one person requires the ending -ni. Vulani izincwadi zenu! (Open your books! to more than one person) is correct. Review the classroom notice in the Reading section for further examples of plural commands.

Common Mistake — Using -a instead of -e after ngicela

Error: Writing *Ngicela uvula ifasitela instead of Ngicela uvule ifasitela, by keeping the ordinary final vowel -a instead of changing it to -e.

Correction: The verb that follows Ngicela u- must end in -e, not -a. Ngicela uvule ifasitela. (Please open the window.) is correct. Treat the whole frame Ngicela u-[verb]-e as a fixed pattern at this stage.

Common Mistake — Confusing Musa with Ngicela in a negative request

Error: Writing *Ngicela ungavuli isitofu in an attempt to say "Please don't open the stove," by mixing the polite request frame with negative grammar that has not yet been activated.

Correction: At this stage, use the fixed negative command frame instead: Musa ukuvula isitofu. (Don't open the stove.) Combining Ngicela with a negative verb form is not yet part of the active productive scope; that combination is developed in a later volume once the subjunctive system is fully analyzed.

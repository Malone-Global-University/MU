Ukuthenga

Shopping · Speak and Write Zulu: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify common clothing items and household or personal goods using nouns from active classes, including new class 9/10 and class 7/8 shopping vocabulary.
  2. Ask for and state a price using numbers already studied, including amounts above one hundred.
  3. Describe color, size, and quality using adjective concords across active noun classes.
  4. Use near and far demonstratives across classes 1/2, 1a/2a, 5/6, 7/8, and 9/10 to point out and refer back to items.
  5. Ask to see or try on an item using a polite request frame.
  6. Refer back to a person or item using an expanded set of object concords with a clear antecedent.
  7. Make a simple comparison between two items using an approved beginner construction.
  8. Complete a purchase or a simple return exchange, including basic payment language.
  9. Distinguish voiced and nasalized click accompaniments in active vocabulary, alongside the plain and aspirated accompaniments already studied.
  10. Write a short product description or customer message using standard conjunctive isiZulu orthography.

Communication Scenario

After breakfast at the café, Sipho and Lindiwe walk with you to a small clothing and general goods shop near the Durban beachfront, where Lindiwe wants to look for a new shirt before a family event next week. The shop assistant greets customers in isiZulu and English, and price tags are marked clearly. You decide to look around as well, since you need a new bag for your isiZulu class. This is your first chance to ask about size, color, and price, to ask to try something on, and to politely decide not to buy an item the assistant shows you.

  1. In English, list two clothing items or goods you would shop for, including a color and a size if relevant. Keep this list, since you will use it in the Writing Workshop later.
  2. Predict how you might ask "How much does this cost?" in isiZulu based on the price question you learned in Chapter 8. Compare your prediction with the Grammar section.

Pronunciation and Tone

Voiced and nasalized click accompaniments: gc, gq, gx, nc, nq, nx

You have already learned the three plain click types from Chapters 2 through 4, and the aspirated accompaniment from Chapter 8. This chapter introduces the voiced accompaniment, written gc, gq, and gx, and the nasalized accompaniment, written nc, nq, and nx.

Pronunciation Focus — Producing voiced and nasalized clicks

A voiced click adds vibration of the vocal folds during the click release, similar to the difference between English s and z. A nasalized click adds nasal airflow before the click release, similar to the nasal quality you already produce in ng. Both accompaniments change the quality of the click rather than its place of articulation.

Practise in this order:

  1. Produce the plain click alone, in the correct place: c, q, or x.
  2. For the voiced accompaniment, add vocal-fold vibration during the release, and combine with a vowel: gca, gqa, gxa.
  3. For the nasalized accompaniment, prepare the nasal airflow first, then release the click into the vowel: nca, nqa, nxa.
  4. Practise in real words such as ingubo (garment, related vocabulary) and ukungcamfa, then listen for contrast with the plain and aspirated forms already studied.

Do not confuse the nasalized click with simply adding ng before any click letter. The nasalization is a single coordinated articulation, not two separate sounds pronounced in sequence. As always, listen to a verified model before attempting production, and review the plain and aspirated accompaniments from earlier chapters alongside these new contrasts.

b and bh consolidation

This chapter recycles and consolidates the contrast between plain b and aspirated or breathy bh, first introduced in Chapter 2, since both consonants appear frequently in shopping vocabulary, including ibhantshi, ibhegi, and -bomvu.

Pronunciation Focus — b versus bh in context

Plain b in isiZulu is produced with an implosive quality in many varieties, distinct from the plain English b. The digraph bh represents a breathy or aspirated bilabial stop, with audible airflow following the release. Practise the contrast in word pairs drawn from active vocabulary, such as -bomvu (red, plain b) against ibhantshi (jacket, bh), listening closely before producing each word aloud.

Descriptive phrase rhythm and contrastive tone

When a noun is followed by a color or size adjective, as in ihembe elibomvu (the red shirt), the phrase carries its own connected rhythm, with the adjective concord linking smoothly to the noun before it. When comparing two items, as in Ngithanda leli kunaleliya, listen for the contrastive tone and slightly heavier stress-like prominence on the items being compared, distinct from a plain statement. Use verified whole-phrase audio or a qualified speaker, and continue to treat tone as a feature of listening and imitation rather than written notation.

Purpose: point out and refer back to items in a shop using near demonstratives, expanding their use across classes 1/2, 1a/2a, 5/6, 7/8, and 9/10.

You have already encountered demonstratives in supported patterns since Chapter 2, including nansi (here it is) in Chapter 8's dialogue. This chapter activates the near demonstrative series productively across the classes you already know, so you can point to a specific item while shopping.

9.2 Adjective and relative concords for description

Purpose: describe an item's color, size, and quality using the correct concord type, distinguishing true adjective stems from relative stems.

IsiZulu has a limited set of canonical adjective stems, which take the adjective concord, a form related to but not identical with the noun prefix. Other descriptive words, including most color terms borrowed or extended from nouns, are relative stems and take the relative concord instead. Both strategies are active in this chapter.

9.3 Asking to see or try an item

Purpose: politely ask to view or try on an item using the object concord introduced in Chapter 8, expanded here to class 5 and class 9 referents.

Chapter 8 introduced object concords productively for first and second person and receptively for class 5 and class 9. This chapter activates the class 5 and class 9 object concords productively in the specific context of asking to see or try something, since these are the classes most shopping items belong to.

Class Object concord Example English
5 (it)-li-Ngingalibona?May I see it? (referring to a class 5 item, such as ihembe)
9 (it)-yi-Ngingayibona?May I see it? (referring to a class 9 item, such as ibhegi)

As in Chapter 8, the object concord must have a clear antecedent. Use these forms only after the item has already been mentioned or pointed to, for example with a demonstrative. The frame Ngingalilinga? (May I try it on?, class 5) and Ngingayilinga? (class 9) follow the same pattern and are introduced as formulaic at this stage, since the full subjunctive grammar behind -nga- requesting forms is developed in a later chapter.

  • Lesi sketi, ngingasibona? ngi-nga-si-bon-a This skirt, may I see it? (class 7 object concord -si-, receptive)
  • Leli hembe, ngingalibona? ngi-nga-li-bon-a This shirt, may I see it?
  • Le bhegi, ngingayibona? ngi-nga-yi-bon-a This bag, may I see it?

9.4 Prices above one hundred

Purpose: ask for and understand a price using numbers above one hundred, extending the numeral system already active since Chapter 3.

You have already studied numbers 0 to 100 across Chapters 1 through 4. This chapter recycles the price question from Chapter 8, Yimalini?, and extends it to amounts above one hundred, which are common for clothing and goods.

  • Yimalini leli hembe? How much is this shirt?
  • Libiza amakhulu amabili. It costs two hundred. (amakhulu amabili, formulaic at this stage; full hundreds system develops later)
  • Le bhegi ibiza amakhulu amathathu namashumi amahlanu. This bag costs three hundred and fifty.

The verb -biza (to cost; literally, to call for) was introduced formulaically in Chapter 8's reading passage and is now productive. Numbers in the hundreds follow a regular pattern built on ikhulu (hundred), but full productive control of the hundreds and combined-number system is not required until later chapters. For now, learn the example amounts above as complete units for shopping purposes, alongside the numbers 0 to 100 you already control independently.

9.5 Simple comparison

Purpose: compare two items using an approved beginner construction, without requiring a full comparative grammar system.

IsiZulu does not use a single word equivalent to English "more" attached to every adjective. At the beginner level, comparison between two specific items is expressed using kuna- (more than; compared to), attached to the second item being compared.

Language Building Block — Comparison with kuna-
[preference statement] + kuna- + [second item]
Kuna- is taught here as a fixed comparative formula. It is built from the locative-like element ku- and na- (and; with), but learners are not expected to derive this analysis independently at this stage.
Ngithanda leli kunaleliya.  |  Le bhegi inkulu kunaleya.
I like this one more than that one.  |  This bag is bigger than that one.
  • Ihembe elimhlophe lihle kunelibomvu. The white shirt is nicer than the red one.
  • Le bhegi incane kunaleya. This bag is smaller than that one.

Comparison is limited in this chapter to direct, two-item statements using vocabulary already active. A fuller comparative and superlative system, including comparison without a stated second item, is outside this chapter's scope and will be developed in later chapters.

Language Focus

This chapter does not introduce a new noun class. Instead, it expands demonstrative, adjective-concord, and object-concord agreement across the classes you already control, classes 1/2, 1a/2a, 5/6, 7/8, and 9/10, while adding the relative-concord distinction, price language above one hundred, and a simple comparison frame. The productive load centers on near demonstratives, true adjective stems for color and size, and class 5/9 object concords in viewing and fitting requests. Relative-stem color terms, far demonstratives, and the internal structure of kuna- remain receptive or formulaic, and will return for fuller treatment in later chapters, particularly Chapter 14.

(Emuva kwesikhashana. — A short while later.)

Writing Workshop

In this chapter you will write a short product description or customer message. Follow the full planning-drafting-revision sequence.

  1. Plan. Return to the list you made in the Communication Scenario: two items you would shop for, with a color and size. Add a comparison between two items, using kuna-.
  2. Study the model. Read the passage Isitolo sezingubo eThekwini in the Reading section once more.   3. Draft. Write five to seven connected sentences in isiZulu describing items for sale, or a purchase you made. Include: at least two noun phrases with an adjective concord for color or size, one demonstrative referring to a specific item, one object concord with a clear antecedent, and one comparison using kuna-.
  4. Add a price exchange. Include one short question-and-answer pair asking and stating a price using Yimalini? and a number above one hundred.
  5. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist below before writing your final version.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • Adjective concords match the class of the noun they describe, for example eli- for class 5, esi- for class 7.
  • Demonstratives match the class of the noun they accompany, for example leli for class 5, le for class 9.
  • Object concords, where used, are written conjunctively inside the verb word, with a clear antecedent.
  • The comparison frame kuna- attaches directly to the second item compared.
  • Each sentence begins with a capital letter and ends with appropriate punctuation.
  • No analytical hyphens appear in the ordinary writing of any verb form.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Shopping and service interaction

Shopping practices among isiZulu-speaking communities vary by region, by the type of establishment, and by individual relationship. In larger retail stores in cities such as Durban and Johannesburg, service is often brief and transactional, while smaller, independent, or family-run shops may involve more extended greeting and conversation before a purchase is discussed. Payment methods, including cash, card, and mobile payment options, vary by establishment and should be verified rather than assumed.

As in earlier chapters, softened request frames such as Ngingayibona? (May I see it?) are generally preferred over bare commands when speaking with an unfamiliar shop assistant, reflecting the same politeness pattern introduced with cela in Chapter 8.

Culture in Context — Comparing and decision-making

Taking time to compare items, ask to try them on, and decide without immediate pressure is a normal part of shopping interactions in many isiZulu-speaking communities, just as it is in many other contexts. A respectful shop assistant will generally allow a customer time to compare options, as Lindiwe does in this chapter's dialogue, rather than expecting an immediate decision.

Regional Note — -luhlaza and color terminology

The adjective stem -luhlaza covers both "green" and "blue" in standard usage, with context, or an additional descriptive word, clarifying the precise shade when necessary. Some speakers further distinguish -luhlaza okwesibhakabhaka (sky blue) from -luhlaza okwotshani (grass green) in more detailed description; these compound forms are receptive at this stage. This pattern, where one core color term covers a broader range than its closest English equivalent, is common across many languages and is not a gap or deficiency in isiZulu.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using the noun prefix instead of the adjective concord

Error: Writing *ihembe ibomvu instead of ihembe elibomvu, by mistakenly repeating the noun prefix pattern instead of using the class 5 adjective concord eli-.

Correction: The adjective concord is related to the noun prefix but is not always identical to it, as you saw with the class 5 subject concord li- in Chapter 8. For class 5, the adjective concord is eli-: ihembe elibomvu, ihembe elimhlophe. Learn each class's adjective concord as a distinct form from its noun prefix and subject concord, rather than assuming they match.

Common Mistake — Treating every color word as a true adjective stem

Error: Applying the adjective concord pattern to -phuzi (yellow; brown), producing an incorrect form such as *ihembe eliphuzi with the wrong concord type, or assuming every descriptive word follows one rule.

Correction: Not every English adjective corresponds to an isiZulu adjective stem. -phuzi is a relative stem and requires a relative concord and the relative marker, which you will study productively in Chapter 14. At this stage, recognize eliphuzi receptively, and produce only the confirmed adjective stems from this chapter's table: -mhlophe, -mnyama, -bomvu, -luhlaza, -khulu, -ncane, and -hle.

Common Mistake — Mismatching the demonstrative and the noun class

Error: Writing *lesi bhegi instead of le bhegi, by applying the class 7 demonstrative lesi to a class 9 noun.

Correction: Each demonstrative must match the class of its noun, exactly as concords do. Ibhegi is class 9, so its near demonstrative is le: le bhegi. Before choosing a demonstrative, first confirm the noun's class using the vocabulary tables, then select the matching form from the demonstrative table in Grammar 9.1.

Common Mistake — Using an object concord with no antecedent in the conversation

Error: Asking Ngingayibona? as an opening line in a shop with no item named, pointed to, or otherwise established, leaving the listener unable to identify what "it" refers to.

Correction: As in Chapter 8, an object concord must always have a clear antecedent. First name or point to the item, often using a demonstrative, as in Le bhegi, ngingayibona? Only use the object concord alone once the item is unmistakably established in the conversation.

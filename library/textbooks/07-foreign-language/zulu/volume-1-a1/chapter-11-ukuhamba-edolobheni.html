Ukuhamba edolobheni

Getting Around · Speak and Write Zulu: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common city and community places, transportation types, and route landmarks using active noun-class nouns.
  2. State where someone is located and where someone is going using approved locative expressions.
  3. Ask for and give simple directions using productive lexical and derived locative forms.
  4. Use singular and plural positive commands, and recognize negative commands, in route and direction contexts.
  5. Make a polite request using cela-based constructions in transport situations such as buying a ticket.
  6. Use object concords in commands where a clear antecedent is available, consolidating the object concord work of Chapter 8 and 9.
  7. Recognize class 3/4 place and transport nouns in supported input without being required to control their full concord system productively.
  8. Distinguish command intonation from request intonation and produce connected direction sequences with appropriate phrase boundaries.
  9. Read a map, schedule, sign, and short set of directions in standard isiZulu orthography.
  10. Write a step-by-step set of directions using correct conjunctive verb spelling, locative forms, and sequencing expressions.

Communication Scenario

It is a weekday morning in Johannesburg. Your isiZulu class has been given an independent navigation task: reach the Newtown Cultural Precinct from Park Station using public transport, then find a specific building inside the precinct. Your group has a printed bus map written in isiZulu and English, but the route numbers have been cut off. You will need to ask at least two people for directions along the way, read a bus-stop sign, and buy a single trip at the ticket counter, all in isiZulu. Sipho, who you know from earlier chapters, is waiting for you at the destination.

  1. In English, write the three questions you would most need to ask if you were lost in an unfamiliar city. Then predict which isiZulu words or patterns from previous chapters might appear in those questions.
  2. Think about how you would politely stop a stranger to ask for help. What expression from earlier chapters could you use to soften the request?

Note on minibus taxis: In South African cities, including Johannesburg and Durban, ithekisi (plural amathekisi) refers primarily to the shared minibus taxi, which is the most widely used form of public transport among working South Africans. Pronunciation and Tone

Command and request intonation

This chapter provides the first sustained opportunity to produce and compare two closely related patterns: the positive command and the polite request. These differ not only in form but in intonation. A bare positive command, such as Hamba phambili. (Go straight ahead.), typically has a relatively level or gently falling phrase contour with clear penultimate lengthening at the end. A cela-based request, such as Ngicela ungikhombise indlela. (Please show me the way.), carries a softer falling contour that extends over a longer phrase, with the pitch dropping gradually across the whole utterance rather than falling sharply at the penultimate syllable.

Pronunciation Focus — Distinguishing commands from requests

Listen carefully to the following minimal pair of interactions and try to imitate each one as a complete phrase, not word by word:

  1. Hamba phambili. — a direct instruction, relatively brisk, falling at the end
  2. Ngicela uhhambe phambili. — a softened request, longer, gradually falling across the phrase

In a real navigating situation, a direct command is appropriate when, for example, a tour guide or a friend is directing you quickly. A request frame is more appropriate when asking a stranger or a service worker for help. Producing the right intonation signals the right level of politeness even before the listener processes every word.

Do not separate direction words into individual stressed syllables as you might in English. Keep a steady syllable rhythm throughout the whole phrase, and let the penultimate syllable of the phrase stretch slightly before the final syllable lands.

Locative tone and rhythm

Locative forms such as ekhoneni, esikhulwini, and esiteshini are longer words than most of what you have practiced so far. Longer words are parsed syllable by syllable at an even pace in isiZulu, and the penultimate lengthening falls on the second-to-last syllable of the word or phrase boundary, not on the syllable you might stress in English. Practise reading each locative aloud from left to right at a steady pace: e-si-te-shi-ni, keeping all five syllables equally timed, then allow the -shi- syllable to lengthen as the word ends a phrase. Use a qualified speaker or verified audio to confirm your production before treating your own model as correct.

Clicks and prenasalized consonants in place vocabulary

Several place and direction words in this chapter contain click consonants and prenasalized consonants recycled from Chapters 2 through 9. In inkundla, the initial nk is a prenasalized consonant, not two separate sounds. In ngqo (straight), the ngq cluster begins with a nasal, moves directly into the alveolar click. Both require the preceding nasal to be produced in the same breath as the click release, without a vowel in between. Review Chapter 4's pronunciation section on the alveolar click q if this sequence feels unfamiliar.

Route numbers and phrasing clarity

When giving a route number or an address in isiZulu, numbers are stated as you have learned them in Chapters 1 through 10. Each digit or group is said at a moderate pace. Avoid rushing through numbers inside a direction sequence, since listeners depend on clear number production to identify the right vehicle or stop. Practise saying a two-digit number followed by a place name in a single connected phrase: for example, ibhasi lenombolo amathupha namathathu (bus number sixty-three) at a measured pace. The goal is smooth phrasing rather than word-by-word hesitation.

Grammar Instruction

11.1 Locatives: lexical forms and productive e-…-ini

Purpose: state where something is and where someone is going using common lexical locatives and the productive e-/o-…-ini pattern for active nouns.

IsiZulu expresses location in more than one way. Some location words are independent adverbs or preposition-like particles that do not change according to noun class: phambili (ahead), emuva (behind), phansi (below), eduze (nearby). These you can use directly without worrying about class agreement. A second group consists of locative nouns, formed from ordinary nouns by adding a locative prefix and, most commonly, a locative suffix. The most productive pattern for active classes uses the prefix e- (or o- for class 1a nouns) and the suffix -ini.

Language Building Block — Productive locative formation: e-…-ini
e- + noun (with class prefix) + -ini
The prefix e- replaces the augment of the noun. The suffix -ini is added to the end of the noun. Some nouns undergo phonological changes at the boundary: a final -a often changes to -e before -ini, and some consonant changes also occur at the suffix boundary. Learn frequent locative forms as whole units first, then connect them to the pattern.
isiteshi → esiteshini  |  isikole → esikoleni  |  isibhedlela → esibhedlela
at / in the station  |  at / in the school  |  at / in the hospital

Not every noun forms a locative through the e-…-ini pattern without irregularity or unpredictable change. Learn the locatives in this chapter's vocabulary table as whole forms. The pattern explanation helps you recognize new locatives in input, but you should not attempt to derive a locative mechanically from every noun you know. When in doubt, use a lexical locative adverb or a phakathi kwa- or eduze kwa- construction instead.

  • UZinhle usekhaya. Zinhle is at home. (lexical locative ekhaya)
  • USipho usesiteshini. u-se-si-te-shi-ni Sipho is at the station. (e-…-ini locative; use- = is at, subject copulative with locative)
  • Siyahamba esikoleni. We are going to school.
  • Ibhasi lisesiteshini. The bus is at the station. (class 5 subject concord + locative)

11.2 Motion verbs: location, source, route, and destination

Purpose: express where someone is coming from, moving through, and going to, using active motion verbs and locative expressions.

IsiZulu motion verbs work together with locative expressions to communicate precise spatial meaning. The most important productive motion verbs at this stage are -hamba (to go; to travel; to walk), -fika (to arrive; to reach), -buya (to return; to come back), and -phuma (to exit; to come out of; to leave from). Each of these combines naturally with the locative forms you have already seen.

Verb Meaning Typical locative partner Example
-hamba go; travel; walk destination locative (where going to) Siyahamba esiteshini. We are going to the station.
-fika arrive; reach destination locative Sifika esikoleni ngo-8. We arrive at school at 8.
-buya return; come back source locative (where coming back from) Ubuya emsebenzini. He/she is returning from work.
-phuma exit; come out; leave source locative Siphuma esiteshini. We are leaving the station.
-khomba point; show; indicate direction or object Ungikhombise indlela. Show me the way. (command, receptive)

Notice that the source of motion ("coming from") and the destination ("going to") are both expressed through locative forms in isiZulu, not through separate prepositions as in English. Context and the choice of motion verb tell the listener which locative role is meant. Siphuma signals that the locative that follows is the place of departure; siyahamba or sifika signals that the locative is the destination.

  • Ngiphuma ekhaya ngo-7, ngemuva kwalokho ngifika esiteshini ngo-7:30. I leave home at 7, then I arrive at the station at 7:30.
  • Siyahamba ngethekisi edolobheni. We are going to town by taxi.
  • Ubuya esibhedlela namuhla. He/she is returning from the hospital today.

11.3 Singular and plural positive commands

Purpose: give clear instructions and directions to one person and to more than one person using positive command forms.

Commands in isiZulu are formed directly from the verb root plus the final vowel -a, with no subject concord. The singular command addresses one person; the plural command adds the suffix -ni to address more than one person.

Language Building Block — Positive commands
Singular: verb root + -a   |   Plural: verb root + -a + -ni → verb root + -ani
For verbs whose root ends in a vowel, a glide may appear or the vowel may change. Focus on the forms given for direction-giving verbs below and treat irregular forms as whole units at this stage.
Hamba!  |  Hambani!      Phuma!  |  Phumani!      Yima!  |  Yimani!
Go! / Walk!  |  Go! (plural)      Come out! / Leave!  |  Leave! (plural)      Stop!  |  Stop! (plural)
Verb root Singular command Plural command English
-hambaHambaHambaniGo; walk
-phumaPhumaPhumaniCome out; exit
-yimaYimaYimaniStop; stand
-bukaBukaBukaniLook
-landelaLandelaLandelaniFollow
-phendukaPhendukaPhendukaniTurn

The command form is used frequently in direction-giving contexts because it is efficient and direct. When combined with a locative or direction word, a command produces a complete step in a set of directions. In contexts where you do not know the person or where politeness is important, use the cela frame described in section 11.4 below.

  • Hamba ngqo phambili, ngemuva kwalokho uphenduke esokunene. Go straight ahead, then turn right.
  • Phuma esiteshini, buka esobunxele. Exit the station, look to the left.
  • Landelani indlela le kuze nifike ekhoneni. Follow this road until you (plural) reach the corner.

11.4 Negative commands

Purpose: recognize negative commands in transport signs and public contexts; produce the most essential negative command forms in supported practice.

A negative command in isiZulu is formed using the prefix musa (do not) or, in plural address, musani, followed by the infinitive with uku-. Alternatively, the negative imperative prefix nga- may be added to the second-person form of the negative verb; this structure is receptive at this stage.

Language Building Block — Negative commands with musa / musani
Musa + uku- + verb root + -a (singular)   |   Musani + uku- + verb root + -a (plural)
Musa is the most straightforward negative command frame for beginners. It uses the class 15 infinitive, which you already know from Chapter 7.
Musa ukuhamba lapha.  |  Musani ukubhema lapha.
Do not walk here.  |  Do not smoke here. (plural)

You will encounter negative commands on signs and public notices in South African cities written in isiZulu. Recognition of musa and musani is essential for navigating those environments. Active productive use is limited to the most frequent forms in this chapter; the fuller negative-command paradigm is developed in Chapter 12 and beyond.

11.5 Polite requests with cela in a transport context

Purpose: ask for help with directions and buy a ticket using the polite cela-based frame already introduced in Chapter 8, now applied to transport situations.

You already know the frame Ngicela ___ from Chapter 8. The same structure applies in transport and direction-asking contexts. When you need to ask a stranger for help with directions, combining Ngicela with a direction question or a request for information is the standard polite approach.

  • Ngicela ungisize. Ngidinga ukuya esiteshini. Please help me. I need to go to the station.
  • Ngicela itikethi elilodwa eya eNkululekweni. I would like one ticket to Newtown, please. (itikethi elilodwa = one ticket, class 5 quantitative form recycled from Chapter 8)
  • Ngicela ungikhombise lapho ibhasi lemi khona. Please show me where the bus stops. (receptive structure; formulaic at this stage)
  • Sicela ukwazi: ibhasi lasesiteshini lisuka nini? We would like to know: when does the bus from the station depart? (receptive)

11.6 Object concords in commands

Purpose: refer back to a person or thing in a command using the first- and second-person object concords from Chapter 8 and the active class 5 and class 9 object concords from Chapter 9, now placed inside command forms.

Object concords learned in Chapters 8 and 9 can appear inside command forms when a clear antecedent exists. The object concord is placed directly before the verb root in the command word, producing a longer conjunctive form. This is productive only for first- and second-person concords and the class 5 and class 9 forms at this stage.

Grammar Note — Object concords inside commands

When a command refers to a specific person or thing already mentioned, the object concord moves inside the verb word. The object concord always comes before the verb root, so the structure is: verb root + object concord + root + final vowel, all written as one word.

Examples:

  • Ngikhombise indlela. — Show me the way. (ngi- = me)
  • Ngikhokhele itikethi. — Pay for my ticket for me. (ngi- = me, with applicative, receptive)
  • Ubonise la. — Show it here. (class 5 object concord -li-, receptive)

As always, the object concord must have a clear, already-stated antecedent. Do not insert one without something to refer back to.

Language Focus

This chapter consolidates and extends locative communication, which has been building since Chapter 6, and introduces commands as a natural partner to the polite request frames already in place since Chapter 8. The key productive items are: locative forms for active class 7/8 and class 9/10 nouns using e-…-ini, the four core motion verbs with their locative partners, and the singular and plural positive command forms for the direction-giving verbs in this chapter. Everything else, including class 3/4 place nouns, complex locative concords, and negative command paradigms, is receptive or supported. Keep your productive energy on giving a clear, complete set of directions and asking a polite transport question.

Model Dialogue

Isimo: ULindiwe uhleli esiteshini sakwaPark eGoli. Ufuna ukuya eNkululekweni kodwa akazi ukuthi ibhasi liphi. Ukhuluma nomuntu ongamazi, uMnumzane Dlamini, obukeka esazi indawo. Ubudlelwane: umuntu ongamazi, endaweni yesizwe, esimeni esifuna usizo.
Setting: Lindiwe is at Park Station in Johannesburg. She wants to get to Newtown but does not know which bus to take. She speaks to a stranger, Mr. Dlamini, who appears to know the area. Relationship: two strangers in a public space, one seeking help.

ULINDIWE Sawubona Mnumzane. Ngicela ungisize. Ngidinga ukuya eNkululekweni. Good morning, sir. Please help me. I need to go to Newtown.
UMNUMZANE DLAMINI Yebo, ngiyakusiza. Hamba phambili kuze ufike esangweni elikhulu, ngemuva kwalokho uphenduke esobunxele. Yes, I will help you. Go straight ahead until you reach the big gate, then turn left.
ULINDIWE Esobunxele. Ngiyaqonda. Ngifika khona kanjani? Ngibhasi noma ngethekisi? To the left. I understand. How do I get there? By bus or by taxi?
UMNUMZANE DLAMINI Ngethekisi kuseduze. Yima lapha, ithekisi liyeza manje. Likhokha amaRandi amane kuphela. By taxi it is close. Wait here, the taxi is coming now. It only costs four rand.
ULINDIWE Ngiyabonga kakhulu! Ngifike kanjani esakhiweni seSintu? Thank you very much! How do I get to the Sintu building once I arrive?
UMNUMZANE DLAMINI Lapho ufika khona, phuma ethekisini, uphenduke esokunene, ngemuva kwalokho hamba ngqo. Isakhiwo sikakhulu, uzosibona kalula. When you arrive, get out of the taxi, turn right, then go straight. The building is big, you will see it easily.
ULINDIWE Kulungile. Ngiyabonga, Mnumzane Dlamini. Uhambe kahle! Very well. Thank you, Mr. Dlamini. Travel well!
UMNUMZANE DLAMINI Sala kahle! Fika kahle eNkululekweni. Stay well! Arrive well in Newtown.

Several expressions in this dialogue, including kuze ufike (until you reach), uzosibona (you will see it), and ngiyakusiza (I will help you), contain patterns that go beyond the productive scope of this chapter. They are provided as recognition items to make the conversation sound natural. Focus your productive study on the commands, locatives, and motion verbs introduced in the Grammar section.

Writing Workshop

In this chapter you will write a step-by-step set of directions. Follow the full planning-drafting-revision sequence.

  1. Choose a route. Think of a short route you know well, either in a real city you know, or a fictional city from the scenario at the start of the chapter. Your route should have at least four steps and should take a person from one named place to another named place.
  2. Identify the landmarks and turns. For each step in your route, decide: which direction word you will use (phambili, esobunxele, esokunene, and so on), which command verb fits (hamba, phenduka, yima, buka, etc.), and whether you will name a landmark using a locative (esiteshini, ekhoneni, and so on).
  3. Draft your directions. Write six to eight connected sentences in isiZulu that take a reader from starting point to destination. Include: at least four positive commands, at least two locative forms using the e-…-ini pattern, one sequence connector such as okokuqala or ngemuva kwalokho, and one polite opening using Ngicela or a greeting before giving the directions.
  4. Add a transport sentence. Include one sentence about how to travel part of the route, using a motion verb and a transport noun with correct class agreement.
  5. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist before writing your final version.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • Each command uses only the verb root plus the final vowel, with no subject concord prefix.
  • Plural commands add -ni correctly to the command form.
  • Locatives for class 7/8 nouns use the e-…-ini pattern with verified spellings.
  • Motion verbs (-hamba, -fika, -phuma, -buya) are used with the correct locative partner (destination or source).
  • Direction words (phambili, esobunxele, esokunene) are spelled correctly as complete units without internal spaces or hyphens.
  • Sequence connectors link steps naturally and do not repeat the same connector at every step.
  • Each sentence begins with a capital letter and ends with appropriate punctuation.
  • No analytical hyphens appear in ordinary verb words.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Transport systems in South African cities

Transport systems differ significantly by city, region, and socioeconomic context across South Africa. In major urban centers like Johannesburg and Durban, the most widely used everyday transport is the shared minibus taxi (ithekisi), which follows fixed routes and is hailed from the roadside or boarded at a taxi rank. Bus services (amabhasi), including the Metrobus in Johannesburg and Durban's municipal bus services, serve scheduled routes; the Rea Vaya bus rapid transit system in Johannesburg provides more structured routes with fixed stops. Train services (uloliwe, receptive) also exist in some corridors. In Durban, the People Mover bus and older municipal routes serve different parts of the city. Not every neighborhood is equally served by all transport types, and access varies considerably between wealthier and working-class areas.

Avoid presenting one transport option as universal or as the only option. A learner asking for directions may receive guidance to a taxi rank, a bus stop, or a walking route depending on where they are, who they ask, and what transport is available at that time of day.

Culture in Context — Asking a stranger for help

Stopping a stranger to ask for directions is a common, socially accepted action in South African cities, and isiZulu speakers generally respond helpfully to someone who makes the effort to ask in isiZulu, even imperfectly. The standard opening is a greeting before any request. Jumping directly into a request without greeting first can feel abrupt. A brief Sawubona or Sanibonani, followed by Ngicela ungisize (Please help me) or Ngicela usizo (I would like help), is the expected pattern. In multilingual cities like Johannesburg and Durban, the person you ask may switch to English to help you, particularly if they sense you are a learner. You can politely continue in isiZulu or accept the switch depending on what is most useful for communication in that moment.

Regional Note — Place names and multilingual naming

Many South African place names exist in more than one official form. Johannesburg may be referred to as eGoli in isiZulu, a name derived from the Zulu word for gold, reflecting the city's history as a gold-mining center. Durban is often called eThekwini in isiZulu, which is also the official name of the metropolitan municipality. Place names on signs and public announcements in South African cities may appear in several languages simultaneously. When reading or writing place names in isiZulu, use the verified isiZulu form, not a transliteration of the English form. Current official names should be verified from South African government or municipal sources rather than assumed.

Culture in Context — Accessibility and varied mobility needs

When giving directions or describing transport options in any language, it is good practice to consider that the person you are helping may have different mobility needs from your own. Not all entrances, routes, or vehicles are equally accessible. South African public transport infrastructure varies widely in its accessibility provisions. Avoid assuming that a route involving stairs, unpaved paths, or rapid vehicle boarding is straightforwardly usable by everyone. In an isiZulu navigation context at this level, simply offering to walk with someone part of the way (Ngingahamba nawe kuze ufike lapho, receptive) or pointing clearly to a visible landmark is often more helpful than a complicated verbal description.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Adding a subject concord to a command

Error: Writing *Uhamba phambili. instead of Hamba phambili. by adding the third-person subject concord u- to the command.

Correction: A positive command in isiZulu consists only of the verb root plus the final vowel -a. No subject concord is added. *Uhamba is a present-tense statement meaning "he/she is going," not a command. If you want to say "Go straight ahead," the correct form is Hamba ngqo phambili. without any prefix.

Common Mistake — Confusing the source and destination of motion verbs

Error: Using -fika where -phuma is needed, for example writing *Ngifika esiteshini to mean "I am leaving the station."

Correction: Ngifika esiteshini means "I arrive at the station." To say "I am leaving the station," use Ngiphuma esiteshini. The motion verb carries the spatial meaning; the locative alone does not distinguish arrival from departure. Check which verb signals the direction of movement before combining it with a locative.

Common Mistake — Inserting spaces or hyphens inside direction words

Error: Writing *e-sokunene or *e so ku nene instead of esokunene.

Correction: Direction locatives such as esobunxele and esokunene are written as single, unbroken words in standard isiZulu orthography. They do not contain internal spaces or analytical hyphens in ordinary writing. Learn them as complete units and write them in full without breaking them apart.

Common Mistake — Applying the e-…-ini pattern mechanically to every noun

Error: Forming *eindleleni instead of the correct endleleni for "on the road," by applying the e- prefix without accounting for the initial vowel change of the noun prefix in class 9.

Correction: The e-…-ini locative pattern involves replacing the augment of the noun, not simply attaching e- to the front of the full noun form. Class 9 nouns whose prefix begins with in- produce locatives beginning end- or emn- through nasal assimilation and other phonological changes. Do not try to derive every locative mechanically. Learn the frequently used locatives in this chapter as whole forms, such as endleleni (on the road) and esiteshini (at the station), before attempting to generalize the pattern.

Common Mistake — Using musa with a bare verb root instead of an infinitive

Error: Writing *Musa hamba lapha. instead of Musa ukuhamba lapha.

Correction: The negative command frame musa requires the class 15 infinitive with uku- before the verb, not the bare command root. Think of it as "don't [do the action of] going": Musa ukuhamba lapha. You already know the class 15 infinitive pattern from Chapter 7, so connect musa to that familiar form rather than to the command root.

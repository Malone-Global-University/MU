Ekhaya nasendaweni

Home and Neighborhood · Speak and Write Zulu: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify rooms, furniture, and household objects using classes 7/8 and 9/10 nouns.
  2. State what is present in a place using the existential construction with khona.
  3. Ask and answer whether a place or service is nearby using basic location questions.
  4. Describe a home using a small set of common lexical locatives.
  5. Form productive locatives for selected nouns using the e-/o-...-ini pattern.
  6. Apply subject concords for classes 7/8 and 9/10 in simple statements.
  7. Use near demonstratives for classes 7/8 and 9/10 to point out objects and places.
  8. Recognize nasal prefix variation in class 9/10 nouns.
  9. Distinguish identity, existence, and location as three separate communicative functions.
  10. Write a short connected description of a home or neighborhood using standard conjunctive orthography.

Communication Scenario

Lindiwe has invited you to visit her family's home in Umlazi for the first time. Before you arrive, she sends you a voice note describing the house and the neighborhood so you will know what to expect: where the kitchen is, whether there is a shop nearby, and what the street looks like. When you arrive, you will want to comment on her home and ask about her neighborhood in return. Talking about home and place is one of the most frequent things speakers do in daily isiZulu conversation, and the patterns you learn here, especially existence and location, will return constantly through the rest of the course.

  1. In English, list four rooms or areas in a home and two things you might find in each one. Keep this list, you will use it in the Writing Workshop later.
  2. Think about how you would ask in English whether something is "in" or "at" a place. Predict whether isiZulu marks this idea on the noun itself or with a separate word. Compare your prediction with the Grammar section.

Pronunciation and Tone

Nasal prefixes and prenasalized consonants

This chapter activates class 9/10 nouns as a productive system. Many class 9/10 nouns begin with a nasal-containing prefix, conventionally represented as iN- in the singular and iziN- in the plural. The capital N stands for a nasal sound that changes depending on the consonant that follows it.

Pronunciation Focus — Hearing the class 9/10 nasal prefix

In words such as indlu (house) and inkomishi (cup), the nasal element of the prefix merges closely with the consonant that follows, producing a single, tightly fused syllable onset rather than two separate sounds. This is called a prenasalized consonant. Listen for a brief nasal hum immediately before the main consonant, rather than a fully separate syllable.

Practise in this order:

  1. Listen to a verified model say indlu slowly, noting the nasal hum before dl.
  2. Repeat the word at a natural pace, keeping the nasal brief.
  3. Compare indlu with inkomishi, noting that the nasal adapts to the following consonant.
  4. Practise the plural forms izindlu and izinkomishi, where the same nasal pattern recurs after izi-.

Do not pronounce the nasal as a fully separate syllable, as in an English "in-dlu" with a pause. The nasal belongs to the same syllable as the consonant that follows it.

Syllabic nasals

Some isiZulu words contain a nasal sound that functions as its own syllable, carrying its own tone, even though it is written with a single letter such as m or n. You will encounter this pattern more fully in later chapters. For now, listen for words where a nasal sound seems to "stand alone" briefly before the rest of the word, and imitate the whole-word model rather than analyzing the nasal in isolation.

Recycling: c, q, and x in home vocabulary

Several words in this chapter recycle the three click types introduced in Chapters 2 through 4. Notice isiqgoki (cupboard), which combines the alveolar click family with a voiced accompaniment you have not yet studied in detail; treat the whole word as a recognition item for now and focus your active click production on words you can already analyze, such as igceke if your instructor confirms a click realization in your model variety, and previously learned items like icici and ixoxo.

Tone and rhythm in locative statements

Locative statements, such as Ikhishi likhona endlini. (The kitchen is there in the house), carry a phrase-level tonal and rhythmic shape that differs from the individual words spoken alone. As in earlier chapters, your goal is accurate imitation of verified whole-phrase models rather than mechanical tone marking. Listen for where the voice falls toward the end of the phrase, especially on the locative-marked noun.

6.1 Classes 7/8 and 9/10

Purpose: recognize and use two new noun-class pairs that are extremely common in household and neighborhood vocabulary.

This chapter activates two new noun-class pairs. Class 7 uses the prefix isi- in the singular and class 8 uses izi- in the plural. Class 9 uses a nasal-containing prefix, represented as iN-, and class 10 uses iziN- in the plural.

Class 7: isi- + stem  |  Class 8: izi- + stem     Class 9: iN- + stem  |  Class 10: iziN- + stem

6.2 Existence with khona

Purpose: state that something is present, available, or "there" using the existential particle khona.

To say that something exists or is present in a place, isiZulu uses the existential particle khona together with a subject concord that agrees with the noun in question. This construction answers questions such as "Is there a shop nearby?"

Grammar Note — Existence, identity, and location are separate systems

English speakers often expect one word, "is," to cover identity ("She is a teacher"), existence ("There is a shop"), and location ("The shop is nearby"). IsiZulu treats these as three separate communicative functions with separate constructions. You have already studied identity copulatives in earlier chapters. This chapter introduces existence with khona, and location through lexical and productive locatives in sections 6.3 and 6.4. Keep these three functions distinct as you build sentences.

Class Subject concord + khona Example English
7sikhonaIsitolo sikhona.There is a shop. / The shop is there.
8zikhonaIzitolo zikhona.There are shops.
9ikhonaIndlu ikhona.There is a house. / The house is there.
10zikhonaIzindlu zikhona.There are houses.

The negative of this construction, used to say something is not present or does not exist, is introduced in Chapter 7 alongside present negation generally. For this chapter, focus on the positive existential pattern and on the question form, which uses the same words with question intonation: Isitolo sikhona? (Is there a shop? / Is the shop there?)

  • Isitolo sikhona eduze? Is there a shop nearby?
  • Yebo, sikhona. Yes, there is. / Yes, it is there.
  • Izindlu zikhona endaweni. There are houses in the area.

6.4 Productive locatives with e-...-ini

Purpose: form a productive locative for selected nouns using the common e-...-ini pattern.

Beyond the lexical locatives in section 6.3, isiZulu has a productive pattern for forming locatives from many nouns. A frequent pattern adds e- to the front of the noun stem and -ini to the end, while the noun's ordinary class prefix is replaced or altered.

Language Building Block — The e-...-ini locative
e- + noun stem + -ini
This is one common, productive locative pattern, not the only one. Not every noun forms a locative this way, and some surface forms involve additional sound changes. Learn the verified forms below and expect more locative patterns in later chapters.
indawo → endaweni   |   ikhishi → ekhishini   |   isitolo → esitolo
place → at/in the place   |   kitchen → in the kitchen   |   shop → at/in the shop
NounLocative formEnglish
indawo (place)endaweniat/in the place
ikhishi (kitchen)ekhishiniin the kitchen
isitolo (shop)esitoloat/in the shop
indlu (house)endliniin the house

Notice that isitolo does not add a final -ini in its common locative form, while indawo and ikhishi do. This kind of variation is a normal part of locative formation in isiZulu and is not something you are expected to predict perfectly at this stage. Treat the verified forms in this table as the productive set for this chapter, and expect the system to be developed further in Chapters 11 and beyond, where locatives are reviewed and expanded.

  • Ikhishi likhona endlini. The kitchen is there in the house.
  • Ngihlala endaweni enhle. I live in a nice area.
  • Sithenga ekhishini. We buy/shop in the kitchen. (Used loosely here for practice; more natural shopping language appears in Chapter 9.)

6.5 Near demonstratives for active classes

Purpose: point out an object or place close to the speaker using the correct demonstrative form for classes 7/8 and 9/10.

Demonstratives agree with the class of the noun they point to, just like subject concords. This chapter introduces the near demonstrative, meaning "this" or "these," for classes 7/8 and 9/10, alongside the class 1/2 and 1a/2a forms recycled from earlier chapters.

Class Near demonstrative Example English
7lesilesi sihlalothis chair
8lezilezi zihlalothese chairs
9lele ndluthis house
10lezilezi zindluthese houses

Demonstratives normally come before the noun in this near-demonstrative series, which is the opposite of the adjective and possessive position you learned in Chapter 4. You are only expected to produce the near, "this/these," series at this stage. Other demonstrative series, such as those meaning "that" at varying distances, are introduced progressively in later chapters.

Language Focus

Two new noun-class pairs are active in this chapter, and they unlock three communicative tools at once: subject concords for verbs and existential statements, demonstratives for pointing things out, and locative forms for saying where something is. As in Chapter 4, the noun's class is the single key that controls all of this agreement. With classes 1/2, 1a/2a, 7/8, and 9/10 now active, you can identify, describe, locate, and point out the great majority of everyday household and neighborhood nouns you will need.

A second key principle: existence and location are not the same thing as identity. Sikhona (it exists/is present) and endlini (in the house) do different communicative work. Keep these systems separate as you build longer descriptions.

6.6 Regional and informal variation in housing vocabulary

Purpose: recognize that some housing and neighborhood terms vary by region and register.

A few terms in this chapter's vocabulary, such as isitobhi, are informal and regionally associated rather than fully neutral Standard isiZulu. Use the Standard forms presented as the default for your own production, and treat labeled informal or regional items as receptive vocabulary that will help you understand real speech.

Model Dialogue

Isimo: ULindiwe ushaya ucingo kuSipho ukuze amchazele ngekhaya lakhe ngaphambi kokuba avakashele. Ubudlelwane: abangane abafunda ndawonye.
Setting: Lindiwe is on a phone call with Sipho, describing her home before he visits. Relationship: classmates and friends.

LINDIWE Sipho, ngifuna ukukuchazela indlu yethu ngaphambi kokuba ufike. Sipho, I want to describe our house to you before you arrive. [ngaphambi kokuba = before; formulaic at this stage]
SIPHO Kulungile, ngilalele. Okay, I'm listening.

(ULindiwe uqala ukuchaza indlu yakhe. — Lindiwe begins describing her house.)

LINDIWE Indlu yethu inkulu. Igumbi lokuhlala likhona phansi, kanye nekhishi. Our house is big. The living room is downstairs, along with the kitchen.
SIPHO Kuhle. Igumbi lokulala likuphi? Nice. Where is the bedroom?
LINDIWE Amagumbi okulala akhona phezulu. Isitezi sethu sincane, kodwa sihle. The bedrooms are upstairs. Our upper storey is small, but it's nice.
SIPHO Isitolo sikhona eduze nekhaya lakho? Is there a shop near your home?
LINDIWE Yebo, sikhona. Isitolo esikhulu sikhona endaweni, futhi isibhedlela sikhona naso, kodwa singaphezu kancane. Yes, there is. There is a big shop in the area, and there is a hospital too, but it's a bit far. [singaphezu kancane = it is somewhat far; formulaic at this stage]
SIPHO Lokho kuhle ukuzwa. Igceke lenu likhulu? That's good to hear. Is your yard big?
LINDIWE Yebo, ikhulu. Abantwana badlala khona phandle njalo ngempelasonto. Yes, it's big. The children play there outside every weekend.
SIPHO Ngizofika ngehora lesithathu. Isango likhona phambi kwendlu? I'll arrive at three o'clock. Is the gate in front of the house? [ngizofika = future form, recognition only; fully introduced in Chapter 12]
LINDIWE Yebo, lesi sango esimnyama. Ngizokulinda khona. Yes, this black gate. I'll wait for you there.

Several expressions in this dialogue, including ngaphambi kokuba (before), singaphezu kancane (it is somewhat far), and the future forms ngizofika and ngizokulinda, contain grammar beyond the current chapter scope. They are included as recognition items to keep the conversation natural. Focus your productive study on the existential, locative, and demonstrative items from the Grammar section.

Read the following short neighborhood notice. All text is in Standard isiZulu. Read the passage aloud twice before answering the questions.

Indawo yaseNdwedwe

Sawubona! Le ndawo ibizwa ngeNdwedwe futhi ikhona eduze neThekwini. Endaweni le, kukhona izindlu eziningi ezinhle, izitolo ezimbili ezinkulu, kanye nesikole esisodwa.

Writing Workshop

In this chapter you will write a short connected paragraph describing a home and its surrounding neighborhood. Follow the full planning-drafting-revision sequence.

  1. Plan. Return to the list you made in the Communication Scenario: four rooms or areas and two items found in each. Add two more details: which class 7/8 or 9/10 nouns from this chapter apply, and one nearby neighborhood place using khona or eduze.
  2. Study the model. Read the passage Indawo yaseNdwedwe in the Reading section once more. Notice how the writer: (a) names the area, (b) states what is present using existential constructions, (c) locates specific rooms and objects, and (d) closes with a general comment about the area. Your paragraph will follow a similar structure.
  3. Draft. Write five to seven connected sentences in isiZulu about a home and its neighborhood, real or imagined. Include: at least two existential sentences with khona, one productive locative formed with e-...-ini, one lexical locative from section 6.3, and one sentence using a near demonstrative.
  4. Locate your rooms. Include at least one sentence stating where a specific room or object is located, for example: Ikhishi likhona endlini.
  5. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist below before writing your final version.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • Subject concords match the class of each noun: si- for class 7, zi- for classes 8 and 10, i- for class 9.
  • Existential statements use khona with the correct subject concord, not a copulative form.
  • Locative forms are written as one conjunctive word: endlini, not *e ndlini or *e-ndlini.
  • Near demonstratives come before the noun and match its class: le ndlu, not *ndlu le.
  • Identity, existence, and location are not confused with one another.
  • Each sentence begins with a capital letter and ends with a full stop.
  • No analytical hyphens appear in the ordinary writing of any verb, locative, or existential form.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: where is it?

Your partner names a household object or room in English. You respond with a complete isiZulu sentence stating that it exists or is located somewhere, using khona or a locative form. Switch roles after six rounds.

Example: Partner says "chair, living room." You say: Isihlalo sikhona egumbini lokuhlala.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: describe your home

Using a drawing, a photo, or an imagined floor plan, describe your home to your partner in isiZulu. For each room or area, state: (a) what is present using khona, (b) where it is located using a lexical or productive locative, and (c) one description using vocabulary from earlier chapters. Your partner listens and then asks at least two follow-up questions about your neighborhood. Switch roles and repeat.

Useful follow-up questions: Isitolo sikhona eduze? (Is there a shop nearby?)  |  Indlu yakho inkulu? (Is your house big?)  |  Ihlala kuphi indawo yakho? (Where is your area located?)

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself giving a sixty- to ninety-second description of your home and neighborhood, real or imagined. Produce at least four complete sentences: what rooms exist (using khona), where two specific objects or rooms are located (using locatives), and one statement about a nearby place. Listen back and check your subject concords and locative forms against the Grammar section tables.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Housing diversity across isiZulu-speaking communities

Housing types and household compositions vary widely across isiZulu-speaking communities, including those in KwaZulu-Natal, Gauteng, and other South African provinces. Some households live in single-storey homes within larger homestead compounds, imizi, that may include several separate buildings shared by an extended family. Others live in townhouses, flats, or multi-storey apartment buildings in urban centres such as Durban or Johannesburg. Neither pattern is more typical or more authentic than the other.

Urban and peri-urban communities frequently combine features of different housing traditions, and household size and structure continue to change with employment patterns, education, and migration. Avoid presenting any single household arrangement, rural or urban, as representative of all isiZulu-speaking communities.

Culture in Context — Asking about someone's home

Asking about someone's home and neighborhood is a common and friendly topic of conversation in many isiZulu-speaking contexts, often following naturally from a greeting exchange. At the same time, questions about household size, ownership, or living arrangements can be sensitive depending on the relationship and setting. As with personal questions introduced in Chapter 3, follow the other speaker's lead and avoid treating detailed questions about property or finances as routine small talk.

Regional Note — Housing and neighborhood vocabulary

Some housing and neighborhood vocabulary varies by region and by the urban or rural character of a community. The informal term isitobhi, used in this chapter for a bus stop or taxi rank area, is common in some urban communities but is not universal Standard isiZulu. Likewise, terms for "window" vary regionally, and this chapter has treated that vocabulary as receptive rather than productive. Verify locally preferred terms with a qualified isiZulu speaker connected to the specific community you are engaging with.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using a copulative form instead of khona for existence

Error: Writing *Isitolo yindlu eduze, attempting to use an identity copulative pattern to express that something is present nearby.

Correction: To say something exists or is present, use the existential construction with khona: Isitolo sikhona eduze. Identity copulatives, which you studied in Chapters 2 and 4, are used for statements like "this is a shop," not for statements about whether something is present somewhere. Keep these two constructions separate.

Common Mistake — Writing the locative with a space or hyphen

Error: Writing *e ndlini or *e-ndlini with a space or hyphen between the locative prefix and the noun.

Correction: The locative form is written as a single conjunctive word: endlini. As with the verb words and possessive forms you studied in earlier chapters, analytical hyphens belong only in grammar explanations, never in ordinary writing. Preserve standard word boundaries at all times.

Common Mistake — Mismatching the subject concord with class 8 or class 10 nouns

Error: Writing *Izitolo sikhona instead of Izitolo zikhona, applying the class 7 concord si- to a class 8 plural noun.

Correction: Class 8 and class 10 plural nouns both take the subject concord zi-: Izitolo zikhona. (The shops are there.) and Izindlu zikhona. (The houses are there.) Always check whether a noun is singular or plural before selecting its concord, since the singular and plural forms in this system differ from one another even though several singular classes appear similar at first glance.

Common Mistake — Placing the near demonstrative after the noun

Error: Writing *ndlu le by transferring English word order, where the demonstrative typically follows in some structures.

Correction: The near demonstrative series, "this/these," comes before the noun in isiZulu: le ndlu (this house), lesi sihlalo (this chair). Note that this is different from the adjective and possessive position from Chapter 4, which come after the noun. Learn each modifier's correct position as a feature of that specific construction rather than applying one universal rule to every modifier.

Looking Ahead

Okungakwenzeka manje — What you can do now

You can identify rooms and household objects using classes 7/8 and 9/10, state what is present in a place using khona, and locate people, objects, and neighborhood places using both lexical locatives and the productive e-...-ini pattern. You can point things out using the near demonstrative series for these classes, and you can distinguish identity, existence, and location as separate communicative tools. You have also begun listening for nasal prefixes and prenasalized consonants in class 9/10 nouns. All of these patterns, especially existence and location, will be recycled and expanded constantly throughout the rest of the course.

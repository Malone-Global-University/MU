Reading Notices and Writing Messages · Speak and Write Zulu: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Read and understand short public notices, signs, and announcements built from vocabulary and grammar you already know.
  2. Recognize common notice vocabulary, including vala (closed), vuliwe (open), ngena (enter), and phuma (exit), using the command forms recycled from Chapter 16.
  3. Identify the purpose and audience of a notice, such as a school announcement, a shop sign, or a community notice board posting.
  4. Write a short practical message to a friend, family member, or colleague, using a simple greeting, body, and closing.
  5. Use the requests and polite forms from Chapter 16, and the sequencing connectors from Chapter 17, inside a written message.
  6. Read a short notice board text and a short written message, and answer comprehension questions about each.
  7. Consolidate spelling, punctuation, and capitalization conventions for short practical texts, as established since Chapter 1.
  8. Distinguish, without full analysis, a small number of set phrases used only in formal public notices, understanding that this chapter's productive writing continues to rely on grammar already active in the course.

Communication Scenario

It is Wednesday morning at Zanele's school. A notice has appeared on the board outside the office, and Zanele and Thandeka stop to read it together before class. Later that day, Zanele's mother asks her to leave a short written message for her older brother, who is out, reminding him about a family matter. Zanele must read carefully, and then write clearly, so that both the notice and her message are understood correctly.

  1. Think of a notice you have seen recently, in English, on a wall, a shop window, or a school board. Write down, in English, what it said and who it was for.
  2. Think of a short message you have written or received recently, such as a text message or a note. Write down, in English, its greeting, its main point, and its closing.

Pronunciation and Reading Aloud

Reading short public texts clearly

A notice is usually short and direct, so each word carries more weight than in a longer narrative. When reading a notice aloud, slow down slightly on the key instruction word, such as vuliwe or ungangeni, and let the penultimate lengthening you have practised since Chapter 1 fall clearly on that word.

Practise saying Ibhodi lezaziso, vuliwe kusukela ngo-08:00. with a brief pause after the comma, and a clear, even stress on vuliwe, since this is the word a reader most needs to notice.

Pronunciation Focus — Reading a message aloud to a listener

When reading a written message aloud, such as to check it with a family member before sending it, keep the greeting, body, and closing as three distinct short phrases, each with its own brief pause, similar to how you separated connectors in Chapter 17. This helps the listener follow the structure of the message, not only its content.

Grammar Instruction

18.1 Reviewing commands and polite requests in notices

Purpose: recognize and use the command forms from Chapter 16 as they appear in short public notices.

Public notices frequently use the imperative and negative imperative forms you learned in Chapter 16, since a notice usually gives an instruction. Ngena (enter), phuma (exit), and ungangeni (do not enter) are common examples. This chapter does not introduce new command morphology. Instead, it teaches you to read and produce these familiar forms inside the short, direct sentences typical of public notices.

Language Building Block — Reviewing the command form
[verb stem]-a (affirmative) / u-nga-[verb stem]-i (negative)
This is the same command pattern from Chapter 16. No new verb morphology is introduced in this chapter. The new work is applying these familiar forms to the short, public style of notices and signs.
Ngena. Ungangeni.
Enter. Do not enter.

18.2 Stative forms for signage: vuliwe and valiwe

Purpose: recognize and use two common formulaic signage words that describe a current state rather than an action.

Vuliwe (open) and valiwe (closed) describe the current state of a place, such as a shop, office, or gate. At this stage, treat these two words as fixed, formulaic vocabulary items for recognition and limited production on signs and notices, without analyzing their internal verb structure. Full analysis of this pattern is developed in a later volume.

  • Ihhovisi, vuliwe. The office is open.
  • Isitolo, valiwe namuhla. The shop is closed today.

18.3 Structuring a short written message

Purpose: organize a short written message using a clear greeting, body, and closing, recycling vocabulary and grammar from earlier chapters.

A short isiZulu message typically opens with a greeting recycled from Chapter 1, states its main point using vocabulary and structures already active in the course, and closes with a leave-taking expression or thanks. The polite request frame ngicela ukukukhumbuza (I would like to remind you), built on the request patterns from Chapter 16, is useful for reminder messages.

Language Building Block — A short written message
[Greeting]. [Main point, using known vocabulary and grammar]. [Closing].
The greeting and closing recycle formulaic expressions from Chapter 1. The main point should use only vocabulary and grammar already active in the course, including sequencing connectors from Chapter 17 where useful for ordering more than one point.
Sawubona Bhuti. Ngicela ukukukhumbuza ngomhlangano wakusasa. Usale kahle.
Hello, Brother. I would like to remind you about tomorrow's meeting. Stay well.
  • Sawubona Mama. Ngiphumele nabangane bami. Ngizobuya ntambama. Hello, Mother. I have gone out with my friends. I will return in the afternoon.
  • Sanibonani. Kuqala, sicela nifike ngesikhathi. Ngemva kwalokho, sizoqala umhlangano. Ngiyabonga ngokufunda. Hello, everyone. First, please arrive on time. After that, we will begin the meeting. Thank you for reading.

Note on scope: This chapter's productive writing relies entirely on grammar already active since Chapter 1 through Chapter 17. A small number of formal notice phrases used in official public signage, such as certain legal or municipal wording, exist in fuller isiZulu public life and are receptive only at this stage; fuller register work with public and institutional texts is developed in Volume II and later volumes.

Language Focus

This chapter's central development is applying familiar grammar to two new text types: the short public notice and the short personal message. No new verb tense or command form is introduced. The command forms from Chapter 16 and the sequencing connectors from Chapter 17 continue to recur here, showing how earlier systems remain active and become more useful as they combine with new reading and writing purposes.

Model Dialogue

Isimo: Ekuseni, phandle kwehhovisi lesikole, uZanele noThandeka bafunda isaziso ebhodini.
Setting: In the morning, outside the school office, Zanele and Thandeka read a notice on the board.

UTHANDEKA Sawubona, Zanele. Bheka lesi saziso. Sithini? Hello, Zanele. Look at this notice. What does it say?
UZANELE Sawubona, Thandeka. Sithi, "Ihhovisi, valiwe namuhla emini. Vuliwe ntambama." Hello, Thandeka. It says, "The office is closed today at midday. Open in the afternoon."
UTHANDEKA Kungani livaliwe emini? Why is it closed at midday?
UZANELE Angazi ngempela, kodwa isaziso sithi kunomhlangano wothisha emini. I do not really know, but the notice says there is a teachers' meeting at midday.
UTHANDEKA Kulungile. Kuzofanele sifike emuva kwentambama, uma sifuna ukukhuluma nomabhalane. All right. We will need to come after the afternoon, if we want to speak with the secretary.
Ntambama, ekhaya, uZanele ubhala umlayezo omfishane kumnewabo, uSipho, ngoba akekho. — In the afternoon, at home, Zanele writes a short message for her older brother, Sipho, because he is not there.
UZANELE (Ebhala) "Sawubona Sipho. Ngicela ukukukhumbuza ngomhlangano womndeni kusasa ekuseni. Usale kahle. — Zanele." (Writing) "Hello, Sipho. I would like to remind you about the family meeting tomorrow morning. Stay well. — Zanele."
UMAMA Ngiyabonga, Zanele. Umlayezo wakho mfishane, kodwa ucacile. Thank you, Zanele. Your message is short, but it is clear.

One expression in this dialogue extends slightly beyond full productive analysis at this stage: kuzofanele sifike (we will need to arrive, a future obligation frame) is receptive. Focus your productive study on the notice vocabulary, the command forms, and the message structure identified in the Grammar section.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Sithini isaziso ngehhovisi emini? (What does the notice say about the office at midday?)
  2. Kungani ihhovisi livaliwe emini, ngokwesaziso? (Why is the office closed at midday, according to the notice?)
  3. Ubani obhalelwa umlayezo kaZanele? (Who is Zanele's message written for?)
  4. Uthini umlayezo kaZanele? (What does Zanele's message say?)

Isaziso

Writing Workshop

In this chapter you will write a short practical notice or message. Choose one of the two tasks below.

Isikhungo sokukhethwa — Choose your writing task:

Umsebenzi A (A short notice): Write a short notice of two to three sentences for a school, shop, or home setting, using at least one command form from Chapter 16, and the vocabulary vuliwe or valiwe.

Umsebenzi B (A short message): Write a short message of three to five sentences to a friend or family member, using a greeting, a main point built from vocabulary and grammar already active in the course, at least one sequencing connector from Chapter 17, and a closing.

  1. Plan your content. Decide on your reader, your purpose, and your two or three main points, in English if that helps you plan.
  2. Write using active vocabulary. Use only vocabulary, grammar, and connectors from this chapter and earlier chapters.
  3. Check your structure. Confirm that a message includes a greeting, body, and closing, or that a notice states its instruction clearly and directly.
  4. Check your command forms. Confirm that any instruction uses the correct affirmative or negative imperative from Chapter 16.
  5. Check your punctuation and capitalization. Confirm that each sentence begins with a capital letter and ends with appropriate punctuation.
  6. Revise and write your final version. Check your draft against the editing checklist below before writing or typing the final version.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • A message opens with a greeting and closes with a leave-taking expression or thanks.
  • A notice states its instruction or information clearly, in one or two short sentences.
  • Any command form uses the correct affirmative or negative imperative from Chapter 16.
  • Any sequencing connector from Chapter 17 is used correctly, with a comma pause where appropriate.
  • No unscheduled vocabulary or grammar appears beyond this chapter and earlier chapters.
  • Each sentence begins with a capital letter and ends with appropriate punctuation.
  • No analytical hyphens appear inside ordinary verb words or compound nouns.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: reading a notice aloud

Your partner writes a short notice, two sentences long, using vocabulary from this chapter. Read the notice aloud clearly, then explain its meaning in your own words. After three rounds, swap roles.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: leaving a spoken message

Partner A imagines they must leave the house and cannot find Partner B. Partner A leaves a short spoken message, three to four sentences long, including a greeting, a reason for leaving, and a closing. Partner B listens, then repeats back the key details to check understanding. Swap roles after each message.

Useful checking expressions: Ngiyaqonda. (I understand.)  |  Ngicela uphinde. (Please repeat.)  |  Ngiyabonga ngomlayezo. (Thank you for the message.)

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself reading aloud a notice or message you have written for this chapter's writing task. Listen back and check that your pronunciation of key instruction words, such as vuliwe or ungangeni, is clear and accurate.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Notice boards in schools and communities

Notice boards remain a common and trusted way of sharing information in many South African schools, workplaces, and community centres, alongside newer digital methods. As seen in this chapter's dialogue, checking the notice board is often a normal part of the school day, and the information found there, such as meeting times or office hours, is generally treated as reliable and worth acting on.

Culture in Context — Leaving a message for a family member

Leaving a short written note for a family member who is out, as Zanele does for her brother, is a common courtesy in many households, connecting to the respect-language and family-communication patterns introduced since Chapter 1. A brief, clear message, as Zanele's mother notes, is generally valued over a long or vague one.

Regional Note — Variation in notice-board wording

Some notices, particularly official or municipal ones, use a more formal register with set legal or administrative phrasing that goes beyond this chapter's scope. This textbook establishes the everyday notice and message patterns above as the primary productive forms for Volume I, and introduces more formal public-notice register receptively in later volumes.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Omitting the greeting or closing in a message

Error: Writing *Ngiphumele nabangane bami. as a complete message, without any greeting or closing.

Correction: A short message should generally include a greeting and a closing, even when brief: Sawubona Baba. Ngiphumele nabangane bami. Usale kahle. (Hello, Father. I have gone out with my friends. Stay well.)

Common Mistake — Confusing vuliwe and valiwe

Error: Writing *Isitolo, vuliwe namuhla. to mean "The shop is closed today," by using vuliwe (open) instead of valiwe (closed).

Correction: Vuliwe means open, and valiwe means closed. Isitolo, valiwe namuhla. (The shop is closed today.) is correct. Review both words together to keep the contrast clear.

Common Mistake — Using an affirmative command where a negative is needed

Error: Writing *Ngena ehhovisini ngaphandle kwemvume. to mean "Do not enter the office without permission," by using the affirmative command ngena instead of the negative form.

Correction: A prohibition requires the negative imperative from Chapter 16: Ungangeni ehhovisini ngaphandle kwemvume. (Do not enter the office without permission.)

Common Mistake — Writing a notice in full sentence style instead of short, direct style

Error: Writing a long, conversational sentence for a notice, such as *Ngicela nazi ukuthi ihhovisi livaliwe namuhla emini ngoba kunomhlangano wothisha, futhi ngithemba ukuthi anizukhathazeka ngalokho., which is far longer and more conversational than a notice normally requires.

Correction: Keep notices short and direct: Ihhovisi, valiwe namuhla emini. Kunomhlangano wothisha. (The office is closed today at midday. There is a teachers' meeting.) A notice states information plainly, without conversational framing.

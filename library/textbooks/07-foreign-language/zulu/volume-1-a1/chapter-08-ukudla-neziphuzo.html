Ukudla neziphuzo

Food and Drink · Speak and Write Zulu: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common foods, drinks, and meals using correct class 5/6 and recycled class 9/10 nouns.
  2. State what you want, need, like, and dislike using approved beginner constructions.
  3. Order food and drink politely in a café or simple restaurant setting.
  4. Request an item using cela, and politely decline an offer.
  5. Express basic quantities and containers, such as a cup, a plate, or a little of something.
  6. Use introductory object concords for first and second person and for selected active noun classes inside a verb word.
  7. Give a singular or plural positive command in a service context, and recognize a negative command.
  8. Ask for and understand a price using numbers already studied.
  9. Distinguish plain and aspirated click accompaniments in active vocabulary, and produce ng accurately.
  10. Write a short order, list, or paragraph stating food preferences using standard conjunctive isiZulu orthography.

Communication Scenario

It is Saturday morning in Durban, and your isiZulu class has arranged to meet at a small café near the beachfront for breakfast before their weekly conversation practice. The menu is written in both English and isiZulu, and the server, who is a fluent isiZulu speaker, is happy to take orders in either language. Sipho, who you met in Chapter 4, is already at a table, and he invites you to order with him. You realize this is your first real chance to ask for what you want, check a price, and decline something politely, all without switching back to English.

  1. In English, list three foods or drinks you would order for breakfast, and one item you would politely decline. Keep this list, since you will use it in the Writing Workshop later.
  2. Predict where the word for "please" might appear in an isiZulu request: at the beginning, the middle, or the end of the sentence. Compare your prediction with the Grammar section.

Pronunciation and Tone

Aspirated click accompaniments: ch, qh, xh

You have already learned the three plain click types: c, q, and x, from Chapters 2 through 4. This chapter introduces the aspirated accompaniment of each click, written ch, qh, and xh. An aspirated click is released with a strong puff of breath immediately after the click sound, similar to the difference you may already notice between unaspirated and aspirated stops.

Pronunciation Focus — Producing aspirated clicks

Begin with the plain click in its correct place of articulation: dental for c, alveolar or postalveolar for q, lateral for x. Immediately after the tongue releases, allow a strong burst of air to escape, as you would for an aspirated p, t, or k. The aspiration follows the click release; it does not replace the click itself.

Practise in this order:

  1. Produce the plain click alone: c, q, or x.
  2. Add a strong puff of air immediately after the release.
  3. Combine with a vowel: cha, qha, xha.
  4. Practise in real words such as ichizi (cheese, an adapted loanword) and listen for the contrast with plain c in icici (earring).

Do not confuse aspiration with simply saying the click more loudly. Aspiration is a distinct airflow event after the click release, not increased volume. As always, listen to a verified model before attempting production.

The consonant ng

The digraph ng represents a velar nasal, similar to the sound at the end of English sing, but it occurs in isiZulu at the beginning of syllables as well as within words. It appears frequently in this chapter's vocabulary, including ngicela and inkomishi.

Pronunciation Focus — ng at the start of a syllable

Unlike English, where this nasal sound never begins a word, isiZulu allows ng to begin a syllable, as in ngi- (the first-person singular subject concord you already use constantly). Position the back of the tongue against the soft palate as for English sing, then release directly into the following vowel without inserting an extra vowel sound beforehand. Practise nga, ngi, ngu in isolation, then in the words ngicela, ngifuna, and ngiyathanda, all of which you will use heavily in this chapter's ordering practice.

Tone in polite requests

Polite request phrases such as Ngicela amanzi. carry a characteristic falling phrase melody toward the end of the request, distinct from the rising pattern of a yes-or-no question. Listen for the difference between a request and a question using verified whole-phrase audio or a qualified speaker. As in earlier chapters, your goal is accurate imitation of the phrase as a whole rather than marking individual syllables, since standard isiZulu spelling does not mark tone.

Grammar Instruction

8.1 Class 5 and class 6 nouns

Purpose: recognize and use the class 5/6 noun pair, which contains many food items, alongside the human and nonhuman classes already active.

Class 5 nouns typically take the prefix i- or ili-, while their class 6 plurals take the prefix ama-. You have already met classes 1/2, 1a/2a, 7/8, 9/10, and class 15. Class 5/6 is the next pair to become productive, and several foods in this chapter's vocabulary, including iqanda, ibhodlela, and ipuleti, belong to it.

Language Building Block — Class 5 and class 6
Class 5: i- (or ili-) + noun stem   |   Class 6: ama- + noun stem
Most class 5 nouns in everyday use take the short prefix i-, with the full ili- form appearing in fewer items. The class 6 plural prefix ama- is highly regular.
iqanda → amaqanda   |   ibhodlela → amabhodlela   |   ipuleti → amapuleti
egg → eggs   |   bottle → bottles   |   plate → plates
Class Prefix Subject concord (singular) Subject concord (plural) Example noun
5 / 6 i- / ama- li- a- iqanda / amaqanda

Notice that the class 5 subject concord li- does not visibly match the noun prefix i-. This is a feature of the class, not an error, and you should learn the concord as part of the noun's grammatical profile. The class 6 subject concord a- is short, and often combines with a following vowel; this combination is shown in the examples below.

  • Iqanda likhulu. i-qanda + li-khulu The egg is big.
  • Amaqanda makhulu. ama-qanda + a-makhulu The eggs are big. (a- + makhulu, vowel coalescence)
  • Ibhodlela likhona. i-bhodlela + li-khona The bottle is here / available.

The class 6 example shows vowel coalescence between the subject concord a- and the adjective concord a- on -khulu, producing amakhulu as one written word with the subject concord fused in. You are not expected to derive this independently; the example is provided for recognition. Focus your productive practice on the singular class 5 forms and on identifying class 6 plurals.

8.2 Wants, needs, and likes

Purpose: state preferences and needs using approved beginner constructions, and contrast them with the polite request frame.

IsiZulu distinguishes several related but separate ways of expressing desire, necessity, and preference. The verbs -funa (want), -dinga (need), and -thanda (like) follow the present positive and negative patterns you already know from Chapters 3 and 7.

Verb Positive (I ___) Negative (I do not ___) Meaning
-funaNgifunaAngifuniwant
-dingaNgidingaAngidingineed
-thandaNgiyathandaAngithandilike

Notice that -funa and -dinga use the short present form productively in this construction, while -thanda commonly appears with the long -ya- form when no object follows. This distribution follows the long-short pattern from Chapter 3: the long form is typical when nothing follows the verb, and the short form is typical when an object or complement follows directly. Compare Ngiyathanda itiye. with Ngifuna itiye. Both are correct; the difference in form reflects the verb, not a different rule.

  • Ngifuna ikhofi. I want coffee.
  • Ngiyathanda izithelo. I like fruit.
  • Angithandi inyama. I do not like meat.
  • Ngidinga amanzi. I need water.

8.3 The polite request frame: cela

Purpose: make a polite request using the formulaic cela construction, appropriate for service interactions such as ordering food.

The most common polite way to request something in isiZulu is Ngicela ___, which functions much like "May I please have ___" or "I would like ___, please" in English. The construction is built from the first-person subject concord ngi- and the verb stem -cela (to ask for; to request), followed directly by the item requested.

Language Building Block — Polite request
Ngicela + noun phrase
Ngicela is a fixed, formulaic opening for a polite request in this chapter. The full subjunctive grammar behind cela is developed productively in a later chapter; for now, treat the frame as a complete unit.
Ngicela itiye.  |  Ngicela ipuleti lesinkwa.  |  Ngicela amanzi.
I would like tea, please.  |  I would like a plate of bread, please.  |  I would like water, please.

To request something on behalf of more than one person, or as a group, use Sicela ___ (we would like ___, please), built on the first-person plural subject concord si-.

  • Ngicela ikhofi nesinkwa. I would like coffee and bread, please.
  • Sicela amaqanda amabili. We would like two eggs, please.
  • Ngicela ibhodlela lamanzi. I would like a bottle of water, please.

Notice that nesinkwa in the first example is na- (and; with) fused with isinkwa, following the same vowel-combination pattern you saw with possessives in Chapter 4. You will see this na- pattern used for "and" between nouns throughout the course.

  2. two eggs (for yourself)
  3. bread and coffee (for your group)
  4. a bottle of water (for yourself)

8.4 Introductory object concords

Purpose: refer back to a person or thing already mentioned by placing the correct object concord inside the verb word, for first and second person and selected active noun classes.

Until now, your verbs have referred to their object by stating the object noun directly after the verb, as in Ngicela itiye. IsiZulu also allows the object to be referenced inside the verb word itself through an object concord, placed between the subject concord or tense marker and the verb root. This chapter introduces the object concord productively for first and second person, and receptively for a small set of active noun classes.

Grammar Note — Object concords occur inside the verb word

An object concord is not a free-standing word. It is written conjunctively as part of the verb, immediately before the verb root. The object concord must have a clear antecedent: a person or thing already mentioned or obvious from context. Do not use an object concord without something for it to refer back to.

Person / class Object concord Example English
me (1st singular)-ngi-Uyangibonisa.He/she is showing me.
you, singular (2nd singular)-ku-Ngiyakucela.I am asking you, please.
us (1st plural)-si-Uyasibonisa.He/she is showing us.
you, plural (2nd plural)-ni-Ngiyanicelela.I am asking on your behalf. (receptive)
class 5 (it)-li-Ngiyalithanda iqanda.I like the egg. (receptive)
class 9 (it)-yi-Ngiyayithanda inyama.I like the meat. (receptive)

The first-person and second-person object concords are productive at this stage: you should be able to use -ngi-, -ku-, and -si- independently in simple sentences. The class 5 and class 9 object concords shown above are receptive: recognize them in context, but you are not yet expected to select them independently across all classes. Notice that the long form -ya- appears before the object concord, since an object concord normally requires the long form when nothing else follows the verb.

  • Sipho uyangibonisa imenyu. u-ya-ngi-bonis-a Sipho is showing me the menu.
  • Ngiyakubonga ngosizo lwakho. ngi-ya-ku-bong-a I thank you for your help. (formulaic at this stage)
  • Umsizi uyasinika amenyu. u-ya-si-nik-a The waiter is giving us menus.

8.5 Quantitative concords and commands in service contexts

Purpose: express small quantities using an introductory quantitative concord, and give or recognize commands appropriate to ordering food.

The quantitative stem -nye (one; some; another) takes a concord matching the class of the noun it quantifies. This chapter introduces the class 5 and class 9 forms productively, since they occur with frequently ordered items.

Class Quantitative form Example English
5elinyeNgicela elinye iqanda.I would like one more egg, please.
9enyeNgicela enye inkomishi yetiye.I would like another cup of tea, please.

Quantitative forms for the remaining active classes will be introduced progressively, and the full quantitative system is consolidated in Chapter 9. For now, learn the class 5 and class 9 forms above as complete units for ordering purposes.

Commands recycle the singular and plural patterns from Chapters 1 and 2, now extended to service vocabulary. A polite command in a service context is typically softened with cela rather than given as a bare command.

  • Ngicela usinike imenyu. Please give us the menu. (softened command, formulaic at this stage)
  • Linda kancane, ngiyacabanga. Wait a little, I am thinking. (singular command, recycled pattern)
  • Ningalindi, sizofika manje. Do not wait, we will arrive now. (negative plural command; receptive)
Language Focus

This chapter introduces two new noun classes worth of agreement, class 5/6, alongside an early window into reference inside the verb word through object concords. The two systems connect directly to communication you actually need in a café: naming and counting food items with class 5/6, and referring back to those items politely without repeating the full noun every time. Keep the productive load focused on first- and second-person object concords and the class 5/9 quantitative forms shown above; the remaining class patterns will return for fuller practice in Chapter 9.

Model Dialogue

Isimo: USipho noLindiwe baseKafini ngasolwandle eThekwini. Umsizi uyeza ukuzothatha iodolo yabo. Ubudlelwane: ikhasimende nomsizi, kanye nabangane ababili abahleli ndawonye.
Setting: Sipho and Lindiwe are at a café near the beachfront in Durban. A server approaches to take their order. Relationship: customers and a server, plus two friends sitting together.

UMSIZI Sawubona! Ningathanda ukuodola manje? Hello! Would you like to order now?
SIPHO Yebo, ngiyabonga. Ngicela ikhofi nesinkwa. Yes, thank you. I would like coffee and bread, please.
UMSIZI Kulungile. Wena ufunani? Very well. What would you like? [addressed to Lindiwe]
LINDIWE Ngicela amaqanda amabili nesaladi. Ngiyathanda izithelo nazo, uma ninazo. I would like two eggs and a salad, please. I also like fruit, if you have it.
UMSIZI Sinazo izithelo. Niyathanda itiye noma ikhofi? We have fruit. Do you like tea or coffee?
LINDIWE Ngicela itiye. Angithandi ikhofi kakhulu. I would like tea, please. I do not like coffee very much.

(Umsizi uyabhala iodolo, abuye emuva nemiphathi. — The server writes down the order and returns later with the trays.)

UMSIZI Nansi ikhofi yakho, nesinkwa. Nansi ne-tiye yakho nesaladi. Here is your coffee, and bread. And here is your tea and salad. [nansi = here it is, recycled demonstrative formula]
SIPHO Ngiyabonga kakhulu. Ngicela elinye iqanda futhi, uLindiwe ulambile. Thank you very much. May I have one more egg, please, Lindiwe is hungry. [ulambile = is hungry; formulaic, receptive]
LINDIWE Hhayi, Sipho, ngiyabonga, kodwa kuyanele. Ngicela amanzi nje. No, Sipho, thank you, but that is enough. I would just like water.
UMSIZI Kulungile. Nansi inkomishi yamanzi. Nina nidla kahle! Very well. Here is a cup of water. Enjoy your meal!
SIPHO Bill, ngiyacela. Yimalini sonke? The bill, please. How much is everything?
UMSIZI Ngamashumi amabili nantathu, ngokuphelele. It is twenty-three rand altogether. [currency unit omitted in this exercise; ngokuphelele = altogether, formulaic]

Several expressions in this dialogue, including kuyanele (that is enough), ulambile (is hungry), and ngokuphelele (altogether), contain grammar beyond the current chapter scope. They are included as recognition items to make the conversation sound natural. Focus your productive study on the items in the Grammar section.

Writing Workshop

In this chapter you will write a short food order or preference paragraph. Follow the full planning-drafting-revision sequence.

  1. Plan. Return to the list you made in the Communication Scenario: three items you would order, and one item you would politely decline. Add a quantity to at least one item, such as two eggs or a cup of tea.
  2. Study the model. Read the passage Imenyu yasekuseni in the Reading section once more. Notice how the text: (a) welcomes the reader, (b) lists what can be ordered, (c) gives a price for two items, and (d) closes politely. Your paragraph will follow a similar pattern, written from the customer's point of view.
  3. Draft. Write five to seven connected sentences in isiZulu placing an order or describing your food preferences. Include: at least two polite requests using Ngicela, one sentence stating a like or dislike using -thanda, one sentence using a quantity or a class 5/6 noun, and one sentence politely declining something using Cha, ngiyabonga.
  4. Add a price exchange. Include one short question-and-answer pair asking and stating a price, using Yimalini? and a number you have studied.
  5. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist below before writing your final version.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • Polite requests use Ngicela or Sicela followed directly by the noun phrase.
  • Class 5 nouns use the prefix i-, and their class 6 plurals use ama-.
  • Likes and dislikes use -thanda in the correct positive or negative form.
  • Object concords, where used, are written conjunctively inside the verb word, with no spaces or hyphens.
  • Quantities such as elinye or enye match the class of the noun they quantify.
  • Each sentence begins with a capital letter and ends with appropriate punctuation.
  • No analytical hyphens appear in the ordinary writing of any verb form.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Food practices across isiZulu-speaking communities

Food practices among isiZulu-speaking communities vary widely by region, household, religion, health need, and personal preference. Staple foods such as izinkobe (cooked maize kernels) and maize-based porridge are widely eaten across South Africa, including in KwaZulu-Natal and Gauteng, but daily diets in urban centers like Durban and Johannesburg often include a broad range of international and fusion foods as well. Café culture, including coffee shops and breakfast spots, is common in cities and reflects contemporary, cosmopolitan South African life rather than a single "traditional" cuisine.

Avoid treating any single dish as representative of all isiZulu-speaking communities. Dietary practices connected to religion, health conditions, and personal choice, including vegetarian and other selective diets, are present and increasingly common, and a respectful server or host will generally ask about preferences rather than assume them.

Culture in Context — Politeness in requests

The cela-based request frame reflects a broader isiZulu pattern in which softened, indirect requests are generally preferred over bare commands, particularly between people who are not close friends or family, and especially in service or formal settings. A bare command such as Ngipha amanzi. (Give me water.) is grammatically correct but can sound abrupt in many contexts; Ngicela amanzi. is the more broadly appropriate choice in a café, shop, or unfamiliar setting. Among close friends and family, more direct requests are often comfortable and common. Pay attention to the relationship between speakers when choosing how directly to ask for something.

Regional Note — Loanwords in food vocabulary

Several words in this chapter, including ikhofi (coffee), itiye (tea), ijusi (juice), and isaladi (salad), are established loanwords adapted into isiZulu noun-class patterns. These forms are widely used and understood across isiZulu-speaking communities, including in KwaZulu-Natal and Gauteng, and are the standard productive choices for this textbook. Some communities and informal speech may use alternative or further code-switched terms for certain foods and drinks; where this is the case, the standard forms taught here remain broadly understood and appropriate for a learner.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using umu- or other wrong prefixes for class 5 nouns

Error: Writing *umuqanda instead of iqanda for "egg," by applying a class 1 prefix to a class 5 noun.

Correction: Class 5 nouns take the prefix i- in everyday productive use: iqanda, ibhodlela, ipuleti. Their class 6 plurals take ama-: amaqanda, amabhodlela, amapuleti. Learning the correct noun prefix from the start prevents persistent concord errors, since the subject concord li- for class 5 depends on correctly identifying the noun's class.

Common Mistake — Placing an object concord without a clear antecedent

Error: Using an object concord such as -ngi- or -ku- when it is unclear what or whom it refers to, producing a confusing sentence.

Correction: An object concord must always have a clear antecedent already established in the conversation, such as a person named in an earlier sentence. Before using an object concord, check that a listener could identify exactly who or what it refers to. When in doubt at this stage, use the full noun phrase instead, as in Ngicela itiye. rather than relying on an unclear object concord.

Common Mistake — Treating ngicela as a bare command

Error: Dropping ngicela and using only a bare command in a service context, such as *Nika amanzi. (Give water.), where a more polite frame is expected.

Correction: In service and unfamiliar settings, prefer the softened request frame: Ngicela amanzi. A bare command is not incorrect grammar, but it can sound abrupt to a server or shopkeeper you do not know. Reserve direct commands for contexts where directness is appropriate, such as among close friends and family, or in instructions you have already learned, such as classroom commands.

Common Mistake — Writing spaces inside the verb word with an object concord

Error: Writing *ngi ya ngi cela or inserting a hyphen as in *ngi-ya-ngi-cela in ordinary text.

Correction: The subject concord, long-form marker, object concord, and verb root combine into a single conjunctive word in standard isiZulu writing. Write ngiyangicela as one unbroken word. Analytical hyphens belong only inside the labeled grammar explanations in this textbook, never in ordinary writing tasks or final drafts.

Looking Ahead

Okungakwenzeka manje — What you can do now

You can name common foods and drinks using the new class 5/6 system alongside the classes you already know, state what you want, need, and like, and order politely in a café using Ngicela. You can express small quantities with the class 5 and class 9 quantitative forms, refer back to people using first- and second-person object concords, and ask for and understand a simple price. All of these patterns, including class 5/6 agreement, polite requests, and object concords, will be recycled and expanded in every chapter that follows, especially in Chapter 9 as you move into shopping for clothing and other goods.

Isahluko 9: Ukuthenga

Chapter 9 turns from food to shopping more broadly. You will learn to ask for and state prices, identify clothing and common goods, describe color, size, quality, and quantity, and refer back to items using demonstratives and an expanded set of object concords. The object concord and quantitative work from this chapter will return and grow across the active noun classes, and the polite request frames you have just learned will support a full shopping and purchasing interaction.

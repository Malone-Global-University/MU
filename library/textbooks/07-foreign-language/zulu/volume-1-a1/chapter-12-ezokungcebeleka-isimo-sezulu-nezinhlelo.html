Ezokungcebeleka, isimo sezulu nezinhlelo

Leisure, Weather, and Plans · Speak and Write Zulu: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common leisure activities, hobbies, and recreational places using active vocabulary from established noun classes.
  2. Describe basic weather conditions using the copulative predicate with weather nouns and adjectival concords from class 7/8 and class 9/10.
  3. State what someone will do using the positive future form with -zo- in first-, second-, and third-person singular and plural.
  4. State basic ability or inability using ngikwazi (I am able to) and angikwazi (I am not able to) followed by an infinitive, treating these as productive formulaic frames.
  5. Make a simple invitation and respond positively or negatively using the established cela and greeting frames, supplemented by new formulaic acceptance and refusal expressions.
  6. Use the locative and motion-verb patterns from Chapter 11 in leisure and planning contexts, confirming where activities take place and how participants will get there.
  7. Recognize the inclusive suggestion pattern using ake si- (let us), treating it as a formulaic expression at this stage.
  8. Distinguish the future marker -zo- from the present-tense marker in connected speech through listening discrimination practice.
  9. Read a simple weekly plan, a short social message, and a weather description written in standard isiZulu.
  10. Write a short paragraph describing a leisure plan, including a weather reference, a future-tense statement, and at least one invitation or response.

Communication Scenario

It is Friday afternoon in Durban. Your isiZulu study group is planning the weekend. Sipho wants to go to the beach on Saturday morning. Zinhle is not sure the weather will be good. Lindiwe suggests a different activity at the community sports centre if it rains. The group needs to agree on a plan, decide how they will get there, and send a short message to Nomvula, who is not with them, explaining what they will do and inviting her to join. Everything must happen in isiZulu.

  1. In English, list three things you would need to say to plan a weekend outing with friends. Then predict which isiZulu patterns from earlier chapters might appear in those statements.
  2. Think about how weather affects plans in daily life. What words or phrases do you already know in isiZulu that could help you discuss conditions such as rain, sunshine, or cold?

Pronunciation and Tone

The future marker -zo- in connected speech

The positive future marker -zo- is inserted between the subject concord and the verb root. In slow or careful speech, the -zo- slot is distinct and easy to hear. In natural connected speech, it shortens and blends with surrounding syllables. Compare the following:

Pronunciation Focus — Hearing and producing -zo- in natural speech

Listen carefully to the contrast between a present-tense form and its future-tense equivalent. The future adds exactly one syllable, -zo-, which carries a relatively level or falling tone before the raised penultimate syllable of the phrase:

  1. Ngiyahamba. — I am going. (present; four syllables: ngi-ya-ha-mba)
  2. Ngizohamba. — I will go. (future; four syllables: ngi-zo-ha-mba)

Notice that in the present form, -ya- appears before the root; in the future form, -zo- replaces -ya-. The two syllables occupy the same slot. When listening, focus on whether you hear -ya- or -zo- in that second position within the verb word. With practice you will distinguish them reliably.

When producing future forms, say the full word at a steady syllable-timed pace without pausing at the slot boundary: ngi-zo-ha-mba, not ngi-zo ... hamba. The verb word is written and spoken as a single unit.

Weather expressions and impersonal tone contours

Weather expressions in isiZulu often use a subjectless or impersonal construction: kushisa, kuyabanda, kuyanetha. These begin with the locative-like element ku-, which carries a relatively level initial tone. The penultimate syllable of the expression then receives lengthening at a phrase boundary. Practice saying ku-ya-ne-tha with equal syllable timing and a slight stretch on -ne- before the final -tha. Do not stress the first syllable as you might in English.

Invitation intonation

Invitations in isiZulu tend to carry a rising or level phrase-final contour when the speaker expects a positive response or is genuinely open to refusal. A falling contour at phrase end can sound more pressured or expectant. Practise the formulaic invitation phrase Ake sihambe ebhishini! first with a clearly rising end, suggesting warm openness, then with a level end, suggesting a confident group decision. Both are natural; the speaker's relationship to the listener and the context determine which is more appropriate.

Vowel length in class 5 weather phrases

The class 5 adjective concord eli-, as in izulu elihle (good weather), contains three syllables: e-li-hle. The vowel in the first syllable e is short and unstressed. Do not lengthen it or reduce it to a schwa as in English. Keep all three syllables even: e-li-hle. A common learner error is to contract this to two syllables or to lengthen the first vowel. Use a qualified speaker or verified audio to confirm your production of this concord before treating your model as reliable.

Grammar Instruction

12.1 The positive future tense with -zo-

Purpose: state what someone will do or what will happen using the positive future marker -zo- in everyday planning and leisure contexts.

IsiZulu expresses future meaning by inserting the marker -zo- between the subject concord and the verb root. The -ya- present-tense marker does not appear in the positive future. The structure is clean and direct, and it parallels the verb-template slot order you already know.

Language Building Block — Positive future with -zo-
Subject concord + -zo- + verb root + -a
The future marker -zo- occupies the tense/aspect/mood slot. The present -ya- is absent. The final vowel -a remains. The whole structure is written as one conjunctive verb word. No subject noun is required when the subject is a pronoun or has just been stated.
Ngizohamba.  |  Uzodlala.  |  Sizobhukuda.  |  Bazofika.
I will go.  |  You / He / She will play.  |  We will swim.  |  They will arrive.
Person Subject concord Future form English
1sg (I)ngi-ngizo-I will [verb]
2sg (you)u-uzo-You will [verb]
3sg (he/she)u-uzo-He / She will [verb]
1pl (we)si-sizo-We will [verb]
2pl (you all)ni-nizo-You all will [verb]
3pl (they)ba-bazo-They will [verb]

For nonhuman noun-class subjects, the future marker -zo- is added to the appropriate subject concord. At this stage, focus on personal subject concords (first, second, third person singular and plural) and the class 5 concord li- for weather expressions such as ilanga lizophuma (the sun will come out). Other noun-class future forms are receptive at this stage.

  • Ngizohamba ebhishini ngoMgqibelo. I will go to the beach on Saturday.
  • USipho uzodlala ibhola namuhla ntambama. Sipho will play football this afternoon.
  • Sizobhukuda uma ilanga liyaphuma. We will swim if the sun comes out.
  • Bazofika esiteshini ngo-9. They will arrive at the station at 9.
  • Ilanga lizophuma kusasa ekuseni. The sun will come out tomorrow morning.

12.2 Basic ability and inability: ngikwazi and angikwazi

Purpose: state what you are able or unable to do using the productive formulaic frames ngikwazi uku- and angikwazi uku-.

IsiZulu expresses ability using the verb -kwazi, which derives from the root -azi (to know) with an object concord. For the purposes of this chapter, treat ngikwazi (I am able to; I know how to) and angikwazi (I am not able to; I do not know how to) as productive formulaic frames. They are followed by the class 15 infinitive with uku-, which you already know from Chapter 7 and Chapter 11.

Language Building Block — Ability frames
Ngikwazi + uku- + verb root + -a (I can / I am able to)
Angikwazi replaces ngikwazi for the negative: I cannot / I am not able to. The infinitive with uku- follows directly. These two frames are productive at this stage. Other person forms (ukwazi, sikwazi, etc.) are receptive and for recognition only.
Ngikwazi ukubhukuda.  |  Angikwazi ukugijima.
I can swim. / I know how to swim.  |  I cannot run. / I do not know how to run.
  • Ngikwazi ukucula kahle. I can sing well.
  • Angikwazi ukubhukuda. I cannot swim.
  • Ngikwazi ukupheka ukudla kwesintu. I can cook traditional food.
  • Angikwazi ukufika namuhla. I cannot come today.

Purpose: invite someone to join a leisure activity and accept or decline politely using established frames and new formulaic expressions.

12.4 Weather descriptions using copulative predicates

Purpose: describe current and expected weather conditions using impersonal weather expressions and the class 5 adjective pattern for weather quality.

IsiZulu describes weather in two main ways at this stage. The first is through impersonal expressions using the prefix ku-, which behaves like a locative subject and does not require a noun: kushisa (it is hot), kuyabanda (it is cold), kuyanetha (it is raining). These are among the most frequent weather expressions and should be learned as productive wholes.

The second pattern uses the class 5 noun izulu (weather) with a class 5 adjectival concord. Class 5 adjective concords use the prefix eli- for predicate adjectives following the copular structure. The forms izulu elihle (good weather) and izulu elibi (bad weather) are productive at this stage.

Grammar Note — Class 5 adjectival concord eli- with izulu

The noun izulu belongs to class 5 (singular), which uses the subject concord li- and the adjectival concord eli-. You saw the class 5 subject concord li- in Chapter 11 with ibhasi (also class 5): ibhasi lisesiteshini. The same concord system applies to izulu.

  • Izulu elihle namuhla. — The weather is fine today.
  • Izulu elibi kusasa. — The weather will be bad tomorrow. (receptive future inference from context)
  • Ilanga liyashisa. — The sun is hot / It is sunny. (class 5 subject concord li- with present -ya-)

You do not need to control the full class 5 concord paradigm productively at this stage. Focus on using izulu elihle, izulu elibi, and ilanga liyaphuma / liyashisa as productive units, and on recognizing other class 5 forms in listening and reading input.

  • Kuyanetha namuhla, ngakho-ke angizuhamba ebhishini. It is raining today, so I will not go to the beach. (receptive negative future; focus on the weather clause)
  • Kushisa kakhulu! Ake sihambe ebhishini. It is very hot! Let us go to the beach.
  • Izulu elihle kusasa. Sizobhukuda. The weather will be fine tomorrow. We will swim.
  • Ilanga liyaphuma. Ngizogijima enkundleni. The sun is coming out. I will run at the sports centre.

Language Focus

This chapter introduces the positive future marker -zo- as the primary new productive grammar system. Everything else, including ability frames, invitation formulae, and weather expressions, builds directly on systems already active from Chapters 5 through 11. The future marker sits in the same slot as the present -ya- marker, so learners who have internalized the verb template can add -zo- without disrupting their existing knowledge. The key productive targets for this chapter are: future forms for all six personal subject concords, the ability frames ngikwazi and angikwazi with an infinitive, the weather formulae kushisa, kuyabanda, and kuyanetha, and the invitation and response expressions in the vocabulary table above. Conditional structures such as uma kuyanetha are formulaic and receptive at this stage; do not require learners to construct original conditional clauses independently.

Model Dialogue

Isimo: UZinhle noSipho bakhuluma ngoMgqibelo. BahlangeneThekwini (eThekwini). Isimo sezulu asisobala namuhla, kodwa uSipho ufuna ukuhamba ebhishini ngoMgqibelo. UZinhle unemibuzo mayelana nezinhlelo. Ubudlelwane: abangane abaqhelile, inkulumo engenasimilo esijwayelekile.
Setting: Zinhle and Sipho are talking about Saturday. They are together in Durban. The weather is not clear today but Sipho wants to go to the beach on Saturday. Zinhle has questions about the plans. Relationship: close friends, informal register.

USIPHO Sawubona Zinhle! Uyavuma ukuhamba nami ebhishini ngoMgqibelo? Hello Zinhle! Do you agree to go with me to the beach on Saturday?
UZINHLE Sawubona Sipho! Mhlawumbe. Izulu linjani ngoMgqibelo? Namuhla kuyabanda. Hello Sipho! Maybe. What will the weather be like on Saturday? Today it is cold.
USIPHO Ngicabanga ukuthi izulu elihle ngoMgqibelo. Ilanga lizophuma ekuseni. Kuzoshisa! I think the weather will be fine on Saturday. The sun will come out in the morning. It will be hot!
UZINHLE Kulungile. Uma kushisa, ngizovuma. Uma kuyanetha, angizuya. Ngikwazi ukubhukuda, kodwa angikwazi ukugijima ebhishini elishayo! All right. If it is hot, I will agree. If it rains, I will not go. I can swim, but I cannot run on a burning beach!
USIPHO Kulungile! Sizohamba ngoMgqibelo ngo-8 ekuseni. ULindiwe naye uzoza nathi. All right! We will go on Saturday at 8 in the morning. Lindiwe will also come with us.
UZINHLE Sihamba kanjani? Ngethekisi noma ngamabhasi? How will we travel? By taxi or by bus?
USIPHO Ngethekisi. Ithekisi liyeza ngo-7:45 esiteshini. Sizolinda khona. Ake sihambe! By taxi. The taxi comes at 7:45 at the station. We will wait there. Let us go!
UZINHLE Ngizojabula kakhulu! Ngizothumela umlayezo kuNomvula naye. I will be very happy! I will also send a message to Nomvula.

Several expressions in this dialogue are receptive at this stage: mhlawumbe (maybe), ngicabanga ukuthi (I think that), kulungile (all right; previously introduced formulaically), naye (also; as well), and ngizothumela umlayezo (I will send a message). These are provided as natural input. Focus your productive study on the future forms, the ability frames, the weather expressions, and the invitation and acceptance patterns identified in the Grammar section.

Writing Workshop

In this chapter you will write a short leisure-plan message to a friend. Follow the full planning, drafting, and revision sequence below.

  1. Choose a leisure activity and a day. Decide on one leisure activity from this chapter's vocabulary and a specific day or time. Your plan must involve at least one other person and must include a weather-dependent element, meaning the plan changes slightly depending on the weather.
  2. Describe the weather today and your expectation for your planned day. Use at least one impersonal weather expression (kushisa, kuyabanda, or kuyanetha) and one class 5 weather phrase (izulu elihle or ilanga liyaphuma).
  3. State your future plan. Write at least three sentences using the positive future marker -zo-. At least one sentence should name a location using a locative form from Chapter 11.
  4. State what you can or cannot do. Include one sentence using ngikwazi uku- or angikwazi uku- that is relevant to your leisure activity.
  5. Invite your friend. Include one invitation expression, either a question or a ngicela frame, and one alternative plan using a receptive conditional phrase (uma kuyanetha or uma kushisa) that you can treat as a memorized unit.
  6. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist before writing your final version.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • All future-tense forms use -zo- in the correct slot, with no -ya- present-tense marker appearing in the same verb word.
  • Ability frames use ngikwazi uku- or angikwazi uku- followed correctly by the class 15 infinitive.
  • Weather expressions are spelled as complete single words without internal spaces or hyphens.
  • The class 5 adjective concord eli- is spelled correctly in izulu elihle or izulu elibi.
  • Locative forms from Chapter 11 are spelled correctly using the e-…-ini pattern where appropriate.
  • Any invitation expression is followed by a clear, grammatically consistent response or alternative plan.
  • Each sentence begins with a capital letter and ends with appropriate punctuation.
  • No analytical hyphens appear inside ordinary verb words.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: future-form drill

Your partner reads a present-tense sentence. You convert it into a future-tense sentence with -zo- and say it aloud. Your partner checks it against the verb table in section 12.1. Swap roles after six turns. Use a variety of subject concords across rounds so that all six personal forms receive practice.

Example: Partner says "Ngiyahamba ebhishini." You say: Ngizohamba ebhishini.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: plan the weekend

Partner A wants to do a leisure activity this weekend but needs to check the weather and invite Partner B. Partner A must mention a weather condition, state a future plan, express an ability or inability related to the activity, and give an alternative plan in case the weather changes. Partner B listens and responds: accepts, declines with a reason using angikwazi, or proposes a different day. Both partners use only isiZulu throughout the exchange.

Useful expressions during the task: Ngiyaqonda (I understand)  |  Ngizocabanga. (I will think about it.)  |  Kulungile, ngizoza. (All right, I will come.)

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself describing your ideal weekend in isiZulu, aiming for sixty to ninety seconds. Include: the current or expected weather, at least two leisure activities using future forms, one ability or inability statement, and one invitation to an imagined listener. Listen back and check that future forms use -zo- without a -ya- marker, and that weather expressions are spoken as complete, unbroken words.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Leisure and community in South African everyday life

Leisure activities in South African communities vary significantly by urban or rural setting, region, generational group, and economic context. In coastal cities such as Durban and Cape Town, beach culture is highly visible and the beach is a shared public space used by diverse communities for swimming, socializing, and sport. In Johannesburg and other inland cities, public parks, sports centres, community halls, and shopping centres are more commonly used leisure spaces. Avoid assuming that any single activity is universally accessible. Cost, transport, and local infrastructure all affect what kinds of leisure are realistic for different families and communities.

Football (ibhola, sometimes ibhola lezinyawo) is among the most widely enjoyed sports across many communities, played both formally and informally in open spaces. Music and church activities also occupy significant time in many households and communities, and the verb ukucula (to sing) carries weight in both religious and social entertainment contexts. In conversations about weekend plans, it is natural to reference church attendance (ukuyachurch, urban code-switching; or ukuya esontweni, standard isiZulu) on Sunday mornings as part of a person's schedule.

Culture in Context — Invitations and group planning

In many isiZulu-speaking social contexts, invitations to group activities carry an expectation of warmth and genuine interest rather than a transactional exchange. If someone declines your invitation, a brief expression of regret on your part before moving on is appropriate. Offering an alternative plan for another time (Mhlawumbe ngesonto elizayo? — Maybe next week?) signals continued goodwill without pressure. Accepting an invitation with Ngizojabula kakhulu (I will be very happy) rather than a bare yebo (yes) adds warmth that is socially valued in many informal contexts.

Group planning often involves coordination through mobile messaging, and learners should expect that the patterns in this chapter, including future statements, weather references, and arrival times, appear frequently in everyday isiZulu text messages among friends and family members.

Regional Note — Weather and coastal versus inland settings

Weather references in isiZulu conversations naturally reflect the speaker's location. In Durban and the KwaZulu-Natal coast, heat and humidity are the dominant concerns, and comments about ilanga (the sun) and ubushisa (heat) appear frequently in everyday speech. In Johannesburg and the Highveld, afternoon thunderstorms in summer and dry cold in winter shape how people discuss weather. The expression kuyanetha (it is raining) may signal a brief summer shower in one context and heavy seasonal rain in another. When reading or listening to weather discussions in isiZulu, consider the speaker's likely location as part of interpreting meaning.

Culture in Context — Sending a message and digital communication

Written isiZulu in digital messaging tends to be shorter and more casual than formal standard isiZulu, with frequent abbreviations, code-switching with English, and omitted subject nouns where context is clear. The writing tasks in this chapter model more complete, standard isiZulu sentences appropriate for a learner at this level. In authentic South African digital messaging contexts, you may encounter forms that differ from the standard you are producing. Both have their place: standard isiZulu is the appropriate target for learning, and digital variation is something to recognize as you develop reading fluency over time.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using -ya- with -zo- in the same verb word

Error: Writing *Ngiyazohamba instead of Ngizohamba, by combining the present-tense marker -ya- with the future marker -zo-.

Correction: The present marker -ya- and the future marker -zo- occupy the same slot in the verb template. They cannot appear together in the same verb word. When using the future, write only the subject concord, then -zo-, then the verb root and final vowel: Ngizohamba. Think of -zo- as replacing -ya-, not adding to it.

Common Mistake — Separating the future marker from the verb word

Error: Writing *Ngi zo hamba with spaces between morpheme positions, as if zo were a separate word.

Correction: IsiZulu orthography writes the full verb word as a single unbroken unit. All morpheme positions, including subject concord, tense/aspect/mood marker, and verb root, are fused into one conjunctive word. The correct spelling is always Ngizohamba without spaces or hyphens between the parts. The same principle applies to all verb forms you have already learned: ngiyahamba, not *ngi ya hamba.

Common Mistake — Omitting uku- after ngikwazi or angikwazi

Error: Writing *Ngikwazi bhukuda instead of Ngikwazi ukubhukuda, by omitting the infinitive prefix uku-.

Correction: The ability frames ngikwazi and angikwazi must be followed by the full class 15 infinitive with uku-. A bare verb root cannot follow these frames directly. Connect the pattern to what you already know from musa ukuhamba (Chapter 11) and other infinitive constructions: the pattern after an ability or prohibitive frame is always uku- plus the root plus -a. Write Ngikwazi ukubhukuda, not *Ngikwazi bhukuda.

Common Mistake — Using the wrong class 5 adjectival concord with izulu

Error: Writing *izulu elikhulu with the class 7 adjectival concord esi- instead of the class 5 concord eli-, producing forms such as *izulu esihle.

Correction: The noun izulu belongs to class 5 (singular), not class 7. Class 5 nouns use the adjectival concord eli-. The correct forms are izulu elihle (good weather) and izulu elibi (bad weather). Class 7 nouns such as isitolo use esi-, as in isitolo esikhulu (the big shop). Do not transfer the class 7 concord to izulu simply because both nouns begin with the vowel i. The class prefix is the key: iz- signals class 5, not class 7.

Common Mistake — Treating weather expressions as needing a noun subject

Error: Adding an unnecessary noun subject to impersonal weather expressions, such as writing *isimo sezulu kuyanetha or *izulu kuyanetha instead of simply Kuyanetha.

Correction: The weather expressions kushisa, kuyabanda, and kuyanetha are impersonal: the prefix ku- functions like a dummy subject, and no noun is required. These expressions stand alone as complete utterances. If you want to specify what the weather is doing, use the noun-subject pattern instead: Imvula iyanetha (the rain is falling) or Ilanga liyashisa (the sun is hot). Do not mix the impersonal and the noun-subject patterns in the same clause.

Practice — Weather and future plans

  1. Namuhla kushisa. Sizobhukuda. (Today it is hot. We will swim.)
  2. Uma kuyanetha, ngizohamba enkundleni yezemidlalo. (If it rains, I will go to the sports centre.)
  3. Izulu elibi. Ngeke sihambe ebhishini. (The weather is bad. We will not go to the beach.) — Note: ngeke (will not) is provided as a receptive negative future at this stage; accept the sentence with a note that negative future forms are developed later.
  4. Ilanga liyaphuma. Ake sihambe! (The sun is coming out. Let us go!)
  5. Kuyabanda namuhla, kodwa kusasa kuzoshisa. (It is cold today, but tomorrow it will be hot.) — Receptive; accept with a note that the full impersonal future form kuzoshisa previews patterns developed in Chapter 13.

Dialogue — Comprehension

  1. USipho ufuna ukuya ebhishini ngoMgqibelo. (Sipho wants to go to the beach on Saturday.)
  2. Namuhla kuyabanda. (Today it is cold.)
  3. Cha, uma kuyanetha, angizuya. (No, if it rains, she will not go.) — Note: angizuya is a negative future form; treat it as receptive and accept a reasonable English paraphrase.
  4. Bazohamba ngoMgqibelo ngo-8 ekuseni. ULindiwe naye uzoza. (They will go on Saturday at 8 in the morning. Lindiwe will also come.)
  5. Bazohamba ngethekisi. (They will travel by taxi.)

Ungubani? Uvelaphi?

Identity, Origin, and Languages · Speak and Write Zulu: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Ask and state where someone comes from and where they live.
  2. Name languages you speak, study, or understand.
  3. Ask and answer common personal-information questions about name, origin, residence, and study or work.
  4. Use first- and second-person singular and plural subject concords with regular verbs in the present tense.
  5. Distinguish and use the present long form (with -ya-) and the present short form (without -ya-) in appropriate clause environments.
  6. Provide a telephone number or email address in isiZulu.
  7. Introduce another person using third-person reference.
  8. Recognize the lateral click x and begin to distinguish it from the dental click c.
  9. Write simple personal-information sentences using standard conjunctive isiZulu orthography.
  10. Complete a short personal profile form and write a brief written introduction.

Communication Scenario

You are attending an introductory session at a community language centre in Durban. The facilitator asks everyone to introduce themselves to the person sitting beside them: their name, where they are from, where they live now, which languages they speak, and why they are learning isiZulu. Your partner is Nomsa, who grew up in Pietermaritzburg but now works in Johannesburg. She asks you the same questions in return. You want to respond accurately, ask follow-up questions, and then report a few things about Nomsa to the group. This chapter gives you the language to do exactly that.

  1. Before you open the Vocabulary section, write down in English what you would want to say about yourself in such an introduction. Include your name, where you are from, where you live, two or three languages you know, and what you do. Keep this list, because you will use it in the Writing Workshop.
  2. In English, think about how questions like "Where are you from?" and "Where do you live?" are structured. Are they the same question in your language? Make a prediction about how isiZulu might handle them differently, and then check your prediction against the Grammar section.

Pronunciation and Orthography

The lateral click: x

In Chapter 2 you learned that c represents the dental click, produced by placing the tip of the tongue against the upper teeth and releasing a suction-like sound. This chapter introduces the second click type: x, the lateral click.

To produce the lateral click, the tongue blade touches the upper palate while the sides of the tongue create the suction. The release happens at the sides of the mouth, producing a sound rather like a sideways pop. English speakers sometimes associate it with a "clicking" sound used when urging a horse, though the specific placement and release in isiZulu must be trained through listening and careful practice.

Pronunciation Focus — Producing the lateral click x

Step 1. Place the tip of your tongue lightly at the ridge behind your upper teeth, as if you were about to say the English "l."

Step 2. Press the sides of your tongue gently against your upper back molars to create a sealed chamber in your mouth.

Step 3. Lower the back of your tongue to create suction in that sealed space.

Step 4. Release the suction by dropping one or both sides of the tongue, allowing air to rush in from the side.

The result should be a crisp lateral pop. Practice moving from the dental click c to the lateral click x and back, since distinguishing these two sounds is essential for clear communication in isiZulu. Your instructor or audio model will demonstrate the contrast.

Caution: Do not substitute a hissing "ks" sound or an English "x" for the lateral click. The letters x and c are click letters in isiZulu and must not be read with their English sound values.

Aspiration awareness: aspirated stops

This chapter introduces awareness of aspirated consonant contrasts. In isiZulu, the sounds written as ph, th, and kh are aspirated stops, produced with a noticeable puff of air. They are distinct sounds from the plain stops p, t, and k.

English speakers must be careful here: English does not use aspiration to distinguish word meanings, but isiZulu does. The difference between a plain stop and an aspirated stop in isiZulu can change the meaning of a word entirely. Begin developing awareness of this contrast by listening to model speech carefully.

Pronunciation Focus — Aspiration awareness

Hold your hand in front of your mouth. When you say the English word "pot," you feel a noticeable puff of air on your hand because the p is aspirated in this position in English. When you say "spot," the puff is absent or much smaller.

In isiZulu, this difference is meaningful. The letters ph, th, and kh always have that puff of air. The letters p, t, and k do not. At this stage, your goal is to notice the contrast and begin imitating it. The full productive contrast will be reinforced in later chapters.

Vowels in longer verb words

The present-tense verb forms introduced in this chapter contain several syllables. Practice keeping all vowels full and clear, especially the vowels in subject concords and the final vowel -a. Do not reduce any vowel to a weak, indistinct sound, as you might in English in unstressed syllables.

For example, in the form ngiyafunda (I am studying), each syllable ngi-ya-fun-da contains a full vowel. Pronounce each one clearly, and give the penultimate syllable fun a slight lengthening when it is phrase-final: ngiyafun–da.

Grammar Instruction

3.1 Personal subject concords

Purpose: attach the correct subject prefix to a verb to express who is performing the action.

In isiZulu, the subject of a verb is normally expressed inside the verb word itself, through a prefix called the subject concord. An independent pronoun is not required before every verb. The subject concord is the essential signal of who is doing something.

Person Subject concord Independent pronoun (for emphasis) Example verb: -funda- (study) English
1st singular (I) ngi- mina ngifunda I study / I am studying
2nd singular (you) u- wena ufunda you study / you are studying
3rd singular: class 1/1a (he, she) u- yena ufunda he/she studies / is studying
1st plural (we) si- thina sifunda we study / we are studying
2nd plural (you all) ni- nina nifunda you all study / you are studying
3rd plural: class 2/2a (they) ba- bona bafunda they study / they are studying

Note on 2nd singular and 3rd singular: Both use u- as a subject concord. Context usually makes clear which person is meant. When speaking face to face, addressing someone directly (ufunda = you study) is distinct from speaking about someone (ufunda = he/she studies). The independent pronouns wena and yena can be added for emphasis or contrast, but are not required in ordinary statements.

Grammar Note — Independent pronouns are used for emphasis, not by default

English requires a subject pronoun before almost every verb: "I study," "you study," "she studies." In isiZulu, the subject concord inside the verb already expresses who is doing the action. Adding the independent pronoun is optional and signals emphasis or contrast:

  • Ngifunda isiZulu. — I am studying isiZulu. (ordinary statement)
  • Mina ngifunda isiZulu. — I am studying isiZulu. (emphasizing that it is me, not someone else)

Do not add the independent pronoun to every sentence simply because English would require a pronoun there. This is one of the most important adjustments for English speakers learning isiZulu.

Practice — Subject concords

Write the correct present-tense verb form for each prompt. Use the short form (without -ya-) because an object or complement follows in each case.

  1. We / speak (root: -khulum-)
  2. I / come from (root: -vel-)
  3. You (singular) / live (root: -hlal-)
  4. They / study (root: -fund-)
  5. You (plural) / work (root: -sebenz-)
  6. He / know (root: -azi-) — [Note: this root begins with a vowel; the subject concord combines: u + azi → wazi. Learn this form as an irregular.]

3.2 The present positive verb: long and short forms

Purpose: understand when to use the long form with -ya- and when to use the short form without it.

IsiZulu has two present positive forms. They convey the same basic meaning, but they are not freely interchangeable. Their distribution depends on what follows the verb in the clause.

Long form: subject concord + -ya- + verb root + final vowel -a
ngi + ya + fund + a = ngiyafunda
Used when the verb ends the clause, with no noun, pronoun, adverb, or complement directly following it
Ngiyafunda.  |  Uyahlala.  |  Siyakhuluma.
I am studying.  |  He/she is living (there).  |  We are speaking.
Short form: subject concord + verb root + final vowel -a
ngi + fund + a = ngifunda
Used when a noun, object, adverb, or complement follows the verb in the same clause
Ngifunda isiZulu.  |  Uhlala eThekwini.  |  Sikhuluma isiNgisi.
I study isiZulu.  |  He/she lives in Durban.  |  We speak English.

The practical guide for beginners: if something follows the verb (a place name, a language name, an object), use the short form. If the verb stands alone at the end of the clause, use the long form.

Grammar Note — -ya- does not mean "is -ing"

English learners sometimes assume that the long form with -ya- translates as the English progressive ("is studying") while the short form translates as the simple present ("studies"). This is not correct. Both isiZulu forms can be translated with either English present meaning, depending on context. The choice between long and short form in isiZulu is determined by clause structure, not by whether the action is ongoing or habitual. Do not use -ya- simply to show that something is happening right now.

  • Ngiyafunda. ngi-ya-fund-a  |  long form: verb ends the clause I am studying. / I study.
  • Ngifunda isiZulu. ngi-fund-a + complement  |  short form: language name follows I study isiZulu.
  • Uyakhuluma. u-ya-khulum-a  |  long form: verb ends the clause He/she is speaking. / He/she speaks.
  • Ukhuluma isiNgisi. u-khulum-a + complement  |  short form: language name follows He/she speaks English.
  • Sihlala eGoli. si-hlal-a + locative  |  short form: place follows We live in Johannesburg.
  • Niyasebenza. ni-ya-sebenz-a  |  long form: verb ends the clause You all are working. / You all work.

Practice — Long form or short form?

Decide whether each sentence requires the long form or the short form. Write the complete verb word.

  2. She speaks isiZulu. (language follows)
  3. We work. (nothing follows)

  5. You (singular) study. (nothing follows)

3.3 Asking and answering personal-information questions

Purpose: ask and answer questions about name, origin, residence, languages, and work or study.

The following question-answer pairs are the core communication patterns of this chapter. Each question uses a personal subject concord with a verb root, often followed by a question word.

QuestionLiteral structureNatural EnglishExample answer
Ungubani? You-are-who? What is your name? / Who are you? Igama lami ngu-Sipho. My name is Sipho.
Uvelaphi? You-come-from-where? Where do you come from? Ngivela eThekwini. I come from Durban.
Uhlala phi? You-live where? Where do you live? Ngihlala eGoli. I live in Johannesburg.
Ukhuluma ziphi izilimi? You-speak which languages? Which languages do you speak? Ngikhuluma isiZulu nesiNgisi. I speak isiZulu and English.
Wenzani? You-do-what? What do you do? Ngifunda ezikoleni. I study at school. / Ngisebenza ehhovisi. I work at an office.

Note on Ungubani?: This question uses the copulative structure introduced in Chapter 2 (ngu- + name). Ungubani? literally means "You-are-who?" and is the standard way to ask someone's name. The answer Igama lami ngu-Sipho (My name is Sipho) uses the same copulative pattern. Practice this as a complete, formulaic expression.

3.4 Introducing another person

Purpose: refer to a third person using the class 1 subject concord u- and report their personal information.

To introduce or talk about someone else, use the third-person singular subject concord u- (same form as second singular). When a name follows, it becomes clear you are talking about that person, not addressing them.

  • Nguye uSipho. Uvela eThekwini. This is Sipho. (copulative)  |  He comes from Durban. (u- + -vel- + -a + locative) This is Sipho. He comes from Durban.
  • UNomsa uhlala eGoli manje. Nomsa + she-lives + in Johannesburg + now Nomsa lives in Johannesburg now.
  • Ukhuluma isiZulu nesiNgisi. she-speaks + isiZulu + and + isiNgisi She speaks isiZulu and English.
  • Usebenza eGauteng. he-works + in Gauteng He works in Gauteng.
Language Focus

The verb word in isiZulu is a complete grammatical unit. The subject concord, any tense-aspect markers, the verb root, and the final vowel are all written together as one word, without spaces or hyphens in ordinary text. Writing ngi funda with a space, or ngi-funda with a hyphen, is an error. Analytical segmentation like ngi-ya-fund-a is only used in grammar explanations, never in normal writing or messages.

The long-short distinction is one of the most important verb contrasts in isiZulu for beginning learners. The choice is not optional. If you use the long form (ngiyafunda) but then add a language name, the result is ungrammatical. If you use the short form (ngifunda) with nothing following, the result is also non-standard for most contexts. Pay careful attention to what follows the verb in every clause you produce.

3.5 Giving a telephone number or email address

Purpose: share contact information in an isiZulu conversation.

Telephone numbers in isiZulu are read digit by digit or in pairs, using the numbers from 0 to 9 that you learned in Chapter 1. The phrase used to introduce a number is:

  • Inombolo yami yocingo ngu-... My telephone number is... (followed by the digits)
  • Ikheli lami le-imeyili ngu-... My email address is... (followed by the address)

When giving a telephone number, read each digit clearly and allow a natural pause between pairs. Practice the groupings common in South African numbers, such as 083 123 4567, by reading: "zero-eight-three, one-two-three, four-five-six-seven."

Model Dialogue

Indawo: Umphakathi wolimi eThekwini, ngemuva kwesifundo sokuqala. UNomsa noRohan baxoxa ngabo. Ubudlelwano: amalungu amasha, axoxa okokuqala.
Setting: A community language centre in Durban, after the first session. Nomsa and Rohan are getting to know each other. Relationship: new classmates, meeting for the first time.

NOMSA Sawubona! Igama lami nguNomsa. Wena ungubani? Hello! My name is Nomsa. And you, who are you? / What is your name?
ROHAN Sawubona, Nomsa! Igama lami nguRohan. Ngiyajabula ukukwazi. Hello, Nomsa! My name is Rohan. I am pleased to meet you. [formulaic expression; the full grammar of ngiyajabula will be developed later]
NOMSA Nami ngiyajabula. Uvelaphi, Rohan? I am also pleased. Where do you come from, Rohan?
ROHAN Ngivela eMgungundlovu, kodwa ngiyahlala eThekwini manje. Wena uvelaphi? I come from Pietermaritzburg, but I live in Durban now. And you, where do you come from?
NOMSA Mina ngivela eMgungundlovu nanjalo! Kodwa ngisebenza eGoli manje. Ngihlala lapho. I also come from Pietermaritzburg! But I work in Johannesburg now. I live there.
ROHAN Uyakhuluma ziphi izilimi? Which languages do you speak? [long form used here because the question phrase follows in the next clause, not directly after the verb; accept short form ukhuluma as equally natural in this position]
NOMSA Ngikhuluma isiZulu, isiNgisi, nesiSotho kancane. Wena ukhuluma ziphi? I speak isiZulu, English, and a little Sesotho. And you, which ones do you speak?
ROHAN Ngikhuluma isiNgisi, isiBhunu, nangifunda isiZulu manje. I speak English, Afrikaans, and I am learning isiZulu now.

(UNomsa uyahleka, uphinde abuze — Nomsa laughs and asks another question.)

NOMSA Kahle kakhulu! Inombolo yakho yocingo iyini? Ngizoqopha. Very good! What is your telephone number? I will write it down. [ngizoqopha = I will write it down; formulaic; the future -zo- is introduced in Chapter 12]
ROHAN Yebo. Inombolo yami yocingo ngu-zero-eight-two, five-five-five, one-two-three-four. Yes. My telephone number is 082 555 1234. [digits read in English or isiZulu depending on speaker preference; both are natural in contemporary South African multilingual contexts]
NOMSA Ngiyabonga, Rohan. Sifunde kahle namuhla! Thank you, Rohan. We studied well today! [Sifunde = we studied/learned; perfect form introduced in Chapter 16; used here as formulaic]
ROHAN Yebo, ngiyavuma! Sisibonani. Yes, I agree! See you. [Sisibonani = See you; formulaic leave-taking from Chapter 1]

Several expressions in this dialogue, including ngizoqopha, sifunde kahle, and ngiyavuma, contain grammar beyond the current chapter. They are included as formulaic recognition items that make the dialogue sound natural. Focus your productive study on the question-answer patterns, the present verb forms, and the origin and residence expressions.

Imibuzo yokuqonda inkulumo — Comprehension questions:

Writing Workshop

In this chapter you will write a short personal profile in isiZulu, similar to the profile of Sibusiso in the Reading section. You will then use it to introduce yourself in the Speaking Practice.

  1. Plan. Use the notes you made in the Communication Scenario: your name, where you are from, where you live, what you do, and which languages you speak. Add your telephone number or email address if you are comfortable doing so. If you prefer privacy, invent a fictional set of details for this exercise.
  2. Model study. Read the profile of USibusiso again. Notice the sentence order: name first, then origin, then current residence, then work or study, then languages, then contact details. Your profile will follow the same order.
  3. Draft. Write four to six sentences in isiZulu about yourself. Include at least: one origin statement using ngivela, one residence or work statement using ngihlala or ngisebenza, one language statement using ngikhuluma, one long-form verb in a clause where the verb ends the thought, and one short-form verb followed by a complement.
  4. Check your verb forms. Go through your draft and circle each verb word. For each one, ask: does anything follow this verb in the same clause? If yes, confirm you used the short form. If no, confirm you used the long form.
  5. Revise. Use the editing checklist below before writing your final version.

Uhlelo lokubheka — Editing checklist:

  • Each verb word is written as one continuous word, with no internal spaces or hyphens.
  • I used the short form when a complement, place name, or language name follows the verb.
  • I used the long form with -ya- when the verb ends the clause with nothing following it.
  • My name is introduced with the copulative pattern: Igama lami ngu—.
  • Origin is expressed with ngivela and a place name.
  • Residence or work is expressed with ngihlala or ngisebenza and a place name.
  • I have written the isiZulu names of languages correctly: isiZulu, isiNgisi, isiBhunu.
  • Each sentence begins with a capital letter and ends with a full stop.
  • I have not inserted analytical hyphens into ordinary text.

Questions: Ungubani? Uvelaphi? Uhlala phi? Ukhuluma ziphi izilimi? Wenzani?

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: interview and report

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Origin, residence, and identity in South Africa

In South Africa, the questions "Where do you come from?" and "Where do you live?" often carry more information than they do in many other contexts. South Africa's history of internal migration, the movement of people between rural and urban areas during and after apartheid, and the continued significance of regional and community identity mean that origin is often understood as distinct from current residence. Many people in Gauteng, for example, identify their "home" as a place of family origin in another province, even if they have lived in Johannesburg for many years.

When someone says ngivela (I come from), they may mean the place where they were born, the place where their family is from, or the place they grew up, depending on context. Similarly, ngihlala (I live) typically refers to current residence. Paying attention to this distinction helps you understand what someone is really telling you about themselves.

Culture in Context — Multilingualism and language identity

South Africa has eleven official languages, and most South Africans speak more than one of them. Asking which languages someone speaks (Ukhuluma ziphi izilimi?) is a common and natural part of introduction in many contexts, and the answer often reflects a person's life history, community, education, and professional experience.

Language, ethnicity, nationality, and citizenship are not interchangeable. A person who speaks isiZulu fluently may not identify as Zulu, and a person who identifies as Zulu may be equally fluent in isiNgisi and isiBhunu. When someone tells you which languages they speak, they are sharing a significant aspect of their life and background. Respond with genuine interest and avoid making assumptions about the person's identity from their language choices alone.

The expression kancane (a little; a small amount) is frequently used when describing a language one knows only partially. Using kancane is a form of honest self-assessment and should not be understood as false modesty. It is also a useful and appropriate thing to say about your own isiZulu when people ask.

Regional Note — Place names and their isiZulu forms

Many South African place names have official forms in more than one language, and the isiZulu locative forms of these names vary. eThekwini (Durban) and eMgungundlovu (Pietermaritzburg) are the isiZulu names for these cities and appear in official usage alongside the English names. eGoli is the widely used informal locative for Johannesburg in isiZulu conversation; the more formal form eJohannesburg also occurs. eGauteng is used for the province of Gauteng.

When you encounter a place name in South African isiZulu communication, you may find multiple forms in use. In this textbook, we use the most broadly recognized forms. Always verify local or community-specific forms when communicating with speakers from a particular area.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using the long form when a complement follows

Error: Writing *Ngiyafunda isiZulu. with the long form, even though the language name isiZulu directly follows the verb.

Correction: When a complement or object follows the verb in the same clause, use the short form: Ngifunda isiZulu. The -ya- marker belongs in the long form, which is used only when the verb ends the clause with nothing following. Check every sentence: if something comes after the verb, drop the -ya-.

Common Mistake — Adding a space inside the verb word

Error: Writing *ngi ya funda or *ngi-ya-funda with spaces or hyphens in ordinary text.

Correction: In standard isiZulu orthography, the verb complex is written as one conjunctive word: ngiyafunda or ngifunda. Analytical hyphens are used only in grammar explanations to show the components, and must never appear in a learner's ordinary writing, messages, or profile texts. Learn to write verb forms as single units from the beginning of your study.

Common Mistake — Reading x as an English sound

Error: Pronouncing the letter x in isiZulu words as "ks," as it is often used in English, or as "z," as it is used in some other languages.

Correction: In isiZulu, x is the lateral click. It is produced by releasing suction at the sides of the tongue, not by combining two consonant sounds. Similarly, c is the dental click, not the English "s" or "k" sound. When you see a click letter in an isiZulu word, prepare for the click articulation, not an English letter value. Practice with a qualified speaker or audio model until these sounds become natural.

Common Mistake — Adding an independent pronoun before every verb

Error: Writing *mina ngifunda or *wena ufunda before every verb, because English requires "I" or "you" before each one.

Correction: The subject concord inside the verb already identifies the person. In ordinary isiZulu statements, the independent pronoun is optional and carries emphasis. It is not required in every sentence. Ngifunda isiZulu is a complete, correct sentence. Adding mina before it is possible but signals emphasis: I am studying isiZulu (not someone else). Use independent pronouns selectively, when contrast or emphasis is genuinely needed.

Common Mistake — Treating isiXhosa and isiZulu as the same language

Error: Assuming that because isiXhosa and isiZulu are related Nguni languages, words, click spellings, or grammatical forms from one can be used freely in the other.

Correction: IsiXhosa and isiZulu are distinct languages with their own grammars, vocabularies, and orthographic standards. They share significant historical and linguistic relationship, but they are not dialects of each other. A click letter in isiXhosa may represent a different sound or a different word than the same letter in isiZulu. When you label a language in your isiZulu practice, always use the correct name: isiXhosa for isiXhosa, and be aware that importing forms from one language into the other creates errors in both.

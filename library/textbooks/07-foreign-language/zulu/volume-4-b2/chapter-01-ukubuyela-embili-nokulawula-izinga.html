Ukubuyela Embili Nokulawula Izinga

Volume IV, Chapter 1: B2 Re-entry and Register Control · Speak and Write Zulu: Advanced Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Reactivate narration, explanation, and discussion skills consolidated in Volume III, applying them confidently in new formal and semi-formal settings.
  2. Distinguish formal and informal register choices in vocabulary, address, and sentence construction, and select the appropriate register for a given setting.
  3. Maintain consistent register within a single turn of speech or a single piece of writing, without slipping between formal and casual forms.
  4. Understand and use vocabulary connected with university, professional, and institutional settings, including modern loanwords adapted to Zulu noun classes.
  5. Read and interpret a short formal notice or announcement, identifying its register and purpose.
  6. Reflect, in isiZulu, on the transition from one life stage to another, using accurate concord, verb template control, and at least one relative clause, carried forward from Volume III.

Communication Scenario

Nomvula, whose family celebration formed the center of Volume III, has now begun her first year at university. She must greet new classmates and lecturers appropriately, read a formal notice posted on a residence noticeboard, write a short message to a lecturer requesting a meeting, and reflect on how her way of speaking must change between a conversation with her roommate and a conversation with a lecturer. Every system she uses this chapter was already active by the end of Volume III. What is new is her growing awareness of when to use each one.

  1. This chapter introduces no new grammar system beyond register itself. It reactivates narration, explanation, discussion, and relative-clause writing at a B2 level, in university and professional contexts.
  2. Learners complete Nomvula's tasks themselves, greeting appropriately in two different settings, reading the notice, writing the message, and producing their own reflection on register.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 20 of Volume III asked learners to integrate narration, explanation, discussion, and practical writing into one connected performance, closing the B2 bridge. Chapter 1 of Volume IV opens with the same integrated systems, but places them inside university, professional, and institutional settings, and asks learners to control register consciously rather than by habit. No system in this chapter is new. What is new is the deliberate attention to formal and informal choices within already familiar grammar.

Coming From Volume III, Chapter 20

Narration with consistent subject concords, explanation with object concords and relative clauses, polite discussion with accurate polarity, and self edited practical writing.

New in This Chapter

No new grammar. Learners consciously distinguish formal and informal register, and apply consolidated systems inside university and professional settings.

Pronunciation and Prosody in Formal Speech

No new sound appears in this chapter. Instead, learners practice maintaining accurate click production, penultimate lengthening, and calm, measured phrase rhythm in formal speech, since a formal register is often carried as much by pacing and intonation as by word choice.

Pronunciation Focus — Slower pacing signals formality

Formal isiZulu speech, such as an introduction to a lecturer or a statement at an institutional gathering, is typically delivered more slowly and evenly than casual conversation, with clear penultimate lengthening on each phrase. Rushing a formal greeting can make it sound casual even when the words themselves are correct.

  • uSolwazi (Professor): practice even syllable timing and a clear vowel in the final syllable, since this title often opens a formal turn.
  • ngiyabonga kakhulu (thank you very much): practice with slightly slower pacing than in casual speech, letting the penultimate syllable of kakhulu lengthen clearly.

Grammar: Reactivating the Volume III System

Purpose: this section does not introduce new grammar. It reviews, side by side, the systems consolidated in Volume III, and shows how the same forms shift in feel, though not in underlying structure, when used in a formal setting.

1.1 Register and the subject concord system

Language Focus

Subject concords do not change between formal and informal registers. What changes is vocabulary choice, sentence length, and the presence of respectful titles before a name. A formal turn tends to use fuller sentences and avoids shortened casual forms, while an informal turn may drop words that context supplies.

  • USolwazi Mkhize ufundisa isifundo esithakazelisayo. Professor Mkhize teaches an interesting course. ufundisa keeps Class 1 subject concord u- for uSolwazi Mkhize, exactly as taught in Volume II and III

1.2 Register and object concord placement

Language Focus

Object concords are placed inside the verb template in exactly the same slot in formal and informal speech. Formal writing, however, tends to avoid repeating a full noun unnecessarily and relies on the object concord more consistently once the noun has already been named, which produces a more polished, professional style.

  • Isifundo lesi ngiyasithanda kakhulu, futhi ngizosifunda ngokucophelela. This course, I like it very much, and I will study it carefully. ngiyasithanda and ngizosifunda both use object concord si- for Class 7 isifundo, avoiding repetition of the noun

1.3 Register and polite requests

Language Focus

A formal request typically opens with a respectful title, states the request clearly using ngicela or ngingathanda, and closes with an expression of thanks. An informal request may use the same underlying subjunctive or potential forms but with simpler phrasing and no title.

  • USolwazi, ngingathanda ukucela isikhathi sokuhlangana nawe ngesonto elizayo. Professor, I would like to request time to meet with you next week. ngingathanda uses the potential mood already consolidated in Volume III, framed here with a respectful title for formal register

1.4 Register and the relative clause

Language Note — The relative clause, reviewed for formal use
relative concord + verb stem, agreeing with the class of the noun described
Formal writing, such as a reflection or a request, often uses a relative clause to add precision without lengthening the sentence unnecessarily, exactly as practiced with isizathu in Volume III, Chapter 20.
Isifundo engisithandayo kunazo zonke enyuvesi yileso esifundisa ulimi.
The course that I like most at university is the one that teaches language. Isifundo engisithandayo uses the Class 7 relative concord esi-, contracted here to form engisithandayo, matching isifundo.

Model Text: Nomvula's First Week at the University

Read Nomvula's short reflection below. It moves between describing a formal setting and describing an informal one, and it shows how her language shifts accordingly, though her underlying grammar remains the same throughout.

Isonto lokuqala likaNomvula enyuvesi (Nomvula's first week at university)

Ngosuku lokuqala, ngahlangana noSolwazi Dlamini ehholo elikhulu, futhi ngamthobela ngokumbingelela ngokusemthethweni, ngithi, "Sawubona Solwazi, ngiyaziqhenya ngokuba yingxenye yesifundo sakho." Ngemva kwesifundo, ngahlangana nabangani bami ekamelweni lokuhlala, futhi lapho khona ukukhuluma kwaba mnene kakhulu, singahleki, sixoxa ngezinto ezimnandi zosuku. Isaziso esasiphostiwe eholo lokuhlala sasisitshela ukuthi kufanele sibhalise izinsuku zokuhlala ngaphambi kwephasika. Kimina, into ebalulekile ukwazi ukuthi nini ngingasebenzisa isikhathi esisemthethweni nokuthi nini ngingakhululeka, ngoba isifundo sami sonke sizoncika kulokho.

The reflection narrates a formal greeting with a respectful title, then an informal exchange with friends, then a formal notice, and closes by naming the register skill itself, exactly as this chapter's tasks require.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. UNomvula wambingelela kanjani uSolwazi Dlamini, futhi kungani kwakubalulekile ukwenza kanjalo? (How did Nomvula greet Professor Dlamini, and why was doing so in that way important?)
  2. Yiziphi izinto ezimbili ezehlukile phakathi kokukhuluma kwakhe noSolwazi nokukhuluma kwakhe nabangani bakhe? (What are two things that differ between her speech with the Professor and her speech with her friends?)
  3. Yini eyayiphakathi kwesaziso esasiphostiwe eholo lokuhlala? (What was inside the notice posted at the residence hall?)

Writing Workshop

Write your own short message to an instructor or supervisor, requesting a meeting, followed by a brief reflection on a moment when you had to shift your own register between a formal and an informal setting.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write a message of three to five sentences requesting a meeting, using a respectful title, ngingathanda or ngicela, and a closing thanks. Then write a reflection of four to six sentences describing one formal and one informal moment from your own recent experience, using at least one relative clause. Apply the Chapter 19 self editing routine from Volume III before sharing your final version.

  1. Draft the message. Open with a respectful title, state your request clearly, and close with thanks.
  2. Draft the formal moment. Describe it using full sentences and at least one relative clause.
  3. Draft the informal moment. Describe it using shorter, more relaxed phrasing, while keeping your grammar accurate.
  4. Name the register skill. Add one closing sentence naming what you noticed about shifting between the two registers.
  5. Audit your draft. Apply the four Chapter 19 repair categories, concord, object concord placement, TAM and polarity, and relative clause accuracy.
  6. Finalize and present. Rewrite any corrected sentences into a clean final version.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • The message opens with an appropriate respectful title.
  • The request uses ngicela or ngingathanda accurately.
  • The formal moment includes at least one accurate relative clause.
  • Object concords are built into the verb template, not placed as separate words.
  • Negative markers and final vowels agree consistently throughout.
  • The two moments show a clear, consistent difference in register.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: reading the model reflection aloud

Read Nomvula's reflection aloud in full, then again identifying, out loud, where each register shift begins.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: register role play

With a partner, take turns presenting the same short piece of news once formally, as if speaking to a lecturer, and once informally, as if speaking to a close friend. Your partner should identify which register you are using and explain how they knew.

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself presenting the same short piece of news twice, once formally and once informally, without reading directly from your notes. Listen back and identify two specific choices, vocabulary, pacing, or title use, that marked each version.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Addressing lecturers and professionals

In many South African academic and professional settings, a title such as uSolwazi or uMnumzane is commonly used before a surname when greeting or addressing a lecturer, supervisor, or senior colleague, particularly at a first meeting. This practice signals respect and is generally expected in formal correspondence and introductions.

Culture in Context — Moving between formal and informal circles in one day

Many isiZulu speakers move fluidly between several registers within a single day, greeting an elder or a lecturer formally in the morning and relaxing into informal speech with friends soon afterward. This flexibility is often treated as an ordinary and valued social skill rather than as inconsistency.

Regional Variation — Notices and institutional writing

Formal notices at universities and workplaces sometimes include English terms alongside isiZulu, particularly for administrative categories such as registration periods or fee deadlines. This mixed practice is common in institutional writing, though the volume of code-switching varies by institution and setting.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Mixing a respectful title with casual phrasing

Error: Opening a message with uSolwazi, then immediately switching to shortened, casual verb forms or informal vocabulary within the same message.

Correction: Once a formal title has opened a message or turn, maintain full verb forms and formal vocabulary throughout that same message or turn.

Common Mistake — Treating register as a vocabulary swap only

Error: Assuming that formal register means only choosing fancier words, while leaving sentence structure, pacing, and title use unchanged.

Correction: Formal register involves vocabulary, sentence fullness, pacing, and the presence of respectful address together, not vocabulary alone.

Common Mistake — Omitting the closing thanks in a formal request

Error: Ending a formal written request immediately after stating what is wanted, without any closing expression of thanks or respect.

Correction: Close a formal request with ngiyabonga or a similar expression of thanks, signaling that the message is complete and respectful.

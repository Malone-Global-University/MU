Volume IV, Chapter 3: Complex Verb Architecture · Speak and Write Zulu: Advanced Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Build a single verb that combines an object concord, an extension, and an accurate final vowel, without losing control of any one slot.
  2. Use the causative extension productively to describe making or causing someone to do something.
  3. Use the passive extension productively to describe an action from the point of view of the person or thing affected by it, rather than the person doing it.
  4. Combine the applicative extension, already developed in Volume III, with a causative or passive extension in a single verb where the meaning requires it.
  5. Explain, in isiZulu, a practical problem and the steps taken to resolve it, using extension combinations accurately.
  6. Apply the Volume III, Chapter 19 self editing routine to a paragraph built around complex verb forms, checking each slot in the verb template in turn.

Communication Scenario

A clash has appeared in Nomvula's class timetable, and she must sort it out with the faculty office before the week is over. Explaining the problem clearly means describing what was done, what caused the clash, who must be asked to fix it, and what was eventually arranged on her behalf. Nomvula cannot rely on simple verbs alone. She needs a single verb that can show, at once, who acted, who was affected, what caused the action, and whether the action is still pending or already complete.

  1. This chapter introduces the causative and passive extensions for full productive use, and asks learners to combine them with the applicative extension already developed in Volume III.
  2. Learners then explain a practical problem of their own, describing its cause and its resolution, using complex verb forms accurately throughout.

Pronunciation and Prosody in Dense Verbs

No new sound appears in this chapter. Instead, learners practice keeping a long, extension heavy verb clear and evenly paced, since stacking an object concord, an applicative, and a causative or passive extension inside one word can tempt a speaker to rush the middle of the verb and blur its internal structure.

Pronunciation Focus — Keeping a long verb clear

When a verb carries several morphemes in sequence, it helps to say the word slowly at first, noticing where the subject concord ends, where the object concord begins, and where the extension attaches to the root, before returning to a natural speaking pace.

  • ukuxazululelwa (to have something resolved on one's behalf): a useful word to say slowly at first, separating the root xazulul, the applicative lel, and the passive final vowel wa.
  • ukucaciswa (to be made clear): worth rehearsing so the causative is, sits clearly before the passive final vowel wa, rather than merging into a single blurred syllable.

Grammar: Complex Verb Architecture

Purpose: this section activates the causative and passive extensions for full productive use, and shows how they combine with the applicative extension, already developed in Volume III, inside a single verb, without disturbing the object concord placement and final vowel control already consolidated.

3.1 The causative extension

Language Focus

The causative extension shows that the subject causes or makes someone else perform an action, rather than performing it directly. In its common productive form, the extension attaches as is or z before the final vowel, and the verb's argument structure changes to include the person made to act.

  • Uthisha wenza abafundi baqonde isisombululo. The teacher makes the students understand the solution. wenza is a periphrastic causative using enza, a common alternative to the morphological causative when the caused action is itself a full clause
  • Umsizi ucacisa isisombululo kubafundi. The helper makes the solution clear to the students. ucacisa uses the morphological causative is on the root cac, meaning to be clear, producing to make clear

3.2 The passive extension

Language Focus

The passive extension shifts the focus of a sentence from the person performing an action to the person or thing affected by it. The extension attaches as w before the final vowel, and the original subject, if mentioned at all, appears after nga, meaning by.

  • Isicelo sami sicaciswa yihhovisi lezifundo. My request is clarified by the faculty office. sicaciswa combines the causative is with the passive final vowel wa, and the agent ihhovisi lezifundo follows the copulative yi rather than a direct subject position

3.3 Combining applicative, causative, and passive extensions

Language Focus

More than one extension may appear on a single root when the meaning genuinely requires it, and each extension keeps its own regular position in the sequence between the root and the final vowel. The applicative, developed in Volume III, typically appears closest to the root, with a causative or passive extension following it toward the final vowel.

  • Isicelo sami saxazululelwa uthisha wami. My request was resolved on my behalf by my teacher. xazululelwa combines the root xazulul, the applicative el for on my behalf, and the passive final vowel wa, with the agent uthisha wami following as the demoted subject

3.4 The full verb template with extension combinations

Language Note — Extension combinations inside the verb template
subject concord + TAM marker + object concord + verb root + applicative + causative or passive + final vowel
Every slot from Volume III remains in place. Extension combinations do not replace the template, they simply fill more of it at once, so accuracy depends on building the verb slot by slot rather than guessing at the whole word.
Ihhovisi lezifundo lasixazululela isicelo ngokushesha.
The faculty office resolved the request for us quickly. lasixazululela uses Class 5 subject concord la, object concord si, root xazulul, and applicative ela, with the beneficiary understood from context.

Model Text: Nomvula Sorts Out a Timetable Problem

Read Nomvula's account of her timetable problem below. It uses the causative, the passive, and a combined applicative and passive form, each accurately built into the verb template.

Uxoxo lukaNomvula (Nomvula's account)

Ishentjuli yami yayinesinqumo esibi, ngoba izifundo ezimbili zangqubuzana ngesikhathi esifanayo. Ngesikhathi ngibona lokhu, ngahamba masinyane ngaya ehhovisi lezifundo ukuze isicelo sami sicaciswe. Umsizi engahlangana naye wangenza ngaqonda kahle ukuthi kungani lolu daba lwenzeka, futhi wangitshela ukuthi izicelo ezinjenge zami zivame ukuxazululwa ngokushesha. Ngemuva kwezinsuku ezimbili, isicelo sami saxazululelwa uthisha wami, futhi ishentjuli yami yaguqulwa ukuze izifundo zingabe zisangqubuzana. Manje sengikwazi ukuya kuzo zombili izifundo ngaphandle kwenkinga.

The account uses ngahamba, wangenza, and wangitshela with consistent Class 1 subject concords, builds sicaciswe as a passive verb of clarification, and closes with saxazululelwa, a combined applicative and passive form, exactly as this chapter's grammar section requires.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Yini eyabangela inkinga kaNomvula neshentjuli yakhe? (What caused Nomvula's timetable problem?)
  2. Wenzenjani umsizi ukuze uNomvula aqonde isimo sakhe? (What did the helper do to make Nomvula understand her situation?)
  3. Yaxazululwa kanjani inkinga kaNomvula ekugcineni? (How was Nomvula's problem resolved in the end?)

Writing Workshop

Write your own account of a practical problem you needed help resolving, such as an administrative mix up, a scheduling clash, or a service request, following the same pattern Nomvula used to explain her timetable clash.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write a paragraph of six to eight sentences. Describe the problem and its cause, describe what someone did to help you understand or resolve it using at least one causative verb, describe how the matter was resolved using at least one passive verb, and close by describing the outcome. Then apply the Chapter 19 self editing routine from Volume III before sharing your final version.

  1. Describe the problem. State what went wrong and why, using vocabulary from this chapter's table.
  2. Draft a causative sentence. Describe what someone did to make you understand the situation or to make something happen.
  3. Draft a passive sentence. Describe an action from the point of view of the person or thing affected, mentioning the agent with nga where useful.
  4. Draft a combined form, if appropriate. If your problem was resolved on your behalf by someone else, try building one verb that combines the applicative and the passive.
  5. Audit your draft. Apply the four Chapter 19 repair categories, concord, object concord placement, TAM and polarity, and relative clause accuracy, then check each extension slot in your complex verbs individually.
  6. Finalize and present. Rewrite any corrected sentences into a clean final version.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • At least one verb uses the causative extension accurately.
  • At least one verb uses the passive extension accurately, with the agent introduced by nga where relevant.
  • Any extension combination follows the correct order, applicative before causative or passive.
  • Object concords are built into the verb template, not placed as separate words.
  • Negative markers and final vowels agree consistently throughout.
  • The paragraph reads smoothly aloud, with clear pacing through each complex verb.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Approaching an office for help

When approaching an administrative office for help, as Nomvula does, it is common to explain the situation calmly and to acknowledge the assistance received afterward, rather than expressing frustration directly to the staff member involved, even when the underlying problem is genuinely frustrating.

Culture in Context — Crediting the person who helped

When describing how a problem was resolved, it is common to name or clearly credit the person who helped, as Nomvula does with uthisha wami, rather than describing the resolution as if it happened on its own, since this reflects a broader value placed on acknowledging assistance.

Regional Variation — Periphrastic versus morphological causatives

Some speakers favor the periphrastic causative with enza, meaning to make or to do, especially when the caused action is a full clause, while others prefer the morphological causative extension wherever it is available. Both approaches are widely used, and the choice often depends on how complex the caused action is.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Placing extensions in the wrong order

Error: Attaching the passive final vowel directly to the root, then adding the applicative afterward, reversing the required order of the two extensions.

Correction: Build the verb from the root outward, attaching the applicative first, then the causative or passive extension, and only then the final vowel.

Common Mistake — Forgetting the agent marker nga in a passive sentence

Error: Mentioning who performed a passive action as if it were a direct subject, without the required nga or an equivalent marker.

Correction: When a passive sentence names the agent, introduce that agent with nga, or with a copulative such as yi, rather than placing it as an ordinary subject.

Common Mistake — Overusing extension combinations where a simple verb is clearer

Error: Stacking an applicative, a causative, and a passive extension onto every verb in a paragraph, even when a simple verb would communicate the same meaning more clearly.

Correction: Reserve extension combinations for sentences where the meaning genuinely requires more than one layer, and use simple verbs for straightforward actions.

Imibiko Nezingxoxo Zabezindaba: Ukuhlaziya Nokuphawula

Volume IV, Chapter 7: Media, Interview, and Commentary · Speak and Write Zulu: Advanced Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Interpret a short broadcast interview, identifying who is speaking, what position each speaker holds, and how the interview is organized.
  2. Report what a speaker said, using wathi and related quotative patterns, without shifting into your own opinion by accident.
  3. Summarize an interview accurately, in your own words, using no more than a few sentences for each speaker's main point.
  4. Distinguish reporting a speaker's words from commenting on them, so that a listener or reader always knows which is which.
  5. Produce a short spoken or written commentary that responds to an interview, agreeing, disagreeing, or partially agreeing with what was said.
  6. Maintain the argumentative discourse markers from Chapter 6, claim, evidence, counterpoint, and conclusion, while commenting on someone else's argument rather than only your own.

Communication Scenario

Word of the study room booking proposal has reached the campus radio station, and the student council chairperson has agreed to a short interview about it. Nomvula was not asked to appear on air, but her supervisor wants a written summary of the interview for the staff newsletter, along with a short commentary noting where the chairperson's position agrees with Nomvula's written argument and where it differs. Nomvula must listen carefully, report the chairperson's words accurately, and keep her own commentary clearly separate from what was actually said on air.

  1. This chapter activates reported speech markers and commentary discourse markers for full productive use inside connected prose.
  2. Learners then listen to or read a short interview, write an accurate summary of it, and add a short commentary responding to the speaker's position.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 6 taught learners to build their own argument from a claim, through evidence and a counterpoint, to a conclusion. Chapter 7 keeps every one of those discourse markers active, since a commentary on someone else's interview still needs a claim, some evidence, an acknowledged counterpoint, and a conclusion. What changes here is the source of the position being discussed. In Chapter 6, the claim belonged to the writer. In Chapter 7, the claim first belongs to someone else, a radio guest, and the learner must report it faithfully before responding to it. Reporting and commenting are treated as two separate, sequential tasks, not as one blended paragraph.

Coming From Chapter 6

Productive control of claim, evidence, counterpoint, and conclusion markers, built into the writer's own argument.

New in This Chapter

Productive reported speech markers such as wathi, together with commentary markers that respond to someone else's reported position.

Looking Forward

Chapter 8 moves from spoken media into professional correspondence, asking learners to write formal reports and messages summarizing exactly this kind of material.

Amagama Ezindaba — Vocabulary for Media and Commentary

Vocabulary: Media, Interview, and Commentary

This chapter introduces the discourse markers that let a learner move cleanly between three tasks, reporting what someone said, summarizing it briefly, and then commenting on it in the learner's own voice, since blurring these three tasks together is one of the most common sources of confusion in media-based writing.

wathi, ngokusho kuka-, umbono, ngokubona kwami, ngiyavumelana no-, and angivumelani ncamashi no- form the working markers for the reporting and commentary tasks in this chapter.

Pronunciation and Pacing in Reported Speech

No new sound appears in this chapter. Instead, learners practice a small shift in pace that signals the boundary between reported speech and commentary, since wathi and ngokusho kuka- should be spoken at a steady, neutral pace that sounds like relaying information, while ngokubona kwami and ngiyavumelana no- can carry a slightly warmer, more personal tone, since they mark the commentator's own voice entering the text.

Pronunciation Focus — Separating the reporting voice from the commentary voice

A brief pause after the reported material, before ngokubona kwami or a similar marker, helps a listener hear that the speaker is stepping out of the role of reporter and into the role of commentator.

  • wathi (he or she said that): worth rehearsing with a level, even pace, since a rising or falling pitch here can make it sound like the reporter's own opinion rather than a faithful report.
  • ngokubona kwami (in my view): worth rehearsing with a clear pause before it, so the shift into personal commentary is unmistakable.

Grammar: Reporting and Commenting on What Others Say

Purpose: this section activates reported speech and commentary discourse markers for full productive use, and shows how these markers work together with the claim, evidence, counterpoint, and conclusion markers already active since Chapter 6, so that a learner can move cleanly from reporting a speaker's words, to summarizing them, to commenting on them.

7.1 Reporting a speaker's words with wathi

Language Focus

wathi introduces reported speech, followed either by a direct quotation or by a complement clause with ukuthi. The reporter's task here is accuracy, not evaluation, so the sentence should stay close to what the speaker actually said, without adding the reporter's own view.

  • Umongameli wesigungu sabafundi wathi uhlelo lokubhukha luzokwenza izinto zibe lula kubafundi abaningi. The student council chairperson said that the booking system will make things easier for many students. wathi introduces the complement clause carrying the reported position, without any comment from the reporter

7.2 Attributing a position with ngokusho kuka-

Language Focus

ngokusho kuka- attributes a stated position directly to a named speaker, and is especially useful at the start of a summary, since it identifies the source of the view before the view itself is reported.

  • Ngokusho kukamongameli wesigungu sabafundi, uhlelo lolu luzosiza kakhulu labo abahlela kusengaphambili. According to the student council chairperson, this system will help a great deal those who plan in advance. ngokusho kuka- attributes the claim to a named source before the claim is stated

7.3 Naming the speaker's view with umbono

Language Focus

umbono names the position itself as a noun, useful when a summary needs to refer back to what was said without repeating the full reported clause each time.

  • Umbono kamongameli wesigungu sabafundi uyahambisana nombono kaNomvula ngokuningi. The student council chairperson's view largely agrees with Nomvula's view. umbono lets the summary compare two positions without restating either one in full

7.4 Stepping into commentary with ngokubona kwami

Language Focus

ngokubona kwami marks the clear boundary where reporting ends and the commentator's own view begins. Everything before this marker should be a faithful account of what was said, and everything after it belongs to the commentator.

  • Ngokubona kwami, umbono kamongameli uyaqondakala, kodwa awuveli ukuthi kuzokwenzeka kanjani uma abafundi bengavumelani ngezikhathi zokubhukha. In my view, the chairperson's position is understandable, but it does not make clear what will happen if students disagree about booking times. ngokubona kwami opens the commentator's own evaluation, separate from the reported material above

7.5 Agreeing and disagreeing with ngiyavumelana no- and angivumelani ncamashi no-

Language Focus

A commentary rarely agrees or disagrees completely. ngiyavumelana no- signals agreement, while angivumelani ncamashi no- signals disagreement that stops short of outright rejection, both of which keep the commentary fair to the original speaker.

  • Ngiyavumelana nomongameli ngokuthi uhlelo luzosiza abafundi abaningi, kodwa angivumelani ncamashi naye ngokuthi akudingeki ukuhlelwa kwezikhathi zokugcina. I agree with the chairperson that the system will help many students, but I do not entirely agree with him that closing times need no planning at all. agreement and partial disagreement appear in the same sentence, keeping the commentary balanced

7.6 The verb template inside a reporting-and-commentary sentence

Language Note — Reporting markers and commentary markers frame the sentence, they do not replace the verb template
discourse marker (reporting or commentary) + subject concord + TAM marker + object concord + verb root + extension or extension sequence + final vowel
A reporting marker such as wathi or ngokusho kuka- tells the listener that faithful reporting is underway. A commentary marker such as ngokubona kwami, ngiyavumelana no-, or angivumelani ncamashi no- tells the listener that personal evaluation has begun. Everything that follows still obeys the verb template from Chapter 3 and the subordination and argument markers from Chapters 5 and 6. The discourse marker changes whose voice the sentence carries, it does not remove the subject concord, the object concord, or any extension the sentence genuinely needs.
Umongameli wesigungu sabafundi wathi uhlelo lokubhukha luzosiza abafundi, futhi ngokusho kwakhe amakamelo azoqondiswa kangcono uma abafundi behlela kusengaphambili. Ngokubona kwami umbono wakhe uyaqondakala, kodwa angivumelani ncamashi naye ngokuthi akudingeki ukubhekwa izikhalo zalabo abangazi ukuhlela kusengaphambili, ngakho-ke ngiyaphakamisa ukuthi uhlelo luqale ngokwesivivinyo, njengoba uNomvula ephakamisile.
The student council chairperson said that the booking system will help students, and according to him the rooms will be better managed if students plan in advance. In my view his position is understandable, but I do not entirely agree with him that the concerns of those who cannot plan in advance need not be considered, and so I propose that the system begin as a trial, as Nomvula has proposed. wathi and ngokusho kwakhe keep the reporting faithful, ngokubona kwami and angivumelani ncamashi no- keep the commentary clearly separate, and ngakho-ke reconnects to the conclusion marker from Chapter 6.

Model Text: The Campus Radio Interview

Read the interview transcript below, followed by Nomvula's summary and commentary. Notice how the summary stays close to the chairperson's words, while the commentary, marked clearly with ngokubona kwami, adds Nomvula's own evaluation.

Inhlolokhono yomsakazo wekampasi (The campus radio interview)

Umsakazi: Sikhuluma namuhla nomongameli wesigungu sabafundi ngodaba lohlelo lokubhukha amakamelo okufunda. Ungasitshela yini ukuthi kungani sikhungo sabafundi sicabanga ukuthi lolu hlelo luyadingeka?

Umongameli: Ngiyabonga. Sikholwa ukuthi uhlelo lokubhukha luzokwenza izinto zibe lula kubafundi abaningi, ikakhulukazi ngesikhathi sokuhlolwa. Abafundi abaningi baye basichazele ukuthi kwakunzima ukuthola ikamelo elivulekile, futhi lokhu kwakubangela ukucindezeleka okungadingekile.

Umsakazi: Kodwa abanye bathi lolu hlelo lungenza izinto zibe nzima kwabafundi abangahleli kusengaphambili. Ningaphendula kanjani lokho?

Umongameli: Siyakuqonda lokho, futhi asicabangi ukuthi umuzwa lowo awunangqondo. Yingakho sicabanga ukuthi izindawo ezimbalwa kuphela okumele zibhukhwe, kuyilapho amanye amakamelo esala evulekile njengakuqala.

Isifinyezo nokuphawula kukaNomvula (Nomvula's summary and commentary)

Ngokusho komongameli wesigungu sabafundi, uhlelo lokubhukha luzosiza abafundi abaningi ngesikhathi sokuhlolwa, ngoba wathi ukungatholakali kwamakamelo bekubangela ucindezelo olungadingekile. Uphinde wathi umuzwa wabafundi abangahleli kusengaphambili unengqondo, futhi wathi izindawo ezimbalwa kuphela okufanele zibhukhwe, kuyilapho amanye amakamelo esala evulekile.

Ngokubona kwami, umbono kamongameli uyahambisana kakhulu nombono wami owaziswa esahlukweni 6, ikakhulukazi ekutheni izindawo ezimbalwa kuphela kufanele zibhukhwe. Ngiyavumelana naye ukuthi lokhu kungasiza ngaphandle kokulimaza abangahleli kusengaphambili. Nokho, angivumelani ncamashi naye ekutheni akakhulumanga ngokuthi ubani ozothatha isinqumo sokuthi yimaphi amakamelo azobhukhwa. Ngakho-ke, ngicabanga ukuthi lolu daba kufanele lucaciswe ngaphambi kokuthi uhlelo luqale.

The summary reports the chairperson's words faithfully using ngokusho kwakhe and wathi. The commentary begins clearly with ngokubona kwami, states agreement with ngiyavumelana naye, states a limited disagreement with angivumelani ncamashi naye, and closes with a conclusion using ngakho-ke, exactly as this chapter's grammar section requires.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Yini umongameli wesigungu sabafundi ayisho ngesizathu sohlelo lokubhukha? (What does the student council chairperson say is the reason for the booking system?)
  2. Ingxenye ephi yombono kaNomvula evumelana nombono womongameli? (Which part of Nomvula's view agrees with the chairperson's view?)
  3. Ingxenye ephi uNomvula angavumelani ncamashi nayo? (Which part does Nomvula not entirely agree with?)

Writing Workshop

Write your own summary and commentary responding to a short interview or media statement, either the one in this chapter or another one your instructor provides, following the same two-stage pattern Nomvula used, reporting first, then commenting.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write a summary and commentary of eight to ten sentences in total. First, report the speaker's position accurately using wathi and ngokusho kuka-, without adding your own opinion. Then, marked clearly with ngokubona kwami, add your own commentary, stating at least one point of agreement with ngiyavumelana no- and one point of limited disagreement with angivumelani ncamashi no-, and close with a conclusion using ngakho-ke or sengiphetha ngokuthi. Then apply the Chapter 19 self editing routine from Volume III before sharing your final version.

  1. Report the position. Use wathi and ngokusho kuka- to state, faithfully, what the speaker said.
  2. Name the view as a whole. Use umbono if you need to refer back to the speaker's position without repeating it in full.
  3. Mark the shift to commentary. Use ngokubona kwami to signal that your own evaluation is beginning.
  4. State agreement and limited disagreement. Use ngiyavumelana no- for what you accept, and angivumelani ncamashi no- for what you do not fully accept.
  5. Reach your conclusion. Use ngakho-ke or sengiphetha ngokuthi to draw your commentary together into a final position.
  6. Audit your draft. Apply the Chapter 19 repair categories, then check that the reporting section and the commentary section remain clearly separated throughout.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • The reported section stays close to what the speaker actually said, with no unmarked opinion mixed in.
  • ngokubona kwami, or an equivalent marker, clearly signals where commentary begins.
  • At least one point of agreement and one point of limited disagreement are stated using the markers from this chapter.
  • The conclusion follows from the commentary given, rather than introducing a new, unsupported idea.
  • Object concords, extensions, subordinate clauses, and the argument markers from Chapter 6 remain accurate throughout.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: reading the interview aloud in two roles

Read the interview aloud, taking both the broadcaster's and the chairperson's parts, then read Nomvula's summary and commentary aloud, pausing clearly at ngokubona kwami.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: reporting and commenting live

With a partner, one person plays a guest being interviewed about a real or invented topic, stating a short position in two or three sentences. The other listens, then reports the position aloud using wathi, and finally adds a short spoken commentary using ngokubona kwami.

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Listen to or read a short opinion from a real or invented source, then record yourself reporting it faithfully, followed by your own commentary. Listen back and check that a listener could tell exactly where your reporting ended and your own opinion began.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Fair reporting before personal comment

In many South African media and institutional contexts, a written summary of someone else's position is expected to be fair and accurate before any personal commentary is added, since a summary widely seen as one sided can damage the credibility of the person writing it, regardless of how reasonable their later commentary may be.

Culture in Context — Commenting on a public figure's words

When Nomvula comments on the student council chairperson's interview, she engages directly with what he said rather than with assumptions about his character or motives, a distinction commonly expected in professional and institutional commentary.

Regional Variation — wathi and ngokusho kuka- in written summaries

Some writers prefer wathi for briefer, more direct reporting within a sentence, while reserving ngokusho kuka- for the opening of a longer summary that attributes an entire position to a named speaker. Others use the two more interchangeably. Both patterns are widely accepted in formal writing.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Blending reporting and commentary without a marker

Error: Moving from a reported statement directly into personal opinion within the same sentence, with no marker such as ngokubona kwami to signal the shift, so a reader cannot tell where the speaker's words end and the writer's opinion begins.

Correction: Always mark the shift from reporting to commentary clearly, using ngokubona kwami or a similar phrase, even inside a single paragraph.

Common Mistake — Reporting only what supports the commentator's own view

Error: Leaving out part of what a speaker said because it does not fit the commentary the writer wants to give, producing a summary that is technically accurate about what remains, but misleading about the speaker's overall position.

Correction: Report the speaker's position in full first, including the parts that will later be questioned in the commentary, then respond to all of it.

Common Mistake — Disagreeing without acknowledging any valid point

Error: Using angivumelani ncamashi no- to reject a speaker's entire position, when the commentator actually agrees with part of it.

Correction: Use ngiyavumelana no- for the parts genuinely accepted, and reserve angivumelani ncamashi no- only for the specific part being questioned.

Chapter 7 Checkpoint

Reporting and Commenting on Media, With Accuracy and Fairness

Complete the following three connected tasks, in order, as a single integrated performance sequence, reporting and commenting on an interview as Nomvula reports and comments on the campus radio interview.

  1. Report the speaker's position. Greet your audience formally, then report the speaker's main points accurately using wathi and ngokusho kuka-, without adding your own opinion.
  2. Mark and deliver your commentary. Signal the shift clearly using ngokubona kwami, then state at least one point of agreement using ngiyavumelana no- and one point of limited disagreement using angivumelani ncamashi no-.
  3. Reach your conclusion. Draw your commentary together using ngakho-ke or sengiphetha ngokuthi, then write and present a short final version, self edited using the Volume III Chapter 19 routine.

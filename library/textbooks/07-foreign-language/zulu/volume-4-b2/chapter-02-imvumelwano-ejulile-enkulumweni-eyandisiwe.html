Volume IV, Chapter 2: Advanced Concord in Extended Discourse · Speak and Write Zulu: Advanced Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Maintain accurate agreement across a long, stacked noun phrase, in which a noun is followed by more than one modifier of a different agreement type.
  2. Carry a single subject concord correctly across several connected sentences in a paragraph, without drifting toward a nearby noun's class.
  3. Track more than one participant across a paragraph or short presentation, using subject and object concords to keep each participant distinct.
  4. Describe a place or a course of study using extended noun phrases that stack an adjective, a possessive, and a relative clause on a single noun.
  5. Read a longer descriptive passage and identify which class each concord in it belongs to, even when several different classes appear close together.
  6. Produce a paragraph of extended description in isiZulu, applying the Chapter 19 self editing routine from Volume III to check concord accuracy across the whole paragraph, not only within single sentences.

Communication Scenario

Nomvula has settled into her first weeks at university, and a friend from home has asked her to describe, in detail, where she lives and what she is studying. To answer well, Nomvula must describe her residence, her room, her course, and her lecturers using long, layered noun phrases, and she must keep her descriptions consistent across several connected sentences, rather than losing track of which noun each concord refers back to as her paragraphs grow longer. This chapter introduces no new grammar system. It asks learners to control the concord system, already fully consolidated in Volume III, across a larger span of language than a single sentence.

  1. Learners study how a single noun can carry several stacked modifiers at once, each with its own agreement pattern, and how a subject concord must remain stable across a full paragraph even when other nouns of different classes appear nearby.
  2. Learners then produce their own extended description of a place and a course of study, applying the same discipline Nomvula uses in her message home.

Where This Chapter Fits

Pronunciation and Prosody Across Long Phrases

No new sound appears in this chapter. Instead, learners practice maintaining clear phrase breaks and even pacing when producing a long, stacked noun phrase, since running several modifiers together without natural pauses can make it difficult for a listener to track which word each concord agrees with.

Pronunciation Focus — Pausing inside a long noun phrase

When a noun carries several stacked modifiers, such as an adjective, a possessive, and a relative clause, a slight pause after the head noun and a slightly longer breath before the relative clause can help a listener follow the structure without losing the thread.

  • uhlelo lwezifundo (course programme): practice a small pause after uhlelo before adding lwezifundo, so the possessive concord lwe- is heard clearly rather than rushed.
  • abafundi abangenayo (incoming students): practice keeping the relative concord aba- distinct in pace from the plain plural aba- of abafundi, since these two syllables can blur together if spoken too quickly.

Grammar: Concord Across Long Noun Phrases and Paragraphs

Purpose: this section does not introduce new grammar. It extends the concord system consolidated in Volume III, showing how the same agreement rules already known must be applied consistently as noun phrases grow longer and as description moves from single sentences into full paragraphs.

2.1 Stacking modifiers on a single noun

Language Focus

A single noun may carry an adjective, a possessive, and a relative clause at the same time, and each of these modifiers must agree with the noun's class independently. Adding modifiers does not change which class the noun belongs to, so every concord in the stack, however many modifiers appear, must point back to the same class.

  • Ikamelo lami elisha elisenyuvesi lihle kakhulu. My new room, which is at the university, is very nice. lami, elisha, and elisenyuvesi all carry Class 5 concord forms agreeing with ikamelo, stacked one after another on the same noun

2.2 Holding a subject concord steady across a paragraph

Language Focus

When one participant remains the subject of several connected sentences, the subject concord for that participant's class must remain the same throughout, even when other nouns of different classes are mentioned nearby as objects or descriptions. A common error appears when a writer accidentally shifts to the concord of the most recently mentioned noun instead of the noun that is actually still the subject.

  • Uhlelo lwami lwezifundo lubandakanya izifundo eziningi, futhi lunikeza abafundi amathuba amaningi okuzilolonga. My course programme includes many subjects, and it gives students many opportunities to practice. lubandakanya and lunikeza both keep Class 11 subject concord lu- for uhlelo, even though izifundo, Class 8, and abafundi, Class 2, appear as objects in between

2.3 Tracking more than one participant across description

Language Focus

When a paragraph describes two participants, such as a place and the people in it, each participant needs its own consistent subject concord, and object concords must be used carefully to keep the two participants distinct, rather than allowing one concord to become ambiguous between them.

  • Umtapo wolwazi mkhulu kakhulu, futhi othisha bawusebenzisa njalo, ngoba unezinsizakusebenza abazidingayo. The library is very large, and the lecturers use it often, because it has the resources they need. mkhulu and unezinsizakusebenza keep Class 3 concord for umtapo wolwazi; bawusebenzisa uses Class 2 subject concord ba- for othisha and Class 3 object concord wu- for the library, keeping the two participants clearly distinct

2.4 Relative clauses inside extended noun phrases

Language Note — Relative clauses as one layer in a modifier stack
noun + adjective concord + possessive concord + relative concord + verb stem
A relative clause can be added as the final layer on a noun that already carries an adjective and a possessive, provided the relative concord still agrees with the noun's original class, exactly as practiced with isizathu in Volume III, Chapter 20.
Abafundi abasha abangenayo enyuvesi babhalisa ngokushesha.
The new incoming students at the university register quickly. Abafundi abasha abangenayo stacks an adjective abasha and a relative clause abangenayo on abafundi, and both concords match Class 2.

Model Text: Nomvula Describes Her Residence and Her Course

Read Nomvula's message to her friend below. It stacks modifiers on several nouns and carries two participants, her residence and her course, consistently across the whole paragraph.

Umyalezo kaNomvula kumngane wakhe (Nomvula's message to her friend)

Ikamelo lami elisha elisenyuvesi lincane, kodwa lihlelekile futhi linokukhanya okuningi okungena ngefasitela. Ngihlala nomngani omusha engimazi kule nyanga kuphela, kodwa sesivumelana kahle. Uhlelo lwami lwezifundo lubandakanya izifundo eziningi ezithinta ulimi nemibhalo, futhi luyangijabulisa ngoba lunikeza abafundi amathuba amaningi okuzilolonga ekukhulumeni nasekubhaleni. Othisha abangifundisayo bacophelela kakhulu, futhi bawusebenzisa umtapo wolwazi njalo lapho befundisa, ngoba unezinsizakusebenza abazidingayo eziningi. Ikhampasi yonke inkulu kunalokho ebengikucabanga, futhi kuthatha isikhathi ukuyazi kahle, kodwa sengiqala ukuzizwa ngathi ngingowayo.

The message stacks an adjective, a possessive, and a locative on ikamelo, keeps Class 11 subject concord steady across uhlelo lwezifundo, and distinguishes othisha from umtapo wolwazi throughout, exactly as this chapter's grammar section requires.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Uchaza kanjani uNomvula ikamelo lakhe, futhi yimaphi amagama amabili achaza lona? (How does Nomvula describe her room, and what two words describe it?)
  2. Kungani othisha besebenzisa umtapo wolwazi njalo, ngokusho kukaNomvula? (Why do the lecturers use the library often, according to Nomvula?)
  3. Uzizwa kanjani uNomvula ngekhampasi manje, uma kuqhathaniswa nokuqala kwakhe? (How does Nomvula feel about the campus now, compared to when she started?)

Writing Workshop

Write your own extended description of a place important to you, such as your home, your workplace, or your school, and of one activity or programme connected to it, following the same pattern Nomvula used to describe her residence and her course.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write a paragraph of six to eight sentences. Describe one place using at least one noun with a stacked adjective, possessive, and relative clause together, and describe one activity or programme connected to that place, keeping its subject concord consistent across at least three connected sentences. Then apply the Chapter 19 self editing routine from Volume III before sharing your final version.

  1. Choose your place and your activity. Select one place and one connected activity or programme to describe.
  2. Draft the place description. Build at least one noun phrase that stacks an adjective, a possessive, and a relative clause, checking that every concord matches the head noun's class.
  3. Draft the activity description. Write at least three connected sentences keeping the same subject concord throughout, even if other nouns of different classes appear as objects.
  4. Check for participant confusion. If you mention a second participant, confirm that its concords remain clearly distinct from the first.
  5. Audit your draft. Apply the four Chapter 19 repair categories, concord, object concord placement, TAM and polarity, and relative clause accuracy, paying particular attention to concord drift across sentence boundaries.
  6. Finalize and present. Rewrite any corrected sentences into a clean final version.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • At least one noun carries a stacked adjective, possessive, and relative clause, with every concord matching its class.
  • The subject concord for the main participant remains consistent across at least three connected sentences.
  • If a second participant appears, its concords remain clearly distinct from the first participant's concords.
  • Object concords are built into the verb template, not placed as separate words.
  • Negative markers and final vowels agree consistently throughout.
  • The paragraph reads smoothly aloud, with natural pauses after stacked modifiers.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: reading the model message aloud

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: describing two places

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Describing new surroundings to family and friends

When a young person moves away for study or work, sending a detailed description of their new surroundings to family and friends back home is a common and appreciated gesture, since it lets those who could not travel there share in the experience and feel reassured about the person's wellbeing.

Culture in Context — Speaking respectfully about lecturers

When describing lecturers or instructors to others, it is common to mention their care or diligence, as Nomvula does, rather than focusing only on their academic titles, since this reflects a broader value placed on relationships of care between students and those who teach them.

Regional Variation — Describing shared or crowded living spaces

Some communities describe a shared residence room primarily in terms of the people living there, while others focus first on the physical space itself, as Nomvula does. Both approaches are common, and the choice often depends on what the listener is most likely to want to know first.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Letting the last modifier set the concord instead of the head noun

Error: Choosing the concord for a relative clause or final modifier based on the noun that appears immediately before it, rather than the head noun the entire stack describes.

Correction: Before adding each modifier in a stack, return mentally to the head noun and its class, and select every concord in the stack from that same class, regardless of how many modifiers come before it.

Common Mistake — Shifting subject concord partway through a paragraph

Error: Switching the subject concord to match a nearby object noun's class, even though the original subject is still the one being discussed.

Correction: Before each new sentence in an extended description, confirm which participant is still the subject, and keep using that participant's concord until the subject genuinely changes.

Common Mistake — Blurring two participants together

Error: Using the same or similar-sounding concords for two different participants in the same paragraph, making it unclear which one a sentence is actually describing.

Correction: When describing two participants of different classes, use object concords deliberately to keep reference clear, and reread the paragraph aloud to confirm a listener could tell the two apart.

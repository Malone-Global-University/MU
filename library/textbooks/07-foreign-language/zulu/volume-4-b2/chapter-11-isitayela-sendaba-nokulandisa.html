Isitayela Sendaba Nokulandisa

Volume IV, Chapter 11: Literary and Narrative Style · Speak and Write Zulu: Advanced Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Read and interpret a short narrative text, identifying narrative voice, point of view, and the ordering of events.
  2. Recognize how dialogue is woven into narration, distinguishing a narrator's account from a character's reported words.
  3. Interpret expressive language, including ideophones and reduplication, as tools that make narration vivid rather than as isolated vocabulary items.
  4. Reuse the reported speech markers from Chapter 7 inside a narrative frame, rather than inside correspondence or public address.
  5. Reuse relative and participial clauses from earlier chapters to describe characters, settings, and unfolding events within a story.
  6. Write and read aloud a short narrative retelling of a familiar set of events, maintaining a consistent narrative voice from beginning to end.

Communication Scenario

Nomvula's isibongo has become something of a small local story. A member of her student council asks her to write it down properly, not as a summary and not as a formal letter, but as a short piece of narrative writing that could be read aloud at the next gathering or shared with new students who were not there. Nomvula realizes this is a different kind of task altogether. The facts have not changed, but now she must decide who is telling the story, in what order the events unfold, and how to bring the imbizo itself to life for someone who never stood in the hall that evening.

  1. This chapter does not introduce new grammar. Instead, it develops the relative clauses, reported speech markers, and expressive language already active since earlier chapters, now applied to narrative writing and reading.
  2. Learners read a model narrative, identify how it is built, and then retell a set of events in their own connected narrative.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 10 asked learners to carry one set of facts through three separate registers, informal, correspondence, and ceremonial, keeping each one clean and consistent. Chapter 11 asks something different. Here, one voice, the narrator, carries the whole story from beginning to end, and that voice may include dialogue, description, and reported speech all within a single flowing text. Narrative writing does not replace the registers practiced in Unit 2. Instead, it draws on all of them, reported speech from Chapter 7 for what characters said, description from earlier noun phrase work for people and places, and now a narrator's own connecting voice that holds everything together.

Coming From Chapters 6 Through 10

Productive control of argument markers, faithful reported speech, correspondence structure, ceremonial address, and the ability to select the correct register for a given communicative moment.

New in This Chapter

No new grammar. The chapter develops narrative voice, point of view, dialogue embedded in narration, and expressive language for vivid storytelling.

Looking Forward

Pronunciation: Voice and Vividness in Narration

A narrator's voice is steadier and more even than the ceremonial pausing practiced in Chapter 9, but it is not flat. Expressive words, ideophones in particular, call for a small burst of energy that sets them apart from the surrounding narration, the way a storyteller's voice might rise slightly at the exact moment something happens.

Pronunciation Focus — Giving weight to ideophones inside a flowing narration

Read the connecting narration at a steady, even pace, then let your voice sharpen briefly on an ideophone or a moment of reported speech, before returning to the steady narrative pace. This small shift signals to a listener that something notable is happening at that point in the story, without requiring any change in vocabulary or grammar.

  • Narration sentences: steady pace, even intonation, similar to the informal summary register from Chapter 10.
  • Ideophones and moments of reported speech within the story: a brief lift in energy, then a return to the narrator's steady voice.

Grammar: Narrative Voice, Point of View, and Expressive Language

Purpose: this section does not activate new grammar. It develops the relative clauses, reported speech markers, and expressive language already active since earlier chapters, showing how they combine inside a single narrative voice, so a learner can read a story with understanding and retell one with a consistent point of view.

11.1 Choosing and holding a narrative voice

Language Focus

A narrative text is usually told from one consistent point of view, most often a narrator describing events from the outside, or a character telling their own story in the first person. Once a learner chooses a point of view for a retelling, that point of view should not shift without a clear reason. A story that drifts between an outside narrator and a character's own voice, without signalling the shift, becomes difficult for a reader to follow.

  • UNomvula wakhuluma embizweni, echaza ukukhathazeka kwabahlali ngomshini owodwa. Nomvula spoke at the gathering, explaining the residents' worry about the single computer. outside narrator, third person, describing Nomvula's action from a distance
  • Ngathi ngisukuma ukuze ngikhulume, ngangizizwa ngithukile kancane. When I stood up to speak, I felt a little nervous. first person narrator, Nomvula telling her own story from the inside

11.2 Weaving reported speech into narration

Language Focus

Inside a narrative, a character's actual words may appear briefly, framed by the same reporting markers practiced in Chapter 7, wathi and ngokusho kwa-. The narrator's own voice then continues around that reported moment, rather than stopping the story to deliver a separate formal report.

  • Omunye wabahlali wathi, "Umshini uzoba khona nini ngempelasonto?" abanye bavuma ngokuhleka kancane. One of the residents said, "When will the computer be available on weekends?" and the others agreed with a small laugh. wathi introduces a brief quoted moment, then the narration continues describing the room's reaction

11.3 Relative clauses for description inside a story

Language Focus

Relative clauses, developed since Chapter 2 and expanded further in Chapters 3 and 5, do quiet but essential work inside narration. They let a narrator describe a person, a place, or an object in passing, without stopping the flow of events to open a separate sentence.

  • Ikhansela, elalihlezi ngaphambili, lalilalele ngokucophelela zonke izikhalo zabahlali. The councillor, who was sitting at the front, listened carefully to all the residents' concerns. relative clause elalihlezi ngaphambili adds description without interrupting the sentence's main action

11.4 Ideophones and reduplication for vividness

Language Note — Expressive language sharpens a moment without changing the underlying grammar
narrative clause + ideophone or reduplicated form, marking sound, motion, manner, or intensity at a single moment
An ideophone or a reduplicated form does not replace the verb template, the subject concord, or the object concord. It sits alongside the ordinary sentence, adding a burst of sensory detail at exactly the moment the narrator wants a listener to notice. Overusing these forms weakens their effect, so a narrator generally reserves them for the story's more important moments.
Ikhanda lakhe laqhaqhazela kancane ngesikhathi esukuma, kepha lapho eseqedile ukukhuluma, iholo lonke lathula cwaka.
Her head trembled slightly as she stood up, but once she had finished speaking, the whole hall fell completely silent. The ideophone cwaka intensifies the description of silence at the story's turning point, while the surrounding sentences remain built on the same verb template studied since Chapter 3.

Model Text: Indaba KaNomvula

Read the short narrative below. It retells the same underlying facts from Chapter 10, but now as a single connected story told from Nomvula's own point of view, in the first person, weaving together description, dialogue, and a few moments of expressive language.

Indaba: Ubusuku Bombizo

Ngangimile ngaphambi kwabantu abaningi, inhliziyo yami ishaya ngamandla. Iholo, elalizele ngabantu abadala nabancane, lalithule kancane ngenkathi ngiqala ukukhuluma. Ngathi ngiyabingelela, ngazizwa sengathi wonke umuntu uyangibuka. Emva kwemizuzu embalwa, ngangesabile, kepha ngaqhubeka ngikhuluma ngokukhathazeka kwabahlali ngomshini owodwa wamakhompyutha.

Omunye wabahlali, owayehlezi eduze komnyango, wathi, "Umshini uzoba khona nini ngempelasonto?" Abanye bavuma ngokuhleka kancane, kanti ikhansela, elalihlezi ngaphambili, lalilalele ngokucophelela. Ngachaza ukuthi izingane eziningi kufanele zisebenzise ikhompyutha ngasikhathi sinye, futhi lokhu kudala inkinga kwimindeni emikhulu.

Lapho ngiseqeda ukukhuluma, iholo lonke lathula cwaka okomzuzwana, ngaphambi kokuthi kuqhume ihlombe. Ngazizwa ikhanda lami liqhaqhazela kancane, kepha ngesikhathi esifanayo, ngazizwa neqiniso lokuthi amazwi ami ayewile kahle. Ikhansela lasukuma, lathi lizosidlulisela isicelo sethu kumnyango. Ngesikhathi ngiphuma eholo, ngaba nombono wokuthi indaba yethu isizoqala uhambo olude, kepha oluqalile ngokuhle.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Ubani okhuluma le ndaba, futhi kungenzeka kanjani ukuthi sazi lokhu? (Who tells this story, and how can we tell?)
  2. Yiziphi izinkulumo eziqondene nabantu abanye ezifakwe ngaphakathi endabeni? (Which quoted words of other people are embedded inside the narration?)
  3. Chaza ukuthi igama elithi cwaka lisiza kanjani ukwakha isithombe sesenzakalo. (Explain how the word cwaka helps build a picture of the moment.)

Writing Workshop

Retell a set of familiar events, either continuing Nomvula's story from a different point of view, or narrating a similar community event of your own choosing, as one connected narrative text.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write a short narrative of eight to ten sentences, choosing either a first person point of view, a narrator telling their own story, or a third person point of view, a narrator describing someone else's actions from the outside. Include at least one moment of reported speech using wathi or ngokusho kwa-, at least one relative clause describing a person or place, and at least one ideophone or reduplicated form placed at the story's most important moment. Then apply the Chapter 19 self editing routine from Volume III before presenting your final version.

  1. Choose your point of view. Decide whether the narrator is a participant in the story or an outside observer, and hold that choice steady from beginning to end.
  2. Draft the opening. Set the scene, naming the place, the people present, and the moment the story begins.
  3. Embed one quoted moment. Use wathi or ngokusho kwa- to bring another person's exact words briefly into the narration.
  4. Add description with a relative clause. Describe one person, place, or object using a relative clause, without opening a separate sentence.
  5. Mark the turning point. Place one ideophone or reduplicated form at the moment in the story that matters most.
  6. Audit your draft. Apply the Chapter 19 repair categories, checking especially that the point of view has not drifted partway through.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • The narrative holds one consistent point of view from beginning to end.
  • At least one quoted moment is introduced using wathi or ngokusho kwa-.
  • At least one relative clause describes a person, place, or object within the story.
  • At least one ideophone or reduplicated form appears at the story's most important moment, and no more than two appear overall.
  • Concords, verb templates, and subordinate clauses remain accurate throughout the narration.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: reading the model narrative aloud

Read the model narrative aloud at a steady pace, lifting your voice briefly at the ideophone and the quoted question, then check that a listener could identify those two moments without seeing the text.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: telling your own story aloud

With a partner, tell your own written narrative aloud from memory, without reading directly from the page. The listener then retells the story back to you in their own words, checking that the main events and the quoted moment survived the retelling.

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself telling your narrative aloud from memory. Listen back and check that your point of view stayed consistent, and that the ideophone or reduplicated form still lands with the right weight.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Storytelling as a way of preserving community events

In many isiZulu speaking communities, retelling a meeting, a gathering, or a shared event as a story is a common way of preserving it for people who were not present, and of passing it on to younger community members. A well told narrative version of an event often circulates alongside the formal written record, carrying the same facts in a form that is easier to remember and to share aloud.

Culture in Context — Expressive language as a marker of a skilled storyteller

The careful, well placed use of ideophones and reduplication is often recognized as a sign of a skilled and engaging storyteller, in contrast with a narration that lists events without any vividness at all. This is a matter of degree rather than a fixed rule, and expectations around how much expressive language suits a given story can differ between communities, generations, and settings.

Regional Variation — Storytelling occasions

The settings in which a story like Nomvula's might actually be retold, a family gathering, a school assembly, a community meeting, or a media interview, can differ between urban and rural communities, and between more traditional and more institutional settings. This chapter's narrative skills apply broadly, but the exact occasion for using them will vary by context.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Letting the point of view drift partway through the story

Error: Beginning a narrative in the first person, ngathi ngisukuma, and then sliding without warning into third person description of the same narrator, as though describing someone else.

Correction: Choose either a first person or a third person point of view before drafting, and check during self editing that every sentence still matches that original choice.

Common Mistake — Overloading a narrative with ideophones

Error: Placing an ideophone or a reduplicated form in nearly every sentence, so that the story loses the contrast that makes these forms effective.

Correction: Reserve expressive language for the story's genuinely important moments, generally no more than one or two per short narrative, so each one still carries weight.

Common Mistake — Reporting speech without the Chapter 7 markers

Error: Inserting another person's words directly into the narration with no wathi, no ngokusho kwa-, and no clear signal that a quotation has begun, leaving a reader uncertain whether the narrator or another character is speaking.

Correction: Introduce every embedded quotation with wathi or ngokusho kwa-, exactly as practiced in Chapter 7, so the boundary between narration and quoted speech stays clear.

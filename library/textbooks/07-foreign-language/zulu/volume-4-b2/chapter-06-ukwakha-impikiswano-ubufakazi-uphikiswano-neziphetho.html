Ukwakha Impikiswano: Ubufakazi, Uphikiswano, Neziphetho

Volume IV, Chapter 6: Argumentation and Evidence · Speak and Write Zulu: Advanced Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. State a claim clearly, so that a listener or reader knows exactly what position is being argued.
  2. Support a claim with evidence, using ngokwesibonelo and related phrases to introduce a concrete reason or example.
  3. Anticipate a counterpoint, using ngakolunye uhlangothi to acknowledge an opposing view before responding to it.
  4. Respond to a counterpoint respectfully, without abandoning the original claim or dismissing the opposing view.
  5. Reach a reasoned conclusion, using ngakho-ke to connect evidence and reasoning to a final position.
  6. Maintain causal, temporal, conditional, and purpose subordination from Chapter 5 while building a longer argumentative text.

Communication Scenario

Nomvula's explanation of the attendance policy was well received, and now a different task has landed on her desk. The student council has proposed a new booking system for the shared study rooms, and opinions are divided. Some staff members believe the current first come, first served arrangement works well enough. Others, including Nomvula, believe a booking system would reduce conflict and make better use of a limited resource. Nomvula has been asked to prepare a short written case for the booking system, one that does more than state her opinion. She must give a reason grounded in evidence, acknowledge the objection she expects to hear, respond to it fairly, and close with a conclusion that follows naturally from what she has argued.

  1. This chapter activates argumentative discourse markers, claim, evidence, counterpoint, and conclusion, for full productive use inside connected prose.
  2. Learners then write and present a short argument in support of a position, using at least one claim, one piece of evidence, one counterpoint, and one conclusion.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 5 taught learners to connect a reason, a time reference, a condition, and a purpose inside a single formal explanation. Chapter 6 keeps every one of those subordinate clause types active, since a good argument still needs to explain why something is true and what a proposal is meant to achieve. What changes here is the larger shape of the text. An explanation states what is true and why. An argument goes further, it takes a position, defends it with evidence, and answers an objection before reaching a conclusion. The grammar does not change, the discourse structure around it does.

Coming From Chapter 5

Productive control of causal, temporal, conditional, and purpose subordination, sustained inside a single formal explanation.

New in This Chapter

Productive argumentative discourse markers for claim, evidence, counterpoint, and conclusion, layered onto the subordination already active.

Looking Forward

Amagama Empikiswano — Vocabulary for Argument

Vocabulary: Claims, Evidence, Counterpoints, and Conclusions

This chapter introduces the discourse markers that give an argument its shape, since a position becomes persuasive only when a claim, its support, the objection it must answer, and the conclusion it reaches are all clearly signaled, not left for the reader to guess.

Pronunciation and Pacing in Argument

No new sound appears in this chapter. Instead, learners practice signaling a shift in stance with the voice, since a counterpoint introduced by ngakolunye uhlangothi needs a small change in pace or pitch to tell a listener that the argument has turned toward an opposing view, and the return to nakuba kunjalo needs a similar signal to show the original position is being reaffirmed.

Pronunciation Focus — Signaling a turn in the argument

A brief pause before ngakolunye uhlangothi, together with a slightly lower pitch, helps a listener hear that a counterpoint is coming rather than a continuation of the same point. Raising the pitch slightly again at nakuba kunjalo signals the return to the speaker's own position.

  • ngakolunye uhlangothi (on the other hand): worth rehearsing as a single phrase with a clear pause before it, since learners sometimes run it directly into the previous sentence and lose the contrast it signals.
  • sengiphetha ngokuthi (I conclude by saying that): worth rehearsing with a slightly slower, more deliberate pace, since a conclusion benefits from sounding settled rather than rushed.

Grammar: Building an Argument With Subordinate Clauses

Purpose: this section activates claim, evidence, counterpoint, and conclusion markers for full productive use, and shows how these discourse markers work together with the causal, temporal, conditional, and purpose subordination already active since Chapter 5, so that an argument can move from position to support to objection to conclusion in one connected piece of writing.

6.1 Stating a claim with ngiyakholwa ukuthi

Language Focus

A claim states the position an argument will defend. ngiyakholwa ukuthi introduces the claim as a complement clause, using ukuthi in the same way learners have already met it in earlier volumes. The claim should be stated once, clearly, near the beginning of the argument, so a reader knows immediately what is being argued.

  • Ngiyakholwa ukuthi uhlelo lokubhukha amakamelo okufunda luzosiza kakhulu abafundi bethu. I believe that a booking system for the study rooms will help our students a great deal. ngiyakholwa ukuthi introduces the complement clause carrying the claim itself

6.2 Supporting a claim with ngokwesibonelo

Language Focus

A claim on its own is only an opinion. Evidence gives it weight. ngokwesibonelo introduces a concrete example, an observation, or a fact that supports the claim, often followed by a causal or temporal clause explaining the connection.

  • Ngokwesibonelo, ngesikhathi sokuhlolwa okudlule, abafundi abaningi babengakwazi ukuthola ikamelo lokufunda ngoba wonke amakamelo ayesegcwele. For example, during the last examination period, many students could not find a study room because all the rooms were already full. ngokwesibonelo introduces the evidence, ngoba then explains the reason inside the evidence itself

6.3 Anticipating a counterpoint with ngakolunye uhlangothi

Language Focus

A strong argument acknowledges the strongest objection to it, rather than ignoring it. ngakolunye uhlangothi introduces this opposing view fairly, without exaggerating it or dismissing it before it has been stated.

  • Ngakolunye uhlangothi, abanye abasebenzi bakholwa ukuthi uhlelo lokubhukha lungenza izinto zibe nzima kakhulu kwabafundi abangazi ukuthi bazodinga ikamelo nini. On the other hand, some staff members believe that a booking system could make things too difficult for students who do not know in advance when they will need a room. ngakolunye uhlangothi introduces the counterpoint as a fair statement of the opposing belief

6.4 Responding to a counterpoint with nakuba kunjalo

Language Focus

Acknowledging a counterpoint is not the same as agreeing with it. nakuba kunjalo signals that the speaker has heard the objection and is now responding to it, usually by showing that the original claim still holds, or by proposing a way to address the objection directly.

  • Nakuba kunjalo, uhlelo lungahlela izindawo ezimbalwa ezingabhukhwa, kuyilapho amanye amakamelo esala evulekile kubafundi abangahlelanga kusengaphambili. Even so, the system could set aside a small number of rooms that can be booked, while other rooms remain open to students who have not planned in advance. nakuba kunjalo introduces the response, which proposes a compromise rather than dismissing the objection

6.5 Reaching a conclusion with ngakho-ke and sengiphetha ngokuthi

Language Focus

A conclusion draws the argument together, showing how the claim, the evidence, and the response to the counterpoint lead to a final position. ngakho-ke is a general connector suitable inside the body of an argument, while sengiphetha ngokuthi is a more formal phrase often reserved for the final sentence.

  • Sengiphetha ngokuthi uhlelo lokubhukha, uma lwakhiwa kahle, lungasiza abafundi abaningi ngaphandle kokulimaza labo abangahleli kusengaphambili. I conclude by saying that a booking system, if it is designed well, can help many students without harming those who do not plan in advance. sengiphetha ngokuthi introduces the conclusion, and uma reappears here carrying a conditional clause from Chapter 5

6.6 The verb template inside an argumentative sentence

Language Note — Argument markers frame the sentence, they do not replace the verb template
discourse marker + connector (if any) + subject concord + TAM marker + object concord + verb root + extension or extension sequence + final vowel
A discourse marker such as ngiyakholwa ukuthi, ngokwesibonelo, ngakolunye uhlangothi, nakuba kunjalo, or ngakho-ke opens a sentence and tells the listener what role that sentence plays in the argument. Everything that follows still obeys the verb template from Chapter 3 and the subordination patterns from Chapter 5. The discourse marker changes how the sentence functions inside the larger argument, it does not remove the subject concord, the object concord, or any extension the sentence genuinely needs.
Ngiyakholwa ukuthi uhlelo lolu luzosiza abafundi ngoba amakamelo amaningi ayevame ukugcwala ngesikhathi sokuhlolwa, futhi ngakolunye uhlangothi kuyaqondakala ukuthi abanye bengakwazi ukuhlela kusengaphambili, kodwa nakuba kunjalo izindawo ezimbalwa ezingabhukhwa zingaxazulula le nkinga ngaphandle kokuphazamisa abanye, ngakho-ke ngiyaphakamisa ukuthi uhlelo lolu luqale ngokwesivivinyo.
I believe this system will help students because the rooms were often full during examination periods, and on the other hand it is understandable that some cannot plan in advance, but even so a small number of bookable spaces could solve this problem without disturbing others, and so I propose that this system begin as a trial. luzosiza keeps the applicative sense of benefit, kuyaqondakala keeps the neuter-stative, and zingaxazulula keeps the potential mood, all inside a single argumentative sentence.

Model Text: Nomvula's Case for the Study Room Booking System

Read Nomvula's written argument below. It sustains the formal register from Chapters 4 and 5, and it moves through a claim, evidence, a counterpoint, a response, and a conclusion, exactly as this chapter's grammar section describes.

Impikiswano kaNomvula ngohlelo lokubhukha amakamelo okufunda (Nomvula's argument for the study room booking system)

Ngiyakholwa ukuthi uhlelo lokubhukha amakamelo okufunda luzosiza kakhulu abafundi bethu, ikakhulukazi ngesikhathi sokuhlolwa lapho isidingo sikhula khona. Ngokwesibonelo, ngesikhathi sokuhlolwa okudlule, abafundi abaningi babebika ukuthi bekungenzeki ukuthola ikamelo elivulekile, ngoba wonke amakamelo ayesegcwele kusukela ekuseni kakhulu. Le nkinga yenzeka ngenxa yokuthi akekho oqondile ukuthi ngubani ozosebenzisa ikamelo nini, ngakho-ke abafundi abaningi babelinda ngaphandle kwesiqiniseko. Ngakolunye uhlangothi, abanye abasebenzi bakholwa ukuthi uhlelo lokubhukha lungenza izinto zibe nzima kwabafundi abangazi ukuthi bazodinga ikamelo nini, futhi kukhona iqiniso kulokho. Nakuba kunjalo, uhlelo lungahlela izindawo ezimbalwa kuphela ezingabhukhwa, kuyilapho amanye amakamelo esala evulekile njengakuqala kubafundi abangahlelanga kusengaphambili. Ngale ndlela, akekho oyolahlekelwa yithuba lokufunda, kodwa abafundi abahlela kusengaphambili nabo bazokwazi ukuqinisekisa indawo yabo. Sengiphetha ngokuthi uhlelo lokubhukha, uma lwakhiwa kahle futhi lwahlolwa okwesikhashana, lungasiza abafundi abaningi ngaphandle kokulimaza labo abangahleli kusengaphambili.

The argument opens with a claim using ngiyakholwa ukuthi, supports it with evidence using ngokwesibonelo, states a counterpoint fairly using ngakolunye uhlangothi, responds to it using nakuba kunjalo, and closes with a conclusion using sengiphetha ngokuthi, exactly as this chapter's grammar section requires.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Yini ekholwa uNomvula ngayo mayelana nohlelo lokubhukha amakamelo? (What does Nomvula believe about the room booking system?)
  2. Yiziphi ubufakazi asebenzisa ukusekela impikiswano yakhe? (What evidence does she use to support her argument?)
  3. Uphendula kanjani uNomvula uphikiswano lwabasebenzi abangavumelani naye? (How does Nomvula respond to the counterpoint raised by staff who disagree with her?)

Writing Workshop

Write your own short argument in support of a position you hold, such as a change you would like to see at your workplace, in your studies, or in your community, following the same reasoning pattern Nomvula used to argue for the booking system.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write an argument of eight to ten sentences, addressed to an audience who may not already agree with you. Open with a claim using ngiyakholwa ukuthi, support it with evidence using ngokwesibonelo, state a fair counterpoint using ngakolunye uhlangothi, respond to it using nakuba kunjalo, and close with a conclusion using ngakho-ke or sengiphetha ngokuthi. Then apply the Chapter 19 self editing routine from Volume III before sharing your final version.

  1. State your claim. Use ngiyakholwa ukuthi to state the position you will defend.
  2. Give your evidence. Use ngokwesibonelo to introduce a concrete example or observation supporting your claim.
  3. State the counterpoint fairly. Use ngakolunye uhlangothi to describe the strongest objection to your position, without exaggerating it.
  4. Respond to the counterpoint. Use nakuba kunjalo to show why your claim still holds, or to propose a way of addressing the objection.
  5. Reach your conclusion. Use ngakho-ke or sengiphetha ngokuthi to draw the argument together into a final position.
  6. Audit your draft. Apply the Chapter 19 repair categories, then check that each discourse marker introduces the function it is meant to, and that the register remains formal throughout.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • The argument opens and closes in a consistently formal register, with no casual forms mixed in.
  • The claim is stated once, clearly, near the beginning of the text.
  • At least one piece of evidence is introduced with ngokwesibonelo and connects clearly to the claim.
  • The counterpoint is stated fairly, without dismissing it before it has been described.
  • The response to the counterpoint strengthens the claim rather than abandoning it.
  • The conclusion follows naturally from the evidence and the response given, rather than introducing a new idea.
  • Object concords, extensions, and subordinate clauses built into earlier chapters remain accurate inside the longer argumentative sentences.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: reading the model argument aloud

Read Nomvula's argument aloud in full, then again identifying, out loud, each discourse marker, ngiyakholwa ukuthi, ngokwesibonelo, ngakolunye uhlangothi, nakuba kunjalo, and sengiphetha ngokuthi, and the function each one plays in the argument.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: arguing a position to a skeptical audience

With a partner, take turns presenting your own argument. Your partner should listen for the claim, the evidence, and the counterpoint, then respond as the skeptical audience by restating the counterpoint in their own words before you offer your response again.

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself presenting your argument in full, without reading directly from your notes. Listen back and identify whether your counterpoint sounds like a fair description of an opposing view or like a weak version created only to be knocked down, then revise and record it again if needed.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Disagreeing while preserving relationship

In many South African institutional settings, a written or spoken argument is expected to acknowledge an opposing view respectfully, since preserving a good working relationship with colleagues who disagree is often considered as important as winning the specific point being argued.

Culture in Context — Proposing a trial rather than a permanent change

When Nomvula proposes that the new system begin ngokwesivivinyo, as a trial, she is drawing on a common institutional pattern in which a contested change is introduced provisionally, with review, rather than presented as final from the outset.

Regional Variation — ngakho-ke and sengiphetha ngokuthi in written argument

Some writers favor sengiphetha ngokuthi only for the very last sentence of a document, reserving ngakho-ke for conclusions that appear earlier within the body of the argument. Others use ngakho-ke throughout, including at the very end, without this being considered less correct. Both patterns are widely accepted.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Stating a counterpoint too weakly

Error: Describing the opposing view in a way that makes it sound obviously wrong, so that responding to it proves nothing about the strength of the original claim.

Correction: State the counterpoint the way someone who actually holds that view would state it, so that the response that follows genuinely engages with a real objection.

Common Mistake — Skipping the evidence and moving straight to the conclusion

Error: Stating a claim, then moving directly to ngakho-ke without ever introducing ngokwesibonelo or any concrete support for the claim.

Correction: Check that every claim is followed by at least one piece of evidence before the argument moves toward its conclusion, since a conclusion without evidence is simply a repeated opinion.

Common Mistake — Letting the conclusion introduce a new idea

Error: Using ngakho-ke or sengiphetha ngokuthi to introduce a completely new point that was never supported earlier in the argument.

Correction: Check that the conclusion only draws together the claim, the evidence, and the response to the counterpoint already given, rather than adding a new, unsupported idea at the last moment.

Answer Key

Model Text — Comprehension

  1. Nomvula believes that a booking system for the study rooms will help students a great deal, especially during examination periods when demand grows.
  2. She uses the evidence that during the last examination period, many students reported being unable to find an open room because all the rooms were already full from early in the morning.

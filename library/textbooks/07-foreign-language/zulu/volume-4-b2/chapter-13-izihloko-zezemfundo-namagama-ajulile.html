Izihloko Zezemfundo Namagama Ajulile

Volume IV, Chapter 13: Academic Topics and Abstract Vocabulary · Speak and Write Zulu: Advanced Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use Class 14 ubu- abstract nouns productively, to name qualities, conditions, and concepts rather than only concrete things.
  2. Build complement clauses with ukuthi to introduce an opinion, a claim, or a piece of reported thinking within a longer sentence.
  3. Hold a structured discussion in Standard isiZulu about a concept such as ubuntu, community, or development, rather than only a concrete event.
  4. Maintain formal register across a short prepared talk, including register choices appropriate to a public or academic setting.
  5. Support an abstract claim with a specific example, connecting conceptual vocabulary to concrete detail.
  6. Read and interpret a short academic-style passage, identifying its main claim and the reasons given for it.

Communication Scenario

Word of Nomvula's story, and of the different way her cousin's community told it, has reached her old teacher. The teacher invites Nomvula to speak at a community forum, not about the specific evening in the hall this time, but about the idea behind it. She is asked to talk for a few minutes about ubuntu, the value that one person's wellbeing is bound up with that of the community, and about how a small local project can support intuthuko, development, for a whole neighbourhood. Nomvula realizes that talking about an idea is different from narrating an event. She needs words for qualities and conditions that have no single physical shape, and she needs a way to state a claim clearly and then explain why she believes it.

  1. This chapter develops the abstract noun class, the complement clause with ukuthi, and formal register, all already active from earlier chapters, and applies them to conceptual discussion rather than narrative.
  2. Learners prepare and deliver a short structured talk stating a claim about an abstract topic and supporting it with a concrete example.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 12 asked learners to listen closely and describe, neutrally, how someone else's speech differed from their own, while their own production stayed standard throughout. Chapter 13 keeps that same discipline of standard, careful production, but moves the subject matter from a concrete retelling to an abstract idea. Instead of describing what happened in a hall on one evening, learners now state what they believe about a concept, and why. The relative clauses, reported speech markers, and complement structures built since earlier chapters do the same grammatical work here that they did in narrative, they now simply carry claims and reasons instead of events.

Coming From Chapter 12

Receptive recognition of regional and urban variation, together with continued standard production, careful listening, and neutral description of differences.

New in This Chapter

Productive development of Class 14 abstract nouns and ukuthi complement clauses, applied to structured discussion of concepts, together with sustained formal register.

Pronunciation: Weight and Pace in Formal Speech

A prepared talk on an idea is usually slower and more evenly weighted than a narrated story. Where a narrative can speed up at an exciting moment, a structured talk about a concept tends to slow down at the moment a claim is stated, so that the listener has time to absorb it, and then to pick up pace again through the supporting example. Penultimate lengthening, already practiced since Volume I, becomes especially noticeable at the end of a stated claim, where the speaker often pauses briefly before continuing.

Pronunciation Focus — Pacing a claim and its support

Practice reading a claim sentence, pausing briefly, then reading its supporting example at a slightly faster, more conversational pace. The contrast in pacing helps a listener hear the difference between the idea being stated and the evidence being offered for it, even before the grammar of the sentence makes that difference explicit.

  • Claim: read slowly and evenly, with a full pause at the end.
  • Supporting example: read a little faster, with the more connected rhythm used for narration in earlier chapters.

Grammar: Abstract Nouns and Structured Opinion

Purpose: this section develops two systems already active since earlier volumes, the Class 14 ubu- abstract noun pattern and the ukuthi complement clause, and applies them productively to conceptual discussion, argument, and formal register.

13.1 Class 14 abstract nouns: naming qualities and conditions

Language Focus

Class 14 nouns, formed with the prefix ubu-, name qualities, conditions, and states rather than countable things. Many are built from an adjective stem or a Class 1/2 noun stem, so a learner who already controls those forms can often recognize the relationship. These nouns behave like other noun-class members for concord, but they are usually singular only, since a quality is not counted the way an object is.

  • ubuntu humanity, the quality of being humane toward others ubu- + -ntu, related to umuntu, a person
  • intuthuko development, progress a Class 9/10 abstract noun frequently paired with ubu- nouns in public discussion
  • ubunzima difficulty, hardship ubu- + -nzima, related to the adjective stem -nzima, heavy or difficult
  • ubuhlobo friendship, relationship ubu- + -hlobo, related to umhlobo, a friend

13.2 Complement clauses with ukuthi: stating a claim

Language Note — Ngicabanga ukuthi as the base pattern for a stated claim
subject concord + verb of thinking, saying, or believing + ukuthi + full clause carrying the claim
The complement clause with ukuthi, already active since Chapter 7 for reported speech, is the same structure a speaker uses to introduce a personal claim or opinion. The verb before ukuthi changes the force of the statement, from a neutral ngicabanga, I think, to a firmer ngikholelwa, I believe, to a more distanced abanye bathi, some people say.
Ngikholelwa ukuthi ubuntu buseyisisekelo esibalulekile empilweni yomphakathi.
I believe that ubuntu remains an important foundation for community life. The subject concord bu- on buseyisisekelo agrees with ubuntu, a Class 14 noun, exactly as concord has agreed with every other noun class since Volume I.
  • Ngicabanga ukuthi iphrojekthi encane ingaba nomthelela omkhulu entuthukweni yomphakathi. I think that a small project can have a major impact on community development. ngicabanga + ukuthi + full clause, a measured claim rather than a strong assertion
  • Abanye bathi intuthuko iqala ngokuxhumana kwabantu, hhayi ngemali kuphela. Some people say that development begins with how people connect with one another, not with money alone. abanye bathi introduces a claim attributed to others, useful for presenting more than one viewpoint

13.3 Supporting a claim with a concrete example

Language Focus

An abstract claim becomes persuasive when it is followed by a specific, concrete example, often introduced with isibonelo, an example, or with a relative clause naming a particular person, place, or event. This pairing of claim and example is the basic unit of a structured academic-style talk, and it reuses the relative clauses and narrative verbs already active since Chapter 2 and Chapter 11.

  • Isibonelo salokho ukuthi umphakathi wethu wakha isikole esisha ngokusebenzisana, hhayi ngoxhaso lukahulumeni kuphela. An example of that is that our community built a new school through working together, not through government funding alone. isibonelo salokho ukuthi introduces a concrete instance that supports the claim stated before it

Learners are asked to study the model text below, then complete the linked tasks in the writing workshop and performance sections.

Model Text: Inkulumo KaNomvula Ngobuntu Nentuthuko

Read Nomvula's short prepared talk below. Notice how each claim, introduced with a verb of thinking or believing plus ukuthi, is followed by a concrete example that supports it.

Inkulumo emfushane ephathelene nobuntu nentuthuko yomphakathi

Ngikholelwa ukuthi ubuntu buseyisisekelo esibalulekile empilweni yethu yansuku zonke, ngisho nasezweni elishintshayo. Ubuntu abusho ukuthi umuntu akanakho ukuzimela, kepha kusho ukuthi impilo yakhe ihlobene nempilo yabanye. Ngicabanga ukuthi intuthuko yeqiniso ayiqali ngezimali ezinkulu, kepha iqala ngokuthi abantu bakhuluma, basebenzisane, futhi babambisane ekwenzeni okuthile okuzuzisa wonke umuntu. Isibonelo salokho ukuthi umphakathi wakithi wakha isikole esisha ngokusebenzisana, hhayi ngoxhaso lukahulumeni kuphela. Abazali, othisha, kanye nezinye izinhlangano zomphakathi, base bebambisene ukuqoqa izinsiza kanye nokwakha kanye. Ngenxa yalokho, izingane eziningi manje zifunda esikoleni esiseduze nomuzi wazo. Ngikholelwa ukuthi lesi sibonelo sikhombisa ukuthi lapho abantu bezwana futhi bebambisene, intuthuko iyenzeka, ngisho nangaphandle kwezimali eziningi.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Yisiphi isimangalo esiyinhloko esishiwo nguNomvula mayelana nobuntu? (What is the main claim Nomvula makes about ubuntu?)
  2. Yisiphi isibonelo esiqinisekisa isimangalo sakhe ngentuthuko? (What example supports her claim about development?)
  3. Bhala amagama amabili asuselwa ku-ubu- akhulunywa kule ndima. (Write two ubu- abstract nouns used in this passage.)

Writing Workshop

Write your own short structured passage on an abstract topic of your choice, following the claim and example pattern practiced in the model text.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write six to eight sentences, in Standard isiZulu, stating one claim about an abstract concept, such as ubuntu, intuthuko, imfundo, or ubuhlobo, and supporting it with at least one concrete example. Use at least one ubu- abstract noun, at least one ukuthi complement clause, and at least one relative clause from Chapter 2 or Chapter 5. Then apply the Chapter 19 self editing routine from Volume III before presenting your final version.

  1. Choose your concept. Select one abstract idea you want to discuss, and note two or three Class 14 or related abstract nouns connected to it.
  2. Draft your claim. Write one clear sentence stating what you believe about the concept, using a verb of thinking or believing plus ukuthi.
  3. Draft your example. Write two or three sentences describing a specific, concrete instance that supports your claim.
  4. Add a relative clause. Describe a person, place, or project connected to your example using a relative clause.
  5. Connect the parts. Use isibonelo salokho ukuthi or a similar connector to link the claim to the example clearly.
  6. Audit your draft. Apply the Chapter 19 repair categories, checking especially that concords agree correctly with any Class 14 nouns you have used.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • At least one ubu- abstract noun is used with correct concord.
  • At least one ukuthi complement clause introduces a clear claim.
  • At least one relative clause describes a person, place, or project in the example.
  • The claim and the example are clearly connected, not simply placed side by side.
  • Register remains formal and consistent throughout the passage.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: reading the model talk aloud

Read the model text aloud, applying the slower pace for each claim and the faster pace for each example, exactly as practiced in the pronunciation section.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: delivering a short talk

With a partner, take turns delivering your written claim and example aloud, as though speaking to a small community forum. The listener names the claim and the example separately afterward, to check that the structure came through clearly.

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself delivering your talk aloud. Listen back and check that your claim and your example are both clear, and that your register stayed formal from beginning to end.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Ubuntu as a widely referenced value

Ubuntu is a widely referenced value in many Southern African communities, often summarized in the idea that a person is a person through other people. It is invoked in many different settings, from everyday conversation to public and political life, and speakers may emphasize different aspects of it depending on context. A learner should treat it as a rich, commonly discussed concept rather than a single fixed slogan with only one meaning.

Culture in Context — Community forums and structured public speech

Community forums, school gatherings, and similar public settings often expect a speaker to move between a formal register and moments of warmer, more personal address, particularly when a speaker is known to the audience. A prepared talk in such a setting commonly opens and closes with a respectful acknowledgement of the audience, even when its main body is structured around a claim and supporting evidence.

Regional Variation — Academic and public vocabulary can vary by setting

Terms used for concepts such as development, community, and progress can vary somewhat between educational, governmental, and everyday contexts, and between different regions. This chapter's vocabulary reflects a common, widely understood standard suited to public and academic discussion, not the only acceptable set of terms a learner may encounter.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Treating ubu- nouns as countable

Error: Attempting to pluralize a Class 14 abstract noun such as ubuntu or ubuhlobo, as though it named a countable object rather than a quality or condition.

Correction: Treat Class 14 abstract nouns as singular only, and use quantity or degree words such as kakhulu, greatly, or kancane, a little, to express more or less of the quality instead.

Common Mistake — Stating a claim with no supporting example

Error: Presenting an abstract claim such as intuthuko ibalulekile, development is important, and moving on without any concrete example, leaving the claim unsupported.

Correction: Follow every claim with at least one specific example, introduced with isibonelo salokho ukuthi or a similar connector, so the listener can see the claim in practice.

Common Mistake — Letting register slip during the supporting example

Error: Stating the claim in careful formal register, then shifting into casual, conversational phrasing once the speaker reaches the example, so the talk sounds uneven.

Correction: Maintain the same formal register across the claim and the example, adjusting only pace and emphasis, not vocabulary or grammar formality.

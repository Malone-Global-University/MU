Ukuxoxisana Nokuxazulula Izinkinga

Volume IV, Chapter 14: Debate, Negotiation, and Problem Solving · Speak and Write Zulu: Advanced Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Disagree respectfully with a claim, using a formal register that acknowledges the previous speaker before offering an alternative view.
  2. Use reciprocal verb forms to describe people acting together, agreeing, or working things out with one another.
  3. Build contrastive clauses with kodwa and nakuba to weigh one option against another.
  4. Build conditional clauses with uma to propose alternatives and describe likely outcomes.
  5. Present a proposal, respond to a counter-proposal, and move a small group toward a shared decision.
  6. Read and interpret a multi-voice discussion, identifying where speakers agree, where they differ, and how the disagreement is resolved.

Communication Scenario

After Nomvula finishes her talk on ubuntu and community development, the forum opens for discussion. Not everyone agrees with her example. One neighbour, Mr Zondi, believes the new school should have waited for government funding rather than relying on volunteer labour, since he worries that unpaid work cannot be sustained. Another speaker, a young parent named Thandeka, proposes a middle path, a small paid coordinator role funded jointly by the community and the local council. Nomvula must listen carefully, acknowledge the concern behind each position, and help the room move from disagreement toward a decision that most people can accept.

  1. This chapter develops the claim and evidence structures from Chapter 13, and the relative and subordinate clauses active since earlier chapters, and applies them to disagreement, proposal, and negotiated decision-making.
  2. Learners practice stating a counter-position, proposing an alternative, and reaching a compromise in a small-group discussion.

Pronunciation: Voice and Turn-Taking in Disagreement

Disagreeing well in Standard isiZulu depends as much on pacing and tone as on word choice. A respectful counter-position is usually introduced at a measured pace, often preceded by a short acknowledging phrase spoken evenly, before the speaker's own view is stated with slightly firmer stress. A proposal that follows a disagreement is typically delivered a little more warmly and a little faster, signalling that the speaker is offering a way forward rather than continuing the objection.

Pronunciation Focus — Softening a disagreement, then firming a proposal

Practice reading an acknowledgement phrase gently and evenly, then reading the counter-position with a touch more stress on the key contrasting word, and finally reading the proposal at a warmer, slightly quicker pace. This three-part pacing pattern helps a listener follow the shift from acknowledgement to disagreement to proposal without needing every connecting word spelled out.

  • Acknowledgement: even, unhurried, respectful.
  • Counter-position: measured, with light emphasis on the point of contrast.
  • Proposal: warmer and a little faster, inviting agreement.

Grammar: Reciprocal Forms, Contrast, and Condition

Purpose: this section consolidates the reciprocal verb extension already active since earlier chapters, and develops contrastive and conditional subordination, so that learners can weigh positions against one another and propose a workable alternative.

14.1 The reciprocal extension: acting together, agreeing, and working things out

Language Focus

The reciprocal extension, formed by adding -ana to the verb stem, describes an action shared between two or more people rather than performed by one person on another. In negotiation and problem solving, reciprocal verbs describe the process itself, people meeting, agreeing, disagreeing, and reaching a decision together, rather than describing what one side does to the other.

  • -bonana to see one another, to meet -bona, to see, plus -ana, reciprocal
  • -vumelana to agree with one another -vuma, to agree, plus -ela, applicative, plus -ana, reciprocal
  • -phikisana to disagree with one another, to argue -phika, to deny or contest, plus -isa, causative, plus -ana, reciprocal
  • -xoxisana to discuss something together, to negotiate -xoxa, to converse, plus -isa, causative, plus -ana, reciprocal, the source of this chapter's title

14.2 Contrastive clauses with kodwa and nakuba: weighing one position against another

Language Note — Two ways to mark contrast
clause one + kodwa + clause two, or nakuba + clause one + clause two
Kodwa, but, joins two independent clauses and places equal weight on each. Nakuba, although, opens the chapter with a concession before the main point, so the two options are not weighted the same way. A speaker who wants to sound balanced often uses kodwa, while a speaker moving toward a firmer conclusion often opens with nakuba.
Ngiyabuqonda ubunzima bakho, kodwa ngikholelwa ukuthi umsebenzi wamahhala ungaqhutshwa kahle uma uhlelwe kahle.
I understand your difficulty, but I believe that volunteer work can be run well if it is properly organized. Kodwa balances the acknowledgement of Mr Zondi's concern against Nomvula's continuing position.
  • Nakuba umsebenzi wamahhala uwusizo, abantu abaningi abakwazi ukuwenza isikhathi eside. Although volunteer work is helpful, many people cannot sustain it for a long time. nakuba opens with a concession before the speaker's main point about sustainability

14.3 Conditional clauses with uma: proposing an alternative

Language Focus

A conditional clause with uma, if, states a condition, and the following clause states what would follow from it. In negotiation, this pattern is the basic shape of a proposal, since a proposal usually names a condition the group could accept, and then names the benefit that would follow.

  • Uma umphakathi noMasipala bebambisana ukukhokhela umuntu oyedwa ukuqondisa umsebenzi, sonke singaphumelela. If the community and the municipality work together to pay one person to coordinate the work, we could all succeed. uma introduces the proposed condition, singaphumelela states the potential outcome, bebambisana carries the reciprocal extension

Learners are asked to study the model dialogue below, then complete the linked tasks in the writing workshop and performance sections.

Model Dialogue: Emhlanganweni Womphakathi

Read the discussion below. Notice how each speaker acknowledges the previous view before disagreeing, and how Thandeka's proposal uses a conditional clause to offer a way forward.

Setting: a community forum after Nomvula's talk. Mr Zondi is an older community member, Thandeka is a young parent, and Nomvula is the speaker from Chapter 13. Register: formal public discussion, with respectful address to elders.

Mnu Zondi: Ngiyabonga ngenkulumo yakho, Nomvula, kodwa nginombono ohlukile ngendlela isikole esakhiwa ngayo. Thank you for your talk, Nomvula, but I have a different view about how the school was built.
Mnu Zondi: Ngicabanga ukuthi umsebenzi wamahhala ubulukhuni kubantu abadinga ukuzondla imindeni yabo. I think that unpaid work was hard for people who need to support their families.
Nomvula: Ngiyabuqonda ubunzima obushiwo nguMnu Zondi, kodwa ngicabanga ukuthi ukubambisana kwethu kwaba nomthelela omuhle. I understand the difficulty Mr Zondi mentions, but I think our working together had a positive effect.
Thandeka: Mhlonishwa Mnu Zondi, noNomvula, ngicela ukuphakamisa isu elisha. Respected Mr Zondi, and Nomvula, I would like to raise a new proposal.
Thandeka: Uma umphakathi noMasipala bevumelana ukukhokhela umuntu oyedwa ukuqondisa imisebenzi enjalo esikhathini esizayo, singagwema ubunzima obukhulunywa nguMnu Zondi. If the community and the municipality agree to pay one person to coordinate future work like this, we could avoid the difficulty Mr Zondi mentions.
Mnu Zondi: Lokho kungasebenza, uma singaqinisekisa ukuthi umuntu lowo ukhethwa ngendlela efanele. That could work, if we can ensure that that person is chosen in the proper way.
Nomvula: Sivumelene-ke, sizoxoxisana noMasipala ngesu likaThandeka esontweni elizayo. We have agreed, then, we will discuss Thandeka's proposal with the municipality next week.

Abantu bahleka kancane, futhi kuyavunywa ngokunikina amakhanda emhlanganweni. (The people laugh softly, and there is agreement shown by nodding around the meeting.)

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Yisiphi isikhathazo sikaMnu Zondi mayelana nomsebenzi wamahhala? (What is Mr Zondi's concern about volunteer work?)
  2. Yisiphi isu likaThandeka, futhi limi kanjani? (What is Thandeka's proposal, and what condition does it depend on?)
  3. Bhala amagama amabili asetshenziswa kule ngxoxo asuselwa esakhiweni esiphindaphindiwe, -ana. (Write two words used in this dialogue that are built from the reciprocal extension, -ana.)

Writing Workshop

Write your own short negotiation exchange, following the acknowledge, disagree, and propose pattern practiced in the model dialogue.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write six to eight sentences, in Standard isiZulu, in which one speaker raises a concern, a second speaker acknowledges it and states a different view, and a third speaker proposes a compromise using a conditional clause with uma. Use at least one reciprocal verb, at least one contrastive clause with kodwa or nakuba, and at least one conditional clause with uma. Then apply the Chapter 19 self editing routine from Volume III before presenting your final version.

  1. Choose your topic. Select a small community, workplace, or family disagreement, and note the two positions involved.
  2. Draft the concern. Write one or two sentences stating the first position and its underlying worry.
  3. Draft the counter-position. Write one or two sentences that acknowledge the concern, then state a different view, using kodwa or nakuba.
  4. Draft the proposal. Write one or two sentences offering a compromise, using uma to state the condition and its likely benefit.
  5. Close the exchange. Write one sentence in which the group reaches, or moves toward, a shared decision.
  6. Audit your draft. Apply the Chapter 19 repair categories, checking especially that reciprocal verbs and conditional clauses are formed correctly.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • At least one reciprocal verb form is used correctly.
  • At least one contrastive clause with kodwa or nakuba marks the shift between positions.
  • At least one conditional clause with uma states the proposal and its outcome.
  • Each speaker's turn acknowledges the previous speaker before disagreeing or proposing.
  • Register remains formal and respectful throughout the exchange.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: reading the model dialogue aloud

With a partner, read the model dialogue aloud, taking one role each, applying the acknowledgement, disagreement, and proposal pacing practiced in the pronunciation section.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: negotiating a compromise

In groups of three, take the roles of the concerned speaker, the counter-speaker, and the proposer, using your own written exchange or a new topic. Work toward a decision the whole group can state in one sentence at the end.

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself delivering all three roles from your written exchange, adjusting your pacing and tone for each position. Listen back and check that the shift from disagreement to proposal is clear.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Respectful disagreement in community settings

In many community and family settings, disagreement is expected to be voiced respectfully, particularly toward elders or people in positions of responsibility. A speaker who disagrees commonly opens with an acknowledgement of the previous speaker's point before stating a different view, and closes a discussion by naming the shared decision clearly, so that the group leaves with a common understanding.

Culture in Context — Consensus-building in public forums

Many community forums favour a consensus-building style of decision-making, where a proposal is refined through discussion until most participants can accept it, rather than settling matters by a strict vote alone. This style values patience and a willingness to adjust a position, and a speaker's standing in the community can depend on how well they manage disagreement, not only on the strength of their original claim.

Regional Variation — Negotiation vocabulary can vary by setting

Vocabulary for proposals, coordination, and compromise can vary between community, workplace, and governmental settings, and between regions. This chapter's vocabulary reflects a common, widely understood standard suited to community and public negotiation, not the only acceptable set of terms a learner may encounter.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Disagreeing without acknowledgement

Error: Moving directly into a counter-position without first acknowledging the previous speaker's concern, which can sound abrupt or dismissive in a formal public setting.

Correction: Open with a brief acknowledgement such as ngiyabuqonda ubunzima, I understand the difficulty, before introducing the contrasting view with kodwa or nakuba.

Common Mistake — Confusing the reciprocal and passive extensions

Error: Using a passive form such as -vunyelwa, to be agreed to, when a reciprocal meaning such as -vumelana, to agree with one another, is intended.

Correction: Use the reciprocal extension -ana when the action is shared between two or more people acting together, and reserve the passive for describing an action done to someone by an unnamed or backgrounded agent.

Common Mistake — A proposal with no clear condition

Error: Stating a proposal as a flat assertion, such as kufanele sikhokhele umuntu oyedwa, we must pay one person, without the uma clause that shows what the proposal depends on.

Correction: Frame the proposal with uma to name the condition clearly, so the listener can see exactly what agreement is being asked for.

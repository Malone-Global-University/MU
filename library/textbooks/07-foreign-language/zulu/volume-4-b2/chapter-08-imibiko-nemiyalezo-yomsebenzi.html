Volume IV, Chapter 8: Professional Correspondence and Reports · Speak and Write Zulu: Advanced Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Structure a formal message or report with an appropriate opening, body, and closing, in a consistently professional register.
  2. Convert a spoken summary and commentary into a written report suited to a workplace or institutional reader.
  3. Use formal opening and closing formulas correctly, matching the level of formality to the reader's role.
  4. Present findings, recommendations, and next steps clearly, in an order a busy reader can follow quickly.
  5. Maintain the reported speech, commentary, argument, and subordination markers from Chapters 5, 6, and 7, while adapting them to a written, professional format.
  6. Apply the Volume III Chapter 19 self editing routine to a completed report before submitting it.

Communication Scenario

Nomvula's supervisor has read her summary and commentary on the campus radio interview, and now asks for something more formal, a short written report for the department, setting out what the chairperson said, how it relates to Nomvula's own proposal, and what she recommends the department do next. A report, her supervisor reminds her, is not a summary with an opinion attached. It needs a clear structure a reader can scan quickly, and it needs to end with a recommendation the reader can act on.

  1. This chapter activates formal correspondence structure and report writing for full productive use.
  2. Learners then convert reported speech and commentary material into a structured written report, ending with a clear recommendation.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 7 taught learners to keep reporting and commentary in two separate stages, first stating faithfully what a speaker said, then adding a personal response marked clearly with ngokubona kwami. Chapter 8 keeps both of those stages fully active, since a report still needs to report accurately before it recommends anything, but it adds a third layer on top, a formal document structure with its own opening, body, and closing, built for a reader who wants information quickly rather than a narrated account. The material has not changed, Nomvula is still working from the same interview, but the container for that material has changed from a spoken and written summary into a structured professional report.

Coming From Chapter 7

Productive control of reported speech markers such as wathi and ngokusho kuka-, together with commentary markers such as ngokubona kwami, ngiyavumelana no-, and angivumelani ncamashi no-.

New in This Chapter

Productive control of formal opening and closing formulas, section headings, and a recommendation structure suited to workplace and institutional reports.

Amagama Emibiko — Vocabulary for Correspondence and Reports

Vocabulary: Correspondence and Reports

This chapter introduces the structural markers a formal report needs, an opening that names the reader and the purpose, a body that presents findings in order, and a closing that states a clear recommendation, so that a busy reader can locate exactly what they need without reading the whole document closely.

Pronunciation and Register in Formal Writing

Formal reports are read silently far more often than they are read aloud, so this chapter's pronunciation focus is really a register focus, learning to hear the difference between a spoken commentary voice and a written report voice when a report is read aloud, for example in a meeting or a formal presentation of findings.

Pronunciation Focus — Reading a report aloud at an even, measured pace

When a report is read aloud in a meeting, each section should be read at an even, measured pace, with a clear pause between the findings section and the recommendation, so that listeners can tell the writer has finished describing the situation and has moved on to proposing an action.

  • ngokwesigaba (as a section): worth rehearsing with a brief pause afterward, since it signals a new labeled part of the document is beginning.
  • ngiyancoma ukuthi (I recommend that): worth rehearsing with a slightly firmer, more deliberate tone, since it marks the point where the report moves from description to action.

Grammar: Structuring Formal Messages and Reports

Purpose: this section activates formal correspondence structure and report writing for full productive use, and shows how a report's opening, body, and closing work together with the reported speech markers from Chapter 7 and the argument markers from Chapter 6, so that a learner can turn spoken or informally written material into a structured professional document.

8.1 Opening a formal report with ngokuhlonipha

Language Focus

ngokuhlonipha opens a report or letter respectfully, and is usually followed by a clear statement of purpose, naming the reader, the topic, and why the document was written, within the first sentence or two.

  • Ngokuhlonipha, ngibhalela umnyango ngombiko wenhlolokhono yomsakazo wekampasi mayelana nohlelo lokubhukha amakamelo okufunda. Respectfully, I am writing to the department with a report on the campus radio interview concerning the study room booking system. ngokuhlonipha opens the report, followed immediately by the purpose and topic

8.2 Presenting findings with okutholakele

Language Focus

okutholakele introduces the body of the report, where the reported speech markers from Chapter 7 continue to do their normal work, faithfully stating what was said, now organized under a labeled section rather than presented as connected prose.

  • Okutholakele: umongameli wesigungu sabafundi wathi uhlelo lokubhukha luzosiza abafundi abaningi, futhi ngokusho kwakhe umuzwa wabafundi abangahleli kusengaphambili unengqondo. Findings: the student council chairperson said that the booking system will help many students, and according to him the concern of students who cannot plan in advance is reasonable. okutholakele labels the section, wathi and ngokusho kwakhe continue to report faithfully inside it

8.3 Referencing an earlier position with ngokwesigaba

Language Focus

ngokwesigaba lets a report point back to an earlier section, or to an earlier document such as Nomvula's own proposal, without repeating it in full, which keeps a report short and easy to scan.

  • Ngokwesigaba sombono kaNomvula owethulwa esahlukweni 6, lokhu kuyahambisana nesincomo sokuqala sokuthi uhlelo luqale ngokwesivivinyo. In accordance with the section of Nomvula's view presented in Chapter 6, this agrees with the first recommendation that the system begin as a trial. ngokwesigaba links the current report back to earlier argued material without restating it

8.4 Stating a recommendation with ngiyancoma ukuthi

Language Focus

ngiyancoma ukuthi introduces a specific, actionable recommendation, followed by a subjunctive clause naming exactly what should happen next. A recommendation should be concrete enough that a reader knows what action is being proposed.

  • Ngiyancoma ukuthi umnyango ucacise kuqala ukuthi ubani ozothatha isinqumo sokuthi yimaphi amakamelo azobhukhwa, ngaphambi kokuba uhlelo luqale ngokugcwele. I recommend that the department first clarify who will decide which rooms are booked, before the system begins in full. ngiyancoma ukuthi introduces a subjunctive clause naming a specific, actionable step

8.5 Closing a formal report with ngiyabonga ngesikhathi sakho

Language Focus

ngiyabonga ngesikhathi sakho closes a report respectfully, and is usually followed by the writer's name and role, keeping the closing brief rather than repeating the recommendation already stated above.

  • Ngiyabonga ngesikhathi sakho. UNomvula, ummeli wesigungu sabafundi sesikole sokuqeqesha. Thank you for your time. Nomvula, representative of the training school's student council. the closing stays brief, naming only the writer and role, without restating the report's content

8.6 The verb template inside a formal report sentence

Language Note — Report structure frames the sentence, it does not replace the verb template
section marker (opening, findings, or recommendation) + subject concord + TAM marker + object concord + verb root + extension or extension sequence + final vowel
A section marker such as ngokuhlonipha, okutholakele, or ngiyancoma ukuthi tells the reader which part of the report they are in. Everything that follows still obeys the verb template from Chapter 3, the reported speech markers from Chapter 7, and the subordination and argument markers from Chapters 5 and 6. The section marker organizes the document, it does not remove the subject concord, the object concord, or any extension the sentence genuinely needs.
Ngokuhlonipha, ngibhalela umnyango ngombiko wenhlolokhono yomsakazo. Okutholakele: umongameli wathi uhlelo luzosiza abafundi abaningi, kodwa akakhulumanga ngokuthi ubani ozothatha isinqumo sokuthi yimaphi amakamelo azobhukhwa. Ngiyancoma ukuthi umnyango ucacise lokhu ngaphambi kokuba uhlelo luqale. Ngiyabonga ngesikhathi sakho.
Respectfully, I am writing to the department with a report on the radio interview. Findings: the chairperson said that the booking system will help many students, but he did not say who will decide which rooms are booked. I recommend that the department clarify this before the system begins. Thank you for your time. ngokuhlonipha opens the report, okutholakele labels the findings drawn from Chapter 7's reporting markers, ngiyancoma ukuthi introduces the recommendation, and ngiyabonga ngesikhathi sakho closes it briefly.

Model Text: The Report to the Supervisor

Read Nomvula's formal report below. Notice how it reuses the reported speech and commentary content from Chapter 7, but reorganizes it under labeled sections with a clear opening, findings, and a single actionable recommendation.

Umbiko kaNomvula ophathelene nenhlolokhono yomsakazo (Nomvula's report on the radio interview)

Isihloko: Umbiko ophathelene nenhlolokhono yomsakazo wekampasi ngohlelo lokubhukha amakamelo okufunda.

Isingeniso: Ngokuhlonipha, ngibhalela umnyango ngombiko omfushane wenhlolokhono yomsakazo wekampasi mayelana nohlelo lokubhukha amakamelo okufunda, njengoba kwakuceliwe.

Okutholakele: Umongameli wesigungu sabafundi wathi uhlelo lokubhukha luzosiza abafundi abaningi, ikakhulukazi ngesikhathi sokuhlolwa. Ngokusho kwakhe, umuzwa wabafundi abangahleli kusengaphambili unengqondo, futhi wathi izindawo ezimbalwa kuphela okumele zibhukhwe, kuyilapho amanye amakamelo esala evulekile. Ngokwesigaba sombono kaNomvula owethulwa esahlukweni 6, lokhu kuyahambisana nesincomo sokuqala sokuthi uhlelo luqale ngokwesivivinyo. Akakhulumanga, nokho, ngokuthi ubani ozothatha isinqumo sokuthi yimaphi amakamelo azobhukhwa.

Isincomo: Ngiyancoma ukuthi umnyango ucacise kuqala ukuthi ubani ozothatha isinqumo sokuthi yimaphi amakamelo azobhukhwa, ngaphambi kokuba uhlelo luqale ngokugcwele.

Isiphetho: Ngiyabonga ngesikhathi sakho. UNomvula, ummeli wesigungu sabafundi sesikole sokuqeqesha.

The report reuses wathi and ngokusho kwakhe from Chapter 7 to keep the findings faithful, uses ngokwesigaba to link back to Nomvula's own earlier proposal without restating it, and closes with a single clear recommendation rather than a personal commentary. Notice that ngokubona kwami does not appear here, since this report presents a recommendation rather than a personal evaluation.

Writing Workshop

Write your own formal report, converting a summary and commentary you have already written, either from Chapter 7 or from another interview or media statement your instructor provides, into a structured report with a clear opening, findings, and a single actionable recommendation.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write a report of eight to ten sentences in total, organized under four labeled sections, isingeniso, okutholakele, isincomo, and isiphetho. Open respectfully using ngokuhlonipha, state your findings faithfully using wathi and ngokusho kuka- as needed, then state exactly one recommendation using ngiyancoma ukuthi, and close briefly using ngiyabonga ngesikhathi sakho. Then apply the Chapter 19 self editing routine from Volume III before submitting your final version.

  1. Open the report. Use ngokuhlonipha to open respectfully, naming the topic and reason for writing in the same sentence.
  2. State the findings. Use okutholakele to label the section, then use wathi and ngokusho kuka- to report faithfully what was said.
  3. Link to earlier material where relevant. Use ngokwesigaba to connect the findings to an earlier proposal or argument, without repeating it in full.
  4. State one recommendation. Use ngiyancoma ukuthi, followed by a subjunctive clause naming a specific, actionable step.
  5. Close briefly. Use ngiyabonga ngesikhathi sakho, followed only by your name and role.
  6. Audit your draft. Apply the Chapter 19 repair categories, then check that each section contains only what belongs in it, findings in the findings section, and the recommendation stated only once.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • The report opens with ngokuhlonipha and states its topic and purpose within the first sentence or two.
  • The findings section reports faithfully, with no personal commentary mixed in.
  • Exactly one clear, actionable recommendation is stated using ngiyancoma ukuthi.
  • The closing stays brief and does not repeat the recommendation.
  • Object concords, extensions, subordinate clauses, and the reported speech markers from Chapter 7 remain accurate throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Respectful address in written reports

In many South African institutional settings, a report addressed to a supervisor, department, or committee is expected to open respectfully and to state its purpose clearly, since a report that begins abruptly or without a clear purpose statement can be read as careless, regardless of the quality of the findings that follow.

Culture in Context — One recommendation, clearly stated

A report that offers several vague suggestions instead of one clear recommendation is often seen as less useful in a workplace or institutional context, since a busy reader needs to know exactly what action is being proposed, rather than a general sense of the writer's opinion.

Regional Variation — Section labels in formal documents

Some institutions use the section labels practiced in this chapter, isingeniso, okutholakele, isincomo, and isiphetho, while others prefer numbered sections or English section headings within an otherwise isiZulu document. Both patterns are widely accepted in formal and institutional writing.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Mixing commentary into the findings section

Error: Inserting a personal opinion, such as ngokubona kwami, into the okutholakele section, so that the reader cannot tell which parts of the findings are faithful reporting and which are the writer's own view.

Correction: Keep the findings section limited to faithful reporting using wathi and ngokusho kuka-, and save any personal evaluation, if it belongs in the document at all, for a separate section clearly marked as such.

Common Mistake — Stating several recommendations without prioritizing one

Error: Listing three or four possible actions in the isincomo section with no indication of which one matters most, leaving the reader to guess what the writer actually wants done.

Correction: State one clear, actionable recommendation using ngiyancoma ukuthi. Additional considerations, if necessary, can be mentioned briefly in the findings section instead.

Common Mistake — Repeating the recommendation in the closing

Error: Restating the full recommendation again in the isiphetho section, making the closing longer than necessary and diluting the impact of the recommendation already stated.

Correction: Keep the closing brief, using ngiyabonga ngesikhathi sakho followed only by the writer's name and role.

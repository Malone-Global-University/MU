Volume IV, Chapter 4: Formal Explanation and Public Information · Speak and Write Zulu: Advanced Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Explain a system, a procedure, or a public issue clearly, in a consistently formal register appropriate to an audience of strangers or new students.
  2. Use the reciprocal extension productively, to describe actions that two or more parties do to or with one another.
  3. Use the neuter-stative extension productively, to describe a state that results from an action, rather than the action itself.
  4. Build a relative clause into formal explanatory prose, connecting a description directly to the noun it modifies without breaking the formal tone.
  5. Combine the causative, passive, reciprocal, and neuter-stative extensions accurately, choosing whichever the explanation genuinely requires.
  6. Apply the Volume III, Chapter 19 self editing routine to a piece of formal explanatory writing, checking register consistency alongside verb-template accuracy.

Communication Scenario

Now that Nomvula's timetable clash has been resolved, the faculty office has asked her to help orient a small group of new students by explaining, in isiZulu, how the course registration procedure works. She must describe how the system is organized, how students and the office interact with one another during registration, what condition a completed registration leaves a student in, and what to do if something goes wrong. Because she is speaking to strangers in an official setting, she cannot use the casual tone she used with her own family. She must sustain a formal register from the first sentence to the last.

  1. This chapter activates the reciprocal and neuter-stative extensions for full productive use, and asks learners to build relative clauses directly into formal explanatory prose.
  2. Learners then explain a system or procedure of their own choosing to an audience of strangers, sustaining a formal register throughout.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 3 activated the causative and passive extensions and showed how they combine with the applicative inside a single verb. Chapter 4 keeps that verb architecture and adds two more productive tools, the reciprocal extension and the neuter-stative extension, while shifting the communicative task from a personal account to a formal explanation for strangers. The relative clauses that have supported description since Volume III now move into sustained formal prose, where they must carry real explanatory weight rather than a single descriptive phrase.

Pronunciation and Register in Formal Delivery

No new sound appears in this chapter. Instead, learners practice a steady, unhurried delivery appropriate to addressing strangers in an official setting, since a formal explanation loses credibility when it is rushed, or when penultimate lengthening and click accuracy slip because the speaker is nervous in front of an unfamiliar audience.

Pronunciation Focus — Steady delivery for an unfamiliar audience

When explaining a procedure to people who do not yet know you, it helps to slow down slightly more than you would with family or classmates, and to pause briefly between each step of the explanation rather than running steps together.

  • ukubonisana (to consult with one another): a word likely to repeat throughout this chapter's explanations, worth rehearsing for the reciprocal ending -ana and its penultimate lengthening.
  • ukugcwaliseka (to be complete): worth rehearsing so the neuter-stative -ek- is clearly separated from the surrounding syllables, rather than blurred into the root.

Grammar: Formal Explanation and New Extensions

Purpose: this section activates the reciprocal and neuter-stative extensions for full productive use, and shows how a relative clause can carry real explanatory weight inside sustained formal prose, without disturbing the verb-template accuracy already consolidated in Chapter 3.

4.1 The reciprocal extension

Language Focus

The reciprocal extension shows that two or more parties perform an action to, with, or toward one another, rather than one party acting on a separate object. In its common productive form, the extension attaches as an before the final vowel, and the subject is usually plural or a phrase joined by na, meaning with.

  • Abafundi nabasebenzi behhovisi bayabonisana ngezinsuku zokubhalisa. Students and office staff consult with one another about registration dates. bayabonisana uses the reciprocal an on the root bon, meaning to see or to advise, producing to consult with one another

4.2 The neuter-stative extension

Language Focus

The neuter-stative extension describes a state that results from an action, rather than the action itself. In its common productive form, the extension attaches as ek before the final vowel, and the subject is the thing or person left in that state, not the party who caused it.

  • Uhlelo lokubhalisa lugcwaliseka uma isicelo sithunyelwe kanye nemibhalo yonke edingekayo. The registration process is complete when the application is submitted along with all the required documents. lugcwaliseka uses the neuter-stative ek on the root gcwal, meaning to fill, producing to be complete, describing the resulting state of the process

4.3 Relative clauses in sustained formal prose

Language Focus

Formal explanation depends on relative clauses that identify exactly which system, policy, or document is under discussion, rather than repeating a full noun phrase in every sentence. In sustained prose, the relative concord must continue to match the noun class of the antecedent, even several clauses later, and even when other nouns of a different class appear nearby.

  • Umgomo omusha okhishwe yihhovisi lezifundo uchaza kabanzi ngezinsuku ezidingekayo ekugcwaliseni inqubo. The new policy that was issued by the faculty office explains in detail the days required to complete the procedure. omusha and okhishwe both carry the Class 3 relative concord o-, matching umgomo, even though ihhovisi lezifundo, a different noun class, appears later in the same sentence

4.4 The full verb template with four extensions available

Language Note — Choosing among extensions accurately
subject concord + TAM marker + object concord + verb root + extension or extension sequence + final vowel
Four extensions are now available for productive use, the applicative, the causative, the passive, and, as of this chapter, the reciprocal and the neuter-stative. A formal explanation should choose the extension that matches the real relationship being described, mutual action, caused action, affected object, or resulting state, rather than defaulting to whichever extension was practiced most recently.
Iziphakamiso ezikhishwe umgomo lo zisiza abafundi nabasebenzi ukuze babonisane kangcono, futhi inqubo yonke igcwaliseke ngaphandle kwenkinga.
The guidelines issued by this policy help students and staff so that they consult with one another better, and so that the whole procedure is completed without a problem. babonisane uses the reciprocal, and igcwaliseke uses the neuter-stative, inside a single formal sentence.

Model Text: Nomvula Explains the Registration Procedure

Read Nomvula's formal explanation below, prepared for a group of new students. It sustains a formal register throughout, and it uses the reciprocal, the neuter-stative, and a relative clause built into sustained prose.

Incazelo kaNomvula ngohlelo lokubhalisa (Nomvula's explanation of the registration system)

Ngiyanibingelela nonke, futhi ngithemba ukuthi ningizwa kahle. Namuhla ngizonichazela ngokusemthethweni ukuthi luhlelwa kanjani uhlelo lokubhalisa lwaleli khonji. Uhlelo lolu luqalwa uma umfundi ethumela isicelo sakhe ehhovisi lezifundo, futhi isicelo lesi kufanele sihambisane nemibhalo eyisisekelo edingekayo. Ngesikhathi lokhu kwenzeka, abafundi nabasebenzi behhovisi bayabonisana ukuze bathole izinsuku ezifanele nezifundo ezifanele kulowo mfundi ngamunye. Umgomo omusha okhishwe yihhovisi lezifundo uchaza kabanzi ukuthi inqubo yonke kufanele igcwaliseke phakathi kwamasonto amabili emva kokufika kwesicelo. Uma kukhona okungahambanga kahle, umfundi kufanele abonisane nomsizi wehhovisi masinyane, ukuze udaba luxazululelwe ngokushesha. Ngingacela ukuthi noma ubani onemibuzo abuze manje, ukuze sonke sicaciselwe kahle ngaphambi kokuqala kwezifundo.

The explanation opens with a formal greeting, sustains ngokusemthethweni throughout, builds bayabonisana and abonisane as reciprocal verbs, builds igcwaliseke as a neuter-stative verb, and carries the Class 3 relative concord across omusha and okhishwe to describe umgomo, exactly as this chapter's grammar section requires.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Yini okufanele umfundi ayithumele ehhovisi lezifundo ukuze aqale inqubo yokubhalisa? (What must a student submit to the faculty office to begin the registration procedure?)
  2. Kungani abafundi nabasebenzi behhovisi bebonisana ngesikhathi sokubhalisa? (Why do students and office staff consult with one another during registration?)
  3. Kufanele kwenziwe njani uma inqubo ingahambanga kahle? (What should be done if the procedure does not go smoothly?)

Writing Workshop

Write your own formal explanation of a system or procedure you know well, such as a registration process, a workplace policy, a public service, or a club or society procedure, following the same pattern Nomvula used to explain the registration system.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write an explanation of seven to ten sentences, addressed to an audience of strangers. Open with a formal greeting, describe how the system is organized using at least one relative clause built into the prose, describe how two parties consult or interact with one another using the reciprocal extension, describe the resulting state of a completed step using the neuter-stative extension, and close with guidance for what to do if something goes wrong. Then apply the Chapter 19 self editing routine from Volume III before sharing your final version.

  1. Open formally. Greet your audience and state, using ngokusemthethweni or an equivalent phrase, that you are giving an official explanation.
  2. Describe the system. Use at least one relative clause built into sustained prose to identify the specific system, policy, or document under discussion.
  3. Draft a reciprocal sentence. Describe how two parties consult, advise, or interact with one another during the procedure.
  4. Draft a neuter-stative sentence. Describe the state a step or document is left in once it has been completed correctly.
  5. Close with guidance. State what a person should do if the procedure does not go smoothly.
  6. Audit your draft. Apply the Chapter 19 repair categories, then check that the register remains formal throughout, and that each extension matches the relationship it describes.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • The explanation opens and closes in a consistently formal register, with no casual forms mixed in.
  • At least one relative clause is built into the prose, with a relative concord matching its antecedent even several clauses later.
  • At least one verb uses the reciprocal extension accurately, describing mutual action between two parties.
  • At least one verb uses the neuter-stative extension accurately, describing a resulting state.
  • Object concords are built into the verb template, not placed as separate words.
  • Negative markers and final vowels agree consistently throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Opening a formal explanation

When addressing a group of strangers in an official capacity, as Nomvula does with the new students, it is common to open with a clear greeting and a brief statement of purpose before moving into the explanation itself, rather than beginning directly with procedural detail.

Culture in Context — Inviting questions from a group

Closing a formal explanation by inviting questions, as Nomvula does at the end of her account, is a common way of showing respect to an audience of strangers, and of making sure that everyone present, regardless of how confident they feel, has an opportunity to seek clarity.

Regional Variation — Degrees of formality in institutional speech

Some institutions favor a highly formal, somewhat elevated style for public information, while others favor a more direct, plainly worded style that remains respectful without sounding distant. Both approaches are widely used, and the preferred style often depends on the institution and the setting.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing the reciprocal with the applicative

Error: Using the applicative extension when the intended meaning is mutual action between two or more parties, producing a verb that sounds like one party acting for another rather than parties acting toward one another.

Correction: Reserve the reciprocal extension for genuine mutual action, and check that the subject is plural or joined by na before selecting it over the applicative.

Common Mistake — Using the neuter-stative where the passive is required

Error: Describing an action performed by an agent using the neuter-stative extension, when the passive extension is required because an agent is genuinely involved.

Correction: Use the neuter-stative only for a resulting state with no agent in view, and use the passive whenever the sentence could name, or does name, who performed the action.

Common Mistake — Letting register slip partway through a formal explanation

Error: Opening a formal explanation with ngokusemthethweni and a formal greeting, then shifting into casual vocabulary or shortened forms once the explanation becomes more detailed.

Correction: Choose formal register for the entire explanation, and maintain full verb forms and formal vocabulary from the opening greeting through the closing guidance.

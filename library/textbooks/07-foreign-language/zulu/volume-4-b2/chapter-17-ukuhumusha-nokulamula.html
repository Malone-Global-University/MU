Ukuhumusha Nokulamula

Volume IV, Chapter 17: Translation and Mediation · Speak and Write Zulu: Advanced Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Explain, in your own words, why word for word translation distorts meaning between English and isiZulu.
  2. Mediate the content of a written isiZulu text for an English speaking reader, preserving the writer's stance and tone rather than every sentence structure.
  3. Mediate the content of a short English text for an isiZulu speaking listener, adjusting register and cultural framing where needed.
  4. Identify concepts that do not map cleanly between the two languages, and choose an accurate explanatory strategy instead of a false equivalent.
  5. Preserve attribution and the writer's original position when mediating between audiences.
  6. Evaluate a mediated text for accuracy, completeness, and appropriate register in the target language.

Communication Scenario

Nomvula's position essay from Chapter 16 draws a reply. Her English speaking cousin, who works for a nonprofit in Durban, wants to understand the essay well enough to raise it at a funders' meeting, but does not read isiZulu. At the same time, the municipal office asks Nomvula's community committee to summarize, in isiZulu, the English language funding guidelines that any joint project would have to follow. Nomvula finds herself carrying meaning in both directions, for two audiences who will never read the original source themselves.

  1. This chapter asks learners to mediate meaning between English and isiZulu, in both directions, without producing a stiff, word for word rendering that a reader would find unnatural or misleading.
  2. Learners practice recognizing where a concept, a connector, or a cultural reference needs explanation rather than direct substitution.

Pronunciation: Reading Aloud for Two Audiences

A mediator is sometimes asked to read a rendering aloud immediately after producing it, for example summarizing an English guideline to a committee in isiZulu on the spot. This demands the same phrase rhythm and penultimate lengthening practiced in Chapter 16, but under more pressure, since there is no chance to revise before speaking. A mediator who pauses to think should pause at a natural phrase boundary, not mid word, so that a listener does not mistake hesitation for a change in meaning.

Pronunciation Focus — Pausing to reformulate without losing the listener

When a mediator needs a moment to find the right isiZulu expression for an English concept, a short pause after a completed clause is far less disruptive than a pause in the middle of a word or a verb template. Practice finishing the clause you are already saying, then pausing, rather than freezing partway through a verb form.

  • Complete the current clause before pausing to reformulate.
  • Use a brief filler such as okusho ukuthi, meaning "that is to say," to buy time without breaking rhythm.
  • Keep penultimate lengthening intact even in a reformulated sentence, so the listener still hears where each clause ends.

Language Focus: Mediating Meaning, Not Words

Purpose: this section does not introduce a new grammatical system. It applies the sentence patterns, connectors, and register control already active in the course to the specific task of carrying meaning between English and isiZulu.

17.1 Why word for word transfer fails

Language Focus

English and isiZulu organize information differently. English relies heavily on word order and a fixed set of prepositions, while isiZulu carries much of that same information through concord agreement, verb extensions, and locative morphology. A mediator who translates one English word at a time into isiZulu often produces a sentence that is grammatically broken, or grammatically correct but socially strange, because the concord chain, the register, or the expected level of directness has not been carried across as well as the vocabulary.

  • Ukususa amazwi, ukugcina incazelo (drop the words, keep the meaning) Identify the core claim of a sentence before choosing how to express it in the other language, rather than substituting word for word.
  • Ukuhlola isimo (checking the register) Ask whether the target audience expects formal, neutral, or informal language, and adjust word choice and sentence length accordingly.
  • Ukuchaza umqondo ongafani (explaining a concept without a direct equivalent) When no single isiZulu or English word matches a source concept exactly, use a short explanatory phrase rather than forcing an inaccurate one word substitute.

17.2 A working method for mediation

Strategy — Meaning first, form second
Funda konke → Bhala isifinyezo ngamazwi akho → Hlola izimo ezingahambisani kahle → Bhala unyaka wokugcina esilimini sokugcina
Read the whole source text before translating any of it, write a short summary in your own words in either language, identify any concept or connector that will not transfer directly, then produce the final version in the target language, checking it against the summary rather than against the original wording.
Encazelweni yesiNgisi ithi "matched funding," okuwukuthi imali enikelwa uhulumeni ilingana nemali eqoqwe umphakathi. Kunokuba ngihumushe igama ngalinye, ngiyachaza: "uhlelo lapho uMasipala enikela imali elingana nalena umphakathi osuwuqoqile."
The English guideline says "matched funding," meaning that the amount given by government equals the amount raised by the community. Rather than translating word by word, I explain it: "a scheme in which the municipality contributes an amount equal to what the community has already raised."

This method, meaning first and form second, is used directly in the writing workshop below, and again in the performance task at the end of the chapter.

Funders generally expect a short written update before any meeting, showing what has been achieved so far, what the community itself has contributed, and what risk remains if funding is delayed. Writing Workshop

Complete two short mediation tasks, one in each direction, using the meaning first method introduced above.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 1 — Isingisi kuya kusiZulu: A short English funding notice appears below. Mediate it into Standard isiZulu for the community committee, in six to nine sentences, choosing explanatory phrases rather than direct word substitutes for any term without a clean equivalent.

Source text, English
Applications must include a signed community endorsement, a simple budget, and evidence of at least one prior completed project. Late applications will not be considered this cycle, regardless of circumstance.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 2 — IsiZulu kuya kusiNgisi: Using Nomvula's position essay from Chapter 16, write a six to nine sentence English summary for her cousin, preserving the claim, the strongest piece of evidence, the counterpoint, and the conclusion, without translating sentence by sentence.

  1. Read the whole source first. Do not begin producing the target language version until you have read the entire source text or passage once.
  2. Summarize in your own words. Write a short summary, in either language, capturing the core claims before choosing exact wording.
  3. Flag concepts without a clean equivalent. Underline any term, such as a funding category or a cultural reference, that will need an explanatory phrase rather than a direct substitute.
  4. Check the register for the new audience. Decide whether the target reader expects formal, neutral, or informal language, and adjust accordingly.
  5. Produce the final version. Write the mediated text, checking it against your summary rather than against the original wording.
  6. Audit for attribution and completeness. Confirm that every claim in the source appears in your version, and that nothing has been added that was not in the source.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • Every major claim in the source text appears in the mediated version.
  • No claim appears in the mediated version that was not present in the source.
  • At least one concept without a clean equivalent is handled with an explanatory phrase, not a forced word for word substitute.
  • The register suits the new audience, not the register of the original source.
  • Attribution is preserved where the source names a specific speaker or organization.
  • The mediated text reads naturally in its own language, rather than sounding like a translation.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: reformulating on the spot

Listen to a short English sentence read by a partner, or recorded by yourself in advance, and give an isiZulu equivalent aloud within a few seconds, using the pausing strategy from the pronunciation section rather than translating word by word.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: mediating a meeting

With a partner, role play a short exchange in which one person speaks English and the other speaks isiZulu, with a third learner mediating aloud between them, summarizing rather than translating each turn.

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself giving a spoken English summary of Nomvula's isiZulu essay, then listen back and check whether you preserved the claim, the evidence, the counterpoint, and the conclusion without simply translating sentence by sentence.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — The mediator as a trusted go-between

In many South African community settings, a bilingual family member, colleague, or committee member is regularly asked to carry meaning between English language institutions and isiZulu speaking residents, and back again. This role carries real responsibility, since a funder, official, or family member on the other side often has no way to check the mediator's rendering against the original.

Culture in Context — Preserving someone else's voice

When mediating a document such as Nomvula's essay, a careful mediator preserves the original writer's stance and tone, rather than softening a strong position or sharpening a mild one to suit the mediator's own view. This is treated as a matter of basic fairness to the original speaker or writer, not merely a stylistic choice.

Regional Variation — Expectations for funding documents vary

The level of formality, and the specific terms, expected in funding or grant related correspondence can differ between organizations and provinces. This chapter's example reflects one common, widely understood pattern, not a fixed national standard.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Translating word by word instead of meaning by meaning

Error: Moving through the source text one word or phrase at a time, producing a target language sentence that follows the source word order rather than natural isiZulu or English structure.

Correction: Read the whole passage first, identify the core claim, and build a new sentence in the target language that expresses that claim naturally.

Common Mistake — Forcing a false equivalent for a concept without one

Error: Choosing the closest sounding word for a term such as "matched funding," even when that word does not carry the same meaning in the target language.

Correction: Use a short explanatory phrase that accurately describes the concept, rather than a single word that misleads the reader.

Common Mistake — Losing or shifting the original writer's position

Error: Softening, sharpening, or omitting part of the original claim, evidence, or counterpoint while mediating it into the other language.

Correction: Check the mediated version against a summary of the source, confirming that the claim, evidence, counterpoint, and conclusion all survive the mediation intact.

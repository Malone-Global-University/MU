Volume IV, Chapter 16: Writing Extended Arguments · Speak and Write Zulu: Advanced Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Organize an extended piece of writing around a clear central claim, rather than a list of loosely connected observations.
  2. Support a claim with evidence drawn from something you heard, read, or observed, instead of unsupported opinion alone.
  3. Introduce a counterpoint fairly, then respond to it, without dismissing an opposing position.
  4. Use ngakho-ke, ngakolunye uhlangothi, and related connectors to move a reader from point to point.
  5. Write a conclusion that states a position clearly, rather than simply restating the introduction.
  6. Revise a draft essay for organization, attribution, and register, using a self-editing checklist.

Pronunciation: Reading an Essay Aloud

A written essay is not simply a speech laid out on a page, but a learner preparing to present a position essay aloud, as part of this chapter's performance task, still needs to attend to phrase rhythm and penultimate lengthening, the same anchors used for listening in Chapter 15. Reading a long, well organized sentence aloud without pausing in the wrong place is a separate skill from reading it silently, and it deserves separate practice.

Pronunciation Focus — Reading connectors as signals, not as filler

When a connector such as ngakho-ke or ngakolunye uhlangothi opens a sentence, a reader should give it a small, deliberate pause before continuing, since it signals a shift for the listener in the same way it signals a shift for a reader's eye on the page. Rushing past a connector removes the very cue that makes an argument easy to follow aloud.

  • Give a brief pause after an opening connector, then continue at normal pace.
  • Let penultimate lengthening mark the end of each main clause, even in a long sentence.
  • Practice reading your own draft aloud before treating it as finished.

Language Focus: Organizing an Extended Argument

Purpose: this section does not introduce a new grammatical system. It applies the reciprocal, contrastive, and conditional patterns from Chapter 14, together with the discourse markers gathered in Chapter 15, to the specific task of organizing a written position essay.

16.1 The shape of a position essay

Language Focus

A position essay in this chapter follows four connected parts: a clear opening claim, evidence that supports the claim, a counterpoint stated fairly and answered, and a conclusion that restates the position in slightly different words. Each part uses language already active in the course, organized in a new sequence.

  • Isitatimende sokuqala (opening claim) A single sentence stating the writer's position clearly, before any supporting detail is given.
  • Ubufakazi (evidence) One or more sentences drawing on something heard, read, or observed, such as the interview or lecture from Chapter 15.
  • Umbono ophikisayo, uphendulwe (counterpoint, answered) A sentence stating an opposing view fairly, usually opened with kodwa or nakuba, followed by a response to it.
  • Isiphetho (conclusion) A closing sentence, often opened with ngakho-ke, restating the position in the writer's own words rather than repeating the opening sentence exactly.

16.2 Moving from a claim to a supported paragraph

Strategy — From a note-taking column to a paragraph
Isikhulumi, Umbono → Isitatimende → Ubufakazi → Isiphetho esifushane
A single row from the Chapter 15 note-taking table can grow into a full paragraph. State the position as a claim, add the supporting detail as evidence, and close with a short sentence connecting it to the writer's own view, rather than leaving the note as an isolated fragment.
Ngokwami umbono, isu likaThandeka lifanele lesekelwe. Njengoba uDkt Sithole eshilo, umsebenzi wamahhala uyehla uma ungasekelwa uhlelo oluqinile. Ngakho-ke, isikhundla esisodwa esiholelwayo asizona izinsizakalo ezingadingekile, kodwa iyona nto eqinisekisa ukuthi umklamo uyaqhubeka.
In my view, Thandeka's proposal deserves support. As Dr Sithole said, volunteer work declines when it is not supported by a firm structure. Therefore, one paid position is not an unnecessary expense, but the very thing that ensures the project continues.

This paragraph pattern is used directly in the writing workshop below, and again in the performance task at the end of the chapter.

Reading Text: Isibonelo Sesitatimende

Read the model position essay below once for the main idea, then a second time to identify its four parts: the opening claim, the evidence, the counterpoint and response, and the conclusion.

Kungani Ngisekela Isu Likathandeka — Isitatimende SikaNomvula

Ngiyasekela isu likaThandeka lokuqasha umuntu oyedwa ozoqondisa imisebenzi yomphakathi. Ngesikhathi sengxoxo yakulo Isikhathi Sethu, uDkt Sithole wachaza ukuthi umsebenzi wamahhala uyasiza ekuqaleni komklamo, kodwa intshiseko yabantu iyehla njengoba izikhathi zishintsha nemindeni idinga ukuzondla. Lokhu kufakazela lokho umphakathi wakithi asewukubonile ngokwawo: abantu abaningi asebekhathele, futhi umklamo ubungeke uqhubeke ngaphandle kwesikhundla esiqinisekisayo. Ngiyaqonda ukuthi uNkk Mahlangu naye uneqiniso, njengoba uMasipala ungenayo yonke imali edingekayo, futhi kufanele siwuhloniphe lowo mbono. Nakuba kunjalo, ukwesaba izindleko akufanele kuvimbele uhlelo olungasiza umphakathi isikhathi eside. Ngakolunye uhlangothi, uma umphakathi nawo unikela ingxenye, njengoba uNkk Mahlangu esho, izindleko azisona eziphelele zikaMasipala kuphela. Ngakho-ke, ngicabanga ukuthi indlela engcono ukuthi ikomiti yomphakathi kanye noMasipala basebenzisane ngokushesha ekumiseni uhlelo lokuxhasa okuhlanganyelwe, ngaphambi kokuba intshiseko yabantu iqale iphele.

Umsebenzi wokufunda — Reading task:

  1. Yisiphi isitatimende sokuqala uNomvula asibeka ekuqaleni komqulu wakhe? (What opening claim does Nomvula state at the beginning of her essay?)
  2. Yiziphi izinkomba ezimbili azisebenzisa njengobufakazi? (Which two pieces of evidence does she use?)
  3. Uwuphendula kanjani umbono kaNkk Mahlangu, engakaze amchithe ngokuphelele? (How does she respond to Nkk Mahlangu's view, without dismissing it entirely?)
  4. Isiphetho sakhe sisho ini ngokucophelela okungeziwe kunesitatimende sokuqala? (What does her conclusion add beyond simply repeating the opening claim?)

Writing Workshop

Write your own position essay in Standard isiZulu, responding to the coordinator proposal debate from Chapter 15, using the four-part structure introduced above.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write ten to fourteen sentences, organized into an opening claim, at least two sentences of supporting evidence drawn from the Chapter 15 interview or lecture, a counterpoint stated fairly and answered, and a conclusion. Use at least two different connectors from the language focus section, and attribute each borrowed position to the correct speaker.

  1. State your claim. Write one clear sentence giving your own position on the coordinator proposal, before adding any supporting detail.
  2. Gather your evidence. Return to your Chapter 15 notes and select two details, one from the interview and one from the lecture, that support your claim.
  3. State the counterpoint fairly. Write one sentence presenting the opposing concern, usually Nkk Mahlangu's financial position, in terms she herself would recognize as fair.
  4. Answer the counterpoint. Write one or two sentences responding to that concern, without dismissing it outright.
  5. Close with a conclusion. Write one or two sentences restating your position in new words, opened with ngakho-ke or a similar connector.
  6. Audit your draft. Apply the Chapter 19 self-editing routine from Volume III, checking especially that borrowed positions are attributed to the correct speaker and that the counterpoint is not dismissed unfairly.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • The opening sentence states a clear claim, not a general topic statement.
  • At least two pieces of evidence are drawn from the Chapter 15 materials and attributed correctly.
  • The counterpoint is stated fairly before it is answered.
  • At least two connectors organize the movement between paragraphs.
  • The conclusion restates the position in different words rather than repeating the opening sentence.
  • Register remains formal and consistent throughout.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: reading your claim aloud

Read your opening claim and conclusion aloud to a partner, or record yourself, applying the phrase-boundary and connector-pausing strategies from the pronunciation section.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: an editorial exchange

With a partner, exchange draft essays and identify each other's claim, evidence, counterpoint, and conclusion aloud, then discuss one place where the argument could be made clearer.

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Read your finished essay aloud in full, recording yourself, then listen back and note any sentence where the connector or the counterpoint was unclear when spoken rather than read.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — The community newsletter as a forum for written opinion

Community newsletters, church bulletins, and local publications offer a space for residents to put a position in writing, alongside more formal media coverage, and reaching this audience well often depends less on formal argumentative technique than on clearly acknowledging neighbors whose views differ.

Culture in Context — Disagreeing in print without damaging a relationship

A written position that will be read by people the writer knows personally, such as a municipal officer or a neighbor, calls for particular care in how a counterpoint is framed, since a published piece is harder to soften afterward than a spoken remark. Acknowledging the other side's reasoning before disagreeing with it, as the model essay does, is one widely valued way of managing this.

Regional Variation — Newsletter style varies by publication

Expectations for length, tone, and directness in a community newsletter piece can differ between publications and between regions. This chapter's model reflects one common, widely understood style, not the only acceptable approach.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Opening with background instead of a claim

Error: Beginning the essay with general background about the debate, leaving the writer's own position unstated until the final sentence, or never stated at all.

Correction: State the claim in the opening sentence, then use the following sentences to provide background and evidence.

Common Mistake — Dismissing the counterpoint instead of answering it

Error: Presenting the opposing view only to immediately reject it as wrong, without acknowledging why a reasonable person might hold it.

Correction: State the counterpoint in terms its holder would recognize as fair, then explain why the writer's position still stands.

Common Mistake — A conclusion that only repeats the opening sentence

Error: Closing the essay by copying the opening claim word for word, without showing how the evidence and counterpoint have developed it.

Correction: Restate the position in new words, referring briefly to the evidence or the counterpoint discussed in the body of the essay.

Answer Key

Model Essay — Comprehension

  1. Nomvula opens by stating that she supports Thandeka's proposal to employ one coordinator for community projects.
  2. She acknowledges that Nkk Mahlangu's financial concern is reasonable and deserves respect, before explaining why she still holds her own position.
  3. She responds by noting that if the community also contributes a portion, as Nkk Mahlangu herself suggested, the cost is not the municipality's alone to carry.
  4. Her conclusion adds a specific recommendation, that the committee and the municipality act quickly to set up joint funding, rather than simply repeating that she supports the proposal.

Writing Strategy

  1. The four parts are an opening claim, supporting evidence, a fairly stated and answered counterpoint, and a conclusion that restates the position in new words.
  2. Accepted connectors include ngakho-ke, ngakolunye uhlangothi, nakuba, and kodwa.

Ukulawula Amaphutha Ohlelweni Oluphakeme

Volume IV, Chapter 18: Error Control in High-Stakes Systems · Speak and Write Zulu: Advanced Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify concord drift across a long noun phrase, clause, or paragraph, and repair it so agreement is carried correctly from the noun to every dependent form.
  2. Recognize click reduction in your own connected speech, and apply a targeted repair strategy rather than slowing your entire delivery.
  3. Identify tense, aspect, and mood ambiguity created by an underspecified or misplaced marker, and select the form that removes the ambiguity.
  4. Recognize register mismatch between a text's vocabulary, sentence length, and address forms, and revise a passage so the register stays consistent throughout.
  5. Self edit an extended piece of writing using a structured error control checklist rather than a single unfocused read through.
  6. Give a partner specific, respectful feedback on one of these four error zones in their own writing or speech.

Communication Scenario

Nomvula's committee has asked her to prepare the final, clean version of both mediated texts from Chapter 17 before they go to the funders' meeting. Her draft is strong in content, but a committee member who reads carefully points out that the concord on a modifier three clauses back does not match the noun it was meant to describe, that one sentence could mean either "the community has contributed" or "the community will contribute," and that the closing paragraph slips into a much more casual register than the opening. None of these are new grammar problems. They are the kind of small, high stakes errors that appear only once a writer is producing longer, more independent text, and that a careful reader will notice immediately.

  1. This chapter asks learners to read their own extended writing, and their own recorded speech, the way that careful committee member read Nomvula's draft.
  2. Learners practice locating and repairing four specific error zones, rather than treating revision as a vague search for "mistakes."

Pronunciation: Click Reduction Under Pressure

As speech becomes faster, longer, or more spontaneous, exactly the kind of speech this volume now expects, click consonants are the sounds most likely to weaken or disappear, especially in the middle of a long word or a rapid clause. A speaker under pressure often keeps the surrounding vowels and consonants intact while letting the click itself soften into an ordinary stop. A listener may still follow the general meaning, but the word can become unclear, or can shift toward a different word entirely, since click place and manner are often the only feature separating two otherwise similar forms.

Pronunciation Focus — Finding your own reduction points

Rather than practicing clicks in isolation again, this chapter asks you to find where clicks weaken specifically in your own extended speech, since the pattern is often personal and consistent from one speaker to the next.

  • Record yourself reading a paragraph you wrote at ordinary conversational speed, then a second time slightly slower.
  • Mark any click that softened, shortened, or dropped at the faster speed, rather than every click in the passage.
  • Practice those specific words at full speed in isolation before returning them to the sentence, rather than slowing the whole sentence down permanently.

Language Focus: Four Error Zones

Purpose: this section does not introduce a new grammatical system. It organizes four already active systems, concord, click production, tense/aspect/mood, and register, into a repeatable error control method for extended, independent text.

18.1 Concord drift across distance

Language Focus

Concord drift happens when a noun sets an agreement pattern early in a sentence or paragraph, and a modifier, pronoun, or verb several words or clauses later fails to carry that same pattern, often because the writer has, in effect, shifted attention to a different noun without noticing. Drift is rare in short sentences, where the noun and its concord sit close together, and becomes far more common in the long noun phrases, stacked modifiers, and cross clause reference that this volume now expects.

  • Umbiko lo, owabhalwa ngumphakathi, uhlanganisa imininingwane emiqingo eyaziwayo This report, which was written by the community, brings together the details on the known items. concord chain: umbiko (Class 3) → owabhalwa → uhlanganisa, all Class 3
  • Drift example: Umbiko lo, owabhalwa ngumphakathi, bahlanganisa imininingwane Same sentence, but the final verb concord has drifted to a Class 2 pattern that matches "umphakathi" instead of "umbiko," producing an unintended subject. error: uhlanganisa (Class 3, correct) replaced by bahlanganisa (Class 2, drift)

18.2 TAM ambiguity from underspecified marking

Language Focus

Certain tense, aspect, and mood markers carry more than one possible reading when a sentence is short of context, particularly forms that sit near the boundary between a completed and an ongoing action, or between a statement and a polite instruction. In a short, isolated sentence this ambiguity rarely causes a problem, because the surrounding conversation supplies the missing context. In a formal written document read by someone who cannot ask a follow up question, the same ambiguity can change what a reader believes was promised or reported.

  • Umphakathi unikele imali Ambiguous out of context: the community has contributed money, or the community contributes money, depending on the surrounding aspectual cues.
  • Repair: Umphakathi usuwunikele imali ngesikhathi sokubhalwa kombiko lo Repaired for a formal document: the community had already contributed the money by the time this report was written, removing the ambiguity with an explicit resultative frame.

18.3 Register mismatch across a single text

Strategy — Reading for one register at a time
Funda kanye ngamunye wesici → Bhala uhlelo lwesimo ngesigatshana ngasinye → Qhathanisa izigatshana → Lungisa lapho isimo sishintsha ngokungahleliwe
Read the text once for content, then read it again focusing only on register, noting the formality level of each paragraph in turn, compare those notes across the whole text, and revise any paragraph where the register shifts without a deliberate reason.
Isigaba sokuqala sombiko sisebenzisa amagama asemthethweni njengokuthi "isicelo" nokuthi "ukugunyazwa," kodwa isigaba sokugcina sizithela sisebenzisa amagama angaphansi kwesimo, njengokuthi "nje kufanele nje niphendule ngokushesha." Lokhu kuguqula isimo phakathi nombhalo owodwa, ngakho isigaba sokugcina kudingeka silungiswe sifane nesokuqala.
The report's opening section uses formal terms such as "application" and "authorization," but the closing section slips into casual language, such as "you just need to reply quickly." This shifts the register within a single document, so the closing section needs to be revised to match the opening.

This method, reading once for each error zone rather than searching for every kind of error at once, is used directly in the writing workshop below, and again in the performance task at the end of the chapter.

Reading Text: Uhlolo Lombhalo KaNomvula

Read the passage below, an early draft of one paragraph from Nomvula's report to the funders' meeting, alongside the committee member's handwritten notes in the margin. Then read the corrected version that follows.

Uhlaka Lokuqala — First Draft, With Marginal Notes

Umphakathi, osuwuqoqe imali eningi kusukela ngonyaka odlule, bafuna ukubona uMasipala enikela imali elingana nalena. [Umhleli: "bafuna" akufani nesigaba "umphakathi" — drift] Bebekhokhile ngaphambili, futhi bazokhokha futhi ngesikhathi esizayo. [Umhleli: yisiphi isikhathi, esidlule noma esizayo — akucaci]

Uhlaka Olulungisiwe — Corrected Version

Umphakathi, osuwuqoqe imali eningi kusukela ngonyaka odlule, ufuna ukubona uMasipala enikela imali elingana nalena. Ususuqoqile lolu suku, futhi uzoqhubeka nokuqoqa uma isidingo sisekhona.

Umsebenzi wokufunda — Reading task:

  1. Yiliphi igama esigabeni sokuqala eliqondile ngephutha lesimo? Chaza kabanzi ukuthi liphuthelwa kuphi. (Which word in the first draft is flagged for a concord error? Explain exactly where it goes wrong.)
  2. Yisiphi isikhathi lesi esilungisiwe kunyaka olandelayo, esidlule noma esizayo? (What tense does the corrected version settle on, past or future?)
  3. Qhathanisa amagama asetshenziswe esigabeni sokuqala nalawo asesigabeni esilungisiwe. Sishintshe kanjani isimo? (Compare the vocabulary used in the first draft with the corrected version. How does the register change?)

Writing Workshop

Complete an error control pass on a piece of your own extended writing, ideally one of the two mediated texts you produced in Chapter 17.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 1 — Uhlolo lwesimo sokuvumelana: Underline every noun phrase in your text that is modified, referred back to, or that governs a verb more than one clause later. For each one, trace the concord chain from the noun to its final dependent form, and correct any drift you find.

Self edit prompt
Choose one paragraph from your Chapter 17 mediation. Circle the first noun in the paragraph that carries an extended concord chain. Follow that chain through the rest of the paragraph, marking every point where a later form should match it.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 2 — Uhlolo lwesikhathi: Reread the same text a second time, checking only for tense, aspect, and mood ambiguity. Where a sentence could be read two ways by someone who cannot ask you a follow up question, add the explicit marking needed to remove the ambiguity.

  1. Read once for content only. Confirm the text still says what you intended before checking for errors.
  2. Read a second time for concord. Trace every extended concord chain from its noun to its final dependent form.
  3. Read a third time for TAM. Flag any sentence that could be read with more than one tense, aspect, or mood.
  4. Read a fourth time for register. Note the formality level of each paragraph and revise any paragraph that drifts from the rest.
  5. Read the text aloud once. Mark any word where a click weakened or dropped at ordinary speaking speed.
  6. Produce the corrected version. Apply every marked correction and read the result once more for overall coherence.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • Every extended concord chain matches its governing noun from beginning to end.
  • No sentence carries an unintended tense, aspect, or mood reading.
  • The register stays consistent from the opening paragraph to the closing paragraph.
  • Every click in the text has been checked aloud at ordinary speaking speed.
  • Corrections have been applied to a clean final version, not only marked in the margin.
  • The corrected text still reads naturally, not as a list of separately patched sentences.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: the concord drift drill

Read a partner's long sentence aloud, then extend it with your own clause that continues the same noun phrase, keeping the concord chain intact through your addition.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: the register check partner

Read your corrected Chapter 17 mediation aloud to a partner without telling them which paragraph was revised for register. Ask them to identify any point where the formality still feels inconsistent.

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself reading your corrected text at ordinary speaking speed, then listen back specifically for click reduction, marking any word that needs further practice.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Careful reading as a form of respect

In many South African community and institutional settings, a document that reaches a funder, a municipal office, or an elder is expected to have been checked carefully before it is sent, since a careless error can be read as a lack of respect for the reader's time and authority, not merely as a technical mistake. Treating error control as a required stage of writing, rather than an optional final glance, reflects this expectation.

Culture in Context — Giving correction without causing offense

When a committee member points out an error in someone else's draft, the correction is usually framed carefully, often naming the strength of the document before raising the specific problem. Peer feedback in this chapter's speaking tasks should follow the same pattern, identifying what works well before naming a specific error zone.

Regional Variation — Editorial expectations vary by institution

Some institutions expect a single, highly formal register throughout a document, while others accept a warmer, more direct closing paragraph even in a formal report. This chapter's example reflects one common expectation, not a fixed national standard, so learners should adjust their register checks to the expectations of their actual audience where those are known.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Losing the concord chain across a long sentence

Error: Allowing a modifier, pronoun, or verb late in a sentence to agree with the nearest noun rather than the noun the sentence is actually describing.

Correction: Trace the concord chain explicitly from the governing noun to every dependent form, rather than relying on what sounds right at the point of writing.

Common Mistake — Leaving a TAM marker underspecified in formal writing

Error: Using a tense, aspect, or mood form that would be clear in conversation but that leaves a formal, unsupervised reader uncertain about what actually happened or was promised.

Correction: Add an explicit time or aspect frame in formal writing whenever the marker alone could support more than one reading.

Common Mistake — Editing for only one error zone at a time and calling it finished

Error: Reading a text once, correcting whatever is noticed, and treating the document as fully checked.

Correction: Use a separate, focused read for each error zone, concord, TAM, register, and click production, since a single unfocused read reliably misses at least one of them.

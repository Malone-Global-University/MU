Ukulalela Inkulumo Eside

Volume IV, Chapter 15: Listening to Extended Speech · Speak and Write Zulu: Advanced Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Follow a radio interview involving several speakers, without needing every word to be repeated or slowed down.
  2. Identify who is speaking at a given moment, using voice, role, and context rather than a printed speaker label.
  3. Take brief notes while listening, capturing a speaker's position, a stated condition, and an outcome.
  4. Follow a short public lecture delivered at a natural, unscripted pace.
  5. Distinguish the main idea of an extended passage from supporting detail and side comment.
  6. Summarize, in your own words, what was agreed, proposed, or left unresolved in a multi-speaker recording.

Communication Scenario

A week after the community forum, a local radio programme, Isikhathi Sethu, invites Nomvula and a municipal officer, Nkk Mahlangu, to discuss Thandeka's coordinator proposal on air. The presenter, Bab' Ngcobo, opens the segment, brings in each guest, and occasionally interrupts to ask a follow-up question. Later in the same programme, a short recorded lecture by a development researcher, Dokotela Sithole, is played, addressing volunteer labour and paid coordination in community projects more broadly. Unlike the forum in Chapter 14, learners here are not participants. They must follow speech that was not shaped for their response, at a pace they cannot control, while keeping track of who is speaking and what each speaker contributes.

  1. This chapter extends the reciprocal forms, contrastive clauses, and conditional clauses consolidated in Chapter 14 into a purely receptive listening context.
  2. Learners practice tracking multiple speakers, taking notes, and summarizing an extended recording without pausing it for every unfamiliar word.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 14 asked learners to hold the floor themselves, acknowledging, disagreeing, and proposing in real time. Chapter 15 removes that control. The recording plays at its own pace, the presenter interrupts when he chooses, and the lecture segment continues whether or not every phrase has been understood. This is the shift from participating in discourse to processing it, a shift the volume scope schedules deliberately for this point in Unit 3. No new grammar system is introduced here. The reciprocal extension, contrastive clauses, conditional clauses, and formal register from Chapter 14 all continue, now inside unscripted, natural-paced speech.

Coming From Chapter 14

Consolidated reciprocal verbs, contrastive clauses with kodwa and nakuba, and conditional clauses with uma, used to acknowledge, disagree, and propose a compromise.

New in This Chapter

Sustained, unscripted, multi-speaker listening: interviews, presenter interjections, and a short lecture, processed at natural pace with note-taking rather than transcription.

Looking Forward

Chapter 16 turns from listening to extended speech toward producing extended writing, organizing the same kind of material into a structured essay.

Pronunciation: Following Unfamiliar Voices

A studio recording introduces sounds that a classroom dialogue usually avoids: overlapping speech, a presenter's brisker pace, and voices the learner has not heard before. Standard isiZulu prosody still applies, penultimate lengthening still marks the end of a phrase, and each speaker's turn still opens at a more measured pace than its middle, but a learner who expects only slow, evenly spaced classroom speech will be caught off guard by a presenter who speaks quickly and a guest who pauses mid-sentence to choose a word.

Pronunciation Focus — Listening for phrase boundaries, not word boundaries

When speech is fast and unfamiliar, listening word by word causes learners to fall behind. Instead, listen for the lengthened penultimate syllable that marks the end of a phrase, and use that boundary to anchor your understanding, even if a word or two inside the phrase was missed. A presenter's interjections are often short and rise slightly in pitch, marking a question rather than a new statement.

  • Anchor on phrase-final lengthening, not on every syllable.
  • Expect a guest's turn to open more slowly than it continues.
  • Recognize a presenter's brief, rising interjection as a question, even without seeing it written.

Language Focus: Tracking Speech You Cannot Pause

Purpose: this section does not introduce a new grammatical system. It consolidates discourse markers and reference-tracking devices already active in the course, so that learners can follow who is speaking, what position they hold, and how one turn connects to the next, without transcript support.

15.1 Markers that signal a shift between speakers or ideas

Language Focus

A small set of connecting words does most of the work of guiding a listener through extended speech. These words were encountered earlier in the course; here they are gathered as listening anchors, not as new grammar to learn.

  • manje now, at this point — often signals the presenter is moving to a new question or a new guest
  • ngakolunye uhlangothi on the other hand — signals a contrasting view is coming, similar in function to kodwa
  • ngakho-ke therefore, so then — signals a conclusion or a summary point
  • njengoba ngishilo as I said — signals a speaker returning to an earlier point after an interruption

15.2 Note-taking in three columns

Strategy — Isikhulumi, Umbono, Isiphetho
Speaker | Position or claim | Outcome or condition
Rather than writing down full sentences while a recording plays, keep three short columns: who is speaking, what they claim or want, and what condition or result they attach to it. This mirrors the reciprocal, contrastive, and conditional patterns practiced in Chapter 14, applied now to listening rather than production.
Nkk Mahlangu — umasipala ungakwazi ukuxhasa ngokuhlanganyela — uma umphakathi nawo unikela ingxenye
Nkk Mahlangu — the municipality could contribute jointly — if the community also contributes a portion. Notice the pattern is captured without a full transcription.

This note-taking pattern is used directly in the listening tasks below, and again in the performance task at the end of the chapter.

Listening Text: Ohlelweni Lomsakazo

The transcript below represents an unscripted-style radio segment. In this chapter, read it once at normal pace without stopping at every unfamiliar word, then use the tasks that follow to check what you understood. Speaker labels are provided here for study purposes; a genuine recording would not display them on screen.

Setting: Isikhathi Sethu, a community affairs radio programme. Bab' Ngcobo is the presenter, Nkk Mahlangu represents the municipality, and Nomvula is the speaker from Chapters 13 and 14. Register: broadcast, formal but conversational, with occasional interruption.

Bab' Ngcobo: Sanibonani, nilalele Isikhathi Sethu. Namuhla sikhuluma ngesu lokuqasha umuntu oyedwa ozoqondisa imisebenzi yomphakathi. Good day, you are listening to Isikhathi Sethu. Today we are discussing the proposal to employ one person to coordinate community projects.
Bab' Ngcobo: Nkk Mahlangu, manje, uMasipala unombono muni ngalesi sikhundla esisha? Nkk Mahlangu, now, what view does the municipality hold about this new position?
Nkk Mahlangu: Siyasamukela isu likaThandeka, kodwa uMasipala ngeke wakwazi ukukhokhela sonke isamba sodwa. We welcome Thandeka's proposal, but the municipality will not be able to pay the whole amount alone.
Nkk Mahlangu: Uma umphakathi nawo unikela ingxenye, sicabanga ukuthi lokhu kungaphumelela ngonyaka wokuqala. If the community also contributes a portion, we think this could succeed in the first year.
Nomvula: Ngiyabuqonda ubunzima bezimali bukaMasipala, kodwa umphakathi wakithi sewuvele uzinikele kakhulu ngesikhathi nangemali encane. I understand the municipality's financial difficulty, but our community has already contributed a great deal, in time and in small amounts of money.
Bab' Ngcobo: Ngiyaphazamisa nje, Nomvula, ubani ozoqoka lo muntu ozoqondisa umsebenzi? Just interrupting, Nomvula, who will select the person who will coordinate the work?
Nomvula: Njengoba ngishilo emhlanganweni, sivumelene ukuthi ikomiti yomphakathi izosebenzisana noMasipala ekukhetheni umuntu ofanele. As I said at the meeting, we agreed that the community committee will work together with the municipality to select the right person.
Nkk Mahlangu: Ngakolunye uhlangothi, kufanele sicacise ukuthi lesi sikhundla asisona esikaMasipala ngokuphelele, kodwa esokusekela umphakathi. On the other hand, we must clarify that this position does not belong entirely to the municipality, but supports the community.
Bab' Ngcobo: Ngakho-ke, kunethemba lokuthi sizoqala ukubona lo mklamo usebenza kungakapheli nyaka. Therefore, there is hope that we will begin to see this project working before the year ends.

Listen once without pausing, using the phrase-boundary strategy from the pronunciation section. Then complete the three-column notes below from memory, before checking against the transcript.

Listening Text: Inkulumo Emfushane

Later in the same programme, a short recorded lecture is played. Unlike the interview above, this is a single continuous voice, delivered without interruption. Listen for the main idea first, and treat supporting detail as secondary.

Dokotela Sithole ekhuluma ngomsebenzi wamahhala nokuqashwa komuntu oyedwa

Emiphakathini eminingi, umsebenzi wamahhala usiza ukuqala umklamo, kodwa awuhlali ukwazi ukuwuqhuba isikhathi eside. Uma abantu benikela ngesikhathi sabo bengaholi, ekuqaleni bavame ukuzimisela, kodwa lokho kuyehla njengoba izikhathi zishintsha, njengoba imindeni idinga ukuzondla, futhi njengoba abanye behamba bayosebenza kwezinye izindawo. Ngenxa yalokho, imiphakathi eminingi esewaphumelela isebenzisa indlela ehlanganisa umsebenzi wamahhala nesikhundla esisodwa esiholelwayo, lapho lowo muntu eba nguye oqinisekisa ukuthi umsebenzi uyaqhubeka nasemuva kokuba intshiseko yokuqala isiphungukile. Lokhu akusho ukuthi umsebenzi wamahhala awubalulekile, kusho nje ukuthi udinga ukusekelwa uhlelo oluqinile ukuze uphumelele isikhathi eside.

Umsebenzi wokulalela — Listening task:

  1. Chaza ngamafuphi umbono omkhulu kaDokotela Sithole. (State the main idea of Dr Sithole's talk in a few words.)
  2. Ngokusho kwakhe, kungani intshiseko yomsebenzi wamahhala ivama ukwehla? (According to him, why does enthusiasm for volunteer work tend to decline?)
  3. Yiziphi izinkomba ezimbili ezisekela ukuthi lo mbono uhambisana nesu likaThandeka esixoxwe ngaso Isahluko 14? (Name two details that show this talk supports the kind of proposal Thandeka raised in Chapter 14.)

Writing Workshop

Turn your listening notes into a short written summary, in Standard isiZulu, of what you heard across both segments of the programme.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write six to eight sentences summarizing the radio segment. Include at least one sentence reporting Nkk Mahlangu's position, at least one sentence reporting Nomvula's position, and at least one sentence connecting Dokotela Sithole's lecture to the proposal discussed by the two guests. Use ngakho-ke or ngakolunye uhlangothi at least once to organize your summary.

  1. Review your notes. Look back at the three-column notes from the interview and your answers to the lecture questions.
  2. Group by speaker. Draft one or two sentences reporting each guest's position, in your own words rather than copying the transcript.
  3. Connect the segments. Write one or two sentences linking the lecture's general point to the specific proposal discussed in the interview.
  4. Close with an assessment. Write one sentence stating whether, in your view, the two guests are moving toward agreement.
  5. Audit your draft. Apply the Chapter 19 self-editing routine from Volume III, checking especially that reported positions are attributed to the correct speaker.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • Each guest's position is reported accurately and attributed correctly.
  • At least one discourse marker, ngakho-ke or ngakolunye uhlangothi, organizes the summary.
  • The lecture is connected explicitly to the interview, not summarized separately without a link.
  • The summary is written in the learner's own words, not copied from the transcript.
  • Register remains formal and consistent throughout.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: reporting a position aloud

Using your three-column notes, state aloud, in one or two sentences, what Nkk Mahlangu proposed and what condition she attached to it. Then do the same for Nomvula.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: a listener call-in

With a partner, take turns playing a listener who calls the programme to comment on what was said. React to one guest's position, using kodwa or nakuba, and connect your comment to a point from the lecture.

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself giving a thirty-second spoken summary of the whole programme, as if reporting it to someone who missed the broadcast. Listen back and check that both guests' positions are represented fairly.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Community affairs radio as public discourse

Community affairs programmes on local radio are a common forum for public discussion in many isiZulu-speaking areas, giving officials, community members, and specialists a shared platform to explain positions to a wide audience. Listening to this kind of programme is a practical, everyday extension of the skills practiced in classroom dialogues.

Culture in Context — The presenter's role in shaping a discussion

A presenter on this kind of programme is expected to remain fair to all guests while still asking direct, sometimes uncomfortable, questions on the audience's behalf. A brief, polite interruption, such as ngiyaphazamisa nje, just interrupting, is an accepted way for a presenter to redirect a guest without appearing disrespectful.

Regional Variation — Broadcast pace and vocabulary can vary by station

Presenting style, pace, and vocabulary choice can differ between community stations, national broadcasters, and online programmes, and between regions. This chapter's transcript reflects a common, widely understood broadcast register, not the only style a learner may encounter.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Trying to transcribe instead of taking notes

Error: Attempting to write down every word while a recording plays, which causes the listener to fall behind and miss the next speaker's turn entirely.

Correction: Use the three-column strategy, speaker, position, condition or outcome, and accept that some detail will be missed on a first listen.

Common Mistake — Losing track of who is speaking

Error: Attributing a position to the wrong speaker after an interruption, especially when a presenter's brief question separates two parts of the same guest's turn.

Correction: Listen for njengoba ngishilo, as I said, and similar phrases, which signal a speaker resuming an earlier point after interruption.

Common Mistake — Summarizing the lecture and the interview as unrelated

Error: Writing two disconnected summaries, one for the interview and one for the lecture, without stating how the lecture's general point relates to the specific proposal.

Correction: Use a connecting sentence, often opened with ngakho-ke, to state explicitly how the lecture supports, challenges, or explains what the guests discussed.

Looking Ahead

Okungakwenzeka manje — What you can do now

You can follow a multi-speaker interview at natural pace, track who is speaking and what position they hold, take brief three-column notes instead of transcribing, follow a short unscripted lecture, and produce a spoken or written summary that fairly connects several sources of extended speech.

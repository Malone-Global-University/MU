Volume IV, Chapter 5: Advanced Relative and Subordinate Clauses · Speak and Write Zulu: Advanced Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Connect two ideas with a causal subordinate clause, explaining why something happens or why a rule exists.
  2. Connect two ideas with a temporal subordinate clause, showing when one event happens in relation to another.
  3. Build a conditional clause with uma, distinguishing a genuine condition from a simple statement of time.
  4. Build a purpose clause with the subjunctive, explaining what a rule or action is meant to achieve.
  5. Combine a contrast connector with a formal explanation, without weakening the register established in Chapter 4.
  6. Maintain reciprocal, neuter-stative, causative, and passive extension accuracy while working inside longer, subordinated sentences.

Communication Scenario

Nomvula's explanation of the registration procedure went well enough that the faculty office has asked her to prepare a short written explanation of the attendance policy for the same group of new students. This task is harder than the last one, because an attendance policy cannot simply list steps in order. It must explain why the rule exists, when it applies, what happens under a particular condition, and what a student should do if their circumstances change. Nomvula realizes she cannot rely on short, separate sentences the way she did before. She must learn to link her ideas together, so that a reason, a moment in time, a condition, and a purpose can all sit inside one clearly organized explanation.

  1. This chapter activates causal, temporal, conditional, and purpose subordination for full productive use inside formal explanatory prose.
  2. Learners then write and present a short formal explanation of a rule or policy, connecting at least three of these clause types.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 4 activated the reciprocal and neuter-stative extensions, and asked learners to carry a relative clause across several sentences of formal prose. Chapter 5 keeps that same formal register and verb accuracy, but shifts the challenge from naming and describing to reasoning and connecting. Learners now join clauses together directly, rather than relying on separate short sentences placed side by side, so that a single explanation can carry a reason, a time reference, a condition, and a purpose without losing its formal shape.

Amagama Ezizathu, Isikhathi, Nemibandela — Vocabulary for Reasons, Time, and Conditions

Vocabulary: Reasons, Time, Conditions, and Purpose

This chapter introduces the connecting words that carry a formal explanation from one idea to the next, since a policy explanation depends on showing how a reason, a moment, a condition, and a purpose relate to one another, not merely on naming them in isolation.

Pronunciation and Pacing in Connected Reasoning

No new sound appears in this chapter. Instead, learners practice pacing a longer sentence so that a listener can follow the reason, the time reference, and the condition without losing the thread, since a subordinate clause loses its force when it is rushed together with the main clause as if it were an afterthought.

Pronunciation Focus — Marking the joint between clauses

A brief pause, or a slight drop in pitch, at the point where ngoba, uma, ukuze, or nokho begins a new clause helps a listener hear where one idea ends and the next begins, especially in a longer formal sentence.

  • ngenxa yokuthi (because, on account of the fact that): worth rehearsing as a single connected phrase, rather than four separate words, since it functions as one causal connector.
  • ukuya ngokuthi (depending on whether): worth rehearsing slowly, since learners sometimes rush this phrase and lose the sense of a condition-sensitive outcome.

Grammar: Subordinate Clauses of Cause, Time, Condition, and Purpose

Purpose: this section activates causal, temporal, conditional, and purpose subordination for full productive use, and shows how these clause types combine with the extensions and relative clauses already active, so that a formal explanation can reason from one idea to the next rather than presenting ideas as an unconnected list.

5.1 Causal clauses with ngoba and ngenxa yokuthi

Language Focus

A causal clause explains why the main clause is true, or why a rule exists. ngoba is the everyday causal conjunction and remains appropriate in formal prose. ngenxa yokuthi is a fuller, slightly more formal phrase, and is often preferred when the reason itself is a complete idea rather than a short fact.

  • Umgomo lo ukhona ngoba abafundi abaningi babengaqondi kahle izinsuku zabo zokubhalisa. This policy exists because many students did not clearly understand their registration dates. ngoba introduces the causal clause explaining the reason for the policy's existence

5.2 Temporal clauses with uma and ngesikhathi

Language Focus

A temporal clause locates the main clause in time, relative to another event. uma can mean if or when, and the intended meaning depends on tense and context, so learners must check whether a genuine condition or simply a moment in time is intended. ngesikhathi means at the time that, or while, and is used when two events overlap.

  • Ngesikhathi umfundi ethumela isicelo sakhe, kufanele futhi afake imibhalo edingekayo. At the time a student submits their application, they must also include the required documents. ngesikhathi locates the main clause's obligation at the same moment as the act of submitting

5.3 Conditional clauses with uma

Language Focus

When uma introduces a genuine condition rather than a simple time reference, it sets out a circumstance that determines an outcome. Formal policy explanation depends on this structure, since a rule frequently applies only under a stated condition.

  • Uma umfundi engakwazi ukufika ngesikhathi esifanele, kufanele axhumane nehhovisi lezifundo ngaphambi kwesikhathi. If a student is not able to arrive at the required time, they must contact the faculty office in advance. uma introduces the condition, engakwazi carries the negative, setting the circumstance that requires the following obligation

5.4 Purpose clauses with ukuze and the subjunctive

Language Focus

A purpose clause explains what an action or rule is meant to achieve. ukuze is followed by the subjunctive mood, marked by an e final vowel on the verb, rather than the ordinary final vowel used in a main clause.

  • Umgomo lo ubhalwe kabanzi ukuze abafundi abasha bakwazi ukubonisana nehhovisi kalula. This policy has been written in detail so that new students can consult with the office easily. ukuze introduces the purpose clause, and bakwazi carries the subjunctive final vowel e rather than the ordinary a

5.5 Contrast connectors kodwa and nokho

Language Focus

A contrast connector shows that the second idea qualifies, limits, or pushes back against the first. kodwa is common in everyday speech and remains acceptable in formal prose. nokho carries a slightly more formal, written tone, and is common in policy explanation.

  • Umgomo lo uyasebenza kubo bonke abafundi, nokho izimo ezithile zingaphathwa ngokuhlukile uma kudingeka. This policy applies to all students, however certain circumstances may be handled differently if needed. nokho signals a formal contrast, limiting the scope of the general rule stated before it

5.6 The verb template inside a subordinated sentence

Language Note — Subordination does not change the verb template
connector + subject concord + TAM marker + object concord + verb root + extension or extension sequence + final vowel
A connector such as ngoba, uma, ngesikhathi, or ukuze sits before a complete verb, built exactly as the verb template from Chapter 3 and Chapter 4 requires. The connector changes the relationship between clauses, and, for ukuze, changes the final vowel to the subjunctive e, but it does not remove the subject concord, the object concord, or any extension the sentence genuinely needs.
Umgomo lo ukhona ngoba abafundi nabasebenzi kudingeka babonisane kahle, futhi uyasebenza ukuze inqubo yonke igcwaliseke ngaphandle kwenkinga, nokho izimo ezithile zingaphathwa ngokuhlukile uma umfundi engakwazi ukulandela izinyathelo ezijwayelekile.
This policy exists because students and staff need to consult with one another properly, and it applies so that the whole procedure is completed without a problem, however certain circumstances may be handled differently if a student is not able to follow the usual steps. babonisane keeps the reciprocal, igcwaliseke keeps the neuter-stative, and engakwazi keeps the negative, all inside a single subordinated sentence.

Model Text: Nomvula Explains the Attendance Policy

Read Nomvula's written explanation of the attendance policy below. It sustains the formal register from Chapter 4, and it links causal, temporal, conditional, and purpose clauses to carry the reasoning from one idea to the next.

Incazelo kaNomvula ngomgomo wokuya ezifundweni (Nomvula's explanation of the attendance policy)

Ngokusemthethweni, ngifisa ukunichazela ngomgomo wokuya ezifundweni onqunyelwe bonke abafundi abasha balo nyaka. Umgomo lo ukhona ngoba ihhovisi lezifundo lifisa ukubona ukuthi bonke abafundi bathola ithuba elifanele lokufunda ngokugcwele. Ngesikhathi isifundo siqala, kufanele umfundi abe khona endaweni yokufunda, futhi kufanele afike ngaphambi kokuba isifundo siqale. Uma umfundi engakwazi ukufika ngesikhathi esifanele ngenxa yokuthi kukhona isizathu esiqinisekile, kufanele axhumane nomsizi wehhovisi ngaphambi kwesikhathi, ukuze udaba luqondwe kahle. Umgomo lo ubhalwe kabanzi ukuze abafundi nabasebenzi babonisane kalula uma kukhona inkinga. Umgomo uyasebenza kubo bonke abafundi, nokho izimo ezithile ezifana nokugula noma uhambo lomndeni ezingahambanga kahle zingaphathwa ngokuhlukile uma kudingeka. Ekugcineni, uma umfundi elandela lo mgomo ngokwethembeka, inqubo yakhe yonke izogcwaliseka ngaphandle kwenkinga enkulu.

The explanation opens formally, uses ngoba and ngenxa yokuthi for reasons, ngesikhathi for a time reference, uma for both a condition and a closing reflection, ukuze for two purpose clauses, and nokho to introduce a formal contrast, exactly as this chapter's grammar section requires.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Kungani umgomo wokuya ezifundweni ukhona, ngokusho kwencazelo? (Why does the attendance policy exist, according to the explanation?)
  2. Kufanele enzenjani umfundi uma engakwazi ukufika ngesikhathi esifanele? (What must a student do if they are not able to arrive at the required time?)
  3. Yiziphi izimo ezingaphathwa ngokuhlukile ngokusho komgomo? (Which circumstances may be handled differently, according to the policy?)

Writing Workshop

Write your own formal explanation of a rule or policy you know well, such as a workplace rule, a household rule, a club rule, or a rule from your studies, following the same reasoning pattern Nomvula used to explain the attendance policy.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write an explanation of eight to ten sentences, addressed to an audience of strangers. Open formally, state why the rule exists using a causal clause, describe when the rule applies using a temporal clause, describe at least one condition using uma, state the rule's purpose using ukuze, and introduce at least one exception or qualification using kodwa or nokho. Then apply the Chapter 19 self editing routine from Volume III before sharing your final version.

  1. Open formally. Greet your audience and state that you are explaining a rule or policy.
  2. State the reason. Use ngoba or ngenxa yokuthi to explain why the rule exists.
  3. State the time reference. Use ngesikhathi or uma to show when the rule applies.
  4. State a condition. Use uma to describe a circumstance that changes what a person must do.
  5. State the purpose. Use ukuze with the subjunctive to explain what the rule is meant to achieve.
  6. State an exception. Use kodwa or nokho to introduce a qualification or exception, then close with guidance.
  7. Audit your draft. Apply the Chapter 19 repair categories, then check that each connector introduces the clause type it is meant to, and that the register remains formal throughout.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • The explanation opens and closes in a consistently formal register, with no casual forms mixed in.
  • At least one causal clause and one temporal clause are built accurately.
  • At least one conditional clause with uma is built accurately, distinct from a simple time reference.
  • At least one purpose clause with ukuze carries the correct subjunctive final vowel.
  • At least one contrast connector, kodwa or nokho, introduces a genuine qualification.
  • Object concords, extensions, and negative markers built into earlier chapters remain accurate inside the longer subordinated sentences.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Explaining the reason behind a rule

In many South African institutional settings, a rule is presented alongside the reasoning that supports it, rather than as an unexplained instruction, since an audience of strangers is more likely to accept a policy when its purpose has been made clear.

Culture in Context — Naming an exception respectfully

When a formal explanation names an exception, as Nomvula does with illness or family travel, it is common to keep the description general and respectful, rather than listing every possible personal circumstance in detail.

Regional Variation — kodwa and nokho in written policy

Some institutions favor nokho for a more formal, written tone in policy documents, while others use kodwa throughout, even in writing, without this being considered less correct. Both are widely accepted, and the choice often reflects the institution's general house style.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Treating uma as always meaning if

Error: Reading every uma clause as a strict condition, even when the surrounding sentence shows that uma is simply marking a moment in time.

Correction: Check the surrounding context before deciding whether uma introduces a genuine condition or a temporal reference, since both meanings are common and neither is automatically correct.

Common Mistake — Forgetting the subjunctive after ukuze

Error: Following ukuze with the ordinary final vowel a, producing a verb form that does not match the purpose clause it is meant to complete.

Correction: Check that the verb after ukuze carries the subjunctive final vowel e, and rehearse the pattern separately if the ordinary final vowel keeps appearing by habit.

Common Mistake — Stacking too many connectors in a single sentence

Error: Joining three or four subordinate clauses into one very long sentence, so that a listener or reader loses track of which clause supports which idea.

Correction: Allow a subordinated sentence to carry one or two clauses clearly, then begin a new sentence, rather than forcing every reason, time reference, and condition into a single unbroken sentence.

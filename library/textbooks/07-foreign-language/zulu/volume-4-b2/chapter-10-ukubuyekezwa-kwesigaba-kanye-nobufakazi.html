Volume IV, Chapter 10: Unit Consolidation, B2 Performance Evidence · Speak and Write Zulu: Advanced Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Move between argumentative, correspondence, and public address registers within a single connected task, without losing register consistency.
  2. Reuse claim, evidence, counterpoint, and conclusion markers from Chapter 6 inside a formal written report and a spoken community address.
  3. Reuse the correspondence structure from Chapter 8 and the ceremonial opening and closing formulas from Chapter 9 accurately in an integrated task.
  4. Report the words and positions of other people faithfully, using the reported speech markers from Chapter 7, across both written and spoken modes.
  5. Identify and repair the most common register, structural, and reporting errors from Chapters 6 through 9 in your own draft work.
  6. Complete a connected listening, reading, writing, and speaking sequence that serves as the first B2 integrated performance check of Volume IV.

Communication Scenario

The imbizo has now taken place. Nomvula's address was well received, and several residents raised questions about how the new booking system would affect families who share a single computer at home. The ward councillor's office has asked Nomvula to do three things before the matter can move forward. First, prepare a short written summary of the residents' concerns for the student council. Second, respond formally to the department that will decide on the changes, referencing both the residents' concerns and the council chairperson's earlier position. Third, be ready to speak again at a follow up gathering, this time addressing both the concerns and the department's likely response. Nomvula realizes that the same booking system story now needs to travel convincingly through three different registers in one connected effort.

  1. This chapter does not introduce new grammar. Instead, it consolidates the systems activated in Chapters 6 through 9 for confident, connected, register aware use.
  2. Learners complete a linked sequence, a short informal summary, a formal written response, and a spoken address, all drawing on the same set of facts.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapters 6 through 9 each activated a distinct register for the same ongoing story, argument and evidence for a persuasive case, correspondence structure for a formal written report, and ceremonial address for a spoken community gathering. Chapter 10 does not add a new register. Instead, it asks learners to hold all three in view at once, choosing the right one for each part of a single connected task, and to move between them without letting one register's habits leak into another. This is the skill a confident B2 speaker actually needs, not simply knowing four separate systems, but knowing which one a moment calls for.

Coming From Chapters 6 Through 9

Productive control of argument and evidence markers, correspondence structure, reported speech, and ceremonial public address formulas, each developed separately.

New in This Chapter

No new grammar. The chapter consolidates register selection, cross register consistency, and faithful reporting across a single connected task.

Looking Forward

Chapter 11 moves from consolidated formal registers into literary and narrative style, building on the same reported speech and register control skills.

Pronunciation and Register Across Modes

Moving between registers within one task also means moving between delivery styles. A written report can be read at the learner's own pace, but a spoken address still calls for the pausing habits practiced in Chapter 9, and a spoken summary of residents' concerns calls for a more measured, less ceremonial delivery than the imbizo address itself.

Pronunciation Focus — Shifting pace between an informal summary and a ceremonial address

When reading the informal summary aloud, keep a steady, conversational pace suited to reporting facts to a small group. When shifting into the ceremonial closing address, slow down again at the greeting and self identification, exactly as practiced in Chapter 9, so the two parts of the task sound like two different registers rather than one flat delivery.

  • ngiyabingelela and ngingu-: still worth a clear pause afterward, even within a longer connected task.
  • Informal summary sentences: read at a natural, unhurried conversational pace, without ceremonial pausing.

Grammar: Integrating the Unit's Systems

Purpose: this section does not activate new grammar. It shows how the argument markers from Chapter 6, the reported speech markers from Chapter 7, the correspondence structure from Chapter 8, and the ceremonial address formulas from Chapter 9 work together across a single connected task, so that a learner can choose the correct register for each part without confusing one for another.

10.1 Recognizing which register a moment calls for

Language Focus

A single connected task usually contains more than one communicative moment, and each moment calls for its own register. A summary for the student council is informal and direct. A response to the department is formal correspondence. An address to a gathering is ceremonial. Choosing the wrong register for a given moment is a more serious error at this level than any single grammatical slip.

  • Abafundi abaningi bakhathazekile ngokuthi umshini owodwa awusizi wonke umndeni. Many students are worried that a single computer does not help the whole family. informal, direct summary register, no ceremonial or correspondence markers needed
  • Ngokuqondene nalokhu, umnyango kufanele ucabangele izimo lapho umndeni munye usebenzisana nomshini owodwa. Regarding this, the department should consider situations where one family shares a single computer. formal correspondence register, reusing Chapter 8's recommendation structure

10.2 Reporting the residents' concerns faithfully

Language Focus

The residents' concerns must be reported using the same faithful reporting markers practiced in Chapter 7, wathi and ngokusho kuka-, whether the report appears in the informal summary, the formal correspondence, or the spoken address.

  • Ngokusho kwabahlali abaningi, umshini owodwa awusizi ngenkathi izingane ezimbili kumele zisebenzise ikhompyutha ngasikhathi sinye. According to many residents, a single computer does not help when two children need to use the computer at the same time. ngokusho kwa- reuses Chapter 7's reporting pattern, still active across all three registers here

10.3 Closing each part in its own register

Language Focus

Each part of the connected task closes differently. The informal summary needs no ceremonial closing at all. The formal response closes with the Chapter 8 correspondence closing. The spoken address closes with ngiyabonga ngesikhathi senu, exactly as in Chapter 9.

  • Ngiyabonga ngesikhathi senu, ngiyanibingelela futhi ngiyanithanda nonke. Thank you for your time, I greet you again and I wish you all well. this closing belongs only to the spoken address, not to the informal summary or the formal letter

10.4 The verb template stays constant beneath every register

Language Note — Register changes the surface, not the underlying verb template
register marker, informal, correspondence, or ceremonial, as needed + subject concord + TAM marker + object concord + verb root + extension or extension sequence + final vowel
Whatever register a moment calls for, the sentence beneath it still obeys the verb template from Chapter 3, the concord agreement from Chapter 2, and the subordination markers from Chapter 5. Register selection changes which opening and closing markers appear, and how directly a claim is stated, but it never changes whether a subject concord, an object concord, or an extension is required.
Abahlali abaningi bakhathazekile ngokuthi umshini owodwa awusizi wonke umndeni, njengoba ngokusho kwabo izingane ezimbili zidinga ukusebenzisa ikhompyutha ngasikhathi sinye. Ngokuqondene nalokhu, umnyango kufanele ucabangele izinhlelo ezizosiza imindeni enkulu.
Many residents are worried that a single computer does not help the whole family, since according to them two children need to use the computer at the same time. Regarding this, the department should consider arrangements that will help larger families. The underlying verb template, concord agreement, and subordination remain identical whether the surrounding register is informal, correspondence based, or ceremonial.

Model Text: The Imbizo Outcome

Read the three connected texts below. Each covers the same underlying facts, the residents' shared computer concerns, but each is written in the register appropriate to its audience and purpose.

Isifinyezo esingahleliwe (Informal summary, for the student council)

Ngemuva kombiko embizweni, abahlali abaningi baveze ukukhathazeka ngokuthi umshini owodwa awusizi wonke umndeni, ikakhulukazi lapho izingane ezimbili noma ezintathu kufanele zisebenzise ikhompyutha ngasikhathi sinye. Abanye babuza ukuthi umshini uzoba khona nini ngempelasonto, ngenkathi izikole zivalwe.

Impendulo esemthethweni kumnyango (Formal correspondence, for the department)

Ukubingelela: Sawubona Mnyango wezokuFunda,

Isizathu: Ngokulandelana nombiko wangaphambilini kanye nombiko wemibono yabahlali embizweni, ngiyafisa ukuveza ukuthi abahlali abaningi bakhathazekile ngokuthi umshini owodwa awusizi imindeni emikhulu.

Ukudlulisa amazwi: Ngokusho kwabahlali, izingane ezimbili noma ezintathu zivame ukudinga ikhompyutha ngasikhathi sinye, futhi umongameli wesigungu sabafundi wathi lolu daba kufanele lucatshangelwe ngokushesha.

Isincomo: Ngicela umnyango ucabangele ukwengeza amakamelo noma izikhathi zokubhukha ezizosiza imindeni emikhulu ngokukhethekile.

Isiphetho: Ngiyabonga ngesikhathi sakho, ngithemba impendulo evela kuwe maduzane.

Amazwi embizweni yesibili (Spoken address, for the follow up gathering)

Ukubingelela: Ngiyabingelela ikhansela, abadala bomphakathi, kanye nani nonke enisibekele lapha namuhla.

Ukuzethula: Ngingu-Nomvula, wakwaMkhize, ummeli wesigungu sabafundi.

Isicelo semvume: Ngicela imvume yokuthi ngichaze kafushane izimpendulo esizitholile kusukela embizweni yokuqala.

Umyalezo: Ngokusho kwabahlali abaningi, umshini owodwa awusizi imindeni emikhulu, futhi sesikudlulisele lokhu kumnyango. Umnyango usaphenya isincomo sethu.

Isiphetho: Ngiyabonga ngesikhathi senu, ngiyanibingelela futhi ngiyanithanda nonke.

Writing Workshop

Complete a linked three part written task, continuing Nomvula's booking system story or a different community scenario your instructor provides.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write three short connected texts based on the same underlying facts. First, an informal summary of four to five sentences for the student council, with no ceremonial or correspondence markers. Second, a formal response of six to eight sentences to the relevant department, using the Chapter 8 correspondence structure and reporting the residents' concerns faithfully. Third, a short spoken address of six to eight sentences for a follow up gathering, using the Chapter 9 ceremonial formulas. Then apply the Chapter 19 self editing routine from Volume III to each of the three texts before presenting your final version.

  1. Write the informal summary. State the residents' concern directly, with no formal opening or closing.
  2. Write the formal response. Open and close using the Chapter 8 correspondence formulas, report the concerns faithfully using wathi or ngokusho kwa-, and end with one clear recommendation.
  3. Write the spoken address. Open using ngiyabingelela, introduce yourself using ngingu- and your isibongo, request permission using ngicela imvume, deliver the message, and close using ngiyabonga ngesikhathi senu.
  4. Check register consistency. Confirm that no ceremonial marker has appeared in the formal response, and no correspondence marker has appeared in the spoken address.
  5. Check reporting accuracy. Confirm that the residents' concerns are reported the same way, in substance, across all three texts, even though the wording differs by register.
  6. Audit your drafts. Apply the Chapter 19 repair categories to each of the three texts separately.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • The informal summary contains no correspondence or ceremonial opening or closing formulas.
  • The formal response opens and closes using the Chapter 8 structure, and states one clear recommendation.
  • The spoken address uses all five parts practiced in Chapter 9, in the correct order.
  • The residents' concerns are reported faithfully and consistently across all three texts.
  • Object concords, extensions, subordinate clauses, and register choices remain accurate throughout.

Speaking Practice

Read the informal summary, the formal response, and the spoken address aloud in that order, adjusting your pace and tone at each transition, then check that a listener could tell which register they are hearing without seeing the labels.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: presenting the three part task

With a partner, one person presents all three texts as a connected sequence. The other listens and states, after each one, which register it was and one supporting reason. Then switch roles with a different community topic.

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Carrying one issue through several settings

In many community and institutional contexts, a single practical issue is expected to move through several settings before it is resolved, an informal internal discussion, a formal written request to the responsible authority, and a public update to those affected. Handling all three well, and keeping the facts consistent between them, is often seen as a mark of a capable and trustworthy community representative.

Culture in Context — Faithful reporting builds trust across registers

Reporting other people's words and positions accurately, whether in an informal summary, a formal letter, or a public address, is often what determines whether a community representative is trusted with further responsibility. Changing the substance of what residents or officials said, even unintentionally, while adapting the register, can undermine that trust more than an imperfect grammatical choice would.

Regional Variation — Follow up gatherings

Whether a follow up imbizo happens quickly or only after a longer formal process can differ between municipal structures and more traditional community structures, and between urban and rural settings. This chapter's sequence, summary, formal request, and follow up address, is broadly applicable, but the pacing of real events will vary by local convention.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Letting ceremonial markers appear in a formal letter

Error: Opening a formal written response to a department with ngiyabingelela, as though it were a spoken address to a gathering, rather than the Chapter 8 correspondence opening.

Correction: Reserve ngiyabingelela and the other Chapter 9 ceremonial formulas for spoken public address, and use the Chapter 8 correspondence opening for written formal correspondence.

Common Mistake — Changing the substance of a report between registers

Error: Softening or altering what the residents or the council chairperson actually said when moving from the informal summary into the formal response or the spoken address.

Correction: Keep the substance of any reported concern or statement consistent across all three registers, using wathi or ngokusho kwa- to report it faithfully each time, even as the surrounding wording changes.

Common Mistake — Using the same closing for every part of the task

Error: Closing the informal summary, the formal letter, and the spoken address all the same way, for example by using ngiyabonga ngesikhathi senu everywhere.

Correction: Leave the informal summary without a ceremonial or correspondence closing, close the formal letter using the Chapter 8 closing, and close the spoken address using ngiyabonga ngesikhathi senu.

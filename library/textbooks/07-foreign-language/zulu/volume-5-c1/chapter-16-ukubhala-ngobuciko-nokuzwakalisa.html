Ukubhala Ngobuciko Nokuzwakalisa Imizwa

Volume V, Chapter 16: Creative and Expressive Production · Speak and Write Zulu: Advanced Independent Performance

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize how ideophones, reduplication, and vivid description shape narrative and expressive language, using grammar already consolidated in this volume.
  2. Interpret expressive commentary and dialogue in a short narrative without needing to produce every expressive feature independently.
  3. Produce a small set of controlled ideophones and reduplicated forms to add vividness to a description or narrative.
  4. Write a short personal narrative that includes description, commentary, and one piece of reported dialogue.
  5. Present a short piece of creative or expressive language aloud, with attention to pacing and emphasis.
  6. Follow Nomvula as she sets aside the formal voice of Chapter 15 and writes about her grandmother, for a community storytelling evening.

Communication Scenario

After the forum she opened in Chapter 15, Nomvula is asked to contribute something different: a short personal story for a community storytelling evening, the kind of gathering where neighbors share memories rather than reports. She decides to write about her grandmother, who taught her to cook, and about the sound the pot made on the stove, and the particular way her grandmother scolded her for coming home late. This is not a ceremony and it is not an argument. It is a story, and it asks for a warmer, more textured voice than either the report from Chapter 14 or the formal opening from Chapter 15.

  1. This chapter treats expressive language, ideophones, and reduplication as a controlled skill, one that adds color to a narrative already built from consolidated grammar, rather than a new grammatical system.
  2. Most of the expressive material here is receptive, since a learner does not need to produce a wide range of ideophones or reduplicated forms independently to recognize how they work and to use a small, safe set of them.

Pronunciation: Voicing Ideophones and Emphasis

An ideophone that is spoken in a flat, even voice loses most of its function. Ideophones and reduplicated forms depend on pitch, loudness, and timing to carry their vivid, sensory meaning.

Pronunciation Focus — Giving ideophones their weight

A storyteller marks an ideophone by briefly stepping outside the ordinary rhythm of the sentence, so the listener hears it as a small performance rather than an ordinary word.

  • Slow down slightly and raise the pitch just before an ideophone, then let the surrounding sentence return to its normal pace.
  • Hold a reduplicated form, such as one describing a repeated sound or motion, a little longer than a single word, so the repetition itself is heard clearly.
  • Use a pause, not a rush, before reported dialogue, so the listener understands that a different voice is about to be quoted.

Language Focus: Expressive Language in Controlled Use

Purpose: this section activates no new grammatical system. It develops controlled recognition of ideophones, reduplication, and vivid narrative commentary, with a small, safe set of productive targets, using grammar already consolidated in this volume.

16.1 Recognizing ideophones in a narrative

Language Focus

An ideophone is a short, vivid word that evokes a sound, a motion, a manner, or a sudden state, and it is usually set apart from the surrounding sentence by a brief pause and a change in pitch, rather than woven smoothly into the grammar around it.

  • Imbiza yagxumuka, gubu, umsi uphuma ngale nendlela The pot boiled over, gubu, with smoke pouring out on all sides. receptive pattern, the ideophone gubu is set apart by pauses and stands outside the ordinary clause structure

Recognizing this pattern is receptive. Learners are expected to interpret ideophones like this one in a passage, but are not required to invent new ideophones independently.

16.2 A controlled, productive way to add vividness

Pattern — Productive vivid description
[predicate] kakhulu, ngendlela ethile
Rather than reaching for an unfamiliar ideophone, a learner at this stage can safely add vividness with an intensifier plus a short manner phrase, built entirely from grammar already consolidated in this volume. This pattern is reliable and appropriate for a first attempt at expressive narrative.
Isibonelo: Ugogo wayekhuluma kakhulu, ngendlela eyayingishaqisa njalo
Example: Grandmother used to speak forcefully, in a way that always startled me.

16.3 Recognizing reduplication for repeated or intensified action

Language Focus

Reduplication repeats part of a word to show that an action happens repeatedly, briefly, or with added intensity, and it appears frequently in storytelling and description.

  • Wayehamba ehambahamba engazi ukuthi uyaphi He kept wandering about, not knowing where he was going. receptive pattern, hambahamba reduplicates the verb root to show repeated, aimless movement

16.4 Weaving in one line of reported dialogue

Language Focus

A short personal narrative often becomes more vivid with a single quoted line, introduced by a verb of saying and set apart clearly from the surrounding narration.

  • Ugogo wathi, ungayifaki isandla kulokho okungesikho okwakho Grandmother said, do not put your hand into what does not belong to you. controlled pattern, a verb of saying introduces one clearly marked quoted line, built from grammar already consolidated in this volume

Model Text: Indaba Yasekhaya

Read the short story Nomvula wrote about her grandmother for the storytelling evening. Notice the ideophone, the reduplicated form, the controlled intensifier pattern, and the single line of quoted dialogue.

Indaba Yasekhaya

Ugogo wayekhuluma kakhulu, ngendlela eyayingishaqisa njalo, ikakhulukazi uma ebona umuntu esebenzisa okuthile okungesikho okwakhe. Imbiza yakhe yayihlala isephini, ivutshelwa isiphuzo lesi neselaso isikhathi, futhi uma ingxenye yamanzi ifika esiphethweni, imbiza yayigxumuka, gubu, umsi uphuma ngale nendlela. Ngangihlala ngimlalele ngimbukile, ehambahamba endlini, ephethe intshebe encane yakhe kanti nalokho okushoyo kubalulekile. Ngelinye ilanga, ngithe sengiphuma nabangani bami, ugogo wathi, ungayifaki isandla kulokho okungesikho okwakho, futhi ngangazi ukuthi ubekhuluma ngenkulumo eyayidlula lokho engangikwenza ngaleso sikhathi.

English Reference

Grandmother used to speak forcefully, in a way that always startled me, especially when she saw someone using something that did not belong to them. Her pot always sat on the stove, simmering whatever needed to simmer at that hour, and when the water reached its edge, the pot would boil over, gubu, with smoke pouring out on all sides. I would sit and watch her, listening closely, as she moved about the house, holding her small chin, and whatever she said mattered. One day, as I was leaving with my friends, grandmother said, do not put your hand into what does not belong to you, and I knew she was speaking about something far beyond what I was doing right then.

Umsebenzi wokufunda — Reading task:

  1. Khomba i-ideophone endabeni kaNomvula, futhi uchaze ukuthi ithini. (Point to the ideophone in Nomvula's story, and explain what it evokes.)
  2. Ubani igama eliphindaphindiwe endabeni, futhi lisho ukuthini? (Which word in the story is reduplicated, and what does it mean?)
  3. Ubani amazwi kagogo aqokwe endabeni, futhi kungani abalulekile? (Whose quoted words appear in the story, and why do they matter?)

Writing Workshop

Complete the following linked writing tasks in order, producing a short personal narrative about a family member, a place, or a memory of your choice.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 1 — Isethulo: Write one or two sentences introducing the person or memory your story is about, using grammar you have already consolidated.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 2 — Ukuchaza ngokuzwakalisayo: Write one sentence of vivid description, using the controlled intensifier pattern from Section 16.2.

Self edit prompt
Check that your descriptive sentence uses the safe intensifier pattern, rather than an invented ideophone you are not confident about spelling or using correctly.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 3 — Amazwi aqokiwe: Write one line of reported dialogue, following the pattern in Section 16.4, that captures something the person in your story said.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 4 — Isiphetho: Write a closing sentence that reflects briefly on why this memory still matters to you.

  1. Draft your introduction. Confirm it names the person, place, or memory clearly.
  2. Draft your vivid description. Confirm it uses the controlled pattern rather than an unfamiliar ideophone.
  3. Draft your quoted line. Confirm the verb of saying is clear and the quoted words are set apart.
  4. Draft your closing reflection. Confirm it stays within grammar already consolidated in this volume.
  5. Read your story aloud. Confirm the pacing from the pronunciation focus gives your descriptive phrase and your quoted line their own space.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • The description uses a controlled, appropriate pattern rather than an unfamiliar or misspelled ideophone.
  • The quoted line is clearly introduced by a verb of saying.
  • The closing reflection remains within already consolidated grammar.
  • The story reads naturally when spoken aloud, with clear pacing.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: one vivid sentence

With a partner, take turns reading aloud the model sentence from Section 16.2, then substitute your own subject and detail, keeping the same controlled pattern.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: telling your story

Tell your partner or small group a short, one minute personal story, including an introduction, one vivid descriptive sentence, one line of reported dialogue, and a brief closing reflection.

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself telling the story, then listen back and check whether your descriptive sentence and your quoted line each receive their own clear pacing, rather than blending into the rest of the narration.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Storytelling evenings

Community storytelling gatherings, where neighbors and family members share personal memories, follow a different rhythm than a formal report or a ceremonial opening. Listeners generally expect warmth, personal detail, and a memorable line or image, rather than the structured argument of a professional presentation.

Culture in Context — Elders as a source of memorable speech

In many families, a grandparent's sayings and turns of phrase are remembered and repeated long after the original moment has passed. Quoting an elder directly, as in Section 16.4, is a common and respectful way to bring their voice into a personal narrative.

Regional Variation — Ideophones across communities

Some ideophones are widely shared across isiZulu speaking communities, while others are more closely associated with a particular family, region, or generation. A learner at this stage should expect variation in which ideophones a given storyteller favors, and should treat unfamiliar ones as receptive vocabulary to interpret from context rather than as errors.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Inventing an unfamiliar ideophone

Error: Reaching for an ideophone the learner has not actually encountered, and guessing at its form or spelling to sound more vivid.

Correction: Use the controlled intensifier pattern from Section 16.2 for original description, and reserve ideophones for ones already met in reading or listening.

Common Mistake — Quoting without a clear verb of saying

Error: Dropping a quoted line into a narrative without a verb such as wathi, so the listener or reader cannot tell whose words are being reported.

Correction: Follow the pattern in Section 16.4, introducing the quoted line clearly with a verb of saying before the words themselves.

Common Mistake — Rushing past reduplicated and expressive forms

Error: Reading a reduplicated form such as hambahamba at the same speed and pitch as an ordinary word, so its repeated, drawn out sense is lost.

Correction: Slow down slightly and hold the repeated syllables a little longer, as described in the pronunciation focus for this chapter.

Volume V, Chapter 19: Independent Speaking and Writing Portfolio · Speak and Write Zulu: Advanced Independent Performance

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Assemble a small portfolio of your own independent isiZulu speaking and writing, drawn from personal, cultural, and professional domains.
  2. Plan and deliver a short spoken presentation without reading from a full script.
  3. Produce an original written text that stands on its own, without relying on a model sentence for every line.
  4. Apply self-editing and self-monitoring habits, drawn from earlier chapters, to your own extended production rather than to a text someone else wrote.
  5. Write a short reflective commentary that explains the choices you made as a speaker and a writer, not only what you produced.
  6. Follow Nomvula as she records a spoken reflection, drafts an original piece of writing, and prepares a short presentation for her own production portfolio.

Pronunciation: Speaking Without Notes in Hand

A speaker reading directly from a script can lean on the eyes rather than the ear, in much the same way a listener leans on a transcript. Independent speaking asks for the opposite habit, using penultimate lengthening, phrase rhythm, and intonation to guide the listener through unscripted or lightly scripted speech, rather than reading each word off a page in a flat, unbroken line.

Pronunciation Focus — Planning pauses before planning words

Before rehearsing exact wording, plan where a presentation's natural pauses fall, at the end of an idea, before a new point, and before a closing statement. A prepared pause, held briefly and confidently, communicates more control than a rushed sentence delivered without a single break.

  • Mark the two or three points in your presentation where a short pause will help a listener follow the shift from one idea to the next.
  • Let penultimate lengthening fall naturally at the end of each sentence, rather than clipping the final syllable to move on quickly.
  • Rehearse aloud at least once before recording or presenting, since silent rehearsal cannot reveal where the rhythm of a sentence breaks down.

Portfolio Skills: Producing Independently

Purpose: this section activates no new grammatical system. It develops a small set of planning and self-monitoring strategies that let a learner produce extended, original speech and writing, using grammar, vocabulary, and register already consolidated in this volume.

19.1 Planning before producing

Language Focus

An independent speaker or writer does not begin with a full sentence. A short plan, three or four ideas in the order they will appear, gives shape to a presentation or a piece of writing before a single sentence is drafted. The plan protects the final product from wandering away from its own purpose partway through.

  • Planning question: Yini engifuna ukuyisho, futhi kungani kubalulekile ukuthi ngiyisho? Planning question: What do I want to say, and why does it matter that I say it? purpose level, answered before any drafting begins
  • Planning question: Yiziphi izinhlamvu ezintathu ezizoqondisa lokhu engikushoyo? Planning question: What three points will guide what I say? structure level, turns a purpose into a short usable outline

This planning habit protects a speaker or writer from producing a piece that sounds fluent sentence by sentence but does not add up to a coherent whole.

19.2 Self-monitoring while drafting

Pattern — Checking your own sentence
[umusho wami] — ngabe izikhombamqondo ziyahambisana, futhi ngabe isikhathi nesimo sesenzo kufanele?
After drafting a sentence, rather than immediately moving to the next one, an independent writer pauses to check the concords against the noun class of the subject, and to check that the tense or aspect matches the intended meaning. This is the same checking habit practiced on someone else's work in Chapter 17, now turned on the writer's own draft.
Isibonelo: Ngithe ngibhala ngengane yami, ngahlala ngiwuhlolisisa umusho ngamunye ngaphambi kokuqhubekela phambili
Example: As I wrote about my child, I kept checking each sentence carefully before moving forward

19.3 Rehearsing before delivering

Language Focus

A presentation improves noticeably between a first silent read through and a second reading aloud, since spoken rhythm exposes sentences that look fine on paper but are difficult to say naturally. Rehearse a short presentation aloud at least twice, adjusting wording where the spoken version stumbles, before delivering or recording it.

19.4 Naming your own choices

Language Focus

Independent production does not mean production without reflection. A mature independent speaker or writer can explain why a particular word, structure, or register was chosen over an available alternative. Naming those choices, rather than treating a finished piece as simply what happened to come out, is itself part of the skill this chapter builds.

Portfolio Piece One: A Recorded Reflection

Nomvula's first portfolio item is a short spoken reflection on what contributing her grandmother's story to the collection has meant to her. She plans three points, rehearses aloud twice, and records herself without reading directly from a full script. Read her planning notes and the reflection she eventually delivered, then answer the portfolio questions that follow as if you were assembling your own portfolio.

Amaphuzu okuhlela — Planning notes

1. Ukuthi kungani ngikhethe ukubhala ngogogo. 2. Ukuthi ngizizwa kanjani ngokwamukelwa kwendaba yami. 3. Ukuthi lokhu kungisiza kanjani ukugcina ulimi lwesiZulu ngokwami.

1. Why I chose to write about my grandmother. 2. How it feels to have my story accepted. 3. How this helps me keep isiZulu alive in my own life.

Umbono Ongokuzwakalayo — Spoken reflection, delivered from the plan above

Ngikhethe ukubhala ngogogo ngoba yena owangifundisa okuningi engikwaziyo namuhla, hhayi ngokungitshela kuphela, kodwa ngokungibonisa ngendlela yakhe yokuphila. Ngithe lapho indaba yami yamukelwa kulo mqulu, ngazizwa ngithokoza kakhulu, ngoba kwakungasho ukuthi amagamaami ayefanele ukufundwa ngabanye abantu, hhayi mina ngedwa. Lokhu kungisize ukuthi ngiqonde ukuthi ukubhala ngesiZulu akusiwo umsebenzi wesikole nje, kuyindlela yokugcina abantu bami nokuxhumana nomndeni wami ngokwedlulele.

Ikhaya Elisha

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task:

Portfolio Piece Three: A Short Presentation

The third item is the short presentation Nomvula prepares to give at the launch event the editor described in Chapter 18. No audio recording accompanies this textbook, so learners should deliver this presentation aloud, to a partner, an instructor, or a recording of their own voice, following the rehearsal habit from Section 19.3. The presentation script below is provided for practice and for the answer key only, and should be rehearsed aloud rather than simply read silently.

Presentation script — Two minute launch event presentation
Sanibonani nonke. Ngingu Nomvula, futhi indaba yami ekulo mqulu ikhuluma ngogogo. Ngikhethe ukubhala ngaye ngoba yena owangifundisa ukuthi izwi lomuntu munye, uma lilalelwa ngokucophelela, lingashintsha indlela abanye ababona ngayo impilo. Ngesikhathi ngibhala le ndaba, ngathola ukuthi izinto ezincane engangizikhumbula, indlela imbiza yakhe eyayivutshelwa ngayo, indlela ayehambahamba ngayo endlini, ziyona ezaziqukethe intsika yesifundo asifundisa. Ngithemba ukuthi uma nilalele le ndaba, nani nizocabanga ngothile owanifundisa okuthile okusabalulekile kini namuhla. Ngiyabonga.
Hello everyone. I am Nomvula, and my story in this collection is about my grandmother. I chose to write about her because she taught me that one person's voice, listened to carefully, can change how others see life. While writing this story, I found that the small things I remembered, the way her pot boiled over, the way she moved around the house, carried the core of the lesson she taught. I hope that as you listen to this story, you too will think of someone who taught you something still important to you today. Thank you.

Umsebenzi wokukhuluma — Speaking task, complete before rereading the gloss above:

  1. Landelani uhlelo lwamaphuzu amathathu esifundo esithi 19.1, bese nilungisa uhlaka lwenkulumo yenu ngenkathi nihlela eyenu. (Follow the three point planning approach from Section 19.1, and prepare an outline for your own presentation while planning yours.)
  2. Khomba indawo eyodwa kule nkulumo lapho isikhulumi isebenzisa isibonelo esincane ukuze ichaze iphuzu elikhulu. (Point to one place in this presentation where the speaker uses a small example to explain a larger point.)
  3. Zilolonge ukusho le nkulumo kabili ngaphambi kokuba niyiqophe noma niyethule, bese nibhala iphuzu elilodwa enalilungisa phakathi kokuzilolonga okokuqala nokwesibili. (Rehearse delivering this presentation twice before recording or presenting it, then write one point you adjusted between the first and second rehearsal.)

Writing Workshop: The Portfolio Reflection

Assemble your own three item production portfolio, one recorded or delivered spoken reflection, one original piece of writing on a topic of your own choosing, and one short presentation. Then write a short reflection on the choices behind each item.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 1 — Ukuhlela izinto ezintathu: Plan your three portfolio items using the three point planning habit from Section 19.1, confirming that each plan is specific enough to guide a full piece, not so broad that it produces a vague or wandering result.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 2 — Ukubhala umbono ngombhalo ngamunye: For each item, write two or three sentences explaining a specific word, structure, or register choice you made, and why you made it rather than an available alternative.

Self reflection prompt
Yiziphi izinto engizikhethile ngamabomu kulo mbhalo noma kule nkulumo, futhi kungani ngingazikhethanga ezinye izindlela? (What did I choose deliberately in this piece of writing or speech, and why did I not choose the other available options?)

Umsebenzi wokubhala 3 — Ukuhlanganisa umqulu: Arrange your three items together with your written reflections, in an order that makes sense to someone encountering your production portfolio for the first time.

  1. Select three items. One spoken reflection, one original written piece, and one short presentation.
  2. Plan before producing. Apply the planning habit from Section 19.1 to each item before drafting or rehearsing it.
  3. Self-monitor while drafting. Check your own concords and verb forms as you write, the way you checked someone else's writing in Chapter 17.
  4. Rehearse aloud. Deliver spoken items at least twice before recording or presenting them, adjusting wording where the spoken version stumbles.
  5. Write a short reflection for each item. Name at least one specific choice you made, and why you made it.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Portfolio checklist:

  • Each of the three items follows a short plan rather than beginning from a blank page with no direction.
  • Each reflection names at least one specific word, structure, or register choice, rather than describing the piece only in general terms.
  • Spoken items were rehearsed aloud at least twice, and were not simply written out and read from a full script.
  • Each written item has been self-checked for concord and verb form accuracy before submission.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: outlining aloud

With a partner, take turns describing your three point plan for one portfolio item aloud in isiZulu, before either of you has drafted a single full sentence of it.

Umsebenzi wokusiza iqabane — Communicative task: presentation rehearsal

Deliver your short presentation to a partner. Ask your partner to identify one place where a pause helped them follow your point, and one place where an additional pause might have helped.

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself delivering each portfolio item aloud in isiZulu, then listen back and compare your recorded delivery to your written plan, noting any place where the spoken version departed usefully from the original outline.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Speaking and writing as forms of contribution

Delivering a short presentation at a community event, or contributing an original piece of writing to a shared collection, is not only a display of language ability but also a genuine contribution to a shared undertaking. Independent production carries a social weight that a purely private exercise does not.

Culture in Context — Acknowledging influence and gratitude in public speech

Public presentations in many isiZulu speaking settings commonly open by acknowledging the audience and close by expressing thanks, and often name the person or people whose influence shaped the speaker's contribution. A presentation that skips these conventions may sound abrupt even when its grammar is otherwise accurate.

Regional Variation — Presentation style varies by setting

A community literary event, a workplace briefing, and an academic presentation may each favor a different balance of formality, personal narrative, and structured argument. Independent speakers should adjust their planning and delivery to the setting, rather than applying a single presentation style to every occasion.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Drafting without a plan

Error: Beginning a piece of writing or a presentation with a full sentence before deciding what the piece is meant to accomplish or how its ideas will be ordered.

Correction: Apply the planning habit from Section 19.1 first, and treat the three point outline as a required step, not an optional one.

Common Mistake — Reading a presentation instead of delivering it

Error: Writing out a presentation in full and reading it aloud word for word, with flat, unbroken rhythm and no rehearsal beforehand.

Correction: Rehearse aloud at least twice, as described in Section 19.3, and let the outline, rather than a full script, guide your delivery.

Common Mistake — Skipping self-monitoring on your own work

Error: Applying careful concord and verb checking to someone else's text, as in earlier chapters, but skipping that same checking when reviewing your own writing.

Correction: Follow Section 19.2, and check your own draft with the same attention you would give another writer's text.

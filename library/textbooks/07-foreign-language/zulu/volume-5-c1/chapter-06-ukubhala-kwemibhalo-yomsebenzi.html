Volume V, Chapter 6: Professional Writing and Documentation · Speak and Write Zulu: Advanced Independent Performance

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Plan a professional document, a memo, a short report, or a summary, before drafting it, identifying its purpose, its intended reader, and the outcome it should produce.
  2. Structure a professional document using clear sections, a stated purpose, supporting detail, and a closing recommendation or request.
  3. Use consistent formal register and accurate concord across a document long enough to require sustained agreement, building on the reading skills developed in Chapter 5.
  4. Distinguish a document's factual content from its recommendation, so a reader can evaluate each separately.
  5. Present a short written report aloud in a meeting setting, summarizing its content without simply reading it word for word.
  6. Follow Nomvula as she moves from commenting on a colleague's document to producing her own report for the departmental planning meeting.

Communication Scenario

The planning meeting Nomvula prepared for in Chapter 5 goes well, and her supervisor asks her to take the next step herself. Nomvula must now write a short report of her own, summarizing what the training programme has achieved this term, noting where access remains difficult, and recommending what the department should do next. She has read enough policy documents by now to recognize their shape, a stated purpose, supporting detail, and a closing position, but producing one herself feels different from reading one. She drafts an outline first, listing what she knows, what she needs to check, and what she wants to recommend, before writing a single full sentence. When her first draft reads more like a diary of everything that happened than a report a busy colleague could use in five minutes, she revises it, cutting detail that does not support her recommendation and sharpening the sentence that states her main request. She then prepares to present the report's key points aloud at the meeting, without reading the document line by line to a room that has already received a copy.

  1. Professional writing was supported receptively through the reading you practiced in Chapter 5. This chapter asks you to produce that same kind of structured, purposeful document yourself, not only to interpret one written by someone else.
  2. A report is not a narrative of everything that happened. A useful report states its purpose early, supports that purpose with selected detail, and closes with a clear recommendation or request the reader can act on.

Pronunciation: Reading Documents Aloud in Meetings

Presenting a report aloud is not the same task as reading a passage aloud for practice. A colleague listening in a meeting needs the main point signaled clearly, with supporting detail delivered at a pace that allows note taking, and the closing recommendation set apart so it is not lost among the detail that came before it.

Pronunciation Focus — Signaling structure through pacing and pitch

Slow slightly and lower pitch marginally when stating a document's purpose at the opening, so a listener registers it as the frame for everything that follows.

  • Use a brief pause before a recommendation or request, distinct from the pauses used for ordinary clause boundaries, so the listener hears it as a shift from description to action.
  • Keep supporting figures and details evenly paced rather than rushed, since a listener who cannot pause to reread depends entirely on your delivery to register each one.
  • Practice presenting your own report's opening sentence three times, adjusting pacing until the purpose is unmistakable on a single hearing.

Language Focus: Structuring a Professional Document

Purpose: this section develops professional writing as an active productive skill, drawing on the concord, extension, and clause systems already active, and introduces the vocabulary of purpose, summary, and recommendation needed to plan and draft a short report.

6.1 Stating a document's purpose early

Language Focus

A professional document should state its purpose in its opening sentence or two, so a reader who has only a moment can still identify why the document exists before deciding whether to read further. This mirrors the claim-first structure you learned to locate as a reader in Chapter 5, now applied to your own drafting.

  • Lo mbiko unikeza uhlolojikelele lwenqubekelaphambili yohlelo lokuqeqeshwa kulesi sigamu This report provides an overview of the training programme's progress this term. unikeza uhlolojikelele (provides an overview) states the document's purpose in the opening sentence
  • Injongo yalo mbiko wukuveza izinselelo ezisele futhi kuphakanyiswe izinyathelo ezilandelayo The purpose of this report is to highlight the remaining challenges and propose the next steps. injongo yalo mbiko wukuveza (the purpose of this report is to reveal) names both a descriptive and a recommending function

6.2 Separating fact from recommendation

Language Focus

A reader must be able to tell where a report is stating something that happened, and where it is recommending something that should happen. Marking this shift clearly, rather than letting fact and recommendation blend together, allows a reader to evaluate the evidence and the proposal separately.

  • Abafundi abangamashumi ayisithupha baphothule uhlelo kulesi sigamu, kepha abaningi kwabo basezindaweni ezikude Sixty learners completed the programme this term, but most of them are in remote areas. baphothule (completed) states a fact using the perfect/resultative already active from earlier chapters
  • Ngenxa yalokho, kuyacetshiswa ukuthi uhlelo lokusiza ngezimali lwandiswe kulezo zifunda Because of that, it is recommended that financial support be expanded to those districts. kuyacetshiswa ukuthi (it is recommended that) marks the shift from fact to recommendation

6.3 Summarizing without narrating everything

Pattern — Selecting what belongs in a summary
okubalulekile kakhulu / ngokufingqiwe / okusalele
What matters most, in summary, and what remains outstanding are three phrases that help a writer signal selection rather than exhaustive narration, guiding a reader toward the points the writer judges essential.
Isibonelo: Okubalulekile kakhulu kulesi sigamu wukuthi izinga lokuphothula lenyukile, nakuba ukufinyelela kusesengumqoka
Example: What matters most this term is that the completion rate has risen, although access remains a key concern.

Notice that okubalulekile kakhulu does not simply mean "important." It signals that the writer has already weighed several facts and is presenting only the ones judged most relevant, a judgment worth making deliberately before you draft.

6.4 Closing with a clear request or recommendation

Language Focus

A report should close with a specific, actionable statement, not a general observation. A reader should be able to say, in one sentence, exactly what the writer is asking the department to do next.

  • Ngicela ukuthi ikomiti liqinisekise isabelomali esengeziwe ngaphambi kwenyanga ezayo I request that the committee confirm the additional budget allocation before next month. ngicela ukuthi (I request that) closes the report with one specific, actionable request

Model Document: Umbiko Wehhovisi

Read Nomvula's short report below, followed by her spoken presentation notes for the meeting. Notice where the report states its purpose, where it separates fact from recommendation, and how her spoken notes differ from the full written document.

Umbiko Wesigamu — Term Report, Excerpt

Lo mbiko unikeza uhlolojikelele lwenqubekelaphambili yohlelo lokuqeqeshwa kulesi sigamu. Abafundi abangamashumi ayisithupha baphothule uhlelo, okuyinyuko uma kuqhathaniswa nesigamu esidlule. Okubalulekile kakhulu wukuthi ukuphothula sekwenyuke, nakuba ukufinyelela kusesengumqoka ezifundazweni ezisemakhaya. Izinsizakusebenza ezengeziwe azikafiki kuzo zonke izikhungo, futhi lokhu kuyaphazamisa amaphuzu okuqeqeshwa asezindaweni ezikude. Ngenxa yalokho, kuyacetshiswa ukuthi uhlelo lokusiza ngezimali lwandiswe kulezo zifunda ngokushesha okukhulu. Ngicela ukuthi ikomiti liqinisekise isabelomali esengeziwe ngaphambi kwenyanga ezayo.

Amanothi okwethula umbiko — Nomvula's spoken presentation notes
Umbiko wami ubika izinga eliphezulu lokuphothula kulesi sigamu, kepha ubalula inkinga eyodwa enkulu, ukufinyelela ezindaweni ezikude. Isicelo sami esikhulu wukuthi sithole isabelomali esengeziwe ngaphambi kwenyanga ezayo, ukuze lezo zikhungo zingasali emumva.
My report reports a high completion rate this term, but highlights one major problem, access in remote areas. My main request is that we obtain additional budget allocation before next month, so that those institutions are not left behind.

Umsebenzi wokufunda — Reading task:

  1. Khomba umusho lapho umbiko unikeza khona injongo yawo. (Point to the sentence where the report gives its purpose.)
  2. Thola umusho lapho umbiko ushintsha khona kusukela ekuchazeni iqiniso kuya ekunikezeni isincomo. (Find the sentence where the report shifts from stating fact to giving a recommendation.)
  3. Qhathanisa amanothi okwethula kaNomvula nombiko wakhe obhaliwe. Yini eshiywe ngaphandle emanothini akhe okukhuluma? (Compare Nomvula's spoken notes with her written report. What has she left out of her speaking notes?)

Writing Workshop

Complete the following linked writing tasks in order, drafting your own short professional report on a training programme, a community project, or a similar topic your instructor provides.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 1 — Ukuhlela: Before writing full sentences, list what you know, what you still need to check, and what you want to recommend. Do not draft prose yet.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 2 — Ukusho injongo: Write one or two opening sentences that clearly state your report's purpose, following the pattern practiced in this chapter.

Self edit prompt
Read only your opening sentence aloud to a colleague who has not seen the rest of your report. Ask, could you tell me why this document exists from that sentence alone. If not, revise the opening before continuing.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 3 — Ukwahlukanisa iqiniso nesincomo: Write two or three sentences of supporting fact, then one clearly marked sentence that shifts to a recommendation, using a phrase such as ngenxa yalokho or kuyacetshiswa ukuthi.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 4 — Ukuvala ngesicelo esiqondile: Write one closing sentence that states a specific, actionable request rather than a general observation.

  1. Complete your planning list. Confirm you have facts, an open question, and a recommendation before drafting prose.
  2. Draft your opening. Confirm the purpose is stated clearly within the first two sentences.
  3. Draft your supporting detail. Confirm each fact you include supports the recommendation you plan to make, and remove any that does not.
  4. Draft your recommendation and closing request. Confirm the request is specific enough that a reader could act on it directly.
  5. Read the whole report aloud once. Confirm a listener could identify the purpose, the key fact, and the request from hearing it a single time.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • The opening states the document's purpose clearly, within the first one or two sentences.
  • Supporting detail is selective, not an exhaustive narration of everything that happened.
  • The shift from fact to recommendation is clearly marked and easy to locate.
  • The closing request is specific and actionable, not a vague general observation.
  • Register remains formal and consistent throughout, as established in Chapters 4 and 5.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: presenting an opening statement

Practice saying your report's opening purpose statement aloud three times, adjusting pacing and pitch so a listener registers it as the frame for what follows.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: meeting presentation

Present the key points of your report to a partner in under two minutes, without reading the full document aloud. Ask your partner to state back your main recommendation, then compare it with what you intended.

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself presenting your report's key points in under two minutes, then listen back and note whether the purpose, the key fact, and the request are each clearly audible.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Presenting a report without reading it verbatim

In many South African professional and institutional settings, a written report is circulated in advance, and a spoken presentation is expected to summarize its key points rather than read the document aloud in full, respecting colleagues' time while still allowing space for discussion.

Culture in Context — Framing a request respectfully within a report

Closing a report with a direct request, as Nomvula does regarding the budget, is generally considered appropriate and professional when the request follows evidence that has already been presented fairly, rather than appearing as a demand disconnected from the report's content.

Regional Variation — Report format across institutions

Government departments, schools, and community organizations may each favor a slightly different report structure or level of formality. Learners should expect to adapt the general pattern taught here to the conventions of the specific institution they are writing for, rather than assuming one fixed template applies everywhere.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Narrating events instead of summarizing them

Error: Listing every event of the term in chronological order, so the reader cannot tell which details matter most to the report's purpose.

Correction: Select only the facts that support your recommendation, and use a phrase such as okubalulekile kakhulu to signal that you have already judged what matters most.

Common Mistake — Burying the request inside a paragraph of detail

Error: Placing the recommendation in the middle of a sentence full of supporting detail, so a busy reader can miss it entirely.

Correction: Give the recommendation its own sentence, introduced clearly with a phrase such as kuyacetshiswa ukuthi or ngicela ukuthi, and place it where a reader expects a closing statement.

Common Mistake — Reading the full report aloud in a presentation

Error: Reading a written report word for word to a meeting that has already received a copy, rather than summarizing its key points.

Correction: Prepare separate, shorter speaking notes that highlight the purpose, the key fact, and the request, leaving full detail for the written document itself.

Answer Key

Model Document — Comprehension

  1. The report notes that additional resources have not yet reached all institutions, disrupting training points in remote areas.
  2. The report requests that the committee confirm additional budget allocation before next month.

Integration

  1. Reporting a fact means stating something that has already occurred or been observed, without proposing an action. Making a recommendation means proposing what should happen next, usually signaled by a phrase such as kuyacetshiswa ukuthi or ngenxa yalokho.
  2. Accepted examples include any natural sentence using "kuyacetshiswa ukuthi" to introduce a specific, actionable proposal related to the report's content.

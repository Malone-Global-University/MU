Ukuhlanganisa Imibiko Nezinqumo: Ukuqoqa Iyunithi II

Volume V, Chapter 10: Unit Consolidation, C1-Oriented Production · Speak and Write Zulu: Advanced Independent Performance

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Combine a fair report of what other people said with a clear account of the position you yourself took, in a single organized briefing.
  2. Move between reported speech and direct, first-person commitment without blurring the two or losing the reader.
  3. Summarize a negotiation's outcome for a supervisor who was not present, including what was agreed, what remains unresolved, and what happens next.
  4. Sustain accurate concord, register, and verb-template control across a longer, multi-part spoken or written turn.
  5. Complete an integrated listening, speaking, reading, and writing check that draws on the reporting skills from Chapter 8 and the negotiation skills from Chapter 9.
  6. Follow Nomvula as she prepares a combined briefing and follow-up plan for her supervisor, reporting on the planning meeting and setting out her office's next steps.

Communication Scenario

The planning meeting is over, but Nomvula's work is not. Her supervisor was not in the room and needs two things by the end of the day, a fair account of what the engineer, the community representative, and the councillor actually said and did, and a clear statement of what Nomvula committed her office to and why. The two documents cannot simply sit side by side. Her supervisor needs one connected briefing that moves smoothly from reporting other people's words to owning her own decisions, so that a reader can tell, at every point, whether a sentence describes someone else's claim or Nomvula's own commitment. Before she writes, her supervisor also asks her to explain the meeting aloud in five minutes, since a second, unrelated meeting starts shortly and there is no time to read a long document first.

  1. Chapter 8 built the skill of reporting fairly. Chapter 9 built the skill of proposing, conceding, and committing. This chapter asks you to combine both skills inside one connected performance, spoken and written.
  2. A briefing that blurs report and commitment leaves a supervisor unable to tell what was said by others and what the office is now bound to do. This chapter treats that distinction as a skill to be checked, not assumed.

Where This Chapter Fits

This chapter does not introduce a new communicative function. Instead, it asks you to draw on two systems you have already practiced, the reported speech and fair-summary skills from Chapter 8, and the proposing, conceding, and committing skills from Chapter 9, and to combine them accurately within a single connected briefing. No new grammar, concord type, or verb pattern is activated here. The work is integration, sustaining accuracy and clarity across a longer stretch of connected discourse.

Coming From Chapter 8

Fair, accurate reported speech, separating claim from support from framing, and reporting on a discussion without inserting your own opinion.

Coming From Chapter 9

Proposing a position, responding to challenge, offering a genuine concession, and closing with a concrete, checkable commitment.

New in This Chapter

Moving cleanly between the two systems inside one briefing, and sustaining that control across a longer spoken or written turn.

Pronunciation: Holding Register Across a Long Turn

A short exchange is easy to keep steady. A five-minute spoken briefing is harder, since pace, register, and clarity all tend to drift as a speaker moves from one part of the account to the next.

Pronunciation Focus — Marking the shift between reporting and committing

Listeners rely on more than word choice to know when a speaker has moved from reporting someone else's words to stating their own position. A brief pause, a slightly firmer and more level pitch, and a small drop in pace at the start of a first-person commitment all help a listener track the shift without needing extra framing words.

  • Keep the penultimate lengthening and even phrase rhythm practiced since earlier chapters consistent across the full five minutes, rather than letting it flatten as fatigue sets in toward the end of a long turn.
  • Mark the transition into direct commitment with a short pause and a slightly firmer pitch, distinct from the more even, narrating tone used for reporting what others said.
  • Rehearse the full briefing aloud at least twice before treating it as ready, once slowly to check every transition, and once at a natural meeting pace to confirm it still fits within five minutes.

Language Focus: Combining Report and Position

Purpose: this section develops no new grammatical system. It draws together the reported-speech patterns from Chapter 8 and the proposing, conceding, and committing patterns from Chapter 9, and practices moving between them clearly inside one connected account.

10.1 Framing the shift from report to commitment

Language Focus

A short framing phrase at the start of a first-person section signals to the listener or reader that the account has moved from what others said to what the speaker herself decided or agreed to do.

  • Ngokwami, ngenze isinqumo sokuthi ... For my part, I made the decision that ... ngokwami (for my part) marks the shift from reported material to the speaker's own position
  • Kepha lokho okwenzeka ngempela ukuthi ... But what actually happened was that ... signals a move from summarizing positions to stating an outcome the speaker is reporting on directly

10.2 Keeping reported claims and personal commitments distinct

Language Focus

A combined briefing should let a reader tell, sentence by sentence, whether a claim belongs to Unjiniyela, to the community representative, or to Nomvula herself. Losing that distinction is the most common weakness in this kind of writing.

  • Unjiniyela uthe imali ingaba yinkinga, kepha mina ngiye ngazibophezela ukuthi hhovisi lami lizoyithola The engineer said the funding could be a problem, but I committed that my office would find it. uthe (he said) attributes the claim to the engineer, while ngiye ngazibophezela (I committed) attributes the decision to Nomvula alone

10.3 Closing a combined briefing with next steps

Pattern — Separating what was agreed from what is still open
Okuvunyelwene ngakho ... / Okusasele engxoxweni ...
A useful closing separates settled matters from unresolved ones, so a supervisor knows exactly what still needs attention rather than assuming everything discussed was decided.
Isibonelo: Okuvunyelwene ngakho ukuthi umkhankaso uzoqala ngemva kwenyanga. Okusasele engxoxweni ukuthi ubani ozokhokhela izindleko zokuqhubeka
Example: What was agreed is that the campaign will start a month later. What remains open is who will cover the ongoing costs.

A briefing that lists only agreements can mislead a supervisor into thinking a matter is fully closed. Naming what is still unresolved is part of a fair, complete report.

Model Text: Umbiko Kasodolobha

Read the combined briefing Nomvula prepares for her supervisor. Notice where she reports fairly on what others said, where she shifts into her own first-person account, and where she separates settled matters from open ones.

Umbiko Womhlangano Wokuhlela Uhlelo Lwamanzi

Ngesikhathi somhlangano wokuhlela uhlelo lwamanzi, uNjiniyela ubuzile ukuthi umkhankaso wokwazisa umphakathi ungakhokhelwa kanjani ngesikhathi esifushane kangaka, kanti ummeli womphakathi wagcizelela ukuthi umphakathi awukwazi ukulinda kuze kufike isikhathi esizayo. Ngokwami, ngenze isinqumo sokuthi ngivumele ukuthi umkhankaso uqale ngemva kwenyanga, kodwa kuphela uma leso sikhathi sibhalwa ohlelweni oluphelele hhayi njengesithembiso esingabhaliwe. Ngesikhathi inkulumo-mpikiswano yasemsakazweni iphindwa emhlanganweni, ngiye ngabuyisela ingxoxo esisombululweni esifunwa yithu sonke, futhi ekugcineni ikhansela lingicele ukuthi ngisho ngempela ukuthi hhovisi lami lizimisele ukwenzani. Ngizibophezele ukuthi hhovisi lami lizoqala ukubhala uhlelo lomkhankaso kusasa, futhi ngizosilethela isigungu isikhathi esiqondile ngaphakathi kwezinsuku ezinhlanu. Okuvunyelwene ngakho emhlanganweni ukuthi umkhankaso uzoqala ngemva kwenyanga, futhi ukuthi hhovisi lami lizosingatha ukubhalwa kohlelo. Okusasele engxoxweni ukuthi ubani ozokhokhela izindleko zokuqhubeka nomkhankaso ngemva kwezinyanga ezintathu zokuqala, futhi lokhu kudinga ukuxoxwa kabusha nomnyango wezezimali.

English Reference

During the water-schedule planning meeting, the engineer asked how the public awareness campaign could be funded on such a short timeline, while the community representative stressed that the community could not wait for some future date. For my part, I decided to agree that the campaign could start a month later, but only if that timeline was written into the full plan rather than left as an unwritten promise. When the earlier radio disagreement resurfaced at the meeting, I redirected the discussion toward the solution we all wanted, and the councillor eventually asked me to state exactly what my office was prepared to do. I committed that my office would begin drafting the campaign plan tomorrow and would bring the committee a precise timeline within five days. What was agreed at the meeting is that the campaign will start a month later and that my office will handle drafting the plan. What remains open is who will cover the ongoing costs of the campaign after the first three months, and this will need further discussion with the finance department.

Umsebenzi wokufunda — Reading task:

  1. Khomba lapho umbiko udlulela khona ekubikeni imibono yabanye uye engxenyeni yokuzibophezela kukaNomvula. (Point to where the briefing shifts from reporting others' views to Nomvula's own commitment.)
  2. Yikuphi okubalulwe njengokuvunyelwene ngakho, futhi yikuphi okubalulwe njengokusasele engxoxweni? (What is listed as agreed, and what is listed as still open?)
  3. Kungani kubalulekile ukuthi umbiko ukhombise ngokucacile umahluko phakathi kwalokho abanye abakushilo nalokho uNomvula ngokwakhe azibophezele kukho? (Why does it matter that the briefing clearly distinguishes what others said from what Nomvula herself committed to?)

Writing Workshop

Complete the following linked writing tasks in order, producing your own combined briefing based on the Chapter 9 meeting, a discussion your instructor provides, or a negotiation you role-played with a partner.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 1 — Ukubika ngokungathathi hlangothi: Write two or three sentences fairly reporting the positions of two other participants in a negotiation, without inserting your own opinion.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 2 — Ukugcina umahluko ucacile: Write one sentence that reports someone else's claim and one sentence that states your own commitment, and check that a reader could not confuse the two.

Self edit prompt
Underline every sentence in your draft that reports someone else's words, and circle every sentence that states your own decision or commitment. If a sentence could belong to either category, revise it until it clearly belongs to only one.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 3 — Ukwahlukanisa okuvunyelwene ngakho nokusasele: Write two closing sentences, one naming what was agreed and one naming what remains unresolved and needs further discussion.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 4 — Ukuhlanganisa umbiko: Combine your sentences from tasks one through three into a single connected briefing of six to ten sentences, using the framing phrases practiced in this chapter to mark the shift between reporting and committing.

  1. Draft the reporting section first. Confirm every claim is clearly attributed to the speaker who made it.
  2. Draft the commitment section. Confirm it uses first-person, direct language rather than reported forms.
  3. Insert a framing phrase at the transition. Confirm a reader can find the exact sentence where the account shifts.
  4. Draft the closing summary. Confirm it separates agreed matters from open ones.
  5. Read the full briefing aloud. Confirm it stays within a natural five-minute spoken length.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • Every reported claim is clearly attributed to the speaker who made it.
  • The shift from reporting to first-person commitment is marked and easy to find.
  • No sentence blurs a reported claim with the writer's own decision.
  • The closing separates what was agreed from what remains open.
  • Register stays consistent with a formal briefing to a supervisor throughout.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: report, then commit

With a partner, take turns giving two connected sentences aloud, one reporting a position and one stating your own commitment, using a clear framing phrase to mark the shift.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: the five-minute briefing

Deliver a five-minute spoken briefing to a partner or small group, summarizing a negotiation you role-played or one your instructor provides. Include fair reporting, a clearly marked personal commitment, and a closing that separates settled matters from open ones.

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself delivering the five-minute briefing alone. Afterward, listen back and mark the exact point where you shifted from reporting to committing. Confirm the shift is audible without needing to see the words.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Briefing a supervisor who was not present

A supervisor who was absent from a meeting typically depends entirely on the briefing they receive to understand what happened and what is now expected of their office. In many South African workplace settings, a briefing that omits unresolved issues, or that makes settled matters sound more final than they are, can create real problems later when those gaps surface unexpectedly.

Culture in Context — Owning a commitment made on behalf of others

When a briefing reports a commitment made on behalf of an office, as Nomvula's does here, the person delivering the briefing is generally understood to be personally accountable for making sure that commitment is communicated accurately upward, even though the underlying obligation belongs to the office as a whole.

Regional Variation — Written versus spoken briefing expectations

Some institutions expect a written briefing before any spoken summary is given, while others, as in Nomvula's case, expect a spoken account first because of time pressure, with the written version following afterward. Learners should expect to adjust the order and level of detail in a combined briefing to match the expectations of the specific institution they are working within.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Blurring reported claims with personal commitments

Error: Moving from a reported claim into a personal commitment within the same sentence, without a clear attribution word or framing phrase, leaving the reader unsure who is speaking.

Correction: Keep reported claims and personal commitments in separate sentences, and mark the transition between them with a framing phrase such as ngokwami (for my part).

Common Mistake — Treating every discussed point as settled

Error: Listing every matter raised in the meeting under what was agreed, even when some points were only discussed and never actually resolved.

Correction: Separate settled matters from open ones explicitly, using contrasting phrases such as okuvunyelwene ngakho (what was agreed) and okusasele engxoxweni (what remains open).

Common Mistake — Letting register slip across a long turn

Error: Beginning a briefing in formal register and drifting into casual phrasing partway through, especially in the closing summary.

Correction: Check the closing sentences of a briefing with the same care given to the opening, since a supervisor's final impression often depends on the last section read or heard.

Answer Key

  1. The briefing shifts to Nomvula's own commitment at the sentence beginning ngokwami, ngenze isinqumo sokuthi, which follows the reported positions of the engineer and the community representative.
  2. What was agreed is that the campaign will start a month later and that Nomvula's office will handle drafting the plan. What remains open is who will cover the ongoing costs of the campaign after the first three months.
  3. Accepted answers should note that a clear distinction lets the supervisor know exactly what her office is now bound to do, separate from opinions or positions that belong to other participants, which matters because unclear attribution could lead the supervisor to think the office committed to something it did not, or failed to commit to something it did.

Integration

  1. Accepted answers should explain that separating the two sections prevents a reader from mistaking someone else's claim for the writer's own decision, and allows a supervisor to hold the correct party accountable for each part of the account.
  2. Accepted examples include any two natural sentences that use ngokwami to introduce a first-person decision or commitment.

Ukuhumusha Nokulamula Okuthuthukisiwe

Volume V, Chapter 12: Advanced Translation and Mediation · Speak and Write Zulu: Advanced Independent Performance

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Summarize the main content of an English passage in clear, accurate Standard isiZulu, without adding or omitting meaning.
  2. Adapt a message for a new audience, adjusting register and detail while preserving the speaker's intended meaning.
  3. Mediate a short exchange between an English speaking specialist and an isiZulu speaking community member, relaying meaning accurately in both directions.
  4. Identify and avoid common mediation errors, such as adding personal opinion, softening a difficult message inappropriately, or omitting a detail that changes the meaning.
  5. Explain, in Standard isiZulu, the responsibilities and limits of a person who mediates between languages.
  6. Follow Nomvula as she mediates a community meeting where a health specialist explains a new water safety procedure to residents.

Communication Scenario

Back from the district office, Nomvula is asked to support a community meeting where an English speaking water safety specialist will explain a new testing procedure to residents who are more comfortable in isiZulu. Nomvula is not simply translating word for word, she must listen to a full explanation, decide what the residents actually need to understand, and deliver that meaning clearly, in a register that respects both the specialist's technical content and the residents' right to ask questions in their own language. Partway through, an elderly resident asks a pointed question about safety, and Nomvula must carry that concern back to the specialist accurately, without softening it into something smaller than it is, and without exaggerating it either.

  1. This chapter treats mediation as a skill with real responsibility, not as a mechanical word swap. A mediator's task is to preserve meaning, not to decide what either side should have said.
  2. Your own mediating language stays in Standard isiZulu, and your summaries and adaptations must remain faithful to the original content in both directions.

Pronunciation: Carrying Meaning Across Languages

A mediator must speak clearly enough that neither party has to ask for repetition, even while thinking quickly about meaning rather than about individual words.

Pronunciation Focus — Clarity under time pressure

When mediating in real time, a speaker can be tempted to rush, letting penultimate lengthening and vowel clarity slip. Deliberately slowing your delivery slightly, and pausing briefly at clause boundaries, helps both parties follow a mediated message without losing any of its content.

  • Mark the end of each mediated clause with a clear penultimate lengthening, so listeners can tell where one idea ends and the next begins.
  • When relaying a technical term borrowed from English, pronounce it clearly and, where useful, follow it with a brief isiZulu explanation rather than assuming it is understood.
  • Keep your own intonation steady and neutral when relaying a difficult or sensitive message, so that your tone does not add emphasis the original speaker did not intend.

Language Focus: Summarizing and Adapting Responsibly

Purpose: this section activates no new grammatical system. It develops the vocabulary and framing patterns needed to summarize, adapt, and mediate content faithfully, in Standard isiZulu.

12.1 Marking a summary as a summary

Language Focus

A responsible mediator signals clearly when they are summarizing rather than reporting every word, so that listeners understand the level of detail they are receiving.

  • Ngizonichazela isifinyezo salokho okushiwo ngusosayensi, hhayi wonke amagama akhe ngamunye I will explain to you a summary of what the scientist said, not every one of his individual words. isifinyezo (summary) is named explicitly, so listeners do not mistake it for a full transcript
  • Ngokusho kukadokotela, amanzi kufanele ahlolwe kabili ngesonto, hhayi kanye kuphela According to the doctor, the water must be tested twice a week, not just once. ngokusho kuka- (according to) attributes the content clearly to its original source

12.2 Adapting register without changing meaning

Language Focus

Adapting a message for a new audience may mean simplifying technical vocabulary or softening tone for delivery, but the core meaning, including any warning, requirement, or number, must stay exactly the same.

  • Ngamafuphi, lokhu kusho ukuthi amanzi awakahlolwa ngokwanele, ngakho akadliwa okwamanje In short, this means the water has not been tested sufficiently, so it should not be consumed for now. ngamafuphi (in short) marks a simplified restatement while preserving the safety warning intact

12.3 Relaying a question or concern accurately

Pattern — Carrying a question back to the original speaker
U- [igama] ubuza ukuthi ... / U- [igama] ukhathazekile ngokuthi ...
These framing patterns let a mediator carry a question or concern back to the original speaker, naming the person asking and stating the concern precisely, without adding or removing weight.
Isibonelo: Ugogo lo, uyabuza ukuthi kungani sekuthathe isikhathi eside kangaka ukutholakala kwemiphumela yokuhlolwa kwamanzi
Example: This grandmother is asking why it has taken this long for the water test results to become available.

A mediator names who is asking and what is actually being asked, and does not soften a direct question into a vaguer one, or sharpen a mild question into a confrontational one.

Model Text: Ukulamula Emhlanganweni

Read Nomvula's account of mediating the community meeting. Notice how she distinguishes her own summarizing choices from the original speakers' exact words, and how she relays the elderly resident's question without changing its weight.

Umhlangano Womphakathi Ngokuphepha Kwamanzi

Usosayensi wachaza ngesiNgisi indlela entsha yokuhlola amanzi, ekhuluma ngokushesha futhi esebenzisa amagama obuchwepheshe amaningi. Ngaphambi kokuba ngiphendulele lokho emphakathini, ngahlala isikhashana ngicabanga ukuthi yiziphi izingxenye eziyisisekelo kulokho okushiwo, base ngazichaza ngesiZulu esicacile, ngamazwi ami, kepha ngingashintshanga nezinombolo noma izixwayiso azishilo. Ngesikhathi ngiqedile isifinyezo sami, ugogo othile emhlanganweni waphakamisa isandla, ebuza ukuthi kungani kuthatha isikhathi eside kangaka ukuthola imiphumela yokuhlolwa kwamanzi. Angizange ngiyehlise leyo mibuzo, ngayidlulisela ngqo kusosayensi, ngamazwi acacile, futhi ngesikhathi esibuyisela impendulo yakhe, ngayihumushela umphakathi ngokugcwele, kuhlanganise nezizathu ezingaphelele azichazile. Ngasekugcineni komhlangano, ngangazisa bonke ababekhona ukuthi lokho engikuchazile kuwukufinyeza nje, futhi noma ubani onemibuzo eqondile angathinta ihhovisi lethu ngokuqondile.

English Reference

The scientist explained the new water testing procedure in English, speaking quickly and using many technical terms. Before relaying this to the community, I paused briefly to consider which parts were essential, and then explained them in clear isiZulu, in my own words, without changing any of the numbers or warnings he had given. When I finished my summary, an elderly woman at the meeting raised her hand, asking why it takes this long to get the water test results. I did not soften that question, I relayed it directly to the scientist, in clear words, and when I brought back his answer, I translated it fully for the community, including the incomplete reasons he gave. Near the end of the meeting, I let everyone present know that what I had explained was only a summary, and that anyone with a specific question could contact our office directly.

Umsebenzi wokufunda — Reading task:

  1. Khomba lapho uNomvula echaza khona ukuthi lokho akushilo kuwukufinyeza nje. (Point to where Nomvula explains that what she said was only a summary.)
  2. Kungani uNomvula engazange ayehlise umbuzo kagogo? (Why did Nomvula not soften the grandmother's question?)
  3. Yini uNomvula ayenzayo ngaphambi kokuphendulela lokho okushiwo ngusosayensi? (What does Nomvula do before relaying what the scientist said?)

Writing Workshop

Complete the following linked writing tasks in order, producing a short written mediation summary based on this chapter's scenario or a similar one you choose.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 1 — Ukufinyeza: Write two or three sentences that summarize a short English explanation, marking clearly that the sentences are a summary, using isifinyezo or ngokusho kuka-.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 2 — Ukuguqula izinga lokukhuluma: Write one or two sentences that adapt a technical statement into simpler language, keeping every number, warning, or requirement unchanged.

Self edit prompt
Compare your adapted sentence to the original meaning point by point. If any number, warning, or requirement has changed or disappeared, revise the sentence before continuing.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 3 — Ukudlulisela umbuzo: Write two or three sentences that relay a resident's question back to a specialist, naming the person asking and stating the concern precisely.

  1. Draft your summary. Confirm it is marked clearly as a summary, and check it against the original content.
  2. Draft your adapted sentence. Confirm the register is simpler, but the meaning is identical.
  3. Draft your relayed question. Confirm it neither softens nor sharpens the original concern.
  4. Read the full mediation aloud. Confirm the tone stays neutral and clear throughout.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • Every summary is explicitly marked as a summary.
  • Numbers, warnings, and requirements remain unchanged after adaptation.
  • Relayed questions and concerns keep their original weight.
  • Register remains clear, neutral, and consistent throughout.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: summarize, then mark it

With a partner, take turns giving a short English style explanation from memory, then produce a two sentence isiZulu summary that is clearly marked as a summary.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: the community meeting

Role-play a three way exchange with two partners, one playing the specialist, one playing a community member, and the mediator relaying meaning accurately in both directions.

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself summarizing a short English passage into isiZulu, then record yourself relaying an imagined listener's question back in English, checking that both directions stay faithful to the original meaning.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — The mediator's responsibility

In many South African public and community settings, a bilingual staff member is regularly asked to mediate informally between English speaking specialists and isiZulu speaking residents. This role carries real responsibility, since a distorted summary can lead to confusion, mistrust, or unsafe decisions, so accuracy and neutrality matter more than smoothness of delivery.

Culture in Context — Respecting the questioner

Community members, particularly older residents, are generally shown respect when their questions are relayed exactly as asked, rather than reworded into something more convenient for the specialist to answer. A mediator who softens a direct question risks undermining the questioner's authority in the room.

Regional Variation — Technical vocabulary in mediated settings

The isiZulu equivalents available for technical or scientific terms can vary between districts and institutions, and some settings rely more heavily on English loanwords than others. A mediator should be prepared to explain a borrowed term briefly rather than assume every audience shares the same technical vocabulary.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Adding personal opinion while mediating

Error: Inserting the mediator's own view, reassurance, or criticism into a summary or relayed answer, rather than the original speaker's actual content.

Correction: Keep mediated language limited to what the original speaker said, and clearly separate any personal comment by marking it explicitly as your own, if it is offered at all.

Common Mistake — Softening a difficult message

Error: Weakening a warning, a requirement, or a direct question during mediation, in an attempt to make the exchange feel more comfortable.

Correction: Preserve the original weight of warnings, requirements, and questions, and address any discomfort through tone and delivery rather than by changing the content.

Common Mistake — Omitting an unmarked summary

Error: Delivering a shortened version of a longer explanation without telling listeners that it is a summary, leaving them to assume they received the complete original.

Correction: Mark every summary explicitly, using isifinyezo or a similar framing phrase, and offer a path to the fuller original content when one is needed.

Answer Key

  1. She pauses briefly to consider which parts of the explanation are essential, before explaining them in clear isiZulu, in her own words, without changing numbers or warnings.
  2. She relays the question directly to the scientist, in clear words, without softening it, and later translates his full answer for the community.

Integration

  1. Accepted answers should explain that listeners need to know a summary may leave out detail, so they can seek fuller information when a decision depends on it, and so they are not misled into thinking they received the complete original explanation.

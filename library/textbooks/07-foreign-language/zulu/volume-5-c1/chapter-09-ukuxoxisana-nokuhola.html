Ukuxoxisana Nokuhola: Ukuphendula Ngqo Emhlanganweni

Volume V, Chapter 9: Negotiation, Leadership, and Strategic Interaction · Speak and Write Zulu: Advanced Independent Performance

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Put forward a proposal clearly and support it under direct questioning, in real time rather than after the fact.
  2. Push back on a colleague's proposal respectfully, using language that challenges the idea without attacking the person.
  3. Offer and recognize a genuine concession, distinguishing it from simply repeating your original position more politely.
  4. Manage a disagreement that grows heated, redirecting the discussion toward a workable outcome.
  5. Summarize a meeting's decisions and remaining disagreements clearly enough for someone who was not present to act on them.
  6. Follow Nomvula as she moves from reporting on a discussion to participating directly in one, representing her department's position in a planning meeting about the water schedule.

Communication Scenario

Nomvula's briefing on the radio debate reaches further than she expected. Her supervisor asks her to represent the office at a planning meeting where the engineer, the community representative, and two ward councillors will try to agree on a workable water schedule before the next dry season. This time Nomvula cannot simply listen and report afterward. She must speak for her office's position, that any schedule needs a public information campaign built in from the start, respond when the engineer questions whether that campaign is really necessary, and decide, in the moment, whether a compromise on timing is worth accepting if it means the campaign stays in the plan. Partway through, the community representative and the engineer begin repeating their earlier radio disagreement more sharply than before, and one of the councillors turns to Nomvula and asks directly what her office is prepared to commit to.

  1. Chapter 8 asked you to report fairly on other people's claims after the fact. This chapter asks you to make, defend, and adjust your own claims while the conversation is still moving.
  2. Negotiation is not simply repeating a position more forcefully when challenged. This chapter treats knowing when to hold firm and when to concede as a distinct, practiced skill.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 8 developed your ability to track other people's claims and report them fairly once the exchange was over. This chapter asks something harder, to hold your own position inside a live exchange, to hear a challenge and respond to it without either collapsing your position or refusing to move at all. You are still relying on the reported-speech and complement-clause systems from Chapter 8, now redirected toward your own proposals rather than someone else's, and on the concord and register control consolidated across this unit.

Coming From Chapter 8

Accurate reported speech, separation of claim from support from framing, and a fair written and spoken summary of what others said.

New in This Chapter

Proposing a position, defending it under challenge, offering a genuine concession, and de-escalating a disagreement in real time.

Pronunciation: Firmness Without Aggression

A proposal delivered too flatly can sound uncertain, while one delivered too sharply can sound like an attack. Meetings that stay productive usually depend on speakers who can sound firm, measured, and audibly willing to be questioned, all at once.

Pronunciation Focus — Steady pitch under challenge

When a proposal is challenged, resist the pull to raise pitch and speed sharply in response, since a sudden rise often reads as defensiveness rather than confidence.

  • Keep the penultimate lengthening and phrase rhythm you have practiced since earlier volumes steady even under a direct question, rather than clipping the ends of words as pace increases.
  • A short, deliberate pause before answering a sharp question, rather than an immediate rushed reply, generally sounds more confident and is easier for other listeners to follow.
  • Practice the model exchange in this chapter twice, once at a comfortable pace to internalize the shape of each turn, and once at a faster pace closer to a real meeting, without losing clear articulation.

Language Focus: Proposing, Conceding, and Holding a Position

Purpose: this section develops the language of proposing, defending, and adjusting a position in real time, building directly on the reported-speech and complement-clause systems from Chapter 8 and redirecting them toward the learner's own contributions rather than another speaker's.

9.1 Putting forward a proposal

Language Focus

A proposal is easiest to defend later when it is stated plainly at the outset, with the reasoning attached rather than left implicit. Stating a proposal clearly is not the same as stating it inflexibly.

  • Sicela ukuthi uhlelo lwazo zonke izinhlelo lufake nomkhankaso wokwazisa umphakathi kusukela ekuqaleni We are asking that the overall plan include a public awareness campaign from the very start. sicela ukuthi (we are asking that) states the proposal directly and attaches it to a complement clause
  • Sikusho lokhu ngoba umphakathi ongaziswanga uphendula kabi uma amanzi eqedwa ngokungalindelekile We say this because an uninformed community responds badly when water is cut without warning. sikusho lokhu ngoba (we say this because) attaches the supporting reason to the proposal already made

9.2 Challenging a proposal without attacking the speaker

Language Focus

A respectful challenge questions the proposal's substance, its cost, its timing, its evidence, while keeping the speaker's standing in the discussion intact.

  • Angiphikisani nesizathu sakho, kepha ngiyabuza ukuthi singayithola kanjani imali yalowo mkhankaso ngesikhathi esifushane kangaka I do not dispute your reasoning, but I am asking how we would fund that campaign in such a short time. angiphikisani nesizathu sakho (I do not dispute your reasoning) separates the challenge from a personal objection

9.3 Offering a genuine concession

Pattern — Marking a real shift in position
ngiyavuma ukuthi ... / singakwazi ukwenza ... uma ...
A genuine concession changes something about the original proposal, timing, scope, or cost, rather than simply restating the same position using softer wording.
Isibonelo: Ngiyavuma ukuthi umkhankaso ungaqala ngemva kwenyanga, uma isikhathi salokho sifakwa ohlelweni oluphelele
Example: I agree the campaign could start a month later, if that timeline is written into the overall plan.

Notice that ngiyavuma ukuthi (I agree that) is followed by an actual change, a start date moving, rather than a repeated version of the original request. A listener should be able to point to what moved.

9.4 De-escalating a sharpening disagreement

Language Focus

When two other speakers begin repeating an earlier disagreement more sharply, a third participant can redirect the discussion by naming the shared goal and asking a concrete question, rather than taking a side in the sharpened exchange.

  • Ake sibuyele esisombululweni esifunwa yithu sonke, singakwazi yini ukuvumelana ngesinyathelo esisodwa esilandelayo Let us return to the solution we all want, could we agree on a single next step. ake sibuyele (let us return to) redirects the room toward shared ground without endorsing either sharpened position

Model Text: Emhlanganweni Wesigungu

Read the transcript excerpt below from the planning meeting Nomvula attends on behalf of her office, followed by the closing meeting note she drafts before leaving. Notice where she states her proposal, where she is challenged directly, where she offers a real concession, and where the councillor's question forces her to commit to something specific.

Umhlangano Wokuhlela Uhlelo Lwamanzi — excerpt from the water-schedule planning meeting
Nomvula:
Sicela ukuthi uhlelo lufake nomkhankaso wokwazisa umphakathi kusukela ekuqaleni, hhayi njengento eyengezwa ekugcineni. We are asking that the plan include a public awareness campaign from the very start, not as something added at the end.
Unjiniyela:
Angiphikisani nesizathu sakho, kepha singayithola kanjani imali yalokho ngesikhathi esifushane kangaka? I do not dispute your reasoning, but how would we fund that in such a short time?
Nomvula:
Ngiyavuma ukuthi umkhankaso ungaqala ngemva kwenyanga, uma isikhathi salokho sifakwa ohlelweni oluphelele hhayi njengesithembiso esingabhaliwe. I agree the campaign could start a month later, if that timeline is written into the overall plan and not left as an unwritten promise.
(Ummeli womphakathi noNjiniyela baphinda inkulumo-mpikiswano yasemsakazweni, izwi labo liphakama.)
Nomvula:
Ake sibuyele esisombululweni esifunwa yithu sonke, singakwazi yini ukuvumelana ngesinyathelo esisodwa esilandelayo? Let us return to the solution we all want, could we agree on a single next step?
Ikhansela:
Kulungile. Hhovisi lakini lizimisele ukwenzani ngempela, ngaphambi kokuba sesiqedele lo mhlangano? Very well. What exactly is your office prepared to commit to, before we close this meeting?
Nomvula:
Hhovisi lami lizimisele ukuqala ukubhala uhlelo lomkhankaso kusasa, futhi ngizosilethela isikhathi esiqondile ngaphakathi kwezinsuku ezinhlanu. My office is prepared to begin drafting the campaign plan tomorrow, and I will bring the committee a precise timeline within five days.
Inothi lomhlangano — Nomvula's closing meeting note
Emhlanganweni wanamuhla, hhovisi lami licele ukuthi umkhankaso wokwazisa umphakathi ufakwe ohlelweni kusukela ekuqaleni. Unjiniyela ubuze ukuthi lokho kungakhokhelwa kanjani ngesikhathi esifushane, futhi ngiye ngavuma ukuthi umkhankaso ungaqala ngemva kwenyanga uma lesi sikhathi sibhalwe ohlelweni oluphelele. Ngesikhathi inkulumo-mpikiswano yasemsakazweni iphindwa, ngiye ngabuyisela umhlangano esisombululweni esivumelekile. Ngiyazibophezela ukuthi hhovisi lami lizoqala ukubhala uhlelo lomkhankaso kusasa, futhi sizolethela isigungu isikhathi esiqondile ngaphakathi kwezinsuku ezinhlanu.
At today's meeting, my office asked for a public awareness campaign to be included in the plan from the start. The engineer asked how that would be funded on such a short timeline, and I agreed the campaign could begin a month later if that timeline is written into the full plan. When the earlier radio disagreement resurfaced, I redirected the meeting toward the agreed solution. I have committed that my office will begin drafting the campaign plan tomorrow and will bring the committee a precise timeline within five days.

Umsebenzi wokufunda — Reading task:

  1. Khomba lapho uNomvula enza khona isiphakamiso sakhe okokuqala. (Point to where Nomvula first states her proposal.)
  2. Yisiphi isivumelwano uNomvula asenzayo, futhi yini eyashintsha ngempela kuso? (What concession does Nomvula make, and what actually changed as a result of it?)
  3. Qhathanisa inkulumo-mpikiswano nenothi lomhlangano. Yini uNomvula angayifakanga enothini engaba nembiwe nokungathembeki? (Compare the meeting exchange with the meeting note. What did Nomvula leave out of her note that would have made it seem less complete?)

Writing Workshop

Complete the following linked writing tasks in order, producing a short meeting note that fairly and precisely records a negotiation, either the model meeting above, a discussion your instructor provides, or a negotiation you role-play with a partner.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 1 — Ukubeka isiphakamiso: Write one or two sentences stating your own proposal clearly, including the reasoning behind it.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 2 — Ukuphendula inselelo: Write one or two sentences responding to a challenge to your proposal, without abandoning it and without dismissing the challenge.

Self edit prompt
Read your concession sentence aloud to a colleague who did not hear the original proposal. Ask, can you tell exactly what changed between my original position and this one. If your colleague cannot point to a specific change, revise before continuing.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 3 — Ukubuyisela umhlangano ezingxoxweni: Write one sentence redirecting a sharpening disagreement between two other participants toward a shared next step.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 4 — Ukuzibophezela: Write one closing sentence stating a specific, checkable commitment, including who will do what, and by when.

  1. Identify the proposal at stake. State it in one clear sentence before drafting anything else.
  2. Draft the challenge and response. Confirm the response engages the substance of the challenge rather than repeating the proposal unchanged.
  3. Draft the concession. Confirm a reader could name exactly what moved.
  4. Draft the redirection. Confirm it names a shared goal rather than taking either side.
  5. Draft the commitment. Confirm it includes a specific action and a specific timeframe.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • The proposal is stated plainly, with its reasoning attached.
  • The challenge is addressed on its substance, not brushed aside.
  • The concession names a specific, checkable change.
  • Any redirection of a sharpening disagreement stays neutral between the two other speakers.
  • The closing commitment states who will do what, and by when.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: proposal and challenge

With a partner, take turns stating a short proposal aloud and responding to a direct challenge to it, using the reasoning and response patterns from this chapter.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: the planning meeting role-play

In a group of three or four, role-play a short planning meeting in which each participant holds a distinct position. Each speaker must state a proposal, respond to at least one challenge, and either hold their position with a clear reason or offer a genuine, specific concession.

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself stating a proposal, then responding to two challenges you write for yourself in advance, one you decide to hold firm against and one you decide to concede on. Confirm afterward that a listener could tell which was which.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Consensus-seeking in committee settings

Many South African committee and community-planning settings place real weight on reaching a solution that all parties can accept, even when that takes longer than a simple majority decision. A participant who pushes only for their own preferred outcome, without visibly working toward shared ground, may be seen as poorly suited to future leadership roles, regardless of how strong their individual argument was.

Culture in Context — Speaking for an office rather than oneself

When a speaker represents an office or department rather than only themselves, as Nomvula does here, commitments made in the meeting are generally understood to bind the office, not merely the individual speaking. This raises the stakes of a concession and is part of why concrete, checkable commitments matter more than general reassurance.

Regional Variation — Meeting conventions across institutions

The degree of formality, the use of a chairperson to manage turns, and the tolerance for open disagreement in a meeting can differ noticeably between municipal committees, workplace meetings, and community forums. Learners should expect to adapt the negotiation language in this chapter to the specific institutional setting they encounter, rather than assuming one fixed format.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Repeating a position instead of responding to the challenge

Error: Answering a direct challenge by restating the original proposal in slightly different words, without engaging with the substance of the objection raised.

Correction: Address the specific concern the challenge raises, such as cost or timing, directly, even if the answer is that the concern is outweighed by the original reasoning.

Common Mistake — Offering a concession that concedes nothing

Error: Using softening language such as ngiyavuma ukuthi (I agree that) while restating the original position unchanged, which other participants will notice quickly.

Correction: Ensure a concession names an actual, specific change to timing, scope, cost, or another concrete element of the proposal.

Common Mistake — Taking a side while trying to de-escalate

Error: Attempting to calm a sharpening disagreement between two other speakers by quietly endorsing one of their positions, which tends to intensify rather than reduce the conflict.

Correction: Redirect toward a shared goal or a concrete next step without endorsing either side's sharpened position, as modeled in this chapter's language focus.

Practical Performance Task

Chapter 9 Practical Check

A Negotiation That Holds Ground and Moves Forward

Complete the following connected tasks, in order, treating them as a short role-played negotiation and a written meeting note that records its outcome.

  1. Prepare a short proposal. Answer the comprehension questions above before continuing.
  2. State your proposal aloud to a partner or group. Include your reasoning in the same turn.
  3. Respond to at least one direct challenge. Either hold your position with a clear reason or offer a specific, genuine concession.
  4. Redirect any sharpening disagreement. If your role-play includes more than two speakers, practice naming shared ground without taking a side.
  5. Write a closing meeting note. Record the proposal, the challenge, the outcome, and one specific, checkable commitment.
CriterionWhat is assessed
Clear proposalThe proposal and its reasoning are stated plainly and audibly.
Substantive responseA challenge is answered on its substance, not deflected or merely restated against.
Genuine concessionAny concession offered names a specific, checkable change.
De-escalationA sharpening disagreement, where present, is redirected without favoring either side.
Concrete commitmentThe closing note states who will do what, and by when.
Register consistencyLanguage remains appropriate to a formal planning meeting, consistent with this chapter.

Answer Key

Model Text — Comprehension

  1. Nomvula's proposal first appears in her opening turn, where she asks that the awareness campaign be included in the plan from the start rather than added at the end.
  2. The councillor asks Nomvula to state exactly what her office is prepared to commit to before the meeting closes.
  3. Accepted answers should note that Nomvula's meeting note omits the moment when the engineer and community representative's disagreement sharpened, focusing instead on the agreed outcome, which keeps the note useful but leaves out some of the tension present in the live exchange.

Integration

  1. Restating a proposal in different words leaves the substance of the position unchanged and does not respond to the challenge. A genuine concession changes something specific and checkable, such as a timeline, in response to the concern raised.
  2. Accepted examples include any natural sentence using "ake sibuyele esisombululweni esifunwa yithu sonke" to redirect a sharpening disagreement toward shared ground.

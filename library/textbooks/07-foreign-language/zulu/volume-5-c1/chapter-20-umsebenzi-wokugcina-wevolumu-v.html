Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Bring listening, speaking, reading, and writing together in a single integrated performance, rather than practicing each skill separately.
  2. Listen to an extended, unscripted account of a public event and report accurately on what you understood.
  3. Read a complete collection entry independently, without a supporting glossary attached to every line.
  4. Deliver a short, unscripted spoken contribution at a public event, drawing on the planning and rehearsal habits built across this volume.
  5. Write a closing reflection that draws together your reading, listening, speaking, and writing portfolios from Chapters 18 and 19.
  6. Follow Nomvula through the launch event itself, as her collection contribution, her spoken reflection, her original writing, and her presentation come together in one closing performance.

Pronunciation: Delivery Under Real Conditions

A rehearsed presentation delivered to a patient partner is not quite the same task as a short contribution delivered to a room, with background noise, an unfamiliar microphone, or an unexpected question from someone in the audience. The pronunciation habits built across this volume, penultimate lengthening, phrase rhythm, and planned pauses, matter most precisely when conditions are least controlled.

Pronunciation Focus — Recovering rhythm after an interruption

An unplanned interruption, a question, a noise, a pause to find the right word, can break a speaker's rhythm. Recovering that rhythm matters more than avoiding the interruption altogether.

  • After any interruption, take one breath before continuing, rather than rushing back into speech at a faster pace than before.
  • Restate, briefly, the point you were making before the interruption, so both you and your listener return to the same place.
  • Let the sentence that follows an interruption carry its own penultimate lengthening and closing rhythm, rather than treating it as an afterthought.

Capstone Skills: Bringing the Portfolios Together

Purpose: this section activates no new grammatical system. It develops the habit of connecting comprehension and production into a single performance, using grammar, vocabulary, and register already consolidated in this volume.

20.1 Listening and responding in sequence

Language Focus

At a real event, listening does not end before speaking begins. A capstone speaker often needs to listen to an introduction, a question, or another contributor's remarks, and then respond using language shaped by what was just heard, not only by what was planned in advance.

  • Response habit: Ake ngiphinde ngithi, njengoba umhleli eshilo manje... Response habit: Let me say again, as the editor has just said... connects a planned point to something just heard, rather than ignoring it
  • Response habit: Lowo ngumbuzo omuhle, futhi ake ngizame ukuwuphendula ngamafuphi. Response habit: That is a good question, and let me try to answer it briefly. acknowledges an unplanned question before answering it

These response habits do not replace planning. They extend it, giving a speaker a way to fold new, unplanned material into an existing structure.

20.2 Reading without a supporting glossary

Habit — Reading a complete text on its own terms
[umbhalo] — ngiyaqonda yini isihloko, izinhloso, nomlandela wemicabango, ngaphandle kwencazelo yamagama ngamunye?
Independent reading, at this level, means asking whether the topic, purpose, and flow of ideas are clear from context, rather than pausing at every unfamiliar word. A reader who stops for every word loses the shape of the whole text.
Isibonelo: Ngathi ngifunda indaba yonke kuqala, ngaphandle kokuma emagameni engingawajwayele
Example: I read the whole story first, without stopping at words I was not familiar with

20.3 Reflecting across a whole body of work

Language Focus

A closing reflection is different from the smaller reflections written in Chapter 19. Rather than commenting on a single item, it looks across a comprehension portfolio and a production portfolio together, asking what pattern connects them and what has changed since the volume began.

Capstone Task One: Listening at the Launch

Nomvula's first capstone task is listening. The editor opens the launch event with unscripted remarks, occasionally glancing at notes but mostly speaking directly to the room. Read the transcript of the opening remarks once for gist, then a second time for detail, before answering the questions that follow, exactly as Nomvula listened before responding.

Transcript, the editor's opening remarks at the launch event, delivered mostly without notes.

Umhleli:
Sanibonani nonke, ngiyajabula kakhulu ukunibona lapha namuhla. Lo mqulu esiwuvulayo namuhla awuveli emsebenzini womuntu oyedwa, uveli emsebenzini wabantu abaningi abazithobayo ukwabelana ngezindaba zabo. Hello everyone, I am very glad to see you all here today. This collection we are opening today does not come from the work of one person, it comes from the work of many people who were humble enough to share their stories.
Umhleli:
Abanye babhalile ngokwabo, abanye badiketile amazwi abo, kodwa bonke balandele indlela efanayo, ukulalela kuqala, ukubhala ngemva kwalokho. Some wrote for themselves, some dictated their words, but all followed the same path, listening first, and writing afterward.
Umhleli:
Namuhla, sizolalela abanye babo bekhuluma ngamabomu ngalokho abakubhalile, futhi ngiyanicela ukuthi nibuze imibuzo uma ininike. Today, we will listen to some of them speaking deliberately about what they wrote, and I invite you to ask questions if you have any.

Umsebenzi wokulalela — Listening task:

  1. Khomba umusho owodwa lapho umhleli echaza ukuthi lo mqulu wenziwe kanjani. (Point to one sentence where the editor explains how this collection was made.)
  2. Ngabe umhleli usho ukuthi bonke abalimbi babhalile ngokwabo? Sekela impendulo yakho ngokuveza umusho ohambisana nayo. (Does the editor say all contributors wrote by themselves? Support your answer by pointing to the sentence it comes from.)
  3. Yini umhleli acela ukuthi izethameli zenze uma zinemibuzo? (What does the editor ask the audience to do if they have questions?)

Capstone Task Two: Reading the Full Collection Entry

Before her turn to speak, Nomvula reads another contributor's complete entry printed in the launch programme, on her own, without the running glosses that supported earlier chapters. Read the passage once for the overall topic and purpose, and only afterward check the small number of glossed items provided beneath it.

Indawo Yokuqala Engangiyihlala Kuyo

Ngikhule endaweni encane, lapho wonke umuntu ayemazi khona wonke umuntu, futhi lapho izindaba zabantu zazihamba ngokushesha kunanoma yiziphi izindaba zomabonakude. Ngangicabanga ukuthi impilo yami yayingafani nekaanoma ubani, kodwa ngokuhamba kweminyaka, ngaqaphela ukuthi izinto engangizibona njengezingananazi, umculo owawudlala emsakazweni ngamaSonto, indlela abantu ababevakashelana ngayo ngaphandle kokushayela ucingo kuqala, zaziyingxenye yobuhle bendawo yami engangingakaze ngibuqonde. Angisayihlali khona manje, kodwa lapho ngiyivakashela, ngiyithola ilokhu ikhona, ishintshile kancane, kodwa ingakalahli lokho okwakuyenza ibe seyakhona.

Note, this passage is a textbook created reading sample, adapted for instructional use, and is not drawn from an actual published collection. Glossed items: ngokushesha quickly; ucingo telephone.

Umsebenzi wokufunda — Reading task, complete before rereading:

  1. Ngamazwi akho, chaza ngokufushane ukuthi le ndatshana ikhuluma ngani. (In your own words, briefly explain what this short piece is about.)
  2. Khomba umusho owodwa okhombisa ukuthi umbhali usebona indawo yakhe ngendlela ehlukile kunaleyo ayeyibona ngayo esemncane. (Point to one sentence showing that the writer now sees their hometown differently than they did as a child.)
  3. Ngaphandle kokusebenzisa uhlu lwamagama, chaza ukuthi umqondo wamagama ngokushesha no ucingo uwuqonde kanjani ngokusekelwe emongweni. (Without using a glossary, explain how you worked out the meanings of ngokushesha and ucingo from context.)

Capstone Task Three: Speaking at the Launch Event

When her turn comes, Nomvula delivers her presentation from Chapter 19, but the launch event does not end there, an audience member asks her an unplanned question afterward. No audio recording accompanies this textbook, so learners should deliver this exchange aloud, following the response habit from Section 20.1.

Transcript, the unplanned exchange following Nomvula's presentation from Chapter 19.

Isethameli:
Ngabe bekulula yini kuwe ukuthola loyo mlando wogogo wakho, noma bekukhona okwakunzima ukukuqoqa? Was it easy for you to gather your grandmother's story, or was there something difficult about collecting it?
Nomvula:
Lowo ngumbuzo omuhle, futhi ake ngizame ukuwuphendula ngamafuphi. Kwakunzima ukukhumbula amagama akhe ngokuqondile, ngakho-ke ngagcina ngibhala ngendlela engangikhumbula ngayo indlela ayekhuluma ngayo, hhayi amagama akhe ngamunye. That is a good question, and let me try to answer it briefly. It was difficult to remember her exact words, so in the end I wrote in the way I remembered her manner of speaking, not her exact words one by one.

Umsebenzi wokukhuluma — Speaking task:

  1. Zilolongeleni le nkulumo entsha noqabane lwenu, elilodwa liphendula njengesethameli, elinye njengoNomvula. (Practice this new exchange with a partner, one of you responding as the audience member, the other as Nomvula.)
  2. Bese cabanga umbuzo ongazenzekela kuwe emcimbini wakho wokwethula, bese uzilolonga ukuwuphendula usebenzisa isu lokuphendula esifundweni sika 20.1. (Then think of a question that could happen to you at your own presentation event, and practice answering it using the response strategy from Section 20.1.)

Capstone Task Four: A Closing Written Reflection

After the launch event, Nomvula writes a closing reflection that looks across both of her portfolios from Chapters 18 and 19, her comprehension of other people's reading and listening material, and her own independent speaking and writing. Read her reflection, then answer the questions that follow.

Umbono Wokuvala

Uma ngiqhathanisa lapho ngiqale khona nalapho sengikhona manje, into engiyibona kakhulu ukuthi angisenqeni ukuzama ukuqonda into engingayiqondi ngokuphelele kwesokuqala. Ngesikhathi ngifunda indaba yesethulo esithi 18, ngangesaba ukuthi ngizoshiya izindawo eziningi ezingacaci, kodwa ngafunda ukukhomba ngokuthembekile lapho ngiqonda khona nalapho ngingaqondi khona, hhayi ukuzenzisa ukuthi ngiqonda konke. Ekubhaleni nasekukhulumeni kwami, into eshintshile kakhulu ukuthi manje ngiyahlela ngaphambi kokuqala, futhi ngiyaziphinda izinto zami ngaphambi kokuba nginikeze omunye umuntu ukuthi azifunde noma azizwe. Angikaze ngicabange ukuthi ngizoke ngikwazi ukuma phambi kwabantu ngikhulume ngesiZulu ngaphandle kwephepha, kodwa namuhla sengikwenzile, futhi lokho kungikhumbuza ukuthi ukufunda ulimi akusiwo umsebenzi ophelayo, kuyindlela yokuphila.

Gloss: ukuzenzisa, to pretend.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task, complete before rereading:

  1. Yiziphi izinto ezimbili uNomvula asho ukuthi zishintshile phakathi kokuqala kwakhe nalapho ekhona manje? (What two things does Nomvula say have changed between when she started and where she is now?)
  2. Khomba umusho owodwa okhombisa ukuthi uNomvula usebenzisa isu afunda ngaso esahlukweni 18, ukukhomba ngokuthembekile lapho eqonda khona nalapho engaqondi khona. (Point to one sentence showing Nomvula using the strategy she learned in Chapter 18, honestly naming where she does and does not understand.)

Writing Workshop: The Capstone Reflection

Self reflection prompt

Umsebenzi wokubhala 3 — Ukuhlanganisa umqulu wonke: Arrange your Chapter 18 comprehension portfolio, your Chapter 19 production portfolio, and this closing reflection together as one complete Volume V capstone submission.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: recovering from an interruption

With a partner, take turns delivering a short prepared statement while the other person interrupts once with an unplanned question. Practice using the recovery habit from Section 20.1 before continuing.

Umsebenzi wokusiza iqabane — Communicative task: launch event role play

In a small group, role play a short launch event, one person as the editor giving unscripted opening remarks, one or more as contributors presenting briefly, and one as an audience member asking an unplanned question.

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself delivering your presentation from Chapter 19, then record yourself answering an unplanned question you write for yourself in advance without rehearsing the answer beforehand.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Community ownership of a shared collection

A launch event for a community collection is typically framed as belonging to everyone who contributed, not to any single author or editor. Opening remarks at such events commonly emphasize the collective nature of the work before individual contributions are introduced.

Culture in Context — Responding to audience questions with respect

Acknowledging a question before answering it, rather than answering immediately without comment, is a common courtesy in many formal isiZulu speaking settings, and signals that the question has been taken seriously.

Regional Variation — Q&A conventions vary by setting

Some public events invite questions during a presentation, while others reserve all questions for the end. Independent speakers should observe the convention in use at a given event rather than assuming one fixed pattern applies everywhere.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Losing rhythm entirely after an interruption

Error: Abandoning penultimate lengthening and phrase rhythm after an unplanned question or interruption, and speaking in a flat, rushed manner for the rest of the response.

Correction: Apply the recovery habit from Section 20.1, taking one breath, restating the point briefly, and letting the following sentence carry its normal rhythm.

Common Mistake — Stopping at every unfamiliar word while reading

Error: Pausing at each unfamiliar word during an independent reading task, losing the overall topic and purpose of the text in the process.

Correction: Follow Section 20.2, reading for the whole shape of the text first, and returning only afterward to specific words that remain unclear.

Common Mistake — Writing a closing reflection about only one portfolio

Error: Writing a closing reflection that discusses only the production portfolio from Chapter 19 or only the comprehension portfolio from Chapter 18, rather than connecting both.

Correction: Follow Section 20.3, and treat both portfolios as one connected body of work when writing the closing reflection.

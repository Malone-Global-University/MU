Communication Scenario

The community storytelling evening went well, and one of the organizers now asks Nomvula whether she would be willing to submit her story about her grandmother for a small printed collection, one that will be read rather than heard, by people who did not grow up in her family and will not have her voice or her pacing to guide them. Nomvula reads her own draft again and notices things a listening audience forgave that a reading audience will not, a pronoun whose reference has drifted, a sentence that runs on longer than it should, a closing line that leans a little too casually for print. She sets out to revise, not to rewrite, keeping the story's warmth while sharpening its clarity for a reader who was not in the room.

  1. This chapter treats editing and revision as a controlled skill applied to writing already produced, rather than a new grammatical system, and every correction in this chapter draws on concord, tense, aspect, mood, and register control already consolidated earlier in this volume.
  2. Learners revise their own Chapter 16 narrative, or a comparable short narrative, moving it from a spoken or informally written draft toward a version suited to a reading audience.

Pronunciation: Reading Your Own Work Aloud

Reading a draft aloud, slowly and at a steady pace, remains one of the most reliable ways to catch a concord error, a missing final vowel, or a sentence that has grown too long for a listener, or a reader, to follow comfortably.

Pronunciation Focus — Editing by ear

A writer's eye tends to skip over small errors that a writer's ear will not.

  • Read each sentence aloud on its own, pausing fully at the end, before moving to the next one.
  • Listen for a subject concord that no longer matches the noun it once agreed with, especially after a sentence has been shortened or combined with another.
  • Notice where your voice runs out of breath before the sentence ends, since that is often a sign the sentence should be divided in the written version as well.

Language Focus: Editing for Concord, Clarity, Tone, and Register

Purpose: this section activates no new grammatical system. It develops a controlled editing process that checks concord accuracy, reference clarity, tonal consistency, and register appropriateness, using grammar already consolidated in this volume.

17.1 Checking concord accuracy across a longer text

Language Focus

In a short sentence, a concord error is easy to notice, but in a longer narrative, a subject can drift several clauses away from its original noun, and a later concord can quietly fail to match it. Editing for concord means tracing each subject and object back to the noun it represents, clause by clause, rather than trusting that the sentence sounds correct.

  • Draft: Ugogo wayekhuluma kakhulu, futhi babengishaqisa njalo Draft: Grandmother used to speak forcefully, and they always startled me error, the second clause shifts to a plural subject concord although the subject, ugogo, remains singular class 1a
  • Revised: Ugogo wayekhuluma kakhulu, futhi wayengishaqisa njalo Revised: Grandmother used to speak forcefully, and she always startled me corrected, the subject concord wa- is restored across both clauses to agree with the class 1a subject

This is a controlled editing skill. Learners identify and correct concord drift in a text they or a partner has written, rather than producing new concord paradigms.

17.2 Clarifying pronoun and object concord reference

Pattern — Checking what a concord refers to
[object concord] — kubhekiswa kubani, noma kuyini?
For every object concord in a sentence, a careful editor asks a simple question, who or what does this refer to, and whether a reader unfamiliar with the story could answer that question from the surrounding text alone. If the answer is not clear, the sentence should name the noun again, rather than relying on the concord to carry the full weight of reference.
Isibonelo: Imbiza yagxumuka, futhi ugogo wayesuka wayithatha ngokushesha
Example: The pot boiled over, and grandmother immediately went to take it, where yi- clearly refers back to imbiza and not to some other item in the story

17.3 Adjusting tone and register for a new audience

Language Focus

A story told aloud to friends can end on a light, conversational line, but the same story, printed for readers who do not know the storyteller, often benefits from a slightly more considered closing, one that still feels personal but does not depend on shared context or a listener's laughter to land well.

  • Spoken draft: Nokho, kwakumnandi, angithi? Spoken draft: Anyway, it was nice, wasn't it? appropriate for a live audience, but too casual and too dependent on a shared moment for a printed collection
  • Revised for print: Namuhla, ngisadla izinkulumo zikagogo, futhi angikaze ngiyifake isandla kulokho okungesikho okwami Revised for print: Today, I still live by grandmother's words, and I have never put my hand into what does not belong to me controlled revision, the closing line is rewritten to stand on its own for a reader who was not present at the telling

17.4 Distinguishing correction from revision

Language Focus

A correction fixes something that is grammatically wrong, such as a concord that does not match its noun. A revision changes something that is grammatically correct but not yet as clear, as well organized, or as suited to its audience as it could be. A thorough edit does both, but a writer should know which kind of change is being made at each step.

Model Text: Indaba Kagogo, Ibuyekeziwe

Indaba Kagogo, Ibuyekeziwe

Ugogo wayekhuluma kakhulu, ngendlela eyayingishaqisa njalo, ikakhulukazi uma ebona umuntu esebenzisa okuthile okungesikho okwakhe. Imbiza yakhe yayihlala isephini, ivutshelwa isiphuzo lesi neselaso isikhathi, futhi uma ingxenye yamanzi ifika esiphethweni, imbiza yayigxumuka, gubu, umsi uphuma ngale nendlela, futhi ugogo wayesuka wayithatha ngokushesha. Ngangihlala ngimlalele ngimbukile, ehambahamba endlini, ephethe intshebe encane yakhe, kanti nalokho okushoyo kubalulekile. Ngelinye ilanga, ngithe sengiphuma nabangani bami, ugogo wathi, ungayifaki isandla kulokho okungesikho okwakho, futhi ngangazi ukuthi ubekhuluma ngenkulumo eyayidlula lokho engangikwenza ngaleso sikhathi. Namuhla, ngisadla izinkulumo zikagogo, futhi angikaze ngiyifake isandla kulokho okungesikho okwami.

English Reference

Grandmother used to speak forcefully, in a way that always startled me, especially when she saw someone using something that did not belong to them. Her pot always sat on the stove, simmering whatever needed to simmer at that hour, and when the water reached its edge, the pot would boil over, gubu, with smoke pouring out on all sides, and grandmother would immediately go to take it off. I would sit and watch her, listening closely, as she moved about the house, holding her small chin, and whatever she said mattered. One day, as I was leaving with my friends, grandmother said, do not put your hand into what does not belong to you, and I knew she was speaking about something far beyond what I was doing right then. Today, I still live by grandmother's words, and I have never put my hand into what does not belong to me.

Umsebenzi wokufunda — Reading task:

  1. Qhathanisa lesi sigaba sokugcina nesigaba sokugcina esisukela esahlukweni 16. Yiziphi izinguquko ozibonayo? (Compare this closing paragraph with the closing paragraph from Chapter 16. What changes do you notice?)
  2. Kungani umusho othi ugogo wayesuka wayithatha ngokushesha wengezwa kulesi sigaba? (Why has the sentence saying grandmother immediately went to take it off been added to this version?)
  3. Ubuyekeze kanjani umusho wokugcina, futhi kungani lolu shintsho lwenziwa? (How has the closing sentence been revised, and why was this change made?)

Writing Workshop

Return to the short personal narrative you wrote in Chapter 16, and revise it for a reader who was not present when you first told or drafted it.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 1 — Ukuhlola izivumelwano: Read your Chapter 16 draft aloud, sentence by sentence, and mark any place where a subject or object concord no longer clearly matches the noun it should agree with.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 2 — Ukuhlola ukucaca: Underline every independent pronoun and object concord in your draft, and check that a reader unfamiliar with your story could tell what each one refers to.

Self edit prompt
If you cannot answer, in one short phrase, what a pronoun or object concord refers to, your reader probably cannot either. Name the noun again rather than leaving the reference unclear.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 3 — Ukuhlola isimo nesigaba sokugcina: Read your closing line again, and decide whether it depends on a listener's presence or shared context. If it does, revise it into a sentence that stands on its own for a reader.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 4 — Uhlelo oluphelele: Produce a clean, revised version of your narrative, incorporating your concord corrections, your reference clarifications, and your adjusted closing line.

  1. Read your draft aloud. Mark any sentence where a concord feels uncertain or where your breath runs out before the sentence ends.
  2. Trace every pronoun and object concord. Confirm each one clearly points back to a specific noun already introduced.
  3. Reconsider your closing line. Confirm it would make sense to a reader who was not present at the original telling.
  4. Separate correction from revision. Note which changes fix an error and which changes improve clarity or tone.
  5. Produce a clean final version. Read it aloud once more to confirm the pacing still feels natural.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • Every subject concord matches its noun consistently across the whole narrative, not only within a single sentence.
  • Every pronoun and object concord has a clear, traceable reference for a reader unfamiliar with the story.
  • The closing line suits a reading audience rather than depending on a listener's shared context.
  • Corrections and revisions have each been made for a clear, identifiable reason.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — From the fireside to the printed page

A story shared aloud among family or neighbors often relies on shared history, familiar gestures, and the storyteller's own presence to carry meaning. When such a story moves into print, for readers who were not there, some of that shared context has to be written back into the text itself, through added detail or a more self contained closing, without losing the warmth of the original telling.

Culture in Context — Giving and receiving feedback respectfully

Offering feedback on someone's personal story calls for particular care, since the writer is sharing something drawn from family memory. Framing feedback around clarity for the reader, rather than around personal criticism, keeps the exchange constructive and respectful of what the writer has chosen to share.

Regional Variation — Editorial conventions vary by publication

Different publications, community newsletters, literary collections, and online platforms often apply somewhat different conventions for register, punctuation, and how much explanatory detail a personal narrative should carry for an unfamiliar reader. A learner preparing writing for publication should expect to adapt a piece further to a given publication's own house style, when one is provided.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Losing concord agreement across a longer sentence

Error: Starting a sentence with one subject and drifting, partway through, into a concord that matches a different noun class, often after combining two shorter sentences into one.

Correction: Trace the subject through the whole sentence, as shown in Section 17.1, and confirm every concord still matches the original noun.

Common Mistake — Leaving a pronoun or object concord without a clear referent

Error: Using an object concord so many sentences after its noun was last mentioned that a reader can no longer confidently identify what it refers to.

Correction: Ask the reference question from Section 17.2 for each object concord, and name the noun again if the answer is not immediately clear.

Common Mistake — Rewriting a whole story instead of editing it

Error: Discarding a working draft and starting over from nothing, rather than working through it systematically for concord, reference, and tone.

Correction: Follow the staged process in the Writing Workshop, addressing one kind of issue at a time, and preserve what already works in the draft.

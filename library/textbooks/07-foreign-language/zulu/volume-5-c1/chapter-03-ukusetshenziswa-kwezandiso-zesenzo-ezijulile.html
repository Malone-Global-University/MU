Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize and produce the applicative, causative, and passive extensions with control, choosing the correct extension for a given communicative purpose rather than a single memorized pattern.
  2. Combine two extensions on a single verb root, applying the correct ordering and final vowel behavior that the combination requires.
  3. Recognize reciprocal and neuter, stative forms in professional description, understanding what each contributes to meaning even before producing every form independently.
  4. Track how an extension changes the argument structure of a verb, that is, who or what becomes the subject, the object, or the beneficiary once an extension is added.
  5. Read and discuss a continuation of Nomvula's monthly report, one that describes actions done for others, actions that caused a result, and actions expressed without naming who performed them.
  6. Write a short passage of your own that uses at least one extension combination accurately in context.

Communication Scenario

Nomvula's supervisor returns her draft report with one comment written in the margin, next to a sentence describing how the health programme reached its participants. The comment asks her to say more precisely who did what, for whom, and why, rather than describing the action in the plain, unextended form she first used. Revising that single sentence sends Nomvula back through the report, verb by verb, asking a new question of each one, does this action need a beneficiary marked directly on the verb, does it need to show that someone caused something to happen, and does it need to be expressed without naming an agent at all, because the agent is unknown, unimportant, or better left unstated in a professional context. This chapter follows that revision, using it to build control over verb extensions and their combinations.

  1. The individual extensions were introduced in earlier volumes, together with reciprocal and neuter, stative forms as formulaic or receptive material. What this chapter adds is combination, precision, and confident use in professional description.
  2. A verb's argument structure, meaning who fills the subject, object, and beneficiary roles, shifts predictably once an extension is added, and a writer who understands that shift can describe an event far more precisely than the plain verb allows.

Pronunciation: Extension Chains and Syllable Weight

Adding one or more extensions to a verb root lengthens the word, and that added length changes where the penultimate lengthening falls once the final vowel is attached. A speaker who has practiced the plain verb but not its extended forms often stresses the wrong syllable, placing the lengthening where it fell in the shorter word rather than where it now falls in the longer one.

Pronunciation Focus — Locating the penultimate syllable after an extension

Before reading an extended verb aloud, count backward from the final vowel to identify the penultimate syllable in the extended word itself, not in the plain root you may know better.

  • Say the plain root first, quietly, to confirm the meaning you intend.
  • Add the extension or extensions, then recount the syllables from the end of the new word.
  • Lengthen the new penultimate syllable, even when it falls on a syllable that felt unstressed in the shorter form.

Language Focus: Extension Combinations

Purpose: this section develops the applicative, causative, and passive extensions introduced in earlier volumes, adds combination as productive control, and introduces reciprocal and neuter, stative forms receptively, as meaningful patterns to recognize in professional and literary description.

3.1 The applicative extension, precision about beneficiary and reason

Language Focus

The applicative extension, formed with -ela or -ela variants depending on vowel harmony, adds a beneficiary, a goal, a reason, or an instrument directly onto the verb, rather than describing that role in a separate phrase. Choosing the applicative over a plain verb plus a preposition is often the difference between an adequate sentence and a precise one in professional writing.

  • Umnyango wakhela abantu izindlu The department builds houses for the people. -akh- (build) plus applicative -ela, the beneficiary "abantu" is licensed directly by the extension
  • Isekela likamphathi lisebenzela intsha ithuba lokuqeqeshwa The deputy manager works to secure a training opportunity for the youth. -sebenz- (work) plus applicative -ela, marking purpose and beneficiary together

3.2 The causative extension, showing who made something happen

Language Focus

The causative extension, most often -isa or -isa related forms, promotes a new participant to subject position, the one who causes the action, while the original doer of the action moves into the object position. This restructuring is exactly what a report needs when the point is not simply that something happened, but that a specific office, person, or programme made it happen.

  • Uhlelo lwenza intsha ifunde amakhono amasha, ngaleyo ndlela luyayifundisa The programme makes young people learn new skills, and in that way it teaches them. -fund- (learn) plus causative -isa becomes -fundisa (teach), the learner shifts to object position
  • Umsebenzi omusha wenze abantu bathuthuke kakhulu The new project has made people develop considerably. -thuthuk- (develop) plus causative -isa, the department becomes the source of the development rather than merely its observer

3.3 The passive extension, when the agent stays unnamed

Language Focus

The passive extension, formed with -wa attached after the root and any preceding extension, allows a writer to describe an action without naming who performed it, because the agent is unknown, understood, or deliberately left in the background. Institutional writing uses this extension often, particularly when the focus belongs on the outcome rather than on the specific person responsible.

  • Izindlu ezingamashumi amabili zakhiwe kulo nyaka Twenty houses were built this year. -akh- (build) plus passive -wa, no builder is named, the outcome is foregrounded
  • Imibiko yenyanga ihlolwa ngaphambi kokuba ithunyelwe ehhovisi elikhulu Monthly reports are checked before being sent to the head office. -thumel- (send) plus applicative -ela plus passive -wa, two extensions stacked on one root

3.4 Combining extensions on one root

Pattern — Applicative plus passive on a single root
isiqu + applicative -el- + passive -wa + isignycelo sokuphela
The extension order matters, the applicative attaches closest to the root, and the passive attaches after it, then the final vowel closes the word.
Isibonelo: -sebenz- → -sebenzela (worked for) → -sebenzelwa (was worked for)
Example: work, becomes work for someone, becomes had someone work for them, with the beneficiary now expressed as the subject of a passive sentence.

Two extensions rarely stack without a clear communicative reason. Before combining extensions, confirm the sentence actually needs both a beneficiary and a backgrounded agent, rather than adding a second extension out of habit.

3.5 Recognizing reciprocal and neuter, stative forms

Language Focus, Receptive

The reciprocal extension, -ana, describes an action that participants do to one another, while the neuter, stative extension, most often -eka or -akala, describes a state that results from an action rather than the action itself. Volume V asks you to recognize these forms confidently in professional and literary reading, and to use a small set of high frequency examples formulaically, without full independent production of every possible form.

  • Amaqembu womabili ayasizana ekufezeni umsebenzi Both groups help one another in completing the work. -siz- (help) plus reciprocal -ana, marking mutual action
  • Inqubo entsha yenza umsebenzi wakhonakala kalula The new procedure makes the work easy to accomplish. -khon- plus neuter, stative -akala, describing a resulting state, that is, being possible or accomplishable, rather than an action performed by someone

Writing Workshop

Complete the following linked writing tasks in order, continuing your own report from Chapter 2 or adapting Nomvula's continuation to your own project.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 1 — Ukusebenzisa isandiso esenzelwayo: Write one sentence describing an action done for the benefit of a specific group, using the applicative extension.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 2 — Ukusebenzisa isandiso esenzisayo: Write one sentence in which a person, office, or programme causes another participant to do or become something, using the causative extension.

Self edit prompt
Underline every extension in your sentences, then ask, for each one, what role it added or removed, a beneficiary, a cause, or a backgrounded agent. If a sentence would be clearer without the extension, remove it.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 3 — Ukusebenzisa isandiso esenzelwa: Write one sentence in which the agent is deliberately left unnamed, using the passive extension, because the outcome matters more than who performed it.

  1. Draft the applicative sentence first. Confirm the beneficiary is licensed directly by the verb, not by a separate phrase alone.
  2. Draft the causative sentence. Confirm the original doer of the action now sits in object position.
  3. Draft the passive sentence. Confirm no agent is named, and that the sentence still reads naturally.
  4. Attempt one combination. If your topic allows it, combine two extensions on a single root, checking the order carefully.
  5. Read all four sentences aloud once. Confirm the penultimate lengthening lands correctly on each extended verb.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • The applicative sentence licenses a beneficiary, goal, reason, or instrument directly on the verb.
  • The causative sentence correctly repositions the original doer of the action as the object.
  • The passive sentence leaves the agent unnamed without becoming unclear.
  • Any combined extension follows the correct order before the final vowel.
  • Every extended verb was read aloud with the penultimate lengthening in its correct place.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Why the passive matters in institutional writing

Institutional writing frequently favors the passive extension when describing outcomes that belong to a team rather than to one individual, since naming a single person as responsible for a collective achievement can misrepresent how the work was actually done.

Culture in Context — The causative and accountability

Using the causative extension to state plainly that an office or programme caused a specific result is often read as a sign of confidence and accountability, provided the claim is accurate and can be supported by what actually happened.

Regional Variation — Extension choice across registers

Some speakers favor a plain verb with a separate phrase over an applicative form in relaxed conversation, even where a formal report would use the extension. Learners should expect greater use of extensions in written, professional, and public registers than in casual speech.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Stacking extensions without a communicative reason

Error: Adding a second extension to a verb simply because the writer has just learned it, without the sentence actually requiring the added meaning.

Correction: Before combining extensions, confirm the sentence genuinely needs both roles, a beneficiary and a backgrounded agent, for example, rather than adding complexity for its own sake.

Common Mistake — Leaving the causative's new object unmarked

Error: Using the causative extension correctly on the verb, but failing to adjust the object concord to reflect the participant who now occupies the object role.

Correction: After adding the causative, identify who or what the original doer of the action has become, then mark that participant with the correct object concord if the sentence requires one.

Common Mistake — Confusing the passive with the neuter, stative

Error: Using -wa where -eka or -akala would better express a resulting state rather than an action performed by an unnamed agent.

Correction: Ask whether the sentence describes something being done, which calls for the passive, or something becoming a certain way as a result, which calls for the neuter, stative extension.

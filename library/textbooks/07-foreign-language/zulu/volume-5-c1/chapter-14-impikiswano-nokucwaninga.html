Volume V, Chapter 14: Extended Argument and Research Communication · Speak and Write Zulu: Advanced Independent Performance

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Build an extended isiZulu argument that states a position, supports it with evidence, and addresses a counterpoint, in Standard isiZulu.
  2. Produce an annotated summary of a source, distinguishing the source's claim from your own comment on it.
  3. Sequence evidence and reasoning across several linked sentences, rather than presenting isolated claims.
  4. Deliver a short, evidence based presentation that moves logically from question, to evidence, to conclusion.
  5. Read and respond to an extended informational or argumentative text drawn from a specialized domain.
  6. Follow Nomvula as she turns a week of domain work into a structured presentation for a community forum.

Communication Scenario

A community organization has asked Nomvula to present, at a forum, what she has observed moving between the school, the government office, the clinic, and the small business from Chapter 13. It is not enough, this time, to describe each visit separately. Nomvula must build a single, connected argument, one that states a clear position about what would make these institutions easier for ordinary people to deal with, supports that position with specific evidence from her week, and responds honestly to the objection that institutions are already doing their best with limited resources.

  1. This chapter treats argument as structure, not as vocabulary, since a well organized argument uses grammar you have already consolidated, arranged with discipline.
  2. Your extended writing and speaking stay anchored in Standard isiZulu, with the summarizing and register skills from Chapters 12 and 13 carried forward rather than replaced.

Pronunciation: Stress Across a Long Argument

A short sentence carries its rhythm easily. A long, connected argument, with several clauses in sequence, asks the speaker to manage pace and emphasis across a much wider stretch of speech.

Pronunciation Focus — Pacing a multi clause argument

When a sentence carries a claim, a piece of evidence, and a conclusion together, the speaker's pacing helps the listener hear where one part ends and the next begins, even without a visible pause mark on the page.

  • Place a brief breath pause at each major clause boundary, so the claim, the evidence, and the conclusion remain audibly distinct from one another.
  • Keep penultimate lengthening consistent even in a long sentence, since a rushed final phrase can make the conclusion of an argument sound like an afterthought rather than the point.
  • Lower pitch slightly and slow down on a concluding clause introduced by words such as ngakho or kungakho, so the listener recognizes it as the point the argument has been building toward.

Language Focus: Building an Extended Argument

Purpose: this section activates no new grammatical system. It develops organizational patterns, claim, evidence, counterpoint, and conclusion, using grammar already consolidated in this volume.

14.1 Stating a position clearly

Language Focus

An extended argument needs a position the listener can hold onto from the first sentence. A vague opening forces the listener to guess where the argument is heading, so a clear claim, stated early, gives every sentence that follows a purpose.

  • Ngicabanga ukuthi izinhlelo zemikhakha eminingi zingaba lula kakhulu, uma nje kwaqinisekiswa ukuthi umuntu ngamunye uyachazelwa ngesiqephu esilandelayo I think that the procedures of many domains could be made much simpler, provided that each person is clearly told what the next step is. the position is stated first, with a clear condition attached, before any evidence follows

14.2 Supporting a claim with sequenced evidence

Language Focus

Evidence gains force when it is linked to the claim, and to the piece of evidence before it, rather than listed as separate, unconnected facts.

  • Esikoleni, abazali abaningi babengaqondi kahle isikhathi sokubhalisa, futhi lokhu kwakubangela ukulibala kwezinye izingane. Ehhovisi likahulumeni, into efanayo yenzeka ngesicelo sendlu, lapho abantu bengachazelwa khona ukuthi isicelo sabo sisephi isigaba At the school, many parents did not properly understand the enrollment period, and this caused some children to be missed. At the government office, the same thing happened with the housing application, where people were not told which stage their application was at. futhi lokhu kwakubangela (and this caused) and into efanayo yenzeka (the same thing happened) link the two pieces of evidence to one claim

14.3 Acknowledging a counterpoint honestly

Pattern — Conceding a point before returning to your position
Yebo, kuyiqiniso ukuthi [ipoyinti eliphambene], kodwa/nokho [ukubuyela esimweni sakho]
This pattern lets a speaker admit a genuine limitation before restating their own position, which makes the overall argument sound honest and considered rather than one sided.
Isibonelo: Yebo, kuyiqiniso ukuthi lezi zikhungo zisebenza ngezimali ezilinganiselwe, kodwa lokhu akuchazi ukuthi umuntu akafanele achazelwe ngendlela ecacile ukuthi isicelo sakhe sisephi isigaba
Example: Yes, it is true that these institutions operate with limited funds, but this does not mean that a person should not be told clearly which stage their application is at.

A counterpoint that is acknowledged and then answered is far more persuasive than a counterpoint that is ignored, since an audience notices when an argument avoids the obvious objection.

14.4 Reaching a conclusion the listener can act on

Language Focus

An extended argument should end with a conclusion that follows clearly from the evidence, and, where appropriate, a specific recommendation rather than a general wish.

  • Ngakho-ke, ngiyaphakamisa ukuthi izikhungo ezisebenzelana nomphakathi zisebenzise uphawu olulodwa, olukhombisa isigaba sohlelo, kunoma yiluphi uhlelo Therefore, I propose that institutions that deal with the community use a single, consistent marker that shows the stage of a process, no matter which procedure it is. ngakho-ke (therefore) signals that the sentence draws a conclusion, not simply another piece of evidence

Ukwenza Izinhlelo Zibe Lula Kakhulu Kubantu Abazisebenzisayo

Ngicabanga ukuthi izinhlelo zemikhakha eminingi zingaba lula kakhulu, uma nje kwaqinisekiswa ukuthi umuntu ngamunye uyachazelwa ngesiqephu esilandelayo. Ngesonto engalichaza esahlukweni esidlule, ngibonile lokhu kuphinda-phindeka ezindaweni ezine ezahlukene. Esikoleni, abazali abaningi babengaqondi kahle isikhathi sokubhalisa, futhi lokhu kwakubangela ukulibala kwezinye izingane. Ehhovisi likahulumeni, into efanayo yenzeka ngesicelo sendlu, lapho abantu bengachazelwa khona ukuthi isicelo sabo sisephi isigaba. Emtholampilo, isiguli esasilandela udokotela ngesifo somfutho wegazi asizange siqonde ukuthi kungani kudingeka amaphilisi asetshenziswe njalo, hhayi kuphela lapho umuntu ezizwa engaphathekile kahle. Ebhizinisini elincane, umnikazi wayefuna ukuqonda isivumelwano esasibhalwe ngamagama obuchwepheshe ezomthetho amaningi, kepha akekho owayemchazele lawo magama ngaphambi kokuba asayine. Yebo, kuyiqiniso ukuthi lezi zikhungo zisebenza ngezimali ezilinganiselwe, futhi izisebenzi zazo zisindwa umsebenzi omningi ngosuku olulodwa, kodwa lokhu akuchazi ukuthi umuntu akafanele achazelwe ngendlela ecacile ukuthi udinga ukwenzenjani, nokuthi usesigabeni bani sohlelo. Ngakho-ke, ngiyaphakamisa ukuthi izikhungo ezisebenzelana nomphakathi zisebenzise uphawu olulodwa, olukhombisa isigaba sohlelo, kunoma yiluphi uhlelo, ukuze umuntu angahlali emangele ukuthi kwenzekani kusukela lapho efaka khona isicelo sakhe.

English Reference

I think that the procedures of many domains could be made much simpler, provided that each person is clearly told what the next step is. In the week I described in the previous chapter, I saw this pattern repeat across four different settings. At the school, many parents did not properly understand the enrollment period, and this caused some children to be missed. At the government office, the same thing happened with the housing application, where people were not told which stage their application was at. At the clinic, a patient following the doctor's instructions about high blood pressure did not understand why the medication needed to be taken regularly, not only when they felt unwell. At the small business, the owner wanted to understand an agreement written with many technical legal terms, but no one had explained those terms before it needed to be signed. Yes, it is true that these institutions operate with limited funds, and their staff carry a heavy workload each day, but this does not mean that a person should not be clearly told what they need to do, and which stage of the process they are at. Therefore, I propose that institutions that deal with the community use a single, consistent marker that shows the stage of a process, no matter which procedure it is, so that a person is never left wondering what happens next after they submit their application.

Umsebenzi wokufunda — Reading task:

  1. Khomba lapho uNomvula evuma khona ipoyinti eliphambene naye, ngaphambi kokuba abuyele esimweni sakhe. (Point to where Nomvula concedes an opposing point, before returning to her own position.)
  2. Iziphi izibonelo ezine uNomvula azisebenzisa ukusekela ipoyinti lakhe? (What four examples does Nomvula use to support her point?)
  3. Yini uNomvula ayiphakamisayo ekugcineni kombiko wakhe? (What does Nomvula propose at the end of her report?)

Writing Workshop

Complete the following linked writing tasks in order, producing a short extended argument set in one of the domains from Chapter 13, or a similar domain you choose.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 1 — Ukubhala isimo sakho: Write one clear sentence stating your position on a specific problem in your chosen domain, following the pattern in Section 14.1.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 2 — Ukwakha ubufakazi: Write two or three sentences of sequenced evidence that support your position, linking each piece of evidence to the one before it.

Self edit prompt
Check that a reader could not mistake your evidence for a separate, unrelated observation. Each piece of evidence should visibly connect back to your stated position.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 3 — Ukuvuma ipoyinti eliphambene: Write one or two sentences using the pattern from Section 14.3, honestly acknowledging a reasonable objection to your position before returning to it.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 4 — Isiphetho nesincomo: Write a closing sentence, introduced by ngakho-ke or a similar connector, that states a clear conclusion or recommendation.

  1. Draft your position. Confirm it is stated clearly and could not be mistaken for a piece of evidence.
  2. Draft your evidence. Confirm each sentence links back to the position and to the evidence before it.
  3. Draft your counterpoint. Confirm you have named a genuine objection, not an easy one you invented to dismiss.
  4. Draft your conclusion. Confirm it follows from the evidence and offers something the reader could act on.
  5. Read your full argument aloud. Confirm the pacing from Section 14 pronunciation focus lets a listener follow each part.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • The position is stated clearly in the opening sentence.
  • Each piece of evidence is linked to the position and to the evidence before it.
  • A genuine counterpoint is acknowledged before the argument returns to its own position.
  • The conclusion follows logically from the evidence presented.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: claim, then evidence

With a partner, take turns stating a one sentence claim about a domain from Chapter 13, then giving one linked piece of evidence to support it.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: the forum presentation

Deliver a short, two to three minute presentation to a partner or small group, stating a position, giving sequenced evidence, acknowledging a counterpoint, and closing with a recommendation.

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself delivering the presentation from the communicative task, then listen back and check whether a listener unfamiliar with your notes could follow your position, evidence, and conclusion.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Argument as shared problem solving

In many South African community and institutional settings, an argument presented at a forum is expected to move toward a shared solution, not only toward winning a point. A speaker who names a workable next step, rather than only criticizing, is generally heard more favorably.

Culture in Context — Respecting the institutions you critique

Raising a limitation in how an institution operates, while acknowledging the constraints its staff work under, is generally seen as more constructive than a purely critical tone. This balance matters especially when the speaker hopes to be invited back to that institution again.

Regional Variation — Forum and meeting conventions

The expected length, tone, and structure of a community forum presentation can vary between urban and rural settings, and between different organizations. A speaker moving between forums should expect to adjust pacing and formality rather than assuming one format fits every audience.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Listing evidence without linking it

Error: Presenting several pieces of evidence as separate, disconnected statements, leaving the listener to work out how each one supports the claim.

Correction: Use connecting language, such as futhi lokhu kwakubangela or into efanayo yenzeka, so each piece of evidence visibly builds on the one before it.

Common Mistake — Ignoring the obvious counterpoint

Error: Presenting only evidence that supports the speaker's position, without acknowledging a reasonable objection the audience is already thinking of.

Correction: Name the objection honestly, using the pattern from Section 14.3, before returning to your position, since an unacknowledged objection weakens the argument more than a well answered one.

Common Mistake — Ending without a conclusion

Error: Stopping after the last piece of evidence, without drawing the argument together into a clear conclusion or recommendation.

Correction: Close with a sentence introduced by ngakho-ke or a similar connector, stating what follows from the evidence and, where appropriate, what should happen next.

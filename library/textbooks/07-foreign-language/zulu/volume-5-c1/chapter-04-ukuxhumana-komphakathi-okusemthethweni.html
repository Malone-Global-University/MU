Volume V, Chapter 4: Formal Public Communication · Speak and Write Zulu: Advanced Independent Performance

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize the features that separate a formal public register from ordinary conversational isiZulu, including sentence length, word choice, and the deliberate use of the passive and applicative extensions you developed in Chapter 3.
  2. Produce a short formal announcement, opening statement, or briefing segment that maintains a consistent formal register from beginning to end.
  3. Use subject and object concords accurately across the longer, more tightly organized sentences that public communication requires.
  4. Address an audience appropriately, choosing address forms and framing that suit an institutional or public setting rather than a private conversation.
  5. Read and discuss a departmental announcement and the opening of Nomvula's briefing, noticing how formal texts organize information for listeners who cannot ask immediate questions.
  6. Deliver a short spoken segment of a briefing aloud, sustaining register, pacing, and clarity for an audience rather than a single conversation partner.

Communication Scenario

Nomvula's supervisor asks her to present the revised report to the full department at next week's briefing, rather than simply filing it. Speaking to twenty colleagues at once is not the same task as writing a report that one supervisor will read alone. Nomvula must open with a greeting appropriate to a mixed audience of colleagues and visitors, state her purpose clearly before offering any detail, keep her sentences organized enough that a listener can follow without rereading, and close in a way that invites questions without inviting disorder. As she drafts her opening remarks, she notices that the plain, unextended verbs she reached for automatically in conversation now feel too casual, and that the applicative and passive forms she practiced in the report serve her again here, this time spoken aloud to a room rather than written for one reader.

  1. Formal register was introduced receptively in earlier volumes. This chapter asks you to produce it, choosing formal vocabulary, sentence structure, and address forms with confidence rather than recognizing them only when someone else uses them.
  2. A briefing or announcement succeeds when the audience can follow it without asking the speaker to repeat themselves, which means organizing information, not simply speaking correctly sentence by sentence.

Where This Chapter Fits

Pronunciation: Rhythm and Pacing in Public Speech

A briefing spoken to a room asks more of pacing and phrase grouping than a conversation between two people does. A listener who cannot interrupt to ask for repetition depends on the speaker to pause in the right places, and to let penultimate lengthening land clearly at the end of each phrase rather than rushing through it.

Pronunciation Focus — Phrasing a briefing for a room, not a partner

Before speaking a prepared segment aloud, mark the natural phrase breaks in the written text, then practice pausing at those breaks rather than at the end of every clause automatically.

  • Group words that belong together in meaning, and pause between groups rather than between every few words.
  • Let the penultimate syllable of the phrase, or sentence, receive its full lengthening before the pause, rather than clipping it short.
  • Slow down slightly at the opening greeting and the closing statement, since these carry the most weight for an audience that is still settling in or preparing to ask questions.

Language Focus: Register and the Formal Voice

Purpose: this section develops formal, public register as productive control, building on the concord and extension systems already active, and introduces the sentence organization patterns that formal announcements and briefings require.

4.1 Opening a briefing, formal greeting and framing

Language Focus

A formal opening greets the audience as a group, states the speaker's role or purpose briefly, and signals what is about to happen, before any detail is given. This framing sentence matters more in public speech than in conversation, since a listener who joins without framing has no way to recover the purpose of what follows.

  • Sanibonani nonke, ngingu Nomvula, futhi namuhla ngizonethulela umbiko wenyanga Greetings to you all, I am Nomvula, and today I will present the monthly report to you. plural greeting sanibonani, applicative -ela on -ethul- (present) licensing the audience as beneficiary
  • Ngaphambi kokuba ngiqale, ngicela ukunithatha kafushane kulokho esesikuhlangabezene nakho kulenyanga Before I begin, allow me to take you briefly through what we have encountered this month. framing clause with ngaphambi kokuba, signaling structure before content begins

4.2 Organizing a briefing, sequencing and signposting

Language Focus

Formal spoken texts announce their own structure more often than conversation does, using phrases that tell the listener where the speaker is in the presentation, such as moving to a first point, moving to a second point, or arriving at a conclusion. This signposting compensates for the listener's inability to reread.

  • Okokuqala, ake sibheke izindlu ezakhelwe imindeni kulo nyaka First, let us look at the houses that were built for families this year. okokuqala (firstly) signposts the first section, zakhelwe carries applicative plus passive from Chapter 3
  • Manje ake ngiphenduke kulokho okuhlangabezane nathi ngokuqeqeshwa kwentsha Now let me turn to what we encountered regarding the training of young people. manje ake ngiphenduke (now let me turn) signals a transition between sections
  • Ekugcineni, ngifisa ukugcizelela ukuthi lo msebenzi awuzange wenziwe umuntu oyedwa Finally, I wish to emphasize that this work was not done by one person alone. ekugcineni (finally) signals closing, passive wenziwe again backgrounds the agent deliberately

4.3 Addressing the audience appropriately

Pattern — Choosing an address form for a mixed audience
isibingelelo esivamile + isikhundla noma inhloso + isimemezelo sesihloko
A public opening greets the group, states the speaker's role or reason for speaking, and names the topic, in that order, before any supporting detail is offered.
Isibonelo: Sanibonani, ngingummeleli walo msebenzi, futhi namuhla sizoxoxa ngentuthuko yohlelo
Example: Greetings, I am a representative of this project, and today we will discuss the programme's progress.

Notice that the plural greeting sanibonani, not the singular sawubona, is required whenever the audience is a group, even when the speaker knows several individuals in the room personally.

4.4 The passive extension in public announcement

Language Focus

Institutional announcements favor the passive extension even more strongly than written reports do, since a spoken announcement to a mixed audience rarely benefits from naming a specific individual responsible for an outcome. The passive keeps the announcement focused on what the audience needs to know, rather than on internal departmental detail that does not concern them.

  • Uhlelo lokuqeqeshwa luzokwenyuselwa amasonto amabili ngenxa yesidingo esikhulu The training programme will be extended by two weeks due to significant demand. passive -wa on -enyusel- (raise, extend for), no decision maker named, appropriate for a public announcement

Umbiko Wenyanga, Isethulo Sasesikhungweni — Monthly Report, Departmental Briefing Opening

Sanibonani nonke, futhi ngiyabonga ngesikhathi senu. Ngingu Nomvula, futhi namuhla ngizonethulela umbiko wenyanga womnyango. Ngaphambi kokuba ngiqale, ngicela ukunithatha kafushane kulokho esesikuhlangabezene nakho kulenyanga. Okokuqala, ake sibheke izindlu ezakhelwe imindeni kulo nyaka, nendlela intsha eyathola ngayo amathuba amasha okuqeqeshwa. Manje ake ngiphenduke kulokho okuhlangabezane nathi ngokuqeqeshwa kwentsha, lapho uhlelo lwanda khona ngenxa yesidingo esikhulu esibonakele kubafundi. Ekugcineni, ngifisa ukugcizelela ukuthi lo msebenzi awuzange wenziwe umuntu oyedwa, kepha wenziwe amaqembu ambalwa asebenza ndawonye ngokuzimisela.

  2. Thola amabinzana amabili amemezela izigaba ezahlukene zesethulo. (Find two phrases that signpost different sections of the opening.)
  3. Yiziphi izenzo ezisebenzisa isandiso esenzelwayo kule ndima, futhi kungani lokho kufaneleka esethulweni esisemthethweni? (Which verbs in this passage use the passive extension, and why is that appropriate for a formal opening?)

Writing Workshop

Complete the following linked writing tasks in order, drafting the opening of your own briefing, either continuing Nomvula's report from Chapters 2 and 3 or adapting it to your own project.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 1 — Isethulo esisemthethweni: Write a formal opening of three to four sentences that greets a mixed audience, states your role or purpose, and names the topic of your briefing.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 2 — Ukuhlela ngezigaba: Write two signposting sentences, one that introduces a first point and one that introduces a second point, using okokuqala and manje ake or similar formal transitions.

Self edit prompt
Read your opening aloud and ask, would a listener who cannot see the page know exactly where one point ends and the next begins. If not, add or strengthen a signposting phrase.

Umsebenzi wokubhala 3 — Isiphetho: Write a closing sentence that uses the passive extension to summarize an outcome without naming a single individual as responsible.

  1. Draft the greeting and framing sentence first. Confirm you have used the plural greeting and stated your purpose before any detail.
  2. Draft your two signposted points. Confirm each signposting phrase clearly separates one section from the next.
  3. Draft your closing sentence. Confirm the passive extension keeps the focus on the outcome, not on a named individual.
  4. Read the whole opening aloud once. Confirm your pacing pauses at phrase breaks, not at every clause.
  5. Check register throughout. Underline any word or phrase that feels closer to casual conversation than to public address, and replace it if needed.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • The greeting addresses the audience as a group, not as a single listener.
  • Purpose is stated before supporting detail is given.
  • At least two signposting phrases mark the movement between sections.
  • The closing sentence uses the passive extension to background an individual agent appropriately.
  • The whole passage was read aloud with pauses placed at phrase boundaries.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: greeting a room

Practice saying a formal group greeting, followed by a one sentence statement of purpose, three times, adjusting your pacing until the pause after the greeting feels natural rather than rushed.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: delivering an opening

Deliver your written briefing opening aloud to a partner, or record it, as though addressing a room of colleagues. Ask your partner, or listen back, for whether the structure was easy to follow.

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself delivering your opening twice, once at a conversational pace and once with deliberate phrase pauses, then compare which version a listener could follow more easily.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Group greetings in institutional settings

Addressing a mixed group with the plural greeting, and briefly acknowledging their time or presence, is expected at the opening of most institutional briefings, meetings, and announcements, regardless of how well the speaker knows individual audience members.

Culture in Context — Crediting collective effort

Closing a briefing by attributing success to a team rather than to oneself, often supported by the passive extension, is commonly regarded as appropriate professional conduct, particularly when the outcome genuinely resulted from shared work.

Regional Variation — Formality expectations across settings

Some workplaces and community settings expect a more elaborate formal opening than others, particularly where elders or officials are present. Learners should adjust the length and elaborateness of a formal opening to the setting rather than applying one fixed formula everywhere.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using a singular greeting for a group audience

Error: Opening a briefing to a room of listeners with "sawubona" as though addressing one person.

Correction: Use "sanibonani" whenever the audience is a group, reserving "sawubona" for one to one address.

Common Mistake — Giving detail before purpose

Error: Beginning a briefing with a specific figure or fact before telling the audience what the briefing is about or why it matters to them.

Correction: State the purpose and topic in a brief framing sentence before offering the first piece of supporting detail.

Common Mistake — Dropping register mid briefing

Error: Opening formally, then sliding into conversational phrasing once the speaker relaxes into the material.

Correction: Reread a drafted briefing specifically for register, checking that the vocabulary and sentence structure remain consistent from the opening greeting through the closing statement.

Answer Key

Model Text — Comprehension

  1. "Sanibonani nonke" is the phrase used to greet the audience as a group.
  2. Significant demand shown by students, described in the passage as "isidingo esikhulu esibonakele kubafundi", caused the training programme to be extended.

Integration

  1. Departmental announcements favor the passive extension because the audience needs to know the outcome, such as a programme being extended or a decision being made, and naming one internal individual as responsible is usually unnecessary and can seem to overstate one person's role in a collective decision.
  2. Accepted examples include any two natural signposting phrases such as "okwesibili" (secondly) or "ekugcineni" (finally) used to mark a transition between sections.

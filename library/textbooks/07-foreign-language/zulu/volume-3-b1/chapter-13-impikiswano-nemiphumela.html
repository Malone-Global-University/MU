Impikiswano, Izimbangela Nemiphumela

Volume III, Chapter 13: Argument, Cause, and Consequence · Speak and Write Zulu: Discourse Expansion

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Connect a claim to a reason, using connectors such as ngoba and ngenxa yokuthi, within controlled argument.
  2. Connect a cause to a consequence, using connectors such as ngakho and ngenxa yalokho, to explain outcomes.
  3. Develop the causative extension, -is-, or -isa, beyond recognition, to describe how one person or event makes another happen.
  4. Develop the reciprocal and neuter, stative extensions introduced in Chapter 12, using them productively in new argument contexts rather than only in the fixed verbs practiced there.
  5. Support an opinion with at least one reason and one example, in a short spoken or written argument.
  6. Politely agree, partly agree, or disagree with a claim, while giving a reason for your position.

Communication Scenario

After the folktale segment airs on the community radio station, Thandeka receives a message from a listener, Bab' uNzimande, who disagrees with the way the story was framed. He argues that the story teaches caution, not cleverness. Thandeka must respond on air, stating her own claim, giving reasons and an example, and acknowledging his view politely without dismissing it. She also explains, using the causative extension, how the grandmother's telling of the story made the younger listeners think more carefully about the characters.

  1. This chapter moves the causative extension, formulaic since earlier volumes, into controlled productive use across new verbs and contexts.
  2. The reciprocal and neuter, stative extensions, introduced in Chapter 12 within a small fixed set, are developed here toward broader, though still guided, productive use in argument.

Pronunciation and Prosody Review

Spoken argument in isiZulu, as in English, often uses a falling intonation on the main claim, followed by a slight pause before the reason, marked by ngoba or ngenxa yokuthi. This chapter asks learners to notice that pause and rhythm, connecting prosody to the logical structure of an argument rather than treating them as separate skills.

Pronunciation Focus — Pausing before a reason

When a speaker states a claim and then gives a reason, a brief pause commonly falls just before ngoba or ngenxa yokuthi. This pause signals to the listener that a supporting reason is coming, and it does not change the click quality or vowel length of the surrounding words.

Grammar: Cause, Consequence, and Extension Development

13.1 Connecting a claim to a reason

Purpose: state a claim, then support it with a reason, using ngoba or the more formal ngenxa yokuthi.

Language Focus

ngoba introduces a reason directly after the clause it explains, and is common in everyday spoken argument. ngenxa yokuthi introduces a fuller subordinate clause and is more common in formal speech and writing. Both connectors follow the claim, and the clause that follows either connector keeps its own normal subject concord and tense.

  • Ngicabanga ukuthi ugwayi uhlakaniphile, ngoba wasinda impisi kaningi. I think the rabbit is clever, because he escaped the hyena many times. claim plus ngoba plus reason, controlled production
  • Indaba le ithakazelisa abaningi, ngenxa yokuthi ifundisa ngobuhlakani nangokuqaphela. This story interests many people, because it teaches about cleverness and caution. claim plus ngenxa yokuthi plus reason, formal register

13.2 Connecting a cause to a consequence

Purpose: state a cause, then describe its outcome, using ngakho or ngenxa yalokho.

Language Note — Cause plus consequence
[cause clause]. Ngakho, [consequence clause]. / [cause clause]. Ngenxa yalokho, [consequence clause].
Ngakho and ngenxa yalokho both point back to a cause already stated, usually in the previous sentence, and introduce the resulting consequence. Ngakho is common in everyday speech, while ngenxa yalokho suits more formal or written explanation.
Ugwayi wayehlakaniphile futhi eqaphela. Ngakho, akaze abanjwa impisi.
The rabbit was clever and careful. Therefore, he was never caught by the hyena.

13.3 Developing the causative extension, -is-, or -isa

Purpose: move the causative extension from formulaic or recognition status toward controlled productive use, describing how one participant makes something happen to another.

Language Focus

The causative extension, -is- or -isa, added to a verb root, shows that the subject causes the action to happen, rather than performing it directly. Ukufunda, to learn or to read, becomes ukufundisa, to teach, to cause to learn. Ukwazi, to know, becomes ukwazisa, to inform, to cause to know. This chapter asks learners to apply the causative extension to a small set of familiar roots in new sentences, rather than only recognizing it in fixed vocabulary.

  • Ugogo wasifundisa ukuthi ukuqapha kubaluleke kakhulu. Grandmother taught us that being careful matters a great deal. causative -is-, developed for controlled productive use
  • Le ndaba yenza abantwana bazi ngokuqapha, ngakho iyabasiza. This story causes children to know about caution, so it helps them. causative meaning expressed periphrastically with -enza, plus cause, consequence connector

13.4 Developing the reciprocal and neuter, stative extensions

Purpose: move beyond the fixed verbs of Chapter 12, applying the reciprocal, -an-, and the neuter, stative, -ek- or -akal-, to additional familiar roots within argument.

Language Focus

Chapter 12 introduced ukubonana and ukwazeka as fixed examples of the reciprocal and neuter, stative extensions. This chapter develops the same two patterns further. The reciprocal extension applies to verbs of interaction, such as ukuxoxa, to narrate, giving ukuxoxisana, to discuss with each other, or ukuphikisa, to argue against, giving ukuphikisana, to argue with each other. The neuter, stative extension applies to verbs describing a result, such as ukuqonda, to understand, giving ukuqondakala, to be understandable, or to make sense.

  • Mina noBab' uNzimande siphikisana ngaleli phuzu, kodwa siyavumelana ngokunye. I and Mr. Nzimande argue with each other about this point, but we agree with each other about other things. reciprocal extension -an-, applied to new roots for controlled production
  • Umbono wami uyaqondakala uma ngichaza ngesibonelo. My view becomes understandable when I explain it with an example. neuter, stative extension -akal-, applied to a new root for controlled production

Learners are asked to apply these two extensions productively to the roots practiced in this chapter. Building new reciprocal or neuter, stative forms independently from unfamiliar roots continues to develop through Chapters 14 to 20.

Model Dialogue

Isimo: UThandeka ukhuluma emsakazweni, ephendula umlaleli, uBab' uNzimande, othumele umyalezo ngendaba kagogo.
Setting: Thandeka is speaking on the radio, responding to a listener, Mr. Nzimande, who sent a message about grandmother's story.

UTHANDEKA Sisebenzisa umyalezo kaBab' uNzimande, othi indaba le ifundisa ukuqapha, hhayi ubuhlakani. We are using a message from Mr. Nzimande, who says this story teaches caution, not cleverness.
UTHANDEKA Ngiyawuqonda umbono wakhe, ngoba ugwayi uyaqapha ngempela endabeni. I understand his view, because the rabbit really is careful in the story.
UTHANDEKA Kodwa, ngenxa yokuthi ugwayi ucabanga ngokushesha futhi enze icebo, ngithi indaba ifundisa ubuhlakani nokuqapha kanyekanye. But, because the rabbit thinks quickly and makes a plan, I say the story teaches cleverness and caution together.
UTHANDEKA Isibonelo esihle yilesi, ugwayi wagijima nya wangena emgodini, kodwa hhayi ngephutha, wayeselindele lelo thuba. A good example is this, the rabbit ran off swiftly and entered his burrow, but not by mistake, he had already been waiting for that chance.
UTHANDEKA Ngakho, ngithi thina sobabili siyavumelana ukuthi ukuqapha kubalulekile, kodwa siyaphikisana kancane ngokuthi ikuphi okubaluleke kakhulu. Therefore, I say the two of us agree that caution matters, but we disagree a little about which one matters most.
UTHANDEKA Sibonga uBab' uNzimande ngokusifundisa ukubuka indaba ngeso elehlukile. We thank Mr. Nzimande for teaching us to look at the story from a different angle.

This monologue combines a claim, a reason, an example, a cause, consequence connector, and both developed verb extensions, reactivating patterns from Chapters 6, 10, and 12 while extending them to new roots.

Writing Workshop

Write a short argument, drawing only on vocabulary and grammar already active from Chapters 1 through 13.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write a short paragraph, six to eight sentences, stating your own opinion about the role of traditional stories today. Include a claim, a reason using ngoba or ngenxa yokuthi, an example, a consequence using ngakho or ngenxa yalokho, and at least one sentence using the developed causative, reciprocal, or neuter, stative extension.

  1. State your claim. Begin with ngicabanga ukuthi, or a similar opening, to introduce your opinion.
  2. Give a reason. Use ngoba or ngenxa yokuthi to explain why you hold this view.
  3. Add an example. Use isibonelo to support your reason with a specific case.
  4. State a consequence. Use ngakho or ngenxa yalokho to describe an outcome that follows from your reasoning.
  5. Include a developed extension. Use one causative, reciprocal, or neuter, stative form practiced in this chapter.
  6. Audit your draft. Confirm that your claim, reason, example, and consequence connect logically, and that every grammar system used is already active.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • The paragraph states a clear claim early on.
  • At least one reason is given, using ngoba or ngenxa yokuthi correctly.
  • At least one example is included, introduced with isibonelo.
  • At least one consequence is stated, using ngakho or ngenxa yalokho correctly.
  • At least one developed causative, reciprocal, or neuter, stative form is included correctly.
  • Standard isiZulu spelling, capitalization, and punctuation are used throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Disagreement without disrespect

In many isiZulu speaking communities, disagreement in public conversation, including on radio programs, is often expressed with care for the other person's dignity, acknowledging their view before offering a different one. Phrases such as ngiyawuqonda umbono wakhe, I understand his view, commonly appear before a speaker introduces a differing opinion.

Culture in Context — Community radio as public discussion

Community radio stations across South Africa often serve as a space for public discussion of local issues, cultural questions, and listener opinions, and presenters commonly model respectful, reasoned argument for their audience, rather than simply broadcasting one fixed view.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing ngoba and ngakho

Error: Using ngakho to introduce a reason, or ngoba to introduce a consequence, reversing the direction of the logical connection.

Correction: Remember that ngoba and ngenxa yokuthi look backward to explain a reason, while ngakho and ngenxa yalokho look forward to a resulting consequence.

Common Mistake — Overusing the causative where a simple verb is meant

Error: Adding -is- or -isa to a verb root when the intended meaning is the plain action, not one participant causing another to act.

Correction: Use the causative only when the meaning is genuinely about making, causing, or bringing about an action in someone or something else, as in ukufundisa, to teach, compared with ukufunda, to learn.

Common Mistake — Stating a claim without a reason or example

Error: Giving an opinion without any supporting reason or example, leaving the argument unconvincing.

Correction: Follow the writing workshop steps above, and always connect a claim to at least one reason and, where possible, one example.

Volume III, Chapter 8: Education, Work, and Professional Tasks · Speak and Write Zulu: Discourse Expansion

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Understand and give simple spoken and written instructions in a school or workplace setting, using the imperative and the subjunctive appropriately.
  2. Describe a task, a procedure, or a set of steps in a logical order, using sequencing language already active from earlier chapters.
  3. Report on completed or ongoing work, using the perfect, resultative, and progressive meanings already active from earlier chapters.
  4. Continue combining the causative, applicative, and passive extensions on a single verb root, now inside instructions, reports, and procedural sentences.
  5. Continue recognizing, receptively, the internal shape of the -an- reciprocal pattern and the -ek- neuter-stative pattern in familiar formulaic verbs, without yet building new forms from a root.
  6. Maintain relative clauses and object relative clauses already active from earlier chapters, now inside descriptions of tasks, roles, and procedures.
  7. Express polite requests, obligations, and permissions in a school or workplace context, using kufanele and related expressions.
  8. Read, listen to, write, and speak a short connected exchange in which a supervisor or teacher explains a task and a colleague or student responds.

Communication Scenario

Nomvula has taken on a new role at the community project she and Lindiwe discussed in the previous chapter. She is meeting with a colleague, Sipho, to explain how a small task should be completed and to check on his progress with an earlier assignment. Giving clear instructions, describing steps in order, and reporting honestly on finished or unfinished work are everyday skills in classrooms, offices, and workplaces alike.

  1. This chapter introduces controlled ways to instruct, sequence, and report on tasks, while continuing to compare, agree, and disagree politely as practiced in Chapter 7.
  2. Learners continue their receptive look at the -an- reciprocal pattern and the -ek- neuter-stative pattern, now noticing them inside procedural and workplace language, while still treating them as formulaic whole words for production.

Pronunciation and Click Focus

This chapter continues nasalized click practice, and adds attention to the rhythm of sequencing words such as okokuqala, okwesibili, and okokugcina, which are often produced quickly in natural speech. Keep each word's penultimate syllable slightly longer, rather than flattening the whole phrase into one even rhythm.

Pronunciation Focus — Sequencing words okokuqala, okwesibili, okokugcina

Say each sequencing word slowly on its own first, giving the penultimate syllable its natural length, then practice all three in a row at a natural pace, keeping a short, clear pause between each one so that a listener can follow the order of steps.

Grammar: Instructions, Reports, and Procedural Language

8.1 Giving instructions with the imperative and subjunctive

Purpose: give a direct instruction using the imperative, and a softer or shared instruction using the subjunctive, both already active from earlier chapters.

Language Focus

A direct instruction to one person often uses the bare imperative form of the verb, while an instruction addressed to more than one person, or a softer suggestion, uses the subjunctive with a subject concord. Ake, please, or just a moment, is often added before the subjunctive to soften a request further, in the same way that kunjalo, kodwa softened a disagreement in Chapter 7.

  • Qeda lo msebenzi ngaphambi kwesikhathi sasemini. Finish this task before midday. bare imperative, singular addressee
  • Ake nilandele lezi zinyathelo ngokulandelana. Please follow these steps in sequence. ake plus subjunctive, plural addressee, softened instruction

8.2 Sequencing a task step by step

Purpose: describe a procedure in a clear, logical order, using sequencing connectors already introduced.

Language Building Block — Sequencing with okokuqala, okwesibili, okokugcina
Okokuqala, ... . Okwesibili, ... . Okokugcina, ...
These three sequencing words mark the beginning, middle, and end of a procedure. Each one normally opens its own sentence or clause, followed by a comma, so that the steps remain clearly separated for a listener or a reader.
Okokuqala, faka isicelo. Okwesibili, linda impendulo. Okokgcina, qinisekisa ukuthi konke kulungile.
First, submit the application. Second, wait for a response. Last, make sure everything is in order.
  • Okokuqala, faka isicelo sakho kumqondisi. First, submit your application to the supervisor. okokuqala plus imperative, first step
  • Okwesibili, hlela izinsuku zokuqeqeshwa ngokulandelana. Second, arrange the training days in sequence. okwesibili, second step, ngokulandelana as adverbial phrase

8.3 Reporting on progress

Purpose: report on completed or ongoing work, using the perfect, resultative, and progressive meanings already active from earlier chapters, together with the passive of ukuqeda.

  • Umsebenzi sewuqediwe, ngaphandle kombiko wokugcina. The work is already finished, except for the final report. sewu-, already, plus qediwe, passive resultative of qeda
  • Ngisawubhala umbiko, kodwa ngizowuqeda ngomuso. I am still writing the report, but I will finish it tomorrow. ngisa-, still, progressive, followed by future ngizo-

8.4 Extension combinations inside instructions and reports

Purpose: continue combining the causative, applicative, and passive extensions on a single root, now inside instructions and reports.

  • Umsebenzi kufanele wenziwe ngendlela echaziwe emyalelweni. The task should be done in the way explained in the instruction. passive wenziwe, plus relative clause echaziwe, relative concord agreeing with indlela
  • Uma singabaqeqeshi kahle, singabadidekisa emsebenzini wabo. If we do not train them well, we might confuse them in their work. causative on the neuter-stative root -dideka-, -didekisa-, reused from Chapter 7

Wenziwe, done, combines the passive with the applicative-derived root -enzelwa-, familiar from Chapter 7's wenzelwe. Learners may recognize the shared root, but should continue treating both forms as reliable whole words.

8.5 Continuing to look inside: -an- and -ek- in workplace verbs

Purpose: continue recognizing, receptively only, the -an- reciprocal pattern and the -ek- neuter-stative pattern, now inside familiar workplace vocabulary, without yet producing new forms.

Language Building Block — Looking inside workplace verbs, for recognition only
Root + -ek- + Final vowel
Ukuqedwa, to be finished, and ukuqediwe, its resultative form, are passive rather than neuter-stative, and are not built with -ek-. However, ukwenzeka, to happen or to occur, is built from the root -enza-, to do, plus -ek-, plus the final vowel -a, in the same way that ukudideka was built in Chapter 7. Learners are still not asked to build new -ek- forms from an unfamiliar root.
enza + ek + a → enzeka
to do, to make → to happen, to occur

This box remains recognition-only. No exercise in this chapter requires learners to build a new -an- or -ek- form from an unfamiliar root.

8.6 Obligation and permission with kufanele and related expressions

Purpose: express obligation and permission in a school or workplace context, extending kufanele, already active from earlier chapters, to procedural and professional situations.

  • Bonke abasebenzi kufanele bafike ngesikhathi. All employees must arrive on time. kufanele plus subjunctive, general workplace obligation
  • Awuvunyelwe ukuphuma ngaphandle kwemvume yomqondisi. You are not permitted to leave without the supervisor's permission. negative passive of vumela, permission structure

Model Dialogue

Isimo: UNomvula uchazela uSipho umsebenzi omusha, futhi uyambuza ngengqubekela phambili yomsebenzi wangaphambilini.
Setting: Nomvula explains a new task to Sipho, and asks him about progress on an earlier task.

UNOMVULA Sipho, ake ngikuchazele umsebenzi omusha. Okokuqala, faka isicelo kumqondisi. Okwesibili, linda impendulo yakhe. Sipho, let me explain the new task to you. First, submit the application to the supervisor. Second, wait for his response.
USIPHO Kulungile. Kodwa ngaphambi kokuba ngiqale, ngingabuza umbuzo omunye? Good. But before I start, may I ask one question?
UNOMVULA Yebo, ubuze. Yes, ask.
USIPHO Umsebenzi wangaphambilini, sewuqediwe, kodwa ngisawubhala umbiko wokugcina. Kufanele ngiwuqede namuhla, noma ngingawuqeda kusasa? The earlier task is already finished, but I am still writing the final report. Should I finish it today, or may I finish it tomorrow?
UNOMVULA Ungawuqeda kusasa ekuseni, kodwa qiniseka ukuthi umbiko wenziwe ngendlela echaziwe emyalelweni. You may finish it tomorrow morning, but make sure the report is done in the way explained in the instruction.
USIPHO Ngizokwenza njalo. Ngicabanga ukuthi lo msebenzi omusha ungcono kunowangaphambilini, ngoba izinyathelo zawo zicacile. I will do that. I think that this new task is better than the earlier one, because its steps are clear.
UNOMVULA Ngiyavumelana nawe. Uma singabaqeqeshi abasebenzi kahle, singabadidekisa, ngakho izinyathelo ezicacile zisiza wonke umuntu. I agree with you. If we do not train employees well, we might confuse them, so clear steps help everyone.

This exchange introduces instructions, sequencing, reporting on progress, and obligation and permission language, while reactivating the opinion frame, comparison, and formulaic reciprocal verbs from Chapter 7.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Ziyiphi izinyathelo ezimbili uNomvula azichazela uSipho? (What two steps does Nomvula explain to Sipho?)
  2. Kungani uSipho engakawuqedi umbiko wakhe? (Why has Sipho not yet finished his report?)
  3. UNomvula ucabanga ukuthini ngokuqeqesha abasebenzi kahle? (What does Nomvula think about training employees well?)

Writing Workshop

Write a short account of a task, its steps, and a report on its progress, drawing only on vocabulary and grammar already active from this chapter and earlier chapters.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write a description of eight to twelve sentences, including at least three steps introduced with okokuqala, okwesibili, and okokugcina, one instruction using the imperative or subjunctive, one report on progress using the perfect, resultative, or progressive, and one verb combining two extensions.

  1. Choose your task. In English, note a school or workplace task, and who is responsible for each part of it.
  2. State the first step. Use okokuqala with an imperative or subjunctive verb.
  3. State the second step. Use okwesibili, continuing the sequence.
  4. State the last step. Use okokugcina to close the sequence.
  5. Report on progress. Use the perfect, resultative, or progressive to say whether the task is finished, in progress, or waiting.
  6. Combine two extensions. Use the causative and applicative, or the applicative and passive, together on a single root.
  7. Audit your draft. Confirm that every reciprocal or neuter-stative verb is used as a whole word, and that no new -an- or -ek- form has been built from an unfamiliar root.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • At least three steps are stated clearly using okokuqala, okwesibili, and okokugcina.
  • At least one instruction uses the imperative or subjunctive correctly.
  • At least one report on progress uses the perfect, resultative, or progressive correctly.
  • At least one verb correctly combines two extensions already active from earlier chapters.
  • No new reciprocal or neuter-stative verb has been built from an unfamiliar root by analogy.
  • Standard isiZulu spelling, capitalization, and punctuation are used throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Respect toward supervisors and teachers

In many isiZulu speaking school and workplace settings, instructions from a supervisor, employer, or teacher are commonly received with a respectful acknowledgment before any question is asked, reflecting the broader respect norms already introduced in earlier chapters. This pattern varies by workplace, generation, and setting.

Culture in Context — Learning from elders and community knowledge

School projects that draw on the knowledge of elders and community members, as in the reading passage above, are commonly valued alongside textbook learning in many communities, reflecting the broader cultural importance placed on oral knowledge and community memory.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing the imperative and the subjunctive

Error: Using a bare imperative form when addressing more than one person, or when the instruction should be softened with ake.

Correction: Use the subjunctive with the correct plural subject concord for instructions to more than one person, and add ake when a softer, more polite instruction is appropriate.

Common Mistake — Leaving out the sequencing words

Error: Listing steps of a procedure one after another without okokuqala, okwesibili, or okokugcina, leaving the order unclear to a listener or reader.

Correction: Mark each major step clearly with okokuqala, okwesibili, or okokugcina, especially in spoken instructions, where the listener cannot see paragraph breaks.

Common Mistake — Building a new -an- or -ek- form from an unfamiliar root

Error: Attaching -an- or -ek- to an unfamiliar workplace root by analogy with ukuvumelana, ukudideka, or ukwenzeka, before this pattern has been introduced for production.

Correction: Continue using only the reciprocal and neuter-stative verbs already introduced as fixed vocabulary items. Wait for the -an- and -ek- patterns to be introduced for production in a later chapter.

Volume III, Chapter 12: Stories, Oral Tradition, and Expressive Language · Speak and Write Zulu: Discourse Expansion

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Understand the main events and sequence of a short traditional story, using narration already active since Chapter 3.
  2. Recognize ideophones as vivid, sound and manner rich words that bring description to life in storytelling and everyday speech.
  3. Recognize reduplication patterns, and understand how repeating part of a word can add intensity, repetition, or emphasis to a description.
  4. Begin to recognize reciprocal and neuter, stative verb forms in short story contexts, connecting them to the argument, structure patterns already noticed as recognition items in Chapter 6.
  5. Retell a short story in your own words, using vocabulary and grammar already active from earlier chapters, while incorporating one or two ideophones or reduplicated forms for expressive effect.
  6. Discuss the cultural role of oral storytelling in isiZulu speaking communities.

Communication Scenario

Thandeka, whose work at the community radio station learners followed in Chapter 11, is now helping to record a segment in which an elder from the neighborhood shares a short traditional story for younger listeners. Before the recording, Thandeka's grandmother tells her the story at home, using vivid ideophones and repeated words to bring the animal characters to life. Thandeka wants to retell the story clearly for the radio audience, keeping some of that expressive color without losing the thread of events.

  1. This chapter does not introduce ideophones or reduplication as a fully productive grammar system. Learners recognize a focused set of high frequency examples, and may use one or two of them for expressive color in speaking and writing.
  2. Reciprocal and neuter, stative verb patterns, noticed only as recognition items in Chapter 6 and Chapter 10, are introduced here for the first time as a small, controlled set, connected to storytelling contexts where they occur naturally.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 11 developed the skill of comprehending and summarizing media language. Chapter 12 turns to a different kind of source text, the traditional story, and asks learners to notice how expressive language, ideophones, reduplication, and a small set of verb extensions, gives narration its vividness. Reciprocal and neuter, stative patterns move here from pure recognition toward controlled introduction, but only within a small, familiar set of verbs.

Coming From Chapter 11

Comprehension and summary of formal media language, reporting frames, and recognition only exposure to formal register and stacked relative clauses.

New in This Chapter

Pronunciation and Click Review

Ideophones are often pronounced with extra emphasis, a longer vowel, or a sharper click than the same sound elsewhere in a sentence, because they exist to create a vivid effect. This chapter asks learners to notice that expressive emphasis without losing the underlying click accuracy built since Volume I.

Pronunciation Focus — Expressive emphasis in ideophones

When a storyteller uses an ideophone such as qephu qephu or gqi, expect the click or the vowel to be produced with more force and a slightly longer duration than in ordinary conversation. This is a normal, expected feature of expressive storytelling, not a mispronunciation, and it does not change the underlying click quality you have already learned.

Grammar: Ideophones, Reduplication, and Verb Extensions

12.1 Ideophones for vivid description

Purpose: recognize a small set of high frequency ideophones, and use one or two of them for expressive color when narrating a simple story.

Language Focus

An ideophone is a vivid word that captures a sound, a movement, or a manner in a single, often repeated, syllable or short sequence. Ideophones usually follow a verb of motion, sound, or state, and they are not conjugated the way ordinary verbs are. At this stage, learners recognize ideophones in context and may use a small, listed set for expressive effect, rather than creating new ideophones freely.

  • Impisi yagijima nya, yashabalala ehlathini. The hyena ran off swiftly, and vanished into the forest. ideophone nya, recognition and limited expressive use
  • Umkhulu wavula umnyango, gqi, kwase kungena umoya obandayo. The old man opened the door, suddenly, and then a cold wind came in. ideophone gqi, recognition and limited expressive use

12.2 Reduplication for repetition and intensity

Purpose: recognize how repeating part of a word can show that an action happened repeatedly, gradually, or with added intensity, and use a small set of familiar reduplicated forms in your own narration.

Language Note — Recognition and limited production, reduplication
-hamba- to -hambahamba-, -bheka- to -bhekabheka-
Repeating the verb stem, or part of it, commonly shows that an action was done slowly, repeatedly, or without a clear single goal, such as wandering about or glancing around repeatedly. Learners recognize this pattern and may use the small set of reduplicated forms listed in this chapter, without building new reduplicated forms independently from unfamiliar roots.
Ingane yayihambahamba ehlathini, ifuna indlela yokuya ekhaya.
The child was wandering about in the forest, looking for the way home.

12.3 First introduction: reciprocal and neuter, stative verb forms

Purpose: move from pure recognition, as in Chapter 6 and Chapter 10, to controlled introduction of a small set of reciprocal and neuter, stative verbs commonly found in storytelling.

Language Focus

The reciprocal extension, -an-, shows that two or more participants do something to each other, as in ukubonana, to see each other. The neuter, stative extension, -ek- or -akal-, shows a state or a quality that results from an action, rather than the action itself, as in ukwazeka, to be known, or to become known. This chapter introduces a small set of these forms for controlled, guided use, connected directly to the story below. A fuller treatment of extension combinations continues in later chapters.

  • Impisi nogwayi babonana endleleni, kodwa abaxoxanga. The hyena and the rabbit saw each other on the path, but they did not speak to each other. reciprocal extension -an-, introduced for controlled use
  • Emva kwesikhathi, indaba yakhe yayeka yaziwa kubantu bonke basemzini. After some time, his story became known to all the people in the village. neuter, stative extension -w- plus -ek- pattern, introduced for controlled use

Learners are asked to recognize and use only these two patterns, in the specific verbs practiced in this chapter. Building new reciprocal or neuter, stative forms independently from unfamiliar roots remains outside this chapter's scope, and continues to develop across Chapters 13 through 20.

Model Dialogue

Isimo: UThandeka usekhaya kagogo wakhe, futhi ugogo umxoxela inganekwane engemuva kokudla kwakusihlwa.
Setting: Thandeka is at her grandmother's home, and her grandmother tells her a folktale after the evening meal.

UGOGO Ake ngikuxoxele indaba yasendulo, ngempisi nogwayi. Let me tell you an old story, about the hyena and the rabbit.
UTHANDEKA Yebo Gogo, ngiyafisa ukuzwa. Yaqala kanjani? Yes Grandmother, I would love to hear it. How did it begin?
UGOGO Impisi nogwayi babonana endleleni, kodwa abaxoxanga. Ugwayi wahambahamba ehlathini, efuna okokudla. The hyena and the rabbit saw each other on the path, but they did not speak to each other. The rabbit was wandering about in the forest, looking for food.
UTHANDEKA Kwenzekani emva kwalokho? What happened after that?
UGOGO Impisi yagijima nya, yashabalala ehlathini, kodwa ugwayi wayihlakanipha kakhulu, akaze abanjwa. The hyena ran off swiftly, and vanished into the forest, but the rabbit was very clever, and was never caught.
UTHANDEKA Ngiyayithanda le ndaba, Gogo. Ngicabanga ukuthi izofaneleka kahle emsakazweni. I love this story, Grandmother. I think it will suit the radio broadcast well.
UGOGO Emva kwesikhathi, indaba yakhe yayeka yaziwa kubantu bonke basemzini. Yingakho kusafanele siyixoxe namuhla. After some time, his story became known to all the people in the village. That is why it is still fitting to tell it today.

This exchange combines narration, an ideophone, a reduplicated form, and both target verb extensions, reactivating patterns from Chapters 3, 6, and 10 while introducing them together for the first time in a single storytelling context.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Indaba kagogo iphathelene nobani? (Who is grandmother's story about?)
  2. Ugwayi wenzani ehlathini? (What was the rabbit doing in the forest?)
  3. Kungani uThandeka ecabanga ukuthi indaba izofaneleka emsakazweni? (Why does Thandeka think the story will suit the broadcast?)

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Oral storytelling and the role of grandparents

In many isiZulu speaking households, grandparents play an important role in passing down folktales, proverbs, and family history to younger generations, often in the evening after a meal. Stories about clever animal characters, such as the rabbit and the hyena, are common across many Southern African oral traditions, and often carry a lesson about wisdom, patience, or caution.

Culture in Context — Expressive language as a storytelling skill

Skilled storytellers commonly use ideophones, reduplication, and vocal variation to hold a listener's attention and bring characters to life. Learning to recognize and use a small set of these expressive tools, as this chapter introduces, supports genuine participation in this living oral tradition, rather than treating it as a purely historical curiosity.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Inventing new ideophones or reduplicated forms

Error: Creating a new ideophone or reduplicating an unfamiliar verb root by analogy with the examples in this chapter, before the underlying pattern has been fully taught.

Correction: Use only the ideophones and reduplicated forms listed in this chapter for now, and wait for later chapters to expand this set with fuller grammatical guidance.

Common Mistake — Confusing the reciprocal and neuter, stative extensions

Error: Using -an- when the intended meaning is a resulting state rather than a mutual action, or the reverse.

Correction: Remember that -an- shows an action between two or more participants, such as ukubonana, to see each other, while -ek- or -akal- shows a state that results from an action, such as ukwazeka, to become known.

Common Mistake — Copying the story word for word instead of retelling it

Error: Reproducing the reading or listening passage sentence by sentence, in the same order and wording, rather than retelling it in the learner's own words.

Correction: Follow the writing workshop steps above, and restate the main events, characters, and outcome using your own sentence structure.

Ukubuyela Emuva Nokuhlolwa Kohlelo

Volume III, Chapter 1: B1 Re-entry and System Audit · Speak and Write Zulu: Discourse Expansion

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Review and confirm control of the noun classes, subject concords, object concords, possessives, demonstratives, and adjectival concords that Volume II established.
  2. Audit your own control of the full verb template, including tense, aspect, mood, and polarity, across connected sentences.
  3. Recognize, without being required to produce, a small set of forms that Volume III will later develop, including the applicative extension and basic relative clauses.
  4. Identify specific gaps in your own concord, click, or verb-template accuracy, and use targeted practice to close them.
  5. Read and listen to connected, multi-sentence isiZulu drawing only on systems already active from Volume II.
  6. Write and speak a short, organized self-introduction that reactivates community, family, and daily-life vocabulary from the previous volume.
  7. Complete a diagnostic self-audit task that identifies your starting point for Volume III.

Communication Scenario

It has been some time since Sipho's class finished Volume II. Now the same learners are gathering again, in a new term, with a new instructor named Nomvula. Before she introduces anything new, Nomvula asks each learner to reintroduce themselves, describe their community and family briefly, and answer a few questions about a short notice, exactly as they did at the end of the last volume. She is listening for what still feels solid, and what needs another look, before the course moves forward.

  1. This chapter does not introduce new grammar, vocabulary, or cultural content beyond what Volume II already activated. It gives you a structured audit, similar to Nomvula's opening class, so that you and your instructor can see clearly what is ready to build on.
  2. A few forms appear in this chapter for recognition only, marked clearly, so that you begin to notice them before Volume III develops them for production later in the sequence.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 1 opens Volume III and Unit 1. It introduces no new noun classes, concords, click series, verb extensions, or clause types for production. Instead, it reactivates and audits everything Volume II established, so that Chapters 2 through 5 can build on a confirmed foundation.

Coming From Volume II

Full noun-class and concord control, the complete verb template with tense, aspect, mood, and polarity, basic locatives, and the volume's cultural and pragmatic routines, including greetings, respect language, and proverbs.

New in This Chapter

No new grammar, vocabulary, or cultural content for production. A small number of items, including the applicative extension and basic relative clauses, appear for recognition only, ahead of their formal introduction later in the volume.

Looking Forward

Chapter 2 develops extended description and classification, and Chapters 3 through 5 begin the volume's work on narration, relative clauses, and causative meaning, once this audit confirms the starting point.

Pronunciation and Click Audit

No new click series is introduced. This chapter asks you to audit your own production of the plain, aspirated, voiced, and nasalized click series that Volume II built, across connected sentences rather than isolated words.

Pronunciation Focus — Auditing click accuracy in your own speech

Record yourself reading the model dialogue aloud, at a natural pace. Listen back, and mark any click that softened or disappeared as the sentence grew longer. This is diagnostic, not corrective. Simply note where your attention drifted, so you know where to focus in the speaking task later in this chapter.

Grammar: System Audit

1.1 Noun classes and concord agreement, confirmed

Purpose: confirm that concord agreement across a full sentence still holds, without a new pattern to learn.

Volume II established subject concords, object concords, possessives, demonstratives, and adjectival concords across the major noun classes. This chapter does not add a class or a concord type. It asks you to check your own sentences against the classes you already control.

Language Focus

No new concord type appears here. The task is confirmation, checking that every concord in a sentence still agrees with the same noun class, the same way it did at the end of Volume II.

  • Umndeni wami omkhulu uhlala kule ndawo eseduze nesikole. My big family lives in this place, near the school. possessive, adjectival concord, and demonstrative, all reactivated from Volume II

1.2 The verb template, audited across tense and polarity

Purpose: confirm accurate use of the subject concord, tense and aspect marker, object concord, verb root, and final vowel, in both affirmative and negative sentences.

Language Building Block — The verb template, reviewed
Subject concord + TAM marker + (object concord) + verb root + final vowel
This chapter does not add a new tense or a new extension. It checks whether present, past, future, and negative forms remain accurate when used together in one connected passage.

1.3 A first look ahead, for recognition only

Purpose: notice two forms that Volume III will teach for production later, without being asked to produce them now.

Language Focus — Recognition only

The two items below are not taught for production in this chapter. They appear so that you begin to notice them in context. Chapter 3 will introduce the applicative extension for production, and Chapter 4 will develop relative clauses further. For now, simply notice how they are used.

  • Umuntu owangisiza wayehlala eduze nathi. The person who helped me used to live near us. recognition only, a relative clause identifying "the person"
  • Ugogo wangenzela ukudla, ngisho nasezinsukwini ezimatasa. Grandmother made food for me, even on busy days. recognition only, an applicative extension marking the beneficiary of the action

Model Dialogue

Isimo: Uthisha uNomvula ubuza abafundi bakhe ukuthi bazazise futhi, ekuqaleni kwesikole esisha.
Setting: The teacher, Nomvula, asks her students to introduce themselves again, at the start of the new term.

UNOMVULA Ngicela nizazise futhi, nisho igama lenu, nomndeni wenu, kanye nendawo enihlala kuyo. Please introduce yourselves again, saying your name, your family, and the place you live.
USIPHO Igama lami nginguSipho. Ngihlala nomndeni wami omkhulu, eduze nesitolo esidala. My name is Sipho. I live with my big family, near the old shop.
UNOMVULA Ngiyabonga, Sipho. Manje sitshele ngomsebenzi owenzayo, noma owawenza ngonyaka odlule. Thank you, Sipho. Now tell us about the work you do, or the work you did last year.
USIPHO Ngonyaka odlule bengisebenza esikoleni, kodwa manje angisebenzi khona, ngisebenza emphakathini wesibhedlela. Last year I was working at the school, but now I do not work there, I work at the community hospital.
ULINDIWE Nami ngicela ngisho, uthisha. Igama lami nginguLindiwe, futhi ngihlala noyihlomkhulu wami omdala. May I also say, teacher. My name is Lindiwe, and I live with my elderly grandfather.

This exchange reactivates concord agreement, the verb template in present, past, and negative forms, and possessive and adjectival patterns, all without introducing anything new.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. USipho uhlala nobani, futhi kuphi? (Who does Sipho live with, and where?)
  2. USipho wayesebenza kuphi, ngonyaka odlule? (Where did Sipho work, last year?)
  3. ULindiwe uhlala nobani? (Who does Lindiwe live with?)

Reading Practice

Read the following short account, written by another learner at the start of the new term. It reactivates family and community description, past and present tense, and basic negation, in a slightly different arrangement from Thandeka's introduction above. Read it aloud twice before answering.

Indaba Kababa Ka-Sipho — Sipho's Father's Account

Writing Workshop

Write your own short self-introduction, as if you were a new arrival in Nomvula's class. Your introduction should give your name, describe your family or household, mention where you live, and briefly compare your life now with your life last year, drawing only on vocabulary and grammar already active from Volume II.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write a short introduction, of five to eight sentences, combining at least one possessive, one locative expression, one past-tense verb, and one negative sentence.

  1. Plan your introduction. In English, note your name, your family or household, where you live, and one thing that has changed since last year.
  2. Open with your name. Begin with a simple, clear self-identification.
  3. Describe your family or household. Use a possessive and, if appropriate, an adjectival concord.
  4. State where you live. Use a locative expression and a demonstrative if appropriate.
  5. Compare then and now. Use one past-tense sentence and one present-tense sentence, connected clearly.
  6. Include one negative sentence. Say something that is no longer true, or that you do not do.
  7. Audit your draft. Confirm that every concord agrees with its noun class, and that your verb forms are accurate in both tense and polarity.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • The introduction opens clearly with a name and identity statement.
  • A possessive and, where used, an adjectival concord agree with the correct noun class.
  • At least one locative expression is used naturally.
  • Past tense and present tense are both used accurately, in a clear comparison.
  • At least one negative sentence is grammatically accurate.
  • Standard isiZulu spelling, capitalization, and punctuation are used throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Reintroducing yourself in a returning group

When a class, workplace, or community group reconvenes after time apart, a short, respectful self-introduction is common among many isiZulu speaking communities, even among people who already know one another. It signals respect for the group, and gives space for news about family, work, or circumstances to be shared naturally.

Culture in Context — Naming change respectfully

Both Thandeka and Sipho's father describe a change in work or circumstance without dwelling on hardship. This measured way of naming change, stating what was true, what is true now, and moving on, reflects a broader pattern of speaking about personal circumstances that you will meet again in later chapters.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Letting concord agreement drift in longer sentences

Error: Starting a sentence with the correct noun class, then losing agreement by the time a possessive, adjective, or demonstrative appears later in the sentence.

Correction: Identify the noun class first, then check every following concord against that same class, even in a longer, more connected sentence.

Common Mistake — Mixing past and present tense markers within one comparison

Error: Beginning a "then and now" comparison correctly in the past tense, then slipping back into a present-tense marker for the second half without intending to.

Correction: Decide in advance which half of the comparison is past and which is present, and check both halves of the sentence before moving on.

Common Mistake — Treating the recognition-only examples as required patterns

Error: Trying to use the applicative extension or a relative clause productively in this chapter's writing or speaking tasks, because it appeared in the grammar section.

Correction: Notice these forms when you meet them in reading or listening, but wait for Chapters 3 and 4, where they are introduced for production, before using them yourself.

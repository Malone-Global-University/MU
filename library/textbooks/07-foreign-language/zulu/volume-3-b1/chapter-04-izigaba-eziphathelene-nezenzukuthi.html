Volume III, Chapter 4: Relative and Participial Clause Development · Speak and Write Zulu: Discourse Expansion

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Build subject relative clauses across a wider range of noun classes, identifying a person or thing by what it does or did.
  2. Build object relative clauses, identifying a person or thing by what was done to it or with it.
  3. Use a participial clause to describe a situation, a state, or an accompanying action within a larger sentence.
  4. Use the causative extension to describe making someone do something, or making something happen.
  5. Use the passive extension to describe an action when the person who did it is unknown, unimportant, or intentionally left in the background.
  6. Add a purpose clause to an existing time, cause, and condition subordination pattern, explaining why an action was carried out.
  7. Maintain reference continuity across several sentences of dialogue or narration, so that listeners never lose track of who is being discussed.
  8. Read, listen to, write, and speak a short connected passage that identifies people and things by what they do, what happens to them, and why.

Communication Scenario

Sipho's story about his first day at the hospital left Nomvula's class wanting more detail, not just about what happened, but about the people involved. Who is the nurse who helped him. What is the work that made him tired. Lindiwe wants to describe the grandfather whose house was locked, and the neighbor who explained everything to her. The class has already met a basic subject relative clause, but now they need more, ways to identify someone by what was done to them, ways to describe a situation rather than a single action, and ways to say that someone made something happen, or that something happened without naming who caused it.

  1. This chapter develops the relative clause into a fuller system, adding object relatives and participial clauses, and introduces the causative and passive extensions for production, building directly on the basic subject relative and the object concord practiced in Chapter 3.
  2. Learners also add a purpose clause to their subordination toolkit, and practice keeping references clear across several connected sentences, so a description can identify exactly who did what, to whom, and why.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 4 takes the narration skills from Chapter 3 and turns them toward description within a story, identifying people and things more precisely, and explaining cause and effect more fully. The object concords and subject relative clauses introduced in Chapter 3 are now the foundation for a wider relative and participial system.

Pronunciation and Click Focus

This chapter continues developing the nasalized click series introduced in Chapter 3, moving from careful, separated practice toward natural connected speech, and adds attention to consonant contrasts that commonly cause confusion for English speaking learners.

Pronunciation Focus — Nasalized clicks in connected relative clauses

Relative clauses often place a nasalized click word directly after a relative concord, which can tempt a learner to insert a small pause. Practice owangibonela alongside inqola engiyibonile, keeping the nasal and click connected within the word, and keeping the whole clause connected in one smooth breath group, without a pause between the relative concord and the word that follows it.

Grammar: Clauses That Describe

4.1 Subject relative clauses, developed across more classes

Purpose: identify a wider range of people and things by what they do or did, using the relative concord that matches the correct noun class.

Language Focus

Chapter 3 introduced the subject relative with human nouns of Class 1 and 2. This chapter extends the same pattern to Class 7 and 8 objects, Class 9 and 10 animals and common nouns, and Class 3 and 4 nouns, so that the relative concord can identify almost any noun by an action it performs.

Language Building Block — Subject relative across classes
Noun + Relative concord, class matched + Verb
The relative concord changes with the noun class of the head noun, while the underlying pattern stays the same across every class.
isitolo esivulekile; izinja ezikhonkothayo
the shop that is open; the dogs that are barking
  • Incwadi engibhale yona iphelele manje. The book that I wrote is complete now. e- relative concord, class 9, combined with object concord -yi-
  • Izitolo ezivulekile kusasa zisemgwaqweni omkhulu. The shops that are open tomorrow are on the main road. ezi- relative concord, class 8, subject relative

4.2 Object relative clauses, introduced for production

Purpose: identify a person or thing by what was done to it, or with it, rather than by what it did.

Language Building Block — Object relative pattern
Noun + Relative concord + Subject concord + Verb
In an object relative clause, the relative concord still agrees with the head noun, but the verb also carries a subject concord for the person or thing performing the action, since the head noun is now the object of that action.
indoda engiyibonile izolo
the man whom I saw yesterday
  • Udade engangingakaze ngimazi ngaphambili wangisiza kakhulu. The nurse whom I had not known before helped me greatly. e- relative concord, class 1, combined with subject concord ngi-, object relative
  • Isipho ugogo asithengile sasisemsakazweni. The gift that grandmother bought was in the package. a- relative concord, class 7, combined with subject concord u-, object relative

Compare the subject relative in section 4.1, where the head noun performs the action, with the object relative here, where the head noun receives the action. Checking which role the head noun plays is the first step in choosing the correct pattern.

4.3 Participial clauses, introduced for production

Purpose: describe a situation, a state, or an accompanying action that frames the main event of a sentence.

Language Building Block — Participial clause
Participial subject concord + Verb stem, without the ordinary tense marker
The participial verb form describes a background situation or an action happening at the same time as the main clause, rather than a separate, independently timed event.
ehamba emgwaqweni, wabona ingozi
while walking on the road, he saw an accident
  • Ekhathele kakhulu, uSipho wagoduka ngokushesha. Being very tired, Sipho went home quickly. participial subject concord e-, describing a state
  • Sifika esibhedlela, sabona umqondisi elinde thina. Arriving at the hospital, we saw the manager waiting for us. participial subject concord si-, framing the main event

4.4 The causative extension, introduced for production

Purpose: describe making someone do something, or causing an event or a change to happen.

Language Building Block — Causative extension
Verb root + -is- variant + Final vowel
The causative extension is inserted after the verb root and before the final vowel, adding a causer to the action, so that the subject of the sentence brings about the action rather than performing it directly.
ukuhamba → ukuhambisa; ukwazi → ukwazisa
to go → to make go, to send; to know → to inform
  • Umqondisi wangazisa ngesikhathi sokuqala sami. The manager informed me about my starting time. -is- causative, combined with the -ngi- object concord
  • Udade wami wangihambisa ekamelweni lami. The nurse walked me over to my room. -is- causative on ukuhamba, adding a causer

4.5 The passive extension, introduced for production

Purpose: describe an action when the person who performed it is unknown, unimportant, or intentionally left in the background.

Language Building Block — Passive extension
Verb root + -w- variant + Final vowel
The passive extension is inserted after the verb root and before the final vowel, moving the person or thing that would normally be the object into the subject position, while the original doer of the action becomes optional or unstated.
ukubona → ukubonwa; ukuchazela → ukuchazelwa
to see → to be seen; to explain to → to be explained to
  • Umsebenzi wonke wangichazelwa ngosuku lokuqala. The whole job was explained to me on the first day. -w- passive, combined with applicative -el-
  • Indlu yagogo yatholakala ivaliwe. Grandmother's house was found closed. -w- passive on ukuvala, describing the resulting state

Compare the passive here with the object relative in section 4.2. Both can describe something happening to a noun, but the passive changes the verb structure itself, while the object relative adds a describing clause around an unchanged verb.

4.6 Purpose subordination, added to the pattern from Chapter 3

Purpose: explain why an action was carried out, extending the time, cause, and condition subordination already active from Chapter 3.

  • Umqondisi wangichazela wonke umsebenzi, ukuze ngingaphuthelwa yilutho. The manager explained the whole job to me, so that I would not miss anything. ukuze, purpose subordination
  • Ngahambisa incwadi ngokushesha, ngenxa yalokho ugogo wayeyilindele. I sent the letter quickly, because of that, grandmother had been waiting for it. ngenxa yalokho, result connector, linked to the earlier cause

4.7 Reference continuity across connected sentences

Purpose: keep every reference clear across several sentences, so a listener or reader never has to guess who or what is being discussed.

Language Focus

Once a person or thing has been introduced by name, later sentences may refer back with an object concord, a relative clause, a demonstrative such as lowo, leyo, lelo, or a phrase such as owesibili. Choosing among these options, rather than repeating the full noun each time, is what keeps a connected passage sounding natural.

Model Dialogue

Isimo: ULindiwe uchazela uNomvula ngezinsizakalo ezimbili ezamsiza ekhaya likagogo wakhe.
Setting: Lindiwe describes to Nomvula the two people who helped her at her grandfather's home.

UNOMVULA Ngitshele, ubani owakuvulela indlu, njengoba ugogo wayengekho? Tell me, who opened the house for you, since your grandfather was not there?
ULINDIWE Umakhelwane engangingakaze ngimazi ngaphambili nguye owangivulela. Ehlangana nami emnyango, wangazisa ukuthi ugogo usuke wayovakashela udadewabo. The neighbor whom I had not known before is the one who let me in. Meeting me at the door, he informed me that grandfather had gone to visit his sister.
UNOMVULA Kwakukhona omunye owakusiza, angithi? There was another one who helped you, wasn't there?
ULINDIWE Yebo. Owesibili kwakungumzukulu wakhe, owangithengela ushayi ukuze ngingalambi. Wonke umsebenzi wami wenziwa lula yibo bobabili. Yes. The second one was his grandson, who bought me tea so that I would not be hungry. All my worry was made easy by both of them.
UNOMVULA Kwathiwani ngesikhathi ugogo ebuya? What was said when your grandfather came back?
ULINDIWE Efika, wamangala ukubona mina ngihleli endlini yakhe, kodwa wathokoza kakhulu ngenxa yosizo engangilutholile. Lolo suku olwaluqale kabi, lwaphenduka olumnandi ngenxa yalabo bantu ababili. Arriving, he was surprised to see me sitting in his house, but he was very glad because of the help I had received. That day, which had started badly, became a good one because of those two people.

This exchange develops the subject relative across a wider class range, introduces the object relative, participial clauses, the causative and passive extensions, and purpose subordination, all inside one connected description.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Ubani owavulela uLindiwe indlu? (Who opened the house for Lindiwe?)
  2. Umzukulu wenzeni ukuze uLindiwe angalambi? (What did the grandson do so that Lindiwe would not be hungry?)
  3. Ugogo wazizwa kanjani ngesikhathi ebuya? (How did the grandfather feel when he returned?)

Umngane Engingasoze Ngamkhohlwa — A Friend I Will Never Forget

Umngane engangifunda naye eyunivesithi wayehlala eduze kwami, futhi wayeyintombazane engangingakaze ngiyazi ngaphambili ngaphambi kokuqala kwesikole. Sihlangana ngosuku lokuqala, ebonakala ekhathazekile, ngamazisa ukuthi nami ngangisha kuleyo dolobha. Kusukela lapho, saba abangane abakhulu. Kwathi ngesikhathi sesiqeda unyaka wokuqala, wangenza ngaqonda ukuthi ubungane bufana nesipho, futhi akubuyele emuva. Umngane lo, engangimazi kalula manje, waba yingxenye enkulu yosuku lwami lonke lokufunda.

Writing Workshop

Write a short description of a real or realistic person who helped you or influenced you, drawing only on vocabulary and grammar already active from this chapter and earlier chapters.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write a description of eight to twelve sentences, including at least one subject relative clause, one object relative clause, one participial clause, and one verb using either the causative or the passive extension.

  1. Choose your person. In English, note who this person is, and what they did for you or with you.
  2. Introduce the person. Name them once by their full noun, then plan how you will refer back to them.
  3. Add a subject relative clause. Identify the person by an action they performed.
  4. Add an object relative clause. Identify something that was done to them, or that they received.
  5. Add a participial clause. Describe a situation or state that frames one of your main events.
  6. Add a causative or passive verb. Show either that someone made something happen, or that something happened without naming the doer.
  7. Explain a purpose. Use ukuze to say why an action was carried out.
  8. Close with a reference back. Use a demonstrative such as lowo, leyo, or lelo, or a phrase such as owesibili, to refer to your person one final time without repeating the full noun.
  9. Audit your draft. Confirm that every relative concord agrees with the correct noun class, and that each causative or passive form is built on the correct verb root.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • At least one subject relative clause correctly identifies a person or thing by an action performed.
  • At least one object relative clause correctly identifies a person or thing by an action received.
  • At least one participial clause correctly frames a situation or accompanying action.
  • At least one causative or passive verb is used correctly.
  • A purpose clause with ukuze explains why an action was carried out.
  • Standard isiZulu spelling, capitalization, and punctuation are used throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Naming a helper by role before by name

In many isiZulu speaking communities, a speaker will often identify a helper by role or relationship, such as umakhelwane, before giving a personal name, or sometimes instead of a personal name altogether. Notice how Lindiwe describes the neighbor and the grandson in this way, since this pattern is common and appropriate, rather than a sign of vagueness.

Culture in Context — Acknowledging elders with care in description

When a description involves an elder, such as ugogo in this chapter's dialogue, learners should notice that the tone remains respectful even when describing a surprising or awkward moment, such as the grandfather's surprise at finding Lindiwe in his home. Respect language and warmth generally combine, rather than one replacing the other.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using a subject relative when an object relative is needed

Error: Building a relative clause as though the head noun performed the action, when the head noun actually received the action.

Correction: Ask whether the head noun did the action or had the action done to it, and add the subject concord for the true doer when the head noun is the receiver.

Common Mistake — Confusing the causative and the passive extension

Error: Using the causative extension when the sentence actually needs to background the doer of an action, or the reverse.

Correction: Ask whether the subject is making something happen, which needs the causative, or whether the subject is receiving an action whose doer is unstated, which needs the passive.

Common Mistake — Repeating a full noun instead of using a discourse-reference demonstrative

Error: Naming a person or thing in full every time it returns in a passage, even after it has already been clearly established.

Correction: Once a person or thing is clearly established, use a demonstrative such as lowo, leyo, or lelo, or a phrase such as owesibili, instead of repeating the full noun.

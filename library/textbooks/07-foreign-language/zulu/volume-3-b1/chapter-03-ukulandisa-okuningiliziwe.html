Ukulandisa Okuningiliziwe Ngesipiliyoni

Volume III, Chapter 3: Narrating Experience in Detail · Speak and Write Zulu: Discourse Expansion

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Narrate a personal experience across several connected sentences, ordering events clearly in time.
  2. Use object concords to refer to people and things already mentioned in a narrative, without repeating the full noun.
  3. Use the applicative extension productively, to say that an action was done for, to, or because of someone.
  4. Build simple relative clauses, identifying a person or thing by what they did or what happened to them.
  5. Join clauses using subordination for time, cause, and condition, so that a narrative reads as connected prose rather than a list of separate sentences.
  6. Recognize and produce the far distance demonstrative, referring to people, places, or things located away from both speaker and listener.
  7. Give a short instruction or warning inside a narrative using the negative command, and recognize a small set of expressive, formulaic narrative openers.
  8. Read, listen to, write, and speak a short multi paragraph narrative that uses these systems together.

Communication Scenario

Nomvula's class has learned to describe, classify, and compare with confidence. Now she asks for something longer. She wants each learner to tell a true story from their own life, not just a single sentence, but a short account with a beginning, a middle, and an end. Sipho tells the story of the day he started his new job at the hospital. Lindiwe tells the story of a visit to her grandfather's house that did not go as planned. The class is moving from naming and describing things, into telling what happened, and why, and to whom.

  1. This chapter develops narration as a connected skill, introducing object concords, the applicative extension, basic relative clauses, and subordination for production, building directly on the recognition only items from Chapter 2.
  2. Learners also meet the far distance demonstrative, the negative command, and a small set of formulaic narrative openers, so a personal story can be told smoothly from start to finish.

Where This Chapter Fits

Pronunciation and Click Focus

This chapter introduces the nasalized click series for controlled production, following the plain, aspirated, and voiced clicks already active. Nasalized clicks combine a click with a preceding nasal sound, and appear frequently in narrative vocabulary.

Pronunciation Focus — Nasalized clicks nc, nq, nx

The nasalized click series adds a nasal n sound immediately before the click release, without inserting a full syllable between them. Practice incwadi (letter, book), inqola (cart), and inxeba (wound), first slowly, separating the nasal and the click in your mind but not in your speech, then at a natural narrating pace. Listen for the nasal and the click as one connected sound, not two separate ones.

Grammar: Building the Narrative

3.1 Object concords, introduced for production

Purpose: refer back to a person or thing already named in a narrative, without repeating the full noun each time.

Language Focus

An object concord attaches to the verb, between the subject concord and the verb root, and agrees with the noun class of the object. Once a person or thing has been introduced by name, later sentences can refer back to it with the object concord alone.

Language Building Block — Object concord slot
Subject concord + TAM marker + Object concord + Verb root + Final vowel
The object concord sits directly before the verb root, marking who or what receives the action.
Ngambona umuntu omusha. Ngamkhumbula kamuva.
I saw a new person. I remembered him later.
  • Sipho wathenga incwadi. Wayifunda ebusuku. Sipho bought a book. He read it at night. -yi- object concord, class 9, referring back to incwadi
  • Ngabona abangane bami. Ngababingelela ngokushesha. I saw my friends. I greeted them quickly. -ba- object concord, class 2, referring back to abangane

3.2 The applicative extension, introduced for production

Purpose: say that an action was done for someone, to someone, because of a reason, or with a particular instrument, expanding the plain verb root.

Chapter 2 asked you to notice, without analyzing, a fixed expression built on the applicative extension. Here is how that extension works, so you can build new sentences with it.

Language Building Block — Applicative extension
Verb root + -el- or -ел variant + Final vowel
The applicative extension is inserted directly after the verb root and before the final vowel, adding a beneficiary, goal, reason, or instrument to the action.
ukuchaza → ukuchazela; ukuthenga → ukuthengela
to explain → to explain for someone; to buy → to buy for someone
  • Ngachazela umngane wami isizathu. I explained the reason to my friend. -el- applicative, adding a beneficiary to ukuchaza
  • Ugogo wangithengela izinto zesikole. Grandmother bought school things for me. -el- applicative, combined with the -ngi- object concord

Compare this with the fixed expression from Chapter 2, "ngikuchazela ngoba ufuna ukwazi." You can now build new sentences using the same -el- pattern, instead of treating it only as a memorized phrase.

3.3 Basic relative clauses, introduced for production

Purpose: identify a person or thing in a narrative by referring to what they did, or what happened to them.

Language Building Block — Subject relative pattern
Noun + Relative concord + Verb
The relative concord agrees with the noun class of the noun it describes, and attaches to a verb form describing an action that noun performed.
umuntu owafika izolo
the person who arrived yesterday
  • Le ndoda eyangibingelela izolo ingumakhelwane wami. The man who greeted me yesterday is my neighbor. e- relative concord, class 9, subject relative
  • Umsebenzi engiwenzayo manje unzima kunowokuqala. The work that I am doing now is harder than the first. e- relative concord, class 3, combined with object concord -wu-

Compare this with the fixed expression from Chapter 2, "le ndawo eyaziwa kakhulu." You can now build new relative clauses of this basic subject type, in addition to recognizing the fixed phrase.

3.4 Subordination for time, cause, and condition

Purpose: connect clauses so that a narrative flows as one organized account, rather than as separate, disconnected sentences.

  • Ngesikhathi ngifika emsebenzini, bonke abasebenzi babevele bekhona. When I arrived at work, all the workers were already there. ngesikhathi, time subordination
  • Ngoba ngangisanda kuqala, ngangikhathazekile kancane. Because I had just started, I was a little worried. ngoba, cause subordination
  • Uma ufika kusasa, ungasho ukuthi ngithumele wena. If you arrive tomorrow, you may say that I sent you. uma, condition subordination

3.5 The far distance demonstrative

Purpose: refer to a person, place, or thing that is away from both the speaker and the listener, often useful in narration of a past event at another location.

  • Lelo sitolo esasiseduze nomuzi wami sesivaliwe manje. That shop, far from here, which used to be near my home, is closed now. lelo, far distance demonstrative, class 7

3.6 The negative command, introduced for production

Purpose: give a warning or instruction not to do something, often useful when a narrative includes advice given to someone else.

  • Ungahambi wedwa ebusuku. Do not walk alone at night. nga- negative imperative marker with second person singular
  • Ningakhohlwa ukuvala isango. Do not (all of you) forget to close the gate. nga- negative imperative marker with second person plural

3.7 Formulaic narrative openers, for recognition and limited production

Purpose: recognize and use a small set of fixed expressions that open or move a narrative forward, without analyzing their full internal grammar yet.

Language Focus — Formulaic use

Kwathi at the start of a sentence is a fixed narrative opener meaning roughly "then it happened that," and may be used to begin a new stage of a story. Use it as a memorized opening device for now.

Model Dialogue

Isimo: UNomvula ucela uSipho ukuthi alandise ngosuku lwakhe lokuqala emsebenzini.
Setting: Nomvula asks Sipho to narrate his first day at work.

UNOMVULA Sipho, sitshele ngosuku lwakho lokuqala esibhedlela. Kwenzekani? Sipho, tell us about your first day at the hospital. What happened?
USIPHO Kwathi ekuseni, ngafika ngesikhathi. Ngesikhathi ngingena, umqondisi wangibingelela wangichazela izinto engangizozenza. Then, in the morning, I arrived on time. When I entered, the manager greeted me and explained to me the things I would do.
UNOMVULA Ubona bani owakusiza kakhulu ngalolo suku? Who do you think helped you the most that day?
USIPHO Kunodade omunye owangisiza kakhulu, engingakaze ngimazi ngaphambili. Wangichazela wonke umsebenzi, wangikhombisa nendlela eya ekamelweni lami. There is a nurse who helped me a great deal, whom I had not known before. She explained the whole job to me, and she also showed me the way to my room.
UNOMVULA Kwathi ekugcineni kosuku, wawuzizwa kanjani? Then in the end of the day, how did you feel?
USIPHO Ngaleso sikhathi, ngangikhathele kakhulu, kodwa ngangijabule ngokuthi ngiqedile usuku lwami lokuqala ngaphandle kwenkinga. Udade wami wathi kimi, "Ungakhathazeki, konke kuzoba lula ngokuhamba kwesikhathi." At that time, I was very tired, but I was happy that I had finished my first day without a problem. My nurse friend said to me, "Do not worry, everything will become easier as time passes."

This exchange develops object concords, the applicative extension, basic relative clauses, subordination, the far distance demonstrative, and the negative command together, inside one connected narrative.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Ngubani owabingelela uSipho ngesikhathi engena? (Who greeted Sipho when he entered?)
  2. Ngubani omunye owamsiza kakhulu ngalolo suku? (Who else helped him a great deal that day?)
  3. Udade wami wathini kuSipho ekugcineni? (What did the nurse say to Sipho at the end?)

Reading Practice

Read the following short narrative, written by another learner, telling the story of a memorable journey. It reactivates object concords, the applicative extension, and relative clauses in a slightly different arrangement from the model dialogue above. Read it aloud twice before answering.

Uhambo Engingasoze Ngalukhohlwa — A Journey I Will Never Forget

Ngonyaka odlule, ngahamba nomndeni wami saya kulelo dolobha elikude nathi. Ngesikhathi sifika, umzala wami esasizombona wasibingelela ngothando, wasithengela ukudla, wasikhombisa nendawo esasizolala kuyo. Kwathi ngobusuku bokuqala, indawo esasilele kuyo yaba nomsindo omkhulu, futhi sonke asizange silale kahle. Ekuseni, umzala wami wasichazela ukuthi lowo msindo wawuvela emgwaqweni oseduze. Wasixwayisa wathi, "Ningakhathazeki ngalokho, sizoshintsha amakamelo namuhla." Ngemuva kwalokho, konke kwahamba kahle, futhi uhambo lonke lwaphenduka isipiliyoni esimnandi engingasoze ngasikhohlwa.

"esasizombona," "wasithengela," and "wasixwayisa" each combine a subject concord, an object concord, and in two cases the applicative extension, exactly as practiced in sections 3.1 and 3.2.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Ngubani owabingelela umndeni ngesikhathi befika? (Who greeted the family when they arrived?)
  2. Kwakukhona nkathazo bani ngobusuku bokuqala? (What problem was there on the first night?)
  3. Ngokubona kwakho, kungani lolu hambo lwaphenduka isipiliyoni esimnandi, ngisho noma kwaba nenkinga? (In your view, why did this journey become a good experience, even though there was a problem?)

Writing Workshop

Write a short narrative about a real or realistic personal experience, drawing only on vocabulary and grammar already active from this chapter and earlier chapters.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write a narrative of eight to twelve sentences, with a clear beginning, middle, and end, including at least one object concord, one applicative verb, one relative clause, and one subordinate clause of time or cause.

  1. Choose your experience. In English, note the beginning, middle, and end of something that happened to you.
  2. Open the narrative. Use kwathi or a similar opener to set the scene.
  3. Introduce the people involved. Name each person once by their full noun, then plan how you will refer back to them.
  4. Refer back using object concords. Once a person or thing is introduced, use the object concord instead of repeating the noun.
  5. Add at least one applicative verb. Show that an action was done for, to, or because of someone.
  6. Add at least one relative clause. Identify someone by what they did.
  7. Connect at least two clauses with subordination. Use ngesikhathi, ngoba, or uma to join events smoothly.
  8. Close the narrative. Use ekugcineni or a similar closer to mark the end of the story.
  9. Audit your draft. Confirm that every object concord and relative concord agrees with the correct noun class.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • The narrative has a clear beginning, middle, and end.
  • At least one object concord correctly refers back to a previously named noun.
  • At least one applicative verb is used correctly.
  • At least one relative clause correctly identifies a person or thing.
  • At least one subordinate clause connects two events using time, cause, or condition.
  • Standard isiZulu spelling, capitalization, and punctuation are used throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Acknowledging help within a narrative

In many isiZulu speaking communities, a personal narrative commonly includes clear acknowledgment of anyone who helped along the way, as Sipho and the learner in the reading passage both do. Leaving out this acknowledgment can make a narrative sound incomplete, even if the main events are told accurately.

Culture in Context — Giving advice gently inside a story

When a narrative includes advice given by one person to another, such as "ungakhathazeki," the tone is generally reassuring rather than commanding. Notice how the negative command in this chapter's dialogue and reading passage softens a difficult moment, rather than criticizing the listener.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Repeating the full noun instead of using an object concord

Error: Naming a person or thing once, then naming it again in full in every following sentence, instead of using the object concord.

Correction: After the first mention, check whether the object concord alone can carry the reference clearly, and use it instead of repeating the noun.

Common Mistake — Confusing the applicative extension with the plain verb

Error: Using the plain verb root where the sentence actually needs a beneficiary, so the meaning of "for someone" is lost.

Correction: Ask whether the action was done for, to, or because of a specific person, and add the -el- extension when that meaning is present.

Common Mistake — Using the wrong relative concord for the noun class

Error: Attaching a relative concord that matches a different noun class than the noun actually being described.

Correction: Identify the noun class of the head noun first, then choose the matching relative concord, checking it against the noun class table if needed.

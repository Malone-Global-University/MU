Volume III, Chapter 19: System Consolidation and Accuracy Repair · Speak and Write Zulu: Discourse Expansion

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify and correct persistent click articulation errors, including confusion between the plain, aspirated, and nasalized series.
  2. Identify and correct concord agreement errors across subject concords, adjectival concords, and possessive concords.
  3. Identify and correct object concord placement errors within the verb template.
  4. Identify and correct tense, aspect, mood, and polarity errors, including negative final vowel mistakes.
  5. Identify and correct relative and participial clause errors, including incorrect relative concord selection.
  6. Apply a self editing routine to your own writing from Chapters 16 through 18, using the error categories practiced in this chapter.

Communication Scenario

Thandeka finished the report about Nomvula's celebration, but before sending it to family members far away, she asks a friend, Sipho, to check her draft. Sipho reads carefully and finds a few recurring errors, the kind that many learners make at this stage, in concord, in object concords, in tense choice, and in one relative clause. Thandeka must understand each correction, not merely accept it, so that her next report avoids the same mistakes.

  1. This chapter introduces no new grammar system. Instead, it isolates and repairs the systems already active across Chapters 1 through 18, so that learners carry stronger accuracy into the Chapter 20 capstone.
  2. Learners work directly with flawed sentences, identify what is wrong, and produce the corrected form, applying the same reasoning Sipho uses when he reads Thandeka's draft.

Pronunciation and Click Repair

No new click sound appears in this chapter. Instead, learners revisit the plain, aspirated, and nasalized click series introduced earlier in the volume, and practice the specific contrasts that most often cause confusion at this stage.

Pronunciation Focus — Separating plain, aspirated, and nasalized clicks

A common error at this stage is producing a plain click, such as c, when the word requires an aspirated click, such as ch, or a nasalized click, such as nc. Listen for the small puff of air that marks an aspirated click, and for the nasal quality that marks a nasalized click, before choosing which series a word requires.

  • caca (to be clear) versus chaza (to explain): plain versus aspirated click at the start of the word.
  • gqoka (to get dressed) versus ngqongqoza (to knock): voiced click versus nasalized voiced click.

Grammar: Repairing Concord, Object Concord, TAM, and Clauses

19.1 Repairing concord agreement errors

Purpose: identify a subject, adjectival, or possessive concord that does not match its noun class, and replace it with the correct concord.

Language Focus

A concord error usually happens when a learner selects a concord that matches a different noun class from the one actually being described, often the class of a nearby word rather than the head noun. Before correcting a sentence, first identify the noun class of the head noun, then check every concord attached to it.

  • Okungalungile: Umndeni bahlela umcimbi omkhulu. Incorrect: The family, they are organizing a large celebration. umndeni is Class 3, so it requires the Class 3 subject concord u-, not the Class 2 concord ba-
  • Okulungile: Umndeni uhlela umcimbi omkhulu. Correct: The family is organizing a large celebration. Class 3 subject concord u- correctly matches umndeni

19.2 Repairing object concord placement errors

Purpose: identify an object concord that has been placed outside the verb template, or omitted where a definite object requires it, and correct its position.

Language Note — Where the object concord belongs
subject concord + TAM marker + object concord + verb root + extension + final vowel
The object concord always sits inside the verb word, immediately before the root, never as a separate word before or after the verb. A frequent error places a pronoun outside the verb, imitating English word order, instead of building it into the verb template.
Okungalungile: Ngithanda yena umzukulu. Okulungile: Ngiyamthanda umzukulu.
Incorrect, following English order: I like him the grandchild. Correct: I like the grandchild, using the Class 1 object concord -m- inside the verb.
  • Okungalungile: Isigaba sokuqala sikhuluma ngezizathu ezenza sona ubaluleke. Incorrect: The first section discusses the reasons that make it important, with a doubled, misplaced reference. the relative construction already carries the reference, so the extra pronoun sona is redundant and misplaced
  • Okulungile: Isigaba sokuqala sikhuluma ngezizathu ezenza umcimbi ubaluleke. Correct: The first section discusses the reasons that make the celebration important. the noun umcimbi is restated instead of doubling the reference with a stray pronoun

19.3 Repairing TAM and polarity errors

Purpose: identify a tense, aspect, mood, or polarity marker that does not match the intended meaning, and correct the final vowel and marker together.

Language Focus

A common polarity error keeps the affirmative final vowel while adding a negative marker, or the reverse, leaving the two parts of the verb in conflict. Negative present forms require both a negative marker and a corresponding final vowel change, not one without the other.

  • Okungalungile: Umndeni awuhlelile umcimbi ngoba awukho isizathu. Incorrect: mismatched negative marking, producing an unclear meaning. the negative present requires a consistent final vowel change alongside the negative marker
  • Okulungile: Umndeni awuhleli umcimbi ngoba akukho isizathu. Correct: The family is not organizing a celebration because there is no reason. negative marker and final vowel now agree, and the negative existential akukho is used correctly

19.4 Repairing relative and participial clause errors

Purpose: identify a relative concord that does not match the class of the noun it describes, and replace it with the correct relative concord.

Language Focus

A relative clause error often happens when the relative concord matches the class of the object rather than the noun actually being described. Before choosing a relative concord, identify which noun the clause is describing, then select the concord for that noun's class, not for any other word nearby.

  • Okungalungile: Isizathu ezenza umndeni uhlele lo mcimbi ubaluleke kakhulu. Incorrect: the relative concord does not match isizathu, the noun it describes. isizathu is Class 7, so it requires the Class 7 relative concord esi-, not eze-
  • Okulungile: Isizathu esenza umndeni uhlele lo mcimbi sibaluleke kakhulu. Correct: The reason that makes the family organize this celebration is very important. Class 7 relative concord esi- and subject concord si- both correctly match isizathu

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Reread your own Chapter 18 report. Find at least one place where you can check for a concord error, an object concord error, a TAM or polarity error, and a relative clause error, even if your original sentence was already correct. Rewrite any sentence that needs correction, and write one sentence explaining the repair you made, or noting that no repair was needed.

  1. Reread your isingeniso. Check that every subject concord matches its noun's class.
  2. Reread your isigaba sections. Check each object concord is built into the verb, not placed as a separate pronoun.
  3. Check your TAM and polarity choices. Confirm that negative markers and final vowels agree throughout.
  4. Check any relative clause. Confirm the relative concord matches the class of the noun it describes, not a nearby word.
  5. Rewrite corrected sentences. Replace any flawed sentence with its repaired form.
  6. Write a repair note. For each correction, write one sentence naming the error category you fixed.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • Every subject concord matches the noun class of its head noun.
  • Every object concord is built into the verb template, not placed as a separate word.
  • Negative markers and final vowels agree consistently throughout the text.
  • Every relative concord matches the class of the noun it describes.
  • At least one repair note names the error category corrected.
  • The register remains consistently formal throughout the revised text.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Asking a trusted reader for help

Asking a friend or family member to check an important piece of writing before it is shared is a common and welcome practice, not a sign of weakness. A careful reader who points out an error respectfully is generally seen as offering a genuine kindness, particularly when the writing will be read by relatives who cannot ask questions in person.

Culture in Context — Correcting respectfully

When pointing out an error in someone else's writing or speech, it is common to frame the correction gently, often by first acknowledging what the writer did well before naming what needs to change. This approach is often preferred over a blunt list of faults, especially between people of different ages or status.

Regional Variation — How feedback is given

Some communities favor direct, detailed written feedback, marked clearly on the page, while others prefer discussing corrections aloud, in conversation, rather than in writing. Both approaches serve the same purpose, helping a writer notice and repair a recurring error, though the preferred style varies by family, school, and workplace.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Correcting a concord by guessing rather than checking the noun class

Error: Replacing an incorrect concord with another concord that still does not match the head noun's class, because the noun class was never actually identified.

Correction: Before changing any concord, first name the noun class of the head noun, then select the concord table entry for that class specifically.

Common Mistake — Adding a separate pronoun instead of repairing the verb template

Error: Fixing an object concord error by inserting a separate pronoun word near the verb, rather than placing the object concord inside the verb template.

Correction: Build the object concord into its correct slot inside the verb, between the TAM marker and the root, exactly as shown in the verb template pattern.

Common Mistake — Repairing only half of a polarity error

Error: Changing the negative marker to match the intended meaning while leaving the final vowel unchanged, or the reverse, so the verb still shows conflicting polarity signals.

Correction: Always check the negative marker and the final vowel together as a single repair, since Zulu polarity changes both at once.

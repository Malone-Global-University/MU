Volume III, Chapter 14: Conditionals and Hypotheticals · Speak and Write Zulu: Discourse Expansion

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. State a real, likely condition and its result, using uma with the present tense.
  2. Distinguish a real condition from a less certain or hypothetical one, using context and verb choice rather than English word order.
  3. Introduce the potential mood, using the -nga- marker, to talk about what could or might happen.
  4. Combine a conditional clause with a cause, consequence connector from Chapter 13, to reason about a plan or scenario.
  5. Discuss simple future plans that depend on a stated condition, in both speech and writing.
  6. Continue developing the causative, reciprocal, and neuter, stative extensions from Chapters 12 and 13, inside conditional sentences.

Communication Scenario

Following the on air exchange with Bab' uNzimande, Thandeka's producer suggests a new radio segment, where listeners phone in to discuss what they would do in a given situation. Thandeka must plan this segment with her co host, Sipho, weighing several conditions. If enough listeners phone in, they will run the segment weekly. If the phone lines are quiet, they will try a written message format instead. As they plan, Thandeka and Sipho also imagine what might happen if a well known elder joined the program as a guest.

  1. This chapter introduces the real conditional pattern with uma, and the potential mood with -nga-, for the first time as productive, controlled systems.
  2. The causative, reciprocal, and neuter, stative extensions, developed in Chapter 13, continue to appear here inside new conditional sentences, without introducing any new extension.

Pronunciation and Prosody Review

A conditional sentence in isiZulu, as in English, is usually spoken as two connected units, a rising or level pitch on the condition, followed by a brief pause, then a falling pitch on the result. This chapter asks learners to carry that pattern into their own conditional sentences, connecting it to the pause before ngoba and ngakho practiced in Chapter 13.

Pronunciation Focus — Pausing after uma

When uma opens a sentence, a brief pause commonly falls at the end of the condition clause, just before the result begins. This pause helps the listener recognize where the condition ends and the result starts, and it does not affect click quality or vowel length in the surrounding words.

Grammar: Conditionals and Hypotheticals

14.1 Real conditions with uma

Purpose: state a likely or real condition, then describe what follows, using uma with the present tense in both clauses.

Language Focus

uma introduces a condition that the speaker considers likely or realistic. The verb in the uma clause keeps its normal subject concord and appears in the present tense, even when the meaning refers to the future. The result clause also uses a normal tense, most often present or future, depending on the intended meaning. uma can be translated as if or as when, depending on context.

  • Uma abalaleli beshaya ucingo abaningi, sizoqhubeka nalesi sigaba njalo sonto. If many listeners phone in, we will continue this segment every week. uma plus present tense condition, plus future tense result
  • Uma amaline engaxakekile, sizama indlela yemiyalezo ebhaliwe. If the lines are not busy, we try the written message method instead. uma plus negative present condition, plus present tense result

14.2 The potential mood with -nga-

Purpose: describe what could, might, or would possibly happen, using the potential marker -nga- inserted into the verb, often together with mhlawumbe.

Language Note — Potential mood pattern
[subject concord] + nga + [verb root] + [final vowel]
The potential marker -nga- slots into the verb template after the subject concord, in the same position where tense markers usually appear. The resulting form expresses possibility rather than certainty, and often pairs naturally with mhlawumbe, perhaps.
Mhlawumbe umkhulu angeza njengesivakashi kulesi sigaba.
Perhaps the elder could come as a guest on this segment.
  • Uma singamema umkhulu, abalaleli bangathokoza kakhulu. If we invite the elder, the listeners could be very pleased. uma condition plus potential mood -nga- in the result clause
  • Sipho ucabanga ukuthi singaxhumana naye ngocingo kusasa. Sipho thinks that we could get in touch with him by phone tomorrow. potential mood -nga-, applied to the reciprocal extension -xhumana-

14.3 Combining a condition with a cause, consequence connector

Purpose: connect a condition to a further consequence, using ngakho or ngenxa yalokho from Chapter 13 inside a longer conditional sentence.

Language Focus

A conditional sentence can be extended with ngakho or ngenxa yalokho, to add a second, resulting consequence beyond the immediate result of the uma clause. This lets learners describe a short chain of reasoning, condition, immediate result, then further consequence, without needing any new grammar beyond what Chapters 13 and 14 have already introduced.

  • Uma isigaba lesi siphumelela, abalaleli bazoxhumana nathi njalo. Ngakho, singathola amathuba amaningi okufunda ngemibono yabo. If this segment succeeds, listeners will connect with us regularly. Therefore, we could get many opportunities to learn about their views. uma condition, plus future result, plus ngakho, plus potential mood consequence

Learners are asked to build short conditional sentences using uma and the potential mood on the roots and topics practiced in this chapter. Building conditional sentences on unfamiliar roots and topics continues to develop through Chapters 15 to 20.

Model Dialogue

Isimo: UThandeka noSipho bahlela isigaba esisha somsakazo, lapho abalaleli bezoshaya ucingo bezokhuluma ngezinto ezingenzeka empilweni yabo.
Setting: Thandeka and Sipho are planning a new radio segment, where listeners will phone in to talk about possible things in their lives.

USIPHO Uma sisungula isigaba esisha, kufanele sicabange ngamacebo amabili, elokushaya ucingo nelemiyalezo ebhaliwe. If we start a new segment, we need to think about two plans, one for phoning in and one for written messages.
UTHANDEKA Uma abalaleli beshaya ucingo abaningi, sizoqhubeka nendlela yocingo njalo sonto. If many listeners phone in, we will continue the phone method every week.
USIPHO Kodwa uma amaline engaxakekile, singazama indlela yemiyalezo, mhlawumbe abalaleli bangathanda ukubhala kunokukhuluma. But if the lines are not busy, we could try the message method, perhaps listeners would prefer writing to speaking.
UTHANDEKA Ngicabanga nokuthi singamema umuntu omdala othile, mhlawumbe umkhulu esake sakhuluma naye. I am also thinking that we could invite a certain elder, perhaps the elder we once spoke with.
USIPHO Uma singamema umkhulu, abalaleli bangathokoza kakhulu, ngoba uyakwazi ukuxoxa kamnandi. If we invite the elder, the listeners could be very pleased, because he knows how to narrate pleasantly.
UTHANDEKA Ngakho, uma isigaba lesi siphumelela, singaxhumana naye njalo, futhi singathola amathuba amaningi okufunda ngemibono yakhe. Therefore, if this segment succeeds, we could connect with him regularly, and we could get many opportunities to learn about his views.

This dialogue combines the real conditional with uma, the potential mood with -nga-, and a cause, consequence connector from Chapter 13, reactivating patterns from Chapters 7 and 13 while extending them to new plans.

Writing Workshop

Write a short plan, drawing only on vocabulary and grammar already active from Chapters 1 through 14.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write a short paragraph, six to eight sentences, describing a plan of your own that depends on a condition, such as a plan for study, work, or community activity. Include at least one uma clause with a present tense result, at least one sentence using the potential mood with -nga-, and at least one cause, consequence connector from Chapter 13.

  1. State your plan. Begin with icebo lami, or a similar opening, to introduce your plan.
  2. State a real condition. Use uma with the present tense to describe a likely situation.
  3. Describe the immediate result. Use present or future tense in the result clause.
  4. Add a possibility. Use the potential mood, -nga-, to describe something that could happen next.
  5. Add a further consequence. Use ngakho or ngenxa yalokho to connect a second, resulting outcome.
  6. Audit your draft. Confirm that your condition, result, and consequence connect logically, and that every grammar system used is already active.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • The paragraph states a clear plan early on.
  • At least one real condition is given, using uma with the present tense correctly.
  • At least one sentence uses the potential mood, -nga-, correctly.
  • At least one further consequence is stated, using ngakho or ngenxa yalokho correctly.
  • Any causative, reciprocal, or neuter, stative form used is correct and already active.
  • Standard isiZulu spelling, capitalization, and punctuation are used throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Planning around community input

In many isiZulu speaking communities, plans for shared activities, including radio programming, community events, and family decisions, are often shaped around anticipated participation, with organizers stating a condition, such as sufficient interest or attendance, before committing fully to a plan.

Culture in Context — Inviting elders into public discussion

Inviting a respected elder to participate in a public discussion, such as a radio segment, is often seen as a way of adding credibility and depth, and such invitations are commonly extended with visible respect for the elder's time and standing in the community.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using the future tense inside the uma clause

Error: Placing a future tense marker inside the uma clause itself, following the English pattern of if plus will.

Correction: Keep the uma clause in the present tense, even when the meaning refers to the future, and reserve the future tense, where needed, for the result clause.

Common Mistake — Confusing the potential mood with the future tense

Error: Using the future tense marker where the meaning is possibility rather than certainty, or using -nga- where a plain, certain future is meant.

Correction: Use the future tense for events the speaker considers certain or planned, and use the potential mood, -nga-, for events the speaker considers merely possible.

Common Mistake — Leaving a condition without a stated result

Error: Stating a condition with uma and never completing it with a result clause, leaving the sentence incomplete.

Correction: Follow the writing workshop steps above, and always pair a stated condition with a clear result, whether certain or merely possible.

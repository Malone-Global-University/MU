Umbono, Ukuvumelana, Nokungavumelani

Volume III, Chapter 7: Opinion, Agreement, and Disagreement · Speak and Write Zulu: Discourse Expansion

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Express a personal opinion using ngicabanga ukuthi, I think that, and related complement structures with ukuthi.
  2. Agree with another speaker's opinion, and register partial agreement, using controlled expressions of agreement.
  3. Disagree politely, softening a disagreement rather than stating it bluntly, in a way appropriate to Standard isiZulu conversation.
  4. Compare two or more options, using comparative expressions built on adjectival and verbal concord already active from earlier chapters.
  5. Continue combining the causative, applicative, and passive extensions on a single verb root, now inside opinion, comparison, and disagreement sentences.
  6. Recognize, for the first time, the internal shape of the -an- reciprocal pattern and the -ek- neuter-stative pattern in familiar formulaic verbs, without yet building new forms from a root.
  7. Maintain subject relative clauses and object relative clauses already active from earlier chapters, now inside opinion and comparison sentences.
  8. Read, listen to, write, and speak a short connected exchange in which two speakers compare views and manage a polite disagreement.

Communication Scenario

Lindiwe and Nomvula are now discussing something less settled than a relationship or a garden: what to do about a small community project that needs a decision. Nomvula thinks the group should meet weekly. Lindiwe is not so sure. Voicing an opinion, weighing someone else's view, and disagreeing without causing offense are everyday skills that Zulu speakers rely on constantly, in families, workplaces, and community meetings alike.

  1. This chapter introduces controlled ways to state an opinion, agree fully or partly, and disagree politely, while comparing two or more options in plain, natural isiZulu.
  2. Learners also take a first, receptive look at how the -an- reciprocal pattern and the -ek- neuter-stative pattern are built from a verb root, preparing the ground for full productive use in a later chapter, while continuing to use the formulaic set from Chapters 5 and 6 as before.

Amagama Ombono Nokuvumelana — Vocabulary of Opinion and Agreement

Core Vocabulary: Opinion and Agreement

This chapter adds vocabulary for stating an opinion, agreeing, disagreeing politely, and comparing options, while keeping the reciprocal and neuter-stative set from Chapters 5 and 6 fully active.

Ukuvumelana and ukungavumelani remain formulaic reciprocal items, used here as whole words. The internal -an- pattern that builds them is examined for the first time in this chapter, but only receptively.

Pronunciation and Click Focus

This chapter continues nasalized click practice, and adds attention to the rhythm of opinion frames such as ngicabanga ukuthi, which learners will use frequently in this chapter and beyond. Keep the vowels in ukuthi crisp and separate, rather than collapsing them into a single blended sound.

Pronunciation Focus — The opinion frame ngicabanga ukuthi

Say ngicabanga ukuthi slowly first, giving each syllable equal weight, then speed up while keeping the -ng- of ngicabanga and the -th- of ukuthi clearly distinct from one another, since these two sounds are easy to blur at natural speaking speed.

Grammar: Opinion, Comparison, and Polite Disagreement

7.1 Stating an opinion with ngicabanga ukuthi

Purpose: introduce a personal opinion using a complement clause with ukuthi, already active from earlier chapters for other complement functions.

Language Focus

Ngicabanga ukuthi, I think that, opens a complement clause in exactly the same way as other ukuthi clauses already practiced. Whatever follows ukuthi is a full sentence in its own right, carrying its own subject concord, tense, and object concord where needed.

  • Ngicabanga ukuthi kufanele sihlangane njalo ngeviki. I think that we should meet every week. ngicabanga ukuthi, opinion frame, plus complement clause
  • UNomvula ucabanga ukuthi leli thuba lingcono. Nomvula thinks that this option is better. subject concord u-, opinion verb, ukuthi complement

7.2 Agreement and polite disagreement

Purpose: agree fully or partly with another speaker, and disagree without causing offense.

Language Focus

Full agreement can be stated simply, as in ngiyavumelana nawe, I agree with you. Partial agreement or polite disagreement is usually softened first, often with kunjalo, kodwa, that is so, but, before the speaker's own view is added. This keeps the exchange respectful, rather than stating a flat contradiction.

  • Ngiyavumelana nawe ngokuphelele. I agree with you completely. full agreement, reciprocal verb used with nawe, with you
  • Kunjalo, kodwa ngicabanga ukuthi kufanele siqale kancane. That is so, but I think that we should start slowly. softened disagreement, followed by an opinion frame
  • Asikavumelani kahle kulolu daba. We have not yet fully agreed on this matter. negative perfect on the formulaic reciprocal ukuvumelana

7.3 Comparing two or more options

Purpose: compare options using comparative expressions built on adjectival concord and the applicative extension already active from earlier chapters.

Language Building Block — Comparing with -ngcono and ukuqhathanisa
Noun + concord + ngcono, kuna + noun compared
To compare two options directly, isiZulu often places the adjective ngcono, better, after the concord agreeing with the option being praised, and follows it with kuna, than, before the option being compared against. Ukuqhathanisa, to compare, itself uses the applicative -is- already familiar from earlier chapters, added to the root -qhatha-.
Leli thuba lingcono kunalelo. / Sizoqhathanisa amathuba amabili.
This option is better than that one. / We will compare two options.
  • Ukuhlangana njalo ngeviki kungcono kunokuhlangana kanye ngenyanga. Meeting every week is better than meeting once a month. comparative construction with kuna, comparing two infinitive-noun phrases
  • Ake siqhathanise amathuba omabili ngaphambi kokuthi sinqume. Let us compare the two options before we decide. ukuqhathanisa, applicative-derived verb, subjunctive frame ake si-

7.4 Extension combinations inside opinion sentences

Purpose: continue combining the causative, applicative, and passive extensions on a single root, now inside sentences that state an opinion or manage a disagreement.

  • Ngicabanga ukuthi umhlangano kufanele wenzelwe abantu bonke, hhayi abambalwa nje. I think that the meeting should be arranged for everyone, not just a few people. passive plus applicative on -enza-, wenzelwe, done: for, participants clarified in context
  • Uma sinqumela abanye ngaphandle kokubabuza, singabadidekisa. If we decide for others without asking them, we might confuse them. causative on the neuter-stative root -dideka-, -didekisa-, to make confused, applicative -el- on -nquma-

Ukudidekisa, to make confused, shows the causative -is- attached directly to the neuter-stative root -dideka-. Learners may recognize this combination now, but should continue treating ukudideka itself as a formulaic whole word.

7.5 A first look inside: the -an- and -ek- patterns

Purpose: recognize, receptively only, how the -an- reciprocal pattern and the -ek- neuter-stative pattern are built from a verb root, without yet producing new forms.

Language Building Block — Looking inside a formulaic word, for recognition only
Root + -an- + Final vowel, root + -ek- + Final vowel
Ukuvumelana is built from the root -vuma-, to agree, plus -el-, plus -an-, plus the final vowel -a. Ukudideka is built from the root -dida-, to confuse, plus -ek-, plus the final vowel -a. Learners are not asked to build new words this way yet. This box exists only so that the shape of familiar formulaic words becomes visible, ahead of full production in a later chapter.
vuma + el + an + a → vumelana / dida + ek + a → dideka
to agree, with each other, about it / to become confused

This box is recognition-only. No exercise in this chapter requires learners to build a new -an- or -ek- form from an unfamiliar root.

Model Dialogue

Isimo: ULindiwe noNomvula baxoxa ngokuthi umhlangano womphakathi kufanele ubanjwe kangaki.
Setting: Lindiwe and Nomvula discuss how often the community meeting should be held.

UNOMVULA Ngicabanga ukuthi kufanele sihlangane njalo ngeviki. Ithuba lokuqhubeka phambili likhulu uma sisebenzisana njalo. I think that we should meet every week. The opportunity to move forward is greater if we work together regularly.
ULINDIWE Kunjalo, kodwa ngicabanga ukuthi kufanele siqale kancane. Abanye abantu abakwazi ukuza njalo ngeviki. That is so, but I think that we should start slowly. Some people cannot come every week.
UNOMVULA Ake siqhathanise amathuba omabili. Ukuhlangana njalo ngeviki kungcono kunokuhlangana kanye ngenyanga, kodwa uma kunzima kubanye, singahlangana kabili ngenyanga. Let us compare the two options. Meeting every week is better than meeting once a month, but if it is difficult for others, we could meet twice a month.
ULINDIWE Ngiyavumelana nalokho ngokuphelele. Umhlangano kufanele wenzelwe abantu bonke, hhayi abambalwa nje. I agree with that completely. The meeting should be arranged for everyone, not just a few people.
UNOMVULA Kulungile. Asikavumelani kahle ngendawo yomhlangano, kodwa ngokwengeziwe, singaxoxa ngalokho ngesonto elizayo. Good. We have not yet fully agreed on the meeting place, but additionally, we can discuss that next week.
ULINDIWE Uma sinqumela abanye ngaphandle kokubabuza, singabadidekisa. Kufanele sibabuze kuqala. If we decide for others without asking them, we might confuse them. We should ask them first.

This exchange introduces the opinion frame, agreement and polite disagreement, and comparison of options, while reactivating the causative, applicative, and passive extensions already active from Chapters 5 and 6.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Yiziphi izinto ezimbili uLindiwe noNomvula abaqhathanisayo? (What two things are Lindiwe and Nomvula comparing?)
  2. Bavumelana ngani ngokuphelele? (What do they agree on completely?)
  3. Basakhathazekile ngani, ngokusho kwendaba? (What are they still uncertain about, according to the conversation?)

Ukukhetha Isikole Esisha — Choosing a New School

Umndeni wami ucabanga ukuthi kufanele sishintshe isikole sengane yami. Ubaba ucabanga ukuthi isikole esiseduze singcono, ngoba asikho kude, futhi izindleko zokuthutha zizoncipha. Writing Workshop

Write a short account of an opinion, a comparison, and a polite disagreement, drawing only on vocabulary and grammar already active from this chapter and earlier chapters.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write a description of eight to twelve sentences, including at least one opinion stated with ngicabanga ukuthi, one comparison using ngcono and kuna, one instance of agreement or polite disagreement using the formulaic reciprocal verbs from this chapter, and one verb combining two extensions.

  1. Choose your decision. In English, note two options being compared, and who holds which opinion.
  2. State the first opinion. Use ngicabanga ukuthi, or a similar frame with a named speaker's subject concord.
  3. Compare the options. Use ngcono and kuna to say which option is better, and why.
  4. Register agreement or disagreement. Use a formulaic reciprocal verb such as ukuvumelana or ukungavumelani, softened with kunjalo, kodwa if needed.
  5. Combine two extensions. Use the causative and applicative, or the applicative and passive, together on a single root.
  6. Close with a decision. State, in your own words, which option was finally chosen, and why.
  7. Audit your draft. Confirm that every reciprocal verb is used as a whole word, and that no new -an- or -ek- form has been built from an unfamiliar root.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • At least one opinion is stated clearly using ngicabanga ukuthi or a similar frame.
  • At least one comparison uses ngcono and kuna correctly.
  • At least one instance of agreement or polite disagreement uses a formulaic reciprocal verb correctly.
  • At least one verb correctly combines two extensions already active from earlier chapters.
  • No new reciprocal or neuter-stative verb has been built from an unfamiliar root by analogy.
  • Standard isiZulu spelling, capitalization, and punctuation are used throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Softening disagreement

In many isiZulu speaking settings, a flat contradiction is often avoided in favor of a softened opening, such as kunjalo, kodwa, that is so, but, which acknowledges the other person's view before offering a different one. This pattern is common in family discussions, workplace meetings, and community gatherings alike.

Culture in Context — Group decisions and consensus

Community and family decisions, such as the meeting schedule discussed by Lindiwe and Nomvula, are commonly reached through extended discussion aimed at consensus, rather than through a quick majority vote, though practices vary by community and context.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Stating disagreement too bluntly

Error: Opening a disagreement directly with angivumelani, I do not agree, without any softening, which can sound abrupt in many contexts.

Correction: Open with kunjalo, kodwa, that is so, but, or a similar softening phrase, before stating a different opinion, unless the setting clearly calls for direct disagreement.

Common Mistake — Building a new -an- or -ek- form from an unfamiliar root

Error: Attaching -an- or -ek- to an unfamiliar root by analogy with ukuvumelana or ukudideka, before this pattern has been introduced for production.

Correction: Continue using only the reciprocal and neuter-stative verbs already introduced as fixed vocabulary items. Wait for the -an- and -ek- patterns to be introduced for production in a later chapter.

Common Mistake — Confusing ngcono with a plain adjective of quality

Error: Using ngcono alone to describe a single option, without kuna and the option being compared against, so the comparison is left incomplete.

Correction: When comparing two named options, include kuna before the second option, as in kungcono kunalokho, it is better than that, so the comparison is explicit.

Ukuxhumana Nokuzenzakalela

Volume III, Chapter 6: Reciprocal, Stative, and Valency Control · Speak and Write Zulu: Discourse Expansion

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize and use a wider, still formulaic, set of reciprocal verbs, describing actions that two or more people do to or with one another.
  2. Recognize and use a wider, still formulaic, set of neuter-stative verbs, describing a state or condition that a person or thing simply finds itself in.
  3. Distinguish, in your own words, a reciprocal meaning, a neuter-stative meaning, a causative meaning, and a passive meaning, without yet building new reciprocal or neuter-stative forms from scratch.
  4. Track how many participants a verb takes, and how the causative, applicative, and passive extensions each change that number, in a skill called valency control.
  5. Combine two or three extensions on a single verb root, in the correct order, while keeping the sentence's participants clear.
  6. Maintain subject relative clauses, object relative clauses, and participial clauses already active from earlier chapters, now working alongside extension combinations.
  7. Sustain reference continuity across a full paragraph describing a relationship or a settled state, not only a single event.
  8. Read, listen to, write, and speak a short connected passage describing a relationship between people, or a state that a person or thing has settled into.

Communication Scenario

Lindiwe and Nomvula have kept talking after their earlier conversation about Lindiwe's grandfather. This time, Nomvula wants to know more about the people involved rather than the events themselves. How often do Lindiwe and her grandfather see one another now that he is stronger? Are the neighbors satisfied with how the garden turned out? Talking about relationships and settled states means naming who is involved with whom, and describing a condition someone has simply arrived at, rather than a single action someone performed.

  1. This chapter widens the formulaic set of reciprocal and neuter-stative verbs introduced in Chapter 5, so that learners can recognize and use more of these fixed items in context, still without analyzing how they are built.
  2. Learners also begin tracking valency directly, that is, how many participants a verb involves, and how the causative, applicative, and passive extensions already active from earlier chapters raise or lower that number when they combine on a single verb.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 5 introduced a first, small set of reciprocal and neuter-stative verbs as fixed vocabulary, and developed the causative, applicative, and passive extensions across a longer narrative. Chapter 6 widens that same formulaic set of reciprocal and neuter-stative verbs, and turns explicit attention to valency, so that learners can explain in plain terms what changes when an extension is added to a verb. The internal patterns that build new reciprocal and neuter-stative forms remain scheduled for later chapters, where they will be introduced for full productive use.

Coming From Chapter 5

The causative extension developed across a wider set of verb roots, change-of-state description, a first small set of formulaic reciprocal and neuter-stative verbs, the applicative extension for reason and instrument, and the passive extension across more verb roots.

New in This Chapter

A wider formulaic set of reciprocal and neuter-stative verbs, explicit valency tracking, extension combinations built in the correct order, and reference continuity across a paragraph about a relationship or a settled state.

Looking Forward

Later chapters in this volume introduce the internal -an- reciprocal pattern and the -ek- neuter-stative pattern for full productive use, once the discourse foundations built here are secure.

Pronunciation and Click Focus

This chapter continues nasalized click practice from earlier chapters, and adds attention to the rhythm of longer verbs built from two or three extensions, so that each extension stays clearly audible even as the whole word grows longer.

Pronunciation Focus — Rhythm across a three-extension verb

A verb carrying an applicative extension and a causative extension together, such as wangisizela, already stretches across several syllables. Practice it slowly, syllable by syllable, then build up speed while keeping the applicative -el- and the surrounding vowels clearly distinct, rather than letting them blur together at natural speed.

Grammar: Valency and Argument Structure

6.1 A wider formulaic set of reciprocal verbs

Purpose: recognize and use more reciprocal verbs as fixed vocabulary items, describing actions that two or more people do to or with one another.

Language Focus

Verbs such as ukuxhumana, to be in contact with one another, ukusizana, to help one another, and ukuthandana, to love one another, all describe a shared action between participants. Learners may use each of these as a whole, memorized item now. The -an- pattern that builds these forms from a verb root is not taught for production until a later chapter.

  • Mina nogogo sixhumana njalo ngocingo. My grandfather and I stay in contact regularly by phone. ukuxhumana, formulaic reciprocal, used as a whole item
  • Umama nomakhelwane basizana kaninginingi ngesivande. Mother and the neighbor help one another often with the garden. ukusizana, formulaic reciprocal, used as a whole item

6.2 A wider formulaic set of neuter-stative verbs

Purpose: recognize and use more neuter-stative verbs as fixed vocabulary items, describing a state or condition a person or thing has simply arrived at.

Language Focus

Verbs such as ukudideka, to be confused, and ukuvuleka, to be open, describe a condition rather than an action someone performs. Learners may use each of these as a whole, memorized item now. The -ek- pattern that builds these forms from a verb root is not taught for production until a later chapter.

  • Ngavuleka kabi umnyango, kwaze kwangena umoya omningi. The door came open badly, and a lot of wind got in. ukuvuleka, formulaic neuter-stative, used as a whole item
  • Ngadideka kancane ngesikhathi engichazela inqubo entsha. I was a little confused while he was explaining the new procedure to me. ukudideka, formulaic neuter-stative, used as a whole item

6.3 Tracking valency: how many participants does the verb involve?

Purpose: name, in plain terms, how many participants a verb takes, and explain how each active extension raises or lowers that number.

Language Building Block — Valency in plain terms
Base verb → one participant added by causative, applicative → one participant removed by passive
A plain verb such as ukukhula, to grow, involves one participant, the thing that grows. Adding the causative -is-, as in ukukhulisa, introduces a second participant, the one who causes the growth. Adding the applicative -el-, as in ukukhulisela, introduces a further participant, such as a reason or instrument. The passive -w- does the opposite: it removes the causer from the sentence, leaving only the process and its result.
ukukhula (1) → ukukhulisa (2) → ukukhulisela (2, plus reason/instrument) → ukukhuliswa (1, doer removed)
to grow → to make grow → to make grow using/for → to be made to grow
  • Isivande sikhulile. The garden has grown. (one participant: the garden) plain verb, one core participant
  • Umakhelwane wakhulisa isivande ngamanzi amaningi. The neighbor grew the garden using plenty of water. (three participants: neighbor, garden, water) causative plus applicative, participants added
  • Isivande sakhuliswa ngamanzi amaningi. The garden was made to grow using plenty of water. (the causer is removed; two participants remain) passive on a causative-applicative base, causer removed

6.4 Combining extensions in the correct order

Purpose: build a single verb carrying two or three extensions together, in the order the language requires, while keeping every participant clear.

Language Building Block — Extension order on one root
Verb root + causative -is- + applicative -el- + passive -w- + Final vowel
When more than one extension attaches to the same root, the causative and applicative extensions attach closer to the root, and the passive extension attaches last, just before the final vowel. This order stays constant, no matter how many participants the finished sentence names.
wangisizela → ukusizela + causative sense already built in by context; wasizelwa
helped me with it; was helped with it (doer left unstated)
  • Udokotela wamlulamisela ngemithi efanele. The doctor helped heal him using the correct medicine. causative -is- plus applicative -el-, three participants: doctor, patient, medicine
  • Wayelulanyiselwa ngemithi efanele nguDokotela uZondi. He was being helped to heal using the correct medicine, by Doctor Zondi. causative plus applicative plus passive, agent reintroduced with nguDokotela when needed for clarity

When a passive sentence still needs to name the agent for clarity, isiZulu reintroduces it with ngu- or ng- before the agent's name, rather than abandoning the passive form.

6.5 Reference continuity across a relationship or a settled state

Purpose: keep every reference clear across a full paragraph describing a relationship between people, or a state someone or something has settled into, not only a single event.

Language Focus

A paragraph about a relationship or a settled state usually names the participants once, then tracks them with subject concords, object concords, and words such as njengamanje, as things stand, before returning to the full noun only when a new participant enters, or when confusion might otherwise arise.

Model Dialogue

Isimo: UNomvula ubuza uLindiwe ngobudlelwane bakhe nogogo wakhe, nangesimo sikamakhelwane njengamanje.
Setting: Nomvula asks Lindiwe about her relationship with her grandfather, and about the neighbor's current state.

UNOMVULA Manje osekelamile ugogo wakho, nixhumana kanjani? Now that your grandfather has recovered, how do you two stay in contact?
ULINDIWE Sixhumana kabili ngeviki ngocingo, futhi sibonana njalo ngempelasonto. Sisizana kakhulu, mina ngimlekelela ngezinto ezinzima, yena angichazele izinto ezindala. We stay in contact twice a week by phone, and we see one another every weekend. We help one another a great deal, I assist him with heavy things, and he explains old things to me.
UNOMVULA Kuzwakala ubudlelwane obuhle kakhulu. Umakhelwane wakho onesivande, uneliseke yini njengamanje? That sounds like a very good relationship. Is your neighbor with the garden satisfied now, as things stand?
ULINDIWE Waneliseke kakhulu. Isivande sakhe salulanyiselwa ngamanzi amaningi nguye ngokwakhe, futhi manje singumthombo wemifino yomndeni wonke. He is very satisfied. His garden was helped along using plenty of water by him personally, and it is now a source of vegetables for the whole family.
UNOMVULA Kwake kwaba nesikhathi lapho nididekile khona, ningazi ukuthi senzenjani? Was there ever a time when you two were confused, not knowing what to do?
ULINDIWE Yebo, ekuqaleni sasididekile kakhulu, kodwa sasizana, saze savuleka ngempela isixazululo. Yes, at the beginning we were very confused, but we helped one another, until a solution finally opened up.

This exchange widens the formulaic reciprocal and neuter-stative set, names participants explicitly, and reactivates causative, applicative, and passive forms already active from Chapter 5.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Bangaki abahlanganyeli ababalulekile kule ngxoxo? (How many important participants are there in this conversation?)
  2. ULindiwe nogogo wakhe basizana kanjani? (How do Lindiwe and her grandfather help one another?)
  3. Yini eyenzeka ngesikhathi uLindiwe nogogo wakhe bedidekile? (What happened while Lindiwe and her grandfather were confused?)

Writing Workshop

Write a short account of a relationship or a settled state, drawing only on vocabulary and grammar already active from this chapter and earlier chapters.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write a description of eight to twelve sentences, including at least one formulaic reciprocal verb, one formulaic neuter-stative verb, one verb combining two extensions such as causative and applicative, and a clear statement of how many participants are involved in at least one sentence.

  1. Choose your relationship or state. In English, note who is involved, and what has settled into place between or within them.
  2. Name the participants. Introduce each person or thing once, in full, before referring to them with concords.
  3. Use a formulaic reciprocal verb. Describe an action the participants do to or with one another.
  4. Use a formulaic neuter-stative verb. Describe a state or condition someone or something has settled into.
  5. Combine two extensions on one verb. Use the causative and applicative, or the applicative and passive, together on a single root.
  6. Count the participants. In at least one sentence, briefly note in your own words how many participants that verb involves.
  7. Close with njengamanje. Use njengamanje, as things stand, to describe the present state of the relationship.
  8. Audit your draft. Confirm that every reciprocal and neuter-stative verb is used as a whole word, and that no new one has been built from a root.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • Every participant is introduced clearly before being referred to with a concord.
  • At least one formulaic reciprocal verb is used correctly as a whole word.
  • At least one formulaic neuter-stative verb is used correctly as a whole word.
  • At least one verb correctly combines two extensions already active from earlier chapters.
  • No new reciprocal or neuter-stative verb has been built from a root by analogy.
  • Standard isiZulu spelling, capitalization, and punctuation are used throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Naming mutual help explicitly

In many isiZulu speaking communities, a good relationship, whether between family, neighbors, or colleagues, is often described in terms of mutual help and mutual contact, rather than only in terms of affection. Notice how Lindiwe describes her relationship with her grandfather by naming what each of them does for the other.

Culture in Context — Neighborliness as an ongoing relationship

Neighborly relationships, such as the one between Mama and MaDlamini in the reading passage, are commonly described as lasting arrangements built over years, marked by regular contact and reciprocal help, rather than as occasional or incidental encounters.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Building a new reciprocal or neuter-stative verb by analogy

Error: Trying to create a new reciprocal or neuter-stative verb from a familiar root, by copying the ending of a word such as ukusizana or ukudideka.

Correction: Use only the reciprocal and neuter-stative verbs already introduced as fixed vocabulary items. Wait for the pattern to be introduced for production in a later chapter before building new ones.

Common Mistake — Losing track of participants in a combined-extension verb

Error: Adding a causative and an applicative extension to the same verb, but losing track of who or what each participant is, so the sentence becomes unclear.

Correction: Before writing or speaking a combined-extension verb, name each participant in your own mind first, in English if necessary, then build the isiZulu sentence around them.

Common Mistake — Reintroducing the agent incorrectly in a passive sentence

Error: Naming the agent of a passive verb without the correct nga-/ngu- form, so the sentence sounds unfinished or unclear.

Correction: When a passive sentence needs to name its agent for clarity, reintroduce that agent with ngu- before a name or noun, as in ngu-Dokotela uZondi, rather than adding the name directly after the verb.

Ukwenza Nokuguquka Kwesimo

Volume III, Chapter 5: Causative and Change-of-State Meaning · Speak and Write Zulu: Discourse Expansion

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use the causative extension across a wider range of verb roots, describing how one person or event brings about an action in another.
  2. Describe change of state, that is, a person or thing moving from one condition into another, using causative and neuter forms together.
  3. Recognize a small set of neuter-stative and reciprocal verb forms as formulaic vocabulary, without yet analyzing their internal structure.
  4. Expand the applicative extension to describe reason, instrument, and manner, alongside the beneficiary and goal meanings already active.
  5. Expand the passive extension across more verb roots, keeping the doer of an action unstated when it is unknown or unimportant.
  6. Combine subject relative clauses, object relative clauses, and participial clauses with causative and passive verbs in a single description.
  7. Maintain reference continuity across a longer stretch of narration, describing a process that unfolds over several connected sentences.
  8. Read, listen to, write, and speak a short connected passage that explains how a change came about, and who or what caused it.

Communication Scenario

A week after Lindiwe's visit to her grandfather, the same class wants to know what happened next. Her grandfather's leg, injured months earlier, is finally healing. The neighbor's small vegetable garden, once struggling, is now thriving. Nomvula wants to know exactly what made these changes happen, who did what, and what changed as a result. Telling this kind of story means moving beyond a single helpful action, toward a fuller account of cause, change, and outcome, stretched across several connected sentences.

  1. This chapter develops the causative extension already introduced in Chapter 4, and applies it specifically to describing influence, change, and process, rather than a single isolated event.
  2. Learners also meet a small, formulaic set of reciprocal and neuter-stative verbs for recognition and limited use, expand the applicative and passive extensions, and practice keeping a longer narrative clear from beginning to end.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 4 introduced the causative and passive extensions for a single event within a short exchange. Chapter 5 takes those same tools and stretches them across a longer, connected account of change over time, while giving learners their first formulaic taste of reciprocal and neuter-stative forms, which will not be analyzed in depth until Chapter 6.

Coming From Chapter 4

Subject and object relative clauses across several noun classes, participial clauses, the causative and passive extensions for a single event, and purpose subordination with ukuze.

New in This Chapter

Causative use across a wider set of verb roots, change of state description, a formulaic set of reciprocal and neuter-stative verbs, expanded applicative meanings, and reference continuity across a longer narrative.

Looking Forward

Chapter 6 develops the reciprocal and neuter-stative extensions for full productive use, alongside continued attention to valency and argument structure.

Pronunciation and Click Focus

This chapter continues the nasalized click work from Chapter 4, and adds attention to keeping the causative -is- and the passive -w- clearly and correctly pronounced when they combine with other extensions in the same verb.

Pronunciation Focus — Extensions stacked within one verb

When a causative or passive extension combines with an applicative extension, the verb becomes noticeably longer, and learners sometimes drop a syllable under pressure. Practice wangichazisela and wangichazelwa slowly, syllable by syllable, then at natural speed, making sure every extension is still audible once the pace increases.

Grammar: Causing and Changing

5.1 The causative extension, developed across more verb roots

Purpose: describe how one person, event, or condition brings about an action or a state in someone or something else, across a wider range of verbs than Chapter 4 introduced.

Language Focus

Chapter 4 introduced the causative on a small set of everyday verbs. This chapter extends the same -is- pattern to verbs describing healing, growth, learning, and emotional change, so that learners can describe influence over a process, not only over a single action.

Language Building Block — Causative across a process
Verb root + -is- variant + Final vowel, describing a caused process
The same causative pattern from Chapter 4 now attaches to verbs of healing, growth, and change, letting the subject of the sentence describe itself as the cause of someone else's improvement or transformation.
ukululama → ukululamisa; ukukhula → ukukhulisa
to heal → to make heal, to nurse back to health; to grow → to make grow, to raise
  • Udokotela wamlulamisa kancane kancane. The doctor made him heal little by little. -is- causative on ukululama, describing a gradual process
  • Umakhelwane wakhulisa imifino yakhe ngokunakekela okukhulu. The neighbor grew his vegetables with great care. -is- causative on ukukhula, combined with the instrumental applicative meaning below

5.2 Describing change of state

Purpose: describe a person or thing moving from one condition into another, using the perfect and the causative together.

Language Building Block — Change of state
Subject + perfect form of a change verb, often paired with a causative agent elsewhere in the sentence
A change-of-state description usually names the earlier condition, names the process or cause, and then reports the resulting condition using the perfect, so the listener follows the whole arc from before to after.
ubelimele, kodwa manje usesindile
he was injured, but now he has recovered
  • Umlenze kababa ubelimele kakhulu, kodwa manje usululamile ngenxa yokunakekelwa kukadokotela. My father's leg was badly injured, but now it has healed because of the doctor's care. perfect ubelimele, contrasted with perfect usululamile, cause named with ngenxa ya-
  • Isivande sikamakhelwane besingakhuli kahle, kodwa manje sesikhulile kakhulu. The neighbor's garden was not growing well, but now it has grown a great deal. negative perfect contrasted with affirmative perfect, describing change

5.3 Reciprocal and neuter-stative verbs, formulaic only

Purpose: recognize a small set of reciprocal and neuter-stative verbs as fixed vocabulary items, without yet analyzing the -an- or -ek- patterns that build them.

Language Focus

Words such as ukubonana, to see one another, and ukwaneliseka, to be satisfied, are useful now, and learners may use them as whole, memorized items. The internal patterns that build reciprocal and neuter-stative meaning are not taught for production until Chapter 6, so do not attempt to build new words of this type yet.

  • Sibonane ngempelasonto, sixoxa kabanzi ngengane zethu. We saw one another over the weekend, and talked at length about our children. ukubonana, formulaic reciprocal, used as a whole item
  • Ugogo wayeneliseke kakhulu ngosizo abelutholile. Grandfather was very satisfied with the help he had received. ukwaneliseka, formulaic neuter-stative, used as a whole item

5.4 The applicative extension, expanded to reason, instrument, and manner

Purpose: describe why, with what, or how an action was carried out, in addition to the beneficiary and goal meanings already active from earlier chapters.

Language Building Block — Applicative for reason and instrument
Verb root + -el- variant + Final vowel, with the reason or instrument named after the verb
The same -el- applicative pattern already active for beneficiary and goal meanings can also introduce a reason, an instrument, or a manner, without changing its form, only its surrounding context and the noun that follows it.
wamnakekela ngothando; wamsizela ngemali yakhe
she cared for him with love; she helped him with her own money
  • Udokotela wamlulamisela ngezidakamizwa ezifanele. The doctor helped heal him with the correct medicine. -el- applicative, instrumental meaning, combined with -is- causative
  • Umakhelwane wakhulisela imifino yakhe ngamanzi amaningi. The neighbor grew his vegetables using plenty of water. -el- applicative, instrumental meaning, combined with -is- causative

5.5 The passive extension, expanded across more verb roots

Purpose: describe a change or an outcome when the person responsible is unknown, unimportant, or intentionally left unstated, across a wider set of verbs than Chapter 4 introduced.

  • Umlenze wagqugquzelwa kaninginingi ngesikhathi sokululama. The leg was exercised many times during the recovery. -w- passive, doer left unstated, process framed as an outcome
  • Isivande sanakekelwa kahle, kwaze kwavela imiphumela emihle. The garden was cared for well, until good results appeared. -w- passive on ukunakekela, combined with a sequential clause

Compare these passive forms with the causative forms in section 5.1. The causative names a causer directly. The passive removes the causer from the sentence entirely, leaving only the process and its result.

5.6 Reference continuity across a longer narrative

Purpose: keep every reference clear across a full paragraph describing change over time, not only across two or three sentences.

Language Focus

A longer account of change usually names the person or thing once, tracks it through several sentences using subject concords, object concords, and demonstratives such as lowo, leyo, lelo, and only returns to the full noun when a new stage of the process begins, or when confusion might otherwise arise.

Model Dialogue

Isimo: UNomvula ubuza uLindiwe ngokuthi umlenze kagogo wakhe usululame kanjani.
Setting: Nomvula asks Lindiwe how her grandfather's leg has healed.

UNOMVULA Umlenze kagogo wakho ususilulame yini manje? Has your grandfather's leg healed now?
ULINDIWE Ubelimele kakhulu, kodwa manje usululamile kancane kancane. Udokotela owamnakekelayo wamlulamisela ngezidakamizwa ezifanele, wamgqugquzela ukuthi ahambe njalo. It was badly injured, but now it has healed little by little. The doctor who cared for him helped heal him with the correct medicine, and encouraged him to walk regularly.
UNOMVULA Kwathathe isikhathi esingakanani, ukuze aphile kahle kabusha? How long did it take for him to become well again?
ULINDIWE Izinyanga ezimbili, kodwa umphumela muhle kakhulu. Manje uyahamba yedwa, angathokoza ngaphandle kosizo. Two months, but the result is very good. Now he walks alone, and is glad without needing help.
UNOMVULA Nezitshalo zomakhelwane wakho, kade zibukeka zibi kakhulu. Zenzenjani manje? And your neighbor's plants, they used to look very bad. What has become of them now?
ULINDIWE Ubezingakhuli kahle, kodwa manje sezikhulile kakhulu. Wazinakekela ngamanzi amaningi nangothando, kwaze kwaba nemiphumela emihle. Isivande sonke sanakekelwa kahle, saguquka saba yisimo esimnandi ukusibuka. They were not growing well, but now they have grown a great deal. He cared for them with plenty of water and with love, until there were good results. The whole garden was cared for well, and it changed into a pleasant sight.

This exchange develops the causative and passive extensions across a longer stretch of narration, contrasts an earlier condition with a later one, and reactivates the applicative and relative structures already active from Chapter 4.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Kwathatha isikhathi esingakanani, ukuze umlenze kagogo kaLindiwe ululame? (How long did it take for Lindiwe's grandfather's leg to heal?)
  2. Umakhelwane wenzeni, ukuze isivande sakhe sikhule kahle? (What did the neighbor do, so that his garden would grow well?)
  3. Yiziphi izinto ezimbili ezashintsha kule ngxoxo? (What two things changed in this conversation?)

Read the following short passage, written by another learner, describing how a family member recovered from illness. It reactivates the causative, the passive, and the applicative extensions across a full paragraph. Read it aloud twice before answering.

Ukululama Kukamama — Mother's Recovery

Umama wami ubegula kabi ekuqaleni konyaka, futhi sonke sasikhathazekile ngaye. Udokotela owamhlolayo wamlulamisela ngemithi efanele, wamgqugquzela futhi ukuthi aphumule kahle. Writing Workshop

Write a short account of a change you witnessed or experienced, drawing only on vocabulary and grammar already active from this chapter and earlier chapters.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write a description of eight to twelve sentences, including at least one causative verb describing a process, one applicative verb with a reason or instrument meaning, one passive verb, and a clear contrast between an earlier condition and a later condition.

  1. Choose your change. In English, note what changed, who or what caused it, and over what period of time.
  2. Describe the earlier condition. Use the perfect or a negative perfect to establish how things were before.
  3. Name the cause. Use a causative verb to show who or what brought the change about.
  4. Add an applicative reason or instrument. Explain why the action was taken, or with what it was carried out.
  5. Add a passive verb. Describe part of the process without naming who did it.
  6. Describe the present condition. Use the perfect to show the result, and contrast it clearly with the earlier condition.
  7. Close with a reference back. Use kusukela lapho, or a demonstrative such as lowo, leyo, or lelo, to refer to your subject one final time.
  8. Audit your draft. Confirm that every causative and passive form is built on the correct verb root, and that your earlier and later conditions are clearly distinguished.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • An earlier condition is clearly established using the perfect or a negative perfect.
  • At least one causative verb correctly describes a process or a caused change.
  • At least one applicative verb correctly introduces a reason or an instrument.
  • At least one passive verb is used correctly, with the doer left unstated.
  • A later condition is clearly described, and the contrast with the earlier condition is easy to follow.
  • Standard isiZulu spelling, capitalization, and punctuation are used throughout.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: reading the model dialogue aloud

Read the model dialogue above aloud twice, once at a slow, careful pace, and once at a natural, connected pace. Pay particular attention to keeping every causative and passive verb clearly pronounced, even when it combines with an applicative extension.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: describing a change over time

With a partner or small group, describe a change you have witnessed, using at least one causative verb, one applicative verb, and one passive verb, and contrasting an earlier condition with a later one, following the pattern in the model dialogue.

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself describing the same change alone, as if updating a friend who has not seen the situation in some time. Listen back, checking that your earlier and later conditions are clearly distinguished, and that every causative and passive form agrees correctly.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Discussing illness and recovery with care

In many isiZulu speaking communities, discussion of illness or injury, especially involving an elder, is often paired with expressions of gratitude toward those who provided care, rather than dwelling only on the difficulty itself. Notice how Lindiwe balances the description of her grandfather's injury with warm acknowledgment of the doctor's help.

Culture in Context — Crediting effort and care in a shared outcome

When describing a positive change, such as a healed leg or a thriving garden, speakers commonly credit the people whose effort and care contributed to it, rather than describing the outcome as happening on its own. This pattern of shared credit is common in narration about recovery, growth, and community support.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using the passive when the causative is needed

Error: Describing an outcome with the passive extension when the sentence actually needs to name who or what caused the change.

Correction: Ask whether the sentence needs to credit a causer, which needs the causative, or whether the causer is genuinely unknown or unimportant, which needs the passive.

Common Mistake — Treating a formulaic reciprocal or neuter-stative verb as a productive pattern

Error: Trying to build a new reciprocal or neuter-stative verb by analogy with ukubonana or ukwaneliseka, before this pattern has been taught for production.

Correction: Use only the reciprocal and neuter-stative verbs already introduced as fixed vocabulary items, and wait for Chapter 6 before building new ones.

Common Mistake — Losing the contrast between earlier and later conditions

Error: Describing only the present condition of a person or thing, without first establishing what the earlier condition was.

Correction: Begin a change-of-state description with the earlier condition, using the perfect or a negative perfect, before introducing the cause and the resulting condition.

Ukubhala Imibiko Nezincazelo

Volume III, Chapter 18: Writing Reports and Explanations · Speak and Write Zulu: Discourse Expansion

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Organize a short written report into an introduction, a body of clearly labeled sections, and a conclusion.
  2. Use isihloko and isigaba productively as the framework for a piece of writing you compose yourself, not only for a text you read.
  3. Use signposting language, including okokuqala, okwesibili, okokugcina, and ngokulandelayo, to connect the sections of your own writing.
  4. Introduce a reason, cause, or result inside an explanation, using ngenxa yalokho and related connectors.
  5. Write a brief concluding statement that summarizes a report's main point, using isiphetho.
  6. Continue producing Standard isiZulu in a consistently formal register, applying kinship, clan, and respect vocabulary from Chapter 16 where relevant to the report's content.

Communication Scenario

Thandeka read the notice and the article that Nomvula gave her in Chapter 17. Now Nomvula has asked Thandeka to write a short report for family members who live far away and could not read the original documents themselves. The report must explain what the celebration is, when and where it will happen, and why the family considers it important, organized clearly enough that a reader can find any detail quickly.

  1. This chapter introduces writing a structured report as productive output, using the same organizational vocabulary, isihloko and isigaba, that Chapter 17 introduced for reading.
  2. Learners are not asked to produce new grammar systems in this chapter. Instead, they apply already active systems, including relative clauses, possessive concords, complement clauses, and the potential mood, to the specific task of composing an organized written text.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 17 taught learners to recognize isihloko, isigaba, and signposting language inside texts written by someone else. Chapter 18 asks learners to build that same structure themselves, moving from reading organization to producing it. The underlying grammar does not change, but the task now requires planning a text before writing it, rather than only interpreting one.

Coming From Chapter 17

Recognizing headings, sections, and signposting language, and separating a main idea from supporting details inside a text written by someone else.

New in This Chapter

Planning and writing an original report with its own isihloko, its own labeled isigaba sections, reason and result connectors, and a closing isiphetho.

Looking Forward

Pronunciation and Prosody Review

This chapter does not introduce a new sound. When reading a report aloud, a clear pause after the isingeniso, and again before the isiphetho, marks the boundaries of the text for a listener, in the same way that pausing between isigaba sections helped a listener in Chapter 17.

Pronunciation Focus — Marking the introduction and conclusion aloud

A short pause after the isingeniso signals that the body of the report is about to begin, and a similar pause before the isiphetho signals that the report is coming to a close. This pacing extends the same careful, formal pause pattern practiced for section boundaries in Chapter 17.

Grammar: Organizing a Written Report

18.1 Planning an introduction, a body, and a conclusion

Purpose: divide a short report into an isingeniso, several labeled isigaba sections, and an isiphetho, before writing any sentences.

Language Focus

An isingeniso states what the report is about, in one or two sentences, before any detail is given. Each isigaba then develops one part of that topic, labeled clearly, the same way the readings in Chapter 17 were labeled. An isiphetho closes the report with a short statement summarizing its main point, rather than introducing new information.

  • Isingeniso salo mbiko lichaza ukuthi umndeni uhlela umcimbi omkhulu. The introduction of this report explains that the family is planning a large celebration. possessive concord salo, complement clause with ukuthi from Chapter 8
  • Isiphetho sichaza ukuthi umcimbi lo ubaluleke kanjani emndenini. The conclusion explains how important this celebration is to the family. complement clause with ukuthi, reactivated adverb kanjani

18.2 Connecting a reason to a result

Purpose: state a reason using isizathu, then introduce the result that follows from it, using ngenxa yalokho.

Language Note — Reason plus result
isizathu + [reason clause], ngenxa yalokho + [result clause]
A useful explanatory habit is to state a reason first, then mark the result that follows from it using ngenxa yalokho, because of that. This pattern reuses the complement clause and relative clause systems already active, applied here to the specific task of explaining why something is done.
Isizathu esenza umndeni uhlele lo mcimbi ukubonga ukuzalwa komzukulu, ngenxa yalokho, wonke umuntu emndenini uyacelwa ukuba abekhona.
The reason the family is organizing this celebration is to give thanks for the birth of the grandchild, and because of that, everyone in the family is asked to be present.
  • Ngakho-ke, umndeni uzobonga ngezibongo zawo ngesikhathi somcimbi. And so, the family will give thanks with its praises during the celebration. connector ngakho-ke, reactivated possessive concord zawo

18.3 Naming each section as you write it

Purpose: use isigaba sokuqala, isigaba sesibili, and isigaba sokugcina as productive labels while composing an original report, applying the relative clauses already active from Chapter 4.

Language Focus

Naming a section as you write it, rather than only after reading it, helps a writer keep a report organized. A writer can open a section with a phrase such as isigaba sokuqala sikhuluma nga-, the first section discusses, followed by the topic of that section, using a relative clause where useful to add detail.

  • Isigaba sokuqala sikhuluma ngesizathu esenza lo mcimbi ubaluleke. The first section discusses the reason that makes this celebration important. relative clause esenza, reactivated from Chapter 4

Learners are asked to study a short model report below, then to plan and write their own report about Nomvula's celebration, using isingeniso, labeled isigaba sections, ngenxa yalokho, and isiphetho.

Model Text: A Short Report

Read the following short model report. It reuses the notice and article from Chapter 17, together with kinship and clan vocabulary from Chapter 16, organized into an introduction, three labeled sections, and a conclusion.

Umbiko: Umcimbi Womndeni KaNomvula

Isingeniso. Lo mbiko uchaza ngomcimbi omkhulu umndeni kaNomvula ohlela wona, ukubonga ukuzalwa komzukulu wabo omusha. Umbiko lona ubhalelwe amalungu omndeni angeke akwazi ukufika emcimbini ngokwawo.

Isigaba Sokuqala: Isizathu Somcimbi. Isizathu esenza umndeni uhlele lo mcimbi ukubonga ukuzalwa komzukulu, kanye nokuhlonipha izibongo zomndeni. Ngenxa yalokho, umndeni ubona kubalulekile ukuthi wonke umuntu abekhona lapho izibongo zibongwa.

Isigaba Sesibili: Isikhathi Nendawo. Umcimbi uzoba ngeSonto, emzini kaNomvula. Bonke abomndeni, kanye nabangani abasondelene nomndeni, bayamenywa. Ngakho-ke, kucelwa ukuthi wonke umuntu afike ngesikhathi.

Isigaba Sokugcina: Indima Yezibongo. Ngesikhathi somcimbi, umndeni uzobonga ngezibongo zawo, ukuhlonipha bonke abakhokho abaqala lo mndeni. Lokhu kwenzeka ngaphambi kokuthi abantu badle.

Isiphetho. Ngakho-ke, lo mcimbi ubaluleke kakhulu emndenini, ngoba uxhumanisa isizukulwane esisha nomlando wesibongo sawo.

Notice that each isigaba opens with its own label, and that ngenxa yalokho and ngakho-ke connect a stated reason to its result, in the same way the Language Note above described.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Isingeniso sichaza ukuthi umbiko lona ubhalelwe bani? (Whom does the introduction say this report is written for?)
  2. Ngokwesigaba sokuqala, yisiphi isizathu esenza umndeni uhlele umcimbi? (According to the first section, what reason leads the family to organize the celebration?)
  3. Isiphetho sisho ukuthini ngokubaluleka komcimbi? (What does the conclusion say about the importance of the celebration?)

Writing Workshop

Plan and write your own short report, drawing only on vocabulary and grammar already active from Chapters 1 through 18.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write a short report, six to eight sentences, in Standard isiZulu, about a family or community event you know, real or invented for practice. Include an isingeniso, at least two labeled isigaba sections, one sentence using ngenxa yalokho or ngakho-ke, and an isiphetho.

  1. Choose the event. Decide what the report will be about, and note it as your isihloko.
  2. Draft the isingeniso. Write one or two sentences stating what the report is about and whom it is for.
  3. Plan your isigaba sections. Decide what each section will cover before writing full sentences.
  4. Write the first isigaba. State its main idea, labeling it isigaba sokuqala.
  5. Write a second isigaba. State its main idea, labeling it isigaba sesibili, and connect a reason to a result using ngenxa yalokho or ngakho-ke.
  6. Write the isiphetho. Close the report with a summarizing statement, without introducing new information.
  7. Audit your draft. Confirm that each part of the report is labeled correctly, and that the register remains consistently formal.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • The report has a clear isingeniso, stating what it is about.
  • At least two isigaba sections are labeled and each states one main idea.
  • At least one sentence connects a reason to a result, using ngenxa yalokho or ngakho-ke.
  • The isiphetho summarizes the report without introducing new information.
  • Kinship, clan, or respect vocabulary from Chapter 16 is used correctly where relevant.
  • The register remains consistently formal throughout, and Standard isiZulu spelling and punctuation are used.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: reading the model report aloud

Read the model report aloud twice, pausing clearly at the isingeniso, each isigaba, and the isiphetho, matching the careful, formal pace practiced in Chapter 17.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: presenting your report

With a partner or small group, take turns presenting your written report aloud, section by section, without reading every word directly from the page.

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself presenting your report from beginning to end, in one to two minutes. Listen back, checking that each section is clearly signposted and that your register remains consistently formal.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Reports for family members far from home

A written report is a common way to share family or community news with relatives who live far away, and cannot attend an event in person. A clear, well organized report allows a reader who was not present to understand what happened and why it mattered.

Culture in Context — Explaining reasons respectfully

When a report explains a family's reasons for holding a celebration, it commonly connects the event to respect for ancestors, family history, and the clan name, rather than presenting the celebration only as a social gathering. This framing is often seen as showing appropriate respect for what the event represents.

Regional Variation — How reports are shared

Some families share written reports by letter or printed page, while others increasingly share similar information through messaging platforms or social media. The underlying organization, an introduction, clearly labeled points, and a conclusion, remains useful across these different media, though the specific channel varies by family and community.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Writing without planning the sections first

Error: Beginning to write full sentences before deciding what each isigaba will cover, which often produces a report with overlapping or missing information.

Correction: Decide the topic of each isigaba before writing any sentences, the same way a reader in Chapter 17 identified each section's main idea before summarizing it.

Common Mistake — Introducing new information in the isiphetho

Error: Adding a new fact or detail inside the isiphetho, as though it were another isigaba rather than a closing summary.

Correction: Reserve all new facts for the labeled isigaba sections, and use the isiphetho only to summarize the report's main point.

Common Mistake — Stating a result without marking it as a result

Error: Placing a reason and a result next to each other without a connector, leaving a reader uncertain whether the second sentence follows from the first.

Correction: Use ngenxa yalokho or ngakho-ke to mark the result clearly, the same way the model report connects a stated reason to what follows from it.

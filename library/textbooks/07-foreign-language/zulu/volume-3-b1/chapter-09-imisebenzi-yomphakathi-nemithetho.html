Imisebenzi Yomphakathi Nemithetho

Volume III, Chapter 9: Public Services, Rules, and Obligations · Speak and Write Zulu: Discourse Expansion

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Interpret spoken and written procedures, requirements, and rules in public-service settings, such as clinics, home affairs offices, and municipal offices.
  2. Express obligation, permission, and prohibition clearly, extending kufanele and related expressions from Chapter 8 to civic and institutional contexts.
  3. Use relative clauses, already active since Chapter 4, to describe documents, offices, requirements, and people involved in a public-service procedure.
  4. Continue combining the causative, applicative, and passive extensions on a single verb root, now inside formal notices and procedural explanations.
  5. Continue recognizing, receptively, the internal shape of the -an- reciprocal pattern and the -ek- neuter-stative pattern in a slightly wider set of formulaic verbs, without yet building new forms from an unfamiliar root.
  6. Ask for clarification politely when a rule or requirement is unclear, using question forms already active from earlier chapters.
  7. Read, listen to, write, and speak a short connected exchange in which a member of the public asks about a public-service requirement and a clerk or official responds.

Communication Scenario

Sipho, having finished the report discussed in the previous chapter, now needs to renew a document at the local Home Affairs office before he can complete an unrelated task for the community project. He arrives without knowing exactly which documents are required, and asks a clerk, Nkosazana Mthembu, to explain the requirements and the steps involved. Understanding rules, requirements, and obligations, and asking for clarification when they are unclear, are everyday skills in public offices, clinics, and municipal settings alike.

  1. This chapter introduces controlled ways to interpret and explain public-service requirements, while continuing the instruction, sequencing, and reporting language introduced in Chapter 8.
  2. Learners continue their receptive look at the -an- reciprocal pattern and the -ek- neuter-stative pattern, now noticing them inside a slightly wider set of formal and institutional vocabulary, while still treating them as formulaic whole words for production.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 8 introduced instructions with the imperative and subjunctive, sequencing a task with okokuqala, okwesibili, and okokugcina, and reporting on progress with the perfect, resultative, and progressive. Chapter 9 keeps all of that fully available, and turns it toward a new domain: public services, rules, and obligations, where the same instruction, sequencing, and reporting language is used to interpret institutional requirements rather than workplace tasks.

Coming From Chapter 8

Instructions using the imperative and subjunctive, sequencing language, reporting on progress, extension combinations on a single root, and continuing receptive recognition of the -an- and -ek- patterns.

New in This Chapter

Interpreting public-service requirements and rules, expressing obligation, permission, and prohibition in civic contexts, and asking for polite clarification when a requirement is unclear.

Pronunciation and Click Focus

This chapter continues nasalized click practice from Chapter 8, and adds attention to the rhythm of formal notice language, which is often read aloud slowly and clearly in public offices. Practice reading a short rule or requirement the way a clerk might read it aloud to a member of the public, keeping each phrase distinct rather than running the whole sentence together.

Pronunciation Focus — Reading formal notices clearly

Say each clause of a formal notice as its own short unit, pausing briefly at commas and full stops, and giving the penultimate syllable of the final word in each clause its natural length, so that a listener can follow each requirement in turn.

Grammar: Requirements, Permissions, and Obligations

9.1 Stating a requirement with kudingeka and isidingo

Purpose: state what a procedure requires, using kudingeka, it is required, alongside kufanele, already active from Chapter 8.

Language Focus

Kudingeka, it is required, behaves like kufanele, and is followed by the subjunctive when a specific person must carry out the requirement, or by an infinitive phrase when the requirement is stated generally. Kudingeka is slightly more formal in tone than kufanele, and is common in public-office and institutional language.

  • Kudingeka ulethe umazisi wakho nesitifiketi sokuzalwa. You are required to bring your identity document and birth certificate. kudingeka plus subjunctive, specific addressee
  • Isidingo esisha yileso sokuletha inombolo yesihloko sakho. The new requirement is that you bring your reference number. isidingo, requirement, as noun subject

9.2 Permission and prohibition with kuvunyelwe and ukwenqabela

Purpose: state what is permitted and what is prohibited, extending the passive of vumela, already active from Chapter 8, alongside the new verb ukwenqabela.

  • Kuvunyelwe ukungenisa isicelo ngeposi noma ngokuya ehhovisi. It is permitted to submit the application by post or by visiting the office. kuvunyelwe, passive, plus infinitive subject
  • Umthetho wenqabela ukuqhamuka ehhovisi ngaphandle kwesitifiketi. The rule prohibits arriving at the office without a certificate. umthetho as subject, wenqabela, verbal predicate

9.3 Relative clauses describing documents, offices, and requirements

Purpose: continue using relative clauses, active since Chapter 4 and developed through Chapters 6 to 8, now to describe public-service documents, offices, and requirements.

Language Building Block — Relative clauses in institutional description
Noun + relative concord + verb/adjective stem
A relative clause identifies which document, office, or requirement is meant, using a relative concord that agrees with the noun class of the noun being described, in the same pattern already used in Chapter 8 to describe indlela echaziwe, the way explained.
iphepha eliphelele → iphepha elicelwayo
a complete document → the document that is being requested
  • Iphepha elicelwayo alikho ehhovisi lakithi. The document that is being requested is not available at our local office. relative concord eli-, agreeing with iphepha, class 5
  • Isidingo esingafinyelelwa kalula kufanele sichazwe kahle. A requirement that cannot easily be met must be explained well. negative relative esingafinyelelwa, plus passive predicate sichazwe

9.4 Extension combinations inside formal notices

Purpose: continue combining the causative, applicative, and passive extensions on a single root, now inside formal notices and institutional explanations.

  • Isicelo esingafakwanga ngesikhathi asikwazi ukucutshungulwa. An application that has not been submitted on time cannot be processed. negative passive esingafakwanga, plus passive kucutshungulwa
  • Umqondisi wehhovisi ubasizile abantu abadidekile ngokubachazela umthetho kahle. The office manager helped confused people by explaining the rule to them well. applicative ubachazela, reused pattern from wangiyalela, Chapter 8

Kucutshungulwa, to be processed, combines the passive with an applicative-derived root, in the same way as kwaqhathaniswe in Chapter 8. Learners may recognize the shared pattern, but should continue treating both forms as reliable whole words.

9.5 Continuing to look inside: -an- and -ek- in institutional verbs

Purpose: continue recognizing, receptively only, the -an- reciprocal pattern and the -ek- neuter-stative pattern, now inside a slightly wider set of formal and institutional vocabulary, without yet producing new forms.

Language Building Block — Looking inside institutional verbs, for recognition only
Root + -ek- + Final vowel
Ukufinyelela, to reach or to access, is a familiar applicative-based verb rather than an -ek- form. However, its negative relative form, esingafinyelelwa, shown above, contains the passive -w-, not -ek-. A true -ek- example already familiar to learners is ukwenzeka, to happen, from Chapter 8. Learners are still not asked to build new -ek- forms from an unfamiliar root, including institutional roots such as -cutshungulwa- or -finyelela-.
enza + ek + a → enzeka
to do, to make → to happen, to occur

This box remains recognition-only. No exercise in this chapter requires learners to build a new -an- or -ek- form from an unfamiliar root, including any of the new public-service vocabulary introduced in this chapter.

9.6 Asking for clarification politely

Purpose: ask politely for clarification when a rule or requirement is unclear, using question forms already active from earlier volumes, together with ake, already active from Chapter 8.

  • Ake ungichazele kabanzi ngalesi sidingo, ngicela? Please could you explain this requirement to me in more detail? ake plus applicative ngichazele, polite clarification request
  • Ngingawuqeda nini lo msebenzi, uma isitifiketi sami singekafiki? When may I finish this task, if my certificate has not yet arrived? question word nini, plus conditional clause using negative -nge-ka- pattern already active

Model Dialogue

Isimo: USipho ufike ehhovisi lakwaHomu Afeya, futhi ubuza uNkosazana Mthembu ngezidingo zesicelo sakhe.
Setting: Sipho has arrived at the Home Affairs office, and asks Nkosazana Mthembu about the requirements for his application.

USIPHO Sawubona, ngingathanda ukuvuselela umazisi wami. Ngicela ungichazele ukuthi yiziphi izidingo. Hello, I would like to renew my identity document. Please explain to me what the requirements are.
UNKOSAZANA Sawubona. Kudingeka ulethe isitifiketi sokuzalwa, kanye nesithombe esisha esilandelayo imigomo yehhovisi. Hello. You are required to bring your birth certificate, along with a new photo that follows the office's guidelines.
USIPHO Ake ungichazele kabanzi ngalesi sithombe, ngicela. Kufanele sibe sinjani? Please could you explain this photo to me in more detail. What must it be like?
UNKOSAZANA Kuvunyelwe isithombe esithathwe kule nyanga kuphela, futhi umthetho wenqabela izithombe ezithathwe ngefoni ngaphandle kwemvume. Only a photo taken this month is permitted, and the rule prohibits photos taken with a phone without permission.
USIPHO Kulungile, ngiyabonga. Ngingawuqeda nini umsebenzi, uma isitifiketi sami sokuzalwa singekafiki? Alright, thank you. When may I finish this process, if my birth certificate has not yet arrived?
UNKOSAZANA Isicelo esingafakwanga ngaphandle kwesitifiketi asikwazi ukucutshungulwa, ngakho kufanele ulinde size sifike. An application submitted without the certificate cannot be processed, so you must wait until it arrives.
USIPHO Ngiyezwa. Ngicabanga ukuthi lo mthetho ubalulekile, ngoba usiza ukugcina ulwazi lwabantu luphephile. I understand. I think this rule is important, because it helps keep people's information safe.
UNKOSAZANA Ngiyavumelana nawe. Uma singabachazeli kahle abantu izidingo, singabadidekisa, futhi lokho kubalibazisela isikhathi eside. I agree with you. If we do not explain the requirements to people well, we might confuse them, and that delays them for a long time.

This exchange introduces requirements, permission, and prohibition language, while reactivating the polite clarification, opinion, and extension-combination patterns from Chapter 8.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Yiziphi izidingo ezimbili uNkosazana Mthembu azichazela uSipho? (What two requirements does Nkosazana Mthembu explain to Sipho?)
  2. Kungani uSipho engakawenzi umsebenzi wakhe manje? (Why can Sipho not complete his task right now?)
  3. UNkosazana Mthembu ucabanga ukuthini ngokuchazela abantu izidingo kahle? (What does Nkosazana Mthembu think about explaining requirements to people well?)

Reading Practice

Read the following short passage, written by another learner, describing a visit to a clinic. It reactivates the causative, applicative, and passive extensions, and introduces the requirement, permission, and prohibition language from this chapter across a full paragraph.

Ukuvakashela Umtholampilo — A Visit to the Clinic

Ngesonto eledlule, ngaya emtholampilo waseduze nendawo engihlala kuyo, ngoba ngangidinga ukuhlolwa. Ngaphambi kokungena, kudingeka ubhalise igama lakho kumklala osesangweni. Isidingo esibalulekile ukuletha ikhadi lomtholampilo, kodwa uma ungenalo, uklala angakusiza ngenye indlela. Umthetho wenqabela ukungena ehhovisi likadokotela ngaphandle kokubizwa igama lakho, ngakho kwakufanele ngilinde ngize ngibizwe. Ngesikhathi ngilinde, ngaqaphela isaziso esithi izisulu zomkhuhlane kudingeka zihlukaniswe nabanye abantu, ukuze bangatheleleki. Ngicabanga ukuthi lo mthetho ubalulekile kakhulu, ngoba usiza ukuvikela impilo yomphakathi wonke. Ngemva kwesikhathi, ikhadi lami lalitholwa uklala futhi ngahlolwa idokotela ngokushesha. Namuhla, sengiyaqonda kangcono ukuthi izidingo zomtholampilo zikhona ngenxa yani.

"kudingeka zihlukaniswe" combines a passive with an applicative-derived root, familiar in shape from kucutshungulwa in this chapter. "ngangidinga" is a plain past-progressive form of -dinga-, to need, reused from earlier volumes.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Yiziphi izidingo ezimbili ezichazwe kule ndaba? (What two requirements are described in this passage?)

  3. Ngokubona kwakho, kungani kubalulekile ukuhlukanisa izisulu zomkhuhlane nabanye abantu? (In your view, why is it important to separate people with an illness from others?)

Writing Workshop

Write a short account of a public-service visit, its requirements, and any permissions or prohibitions involved, drawing only on vocabulary and grammar already active from this chapter and earlier chapters.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write a description of eight to twelve sentences, including at least two requirements introduced with kudingeka or kufanele, one statement of permission using kuvunyelwe, one statement of prohibition using ukwenqabela, one relative clause describing a document or office, and one verb combining two extensions.

  1. Choose your setting. In English, note a public office, clinic, or municipal service, and what you needed there.
  2. State the first requirement. Use kudingeka or kufanele with the subjunctive.
  3. State a permission. Use kuvunyelwe to describe what is allowed.
  4. State a prohibition. Use ukwenqabela to describe what is not allowed.
  5. Describe a document or office. Use a relative clause with the correct relative concord.
  6. Combine two extensions. Use the causative and applicative, or the applicative and passive, together on a single root.
  7. Audit your draft. Confirm that every reciprocal or neuter-stative verb is used as a whole word, and that no new -an- or -ek- form has been built from an unfamiliar root.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • At least two requirements are stated clearly using kudingeka or kufanele.
  • At least one permission is stated correctly using kuvunyelwe.
  • At least one prohibition is stated correctly using ukwenqabela.
  • At least one relative clause correctly describes a document, office, or requirement.
  • At least one verb correctly combines two extensions already active from earlier chapters.
  • No new reciprocal or neuter-stative verb has been built from an unfamiliar root by analogy.
  • Standard isiZulu spelling, capitalization, and punctuation are used throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Patience and queueing in public offices

In many South African public offices and clinics, waiting in a queue and being called by name or number is a common part of the visit, and patience is generally valued alongside asking clear, polite questions when a requirement is unclear. This pattern varies by office, region, and the specific service being requested.

Culture in Context — Explaining rules with care

Clerks and officials in many settings are commonly expected to explain requirements clearly and patiently, particularly to members of the public who may be unfamiliar with a procedure, reflecting the broader value placed on clear communication discussed in Chapter 8.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing kuvunyelwe and kudingeka

Error: Using kudingeka, it is required, to describe something that is merely permitted, or using kuvunyelwe, it is permitted, to describe something that is compulsory.

Correction: Use kudingeka or kufanele only for genuine requirements, and kuvunyelwe only for optional permissions. Check the intended meaning carefully before choosing between them.

Common Mistake — Omitting the relative concord in institutional description

Error: Describing a document or office without the correct relative concord, producing a sentence that sounds like two separate statements rather than one description.

Correction: Choose the relative concord that agrees with the noun class of the item being described, as in iphepha elicelwayo, following the pattern already practiced since Chapter 4.

Common Mistake — Building a new -an- or -ek- form from an unfamiliar root

Error: Attaching -an- or -ek- to a new institutional root, such as -cutshungulwa- or -finyelela-, by analogy with ukuvumelana, ukudideka, or ukwenzeka, before this pattern has been introduced for production.

Correction: Continue using only the reciprocal and neuter-stative verbs already introduced as fixed vocabulary items. Wait for the -an- and -ek- patterns to be introduced for production in a later chapter.

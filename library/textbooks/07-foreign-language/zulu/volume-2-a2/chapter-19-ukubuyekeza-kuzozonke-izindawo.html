Ukubuyekeza Kuzozonke Izindawo

Volume II, Chapter 19: Review Across Domains · Speak and Write Zulu: Practical Independence

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use noun classes, subject concords, object concords, possessives, demonstratives, and adjectival concords together with increasing independence, across the domains covered in Chapters 1 through 18.
  2. Control the verb template, including tense, aspect, mood, polarity, and object concord placement, in connected speech and writing.
  3. Use applicative and passive extensions accurately in familiar, controlled contexts.
  4. Use basic relative clauses to identify and describe people, places, and things.
  5. Use locative expressions to describe places, routes, and institutional spaces.
  6. Recognize and apply the culturally governed speech routines introduced across the volume, including greetings, respect language, kinship terms, and proverbs.
  7. Complete an integrated review task drawing on family, work, transactions, health, narration, and culture, in preparation for the Volume II capstone.

Communication Scenario

A month has passed since Sipho's brother recovered. Sipho's isiZulu class has asked him to prepare a short account of what happened, drawing on everything the class has learned this term. As he plans his account, Sipho realizes he needs to describe his family, explain what happened at the clinic, narrate the sequence of events, mention where things took place, quote what people said, and close with the proverb his grandmother offered. He is no longer relying on any single chapter. He is drawing on all of them together.

  1. Before you begin, look back briefly through Chapters 1 through 18. Notice which systems you feel confident using, and which ones you would like to review more carefully in this chapter.
  2. This chapter does not introduce new grammar. It brings together the noun class, concord, verb, locative, relative, and cultural systems from the whole volume, so that you can use them together in one connected account.

Instead, it draws together everything active by Chapter 18, so that Chapter 20 can assess integrated, independent performance across listening, speaking, reading, and writing.

Grammar: Consolidated Systems

19.1 Concord control across the noun phrase

Purpose: use subject concords, object concords, possessives, demonstratives, and adjectival concords together, accurately, in one connected sentence.

By this point in the volume, you have practiced each concord type separately. This chapter asks you to combine them, so that a single sentence carries correct agreement across several elements at once.

Language Focus

No new concord type appears here. The goal is fluency in combining concords you already control, rather than learning a new pattern.

  • Umama wami omdala wamsiza kahle umfowethu ogulayo. My elderly mother helped my sick brother well. possessive, adjectival concord, object concord, and relative concord, in one sentence
  • Sahlala kuleyo ndlu enkulu eseduze nesibhedlela. We stayed in that big house near the hospital. demonstrative, adjectival concord, and locative reference, combined

19.2 Verb-template and TAM control in narration

Purpose: choose the correct tense, aspect, and polarity marker for a given point in a connected story, and place object concords correctly within the verb.

Language Building Block — The full verb template, reviewed
Subject concord + TAM marker + (object concord) + verb root + (extension) + final vowel
Every verb you have produced this volume fits this template. Reviewing it as a whole, rather than tense by tense, helps you build new sentences confidently in the capstone.
Wamenzela ukudla ngoba wayekhathele.
She made food for him, because he was tired.
  • Angikaze ngimbone umuntu obekezela kanjalo. I have never seen a person who endures like that. negative perfect, object concord, and relative clause, combined

19.3 Applicative and passive extensions in review

Purpose: recognize when to use the applicative extension for a beneficiary or reason, and when to use the passive to background an agent.

  • Udokotela wamsizela ngemithi efanele. The doctor helped him with the right medicine. applicative extension, beneficiary marked on the verb
  • Umfowethu wanakekelwa kahle esibhedlela. My brother was looked after well at the hospital. passive extension, agent backgrounded

19.4 Relative clauses and locatives together

Purpose: use a relative clause to identify a place, and a locative expression to say where an event happened.

  • Sibonene esibhedlela esiseduze nasekhaya. We met each other at the hospital that is near home. locative reference, combined with a relative clause describing the place

Model Dialogue

Isimo: USipho ulungiselela ukwethula indaba yakhe kwikilasi lakhe lesiZulu, exoxa nomngane wakhe uLindiwe ngalokho azokusho.
Setting: Sipho prepares to present his account to his isiZulu class, talking it through with his friend Lindiwe.

ULINDIWE Uzoqala kanjani indaba yakho? How will you start your account?
USIPHO Ngizoqala ngokuchaza umndeni wami omncane, base ngichaza indawo esihlala kuyo, eseduze nesibhedlela esikhulu. I'll start by describing my small family, then describe the place we live, near a large hospital.
ULINDIWE Ubani owasizayo, ngesikhathi umfowenu egula? Who was the one who helped, when your brother was sick?
USIPHO UThandeka wamsizela ngokushayela udokotela, base ekugcineni umfowethu wanakekelwa kahle esibhedlela. Thandeka helped him by calling the doctor, and in the end my brother was looked after well at the hospital.
ULINDIWE Uzokuvala kanjani indaba yakho? How will you close your account?
USIPHO Ngizoyivala ngesaga esithi, ukubekezela kuyanqoba, ngoba yiso esichaza kahle konke okwenzekile. I'll close it with the proverb, patience conquers, because that one describes well everything that happened.

This dialogue combines possessives, locatives, applicative and passive verbs, a relative clause, and the closing proverb from Chapter 18, in one connected exchange.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. USipho uzoqala kanjani indaba yakhe? (How will Sipho start his account?)
  2. Ubani owamsizela udokotela? (Who helped him by calling the doctor?)
  3. Uzoyivala ngani indaba yakhe? (What will he close his account with?)

Writing Workshop

In this chapter, you will write a short, connected account that combines family description, locative reference, at least one applicative or passive verb, a relative clause, and a closing proverb, in preparation for the Volume II capstone.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write a short account of your own, or an invented family's, experience of helping one another through a difficulty, drawing on the vocabulary, grammar, and cultural patterns reviewed in this chapter.

  1. Plan your account. In English, note the family members involved, the place, the problem, and who helped whom.
  2. Describe the family and place. Use possessives, adjectives, and a locative expression to set the scene.
  3. Narrate the help given. Use at least one applicative verb, for helping someone with or for something, and one passive verb, to background who did the action.
  4. Identify someone with a relative clause. Describe a person or place using a relative clause from Chapter 11.
  5. Close with a proverb. Choose a proverb from Chapter 18 that fits the lesson of your account.
  6. Audit your draft. Confirm that your concords, verb template, locatives, and relative clause are used correctly, and that the account reads as one connected passage, not a list of separate sentences.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • The account describes family and place with correct concord agreement.
  • At least one applicative verb and one passive verb are used correctly.
  • A relative clause identifies a person or place appropriately.
  • A fitting proverb closes the account.
  • Standard isiZulu spelling, capitalization, and punctuation are used throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Bringing systems together in real accounts

Real isiZulu communication rarely isolates one grammar point at a time. A single family account, like Sipho's, naturally calls on concord agreement, verb morphology, locatives, relative clauses, and a closing proverb, all working together. This chapter's review reflects that reality, rather than treating each system as a separate, disconnected topic.

Culture in Context — Respect and register across the volume

Across Chapters 1 through 18, respectful address, kinship terms, and appropriate register have appeared consistently, from greetings in early chapters to the proverb exchange with a grandmother in Chapter 18. Reviewing these systems together highlights how respect is maintained across many different kinds of interaction, not only in a single greetings lesson.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Losing concord agreement across a longer sentence

Error: Getting the first concord in a sentence correct, but losing agreement by the time a possessive, adjective, or demonstrative appears later in the same sentence.

Correction: Identify the noun class first, then check every concord in the sentence against that same class, for example umama wami omdala, my elderly mother, where wami and omdala both agree with umama.

Common Mistake — Confusing applicative and passive meaning

Error: Using a passive verb where an applicative meaning, doing something for or because of someone, is intended, or the reverse.

Correction: Use the applicative when a beneficiary or reason is added, for example wamsizela, helped for him, and the passive when the agent is backgrounded, for example wanakekelwa, was looked after.

Common Mistake — Overloading a single sentence with every system at once

Error: Trying to fit a possessive, a relative clause, an extension, and a locative into one sentence that becomes difficult to follow.

Correction: Spread these systems naturally across two or three connected sentences, as in the model dialogue and reading passage, rather than forcing every system into a single line.

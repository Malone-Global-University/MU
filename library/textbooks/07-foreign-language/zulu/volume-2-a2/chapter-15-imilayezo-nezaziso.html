Imilayezo Nezaziso

Volume II, Chapter 15: Media, Messages, and Announcements · Speak and Write Zulu: Practical Independence

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Read short public notices, announcements, and messages, and identify their main purpose.
  2. Recognize passive verbs, from Chapter 14, as they appear naturally in public and media texts.
  3. Listen to short announcements and radio-style items, and extract key information such as time, place, and what has happened.
  4. Understand a short written message, such as a note or a text message, and respond to it appropriately.
  5. Identify the difference between a formal public notice and an informal personal message, and choose language suited to each.
  6. Write a short notice or message of your own, using vocabulary and grammar already under your control.
  7. Connect the passive forms from Chapter 14, and the applicative forms from Chapter 13, to authentic-style public communication.

Communication Scenario

A few days after the visit described in Chapter 14, Thandeka checks the community noticeboard near her home, and Nomvula sends her a short message on her phone. Between the printed notice on the wall and the message on the screen, Thandeka has to work out what is being announced, what has already happened, and what she still needs to do. Public and personal texts like these are usually short, but they pack a great deal of meaning into a few sentences, and much of that meaning depends on the passive forms you have just learned.

  1. Before you begin, think, in English, about the last public notice or text message you read quickly, perhaps a sign about a delayed train, or a note left on a door. Notice how little language such notices actually use, and how much they still manage to communicate.
  2. Look back briefly at the passive verbs from Chapter 14, thengwa, phekwa, bhalwa, and enziwa. This chapter shows you how the same passive pattern extends easily to new, everyday verbs that appear often in public notices.

Pronunciation Review

No new click series is introduced in this chapter. This chapter reviews the plain and aspirated click series together, c, q, x, ch, qh, and xh, since these appear often in the names of places, offices, and institutions mentioned in public notices.

Pronunciation Focus — Reading notices aloud at a natural pace

Public notices are often read aloud quickly, over a loudspeaker or on the radio, so the click consonants and the passive -w- can pass by fast. Practice reading each notice in this chapter aloud twice, once slowly, checking every click and every -w-, and once at a faster, more natural pace, the way an announcer might read it.

Grammar: Media, Messages, and Announcements

15.1 Extending the passive pattern to new verbs

Purpose: recognize and use the same passive pattern from Chapter 14 on a small new set of verbs common in public notices.

Public notices describe results, a meeting has been arranged, a time has been changed, a road has been closed, far more often than they describe who did the arranging, changing, or closing. The passive pattern you learned in Chapter 14 extends directly to these new verbs, adding -w- before the final vowel, exactly as before.

Language Focus

The same -w- pattern applies to new roots. Compare vala, to close, with valwa, to be closed. Hlela, to arrange, becomes hlelwa, to be arranged. Shintsha, to change, becomes shintshwa, to be changed. Misa, to stop or postpone, becomes miswa, to be postponed.

Plain verbMeaningPassive formMeaning
valato closevalwato be closed
hlelato arrangehlelwato be arranged
shintshato changeshintshwato be changed

These four verbs are recognition and controlled production items in this chapter, since the underlying passive pattern is already active from Chapter 14. Building fully independent passive forms from any verb root remains a later-chapter target.

15.2 Reading public notices for key information

Purpose: pull out the most important facts from a short public notice, without needing to understand every word.

A public notice usually answers a small number of questions, what has happened or will happen, when, and where. Reading strategically means looking first for these anchors, rather than trying to translate the notice word by word.

Language Building Block — Reading a notice in three questions
Kwenzekani? + Nini? + Kuphi?
Ask yourself, in order, what has happened or what will happen, when it happens, and where it happens. A notice built around a passive verb usually answers the first question directly, in the verb itself.
Umhlangano usumiswe kuze kube ngoLwesihlanu.
The meeting has now been postponed until Friday.

Notices and messages also frequently use the reason clauses from Chapter 12 to explain why something has changed.

  • Isikole sivaliwe namuhla, ngoba kunemvula enkulu. The school is closed today, because there is heavy rain. Passive valiwe (resultative) + ngoba reason clause
  • Isikhathi somhlangano sesishintshiwe, kusuka ekuseni kuya emini. The meeting time has been changed, from the morning to midday. Passive shintshiwe (resultative)

15.3 Formal notices and informal messages compared

Purpose: notice how the same passive grammar appears in both a formal public notice and an informal personal message, while the surrounding language changes with the setting.

A formal notice on a board or in a newspaper tends to use complete sentences, a clear opening such as isaziso, and no direct address to a particular person. An informal message to a friend or family member is shorter, may drop some grammatical words, and often opens with a greeting or the person's name.

  • Isaziso: Umhlangano wesikole usumiswe kuze kube ngoLwesihlanu. Notice: The school meeting has now been postponed until Friday. Formal register, no direct address
  • Sawubona Thandeka, umhlangano usumisiwe, size ngoLwesihlanu. Hello Thandeka, the meeting has been postponed, we'll go on Friday. Informal register, direct address and greeting

Both sentences use the same passive verb, miswa, but the formal notice states the fact plainly, while the informal message speaks directly to a known person.

Model Dialogue

Isimo: UThandeka usebona isaziso ebhodini lasendaweni, futhi ubuye athole umlayezo kaNomvula ocingweni lwakhe.
Setting: Thandeka reads a notice on the community board, and then finds a message from Nomvula on her phone.

UTHANDEKA Nangu isaziso, kuthiwa umhlangano wesikole usumiswe. Angazi ukuthi kungani. Here's a notice, it says the school meeting has now been postponed. I don't know why.
Ucingo lukaThandeka luyakhala, futhi ubona umlayezo omusha ovela kuNomvula.
Thandeka's phone rings, and she sees a new message from Nomvula.
UMLAYEZO Sawubona Thandeka, umhlangano usumisiwe ngoba kunemvula enkulu. Sizohlangana ngoLwesihlanu esikhundleni salokho. Hello Thandeka, the meeting has now been postponed because there is heavy rain. We'll meet on Friday instead.
UTHANDEKA Ngiyabonga ngomlayezo, Nomvula. Manje sengiyazi ukuthi kungani, futhi ngiyazi nosuku olusha. Thank you for the message, Nomvula. Now I know why, and I also know the new day.
UThandeka ubhala impendulo emfushane.
Thandeka writes a short reply.
UMLAYEZO Kulungile, ngizobe ngikhona ngoLwesihlanu. Ngiyabonga ngokungitshela. Alright, I will be there on Friday. Thank you for telling me.

This dialogue moves between a formal public notice and an informal text message, and combines the passive forms miswa and shintshwa with a reason clause and a plan for the future.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Kwenzekeni kumhlangano wesikole? (What happened to the school meeting?)
  2. Kungani umhlangano umisiwe? (Why has the meeting been postponed?)
  3. Bazohlangana nini esikhundleni salokho? (When will they meet instead?)

Reading

Read the following notice and message, which show the same information presented in a formal and an informal way. Read each one aloud twice before answering the comprehension questions.

Isaziso Esisemthethweni — Formal Notice
Isaziso: Umtapo wolwazi wasendaweni uzovalwa ngoLwesibili, ngenxa yokuthi izinsimbi zikagesi sezihlelwa kabusha. Uzovulwa futhi ngoLwesithathu, ehoreni lesikhombisa ekuseni.
Notice: The local library will be closed on Tuesday, because the electrical wiring is being rearranged. It will open again on Wednesday, at seven o'clock in the morning.
Umlayezo — Text message, from Nomvula to Thandeka
Sawubona Thandeka, ungayi emtapeni wolwazi kusasa, uvaliwe ngenxa yezinsimbi zikagesi. Kulungile ukuya ngoLwesithathu ekuseni.
Hello Thandeka, don't go to the library tomorrow, it's closed because of the electrical wiring. It's fine to go on Wednesday morning.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Kungani umtapo wolwazi uzovalwa? (Why will the library be closed?)
  2. Uzovulwa nini futhi? (When will it open again?)
  3. Yiziphi izinto ezifanayo phakathi kwesaziso nomlayezo, futhi yiziphi izinto ezahlukene? (What is the same between the notice and the message, and what is different?)

Writing Workshop

In this chapter, you will write a short public notice and a short informal message that convey the same information, using at least one passive verb from Chapter 14 or from this chapter.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Choose an everyday change, a meeting time that has changed, a place that has closed, or an event that has been postponed, real or invented. Write a formal notice of three to four sentences, and then a short informal message of two to three sentences, describing the same change to a friend.

  1. Choose your change. In English, note what has happened, when, and why.
  2. Write the formal notice. Begin with isaziso, use a passive verb such as valiwe, shintshiwe, or misiwe, and avoid addressing a particular person directly.
  3. Write the informal message. Open with a greeting or a name, use the same passive verb, and add a brief personal note.
  4. Add a reason. Use a ngoba clause from Chapter 12 in at least one of the two texts.
  5. Audit your draft. Confirm that each passive verb keeps its -w- clearly, and that the formal and informal versions differ in tone, not only in length.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • At least one passive verb is used correctly in both texts.
  • The formal notice avoids direct address to a specific person.
  • The informal message includes a greeting or a name.
  • At least one ngoba reason clause is included.
  • Standard isiZulu spelling, capitalization, and punctuation are used throughout.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: reading a notice aloud

With a partner, take turns reading one of this chapter's notices aloud, at a natural, announcer-like pace, then summarize it in one spoken sentence, for example Umtapo wolwazi uvaliwe ngoLwesibili. (The library is closed on Tuesday.)

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: passing on a message

Working with a partner, one of you reads a short invented notice silently, then tells the other person what it says, as if passing on news, while the other person asks one follow up question, such as nini or kuphi.

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself reading your written notice and message aloud, once in a formal, announcer-like voice, and once in a warmer, informal voice, as if speaking to a friend. Listen back, and check that your tone changes even though some of the words stay the same.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Noticeboards and shared information

Community noticeboards, at schools, clinics, and local offices, remain a common way of sharing practical information in many communities, alongside phone messages and radio announcements. Learning to read these short, information dense texts is a genuinely useful everyday skill, not only a classroom exercise.

Culture in Context — Greeting before the news

Even a short, practical text message to someone you know well, such as Nomvula's message to Thandeka in this chapter, commonly opens with a brief greeting before the main point. Dropping straight into the news without any greeting can come across as abrupt, even in a hurried message.

Regional Note — Radio and public address announcements

Local radio stations and public address systems at taxi ranks, schools, and markets often use exactly this kind of short, passive heavy announcement, read at a brisk pace. Listening to real recordings, if they become available to you, is excellent extra practice once you are comfortable with this chapter's script.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Reading word by word instead of for key information

Error: Trying to translate every word of a notice before answering a comprehension question, and losing track of the main point.

Correction: Ask the three anchor questions first, kwenzekani, nini, kuphi, and only look closely at the surrounding words once you have the main facts, for example noticing quickly that umhlangano usumiswe means the meeting has been postponed, before worrying about every remaining word.

Common Mistake — Using formal notice language in a personal message

Error: Opening a message to a friend with Isaziso, as if writing a public notice, instead of a greeting.

Correction: Reserve isaziso for genuinely public, impersonal notices, and open a personal message with a greeting, such as Sawubona Thandeka, before giving the news.

Common Mistake — Confusing valwa and vala

Error: Saying isikole sivala namuhla to mean the school is closed today, which instead suggests the school itself is doing the closing.

Correction: Use the passive form when the school is the thing affected, not the doer: isikole sivaliwe namuhla, the school is closed today.

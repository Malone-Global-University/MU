Ukwenzela Abanye

Volume II, Chapter 13: Applicative Meanings · Speak and Write Zulu: Practical Independence

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize the applicative extension, and understand how it changes a plain verb into one that means doing something for, to, with, or because of someone.
  2. Use a small, controlled set of applicative verbs to talk about actions done on someone's behalf.
  3. Combine an applicative verb with an object concord, to say exactly for whom an action is done.
  4. Connect an applicative clause to a reason with ngoba, joining what you already practiced in Chapter 12 to this chapter's new verb forms.
  5. Ask who an action was done for, and answer using an applicative verb.
  6. Read and listen to short passages describing everyday favors and services.
  7. Write a short paragraph describing something you did, or something someone did, for another person.

Communication Scenario

Thandeka has decided to introduce Nomvula to her new neighbor properly. As the two of them plan a small visit, the conversation keeps returning to small favors, who bought bread for whom, who is cooking for the gathering, and who will carry the chairs for the elderly guests. Talking about actions done for other people needs a verb form that Thandeka and Nomvula have not yet used together, the applicative.

  1. Before you begin, think, in English, about how often you describe an action as being done for someone, rather than simply done. Notice how naturally that idea appears in everyday planning.
  2. Look back briefly at the reasons and opinions you built in Chapter 12. This chapter asks you to use ngoba again, now paired with a new kind of verb.

Pronunciation Review

No new click series is introduced in this chapter. This chapter reviews the voiced click series, gc, gq, and gx, and reminds you to keep the extra syllable of an applicative verb clear, rather than letting it blur into the syllable before it.

Pronunciation Focus — The extra syllable in an applicative verb

An applicative verb is one syllable longer than the plain verb it comes from, for example thenga becomes thengela. Read the model dialogue below aloud, and take care to pronounce that added syllable clearly, with the penultimate lengthening now falling on the new second-to-last syllable, not on the one you would use for the plain verb.

Grammar: The Applicative Extension

13.1 What the applicative extension does

Purpose: describe an action performed for the benefit of someone, directed toward someone, done with something, or done because of a particular reason or goal.

The applicative extension changes the meaning of a verb by adding a beneficiary, a goal, an instrument, or a reason into the action itself, without needing a separate word for for or to. This chapter introduces the applicative in a small, controlled set of verbs only, so that you can recognize the pattern and use a handful of common applicative forms correctly, without yet building new applicative verbs freely.

Language Focus

The applicative adds -el- before the final vowel. Compare thenga, to buy, with thengela, to buy for. The root thenga stays recognizable, and the extra -el- carries the new meaning. This is why an applicative verb is always one syllable longer than the plain verb it comes from.

Plain verbMeaningApplicative formMeaning
thengato buythengelato buy for
phekato cookphekelato cook for
bhalato writebhalelato write for
enzato do, to makesenzelato do for

Notice the extra -el- added before the final vowel in each applicative form. This chapter asks you only to recognize and use these four applicative verbs correctly. Building new applicative forms from other verb roots will be developed in a later chapter.

13.2 Naming the beneficiary with an object concord

Purpose: say exactly for whom, or for what, the action is being done.

An applicative verb is often paired with the object concord you already practiced in Chapter 7, placed before the verb root to name the beneficiary directly, without repeating the person's name in every sentence.

Language Building Block — The applicative pattern
Subject concord + tense marker + (object concord) + applicative root + -el- + final vowel
Begin with the ordinary subject concord and tense marker you already control. If the beneficiary is known or already mentioned, add the matching object concord before the root. Then use the applicative form of the verb, keeping the usual final vowel.
Umsizi omusha uyasithengela ubisi, ngoba sonke sikhathele.
The new assistant is buying milk for us, because all of us are tired.

You can combine an applicative clause with a reason from Chapter 12, so that a single sentence explains both who benefits from an action, and why it is being done.

  • UThandeka umphekela ukudla umakhelwane wakhe, ngoba ubegula ngesonto eledlule. Thandeka is cooking food for her neighbor, because he was sick last week. Applicative phekela + object concord m- + ngoba reason clause
  • Ngicabanga ukuthi kufanele simbhalele incwadi umsizi, ngoba usisiza njalo. I think that we should write him a letter, the assistant, because he helps us all the time. Opinion clause (Chapter 12) + applicative bhalela + object concord m- + ngoba reason clause

13.3 Asking for whom something was done

Purpose: ask who benefits from an action, or on whose behalf it is done.

To ask for whom an action is done, use an applicative verb together with the question word obani, for whom. The rest of the sentence keeps its ordinary word order, so this question is built from pieces you already know.

  • Uthengela obani ubisi lolu? Who are you buying this milk for? Applicative thengela + obani question word

Model Dialogue

Isimo: UThandeka noNomvula balungiselela ukuvakashela umakhelwane omusha kaThandeka, becabanga ngezinto okufanele bazenzele khona.
Setting: Thandeka and Nomvula are preparing to visit Thandeka's new neighbor, thinking about the things they should do for him.

UNOMVULA Uthengela obani ubisi lolu? Angibonanga ukuthi kwakusekhona okuthengiwe. Who are you buying this milk for? I did not notice that something had already been bought.
UTHANDEKA Ngiyamthengela umakhelwane wami, ngoba ubegula ngesonto eledlule, futhi angikafiki ekhaya ukuze amsize. I am buying it for my neighbor, because he was sick last week, and he has not yet come home so that I could help him.
UNOMVULA Kulungile, nami ngingamsiza. Ngingamphekela ukudla, uma uthanda. Alright, I can help too. I could cook food for him, if you like.
UTHANDEKA Ngicabanga ukuthi lokho kuyisenzo esihle. Ngizombhalela incwadi, ngimtshele ukuthi sizomvakashela ngeSonto. I think that is a good thing to do. I will write him a letter, telling him that we will visit him on Sunday.
UNomvula ubopha ibhege yobisi, ecabanga ngokuthi yini enye abangayenzela umakhelwane.
Nomvula ties up the bag of milk, thinking about what else they could do for the neighbor.
UNOMVULA Kulungile. Manje sesenzela umuntu okuthile okuhle, hhayi nje ukuxoxa ngaye. Good. Now we are doing something good for a person, not just talking about him.

This dialogue combines the applicative forms thengela, phekela, and bhalela, an object concord naming the beneficiary, and a ngoba reason clause from Chapter 12.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. UThandeka umthengela obani ubisi? (Who is Thandeka buying milk for?)
  2. UNomvula uzomenzela ni umakhelwane? (What will Nomvula do for the neighbor?)
  3. UThandeka uzobhalela ini incwadi? (What is the letter Thandeka will write about?)

Umakhelwane osizwayo — A neighbor who is helped

  1. Kungani umakhelwane wenzelwa izinto eziningi? (Why is the neighbor having many things done for him?)
  2. UThandeka wamenzela ni? (What did Thandeka do for him?)
  3. Kungani uNomvula engamphekelanga yena ukudla okuqandile? (Why did Nomvula not cook him cold food?)
  4. Umakhelwane wazizwa kanjani ekugcineni? (How did the neighbor feel in the end?)

Writing Workshop

In this chapter, you will write a short paragraph describing something you did, or something someone else did, for another person, using at least one applicative verb and at least one ngoba reason clause from Chapter 12.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write six to eight sentences describing a favor, real or invented. Name who did the favor, who benefited from it, and use ngoba to explain why it was done. Use at least two of the applicative verbs from this chapter, thengela, phekela, bhalela, or senzela.

  1. Choose your favor. In English, note who did something for whom, and what it was.
  2. Name the people involved. Decide whether you will use names, or an object concord once the person has already been named.
  3. Describe the first favor. Use an applicative verb, and an object concord if the beneficiary is already known from an earlier sentence.
  4. Give the reason. Add a ngoba clause, explaining why the favor was needed.
  5. Describe a second favor. Use a different applicative verb from this chapter's set.
  6. Audit your draft. Confirm that each applicative verb still ends with the ordinary final vowel, and that every object concord matches the noun class of the person or thing it refers to.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • At least two applicative verbs from this chapter are used correctly.
  • Each applicative verb is paired with a clear beneficiary, either named or marked with an object concord.
  • At least one ngoba reason clause explains why a favor was done.
  • Every object concord matches the noun class it refers to.
  • Standard isiZulu spelling, capitalization, and punctuation are used throughout.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: asking for whom

With a partner, take turns naming an everyday action, such as thenga, pheka, or bhala, then ask Wenzela obani? (Who are you doing this for?), and answer using the matching applicative form, such as Ngiphekela umama wami, ngoba usebenza kade namuhla.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: planning a favor

Working with a partner, imagine that a person you both know needs help this week. Decide together, in isiZulu, who will do what for that person, and why, using at least two applicative verbs and one ngoba clause each.

Useful checking expressions: Ngingakwenzela lokho. (I can do that for you.)  |  Ubani ozomenzela lokho? (Who will do that for him?)  |  Kulungile, ngizokusiza. (Alright, I will help you.)

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself describing three favors you have done, or could do, for people you know, each with a reason. Listen back, and check that each applicative verb keeps its extra syllable clear.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Favors as an everyday expectation

In many communities, checking on a neighbor who has been unwell, or offering to cook, buy, or write something on their behalf, is treated as an ordinary part of good neighborliness, rather than as an unusual act of generosity. Describing these small favors accurately is therefore useful everyday language, not only vocabulary for special occasions.

Culture in Context — Offering help without being asked

It is common, in many everyday interactions, to offer a favor before the other person has directly asked for one, particularly among neighbors, family members, and close colleagues. The applicative forms in this chapter give you the tools to describe that kind of unprompted help clearly.

Regional Note — Variation in how favors are announced

Some speakers announce a favor plainly and directly, while others prefer to mention it briefly, almost in passing, so as not to draw attention to their own generosity. Both approaches are common, and this textbook models a direct, clearly stated pattern to give learners solid practice with the applicative form itself.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Dropping the -el- extension

Error: Using the plain verb where the applicative meaning is needed, for example saying ngiyamthenga ubisi to mean I am buying milk for him, instead of using the applicative form.

Correction: Add the applicative extension when the sentence names a beneficiary: ngiyamthengela ubisi, I am buying him milk, with -el- clearly present before the final vowel.

Common Mistake — Forgetting the object concord for a known beneficiary

Error: Repeating the beneficiary's full name in every sentence, for example saying ngiphekela umakhelwane, ngithengela umakhelwane, ngibhalela umakhelwane, instead of using an object concord once the person is already known.

Correction: Once the beneficiary is clear from context, use the matching object concord: ngiyamphekela, ngiyamthengela, ngiyambhalela, all referring back to the same person.

Common Mistake — Building new applicative forms freely

Error: Attempting to add -el- to a verb root not yet covered in this chapter's table, by guessing a pattern from thengela or phekela.

Correction: Use only thengela, phekela, bhalela, and senzela productively at this stage. Building new applicative forms from other roots will be developed in a later chapter.

Together with your existing noun-class, concord, and causative control, this lets you describe not only what people do, but who benefits from it, and why.

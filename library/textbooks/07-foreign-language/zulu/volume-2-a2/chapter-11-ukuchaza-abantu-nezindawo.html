Ukuchaza Abantu Nezindawo

Volume II, Chapter 11: Relative Description · Speak and Write Zulu: Practical Independence

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize the basic relative concord for common noun classes, including Class 1, 1a, 2, 7, and 9.
  2. Form simple relative descriptions that identify a person, object, or place, such as "the woman who works at the clinic."
  3. Attach a relative concord to a verb to create a descriptive clause, in the classes this chapter activates.
  4. Use a small set of formulaic relative expressions for classes not yet formally taught, without analyzing their internal structure.
  5. Read and listen to short passages that use relative description to identify people, objects, and places.
  6. Write short sentences that use a relative clause to identify someone or something already mentioned.
  7. Distinguish, in your own production, between the relative concords this chapter has activated and the ones you are only expected to recognize.

Communication Scenario

A few days after her errands, Thandeka wants to introduce Nomvula to a new neighbor, but she cannot remember his name. Instead, she describes him by what he does and where he is, the way people naturally do when a name has slipped their mind. Nomvula, in turn, describes a shop assistant and a nearby house the same way, so the two of them can identify exactly who and what they mean.

  1. Before you begin, think, in English, about how you identify someone whose name you do not know. Notice that you usually describe them by what they do, where they are, or what they look like, rather than by name.
  2. Look back briefly at the noun-class and concord tables from earlier chapters. This chapter asks you to extend concord agreement into a new function: identifying people, objects, and places through a descriptive clause.

Grammar: Relative Concords

11.1 What a relative concord does

Purpose: identify a person, object, or place by describing what they do, where they are, or what is true about them, instead of, or in addition to, naming them.

In English, a relative clause is introduced by a word such as "who," "which," or "that," for example "the woman who works at the clinic." In isiZulu, there is no separate word like this. Instead, a relative concord, a short prefix that agrees with the noun class of the person, object, or place being described, attaches directly to the verb. The relative concord replaces the ordinary subject concord in the clause it introduces.

Language Focus

The relative concord is not a separate word. Where English adds "who" or "which" before a verb, isiZulu changes the beginning of the verb itself. This is why relative description depends on the noun-class and concord control you have already built. A relative clause is, at its core, a verb wearing a different prefix.

11.2 Relative concords activated in this chapter

This chapter activates the relative concord for five common classes only. Other classes remain receptive until later chapters formally introduce them.

ClassExample nounRelative concordExample relative form
1umuntu (person)o-umuntu osebenzayo (the person who works)
1auThandeka (name)o-uThandeka ohlala eduze (Thandeka, who lives nearby)
2abantu (people)aba-abantu abasebenzayo (the people who work)
7isitolo (shop)esi-isitolo esisha (the shop that is new)
9indlu (house)e-indlu ehle (the house that is nice)

Notice that in the affirmative present, the relative concord often adds the suffix -yo to a verb that would otherwise end plainly, when the verb stands at the end of the clause. You will see this pattern in the examples below, and it will be developed further in later chapters. For now, focus on recognizing and reproducing the whole relative form, rather than analyzing every part of it.

Language Building Block — The relative-description pattern
Noun (the person, object, or place) + relative concord matching its class + verb, describing what is true of it.
Name the person, object, or place first, then attach the matching relative concord to a verb that tells the listener what they do, where they are, or what is true about them. The relative concord takes the place of the ordinary subject concord in that clause.
Umakhelwane osebenza esitolo useduze nendlu yami.
The neighbor who works at the shop is near my house.

11.3 Formulaic relative expressions for other classes

You will meet a small number of useful relative expressions in classes this chapter does not yet activate for full analysis, such as into edingekayo (the thing that is needed) or indawo ekhona (the place that is there). Use these as fixed expressions for now. Do not attempt to build new relative forms in classes beyond the five listed above until a later chapter authorizes them.

  • Ngazi umuntu osebenza emtholampilo. I know a person who works at the clinic. Class 1 relative concord o-, verb sebenza + -yo
  • Sikhuluma ngesitolo esiseduze nesitobhi. We are talking about the shop that is near the transport rank. Class 7 relative concord esi-, locative-based description
  • Yile ndlu ehle engiyithandayo. This is the nice house that I like. Class 9 relative concord e-, demonstrative from Chapter 3, object concord -yi- from Chapter 7

Every relative form above uses a noun class, a concord, or a verb root you have already met. The only new element is the relative concord itself, and only for the five classes listed in the table above.

Model Dialogue

Isimo: Ekuseni, uThandeka nomakhelwane wakhe omusha, uNomvula, bahamba endleleni. UThandeka ubona indoda angayazi igama layo.
Setting: In the morning, Thandeka and her new neighbor, Nomvula, are walking together. Thandeka sees a man whose name she does not know.

UTHANDEKA Nomvula, uyambona lowo muntu osebenza esitolo esiphesheya? Nomvula, do you see that person who works at the shop across the way?
UNOMVULA Yebo, ngiyambona. Ngubani igama lakhe? Yes, I see him. What is his name?
UTHANDEKA Angilazi, kodwa yena ngumakhelwane wami omusha. Uhlala endlini ehle, eduze nomtholampilo. I don't know it, but he is my new neighbor. He lives in the nice house near the clinic.
UNOMVULA Awu, yindlu leyo engiyaziyo. Umakhelwane wakho, usebenza kuphi kanti? Ah, that's a house I know. So where does your neighbor work, then?
UTHANDEKA Usebenza esitolo esisha esiseduze nesitobhi. Ngithanda ukuthenga khona, ngoba umsizi omusha osebenza lapho unesineke. He works at the new shop that is near the transport rank. I like buying there, because the new assistant who works there is patient.
UNomvula uyamamatheka, esazi manje ukuthi umakhelwane omusha kaThandeka ubani.
Nomvula smiles, now knowing who Thandeka's new neighbor is.
UNOMVULA Kuhle. Ngizomthola khona, umuntu osiza ngokushesha uyangithokozisa. Good. I will find him there; a person who helps quickly pleases me.

This dialogue introduces the Class 1, Class 7, and Class 9 relative concords activated in this chapter, alongside object concords, locative nouns, and demonstratives already familiar from earlier chapters.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Umuntu uThandeka ambonayo, wenzani? (What does the person Thandeka points out do?)
  2. Umakhelwane omusha kaThandeka uhlala kuphi? (Where does Thandeka's new neighbor live?)
  3. Kungani uThandeka ethanda ukuthenga esitolo leso? (Why does Thandeka like shopping at that shop?)

Writing Workshop

In this chapter, you will write short sentences that identify a person, an object, or a place using a relative clause from the five classes this chapter has activated.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write six to eight sentences describing three people, objects, or places you know, real or invented, each identified through a relative clause. Use at least two different relative concords from the table in this chapter, and include at least one locative or object concord from an earlier chapter inside a relative clause.

  1. Choose your three subjects. In English, list a person, an object or place, and a second person or place you want to describe.
  2. Match the class. For each one, identify its noun class and the correct relative concord from the table in this chapter.
  3. Build the relative clause. Attach the relative concord to a verb that tells the listener what the person, object, or place does or where it is.
  4. Add one supporting detail. Extend at least one sentence with a locative phrase or an object concord from an earlier chapter.
  5. Check class agreement. Confirm that every relative concord you used matches the noun class of what it describes.
  6. Audit your draft. Compare each relative form against the table in section 11.2, and make sure no relative concord outside the five activated classes has been invented.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • Every relative concord used matches the noun class of the person, object, or place it describes.
  • At least two different relative concords from this chapter's table appear in the writing.
  • At least one relative clause includes a locative or object concord from an earlier chapter.
  • No relative concord outside Classes 1, 1a, 2, 7, and 9 has been produced independently.
  • Standard isiZulu spelling, capitalization, and punctuation are used throughout.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: describing without naming

Your partner thinks of a person, object, or place from earlier chapters without naming it. Ask questions and offer relative descriptions, such as Ingabe umuntu osebenza esitolo? (Is it a person who works at a shop?), until you identify what your partner is thinking of.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: introducing a stranger

Working with a partner, take turns playing Thandeka and Nomvula from the model dialogue. Invent a new person, shop, or house not mentioned in the dialogue, and describe it using a relative clause, before your partner guesses who or what you mean.

Useful checking expressions: Ngubani lowo? (Who is that?)  |  Yikuphi lokho? (Which one is that?)  |  Uqonde bani? (Who do you mean?)

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself describing three people or places you know, real or invented, using a relative clause for each. Listen back, and check that each relative concord matches its noun class correctly.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Identifying people by role rather than by name

In many everyday conversations, it is common and entirely acceptable to identify someone by what they do or where they can be found, rather than by name, particularly when the name is unknown or momentarily forgotten. Describing someone as "the person who works at the shop near the transport rank" is a normal, respectful way to refer to them.

Culture in Context — Speaking well of service providers

When describing a shop assistant, neighbor, or other person who provides a service, it is common to include a brief remark about their character, such as patience or helpfulness, as part of the description itself. This reflects a broader communicative habit of noting not just what someone does, but how they do it.

Regional Note — Variation in descriptive detail

Some speakers favor brief, minimal relative descriptions, offering just enough detail for identification, while others include richer detail as a matter of conversational style. Both approaches are common, and this textbook models a moderately detailed style to give learners full practice with the concords in this chapter.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using the ordinary subject concord instead of the relative concord

Error: Keeping the plain subject concord in a descriptive clause, for example saying umuntu usebenza esitolo when a relative clause is intended, rather than switching to the relative concord.

Correction: Replace the ordinary subject concord with the matching relative concord when the clause is identifying or describing the noun: umuntu osebenza esitolo, not a plain subject-concord clause standing in for a relative one.

Common Mistake — Mismatching the relative concord and the noun class

Error: Applying a Class 1 relative concord to a Class 7 or Class 9 noun, or the reverse, for example saying isitolo osebenza instead of matching the concord to the shop's own class.

Correction: Always match the relative concord to the class of the noun it describes: isitolo esisebenzayo for Class 7, indlu esebenzayo is incorrect for a house, but indlu ehle for Class 9 shows the correct match.

Common Mistake — Extending relative concords beyond this chapter's activated classes

Error: Attempting to construct a relative concord for a class not yet activated, such as Class 5 or Class 11, by guessing a pattern from the five classes already learned.

Correction: Use only the fixed, formulaic expressions given for other classes until a later chapter formally activates them. Do not build new relative forms outside Classes 1, 1a, 2, 7, and 9 at this stage.

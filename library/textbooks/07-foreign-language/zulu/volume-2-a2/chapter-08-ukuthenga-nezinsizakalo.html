Ukuthenga Nezinsizakalo

Volume II, Chapter 8: Shopping, Services, and Transactions · Speak and Write Zulu: Practical Independence

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Conduct a fuller shopping interaction, naming an item once and then referring back to it with the correct object concord.
  2. Ask for the price of an item, and understand a spoken price in reply.
  3. Use object concords for classes 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, and 9 inside transactional exchanges at the market, a clinic, a transport rank, and an office counter.
  4. Request an item, a service, or help, using polite request forms already active from earlier chapters.
  5. Confirm, decline, or ask for a substitute during a transaction, without repeating the item's name in every sentence.
  6. Read and listen to short transactional exchanges set in a market, a clinic, and a transport rank.
  7. Write a short shopping or service exchange that names an item once, then refers back to it using an object concord across at least two tenses.
  8. Continue the concord audit habit, now applied to fast paced transactional speech.

Communication Scenario

On Saturday morning, Nomvula goes to the market near her home to buy vegetables for the week, then stops at the clinic to collect a prescription, and finally waits at the transport rank to travel back into town. At each stop, she names what she wants once, then relies on object concords to keep the exchange short and natural, the same habit she and Zanele practised with the report in Chapter 7.

  1. Before you begin, think, in English, about a recent shopping trip or a visit to a clinic or office. Notice how often you ask for something by name once, then use "it" or "them" for the rest of the conversation.
  2. Think of one item you often buy, and one service you sometimes need, such as a prescription, a ticket, or a form. You will practise asking for both in isiZulu by the end of this chapter.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 7 introduced the object concord and showed how it replaces a noun that has already been named. This chapter keeps that system fully active, and puts it to work in the kind of fast, practical exchanges you meet at a market, a clinic, a transport rank, or an office counter. No new object concord classes are introduced here. Instead, the same six classes from Chapter 7 are practised at greater speed and in a wider range of settings, alongside a small set of new transactional vocabulary.

From Chapters 1 through 7

Concord audit, familiar noun classes, recent and resultative past, simple future, and object concords for classes 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, and 9.

New in This Chapter

Market, clinic, transport, and office vocabulary, price questions, and polite request forms, all combined with the object concords already active.

Looking Forward

Pronunciation and Click Review

Transactional speech moves quickly, and object concords are easy to lose in a fast exchange at a busy counter. Practise the target words below slowly first, then at natural speed, keeping the object concord clear even when you speed up.

Pronunciation Focus — Object concords under pressure

Say ngiyalithenga slowly, broken into ngi-ya-li-theng-a, then again at natural speed. Compare it with ngiyayithenga, broken into ngi-ya-yi-theng-a. Listen for the short vowel of the object concord, -li- for class 5 items such as ithikithi, and -yi- for class 9 items such as imali. A rushed speaker may blur the vowel, but the consonant, l or y, must stay clear so that the listener knows which item is meant.

Grammar: Object Concords in Transactions

8.1 Naming an item once, then asking for it again

Purpose: request, confirm, and pay for a specific item without repeating its name in every sentence.

A shopping or service exchange almost always follows the same shape. A customer names an item once, asks its price, then uses an object concord for every later reference, including when confirming the purchase or asking for a receipt. This is the same discipline from Chapter 7, now applied to a live transaction instead of an office report.

Language Focus

The object concord table from Chapter 7 does not change in this chapter. Only the nouns and the setting change. Before speaking, identify the class of the item you want, then choose -m-, -ba-, -wu-, -yi-, -li-, or -yi- from the Chapter 7 table, exactly as before.

Language Building Block — The full transaction pattern
Ngicela + noun. Yimalini? + subject concord + tense marker + object concord + root + final vowel.
Name the item once with Ngicela, ask the price with the fixed formula Yimalini, then confirm, decline, or pay using the object concord that matches the item's class, inside the verb template you already control.
Ngicela ithikithi. Yimalini? Ngizolibhadala manje.
Please may I have a ticket. How much is it? I will pay for it now.
  • Ngicela isaka lemifino. Yimalini? Please may I have a bag of vegetables. How much is it? isaka, class 5, named for the first time
  • Nginemali eyanele. Ngizolibhadala manje. I have enough money. I will pay for it now. simple future, class 5, object concord -li-, referring back to isaka
  • Udokotela unginike umuthi. Ngiwuthathile ekuseni. The doctor gave me medicine. I took it this morning. resultative past, class 3, object concord -wu-, referring back to umuthi

Notice that the price question, Yimalini, does not itself carry an object concord. It is a fixed question formula. The object concord appears only once the customer moves on to confirm, pay for, or decline the item.

Model Dialogue

Isimo: NgoMgqibelo ekuseni, uNomvula useMakethe, ecela imifino kumthengisi.
Setting: On Saturday morning, Nomvula is at the market, asking a vendor for vegetables.

UNOMVULA Sawubona, mama. Ngicela isaka lemifino, elikhulu kuphela. Hello, mama. Please may I have a bag of vegetables, a large one only.
UMTHENGISI Kulungile, ngane yami. Nasi isaka. Yimalini engikuthengelayo? Alright, my child. Here is the bag. How much am I selling it to you for?
UNOMVULA Nginemali eyanele. Ngizolibhadala manje, bese ngihamba. I have enough money. I will pay for it now, and then I will go.
UMTHENGISI Ngiyabonga. Uzobuya futhi? Thank you. Will you come back again?
UNOMVULA Yebo, ngizobuya ngoMgqibelo ozayo. Ngiyawathanda amasaka akho. Yes, I will come back next Saturday. I like your bags very much.
UNomvula uphuma emakethe, ehamba ngasesitobhini, elinde utekisi.
Nomvula leaves the market, heading toward the transport rank, waiting for a taxi.
UMSHAYELI Sawubona. Uya edolobheni? Hello. Are you going into town?
UNOMVULA Yebo. Ngicela ithikithi. Yimalini? Yes. Please may I have a ticket. How much is it?
UMSHAYELI Ngizolinika manje. Ubhadale khona lapha kimi. I will give it to you now. Pay right here to me.

This dialogue uses object concords for class 5, isaka and ithikithi, using -li-, across resultative past, present, and simple future. Every noun class and sentence pattern used was already active by the end of Chapter 7.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  2. Uzosibhadala nini isaka? (When will she pay for the bag?)
  3. Uzobuya nini emakethe? (When will she come back to the market?)

Writing Workshop

In this chapter, you will write a short shopping or service exchange, naming an item once, then referring back to it using an object concord, across at least two tenses.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write six to eight sentences describing a visit to a market, a clinic, or a transport rank, or an invented equivalent. Name the item or service once, then use at least three different object concords to refer back to it, or to more than one item, across at least two different tenses.

  1. Choose your setting. In English, decide whether your exchange happens at a market, a clinic, or a transport rank.
  2. Name your item once. Introduce the item in your first sentence, with its correct noun class.
  3. Ask its price. Use the fixed formula Yimalini to ask the price of the item you named.
  4. Refer back to it. In your following sentences, replace the noun with the matching object concord, rather than repeating it.
  5. Vary the tense. Use at least one present, one resultative past, and one simple future sentence, each carrying an object concord.
  6. Audit your draft. Check that every object concord matches the class of the noun it replaces, before writing your final version.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • The item or service is named once, then replaced with an object concord in later sentences.
  • At least three object concords are used, matching the correct noun classes.
  • Object concords appear correctly in at least two different tenses.
  • The price question Yimalini is used at least once, correctly.
  • Every subject concord and object concord matches its noun's class.
  • No unscheduled vocabulary or grammar appears beyond Volume I and Chapters 1 through 8.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: naming and confirming

Your partner names an item, using Ngicela. Respond by asking Yimalini, then confirm the purchase out loud using the matching object concord, and check together which class you chose.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: the Saturday errand

Working with a partner, act out a short visit to a market, a clinic, or a transport rank. One of you plays the customer, and the other plays the vendor, the doctor, or the driver. Name at least two items or services, ask the price of each, and confirm or decline using object concords rather than repeating the nouns.

Useful checking expressions: Ngicela isaka. (Please may I have a bag.)  |  Yimalini? (How much is it?)  |  Ngizolibhadala manje. (I will pay for it now.)

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself narrating the exchange you wrote about in the Writing Workshop, as a spoken account. Listen back, and check that every object concord you used still matches its noun's class.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Greeting before transacting

In many isiZulu speaking communities, a market or shop interaction commonly opens with a greeting before any request is made, even in a busy, fast moving setting. Moving straight to a request without a greeting can come across as abrupt, so learners are encouraged to keep the greeting habit active even in short, practical exchanges.

Culture in Context — Addressing vendors and drivers respectfully

Vendors, drivers, and clinic staff are often addressed with respectful terms such as mama, baba, or sisi, rather than by name, particularly on a first meeting. This respectful address commonly continues throughout the transaction, even once an object concord has taken over reference to the item itself.

Regional Note — Variation in price expressions

Some speakers and regions use additional or slightly different price expressions beyond Yimalini, particularly in urban markets with frequent code-switching into English or Afrikaans loanwords for currency. This textbook models the clear, standard Yimalini pattern suitable for learners at this stage, and later volumes will expand your recognition of regional variation.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Adding an object concord to Yimalini

Error: Inserting an object concord into the fixed price question, for example saying Yiliyimalini when asking the price of a class 5 item.

Correction: Keep Yimalini fixed and unchanged, regardless of the noun class of the item: Yimalini?, not Yiliyimalini, since the price question does not carry an object concord.

Common Mistake — Repeating the item name after asking the price

Error: Naming the item again in the confirmation sentence, for example saying Ngizobhadala isaka manje immediately after already naming isaka in the previous sentence.

Correction: Once the item has been named, switch to the object concord: Ngizolibhadala manje, not Ngizobhadala isaka manje, unless you are deliberately emphasizing the item.

Common Mistake — Using an unscheduled object concord for classes 7, 8, or 11

Error: Attempting to build an object concord for isitolo or izimpahla, for example saying Ngiyasithenga for isitolo, before that class's object concord has been introduced.

Correction: For classes not yet active, keep naming the noun directly rather than guessing at an object concord: Ngiyasithanda isitolo only after class 7 is formally introduced. Until then, repeat the noun or restructure the sentence.

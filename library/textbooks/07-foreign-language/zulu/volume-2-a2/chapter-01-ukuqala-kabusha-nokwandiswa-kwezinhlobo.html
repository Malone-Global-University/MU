Volume II, Chapter 1: Re-entry and Concord Expansion · Speak and Write Zulu: Practical Independence

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Reactivate greetings, self-introduction, family description, and respectful address from Volume I in slightly longer, more independent exchanges.
  2. Recognize and confirm your control of the noun classes and subject concords consolidated in Volume I, before those systems expand.
  3. Extend noun-class agreement to a wider set of everyday nouns, including additional class 3/4, class 7/8, and class 9/10 items.
  4. Carry out a short concord audit, checking your own subject concord and adjective concord choices against a noun's class.
  5. Read a short connected passage that recombines Volume I grammar with a slightly wider noun-class range.
  6. Write several organized sentences that describe people, home, and daily life using accurate, audited concord.
  7. Take part in an extended spoken exchange that checks in on a returning topic, such as home or family, at a broader level of detail.
  8. Identify which Volume I systems feel fully secure and which deserve continued attention as Volume II begins.

Communication Scenario

Some months have passed since Zanele first welcomed her new neighbour, Nomvula, to the street. The two have kept in touch, and Nomvula is now settled in and ready to talk about her life in more detail. This morning, Zanele stops by, and the two catch up, moving comfortably across topics both women have discussed before, but now noticing new objects, rooms, and people that neither had mentioned in earlier conversations. Nothing about the grammar is new. What is new is how many nouns, of how many different classes, the conversation now touches.

  1. Before you begin, think back to the Volume I capstone chapter. Write down, in English, three noun classes you feel confident recognizing, and one you would like to review carefully today.
  2. Look around the room you are sitting in right now. Write down, in English, three objects you can see. You will return to these later in the chapter once your class vocabulary has expanded.

Pronunciation Focus — A returning click warm-up

Say each pair slowly, then at a natural pace: c in icici, q in iqanda, x in ixoxo. Then move to gc in ugcina, and nx in inxeba. Keep your penultimate lengthening steady, exactly as you practised throughout Volume I.

Grammar: Concord Expansion

1.1 Auditing your subject concord against noun class

Purpose: confirm accurate control of the subject concords consolidated in Volume I, then apply them to a slightly wider set of nouns from the same classes.

A concord audit is a simple check. Before you speak or write a sentence, identify the noun's class, then confirm that your subject concord, your adjective concord, and your possessive concord all agree with that class. No new concord form appears in this chapter. The task is accuracy and confidence across a wider vocabulary set, not new morphology.

Language Building Block — Concord audit in three steps
1. Identify the class. 2. Choose the matching concord. 3. Check the sentence aloud.
This process reuses the noun-class recognition from Chapters 3 and 4 of Volume I and the subject-concord system from Chapter 5. No new morphology appears here; the pattern simply becomes a habit you apply automatically.
Umnyango mkhulu. Isihlalo sihle. Ingane incane.
The door is big. The chair is nice. The child is small.

1.2 Widening the everyday noun-class range

Purpose: apply familiar noun classes to a broader set of everyday nouns, in preparation for the object concords introduced later in Volume II.

  • Umuzi wami mkhulu, futhi unemithi eminingi. My homestead is big, and it has many trees.
  • Ingane kamakhelwane ihlala endlini encane. The neighbour's child lives in a small house.

Note on scope: Every class shown in this section was fully active by the end of Volume I. This chapter asks you to widen and audit, not to learn a new noun-class system.

Language Focus

The purpose of this opening chapter is confident recall, not expansion for its own sake. Every noun class, subject concord, and adjective concord used here was introduced and consolidated in Volume I. Widening your vocabulary inside these classes builds the stable ground on which object concords, locatives, and verb extensions will be introduced later in this volume.

Model Dialogue

Isimo: Ekuseni, endlini kaNomvula, uZanele uzovakashela.
Setting: In the morning, at Nomvula's house, Zanele has come to visit.

UZANELE Sawubona, Nomvula! Unjani namuhla? Hello, Nomvula! How are you today?
UNOMVULA Ngikhona, ngiyabonga. Ngena, mngane wami. Nangu umuzi wami omusha. I am well, thank you. Come in, my friend. Here is my new homestead.
UZANELE Kuhle lapha! Umnyango wakho muhle, futhi lesi sihlalo sikhulu kakhulu. It is nice here! Your door is beautiful, and this chair is very big.
UNOMVULA Yebo, ngiyathanda leli khaya. Nengane yami iyalithanda. Yes, I love this home. My child loves it too.
UZANELE Ingane yakho inezimpawu zani? Umdala noma umncane? What is your child like? Older or younger?
UNOMVULA Umncane kakhulu, kodwa uyakhuluma kahle isiZulu manje. Very young, but they speak isiZulu well now.
UZANELE Kuhle lokho! Isitolo esisha sivulekile eduze kwendlu yakho, akunjalo? That is nice! The new shop is open near your house, isn't it?
UNOMVULA Yebo, siyavuleka. Sizoya khona sonke ngempelasonto. Yes, it is open. We are all going there over the weekend.

Every grammar pattern in this dialogue was fully active by the end of Volume I. This chapter simply widens the noun-class vocabulary it is applied to.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Ubani ovakashela uNomvula? (Who is visiting Nomvula?)
  2. Sinjani isihlalo sikaNomvula? (What is Nomvula's chair like?)
  3. Ingane kaNomvula imdala noma imncane? (Is Nomvula's child older or younger?)
  4. Bazokwenzani ngempelasonto? (What will they do over the weekend?)

Writing Workshop

In this re-entry chapter, you will write several organized sentences that check your concord accuracy against a slightly wider noun-class vocabulary.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write six to eight sentences describing your own home or homestead, at least one person who lives there, and one recent or upcoming event connected to that home, using only vocabulary and grammar already active from Volume I, plus the small set of new items in this chapter.

  1. Plan your content. List, in English if that helps, the nouns you will use, and note each one's class.
  2. Write your opening. Begin by naming your home or homestead and describing it with an adjective.
  3. Add a person. Describe one person who lives there, using an accurate kinship term and subject concord.
  4. Add an object. Describe one object in or near the home, checking your adjective concord against its class.
  5. Add an event. Report one recent event or state one upcoming plan connected to the home.
  6. Audit your draft. For each sentence, identify the noun class, then check every concord against it before writing your final version.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • Every noun's class has been identified before choosing its concords.
  • Subject concords match the noun class of the subject in every sentence.
  • Adjective concords match the noun class of the noun they describe.
  • At least one recent event or future plan is included, with the correct verb form.
  • No unscheduled vocabulary or grammar appears beyond Volume I and this chapter.
  • Each sentence begins with a capital letter and ends with appropriate punctuation.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: describing a home

Your partner asks you three questions about your home, using words from this chapter's vocabulary list. Answer each question with a full sentence, then check your subject and adjective concords together before swapping roles.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: an extended catch-up conversation

Working with a partner, hold a conversation of at least eight exchanges that catches up on at least four of the following topics: greetings, home, family, a recent event, a future plan, and an object nearby. Your partner should ask at least one follow-up question on two different topics.

Useful checking expressions: Ngiyaqonda. (I understand.)  |  Ngicela uphinde. (Please repeat.)  |  Kuhle lokho. (That is nice.)

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself performing the full conversation as if speaking both roles, or present your Writing Workshop composition aloud as a spoken monologue. Listen back and check that your subject and adjective concords remain accurate across every noun class you use.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Checking in on a neighbour or friend

Returning to check on a neighbour or friend some time after a first meeting, and taking a genuine interest in changes to their home or family, is commonly considered good practice in many isiZulu speaking communities. Such ongoing attention often strengthens the relationship established at a first greeting.

Culture in Context — Homesteads and households

The word umuzi, commonly translated as homestead or household, can refer to more than a single building. Depending on context, it may describe a family's broader living compound. Learners should listen for context clues rather than assuming a single fixed meaning in every situation.

Regional Note — Widening vocabulary responsibly

As your noun-class vocabulary widens in Volume II, you may begin to notice regional or informal alternatives for some everyday words. This textbook continues to establish common, widely recognized standard forms as your foundation. Keep asking isiZulu speaking friends, family members, or instructors about the variation you encounter.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Guessing a concord instead of auditing the noun class

Error: Choosing a subject or adjective concord from memory of a similar-sounding word, without first checking the actual class of the noun in the sentence, for example treating umnyango as though it were class 1/2.

Correction: Pause and identify the class before choosing the concord: Umnyango (class 3/4) muhle. A brief class check prevents the concord error before it happens.

Common Mistake — Letting new vocabulary reset the subject concord

Error: Switching correctly established subject concords when a new noun from this chapter's vocabulary list is introduced mid-conversation, as though the new word required a different grammar system entirely.

Correction: New vocabulary items follow the same class system already learned: Isihlalo sikhulu, futhi sihlala eduze komnyango. A new word inside a familiar class takes the same concord as any other member of that class.

Common Mistake — Skipping the greeting when re-entering a familiar conversation

Error: Moving straight into description or news, without a returning greeting, as though the relationship no longer required one because the two speakers already know each other.

Correction: Open with a greeting even in a familiar, ongoing relationship: Sawubona, Nomvula! Unjani namuhla? A greeting remains appropriate at the start of every conversation, familiar or not.

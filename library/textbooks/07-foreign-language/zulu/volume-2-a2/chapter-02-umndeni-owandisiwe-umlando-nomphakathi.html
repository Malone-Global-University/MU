Umndeni Owandisiwe, Umlando, Nomphakathi

Volume II, Chapter 2: Extended Family, Biography, and Community · Speak and Write Zulu: Practical Independence

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name and describe extended family members, using class 1a/2a kinship terms alongside the terms already active from Volume I.
  2. Distinguish, in simple terms, between events that feel recently relevant and events located further in the past, and choose the correct past-tense form for each.
  3. Introduce a short personal or family history using recent past and remote past narration together.
  4. Use the connector ukuthi to report what someone said, knows, or believes, in a short complement clause.
  5. Describe a community, neighbourhood, or hometown using vocabulary from familiar noun classes.
  6. Read and listen to a short biography and identify its sequence of events.
  7. Write a short, organized biography of a real or invented family member.
  8. Continue applying the concord audit habit from Chapter 1 to a wider range of nouns and a wider range of verb forms.

Communication Scenario

A week after her visit to Nomvula's new homestead, Zanele invites Nomvula to a small family gathering. Over tea, Zanele's grandmother, uGogo, begins telling Nomvula about the family, and about the neighbourhood the family has lived in for many years. Nomvula listens carefully, then shares a little of her own family background in return. As the two families get to know each other more deeply, the conversation naturally moves from the present into the past, and from individual people into the wider community around them.

  1. Before you begin, list, in English, the members of your own extended family that you would want to mention first in a conversation like this.
  2. Think of one event from your family's past that you consider recent, and one that you consider more distant. You will return to this distinction later in the chapter.

Pronunciation and Click Review

This chapter's vocabulary includes several words without clicks, alongside a few click-bearing words from earlier chapters that will reappear in the reading and listening passages. Keep your click production steady as sentences grow longer.

Pronunciation Focus — Kinship words and rhythm

Practise the penultimate lengthening in umkhulu, umalume, and ubabekazi. Longer kinship words can tempt learners to stress the wrong syllable. Say each word slowly, then say it inside a full sentence, keeping the lengthening on the second-to-last syllable of the whole phrase, not just the word in isolation.

Grammar: Two Ways of Narrating the Past

2.1 Recent past and remote past

Purpose: choose between two past-tense forms, depending on whether an event feels closely connected to the present or belongs to a more distant stretch of time.

Volume I introduced a single past-tense pattern for talking about things that already happened. This chapter introduces a second past-tense form and teaches you, for the first time, when isiZulu speakers generally choose one over the other.

The recent past, sometimes called the perfect, is built with the subject concord plus the verb stem plus the suffix -ile. It is generally used for events that feel close to now, or whose result is still relevant.

The remote past is built with the subject concord plus -a- plus the verb stem plus the final vowel -a. It is generally used for events set further back in time, or for narrating a connected sequence of past events, such as in a story or biography.

Language Building Block — Two past-tense shapes
Recent past: SC + stem + -ile  |  Remote past: SC + -a- + stem + -a
Both forms build on the subject-concord system consolidated in Volume I. Neither form requires an object concord, an extension, or a relative clause. This chapter activates the contrast between them; it does not yet activate every irregular or extended past-tense pattern.
Ngifundile namuhla.  |  Ngafunda eNyuvesi yaseThekwini.
I have studied today.  |  I studied at the University of Durban, some time ago.
  • Ugogo wazalwa eMgungundlovu. Grandmother was born in Pietermaritzburg. remote past, distant biographical fact
  • Umzala wami ufikile izolo. My cousin arrived yesterday. recent past, still relevant now

Note on scope: This chapter activates the recent past and remote past for simple, single-clause statements only. Negative past forms, and past-tense relative clauses, remain outside this chapter's scope.

2.2 Reporting with ukuthi

Purpose: introduce a short, connected way to report what someone said, knows, thinks, or believes.

The word ukuthi (that) connects a main clause, such as I know or Grandmother says, to a second clause that reports the content. The second clause keeps its own ordinary subject concord and tense, exactly as it would as an independent sentence.

  • Ugogo uthi umkhulu wafunda eThekwini. Grandmother says that grandfather studied in Durban.
  • Ngiyazi ukuthi umzala wami usebenza edolobheni. I know that my cousin works in town.
Language Focus

A reported clause is simply an ordinary sentence placed after ukuthi. You do not need a new verb form to report speech. You only need a clause that is already correct on its own, joined to the main clause by ukuthi. This keeps reported speech well within the systems this volume has activated so far.

Model Dialogue

Isimo: Ekuseni, ekhaya kaZanele, uNomvula ubingelela uGogo.
Setting: In the morning, at Zanele's home, Nomvula greets Grandmother.

UGOGO Sawubona, Nomvula. Ngiyajabula ukukubona lapha ekhaya. Hello, Nomvula. I am happy to see you here at home.
UNOMVULA Ngiyabonga, Gogo. Kuhle lapha. Kunomndeni omkhulu kule ndawo, akunjalo? Thank you, Grandmother. It is nice here. There is a large family in this area, isn't there?
UGOGO Yebo. Umkhulu wazalwa lapha, futhi wafunda edolobheni eliseduze. Umalume kaZanele nabo bahlala kule ndawo. Yes. Grandfather was born here, and he studied in the nearby town. Zanele's uncle and his family also live in this area.
UNOMVULA Kuhle lokho. Umndeni wami nawo mkhulu. Ubabekazi wami ufikile izolo evela edolobheni. That is nice. My family is also big. My aunt arrived yesterday, coming from town.
UZANELE Ugogo uthi umkhulu wayesebenza kulo mphakathi iminyaka eminingi. Grandmother says that grandfather used to work in this community for many years.
UNOMVULA Ngiyezwa. Nomzala wami usebenza edolobheni manje, kodwa wazalelwa endaweni encane. I hear that. My cousin also works in town now, but was born in a small area.
UGOGO Sicela usiphindele nini futhi, Nomvula? Isakhamuzi esifana nawe siyathandeka lapha. When will you come back to us again, Nomvula? A resident like you is well liked here.

This dialogue uses the recent past, the remote past, and one reported clause with ukuthi. Every noun class and subject concord was already active by the end of Chapter 1.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Ugogo wazalwa kuphi? (Where was Grandmother born?)

  3. Ubabekazi kaNomvula ufike nini? (When did Nomvula's aunt arrive?)
  4. Umzala kaNomvula usebenza kuphi manje? (Where does Nomvula's cousin work now?)

Writing Workshop

In this chapter, you will write a short biography of a real or invented family member, combining recent past, remote past, and at least one reported clause with ukuthi.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write six to eight sentences describing a family member's background, including where they were born, one thing they did in the more distant past, one thing that happened recently, and one detail reported using ukuthi.

  1. Choose your subject. Decide which family member you will describe, and note their kinship term and noun class.
  2. Open with origin. State where they were born, using the remote past.
  3. Add distant history. Describe one thing they did or experienced further in the past.
  4. Add a recent event. Describe one thing that has happened recently, using the recent past.
  5. Report something. Add one sentence reporting what someone says or knows about this person, using ukuthi.
  6. Audit your draft. Check every noun's class, every concord, and every verb form before writing your final version.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • At least one sentence uses the remote past correctly.
  • At least one sentence uses the recent past correctly.
  • At least one sentence includes a clause reported with ukuthi.
  • Every noun's class has been identified before choosing its concords.
  • No unscheduled vocabulary or grammar appears beyond Volume I and Chapters 1 and 2.
  • Each sentence begins with a capital letter and ends with appropriate punctuation.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: recent or remote?

Your partner reads a short statement about a family event. Respond by identifying whether it is a recent-past or remote-past statement, then restate it in the other past tense if the meaning allows. Check each other's forms before swapping roles.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: a family history interview

Working with a partner, interview each other about a family member. Ask at least four questions covering where the person was born, something from their more distant past, something recent, and one thing you have heard someone else say about them, using ukuthi in your answer.

Useful checking expressions: Ngiyaqonda. (I understand.)  |  Ngicela uphinde. (Please repeat.)  |  Kuyathakazelisa lokho. (That is interesting.)

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself narrating the biography you wrote in the Writing Workshop as a spoken monologue. Listen back and check that your recent past, remote past, and reported clause all remain accurate.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Elders as narrators of family history

In many isiZulu speaking families, grandparents and older relatives are commonly treated as the primary narrators of family history. Listening attentively while an elder tells a family story, without interrupting, is often considered an important sign of respect, and is a natural setting in which recent and remote past narration both appear together.

Culture in Context — Extended kinship terms

Kinship terms such as umalume and ubabekazi describe specific relationships on particular sides of the family, in ways that do not always map neatly onto single English words such as uncle or aunt. Learners should listen for how a speaker uses each term in context, rather than assuming a one-to-one translation in every family.

Regional Note — Family narration styles

Some families and communities favour longer, more detailed accounts of family history, while others favour shorter, more compressed accounts. This textbook continues to model common, widely recognized standard forms. Keep asking isiZulu speaking friends, family members, or instructors how their own families typically tell these stories.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using the recent past for a distant biographical fact

Error: Describing where a grandparent was born using the recent past, such as wazelwe treated as though it behaved exactly like ufikile, without considering how far back the event lies.

Correction: Use the remote past for distant biographical narration: Ugogo wazalwa eMgungundlovu. Reserve the recent past for events that feel closely connected to the present moment.

Common Mistake — Dropping ukuthi before a reported clause

Error: Joining two independent statements without a connector, as though English word order alone were enough to signal that the second clause is being reported, for example placing two full sentences side by side with no linking word.

Correction: Connect the two clauses explicitly: Ugogo uthi umkhulu wafunda eThekwini. The word ukuthi or the shorter form uthi signals clearly that a report follows.

Common Mistake — Forcing a new class 1a/2a word into an unfamiliar concord pattern

Error: Treating a new kinship term such as umalume as though it required a different subject concord from ubaba, simply because it is a newly learned word.

Correction: New class 1a/2a nouns take the same concord pattern as any other member of that class: Umalume ufikile izolo. A concord audit prevents this error before it happens.

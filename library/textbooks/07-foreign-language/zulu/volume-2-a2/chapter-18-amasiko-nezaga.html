Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize and interpret a small set of common isiZulu proverbs, izaga, and understand the situations in which they are used.
  2. Use a controlled set of ideophones to add vividness to spoken and written description, particularly in narration.
  3. Recognize reduplicated forms in speech and reading, as a receptive feature of expressive isiZulu, without being required to produce new reduplicated patterns.
  4. Understand how proverbs and expressive language are used to comment indirectly on behavior, character, and community values.
  5. Continue a connected narrative, using sequencing, quoting, and support language from Chapters 16 and 17, now enriched with expressive and cultural detail.
  6. Read a short cultural passage that draws on proverbs and ideophones, and answer comprehension questions about its meaning.
  7. Write a short reflective paragraph that closes a narrative with a fitting proverb.

Communication Scenario

A week after his brother's illness, Sipho visits his grandmother to thank her for her patience, and to tell her, properly this time, how everything turned out. She listens closely, and, as she often does, responds not only with relief, but with a proverb that captures the lesson of the whole experience. Sipho realizes that Thandeka's help, his own worry, and his grandmother's calm each fit into a pattern of speech that his family has used for as long as he can remember, one that says a great deal in only a few words.

  1. Before you begin, think, in English, about a proverb or saying that your own family or community uses often. Notice what situation calls for it, and what it teaches.
  2. Look back briefly at the sequencing and quoting patterns from Chapter 16, and the need, obligation, and advice language from Chapter 17. This chapter shows how a narrative can be closed, and given deeper meaning, through a well chosen proverb.

Ideophones such as gxivu, cwaka, and nyelele are treated here as productive, controlled vocabulary. Reduplicated forms, such as those built from these same ideophones, remain receptive only at this stage, and are not required for independent production.

Pronunciation Review

No new click series is introduced in this chapter. This chapter reviews the plain, aspirated, and voiced click series once again, since ideophones such as gxivu combine a voiced click with an expressive, exaggerated delivery that differs slightly from ordinary speech rhythm.

Pronunciation Focus — Expressive delivery of ideophones

Ideophones are often spoken with more emphasis, a slightly longer vowel, or a sharper attack on the initial consonant, than ordinary words. Practice gxivu, cwaka, and nyelele first at a normal pace, then again with the extra vividness a storyteller would use, without losing the underlying click or consonant accuracy.

Grammar: Proverbs and Expressive Language

18.1 Understanding and using izaga, proverbs

Purpose: recognize common proverbs, understand the situations that call for them, and use a small set of them appropriately in conversation and writing.

A proverb, isaga, states a general truth in a fixed, memorized form. Learners are not expected to construct new proverbs, or to analyze every internal grammatical detail, but rather to recognize a proverb's meaning, and to use a small, controlled set of them at the close of a story, an explanation, or a piece of advice.

Language Focus

Proverbs are treated here as formulaic language, in line with the volume's status codes. Learners may quote a proverb accurately and use it in context, without being required to change its internal structure.

  • Umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu. A person is a person through other people. formulaic proverb, used to comment on community and interdependence
  • Ukubekezela kuyanqoba. Patience conquers, or, patience wins in the end. formulaic proverb, used after a difficult or worrying situation is resolved

18.2 Ideophones for vivid narration

Purpose: add vividness, sound, and movement to a narrative, particularly at a dramatic or turning point in the story.

An ideophone is a vivid, often sound symbolic word that describes a manner, sound, or sudden action. Ideophones usually appear together with a verb such as thi, to say or go, or enza, to do, and take on the expressive force of the moment they describe.

Language Building Block — Introducing an ideophone
Subject concord plus thi, or enza, plus ideophone
The ideophone itself does not take a subject concord. Instead, it follows a small, supporting verb, most often thi, which carries the ordinary subject concord and TAM marking.
Indlu yathi cwaka.
The house fell completely silent.
  • Isitsha sathi gxivu phansi. The dish fell to the ground with a heavy thud. si- (class 7 subject) + thi + gxivu, ideophone
  • Umfana wenza nyelele, wangena ekamelweni. The boy slipped away quietly, and went into the room. u- (class 1 subject) + enza + nyelele, ideophone, followed by sequencing verb

18.3 Reduplication, receptive recognition only

Purpose: recognize reduplicated forms in listening and reading, without being required to produce new reduplicated patterns at this stage.

Reduplication repeats part or all of a word to intensify or extend its meaning. In this volume, reduplicated forms remain receptive only. Learners should be able to recognize a reduplicated word in context, and understand roughly what it adds to the meaning, without needing to construct new reduplicated forms themselves.

  • Wahamba kancane kancane. He walked very slowly, little by little. reduplication of kancane, slowly, for receptive recognition only

Kancane kancane appeared already in Chapter 16 as a fixed, formulaic expression. This chapter simply names the pattern as reduplication, for recognition, without asking learners to build new reduplicated forms.

Model Dialogue

Isimo: USipho uvakashele ugogo wakhe, emtshela ukuthi umfowabo usephile, futhi embonga ngesineke sakhe.
Setting: Sipho visits his grandmother, tells her his brother has recovered, and thanks her for her patience.

USIPHO Gogo, ngifikile ukuzokutshela ukuthi umfowethu usephile. Udokotela wathi angeke aphinde agule kalula. Grandmother, I've come to tell you my brother has recovered. The doctor said he won't easily fall sick again.
UGOGO Ngiyabonga, Nkosi yami. Ngangikhathazekile kakhulu, kodwa ngangazi ukuthi ukubekezela kuyanqoba. Thank you, my Lord. I was very worried, but I knew that patience conquers in the end.
Indlu yathi cwaka okwesikhashana, ugogo ecabanga.
The house falls completely silent for a moment, as grandmother thinks.
UGOGO Uyabona, mzukulu, umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu. UThandeka wenza kahle ngokukusiza, futhi nawe wenza kahle ngokungixoxela. You see, grandchild, a person is a person through other people. Thandeka did well by helping you, and you did well by telling me.
USIPHO Kunjalo, Gogo. Ekuqaleni ngangesaba, ngase ngenza nyelele ngingakutsheli lutho. That's true, Grandmother. At first I was afraid, and I almost slipped away quietly without telling you anything.
UGOGO Yingakho ngithi, kufanele sihlale phansi njalo, sikhulume, kunokuba sithulele izinto ezikhathazayo. That's why I say, we should always sit down and talk, rather than staying silent about worrying things.

This dialogue combines two formulaic proverbs, ukubekezela kuyanqoba and umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu, with the ideophones cwaka and nyelele, alongside the obligation and sequencing patterns from Chapters 16 and 17.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Isaga sokuqala esisho ugogo, sithini? (What does the first proverb grandmother says mean?)
  2. USipho wayecabanga ukwenzani, ekuqaleni? (What was Sipho thinking of doing, at first?)
  3. Ugogo uthi, kufanele senzeni, kunokuthula? (What does grandmother say they should do, instead of staying silent?)

Isaga Nendaba Yaso — A Proverb and Its Story

Kudala, kwakukhona umfana owayehlala eyedwa, engathandi ukucela usizo kubantu. Ngelinye ilanga, wawa phansi wathi gxivu, esemsebenzini, kodwa akekho owayebona. Writing Workshop

In this chapter, you will write a short reflective paragraph, closing a narrative with a proverb, and including at least one ideophone, drawing on the sequencing patterns from Chapter 16.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Continue, or invent, a short story about a moment of worry, help, or hardship, similar to those in this chapter and the last, and close it with a proverb that fits the lesson of the story.

  1. Plan your story. In English, note the situation, the moment of difficulty, and the lesson learned.
  2. Narrate the moment. Use the sequencing connectors from Chapter 16 to move the story forward.
  3. Add an ideophone. Use gxivu, cwaka, or nyelele, with thi or enza, at the story's most dramatic moment.
  4. Close with a proverb. Choose umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu, or ukubekezela kuyanqoba, or another proverb from this chapter, and use it to sum up the lesson.
  5. Audit your draft. Confirm that your sequencing, ideophone, and proverb are used correctly, and that the paragraph reads as a connected reflection, not a list of separate sentences.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • The paragraph narrates a connected sequence of events, using familiar connectors.
  • At least one ideophone is used correctly, with thi or enza.
  • A fitting proverb closes the paragraph, and matches the lesson of the story.
  • Standard isiZulu spelling, capitalization, and punctuation are used throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Proverbs as indirect comment

Proverbs are often used to comment on a situation without stating an opinion too directly, particularly across generations. A grandmother offering ukubekezela kuyanqoba after a worrying event is both comforting a family member and gently affirming a shared value, rather than lecturing.

Culture in Context — Ubuntu and community interdependence

Umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu is closely associated with the wider philosophy of ubuntu, which emphasizes that a person's identity and wellbeing are formed through relationships with others. This proverb often appears at moments when community members have supported one another through a difficulty, as in this chapter's dialogue.

Regional Note — Proverb variation across families and regions

Many isiZulu proverbs exist in slightly different wordings across families, regions, and generations. The forms taught in this chapter are widely recognized and appropriate for learners, though a speaker's own family may phrase a similar idea somewhat differently.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Changing the wording of a proverb

Error: Altering the fixed wording of a proverb, for example rearranging umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu, so that it no longer matches the recognized form.

Correction: Treat proverbs as fixed, formulaic expressions, and reproduce them exactly, for example umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu, rather than a rearranged or partial version.

Common Mistake — Giving the ideophone its own subject concord

Error: Attaching a subject concord directly to an ideophone, as though it were an ordinary verb, for example treating gxivu as if it could stand alone with a subject prefix.

Correction: Keep the subject concord and TAM marking on thi or enza, and let the ideophone follow unchanged, for example isitsha sathi gxivu, the dish fell with a thud, rather than a modified ideophone form.

Common Mistake — Treating reduplication as new productive grammar

Error: Attempting to build new reduplicated forms freely, beyond the formulaic examples already introduced, such as kancane kancane.

Correction: Recognize reduplicated forms receptively when reading or listening, and continue to use only the formulaic reduplicated expressions already taught, such as kancane kancane, slowly, little by little.

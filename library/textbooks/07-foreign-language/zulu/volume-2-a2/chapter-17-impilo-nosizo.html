Impilo Nosizo

Volume II, Chapter 17: Health, Help, and Social Support · Speak and Write Zulu: Practical Independence

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Say what you need, using idinga and related patterns, in a clear and natural way.
  2. Express obligation and necessity, using kufanele and kumele, in everyday and slightly more formal contexts.
  3. Ask for help politely, and offer help to someone else, using controlled request and offer patterns.
  4. Give simple advice, using ungase and kungcono, when a friend or family member describes a problem.
  5. Understand and use a small set of health related vocabulary, for describing common symptoms and everyday concerns.
  6. Continue a connected story, using the sequencing and quoting patterns from Chapter 16, to narrate a moment when help was needed or given.
  7. Write a short supportive message, offering help or advice to someone facing a problem.

Communication Scenario

A few days after hearing Thandeka's story about the meeting, Sipho calls her again, but this time the news is not about a meeting. His younger brother has not been feeling well, and Sipho is not sure whether the family needs to see a doctor, whether they can manage things at home, or what to say to their grandmother, who is worried. Thandeka listens, asks a few careful questions, and offers some practical advice, drawing on the same careful, respectful tone that shaped their earlier conversation. Between them, the two cousins move from describing a need, to weighing an obligation, to settling on a plan.

  1. Before you begin, think, in English, about a time you asked someone for advice about a health or family concern. Notice how you described the problem, and how the other person responded.
  2. Look back briefly at the applicative forms from Chapter 13, and the sequencing and quoting patterns from Chapter 16. This chapter shows you how these systems support a conversation about need and support.

Pronunciation Review

No new click series is introduced in this chapter. This chapter reviews the plain, aspirated, and voiced click series once again, since words such as ukukhathazeka and umtholampilo combine aspiration with familiar syllable patterns, and deserve careful, unhurried practice.

Pronunciation Focus — Aspiration in longer words

Longer words such as ukukhathazeka contain more than one aspirated consonant. Practice the word slowly, syllable by syllable, then at a natural pace, keeping each aspirated sound clearly audible, rather than letting it soften as the word speeds up.

Grammar: Need, Obligation, and Offering Help

17.1 Expressing need with idinga

Purpose: state clearly what a person needs, whether an object, an action, or a form of support.

Dinga functions as an ordinary verb, taking a subject concord in the usual way, and it can be followed either by a noun phrase or by a further clause describing what is needed.

Language Focus

Ngidinga introduces a need belonging to the speaker, and the subject concord changes for other people in the usual way, giving forms such as udinga, you need, or udinga, he or she needs, for class 1 subjects.

  • Umfowethu udinga ukuphumula. My brother needs to rest. u- (class 1 subject) + dinga + infinitive
  • Sidinga usizo lwakho. We need your help. si- (we) + dinga + noun phrase

17.2 Obligation with kufanele and kumele

Purpose: express that an action is necessary, expected, or the right thing to do.

Kufanele and kumele both introduce a clause describing the action that is necessary. The subject of the following clause carries its own subject concord, so the obligation form itself does not change for person or class.

Language Building Block — Stating an obligation
Kufanele, or kumele, plus subject concord plus verb
Kufanele and kumele stay the same regardless of who has the obligation. The following clause carries the subject concord that agrees with the person who must act.
Kufanele simfonele udokotela.
We must phone the doctor for him.
  • Kumele aphumule ekhaya namuhla. He should rest at home today. kumele + a- (class 1 subject) + phumula
  • Kufanele sihambe naye emtholampilo. We must go with him to the clinic. kufanele + si- (we) + hamba, with applicative style companion phrase naye

17.3 Requesting and offering help

Purpose: ask someone for assistance politely, and offer assistance to someone else in a natural way.

Ngicela, I would like to ask, or please, softens a request, and can be followed by a further verb describing the help needed. Offers often use ngingakusiza, may I help you, or ake ngikusize, let me help you, both of which draw on the applicative pattern introduced in Chapter 13.

  • Ngicela ungisize ngalolu daba. Please help me with this matter. ngicela + u- (you) + applicative -el- + sika, to help
  • Ake ngikusize ngokushayela udokotela. Let me help you by calling the doctor. ake, softener + ngi- (I) + ku- (object concord, you) + applicative -el-

17.4 Giving advice with ungase and kungcono

Purpose: suggest a course of action gently, without stating it as a strict obligation.

Ungase softens a suggestion, presenting it as a possibility rather than a requirement, while kungcono introduces the idea that one option would be better than another. Both are common when a friend or family member describes a problem and expects thoughtful, unforced advice rather than an instruction.

  • Ungase umyise emtholampilo, uma engatholi kangcono. You might take him to the clinic, if he does not get better. ungase + object concord m- (him) + yisa, to take
  • Kungcono nimtshele ugogo, ukuze angakhathazeki. It would be better to tell grandmother, so that she does not worry. kungcono + ni- (you plural) + tshela, to tell

Model Dialogue

Isimo: USipho ushayela ucingo kuThandeka, ekhathazekile ngomfowabo ogulayo, futhi ecela iseluleko sakhe.
Setting: Sipho phones Thandeka, worried about his younger brother who is unwell, and asks for her advice.

USIPHO Thandeka, ngicela usizo lwakho. Umfowethu ugula kusukela izolo, futhi angazi ukuthi kufanele senzeni. Thandeka, please, I need your help. My brother has been sick since yesterday, and I don't know what we should do.
UTHANDEKA Kwenzekani kahle, Sipho? Une mikhuhlane, noma kunye enye into? What exactly is happening, Sipho? Does he have a cold, or is it something else?
USipho uphefumula kanzima, ekhuluma kancane kancane.
Sipho breathes with difficulty, speaking slowly.
USIPHO Une mikhuhlane, futhi ukhala ekhanda. Base kwathi izolo ntambama, wathi, "Angisezwa kahle." He has a cold, and a headache. Then yesterday afternoon, he said, "I don't feel well at all."
UTHANDEKA Kumele aphumule kahle, futhi anwabe amanzi amaningi. Kodwa uma engatholi kangcono kusasa, ungase umyise emtholampilo. He should rest properly, and drink plenty of water. But if he does not get better by tomorrow, you might take him to the clinic.
USIPHO Kufanele ngitshele ugogo yini? Ngesaba ukuthi angakhathazeka kakhulu. Should I tell grandmother? I'm afraid she will worry too much.
UTHANDEKA Kungcono nimtshele, ngoba uzokhathazeka kakhudlwana uma engazi lutho. Ake ngikusize, ngishayele udokotela wakithi. It would be better to tell her, because she will worry even more if she knows nothing. Let me help you, I'll call our family doctor.
USIPHO Ngiyabonga kakhulu, Thandeka. Ngathi angeke ngazi ukuthi kufanele ngenzeni. Thank you very much, Thandeka. I really did not know what I should do.

This dialogue combines idinga and dinga forms, the obligation words kufanele and kumele, the advice forms ungase and kungcono, and one applicative offer of help, ake ngikusize, alongside the sequencing connector base from Chapter 16.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Umfowabo kaSipho ugula njani? (How is Sipho's brother sick?)
  2. UThandeka usikeluleka ngani uSipho, uma umfowabo engatholi kangcono? (What does Thandeka advise Sipho, if his brother does not get better?)
  3. UThandeka uzomsiza ngani uSipho? (How will Thandeka help Sipho?)

Reading

Read the following short message, which combines need, obligation, and advice language with a relative clause and a sequencing connector from earlier chapters. Read it aloud twice before answering the comprehension questions.

Umlayezo osusela kuThandeka, oya kuSipho

"Sipho, ngishayele udokotela owelapha umndeni wakithi. Uthe kumele umfowenu aphumule izinsuku ezimbili, futhi anwabe amanzi amaningi. Kufanele nimbheke kahle, base nimtshele uma engakabi ngcono kusasa ntambama. Ungasakhathazeki kakhulu, kodwa yiba nesineke."

Sipho, I called the doctor who treats our family. She said your brother should rest for two days, and drink plenty of water. You must watch him carefully, then tell me if he is not better by tomorrow afternoon. Don't worry too much, but be patient.

"Udokotela owelapha umndeni wakithi" uses a relative clause from Chapter 11 to describe the family doctor.

Writing Workshop

In this chapter, you will write a short supportive message, offering advice or help to someone facing a small, everyday problem, using at least one need form, one obligation form, and one advice form.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Choose a small, everyday problem, being unwell, feeling tired, having too much work, or another situation of your choice, and write a short message offering advice and help to the person facing it.

  1. Plan your message. In English, note the problem, and one or two pieces of advice you want to give.
  2. Name the need. Use ngidinga, udinga, or a related form to state clearly what is needed.
  3. State an obligation. Use kufanele or kumele to describe what the person should do.
  4. Add a softer suggestion. Use ungase or kungcono, to offer a gentler, optional piece of advice.
  5. Offer your help. Use a phrase such as ake ngikusize, or ngingakusiza, to offer direct assistance.
  6. Audit your draft. Confirm that your need, obligation, and advice forms are used correctly, and that the tone remains warm and supportive throughout.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • The message states a clear need, using idinga or dinga.
  • At least one obligation form, kufanele or kumele, is used correctly.
  • At least one advice form, ungase or kungcono, is used correctly.
  • The message includes an offer of direct help.
  • Standard isiZulu spelling, capitalization, and punctuation are used throughout.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: stating obligation

With a partner, take turns describing a small everyday problem, and responding with a sentence using kufanele or kumele. Alternate roles so that each partner both describes a problem and states an obligation.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: asking for and offering help

Working with a partner, one of you describes a problem you are facing, real or invented, and asks for help. The other partner responds with a piece of advice, using ungase or kungcono, and then offers direct help.

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself describing a small problem, then giving yourself advice aloud, as though speaking to a friend. Include at least one need form, one obligation form, and one advice form, then listen back and check your pronunciation.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Sharing worry within the family

Deciding when, and how, to share worrying news with an older family member, such as a grandmother, is often treated as a careful judgment, balancing honesty with a wish not to cause unnecessary distress. Phrases such as kungcono nimtshele, it would be better to tell her, reflect this careful weighing of concern and respect.

Culture in Context — Offering help without being asked

Offering direct, practical help, such as making a phone call on someone's behalf, is commonly seen as a natural and expected form of support among close family and community members, rather than as an intrusion. A phrase such as ake ngikusize, let me help you, is often welcomed rather than questioned.

Regional Note — Kufanele and kumele in different settings

Some speakers use kufanele and kumele almost interchangeably in everyday speech, while others reserve kufanele for slightly more careful or formal contexts, such as written messages or public announcements. Both forms are correct, and either is appropriate for this chapter's tasks.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing need and obligation

Error: Using kufanele where idinga would fit better, so that a simple need is expressed as though it were a strict duty, for example saying kufanele amanzi instead of describing the need directly.

Correction: Use idinga or dinga to describe a plain need, and reserve kufanele or kumele for genuine obligations, for example udinga amanzi amaningi, he needs plenty of water, rather than an obligation form alone.

Common Mistake — Making every suggestion sound like a command

Error: Using kufanele or kumele for every piece of advice, even gentle, optional suggestions, so that the advice sounds more forceful than intended.

Correction: Use ungase or kungcono for softer, optional advice, for example ungase umyise emtholampilo, you might take him to the clinic, keeping kufanele and kumele for genuine necessity.

Common Mistake — Forgetting the object concord in an offer of help

Error: Offering help without including the object concord that refers to the person being helped, for example saying ngingasiza instead of clearly including the person.

Correction: Include the object concord for the person receiving help, for example ngingakusiza, may I help you, where ku- refers clearly to the listener.

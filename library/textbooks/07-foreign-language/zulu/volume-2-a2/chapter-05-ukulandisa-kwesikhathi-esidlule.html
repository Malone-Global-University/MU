Volume II, Chapter 5: Past Narration and Sequence · Speak and Write Zulu: Practical Independence

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Distinguish recent past meaning from remote past meaning, and choose the correct form for each, in familiar work and study contexts.
  2. Form and use the resultative or perfect past, to describe a present state that came from a completed action.
  3. Sequence two or more past events in a short narrative, using a simple connector, rather than a string of unconnected sentences.
  4. Ask and answer simple questions about when something happened, using vocabulary and question patterns already active from Chapters 1 through 4.
  5. Continue using infinitives, noun phrases, and simple commands from Chapter 4, now inside connected past narration.
  6. Read and listen to a short narrative that moves between recent, remote, and resultative past meanings.
  7. Write a short, sequenced account of something that happened recently and something that happened longer ago.
  8. Continue applying the concord audit habit, now checking subject concords across every past form used in a single narrative.

Communication Scenario

On Tuesday, Zanele tells Nomvula about her day, and then, later, about a much older memory from when she first started her job. Nomvula asks careful questions to work out whether Zanele is describing something that happened this morning, something that happened long ago, or something that is still true because of what happened. The two friends move easily between these different kinds of past meaning as they talk, the way people naturally do in real conversation.

  1. Before you begin, think, in English, about the difference between saying "I finished my report" and saying "I have finished my report, so it is ready now." Notice that the second sentence points to a present result.
  2. List, in English, one thing that happened to you today, and one thing that happened to you a long time ago. You will describe both in isiZulu by the end of this chapter.

Pronunciation and Click Review

The words kudala and izolo both reward steady vowel clarity, since a rushed pronunciation can blur the distinction between similar-sounding time words.

Pronunciation Focus — Keeping past-tense endings clear

As you begin producing several different past forms in the same conversation, listen closely to the final vowel and any final syllable of each verb. A small change at the end of the word often carries the difference between recent past, remote past, and resultative past. Say ngafunda, ngafundile, and ngiyafunda slowly, one after another, and notice that each final portion sounds distinct, even though the beginning of the word stays similar.

Grammar: Recent, Remote, and Resultative Past

5.1 Recent past

Purpose: describe something that happened earlier today, yesterday, or within the last few days, still felt close to the present moment.

The recent past is formed with the subject concord, followed by the marker -a-, followed by the verb root, followed by a final vowel that changes according to the root. For the roots used so far in this course, the recent past of -funda is ngafunda, meaning I studied, or I read, referring to something that happened recently.

Language Building Block — Recent past
subject concord + -a- + root + final vowel
The recent past places an action close to the present moment, often within the same day or the last few days. It answers the question, what did you do, when the answer still feels close.
Izolo, ngabhala imibiko emikhulu.
Yesterday, I wrote large reports.

5.2 Remote past

Purpose: describe something that happened further back in time, no longer felt close to the present moment.

The remote past adds the marker -ka- before the verb root, together with the subject concord, and closes with the vowel -a. This form signals distance from the present, and is often used together with a time word such as kudala or ngonyaka odlule.

  • Kudala, ngangisebenza ehhovisi elincane. Long ago, I used to work in a small office. remote past, distant habitual state
  • Ngonyaka odlule, uZanele wakafunda esikoleni esikhulu. Last year, Zanele studied at a big school. remote past, single distant event

Note on scope: This chapter presents the remote past as a controlled system for describing distant single events and distant ongoing states, paired with the time words above. Full paradigm control across every verb class, and combination with object concords, remains outside this chapter's scope.

5.3 Resultative past

Purpose: describe a present state that exists because of a completed action, similar in meaning to the English present perfect.

The resultative past is formed with the subject concord, the verb root, and the ending -ile. It points to a completed action whose result is still true right now. This is different from the recent past, which simply reports that something happened, without necessarily pointing to a present result.

Language Building Block — Resultative past
subject concord + root + -ile
The resultative past connects a finished action to a present state. Compare ngafunda, I studied, with ngifundile, I have studied, meaning the studying is done and the result stands now.
Ngiwuvulile umnyango, manje singena.
I have opened the door, now we can go in.
  • Ngibhalile imibiko, manje ngiyaphumula. I have written the reports, now I am resting.
  • Uthisha uvalile igumbi lokufundela. The teacher has closed the classroom.

5.4 Sequencing two events with bese

Purpose: link two past events into a short, connected narrative, rather than presenting them as two separate, unconnected sentences.

To sequence events, state the first event using a recent past or remote past form, then add bese, meaning and then, followed by the subject concord and verb root for the second event. This pattern already appeared once in the Chapter 4 reading passage, and is now activated for full productive use.

  • Ngavula ihhovisi, bese ngahlala esihlalweni sami. I opened the office, and then I sat in my chair.
  • UZanele wabhala imibiko, bese esiza umqashi wakhe. Zanele wrote reports, and then helped her employer.
Language Focus

A narrative may mix recent past, remote past, and resultative past in the same short passage, the way a speaker naturally moves between them. A narrator might open with a remote past state, move to a recent past sequence of events, and close with a resultative past to show a present result. This chapter's reading and listening passages model that natural movement, without requiring you to produce every combination yourself.

Model Dialogue

Isimo: NgoLwesibili, uZanele uxoxela uNomvula ngosuku lwakhe, kanye nangesikhathi esidala.
Setting: On Tuesday, Zanele tells Nomvula about her day, and about an older memory.

UNOMVULA Sawubona, Zanele. Wenzeni namuhla ehhovisi? Hello, Zanele. What did you do today at the office?
UZANELE Ngavula ihhovisi ekuseni, bese ngabhala imibiko emikhulu. Manje ngiwabhalile wonke, ngiyaphumula. I opened the office in the morning, and then I wrote large reports. Now I have written all of them, I am resting.
UNOMVULA Kuhle. Kudala, wawusebenzela liphi ihhovisi? Good. Long ago, which office did you use to work for?
UZANELE Kudala, ngangisebenza ehhovisi elincane, phansi kwesitolo esikhulu. Ngonyaka odlule, ngathola lo msebenzi omusha. Long ago, I used to work in a small office, below a big shop. Last year, I got this new job.
UNOMVULA Uyawuthanda kakhulu umsebenzi wakho omusha, angithi? You like your new job a lot, don't you?
UZANELE Yebo, ngiyawuthanda kakhulu. Manje, ngicela usebenzise le sihlalo, ngiyakubonisa incwadi engiyibhalile izolo. Yes, I like it very much. Now, please use this chair, I will show you a letter I wrote yesterday.

This dialogue moves between recent past, resultative past, and remote past, and uses bese once to sequence two events. Every noun class and noun phrase pattern used was already active by the end of Chapter 4.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. UZanele wenzeni ekuseni namuhla? (What did Zanele do this morning?)
  2. Manje, uZanele wenzani? (What is Zanele doing now?)
  3. Kudala, uZanele wayesebenzela liphi ihhovisi? (Long ago, which office did Zanele work for?)
  4. Wathola nini umsebenzi wakhe omusha? (When did she get her new job?)

Writing Workshop

In this chapter, you will write a short, sequenced narrative describing something that happened recently and something that happened longer ago, combining these with the noun phrase and infinitive patterns from earlier chapters.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write six to eight sentences describing your day, or an invented day, including at least one recent past sentence, one remote past sentence, one resultative past sentence, and one use of bese to sequence two events.

  1. List your events. In English, jot down one recent event, and one older memory, that you will describe.
  2. Open with the older memory. Use a remote past sentence, paired with a time word such as kudala.
  3. Move to recent events. Use two recent past sentences, linked with bese, to sequence what happened.
  4. Show a present result. Close with a resultative past sentence, showing what is true now, because of what happened.
  5. Add one noun phrase. Include at least one noun phrase with an adjective and a possessive, from Chapter 3.
  6. Audit your draft. Check every subject concord against its noun class, confirm each past form matches its intended meaning, and confirm bese links two related events, before writing your final version.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • At least one sentence uses the recent past correctly.
  • At least one sentence uses the remote past correctly, with a matching time word.
  • At least one sentence uses the resultative past correctly.
  • Bese is used at least once to sequence two events.
  • Every subject concord matches its noun's class.
  • No unscheduled vocabulary or grammar appears beyond Volume I and Chapters 1 through 5.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: sorting past meanings

Your partner says a short past-tense sentence. Decide, out loud, whether it describes something recent, something remote, or a present result, then explain, in English, which word or ending told you so.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: two memories

Working with a partner, tell a short story about something you did recently, and then something you did long ago, using bese at least once to sequence events within each part. Your partner asks at least two follow-up questions using nini or kuphi.

Useful checking expressions: Kwenzeka nini lokho? (When did that happen?)  |  Manje kunjani? (How is it now?)  |  Bese, kwenzekani? (And then, what happened?)

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself narrating the events you wrote about in the Writing Workshop, as a spoken account. Listen back, and check that your recent past, remote past, and resultative past forms all remain accurate.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Sharing memories and daily events

Moving easily between a recent event, an older memory, and a present result is a natural part of everyday storytelling in many isiZulu speaking communities, much as it is in English. Learners should feel comfortable weaving these different kinds of past meaning together in ordinary conversation, rather than treating each one as a separate, isolated exercise.

Culture in Context — Listening patiently to a full account

When someone shares a longer memory, particularly with an older relative, colleague, or friend, patient, attentive listening is generally valued, and interruptions are often kept to natural pauses, rather than the middle of a sentence. This pattern is worth observing as you practise the listening and speaking tasks in this chapter.

Regional Note — Variation in past-tense pronunciation

Some speakers pronounce certain past-tense forms slightly differently depending on region or generation, particularly in fast, connected speech. This textbook models a clear, standard pronunciation suitable for learners. Keep listening for small variations as your listening skills develop in later chapters and volumes.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing recent past with resultative past

Error: Using the recent past when the sentence actually points to a present result, for example saying Ngabhala imibiko when the meaning intended is that the reports are finished and ready now.

Correction: Use the resultative past, with -ile, when the point is a present state caused by a finished action: Ngibhalile imibiko, not simply Ngabhala imibiko, when the reports being ready right now is the main point.

Common Mistake — Leaving out the time word with the remote past

Error: Using a remote past form without a supporting time word, leaving the listener unsure how far back the event happened, for example saying Ngangisebenza ehhovisi with no time reference at all.

Correction: Pair the remote past with a clear time word, such as kudala or ngonyaka odlule: Kudala, ngangisebenza ehhovisi elincane, so the distance in time is clear.

Common Mistake — Stringing events together without bese

Error: Placing two past sentences side by side with no connector at all, for example saying Ngavula ihhovisi. Ngahlala esihlalweni., as two separate, unconnected statements.

Correction: Link related events with bese, to show they happened in sequence: Ngavula ihhovisi, bese ngahlala esihlalweni sami, not two disconnected sentences.

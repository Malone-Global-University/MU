Ukunikeza Izizathu Nemibono

Volume II, Chapter 12: Reasons, Explanations, and Opinions · Speak and Write Zulu: Practical Independence

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Give a reason for a statement or an action, using ngoba to introduce the reason.
  2. State a simple opinion or belief, using ngicabanga ukuthi and similar complement-clause patterns with ukuthi.
  3. Combine a reason or an opinion with a relative clause from Chapter 11, to say exactly what or whom the reason or opinion concerns.
  4. Recognize and use, in a controlled way, the causative extension, for talking about making or causing something to happen.
  5. Ask someone for their reason or their opinion, and respond appropriately.
  6. Read and listen to short passages that explain a decision, a preference, or an opinion.
  7. Write a short paragraph that states an opinion and supports it with a reason.

Communication Scenario

Now that Nomvula knows about Thandeka's new neighbor, the conversation turns from simply describing him to explaining why Thandeka likes him, why she prefers his shop, and what she thinks other people should know. Reasons and opinions, not just descriptions, become the center of the conversation.

  1. Before you begin, think, in English, about how you explain a preference to a friend. Notice that you usually name the thing you prefer, then give a reason, and sometimes add an opinion about it.
  2. Look back briefly at the relative clauses you built in Chapter 11. This chapter asks you to use those same relative clauses to say, precisely, what your reason or opinion is about.

Where This Chapter Fits

The causative extension is introduced here for the first time, in a small, controlled way, so that it can be developed further in later chapters alongside the applicative and passive extensions already underway.

Pronunciation Review

No new click series is introduced in this chapter. This chapter reviews the aspirated click series, ch, qh, and xh, and reminds you to keep aspiration and click articulation both clear when a reason clause lengthens a sentence.

Pronunciation Focus — Rhythm across a reason clause

When a sentence grows longer because it includes a reason introduced by ngoba, learners sometimes rush the second half. Read the model dialogue below aloud, and keep the penultimate lengthening clear in both halves of the sentence, not only at the very end.

Grammar: Reasons and Opinions

12.1 Giving a reason with ngoba

Purpose: explain why something is true, why someone does something, or why you feel a certain way.

To give a reason in isiZulu, add a second clause after the main statement, introduced by ngoba, meaning because. The verb that follows ngoba keeps its ordinary subject concord and tense marking, so you do not need any new verb form to use this pattern. What is new is the connector itself, and the habit of building a two-part sentence around it.

Language Focus

Ngoba does not change the verb that follows it. Unlike the relative concord from Chapter 11, which replaces the subject concord, ngoba simply connects two ordinary clauses. This makes it one of the easiest new connectors to add to your existing sentences.

  • Ngithanda ukuthenga esitolo sakhe, ngoba umsizi wakhe unesineke. I like buying at his shop, because his assistant is patient. Main clause + ngoba + ordinary subject-concord clause
  • UThandeka uyamthanda umakhelwane wakhe omusha, ngoba usiza abantu ngokushesha. Thandeka likes her new neighbor, because he helps people quickly. Main clause with object concord + ngoba + reason clause

12.2 Stating an opinion with ukuthi

Purpose: introduce what you think, believe, or notice, as a complete idea inside your sentence.

To state an opinion, use a verb such as cabanga (think) or bona (see, notice), followed by ukuthi, meaning that, and then a complete clause expressing the opinion itself. You met a small number of fixed expressions using ukuthi in earlier chapters, as formulaic vocabulary. This chapter develops that pattern into a productive tool you can use with new content.

Language Building Block — The opinion pattern
Ngi- (I) + cabanga or bona (opinion verb) + ukuthi + complete clause stating the opinion.
Begin with the opinion verb in the first person, add ukuthi, and then state the opinion as its own clause, with its own subject concord and tense marking. The clause after ukuthi behaves like an ordinary sentence.
Ngicabanga ukuthi umakhelwane omusha kaThandeka uyisenzo esihle kulendawo.
I think that Thandeka's new neighbor is a good addition to this area.

You can combine a reason and an opinion in one sentence, and you can combine either one with a relative clause from Chapter 11, to say exactly what or whom your opinion or reason concerns.

  • Ngicabanga ukuthi isitolo esisha esiseduze nesitobhi siyingcono, ngoba umsizi osebenza lapho unesineke. I think that the new shop near the transport rank is better, because the assistant who works there is patient. Opinion clause + relative clause (Class 7, from Chapter 11) + ngoba reason clause + relative clause (Class 1, from Chapter 11)

12.3 First look at the causative extension

Purpose: talk about making or causing an action to happen, rather than performing the action yourself.

The causative extension changes a verb root so that it means to make someone do something, or to cause something to happen. This chapter introduces the causative in a small, controlled set of verbs only, so that you can recognize the pattern and use a handful of common causative forms correctly, without yet building new causative verbs freely.

Plain verbMeaningCausative formMeaning
fundato learn, to studyfundisato teach, to cause to learn
sindato be saved, to survivesindisato save, to help someone survive
hlalato sit, to livehlalisato seat someone, to settle someone

Notice the extra -is- added before the final vowel in each causative form. This chapter asks you only to recognize and use these three causative verbs correctly. Building new causative forms from other verb roots will be developed in a later chapter.

  • Umsizi omusha ufundisa abantwana esitolo sikayise. The new assistant teaches the children at his father's shop. Causative fundisa, Class 1 relative concord from Chapter 11 in omusha

Model Dialogue

Isimo: Ngemuva kwesikhathi, uThandeka noNomvula bahlala phansi, becabanga ngomakhelwane omusha kaThandeka nesitolo sakhe.
Setting: Some time later, Thandeka and Nomvula sit down, thinking about Thandeka's new neighbor and his shop.

UNOMVULA Ngicabanga ukuthi umakhelwane wakho omusha uyisenzo esihle kulendawo. Yini umbono wakho? I think that your new neighbor is a good addition to this area. What is your opinion?
UTHANDEKA Nami ngiyavuma. Ngithanda kakhulu isitolo sakhe, ngoba umsizi osebenza lapho unesineke, futhi uyabafundisa abantwana uma bezofuna usizo. I agree too. I like his shop very much, because the assistant who works there is patient, and teaches the children if they come looking for help.
UNOMVULA Kungani abantu abaningi bethanda ukuya khona, ucabanga? Why do you think many people like going there?
UTHANDEKA Ngibona ukuthi kungenxa yezizathu ezimbili, isitolo esiseduze, nomsizi osindisa isikhathi sabo ngokusheshisa umsebenzi. I see that it is for two reasons, a shop that is nearby, and an assistant who saves their time by working quickly.
UNomvula uyavuma ekhanda, ecabanga ngesizathu sokuqala uThandeka asichazile.
Nomvula nods in agreement, thinking about the first reason Thandeka explained.
UNOMVULA Kulungile, ngiyaqonda manje. Ngicabanga ukuthi nami ngizoya khona ngoba sengizwile izizathu zakho. Alright, I understand now. I think I will go there too, because I have now heard your reasons.

This dialogue combines ngoba reason clauses, ukuthi opinion clauses, and the Class 1, 7, and 9 relative concords from Chapter 11, alongside the causative verb fundisa and sindisa.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Yini umbono kaNomvula ngomakhelwane kaThandeka? (What is Nomvula's opinion about Thandeka's neighbor?)
  2. Kungani uThandeka ethanda isitolo somakhelwane wakhe? (Why does Thandeka like her neighbor's shop?)
  3. Yiziphi izizathu ezimbili uThandeka azichazile? (What are the two reasons Thandeka explained?)

Reading

Writing Workshop

In this chapter, you will write a short paragraph that states an opinion and supports it with reasons, using at least one relative clause from Chapter 11 to say exactly what your opinion concerns.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write six to eight sentences stating your opinion about a person, a place, or an activity, real or invented. Support your opinion with at least two reasons introduced by ngoba, and include at least one relative clause from the five classes activated in Chapter 11.

  1. Choose your topic. In English, note the person, place, or activity you want to give an opinion about.
  2. State your opinion. Open with ngicabanga ukuthi or ngibona ukuthi, followed by a complete clause.
  3. Give your first reason. Add ngoba and a clause explaining why you hold that opinion.
  4. Give a second reason. Add a second ngoba clause, or connect it with a coordinator from an earlier chapter.
  5. Add one relative clause. Use a relative concord from Chapter 11 to identify exactly what or whom a reason concerns.
  6. Audit your draft. Confirm that every ukuthi clause and every ngoba clause keeps its own ordinary subject concord, and that any relative concord matches its noun class correctly.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • The opinion is introduced clearly with ngicabanga ukuthi or ngibona ukuthi.
  • At least two reasons are given, each introduced by ngoba.
  • At least one relative clause from Chapter 11 identifies what or whom a reason concerns.
  • Every relative concord used matches the noun class it describes.
  • Standard isiZulu spelling, capitalization, and punctuation are used throughout.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: asking why

With a partner, take turns making a simple statement about a person, place, or activity from an earlier chapter, then ask Kungani? (Why?) and answer with a ngoba clause, such as Ngithanda leyondawo, ngoba iseduze nomuzi wami.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: comparing opinions

Working with a partner, each choose an opinion about a shop, a neighbor, or a place familiar from earlier chapters. Share your opinion and two reasons, then listen to your partner's opinion, and note whether you agree or disagree, and why.

Useful checking expressions: Ngiyavumelana nawe. (I agree with you.)  |  Angikaqondi kahle. (I do not quite understand.)  |  Yini isizathu sakho? (What is your reason?)

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself stating an opinion about three different topics, each with at least one reason. Listen back, and check that each ngoba clause and each ukuthi clause keeps its ordinary subject concord.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Offering an opinion respectfully

When sharing an opinion, especially with someone older or with a person you do not know well, it is common to soften the statement with a phrase such as ngicabanga ukuthi (I think that), rather than stating the opinion as an unqualified fact. This leaves space for disagreement without appearing confrontational.

Culture in Context — Supporting an opinion with concrete reasons

In many everyday conversations, an opinion is expected to come with at least one concrete, practical reason, rather than standing alone. Saying that a shop is good, and then explaining why, is considered more persuasive and more complete than the opinion by itself.

Regional Note — Variation in how directly opinions are stated

Some speakers state opinions briefly and directly, while others prefer to build up to an opinion gradually through description and example, arriving at the opinion only near the end. Both approaches are common, and this textbook models a straightforward opinion-then-reasons structure to give learners clear practice with the pattern.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Treating ngoba like the relative concord

Error: Changing the subject concord of the verb after ngoba, as if ngoba worked like a relative concord, for example altering the ordinary subject concord in the reason clause instead of leaving it unchanged.

Correction: Keep the ordinary subject concord after ngoba: ngoba unesineke, with the normal subject concord u-, not a relative form.

Common Mistake — Dropping ukuthi before an opinion clause

Error: Joining an opinion verb directly to the following clause without ukuthi, for example saying ngicabanga umuntu lo muhle instead of including the connector.

Correction: Include ukuthi between the opinion verb and the clause that follows: ngicabanga ukuthi umuntu lo muhle, keeping the two clauses clearly connected.

Common Mistake — Building new causative forms freely

Error: Attempting to add -is- to a verb root not yet covered in this chapter's causative table, by guessing a pattern from fundisa or sindisa.

Correction: Use only fundisa, sindisa, and hlalisa productively at this stage. Building new causative forms from other roots will be developed in a later chapter.

Looking Ahead

You can now give a reason with ngoba, state an opinion with ukuthi, combine either one with a relative clause from Chapter 11, and recognize a small set of causative verbs. Together with your existing noun-class, concord, and locative control, this lets you explain, not only describe, the people and places around you.

Izindawo Nokuqondisa

Volume II, Chapter 9: Locatives and Spatial Description · Speak and Write Zulu: Practical Independence

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Form locative nouns from familiar class 5, 9, and 1a nouns, using the e-...-ini pattern and the related o- pattern.
  2. Ask and answer where a place is, using the locative question Kuphi and simple locative answers.
  3. Describe the position of one place relative to another, using near, far, and in-between spatial terms.
  4. Give simple directions to a shop, a clinic, or a transport rank, using motion verbs already active from earlier chapters.
  5. Place the market, clinic, and transport-rank vocabulary from Chapter 8 firmly on a described map of a neighborhood.
  6. Read and listen to short passages that describe where places are and how to reach them.
  7. Write a short set of directions from one familiar place to another, combining locatives with subject and object concords already active.
  8. Continue the concord audit habit, now checking locative agreement alongside subject and object concords.

Communication Scenario

A few days after her Saturday errands, Nomvula's cousin Thandeka arrives from out of town for a short visit. Thandeka does not know the neighborhood, so Nomvula draws a simple map and explains where the market, the clinic, and the transport rank actually are, and how to walk from the house to each one.

  1. Before you begin, think, in English, about how you usually give someone directions in your own neighborhood. Notice which words you rely on most, such as "near," "behind," "next to," or "straight ahead."
  2. Think of one place near your home and one place you visit often. You will practise describing where each one is by the end of this chapter.

Amagama — Vocabulary

Vocabulary: Places and Directions

This chapter introduces locative forms of nouns you already know, along with a small set of spatial and directional words. Each locative noun below is built from a noun whose class you have already practised, so the new material stays anchored to familiar ground.

Izindawo — Places, in their locative form

Base nounLocative formEnglish
imakethe (class 9)emaketheat, to, from the market
umtholampilo (class 3)emtholampiloat, to, from the clinic
isitobhi (class 7)esitobhiniat, to, from the transport rank
idolobha (class 5)edolobheniin, to, from town
ihhovisi (class 5)ehhovisiat, to, from the office
indlu (class 9)endliniat, to, from the house

Notice the pattern: most nouns take the prefix e- and, where the noun ends in a, add the suffix -ini. Isitobhi, which already ends in i, still takes -ini, giving esitobhini. This locative form does not change for tense, and it does not need a separate object concord, since the locative prefix and suffix already do that work.

Ukubuza indawo — Asking where a place is

isiZuluEnglishUse
Kuphi?Where is it?fixed question formula, does not change for noun class
Ise-...It is at, in...simple locative answer, subject concord plus locative noun

Like Yimalini from Chapter 8, Kuphi is a fixed question formula. It does not take an object concord and does not change according to the class of the place being asked about.

Pronunciation and Click Review

Locative nouns often stack several syllables onto a word you already know well, so the penultimate syllable can shift. Practise each pair below slowly first, then at natural speed, keeping the stressed syllable clear in both forms.

Pronunciation Focus — Stress shift in locative forms

Say idolobha slowly, then say edolobheni slowly, broken into e-do-lo-bhe-ni. Notice that the stressed, lengthened syllable moves to stay on the second-to-last syllable of the new, longer word. Practise the same shift with isitobhi and esitobhini. Keep the click in -tobhi- clear in both the base noun and the locative form, even as the word grows longer.

Grammar: Locatives and Spatial Description

9.1 Building a locative noun

Purpose: say where something is, is happening, or is going, without a separate preposition word.

IsiZulu builds location into the noun itself, rather than adding a separate word like "in" or "at" in front of it. Most nouns form their locative by adding the prefix e- and the suffix -ini, though a small number of nouns, including most class 1a and 2a human nouns, use the prefix ku- instead. This chapter focuses on the e-...-ini pattern, since it covers the market, clinic, transport-rank, office, and house vocabulary you already know.

Language Focus

A locative noun is complete on its own. Emakethe already means "at, to, or from the market," all at once. The exact meaning, at, to, or from, is carried by the surrounding verb, not by a separate word before emakethe.

9.2 Asking and answering where a place is

QuestionAnswerEnglish
Isitolo, sikuphi?Sise eduze nendlu.Where is the shop? It is near the house.
Umtholampilo, ukuphi?Ukude nedolobha.Where is the clinic? It is far from town.
Isitobhi, sikuphi?Sise phakathi kwesitolo nomtholampilo.Where is the transport rank? It is between the shop and the clinic.
Language Building Block — The location pattern
Noun + subject concord + kuphi? → subject concord + -se- + locative position or locative noun.
Name the place with its own subject concord, ask Kuphi, then answer with the copular -se- form, followed either by a spatial word such as eduze or kude, or by a second locative noun such as edolobheni.
Isitolo sikuphi? Sise eduze nendlu.
Where is the shop? It is near the house.
  • Emakethe kuseduze nesitobhi. The market is near the transport rank. locative noun emakethe, spatial word eduze
  • Ngihamba ngiye edolobheni, bese ngiqonda. I am going into town, and then I go straight. motion verb hamba, locative edolobheni, directional verb qonda
  • Jika ngakwesobunxele, bese ubona umtholampilo eceleni kwesitolo. Turn to the left, and then you will see the clinic next to the shop. directional verb jika, locative phrase eceleni kwesitolo

Notice that Kuphi, like Yimalini in Chapter 8, stays fixed. It is the answer, not the question, that carries the locative form and the spatial vocabulary.

Model Dialogue

Isimo: UThandeka ufike endlini kaNomvula, ecela ukwazi ukuthi izindawo ezahlukene zikuphi.
Setting: Thandeka has arrived at Nomvula's house, asking to know where different places are.

UTHANDEKA Sawubona, Nomvula. Ngicela ungitshele, isitolo sikuphi? Hello, Nomvula. Please tell me, where is the shop?
UNOMVULA Isitolo sise eduze nendlu, ngakwesokudla. The shop is near the house, on the right.
UTHANDEKA Kuhle. Kanti umtholampilo ukuphi? Good. And where is the clinic?
UNOMVULA Umtholampilo ukude kancane, kodwa uqonda nje edolobheni, bese ujika ngakwesobunxele. The clinic is a little far, but you just go straight into town, and then you turn to the left.
UTHANDEKA Ngizolithola kanjani isitobhi? How will I find the transport rank?
UNOMVULA Isitobhi sise phakathi kwesitolo nomtholampilo. Uzosibona ngokushesha. The transport rank is between the shop and the clinic. You will see it quickly.
UNomvula udwebela uThandeka imephu encane phezu kwephepha.
Nomvula draws Thandeka a small map on a piece of paper.
UTHANDEKA Ngiyabonga kakhulu. Manje sengiyazi ukuthi konke kukuphi. Thank you very much. Now I know where everything is.

This dialogue uses the fixed question Kuphi alongside the locative forms edolobheni and phakathi kwesitolo nomtholampilo, and reactivates the market, clinic, and transport-rank vocabulary from Chapter 8, now firmly placed on a map.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Isitolo sikuphi? (Where is the shop?)
  2. UThandeka ufanele ajike ngakuphi, ukuze afike emtholampilo? (Which way must Thandeka turn to reach the clinic?)
  3. Isitobhi sise phakathi kwezindawo ezimbili. Yiziphi? (The transport rank is between two places. Which two?)

Reading

Read the following short passage, which places Nomvula's neighborhood on the map, using the locative forms and spatial words from this chapter. One sentence includes a relative clause, given in bold. You are not expected to build a sentence like this yourself yet, only to understand it in context. Read the passage aloud twice before answering the comprehension questions.

Indawo yakwaNomvula — Nomvula's neighborhood

Indlu kaNomvula ise eduze nesitolo, ngakwesokudla. Emakethe, lapho uNomvula athenga khona imifino ngoMgqibelo, ise kude kancane, phesheya kwedolobha. Isitolo esiseduze nendlu simnika izinto azidingayo nsuku zonke. Umtholampilo ukude nendlu, kodwa uqonda nje edolobheni, ujike ngakwesobunxele, bese uwubona. Isitobhi, esisiza uNomvula ukuya edolobheni, sise phakathi kwesitolo nomtholampilo.

The bold sentence uses a relative clause, "esiseduze nendlu," meaning "which is near the house." Relative clauses like this one are introduced formally in a later chapter. For now, simply understand its meaning from context: the shop that is near the house.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Indlu kaNomvula ise kuphi kunesitolo? (Where is Nomvula's house in relation to the shop?)
  2. Emakethe kukuphi kunedolobha? (Where is the market in relation to town?)
  3. Yini okufanele uyenze ukuze uthole umtholampilo? (What must you do to reach the clinic?)

Writing Workshop

In this chapter, you will write a short set of directions from one familiar place to another, combining locatives with subject and object concords already active.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write six to eight sentences that describe where two or three familiar places are, relative to your home or to each other, and then give directions from one place to another. Use at least three locative nouns, and at least two spatial words such as eduze, kude, phambi kwa-, emuva kwa-, phakathi kwa-, or eceleni kwa-.

  1. Choose your places. In English, decide on two or three real or invented places near your home.
  2. Build the locative forms. Convert each place name into its locative form, checking the pattern from this chapter.
  3. Answer Kuphi. Write one sentence for each place, answering where it is, using the -se- pattern.
  4. Add a spatial relationship. Describe how the places relate to one another, using at least two different spatial words.
  5. Give simple directions. Write two or three sentences telling someone how to walk from one place to another, using qonda and jika.
  6. Audit your draft. Check that every locative noun is correctly formed, and that any object concords from earlier chapters still match their noun's class.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • At least three locative nouns are correctly formed, using e-...-ini or the appropriate pattern.
  • The question Kuphi is used at least once, correctly, without an object concord.
  • At least two spatial words are used to relate one place to another.
  • Directions include at least one motion verb, such as qonda or jika.
  • Any object concords carried over from Chapters 7 and 8 still match their noun's class.
  • No unscheduled relative clauses or unscheduled grammar appear beyond Volume I and Chapters 1 through 9.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Giving directions by landmark

In many South African neighborhoods, directions are commonly given by landmark, such as a shop, a church, or a school, rather than by street name or number alone, particularly outside larger city centers. Learners are encouraged to practise the landmark-based directions in this chapter, since this style is often the most natural way to be understood.

Culture in Context — Offering to accompany a visitor

Rather than only describing a route in words, it is common for a host to offer to walk part of the way with a visitor, particularly on a first visit to an unfamiliar area. This gesture is generally seen as helpful and welcoming, not as a sign that the directions themselves were unclear.

Regional Note — Variation in directional vocabulary

Some speakers and regions use additional directional expressions beyond qonda and jika, particularly in urban areas with frequent code-switching into English for terms such as "robot," meaning traffic light. This textbook models the clear, standard directional pattern suitable for learners at this stage, and later volumes will expand your recognition of regional variation.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Adding an object concord to Kuphi

Error: Inserting an object concord into the fixed location question, for example saying Sikuphi na with an extra concord marker before asking where the shop is.

Correction: Keep Kuphi fixed and unchanged, following only the subject concord of the place named: Isitolo sikuphi?, not a form with an added object concord, since the location question does not carry one.

Common Mistake — Dropping the -ini suffix

Error: Using only the e- prefix without the -ini suffix, for example saying edolobha instead of the full locative form.

Correction: Most locative nouns need both the prefix and the suffix together: edolobheni, not edolobha, unless the base noun already ends in a vowel that blocks the suffix in a specific irregular case.

Common Mistake — Building a relative clause too early

Error: Attempting to construct a new relative clause independently, for example trying to say "the clinic that is far" using an unscheduled relative concord, before relative clauses have been formally introduced for production.

Correction: For now, describe the clinic in two separate sentences instead: Umtholampilo ukude. Ukude nendlu., not an independently constructed relative clause, since relative-clause production is not yet active in this volume.

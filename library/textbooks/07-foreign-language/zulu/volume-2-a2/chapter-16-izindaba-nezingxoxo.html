Izindaba Nezingxoxo

Volume II, Chapter 16: Narrative Expansion and Dialogue · Speak and Write Zulu: Practical Independence

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Tell a short connected story, in the past, with events in a clear order.
  2. Use sequencing words such as kwathi, base, and ngemuva kwalokho, to link one event to the next.
  3. Use basic relative clauses, from Chapter 11, to describe people and things inside a story.
  4. Include a short stretch of quoted dialogue inside a narrative, and mark clearly who is speaking.
  5. Recognize a small set of ideophones that add vividness to storytelling, without needing to produce every form independently.
  6. Write a short story of several connected sentences, with a beginning, a middle, and an end.
  7. Connect the passive forms from Chapters 14 and 15, and the applicative forms from Chapter 13, to natural storytelling.

Communication Scenario

The rescheduled meeting from Chapter 15 has now taken place, and Thandeka wants to tell her cousin Sipho, who could not attend, what happened. A short notice or message states only the bare facts, but a story needs more, an order of events, a sense of who said what, and a few vivid touches that make the telling worth listening to. As Thandeka retells the afternoon, she moves between plain narration, quoted speech, and small expressive details, weaving together everything from earlier chapters into something closer to natural, connected speech.

  1. Before you begin, think, in English, about the last time you retold a friend's story to someone else. Notice how you ordered the events, and how you signalled which words were someone else's.
  2. Look back briefly at the relative clauses from Chapter 11, and the passive forms from Chapters 14 and 15. This chapter shows you how these systems combine naturally inside a story.

Pronunciation Review

No new click series is introduced in this chapter. This chapter reviews all click series encountered so far, plain, aspirated, and voiced, since storytelling at a natural pace tends to blur click articulation more than careful, single sentence reading does.

Pronunciation Focus — Keeping clicks clear at storytelling pace

When people tell a story, they often speak faster and with more expression than when they read a notice aloud. Practice telling this chapter's story once slowly, checking each click carefully, and once at a livelier, more natural storytelling pace, without letting the clicks disappear.

Grammar: Narrative Expansion and Dialogue

16.1 Sequencing events with kwathi, base, and ngemuva kwalokho

Purpose: link a series of past events into one connected story, instead of a list of separate sentences.

A story rarely presents events as isolated facts. Instead, one event leads naturally into the next, often marked by a small connecting word rather than by a full new subject and verb every time. Kwathi, base, and ngemuva kwalokho all move a story forward, but they differ slightly in register and feel.

Language Focus

Kwathi often opens a new turn in the story, and can be followed directly by a description of what happened. Base is a lighter, more conversational connector, common in spoken narration. Ngemuva kwalokho is more formal, and works well in writing.

ConnectorMeaningRegister
kwathithen, and so it happened thatneutral, common in both speech and writing
basethen, after thatinformal, conversational
ngemuva kwalokhoafter thatmore formal, common in writing
  • Umhlangano wawuqalile, kwathi kucaca ukuthi izulu lizoba nzima. The meeting had started, then it became clear that the weather would be bad. kwathi + descriptive clause
  • Sahlala phansi, base sabhala amagama ethu ebhodini. We sat down, then we wrote our names on the board. base + past tense

16.2 Quoting speech inside a story

Purpose: mark clearly, inside a narrative, when the words belong to a particular person, rather than to the narrator.

Wathi introduces someone's exact words, in much the same way that English uses “he said” or “she said,” before or after a quotation. The subject concord inside wathi changes with the noun class of the speaker, exactly as in any other verb.

Language Building Block — Introducing quoted speech
Subject concord + -thi + "quoted words"
The subject concord agrees with the speaker, not with the words being quoted. A class 1 speaker such as Nomvula takes the concord u-, giving wathi.
UNomvula wathi, "Ngiyaxolisa, ngifikile kade."
Nomvula said, "I'm sorry, I arrived late."

Quoted speech, once introduced with wathi, may include any grammar the speaker would naturally use, including forms not yet fully active for independent production. Learners are not expected to generate every possible quoted sentence, only to recognize the pattern and reuse it with familiar material.

  • Uthisha wathi, "Umhlangano usuzoqala manje." The teacher said, "The meeting is about to start now." wathi + direct quotation
  • Bathi bonke, "Siyavuma." They all said, "We agree." class 2 subject concord ba- + -thi

16.3 Ideophones for vivid storytelling

Purpose: recognize, and use in a controlled way, a small set of ideophones that make a story more vivid.

An ideophone is a short, expressive word that evokes a sound, a movement, or a sudden state, often placed after a verb such as thi or enza. This chapter introduces only a small, high frequency set, for recognition and light, controlled use. Building an independent, expressive command of ideophones remains a target for later volumes.

IdeophoneEvokesTypical frame
cwakasudden silencekwathi cwaka, it suddenly became silent
bhicia sudden splash or wet impactkwathi bhici, there was a sudden splash
veleappearing suddenly, plainlywavele wafika, he simply appeared

Ideophones such as cwaka and bhici are recognition and light controlled use items in this chapter, not an open, independent expressive system.

Model Dialogue

Isimo: UThandeka utshela umzala wakhe, uSipho, ngomhlangano owenzeke ngoLwesihlanu, njengoba kuvezwe esazisweni nasemlayezweni esahlukweni esidlule.
Setting: Thandeka tells her cousin Sipho about the meeting that took place on Friday, following the notice and message from the previous chapter.

USIPHO Kwenzekani kulowo mhlangano, Thandeka? Ngizwile ukuthi umisiwe kanye, kodwa angazi okunye. What happened at that meeting, Thandeka? I heard it was postponed once, but I don't know anything else.
UTHANDEKA Sahlala phansi ehholo, base kwathi cwaka, uthisha esukuma. Wathi, "Umhlangano usuzoqala manje." We sat down in the hall, then it suddenly went quiet, as the teacher stood up. He said, "The meeting is about to start now."
USipho uhlala phansi, elalele ngokujula.
Sipho sits down, listening intently.
USIPHO Kwathi ngemuva kwalokho, kwenzekani? Then after that, what happened?
UTHANDEKA Uthisha wachaza isaziso esibhalwe ebhodini, base ethula umbuzo kithi sonke. Bonke bathi, "Siyavuma," ngokushesha, base bahleka kancane. The teacher explained the notice written on the board, then asked us all a question. Everyone said, "We agree," quickly, then they laughed a little.
USIPHO Yeka indaba enhle! Ngiyabonga ngokungitshela, Thandeka. What a good story! Thank you for telling me, Thandeka.

This dialogue combines sequencing connectors, a quoted turn with wathi, one controlled ideophone, cwaka, and a relative clause from earlier chapters, isaziso esibhalwe ebhodini.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Kwenzekani lapho uthisha esukuma? (What happened when the teacher stood up?)
  2. Uthisha wathini kubo bonke? (What did the teacher say to everyone?)
  3. Bonke baphendulani, futhi benzeni ngemuva kwalokho? (What did everyone answer, and what did they do after that?)

Writing Workshop

In this chapter, you will write a short story of five to seven sentences, using at least one sequencing connector, one quoted line with wathi, and one relative clause from Chapter 11.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Choose a small event, a meeting, a visit, or a change of plan, real or invented, and tell it as a short story with a clear beginning, middle, and end.

  1. Plan your events. In English, list three to four things that happened, in order.
  2. Open the story. Set the scene in one or two sentences, using the past tense.
  3. Sequence the middle. Use kwathi, base, or ngemuva kwalokho to link at least two events.
  4. Add one quoted line. Use wathi, or a matching subject concord plus -thi, to quote something a person said.
  5. Close the story. End with a short sentence describing the outcome or how people felt.
  6. Audit your draft. Confirm that your sequencing connectors are used correctly, that your quoted line is clearly marked, and that at least one relative clause from Chapter 11 appears somewhere in the story.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • The story has a clear beginning, middle, and end.
  • At least one sequencing connector links two events.
  • At least one quoted line is introduced with wathi or a matching form.
  • At least one relative clause from Chapter 11 is used correctly.
  • Standard isiZulu spelling, capitalization, and punctuation are used throughout.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: retelling in order

With a partner, take turns retelling one of this chapter's stories aloud, in your own words, but keeping the events in the same order. Use at least one sequencing connector each time.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: passing on a story

Working with a partner, one of you invents a short event, then tells it as a story to the other person, including one quoted line. The listener then retells the story back, as briefly as possible, checking that the order of events stays the same.

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself telling your written story aloud, once slowly, checking every connector and quoted line, and once at a livelier, more natural pace. Listen back, and check that the order of events remains clear even when you speak more quickly.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Storytelling as shared information

Retelling the news of a meeting, a visit, or an event to someone who was not there is an everyday act of storytelling, and it commonly matters as much how the story is told as what it contains. A listener often expects some sense of order, some quoted words, and a clear outcome, rather than a bare list of facts.

Culture in Context — Marking someone else's words

Clearly marking whose words are being reported, with a form such as wathi, is treated as an important part of accurate, respectful storytelling in many isiZulu speaking communities, since it keeps the narrator's voice separate from the voice of the person being quoted.

Regional Note — Ideophones in everyday speech

The frequency and choice of ideophones in storytelling can vary somewhat between speakers and regions, with some speakers using a rich, expressive set, and others using a smaller, more restrained set. The three ideophones in this chapter are common and widely understood.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Listing events without any connector

Error: Stringing several past tense sentences together with no connector at all, so that the story sounds like a list of unrelated facts.

Correction: Use kwathi, base, or ngemuva kwalokho to show that one event follows from, or leads into, the next, for example sahlala phansi, base kwathi cwaka, we sat down, then it suddenly went quiet.

Common Mistake — Losing track of who is speaking

Error: Quoting someone's words without a clear wathi, or a matching form, so the listener cannot tell whether the narrator or another person is speaking.

Correction: Always introduce quoted speech clearly, matching the subject concord to the speaker, for example uthisha wathi, "Umhlangano usuzoqala manje."

Common Mistake — Overusing ideophones

Error: Adding an ideophone to almost every sentence, in an attempt to sound more vivid or advanced.

Correction: Use an ideophone sparingly, at one clear high point in the story, so that it keeps its expressive force, for example one single kwathi cwaka at the moment the room goes quiet.

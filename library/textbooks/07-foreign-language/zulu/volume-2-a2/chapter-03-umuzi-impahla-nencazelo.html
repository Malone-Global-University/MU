Volume II, Chapter 3: Household, Possession, and Description · Speak and Write Zulu: Practical Independence

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name rooms, furniture, and everyday household items, using noun classes already active from Volume I and Chapter 1.
  2. Build a fuller noun phrase by combining a noun with an adjective and a possessive in the same sentence.
  3. Use near and medial demonstratives confidently by class, and recognize a small set of far demonstratives as fixed forms.
  4. Describe a room or a homestead in a short connected description.
  5. Ask and answer questions about who owns something, and where an item is located within the home.
  6. Read and listen to a short description of a household and identify its key details.
  7. Write a short paragraph describing your own home or an invented one.
  8. Continue applying the concord audit habit to nouns, adjectives, possessives, and demonstratives together.

Communication Scenario

After breakfast, Zanele shows Nomvula around the family homestead. Room by room, Zanele points out the furniture, explains who each item belongs to, and answers Nomvula's questions about the house. Nomvula, in turn, describes her own home and compares it to what she sees. As they move from room to room, the conversation requires longer noun phrases than either speaker has needed before, phrases that combine a noun, a description, an owner, and sometimes a pointer to exactly which item is meant.

  1. Before you begin, list, in English, three rooms and three pieces of furniture you would want to describe in your own home.
  2. Think about how you would say, in English, that something belongs to a specific person, such as "my mother's chair" or "the neighbour's dog." You will build similar phrases in isiZulu in this chapter.

Pronunciation and Click Review

Several household words in this chapter, such as isihlalo and igceke, include consonant clusters and a click letter that deserve careful attention as your sentences grow longer.

Pronunciation Focus — The lateral fricative hl and the click in igceke

The hl in isihlalo is a lateral fricative, produced by pushing air along the sides of the tongue rather than through the centre of the mouth, as with an ordinary l. Practise isihlalo slowly, then say it inside a full noun phrase, such as isihlalo esikhulu. The gc in igceke is a voiced dental click. Keep the click crisp and short, even when the word is placed in the middle of a longer sentence.

Grammar: Building a Fuller Noun Phrase

3.1 Stacking a noun, an adjective, and a possessive

Purpose: combine a noun with a descriptive adjective and a possessive in the same phrase, so that you can describe an item and say who owns it in one breath.

Volume I and Chapter 1 taught you to attach one adjective, or one possessive, to a noun. This chapter shows you how to place both together, in a fixed and predictable order. In isiZulu, the noun comes first, followed by the adjective, followed by the possessive.

Language Building Block — Noun plus adjective plus possessive
Noun + adjective (with its concord) + possessive (with its concord)
The adjective concord and the possessive concord are both drawn from the noun's class, exactly as in Volume I. Placing two modifiers after the noun does not change how either concord is chosen. Each concord still answers only to the noun's class.
isihlalo esikhulu sikagogo
grandmother's big chair, literally: chair big of grandmother
  • Umbhede omdala wami usekamelweni. My old bed is in the room. noun + adjective + possessive, class 3
  • Itafula elisha likamalume liseKhishini. Uncle's new table is in the kitchen. noun + adjective + possessive, class 5

Note on scope: This chapter activates only one adjective and one possessive per noun phrase. Stacking a third modifier, such as a demonstrative together with both an adjective and a possessive on the same noun, remains outside this chapter's scope, and appears only receptively in the reading passage below.

3.2 Near and medial demonstratives by class

Purpose: point to a specific item that is close to the speaker, or close to the listener, using the correct demonstrative form for that item's noun class.

Volume I introduced a small set of demonstratives as fixed expressions. This chapter develops the full near and medial demonstrative pattern across the classes active in this volume. The near demonstrative points to something close to the speaker. The medial demonstrative points to something close to the listener, or somewhat further from the speaker.

ClassNear (this, these)Medial (that, those, near you)
3 (umbhede)lo mbhedelowo mbhede
5 (itafula)leli tafulalelo tafula
7 (isihlalo)lesi sihlaloleso sihlalo
9 (indlu)le ndluleyo ndlu
  • Lesi sihlalo sikagogo, kodwa leso sihlalo sikamkhulu. This chair is grandmother's, but that chair is grandfather's.
  • Le ndlu inkulu, kodwa leyo ndlu incane. This house is big, but that house is small.
Language Focus

Far demonstratives, such as lowaya or laphaya, exist as a third position in the isiZulu demonstrative system, pointing to something distant from both speaker and listener. This chapter introduces only a small, fixed set of far demonstratives as formulaic expressions, for recognition and limited use, such as laphaya (over there). Full productive control of the far demonstrative across every class is not required until a later chapter.

Model Dialogue

Isimo: Emini, uZanele umbonisa uNomvula umuzi wakubo.
Setting: In the afternoon, Zanele shows Nomvula around the family homestead.

UZANELE Nangu ikhishi lethu. Lesi sitofu esidala kaGogo, kodwa lesi sitsha sami. Here is our kitchen. This old stove is Grandmother's, but this new one is mine.
UNOMVULA Kuhle lapha. Nazi izitsha ezinhle kakhulu. Ngezikabani lezi? It is nice here. Here are some very beautiful dishes. Whose are these?
UZANELE Izitsha zikamama. Woza, ngikubonise igumbi lokulala. Lo mbhede omdala ngokamkhulu. The dishes are my mother's. Come, let me show you the bedroom. This old bed is grandfather's.
UNOMVULA Lo mbhede mkhulu impela. Nalesi sihlalo esincane, ngesikabani? This bed is really big. And this small chair, whose is it?
UZANELE Leso sihlalo sami, sisukela ebuncaneni bami. Manje ake sihambe siyobheka igceke. That chair is mine, it has been mine since I was a child. Now let us go and look at the yard.
UNOMVULA Yebo, kulungile. Umuzi wakini muhle kakhulu, futhi mkhulu kunowami. Yes, alright. Your family's homestead is very beautiful, and bigger than mine.
UZANELE Ngiyabonga. Laphaya, emuva kwegceke, kunesivande sikagogo. Thank you. Over there, behind the yard, there is grandmother's garden.

This dialogue combines noun plus adjective plus possessive phrases with near and medial demonstratives, and includes one formulaic far demonstrative, laphaya. Every noun class was already active by the end of Chapter 1.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. Isitofu esidala ngesikabani? (Whose is the old stove?)
  2. Izitsha ezinhle ngezikabani? (Whose are the beautiful dishes?)
  3. Umbhede omdala ngokabani? (Whose is the old bed?)
  4. Kuyini okukhona ngemva kwegceke? (What is behind the yard?)

Writing Workshop

In this chapter, you will write a short paragraph describing your own home, or an invented one, combining noun plus adjective plus possessive phrases with at least one demonstrative.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write six to eight sentences describing a home, including at least two rooms, at least two pieces of furniture with an adjective and a possessive, and at least one near or medial demonstrative.

  1. List your rooms and items. In English, jot down which rooms and which pieces of furniture you will describe, and who owns each item.
  2. Open with the homestead. State whether the home is big or small, using an adjective you already know.
  3. Describe two rooms. For each room, name at least one item, add an adjective, and add a possessive.
  4. Point at something. Use at least one near or medial demonstrative to point to a specific item.
  5. Close with a feeling. End with one short sentence about how you feel about the home.
  6. Audit your draft. Check every noun's class, and confirm that each adjective concord and possessive concord matches that class, before writing your final version.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • At least two rooms are named correctly.
  • At least two noun phrases combine a noun, an adjective, and a possessive.
  • At least one near or medial demonstrative is used correctly.
  • Every noun's class has been identified before choosing its concords.
  • No unscheduled vocabulary or grammar appears beyond Volume I and Chapters 1 through 3.
  • Each sentence begins with a capital letter and ends with appropriate punctuation.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: whose is this?

Your partner points to an object, real or imagined, and asks Ngesikabani lesi? or a similar question matched to the object's class. Answer using a full noun phrase with an adjective and a possessive. Check each other's concords before swapping roles.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: a home tour

Working with a partner, take turns describing a room in your home, real or invented. Your partner asks at least three follow-up questions about the items in the room, their owners, and their location, using near or medial demonstratives where natural.

Useful checking expressions: Kuseduze noma kukude? (Is it near or far?)  |  Ngesikabani lokho? (Whose is that?)  |  Chaza kabanzi. (Describe it in more detail.)

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself describing the home you wrote about in the Writing Workshop as a spoken description. Listen back and check that your adjective concords, possessive concords, and demonstratives all remain accurate.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — The homestead as a shared, multi-generational space

In many isiZulu speaking households, a single homestead, or umuzi, is commonly shared across generations, with rooms and belongings often understood as connected to particular family members rather than treated as strictly private. This is one reason ownership language, such as who a chair or a bed belongs to, comes up naturally and often in everyday conversation about the home.

Culture in Context — Showing a guest around the home

Offering a guest a tour of the home, and naming who owns or uses particular rooms and items, is often considered a warm and welcoming gesture in many isiZulu speaking communities. Learners should listen for how hosts frame this kind of tour, since the tone is usually informative and proud rather than merely descriptive.

Regional Note — Words for household items

Some households and regions use slightly different words for particular pieces of furniture or household items, especially for items introduced more recently through trade or technology. This textbook continues to model common, widely recognized standard forms. Keep asking isiZulu speaking friends, family members, or instructors what words they use in their own homes.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Matching the adjective or possessive concord to the wrong word

Error: Choosing the adjective concord or the possessive concord based on the most recent word in the sentence, rather than the noun's own class, for example letting a nearby class 1a kinship term influence the concord that belongs to a class 7 noun such as isihlalo.

Correction: Always trace the concord back to the head noun's class: isihlalo esikhulu sikagogo, where both esikhulu and sikagogo agree with isihlalo, not with gogo.

Common Mistake — Reversing the order of adjective and possessive

Error: Placing the possessive immediately after the noun and the adjective at the end of the phrase, as though isiZulu followed the same modifier order as English in every case.

Correction: Keep the noun, then the adjective, then the possessive: umbhede omdala wami, not a reversed order. Reading the pattern box aloud a few times helps fix the order in memory.

Common Mistake — Overusing the far demonstrative

Error: Reaching for a far demonstrative, such as lowaya, in every class, for anything that is simply not right next to the speaker, instead of reserving it for items genuinely distant from both speaker and listener.

Correction: Use the near or medial demonstrative for most everyday pointing, such as lesi sihlalo or leso sihlalo, and treat far demonstratives such as laphaya as fixed expressions for now, rather than a full productive system.

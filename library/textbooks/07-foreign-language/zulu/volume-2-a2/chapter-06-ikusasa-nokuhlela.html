Ikusasa Nokuhlela

Volume II, Chapter 6: Future, Intention, and Planning · Speak and Write Zulu: Practical Independence

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Form and use the simple future tense, to describe actions that have not yet happened.
  2. Express intention and plans using controlled formulaic expressions such as wanting to and planning to.
  3. Talk about appointments, meetings, and arrangements, in familiar work and study settings.
  4. Ask and answer simple questions about when something will happen, using vocabulary already active from Chapters 1 through 5.
  5. Sequence two future events using bese, the same connector already active from Chapter 5, now pointed forward in time instead of backward.
  6. Continue using past narration from Chapter 5 to contrast what already happened with what is still to come.
  7. Read and listen to a short passage that moves between present, past, and future meaning.
  8. Write a short, organized plan for an upcoming day or week, combining future forms with familiar noun phrases.
  9. Continue applying the concord audit habit, now checking subject concords across every future form used in a single plan.

Communication Scenario

On Wednesday, Nomvula stops by Zanele's office to plan the coming week together. Zanele has already written her reports, so now she turns her attention forward, telling Nomvula what she still needs to do, and when she plans to do it. Nomvula, who has an appointment of her own coming up, asks careful questions to work out exactly when each task will happen, and the two friends sequence several plans together, the way colleagues naturally do when organizing a busy week.

  1. Before you begin, think, in English, about the difference between saying "I will write the report" and saying "I want to write the report" or "I am planning to write the report." Notice that each sentence expresses a slightly different relationship to the future.
  2. List, in English, one thing you plan to do later today, and one thing you plan to do next week. You will describe both in isiZulu by the end of this chapter.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 5 opened the recent past, remote past, and resultative past, and taught you to sequence two events with bese. This chapter keeps every one of those systems active, and adds a new direction in time, the future. You will use the same work and study vocabulary, the same noun phrases, and the same bese connector, now pointed toward what has not yet happened. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to move between what you did, what you have finished, and what you still plan to do, inside a single connected conversation.

From Chapters 1 through 5

Concord audit, familiar noun classes, infinitives with uku-, simple questions with nini and kuphi, noun phrases with adjectives and possessives, and recent, remote, and resultative past.

New in This Chapter

Simple future tense, formulaic expressions of intention and planning, and bese used to sequence two future events rather than two past events.

Vocabulary: Plans and Appointments

This chapter's noun vocabulary remains within classes already active from Volume I and Chapters 1 through 5. The new material here is a small set of future time words and two formulaic expressions of intention, which allow you to place events ahead in time and describe your plans clearly.

Pronunciation and Click Review

The word kusasa begins with the same syllable shape as several familiar words from earlier chapters. Keep the vowels short and even, and place the natural lengthening on the second-to-last syllable, so kusasa is not confused with the chapter title word ikusasa, meaning the future.

Pronunciation Focus — Hearing the future marker

The future marker sits between the subject concord and the verb root, and is easy to blend with surrounding sounds if you speak too quickly. Say ngizokwenza slowly, breaking it into its parts, ngi-zo-kwenza, then say it again at a natural, connected speed. Practise the same pattern with ngizofunda and ngizobhala, listening for the same clear -zo- in each word.

Grammar: Future, Intention, and Planning

6.1 Simple future

Purpose: describe something that has not yet happened, but is expected, planned, or certain to happen.

The simple future is formed with the subject concord, followed by the marker -zo-, followed by the verb root, followed by a final vowel. For the roots used so far in this course, the future of -funda is ngizofunda, meaning I will study, or I will read.

Language Building Block — Simple future
subject concord + -zo- + root + final vowel
The simple future places an action ahead of the present moment. It answers the question, what will you do, without saying how strongly the speaker intends it.
Kusasa, ngizobhala imibiko emikhulu.
Tomorrow, I will write large reports.
  • UNomvula uzofika ehhovisi ngeviki elizayo. Nomvula will arrive at the office next week. simple future, single event
  • Sizosebenza kanzima ngonyaka ozayo. We will work hard next year. simple future, plural subject concord

Note on scope: This chapter presents the simple future as a controlled system for single planned events, paired with the future time words above. Combination with object concords, and negative future forms, remain outside this chapter's scope.

6.2 Expressing intention with ngifisa uku- and ngihlela uku-

Purpose: describe a wish or a plan, rather than a certain future event.

To express what you want to do, use ngifisa uku- followed by an infinitive. To express what you are planning to do, use ngihlela uku- followed by an infinitive. Both expressions are treated as fixed formulas in this chapter, and both are followed by the same infinitive pattern with uku- that you learned in Chapter 4.

Language Building Block — Intention formulas
ngifisa uku- / ngihlela uku- + infinitive
These formulas soften a future statement into a wish or a plan. Compare ngizobhala, I will write, with ngifisa ukubhala, I want to write, and ngihlela ukubhala, I am planning to write.
Ngihlela ukuthola omunye umsebenzi ngonyaka ozayo.
I am planning to get another job next year.
  • Ngifisa ukuphumula maduze. I want to rest soon.
  • UZanele uhlela ukuvakashela isikole sakhe esidala. Zanele is planning to visit her old school.

6.3 Sequencing two future events with bese

Purpose: link two future events into a short, connected plan, the same way bese already links two past events.

To sequence future plans, state the first event using a simple future form, then add bese, followed by the subject concord and verb root for the second event. This is the same connector you activated in Chapter 5, now reused to point forward rather than backward.

  • Ngizovula ihhovisi kusasa, bese ngibhala imibiko. I will open the office tomorrow, and then I will write the reports.
  • Sizofika ngeviki elizayo, bese sihlangana nomqashi wethu. We will arrive next week, and then we will meet our employer.
Language Focus

A single conversation may move between past, present, and future, the way real planning conversations naturally do. A speaker might report what already happened, using the past forms from Chapter 5, then state what is planned, using the future forms from this chapter. This chapter's dialogue, reading, and listening passages model that natural movement across time, without requiring you to produce every combination yourself.

Model Dialogue

Isimo: NgoLwesithathu, uNomvula uvakashela ihhovisi likaZanele, ukuze bahlele iviki elizayo ndawonye.
Setting: On Wednesday, Nomvula visits Zanele's office, so they can plan the coming week together.

UNOMVULA Sawubona, Zanele. Sewuyibhalile yonke imibiko yakho, angithi? Hello, Zanele. You have already written all your reports, haven't you?
UZANELE Yebo, ngiyibhalile yonke izolo. Manje, ngifisa ukuphumula maduze, kodwa kusasa ngizosebenza kanzima futhi. Yes, I wrote all of it yesterday. Now, I want to rest soon, but tomorrow I will work hard again.
UNOMVULA Uzokwenzani kusasa? What will you do tomorrow?
UZANELE Ngizovula ihhovisi ekuseni, bese ngibhala eminye imibiko. Ngihlela ukuthola omunye umsebenzi ngonyaka ozayo, ngakho ngisebenza kanzima manje. I will open the office in the morning, and then I will write more reports. I am planning to get another job next year, so I am working hard now.
UNOMVULA Kuhle kakhulu. Nami ngizofika lapha ngeviki elizayo, bese sihlangana futhi. That is very good. I too will arrive here next week, and then we will meet again.
UZANELE Kulungile, ngizolinda ukukubona ngeviki elizayo. Alright, I will look forward to seeing you next week.

This dialogue moves between resultative past, present, and simple future, and uses bese once to sequence two future events. Every noun class and noun phrase pattern used was already active by the end of Chapter 5.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. UZanele uzokwenzani kusasa? (What will Zanele do tomorrow?)
  2. UZanele uhlela ukwenzani ngonyaka ozayo? (What is Zanele planning to do next year?)
  3. UNomvula uzofika nini ehhovisi likaZanele? (When will Nomvula arrive at Zanele's office?)

Writing Workshop

In this chapter, you will write a short, organized plan describing what you will do soon, and what you are planning to do further ahead, combining these with the noun phrase and infinitive patterns from earlier chapters.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write six to eight sentences describing your plans, or invented plans, including at least one simple future sentence, one sentence using ngifisa uku- or ngihlela uku-, and one use of bese to sequence two future events.

  1. List your plans. In English, jot down one thing you will do soon, and one thing you are planning to do later.
  2. Open with a present result. Use a resultative past sentence from Chapter 5, to show what you have already finished.
  3. State an intention. Use ngifisa uku- or ngihlela uku-, followed by an infinitive, to describe a wish or a plan.
  4. Sequence two future events. Use two simple future sentences, linked with bese, to show what will happen in order.
  5. Add one noun phrase. Include at least one noun phrase with an adjective and a possessive, from Chapter 3.
  6. Audit your draft. Check every subject concord against its noun class, confirm each future form matches its intended meaning, and confirm bese links two related events, before writing your final version.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • At least one sentence uses the simple future correctly.
  • At least one sentence uses ngifisa uku- or ngihlela uku- correctly, with a matching infinitive.
  • Bese is used at least once to sequence two future events.
  • At least one earlier past form from Chapter 5 is reused correctly.
  • Every subject concord matches its noun's class.
  • No unscheduled vocabulary or grammar appears beyond Volume I and Chapters 1 through 6.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: sorting time meanings

Your partner says a short sentence in the past, present, or future. Decide, out loud, which time it describes, then explain, in English, which word or marker told you so.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: planning the week

Working with a partner, plan a short week together. Each of you should state one thing you will do soon, and one thing you are planning to do later, using bese at least once to sequence events. Your partner asks at least two follow-up questions using nini or kuphi.

Useful checking expressions: Uzokwenzani kusasa? (What will you do tomorrow?)  |  Uhlelani ngonyaka ozayo? (What are you planning for next year?)  |  Bese, kwenzekani? (And then, what happens?)

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself describing the plans you wrote about in the Writing Workshop, as a spoken account. Listen back, and check that your simple future and intention forms all remain accurate.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Speaking about plans with care

In many isiZulu speaking communities, speakers often soften confident statements about the future, particularly when discussing health, safety, or important personal matters, preferring expressions of hope or intention over flat certainty. Learners should notice how often speakers reach for a wish or a plan, rather than a bare future statement, in everyday conversation.

Culture in Context — Confirming shared plans

When two people agree to meet or work together, restating the plan clearly near the end of a conversation, as Zanele and Nomvula do in the model dialogue, is a common and courteous way to confirm that both sides share the same understanding. This pattern is worth practising as you complete the speaking tasks in this chapter.

Regional Note — Variation in expressing plans

Some speakers favor ngihlela uku-, while others favor alternative planning expressions not yet introduced in this course, depending on region, generation, or setting. This textbook models a clear, standard pattern suitable for learners. Keep listening for small variations as your listening skills develop in later chapters and volumes.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing the future marker with the recent past marker

Error: Blending the future marker -zo- with the recent past marker -a- from Chapter 5, for example saying ngazokwenza when a plain future meaning is intended.

Correction: Use the subject concord directly followed by -zo- for the simple future: ngizokwenza, not ngazokwenza, when describing something that has not yet happened.

Common Mistake — Leaving out the infinitive after ngifisa uku- or ngihlela uku-

Error: Using ngifisa or ngihlela alone, without the infinitive that carries the actual planned action, for example saying Ngifisa umsebenzi omusha when a full infinitive phrase is intended.

Correction: Follow ngifisa uku- or ngihlela uku- with a complete infinitive: Ngifisa ukuthola umsebenzi omusha, not Ngifisa umsebenzi omusha, when describing a wish to do something.

Common Mistake — Stringing future events together without bese

Error: Placing two future sentences side by side with no connector at all, for example saying Ngizovula ihhovisi. Ngizobhala imibiko., as two separate, unconnected statements.

Correction: Link related future events with bese, to show they will happen in sequence: Ngizovula ihhovisi, bese ngibhala imibiko, not two disconnected sentences.

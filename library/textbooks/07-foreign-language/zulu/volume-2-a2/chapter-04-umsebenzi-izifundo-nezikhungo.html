Umsebenzi, Izifundo, Nezikhungo

Volume II, Chapter 4: Work, Study, and Institutional Life · Speak and Write Zulu: Practical Independence

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common workplaces, school spaces, and institutional roles, using noun classes already active from Volume I and Chapters 1 through 3.
  2. Name everyday activities and responsibilities using the infinitive, class 15, formed with uku-.
  3. Ask simple content questions about routines, responsibilities, and schedules.
  4. Give and understand simple, polite commands used in offices, classrooms, and public institutions.
  5. Describe a daily routine at work or school in a short connected description.
  6. Continue building fuller noun phrases, combining a noun, an adjective, and a possessive, in institutional contexts.
  7. Read and listen to a short passage about a workplace or school and identify its key details.
  8. Write a short paragraph describing your own routine, or an invented one, at work or at school.
  9. Continue applying the concord audit habit to nouns, adjectives, possessives, and now infinitives.

Communication Scenario

On Monday morning, Nomvula visits the office where Zanele works, before both of them head to Zanele's evening class. Zanele explains her daily responsibilities, points out the offices and the classroom, and answers Nomvula's questions about her schedule. Later, a receptionist gives Nomvula simple instructions for finding the correct office. The conversation now requires naming activities themselves, such as working, studying, and writing, not only naming people and things.

  1. Before you begin, list, in English, three responsibilities you have at work, school, or home that you would want to describe.
  2. Think about how you would ask someone, in English, what time they start work or what their job involves. You will build similar questions in isiZulu in this chapter.

Amagama — Vocabulary

Vocabulary: Work, Study, and Offices

This chapter's noun vocabulary stays within classes already active from Volume I and Chapters 1 through 3, namely classes 3/4, 5/6, 7/8, and 9/10. No new noun class is introduced here. The verb vocabulary, however, introduces the infinitive form, class 15, for the first time as a productive system, using verb roots you have already met or will recognize easily.

Pronunciation and Click Review

The word isikole and the phrase igumbi lokufundela both reward careful, even syllable rhythm as your sentences grow longer and combine an infinitive with other noun phrase material.

Pronunciation Focus — Keeping the uku- prefix light and clear

The infinitive prefix uku- should be pronounced clearly but without extra stress, since penultimate lengthening still falls near the end of the word, not on the prefix. Practise ukusebenza slowly, noticing that the vowel lengthening happens on the second-to-last syllable of the whole word, not on the first syllable. Then place the infinitive inside a longer sentence, such as Ngithanda ukusebenza ehhovisi, and keep that same rhythm.

Grammar: Infinitives, Questions, and Commands

4.1 The infinitive, class 15, with uku-

Purpose: name an activity itself, such as working, studying, or writing, so that you can talk about routines, responsibilities, likes, and plans without attaching the activity to one particular subject.

Every isiZulu verb root can be turned into a noun that names the activity itself, by adding the prefix uku-. This noun belongs to class 15, the infinitive class. Once formed, the infinitive behaves like any other class 15 noun, and can be used as the subject or object of a sentence, or after a verb such as -thanda, to like.

Language Building Block — Forming and using the infinitive
uku- + verb root = infinitive, class 15
The infinitive is formed the same way for every verb root taught in this chapter. Once formed, it can follow a verb like -thanda, or stand on its own as a short answer to a question about activity or purpose.
Ngithanda ukufunda.
I like studying, literally: I like the-studying.
  • Ukusebenza ehhovisi kumnandi. Working in the office is enjoyable. infinitive as subject, class 15
  • Uthisha uthanda ukubhala kanye nokufunda. The teacher likes writing and reading. two infinitives joined with kanye na-

Note on scope: This chapter activates the infinitive only as a simple noun, used as a subject or after -thanda. Infinitives combined with object concords, or used inside relative or purpose clauses, remain outside this chapter's scope.

4.2 Simple content questions about routines and responsibilities

Purpose: ask when, where, and what someone does at work, school, or another institution.

Volume I introduced a small set of question words as fixed expressions. This chapter develops three of them for controlled, productive use, connected to institutional routines: nini, meaning when; kuphi, meaning where; and ini, meaning what, used after certain verbs.

  • Uqala nini umsebenzi? When do you start work?
  • Ihhovisi likuphi? Where is the office?
  • Wenzani ehhovisi? What do you do at the office?

4.3 Simple polite commands in institutional settings

Purpose: give and understand short, polite instructions of the kind commonly heard in offices, classrooms, and public institutions.

For most verb roots, a simple command to one person is formed from the bare root, with no subject concord. A command to more than one person, or a more polite command to one person, adds -ni to the end of the root.

RootCommand, one personCommand, more than one person or polite
-vulaVula.Vulani.
-valaVala.Valani.
-hlalaHlala.Hlalani.
  • Vula ihhovisi, siyafika. Open the office, we are arriving.
  • Hlalani, ake nilinde umqashi. Please sit, wait for the employer.
Language Focus

A small number of very common roots take an irregular command form, such as -za, to come, which becomes woza rather than a bare root. This chapter presents woza as a fixed, formulaic command form for recognition and limited use. The full pattern of irregular commands is not required for productive control until a later chapter.

Model Dialogue

Isimo: Ekuseni ngoMsombuluko, uNomvula uvakashele ihhovisi likaZanele.
Setting: On Monday morning, Nomvula visits the office where Zanele works.

UNOMVULA Sawubona, Zanele. Uqala nini umsebenzi? Hello, Zanele. When do you start work?
UZANELE Ngiqala ekuseni kakhulu. Ngithanda ukusebenza ekuseni, kungakabi nemisindo eminingi. I start very early. I like working in the morning, before there is a lot of noise.
UNOMVULA Wenzani ehhovisi lakho? What do you do at your office?
UZANELE Ngibhala imibiko emikhulu, futhi ngisiza umqashi wami. Ngiyawuthanda umsebenzi wami omusha. I write large reports, and I help my employer. I like my new job.
UNOMVULA Ihhovisi lakho likuphi kule ndawo? Where is your office in this building?
UZANELE Nali ihhovisi lami. Ngena, hlala. Ngizokubonisa isikole engiya kuso kusihlwa. Here is my office. Come in, sit down. I will show you the school I attend in the evening.
(Emva kwesikhathi, ababili baya esikoleni. Umlindisango uyabingelela.)
(Later, the two go to the school. A receptionist greets them.)
UMLINDISANGO Sanibonani. Vulani lo mnyango, bese nihlale kulesi sihlalo. Hello to you both. Open this door, then sit in this chair.
UZANELE Ngiyabonga. Sifuna igumbi lokufundela likathisha wami. Thank you. We are looking for my teacher's classroom.

This dialogue combines the infinitive with familiar noun phrases and demonstratives from Chapter 3, and adds simple questions and commands. Every noun class used was already active by the end of Chapter 1, and every noun phrase pattern was already active by the end of Chapter 3.

Imibuzo yokuqonda — Comprehension questions:

  1. UZanele uqala nini umsebenzi? (When does Zanele start work?)
  2. UZanele wenzani ehhovisi lakhe? (What does Zanele do at her office?)
  3. Ithini imiyalelo yomlindisango? (What are the receptionist's instructions?)
  4. UZanele nomNomvula bafuna liphi igumbi? (Which room do Zanele and Nomvula want?)

Writing Workshop

In this chapter, you will write a short paragraph describing a daily routine at work or at school, combining infinitives with the noun phrase patterns from Chapter 3.

Umsebenzi wokubhala — Writing task: Write six to eight sentences describing a work or school routine, including at least two infinitives, at least one noun phrase with an adjective and a possessive, and one simple question or command.

  1. List your activities. In English, jot down which activities, rooms, and people you will describe.
  2. Open with the setting. State whether the workplace or school is big or small, using an adjective you already know.
  3. Name two activities. Use the infinitive to name two things you or someone else does there.
  4. Describe an item or a person. Add at least one noun phrase with an adjective and a possessive.
  5. Ask or instruct. Include one simple question, or one simple command.
  6. Audit your draft. Check every noun's class, confirm each infinitive is correctly formed with uku-, and confirm that each adjective concord and possessive concord matches its noun, before writing your final version.

Isikhungo sokuhlola — Editing checklist:

  • At least two activities are named using the infinitive with uku-.
  • At least one noun phrase combines a noun, an adjective, and a possessive.
  • At least one simple question or command is used correctly.
  • Every noun's class has been identified before choosing its concords.
  • No unscheduled vocabulary or grammar appears beyond Volume I and Chapters 1 through 4.
  • Each sentence begins with a capital letter and ends with appropriate punctuation.

Speaking Practice

Ukuzilolonga — Controlled practice: asking about routines

Your partner names a workplace or school activity using an infinitive. Ask them nini or kuphi about that activity, and have them answer in a full sentence. Check each other's infinitive forms before swapping roles.

Umsebenzi wokuxhumana — Communicative task: a workplace or school tour

Working with a partner, take turns describing your workplace or school, real or invented. Your partner asks at least three follow-up questions about activities, rooms, and people, and you respond, giving at least one simple command as part of the tour.

Useful checking expressions: Uqala nini? (When do you start?)  |  Kusetshenziswa kuphi? (Where is it used?)  |  Ake uchaze kabanzi. (Please describe it in more detail.)

Ukuzilolonga wedwa — Solo alternative

Record yourself describing the routine you wrote about in the Writing Workshop as a spoken description. Listen back and check that your infinitives, noun phrases, and question or command forms all remain accurate.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Respectful address in offices and classrooms

In many isiZulu speaking workplaces and schools, using the polite plural command form, such as Hlalani rather than Hlala, is common as a mark of respect toward a visitor, a group, or a person of higher status, even when speaking to a single individual. Learners should listen for this pattern in institutional settings.

Culture in Context — Naming one's work with pride

Describing one's work or studies, and expressing enjoyment of them, is often considered a natural and welcome part of everyday conversation in many isiZulu speaking communities. Learners should feel comfortable naming their own responsibilities and activities using the infinitive patterns from this chapter.

Regional Note — Words for office and institutional roles

Some workplaces and institutions use slightly different terms for roles and spaces, particularly newer terms borrowed from English for modern office equipment or job titles. This textbook continues to model common, widely recognized standard forms. Keep asking isiZulu speaking colleagues, classmates, or instructors what terms they use in their own workplaces.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Forgetting the uku- prefix on the infinitive

Error: Using a bare verb root where an infinitive is required, for example saying Ngithanda sebenza instead of attaching the class 15 prefix.

Correction: Always add uku- before the root when naming the activity itself: Ngithanda ukusebenza, not Ngithanda sebenza.

Common Mistake — Adding a subject concord to a command

Error: Attaching a subject concord to a simple command, as though it were an ordinary statement, for example saying Uvula instead of a true command form.

Correction: Use the bare root for a command to one person, and add -ni for more than one person or for politeness: Vula, or Vulani, not Uvula.

Common Mistake — Placing the question word at the start of the sentence

Error: Moving nini or kuphi to the beginning of the sentence, following English question order, for example saying Nini uqala umsebenzi?

Correction: Keep the question word in its natural position, usually after the verb: Uqala nini umsebenzi?, not a reversed English-style order.

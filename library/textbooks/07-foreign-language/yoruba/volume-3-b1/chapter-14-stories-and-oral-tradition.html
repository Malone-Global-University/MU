Stories and Oral Tradition · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Understand a short adapted story told in connected Yorùbá and follow its sequence of events.
  2. Retell a story orally and in writing using clear narrative sequencing markers.
  3. Track participants across a story using pronoun chains without losing clarity about who did what.
  4. Recognize relative clauses and contrastive focus within a longer narrative (recognition continues from Chapters 12–13), while producing only the relative-clause patterns already active from earlier chapters.
  5. Use a small set of reduplicated forms productively to describe manner, repetition, or intensity in a story.
  6. Recognize ideophones in a story and explain, in general terms, what kind of vivid impression they add, without being required to produce new ideophones independently.
  7. Continue using ceremonial and cultural formulas from Chapter 13 where they fit naturally into a story's opening or closing.

Opening Communication Scenario

After the naming ceremony discussion, Adé's grandmother offers to tell a story she remembers from her own childhood, about a clever tortoise (ìjàpá) who tricks his neighbors but ultimately learns a lesson. Adé wants to be able to follow the story closely, retell it to a cousin who was not present, and understand why certain words in the story sound repeated or especially vivid.

  1. This chapter develops narrative sequencing and pronoun-chain tracking so that a story's events and characters stay clear from beginning to end.
  2. Reduplication is introduced for controlled productive use in describing manner and repetition (for example, "slowly, slowly" or "little by little"), while ideophones remain recognition-only, continuing the pattern from Chapters 12–13.
  3. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to retell a short adapted story clearly, keeping track of who does what at each point.

Pronunciation Focus

Focus — Storytelling rhythm and reduplicated stress

Storytelling in Yorùbá often uses a more musical, rising-and-falling rhythm than plain conversation, especially at moments of suspense or repetition. Reduplicated forms such as rọra-rọra and díẹ̀-díẹ̀ typically carry even, deliberate stress across both halves, reinforcing the sense of gradualness or care.

  • Keep the tone on both halves of a reduplicated word accurate; do not flatten the second half.
  • Practice a short pause before an ideophone in the reading passage, since storytellers often use a brief pause to set off a vivid sound word.
  • Read the model dialogue's story opening slowly first, then at a natural storytelling pace.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops narrative sequencing and pronoun-chain tracking across a multi-sentence story, and introduces a small set of reduplicated forms for controlled productive use. Relative clauses and contrastive focus continue as recognition-only material, as in Chapters 12–13; no new relative-clause pattern is activated for independent production in this chapter.

Narrative sequencing markers

Pattern

Ní ìbẹ̀rẹ̀... Lẹ́yìn náà... Nígbẹ̀yìngbẹ́yín...

These time-sequencing expressions organize a story into a clear beginning, middle, and end without requiring new subordinating grammar. They recycle time expressions already active in the volume.

Ní ìbẹ̀rẹ̀, ìjàpá pinnu láti tan gbogbo àwọn ẹranko ní igbó jẹ. Lẹ́yìn náà, wọ́n bẹ̀rẹ̀ sí fura sí i. Nígbẹ̀yìngbẹ́yín, ó gbọ́dọ̀ jẹ́wọ́ ẹ̀tàn rẹ̀.

At first, the tortoise decided to deceive all the animals in the forest. Afterward, they began to suspect him. In the end, he had to admit his trick.

Pronoun chains and participant tracking

  • Ìjàpá pè àwọn ọ̀rẹ́ rẹ̀ jọ. Ó sọ fún wọn pé òun ní oúnjẹ tí ó pọ̀ jùlọ. Wọ́n gbà á gbọ́, wọ́n sì tẹ̀lé e lọ. The tortoise called his friends together. He told them that he had the most food. They believed him and followed him. subject/object pronoun chain across three clauses (recycled + developed)
  • Nígbà tí wọ́n dé ibẹ̀, wọn kò rí oúnjẹ kankan; ìjàpá nìkan ni ó ti jẹ ẹ́ tán. When they arrived, they found no food at all; the tortoise alone had already finished eating it. reference continuity + recycled focus frame "X nìkan ni"

Reduplication for manner and gradualness

  • Ó ń rìn rọra-rọra kí àwọn ẹlòmíràn má bàa gbọ́ ìró rẹ̀. He was walking slowly and carefully so the others would not hear his footsteps. reduplication (introduced) — manner adverbial before verb
  • Àwọn ẹranko bẹ̀rẹ̀ sí kíyèsi í díẹ̀-díẹ̀, títí wọ́n fi mọ ẹ̀tàn rẹ̀. The animals began to notice, little by little, until they realized his trick. reduplication (introduced) — gradualness marker + result clause (recycled)
Recognition only in this chapter — Do not produce independently yet

The listening and reading passages below contain a relative clause describing which animal first suspected the tortoise, a contrastive-focus phrase distinguishing what the tortoise said from what actually happened, and two ideophones marking a sudden discovery and a slow, dragging retreat. These items remain receptive, continuing the pattern from Chapters 12–13; you are not assessed on producing new instances of them in this chapter.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Ìyá Àgbà ń sọ ìtàn ìjàpá fún Adé àti àbúrò rẹ̀ ní alẹ́ ọjọ́ kan lẹ́yìn oúnjẹ alẹ́.
Setting: Grandmother is telling Adé and his younger sibling the story of the tortoise one evening after dinner.

ÌYÁ ÀGBÀ Ẹ jókòó dáadáa, kí n sọ ìtàn ìjàpá fún yín. Ní ìbẹ̀rẹ̀, ìjàpá pinnu láti tan gbogbo àwọn ẹranko ní igbó jẹ. Sit down properly, so I can tell you the story of the tortoise. At first, the tortoise decided to deceive all the animals in the forest.
ADÉ Kí ni ó ṣe, Ìyá Àgbà? What did he do, grandmother?
ÌYÁ ÀGBÀ Ó ń rìn rọra-rọra kí àwọn ẹlòmíràn má bàa gbọ́ ìró rẹ̀, ó sì pa gbogbo oúnjẹ tí wọ́n kó jọ mọ́ra fún ara rẹ̀ nìkan. He was walking slowly and carefully so the others would not hear his footsteps, and he kept all the food they had gathered together for himself alone.
ÀBÚRÒ ADÉ Ǹjẹ́ àwọn ẹranko yòókù mọ ohun tí ó ṣe? Did the other animals find out what he did?
ÌYÁ ÀGBÀ Àwọn ẹranko bẹ̀rẹ̀ sí kíyèsi í díẹ̀-díẹ̀, títí wọ́n fi mọ ẹ̀tàn rẹ̀. Nígbẹ̀yìngbẹ́yín, ó gbọ́dọ̀ jẹ́wọ́ ohun tí ó ṣe níwájú gbogbo wọn. The animals began to notice, little by little, until they realized his trick. In the end, he had to confess what he had done in front of all of them.
ADÉ Kí ni ẹ̀kọ́ ìtàn yìí, Ìyá Àgbà? What is the lesson of this story, grandmother?
ÌYÁ ÀGBÀ Ẹ̀tàn kì í gbé pẹ́; ohun tí a ṣe ní ìkọ̀kọ̀ máa ń hàn láìpẹ́ tàbí láìpẹ́. Deception does not last long; what is done in secret usually comes to light sooner or later.

Gbá! ó bẹ́ síta, ó sì bá ìjàpá pàdé lọ́wọ́ ẹ̀tàn rẹ̀. Ìjàpá bẹ̀rù, ó sì sá rẹ̀rẹ̀rẹ̀ lọ sínú ẹkùn koríko. Ní ọjọ́ kejì, gbogbo àwọn ẹranko péjọ, ìjàpá sì jẹ́wọ́ ẹ̀ṣẹ̀ rẹ̀ níwájú wọn."

Ìjàpá àti Ẹyẹ Ológbọ́n

Ní ọjọ́ kan, ìjàpá pàdé ẹyẹ kan tí orúkọ rẹ̀ ń jẹ́ ológbọ́n, ẹni tí gbogbo igbó mọ̀ dáadáa nítorí ọgbọ́n rẹ̀. Ìjàpá ní òun fẹ́ láti fò bí i ẹyẹ, torí náà ó bẹ ẹyẹ náà pé kí ó fún òun ní ìyẹ́ díẹ̀. Ẹyẹ ológbọ́n fún un ní ìyẹ́, ṣùgbọ́n ó kìlọ̀ fún un pé kí ó má lo àṣejù. Ìjàpá kò tẹ́tí sí ìmọ̀ràn náà; ó fi ìyẹ́ náà hàn fún gbogbo ẹranko, ó sì ń wí pé òun ti di ọba òfurufú. Díẹ̀-díẹ̀, àwọn ìyẹ́ náà bẹ̀rẹ̀ sí bọ́ nítorí pé kò lò ó bí a ti kọ́ ọ. Gbá! ó ṣubú lulẹ̀ níwájú gbogbo àwọn ẹranko tí ó ń fi í ṣe yẹ̀yẹ́. Ẹyẹ ológbọ́n wí pé, "Ẹnikẹ́ni tí kò bá tẹ́tí sí ìmọ̀ràn a máa rí ìtìjú."

2. What warning did the bird give, and did the tortoise follow it?

3. What happened to the feathers, and why?

4. What is the bird's closing statement, and how does it relate to the story's lesson?

Writing Workshop

Write a short retelling of a story (70–110 words) with full tone marks and dot-under letters throughout. Your retelling must use at least two narrative sequencing markers, a clear pronoun chain, at least one reduplicated form, and a one-sentence statement of the story's lesson.

Sample retelling shell — for reference only
Ní ìbẹ̀rẹ̀, [ohun tí ó ṣẹlẹ̀ àkọ́kọ́]. Lẹ́yìn náà, [ohun tí ó tẹ̀lé e], ó sì ń ṣe é [rọra-rọra/díẹ̀-díẹ̀]. Nígbẹ̀yìngbẹ́yín, [bí ìtàn náà ṣe parí]. Ẹ̀kọ́ ìtàn yìí ni pé [ẹ̀kọ́].   • Choose a story to retell (the tortoise story, the reading passage, or one you already know).

  • Include at least one reduplicated form to describe manner or gradualness.

    • Include

    • at least 2 narrative sequencing markers (beginning, middle, or end)
    • a clear pronoun chain tracking at least two participants
    • at least 1 productive reduplicated form

    Tone and Sequence Checkpoint
    • Every vowel that should carry a high or low tone mark has one.
    • Every mid-tone syllable is left correctly unmarked.

    • Both halves of every reduplicated form carry accurate, matching tone.
    • Each pronoun's referent is clear from context without needing to reread the sentence.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Trickster tales and oral transmission

    Stories featuring ìjàpá the tortoise are widely told in many Yorùbá-speaking communities and often use cleverness, deception, and eventual consequence to teach practical or moral lessons. These stories are traditionally passed down orally, often by grandparents or older relatives, and a single tale may exist in several different versions depending on the storyteller, region, and generation. Ideophones and reduplicated forms are common features of Yorùbá oral storytelling, adding vividness and rhythm; they are not required in ordinary conversation to the same degree. Learners should treat any single version of a story presented here as one telling among many, not the single authoritative version of the tale.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Losing track of who a pronoun refers to

    Error: Stringing together several pronouns across clauses without enough context for a listener or reader to tell which character is meant, for example switching between "he" and "they" without reintroducing names when the story has more than two characters.

    Correction: Reintroduce a character's name at natural points in a longer narrative, especially after a new character has been mentioned, so pronoun reference stays clear, as in "ìjàpá sọ fún ọ̀bọ pé... ọ̀bọ sì dá a lóhùn pé..." ("the tortoise told the monkey that... and the monkey replied that...").

    Common Mistake — Inventing new ideophones instead of using recognized ones

    Error: Creating an unfamiliar sound-symbolic word in writing or speech and expecting it to be understood as an ideophone, since ideophones are recognition-only in this chapter and are not yet an independent production system.

    Correction: Use ordinary descriptive language instead, or recycle a reduplicated form already introduced, such as "rọra-rọra" or "díẹ̀-díẹ̀", until ideophones are formally activated for production in a later volume.

    Answer Key

    Listening Practice Questions

    1. The tortoise and the other animals agreed to gather food together for the dry season.
    2. The monkey first suspected the tortoise; the monkey usually went into the forest to see how things were.
    3. The tortoise claimed he had not touched the food at all, but the truth was that he alone had been eating it every night.
    4. After the monkey caught the tortoise, the tortoise fled in fear, and the next day all the animals gathered and the tortoise confessed his wrongdoing before them.

    Reading Practice Questions

    1. The tortoise asked the wise bird for feathers because he wanted to fly like a bird.
    2. The bird warned him not to overuse the feathers; the tortoise did not follow the warning.
    3. The feathers began to fall out little by little because he did not use them as he had been taught.
    4. The bird's closing statement is that anyone who does not listen to advice will experience shame; it relates directly to the story's lesson about ignoring good counsel.

    Guided Practice — Sample Responses

    1. "Ní ìbẹ̀rẹ̀" introduces the beginning, "lẹ́yìn náà" introduces the middle, and "nígbẹ̀yìngbẹ́yín" introduces the end of the story. 2. Sample pronoun chain: "Ìjàpá pè àwọn ọ̀rẹ́ rẹ̀ jọ. Ó sọ fún wọn... Wọ́n gbà á gbọ́..." — here "ó" refers back to ìjàpá and "wọ́n"/"wọn" refer to the friends. 3. "Rọra-rọra" describes walking slowly and carefully; "díẹ̀-díẹ̀" describes noticing something gradually. 4. Sample restatement: "Ẹ̀tàn kì í gbé pẹ́; ohun búburú tí a ṣe a máa hàn níkẹyìn."

    Chapter Review — Apá A

    1. "Ní ìbẹ̀rẹ̀" (at first) signals the beginning of a story, "lẹ́yìn náà" (afterward) signals a middle event, and "nígbẹ̀yìngbẹ́yín" (in the end) signals the conclusion. 2. Example: "rọra-rọra" (slowly, carefully) adds a sense of deliberate, cautious manner to the verb it modifies.

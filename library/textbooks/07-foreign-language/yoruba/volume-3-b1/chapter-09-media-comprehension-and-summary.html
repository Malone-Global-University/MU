Òye Ìròyìn àti Àkópọ̀

Media Comprehension and Summary · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Understand the main idea and key details of a short adapted news report or interview.
  2. Attribute information to a source using reported-speech formulae such as ó sọ pé and wọ́n ròyìn pé ("he/she said that," "they reported that").
  3. Track tense and aspect across a news report, distinguishing what has already happened, what is ongoing, and what is expected to happen.
  4. Summarize a short media report orally and in writing, preserving its main point and source.
  5. Recognize, without producing independently, embedded reported questions and multi-layered attribution ("she said that he asked whether..."), which later chapters will continue to develop.

Communication Scenario

After the community meeting about the market roof and the borehole, a local radio station broadcasts a short news report about the meeting, followed by a brief interview with one of the residents. Adé's family listens to the broadcast together, and Adé's father asks Adé to summarize what the reporter and the resident said.

  1. This chapter develops reported-speech formulae with sọ pé, wí pé, and ròyìn pé, and source attribution, while consolidating the tense/aspect particles ń, ti, and yóò from earlier chapters for tracking time in a news report.
  2. It introduces vocabulary for media, interviews, and public announcements.
  3. By the end of the chapter, you should be able to understand a short media report, attribute its content to a source, track its tense and aspect, and produce an accurate summary — while continuing to recognize, without producing, embedded reported questions.

Pronunciation and Orthography Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Listening to connected media speech

News presenters and radio announcers typically speak at a steadier, more deliberate pace than ordinary conversation, with clear phrase breaks between the reported statement and the attribution frame that introduces it. This makes media speech a useful bridge between slow, careful classroom speech and the faster pace of natural conversation.

  • Practice ó sọ pé, ó wí pé, and wọ́n ròyìn pé as fixed openings, listening for the short pause that often follows pé before the reported content begins.
  • Listen for the tone-bearing distinction between sọ (say) and similarly shaped words; do not let the attribution verb flatten under reading speed.
  • Read the model dialogue and the listening passage below aloud twice: once phrase by phrase, and once at the measured pace of a radio broadcast.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops reported-speech formulae and source attribution as new productive systems, while consolidating the tense/aspect particles ń, ti, and yóò for tracking time in connected news reports.

Introduced: Reported speech with `sọ pé` and `wí pé`

Building block
[Ẹni tí ó sọ] + sọ pé / wí pé + Gbólóhùn
[Speaker] + sọ pé / wí pé + [full clause reporting what was said] — like mo lérò pé from Chapter 8, these attribution frames attach a complete clause with its own subject and tense marking, not a bare verb. `Wí pé` is somewhat more formal than `sọ pé`, but both are widely used.
Agbéròyìn náà sọ pé ìpàdé àwùjọ kan wáyé ní ọ̀sẹ̀ tó kọjá.
The reporter said that a community meeting took place last week. (Literally: reporter that say that meeting community one happen at week that passed.)
  • Obìnrin kan wí pé kànga omi ṣe pàtàkì jùlọ fún àdúgbò náà. A woman said that a water borehole was most important for the community. wí pé + pé clause reporting an opinion
  • Ìyá Agbà sọ pé ó yẹ kí wọ́n kọ́kọ́ tún ọjà ṣe. Ìyá Agbà said that they should repair the market first. sọ pé + modal clause reporting advice

Introduced: Source attribution with `ròyìn pé` and `gẹ́gẹ́ bí ... ṣe sọ`

  • Wọ́n ròyìn pé àwọn olùgbé àdúgbò ti gbà láti kọ́kọ́ mọ iye owó tí wọ́n ní. They reported that the residents had agreed to first find out how much money they had. wọ́n ròyìn pé + perfective ti reporting a completed decision
  • Gẹ́gẹ́ bí ọkùnrin kan ṣe sọ, ìlera àwọn ọmọdé ṣe pàtàkì ju ọjà lọ. According to one man, children's health matters more than the market. formal source-citing frame + reported opinion

Ròyìn pé is used specifically for media reporting, while sọ pé and wí pé are general reported-speech frames usable in any context. Gẹ́gẹ́ bí ... ṣe sọ is a formal citing frame common in written and broadcast news, useful for naming a specific source before reporting their words.

Consolidated: Tense/aspect tracking with `ń`, `ti`, and `yóò` in news

News reports move between what has already happened, what is currently underway, and what is expected next, and careful listeners track this through the particles already active since earlier volumes: ti for completed or resultative events ("ó ti gbà" — they have agreed), ń for something ongoing ("wọ́n ń jíròrò" — they are discussing), and yóò for what is expected ("wọn yóò pinnu" — they will decide). A summary that flattens all of these into a single tense loses information the report actually contains.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé àti bàbá rẹ̀ ń gbọ́ ìròyìn lórí rédíò nípa ìpàdé àwùjọ tí ó ṣẹlẹ̀ ní ọ̀sẹ̀ tó kọjá.
Setting: Adé and his father are listening to a radio news report about the community meeting that happened last week.

AGBÉRÒYÌN Wọ́n ròyìn pé ìpàdé àwùjọ kan wáyé ní àdúgbò kan ní ọ̀sẹ̀ tó kọjá láti jíròrò bí a ṣe máa lo owó àpapọ̀. It was reported that a community meeting took place in a neighborhood last week to discuss how to use the shared fund.
BÀBÁ Ǹjẹ́ wọ́n ti pinnu ohun tí wọn yóò ṣe? Have they already decided what they will do?
AGBÉRÒYÌN Agbéròyìn náà sọ pé wọn ò tíì pinnu, torí pé wọ́n ń dúró láti kọ́kọ́ mọ iye owó tí wọ́n ní. The reporter said that they had not yet decided, because they were waiting to first find out how much money they had.
ADÉ Ta ni ó sọ pé ìlera àwọn ọmọdé ṣe pàtàkì jù ọjà lọ? Who said that children's health matters more than the market?
BÀBÁ Gẹ́gẹ́ bí agbéròyìn náà ṣe sọ, obìnrin kan ní ìpàdé náà ni ó sọ bẹ́ẹ̀. According to the reporter, it was a woman at the meeting who said that.
AGBÉRÒYÌN A óò tún gbọ́ ìròyìn síi lórí ọ̀rọ̀ yìí lọ́la. We will hear more news on this matter tomorrow.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Community news and the role of radio

    In many Yorùbá-speaking communities, radio remains an important and trusted source of local news alongside newer digital media, and community meetings, town announcements, and public decisions are commonly reported on local stations in the language spoken by the audience. Attribution matters in this context: careful speakers distinguish what a report states directly from what it attributes to a named or unnamed source, a distinction reflected in the frames practiced in this chapter.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Dropping the attribution frame while still reporting

    Error: Summarizing a report as though its content were the learner's own direct statement, such as *Ìlera àwọn ọmọdé ṣe pàtàkì ju ọjà lọ when the learner is actually reporting what someone else said.

    Correction: Keep the attribution frame in place: Obìnrin kan sọ pé ìlera àwọn ọmọdé ṣe pàtàkì ju ọjà lọ ("a woman said that children's health matters more than the market"). A summary should make clear whose view is being reported.

    Common Mistake — Flattening tense/aspect when summarizing

    Error: Reporting a completed event and an expected future event with the same bare-verb form, such as *wọ́n pinnu, wọ́n ṣe é when the report actually distinguished a completed decision from an action still to come.

    Correction: Preserve the original tense/aspect distinctions: wọ́n ti gbà, wọn yóò sì pinnu ohun tí wọn yóò ṣe lẹ́yìn náà ("they have agreed, and they will decide what they will do after that").

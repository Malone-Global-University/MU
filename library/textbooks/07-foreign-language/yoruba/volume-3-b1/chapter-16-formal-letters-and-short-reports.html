Formal Letters and Short Reports · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Open and close a formal letter or message using appropriate conventions of address and courtesy.
  2. State a reason, request, or complaint clearly using developed reason clauses.
  3. Reference where information came from when writing a short report.
  4. Organize a short formal report with a clear beginning, body, and closing.
  5. Distinguish the register of a formal letter from the expressive, informal description practiced in Chapter 15.
  6. Continue recognizing relative clauses and contrastive focus at the level active since Chapter 12, without producing new instances independently.

Opening Communication Scenario

Adé's cousin, who now works at the local council office, has asked him to help draft a short letter to a community office requesting repair of a broken water pipe near their street, and to write a brief report afterward summarizing what happened when the office responded. Adé realizes the vivid, expressive language he practiced for describing the market day in Chapter 15 will not serve him here — a formal letter and report call for measured, precise language, clear structure, and respectful address.

  1. This chapter develops formal openings and closings, reason clauses for requests and complaints, and source information for reporting what was said or found.
  2. Formal register is treated as a distinct communicative tool, separate from the expressive register of Chapter 15.
  3. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to write a short formal letter and a short report with a clear structure.

Pronunciation Focus

Focus — Even, measured delivery in formal reading

Formal letters and reports are read aloud with an even, unhurried rhythm and a narrower pitch range than the expressive delivery practiced in Chapter 15. Tone accuracy remains just as important, but the energy is calm and deliberate rather than dramatic.

  • Keep tone precise and steady on formal opening and closing formulas such as pẹ̀lú ọ̀wọ̀, since these phrases are often read aloud in institutional settings.
  • Avoid the sharp, bursting stress used for ideophones; formal register does not use ideophones.
  • Practice reading a formal sentence and a Chapter 15 expressive sentence side by side to hear the contrast in delivery.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter activates formal opening and closing formulas, develops reason clauses for the specific functions of request and complaint, and introduces source-marking with gẹ́gẹ́ bí for reporting what was said or found. Relative clauses and contrastive focus continue as recognition-only material, unchanged from Chapters 12–15.

Formal opening and closing formulas

Pattern

Pẹ̀lú ọ̀wọ̀, ... [body] ... Ọ̀wọ̀ ẹ,

These fixed formulas frame a formal letter with respect at both the opening and the closing. They are learned as formulaic units and do not require internal grammatical analysis to use correctly.

Pẹ̀lú ọ̀wọ̀, mo ń kọ̀wé yìí láti bẹ̀bẹ̀ pé kí a tún fọ́nà omi tí ó bà jẹ́ ṣe.

With respect, I am writing this letter to request that the damaged water pipe be repaired.

Reason clauses for requests

  • Mo ń bẹ̀bẹ̀ pé kí wọ́n tún fọ́nà omi ṣe nítorí pé omi ti ń jò fún ọ̀sẹ̀ méjì. I am requesting that they repair the pipe because water has been leaking for two weeks. reason clause (developed) — request function
  • Nítorí pé àwọn ọmọdé ń lọ sí ilé-ìwé ní ojú ọ̀nà náà, ó ṣe pàtàkì kí a yára tún un ṣe. Because children pass along that road to school, it is important that it be repaired quickly. reason clause (developed) — supporting urgency

Reason clauses for complaints

  • Ẹ̀dùn ọkàn mi ni pé kò sí ẹnikẹ́ni tí ó dáhùn lẹ́tà tí a kọ ní oṣù tó kọjá. My complaint is that no one responded to the letter we wrote last month. reason clause (developed) — complaint function; recognition-only relative clause "tí ó dáhùn"

Source information with gẹ́gẹ́ bí

Pattern

Gẹ́gẹ́ bí [source] ṣe sọ, ...

This pattern attributes information to a specific source, a skill needed for reporting what an office, official, or document stated. It builds on the reported-speech awareness from Chapter 9 but applies it to written, formal source attribution.

Gẹ́gẹ́ bí ọ́fíìsì náà ṣe sọ, wọn yóò tún fọ́nà náà ṣe láàrin ọjọ́ méje.

According to the office, they will repair the pipe within seven days.

Register limit — Formal register excludes expressive language

Formal letters and reports do not use ideophones, intensifying reduplication such as gan-an, or vivid descriptive language of the kind practiced in Chapter 15. Keep sentences measured, factual, and organized around request, reason, and outcome.

Recognition only in this chapter — Do not produce independently yet

The reading and listening passages below contain a relative clause identifying which office responded and a contrastive-focus phrase distinguishing what was promised from what was delivered. These remain receptive, continuing the pattern from Chapters 12–15; you are not assessed on producing new instances of them here.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé àti ẹ̀gbọ́n rẹ̀ ń jíròrò bí wọ́n ṣe lè kọ lẹ́tà ẹ̀bẹ̀ sí ọ́fíìsì àdúgbò nípa fọ́nà omi tí ó bà jẹ́.
Setting: Adé and his older sibling are discussing how to write a request letter to the community office about a broken water pipe.

ẸGBỌ́N Kí ni a ó kọ ní ìbẹ̀rẹ̀ lẹ́tà náà? What should we write at the beginning of the letter?
ADÉ A ó kọ, "Pẹ̀lú ọ̀wọ̀, mo ń kọ̀wé yìí láti bẹ̀bẹ̀ pé kí a tún fọ́nà omi tí ó bà jẹ́ ṣe." We'll write, "With respect, I am writing this letter to request that the damaged water pipe be repaired."
ẸGBỌ́N Kí ló dé tí ó fi ṣe pàtàkì? Why is it important?
ADÉ Nítorí pé omi ti ń jò fún ọ̀sẹ̀ méjì, àti nítorí pé àwọn ọmọdé ń lọ sí ilé-ìwé ní ojú ọ̀nà náà. Because water has been leaking for two weeks, and because children pass along that road to school.
ẸGBỌ́N Ǹjẹ́ ó yẹ kí a fi ẹ̀dùn ọkàn kan kún un pé? Should we add a complaint as well?
ADÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ni. A lè kọ pé, "Ẹ̀dùn ọkàn mi ni pé kò sí ẹnikẹ́ni tí ó dáhùn lẹ́tà tí a kọ ní oṣù tó kọjá." Lẹ́yìn náà, a ó fi "Ọ̀wọ̀ ẹ," parí lẹ́tà náà. Yes. We can write, "My complaint is that no one responded to the letter we wrote last month." After that, we'll close the letter with "Respectfully yours."

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Formal correspondence and community life

    Written requests and reports to community offices, employers, schools, and institutions commonly follow a respectful, measured register, opening with a courteous formula and stating the request or concern plainly before offering supporting reasons. Many speakers treat a well-organized, polite letter as a sign of seriousness and respect toward the reader, distinct from the more expressive language used in personal storytelling. Learners should treat formal correspondence as one register among several, appropriate to institutional and professional settings, rather than as the only correct way to write.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Mixing expressive and formal register

    Error: Using an intensifier such as gan-an or an ideophone such as gbá! inside a formal letter or report, producing a tone that feels inappropriate for the setting.

    Correction: Keep formal letters and reports measured and factual. State the problem plainly, as in "omi ti ń jò fún ọ̀sẹ̀ méjì" ("water has been leaking for two weeks"), rather than adding expressive emphasis.

    Common Mistake — Omitting the source in a report

    Error: Reporting what an office or official said without marking the source, so the reader cannot tell whether the statement is fact, promise, or the writer's own opinion.

    Correction: Use gẹ́gẹ́ bí ... ṣe sọ to clearly attribute the statement, as in "gẹ́gẹ́ bí ọ́fíìsì náà ṣe sọ, wọn yóò tún fọ́nà náà ṣe" ("according to the office, they will repair the pipe").

    2. The office said they would send workers to repair the pipe within seven days.

Àríyànjiyàn àti Àtakò Onítọ̀wọ̀

Debate, Disagreement, and Polite Challenge · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Acknowledge another speaker's point before disagreeing with it, using a developed polite-disagreement frame.
  2. Concede a point while still maintaining your own position, using a developed concession clause.
  3. Ask challenging questions that invite a speaker to justify their reasoning.
  4. Use consolidated ni focus and subject/object focus to state a position clearly within a debate.
  5. Take turns appropriately in a structured group discussion.
  6. Continue recognizing contrastive focus, question focus, and relative clauses at the level active since Chapter 12, without producing new instances independently.

Opening Communication Scenario

Weeks after Adé and his cousin sent their letter about the broken water pipe, the community office finally responds — but not in the way anyone expected. The office proposes digging an entirely new borehole for the street instead of repairing the old pipe, at greater cost and with a longer wait. At a community meeting, neighbors disagree sharply: some want the quick, cheap repair; others argue the new borehole is the better long-term choice. Adé must listen carefully, respond to his neighbors' points, and state his own position — respectfully, but without simply agreeing with whoever spoke last.

  1. This chapter develops concession and contrast formulae, challenging questions, and polite disagreement, building on the formal register established in Chapter 16.
  2. Ni focus and subject/object focus continue at their consolidated level to help state a clear position; contrastive and question focus remain recognition-only, as they have been since Chapter 12.
  3. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to participate in a structured debate about a community decision.

Pronunciation Focus

Focus — Turn-taking and contrastive tone

Debate speech keeps the calm, measured delivery of formal register from Chapter 16, but adds a distinct rising lift at the end of a turn-holding phrase such as ẹ jọ̀wọ́, ẹ jẹ́ kí n parí ọ̀rọ̀ mi, signaling to the group that the speaker intends to continue rather than yield the floor.

  • Keep tone precise on bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé and síbẹ̀síbẹ̀, since a flattened tone can blur the contrast these words are meant to signal.
  • Pause briefly before ṣùgbọ́n in the disagreement frame, giving the listener a clear signal that a contrasting view is coming.
  • Practice reading a concession sentence and a plain declarative sentence side by side to hear how the concession clause changes the sentence's rhythm.
Gírámà àti Ìlànà Gbólóhùn

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops concession clauses with bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé ... síbẹ̀síbẹ̀ ..., a polite-disagreement frame built on acknowledgment plus contrast, and challenging questions that ask a speaker to justify a position. Consolidated ni focus and subject/object focus continue to state positions clearly. Contrastive focus, question focus, and relative clauses continue as recognition-only material, unchanged from Chapters 12–16.

Concession clauses

Pattern

Bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé [point conceded], síbẹ̀síbẹ̀ [speaker's position].

This pattern grants that another point has some validity before restating the speaker's own position, without simply abandoning it.

Bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé kànga omi tuntun yóò gbowó lórí, síbẹ̀síbẹ̀ mo rò pé ó dára ju títún fọ́nà àtijọ́ ṣe lọ.

Even though a new borehole will cost more money, nevertheless I think it is better than repairing the old pipe.

Polite disagreement frame

  • Mo lóye ohun tí o sọ, ṣùgbọ́n mo rò pé a kò ní owó tó láti kọ́ kànga omi tuntun ní àkókò yìí. I understand what you said, but I think we do not have enough money to build a new borehole at this time. acknowledgment + ṣùgbọ́n contrast — polite disagreement function
  • Mo mọ̀ pé o fẹ́ kí a yára ṣe é, ṣùgbọ́n a nílò láti ronú síi kí a tó pinnu. I know you want us to act quickly, but we need to think further before we decide. acknowledgment + ṣùgbọ́n contrast — recommending caution

Challenging questions

  • Kí ni ìdí tí o fi rò pé kànga omi tuntun yóò sàn ju fọ́nà àtijọ́ tí a bá tún un ṣe lọ? Why do you think a new borehole would be better than the old pipe if we repair it? challenging question (developed) — invites justification
  • Ǹjẹ́ o ti ronú nípa iye owó tí ìdíyelé kànga náà yóò jẹ́ fún àwùjọ wa? Have you thought about how much the borehole's cost would be for our community? challenging question (developed) — raises a concrete concern
Consolidated — Ni focus and subject/object focus in debate

Statements such as owó ni ìṣòro náà ("money is the problem") and àwọn ọmọdé ni a ń ronú nípa wọn ("it is the children we are thinking about") use the ni focus and object focus already consolidated since earlier chapters. In debate, these patterns help a speaker state clearly what the real issue or priority is, without introducing new focus structures.

Recognition only in this chapter — Do not produce independently yet

The reading and listening passages below contain a contrastive-focus phrase distinguishing what one neighbor emphasizes from what another does, a question-focus construction, and a relative clause identifying which proposal is under discussion. These remain receptive, continuing the pattern from Chapters 12–16; you are not assessed on producing new instances of them here.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Ní ìpàdé àwùjọ, Adé àti aládùúgbò rẹ̀ Ìyá Kúnlé ń jiyàn nípa bóyá kí wọ́n bèèrè fún kànga omi tuntun tàbí kí wọ́n kàn tún fọ́nà àtijọ́ ṣe.
Setting: At the community meeting, Adé and his neighbor Ìyá Kúnlé are debating whether to request a new borehole or simply repair the old pipe.

ÌYÁ KÚNLÉ Mo rò pé ó yẹ kí a bèèrè fún kànga omi tuntun, kì í ṣe àtúnṣe fọ́nà àtijọ́ mọ́. I think we should ask for a new borehole, not another repair of the old pipe.
ADÉ Mo lóye ohun tí o sọ, ṣùgbọ́n mo rò pé a kò ní owó tó láti kọ́ kànga omi tuntun ní àkókò yìí. I understand what you said, but I think we do not have enough money to build a new borehole at this time.
ÌYÁ KÚNLÉ Kí ni ìdí tí o fi rò pé fọ́nà àtijọ́ yóò tó dáadáa lẹ́ẹ̀kan sí i? Why do you think the old pipe will hold up well one more time?
ADÉ Bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé fọ́nà àtijọ́ ti bàjẹ́ ní ọ̀pọ̀lọpọ̀ ìgbà, síbẹ̀síbẹ̀ mo rò pé àtúnṣe kíákíá yóò gbà wá là ní báyìí, kí a sì fi ọwọ́ mú ọ̀rọ̀ kànga tuntun nígbà tí owó bá tó. Even though the old pipe has broken many times, nevertheless I think a quick repair will save us for now, and we can take up the matter of a new borehole once the money is available.
ÌYÁ KÚNLÉ Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, ẹ jẹ́ kí n parí ọ̀rọ̀ mi — owó ni ìṣòro náà lóòótọ́, ṣùgbọ́n a lè ṣe àgbàjọ owó díẹ̀díẹ̀ láàrin àwọn olùgbé ìta. Please, allow me to finish my point — money is indeed the problem, but we could raise funds gradually among the residents of the street.

Writing Workshop

Write a short structured argument (70–100 words) with full tone marks and dot-under letters throughout. Your text must state a position, acknowledge an opposing view politely, use at least one concession clause, and end with a clear recommendation.

Sample argument shell — for reference only

Mo rò pé [ipò rẹ] ni ó dára jù.

I think [your position] is the best option.

Mo lóye ohun tí àwọn kan sọ pé [ìpò àtakò], ṣùgbọ́n mo rò pé [ìdí rẹ].

I understand what some say, that [opposing position], but I think [your reason].

Bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé [ẹ̀tọ́ àtakò], síbẹ̀síbẹ̀ mo dúró ṣinṣin pé [ìpinnu tí a dábàá].

Even though [the opposing view has some merit], nevertheless I stand firm that [proposed decision].

Nítorí náà, mo dábàá pé [ìṣe tí ó yẹ kí a gbé].

Therefore, I propose [the action that should be taken].

    • Every vowel that should carry a high or low tone mark has one.

    • The concession clause grants a real point rather than a token one.
    • The recommendation follows clearly from the stated reasons.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Community deliberation and disagreement

    Many community meetings value giving every speaker a full hearing before a decision is made, and phrases that acknowledge another's point before disagreeing, such as mo lóye ohun tí o sọ, are common ways of disagreeing without appearing disrespectful toward an elder or a fellow resident. Deliberation may continue across more than one meeting rather than being resolved immediately, particularly when cost or long-term consequences are involved. Learners should treat patient, acknowledgment-based disagreement as one effective approach among several, rather than assuming all communities debate in exactly the same way.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Disagreeing without acknowledgment

    Error: Responding to an opposing view with only ṣùgbọ́n ("but") and a counterargument, skipping any acknowledgment of what the other speaker said.

    Correction: Open with an acknowledgment such as "mo lóye ohun tí o sọ" ("I understand what you said") before stating the contrasting view, so the disagreement sounds measured rather than abrupt.

    Common Mistake — Treating a concession as full agreement

    Error: Using bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé to grant a point and then failing to restate one's own position, leaving the argument unclear.

    Correction: Always pair the concession with síbẹ̀síbẹ̀ and a restated position, as in "bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé kànga tuntun dára, síbẹ̀síbẹ̀ mo rò pé àtúnṣe kíákíá yóò sàn ju u lọ ní báyìí" ("even though a new borehole is good, nevertheless I think a quick repair is better for now").

    Answer Key

    Listening Practice Questions

    1. The community debated for one hour at the Saturday meeting.
    2. Ìyá Kúnlé argued that a new borehole was the right solution, because the old pipe had broken many times.
    3. Adé thought instead that a quick repair would help them sooner.
    4. The community agreed to first ask for the cost of both the new borehole and the repair, and to meet again to hear the cost information before making a final decision.

    Reading Practice Questions

    1. The traders are debating whether to change the market day from Sunday to Wednesday.
    2. Bàbá Adùké argued that the Wednesday market was more important, because many customers came from other towns on that day.
    3. Ìyá Tọ́pẹ́ raised the concern that changing the day would disrupt those who had known the Sunday market for many years.
    4. The traders' leader proposed holding a vote among all the traders before making a final decision.

    Guided Practice — Sample Responses

    1. The concession clause grants that the new borehole would cost more, then restates that a repair is still better for now. 2. Adé uses the polite-disagreement frame, since he disagrees with Ìyá Kúnlé's proposal after acknowledging it. 3. Ìyá Kúnlé's question asks Adé to justify why he believes the old pipe will hold up well one more time. 4. "Owó ni ìṣòro náà" uses ni focus to emphasize that money, specifically, is the problem.

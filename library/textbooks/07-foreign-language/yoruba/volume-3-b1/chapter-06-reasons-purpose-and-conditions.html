Ìdí, Ète, àti Ipò Kòndíṣọ̀nù

Reasons, Purpose, and Conditions · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Give reasons using torí and torí pé.
  2. Express purpose using kí clauses such as "so that."
  3. Form conditional clauses with bí ("if") and time clauses with nígbà tí ("when").
  4. Express obligation with gbọdọ̀ and advice with ó yẹ kí.
  5. Combine reason, purpose, and condition clauses with relative clauses and serial verbs from earlier chapters in a single explanation.
  6. Recognize, without producing independently, negative and focus-marked reason and condition clauses, which later chapters will continue to develop.

Communication Scenario

Adé's cousin is now settling into the neighborhood and keeps asking why things are done a certain way — why the gate is locked at night, why the shop closes early on market days, what to do if it rains during the walk to school. Simple descriptions are no longer enough; Adé now needs to explain reasons, purposes, and conditions clearly, and to give the cousin advice about what one should and must do.

  1. This chapter develops reason clauses with torí and torí pé, purpose clauses with kí, and condition/time clauses with bí and nígbà tí.
  2. It introduces obligation with gbọdọ̀ and advice with ó yẹ kí, both of which combine with the same kí-clause pattern used for purpose.
  3. By the end of the chapter, you should be able to write and speak a reasoned explanation that gives a reason, states a purpose or condition, and offers advice — while continuing to recognize, without producing, more advanced negative and focus-marked versions of these clauses.

Pronunciation and Orthography Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Clause-level rhythm in explanations

Reason, purpose, and condition clauses often stack two or three clauses in one sentence. Learners sometimes flatten the tone on connectors like torí, kí, and bí because these words are short and feel grammatical rather than meaningful. Each connector still carries its full tone and must be heard clearly, since it signals exactly how the clauses relate to one another.

  • Practice saying mo gbọdọ̀ lọ sí ọjà torí pé ilé wa kò ní ẹ̀wà mọ́ slowly, giving torí pé its full weight before speeding up.
  • Pause briefly at the boundary between clauses when first practicing a sentence with bí or nígbà tí, then smooth the pause into natural connected speech.
  • Read the model dialogue below aloud twice: once identifying every reason, purpose, and condition connector, and once at a natural conversational pace.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops reason clauses, purpose clauses, condition/time clauses, obligation, and advice as productive systems, building on the relative clauses and expanded reference introduced in Chapter 5.

Introduced: Reason clauses with `torí` and `torí pé`

Building block
Gbólóhùn Àkọ́kọ́ + torí pé + Gbólóhùn Ìdí
[Main clause] + torí pé + [clause giving the reason] — torí pé attaches a full clause; torí alone attaches directly to a noun phrase.
Mo dá ilẹ̀kùn dè torí pé ó ti ṣú.
I locked the door because it had gotten dark. (Literally: I locked door because that it has-become dark.)
  • A kì í jáde nígbà òjò torí ààbò. We don't go out during rain, because of safety. torí + noun phrase (ààbò)
  • Ìtajà náà ti ilẹ̀kùn pa ní kùtùkùtù torí pé ọjọ́ ọjà ni. That shop closes early because it is a market day. torí pé + full clause (ọjọ́ ọjà ni)

Introduced: Purpose clauses with `kí`

A purpose clause states why an action is done, in the sense of "so that" or "in order that." The clause after kí uses a subjunctive-like subject pronoun and does not take the ordinary tense/aspect particles of a main clause.

  • Mo ti ilẹ̀kùn kí ọlọ́ṣà má bàa wọlé. I locked the door so that thieves would not enter. kí + subject + verb, purpose clause
  • A gbọdọ̀ kúrò ní kùtùkùtù kí a lè yẹra fún ọ̀pọ̀ ènìyàn. We must leave early so that we can avoid the crowd. kí + a + lè + verb, purpose clause with ability marker lè

Introduced: Condition and time clauses with `bí` and `nígbà tí`

Building block
Bí + Gbólóhùn + bá + Gbólóhùn Kejì
Bí introduces the condition; bá appears with the verb inside the condition clause; the second clause states the result.
Bí òjò bá rọ̀, a kì í jáde.
If it rains, we don't go out. (Literally: If rain bá falls, we don't go-out.)
  • Nígbà tí mo dé ilé, mo rí i pé ẹnu ọ̀nà ti ṣí. When I arrived home, I saw that the gate was already open. nígbà tí + clause, time reference
  • Bí o bá rí ìṣòro kan, ó yẹ kí o sọ fún mi lẹ́sẹ̀kẹsẹ̀. If you encounter a problem, you should tell me right away. bí + bá conditional combined with ó yẹ kí advice clause

Notice that bí ... bá introduces a general or hypothetical condition, while nígbà tí introduces a specific time reference, often for something that already happened or regularly happens. Do not use nígbà tí where a genuine hypothetical condition, rather than a specific occasion, is intended.

Introduced: Obligation with `gbọdọ̀` and advice with `ó yẹ kí`

  • O gbọdọ̀ wẹ ọwọ́ rẹ kí o tó jẹun. You must wash your hands before you eat. gbọdọ̀ + verb, obligation
  • Ó yẹ kí a bọ̀wọ̀ fún àwọn àgbà nínú àdúgbò yìí. We should respect the elders in this neighborhood. ó yẹ kí + subject + verb, advice
Why `kí` does more than one job

English uses different words for purpose ("so that"), advice ("should"), and reported wish ("that"), while Yorùbá reuses the connector kí across several of these functions, always followed by the same subjunctive-like clause pattern. Learners transferring English patterns sometimes look for a separate word for each function; instead, focus on recognizing the pattern kí + subject + verb and interpreting its function from context, whether it follows a purpose statement, an obligation, or a piece of advice.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Ẹ̀gbọ́n Adé ń béèrè nípa àwọn òfin ilé àti ìṣẹ̀lẹ̀ tí ó ṣẹlẹ̀ ní ọjọ́ òjò.
Setting: Adé's cousin is asking about household rules and what happens on a rainy day.

ẸGBỌ́N Torí kí ni a fi ń ti ilẹ̀kùn ní gbogbo alẹ́? Why do we lock the door every night?
ADÉ A ń ti ilẹ̀kùn torí ààbò, kí ọlọ́ṣà má bàa wọlé sí ilé wa. We lock the door because of safety, so that thieves will not enter our house.
ẸGBỌ́N Bí òjò bá rọ̀ ní òwúrọ̀, kí ni a máa ṣe nípa lílọ sí ilé ìwé? If it rains in the morning, what do we do about going to school?
ADÉ Bí òjò bá rọ̀ gidigidi, ó yẹ kí a dúró díẹ̀ kí òjò tó dínkù, kí a má bàa bẹ̀rẹ̀ àìsàn. If it rains heavily, we should wait a little for the rain to reduce, so that we don't fall ill.
ẸGBỌ́N Nígbà tí mo dé ibí, mo rí ọ̀pọ̀ òfin lórí ògiri ilé ìwé. Kí ni ìdí gbogbo rẹ̀? When I arrived here, I saw many rules on the school wall. What is the reason for all of them?
ADÉ Àwọn olùkọ́ ló kọ wọ́n sílẹ̀ kí gbogbo akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ lè mọ ohun tí ó yẹ kí wọ́n ṣe àti èyí tí wọn kò gbọdọ̀ ṣe. The teachers wrote them down so that every student would know what they should do and what they must not do.
ẸGBỌ́N Ó dà bíi pé ìlú yìí ní ọ̀nà àbáyọ fún ọ̀pọ̀ ìṣòro. It seems like this place has a solution for many problems.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Giving reasons and advice respectfully

    When explaining a rule or giving advice to someone younger or in a family setting, many Yorùbá speakers pair an obligation or advice statement with a clear reason, using torí pé or a purpose clause with kí, rather than stating a rule as a bare command. This pattern reflects a broader communicative expectation that guidance should be explained, not merely issued, especially across generations within a household or community.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Using `torí` where a full clause is needed

    Error: Producing a sentence such as *mo dá ilẹ̀kùn dè torí ó ti ṣú, using torí directly before a full clause on the model of English "because."

    Correction: Use torí pé before a full clause, as in mo dá ilẹ̀kùn dè torí pé ó ti ṣú. Reserve plain torí for a noun phrase, as in torí ààbò ("because of safety").

    Common Mistake — Confusing `bí ... bá` with `nígbà tí`

    Error: Using nígbà tí for a general hypothetical condition, as in *nígbà tí òjò bá rọ̀, a kì í jáde when a genuine "if" condition, not a specific past occasion, is intended.

    Correction: Use bí ... bá for hypothetical or general conditions, as in bí òjò bá rọ̀, a kì í jáde ("if it rains, we don't go out"). Reserve nígbà tí for a specific time reference, as in nígbà tí mo dé ilé, mo rí i pé ẹnu ọ̀nà ti ṣí.

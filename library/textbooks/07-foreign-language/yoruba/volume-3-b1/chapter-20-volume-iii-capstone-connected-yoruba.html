Volume III Capstone: Connected Yorùbá · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Narrate a connected personal or community event using consolidated tense-aspect particles, serial verbs, and reference tracking.
  2. Write a short formal report that summarizes credited sources using paraphrase and the gẹ́gẹ́ bí ... ṣe wí attribution pattern.
  3. Summarize a short media-style passage, identifying its main idea and key details.
  4. Participate in a structured debate, stating an opinion, supporting it with a reason clause, and responding to a counterpoint with a concession.
  5. Respond to spontaneous interview questions about your own experience, opinions, and community, drawing on the full range of Volume III productive systems.
  6. Demonstrate consolidated control of tone marking, dot-under orthography, `ni` focus, and relative clauses with `tí` across all five portfolio components.
  7. Reflect on your growth from Chapter 1 to Chapter 20 and identify which Volume III systems you can now use with increasing independence.

Opening Communication Scenario

Adé's class has reached the final week of the term. The instructor explains that instead of one large final test, each learner will complete a **capstone portfolio**: five connected tasks that together demonstrate everything the class has built since Chapter 1 — narrating events, explaining reasons, using serial verbs, focusing information, describing people and places with relative clauses, summarizing media, and holding a respectful debate. Adé decides to build his portfolio around the well project from Chapters 17 through 19, extending it into a fuller account of his community and his own growth as a Yorùbá speaker.

  1. This chapter introduces no new grammar. It assembles the productive systems developed across Chapters 1–19 into five connected portfolio components.
  2. The five components are: a personal-community narrative, a short written report, a media summary, a structured debate turn, and an oral interview.
  3. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to complete an entire connected-discourse portfolio in Standard Written Yorùbá, and to recognize how far your independent communication has developed.

Pronunciation Focus

Focus — Sustained fluency across five connected tasks

The capstone asks you to sustain tone accuracy and phrase rhythm across five different task types in one sitting, moving between narrative, explanation, summary, argument, and spontaneous response.

  • Rehearse each portfolio component separately before attempting the full sequence, so that tone and rhythm remain stable when you shift task type.
  • Pay particular attention to tone on particles and pronouns at the start of each new sentence, since these are the syllables most likely to flatten when moving quickly between tasks.
  • During the interview component, allow yourself brief pauses to plan your tone and word order before speaking, rather than rushing into an under-marked response.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new productive grammar system. It assesses consolidated Volume III systems: narrative sequencing and reference tracking from Chapters 1–2, serial verbs from Chapter 3, `ni` focus from Chapter 4, relative clauses with `tí` from Chapter 5, reason and concession clauses from Chapters 6, 13, and 17, reported speech and source attribution from Chapter 9, and report organization from Chapter 16. Contrastive focus, question focus, and full relative-clause embedding remain recognition-only, as they have been since Chapter 12.

Connecting narrative, reason, and focus in one passage

Pattern

[Ìṣẹ̀lẹ̀ àkọ́kọ́], nítorí pé [ìdí], [ìṣẹ̀lẹ̀ tí ó tẹ̀lé]. [Ẹni/Ohun] ni ó [ìgbésẹ̀ pàtàkì].

This pattern chains an event, a reason clause, a following event, and a focus sentence that highlights the most important participant or action, consolidating narrative sequencing, reason clauses, and `ni` focus from earlier chapters.

Nígbà tí a parí kànga náà, nítorí pé gbogbo àwùjọ ṣiṣẹ́ pọ̀, omi bẹ̀rẹ̀ sí í tú jáde. Àwùjọ wa ni ó fi hàn pé ìṣọ̀kan lè yanjú ìṣòro ńlá.

When we finished the well, because the whole community worked together, water began to flow. It is our community that showed that unity can solve a big problem.

Debate turn: opinion, reason, and concession

  • Mo rò pé ó yẹ kí a kọ́ kànga kejì, nítorí pé omi kan kò tó fún gbogbo àdúgbò. I think we should build a second well, because one well is not enough for the whole neighborhood. opinion frame + reason clause
  • Bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé owó kò pọ̀, síbẹ̀síbẹ̀ a lè bẹ̀rẹ̀ pẹ̀lú ohun tí a ní. Even though there is not much money, nevertheless we can start with what we have. concession clause, consolidated since Chapter 13
Consolidated — Source attribution and relative clauses continue

The media summary and interview components continue to rely on gẹ́gẹ́ bí ... ṣe wí for source attribution, consolidated since Chapter 9, and on productive `tí` relative clauses such as ọkùnrin tí ó kọ́ kànga náà ("the man who built the well"), consolidated since Chapter 5.

Recognition only — No new productive targets this chapter

The reading and listening materials below may contain a contrastive-focus phrase or an embedded relative clause inside another relative clause. These remain receptive, continuing the pattern from Chapters 12–19; your portfolio should rely on the consolidated systems named above rather than on new independent production of these forms.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé ń bá ẹgbọ́n rẹ̀ obìnrin, Bọ́lá, sọ̀rọ̀ nípa àpapọ̀ iṣẹ́ tí ó ń pèsè sílẹ̀.
Setting: Adé is discussing the portfolio he is preparing with his older sister, Bọ́lá.

BỌ́LÁ Adé, báwo ni àpapọ̀ iṣẹ́ rẹ ṣe ń lọ? Adé, how is your portfolio going?
ADÉ Ó ń lọ dáradára. Mo ti kọ ìtàn ìrírí mi nípa kànga náà, mo sì fẹ́ ṣe àkópọ̀ ìròyìn àti àríyànjiyàn ní ọ̀sẹ̀ yìí. It's going well. I've written my personal narrative about the well, and I want to do the media summary and debate this week.
BỌ́LÁ Kí ni apá tí ó ṣòro jù fún ọ nínú gbogbo iṣẹ́ márùn-ún náà? Which part is hardest for you among all five tasks?
ADÉ Ìfọ̀rọ̀wánilẹ́nuwò ni ó ṣòro jù, nítorí pé n kò mọ ìbéèrè tí wọn yóò béèrè lọ́wọ́ mi. The interview is hardest, because I don't know what questions they will ask me.
BỌ́LÁ Má bẹ̀rù. Ìwọ ni ó ti lo èdè Yorùbá fún ọdún mẹ́ta báyìí; o ti ní ìdàgbàsókè ńlá. Don't be afraid. It is you who has used Yorùbá for three years now; you've had great growth.
ADÉ Ó tọ́. Bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé mo máa ń ṣàṣìṣe nígbà mìíràn, síbẹ̀síbẹ̀ mo lè tún ọ̀rọ̀ mi ṣe kí n bàa lè sọ ohun tí mo fẹ́ sọ. True. Even though I sometimes make mistakes, nevertheless I can restate what I say so I can say what I want to say.

Guided Practice

  3. Locate the concession clause near the end of the dialogue and state what Adé concedes.
  4. Write one sentence of your own combining a reason clause and a `ni`-focus sentence about your own progress this volume.

Writing Workshop

Write the two written components of your capstone portfolio with full tone marks and dot-under letters throughout: (1) a personal-community narrative (150–200 words) and (2) a short written report (100–150 words) that summarizes at least two sources with clear attribution. Together, the two pieces must include at least one serial verb construction, one reason clause, one concession clause, one instance of consolidated `ni` focus, and one relative clause with `tí`.

Sample portfolio shell — for reference only

Ìtàn ìrírí: Ní [àkókò], [ìṣẹ̀lẹ̀ àkọ́kọ́] wáyé nínú àwùjọ mi. Nítorí pé [ìdí], [ìṣẹ̀lẹ̀ tí ó tẹ̀lé].

Narrative: At [time], [first event] happened in my community. Because [reason], [following event].

Ìjábọ̀: Gẹ́gẹ́ bí [orísun kìíní] ṣe wí, [àkópọ̀]. Ènìyàn tí ó [ìgbésẹ̀ pàtàkì] ni ó fi hàn wá pé [ìparí].

Report: According to [source one], [summary]. It is the person who [key action] that showed us that [conclusion], using a relative clause and `ni` focus.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Community reflection and collective storytelling

    Presenting a project that combines a personal account, community sources, and a public opinion, as Adé does throughout Chapters 17–20, reflects a common pattern in many Yorùbá-speaking communities: individual experience is generally understood as connected to family and community life, and community concerns are frequently discussed through shared narrative and open, respectful debate. Interviews and question-and-answer exchanges are also familiar and valued ways of demonstrating what one has learned, whether in a classroom, a family setting, or a community gathering.

    Portfolio Topic Bank — Suggestions, not requirements
    • A community project you have followed or contributed to
    • A family story spanning more than one generation
    • A local or diaspora news item and its effect on your community
    • A cultural practice you have researched this volume
    • A public health or public information topic with a personal connection
    • A school, work, or educational initiative you have observed
    • A comparison between two communities or regions
    • Your own experience learning Yorùbá across Volume III

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Treating the five components as unrelated

    Error: Writing or speaking about five completely different topics across the narrative, report, media summary, debate, and interview.

    Correction: Choose one connected topic, as Adé does with the well project, so that each component builds on and refers back to the others.

    Common Mistake — Abandoning tone accuracy under time pressure

    Error: Letting tone marking and dot-under spelling become inconsistent while rushing to finish all five portfolio components.

    Correction: Apply the Capstone Writing Checkpoint to every written piece individually, and rehearse each spoken piece separately before combining them, exactly as recommended in the Pronunciation Focus section.

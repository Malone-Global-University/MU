Narrative Sequencing and Participant Tracking · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Narrate a sequence of personal or community events in a clear, chronological order using time connectors.
  2. Distinguish and correctly apply the perfective/resultative particle ti and the progressive/imperfective particle ń within a single connected narrative.
  3. Track a participant across several linked sentences without repeating the full noun phrase unnecessarily or losing clarity about who is being discussed.
  4. Maintain accurate tone on pronouns and particles as narrative sentences grow longer and more connected.
  5. Write and speak a multi-sentence narrative about a past event, a visit, or a community story with correct sequencing and reference.
  6. Recognize, without producing, richer cross-sentence reference patterns that Volume III will continue to develop.

Communication Scenario

Adé's community newsletter editor liked his personal profile and has now asked him to write a longer piece: the story of a recent community event, a naming ceremony he attended last month. To tell this story well, Adé must keep events in the right order, make clear who is doing what at each point, and show the reader when something was already finished versus still happening as the story unfolds.

  1. This chapter develops two systems that were only recycled in Chapter 1: time connectors for ordering events, and pronoun/reference chains for tracking who is being discussed across a narrative.
  2. It also sharpens the contrast between ti (an event already completed or its result still holding) and ń (an event ongoing at a particular narrative moment), since narratives constantly move between these two states.
  3. By the end of the chapter, you should be able to tell a several-sentence story in which the listener never loses track of who the subject is or when each event happened relative to the others.

Pronunciation and Orthography Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Tone on ti and ń across a narrative chain

Because ti and ń recur many times within a single narrative, learners sometimes let their tone or vowel length drift as the story continues, especially when speaking quickly.

  • Practice saying three or four linked sentences aloud, keeping ti and ń equally clear each time they appear, rather than only on the first occurrence.
  • Check that pronoun tone (for example mo, ó, wọ́n) stays accurate even when the pronoun is repeated many times in a row while tracking the same participant.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops two systems introduced or consolidated earlier: time connectors for sequencing, and pronoun/reference chains for tracking participants. It also sharpens the working contrast between ti and ń.

Developed: Ordering events with time connectors

Building block
Ní àkọ́kọ́, Gbólóhùn kìíní. Lẹ́yìn ìgbà díẹ̀, Gbólóhùn kejì. Ní òpin ọjọ́ náà, Gbólóhùn kẹta.
[At first, event one.] [After a little while, event two.] [At the end of the day, event three.]
Ní àkọ́kọ́, àwọn àlejò dé sí ilé wa. Lẹ́yìn ìgbà díẹ̀, wọ́n bẹ̀rẹ̀ ìkíni. Ní òpin ọjọ́ náà, gbogbo wa jọ jẹ oúnjẹ àsè.
At first, the guests arrived at our house. After a little while, they began the greetings. At the end of the day, we all ate the celebration food together.
  • Ṣáájú kí a tó dé sí ìsomọlórúkọ náà, ìyá ọmọ náà ti pèsè oúnjẹ àsè sílẹ̀. Before we arrived at the naming ceremony, the child's mother had already prepared the celebration food. ṣáájú kí a tó + clause (event before the main narrative point)
  • Bí a ṣe ń sọ̀rọ̀ pẹ̀lú bàbá ọmọ náà, àwọn mìíràn ń pín ẹ̀bùn fún àwọn àlejò. As we were talking with the child's father, others were distributing gifts to the guests. bí a ṣe ń + clause (background ongoing action)

Developed: The ti / ń contrast in narration

In connected narration, ti marks an event that was already completed, or whose result still holds, at the narrative moment being described. Ń marks an event that was ongoing at that same moment. A single narrative sentence often needs both: one clause reporting what had already happened, and another reporting what was still happening.

  • Nígbà tí a dé, àwọn àlejò ti jókòó, àmọ́ oúnjẹ kò tí ì tó. When we arrived, the guests had already sat down, but the food was not yet enough. ti (completed/resultative) contrasted with kò tí ì (not-yet negation)
  • Bí ààrẹ àwùjọ ṣe ń kí àwọn àlejò, mo ti mọ̀ pé ọjọ́ náà máa dùn. As the community leader was greeting the guests, I already knew the day would be enjoyable. ń (ongoing) in one clause, ti (completed knowledge-state) in the linked clause

Developed: Pronoun and reference chains

When the same participant continues across several sentences, Yorùbá narration relies on consolidated pronoun forms rather than repeating the full noun phrase. Once a participant has been named clearly, later sentences may refer back with a pronoun as long as the reference stays unambiguous.

  • Bàbá ọmọ náà dúró ní iwájú gbogbo èniyàn. Ó dúpẹ́ lọ́wọ́ àwọn tí ó wá. Ó tún kí àwọn àgbà àdúgbò ní pàtàkì. The child's father stood in front of everyone. He thanked those who had come. He also greeted the neighborhood elders specially. full NP introduced once, then tracked with ó across three linked sentences

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé ń sọ ìtàn ìsomọlórúkọ kan fún alábòójútó ìwé ìròyìn àdúgbò.
Setting: Adé is telling the story of a naming ceremony to the community newsletter editor.

OLÓÒTÚ Ǹjẹ́ o lè sọ ìtàn ìsomọlórúkọ tí o lọ sí ní ọ̀sẹ̀ tó kọjá? Can you tell the story of the naming ceremony you attended last week?
ADÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ni. Ní àkọ́kọ́, mo dé sí ilé ìdílé náà ní kùtùkùtù òwúrọ̀. Ṣáájú kí n tó dé, ìyá ọmọ náà ti pèsè oúnjẹ àsè sílẹ̀. Yes. At first, I arrived at the family's house early in the morning. Before I arrived, the child's mother had already prepared the celebration food.
ADÉ Bàbá ọmọ náà dúró ní iwájú gbogbo èniyàn. Ó dúpẹ́ lọ́wọ́ àwọn tí ó wá. Ó tún kí àwọn àgbà àdúgbò ní pàtàkì. The child's father stood in front of everyone. He thanked those who had come. He also greeted the neighborhood elders specially.
OLÓÒTÚ Kí ni ó ṣẹlẹ̀ nígbà tí ẹ ń jẹ oúnjẹ? What happened while you were eating?
ADÉ Bí a ṣe ń jẹ oúnjẹ àsè, àwọn mìíràn ń pín ẹ̀bùn fún àwọn àlejò. Lẹ́sẹ̀kẹsẹ̀, wọ́n bẹ̀rẹ̀ orin àti ijó. As we were eating the celebration food, others were distributing gifts to the guests. Immediately, they began song and dance.
ADÉ Ní òpin ọjọ́ náà, gbogbo wa ti rẹ̀wẹ̀sì díẹ̀, àmọ́ inú gbogbo èniyàn dùn. Títí di ìsinsìnyí, mo ṣì ń rántí ọjọ́ náà pẹ̀lú ìdùnnú. At the end of the day, we were all a little tired, but everyone was happy. Up until now, I still remember that day with joy.

Writing Workshop

Write a connected narrative of 90–120 words with full tone marks and dot-under letters throughout, describing a past event, visit, or ceremony, using clear time sequencing and consistent participant reference.

  • Plan four or five key moments of the event in the order they happened.
  • Draft your narrative using at least three time connectors from this chapter's vocabulary table.
  • Include at least two ti clauses, two ń clauses, and one sentence chain in which a single participant is tracked across at least two consecutive sentences by pronoun rather than repeated noun phrase.
  • Read your draft aloud, checking that tone on ti, ń, and pronouns stays accurate throughout, not just in the opening sentences.
  • Apply the Tone and Orthography Checkpoint below before finalizing.

    • Include

    • at least 3 time connectors
    • at least 2 ti clauses and 2 ń clauses
    • one tracked-participant sentence chain
    • consistent chronological order

    Avoid

    • producing new relative clauses with tí
    • producing contrastive focus
    • producing expanded/resultative serial verbs
    • losing track of who the subject is mid-narrative
    Tone and Orthography Checkpoint
    • Every vowel that should carry a high or low tone mark has one.
    • Every mid-tone syllable is left correctly unmarked.
    • ẹ, ọ, and ṣ are distinguished from e, o, and s throughout.
    • Tone on ti, ń, and every pronoun is correct across the whole narrative, not only the first sentence.
    • No unscheduled relative clause, contrastive focus, or expanded serial verb has been produced independently.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — The naming ceremony as community narrative

    A naming ceremony, or ìsomọlórúkọ, is commonly understood among many Yorùbá families and communities as an occasion that brings extended family, neighbors, and community elders together, not only the immediate household. Retelling such an event afterward often follows a recognizable order — arrival, preparation, the naming itself, feasting, and closing well-wishes — though the exact sequence, participants, and formality vary by family, region, and occasion.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Confusing ti and ń in a single narrative

    Error: Using ń to describe an event that was already finished by the narrative moment, or using ti to describe an action that was still ongoing.

    Correction: Before writing each clause, ask whether the event was already complete (or its result still held) at that narrative moment — use ti — or whether it was still happening at that moment — use ń.

    Common Mistake — Repeating the full noun phrase in every sentence

    Error: Repeating a participant's full name or noun phrase in every sentence of a narrative (for example, "bàbá ọmọ náà" three times in a row), which sounds unnatural and choppy.

    Correction: Once a participant is clearly named, switch to the appropriate pronoun for later sentences as long as reference stays unambiguous, as modeled in the Model Dialogue above.

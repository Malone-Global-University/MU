Relative Clauses and Expanded Reference · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Form relative clauses with tí to describe people, things, and places.
  2. Build subject relative clauses, in which tí is followed directly by a verb describing the head noun.
  3. Build object relative clauses, in which tí introduces a clause whose object position refers back to the head noun.
  4. Expand noun phrases by combining relative clauses with possession, demonstratives, and modifiers already under your control.
  5. Track reference clearly across a description that uses more than one relative clause.
  6. Recognize, without producing independently, relative clauses that combine with focus or negation, which later chapters will continue to develop.

Communication Scenario

Adé is helping a visiting cousin get to know the neighborhood, describing the people, houses, and shops nearby so the cousin can find them again later. A simple list of names and places is not enough — Adé needs to say things like "the woman who sells the beans," "the house that has the blue gate," and "the shop that is near the post office," so that each description picks out exactly the right person or place.

  1. This chapter introduces tí relative clauses productively for the first time, building on the receptive and formulaic exposure from Chapters 3 and 4.
  2. It shows how Yorùbá attaches a relative clause directly after the noun it describes, using tí plus an ordinary clause, rather than a separate relative pronoun system with case marking.
  3. By the end of the chapter, you should be able to write and speak descriptive profiles of people, places, and things using relative clauses, and to recognize — without yet producing — relative clauses combined with focus or negation.

Pronunciation and Orthography Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Tone in longer noun phrases

As noun phrases grow longer with a head noun, a relative marker, and a full clause, learners often start to rush or flatten tone in the middle of the phrase, especially on tí itself and on the pronoun that follows it. Tí carries a high tone and must not be reduced to an unmarked or low-toned syllable, even in fast speech.

  • Practice saying obìnrin tí ó ń ta ẹ̀wà ("the woman who sells beans") slowly at first, keeping every tone mark distinct, then again at a natural pace.
  • Pause briefly after the head noun and before tí when first practicing, then gradually smooth the phrase into one connected unit.
  • Read the model dialogue below aloud twice: once noticing where each relative clause begins and ends, and once at natural conversational speed.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops tí relative clauses productively, building on the receptive/formulaic exposure from Chapters 3 and 4. It also expands noun phrases by combining relative clauses with possession, demonstratives, and modifiers.

Introduced: `tí` relative clauses

Building block
Orúkọ Ẹnìkan/Ohun kan + tí + Gbólóhùn
[Head noun] + tí + [clause describing the head noun] — the clause that follows tí attaches directly after the noun, with no separate word for "who," "which," or "that" beyond tí itself.
Obìnrin tí ó ń ta ẹ̀wà ń gbé nítòsí ọjà.
The woman who sells beans lives near the market. (Literally: Woman tí she is-selling beans lives near market.)
  • Ilé tí ó ní ẹnu ọ̀nà aláwọ̀ búlúù ni ti Bàbá Adé. The house that has a blue gate is Bàbá Adé's. subject relative clause: tí + ó ní ... describes ilé
  • Ìwé tí mo rà ní ọjà lánàá ti sọnù. The book that I bought at the market yesterday has gotten lost. object relative clause: tí + mo rà ... describes ìwé, the object of rà

Introduced: Subject relative clauses and object relative clauses

In a subject relative clause, the head noun is understood as the subject of the verb inside the relative clause; a weak subject pronoun such as ó or wọ́n usually appears right after tí to represent that subject. In an object relative clause, the head noun is understood as the object of the verb inside the relative clause; the object position after the verb stays empty, since the head noun outside the clause already fills that role in meaning.

  • Ọkùnrin tí ó ń ṣiṣẹ́ ní ilé iṣẹ́ àfìwéránṣẹ́ ni aládùúgbò mi. The man who works at the post office is my neighbor. subject relative: tí + ó ń ṣiṣẹ́ ... describes ọkùnrin
  • Ọmọdé tí a rí lánàá jẹ́ ọmọ aládùúgbò wa. The child whom we saw yesterday is our neighbor's child. object relative: tí + a rí (empty object slot) ... describes ọmọdé

Notice that the relative clause does not change the basic SVO shape of the sentence that contains it. The relative clause simply expands the noun phrase; the larger sentence around it keeps its own subject, verb, and object in their usual positions.

Why one word does the work of "who," "which," and "that"

English uses different relative pronouns depending on whether the head noun is a person ("who"), a thing ("which"), or either ("that"), and it sometimes drops the relative pronoun entirely ("the book I bought"). Yorùbá uses a single relative marker, tí, regardless of whether the head noun is a person, an animal, a thing, or a place, and tí is not optional the way English "that" sometimes is. Learners transferring English patterns often try to invent separate words for "who" versus "which"; Yorùbá does not require this distinction.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé ń ṣàlàyé fún ẹ̀gbọ́n rẹ̀ tí ó ṣẹ̀ṣẹ̀ dé nípa àwọn ilé àti ènìyàn tí ó wà nítòsí.
Setting: Adé is explaining to a cousin who has just arrived about the houses and people nearby.

ẸGBỌ́N Ilé wo ni tiwa nínú gbogbo àwọn ilé wọ̀nyí? Which house among all these is ours?
ADÉ Ilé tí ó ní ẹnu ọ̀nà aláwọ̀ búlúù, tí ó sì súnmọ́ igi ńlá náà, ni tiwa. The house that has a blue gate, and that is near the big tree, is ours.
ẸGBỌ́N Ta ni obìnrin tí a rí lẹ́gbẹ̀ẹ́ ilé náà? Who is the woman whom we saw beside that house?
ADÉ Ìyẹn ni obìnrin tí ó ń ta ẹ̀wà ní ọjà; gbogbo aládùúgbò mọ̀ ọ́n dáadáa. That is the woman who sells beans at the market; every neighbor knows her well.
ẸGBỌ́N Ǹjẹ́ ìtajà tí ó súnmọ́ ilé iṣẹ́ àfìwéránṣẹ́ jẹ́ tí ẹnikẹ́ni tí a mọ̀? Is the shop that is near the post office owned by anyone we know?
ADÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ ni. Ọkùnrin tí ó ń ṣiṣẹ́ níbẹ̀ ni aládùúgbò tí mo mọ̀ láti ìgbà èwe mi. Yes. The man who works there is a neighbor whom I have known since my childhood.
ẸGBỌ́N Ó dàbí àdúgbò tí ẹnikẹ́ni lè gbọ́kànlé, níbi tí gbogbo ènìyàn ti mọ ara wọn. It seems like a neighborhood that anyone can rely on, a place where everyone knows one another.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Describing people by relationship and role

    When describing a person in conversation, many Yorùbá speakers identify someone first by relationship, role, or a well-known activity — "the woman who sells beans," "the man who works at the post office" — rather than by name alone, especially when the listener may not yet know the person's name. This pattern makes relative clauses a natural, everyday tool for introducing and identifying people in a community, not just a formal grammatical device.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Dropping `tí` the way English drops "that"

    Error: Producing a sentence such as *ìwé mo rà ní ọjà ti sọnù, omitting tí before the relative clause on the model of English "the book I bought is lost."

    Correction: Keep tí in place, as in ìwé tí mo rà ní ọjà ti sọnù ("the book that I bought at the market is lost"). Unlike English "that," Yorùbá tí is not optional in a relative clause.

    Common Mistake — Inventing separate words for "who" and "which"

    Error: Trying to use one word for people and a different word for things, on the model of English "who" versus "which," when describing a person versus an object.

    Correction: Use tí for both, as in obìnrin tí ó ń ta ẹ̀wà ("the woman who sells beans") and ilé tí ó ní ẹnu ọ̀nà aláwọ̀ búlúù ("the house that has a blue gate"). Yorùbá does not distinguish relative markers by whether the head noun is a person or a thing.

    Answer Key

    Listening Practice Questions

    1. She lives near the market, in a house that has a blue gate and is near a big tree that has stood there for many years.
    2. A young boy who has known her since his birth helps her in her shop.
    3. The sentence is "Ilé tí ẹnìkan kò tíì tọ̀ jẹ́ ilé tí ó wà lẹ́gbẹ̀ẹ́ ọjà" (the house that no one has yet visited is the house that is beside the market); no one has visited it because the owner has just returned from a distant journey.
    4. A relative clause describing a place: "ilé tí ó ní ẹnu ọ̀nà aláwọ̀ búlúù" (the house that has a blue gate). A relative clause describing a person: "ọmọ tí ó ń ràn án lọ́wọ́" (the child who helps her).

    Chapter Review — Apá A

    1. In a subject relative clause, the head noun is the subject of the relative clause's verb, as in "obìnrin tí ó ń ta ẹ̀wà" (the woman who sells beans); in an object relative clause, the head noun is the object of the relative clause's verb, as in "ìwé tí mo rà" (the book that I bought). 2. Yorùbá tí is a required relative marker rather than an optional connector; unlike English "that," it cannot be omitted from the clause.

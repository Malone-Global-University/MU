Ẹ̀kọ́, Iṣẹ́, àti Ọ̀nà Ọjọ́ Iwájú

Education, Careers, and Future Pathways · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Talk about future plans using the future particle yóò (contracted á/ó) and time expressions for what has not yet happened.
  2. Express desire, intention, and ability with the modal verbs fẹ́ ("want"), gbèrò láti ("plan to"), and lè ("can").
  3. Use complement clauses after verbs of wanting, planning, hoping, and deciding.
  4. Describe education, training, and career paths using formal vocabulary and relative clauses with tí.
  5. Combine future forms, modality, and complement clauses with reason and purpose clauses from Chapter 6 to state and justify a career or education goal.
  6. Recognize, without producing independently, negative future forms and focus-marked future statements, which later chapters will continue to develop.

Communication Scenario

Adé's cousin has finished secondary school and must now decide what to study and what kind of work to pursue. An older family friend, a career counselor at the community center, sits down with the cousin to talk through options — what the cousin wants to do, what training is required, and what the cousin plans to do in the coming years.

  1. This chapter develops future forms with yóò, modal verbs of desire, intention, and ability, and complement clauses after verbs such as fẹ́, gbèrò láti, and retí pé.
  2. It introduces formal education and career vocabulary and extends relative clauses with tí from Chapter 5 to describe programs, professions, and qualifications.
  3. By the end of the chapter, you should be able to write and speak a short statement about your education or career goals that states a plan, gives a reason for it, and expresses what you hope will happen — while continuing to recognize, without producing, negative future and focus-marked future constructions.

Pronunciation and Orthography Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Formal vocabulary pronunciation

This chapter introduces several loanword-derived and multi-syllabic professional terms, such as yunifásítì and onimọ̀-ẹ̀rọ. Learners sometimes stress these words as though they were English, flattening the Yorùbá tones on later syllables. Every syllable in these words still carries its own tone and must be pronounced with the same care as shorter, more familiar vocabulary.

  • Practice onimọ̀-ẹ̀rọ syllable by syllable first: o-ni-mọ̀-ẹ̀-rọ, then at natural speed, keeping the low tones on mọ̀ and ẹ̀ audible.
  • Practice the future particle yóò with its full mid-high tone contour before reducing it to the short forms á or ó common in ordinary speech.
  • Read the model dialogue below aloud twice: once naming every profession and education word slowly, and once at a natural conversational pace.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops future forms, modal verbs of desire and ability, and complement clauses as productive systems, building on the purpose clauses and advice modals introduced in Chapter 6.

Introduced: Future forms with `yóò` (`á` / `ó`)

Building block
Kókó-ọ̀rọ̀ + yóò + Ìṣe
[Subject] + yóò + [verb] — yóò is the full form used in careful speech and writing; á or ó replace it in ordinary conversation without changing the meaning.
Mo máa lọ sí yunifásítì lọ́dún tí ń bọ̀.
I will go to university next year. (Literally: I future-marker go to university at year that is-coming.)
  • Ó máa kọ́ ẹ̀kọ́ ìṣègùn ní ìlú Èkó. She will study medicine in Lagos. máa (contracted future) + verb
  • A ó rí ìwé ẹ̀rí wa nígbà tí a bá parí ẹ̀kọ́. We will receive our certificates when we finish our studies. future + nígbà tí time clause (Chapter 6 recycling)

In formal written Yorùbá, yóò appears in its full form; in ordinary spoken Yorùbá the reduced forms máa, á, or ó are common. Both are correct within their register; this chapter's writing tasks require the full form yóò or its common formal-written alternative máa.

Introduced: Desire and intention with `fẹ́` and `gbèrò láti`

  • Mo fẹ́ láti di oníṣègùn. I want to become a doctor. fẹ́ + láti + verb, complement clause
  • Ó gbèrò láti kọ́ ẹ̀kọ́ ìṣẹ́ọwọ́ ní ọdún tí ń bọ̀. He plans to pursue vocational training next year. gbèrò láti + verb, formal planning complement

Introduced: Ability with `lè` and hope with `retí pé`

Building block
Kókó-ọ̀rọ̀ + retí pé + Gbólóhùn Kejì
[Subject] + retí pé + [full clause stating what is hoped or expected] — retí pé behaves like torí pé from Chapter 6, attaching a complete clause rather than a noun phrase.
Mo retí pé n óò rí iṣẹ́ rere nígbà tí mo bá parí ẹ̀kọ́ mi.
I hope that I will find good work when I finish my studies.
  • Ọmọdé lè di agbẹjọ́rò bí ó bá kẹ́kọ̀ọ́ ní yunifásítì. A young person can become a lawyer if they study at a university. lè + verb, ability + bí ... bá condition (Chapter 6 recycling)
  • A retí pé àwọn akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ tí ó parí ẹ̀kọ́ ìṣẹ́ọwọ́ á rí iṣẹ́ ní kánkán. We hope that students who complete vocational training will find work quickly. retí pé + tí relative clause + future á

Developed: Relative clauses with `tí` for education and careers

Relative clauses introduced in Chapter 5 now describe programs, institutions, and professions. The pattern remains the same: the head noun is followed by tí and a clause that describes it.

  • Yunifásítì tí mo fẹ́ lọ ní ẹ̀kọ́ ìṣègùn tó dára. The university that I want to attend has a good medical program. tí relative clause modifying yunifásítì, developed for formal domains
  • Iṣẹ́ tí n óò ṣe lọ́la gbọdọ̀ jẹ́ èyí tí ó bá ẹ̀bùn mi mu. The work I will do in the future must be one that suits my talents. tí relative clause + gbọdọ̀ (Chapter 6 recycling)
Why `fẹ́`, `gbèrò láti`, and `retí pé` take different complements

Fẹ́ and gbèrò láti are typically followed by láti plus a bare verb, describing an action the same subject intends to carry out. Retí pé, like torí pé from Chapter 6, is followed by a full clause with its own subject and tense marking, because hoping or expecting is a stance taken toward an entire situation, not simply an action to be performed. Learners transferring English "to"-infinitive patterns sometimes try to follow retí pé with a bare verb; instead, treat retí pé as introducing a complete sentence.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Ẹ̀gbọ́n Adé ń bá alámọ̀ràn iṣẹ́ sọ̀rọ̀ nípa ẹ̀kọ́ àti iṣẹ́ tí yóò ṣe ní ọjọ́ iwájú.
Setting: Adé's cousin is speaking with a career counselor about education and future work.

ALÁMỌ̀RÀN Kí ni o fẹ́ ṣe lẹ́yìn ilé ìwé girama yìí? What do you want to do after this secondary school?
ẸGBỌ́N Mo fẹ́ láti di onimọ̀-ẹ̀rọ, torí pé mo nífẹ̀ẹ́ sí ìmọ̀ ẹ̀rọ àti ẹ̀rọ amóhùnmáwòrán láti kékeré. I want to become an engineer, because I have loved technology and machines since I was small.
ALÁMỌ̀RÀN Ó dára. Yunifásítì tí o fẹ́ lọ ha ní ẹ̀kọ́ ìmọ̀-ẹ̀rọ tó dára? Good. Does the university you want to attend have a good engineering program?
ẸGBỌ́N Bẹ́ẹ̀ ni. Mo gbèrò láti fi ìwé ẹ̀bẹ̀ ránṣẹ́ sí ibẹ̀ lọ́dún tí ń bọ̀, kí n lè bẹ̀rẹ̀ ẹ̀kọ́ gíga ní àkókò. Yes. I plan to send an application there next year, so that I can begin higher education on time.
ALÁMỌ̀RÀN Kí ni o retí pé yóò ṣẹlẹ̀ lẹ́yìn tí o bá parí ẹ̀kọ́ rẹ? What do you hope will happen after you finish your studies?
ẸGBỌ́N N óò retí pé mo máa rí iṣẹ́ ní ilé-iṣẹ́ kan tí ó bá ẹ̀bùn mi mu, kí n sì lè ṣèrànwọ́ fún ìdílé mi. I hope that I will find work at a company that suits my talents, and that I can also help my family.
ALÁMỌ̀RÀN Ète rẹ dára. Ó yẹ kí o pèsè gbogbo ìwé rẹ dáradára kí o tó fi ránṣẹ́. Your goal is good. You should prepare all your documents carefully before you send them.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Family involvement in education and career decisions

    In many Yorùbá families, decisions about education and career are discussed openly with parents, older relatives, and sometimes community elders, rather than made in isolation by the young person alone. Statements of intention such as mo gbèrò láti ("I plan to") are often shared with family early, and advice or blessing from elders commonly accompanies a young person's stated goals. This pattern reflects a broader communicative expectation that important future plans are shared and supported within the family and community, not treated as purely private matters.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Following `retí pé` with a bare verb

    Error: Producing a sentence such as *mo retí pé rí iṣẹ́ rere, dropping the subject and tense marking after retí pé on the model of an English "to"-infinitive.

    Correction: Use a full clause with its own subject and future marker after retí pé, as in mo retí pé n óò rí iṣẹ́ rere ("I hope that I will find good work"). Reserve the bare láti + verb pattern for fẹ́ and gbèrò, as in mo fẹ́ láti rí iṣẹ́ rere.

    Common Mistake — Omitting the subject before a short future form

    Error: Dropping the subject pronoun before a contracted future form, as in *óò lọ sí yunifásítì where the intended subject is "I" rather than "he/she."

    Correction: Keep the subject pronoun explicit before the future marker, as in n óò lọ sí yunifásítì ("I will go to university"). Short future forms still require a clear subject, since ó and n óò are not interchangeable.

Fẹ́ láti and gbèrò láti are followed by a bare verb describing an action the same subject will carry out, as in "mo fẹ́ láti di oníṣègùn" (I want to become a doctor); retí pé is followed by a full clause with its own subject and tense marking, as in "mo retí pé n óò rí iṣẹ́ rere" (I hope that I will find good work). 2. Yóò is preferred in careful, formal, or written Yorùbá, while máa, á, and ó are common in ordinary spoken Yorùbá; this chapter's formal writing tasks require yóò or máa.

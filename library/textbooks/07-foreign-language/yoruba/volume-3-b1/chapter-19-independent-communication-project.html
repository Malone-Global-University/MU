Independent Communication Project · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Select and narrow a community or cultural topic suitable for independent research in Yorùbá.
  2. Gather information from two or more sources (readings, recordings, or interviews) and identify each source clearly.
  3. Summarize source information in your own words using reported-speech patterns consolidated since Chapter 9.
  4. Organize a short written report with an introduction, body, and conclusion, using paragraph structure developed since Chapter 16.
  5. Prepare and deliver an oral presentation that narrates, explains, and supports an opinion using consolidated ni focus, reason clauses, and concession clauses from Chapters 4–17.
  6. Answer follow-up questions about your project spontaneously, drawing on the full range of Volume III productive systems.
  7. Maintain full tone marking and dot-under orthography across every written and spoken component of the project.

Opening Communication Scenario

Adé's Yorùbá class has reached the end of the unit on community life, media, and language awareness. The instructor asks each learner to choose one topic connected to their own community or cultural interest — it might be a local tradition, a community project, a piece of news, or a cultural practice from home or from a Yorùbá-speaking community abroad — and to research it well enough to explain it to the class. Adé decides to build on the borehole debate from Chapter 17 and the language-variation conversation with Túndé from Chapter 18, combining what he has learned about community issues and cross-dialect awareness into one connected project.

  1. This chapter introduces no major new grammar. It integrates the productive Volume III systems developed since Chapter 1 into one extended task.
  2. The project moves through four stages: choosing and narrowing a topic, gathering and summarizing sources, writing a short report, and presenting the findings orally with a question-and-answer period.
  3. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to complete an independent research-and-presentation cycle entirely in Standard Written Yorùbá.

Pronunciation Focus

Focus — Presentation fluency and self-repair

This chapter shifts pronunciation attention from perception to sustained delivery. A presentation of two or three minutes places new demands on tone accuracy and phrase rhythm under time pressure.

  • Practice your presentation aloud at least twice before recording or delivering it, listening for any syllable where tone flattens under speed.
  • When you notice a slip, pause briefly, restate the phrase with correct tone, and continue — this kind of self-repair is a normal and expected B2-level skill, not a failure.
  • Mark your written script with tone and dot-under letters exactly as you will say it, so that reading aloud and independent speech reinforce each other.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new productive grammar system. It integrates systems already consolidated across Volume III: reported-speech and source-attribution patterns from Chapter 9, reason and purpose clauses from Chapter 6, consolidated ni focus from Chapters 4–17, concession clauses and the polite-disagreement frame from Chapters 13 and 17, and paragraph and report organization from Chapter 16. Contrastive focus, question focus, and relative clauses continue as recognition-only material, unchanged since Chapter 12.

Attributing information to a source

Pattern

Gẹ́gẹ́ bí [orísun] ṣe wí, [àkópọ̀ ọ̀rọ̀ orísun náà nínú ọ̀rọ̀ ara-ẹni].

This pattern names the source first, then follows with a paraphrase in the learner's own words rather than a copied sentence, consolidating the reported-speech and source-attribution work from Chapter 9.

Gẹ́gẹ́ bí olùdarí àwùjọ wa ṣe wí, kànga tuntun yóò mú kí àwọn ọmọdé má lọ jìnnà mọ́ láti wá omi mu.

According to our community leader, the new well will mean that children no longer have to travel far to fetch water.

Organizing a short report

  • Ní ìpìlẹ̀ṣẹ̀, mo fẹ́ sọ̀rọ̀ nípa ìdí tí mo fi yàn kókó-ọ̀rọ̀ yìí. In the introduction, I want to explain why I chose this topic. report structure — introduction frame
  • Ní ìparí, a rí i pé àṣàrò yìí fi hàn wá ohun tí a lè ṣe ní àwùjọ. In conclusion, this project shows us something we can do in the community. report structure — conclusion frame, consolidated ni-adjacent emphasis
Consolidated — Reason, concession, and focus continue

Statements such as nítorí pé àwọn ọmọ ilé-ìwé nílò omi tí ó mọ́ ("because the schoolchildren need clean water") continue the reason-clause pattern from Chapter 6. Concession with bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé ... síbẹ̀síbẹ̀ ... and consolidated ni focus such as àwùjọ ni ó ní láti pinnu ("it is the community that must decide") remain available exactly as developed in Chapters 6, 13, and 17.

Recognition only — No new productive targets this chapter

The reading and listening materials below may contain a contrastive-focus phrase or a relative clause naming a source. These remain receptive, continuing the pattern from Chapters 12–18; your project itself should rely on the consolidated systems named above rather than on new independent production of these forms.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé ń bá olùkọ́ rẹ̀ sọ̀rọ̀ nípa àṣàrò tí ó yàn.
Setting: Adé is discussing his chosen project with his instructor.

OLÙKỌ́ Adé, kí ni àṣàrò tí o yàn fún iṣẹ́ yìí? Adé, what project have you chosen for this task?
ADÉ Mo fẹ́ sọ̀rọ̀ nípa ìjíròrò tí ó wáyé nípa kànga tuntun ní àwùjọ wa, àti bí èdè ṣe yàtọ̀ síra láàrin àwọn tí ń gbé nílé àti àwọn tí ń gbé lóde. I want to talk about the discussion that took place about the new well in our community, and how language differs between those who live at home and those abroad.
OLÙKỌ́ Ó dára. Orísun mélòó ni o ní? Good. How many sources do you have?
ADÉ Mo ní méjì: ọ̀rọ̀ olùdarí àwùjọ wa lórí ìpàdé náà, àti ìjíròrò tí mo bá ẹ̀gbọ́n mi Túndé ní Lọndọnu. I have two: our community leader's words at the meeting, and the conversation I had with my cousin Túndé in London.
OLÙKỌ́ Rántí pé kí o kọ àkópọ̀ ọ̀rọ̀ wọn ní ọ̀rọ̀ ara rẹ, kì í ṣe àdàkọ tààrà. Remember to write a summary of their words in your own words, not a direct copy.
ADÉ Mo lóye. Èmi yóò lo gẹ́gẹ́ bí ... ṣe wí, kí n bàa lè fi orísun hàn kedere. I understand. I will use "according to ..." so I can clearly show my source.
OLÙKỌ́ Bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé kókó-ọ̀rọ̀ méjì ni, síbẹ̀síbẹ̀ o lè so wọ́n pọ̀ dáradára nínú ìjábọ̀ kan ṣoṣo. Even though there are two topics, nevertheless you can connect them well into one single report.

Guided Practice

Writing Workshop

Write a short project report (150–200 words) with full tone marks and dot-under letters throughout. Your report must include an introduction stating your topic and why you chose it, a body summarizing at least two sources with clear attribution, at least one reason clause, at least one instance of consolidated ni focus, and a conclusion.

Sample report shell — for reference only

Ìpìlẹ̀ṣẹ̀: Àṣàrò mi ń sọ̀rọ̀ nípa [kókó-ọ̀rọ̀], nítorí pé [ìdí ìyàn].

Introduction: My project discusses [topic], because [reason for choosing it].

Ara ìjábọ̀: Gẹ́gẹ́ bí [orísun kìíní] ṣe wí, [àkópọ̀]. Gẹ́gẹ́ bí [orísun kejì] ṣe wí, [àkópọ̀].

Body: According to [source one], [summary]. According to [source two], [summary].

Ìparí: [Kókó-ọ̀rọ̀] ni ó fi hàn wá pé [àkópọ̀ ìparí].

Conclusion: It is [topic] that shows us that [closing summary], using ni focus.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Community research and shared knowledge

    In many Yorùbá-speaking communities, community leaders, elders, and family members are common and trusted sources of information about local matters, alongside written or broadcast media. Combining a community source with a personal or family source, as Adé does, is a natural and respected way of researching a topic, provided that each source is credited clearly and honestly. Presenting findings to a group — whether a classroom, a family gathering, or a community meeting — is also a familiar communicative practice, and audiences generally expect a speaker to acknowledge where information came from.

    Project Topic Bank — Suggestions, not requirements
    • A local community project or improvement
    • A family tradition or celebration
    • A piece of local or diaspora news
    • A cultural practice from a Yorùbá-speaking community abroad
    • A comparison of two regional customs
    • A public health or public information topic
    • A school or educational initiative
    • A proverb or expressive tradition and its meaning

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Copying source language directly

    Error: Reproducing a source's exact wording as the project's own report content.

    Correction: Use gẹ́gẹ́ bí ... ṣe wí to name the source, then paraphrase the idea in your own words, exactly as modeled in the model dialogue and reading passage.

    Common Mistake — Choosing a topic too broad for the time available

    Error: Selecting a topic so large that it cannot be explained clearly within a short report and a two-to-three-minute presentation.

    Correction: Narrow the topic to one clear question or focus, as recommended in the Reading Practice passage, before beginning research.

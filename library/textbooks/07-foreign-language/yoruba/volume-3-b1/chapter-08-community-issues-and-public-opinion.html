Ọ̀rọ̀ Àdúgbò àti Èrò Gbogbogbò

Community Issues and Public Opinion · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. State an opinion on a community issue using opinion frames such as mo lérò pé and mo rò pé ("I think that").
  2. Support an opinion with a reason using torí pé, recycled from Chapter 6, in an argumentative rather than purely explanatory context.
  3. Mark contrast between two ideas using ṣùgbọ́n and àmọ́ ("but").
  4. Use ni focus, consolidated from Chapter 4, to emphasize the specific point being argued in a discussion.
  5. Disagree politely using softened disagreement frames rather than blunt contradiction.
  6. Recognize, without producing independently, contrastive focus constructions that sharpen a disagreement (such as "it is X, not Y, that...") and question-focus disagreement patterns, which later chapters will continue to develop.

Communication Scenario

The community center where Adé's cousin met the career counselor is holding a meeting about a proposed change to the neighborhood: whether the community should spend its shared funds on repairing the old market roof or on building a new borehole for clean water. Residents of different ages and backgrounds take turns sharing their opinions, agreeing with some points, disagreeing with others, and giving reasons for their positions.

  1. This chapter develops opinion frames with mo lérò pé and mo rò pé, contrast connectors ṣùgbọ́n and àmọ́, and polite disagreement frames, while consolidating ni focus and reason clauses with torí pé from earlier chapters.
  2. It introduces vocabulary for community issues, public services, and shared decision-making.
  3. By the end of the chapter, you should be able to state an opinion, support it with a reason, contrast it with another view, and disagree politely in a structured discussion — while continuing to recognize, without producing, sharpened contrastive-focus disagreement patterns.

Pronunciation and Orthography Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Tone in public discussion and disagreement

In lively discussion, speakers sometimes raise their voices or speed up, and learners can accidentally flatten tone under that pressure — especially on short, high-frequency words like ṣùgbọ́n and àmọ́, which carry meaning-bearing tone just like any other word. Disagreement does not license careless tone.

  • Practice ṣùgbọ́n and àmọ́ slowly first, then at natural conversational speed, keeping every tone audible.
  • Practice mo lérò pé and mo rò pé as fixed openings, noticing that the tone pattern does not change even when the opinion that follows is emphatic or contested.
  • Read the model dialogue below aloud twice: once slowly, phrase by phrase, and once at the pace of an animated but respectful discussion.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops opinion frames and contrast connectors as new productive systems, while consolidating ni focus and reason clauses with torí pé for use in structured argument.

Introduced: Opinion frames with `mo lérò pé` and `mo rò pé`

Building block
Mo lérò pé / Mo rò pé + Gbólóhùn
Mo lérò pé / Mo rò pé + [full clause stating the opinion] — like retí pé from Chapter 7, these opinion frames attach a complete clause with its own subject and tense marking, not a bare verb.
Mo lérò pé ó yẹ ká tún òrùlé ọjà ṣe kí a tó kọ́ kànga tuntun.
I think that we should repair the market roof before building a new borehole. (Literally: I think that it is-fitting we again roof market do before we build well new.)
  • Mo rò pé àwọn ọmọdé àti àgbàlagbà ni yóò jàǹfààní jùlọ nínú kànga omi. I think that children and elders would benefit most from a water borehole. mo rò pé + pé clause + ni focus on àwọn ọmọdé àti àgbàlagbà
  • Ǹjẹ́ o gbà pé ọjà ni ó ṣe kókó jùlọ fún àdúgbò yìí? Do you agree that the market is the most important thing for this community? agreement check + ni focus + pé clause

Introduced: Contrast with `ṣùgbọ́n` and `àmọ́`

  • Ọjà wa ti bàjẹ́ gan an, ṣùgbọ́n omi tí a ń mu kò tíì mọ́ dáradára. Our market is truly in bad shape, but the water we drink is still not clean enough. ṣùgbọ́n links two contrasting statements, formal register
  • Mo gbà pé kànga ṣe pàtàkì, àmọ́ ọjà náà kò gbọdọ̀ di ahoro. I agree that a borehole is important, but the market must not be allowed to fall into ruin. àmọ́ links two contrasting statements, ordinary spoken register

Ṣùgbọ́n and àmọ́ both mean "but." Ṣùgbọ́n is preferred in careful speech and formal writing; àmọ́ is very common in ordinary conversation. Both are correct within their register; this chapter's writing tasks require ṣùgbọ́n.

Introduced: Polite disagreement

Building block
Ó ṣeé ṣe kí ó jẹ́ bẹ́ẹ̀, ṣùgbọ́n...
[Acknowledge the other view as possible] + ṣùgbọ́n + [state the different view] — this frame softens disagreement by first granting the other person's point some validity.
Ó ṣeé ṣe kí ó jẹ́ bẹ́ẹ̀, ṣùgbọ́n mo lérò pé a gbọdọ̀ kọ́kọ́ rí i pé gbogbo ènìyàn ní omi mímọ́ mu.
That may be true, but I think we must first make sure everyone has clean water to drink.
  • Mo ṣe kàyéfì pé bóyá àpapọ̀ méjèèjì ni yóò dára jù. I wonder whether doing both together might be better. softened alternative-viewpoint frame, avoids flat contradiction
Consolidated: `ni` focus and `torí pé` in argument

Ni focus, introduced in Chapter 4 for clarification and correction, and torí pé, introduced in Chapter 6 for explanation, now do argumentative work. Placing ni after the exact element being emphasized ("kànga omi ni a nílò jùlọ" — it is a borehole that we need most) sharpens which part of a claim a speaker is defending. Following a claim with torí pé gives listeners a reason they can accept, question, or counter, which is the basis of structured disagreement.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Àwọn olùgbé àdúgbò kan péjọ pọ̀ ní ìpàdé àwùjọ láti jíròrò bí a ṣe máa lo owó àpapọ̀ wọn.
Setting: A group of neighborhood residents gather at a community meeting to discuss how to use their shared fund.

ÌYÁ AGBÀ Mo lérò pé ó yẹ ká kọ́kọ́ tún òrùlé ọjà ṣe, torí pé ọ̀pọ̀lọpọ̀ àwọn tí ń ta ọjà ń jìyà nígbà tí òjò bá rọ̀. I think we should first repair the market roof, because many of the traders suffer when it rains.
ỌKÙNRIN Ó ṣeé ṣe kí ó jẹ́ bẹ́ẹ̀, ṣùgbọ́n mo rò pé kànga omi ni ó ṣe pàtàkì jùlọ, torí pé àìsàn púpọ̀ ń wá láti ara omi tí kò mọ́. That may be true, but I think a water borehole is more important, because much sickness comes from unclean water.
ÌYÁ AGBÀ Mo gbà pé omi mímọ́ ṣe kókó, àmọ́ tí ọjà bá tún bàjẹ́ síi, kò ní sí ibi tí àwọn èèyàn á ti ra ohun tí wọ́n nílò. I agree that clean water matters, but if the market gets worse, there won't be anywhere for people to buy what they need.
OBÌNRIN Ǹjẹ́ ẹ gbà pé a lè ṣe àwọn méjèèjì síbẹ̀ díẹ̀díẹ̀, kí a má ṣe fi gbogbo owó sí ohun kan ṣoṣo? Do you agree that we could do both, gradually, instead of putting all the money into just one thing?
ỌKÙNRIN Mo ṣe kàyéfì pé bóyá àpapọ̀ méjèèjì ni yóò dára jù, bí owó bá tó. I wonder whether doing both together might be best, if the money is enough.
ÌYÁ AGBÀ Ó dára. Ẹ jẹ́ kí a kọ́kọ́ mọ iye owó tí a ní kí a tó gbé ìpinnu àpapọ̀ kalẹ̀. Good. Let us first find out how much money we have before we make a shared decision.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Consensus-building in community discussion

    In many Yorùbá communities, public discussion about shared resources favors reaching consensus over declaring a clear winner in an argument. Speakers commonly acknowledge the validity of an opposing view before offering their own, using frames like ó ṣeé ṣe kí ó jẹ́ bẹ́ẹ̀, ṣùgbọ́n..., and elders or respected figures often play a role in guiding the group toward a shared decision rather than a vote that produces losers. This pattern reflects a broader communicative expectation that disagreement should preserve relationships and community harmony, not merely settle who is right.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Disagreeing bluntly without an acknowledgment frame

    Error: Producing a flat contradiction such as *ìwọ kò tọ́ ("you are not correct") immediately after another speaker's opinion, with no softening frame.

    Correction: Open with an acknowledgment such as ó ṣeé ṣe kí ó jẹ́ bẹ́ẹ̀, ṣùgbọ́n... ("that may be true, but...") before stating a different view, as modeled throughout this chapter. This keeps disagreement respectful and consistent with community discussion norms.

    Common Mistake — Confusing `ṣùgbọ́n`/`àmọ́` with `torí pé`

    Error: Using torí pé where a contrast is intended, as in *mo gbà pé kànga ṣe pàtàkì torí pé ọjà kò gbọdọ̀ di ahoro, which incorrectly presents the second clause as a reason rather than a contrast.

    Correction: Use ṣùgbọ́n or àmọ́ when linking two contrasting ideas, and reserve torí pé for stating a reason: mo gbà pé kànga ṣe pàtàkì, ṣùgbọ́n ọjà kò gbọdọ̀ di ahoro.

    You can now state an opinion with mo lérò pé or mo rò pé, support it with a reason using torí pé, contrast it with another view using ṣùgbọ́n or àmọ́, and disagree politely using an acknowledgment frame. You continue to recognize, without producing, contrastive-focus and question-focus disagreement constructions.

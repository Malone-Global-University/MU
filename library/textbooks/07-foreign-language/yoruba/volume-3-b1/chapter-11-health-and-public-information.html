Health and Public Information · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Interpret public health notices, advisories, and announcements at a connected-discourse level.
  2. Give and understand prohibitions using má in health and safety contexts.
  3. Express obligation and recommended action using ó yẹ kí and related modal frames.
  4. State conditions using bí and tí clauses in practical guidance.
  5. Explain cause and result using torí pé and nítorí náà in a health context.
  6. Recognize (receptively) how contrastive focus and relative clauses can sharpen a piece of public guidance, without being required to produce them independently in new forms.
  7. Write a short, accurate health information note using full tone marks and diacritics.

Opening Communication Scenario

A community health worker has posted a notice near the borehole that Adé's neighborhood recently repaired, reminding residents how to keep the water safe and what to do if someone falls ill. Your neighbor, who missed the meeting, asks you to explain the notice and tell them what they should and should not do.

  1. This chapter develops prohibitions, obligation, condition, and cause/result clauses already introduced earlier in the volume, now applied to public health and safety guidance.
  2. Contrastive focus, relative clauses with expanded structure, and complement clauses may appear for recognition only in this chapter's readings and listening passages; you are not expected to produce new forms of them independently yet.
  3. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to explain a piece of public health guidance clearly, including what to do, what to avoid, and why.

Pronunciation Focus

Focus — Tone and rhythm in announcements and warnings

Public announcements and warnings often use short, emphatic clauses with clear pauses between the prohibition, the reason, and the recommended action. Keep every tone mark accurate even when speaking with added emphasis; emphasis in Yorùbá is carried by rhythm, pausing, and volume, not by flattening or exaggerating tone.

  • Practice the contrast between má (prohibition particle, low tone) and ma-initial words that are not the prohibition particle.
  • Keep the dot-under vowels in ṣọ́ra, ẹ̀jẹ̀-type words, and ọjà-type words distinct and clear when reading a warning aloud.
  • Read each sentence of the health notice below first slowly, phrase by phrase, then again at a natural announcement pace.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops four connected systems already active in the volume — prohibition, obligation, condition, and cause/result — and applies them to public health guidance. No new particle or clause type is introduced for the first time; each pattern below builds on material already scheduled.

Prohibitions with má

Pattern

Má (+ kí ó) + verb + object

má negates a command; it is distinct from the ordinary declarative negator kò and is used specifically for instructions and warnings.

Má mu omi tí kò mọ́.

Do not drink water that is not clean.

  • Ẹ má fọwọ́ kan omi kànga náà kí ẹ tó fọ ọwọ́ yín. Do not touch the borehole water before you wash your hands. respectful plural prohibition + time clause (recycled)

Obligation with ó yẹ kí

  • Ó yẹ kí gbogbo ènìyàn wẹ ọwọ́ wọn kí wọ́n tó jẹun. Everyone should wash their hands before they eat. obligation frame + purpose/time subordination (recycled from Ch. 7)
  • Kò yẹ kí a fi omi tí a kò tíì sè fún àwọn ọmọdé. We should not give unboiled water to children. negated obligation frame

Condition with bí and tí

  • Bí ara ẹnikẹ́ni bá gbóná jù, kí wọ́n lọ sí ilé ìwòsàn lẹ́sẹ̀kẹsẹ̀. If anyone's body becomes too hot (has a fever), they should go to the clinic immediately. bí conditional clause + obligation-style instruction
  • Tí a bá tọ́jú kànga náà dáadáa, àìsàn yóò dín kù. If we take good care of the borehole, illness will decrease. tí-conditional + future-marked result clause

Cause and result with torí pé and nítorí náà

  • Omi tí kò mọ́ máa ń fa àìsàn ikùn; nítorí náà, ẹ máa sè omi yín kí ẹ tó mu ún. Unclean water often causes stomach illness; therefore, boil your water before drinking it. habitual cause statement + nítorí náà result clause + prohibition-style recommendation
Recognition only in this chapter — Do not produce independently yet

The listening and reading passages below contain a small number of contrastive focus phrases, a longer relative clause, and one complement clause after "wọ́n sọ pé" used only to make the health notice sound natural. These items remain receptive; you are not assessed on producing new instances of them in this chapter.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé ń ṣàlàyé ìwé ìkéde ìlera tí a fi sí ẹ̀gbẹ́ kànga fún aládùúgbò rẹ̀.
Setting: Adé is explaining the health notice posted near the borehole to his neighbor.

ALÁDÙÚGBÒ Kí ni ìwé ìkéde náà sọ nípa omi kànga? What does the notice say about the borehole water?
ADÉ Ó sọ pé ẹ má fọwọ́ kan omi náà kí ẹ tó fọ ọwọ́ yín, torí pé ìdọ̀tí lè bà á jẹ́. It says do not touch the water before washing your hands, because it can be contaminated by dirt.
ALÁDÙÚGBÒ Ǹjẹ́ ó yẹ kí a sè omi náà kí a tó mu ún? Should we boil the water before drinking it?
ADÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ni, ó yẹ. Omi tí a kò sè lè fa àìsàn ikùn; nítorí náà, ẹ máa sè é nígbà gbogbo. Yes, it should be boiled. Unboiled water can cause stomach illness; therefore, always boil it.
ALÁDÙÚGBÒ Kí ni a óò ṣe bí ọmọ kan bá ṣàìsàn? What will we do if a child becomes ill?
ADÉ Bí ara ọmọ náà bá gbóná jù, kí a gbé e lọ sí ilé ìwòsàn àdúgbò lẹ́sẹ̀kẹsẹ̀. Má dúró ní ilé láti fi ewé tàbí oògùn ilé ìbílẹ̀ dán an wò dá nìkan, ẹ̀wọ̀ ìlera ni kí a kọ́kọ́ rí i. If the child's body becomes too hot, take them to the neighborhood clinic immediately. Do not stay home trying only home remedies or herbs; it is a health worker we should see first.
ALÁDÙÚGBÒ Ẹ ṣé, mo ti yé bí a ṣe lè ṣọ́ra. Thank you, I now understand how to be careful.

Writing Workshop

Write a short health information note of 60–100 words with full tone marks and dot-under letters throughout. Your note must include at least one prohibition, one obligation statement, one condition, and one cause/result statement, addressed to your community.

  • Reread the listening script and the reading notice above.
  • Choose a health or safety topic relevant to your community (water, food, a seasonal illness, or child safety).
  • Draft a prohibition using má.
  • Draft an obligation statement using ó yẹ kí.
  • Add a condition with bí or tí and its recommended action.
  • Add a cause/result statement using torí pé and nítorí náà, then apply the Tone and Orthography Checkpoint before finalizing.

    • Include

    • at least 1 prohibition with má
    • at least 1 obligation with ó yẹ kí
    • at least 1 condition clause (bí or tí)
    • at least 1 cause/result pair (torí pé / nítorí náà)

    Avoid

    • producing new contrastive focus or relative-clause structures beyond recycled forms
    • producing an independent new complement clause
    • confusing má (prohibition) with kò (declarative negation)
    • omitting tone marks or dot-under letters
    Tone and Orthography Checkpoint
    • Every vowel that should carry a high or low tone mark has one.
    • Every mid-tone syllable is left correctly unmarked.
    • ẹ, ọ, and ṣ are distinguished from e, o, and s throughout.
    • The prohibition particle má is spelled and toned distinctly from the negator kò.
    • No unscheduled grammar (new focus patterns, new relative-clause types, new particles) has been produced.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Public health communication and shared responsibility

    Public health guidance in many Yorùbá-speaking communities is often communicated as a shared responsibility rather than a set of individual instructions; notices frequently address "gbogbo wa" (all of us) and appeal to collective care for shared resources such as water sources. At the same time, it is common for health notices to explicitly recommend seeking care from a trained health worker or clinic rather than relying solely on home remedies for serious symptoms, while still respecting that home and traditional remedies remain part of everyday health practice for many families.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Confusing má with kò

    Error: Using the declarative negator in a prohibition, such as *Ẹ kò fọwọ́ kan omi náà when giving an instruction not to touch the water.

    Correction: Use the prohibition particle for instructions and warnings: Ẹ má fọwọ́ kan omi náà ("do not touch the water"). Kò states that something is not the case; má instructs someone not to do something.

    Common Mistake — Dropping the condition when giving guidance

    Error: Stating the recommended action without its triggering condition, such as *Kí a gbé e lọ sí ilé ìwòsàn alone, without saying under what circumstance this applies.

    Correction: Keep the condition attached to the instruction: Bí ara ọmọ náà bá gbóná jù, kí a gbé e lọ sí ilé ìwòsàn lẹ́sẹ̀kẹsẹ̀ ("if the child's body becomes too hot, take them to the clinic immediately"). Without the condition, the guidance loses its practical meaning.

    Answer Key

    Listening Practice Questions

    1. Residents are told not to touch the water with unclean hands, because contact with dirty hands can contaminate it.
    2. The water should be boiled before it is given to children to drink, because unboiled water can cause stomach illness.
    3. If someone has diarrhea or a high fever, they should be taken to the neighborhood clinic immediately, rather than trying home remedies first.
    4. The doctor said that children who have not yet been vaccinated should come to the clinic this week.

    Reading Practice Questions

    1. The water must be boiled before it is given to children, because unboiled water can cause a serious illness.
    2. If someone begins to have diarrhea or a high fever, they should be taken to the clinic first, not treated at home.
    3. The prohibition is "ẹ má fi ọwọ́ tí kò mọ́ kan omi náà" ("do not touch the water with unclean hands"); restated, it means residents should keep their hands clean before touching the water.
    4. The notice places responsibility on everyone to stay alert and protect the borehole as shared property.

    Guided Practice — Sample Responses

    1. Prohibition: "ẹ má fọwọ́ kan omi náà kí ẹ tó fọ ọwọ́ yín"; obligation: "ó yẹ kí a sè omi náà kí a tó mu ún"; condition: "bí ara ọmọ náà bá gbóná jù..."; cause/result: "omi tí a kò sè lè fa àìsàn ikùn; nítorí náà..." 2. Sample singular form: "Má fọwọ́ kan omi náà kí o tó fọ ọwọ́ rẹ." 3. Sample combination: "Bí ara ẹnikẹ́ni bá gbóná jù, ó yẹ kí a gbé e lọ sí ilé ìwòsàn lẹ́sẹ̀kẹsẹ̀." 4. The complement clause "pé ìdọ̀tí lè bà á jẹ́" introduces what the notice states as the reason for the prohibition; it functions as the content of "ó sọ."

    Chapter Review — Apá A

    1. Kò negates a statement of fact (something is not the case); má negates a command or instruction (telling someone not to do something). Example with kò: "Omi náà kò mọ́" (the water is not clean). Example with má: "Ẹ má mu omi náà" (do not drink the water). 2. "Bí ara ọmọ náà bá gbóná jù, kí a gbé e lọ sí ilé ìwòsàn lẹ́sẹ̀kẹsẹ̀" combines a condition (a fever occurring) with an obligation-style instruction (taking the child to the clinic); the condition specifies when the obligation applies.

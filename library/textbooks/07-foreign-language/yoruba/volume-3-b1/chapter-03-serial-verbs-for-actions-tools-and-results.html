Serial Verbs for Actions, Tools, and Results · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use serial verb constructions to describe motion and direction, such as going, coming, carrying, and taking something somewhere.
  2. Use serial verb constructions to describe transfer and instrumental actions, such as giving something to someone or using an object to accomplish a task.
  3. Explain, in your own words, why a chain of Yorùbá verbs describing a single event is not simply translated with English "and."
  4. Maintain tense/aspect scope correctly across a short verb series, keeping ti and ń consistent across all verbs in the chain.
  5. Narrate a simple procedure or errand using two or three linked verbs rather than separate disconnected sentences.
  6. Recognize, without producing, resultative serial verbs and full serial-verb-versus-coordination contrasts, which later chapters will continue to develop.

Communication Scenario

Adé's neighbor, Ìyá Kúnlé, has asked him to run a few errands for her while she recovers from a minor illness: taking a package to the post office, buying items at the market with a specific tool she lent him, and bringing food back to her house. To describe these tasks clearly — both while doing them and afterward when reporting back to her — Adé needs to link verbs together the way a Yorùbá speaker naturally would, rather than stringing together short English-style sentences joined by "and."

  1. This chapter introduces motion, direction, transfer, and instrumental serial verb constructions as productive systems for the first time.
  2. It shows how a single Yorùbá clause can pack together "take," "go," "give," or "use" without a separate conjunction for each step, and how tense/aspect marking applies across the whole chain rather than being repeated on every verb.
  3. By the end of the chapter, you should be able to describe a simple errand or task using a short chain of verbs, and to recognize — without yet producing — more complex resultative serial verbs.

Pronunciation and Orthography Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Chunking a verb series without English-style pauses

Learners often insert an English-style pause or stress break between each verb in a Yorùbá serial construction, as if silently inserting "and" between them. A serial verb chain should be spoken as one connected chunk with smooth rhythm across the verbs.

  • Practice saying mú i wá ("bring it," literally "take-it-come") as a single rhythmic unit rather than as two separate stressed words.
  • Keep tone accurate on every verb in the chain, even the shorter ones such as fi and fún, which learners sometimes flatten because they seem grammatically "light."

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces serial verb constructions productively for the first time: motion/direction serialization and transfer/instrumental serialization. It also recycles the ti/ń contrast from Chapter 2, now applied across a verb chain rather than a single verb.

Introduced: Motion and direction serialization

Building block
Kókó-ọ̀rọ̀ + Ọ̀rọ̀-ìṣe kìíní (gbé/mú) + Awùjọ + Ọ̀rọ̀-ìṣe kejì (lọ/wá)
[Subject] [take/carry-verb] [object] [go/come-verb] — a single event described with two linked verbs sharing one subject and one object.
Mo mú páálí náà lọ sí ilé iṣẹ́ àfìwéránṣẹ́.
I took the package to the post office. (Literally: I take package-the go to post-office.)
  • Ó gbé àpò owó rẹ̀ wá sí ọjà. She brought her purse to the market. gbé (carry) + wá (come) share one subject, one object
  • A ti mú páálí náà lọ, àmọ́ a kò tí ì dé ilé iṣẹ́ àfìwéránṣẹ́. We have already taken the package, but we have not yet arrived at the post office. ti scopes over the whole verb chain mú...lọ, not only mú

Introduced: Transfer and instrumental serialization

Yorùbá often introduces the recipient of an action with fún ("give to") and the instrument or means of an action with fi ("use/take as tool"), both linked directly to a main verb rather than expressed with a separate English-style preposition phrase.

  • Mo fi kẹ̀kẹ́ mi lọ sí ọjà, mo sì rà oúnjẹ fún Ìyá Kúnlé. I used my bicycle to go to the market, and I bought food for Ìyá Kúnlé. fi (instrument) + lọ (motion); rà + fún (transfer to recipient)
  • Ó fi ìwọ̀n náà wọn ẹ̀wà náà kí ó tó tà á fún mi. She used the scale to weigh the beans before she sold them to me. fi (instrument) + wọn (measure); fún introduces the recipient of the sale

Notice that neither fi nor fún is translated as a separate English clause joined by "and." Each forms part of a single connected event with the main verb that follows or precedes it.

Why serial verbs are not simply English "and"

In English, "I took the package and went to the post office" describes two separate actions joined by a conjunction, and each verb could carry its own independent tense marking. In Yorùbá, mo mú páálí náà lọ sí ilé iṣẹ́ àfìwéránṣẹ́ describes one connected event: taking-and-going form a single unit, sharing one subject, one implied object, and one tense/aspect scope. Translating every Yorùbá verb chain with English "and" will eventually produce unnatural, overly segmented Yorùbá when you try to speak or write it yourself.

Recycled: The ti / ń contrast across a verb chain

As in Chapter 2, ti marks a completed or resultative event and ń marks an ongoing event. Within a serial verb chain, this marking applies to the chain as a whole rather than to each verb separately.

  • Bí mo ṣe ń fi kẹ̀kẹ́ mi lọ sí ọjà, ó ti bẹ̀rẹ̀ sí rọ̀jò. As I was using my bicycle to go to the market, it had already begun to rain. ń scopes over fi...lọ as one ongoing chain; ti marks the separate completed event of rain beginning

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé ń ròyìn iṣẹ́ tí ó ṣe fún Ìyá Kúnlé, aládùúgbò rẹ̀ tí kò lágbára.
Setting: Adé is reporting back on errands he ran for Ìyá Kúnlé, his neighbor who is unwell.

ÌYÁ KÚNLÉ Ǹjẹ́ o ti mú páálí mi lọ sí ilé iṣẹ́ àfìwéránṣẹ́? Have you already taken my package to the post office?
ADÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ni, mo ti mú un lọ. Mo tún fi kẹ̀kẹ́ mi lọ sí ọjà lẹ́yìn náà. Yes, I have already taken it. I also used my bicycle to go to the market afterward.
ÌYÁ KÚNLÉ Ṣé o rà ẹ̀wà tí mo béèrè fún? Did you buy the beans I asked for?
ADÉ Mo rà á. Ẹni tí ó tà á fi ìwọ̀n rẹ̀ wọn ẹ̀wà náà kí ó tó tà á fún mi. I bought it. The seller used her scale to weigh the beans before she sold them to me.
ADÉ Mo gbé ẹ̀wà náà wá sí ilé yín pẹ̀lú oúnjẹ mìíràn tí mo rà. I brought the beans to your house along with other food I bought.
ÌYÁ KÚNLÉ Ẹ ṣé gan-an, Adé. Kí ni ó ṣẹlẹ̀ nígbà tí ẹ ń padà bọ̀? Thank you very much, Adé. What happened while you were coming back?
ADÉ Bí mo ṣe ń fi kẹ̀kẹ́ mi padà bọ̀, ó ti bẹ̀rẹ̀ sí rọ̀jò, àmọ́ mo ti dé ilé yín kí òjò náà tó pọ̀. As I was using my bicycle to come back, it had already begun to rain, but I had already arrived at your house before the rain got heavy.

Writing Workshop

Write a connected account of 90–120 words with full tone marks and dot-under letters throughout, describing an errand, task, or small procedure, using motion, transfer, and instrumental serial verb constructions.

  • Plan three or four steps of a simple errand or task in the order they happened.
  • Draft your account using at least one motion serialization, one transfer serialization with fún, and one instrumental serialization with fi.
  • Include at least one sentence in which ti or ń scopes over an entire verb chain rather than a single verb.

  • Apply the Tone and Orthography Checkpoint below before finalizing.

    • Include

    • at least 1 motion serialization
    • at least 1 transfer serialization with fún
    • at least 1 instrumental serialization with fi

    Avoid

    • producing new resultative serial verb chains

    Tone and Orthography Checkpoint
    • Every vowel that should carry a high or low tone mark has one.
    • Every mid-tone syllable is left correctly unmarked.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Errands and neighborly obligation

    Running errands for an unwell or elderly neighbor, as in this chapter's scenario, is commonly understood among many Yorùbá communities as an ordinary expression of neighborliness rather than an unusual favor. Reporting back afterward in careful, complete detail — what was done, in what order, and with what result — is often seen as a mark of respect and reliability, though expectations around detail and formality vary by family, community, and generation.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Inserting an English-style conjunction inside a serial verb chain

    Error: Producing a sentence such as *mo mú páálí náà àti lọ sí ilé iṣẹ́ àfìwéránṣẹ́, inserting àti ("and") between two verbs that should form a single serial chain.

    Correction: Link the two verbs directly with no conjunction, as in mo mú páálí náà lọ sí ilé iṣẹ́ àfìwéránṣẹ́. Reserve àti for joining separate nouns or separate independent events, not verbs describing one connected action.

    Common Mistake — Repeating tense/aspect marking on every verb in a chain

    Error: Marking ti or ń separately on each verb in a chain, such as *mo ti mú páálí náà ti lọ, as if each verb needed its own independent aspect marker.

    Correction: Mark aspect once for the whole chain, as in mo ti mú páálí náà lọ. The aspect scopes over the connected event described by the full verb series.

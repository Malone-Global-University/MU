Re-entry: From Sentences to Discourse · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Convert short, separate Volume II sentences into a connected paragraph using time connectors and reference words.
  2. Diagnose your own current control of particles, pronouns, negation, plural/quantifier noun phrases, possession, demonstratives, routine serial verbs, and basic ni focus.
  3. Identify and repair tone-marking and dot-under spelling errors in your own connected writing.
  4. Narrate a short personal history using consolidated Volume II tense-aspect particles inside connected discourse.
  5. Recognize, without yet producing, the discourse-level features that Volume III will develop: relative clauses with tí, contrastive focus, and expanded serial verbs.
  6. Set personal goals for the systems you will strengthen across Volume III.

Communication Scenario

Adé has been asked by a community newsletter to write a short profile of himself and his neighborhood for new residents. In Volume II, Adé could answer separate interview questions about his family, his daily routine, and his neighborhood. Now he must join those separate answers into one connected piece of writing that a stranger could read and understand as a single, flowing account — not as a list of disconnected facts.

  1. This chapter introduces no major new grammar. It is a diagnostic re-entry chapter that recycles the consolidated productive systems of Volume II — particles, pronouns, negation, plural/quantifier noun phrases, possession, demonstratives, routine serial verbs, and basic ni focus — and asks you to use them inside connected paragraphs rather than isolated sentences.
  2. The chapter previews, for recognition only, three systems that Volume III will develop productively: relative clauses with tí, contrastive focus, and a wider range of serial verb types. These appear glossed and labeled; you are not expected to produce them independently yet.
  3. Your primary task across this chapter is to notice where your Volume II Yorùbá becomes choppy when strung together, and to practice smoothing it with the connectors and reference devices presented here.

Pronunciation and Orthography Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Tone accuracy under connected-speech pressure

When sentences are joined into longer stretches of speech, learners commonly let tone accuracy slip on function words — pronouns, particles, and connectors — even while content-word tone stays strong. This chapter targets exactly that weak point.

  • Practice reading each connector above aloud in isolation first, holding its tone pattern steady.
  • Then read it inside a full sentence, checking that the tone does not flatten under speed.
  • Pay particular attention to àmọ́ (mid-high-low) and torí náà (mid-high, mid-mid), which are frequently mispronounced with even stress.
Orthography self-check

Before continuing, copy out the eight connectors from the table above by hand or by typing, marking every tone and every dot-under letter. Compare your copy against the table. This check anchors the Tone and Orthography Checkpoint self-review that recurs across Volume III chapters.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter does not introduce new grammar systems. It recycles Volume II systems at the connected-discourse level and previews three Volume III systems for recognition only.

Recycled: Joining sentences with connectors

Building block
Gbólóhùn kìíní. + Olùsopọ̀ + Gbólóhùn kejì.
[Sentence one.] + [Connector] + [Sentence two.]
Mo bí ní Ìbàdàn. Lẹ́yìn náà, ìdílé mi kó lọ sí Èkó.
I was born in Ibadan. After that, my family moved to Lagos.
  • Mo lọ sí ilé-ìwé alákọ̀ọ́bẹ̀rẹ̀ ní àdúgbò wa. Torí náà, mo ní ọ̀pọ̀lọpọ̀ ọ̀rẹ́ láti ìgbà náà. I attended primary school in our neighborhood. Because of that, I have many friends from that time. clause + torí náà + clause (result)
  • Mo fẹ́ràn iṣẹ́ mi gan-an, àmọ́ ó máa ń ṣòro nígbà mìíràn. I love my work very much, but it is sometimes difficult. clause + àmọ́ + clause (contrast)

Recycled: Particles, negation, and reference inside connected text

The particles ń (progressive), ti (perfective/resultative), negation with kò, and consolidated pronoun forms all remain fully productive in Volume III. In this chapter, practice keeping their tone and placement correct across two or three linked sentences, not only inside a single sentence.

  • Bàbá mi ti fẹ̀yìntì, àmọ́ ó ń ṣe iṣẹ́ àgbẹ̀ ní ẹ̀yìn ilé wa nígbà gbogbo. My father has retired, but he still farms behind our house all the time.
  • Àwọn ọmọdé àdúgbò wa kò ní ibi ìṣeré tí ó tóbi. Torí náà, wọ́n máa ń ṣeré ní pápá ilé-ìwé. The children of our neighborhood do not have a large playground. Because of that, they usually play in the school field.

Recycled: Basic ni focus in a connected account

  • Ìyá mi ni ó kọ́ mi láti kàwé. It was my mother who taught me to read. subject focus, consolidated from Volume II
Preview — Recognition only (Volume III will develop these)

The following three features appear in this chapter's reading and listening material. You are not expected to produce them independently. They are labeled so that you can begin noticing their shape.

  • Relative clauses with tí: ọkùnrin tí ó kọ́ni ní ilé-ìwé wa — "the man who teaches at our school." Full productive instruction begins in Chapter 5.
  • Contrastive focus: using ni to correct or contrast information rather than simply to identify it. Full instruction begins in Chapter 4.
  • Expanded serial verbs: chains describing instrument, result, and multi-step actions beyond the routine motion/transfer patterns of Volume II. Full instruction begins in Chapter 3.
Ìjíròrò Àpẹẹrẹ

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé ń sọ ìtàn ìgbésí ayé rẹ̀ fún alábòójútó ìwé ìròyìn àdúgbò.
Setting: Adé is telling his life story to the editor of the community newsletter.

OLÓÒTÚ Ǹjẹ́ o lè sọ ìtàn ìgbésí ayé rẹ ní ṣókí fún àwọn tuntun tí ó ṣẹ̀ṣẹ̀ dé sí àdúgbò wa? Can you briefly tell your life story for the newcomers to our neighborhood?
ADÉ Mo bí ní Ìbàdàn ní ọdún 1990. Lẹ́yìn náà, nígbà tí mo pé ọmọ ọdún márùn-ún, ìdílé mi kó wá sí àdúgbò yìí. I was born in Ibadan in 1990. After that, when I turned five years old, my family moved to this neighborhood.
ADÉ Mo lọ sí ilé-ìwé alákọ̀ọ́bẹ̀rẹ̀ tí ó wà nítòsí ọjà. Ìyá mi ni ó kọ́ mi láti kàwé kí n tó bẹ̀rẹ̀ ilé-ìwé. I attended the primary school near the market. It was my mother who taught me to read before I started school.
OLÓÒTÚ Ǹjẹ́ àdúgbò yìí ti yí padà pẹ̀lú, láti ìgbà tí o wá sí ibí? Has this neighborhood also changed since you came here?
ADÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ni. Ọ̀nà wa kò dára rí nígbà náà, àmọ́ ìdàgbàsókè ti wà báyìí. Síwájú sí i, àwọn ilé tuntun ti wà lọ́pọ̀lọpọ̀. Yes. Our roads were not good back then, but there has been development now. Furthermore, there are now many new houses.
ADÉ Ní ìparí, mo lè sọ pé àdúgbò yìí ti di ilé mi tòótọ́. In the end, I can say this neighborhood has truly become my home.

If no speaker is available, this is a Listening Script for Independent Use. Machine text-to-speech must be checked by a qualified Yorùbá speaker before use as a pronunciation model.

"Ìyá Kúmọ́lá ti gbé ní àdúgbò yìí fún ọgbọ̀n ọdún. Nígbà tí ó kọ́kọ́ dé, ilé kò tí ì pọ̀ rí, àwọn ọ̀nà sì máa ń bàjẹ́ nígbà òjò. Àmọ́ láti ìgbà náà wá, àwọn ènìyàn ti ṣiṣẹ́ pọ̀ láti mú àdúgbò dára sí i. Ní báyìí, ó ní ọ̀rẹ́ púpọ̀ láàrin àwọn tí ó ti gbé níbẹ̀ pẹ́ àti àwọn tuntun tí wọ́n ṣẹ̀ṣẹ̀ dé. Ó sọ pé inú òun máa ń dùn láti rí bí àwọn ọmọdé ṣe ń dàgbà pẹ̀lú àlàáfíà ní àdúgbò kan náà tí òun náà ti dàgbà sí."

2. What was a problem when she first arrived?

3. What connector does the passage use to introduce the contrast between the past problem and later change?

4. What does she say makes her happy?

Ọjà tí àwọn òbí mi máa ń lọ ra ẹrù ti di ọjà ńlá kan tí ó ní ọ̀pọ̀lọpọ̀ ilé ìtajà. Àmọ́ ohun kan kò yí padà: àwọn aládùúgbò ṣì máa ń kí ara wọn pẹ̀lú ìtara ní gbogbo àárọ̀, gẹ́gẹ́ bí wọ́n ti ń ṣe nígbà tí mo wà kékeré.

Apá Kíkọ

Writing Workshop

Write a connected paragraph of 80–110 words with full tone marks and dot-under letters throughout, narrating your own personal history and describing one way your community has changed or stayed the same.

  • Plan three to four key points: where you were born, where you grew up, one change or continuity in your community, and a closing reflection.
  • Draft your paragraph using at least three of the discourse connectors from this chapter's vocabulary table.
  • Include at least one particle (ń or ti), one negative statement, one plural or quantified noun phrase, and one basic ni focus sentence.
  • Read your draft aloud, checking that every connector and particle keeps its tone under connected-speech pressure.
  • Apply the Tone and Orthography Checkpoint below before finalizing.

    • Include

    • at least 3 discourse connectors
    • at least 1 negative statement
    • at least 1 particle (ń or ti)
    • at least 1 basic ni focus statement

    Avoid

    • producing new relative clauses with tí
    • producing contrastive focus
    • producing expanded/resultative serial verbs
    • dropping tone marks in longer sentences
    Tone and Orthography Checkpoint
    • Every vowel that should carry a high or low tone mark has one.
    • Every mid-tone syllable is left correctly unmarked.
    • ẹ, ọ, and ṣ are distinguished from e, o, and s throughout.
    • Syllabic nasals carry the correct tone mark.
    • No unscheduled relative clause, contrastive focus, or expanded serial verb has been produced independently.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Telling a connected life story

    In many Yorùbá communities, sharing one's personal history in a connected way — rather than as a list of isolated facts — is closely tied to establishing relationships of trust, especially with newcomers or interviewers. A well-told account often moves from origin, to key transitions, to present circumstances, closing with a reflection that connects the speaker's story to the shared life of the community, though the exact shape of such an account varies by speaker, occasion, and region.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Stringing sentences together with no connector at all

    Error: Producing several grammatically correct Volume II sentences in a row with no connecting word, so that the account sounds like a list rather than a narrative.

    Correction: Select an appropriate connector — sequencing, result, or contrast — from this chapter's vocabulary table for every join between sentences in a connected account.

    Common Mistake — Attempting to produce a relative clause with tí before Chapter 5

    Error: Trying to build an original relative clause with tí in speaking or writing this chapter, based only on recognizing it in the reading passage.

    Correction: Treat tí-clauses as recognition-only material for now. If you need to describe something further, use two separate connected sentences instead, and wait for full instruction in Chapter 5.

    Listening Practice Questions

    1. Ìyá Kúmọ́lá has lived in the neighborhood for thirty years.
    2. When she first arrived, there were not many houses, and the roads used to get damaged during the rainy season.
    3. The passage uses àmọ́ ("but") to introduce the contrast between the past problem and the later improvement.

    Reading Practice Questions

    1. Accept any two of: the school has grown larger and has more teachers; the market has become a large market with many shops.
    2. What has stayed the same is that neighbors still greet one another warmly every morning, as they did when the writer was young.
    3. The relative clause is "tí mo ti kọ́kọ́ kàwé sí" (that I first learned to read at), describing the school where the writer first attended.

Ẹ̀rọ Ìbánisọ̀rọ̀ àti Ayélujára

Technology and Digital Communication · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Discuss phones, the internet, apps, and online services in connected Yorùbá.
  2. Give sequenced instructions for completing a digital task, using serial verbs already developed in this volume.
  3. Make polite requests for help with a device, an account, or an online service.
  4. Recognize and correctly label common English loanwords used in Yorùbá technology talk, and identify a formal Yorùbá alternative when one exists.
  5. Write digital messages with full tone marks and dot-under letters, while recognizing that everyday texting sometimes reduces diacritics.
  6. Recognize (receptively) how relative clauses, contrastive focus, and a reported-speech complement clause can appear in a customer-service exchange, without being required to produce new instances of them independently.
  7. Write an accurate, polite digital-service message requesting help with a technology problem.

Opening Communication Scenario

Adé's aunt has a new phone and wants to send money to a relative and check her data balance, but the banking app keeps showing an error message. She asks Adé to help her follow the steps in Yorùbá, and later to help her write a short message to the network company's customer-service line.

  1. This chapter develops modern vocabulary, labeled loanwords, sequencing instructions, and polite requests already active in the volume, now applied to phones, apps, and online services.
  2. Relative clauses, contrastive focus, and one reported-speech complement clause appear for recognition only in this chapter's readings and listening passages; you are not expected to produce new instances of them independently yet.
  3. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to explain a simple digital task step by step and write a polite request for technical help.

Pronunciation Focus

Focus — Diacritics in digital spelling and loanword pronunciation

English-derived words such as fóònù, dátà, and ìhun ẹ̀rọ are pronounced with Yorùbá tone and vowel quality, not with English rhythm. Read each loanword slowly first, keeping every tone mark and dot-under letter accurate, before repeating it at ordinary conversational speed.

  • Practice tone on fóònù (high-mid-high) and àkọsílẹ̀ (recycled from Chapter 10), keeping the dot-under ẹ distinct from plain e.
  • Notice that everyday text messages between friends often drop tone marks and dot-under letters entirely; this is a labeled, receptive-only informal practice and must not be copied into formal instructional writing.
  • Read the sample text message below first as it would be formally written with full diacritics, then note how it might appear informally on a phone screen.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops sequencing instructions and polite requests already active in the volume, applies them to technology tasks, and labels common loanwords. No new particle or clause type is introduced for the first time; each pattern below builds on material already scheduled.

Sequencing instructions with serial verbs

Pattern

Kọ́kọ́ + verb-1 ... kí o tó + verb-2 ... lẹ́yìn náà + verb-3

This ordering pattern reuses the motion, transfer, and result serialization developed earlier in the volume to walk someone through app steps in the correct order.

Kọ́kọ́ ṣí ìhun ẹ̀rọ náà, kí o tó tẹ ọ̀rọ̀ ìpamọ́ rẹ, lẹ́yìn náà tẹ orí "Rán Owó."

First open the app, before you enter your password, then tap on "Send Money."

  • Ẹ kọ́kọ́ tẹ àmì ayélujára náà, kí ẹ tó tẹ orukọ àkọsílẹ̀ yín sínú rẹ̀. First tap the internet icon, before you enter your account name into it. respectful plural sequencing + purpose/time subordination (recycled)

Polite requests for help

  • Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, ẹ ràn mí lọ́wọ́ láti tún ọ̀rọ̀ ìpamọ́ mi ṣe. Please, help me reset my password. polite request frame + purpose infinitive (recycled)
  • Ǹjẹ́ ẹ lè ràn mí lọ́wọ́ láti wo ìdí tí ìhun ẹ̀rọ yìí kò fi ṣiṣẹ́? Could you help me find out why this app is not working? polite question request + labeled recognition-only relative-like frame ("ìdí tí")

Labeling loanwords and offering formal alternatives

  • A máa ń pe fóònù ní ẹ̀rọ ìbánisọ̀rọ̀ nínú ìwé àti ọ̀rọ̀ ìṣàkóso. A phone is often called "ẹ̀rọ ìbánisọ̀rọ̀" in writing and official language. habitual labeling statement + formal register note (recycled habitual, Ch. 9)
  • Dátà jẹ́ ọ̀rọ̀ àyọkà láti èdè Gẹ̀ẹ́sì tí a máa ń lò lójoojúmọ́. "Dátà" is a loanword from English that is commonly used every day. loanword-labeling statement

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé ń ràn ìyá àbúrò rẹ̀ lọ́wọ́ láti lo ìhun ẹ̀rọ báńkì lórí fóònù rẹ̀.
Setting: Adé is helping his aunt use a banking app on her phone.

ÌYÁ ÀBÚRÒ Adé, ẹ jọ̀wọ́, ẹ ràn mí lọ́wọ́; owó tí mo fẹ́ rán kò lọ. Adé, please, help me; the money I want to send is not going through.
ADÉ Kò tọ̀ràn. Ẹ kọ́kọ́ ṣí ìhun ẹ̀rọ náà, kí ẹ tó tẹ ọ̀rọ̀ ìpamọ́ yín, lẹ́yìn náà ẹ tẹ orí "Rán Owó." No problem. First open the app, before you enter your password, then tap on "Send Money."
ÌYÁ ÀBÚRÒ Mo ti ṣe bẹ́ẹ̀, ṣùgbọ́n àṣìṣe kan ń hàn lórí fóònù mi. I have done that, but an error is showing on my phone.
ADÉ Ǹjẹ́ ẹ lè ràn mí lọ́wọ́ láti wo ìdí tí ìhun ẹ̀rọ yìí kò fi ṣiṣẹ́? Ó ṣeé ṣe kí dátà yín ti tán. Could you help me find out why this app is not working? It's possible your data has finished.
ÌYÁ ÀBÚRÒ Bẹ́ẹ̀ni, ó ṣeé ṣe kó rí bẹ́ẹ̀. Kí ni a óò ṣe nísinsìnyí? Yes, that could be so. What shall we do now?
ADÉ Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, ẹ kọ ọ̀rọ̀ ìránṣẹ́ kan sí olùrànlọ́wọ́ oníbàárà, kí ẹ sọ pé àṣìṣe ń hàn nígbà tí ẹ bá ń gbìyànjú láti rán owó. Please, write a message to customer service, saying that an error appears when you try to send money.
ÌYÁ ÀBÚRÒ Ẹ ṣé, èmi yóò kọ ọ̀rọ̀ ìránṣẹ́ náà báyìí. Thank you, I will write the message now.

Writing Workshop

Write a short digital-service message of 60–100 words with full tone marks and dot-under letters throughout. Your message must include a polite request for help, at least two sequenced steps, and at least one labeled loanword with its formal alternative where one exists.

Sample message shell — for reference only
Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, ẹ ràn mí lọ́wọ́ pẹ̀lú [ìṣòro náà]. Mo ti kọ́kọ́ [ìgbésẹ̀ kìíní], kí n tó [ìgbésẹ̀ kejì], ṣùgbọ́n [ìṣòro tí ó ṣẹlẹ̀]. Ẹ jọ̀wọ́ ẹ rán mi lọ́wọ́ láti yanjú ọ̀rọ̀ yìí. Please, help me with [the problem]. I first [step one], before I [step two], but [what went wrong]. Please help me resolve this.

  • Choose a technology problem relevant to you (a password, an app error, a slow connection, or a data balance).
  • Open with a polite request for help.
  • Describe at least two steps you already took, using the sequencing pattern kọ́kọ́ ... kí o tó ... lẹ́yìn náà.
  • Name the problem clearly, labeling any loanword you use and giving a formal alternative if one exists.
  • Close politely, then apply the Tone and Orthography Checkpoint before finalizing.

    • Include

    • at least 1 polite request for help
    • at least 2 sequenced steps using recycled serial-verb ordering
    • at least 1 labeled loanword with a formal alternative, where one exists
    • a clear statement of the problem

    Avoid

    • producing new relative-clause, focus, or complement-clause structures beyond recycled forms
    • writing loanwords without labeling them as loanwords

    Tone and Orthography Checkpoint
    • Every vowel that should carry a high or low tone mark has one.
    • Every mid-tone syllable is left correctly unmarked.
    • ẹ, ọ, and ṣ are distinguished from e, o, and s throughout.
    • Loanwords such as fóònù and dátà carry Yorùbá tone marks, not English spelling habits.
    • No unscheduled grammar (new focus patterns, new relative-clause types, new complement clauses) has been produced.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Loanwords, generations, and digital register

    Everyday Yorùbá conversation about technology commonly borrows English terms such as fóònù, dátà, and ìhun ẹ̀rọ, adapting them to Yorùbá tone and pronunciation; this is an ordinary feature of modern usage rather than a sign of incorrect Yorùbá. At the same time, formal writing, official announcements, and some older or more purist speakers may prefer established Yorùbá alternatives such as ẹ̀rọ ìbánisọ̀rọ̀. Text messages between friends and family also frequently drop tone marks and dot-under letters for speed, which is a recognized informal digital convention rather than an acceptable model for formal instructional writing.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Treating loanwords as needing no label or alternative

    Error: Writing a formal message that uses fóònù or dátà without any awareness that these are loanwords, or assuming no formal Yorùbá alternative exists.

    Correction: Recognize fóònù and dátà as everyday loanwords from English, and note that a more formal alternative, such as ẹ̀rọ ìbánisọ̀rọ̀ for phone, exists for official or highly formal writing, even though the loanword remains completely acceptable in everyday speech.

    Common Mistake — Dropping diacritics in formal digital writing

    Error: Writing a formal digital-service message the way one might text a friend, such as *E jowo, e ran mi lowo without tone marks or dot-under letters.

    Correction: Keep full diacritics in a formal message: Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, ẹ ràn mí lọ́wọ́ ("please, help me"). Informal, undiacritized texting between friends is a labeled receptive pattern, not a model for formal instructional or customer-service writing.

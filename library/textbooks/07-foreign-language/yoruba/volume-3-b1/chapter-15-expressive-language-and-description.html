Ọ̀rọ̀ Onífẹ́ẹ̀ràn àti Àpèjúwe

Expressive Language and Description · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe people, places, movement, and emotion in vivid, controlled detail.
  2. Expand your productive use of reduplicated forms beyond gradualness and manner to cover intensity and repeated action.
  3. Use a small, defined set of ideophones productively, within clearly stated register limits, continuing from the recognition-only stage in Chapters 12–14.
  4. Distinguish expressive language appropriate for storytelling and informal description from language appropriate for neutral or formal registers.
  5. Continue recognizing relative clauses and contrastive focus at the level active since Chapter 12, without producing new instances independently.
  6. Write a short, controlled descriptive passage that uses expressive language without overusing it.

Opening Communication Scenario

Adé wants to describe a market day to a cousin who lives far away. Plain description feels flat to him — he remembers the way the rain fell suddenly, the way a vendor's voice rang out, and the way the crowd moved slowly through the narrow path. He wants his description to feel alive, but his teacher has reminded him that expressive language should be used with judgment: a few well-placed vivid words are more effective than piling up unfamiliar ones.

  1. This chapter develops description of people, places, movement, and emotion using expressive vocabulary and expanded reduplication.
  2. A small set of ideophones is activated for controlled productive use, with explicit limits on where and how often they are appropriate.
  3. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to write and speak a short, vivid description that remains clear and appropriately controlled.

Pronunciation Focus

Focus — Expressive stress and ideophone delivery

Expressive description often uses a wider pitch range than plain narration, especially on intensifiers such as gan-an and on ideophones such as gbá! and wó!, which are typically delivered with a short, sharp burst of stress rather than the even rhythm used for ordinary words.

  • Keep tone accurate on both halves of reduplicated intensifiers even when speaking with more expressive energy.
  • Deliver ideophones briskly and briefly; do not stretch them into a long, sung syllable.
  • Practice contrasting a calm descriptive sentence with an ideophone-marked sentence to hear the difference in energy.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter expands reduplication from manner and gradualness (Chapter 14) to intensity and repeated action, and activates a small, closed set of ideophones for controlled productive use. Relative clauses and contrastive focus continue as recognition-only material, unchanged from Chapters 12–14.

Reduplication for intensity

Pattern

Adjective/Adverb + gan-an

The intensifier gan-an follows a descriptive word to sharpen or emphasize it. It recycles tone-marking skills already active and does not introduce new clause structure.

Ọjà náà kún gan-an; ó sì rú gan-an nítorí àwọn ènìyàn tó pọ̀.

The market was very full; it was very noisy because of the large number of people.

Reduplication for repeated or emphatic action

  • Àwọn ọkọ̀ ń kọjá yára-yára ní ojú ọ̀nà náà. The vehicles were passing quickly, one after another, along the road. reduplication (developed) — manner/speed of repeated motion
  • Òjò bẹ̀rẹ̀ sí rọ̀ yára-yára, gbogbo ènìyàn sì sá lọ sí abẹ́ ibòji. The rain began to fall quickly and heavily, and everyone ran to shelter. reduplication (developed) + narrative sequencing (recycled)

A small set of ideophones for controlled production

  • Òjò bẹ̀rẹ̀ sí rọ̀, gbá! ó dé bí ẹni pé wọ́n dà á láti orí ilé. The rain began, and — gbá! — it arrived as if poured down from the roof. ideophone (productive, limited) — sudden onset
  • Àgọ́ oníṣòwò náà wó, ó sì ṣubú sí ilẹ̀. The trader's stall collapsed and fell to the ground. ideophone (productive, limited) — sudden collapse; used sparingly, once per passage
Register limit — Use ideophones sparingly

Only gbá! and wó! are activated for independent production in this chapter, and each should appear at most once or twice in a short passage, at a genuine turning point in the description. Overusing ideophones, or inventing new ones, makes description sound unnatural rather than vivid. Other ideophones, such as kẹ̀rẹ̀kẹ̀rẹ̀ from Chapter 14, remain recognition-only; do not produce them independently yet.

Recognition only in this chapter — Do not produce independently yet

The listening and reading passages below contain a relative clause identifying which vendor's stall collapsed and a contrastive-focus phrase distinguishing the calm marketgoers from the fleeing crowd. These remain receptive, continuing the pattern from Chapters 12–14; you are not assessed on producing new instances of them here.

Ìjíròrò Àpẹẹrẹ

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé ń ṣàpèjúwe ọjà kan tí ó lọ sí ìgbà ẹ̀ẹ̀rùn fún ẹ̀gbọ́n rẹ̀ tí kò lè lọ pẹ̀lú rẹ̀.
Setting: Adé is describing a market he visited during the rainy season to his older sibling, who could not go with him.

ẸGBỌ́N Báwo ni ọjà náà ṣe rí, Adé? What was the market like, Adé?
ADÉ Ọjà náà kún gan-an, àwọn ènìyàn sì ń rìn yára-yára láàrin àwọn ọjà. The market was very full, and people were moving quickly among the stalls.
ẸGBỌ́N Kí ni ó ṣẹlẹ̀ nígbà tí òjò dé? What happened when the rain came?
ADÉ Òjò bẹ̀rẹ̀ sí rọ̀ yára-yára, gbá! ó dé bí ẹni pé wọ́n dà á láti orí ilé. Àgọ́ oníṣòwò kan wó, ó sì ṣubú sí ilẹ̀. The rain began to fall quickly and heavily — gbá! — it arrived as if poured from the roof. One trader's stall collapsed and fell to the ground.
ẸGBỌ́N Ṣé ẹ̀rù bà ọ́? Were you afraid?
ADÉ Kìí ṣe gbogbo ènìyàn ni ẹ̀rù bà; àwọn kan farabalẹ̀ wọ́n sì bẹ̀rẹ̀ sí kó ẹrù wọn jọ díẹ̀-díẹ̀, nígbà tí àwọn mìíràn sì sáré lọ. Not everyone was afraid; some stayed calm and began gathering their goods little by little, while others ran away.

Apá Kíkọ

Writing Workshop

Write a short vivid description (70–110 words) with full tone marks and dot-under letters throughout. Your description must use at least one intensifier, at least one reduplicated form describing manner or speed, no more than two ideophones used at genuine turning points, and must remain clear rather than overloaded with expressive vocabulary.

Sample description shell — for reference only
[Ibi tàbí ìṣẹ̀lẹ̀] náà [ẹ̀kúnrẹ́rẹ́ àpèjúwe] gan-an. Àwọn ènìyàn ń [ìṣe] yára-yára/rọra-rọra. Láìpẹ́, [ìṣẹ̀lẹ̀ òjijì], gbá!/wó! [ohun tí ó ṣẹlẹ̀]. [Bí àwọn ènìyàn ṣe hùwà sí i]. The [place or event] was very [description]. People were [action] quickly/slowly. Suddenly, [sudden event] — gbá!/wó! — [what happened]. [How people reacted].
  • Reread the model dialogue and the reading passage above.
  • Choose a scene to describe: the market, the windstorm, or one of your own.
  • Open with a description that includes an intensifier.
  • Add a reduplicated form describing manner or speed of movement.
  • Mark one or two genuine turning points with an ideophone, not more.
  • Close by describing how people or characters reacted, then apply the Expressive Balance Checkpoint before finalizing.

    • Include

    • at least 1 intensifier (e.g., gan-an)
    • at least 1 reduplicated manner/speed form
    • 1–2 ideophones at genuine turning points
    • a clear reaction or closing description

    Avoid

    • using more than two ideophones in one short passage
    • inventing new ideophones not activated in this chapter
    • producing new relative-clause or contrastive-focus structures beyond recycled forms
    • omitting tone marks or dot-under letters
    Expressive Balance Checkpoint
    • Every vowel that should carry a high or low tone mark has one.
    • ẹ, ọ, and ṣ are distinguished from e, o, and s throughout.
    • Both halves of every reduplicated form carry accurate, matching tone.
    • No more than two ideophones appear, and each marks a genuine turning point.
    • A reader unfamiliar with the scene could still follow what happened and how people reacted.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Vividness and restraint in everyday description

    Expressive resources such as ideophones and reduplication are common features of everyday Yorùbá description and storytelling, adding color and immediacy to an account of events. At the same time, many speakers reserve dense expressive language for informal storytelling contexts and use plainer, more measured description in formal, professional, or written institutional communication. Learners should treat expressive language as one register option among several, appropriate to audience and setting, rather than as a universal marker of fluency.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Overloading a passage with ideophones

    Error: Inserting an ideophone at every event in a description, so that the passage no longer distinguishes genuine turning points from ordinary narration.

    Correction: Reserve gbá! and wó! for the single most sudden or dramatic moment in a passage, and describe other events with ordinary verbs and, where appropriate, a reduplicated manner form, as in "òjò bẹ̀rẹ̀ sí rọ̀ yára-yára" rather than marking every sentence with an exclamation.

    Common Mistake — Confusing intensifiers with reduplicated manner forms

    Error: Using gan-an where a manner reduplication like rọra-rọra or yára-yára is needed, producing a sentence that emphasizes a quality without describing how an action was performed.

    Correction: Use gan-an after an adjective or adverb to intensify a quality ("ó kún gan-an", "it was very full"), and use a manner reduplication before or after a verb to describe how an action happened ("ó ń rìn yára-yára", "he was walking quickly").

    Answer Key

    Listening Practice Questions

    1. The market was very full, and everyone was moving quickly to find the goods they wanted to buy; no one suspected rain.
    2. The rain suddenly began to fall quickly and heavily; the moment is marked with "gbá!" as it arrived without any warning.
    3. The stall belonging to the woman who sold palm oil collapsed; she stayed calm and did not run, and instead began gathering her goods little by little.
    4. The young people nearby ran quickly to shelter, in contrast to her calm reaction.

    Reading Practice Questions

    1. The children thought the wind would stop soon.
    2. The large tree eventually collapsed and fell onto the field; the moment is marked with "gbá!" as a loud sound was heard.
    3. The children immediately ran back home.
    4. The village leader advised everyone not to walk near large trees when the wind is strong, so that nothing bad would happen.

    Guided Practice — Sample Responses

    1. "Gan-an" intensifies "kún" (full) and "rú" (noisy) in the dialogue. 2. The ideophones are "gbá!" (marking the sudden arrival of heavy rain) and, in the reading passage, "wó" is used as a verb rather than a bare ideophone; in the dialogue, "gbá!" marks the sudden rain and the collapsing stall is described with the verb "wó." 3. "Yára-yára" describes moving or falling quickly; "díẹ̀-díẹ̀" describes gathering goods little by little. 4. Using ideophones only at the most dramatic moment keeps the description clear and prevents every sentence from sounding equally dramatic.

    Chapter Review — Apá A

    1. "Gbá!" marks a sudden, forceful event such as heavy rain arriving; "wó!" (or the verb "wó") marks a sudden collapse or falling. 2. "Gan-an" intensifies a quality already named by an adjective or adverb, while "yára-yára" describes the manner or speed of an action itself.

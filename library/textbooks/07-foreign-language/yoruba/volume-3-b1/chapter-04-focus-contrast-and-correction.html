Ìtẹnumọ́, Ìyàtọ̀, àti Àtúnṣe

Focus, Contrast, and Correction · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use ni to identify and emphasize a person, thing, or piece of information.
  2. Form subject-focus sentences that place the focused participant before ni.
  3. Form object-focus sentences that front a focused object ahead of the rest of the clause.
  4. Use strong (emphatic) pronouns in focus constructions rather than the weak subject pronouns used in ordinary clauses.
  5. Politely correct or clarify information using contrastive focus frames.
  6. Recognize, without producing independently, contrastive focus interacting with questions and negation, which later chapters will continue to develop.

Communication Scenario

Adé stops by the post office to confirm that Ìyá Kúnlé's package actually arrived, but the clerk has the details slightly wrong: the wrong sender, the wrong day, the wrong recipient. Adé needs to clarify exactly who did what, correct the clerk's mistaken assumptions, and make sure the right information is on record — without simply repeating whole sentences over again each time something needs fixing.

  1. This chapter introduces ni focus, subject focus, and object focus as productive systems for the first time.
  2. It shows how Yorùbá uses focus constructions and strong pronouns — not stress or word order alone, as English often does — to identify, emphasize, and correct information.
  3. By the end of the chapter, you should be able to conduct a short clarification interview, correcting mistaken details using focus, and to recognize — without yet producing — contrastive focus combined with questions and negation.

Pronunciation and Orthography Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Focused-phrase intonation without flattening tone

Learners often try to signal focus the way English does: by leaning heavily on stress and pitch while letting the actual tone marks slip. In Yorùbá, the focused phrase before ni still keeps its own high, mid, and low tones exactly as written; emphasis comes from the construction itself, not from distorting the tone.

  • Practice saying Adé ni ó rà á ("It was Adé who bought it") with clear, level phrasing, letting ni mark the emphasis rather than raising your voice on Adé.
  • Keep the tone on strong pronouns such as èmi and òun fully accurate; these are easy to flatten because they already feel emphatic in meaning.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops ni focus, subject focus, and object focus productively, building on the receptive/formulaic exposure from Chapter 3. It also introduces strong pronouns as the forms required inside focus constructions.

Developed: `ni` identification and focus

Building block
Ohun tí a ń tẹnumọ́ + ni + Ìyókù gbólóhùn
[Focused element] + ni + [rest of the clause] — the item before ni is singled out as the answer or correction.
Adé ni ó gbé páálí náà lọ.
It was Adé who took the package. (Literally: Adé ni [he] took package-the go.)
  • Ọjọ́ Ajé ni mo dé ilé iṣẹ́ àfìwéránṣẹ́, kì í ṣe ọjọ́ Ìṣẹ́gun. It was Monday that I arrived at the post office, not Tuesday. focused time phrase + ni; correction with kì í ṣe
  • Èmi ni mo mú páálí náà wá, kì í ṣe ẹnì kejì. It was I who brought the package, not the other person. strong pronoun èmi required before ni, not weak mo alone

Developed: Subject focus and object focus

When the subject of a clause is focused, the strong pronoun or noun phrase moves before ni, and the rest of the clause follows with its ordinary weak subject pronoun retained. When the object is focused, the focused object moves to the front of the clause, followed by ni, and the subject and verb follow in their usual order.

  • Ìwọ ni o yẹ kí o wá, kì í ṣe èmi. It's you who should come, not me. subject focus: strong ìwọ before ni, weak o retained in the clause
  • Ẹ̀wà ni mo rà, kì í ṣe ìrẹsì. It was beans that I bought, not rice. object focus: focused object ẹ̀wà fronted before ni

Notice that focus does not change the basic SVO architecture of the rest of the clause. The focused element is added before ni; everything else keeps its normal position and its normal pronoun forms.

Why focus needs a strong pronoun, not just stress

In English, emphasis on a pronoun is usually done with stress alone: "I bought it, not her." In Yorùbá, the weak subject pronouns (such as mo, o, ó) cannot carry focus by themselves; they must be replaced by the corresponding strong pronoun (èmi, ìwọ, òun) whenever that participant is fronted for focus with ni. Using a weak pronoun in a focus position, or trying to add stress to a weak pronoun instead of switching forms, produces an ungrammatical or unnatural sentence.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé ń bá òṣìṣẹ́ ilé iṣẹ́ àfìwéránṣẹ́ sọ̀rọ̀ láti ṣàtúnṣe àwọn kúlẹ̀kúlẹ̀ tí kò tọ̀nà.
Setting: Adé is speaking with a post office clerk to correct some inaccurate details.

ÒṢÌṢẸ́ Ẹnì kejì yín ni ó mú páálí yìí wá, àbí bẹ́ẹ̀ kọ́? It was the other person who brought this package, wasn't it?
ADÉ Rárá o, èmi gan-an ni mo mú páálí náà wá, kì í ṣe ẹnì kejì. No, it was I myself who brought the package, not the other person.
ÒṢÌṢẸ́ Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, ọjọ́ Ìṣẹ́gun ni ẹ mú un wá, àbí? Please, it was Tuesday that you brought it, right?
ADÉ Kàkà bẹ́ẹ̀, ọjọ́ Ajé ni mo dé ibí yìí, kì í ṣe ọjọ́ Ìṣẹ́gun. Rather, it was Monday that I arrived here, not Tuesday.
ÒṢÌṢẸ́ Àṣìṣe ni mo ṣe nínú ìwé mi, ẹ dárí jì mí. It was a mistake that I made in my records, please forgive me.
ADÉ Kò burú rárá. Ǹjẹ́ Ìyá Kúnlé ni ẹ ní gẹ́gẹ́ bí olùgbà, tàbí ẹnì kejì ni? It's not a problem at all. Do you have Ìyá Kúnlé listed as the recipient, or is it someone else?
ÒṢÌṢẸ́ Ìyá Kúnlé gan-an ni à ń ní gẹ́gẹ́ bí olùgbà, kì í ṣe ẹlòmíràn. It is exactly Ìyá Kúnlé that we have listed as the recipient, not someone else.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Correcting others politely

    Correcting a mistake, especially one made by someone in a position of service or authority such as a clerk or official, is often handled among many Yorùbá speakers with softening expressions such as ẹ jọ̀wọ́ ("please") or kàkà bẹ́ẹ̀ ("rather") before stating the correct information directly. Directness paired with courtesy markers is common, though the exact balance of directness and softening varies by relationship, setting, and individual style.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Using a weak pronoun in a focus position

    Error: Producing a sentence such as *mo ni mo rà á, attempting to focus the subject with the weak pronoun mo instead of switching to the strong form.

    Correction: Replace the weak pronoun with the corresponding strong pronoun before ni, as in èmi ni mo rà á ("it was I who bought it"). Weak subject pronouns cannot occupy the focus position directly before ni.

    Common Mistake — Signaling focus with stress instead of `ni`

    Error: Trying to signal emphasis the English way, by stressing a word without restructuring the sentence, such as saying mo rà ẹ̀wà with heavy stress on ẹ̀wà and expecting it to mean "It was beans that I bought."

    Correction: Restructure the sentence with ni, as in ẹ̀wà ni mo rà. Yorùbá signals focus through construction, not through stress alone.

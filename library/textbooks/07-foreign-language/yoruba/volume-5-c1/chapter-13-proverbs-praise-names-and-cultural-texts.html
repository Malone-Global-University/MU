Òwe, Oríkì, àti Àwọn Ọ̀rọ̀ Àṣà

Proverbs, Praise Names, and Cultural Texts · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Advanced Precision, Interpretation, and Specialization

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify the structural features of a Yorùbá proverb (òwe), including its typical two-part balance, figurative comparison, and function in conversation.
  2. Identify the structural features of oríkì (praise poetry/names), including compressed reference, allusion, and repetition, without claiming expert command of the oral-tradition genre.
  3. Interpret a proverb or short oríkì excerpt by stating its literal content, its figurative or contextual meaning, and the kind of situation in which it might be used.
  4. Explain why proverbs and praise texts require cautious, source-aware interpretation rather than confident, definitive readings from a short excerpt.
  5. Use hedge frames from Chapter 12 together with citation and attribution habits to present a proverb or oríkì excerpt responsibly.
  6. Distinguish reciting or quoting a cultural text from interpreting it, and explain why this course treats interpretation, not composition, as the productive target for this genre.
  7. Write a short interpretive commentary on one proverb or cultural text excerpt, integrating register and mediation skills from Chapters 2 and 11.

Opening Communication Scenario

Adé has been invited to a family naming ceremony (ìsọmọlórúkọ) where an elder will deliver oríkì for the newborn's lineage and offer proverbs of blessing and guidance to the parents. Adé is not asked to compose or perform any of this material himself — that role belongs to the elder reciting it, drawing on years of transmitted knowledge. Adé's task, similar to his work at the radio archive in Chapter 12, is to listen respectfully, ask appropriate clarifying questions afterward, and later write a short interpretive note for his own study journal explaining what he understood of one proverb and one praise line, while being honest about the limits of his understanding.

  1. This chapter does not introduce a new tense, aspect, or clause system. It applies the interpretive, hedging, and mediation habits from Chapters 11 and 12 to a new and more culturally sensitive text type.
  2. The productive goal is careful, attributed interpretation of short excerpts — not composing new proverbs or oríkì, and not claiming to perform this material with authority.
  3. Full diacritics and Standard Written Yorùbá remain the productive baseline for Adé's own written commentary, even when quoting material that may include specialized or archaic vocabulary.

Ìpín Kìíní

Pronunciation Focus — Performance Register

Sound and Style — Recognizing ceremonial delivery

Proverbs and oríkì are often delivered in a performance register distinct from ordinary conversation: a more deliberate rhythm, pronounced tone-height contrasts used for emphasis, occasional elongation of tone-bearing syllables at emotionally significant points, and pauses that mark the boundary between one praise line and the next.

Your task in this chapter is recognition, not reproduction. Listen for where a speaker slows down, raises pitch for emphasis, or repeats a name or phrase, and note that these are meaningful performance choices rather than errors or ordinary conversational habits.

You are not expected to perform proverbs or oríkì in this ceremonial register yourself. Your own spoken commentary on these texts should remain in your ordinary advanced conversational register, with accurate tone production on the words you quote.

Ìpín Kejì

Grammar Focus — Proverb, Oríkì, and Ceremonial Structure

This section develops interpretive skill using systems already consolidated: figurative-comparison vocabulary, register description from Chapter 2, and the hedge frames introduced in Chapter 12. No new particle, tense, or clause type is introduced.

1. The typical two-part structure of a proverb

Many Yorùbá proverbs balance two parts: a concrete image or observation, followed by an application or consequence. Recognizing this balance helps you separate the literal content from the figurative point being made.

  • Bí a bá n gun igi, a a fi ẹsẹ̀ méjèèjì gùn ún; bẹ́ẹ̀ ni a kì í fi ẹsẹ̀ kan ṣe ọ̀rọ̀ pàtàkì. If one is climbing a tree, one climbs it with both legs; likewise, one does not handle an important matter with only half effort. structure: image (climbing with both legs) + application (important matters need full commitment)

When you interpret a proverb, state the literal image first, then propose the figurative application, and mark the application as an interpretation rather than a fixed, singular meaning — proverbs are frequently used flexibly across different situations by different speakers.

2. Compressed reference in oríkì

Oríkì often name ancestors, qualities, animals, places, or historical events in compressed form, assuming shared background knowledge in the listening community. A single line may allude to a story the reciter does not fully narrate.

Interpretive frame: naming an allusion without over-claiming
... ń tọ́ka sí ... (láìsí ẹ̀rí kíkún láti sọ ìtàn náà ní kíkún)
This frame lets you name that a phrase points to something — a quality, a lineage marker, an event — while explicitly acknowledging that you cannot fully reconstruct the underlying story from a short excerpt.
Ọ̀rọ̀ "ọmọ ẹkùn tí kò jẹ́ kí igbó dákẹ́" ń tọ́ka sí ìgboyà àti agbára, àmọ́ n kò lè sọ ìtàn pàtó tí ó so mọ́ ìdílé yìí láìsí àlàyé láti ọ̀dọ̀ olùtọ́jú àṣà.
The phrase "child of the leopard who does not let the forest go quiet" points to courage and strength, but I cannot state the specific story tied to this family without an explanation from a tradition-keeper.

3. Extending Chapter 12's hedge frames to cultural texts

The hedge frames you developed for variation analysis — "ó ṣeé ṣe kí...", "bóyá...", "ó dàbí pé..." — apply directly here, since proverb and oríkì interpretation from a short excerpt is also an inference from limited evidence, not a certainty.

  • Ó dàbí pé òwe yìí ń sọ̀rọ̀ nípa sùúrù, àmọ́ ó ṣeé ṣe kí ó tún ní ìtumọ̀ mìíràn nínú àyíká ọ̀rọ̀ tí n kò tíì gbọ́. It seems this proverb is talking about patience, but it's possible it also has another meaning in a context I haven't heard yet. applies the Chapter 12 hedge pattern to a proverb-interpretation claim

4. Attribution and citation habits

When you quote a proverb or oríkì excerpt in your own writing, name where or from whom you encountered it (an elder, a book, a class example, a described source) rather than presenting it as your own composition or as a text you can attribute with certainty to a specific historical origin.

  • Gẹ́gẹ́ bí àgbà kan ṣe pín in fún mi, àwọn Yorùbá máa ń sọ pé: "Ẹní tí kò mọ ibi tí òjò ti bẹ̀rẹ̀ sí rọ̀ ọ́ kò lè mọ ibi tí yóò dá." As an elder shared it with me, Yorùbá speakers often say: "One who does not know where the rain began cannot know where it will stop." attribution: names the source type ("an elder") rather than presenting the proverb as the writer's own invention or as universally and precisely attributable

Model Dialogue — Explaining a Proverb at a Naming Ceremony

Context: After the naming ceremony, Adé asks the officiating elder, Bàbá Adisa, about a proverb used during the blessing.

Adé: Bàbá, ẹ jọ̀wọ́, ǹjẹ́ ẹ lè ṣàlàyé òwe tí ẹ lò nígbà tí ẹ ń súre fún àwọn òbí ọmọ náà?Sir, please, could you explain the proverb you used when you were blessing the child's parents?
Bàbá Adisa: Mo sọ pé: "Ọwọ́ ọmọdé kò tó pẹpẹ, tí àgbàlagbà, tirẹ̀ kò wọ̀ akèrègbè." Ó ń fi hàn pé olúkúlùkù ìran ní iṣẹ́ tirẹ̀ tí ó bá a mu, kò sì yẹ kí a fi ẹnì kan ṣe iṣẹ́ tí kò bá a mu.I said: "A child's hand cannot reach the high shelf, and an elder's hand does not fit into the small gourd." It shows that each generation has work suited to it, and no one should be made to do work unsuited to them.
Bàbá Adisa gives both the literal image (child's hand, elder's hand) and the applied meaning (generational fit of roles) before Adé responds.
Adé: Ẹ ṣé, Bàbá. Ǹjẹ́ ó bọ́gbọ́n mu bí mo bá kọ ọ́ sínú ìwé-ìkẹ́kọ̀ọ́ mi, mo sì sọ pé àgbà kan ni ó pín in fún mi, kí n má ​​sì so ọ́ pọ̀ mọ́ orúkọ ẹnikẹ́ni mìíràn?Thank you, sir. Would it be appropriate if I write it in my study notebook and say that an elder shared it with me, without attaching it to anyone else's name?
Bàbá Adisa: Ó dára bẹ́ẹ̀. Àmọ́ rántí pé ìtumọ̀ mi kìí ṣe èyí tí gbogbo ènìyàn ń lò; àwọn ẹlòmíràn lè lo òwe kan náà fún ọ̀ràn mìíràn pátápátá.That is fine. But remember that my interpretation is not the one everyone uses; others may use this same proverb for an entirely different situation.
Bàbá Adisa explicitly cautions against treating a single interpretation as the only correct one — a caution Adé should carry into his written commentary.

Writing Workshop

Produce a short interpretive commentary in Standard Written Yorùbá on one proverb or short cultural-text excerpt (provided by an instructor, drawn from this chapter, or a plausible invented example clearly labeled as such).

  1. Choose one proverb or short oríkì excerpt: one from this chapter, an alternative approved by your instructor, or a clearly labeled invented example for practice purposes.
  2. State the literal meaning of the excerpt in one or two sentences.
  3. Propose a figurative or contextual application, hedged using at least one frame from this chapter or Chapter 12.
  4. Write your commentary (90–130 words) in Standard Written Yorùbá, including an attribution of the source (or a clear label if invented) and an explicit note that other readings may exist.
  5. Proofread the note using the Chapter 3 four-pass procedure before submission.
  • The commentary is fully tone-marked with correct dot-under letters throughout.
  • The literal meaning and the figurative meaning are clearly distinguished.
  • At least one hedge frame is used appropriately for the figurative reading.
  • The source is attributed or clearly labeled as an invented practice example.
  • No sentence claims the interpretation as the single, definitive meaning of the text.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Proverbs and oríkì as living, specialized knowledge

Proverbs are used widely in everyday Yorùbá speech by people of many backgrounds, while oríkì and related ceremonial forms are often associated with specialized knowledge held by particular elders, families, or culture-bearers. Approaching both genres with curiosity and humility, rather than assuming a short textbook excerpt gives complete mastery, reflects how these forms are actually transmitted and valued within Yorùbá-speaking communities.

Regional Note — Variation within this genre too

Proverbs and praise forms vary by family, town, and region, consistent with the variation categories introduced in Chapter 12. A proverb's wording, and the specific application given to it, may differ from one community or speaker to another. Treat any single interpretation offered in this chapter as one plausible reading among others, not as a fixed, universal key.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Presenting one interpretation as the only correct meaning

Error: Writing that a proverb "means" one fixed thing, without acknowledging that other applications or readings are possible.

Correction: Use hedge frames from this chapter and Chapter 12 to present your reading as one plausible interpretation, as modeled in the Grammar Focus and dialogue.

Common Mistake — Composing new oríkì or claiming ceremonial authority

Error: Writing an original oríkì-style text for a real person or family, or presenting your own commentary as equivalent to a culture-bearer's authoritative recitation.

Correction: Keep your productive task limited to interpretation and attributed commentary on existing excerpts; this volume does not authorize learners to compose or perform ceremonial praise texts with cultural authority.

Chapter Summary

This chapter introduced no new tense, aspect, particle, or clause system. It extended the hedged, respectful description habits built in Chapter 12 to a more culturally sensitive text type: proverbs and praise poetry. Learners practiced identifying the literal and figurative layers of a proverb, recognizing compressed allusion in oríkì, applying hedge frames to interpretive claims, and attributing quoted material responsibly. The chapter's core productive task was a written interpretive commentary distinguishing literal meaning, hedged figurative reading, and acknowledged uncertainty, without composing new ceremonial texts or claiming cultural authority over the genre.
